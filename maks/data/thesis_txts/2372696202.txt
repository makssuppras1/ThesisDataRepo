ABSTRACT 
The relation between indoor environmental quality (IEQ) and energy use in residential buildings has 
been investigated in this thesis. This has been done by comparing recommendations from 
international standards for the indoor environment. The thesis consists of a comparison of 
international indoor environmental standards as well as a parametric study on the relation between 
indoor environmental parameters and energy use in residential buildings, using variations matching 
the findings in the comparison of standards. 
The results obtained in this thesis will be applicable for property developers, designers, operators 
and building occupants to help decide on IEQ level in buildings based on an informed basis. The 
results can also work as input for further research into cost/benefit of IEQ levels in residential 
buildings. 
The indoor environmental standards compared were ISO 7730, the new standard ISO 17772, EN 
15251 and the superseding standard prEN 16798, ASHRAE Standard 55, ASHRAE Standard 62-1 and 
ASHRAE Standard 62-2, GB/T 50785, ISH 10001 and AS 1668.2. The comparison of the standards 
shows that the standards give different recommendations for the indoor environmental parameters, 
even when aiming for the same comfort level.   
The parametric study on how the energy use is impacted by the indoor environmental quality 
showed that higher quality of the indoor environment generally leads to higher energy use. The 
indoor environmental quality did however not affect the energy use as much as improving the 
building envelope and building installations, or as the climate where the building was placed.  
The factor related to the indoor environmental quality that impacted the energy use the most was 
whether mechanical cooling was used.  Buildings without use of mechanical cooling consumed, on 
average for the tested climates, 48 % of the energy use compared to buildings using mechanical 
cooling. The simulations showed that the thermal comfort was not deteriorated in buildings without 
mechanical cooling, due to an adaptive effect to the outdoor temperature, but that the relative 
humidity indoors was increased for some climates. 
Changing the factors window size, ventilation rate and temperature range at the same time to meet 
different standards and Category of standards impacted the energy use more than changing one of 
the parameters alone. Following ISO 17772 Category 1 for all these parameters lead to the highest 
energy use (22 % higher than ISO 17772 Category 2). Using setpoints as recommended by the 
ASHRAE standards standard lead to 14 % higher energy use than following recommendations from 
ISO 17772 Category 2, even though they aim for the same comfort level. This is probably due to a 
requirement of high demand controlled exhaust rates in the kitchen in ASHRAE Standard 62-2.  
Of the three parameters changed to meet the different indoor environmental standards and 
categories were window size the single parameter that affected the total energy use the most. Using 
windows corresponding to a daylight factor of 6 % resulted in 16 % higher total energy use than 
window sizes corresponding to a daylight factor of 4 %. Large windows should therefore not be used 
uncritically.  
Using different temperature ranges is one of the environmental parameters tested in this thesis that 
affect the energy use the least. Using a temperature range of 21-25.5 °C lead to, on average, 5 % 
higher energy use than a temperature range of 20-26 °C.

1 INTRODUCTION 
 The indoor environment in buildings affects the wellbeing and health of occupants, as well as the 
durability of the building. Different standards have therefore been published giving 
recommendations for the indoor environmental quality (IEQ).  
At the same time is the building sector one of the largest end-use energy sectors worldwide, with 
approximately 30 %  of the global final energy use (Lucon et al. 2014; Ürge-Vorsatz & et al. 2012), 
where upholding a comfortable indoor environment is one of the main aspects influencing the 
energy use in buildings. Space heating represented 32-34 % of the global final energy use in both the 
residential and commercial building sub-sectors in 2010 (Lucon et al. 2014). 
Even though a lot of research has been done on both energy use in buildings and indoor 
environmental quality, little research has been done on how the indoor environmental quality effects 
energy use, and how the energy use is affected by the interaction of different IEQ parameters. 
Research investigating the relationship between energy use and indoor environmental quality often 
revolve around green building classification systems.  
This thesis will examine how different IEQ standards’ recommendations for indoor environmental 
quality parameters affect the energy use of buildings, by a parametric study. The focus of the 
parametric study will be on residential buildings, as residential buildings accounted for 24 % of the 
total global final energy use in 2010 (Lucon et al. 2014) as well as the fact that more than half the of 
the time spent indoors takes place in homes (Frontczak et al. 2012).  
The parametric study is based on recommendations given in different IEQ standards. A comparison 
of IEQ standards has therefore been conducted, and can be found in chapter 5. An introduction to 
different indoor environmental parameters and a review of how the IEQ parameters affect the 
occupant comfort, health and productivity is given in chapter 2, and results from existing studies of 
the effect of the IEQ level on energy use in buildings is presented in chapter 3. This is important 
factors to be aware of when deciding which IEQ level to use in buildings. 
The IEQ in buildings and resulting energy use is a complex system, and is affected by many aspects. 
Some of these aspects are the building type, building envelope and occupant behavior. This thesis 
will give a brief introduction on these aspects in chapter 4.  
Chapter 6 presents the method for the parametric study, chapter 7 presents results from the 
parametric study, while chapter 8 presents a discussion on the results found and the choices done in 
the thesis. Chapter 9 gives an overview of the implications of the study as well as recommendations 
for further studies to be done on the topic. Chapter 10 presents the conclusion of the thesis.  
Because of the complexity of influencers on IEQ parameters and energy use in buildings, all factors 
have not been considered in this thesis. The parametric study has been conducted with variations in 
the IEQ parameters temperature ranges, ventilation rates, illuminance level and daylight factors. 
Other factors varied includes climate, building envelope and the use of mechanical cooling. More 
research taking more of the parameters affecting IEQ and energy use in buildings is therefore 
recommended.

2 INDOOR ENVIRONMENTAL QUALITY 
The indoor environmental quality is the combined condition of different indoor environmental 
quality parameters in a building. Indoor environmental quality parameters are often grouped into 
four major areas: 
 
Thermal comfort 
 
Indoor air quality  
 
Visual comfort 
 
Acoustic comfort 
These parameters are influenced by factors that can be subdivided into four categories, external 
conditions, building characteristics, building services and people and activities (Almeida et al. 2015). 
External conditions can include factors such as climate, outdoor air quality and external sources of 
shading, while building characteristics are made up of e.g. the building envelope, size of windows and 
thermal mass. The people and activities that influence the indoor environmental quality consists of, 
among other factors, occupancy schedule, activity level and use of appliances. The building 
installations factors include installed effect of building installation and efficiency of installed building 
installations. 
2.1 Indoor environmental parameters 
As described above is the indoor environmental quality consists of four major indoor environmental 
parameter areas. These areas affect each other and are affected by the different influencing factors.  
The following sub sections will give a description on how some of the different indoor environmental 
parameters are affected.  
2.1.1 Thermal comfort  
The thermal comfort is one of the indoor environmental parameters that have gained most 
attention. 
A model for determining the overall thermal comfort in a building was developed by Fanger in the 
1970s (Fanger 1970). This is the model often used in IEQ standards. This model takes the air 
temperature, mean radiant temperature, air velocity and water vapor pressure into account, as well 
as the occupant’s activity level and clothing insulation.  
Some factors influencing the thermal environment is building envelope, ventilation rates, installed 
effect of building installations and their set points, possibilities to open windows and heat gains from 
occupants and equipment in the building. 
Though popular, the use of Fanger’s model in standards has been criticized for not taking people’s 
ability to adapt to their environment into account and to favorize highly controlled environments 
(Nicol & Humphreys 2009).  
This has led to the inclusion of an adaptive approach in some standards, based on Brager and de 
Dear’s adaptive comfort theory (de Dear, Richard J., Brager 1998), where the occupant’s possibility to 
affect the environment in different ways allows for the indoor temperature to increase when the 
outdoor temperatures increases without reducing the comfort of the occupants. 
The optimal temperatures can also vary with parameters such as gender, time of day and type of 
building (residential/offices) and climatization of the building (naturally ventilated/air 
conditioned)(Karyono 2000; Choi et al. 2012; Frontczak & Wargocki 2011).

2.1.2 Indoor air quality 
The indoor air quality (IAQ) is affected by, among other factors, the ventilation rate, emissions from 
building components, occupants and processes in a room and the outdoor air quality. The air 
temperature and humidity level also affects occupant perception of the indoor air quality (Toftum et 
al. 2002). 
The ventilation rate, either provided mechanically or naturally, is a common way of quantifying the 
indoor air quality. A common way of controlling the ventilation rate, especially in residential 
buildings, is by opening or closing windows. 
The indoor air quality in buildings with mainly emissions due to occupancy is often quantified by the 
CO2 concentration in the room. It is still important to remember that the CO2 concentration does not 
provide information on indoor air quality due to other polluting sources.  
2.1.3 Humidity 
According to EN 15251, most of adverse health effects and building disorders (condensation, mould) 
are related to humidity (CEN/TC 156 2007).  
The humidity level in the air is often measured in relative humidity, which is the relation between 
water vapor in the air compared to the saturation water vapor at the same temperature. The 
humidity in the air can also be measured in absolute humidity, expressed as g/m3.  
Condensation occurs when the surface temperature is under or at the dew point temperature of the 
air. High relative humidity and/or cold surfaces is therefore risk factors of condensation. Mould can 
occur at different humidity levels, but the higher the relative humidity of the air, the higher is the 
chance for mould-growth.  
The indoor humidity level can also affect the thermal sensation and the perceived indoor air quality 
in buildings (Toftum et al. 2002), while the indoor humidity level is affected by the outdoor air 
humidity level, the temperature and the ventilation rate of the building, as well as processes indoors.  
2.1.4 Lighting 
The experience of the illuminance level in buildings is categorized under visual comfort. Illuminance 
in buildings might be provided by electric light or by daylight. Both light sources may influence the 
indoor temperature, as electric light will emit some of the consumed energy as heat, and some heat 
will radiate through windows. Windows might also cause extra heat-loss during the heating period, 
as the heat transmission through windows is often higher than through exterior walls.  
The necessary illuminance level is different for different type of tasks. Choi, Loftness and Aziz (Choi et 
al. 2012) recommends an upper limit for light on a computer screen to be 300-350 lux. More light will 
however be recommended for paper based tasks. Generally, more detailed work requires higher 
illuminance levels.  
The illuminance level in residential buildings is dependent on the occupants’ requirements and 
habits. According to a survey of occupant behavior and control of indoor environment in Danish 
dwellings (Andersen et al. 2009), the occupants average age, thermal sensation and gender can 
influence the use of lighting in dwellings.   
Daylight 
The use of daylight in buildings is recommended both to save energy used for electric lighting, and 
because daylight seems to have an important effect on humans (Leslie 2003). The daylight in 
buildings is affected by the window sizes, placing of windows and light transmittance through 
==PAGE:17==
4 
 
windows. The use of shading will also affect the daylight level in the building. The daylight level in the 
building is often measured in daylight factor, which is the ratio of daylight illumination indoors 
compared to the daylight illumination outdoors. The daylight factor should be calculated under 
overcast sky conditions.  
2.1.5 Acoustic comfort 
The acoustic comfort in indoor environments is affected by indoor and outdoor sound sources, as 
well as sound insulation and -absorption in the building. Softer surfaces usually have higher degree of 
sound absorption than harder surfaces.   
Sound sources in a building includes the building installations, where mechanical ventilation systems 
are one of the most important sources.  Keeping the noise from the building installations at an 
acceptable level is important, as users often shut off or alter ventilation devices because of 
discomfort due to noise(ISO/TC 163 2016a; CEN/TC 156 2016b).  
2.1.6 IEQ parameters affecting each other 
As described in the previous sections, most of the IEQ parameters affect each other in some ways. 
Some measures to improve one parameter might therefore affect other parameters in a more 
negative way. One example is that hard ceilings and floors, which can improve the indoor air quality, 
can also increase the noise in a room (Sakhare & Ralegaonkar 2014). Another example is that while 
increased ventilation rates was found to reduce the energy use in Brisbane, due to free cooling, the 
increased ventilation rate also lead to an increased number of particles indoor – affecting the indoor 
air quality (Ngoc et al. 2014). 
2.2 Effect of IEQ parameters on comfort, health and productivity 
The focus on the effect of IEQ parameters on occupants generally concern the comfort, health and 
productivity of occupants. While the comfort has traditionally been the main concern on research 
into indoor environmental quality, the focus has shifted towards how the environmental quality 
affects both health and productivity.  
2.2.1 Comfort 
Multiple studies have been conducted to try to pinpoint what IEQ parameters are the most 
important when it comes occupant comfort, and to try to make an IEQ index model, with different 
outcomes. A questionnaire study conducted in Danish homes (Frontczak et al. 2012) found that air 
quality, visual, acoustic and thermal conditions were considered to have the same magnitude of 
relevance to overall comfort, while a literature study (Frontczak & Wargocki 2011) found that 
thermal conditions were the most important factor contributing to a satisfying indoor environment.  
Regression analysis conducted by Kim and de Dear (Kim & de Dear 2012) has found a different 
relation between IEQ parameters and work place satisfaction than the two other cited studies. The 
study by Kim and de Dear has also shown that dissatisfaction with an IEQ parameter can have a 
different impact on the overall sensation of the IEQ than satisfaction with the same parameter. If for 
instance the sensation of the thermal environment is comfortable will this not have as big impact on

control explained an equal amount of variations in thermal comfort as the thermal sensation and 
operative temperature. The same study found a 1.5 °C difference in neutral temperature between 
occupants with high and low degree of control over windows.

respondents answered that they accept both manual and automatic control.

2.2.2 Health 
Other than comfort, how the IEQ relates to health has gotten the most attention in residential 
buildings. The main focus area has been how respiratory symptoms is related to mold or dampness.  
Fisk (Fisk 2000) states that the evidence of a linkage between indoor environmental quality and the 
incidence of allergic and asthma symptoms is relatively strong. Building related risk factors, such as 
moisture problems and mold or environmental tobacco smoke, are associated with 20 % to 100 % 
increases in allergy and asthma symptoms. 
A large study on the relationship between poor indoor air quality and asthma (Bornehag et al. 2004) 
show that low ventilation rates increase the risk of allergic symptoms significantly and that the 
presence of phthalates emitted from polyvinyl chloride, including plasticizers in children’s rooms, 
increases the risk of asthma. An association has also been found between living or working in 
buildings with high humidity and health effects, such as cough, wheeze, allergies and asthma (Olesen 
2005). 
Availability of daylight indoors have also been linked to reduced disruption to occupants circadian 
rhythms (Figueiro 2013), and reduced discomfort due to more attractive window view is found to be 
linked to improved sleep quality (Aries et al. 2010). 
2.2.3 Productivity 
The IEQ parameters have been shown to not only affect the health of occupants, but also the 
productivity. The increase of productivity related to IEQ is mainly examined for offices and schools, 
where the main IEQ parameters studied are temperature, light and ventilation rates. 
A literature review by Fisk (Fisk 2000) conclude that the estimated potential savings and productivity 
gains for the US are $20 to $160 billion from direct improvements in worker performance that are 
unrelated to health.  
According to a REHVA guidebook on indoor environment and productivity in office environment 
(Wargocki et al. 2006), the combined effect of productivity increase due to ventilation rates and due 
to temperature is at least the effect of the parameter with the biggest effect, but not more than the 
sum of the effect of the independent parameters.  
Temperature 
Fisk (Fisk 2000) states that many studies have found that changes in temperature of a few degrees 
Celsius within the 18°C to 30°C range can significantly influence performance in tasks like reading 
speed and comprehension, word memory and typewriting, but that some studies have not found 
such associations. The literature review by Fisk cites studies suggesting that individual control of the 
temperature could increase productivity and increase performance.  
Roelofsen (Roelofsen 2002) has done a study on the payback time of improving the thermal 
environmental quality, based on a standard predictive index on human response to the thermal 
environment (Gagge et al. 1986). The study is based on IEQ categories from the Dutch code of 
practice NPR-CR 1752, which sets PPD and PMV requirements equal to ISO 7730 (as will be described 
later in the thesis), and finds that an improvement of the thermal environment from Category B to 
Category A has a payback time of 1.8 years, while improving the thermal environment from Category 
C to Category B has a payback time of 0.8 years.  
Indoor air quality 
A field-intervention experiment in single-occupancy student dormitory rooms (Strøm-Tejsen et al. 
2016) has shown that indoor air quality in bedrooms affect the sleep quality, which affected the next 
==PAGE:20==
7 
 
day sleepiness and ability to concentrate as well as subject’s performance of a test of logical thinking. 
The productivity increased with the IEQ with an effect of about 3 %. 
Olesen (Olesen 2005) states that for every 10 % decrease in PPD due to indoor air quality (in a PPD-
range of 16–68 %), the productivity can be expected to increase 1 %.  
Lighting 
According to Fisk (Fisk 2000), studies linking light to performance were primarily linked to unusually 
low light levels, or reading material with small, poor quality or low-contrast type. It is therefore no 
data to say that increased illuminance within normal levels leads to higher productivity. The 
availability of daylight has on the other hand been shown to improve occupants productivity (Veitch 
et al. 2008).  
2.2.4 Implications for dwellings 
Ventilation rates, relative humidity and daylight is the most important IEQ factors when it comes to 
effect on occupants in dwellings. These factors influence both health as well as next-day productivity 
as a result of increased sleep quality.

3 EFFECT OF IEQ LEVEL ON ENERGY USE IN BUILDINGS 
As described in the introduction, buildings account for approximately 30 % of the total global final 
energy use, making it one of the largest end-use sectors worldwide (Lucon et al. 2014; Ürge-Vorsatz 
& et al. 2012). A substantial part of this energy use is used to keep the indoor environment inside an 
acceptable range.  
A simulation study on how occupant behavior affects the building energy use (Fabi et al. 2013) found 
that the required IEQ level of the building had a high effect on the building energy use. The IEQ 
categories presented in EN 15251 (CEN/TC 156 2007) (a standard further described later in the 
thesis) was simulated, showing that going from the stricter IEQ Category level 1 to the lowest IEQ 
Category level 3 lead to a reduction of energy use of 77 % for space heating and 27 % for AHU 
primary energy.  
The fact that the indoor environmental quality affects the energy use should be considered when 
making regulations for the energy use of buildings, so that buildings where it has been aimed at 
keeping a better indoor environmental quality will not suffer from this choice.  
The European Energy Performance of Building Directive (EPBD) (European Parliament & Council of 
the European Union 2010) takes this into consideration by requiring that the calculation of energy 
performance of buildings, and measures to improve the building energy performance, should take 
the indoor environment into consideration. The IEQ standard EN 15251 (CEN/TC 156 2007), which is 
one of the standards reviewed later in the thesis, is prepared to support essential requirements in 
the EPBD. EN 15251 will be superseded by EN 16798(CEN/TC 156 2016a), which is also reviewed later 
in this thesis. 
The following sections will describe how the different indoor environmental parameters affect the 
energy use of buildings. 
3.1 Temperature and energy use 
Space heating accounts for 32-34 % of the global final energy use in buildings1, while cooling 
accounts for 2 % of the global final energy use in residential buildings and 7 % in commercial 
buildings, according to Working Group III to the Fifth Assessment Report of the Intergovernmental 
Panel on Climate Change (Lucon et al. 2014). The energy use related to space heating and cooling is 
expected to grow by 79 % in residential buildings, and 84 % in office buildings, in a business-as-usual 
scenario. 
The Working Group (Lucon et al. 2014) does at the same time predict that the energy use for heating 
can be reduced with 10-30 % by lowering the thermostat setting by 2 °C during the heating season. A 
simulation study performed by Manu et al. on Indian climates (Manu 2011) found that an increase in 
thermostat cooling set point from 24 °C to 27 °C can give an annual energy saving of 5-6% per Kelvin, 
and a later Indian field study (Manu et al. 2016) found that the Fanger comfort model consistently 
over predicted the Indian occupants thermal sensation – and that the occupants in naturally 
ventilated Indian offices were more adaptive than the prevailing ASHRAE and EU models suggest. 
This indicates that it would not be affecting the thermal comfort of occupants to increase thermostat 
cooling set point – or to not use cooling. An Indonesian study also found that the set point 
temperature for cooling in air conditioned buildings could be increased without higher discomfort, 
while using less energy (Karyono 2000).

3.2 Ventilation rates and energy use 
According to (Lucon et al. 2014), the use of fans comprised of 3.6 % of the residential energy use in 
OECD-countries in 2010. In Norwegian office buildings, ventilation systems used 10 % of the building 
energy use in 2016 (Langseth 2016). The energy use for ventilation systems is not only dependent on 
the ventilation rate, but also on the use of heat recovery, energy efficient fans and design of the 
system. (Leal & Meldonado 2000) found that use of “best system” ventilation lead to 60 % lower 
energy use than “common system” ventilation.  
3.2.1 Noise in ventilation systems 
To decrease the noise from ventilation systems, silencers are often used. These increase the pressure 
drop in the ventilation system, leading to an increase in the energy use used for the ventilation 
system (Brandstätt & Leistner 2014). This parameter is however not investigated in the parametric 
study in this thesis, as the effect on energy use is not as straight forward as other parameters. The 
level of noise can however have an effect, and would therefore be interesting to investigate in 
further studies. 
3.3 Energy use for electric lighting 
According to Working Group III to the Fifth Assessment Report of the Intergovernmental Panel on 
Climate Change (Lucon et al. 2014), lighting accounts for 2 % of the world building final energy use in 
residential buildings, and 7 % of the world building final energy use in commercial buildings2.  
The use of daylight and optimization of the shading system and of the lighting system through 
daylight responsive control systems can reduce the electricity use used for lighting (Pellegrino et al. 
2017).

4 OTHER FACTORS INFLUENCING ENERGY USE IN BUILDINGS 
Although countries have set requirements for the energy use in buildings, the actual energy use of 
the building will often be higher than the requirements, as the actual energy use of buildings often is 
higher than predicted (Sakhare & Ralegaonkar 2014). (Menezes et al. 2012) found that the 
discrepancy between predicted and actual energy use in non-domestic buildings often were related 
to the use of unrealistic input parameters regarding occupancy behavior and facilities management 
in building energy models. 
4.1 Occupant behavior and energy use in dwellings 
As stated in chapter 2.2, the occupants in buildings prefer the possibility to control their own indoor 
environment. At the same time does a high degree of occupant control of the indoor environment 
open for a big variation in energy use in buildings. 
Especially the operation of residential buildings is hard to predict. For instance, a study by Bahaj and 
James (Bahaj & James 2007) on nine identical low energy social housing units found that the 
electricity use of the highest energy consuming house was three times that of the lowest consuming 
house. According to (Fabi et al. 2013) the total energy use in dwellings can be reduced by 10-30 % by 
changing occupant behavior alone. 
A study on the behavioral patterns effect on energy use (Corgnati et al. 2014) showed that the 
degree which occupants manipulate the thermostat and opens and closes the windows affects the 
energy use in the building.  
A study on occupant behavior and control of indoor environment (Andersen et al. 2009) showed that 
the indoor environmental settings in dwellings, and the chosen set points, is in part affected  by the 
gender and ownership status of the occupants. 
One simulation study on how occupant behavior affects the building energy use (Fabi et al. 2013) 
showed that using a probabilistic occupant behavioral model, based on measurements in 13 
dwellings, lead to a higher calculated energy use than using the traditional deterministic occupant 
behavioral model.  
The occupant behavior in the building is therefore a key aspect to determine before doing 
simulations on energy use in buildings. ISO 17772 (ISO/TC 163 2016b) and prEN 16798 (CEN/TC 156 
2016a), two standards under development that is further described later in the thesis, presents 
standardized schedules for occupants, lighting and appliances for different building types to be used 
for energy calculations.  
The occupancy schedule given in ISO 17772 and EN 16798 were deemed to be exact enough for the 
parametric study presented later in the thesis, although further investigations into how the occupant 
behavior of the building affect the impact the indoor environmental parameters have on the energy 
use could be interesting.

4.2 Building envelope and building installations 
The building envelope and efficiency of building installations is another important factor that 
influences the energy use and indoor environmental quality in buildings. National building 
regulations often require minimum standards of the building envelope and building installations, as 
well as setting energy use criteria. A study measuring the policy steps aiming at reducing energy use 
(Leth-Petersen & Togeby 2001) found that building regulations have been important in reducing 
energy use in new buildings. The standard of the building envelope and building installations has 
generally increased over time, and newer buildings will therefore often have lower energy use than 
older buildings.

5 COMPARISON OF STANDARDS 
Indoor environmental quality standards provide guidelines and suggest requirements to ensure a 
given degree of indoor environmental quality. Upholding the requirements in a standard does not 
mean that the occupants will automatically be satisfied with the indoor environment, but do give 
guidelines to how the indoor environment can be designed to meet a given quality of satisfactory 
environment. 
Standards are not legislative, and is therefore usually voluntary to use. Standards can however often 
be referred to by legislations, making them mandatory.  
There are several different indoor environmental quality standards throughout the world, published 
by different standard organizations. The comparison of IEQ standards was done for standards 
relevant for both residential- and non-residential buildings.  
5.1 Standard organizations 
In this report, a focus has been set on the indoor environmental quality standards provided by ISO 
(international standards), CEN (European standards) and ASHRAE (American standard). Standards by 
ISHRAE (Indian standard), Chinese national standard (GB/T) and Standard Australia (AS) have also 
been reviewed.  
5.1.1 International Organization of Standardization – ISO 
ISO is an independent, non-governmental international organization. It consists of 161 national 
standards bodies, including most of Europe, America, Asia and Oceania. A big part of the African 
countries are also members.  
ISO develops – through use of experts from the member countries – voluntary, consensus-based 
international standards(International Organization for Standardization n.d.).  
The relevant ISO standards reviewed in this thesis are ISO 7730 (ISO/TC 159 2005) and a draft of a 
new standard, ISO 17772-1 and ISO 17772-2 (ISO/TC 163 2016b; ISO/TC 163 2016a) 
5.1.2 European Standard – EN 
European standard is the collective name of documents ratified by one of the three European 
Standardization Organizations. These organizations are CEN, CENELEC and ETSI.  
The European Standard automatically becomes a national standard in each of the 34 CEN-CENELEC 
member countries, and requires the withdrawal of any conflicting national standard. The standards 
are voluntary, but laws and regulations may refer to them(CEN/CENELEC n.d.).   
The relevant European standards reviewed in this thesis are EN 15251(CEN/TC 156 2007), and a draft 
of the standards EN 16798-1 and EN 16798-2 (CEN/TC 156 2016a; CEN/TC 156 2016b) that are under 
development.  
5.1.3 ASHRAE 
ASHRAE, originally the American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers, is 
now a global society working on advancing human wellbeing through sustainable technology for the 
built environment. In 2012, the society started using only ASHRAE instead of the original full name 
(ASHRAE n.d.).  
ASHRAE is accredited by the American National Standards Institute (ANSI), and develops standards 
after ANSI’s requirements for due process and standard development(ASHRAE n.d.).

The relevant standards reviewed in this thesis are standard 55, 62-1 and 62-2(ASHRAE Standing 
Standard Project Committee 55 2013; ASHRAE Standing Standard Project Committee 62-1 2016; 
ASHRAE Standing Standard Project Committee 62-2 2016).   
5.1.4 Australian Standard – AS 
Standards Australia is a non-governmental not-for-profit standard organization established in 
1922(Standards Australia n.d.). The standard reviewed in this paper is AS 1668.2 – The use of 
ventilation and air conditioning in buildings(Committee ME-062 2002). Other relevant standards 
might have been AS 1680 – Interior and workplace lighting and AS 2107 – acoustics – recommended 
design sound levels and reverberation times for building interiors, these were however not available. 
An Australian standard for thermal comfort does not exist, but it is referred to ISO 7730.

recommended, where the prefix GB are used for mandatory standards and GB/T are used for

Administration of Quality Supervision, Inspection and Quarantine (AQSIQ) and headed primarily by 
the Standardization Administration of the People’s Republic of China (SAC).

GB/T 50785 has official English translation, while other standards are available only in Chinese

5.2.1 Thermal environmental standards 
ISO 7730 – Ergonomics of the thermal environment – analytical determination and interpretation of 
thermal comfort using calculation of the PMV and PPD indices and local thermal comfort criteria 
ISO 7730(ISO/TC 159 2005) presents methods for predicting the general thermal sensation and 
degree of discomfort of people exposed to moderate thermal environments, based on indoor 
thermal environmental factors (i.e. air temperature, humidity, air velocity) and personal factors 
(clothing level and metabolic rate). The standard presents three categories (A, B, C) for the thermal 
environment. The standard was developed in parallel with the revised ASHRAE standard 55. 
The version considered in this thesis is the third edition, published in 2005. 
ASHRAE Standard 55 – Thermal Environmental Conditions for Human Occupancy 
ASHRAE standard 55 (ASHRAE Standing Standard Project Committee 55 2013) specifies combinations 
of indoor thermal environmental factors (i.e. air temperature, humidity, air velocity) and personal 
factors (clothing level and metabolic rate) that will result in a given thermal environmental quality. 
The standard does not present indoor environmental categories, but defines acceptable thermal 
environment as a thermal environment that a substantial majority (more than 80 %) of the occupants 
find thermally acceptable.  
The version reviewed in this thesis is published in 2013. 
GB/T 50785 – evaluation standard for indoor thermal environment in civil buildings 
GB/T 50785 (Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development of the People’s Republic of China 
2012) presents ways to build sustainable and healthy indoor thermal environments and standardizes 
the evaluation of indoor thermal environment in civil buildings. The standard is in large based on 
ASHRAE Standard 55. 
==PAGE:29==
16 
 
The standard is applicable for evaluation of indoor thermal environments in residential and public 
buildings, including offices, malls, hotels and educational buildings. The standard applies to healthy 
adults, meaning people over 18 years and with physiological and mental health and with good social 
adaption. The standard specifies that “sun rooms and caves” are not in the scope of the standard, 
without giving further definition of what this is. 
GB/T 50785 presents recommended thermal environment for three thermal environmental classes -  
Category 1, Category 2 and Category 3, where Category 3 is everything that falls outside the two 
stricter categories.  
GB/T 50785 presents, in addition to recommendations about temperature and other thermal 
environmental parameters, requirements for measuring the thermal environment (instruments, 
parameters, conditions, time and position). Example of thermal environment survey, table of 
metabolic rates and clothing insulation is found in the appendixes. 
The version reviewed in this thesis is published in 2012. 
5.2.2 Indoor air quality standards 
ASHRAE Standard 62-1 – Ventilation for Acceptable Indoor Air Quality 
ASHRAE Standard 62-1 (ASHRAE Standing Standard Project Committee 62-1 2016) presents ways of 
ensuring a given indoor air quality that minimizes adverse health effect. The scope of the standard 
does not include residential occupancies, which is covered by Standard 62-2. The standard does not 
present indoor environmental categories, but defines acceptable indoor air quality as air in which 
there are no known contaminants at harmful concentration as determined by cognizant authorities 
and with which a substantial majority (more than 80 %) of the people exposed do not express 
dissatisfaction(ASHRAE Standing Standard Project Committee 62-1 2016). The standard contains 
requirements related to certain sources, including outdoor air, construction process, moisture, and 
biological growth, in addition to ventilation rate. 
The version reviewed in this thesis is published in 2016. 
ASHRAE Standard 62-2 – Ventilation and Acceptable Indoor Air Quality in Residential Buildings 
ASHRAE Standard 62-2 (ASHRAE Standing Standard Project Committee 62-2 2016) presents minimum 
requirements for mechanical and natural ventilation systems to provide acceptable indoor air quality 
in residential buildings. The standard does not present indoor environmental categories, but defines 
acceptable indoor air quality as air in which there are no known contaminants at harmful 
concentration as determined by cognizant authorities and with which a substantial majority of the 
people exposed do not express dissatisfaction(ASHRAE Standing Standard Project Committee 62-2 
2016). 
The version reviewed in this thesis is published in 2016. 
AS 1668.2 - The use of ventilation and air conditioning in buildings 
AS 1668.2 (Committee ME-062 2002) presents minimum permissible ventilation rates to provide 
acceptable indoor air quality. The minimum ventilation rates specified are intended to maintain 
general contaminants (e.g., body odors, volatile organic compounds and the like) at concentrations 
below exposures that have the potential to cause adverse health effects to a substantial majority of 
occupants. The standard includes guidelines on outdoor air intake and sections relating to ventilation 
of enclosures for health care functions and for enclosures used by vehicles with combustion engines. 
The standard does not present indoor environmental categories.   
The version reviewed in this thesis is published in 2002.

5.2.3 Indoor environmental standards for multiple parameters 
ISH 10001 – Indoor Environmental Quality Standard 
 
ISH 10001(ISHRAE 2016) defines the recommended values and methods of measurement including 
technical specification of the measuring instruments for indoor air quality, thermal comfort, lightning 
and acoustics. 
The standard covers regularly occupied built environments in residential and non-residential 
buildings with low to moderate levels of activity. Both naturally ventilated, mixed mode and air-
conditioned buildings are covered. The standard does not cover spaces for high intensity activities or 
special purposes such as gymnasiums, indoor sports facilities wedding halls etc., nor does it cover 
specific requirements for specialized built environments such as operation theatres, intensive care 
units, clean rooms, scientific laboratories, industry and other specialized applications.  
It does not cover indoor environmental quality parameters such as ergonomics and electromagnetic 
radiations, or pollutants not specified in the standard (lead, benzene, styrene, ammonia, radon or 
asbestos for instance is not covered). 
The standard gives recommended values for IEQ parameters for three classes, Class A, Class B and 
Class C. Class A is meant to be comparable with internationally prevalent standards.  
The standard was published in 2016, and the version reviewed in this thesis is an undated draft. 
ISO 17772 – Energy Performance of buildings – Indoor environmental quality 
ISO 17772 is a standard under development, and presents input parameters for system dimensioning 
and energy calculations for different indoor environmental quality categories. The standard presents 
input parameters for thermal environment, lighting, indoor air quality, acoustics and humidity (SAI 
Global 2017; ISO/TC 163 2016b).  
The standard is split into two parts. ISO 17772-1(ISO/TC 163 2016b) presents input parameters for 
the design and assessment of energy performance of buildings, while ISO 17772-2(ISO/TC 163 2016a) 
is a guideline to part 1. ISO 17772-2 also gives additional background information on topics in ISO 
17772-1. It also describes and recommends additional topics related to the evaluation of the indoor 
environmental quality and new possibilities to improve the indoor environmental quality and reduce 
energy use of new buildings (personalized systems, air cleaning technologies, consideration of 
adapted persons etc.)  
ISO 17772 sets up 4 categories (1-4) for thermal environment and lighting, and 3 categories (1-3) for 
air quality and humidity. The standard also provides occupant schedules for different types of 
buildings and spaces, to be used in energy calculations if real use of the building is not known. 
The version of ISO 17772-1 reviewed in this paper is a draft dated 25.07.2016, while the reviewed 
version of ISO 17772-2 is a draft dated 20.10.2016. 
EN 15251 – Indoor environmental input parameters for design and assessment of energy performance 
of buildings addressing indoor air quality, thermal environment, lighting and acoustics 
EN 15251(CEN/TC 156 2007) presents input parameters for system dimensioning and energy 
calculations for different indoor environmental quality categories for thermal environment, lighting, 
indoor air quality and acoustics. The standard sets up 4 categories (1-4) for thermal environment, 
indoor air quality and humidity. There are given information on recommended criteria for lighting 
and indoor system noise in different types of buildings and spaces.  
==PAGE:31==

The standard is applicable mainly in non-industrial buildings where the criteria for indoor 
environment are set by human occupancy and where the production or process does not have a 
major impact on indoor environment.   
The version reviewed in this thesis is the 1st edition, published in 2007. 
prEN 16798 - Energy performance of buildings 
prEN 16798 is a new standard under development. This standard will supersede EN 15251 when 
published (CEN/TC 156 2016a). The major change from EN 15251 is that the standard is split into a 
normative part, prEN 16798-1(CEN/TC 156 2016a), and a technical report part prEN 16798-2(CEN/TC 
156 2016b). The standard is the same as the new ISO standard ISO 17772. 
The standard is applicable where the criteria for indoor environment are set by human occupancy 
and where the production or process does not have a major impact on indoor environment. 
The versions considered in this document is prEN 16798-1 revision 5 published 01.04.2016 and prEN 
16798-2 draft 11 published 22.07.2016.

5.5.2 Local discomfort 
ISO 7730, ISO 17772, prEN 16798, ASHRAE Standard 55, ISH 10001 and GB/T also gives 
recommendations about local discomfort.  
ISO 7730 states that it is mainly people at light sedentary activities with a thermal sensation for the 
whole body close to neutral that are sensitive to local discomfort. At higher activity levels, people are 
less thermally sensitive and consequently the risk of local discomfort is lower. 
ASHRAE Standard 55 states that the requirements for local discomfort is only applicable when 
representative occupants have a clothing insulation less than 0.7 clo and have an activity level of less 
than 1.3 met. The standards present requirements designed to keep PPD with local discomfort under 
10 %. 
The local discomfort requirements are separated into requirements to avoid draught, discomfort due 
to vertical temperature differences, discomfort due to warm and cold floors and discomfort due to 
asymmetrical radiation. More information on recommendations for these parameters is given in 
appendix 0, together with a comparison of ASHRAE Standard 55 and ISO 7730 acceptance of 
temperature variations with time.

5.12 Comparison 
The review of the relevant standards has shown that most of the standards is made with design of 
the system or operation in mind. Only ISO 17772, EN 15251 and prEN 16798 is made to be used for 
energy calculations.  
Most of the standards states which comfort level is aimed at, either by stating the predicted 
percentage satisfied with the IEQ level or by stating the predicted percentage dissatisfied (PPD) with 
the IEQ level. The ASHRAE Standards, ISHRAE standard Class 1 and GB/T 50785 Class 1 aims for the 
same level as ISO/EN standards Category 2, with 10 % dissatisfied with the overall thermal comfort 
and 20 % dissatisfied with the indoor air quality. GB/T Class 2 aims for the same amount of 
dissatisfied with the overall thermal comfort as ISO 17772 /prEN 16798 Category 4 (25 % 
dissatisfied). 
Even though the standards are designed with an aim for the same comfort level, the 
recommendations often vary between the standards.  Both ASHRAE Standard 55 and GB/T 50785 
Class 1 recommends somewhat higher temperatures than the ISO/EN standard Category 2, even 
though they aim for the same number of dissatisfied. GB/T 50798 Class 2 also recommends higher 
temperatures than ISO 17772 /prEN 16798 Category 4.  
The standards giving recommendations for ventilation rates gives different ways of deciding the 
ventilation rates, and ends therefore up giving different recommendations for ventilation rates. 
ASHRAE Standard 62-2 requires a given exhaust rate from kitchen and bathrooms, contrary to ISO/EN 
standards that gives recommendations on exhaust rates from these rooms.

6 PARAMETRIC STUDY - METHOD 
A parametric study was conducted to compare the energy use based on the recommended IEQ in the 
different standards revised in chapter 3.  
The parametric study was conducted by simulating a building in the dynamic multi-zone simulation 
software IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE) (Equa Simulations AB 2016a), changing one or 
more of the tested parameters at a time.  The parameters tested was climates, building regulations, 
ventilation rates, temperature ranges, use of cooling, illuminance set points and window sizes.  
The simulations for building characteristics was only done for Copenhagen climate, while the other 
parameters were tested for all the tested climates.  
The parametric study resulted in comparison of the average relative difference in energy use over 
the simulated climates. For some simulated parameters were also some IEQ parameter levels 
compared.  
6.1 Simulation program 
The simulation program IDA ICE is developed by the Swedish company Equa Simulations AB, and is 
validated according to validation tests such as ASHRAE 140-2004, CEN Standard EN 15255 and 
152565 - 2007 and CEN Standard EN 13791 (Equa Simulations AB n.d.). The version used in this thesis 
is version 4.7.1, released October 2016. 
The simulation program provides results both on energy use and indoor environmental quality, and 
allows for a detailed definition of the building envelope, building installations and internal gains.  
The outdoor climate is based on a climate file for the desired climate, that can be downloaded from 
“IDA location download center” 
IDA ICE offers three models of user face. The simplest level is called the wizard, followed by the 
standard model, then the advanced model. The advanced model user face allows access to the 
model source code (Equa Simulations AB 2016b). The standard level is mainly used in this thesis, with 
some use of the advanced model user face to get an understanding of how different aspects were 
calculated from the model source code.  
The model also offers two zone models – the energy model and the climate model. The climate 
model offers detailed calculations of temperature, while the energy level is based on a mean radiant 
temperature(Equa Simulations AB 2016b). The energy model is used in this thesis. When using the 
energy model is it possible to change the percentage of internal gains used for calculations. This is set 
to 60 % by default, which was used in these simulations although using 100 % would probably give a 
more accurate result based on the input parameters used for the energy calculations. 
The program extension Daylight was also used to calculate the average daylight factor and 
illuminance level for different window sizes.  
IDA ICE offers four different simulation types: Heating load simulation, cooling load simulation, 
energy simulation and a custom simulation. The energy simulation is used in this thesis. The energy 
simulation calculates the energy use, as well as other requested output, for one year using the 
chosen climate file.  
The results are based on the delivered energy report for the whole building, as well as thermal 
comfort report with and without cooling, main temperatures- and indoor air quality diagrams for the 
zone levels.

The thermal comfort report is based on EN 15251:2007, and is only implemented for living spaces 
(Equa Simulations AB 2016b).

6.6 Review of results 
6.6.1 Energy use 
How the IEQ parameters effected the energy use of the building was reviewed by comparing the 
total delivered energy (the sum of delivered energy for lighting, heating, cooling, HVAC auxiliary 
systems and domestic hot water) per m2 for each simulation with the reference model (as described 
in subchapter 6.5) simulated for the same climate. The relative difference in energy use between the 
given simulation variation and the reference model for the different climates were then compared to 
the equivalent simulations for the other climates, and the average relative difference in energy use 
for all the simulated climates, compared to the reference models for each climate, was found.  
For some parameters were the energy use for different energy posts (lighting, heating, cooling etc.) 
also compared. The energy uses for different energy posts were compared in the same way as the 
total delivered energy.  
6.6.2 IEQ level 
To investigate how the different set points affected the IEQ level in the building, some IEQ 
parameters were also compared. The IEQ levels were compared as percentage of time within 
different intervals. The intervals were based on recommended values given in ISO 17772 and prEN 
16798. The time within the intervals were compared using method A as described in chapter 5.10. 
That means that it was only the number of hours within each interval that were compared, it was not 
used any weighing factor based on number of occupants or how much the evaluated parameter 
differed from the optimal.  
The results for the thermal comfort report differs slightly from the results for the other IEQ 
parameters. The thermal comfort report is based only on occupied hours, and is given as a number of 
hours within each Category – corresponding to EN 15251.  
The results for the other IEQ parameters is based on all hours in the simulated year, regardless of 
whether occupants were present or not. These results were presented as a diagram, where the 
duration over or under a certain value could be found. The intervals presented is chosen based on 
recommendations for different IEQ classes from ISO 17772.

The IEQ parameters were compared by finding the percentage of time within the intervals for the 
different simulations. The average percentage within the different intervals were then found across 
the simulated climates.

the energy use.

lowered by not using mechanical cooling, the relative humidity in the building might increase.

window size or temperature set points alone. The change in energy was however not as big as the 
summation of the effect of changing one parameter at a time. That is to say, the sum of the energy

impact on the energy use.

affect how the energy use in buildings varies with change in different IEQ parameters.

8 DISCUSSION 
How the IEQ parameters affect the energy use in buildings was investigated in this thesis. The results 
show that the IEQ parameters do affect the energy use, but not as much as improving the building 
envelope and efficiency of building installations. Better IEQ causes a higher energy use. In the 
following chapter will it be given a discussion on the different standards, in light of both the 
comparison of the standards and the parametric study. This is followed by a discussion on the results 
obtained in the parametric study. Chapter 8.3 gives a discussion on the choices done in this thesis.  
8.1 Standards 
The comparison between the different IEQ standards showed that the standards give different 
recommendations for IEQ set points, even when aiming for similar comfort levels. This is partly 
because the standards use different ways of calculating recommended set points for IEQ parameters, 
partly because the standards make different assumptions for other aspects affecting the IEQ 
parameters.  
The parameter study showed that the IEQ set points recommended in the different standards and 
IEQ categories lead to different energy uses. Using ASHRAE Standard 62-2 for ventilation results in a 
higher energy use than using recommendations from ISO 17772. At the same time did using 
recommended temperature ranges from ASHRAE Standard 55 leads to lower energy use than using 
the recommended temperature range from ISO 17772, both standards aiming for the same comfort 
level (10 % PPD). 
Using recommended temperature ranges from GB/T 50785 Category 2 gives a high variation in 
relative difference in energy use compared to ISO 17772 Category 2 for the different climates. This 
implies that the choice of IEQ standard to follow is important for the resulting energy use in 
buildings. The different set points recommended in the different IEQ standards will however also 
result in different indoor environments, even when the stated comfort aim is the same. 
GB/T 50785 Category 2 leads to high average energy use, with big variations. It can therefore be a 
good standard to use in hotter climates if there is a focus on energy saving. Using GB/T 50785 
Category 2 in colder climates is not recommendable, as it leads to high energy use with uncertain 
effect on the thermal comfort of the occupants.  
The choice of standard, and Category within the standards, should be based on climate and whether 
the focus is on energy savings or increased IEQ. 
8.2 Results 
This section presents a discussion around the results found in the parametric study. Possible 
explanations for the results, the limitations the results are based on as well as possible implications 
of the results will be presented.  
When analyzing the results, it should be considered that the results are based on a limited number of 
simulations with a restricted amount of changed parameters. The simulations are performed using 
only one building layout, and only one set of building use schedules. Different types of buildings, 
room layout and use of the building is some of the parameters that can impact how much each of the 
tested parameters influence the energy use of the building.  
It should also be noted that the energy calculations were performed with the use of only 60 % of 
internal gains. Based on the internal gains and use of building schedule used would probably a 
calculation with 100 % internal gain be more correct. The fact that it was used 60 % internal gains in 
==PAGE:83==
70 
 
the energy calculations instead of 100 % were however discovered too late in the process to produce 
new data.  
8.2.1 Change in amount of internal gains used for simulations 
The results from the simulation using 100 % internal gains for the simulations show that choices done 
on occupancy, use of appliances etc. is of great importance when calculating the energy use in 
buildings, and underlines the need for more research into how these factors affect the change in 
energy use due to IEQ parameters.  
The great impact this one factor has on the energy use in buildings do highlight how complex a 
system the relation between IEQ and energy use in buildings is.  
8.2.2 Other factors than IEQ parameters 
The results show that an improvement in building envelope give the highest impact on the energy 
use, more than changing the IEQ level. Renovating older buildings is therefore more recommendable 
than lowering the standard of the IEQ.  
The climate has the second largest impact on the energy use, although this does not seem to be 
changed towards conditions resulting in lower energy use. Buildings in warmer climates use generally 
more energy on keeping an acceptable indoor environment, followed by buildings in cold climates. 
Temperate climates have the lowest energy use, with Copenhagen climate being the climate with 
lowest energy use.  
The lower energy use in Copenhagen might be influenced by the fact that the building was designed 
after Danish building regulations. These regulations will probably focus more on issues important in 
Copenhagen climate, while other requirements might be used to lower the energy use in other 
climates.  Studies with buildings following building regulations and traditions in other parts of the 
world will therefore be of much interest.   
8.2.3 Use of cooling 
Whether mechanical cooling was used or not had the third largest impact of energy use. This might 
be affected by the fact that lowering the cooling demand was not a focus when designing the 
building. 
The simulations also showed that it was little difference in relative energy savings by avoiding cooling 
for buildings following different building regulations. This might however be a result of the fact that 
Copenhagen less need for cooling than some of the other climates in the study. A warmer climate 
might show a bigger difference in energy savings due to lack of cooling for buildings following 
different building regulations.  
The IEQ results showed that the thermal comfort in the buildings would be better in naturally cooled 
buildings than in mechanically cooled buildings if the adaptive effect is taken into consideration. The 
relative humidity is however higher in naturally cooled buildings. The humidity in residential buildings 
is one of the main concerns when considering health effects of IEQ parameters, and so it is 
recommended to take steps to avoid high relative humidity in buildings. Even so, less energy 
demanding ways of keeping the relative humidity down might exist, and could lead to lower energy 
use in areas where high relative humidity is a problem.  
The fact that the calculations were performed with 60 % internal gain may have influenced the 
results. Higher degree of use of internal gain will probably lead to higher energy use for cooling, as 
well as higher operative temperatures when cooling is not used. The percentage of internal gains also 
affect the humidity in the buildings, as only 60 % of the emitted humidity is taken into account.  
==PAGE:84==
71 
 
The fact that Working Group III to the Fifth Assessment Report of the Intergovernmental Panel on 
Climate Change (Lucon et al. 2014) states that cooling only accounts for 2 % of the global final energy 
use in residential buildings does imply that most buildings even in warm climates does not use 
mechanical cooling of residential buildings. Higher standard of living might however affect this in the 
future, making cooling of buildings a bigger part of the global final energy use in residential buildings.  
8.2.4 IEQ Category 
Simulations aiming at fulfilling the ASHRAE Standards have an energy use in the middle of ISO/EN 
Category 1 and Category 2, even though the ASHRAE standards aims for the same comfort level as 
ISO/EN Category 2. This might be due to the increased ventilation rates recommended by ASHRAE 
Standard 62-2.  
The fact that changing the IEQ category level from ISO 17772 Category 1 to ISO 17772 Category 2 
leads to a decrease in energy use of 76 % for heating and 28 % for HVAC auxiliary fits well with the 
results gained in Influence of occupant's heating set-point preferences on indoor environmental 
quality and heating demand in residential buildings (Fabi et al. 2013), where changing the IEQ 
category level from ISO 17772 Category 1 to ISO 17772 Category 2 lead to a reduction of energy use 
of 77 % for space heating and 27 % for AHU primary energy. 
The fact that the calculations were performed with 60 % internal gain may have influenced the 
results. Higher degree of use of internal gain will probably lead to higher energy use for cooling, and 
lighting, as well as less energy consumption for heating. The overall effect on the change in energy 
use due to a change in IEQ Category is difficult to predict, and an investigation into this is 
recommended for further work. 
8.2.5 Window sizes 
The simulations showed that window sizes affect the energy use in buildings with up to 16 % by 
increasing the daylight factor to correspond to a higher Category. This implicates that one should be 
careful when choosing bigger window sizes, making sure to get the most effect out of the increased 
window areal. 
The results are however only based on simulations with a low number of parameters affecting the 
change in energy use due to window sizes. Different windows and different use of shading might 
impact the energy use for heating and cooling, while different lighting schedule and light efficacy 
might affect the energy use for lighting.  
The fact that the calculations were performed with 60 % internal gain may have influenced the 
results. Higher degree of use of internal gain will probably lead to higher energy use for cooling, and 
lighting, as well as less energy consumption for heating. This might affect the results so that changing 
the window size has a smaller effect on the total energy use than the results in this thesis show. An 
investigation into how this affects the change in energy use due to a change in window sizes is 
recommended for further work. 
8.2.6 Illuminance levels 
The simulations showed that the illuminance levels simulated did not affect the energy use of the 
building noticeably. These results fits well with the fact that only 2 % of the world building final 
energy use in residential buildings is used for lighting (Lucon et al. 2014). 
The impact the illuminance level has on the energy use might be changed if the lighting schedule, use 
of shading or illuminance efficacy was changed, or for different illuminance levels, but based on 
==PAGE:85==
72 
 
these simulations is it no reason to recommend keeping a lower illuminance level in dwellings than 
what feels comfortable.  
The fact that the calculations were performed with 60 % internal gain may have influenced the 
results. Higher degree of use of internal gain will probably lead to higher energy use for lighting and 
cooling. This might affect the results so that changing the illuminance level has a higher effect on the 
total energy use than the results in this thesis show. An investigation into how this affects the change 
in energy use due to a change in window sizes is recommended for further work. 
8.2.7 Ventilation rates 
The simulations show that higher ventilation rates lead to higher energy use.  
The biggest relative difference in energy use compared to ISO 17772 Category 2 was using ventilation 
rates corresponding to ASHRAE Standard 62-2. Although ASHRAE standard 62-2 aims for the same 
percentage of dissatisfied (20 % PPD) as ISO 17772 Category 2, the energy use in buildings with 
ventilation rates corresponding to ASHRAE 62-2 is higher than in buildings with ventilation rates 
corresponding to ISO 17772 Category 2, and Category 1. ASHRAE standard 62-2 requires higher 
ventilation rates than ISO 17772 Category 2, and does also require a high demand controlled 
ventilation rate from the kitchen hoods. To what extent larger ventilation rates lead to improved IEQ 
is difficult to judge from the simulations, but the CO2 level for the bedroom is within Category 1 for a 
longer duration in buildings with ventilation rates corresponding to ASHRAE Standard 62-2 than for 
ventilation rates corresponding to any of the ISO 17772 categories. This implies that the ventilation 
rates corresponding to ASHRAE Standard 62-2 requirements leads to better indoor air quality. The 
relative humidity in the dwelling does not seem to be affected by the ventilation rate.  
The fact that the calculations were performed with 60 % internal gain may have influenced the 
results. A higher occupancy density and more humidity emissions might affect the CO2 level and 
relative humidity level, making the difference in indoor air quality bigger for the different ventilation 
rates. How the use of internal gains affects the variations in energy use and the indoor air quality due 
to variation in ventilation rates is recommended for further work. 
8.2.8 Temperature ranges 
The simulations showed that the temperature range set points were one of the parameters with the 
lowest effect on the energy use. This is interesting, as the temperature set point has gotten a lot of 
attention when researching the energy use in buildings, probably due to the fact that so much of the 
energy use in buildings is used for heating and cooling ( approximately 34 %)(Lucon et al. 2014). The 
change in energy use for heating and cooling does match the estimations described in section 3.1, 
but the simulations have shown that other IEQ parameters affect the energy use for heating and 
cooling as well, as well as energy use for other posts.  
The simulations showed that the temperature set points in GB/T 50768 Category 2 leads to the 
second largest energy use, while also having a wide variation in relative change in energy use. This is 
due to the high temperatures recommended, which requires high energy uses for heating in some 
climates, while resulting in a reduced need for cooling in other climates. Temperature ranges 
recommended by GB/T 50768 should therefore probably not be used in cold climates.  
Temperature ranges corresponding to ASHRAE Standard 55 uses less energy than ISO Category 2, 
even though aiming for the same amount of dissatisfied. Whether people prefer temperature ranges 
recommended by ISO 17772 or ASHRAE Standard 55 is outside the scope of this paper, but could be 
an interesting focus for further work.  
==PAGE:86==
73 
 
The fact that the calculations were performed with 60 % internal gain may have influenced the 
results. The use of a higher percentage of internal gains will probably lead to higher energy use for 
cooling and lower energy use for heating. This might affect the change in energy use due to change in 
temperature set points to be of more importance.  
8.2.9 Ventilation rates and temperature ranges 
The simulations showed that the ventilation rate and temperature range did not affect each other in 
a way that gave impact on the relative change in energy use. This is somewhat surprising, as both IEQ 
parameters affect the energy use for heating and cooling, something that could lead to increased 
effect on the overall energy use. 
Whether a change in the amount of internal gains affects how the two parameters interact is 
uncertain. 
8.2.10 Temperature set points and window sizes 
Altering the setpoints for heating and cooling simultaneously as altering the window size gave a 
bigger change in energy use than what changing one parameter alone would give, as both 
parameters affect the necessary energy due to heating and cooling. It could be interesting to see 
how the two parameters interacted if more simulations were done with i.e. different lighting 
schedule, efficacy, shading etc.  
The fact that the calculations were performed with 60 % internal gain may have influenced the 
results. The use of a higher percentage of internal gains will probably lead to higher energy use for 
lighting and cooling and lower energy use for heating. 
8.2.11 The effect of IEQ parameters on occupants related to results of the parametric study 
The results on how a change in the internal gains used for simulations affects the energy use in 
buildings show that the change in energy use due to a change in parameters is in a great deal 
affected by other factors as well. It should therefore not be drawn to strict conclusions before more 
of the factors affecting the change in energy use due to change in IEQ parameters is investigated.  
Based on the findings in this thesis is it however in this section discussed how the change in energy 
use in buildings due to change in IEQ parameters can affect choices for the IEQ in dwellings.  
As described in chapter 2.2 has different studies found different importance of the IEQ parameters 
for the overall satisfaction with the IEQ. The literature study by Frontczak and Wargocki (Frontczak & 
Wargocki 2011) found that the thermal environment affect the overall sensation of the IEQ the most. 
If this is correct could it be recommended to increase thermal comfort on behalf of other IEQ 
parameters, as the temperature set points affect the energy use to a smaller extent than other IEQ 
parameters.  
At the same time is there little research showing that the thermal environment in dwellings is 
important for the health of the occupants. This is however found for both daylight and ventilation 
rates, as well as for the humidity in homes. As Kim and de Dear (Kim & de Dear 2012) found is it also 
a difference in the importance of the thermal comfort on the overall sensation of the IEQ based on 
whether the occupants are satisfied or dissatisfied with the thermal comfort. Increasing the thermal 
environment from an already satisfactory level will therefore not necessarily increase the overall 
satisfaction with the IEQ if this causes other parameters to be dissatisfactory.  
According to the regression analysis by Kim and de Dear(Kim & de Dear 2012) is a comfortable level 
of lighting more important for the overall comfort than a comfortable thermal environment, indoor 
==PAGE:87==
74 
 
air quality and visual comfort. Considering that the impact the illuminance level has on the energy 
use is very small is it no reason to save energy by keeping the dwelling dimly lit. The results of Kim 
and de Dear is however mainly applicable for work-spaces.  
Improved ventilation rates in dwellings is linked to both increased health, sleep quality and 
productivity. The ventilation rate is however mainly used as an indicator of the indoor air quality, 
where the ventilation rate in itself is probably not that important for these factors. As the increase in 
ventilation rate is related to an increase in energy use is it probably better to first remove factors in 
the dwelling leading to worsened indoor air quality. This might include using less emissive materials, 
avoid environmental tobacco smoke and reduce risk of mould and condensation.   
Window sizes impacts on the energy use in dwellings to a high degree, but is also found to be 
important for the circadian rhythm and productivity of occupants. Windows is also important for the 
visual comfort, one of the parameters found to have highest relevance for occupants satisfaction 
with the overall IEQ in the regression analyses by Kim and de Dear(Kim & de Dear 2012). The 
placement and size of window sizes should therefore be well planned, also because placement and 
window sizes is one of the parameters tested in this thesis that is most difficult to change once the 
building is finished.  
8.3 Choices in the thesis 
The choices done in the thesis has affected the results obtained. The choices done can be separated 
into choices done for the simulations and choices done for comparing the results.  
8.3.1 Simulations 
The fact that the simulations is based on calculations with 60 % of the internal gains will probably 
have an impact on the results obtained in this thesis. How big effect this has is recommended for 
further investigations.  
Parameters simulated 
As the indoor environment is affected by a large number of parameters, a choice in which 
parameters to simulate had to be made. The decision was made to simulate the parameters given 
recommendations for on energy calculations in ISO 17772 and prEN 16798 – temperature range, 
ventilation rate and lighting. It was however decided not to do simulations on humidity control, as 
this is generally not recommended to be used unless necessary, and because it was not found a 
simple way of controlling humidity in IDA ICE. 
 It was also decided to compare window sizes corresponding to different daylight factors. This is also 
a parameter given recommendations for in ISO 17772 and prEN 16798, although not in the energy 
calculations part of the standard. The window sizes and daylight factor does nonetheless effect the 
energy use for heating, cooling and lighting, as was therefore an interesting parameter to investigate.  
Some of these parameters were also combined to investigate how the IEQ parameters affect each 
other and the energy use.  
It was also decided to compare how buildings with and without cooling affected the energy use, as 
most standards include recommendations on thermal environment for buildings without mechanical 
cooling.  
Another parameter discussed in ISO 17772 and prEN 16798 is the noise from building installations. 
This parameter was however not investigated in the thesis, as the recommended values given in the 
standards are not possible to find easily. To compare the energy use due to noise level, it would be 
natural to find the increased pressure loss in the ventilation system due to the necessary silencers. 
==PAGE:88==
75 
 
The silencers needed for a ventilation system, and how these affect the pressure loss in the 
ventilation system, is however dependent on several factors. It was therefore decided to focus on 
other IEQ parameters in this thesis.  
Other parameters varied in the thesis is climate and building envelope- and building installation 
characteristics as recommended in different building regulations. The choice to compare different 
climates was based on a wish to make the findings globally relevant, while the different building 
regulations were compared to see how much effect the building envelope has on the difference in 
energy use for the tested IEQ parameters. 
Variations within the parameters 
The parameters were also not directly comparable, as the units used to describe the different indoor 
environmental parameters are not the same (i.e. a change of 1°C is not directly comparable to a 
change in ventilation rate of 1 m3/h). The choice was therefore made to compare the IEQ parameters 
based on recommendations from different standards and categories within the standards. A different 
way of comparing energy use for IEQ parameters could have been to compare based on PPD. The 
predicted percentage dissatisfied is however not available for all IEQ parameters.  
It was therefore decided to simulate the different parameters based on given recommendations 
from different IEQ standards.  
Building use 
How the building is used by occupants affects the energy use, and how the building is used is 
therefore an important decision for simulations of energy use in buildings. Dwellings will often have a 
less predictable building use pattern than office buildings, as IEQ parameters are controlled more 
locally.  (Fabi et al. 2013) recommends simulating the building use based on a predictive occupant 
behavioral model. This was however deemed to be a time-consuming task that was not directly 
related to the aim for the thesis.  
It was however tested how the amount of internal gain affect the energy use, which gave results 
indicating that the use of the building affects how a change in IEQ parameters leads to change in 
energy use in buildings to a high degree.  
ISO 17772 and prEN 16798 presents examples of schedules to be used for energy calculations for 
different type of buildings. The schedules are given for occupancy, lighting and appliances, and is 
easily applicable. The schedules were however only given for the whole building, where the building 
in the simulation were separated into different rooms. The schedule from ISO 17772 and prEN 16798 
were therefore adapted to the different rooms (i.e. occupants occupying the bedroom 22-06 and 
living room during 06-22).  
The type of lighting sources also varies from dwelling to dwelling. As incandescent lamps are 
increasingly being phased out, the efficacy of the lamps used in the simulations corresponded to 
compact fluorescent lamps with high efficacy or a mid-range LED lamps.  
For the simulation of ventilation rates corresponding to ASHRAE Standard 62-2, it was introduced a 
ventilation schedule to show when the kitchen hood in the kitchen was used. The schedule was 
based on an estimation of how kitchen hoods are used, and different use of the kitchen hood will 
most probably result in a difference in energy use. 
==PAGE:89==
76 
 
Building envelope and building installations 
The simulated building was based on Danish building regulations, and might not be representable for 
buildings in the other climates. Warmer climates usually have less insulated buildings, and it is not 
necessarily as common with heat recovery units in other parts of the world.   
The windows simulated in building envelope simulations corresponding to building regulations from 
2015 and 2020 has a higher energy transfer than required by the building regulations (the windows 
are better than the minimum requirement).  This can probably influence how the energy use varies 
with window size, where windows closer to the requirements lead to a bigger increase in energy use 
due to increased window sizes. A study on how different qualities of the window influences the 
change in energy use due to change in window sizes could be interesting.  
Thermal bridges were not considered in the simulations. This might affect how the energy use varies 
with the different parameters. How thermal bridges affect change in energy use due to other 
parameter variations would be interesting for further studies.  
8.3.2 Comparison of results 
The comparison of the results is based on the total energy use for each simulation. The total energy 
use is based on lighting, cooling, heating, HVAC auxiliary and domestic hot water. These factors were 
included as these are the factors that the Danish building regulations requires considered for the 
calculation of energy use, which also follows the requirements in the European Energy Performance 
of Building Directive (EPBD).  
The total energy use is compared by using the average relative difference from the reference model 
over eight different climates. To pick up big differences between the climates, the variation of 
relative difference in energy use for the different climates is compared, and big differences for single 
climates have been investigated and tried to be explained.  
The relative change in energy use for buildings following different building regulations has also been 
compared for Copenhagen to investigate how much the building envelope and efficiency of building 
installations impacts the change in energy for the different parameters.  
This way of comparing the different results allows for an easily accessible comparison over several 
different parameters. An increased amount of simulations would however have given the results 
even more strength.   
The indoor climate data are presented in duration within intervals corresponding to 
recommendations in ISO 17772, as this is an easy way of showing if the simulated parameters 
fulfilled the IEQ level aimed for.  
The fact that the IEQ results is compared with the requirements in one of the standards tested 
means that it is expected if the simulations following ISO 17772 is within the different IEQ categories 
more than simulations following other standards. ISO is however an international and recognized 
standard organization, and it was therefore decided that it was a practical way of reviewing the IEQ 
results.  
The fact that the IEQ-level was compared by assessing the time within specific intervals also limited 
the possibility to judge which simulations that best fulfilled the recommended IEQ levels, as i.e. the 
CO2 level was within Category 1 at all times for living rooms in simulations using ventilation rates 
corresponding to ISO 17772 and ASHRAE standard 62-2. A more precise comparison of the IEQ 
parameters would however be more complicated when comparing a high degree of parameters.

9 IMPLICATIONS AND FURTHER WORK 
This thesis has shown that IEQ parameters have implications for the energy use in residential 
buildings, and provides information on the impact different IEQ parameters have on energy use in 
buildings. The impact of changing the IEQ parameters is however not as big as improving the building 
quality from 1995 standard to 2015 standard.  
This information can be useful for property developers, designers, operators and building occupants 
to help decide on IEQ level in buildings based on an informed basis. The results can also work as 
input for research into cost/benefit of IEQ levels in residential buildings. 
Further research into this topic could be to investigate how different aspects of the building 
influences the implications of IEQ parameters on the energy use. Such aspects can include type of 
building (single family/town house/multifamily, one story/multiple story), use of building (occupancy 
schedule, lighting schedule etc.) and equipment and building installations (appliances, light efficacy, 
COP of boiler and cooler, efficiency of heat recovery, SFP of fans). Qualities concerning the building 
envelope (U-values of walls and windows, energy transmittance of windows, thermal inertia of walls) 
and different window shading strategies is also concerns that might affect how the IEQ parameters 
influence the energy use of buildings.  
A more thorough investigation into each IEQ parameter, using more variations within each 
parameter, could also be interesting to get a more in-depth understanding on how the IEQ 
parameter effect the energy use in a building. A comparison of how changes in PPD affect the energy 
use could for instance be interesting in this setting.  
Simulations could also be done on more IEQ parameters, such as humidity control and noise from 
building installations. Further investigations into how interactions between IEQ parameters affect the 
energy use is also of interest. Such interactions could be the interaction between ventilation rate and 
noise.  
Studies on which IEQ parameters should be kept at a high-quality level, and where it is possible to 
save energy could also be performed. This study could look into factors such as health, comfort and 
productivity as well as the energy use of buildings.

10 CONCLUSION 
This thesis has shown that the IEQ parameters does affect the energy uses of dwellings, but not as 
much as updating older buildings to follow the newest building regulations. 
Different IEQ standards give different recommendations for the indoor environment. The standards 
tend to aim for the same comfort level. where 20 % dissatisfied due to ventilation and 10 % 
dissatisfied due to the thermal environment seem to be the most common aim. At the same time 
does the standards give different recommendations on how to achieve this aim. ASHRAE Standard 
62-2 requires a high demand controlled exhaust rate for kitchen, opposite ISO and EN standards, that 
give recommendations on exhaust rates, but only includes this as an alternative way of calculating 
necessary ventilation rate. 
The ISO and EN standards also gives recommendation on operative temperatures, based on 
assumptions on other factors affecting the thermal environment. ASHRAE Standard 55 does not 
provide a recommended temperature range in the same way, but presents figures where it is 
possible to find acceptable temperature ranges based on own assumptions on the thermal 
environment.  
The parametric study showed that following the different standards also leads to difference in energy 
use. Using temperature ranges recommended by ASHRAE Standard 55 leads to lower energy use 
than following recommendations in ISO 17772 Category 2, both standards having the same aim for 
PPD. Following ASHRAE Standard 62-2 recommendations for ventilation rates leads to higher energy 
use than using recommendations from ISO 17772 Category 2, both standards having the same aim 
for PPD. This is due to the aforementioned required demand controlled exhaust rate in the kitchen.  
The IEQ parameter that affected the energy use the most was the use of mechanical cooling. 
Avoiding the use of mechanical cooling leads to high energy savings, without affecting the predicted 
thermal comfort of the occupants. Not using mechanical cooling might however lead to humidity 
problems in some climates. Due to the high energy savings avoiding mechanical cooling can impose, 
it is recommended to find less energy demanding ways of dehumidifying dwellings.  
Of the parameters window size, ventilation rates and temperature ranges is window sizes the 
parameter with the highest impact on energy use, while temperature ranges have the lowest effect.  
This implies that increasing the window size uncritically is unwise, but that the increased comfort, 
productivity or health gains increased window sizes will give must be compared to the increased 
energy use it will lead to.  
The illuminance level is one of the factors leading to the lowest change in energy use. It is therefore 
no reason to recommend using less than optimal illuminance levels in dwellings to save energy.  
Further investigations on the impact different IEQ parameters has on the energy use should be 
conducted.

measurements stage and evaluation stage. The design stage is the stage where the building services,

inspection and measurement stage is applicable after the building is finished, to control that the 
actual building fulfils the requirements. The evaluation can be done either before the building is 
built, as a planning stage evaluation of the indoor environment, or after the building is built, as an 
evaluation of the actual indoor environment.

Adaptive temperature model 
Prerequisites 
The different standards have different prerequisites for the use of the adaptive method.  
ISO 17772 and prEN 16798 states that the adaptive method only applies for occupants with 
sedentary activities (1.0 to 1.3 met) without strict clothing policies (possibilities to adapt their 
clothing within a range of at least 0.4 to 1.0 clo. Thermal conditions should be regulated primarily by 
the occupants through opening and closing of elements in the building envelope. EN 15251 states the 
same, without giving numerable ranges for clothing- and activity level. 
ASHRAE Standard 55 requires that the occupants are free to adapt their clothing to the indoor and/or 
outdoor thermal conditions within a range of at least 0.5 to 1 clo, and that the occupants have 
metabolic rates ranging from 1.0 to 1.3 met. 
ISO 17772, prEN 16798 and ASHRAE Standard 55 requires that the building does not have mechanical 
cooling systems installed. A mechanical ventilation system can be running, but the air should not 
==PAGE:101==
88 
 
have conditioned air. ASHRAE Standard 55 requires that there is no heating system in operation 
during the period where the adoptive model is used as well. EN 15251 requires that no mechanical 
cooling is in operation, and the adaptive method only applies during times of the year when the 
heating season is not in operation.   
GB/T 50785 requires that the building is free-running, meaning that natural or mechanical ventilation

Figure 34 Acceptable air speeds to avoid draught

ISO 7730 does not give acceptable air speeds to keep the PPD within the acceptable range, but does 
give figures showing the relation between air temperature, air velocity and turbulence intensity for 
the different PPD-levels.  
ISO 17772 and prEN 16798 gives acceptable air speeds for the different categories, based on an 
assumption of an activity level of 1.2 met, turbulence intensity of 40 %, and air temperature equal to 
the operative temperature, set to 20 °C in winter and 23 °C in summer.

HVAC system shall not exceed 0.15 m/s when operative temperatures are below 22.5 °C. This means

are permitted at higher temperatures, either with or without occupancy control.

should be less than 20 %, and shows a figure to find acceptable air temperature, air velocity and

Some standards give recommendations for natural ventilation as well as for mechanical ventilation 
systems, while some standards require ventilation opening areas such as operable windows even if it

Ventilation during un-occupied periods 
Some of the standards describes acceptability to turn of the ventilation system during un-occupied

ISO 17772, prEN 16798 and EN 15251 requires different ventilation rates during un-occupied periods 
for residential buildings and non-residential buildings.

residential buildings if no value is given at national level. ISO 17772 and prEN 16798 recommends 
between 0.1 l/(s*m2) and 0.15 l/(s*m2) depending on size and occupancy of dwelling. 
For non-residential buildings, ISO 17772 and prEN 16798 recommends the use of the ventilation rate 
corresponding to the building component during unoccupied hours, alternatively full ventilation 
stared a given time before occupation in the technical report part of the standard. In part 1 of the 
standard is it however recommended to ventilate with 1 volume within 2 hours of start of occupancy, 
or 0.15 l/(s*m2) during the un-occupied period.

l/(s*m2) to 0.2 l/(s*m2) during the un-occupied period – if national recommendations ae not available
