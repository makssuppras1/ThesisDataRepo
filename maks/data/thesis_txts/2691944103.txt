ABSTRACT
The damage to highway systems from past earthquake events has highlighted the
need for risk assessment of existing infrastructure such as bridges. It is clear that it
is of high importance to know the degree of damage to the bridge structures due
to earthquake. To estimate the damage level (slight to severe), fragility curves are
considered to be the most useful decision tool. Fragility curves show the relationship
between probability of structural damage and ground motion (intensity measure).
Initially, a set of empirical fragility curves were constructed based on the severe
damage of the infrastructure system that occured in Kobe area in 1995 from
Kobe earthquake.
These fragility curves, do not specify type of the structure
and structural performance and may be applicable only for speciﬁc bridge structures.
It is generally accepted that the motion characteristics (frequency, duration) and
structural parameters have an eﬀect on the damage of the structure and therefore
on fragility curves.
Over the last years, several methods have been used for the estimation of fragility
curves for various structural systems. Particularly, fragility curves can be obtained
experimentally, empirically or analytically. The last one is the most challenging one,
since suﬃcient past data may not be available for the area of interest. To obtain
analytically fragility curves two steps are required. Firstly, probabilistic methods in
order to simulate the random variables and secondly to evaluate the response of a
structure by either push-over analysis or non linear time history analysis.
For the former one, Direct Monte Carlo is the most applicable method due to it’s
simplicity. However, sometimes can be computationally demanding when it comes
to structures of low probability of failure. With complex performance functions
and time demanding computation of structural responses, the estimation of small
probability of failures is a challenging task in engineering. Subset simulation has
been found to be a powerful tool for estimating the small probabilities of failure.
In the present of case study, the use of Subset simulation in the establishment of
fragility curves for a 2 span bridge located in California is investigated.

1
INTRODUCTION
1.1
Motivation
The sophistication of bridge building technology has been developing since the
human civilization and the importance of them is undoubtedly. Bridges can be used
for connecting diﬃcult terrains, reducing travelling time or for military purposes.
However, the design of a bridge structure is a complicated process due to several
aspects that they have to be taken into account. The collapse of a bridge is one
of the most dangerous types of structural failure that can occur since many lives
can be at risk. Some of the causes of a bridge collapse are natural hazards such
as earthquakes, ﬂoods, design defects or poor maintenance. Earthquake events are
extremely uncertain even with the present knowledge, and it is not easy to predict
forthcoming events precisely bin terms of time and frequency.
The relation of the structure’s natural period with the predominant period of the
induced ground motion may lead to disastrous phenomena, as many observations
from past historical earthquakes have demonstrated. The most common way to
predict the risk is to identify the vulnerability of a structure. Vulnerability assessment
of a structure includes structural analysis and subsequently identify how sensitive
is the structure for exposed load. In order to project this vulnerability, considering
the state of damage and describe the probability of collapse or exceeding a speciﬁc
damage state due to seismic loading, fragility curves were developed. In general
fragility curves can be considered as one of the most powerful tool for the vulnerability
assessment, taking into account uncertainties of the variables e.g. by using probability
distributions to describe the properties of the structural model and natural hazards.
Although Monte Carlo simulation belongs to the most commonly used methods
in probabilistic seismic assessment, it turned to be computationally demanding
for low probabilities of failure, that are of the main interest in engineering ﬁeld.
Therefore, there is a need for improvements and enhancements of vulnerability
estimation procedure in terms of the simulation techniques. On the other hand,
Subset simulation has been proposed as one of the most eﬃcient techniques for the
simulation of small probabilities of failure.

1. Introduction
1.2
Objective of project
The main objective of the present thesis is to investigate the applicability of advanced
simulation methods such as Subset simulation for the establishment of fragility curves
of 2 - span box girder concrete bridge. Although, there have a been a numerous
of studies to develop a fragility curve, the majority of them are computationally
demanding or cannot capture wide range of uncertainties that play an important
role in seismic risk assessment. To aid in the assessment of seismic risk assessment,
an elegant method for estimating rare probabilities, called Subset Simulation, will
be employed in this study.
Initially, a literature review was carried out in order to investigate if advanced
simulation methods have been already used for the estimation of the fragility curve
of a structure. However, there is a lack in the available literature concerning the
use of Subset simulation in deﬁning fragility curves. Therefore, it was of crucial
importance to evaluate the accuracy of the aforementioned method in comparison of
Crude Monte Carlo, that is the most robust method for simulating random variables.
Hence, the technique was ﬁrstly applied in a simple engineering example of slope
stability and the results are presented in Chapter 2.
Subsequently, the applicability of the Subset simulation in fragility curve estimation
was conducted. Particularly, in Chapter 3, the theory concerning the fragility analysis
and its implementation to the current study are presented.
Thereafter, the structural model as well as the numerical model, that was carried
out in OpenSees platform, have been introduced in Chapter 4. The uncertainties
associated with the aforementioned structural model and the corresponding seismic
demand, particularly the model uncertainties and uncertainty in applied seismic
motion, are described in Chapter 5.

SIMULATION METHODS
Since the aim of the present study is to determine the fragility curve in an analytical
way it is of high importance to clarify the probabilistic approach and its simulation
methods, implemented in this case.
The probabilistic approach has been developed, since it was proved that the strength
and the demand cannot be deﬁned in deterministic manner. This lack of accuracy,
due to the uncertainty, has been already minimized by introducing safety factors
in the design process. However, the safety factors cannot still precisely capture
the uncertainty and in many cases comes to inaccurate results. For this purpose,
probabilistic techniques have been developed and used to evaluate the life-cycle
performance and the reliability of structures. The structural reliability can be
expressed as a probability of a structure to fulﬁl its function under the certain
condition. In order to assess the structural performance, a limit state function needs
to be deﬁned. The limit state function is a simple function, where the capacity (C)
and the demand (D) of the structure are compared in order to identify if there is any
exceedance of a given threshold. If the capacity is suﬃcient, the limit state takes a
positive value and the structure is stable. Otherwise the failure of the structure is
presented. The limit state function is shown in equation Equation 2.1
G = C −D
(2.1)
Following the form of limit state function and considering the input parameters as a
multivariate vector x (x1, x2, ....xk), the limit state function can be expressed as a
response variable, y = g(x). Once the structure exceeds a given threshold, set to be 0,
the failure occurs and the performance is deﬁned as y > y∗, otherwise y < y∗when
the structure is safe. This allows the failure domain to be deﬁned as Equation 2.2.
F = {x : g(x) > y∗}
(2.2)
Considering the probabilistic approach and deﬁne the variables by their marginal
distributions, the randomness needs to be also incorporated in calculating the
response of the structure. The probability of failure, can be therefore mathematically
determined as an integral of the joint probability density function for x, f(x), where
x represents input variables [X1, X2, ...Xk]. This probability of failure is shown in
Equation 2.3.

2. Simulation methods
2.1
Crude Monte Carlo
Crude Monte Carlo simulation, as one of the most well known technique for reliability
analysis, is a method that enable decision making when uncertain conditions are
employed. The method was invented by Jon von Neumann and Stanislav Ullam
led by Metropolis, during the II World war for analyzing the behavior of neutrons
of radioactive material and other problems in mathematical physics. Since then
Monte Carlo has been used for solving problems, where analytical methods failed
to provide results. The main advantage of this method over numerical integration
is that it cannot inﬂuenced by the dimension of the problem, because The law of
large numbers is applied. Moreover, Monte Carlo is a robust method regarding its
application and can be used for any type of structure, diﬀerent excitation models, or
diﬀerent types of standard distributions (Lognormal, Normal, Exponential, etc).
The aforementioned Law of the large numbers represents the main idea of Monte
Carlo method, and the integral from the reliability analysis can be evaluated as an
expectation of the function h : X →R with respect to PDF π(x).

2. Simulation methods
2.1.1
Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)
Markov Chain Monte Carlo is a set of algorithms that were developed in order to
generate new samples in more eﬀective way instead of sampling the whole input space
independently as it is in the case of Monte Carlo algorithm. In general, Markov Chain
Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms are used for sampling from multi-dimensional
distributions, especially when the number of dimensions is high.
Generation of new samples by these algorithms can be characterized as a stationary
process, where the samples are produced within the chains and the process is limited
by reaching the target distribution π(θ). Thus, all of the samples are progressively
produced towards the failure region and the probability of failure is reached more
eﬀectively. In general the samples produce within the chain are dependent, however
the process is memory-less, therefore the selection of a new sample depends only on
one previous sample.
Among the ﬁrst and most well known algorithms from this class are Gibs sampling
and Metropolis Hastings algorithm. Both of these methods have a long history and
have been modiﬁed by many researches. The ﬁrst oﬃcial publication, "Markov Chain
Monte Carlo in Practice", describing both of these methods comes from professor
Robert Kass (1997).
For the present study, a Modiﬁed Metropolis-Hastings algorithm was chosen, that
has been proved to be the most reliable and accurate in high dimensions while
accounting many random variables.

Subset Simulation
Since the Monte Carlo was proved to be inappropriate for sampling in high dimensions,
there was a need to develop method, that can be as accurate as the robust Monte
Carlo (CMC), however more eﬀective in terms of time and number of simulations.
The subset simulation is an elegant and eﬀective method for simulating rare events
and estimating their rare probabilities, that has been proposed by Siu-Kui Au and
James Beck, [1], for the analysis of the reliability of structures. However, since
then, the Subset Simulation has been applied on many other engineering problems,
resulting in satisfactory results.
Figure 2.1. Schematic diagram of Subset simulation method (after Zhang et al [13] and
Cao et al [3])
With the idea of Subset simulation these rare events can be decomposed to less-
rare nested events (F1,F2,F3....Fn), that progressively approach the smallest target
failure F. The generation of new samples becomes after the ﬁrst level directed by

method becomes more eﬃcient than the Crude Monte Carlo, where the samples are
generated independently. The ﬁnal probability of failure in Subset Simulation can
be subsequently derived as a product of the small bonded probabilities of failure
that correspond to the intermediate thresholds.

The choice of the proposal PDF is crucial for MCMC technique, because it strongly
inﬂuences the distribution and the acceptance rate of generated seeds. The acceptance
or rejection rate consecutively aﬀect the correlation between samples within chain,
when the correlation among the samples is increasing the algorithm becomes less
eﬃcient. Therefore, for feasible subset simulation, a careful choice of proposal
PDF is worthy to examine. A proper choice of PDF means that candidates have
a distribution close to the conditional PDF, and simulation can progress towards
the failure region with minimum rejected samples. Modiﬁed Metropolis algorithm
is a speciﬁc example because the acceptance rate is enhanced by computing its
components (uncertain parameters) separately. The main reason of grouping these
parameters is their independence and in this way one can suppress a zero acceptance
of samples in candidate state, that become complicated in high dimensions. For this
study was chosen non-adaptive sampling, and uniform proposal PDF for all of the
components. In order to deﬁne the parameters for the highest eﬃciency, proposal
PDF was investigated in relation to diﬀerent failure probabilities. As long as, the
variables are in the form of multivariate standard Gaussian vector, the remaining
parameter for generating the seeds from candidates is the standard deviation of
Markov step. Hence, the correlation factor is going to be determined as a function
of Markov step, speciﬁcally diﬀerent values for standard deviation of Markov step.

FRAGILITY ANALYSIS
The term fragility function, was ﬁrstly introduced to the seismic engineering by
Kennedy et al. (1980), [12], who deﬁned the fragility as a probabilistic relationship
between frequency of failure of a component of a nuclear power plant and peak
ground acceleration of an earthquake. Since then, this term has gained a lot of
attention, which resulted to diﬀerent improvements and modiﬁcations of this tool.
These diﬀerent applications were ﬁrstly categorized and summarized into the standard
procedure by Porter et al. (2007), [20]. This research proposed 3 diﬀerent approaches
for determining the fragility, namely, empirical, expert-opinion- based, analytical
curves. By these methods a framework for establishing the fragility analysis based
on diﬀerent criteria has been set up. For instance, location, available data from the
past event, the structure, site conditions, etc. Since the ﬁrst two terms are related
to deterministic approach, for the aim of thesis, analytical fragility function will be
employed. This method as it was mentioned in the beginning, uses a probabilistic
approach, that is of our main interest, to quantify the seismic risk.
The fragility function can be characterized as a probability of occurrence of an
unfavourable event related to the certain excitation. In order to express this deﬁnition
in terms of structural performance under the seismic loading, reliability theory can
be used. Hence, the fragility function can be transformed to the probability of the
demand (D) exceeding a capacity of a structure or certain limit state associated to
the certain intensity measure (IM) of ground motion, see Equation 3.1.
Fragility = P[D ≥C | IM] = P[C −D ≤0.0 | IM]

Damage states
In seismic risk assessment, the performance levels of a structure can be deﬁned
through damage thresholds, commonly named as limit states. The limit states in
fragility analysis deﬁne the boundaries between diﬀerent damage conditions and the
damage states deﬁne the damage conditions themselves. The choice of the type of
the damage states depends mainly on the typology of the investigated element and
the approach for estimating the fragility curve. In this study, the post-earthquake
operability of the bridge structure is based on the fragility curve model, where the
damage scale has 4 main stages: slight, moderate, extensive, severe. These stages
were deﬁned based on the capacity curve computed for this structure.
In order to classify the consequences of earthquake it is necessary to agree on the
engineering demand parameter (EDP), that will be used as a judgemental criterion.
In analytical approaches inter-storey drift is widely used. However, limits associated
with the capacity of corresponding material, similarly to the capacity curve, were
adopted for the aim of this study . Hence, all of the states are represented by collapse
of one part of the investigated component, as it can be seen in Table 3.1. These
limit states can be characterized as sequential because the probability of reaching or
exceeding a lower damage state is (generally) greater than the probability of reaching
or exceeding a higher damage state.

PROBABILISTIC MODEL
The fragility curve based on Monte Carlo method or Subset Simulation is computed
from thousands of simulations for modelling parameter values, deﬁned based on
their statistic properties. By that, one could provide thousands of probabilities of
collapse in relation to diﬀerent intensity measure values (IM). In analytically derived
fragility curves one could observe two distinct sources of uncertainties that greatly
inﬂuence their distribution parameters. In particular, the median and dispersion of
probabilistic distribution, deﬁning the exceeding of given limit state, are dependent
on the aleatory as well as epistemic uncertainties. Aleatory uncertainties are related
to randomness of ground motions, while epistemic uncertainty is associated mainly
with the model uncertainty. As it can be seen in Figure 5.1, the uncertainty from
material properties is also introduced in the damage state assessment.
Figure 5.1. Flowchart of vulnerability analysis, highlighting the uncertainty related to
diﬀerent stages of fragility analysis.
Since the fragility curves are expressed as a probability of exceeding a limit state
related to diﬀerent intensity measure (IM) levels, the ﬁrst selection will be dedicated
to the imposed seismic loads, that can be done by creating synthetic ground motions
or by using records of real earthquakes. For the aim of this thesis data recorded
from past events sourced from high-quality PEER-NGA database were used. The
selection of appropriate ground motions is not a trivial task and therefore will be
described in detail in the next section.
The second type of uncertainty is due to the lack of knowledge regarding a system,

previously deﬁned as epistemic uncertainty. In contrast to the aleatory uncertainties,
epistemic uncertainties can be eliminated by experimental work. In other words, the
reliability of distributions parameters is aﬀected by increasing number of experiments.
For this reason, highly reliable sources for all of the probabilistic models used in the
structure [24] were chosen. The biggest portion of uncertainty is associated with
material characteristics. It is mainly because the performance of a structure depends
on its capacity, that is limited by the materials that the structure is composed of.
Except the random variables related to used material, also damping ratio needs to
be deﬁned with the level of uncertainty. It is mainly because the damping ratio
depends on many parameters, such as, materials, length and deck typology, bearing
systems, non structural elements, abutments and pier dimensions, foundation, etc.
Thus, the most reliable value for damping ratio could be obtained only by physical
observation of real structures and therefore damping ratio has to be characterized
by its distribution parameters in the design stage.
5.1
Model uncertainties
Based on the deﬁnition of fragility curve it is assumed that the main emphasis has
to be given to the assessment of seismic demand due to record-to-record variability.
However, it was proved that the uncertainty related to material properties is not
of lower importance. The main reason for considering the epistemic and aleatory
uncertainties to be equally important for design, is that the behavior of a structure can
be aﬀected by using limited number of ground motions. What is more, inappropriate
number of the random variables employed in structural modelling may have a
signiﬁcant impact on the estimated fragility curve. Therefore, characterizing and
propagating uncertainties in vulnerability analysis plays an important role in deﬁning
the capacity of a given structure.
Marginal distributions
The probabilistic characteristics of the input random variables were deﬁned by using
experimental and analytical tests performed in diﬀerent researches. A marginal
distribution function was assigned to basic material properties such as yield strength
and ultimate strain of reinforcing steel bars, and the concrete is represented by
unconﬁned compressive strength and its density. Among the material characteristics
one could see another parameter that does not describe the material however it
contributes on model uncertainty. This parameter is the damping ratio, that as it
was mentioned before cannot be deﬁned by discrete variable, because it can deviate
based on the geometry and material properties.
For quantifying the aforementioned random variables, lognormal and normal
distributions were used, since they belong to the most frequent ones in application
of fragility, vulnerability and risk assessment. Their wide applicability is attributed

the marginal distribution for the selected random variables is diﬀerent for each of
them. Therefore, it is of high importance to apply one of the transformation method
for introducing the random variables from physical space to standard normal space.
Since, not all the variables are in Gaussian space, the standardization is not available
and more general techniques need to be used, such as the Rosenblatt transformation
and the Nataf transformation.
By this process one should enable the Subset Simulation start from independent
variables to generate dependent samples in physical space. Respectively, the Nataf
transformation is said to map the physical space where X takes its values into the
standard space where U takes its values. On the other hand, the inverse of this
function is used to calculate the performance function with physical variables.
Moreover, by using Nataf transformation one could deﬁne a correlation for each
of the variables. The dependency among the variables is prescribed by correlation
matrix. In reality, among all of the used material variables there are only two that
can depend on each other, and in this case the unconﬁned compressive strength
and density of concrete. In order to sample with chosen non-adaptive proposal
PDF, that is used for this study, one needs to adopt uncorrelated variables, thus the
correlation between density of concrete and its unconﬁned compressive strength will
be neglected.

Seismic loads used in this study are represented by time series of strong ground

Seismic demand for the bridge fragility curves
The probabilistic approach to hazard analysis does not consider speciﬁc scenario
but looks at all feasible scenarios (in terms of magnitude, PGA, distance to the
rupture zone, time, etc) and their consequences in order to quantify the probability
of occurrence (E.I.Katzanos et al (2009), [10]). Hence, in probabilistic manner, the
seismic hazard is assessed by cataloguing past events. This catalogue consists of
independent ground motions, since in fragility analysis one needs to incorporate
uncertainty in both, structural parameters as well as, seismic loading.
Moreover, the uncertainty in seismic loading has been proved to be more important
in performance-based design. In particular, the selection of earthquakes in this
thesis needs to provide a wide range data regarding diﬀerent seismic hazard that can
occur. The reason is to develop a method by which one can derive the probability
of risk on sites, where suﬃcient records from past events are not available. Within
this framework were established selection criteria that specify the requirements
for obtaining unbiased earthquake motion.
The aforementioned soil type will
represent the only common deterministic parameter for all selected events, while the
other parameters were proved to be stochastic and therefore speciﬁc values are not
representative. To conclude, for this fragility analysis was selected a set of 50 ground
motions with diﬀerent metadata following the below listed criteria:
• All the selected data are for speciﬁc soil type - soil type C.
• The records need to be categorized based on their period into 3 groups with
intervals
• The maximum percentage of events from the same earthquake cannot exceed
25%.
• The selected events from the same earthquake correspond to the ones with the
highest PGA.
• Both far and near ﬁeld motions need to included

CONCLUSION
The main objectives of the present study have been the investigation of applicability
of Subset Simulation in vulnerability analysis and in conjunction with that the
estimation of analytical fragility curves for 2-span highway RC box girder bridge.
To do so, 4 fragility curves based on 2 simulation techniques, Subset Simulation and
Crude Monte Carlo, respectively, were conducted and compared.
The validation of the chosen advanced simulation technique, namely Subset
Simulation (Au and Beck 2003), was initially examined by simple example of
slope stability, where the results from this method proved to be unbiased and
they converged to the reference case computed by Monte Carlo. This veriﬁcation
was conducted in terms of probability of failure, and the representative example
reached a probability of failure approximately 10-5.
Additionally the accuracy of Subset Simulation was investigated in regards of its
statistical parameters, namely, coeﬃcient of variation and standard deviation. The
coeﬃcient of variation is considered to be the most signiﬁcant parameter associated
to accuracy due to the Law of large numbers. In Subset Simulation was observed a
trend of decreasing coeﬃcient of variation, when reaching lower failure probabilities,
which is an additional signiﬁcant aspect to use this approach instead of Crude Monte
Carlo, with the opposite trend.
Subsequently after verifying the accuracy of the results, the comparison of the
required number of sample for both of these methods, was conducted and the results
proved that for the Subset Simulation is needed approximately 25[%] of the samples
required for the traditional Crude Monte Carlo method.
Since the Subset Simulation has been proved to be a valid approach in terms of
calculation the probability of failure, the next step in the investigation was to
implement it in the fragility analysis. Fragility curves for 2-span RC bridge were
constructed for 4 damage states by using Multiple Stripes Method and random
variables were introduced by Subset Simulation and subsequently compared with
the Crude Monte Carlo. Based on the results it can be concluded that the Crude
Monte Carlo cannot capture the results accurately, when the number of simulations
is lower than the required one. Inaccuracy of Monte Carlo caused deviation of the
results and led to the underestimated probabilities of failure.
On the other hand, the coeﬃcient of variation for the fragility curves for 3 damage
states (DS1,DS2,DS3) was lower, when Monte Carlo method was used. Although

in the fourth case the eﬃciency in terms of coeﬃcient of variation has been proved
for Subset Simulation, it has to be noted that the last limit state has the biggest
amount of Subset levels, and also the lowest probabilities of failure.
However, it has to be mentioned that even if the Subset Simulation has not been fully
employed, in terms that the samples did not propagate to the conditional levels, the
eﬃciency of this method has been proved. For instance, for the ﬁrst damage state
only Monte Carlo stage was used and no conditional probabilities of failure were
computed. But since the algorithm for Subset Simulation works with the stopping
criterion and the generation of new samples is controlled, the amount of the samples
was decreased while the probabilities of failure converged.
Overall, by implementing the Subset simulation (SuS) to the fragility analysis
one can eﬀectively reduce the number of simulations 60 [%], so subsequently the
computational time was decreased.
7.1
Further studies
Since there is an inaccuracy in the results of fragility curve associated with the Crude
Monte Carlo (CMC) method, the Subset simulation could not be veriﬁed in some
levels, because the Crude Monte Carlo cannot be considered as a reference method
for these levels. This indicates that more simulations by using Crude Monte Carlo
(CMC) should be done for further investigation, since bigger amount of simulations
per Peak Ground acceleration level will ensure convergence of the estimator to the
true value.
In regards to fragility analysis, it could be useful to include more random variables.
As far as currently, the probabilistic model consists only from material characteristics
associated with concrete and reinforcing steel, it is of interest for further investigation
to include the uncertainties related to elastomeric bearings or abutments that play
an important role in bridge design.
One more aspect that one may look into is a diﬀerent selection of GM for Multiples
Stripes Analysis. In order to reduce scaling one needs to make an additional selection
of GM for each IM discrete value. Meaning, that one can ensure that none of the
real GM will be scaled more than of predeﬁned scaling factor. Each discrete value
IM will correspond to distinct suite of GM.
