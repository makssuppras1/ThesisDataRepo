Abstract
Using remote sensing for Earth observation is an ever­expanding part of the commercial­
ized satellite technology sector, in which GOMspace is a key player in CubeSat technology
development. The economy in the market of Earth observation data is estimated to rise to
$11 trillion through the next 20 years, where most markets use data from optical payloads.
This project describes the preliminary design of a CubeSat with an optical payload, which
will suffice the versatility needed, to solve a multitude of tasks in the Earth observation
sector, in order to provide the market a solution that can be produced fast and cheap.
Through the work of a systems engineer, the preliminary design has been developed, for
the integration of a COTS VNIR optical payload, in a GOMspace 16U CubeSat platform,
including all the necessary avionics, to perform an Earth observation mission.
Furthermore, the capabilities have been analyzed, using an example mission, that will
track the development of biodiversity in Scandinavia.

1
Introduction
Through the development of remote sensing technology, Earth observation studies have
become more accessible, but have been reserved in the past for large governmental en­
tities, like NASA and ESA, as space missions have been very expensive.
The industry of space technology has undergone a commercialization, and it has become
more obvious to private companies, that there is a lot to gain, when including observations
only possible through remote sensing, into their respective field of business.
This has allowed for many smaller companies to join the space technology industry, either
by developing equipment that will be able to do remote sensing, buying and processing
data from remote sensing, or by using processed data to gain insight otherwise unavail­
able.
CubeSat technology, which has been a major game­changer for the commercialization of
the space industry, is a streamlined satellite technology that is based on commercial­off­
the­shelf (COTS) parts, and the design will have to comply with some standards. As long
as it does, it is allowed to fill it with as much technology, as possible.
In the beginning, a CubeSat was a 1U, 10x10x10cm, satellite, hence the name, and was
originally a way for university students to get hands­on experience with developing satellite
missions, as they were small and did not need their own separate launch. This meant,
that students could create their own missions, at a fraction of the cost of a large mission.
GOMspace, a private company located in Aalborg, Denmark, saw the opportunities that
the CubeSat technology brought, and are developing CubeSat solutions, on an order ba­
sis, for other companies, as well as governmental entities.
The CubeSat technology is still developing, and the possibilities are expanding.
For
GOMspace, the largest satellites have been 6­12U, but as CubeSat technology devel­
ops, larger constructions are becoming possible.
Being that the possibility of a 16U CubeSat construction has emerged, GOMspace would
like to investigate the possibilities of developing a satellite with an optical payload, that
will be able to do Earth observation.
Ultimately, the desire for GOMspace is to have a solution ready, for possible customers,
that has a high versatility in possible applications in which it can operate, allowing for mul­
tiple customers to use the same solution.
This project is the process of how such a solution is found, by studying how Earth obser­
vation technology and ­data is used throughout the market and the benchmark of CubeSat
technology for optical payloads, in order to find a versatile camera.
Upon finding a feasible imager, that can perform for a variety of missions, the imager
should be integrated into a GOMspace platform, and the performance will be analyzed.
The task has been completed through a systems engineering approach, where a market
analysis has built the foundation for the decision of an imager, and upon having found what
imager to integrate, the preliminary design of a GOMspace platform has been made.
The entire system is evaluated by a use­case example mission, that has been used both
for requirements determination and capability analysis, but the main objective for the
project is to build a versatile solution.

1.1
Project Aim
As the aim for this project is to find a COTS optical imager, and integrate it into a GOMspace
structure, in order to have a versatile solution that will be able to take on a multitude of
Earth observation (EO) tasks.
In order to do so, a systems engineering approach has been used, and as the project has
developed it has been reviewed at different points of the project lifetime, to ensure the
right progress.
1.1.1
Project Phases
The project has more or less been separated into six phases, following the schedule of
figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: The schedule for the development of the project, including the reviews that
have been done throughout
The intended first review was the Literature Study Review. In the literature study, the state
of the art of CubeSat­ and EO technology, were to be investigated, as well as how EO
data is used. This was to be concluded with a report of the findings. The key findings
have been included in the market analysis in this report.
The next review was the Mission Requirement Review, where objectives of a possible
mission, based on the state­of­the­art disclosed in the Literature study, were reviewed.
The key mission requirements, as well as a concept of operations for the mission, were
to be reviewed, to aid the analysis of system requirements.
The following was the System Requirements Review. Having insight into the state­of­the­
art, from the literature study, as well as the mission requirements, the system requirements
could be made. These were to be reviewed at the systems requirements review.
Ultimately, the former reviews was to lead to the Mid­term Review and Design Review,
where all the former stages should be used for the final design, first on a high­level basis,
and then a detailed design basis, respectively.
It ought to be mentioned, that this intended project progress has not developed in such a
strict manner, and some of the phases were skipped only to be revisited at a later time,
but all the phases have been undertaken, and concluded.

1.2
Project Structure
The main focus for the project has been to integrate a COTS imager into
a GOMspace CubeSat platform, using existing GOMspace products.
Finding a versatile imager to move forward with, has been the primary
task. The design of the satellite subsystems, using GOMspace prod­
ucts has been secondary, and the details on the mission proposed, have
been tertiary, and mainly used to aid the decisions on the system design.
To do so, the project has been divided into five phases, as can be seen in
figure 1.2, comprising the initial need research and the market research,
which were the foundation for the considerations, which led to the de­
sign. Having a design, the capabilities of the proposed solution could be
analyzed.
Initial Need
The initial­need­level, has been a research of what has been done with
CubeSat technology, and what the EO market looks like, to gain insight
into the needs.
Research
The needs were then used as an indicator for the market analysis, where
the state­of­the­art for COTS imagers was investigated, as well as how
other companies use EO data.
Considerations
All of this, led to a list of considerations, that was used to narrow down
what imagers, found in the research, would be suitable for the project.
Design of platform
Having found an imager to move forward with, the platform design could
proceed, where mainly GOMspace products were used, but where they
could not suffice, other parts have been included.
Capabilities
And last, alongside the platform design, from the application analysis,
a mission statement has been developed, as seen with the Example
Mission following an adjacent path, to assist the capability study of the
final platform design. As mentioned above, the mission has been used
to aid the requirements determination, but is a tertiary focus.

1.3
GOMspace and CubeSat Technology
This project has been developed in collaboration with GOMspace, which is a nanosatellite
company based in Aalborg, Denmark. They supply academic, governmental, and com­
mercial markets with systems integration solutions, nanosatellite platforms, and miniatur­
ized radio technology.
GOMspace was founded in 2007 and has only grown since, now estimated to own a
market share of 15% in the nanosatellite manufacturing sector, and have subsidiaries in
multiple countries across 3 continents. [GomSpace 2019]
1.3.1
CubeSat Technology
The technology on which GOMspace is based, is the concept of CubeSats, which is a
type of nanosatellite that has a standardized size, form, and weight.
The main idea is that, if a standardized size and weight are used, the cost of the launch
can be reduced significantly, as it is possible to buy in on a launch of some other primary
payload.
This is referred to as a piggyback payload, where the satellite, if designed properly, will
fit in the Poly Picosatellite Orbital Deployer (P­POD) [Johnstone 2020]. The P­POD is
the system that releases the CubeSat into orbit, and the design of the CubeSat has to
perfectly fit into the P­POD to ensure the safety of other payloads.
Hence, the CubeSat has to comply with some restrictions, of which the restrictions that
are essential for this project, are the size and weight.
As stated in the CubeSat Design Specification documentation, from Cal Poly, the standard
is based on units (U), and the number of U’s used for the design of the satellite, determines
the size and weight allowed. [Johnstone 2020]
The standard is based on a 1U CubeSat, which must be a 100mm3 cube with a maximum
weight of 2 kg.
Thus, when increasing the size to 2U, 6U, or 12U, as seen in figure 1.4, the allowed
weight will follow, and a 12U must thereby weigh no more than 24 kg. The weight and
size restrictions can be seen in tables 1.3a, and 1.3b.

1.4
Systems Engineering
This project has been developed using a systems engineering approach, using tools like
the hierarchy of requirements and the V­model.
Russel Ackhoff, a professor and pioneer in systems engineering, said; ”A system is a
whole that cannot be divided into independent parts without losing its essential charac­
teristics as a whole [...] when performance of each part taken separately is improved, the
performance of the system as a whole may not be, and usually isn’t”. [Gill 2015]
Systems engineering is a process to use when designing large or complex systems. It is
the art of steering a project, that involves many different aspects, as well as being able
to communicate said project, to the key individuals, that are interested, as stakeholder
management is one of the important tasks of a systems engineer. [Lohrey n.d.]
Stakeholder management is important, as it helps to determine the constraints of the
project, as well as the needs.
Stakeholders can be both people or objects, who have an interest in, are affected by, or
will have an impact on the project, and the main stakeholder for this project is GOMspace.
Other stakeholders are vaguely present, and have naturally been taken into account,
passive and active, but have not been described further.
1.4.1
The Process of Systems Engineering
”Design is based on requirements. There’s no justification for designing something one
bit ”better” than the requirements dictate.” [Gill 2015]
This statement is the foundation of one of the first steps of a design process. The systems
engineer will have to identify the requirements for the project, both to gain an overview of
the system, but just as well to try to prevent possible failures that might occur down the
line.
The Hierarchy of Requirements
The requirement analysis can be done in a top­down manner, using the hierarchy of re­
quirements model as seen in figure 1.5. [Gill 2015]
The top­level, the need, is what to accomplish, when the project is done, in essence, this
is the foundation for doing the project.
The immediate next step down is the mission statement, which is how one can fulfill the
need. I.e. what should be done to solve the problem, that is stated by the need.
To solve the issues in the mission statement, the stakeholders and their requirements are
used. This is where the stakeholders of the project, should be analyzed, and considered
in regards to how the mission statement can be met.
In short, every time one is to move down one step, a solution is provided to a problem
stated at a higher level, which is illustrated in figure 1.5 with the ”What?”­needs­to­be­
accomplished and ”How?”­is­the­problem­solved arrows.
The first three top­steps, which is the high­level requirements, are the non­technical fields
of requirements to determine, and the technical requirements, the system, and subsys­
tem requirements go into what the system should be able to do, to meet the stakeholder
requirements, mission statement, and ultimately the need.

In order to ensure that the project is progressing correctly and that all stakeholders are

The Mission baseline, reviewed at the Mission Definition Review (MDR) has to fully
define the stakeholder­affirmed mission needs and ­objectives, and how to meet these.
The System baseline, reviewed at the System Requirements Review (SRR) is to de­
scribe the functional­ and performance requirements of the system, further characterizing
the systems to perform the mission concept.
The Functional baseline, reviewed at the System Definition Review (SDR) will include
the system design, as well as the functional­ and performance requirements of the system
elements.
The Design­to baseline, reviewed at the Preliminary Design Review (PDR) is the fully
defined system design, that has the component specifications, software specifications,
ground support equipment specifications (applies for space projects), verification plan,
and manufacturing plan.
The Build­to baseline, reviewed at the Critical Design Review (CDR) has the complete
system design, described by CAD models, software design documents, and plans for
fabrication, assembly, integration, and test.
The As­built baseline, reviewed at the System Acceptance Review (SAR) shows the
system as verified, validated, and ready for deployment.
The As­deployed, reviewed at the Operational Readiness Review (ORR) and Post Launch
Assessment Review, PLAR, with the system launched or otherwise deployed, checked

3.2.2
Spectral Signatures
Since passive detectors are being considered, and image sensors generally work by mea­
suring the amount of incoming light at certain wavelengths, it should be mentioned that all
objects, will either absorb or reflect incoming solar radiation. The amount of light reflected
off of an object will vary with the wavelength. This is why it is possible to distinguish cover
types from one another when capturing an image, and the characteristics of the amount of
radiation reflected by an object, in a specific wavelength is called the spectral signature,
spectral response, or reflectance.
Comparing response patterns, it is possible to distinguish between different types of fea­
tures in the scene, as can be seen in figure 3.2.
The figure shows the spectral signature of the most common types of cover, spanning
through the visible wavelengths, to the infrared. It is seen that, though water and vegeta­
tion reflect somewhat similarly in the visible wavelengths (0.4­0.8µm), vegetation is much
brighter through the infrared spectrum.
Figure 3.2: The spectral response curve of the most common types of cover
Source: Zeeshan, R.R., and Shafiuddin 2017
The spectral response differs, not only between materials, but within the same sort of
material, depending on features, and it is thereby necessary to understand what type of
spectral response one is interpreting.
But with a knowledge of the characteristics of cover types, which can be determined ex­
perimentally or otherwise, it is possible to identify materials in an otherwise unfamiliar
area. In figure 3.2, the spectral response curves of dry soil, vegetation, and water are
shown.
Water, when it is clear, will absorb radiation at longer wavelengths, and look dark, whereas
it will reflect radiation of the shorter blue­green visible spectrum, and will appear blue­ish.
If there are sediment or algae in the water, the reflectance will appear different, as algae,
for instance, comprise chlorophyll which absorbs more of the radiation in the blue spec­
trum, and reflects in the green, making the water appear greener.

Besides sediment and algae, the roughness of the water surface, due to waves or floating

Vegetation in general contains chlorophyll, which mainly reflects in the green spectrum,

3.2.3
Resolution
Another important element, when talking images, are the image sensor’s resolution. In
regards to remote sensing, there are three different types of resolution that have been
considered in this project; Spatial­, spectral­, and temporal resolution.
Spatial resolution is the area covered by each pixel. For a high spatial resolution, one
might think of it as a high ”smallness” possible, for objects in the image. Meaning that a
high spatial resolution allows distinguishing between smaller separate objects.
The spatial resolution depends on the sensor’s capabilities and the distance from the
object, i.e. the height of the satellite.
Spectral resolution is the number of electromagnetic bands in which the imager can
measure. A high number of narrow bands will give a finer image, while fewer, wider
bands will provide a coarser image.
I.g. If the imager has many narrow bands, it will be able to determine more detailed scene
elements.
Temporal resolution has nothing to do with the sensor capabilities, but the repeat time
of the imager’s capturing of the same scene. High temporal resolution would mean a high
frequency at which the same area was captured, while a low temporal resolution would
mean the scene would be captured less frequently. [Zeeshan, R.R., and Shafiuddin 2017]
The temporal resolution needed depends on the mission type.

HS images are not images in the traditional form, but rather an image spectroscopy. Fig­

The advantage of using HS imaging is the large amount of data, that will disclose infor­

Mission basis
According to ”Remote Sensing of Plant Biodiversity” [Cavender­Bares, Gamon, and Townsend

the diversity of traits, using remote sensing.
It is stated that approximately 1 in 5 seed plants and 1 in 6 tree species are threatened.
[Cavender­Bares, Gamon, and Townsend 2020]
The trees are mainly threatened due to a change in land use and forestry, but climate
change and disease are ever­increasing factors as well, whereas plant species are at
risk due to a combination of global change factors like drought stress, exotic species,
application of chemicals, and over­exploitation.
It seems self­explanatory that a high diversity is important to the well­being of our planet,
as every single living organism has a role to play.
An ecosystem that is rich in biodiversity has a long line of positive effects, as well as a

4.2
Mission Requirements and ­Constraints
As this example mission has not been a primary focus of the project, only the requirements
and constraints that have directly influenced the design of the satellite are mentioned.
Much of the research for the requirements have been made by inspiration from the book
”Spacecraft Systems Engineering”. [Fortescue, Swinderd, and Stark 2011]
The first requirement has to do with the imager resolution.
In order to obtain useful data, the resolution of the imager will have to be quite high, both
spatially, and spectrally, to gain enough detail.
The spectral capabilities need to extend outside of the visual spectrum, as most radiation
from vegetation is reflected in the NIR spectrum, thus an MS or HS imager is needed.
Furthermore, the spatial resolution should be medium or high, to get enough detail, but
the temporal resolution, given that the desire is to get the development in the variability,
does not need to be high.
Besides this, the aim is to monitor the development of the biodiversity through the years,
which would affect the orbit height.
The orbit height, however, is constrained by the imager capabilities, as most imagers are
developed to function in a LEO at approximately 500­600 km. Thus, a sweet spot, that
complies with the operational height, and enables a long lifetime, will have to be found,
but this will be part of the future works, as it has been outside of the scope of the project.
4.2.1
Orbit determination
Two main constraints have aided the determination of the orbit.
One constraint is that the payload needs the reflection of solar radiation, to capture the
data.
A second constraint is that a semi­high resolution is desired to get enough detail.
Thus, a sun­synchronous Low Earth Orbit is desirable
The need for a low Earth orbit (LEO) is due to the resolution possibilities of the payload,
and when using a LEO it is necessary to have a near 90◦inclination to get near­global
coverage. This coverage is needed, as it is not possible to single out an area as large as
Scandinavia.

4.2.2
More Requirements
Besides the orbit and the inclination, some other parameters should be considered as
requirements for the mission.
Mission Lifetime
As mentioned above, the orbit height should satisfy the requirement for the resolution of
the imager, but as the mission lifetime is limited by the initial orbit height, this should be
considered as well.
Due to drag, a satellite in LEO will eventually re­enter the atmosphere, terminating the
mission. Upon determination of the number of years, the satellite should be in operation,
the height necessary should be calculated, but this will be put as a task in future works.
Coverage
The coverage of the area of interest is one of the main drivers for the mission. This will
depend on the swath of the imager, as well as the orbit.
For this mission, it is of interest to determine the time it will take to image the entirety of
Scandinavia, and it has been assumed, that upon revisit, of the lowest point of latitude, in
Scandinavia, the entire area has been covered.
Revisit­time, or coverage gap, is the time between the visibility of the same geographical
point, i.e. how long it will take the satellite to fly over the exact same spot.
Communication
Last, when the satellite has to communicate with the ground station(s) (GS), it needs to
be able to ”see” the GS. For simplicity, as instructed by the supervisors of this project, the
common practice is to use only one ground station for missions like this.
The ground station should be visible from the satellite, and will technically be visible as
soon as it has risen above the horizon, but due to the attenuation of the signal through the
atmosphere, the assumption is made, that the GS is visible when the satellite elevation is
greater than 5% above the horizon.
This visibility can be calculated to give an amount of time, the satellite will have to downlink
the captured data, and/or receive updates or commands from the GS.
STK
Many of these requirements calculations can be done through the use of a simulation
program called STK, which is used for many major projects, in the engineering world, and
for this project as well.
Using STK, it is possible to input the orbit type and height, as well as sensor properties,
areas of interest, and GSs.
Having all of this, STK will be able to deliver good­looking orbital visuals in 2D and 3D
and do the analysis of coverage, and visibility, which is then output in text files, with data
like specific times of visibility and how long each passing will take.
Power Supply and Eclipse Period
The power supply capabilities for the satellite, which correlates with the eclipse time of
the orbit, will have to be taken into account, both as constraints and as requirements.
The eclipse time can be determined by some fairly simple equations given in the book,
Spacecraft Systems Engineering [Fortescue, Swinderd, and Stark 2011, pp. 118­120],
but as GOMspace have developed a tool to do these calculations, regarding the eclipse
time from the mission parameters, and the depth­of­discharge for the batteries depending
on the mission phases, this tool has been used instead.
The size of the solar arrays should also be determined based on the mission parameters,
whether fixed arrays or deployable arrays are preferred.

5.1.1
Deployment
The deployment phase covers the time from P­POD detachment, till the CubeSat is in
the orbit, with the desired attitude, ready to operate. The actual deployment has been
excluded, and even though it has not been possible to seek out a source for this informa­
tion, the project supervisor, Daniel Pino, has disclosed that one should expect a possible
satellite tumbling of up to 10 deg/sec, after deployment.
Detumbling
As the satellite is released into its orbit, it might tumble around either of its axes, and it
will need detumbling in order to ever be ready for operation. This can be done in dif­
ferent ways, using propellant­based or non­propellant­based techniques, where a non­
propellant­based technique, using the Earths magnetic field, is a very widely used way
in the CubeSat industry. This is mainly due to the fact, that propellant­based solutions
take up a lot of space, which is undesirable for a CubeSat, due to the size and weight
restrictions.
How to use the Earth’s magnetic field is further described in section 6.1.5.
Position and attitude determination
Before it is possible to move into the operational phases of the mission, it is necessary
to figure out where the satellite is located and make sure that it is directed towards the
Earth. This is done using an Attitude Determination and Control System (ADCS).
Just like for detumbling, this can be done in a propellant­ or non­propellant­based way and
using the same argument as in the detumbling, usually, a non­propellant­based solution
is chosen, based on mechanics and magnetism.
A major advantage to this solution, is the near­unlimited supply, as they run on power
drawn from the electrical power management system, where propellant­based solutions
would be limited and will stop functioning when the propellant runs dry.
5.1.2
Operation
When the satellite has been detumbled, and the position and attitude are determined, it
proceeds into the operation phases, which is the primary focus of this project, and where
the satellite will spend most of its lifetime. This has been divided into four sub­phases, as
each has special focus areas.
Standby/TT&C
The first state of the operational phase is the standby phase, where most subsystems will
run on the lowest power possible, as to, for instance, let the batteries recharge.
The entire system, however, will not run on standby in this phase, as a constant com­
munications link, called Telemetry, Tracking & Command (TT&C), is necessary to ensure
knowledge of the position, attitude, and status of the satellite, also called housekeeping
data.
The housekeeping data lets the people on the ground know that the health of satellite is
well and that it is functioning properly.
This phase is also the standard go­to phase, when the satellite is not actively perform­
ing an operation, implied by the arrows pointing backward from the Data handling and
Downlink phases, in figure 5.1.
Imaging and Data Handling
When the satellite passes over the area of interest, it goes into the imaging phase, where
the optical payload performs its operation and sends the data to a storage unit.
It has been divided into two sections, mainly because the imaging phase needs a lot of
subsystems active, to know the location and pointing, but also as this phase always leads
into the data handling phase, whereas the data handling phase needs the aid of fewer
subsystems, and has the ability to either downlink just moments after the image data has

When the data has been fully downlinked, or the GS is not visible, the satellite will go into
the standby phase, until it can once again proceed with imaging or downlinking.

The requirements stated above provided a new list of requirements, and the system de­

Most of the decisions for the design have been made through a trade­off process, which
is a classic systems engineering tool, that is used to consider many different solutions,
to ultimately end up with a solution that is feasible and will satisfy the requirements and
constraints.
As a systems engineer, the common practice is to not invent everything from the bottom
up, but to do research, gain an understanding of how others have accomplished similar
tasks, and use this to end up with a functional design.
The trade­off process is a series of steps that can be used in high­level­, as well as low­
level design processes, where the aim is to find the most feasible of all the possible solu­
tions that were found during the research phase. The process can be seen in figure 5.2
[Gill 2015]

As a start for the trade­off, the systems engineer will research all the possibilities, that
may or may not work. In the ”Eliminating concepts that won’t work phase”, the systems
engineer will list all the possible options that can solve the desired task.
A task can be high­level, where many subsystems are included superficially, and prefer­
ably with a straw­man for each concept, but it can just as well be a subsystem analysis,
where a straw­man would be unnecessary, thus the first two steps are skipped.
If a solution has no concept, i.e. no possible straw­man to sketch, they can be immediately
eliminated, just as solutions that are obviously non­feasible can be eliminated.
Following these steps will leave a list of the solutions found in the research, that could
possibly solve the task, which will be the list of ”maybes”.
Next, the ones that are not yet ready to be further analyzed or developed, will be added
to a list of ”future” possibilities, making sure that they are not lost. The options still left will
be analyzed for their weaknesses, and listed as feasible candidates, which will conclude
the ”Eliminating concepts that won’t work” phase.
At the end of the process, the feasible candidates will be weighed against each other,
which will ultimately lead to a conceptual design, that will meet the needs of the project,
or a solution to the task at hand.

6.1.1
Payload
The payload of the system, is the heart of the satellite, as it is the main instrument.
For this mission, the payload will be a passive optical sensor that will capture the sun
radiation, reflected off of the surface of the Earth, and it should be able to do this, and

The parameters of the payload have been described in section 7, where the payload has

6.1.2
Communication
The satellite should be able to communicate with the ground station, and will thereby have
to have a communications subsystem.
Generally for communication, a radio, that handles the signal, a front­end, which comprise
all the circuitry between the radio and antenna, and an antenna, is needed.
For this mission, the satellite will need three different links, which are the constant com­
munications link, a high­speed communications link, and a powerful downlink for the data
from the payload.
Constant communication
The satellite should have a link, that should preferably be constantly reachable, indepen­
dent of the orientation and position of the satellite, i.e. it should have an omni­directional
radiation pattern, and should use as little power as possible, to spare the EPS.
This will ensure that the satellite is not lost, due to something like tumbling, and should
be used for housekeeping data communication, and simple commands.
High­speed communication
Should it be necessary to upload software updates, as for instance, the satellite should
have a link for uploading more data, than possible for the constant communication. The
orientation of the radiation pattern is less important, as the satellite will be able to change
attitude upon request, and point the antenna towards the ground station.
Downlink
The data from the payload has to be downlinked to the GS, as the data would otherwise be
inaccessible. Since the amount of data can be quite much, and the time the GS is visible
for the satellite is limited, a powerful downlink communications module is necessary.
This will, just like the high­speed communication, not need a specific radiation pattern,
just the correct pointing.

The OBC will be the brain of the operation. It will take inputs from sensors, and output
commands to the entire system, making sure that everything is going according to sched­
ule.
The OBC will also receive information about the status of the other subsystems, known as
the housekeeping data, and communicate this through a downlinking path, to the ground­
station­personnel, in order to ensure that the satellite is healthy, and the pointing and
position are as it should be.

6.1.4
EPS
The EPS makes sure that all subsystems has the sufficient power delivered. In order
to do so, it has to comprise a primary power supply, a secondary power supply (power
storage), as well as a power management distribution and control system, that will properly
distribute the correct voltage to the subsystems, for them to function.
Primary Power supply
For satellites, the power is supplied through solar arrays. These arrays can either be
attached along the sides of the satellite or as deployable wings, that will deploy during
flight. Using deployable solar arrays will allow for the possibility of having sun­tracking
solar arrays, that will angle themselves towards the sun, automatically, whereas if one is
to use side­mounted arrays, the satellite can be covered on most surfaces.
There is a trade­off to decide on. Generally, solar arrays differ in efficiency by approxi­
mately 40%, depending on the tilt angle of the panel towards the sun, spanning from a
flat tilt through a 90­degree tilt, with a 30% tilt being optimal. [Bas 2010]
With this in mind, solar panels attached to the sides of the satellite might be sufficient,
and with less movable parts, i.e. the need for deployment, the risk reduces.
Though, if the satellite needs more power than what can be supplied through side­attached
arrays, the improved efficiency of the deployable panels is a necessity.
But this is to be determined when the power­need has been estimated.
Batteries
Depending on the mission, the satellite will experience a solar eclipse of a certain duration.
During solar eclipses, or at other times when the primary power supply can not deliver the
necessary amount of power to the system, the satellite will depend on batteries.
For instance, if the satellite is in a geostationary orbit, the eclipses will be short and scarce,
whereas a LEO operation will have the satellite experience an eclipse that will last longer,
and occur at each orbit.
It has more or less become the standard to use Li­Ion batteries, as Li­Ion batteries have
high specific energy (90 W h/kg), and a high depth of discharge (DoD) capability (∼80%)
at a large number of cycles.
As this project concerns itself with the design of a CubeSat, the considerations for the
batteries will depend on the size and DoD capabilities. Larger batteries would result in a
lower DoD capability, but as mentioned in section 1.3.1, size is a major constraint.
6.1.5
ADCS
The ADCS can be divided into two sections. The determination of the attitude, and the
control of it.
The determination depends on the measurement from sensors, and calculations in the
OBC, to determine the pointing of the satellite. The calculations are called control algo­
rithms, which are complex algorithms, that calculate the error between the desired attitude
and the input from the sensors.
Attitude Determination
The attitude cannot be determined using any single measurement, and a need for multiple
sensors is thereby necessary.
Sensors commonly used for attitude determination are horizon sensors, sun sensors,
Earth sensors, and star trackers, as well as gyroscopes and magnetometers.
Attitude Correction
As the attitude has been determined, a correction might be needed, which is where the
control system comes in, and the common practice is to use actuators to do so.
Actuators are torque appliers, that, when used, are capable of rotating the satellite. This
Preliminary Design and Integration of a COTS Optical Payload in a CubeSat Platform
41
==PAGE:54==
can be done using something like reaction/momentum wheels, mass movement, and
magnetic torquers.
Operation­Considerations of the ADCS
Two events have to be taken into account, in the operations of the satellite. These are
the detumbling of the deployed satellite, and the desaturation of reactions wheels when
used for these corrections.
Detumbling
When the satellite is deployed it might tumble with as much a 10 deg/s and will have to
be stabilized.
This can be done, when in LEO, using the BDOT algorithm, which is a detumbling method
that uses the Earth’s magnetic field, as well as a constellation of magnetorquers.
BDOT Algorithm
To perform the BDOT algorithm maneuver, the satellite should be equipped with a mag­
netometer, that will measure the instantaneous direction of the magnetic field twice in a
short time spacing, as well as magnetorquers that are placed directionally independant.
The 3­dimensional change in the direction of the B­field, relative to the satellite, will be
used to calculate the tumbling, and a current will be applied to each of the three mag­
netorquers. The magnetorquers will interact with the Earth’s magnetic field to create an
opposite torque on the satellite, to the one applied by the tumbling, to slow down the ro­
tation.
This process is iterated over and over again until the satellite is detumbled. [ece3sat n.d.]
Desaturation of Reaction Wheels
The functionality of reaction wheels, which is a good actuator to use for attitude correction,
relies on the rotational speed of the reaction wheels. When they are sped up, the satellite
will start rotating due to angular momentum. The problem with reaction wheels is that the
torque accumulates, and the wheels will keep increasing the RPM, to keep the satellite
steady, and at some point, they will be saturated (Unable to gain any more momentum).
To desaturate them, or slow them down, without applying a negative momentum, an ex­
ternal force will have to be applied, while they slow their rotation. This could be done by
the use of, for instance, magnetorquers. [Reaction Wheel 2021]

to be taken into account as well.

The GOMspace Power Budget Tool
The power­budget tool is a tool developed by GOMspace employees, that allows for es­

It is a spreadsheet made up of multiple sheets, each interacting to provide power usage
analysis results and graphs. (The sheets can be seen in appendix D)
It is a much more powerful tool, than what this project has needed, but it has been amazing
to estimate the power production necessary to drive the system, and the way the tool has
been used for this project are as follows:
Power Budget Tool Functionality
• Orbit Definition: The user input the orbit parameters, and the tool calculates the time
spend in sunlight and eclipses, respectively
• Power Consumption ­ Components: All power­consuming components, and how
much power they consume, is listed, and the tool will provide a feedback of the
overall consumption, on a system and subsystem level
• Modes: Like the CONOPS, all the possible modes, the satellite will be in during the
mission duration, is made.
Parts that will be active, during each mode is input, as well as the expected power
production from the solar panels possible in that mode, and the modes will then be
used for the orbit analysis
• Orbit Analysis: The user marks which modes, the satellite will go through, in the
order the satellite is expected to change between them during one orbit, and for
how long it will stay in each mode. Furthermore, the initial DoD is input, and the tool
outputs how much power has been consumed and how much has been produced
throughout the orbit
The interesting function of the tool is the consumption vs. production through the orbit.
The acceptable DoD of the batteries is 20%. This is a GOMspace standard estimate, that
will ensure that the batteries will function troughout the mission lifetime.
The aim for using this tool is to figure out whether the satellite will keep discharging, or if
the charge vs. discharge is stable.

The satellite will be lined with body­mounted arrays, and the estimates made in the budget
tool have been done with both one deployable array added, and two.
The estimates of the power production, are based on earlier 6U designs (The numbers
used, have been provided by the GOMspace supervisors).
Using the body­mounted arrays and a single deployable solar array, the power production
possible, when pointing towards the sun, is 20.7W, whereas it is 29.6W for two deploy­
able arrays.
During operation (Imaging or pointed communication), having an angle to the sun, pro­
duction is 9­12W for a single deployable array, and 12­16W for two.
These estimates have been used, with the battery capability of the single battery pack,
in the power budget tool, to reveal that, in order to ensure that the satellite will recharge
during its operational cycle, and not drop below the allowed limit of 20% for DoD, two
deployable arrays are needed.
Of cause, this is based on a worst­case scenario, where the xScape200 will operate for
as long as possible during the orbit, and the XT8250 will downlink at the average time of
GS visibility, right after.
Thus, two NanoPower DSP (Deployable Solar Panels) have been decided on, alongside
the side­mounted NanoPower MSP (Modular Solar Panels).
This will allow for a production average of approximately 28W, according to the provided
data estimates, which can be seen in appendix E.

8.3
Final ConOps
Knowing the exact parts of the subsystems, the ConOps that was initially sketched up can
be expanded into a detailed ConOps, including the subsystem­parts and their respective
tasks.
As stated in section 5.1, the ConOps is a tool used to gain an overview of the project.
The ConOps can be seen in figure 8.2, where the arrows at the top show what phase the
satellite will go into, after the current phase.
The solid arrows show the planned next phase, and the dotted arrows show a possible
next phase, should it be necessary, as explained in section 5.1, with the initial ConOps.
The pointing­ and power subsystems, seen in the lower right­hand side of figure 8.2, will
be active in almost every phase, and their respective tasks have been described in the
subsystem design section.
Furthermore, the ConOps have been divided into three main phases, which are the de­
ployment phase, the mission duration phase, and the termination phase.
8.3.1
Deployment
The first phase is the deployment phase, where the satellite will detumble and determine
its attitude and position.
For the detumbling, the magnetometer and magnetorquers are used, to perform the BDOT
algorithm, as described in section 6.1.5.
54
Preliminary Design and Integration of a COTS Optical Payload in a CubeSat Platform
==PAGE:67==
As soon as the satellite is detumbled, it uses the sun sensor and star tracker, together with
some a­priori knowledge of the orbit from the mission design, to accurately determine the
position and attitude, after which, the satellite will enter into the mission duration phase.
8.3.2
Mission duration
During the mission duration, in which the satellite will spend most of its lifetime, the satellite
will cycle through four phases.
Standby/TT&C
In the standby phase, only the NanoMind A3200 (OBC) and AM2150­PS/P will really be
active.
The OBC, as it keeps track of everything, sends out commands, and reads the sensors for
housekeeping data. Furthermore, the OBC will be equipped with a Watch­Dog function,
which is a countdown, that will have to be reset through a command from the GS.
If communication with the satellite is not possible, it is assumed that some subsystem
needs rebooting, so if the countdown is not reset, the entire system will restart, hopefully
fixing the problem.
As the satellite needs the possibility for constant communication, independent of its atti­
tude, the AM2150­PS/P will be active, providing an Omni­directional radiation pattern.
And lastly, the ANT2000, for high­speed communication, will be active when needed.
Imaging
For imaging, the xScape200 will activate and start operating. The captured data will most
likely be stored locally in the payload, or be transferred to the SDR, which is able to handle
the data.
Data Handling
The data handling is included as a step, as there are still some future decisions to be
made, of whether the data should be stored locally, and for how long, or if there should
be some automatic algorithms to analyze the data on­board and only store images that
live up to some desired requirements.
The data handling could either be performed by storing the captured data in the SDR or
the xScape200.
Including this step, also better explains how, as the data might not be downlinked im­
mediately, the satellite could go into standby mode before moving into the downlinking
phase.
8.3.3
Downlinking
When the GS is visible, the data should be downlinked, through the XT8250. When the
GS is no longer visible, or all the data has been downlinked, the satellite will go back into
standby mode.
8.3.4
Termination
As the mission reaches the end of its lifetime, it will reach an altitude that will make it
reenter into the atmosphere, making it burn up due to the atmospheric friction.

Discussion
Much of the discussion for this project, have been done throughout the report, as many of
the decisions described throughout have been made upon the considerations, that would
have otherwise been part of the discussion, but some things have to be further considered.
10.1
Imager selection
As described in section 7, on the choice of payload, the imager has been chosen on the
basis that it would do splendidly, as a versatile solution, that could be used for a multitude
of applications in the market of EO.
The market analysis showed that almost all sectors that used value­added services, based
on remote sensing EO data in 2015, would use data from optical sensors in either VIS or
VNIR. The images was used for big data or GIS analysis, to gain insight that is otherwise
unavailable, or statistical purposes.
As the other considered imagers, in the final trade­off, would be able to perform for the
same applications, these should be kept in mind, should it be necessary to change the
payload. A change of payload would even be quite simple to do, as the subsystems would
be able to function with either of the imagers.
The xScape200 is a VNIR imager, that will be able to do MS imaging. The exact method
used for the MS imaging, what exact spectral bands it can capture, or what spectral reso­
lution can be obtained, has not been available in the datasheets, but it has been assumed
that the spectral sensitivity can be modified depending on the phenomena one would want
to observe.
Furthermore, due to the size of the xScape200, it was almost excluded from the trade­off.
It is just within the restrictions of a 12U CubeSat, so the immediate next step for the project,
in the future works, would be to analyse if the needed avionics will fit inside the 4Us that
is left in the structure. This could be done by the use of SolidWorks, where a database of
all GOMspace products, including dimensions and weight, have already been produced.
The GOMspace supervisors have deemed, based on their experience, that there is enough
margin in the structure, to fit the xScape200. Should it not be the case, either of the other
imagers should be reconsidered.
Last, the list of imagers that were found through the benchmark study, included a lot of
HS imagers as well, but these were deemed too advanced for the need of this project,
and not desired by the market. Should it, however, at some point be reconsidered, the
size of the data cubes, would make this project need a complete reconsideration for the
subsystems.

The example mission further aided the analysis of requirements for the system. If not for
a mission, there are a lot of loose ends and decisions, that can not be argued for, like the
orbit height and type, which affects the EPS and communication subsystem, to name a

Some considerations for the subsystem include whether or not the XT8250 transmitter

missions have used it with great success, and it is thereby assumed, that it will do just

Likewise, the remaining communication systems are tested and verified through other
missions, and is thereby assumed to be satisfying.
The ADCS and OBCs are robust solutions, that will be able to do exactly what they have
to, and would not need reconsideration, depending of the mission.
The OBCs as they just need to be simple computers, and are just that.
The ADCS, as whatever mission will be designed, they will be in the same realm regarding
the magnetic field, and access to the stars and sun.
The EPS will have to be reconsidered depending on the mission, as the eclipse­ and
sun­light times will most likely differ. The power distribution system and battery packs,
should suffice, but whether to use one deployable solar panel or two, and whether a sun
tracking panel might be needed, depends on the orbit modes, and this would therefore
need reconsideration.
The GOMspace budget tool does include a possibility of analysing multiple orbits, and
thereby different tasks performed in each orbit, but for this project, an orbit with both
imaging, data handling, and downlinking was considered, which spends as much power
as possible.
The last subsystem task that would need consideration is the data handling.
Through this project, it has been assumed that the data handling has simply been a local
storage in the payload, but the SDR does include a storage as well.
Furthermore, solutions of using AI to process the images in­flight has also appeared during

storage.

10.4
Feasibility
The feasibility will have to be considered from two points of view.
First whether or not the solution satisfies the initial project aim, and secondly if the solution
will satisfy the suggested example mission.
10.4.1
Initial Project Aim
As the initial project, that was agreed upon with GOMspace, was to design a solution that
could be used for a multitude of tasks, the solution has mainly been based on the market
analysis, which suggested, that using a MS imager, would suffice.
The main constraint regarding the capabilities, are that the camera should be able to fit
on a CubeSat, as the limitations of size and weight, naturally provides a limit of what is
possible with the technology available to this day.
The high GSD is tremendous in such a camera, but the swath is still in the low end, which
will create boundaries for the types of missions, the platform could be used for.
But through the market analysis, in the research of how remote sensing was used in the
value­added sectors, is was clear that this imager would be able to provide data for many
of the sectors researched in the market analysis.
10.4.2
Example mission
For the example mission, the requirements stated a need for a high spatial resolution, and
a medium to high spectral resolution.
The xScape200 does indeed live up to the requirement of a high spatial resolution, with
a GSD of 1.5 m, but whether or not the spectral resolution is met is not clear, as the
datasheet did not include these specifications.
The temporal resolution did not need to be high, only the mission lifetime, which was to
be determined on, as a future task.
The repeat time for the satellite, with a sun­synchronous orbit at a height of 500 km, and
a swath of 14 km, was found to be approximately 40 days for the lowest latitude of the
target areas, but due to issues with STK, many different estimates for the repeat time
were found, and this will have to be reevaluated as a future task.
Furthermore, monitoring the entirety of Scandinavia may not be possible with the found
solution.
As stated in section 7, about the xScape200, a quick estimate of the size of a square
image will be about 1.19GB, which will be 14 km by 14 km, just a smidgen of the entire
area of Scandinavia.
It is thereby assumed, that the internal storage of the xScape200 would only allow for
approximately 100 km of capturing, before needing downlinking, due to the 128GB internal
storage limit.
And, as each pass above a GS allows for a downlink of 9GB, if the entire pass is used for
downlinking, 14 passes is needed to downlink 128GB of imaging.
The camera does have the possibility of creating thumbnails, making it possible to pre­
view areas of interest before downlinking, and it should be considered whether to use
one or two GSs, and the location of them, but to caputure and downlink the entirety of
Scandinavia at each pass, does not seem feasible.
The mission would need tweaking, to maybe just capture some of the nature parks at every
second pass, and downlink whenever it is possible, but this needs much more analysis,
to find a solution as to make the mission feasible.

Conclusion
The intend of this project was to find an optical payload, that would suffice as a versatile
solution that would be able to take on many different tasks in the field of remote sensing
Earth observation.
Through a systems engineering approach, the preliminary design of an xScape200 mul­
tispectral imager, integrated into a GOMspace platform, has been made. The design
includes the subsystems, and what COTS parts should be used for each subsystem, as
well as what tasks they should perform.
Through a market analysis, it was found that a large part of the commercialized value­
added market could use the same type of data, which could be delivered by a medium­
to high resolution multispectral camera.
Four cameras, that could do just this, and fit in a CubeSat, have been listed as feasible
candidates, of which the xScape200 has been chosen as the most feasible.
The camera is just within the limits of what can be fit into a 12U structure, and a subsys­
tems pack, which is a very robust solution, has been successfully decided upon, for the
remaining 4Us, allocated for avionics.
The robustness of the subsystems means that it will be able to work with any of the feasible
cameras, though with a few considerations needed, as mentioned in the discussion.
As stated in the discussion, the final platform, using the xScape200, was not a feasible
solution for the use­case example mission, as the initial intention was to image all of
Scandinavia on a repeat basis, and tweaking is definitely necessary if one would want to
monitor the biodiversity of Scandinavia. It could possibly be done by choosing smaller
areas of interest and doing spot checks, but to map out the entirety would need a much
larger satellite.
The aim for the project, however, was not to design a satellite that could solve one specific
mission, but to find a camera that could perform many different tasks, and integrate this
into a GOMspace platform.
The imager does comply with the requirements put forward and would be able to perform
many other missions, that required imaging of smaller areas than Scandinavia. This, as
for instance, could be to aid a much larger mission that would do the monitoring of the
biodiversity, by doing reconnaissance of the area, to find areas that would need extra
attention.
It can thereby be concluded that most of the tasks of the preliminary design for the inte­
gration of a versatile optical payload, in a GOMspace platform, have successfully been
undertaken and finalized, and the design is thereby ready for the PDR.
