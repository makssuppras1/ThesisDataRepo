Abstract
Decision-making in Maintenance Management contains different decision levels, where
higher levels give guidelines for the following decision levels. These higher-level decisions
are commonly made under uncertain outcomes of the circumstances at the following level.
Therefore, there is a high motivation to integrate the uncertainty in the former decision-
making levels.
This thesis studies the decision-making problem of an industrial maintenance service
provider, where the tactical decision of assigning equipment to technicians is made un-
der the uncertainty of the operational circumstances. The operational uncertainties are
unplanned maintenance activities that occur stochastically into the plan, so neither the
task‚Äôs occurrence nor duration is known beforehand. These unplanned tasks additionally
induce uncertainty in the maintenance tours, as the technician must interrupt their route to
answer this unplanned task. The thesis, therefore, studies how to account for this uncer-
tainty when creating the assignments so that the negative consequences of such events
are minimised.
The problem is formalised through a Stochastic Program, where two alternatives for the
recourse are created. One of the recourse functions concludes in a Linear Stochastic
Program. In contrast, the other becomes a Stochastic Program with Recourse, where
the recourse is modelled as a multiple-visit Travelling Salesman Problem. The formalised
problem is solved using a metaheuristic, which is designed for solving large-scale prob-
lem instances of the industrial maintenance provider. The algorithm uses a combination
of Local Search and Large Neighbourhood Search to leverage principles of diversification
and intensification. The neighbourhood functions use a graph formulation to quantify the
qualitative empirical knowledge of the structure of optimal assignments, thereby creating
good candidate solutions. The uncertainty is modelled in the Metaheuristic through sim-
ulation, where depending on the recourse function, either just Monte-Carlo simulation is
used or a combination of Monte-Carlo simulation and Simulation Optimisation is used.
The objective is to minimise the imbalance in workload for the maintenance fleet and the
overtime. For the instances in the case study, the designed metaheuristic is able to im-
prove the objective for both recourse functions on average over 90 %. The two recourse
functions are studied, and it concluded that Simulation Optimisation, which modelled the
recourse decision, creates more reliable results. Solving this model created more bal-
anced workloads in terms of different workload types, had fewer negative effects on the
utilisation of the technicians and generally created smaller service districts compared to
the other recourse function.
Keywords: Maintenance Management, Stochastic Programming, Local Search, Large
Neighbourhood Search, Simulation Optimisation

Generally, the intuitive heuristic of the human mind is successful in creating good-quality
solutions to questions. Still, as addressed in Kahneman‚Äôs book 2012, when a problem
becomes complex enough, we tend to simplify the problem to answer the question. The
same work discusses the problem of algorithm-generated decisions. People have a ten-
dency to forgive human-made errors easier than machine-generated errors, even though
machine-generated errors would be rarer [1]. Indications of scepticism are expected, es-
pecially when the decisions concern human subjects, e.g. how people are organised and
how people should operate.
In maintenance management, the decisions to be made usually concern how to organise
operations and how to utilise resources optimally. These decisions affect the employees
in the maintenance fleet, and accountability for the grounds of the decisions is essential
in good management practice. As maintenance organisations grow, the ability to man-
age these large entities becomes harder, and the ability to take in information is limited.
Thereby it is motivated to use computational tools, including Artificial Intelligence (AI), to
support these management processes. However, the explainability of machine learning
algorithms has been a question of debate ever since the emergence of the technology.
Furthermore, these algorithms are often referred to as ‚ÄĚblack boxes‚ÄĚ, so the input and
output are known, but the internal mechanisms are hard to explain. In Forbes Magazine,
2023, Power addresses the ‚ÄĚblack box‚ÄĚ, the explainability and the problems involved in
the use of AI in recruitment decisions and other individual-related decisions. They ad-
dress the triggered fear response a ‚ÄĚblack-box‚ÄĚ algorithm might cause, which are the loss
of valuable insight and feedback due to a lack of trust, and the triggered fear [2]. For
a decision-maker to be accountable for machine-assisted decisions, they must trust the
output of the algorithm and explainability has a key role in building that trust.
The maintenance decision studied here is a tactical decision, where the base of the deci-
sion lies on the operational circumstances. These circumstances are partly known when
the decision is made, but some parameters are unknown beforehand. The scale of the
problems can be considered to be large, which is why intuition-based decision-making
is not feasible. The problem is studied as a case study of an industrial maintenance
organisation which provides maintenance services for infrastructure equipment. Due to
confidentiality, the concepts are described in general terms from a general maintenance
management perspective. The problem described is applicable to any maintenance or-
ganisation providing service over a geographical area. Similar problems are found, e.g. in
home healthcare, telecommunications, commercial refrigeration, elevators or real-estate
maintenance.
1.1
Problem Statement
Optimisation and decision-making problems usually assume all data is deterministic and
known. This, however, is rarely the case, especially in making strategic and tactical de-
cisions. Therefore, decision-makers aim to mitigate this uncertainty through forecasting,

e.g. predictive maintenance. However, every prediction is associated with a possible
prediction error, where a component could break down before the given prediction. This
breakdown is associated with a cost of maintenance. For example, when a technician
must interrupt their maintenance route, travel to the breakdown location and back to the
route. The cost in this example is the lost work time due to travelling. The problem to be
studied within the framework of the thesis is how to create good assignments of equipment
to technicians so that the impact of such disruptions is minimised and that the workload of
the entire fleet is balanced, so the workload is equally distributed among the technicians.
The case company is an infrastructure equipment manufacturer and supplier, and as part
of their service portfolio, they offer maintenance for their products and competitors‚Äô prod-
ucts. The maintenance offering covers, for example, inspections, scheduled maintenance
and repairs, but also emergency tasks, like customer call-outs or IoT-generated urgent
service needs. The units lie geographically spread over an area, and some equipment can
be located at the same location. The technicians in the maintenance fleet are assigned
to equipment based on their capabilities, and some equipment types require certification
from the technician for conducting maintenance.
The technicians‚Äô tasks contain planned maintenance, which is known beforehand and un-
planned tasks that dynamically occur to the plan. The duration of these unplanned tasks
is not known beforehand. The travel time between the equipment is counted into the total
workload. The travel time is assumed to be deterministic, so the impact of congestion
or other factors affecting the travel time are disregarded. The technicians‚Äô maintenance
routes start and end in a given depot which can differ between technicians.
The aim is, therefore, to assign equipment based on technicians‚Äô capabilities and capac-
ities in terms of workload. Additionally, the assignment must enable a relatively equal
workload for each technician and the workload should be dimensioned so that the as-
signed technician primarily answers the emergency calls of the equipment. Other key
performance indicators that drive the assignment problem are the overtime performed by
the technicians and the utilisation of the technicians. The utilisation is the ratio in which
the technicians work on equipment versus the overall work time.
It has also been found that there is an underlying value in allowing technicians to build
customer relationships. Therefore it is not appropriate to manipulate the assignment con-
tinuously. Instead, the assignments should be reconsidered if there is a change in the
operational circumstances of the technicians. For example, when new pieces of equip-
ment are introduced or removed or organisational changes require reassignments. This
means that the assignment decision must be made prior to the operational execution of
the tasks. The assignment decision gives the foundation on which the operational deci-
sions are made.
1.2
Motivation
This thesis aims to study a large-scale tactical decision-making problem given operational
uncertainty. The problem involves decisions which, both directly and indirectly, through
the assignments, affect the everyday operational circumstances of the technicians in the
maintenance fleet. Therefore, making a well-founded assignment decision will have both
organisational and individual value. The organisational value comes from an increase in
productivity when the technicians use less time on non-value-creating activities, like travel
time between units. Value on the employee level is created by having well-balanced as-
signments for the technicians, where also underlying workload due to unplanned events
is accounted for. A well-balanced workload can reduce the risk of overworking and under-
working the technicians. On top of to individual value, a balanced workload can positively

affect employee satisfaction, which naturally brings organisational value. In addition, ge-
ographically dense assignments will hopefully reduce travel time, which implicitly can de-
crease the environmental impact of the maintenance operations through the reduction of
emissions.
The currently deployed procedures for making these tactical decisions have suffered from
organisational knowledge loss, and, thereby, the trust in the output of the procedure has
decreased. This, on the other hand, has led to intuition-based optimisation of manage-
ment decisions. The risk of this kind of decision-making is decision mechanisms, like
bias and noise, which can lead the decisions to drift away from an optimum. The thesis
will formalise the decision-making problem and facilitate principles of explainability and
simplicity to solve large-scale problems with uncertain components. The aim is to test
alternative procedures, principles and performance metrics and see how the decisions
are affected by these.
A substantial part of the problem is preparing for the uncertain operational circumstances.
So this study aims to enlighten the impact of uncertain parameters on the decision-making
problem and how historical data could be used to approximate the impact and integrate
the impact into the decision-making procedure.
1.3
Structure of Report
The problem is identified to be a combination of two well-studied combinatorial optimisa-
tion problemsÕĺ the Assignment Problem and the Travelling Salesman or the Vehicle Rout-
ing Problem. This report is structured so that Chapter 2 introduces a literature review of
studies of related problems, and it summarises the modelling and solution implementa-
tions found for similar problems. Chapter 3 aims to give background and foundation of
the concepts introduced in the review as well as a theoretical foundation to the methods
and concepts used to facilitate the study presented in this report.
Chapter 4 will introduce the data connected with the case study and aims to give intu-
ition to the available data and needed preprocessing and whether the data support the
motivations set for the study. Following Chapter 5 will formalise the problem through the

Literature Review
The following chapter will introduce a brief literature review which has facilitated the study
reported in this work. The sections will address the recent research in the area of Stochas-
tic Combinatorial Optimization, its application to specific problems and the solution meth-
ods involved to solve the problems.
The specific problem formulations studied here are the well-studied problemsÕĺ the Travel-
ling Salesman Problem (TSP) and its close relative, the Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP).
These two formulations are chosen, due to their relation to the problem description in the
previous chapter.
The selection is supported by the analogy that Blakeley et al. 2003 made, where they in-
troduce an automated route-scheduling and planning system for Schindler Elevator Cor-
poration. The problem statement they have is similar to the one presented here. The
system first divides the locations into geographical clusters or assignments. The assign-
ment is a foundation for the scheduling and routing systems. The scheduling part of the
system uses Periodic VRP to formalise the problem [3]. Blakeley et al. mention emer-
gency callbacks that require ad hoc actions and are not known beforehand. It remains,
however, unclear whether stochasticity is integrated into the actual solver system. But
they mention that the assignment aims to create geographically dense districts and that
the schedule should leave margin for these trouble calls so that answering these calls
would have low operational impact [3].
2.1
The TSP and VRP and their Stochastic Counterparts
Both the TSP and VRP belong to well-studied combinatorial optimisation problems. The
TSP was mathematically formulated in the 19th century by mathematicians T. Kirkmand
and W.R. Hamilton, after whom also the well-known puzzle of finding Hamiltonian cycles
got its name. The general travelling salesman aims at finding the shortest route visiting
a given set of cities [4]. Danzig and Ramser first proposed the closely related problem,
VRP, as a generalisation of the Travelling Salesman problem in 1959, then called the
‚ÄĚtruck dispatching problem‚ÄĚ [5]. Here the aim is to assign routes for vehicles visiting all
customers while minimising the associated cost. In the general VRP, the customers have
a demand that needs to be satisfied by the vehicles. Determining the optimum for the
TSP and VRP, including their variations, is NP-hard [6].
Both of these problems have a number of variations, depending on what constraints are
forced. The most well-known variations of TSP are the asymmetric TSP, where the dis-
tance between cities is not equal but dependent on the start and end points, and the
multiple TSP, where there is a group of salesmen instead of one salesman. The most
known variation of the VRP is the Capacitated VRP (CVRP), where the vehicle capacity
is limited in delivering the demand to the customers.
2.1.1
The stochastic counterparts to routing problems
Optimisation problems generally assume all parameters are deterministic, which contra-
dicts the fact that usually, decisions must be made under uncertain knowledge of the
outcome. Therefore the motivation to study the uncertain counterparts of well-studied
problems is high [7]. When uncertainty is integrated into a VRP, it is generally referred
to as a Stochastic VRP [8]. The stochastic counterpart for the TSP, however, is more
commonly referred to as Probabilistic TSP [9]. The uncertainty in PTSP commonly is

2.2.1
Stochastic Programming and Stochastic Programming with
Recourse
Stochastic programming assumes that the uncertain parameters have some underlying
probability distribution that can be used to describe the average outcome of the uncer-
tainty, so the expected value outcome. The program then optimises over this expected
outcome.
A stochastic program with recourse allows the program to repair the solution after the
uncertainty is revealed. There is no theoretical limit for the number of recourse decisions
a model can have, and those models are generally referred to as multi-stage programs.
However, in practice, due to the complexity of the models, the number of recourse de-
cisions often are restricted to one, which is referred to as a two-stage program [12]. In
routing problems, these could be adding more vehicles or allowing changes to the route
plan [8]. The general mathematical formulation inspired by Shapiro et al. from 2003 [12]
is presented in upcoming chapters with the theoretical foundation used in this report.
Examples of work using Stochastic programs with recourse are by Hvattum et al. in 2006
and Haugland et al. in 2007. The first example, studied by Hvattum et al., deals with a ver-
sion of the VRP where customers can appear in the routing plan throughout the day, and
the customer demand is stochastic. The authors use a stochastic program with recourse
to solve this problem, where the recourse actions are either deploying new vehicles or
rearranging the existing route plan [13].
The second, explored by Haugland et al., deals with a tactical districting problem with
stochastic demand where couriers are assigned to delivery districts based on the ex-
pected cost of second-stage decisions. These second-stage decisions are the VRPs for
each assigned district [14].
Stochastic programs with recourse require that all stages are feasible and that they are
consistent with each other, meaning any first-stage decision must be feasible in any out-
come of uncertainty in the following stage. In Hvattum et al., where an SVRP with time
windows was studied, the existence of time windows can lead to a situation where first-
stage decisions are no longer feasible in the second stage as new customers appearÕĺ
thus the condition of consistency is violated [13]. This is averted in the model by allowing
customers not to be visited against some high cost.
Another workaround for this issue is using chance constraints. In Chance-Constrained
Programming, this issue is solved by enforcing constraints which measure the probability

2.2.2
Robust Optimisation
The objective function in Stochastic Programs evaluates the cost of the average outcome
of uncertainty. However, there can exist outcomes of uncertainty that can lead to the
first-stage decision losing its meaning, for instance, in the case described in Hvattum et
al., where the outcome of stochastic customers and their time windows led to infeasible
first-stage [13]. Additionally, stochastic programs require known probability distributions,
and discovering these is not always straightforward and sometimes requires unrealistic
amounts of observations [16]. Ord√≥√Īez describes in their tutorial different uncertainty sets
found in research in robust routing problems. The uncertain parameters in a Robust VRP
can be the same as for a stochastic program. Though defining the worst-case outcomes
of stochastic customers is not straightforward, studies on applying robust optimization on
uncertain customers are sparse [17]. One such study is from Subramanyam et al. 2021,
where budget uncertainty sets are used for stochastic customer orders. The stochastic
customers are modelled by finite binary vectors, where the values are non-zero if the cus-
tomer order is revealed [18]. Followingly, Sungur et al. in 2010 described a Courier De-
livery Problem model where uncertain customers are modelled through scenario-based
stochastic programming with recourse. In contrast, the service time is defined through
robust optimisation [19]. Other examples where service time uncertainty is described
through robust optimization in a maintenance setting areÕĺ Chen et al., in 2016, studying
road network maintenance [20] and Souyris et al., in 2013, studying technician dispatch-
ing with uncertain service times [21].
2.3
Solution Approaches
The survey from Oyola et al. 2017 summarises different solution methods for Stochastic
Vehicle Routing Problems, including exact solution methods and heuristic approaches.
The most common approaches in solving the problems were heuristic approaches and this
is because deterministic VRPs are challenging, and adding stochastic elements merely
elevates the complexity [22]. The challenge in such problems is the combinatorial nature
of the problems, and incorporating integrality constraints increase complexity.
Exact methods
The exact methods showcased in Oyola et al. were branch-and-cut and branch-and-price
methods [22]. The cutting plane algorithm associated with stochastic programs is an L-
shaped method. The method divides the problem into a master and multiple subproblems.
In a stochastic program with a piece-wise linear recourse function, the subproblems are
defined scenarios for the recourse stage. The problem is then iteratively solved by adding
optimality and feasibility cuts to the master problem [23]. The L-shaped method requires
linearity, and thus a version fitted for MIP-problems is discussed in Oyola et al., namely
the integer L-shaped method, which first ensures integer feasibility of the solutions prior

Heuristic Methods
Most of the literature on stochastic programs refers to heuristics as solution approaches
due to the computational burden of exact methods. In the papers presented in Oyola et
al., the instance sizes for exact solutions were around 100 customers, which limits the
applicability of exact methods in some real problems [22].
Using integer variables in heuristics is less complex than for exact methods, and instead
of discretising the expected cost of the uncertainty, the metaheuristics can use the distri-
butions directly. However, computing the expected cost can, in some cases, be compu-
tationally expensive. Other ways of evaluating the expected cost are approximations and
simulations [7].
Approximations of the fitness / objective function are problem-dependent, and different
approaches can be made depending on the case. Bianchi et al. 2009 mention three
possible approaches [7]:
‚ÄĘ Exact computation through expected value and distributions
‚ÄĘ Ad-hoc approximate computation
‚ÄĘ Approximation through simulation or simulation optimisation
The exact computation of the expected value will be computationally expensive, even if
using continuous distributions. And as pointed out in Bianchi 2006, the complexity of the
PTSP is O(n2) compared to its deterministic version TSP which has the complexity of
O(n) [24].
Haugland et al. 2007 approximated the expected routing cost in the districting problem
by using scenarios to approximate the expected value [14]. Scenarios can be generated
through sampling from a known distribution, and the number of scenarios can be reduced
to a number of significant scenarios by different clustering methods. Zhong et al. 2007
use problem-dependent approximation in approximating the expected tour durations in
the Territory Planning Problem instead of exactly solving the tours [15]. The risk in using
approximations is introducing errors to the evaluation, which could mislead the search
from the actual optimum. To mitigate this risk of misleading the search, the solution eval-
uation can alternate between approximation and exact computation [7].
The other mentioned approach is simulation approaches, which can be problem-dependent
fitness functions (e.g. non-linear functions) or Monte Carlo-based problem-independent
approaches. The sample average is used for Monte Carlo approaches to approximate
the expected value [7].
In Hvattum et al. 2006, the sample average of scenarios in a VRP with time windows is
used to guide the search in a hedging heuristic which gradually builds the solution out
of the solved scenarios [13]. S√∂rensen et al. 2009 use sample average approximation
in a heuristic based on memetic algorithms with a robust VRP framework to hedge away
from the worst-case scenario. They motivate the approach, especially in a situation where
the likelihood of the worst-case realising, thus running sample average approximation will
increase the flexibility of the solutions [25].

3.1
Industry Background
Pintelon et al. address in their article from 1992 the decision-making environment and
elements in maintenance. The decision-making structure can generally be divided into
Strategic, Tactical and Operational decision-making. Strategic level decisions generally
concern the resources allocated, whether larger technological shifts should be executed
and on which criteria investments should be made. In maintenance management, these
could concern, e.g. budgeting and technological upgrades to remote sensing. Tactical
decisions aim to utilise strategically allocated resources in maintenance effectivelyÕĺ this
could cover workforce-related decisions or maintenance policy. Finally, operational deci-
sions are related to daily activity, e.g. in which order tasks should be done. Generally,
the maintenance decisions should be built to reflect the company‚Äôs overall missions and
visions [26].
The governing missions in Maintenance Management are mostly aligned with the funda-
mental thinking of Lean Management. For example, in Kister and Hawkins from 2006, the
mentioned principles in maintenance areÕĺ the elimination of waste, focus on customers,
and that quality is generated at the source [27].
Eliminating waste removes non-value-creating activity, which is at the core of lean think-
ing. Waste can appear as spare processing time or lead time due to travel or reprocessing,
so performing the tasks repeatedly or ineffectively.
A customer focus in maintenance planning aims to understand the value-creating activi-
ties of the customer, where, e.g. timely maintenance and effective maintenance actions
are essential building blocks in the customer‚Äôs operations and increase the service‚Äôs reli-
ability.
Customer relationship management must also not be disregarded when addressing cus-
tomer focus and reliability. Especially in the maintenance of highly technical equipment,
the maintenance staff will not only familiarise themselves with the particularities of the
equipment but create relations and preferably mutual trust with the operative staff of the
customer. The possible benefits of mutual trust can be improved information sharing and
lower transactions cost [28]. The interfirm trust was studied by Laaksonen et al. in 2009,
and they found the benefits of mutual trust in a maintenance setting had a significant mon-
etary value. They also conclude that interfirm trust enables arrangements which benefit
all parties and are hard to imitate due to the complex nature of trust [28].
Lastly, quality should be delivered at the source and the first visit, meaning that the tasks
conducted should be performed at an optimal frequency, with a minimum number of visits,
and with the correct tools [27].
To reach these objectives, maintenance organisations deploy different strategies. The
strategies are often divided into preventive maintenance and corrective maintenance.
Preventive maintenance aims to maintain components before failure, and corrective main-
tenance repairs equipment only post-failure. Maintenance strategies vary depending on
industries and are highly related to the cost structure of the operations and maintenance.
In practice, hybrid strategies are usually deployed, where the two policies are combined.
Generally, however, it can be stated that the corrective maintenance strategy is associ-
ated with higher repair costs and longer downtime [29].
Preventive maintenance policies include time-based and condition-based policies. In a

3.3
Heuristics Background
3.3.1
Construction Heuristics: Nearest Neighbor Heuristic
Given a combinatorial optimisation problem, the fastest way to obtain a feasible solution
is usually by implementing a construction heuristic. The construction starts from an empty
solution or a clean canvas, and the solution is built piece by piece to become an initial
solution. The solution quality tends to be bad, and other improvement algorithms are
usually used to improve the solution [31].
One of the most renowned construction heuristics for the TSP and VRP are the Nearest
Neighbor Construction heuristic, which builds the solution gradually by starting from the
starting city and visiting cities based on their proximity to the city the salesman is in. So
the order of visitations is determined by the distance, and the next city to be visited by the
12

==PAGE:27==
salesman is the city closest to where the salesman is currently. This greedy selection is
repeated until all cities are visited, and the last city is connected with the start city [31].
3.3.2
Local Search
Local Search is a search heuristic which is not guaranteed to find the optimal solutions,
but as the name suggests, searches for local improvements in the search space. Local
Search starts from a given feasible initial solution, for instance, the solution from a con-
struction heuristic and by making small modifications to the current solution, explores the
solutions close to the current solution. The modifications are evaluated by a defined fit-
ness function, which describes the quality of the solution. If the neighbouring solutions
are better compared to the current solution, these are accepted. This is repeated until the
neighbours do not improve the solution anymore [32].
Neighborhoods
The neighbourhood operators are the core of the local search algorithms and describe
what belongs to the neighbourhood of the solution. Resende and Ribeiro, 2016, describe
a neighbourhood as a subset of feasible solutions, which are reachable from the current
solution by subjecting the solution to an operator they call move. Usually, the neighbours
differ from each other only slightly [32].
The neighbours are evaluated by a fitness or cost function, which can, for instance, be the
objective function of the linear programming model, but the method is not limited merely
to that. The evaluation of the objective function is often computationally demanding, es-
pecially for stochastic programming problems. Therefore, only the impact of the small
change by the move-operator can be evaluatedÕĺ this speeds up the evaluation of the so-
lution. [32]. This report will refer to such evaluation as delta-evaluation.
Search Strategies in Local Search
The number of neighbouring solutions can be extremely large, and evaluating all neigh-
bours can be costly. The algorithm can be subjected to different search strategies to
reduce the number of evaluations. A simple strategy is to accept the first neighbouring
solution, which improves the cost, instead of selecting the best improvement out of all the
neighbours. This is usually called the first improvement strategy [32].
Other strategies are so-called candidate lists. These restrict the neighbours to a subset
of neighbours called candidates. The candidate list can be created by limiting the number
of neighbours or by avoiding the recomputation of the same solutions. Such strategies
are, for instance, for TSP problems, 3-opt operations, where three edges from the tour
are deleted, creating three sub-tours. Then all seven feasible reconnections are tested
on fitness. Literature is abundant on these search strategies for a variety of optimisation
problems, and they can incorporate domain-specific knowledge to cater for the specific
problem at hand [32].
3.3.3
Large Neighborhood Search
An analogy used to illustrate the limitations of local search is that of a mountain climber
who is visually impaired. The climber is able to sense the immediate surroundings and
perceives that all nearby points are at a lower elevation than their current position, leading
them to believe that they have reached the highest peak. However, due to the climber‚Äôs
visual impairment, they are unable to see the full scope of the landscape and may miss
out on even greater peaks beyond their immediate vicinity. This analogy highlights the
challenges of local search algorithms in identifying the global optimum in complex opti-
mization problems. [33].
Several algorithms are developed to explore the search space further to escape this lo-
cal optimum. One such algorithm is Large Neighbourhood Search (LNS). As the name

The neighbour is evaluated and accepted based on defined criteriaÕĺ in the simplest form,
all improving solutions are to be accepted. The algorithm records and evaluates whether
the generated neighbour is the best-found solution. The algorithm is repeated until the
termination criterion is met. The termination criterion is a design choice, but common
criteria are either number of iterations or time limit [35].
Acceptance Criteria
Pisinger and R√łpke mention other more sophisticated acceptance criteria besides the
criteria of accepting the solution if it improves the cost function. Such criteria are based on
the principles of diversification and intensification, also familiar from Bianchi et al. [7]. The
aim is to allow the algorithm to accept non-improving solutions, thereby exploring a wider
search space and generating new potential best solutions, hence the term diversification.
However, it is not sensible to simply jump around the search space but also to explore
the neighbouring solutions for better solutions. This action is referred to as intensification,
where only improving solutions are accepted. Usually, alternating between diversification
and intensification captures the best features of the strategies, as stated in Bianchi et.al
2009 [7].
Such acceptance criteria can be borrowed from another metaheuristic, the nature-inspired
Simulated Annealing. Delahaye et al. 2019 explain that the algorithm mimics the be-
haviour of physical annealing. A material is brought to melting temperature and is then
gradually cooled in a controlled manner. As the material approaches the solid state, it
reaches an organised physical state of minimum energy [36].

Mathematical Programming
Formulation
The described problem will be formalised through a mathematical formulation in this chap-
ter. The modelling principle selected is a Linear Mixed Integer Stochastic Program with
recourse. The other modelling foundation found in the literature is Robust Optimisation,
which is shortly presented in the Appendix B.1. Stochastic Programming is selected be-
cause it bases on optimising over the expected value of possible scenarios, whereas
Robust Optimisation optimises over the worst-case scenario. So Robust Optimisation is,
by definition, more risk averting and robust. Since the assignment decision is a tactical
decision, which is a guideline for the operational decisions later on, it is not motivated to
have a high degree of risk aversion but rather to prepare for the likely outcome.
The model is built as a two-stage stochastic program with two decision stages. The first
decision is the assignment of a technician to equipment. The second stage decision is,
given the assignment in the first stage and the outcome of uncertainty, the routes the
technicians take. A technician‚Äôs total workload is assumed to be the sum of planned and
unplanned visits and travel time. This model disregards workload due to internal activity
like internal meetings, training or other activities.
The chapter is divided into two parts, the first part introduces the first stage, and the
second part introduces two possible approaches to the second stage. The formulation
principles follow the theory in Shapiro et al. 2003 [12].

Solution Approach and
Implementations
The model presented in Chapter 5 is a Linear Mixed Integer Stochastic Programming
Problem. The variables are mostly integer or binary, which implies that solving the problem
using exact methods might become challenging. As showcased in Oyola et al. 2018, the
optimal solutions using exact methods are often limited to instance sizes below hundred
nodes [8].
Especially if the recourse function based on potential routes is used to evaluate the im-
pacts of the assignment, the number of binary and integer variables will be significant.
Say, that an instance has five technicians and a hundred pieces of equipment, and the
planning period has ten time-windows. Even if the random vector from the mathematical
formulation in Section 5.3, őĺ2, could be discretised to just five representative scenarios,
the number of integer variables, yt,e
i,j, would add up to 2.5 million variables.
The example above assumes that five representative scenarios can be created based on
the number of unplanned events and their durations. In Section 4.2, the data available is
presented, and the limitation in terms of the number of observations is shown, where ob-
servations for some equipment are missing and at most three observations are available
for a piece of equipment. Therefore, some more general assumptions of the behaviour of
uncertainty must be made. Hence, describing the uncertainty through rigid scenarios will
limit the implementation opportunities.
For these reasons, the selected solution approach is a heuristic approach, where the aim
is to find improvements to the status quo and obtain feasible solutions to all instances,
which is desirable from a business perspective. For the maintenance business, obtaining
near-optimal solutions to all instances is preferred rather than optimal solutions to a subset
of the instances and using the initial solution for the rest.
The chapter is structured as such that first, an overview of the algorithm is given, and
the larger entities are introduced. Then the individual building blocks are opened and
described.
6.1
Overview
The overall strategy of the heuristic search algorithm is built up by two leading principles.
The first principle is the combination of diversification and intensification. Intensification is
incorporated by using domain- and problem-specific knowledge of what an optimal solu-
tion could look like. The diversification is implemented by leaving the domain knowledge
and adding stochastic elements to exploring solutions.
The second principle is familiar from decomposition algorithms, which divide the problem
into a master problem and smaller subproblems. In this implementation, the first-stage
decision could be described as the master problem, and the second stage is the subprob-
lem. A bird‚Äôs-eye view of the procedure is given in the flow chart in Figure 6.1.
The algorithm is built up of four larger entities. Three of these blocks, Initialisation, Local
search and Large Neighbourhood Search (LNS), depend on the Evaluation block, and
this block contains all processes related to evaluating the quality of the solution. The

6.2.1
Neighbour Sets
Another representation used regularly here relates to the precomputed Adjacency Graph.
The graph is used to create so-called Neighbour Sets, which represent the relation be-
tween the assignments and will be used to guide the search.
The idea is inspired by Haugland et al. 2007, where the assumption is made that a good
service district is one where the locations create cohesive clusters. To enable this Haug-
land et al. use so-called Connectivity and Border constraints, prohibiting changes in as-
signments which would create disconnected regions [14].
The Neighbour Sets Na,e matrix is of size A, E, where each row represents a piece of
equipment and the column represents the technician or, to be exact, the assignment to a
certain technician eÕĺ the Neighbour Set has the same cardinality as technicians.
The values in the Neighbour Set matrix are associated with the edges in the Adjacency
Graph. Given a certain assignment xe,a and certain equipment ai, the Neighbour Set is
defined by the edges in the Adjacency Graph A‚Ä≤ connecting equipment ai to its adjacent
equipment af, where (ai, af) ‚ąąA‚Ä≤. An assignment e belongs to equipment‚Äôs ai Neighbour
Set Nai,e, if any adjacent equipment af belongs to that assignment e. So the Neighbour
Set matrix value is the sum of adjacent equipment belonging to a particular assignment e.

6.3.5
Multiple Travelling Salesman Problem
The second stage decision in the recourse function in the model in Chapter 5 is modelled
as a multiple Travelling Salesman Problem (mTSP), where the technician makes a route
through the scheduled visits, and some of the visits can be to the same location. In
this implementation, the problem is solved through a construction heuristic and, more
accurately, a Nearest Neighbour heuristic, where the route is built piece-wise based on the
closest location for the next visit. One time window can contain multiple visits to the same
equipmentÕĺ the conventional Nearest-Neighbour would execute all visits at the equipment
consecutively, which is a rare scenario for the particular case. That is why a Tabu-search
metaheuristic is added to the construction heuristic. Tabu-search uses a so-called tabu-
list which records the B most recent moves, in this case, the visited equipment. This
forces the solution to leave the equipment after a visit is executed and allows it to return
only after the equipment is removed from the tabu-list, so at least B moves are made
before revisiting the equipment.
This operation can be compared to the constraint 5.5p, where two visits to the same unit
were not allowed to be conducted consecutively. However, the constraint only restricts
the immediately following visits. Still, there can be instances where the two pieces of
equipment are closely located in the same building and have the same number of visits.
This would lead to the solved route alternating between the two units, which is also un-
likely for the use case and would make the travel time unrealistic. Therefore, having a
larger tabu-list will more efficiently constrain the tour for the time window and create more
realistic tours. One option to eliminate this Tabu procedure is to increase the number of
time windows so that the level of discreteness would leave only one visit per time window.
This then raises the issue of the trade-off between exact computation and computational
loadÕĺ raising the level of discreteness will require more simulation, and given the inaccu-
racies in the uncertainty modelling, it is arguable whether the added precision actually will
give more reliable results and whether they will impact the larger-scale tactical problem.
The Nearest Neighbour construction heuristic for the simulation of the recourse is de-
scribed in Algorithm 6. The route starts from the technician‚Äôs depot, and the first visit is
made to the equipment closest, which is inserted into the route. The first visited unit is
marked as the current unit. The route is then built piece by piece by selecting the clos-
est equipment which still has visits and is not in the tabu list. The procedure is repeated
until all visits have been made. Note that in each iteration, the list of visits and the set of
equipment is updated to reflect the current visits. As the number of elements still to visit

The strength of local search algorithms is powerful neighbourhood operations, which pro-

specific knowledge of what is a good potential neighbour. The implemented Local search
presented in Algorithm 7 tries to leverage the knowledge of what is perceived as a good
assignment. The procedure in the algorithm is similar to many Local Search algorithms.
Here, the initial solution and the problem instance are inputs, and the neighbourhood is
explored either until the time runs out or there are no longer any improvements in the
neighbourhood, and only improving solutions are accepted.

that the reassigned equipment is not disconnected from the destination assignment. The

6.5
Large Neighbourhood Search
The local search will terminate when the bordering regions no longer have improving
moves, and since the candidate list limits the search space, there might be areas in the
search space where the overall solution is better. That is why the search is widened to
explore larger solution neighbourhoods. This is done by implementing Large Neighbour-
hood Search (LNS). The Algorithm 9 is inspired by the LNS presented in Pisinger and
R√łpke 2019, where the destroy and repair operators explore the neighbourhood [35].
The difference in Algorithm 9 is that Local Search is run after the reconstruct and decon-
struct operators, which have moved the solution to another area in the search space. This
will allow intensified search in the area.
The motivation of the strategy is that the Deconstruct and Reconstruct refrain from conser-
vative moves to explore a larger search neighbourhood. While doing so, the operators can
create incohesive regions that the Local Search elements can correct. Thus the strategy
of diversification and intensification is employed. The generated solution will be accepted
on an acceptance criterion which is, in this implementation, a Simulated Annealing accep-
tance criteria. Each solution is tested on the cost function, and the incumbent solution is
recorded. The algorithm runs until it reaches the termination criterion, which is here time.
The acceptance criteria in the LNS implementation is a Simulated Annealing acceptance
criteria, where in addition to improving solutions, the solution is accepted based on a
probability defined by the current iteration temperature. The temperature deteriorates
with each iteration and is reheated if the search does not result in improving solutions in
many iterations.

The Local Search focused mostly on the bordering regions and isolated equipment with a

here 10 % of the equipment will be removed from their assignment.

7.1.1
Number of samples for estimating uncertainty
The number of needed samples is estimated by using the accumulated mean as described
in Rosetti 2016 [37]. The simulation is run multiple times and the accumulated mean is
recorded over all iterations. The aim is to determine when the number of simulations
reaches a value where the accumulated average converges and reaches a steady state.
The three sample sizes to be studied are Nf, for frequency, Nd, for duration and Nt for
the route approximation.
Instead of presenting all instances, a selected instance and some of its technicians are
used. It should be noted that the test for route approximation will only be conducted for an
assignment instead of simulating routes over the entire instances. All instances showcase
similar behaviour as presented here.
Frequency: Number of unplanned events over planning period
The simulation result for all observations of the number of unplanned events is shown in
Figure 7.1. The blue noise in the background is the generated samples from the distri-
bution, and the red line indicates the accumulated mean over each simulation. As the
number of samples increases, the accumulated average stabilises. It is visually hard to
determine when the line stabilisesÕĺ therefore, the green line calculates a supporting met-
ric. This line indicates when a limit on the standard deviation is overcome. The standard
deviation is calculated on the hundred most recent values for the accumulated mean,
which then becomes smaller as the number of iterations increases. The value for the limit
is set at 0.001. Determining the limit can be complicated since the number of unplanned
events is a discrete random variable, and the actual sample average value will be rounded
to the closest integer.
In the distribution analysis, one finding is that a subset of the overall data might showcase
a different behaviour. Therefore two arbitrarily selected technicians from the subgroup
are selected, and the same test gave the following outputs in Figure 7.2 and 7.3. For the
assignment for Technician 1, the number of samples needed is lower than for the overall
data set. However, for Technician 5, the number of samples needed to reach the same
stability is significantly larger, approaching 15 000 samples. So the number of samples
to reach the same stability in the accumulated average is not the same for all possible
subsets.

Alternating with larger and smaller sample size
The issue which arises with the route approximation is computation time, as it can have
polynomial complexity in the worst case. Therefore, the strategy presented in Bianchi et
al. 2009 is employed where the search alternates between more accurate and less accu-
rate computation [7]. The alternation is implemented by recording the changes for each
technician, and at every nth change, the more accurate value is computed, thereby the
computation becomes quicker. Thereby, the reduced sample size value will not mislead
the search as the larger sample size calibrates the direction at every nth change. For the
results presented here, the threshold for the number of changes is three, so the larger
sample size is used after every third reassignment in a technician‚Äôs assignment.
To show how the Sample Average Approximation of the route approximation behaves
with reduced sample size, Figure 7.9 is presented. Both sample sizes fluctuate around
the same mean, and the smaller sample has a large variationÕĺ however, the values differ
merely on the centesimal scale, which considering the application, can be considered
a fairly acceptable degree of difference. Therefore a factor of eight is selected for the
smaller sample approximation.

7.1.2
Algorithmic User Parameters
From Figure 7.9, it is evident that the result is not fully stable, so there are small marginal
differences when evaluating the uncertainty. This can lead to cases in the local search
where the search continues even though the variance is only in the uncertainty. Therefore,
when evaluating a candidate solution, the candidate‚Äôs improvement level must overcome
a given threshold.
The value for the threshold is determined by evaluating what kind of improvement will bring
business value. The cost function measures the workload imbalance and the overtime.
Thus, an improvement in the cost function of 0.01 would correspond to an improvement of
below a minute in real time. And it is arguable whether that improvement will materialise
given the uncertainty. Therefore, the improvements are limited to be larger than 0.01.
The degree of how many units will be reassigned is determined by evaluating the trade-
off of exploring large areas and the burden of correcting the solution. As previously dis-
cussed, reevaluating the technicians‚Äô workloads can be computationally demanding, and
that is why 10 % of the equipment to be reassigned is deemed appropriate, as it diversifies
the search. At the same time, the run time is not spoiled in evaluating the changes.

to do with the acceptance criteria, so the initial temperature and the level of deterioration

Delahaye et al. 2019, where the algorithm starts by setting the initial temperature and

Discussion and Further Research
8.1
Methods and Limitations
The problem statement and the available data have largely affected the selection of the
methods used to solve this problem. All of the decisions described in the problem are most
naturally described as either binary or integer decisions, and considering the size of the
instance, the most preferable method is a metaheuristic approach. Also, the primary prob-
lem is a tactical decision, which is a framework for the upcoming operational decision, and
hence using methods which integrate the likely outcome of uncertainty is preferable com-
pared to risk-averting modelling methods ‚ÄĒ furthermore, the lack of equipment-specific
data conditions the methods to describe the uncertainty in more general terms. Simu-
lation optimisation is selected to enable more generalised assumptions of the historical
data.
8.1.1
Uncertainty Modelling and Suggested Improvements
When describing the uncertainty of the frequency and duration of unplanned events, prob-
ability distributions are used. The distributions are created using historical data, but as
described in Chapter 4, the data is partly incomplete, and the number of observations
makes it unreliable to describe each piece of equipment by a distribution. Using the same
total historical data to describe each unit would result in the same expected outcome for
all equipment which is known not to reflect the overall data. Therefore, the observations
are aggregated over the assignment, which change when the move operator is used. So
the assumption is that the joint distribution of a particular group of equipment describes
the unplanned workload of that particular assignment.
Suppose a technician is assigned to a group of equipment which all have had one un-
planned event in the three last planning periodsÕĺ hence, it is also fair to assume that
the technician will in the next period have one unplanned event at each equipment in
the upcoming planning period. If the technician is assigned to a piece of equipment with
significantly more unplanned events, say, twenty events in a planning period, the joint dis-
tribution for the equipment becomes skewed towards higher frequencies. The assumption
here is that the joint distribution reflects a total load of unplanned events for that specific
assignment of units over the planning period. So when the unplanned workload is aggre-
gated over the entire planning period, the sum of unplanned activity is approximately the
same when using individual or joint distributions.
However, the problem which emerges with this assumption is associated with travel time.
Let‚Äôs say that the units with only one unplanned event over the period are very closely po-
sitioned, but the problematic equipment with twenty events per period is far away from the
assignment cluster. Then using the total assignment‚Äôs average outcome will underesti-
mate the load of the technician travelling to that far-off location, as the joint distribution will
give values lower than the actual value of the problematic equipment. This assumption is
to be considered when analysing the results and their impact.
Additionally, the data exploration made in Section 4.2 showed that the theoretical distribu-
tion depends on the subset of the historical data, highlighting the point that the distributions
change with the reassignments. Suppose the distributions fit poorly to the data. In that
case, the uncertainty will be described poorly, which in the worst case, could misguide
the search and reduce the credibility of the proposed solution.

By describing uncertainty differently, the limitations described could be eliminated. Possi-
ble methods could be creating equipment-specific models, where the common equipment
features are analysed. The common features could be, for instance, the equipment type
and model, the age of the equipment and when recent upgrades or rebuilds have been
made. This would enable modelling possible unplanned events over the planning period
and creating representative scenarios to be used in the evaluation part of the procedure.
This, however, requires a large amount of research to describe the equipment in a reli-
able way, as each scenario would include a Markov decision process of either maintaining
equipment or not and the following probabilities of unplanned events. Another possible
way to integrate uncertainty is using a Robust Optimisation framework, where the ob-
servations would be used directly in a box uncertainty set, where the outcome can vary
between the maximum and minimum values depending on the worst case. Naturally, us-
ing Robust Optimisation makes the model more risk-averting, but the problem considering
the distributions is eliminated. Using Robust Optimisation will require some assumptions
concerning the data since not all units have observations in the given data, as stated in
Section 4.2.
8.1.2
Algorithm Design Choices
The presented implementation requires the user to determine the number of samples used
to execute the modelling of the uncertaintyÕĺ the analysis showed in Subsection 7.1.1 that
the number of samples is not necessarily stable. Even though the number of samples
was selected with large tolerance in the standard error, there might be cases of insuf-
ficient sample sizes. An alternative approach to having a user parameter is changing
the simulation termination criteria to terminate only when the simulation has reached a
steady state, i.e. a sufficient standard error is reached. This can, of course, result in in-
creased computation time for the simulation, but similarly, for some assignments, fewer
simulations could be sufficient.
In the recourse function based on route approximation, the mTSP is modelled through
a construction heuristic. The advantage of generating a solution through a construction
heuristic is quick computation, which is preferable in a simulation setting. Also, since the
target problem to be solved is not the actual route, it is not necessary to search for any
improving solutions but rather simulate the decision process.
Subsection 6.3.5 discussed the limitation of the mathematical formulation, where it con-
strains consecutive visits to the same equipment but allows the solution to return directly
after it has visited another equipment. This return to the equipment was hindered by a
tabu list which allowed a revisit only after a certain amount of other visits. There are,
however, cases where the routes become unrealistic as the tabu list is simply insufficient.
Such cases appear especially if many units are in the same location, so all visits in the
location are executed consecutively. This could be averted by increasing the number of
time windows in the planning period so that there is only one visit per equipment in the
time window. This, however, increases the computational load, and additionally, the re-
ceived data does not support it, as visits are currently aggregated to multiple visits in a
time window. So implementing more time windows would require additional assumptions
about the data. And on the other hand, the trade-off between more exact modelling and
generating a solution for the tactical problem should be kept in mind. What is the cost of
an exact model and the benefit for the tactical problem to be solved?
The search algorithm uses problem-specific traits to find good neighbouring solutions. De-
scribing the geographical area through these adjacency graphs enabled both the Local
Search and the Large Neighbourhood Search to use the regional connections between
the assignments through the so-called Neighbour Sets, which provided good neighbours.

The Local Search uses a candidate list for testing reassigned equipment. The list is cre-
ated through different criteria. The first criterion is to only have the equipment in the list
with adjacent equipment in the target assignment techt, ensuring that the equipment will
be regionally connected after reassignment. Then among those units, the first units to
be evaluated are units which are geographically disconnected from their current assign-
ment techf. Testing those first will hopefully result in more connected assignments. The
second sorting criterion is the number of assignments in the equipment‚Äôs neighbour set,
where the equipment with the least assignments in the neighbour sets is tested first. The
idea here is that equipment with many assignments in their neighbour sets will likely be
centrally located overall and, therefore, easy to access. The fewer neighbours the equip-
ment has, the farther away from the central areas the equipment is and reassigning that
unit will affect the source technician‚Äôs (techf) workload more. This was proven in the tests
to be a good result as the procedure found good solutions in the proximity of the initial
solution for all of the instances.
The approach in the LNS is the opposite, where operators target centrally located equip-
ment to provide alternative solutions. By doing so, it moves the solution to a completely
another area in the solution space. At the same time, the travel time of the technicians is
not changed drastically as all of the moved units lie centrally in the border regions. Com-
bining the two approaches gave good results, as shown in Chapter 7 as the approach
uses proven principles of diversification and intensification.
Using a Simulated Annealing acceptance criteria supports the principles of diversification
and intensification, and implementing an automatic parameter setting method is beneficial
because it gives intuition as to what is a good value for the parameters and especially in
cases where iterations are executed in a reasonable time, which is the case in the sum of
distances recourse. Even though the automatic parameter setting is not run for the route-
approximation metric, using the parameter setting for the other metric gives intuition for
the parameters for route approximation.
8.1.3
Adjacency Graph and Limitations
The strength of the neighbourhood operators in both Local Search and Large Neighbour-
hood Search depends on the preprocessed Adjacency Graph. The graph describes the
geographical area and how the units are located. Two units can only belong to the same
assignment if they have a shared edge or share adjacent equipment. Therefore it is impor-
tant that the generated adjacency matrix is symmetrical and that all units have a number
of edges. Additionally, the graph should represent the different densities of units in the
geographical area. So centrally located equipment should have more edges compared to
more rural equipment. This is because centrally located units are more easily accessible
than rural equipment. The distances between the central equipment are smaller, so re-
assigning central equipment has less effect on the travel time in the assignments. Using
the graph formulation converted abstract problem-specific traits to quantifiable measures,
enabling the creation of good neighbourhoods for the search algorithms.
The algorithm used to create these graphs was inspired by the work of Haugland et al.
in 2007, where the edges are filtered away based on if they intersect each other or not
[14]. The algorithm shown in Haugland et al. seemed not to be well suited for the large
instances in this study, and the structure of the instances resulted in unexpected cavities
in the graphs. The cavities are likely the result of the difference in the density of units in
the instances, resulting in too many edges being filtered away. The algorithm is modified
to go through edges only once, which makes the algorithm more dependent on the or-
der in which the edges are observed. So the units observed first will have more edges
than the last units. Therefore, additional filtering is made based on instance size, where
the edges are compared once more. This approach is very case-sensitive since each
Case Study: Assignment Problem under Operational Uncertainty in Maintenance
75
==PAGE:90==
instance‚Äôs size and geographical structure differ. In some instances, the preprocessing
creates sufficient results, whereas, in other instances, the approach leads to graphs that
do not present the geographical area well.
To reduce the case dependency of the Adjacency Graphs, other methods for generating
the graphs could be explored. One method could be leveraging the k-nearest neigh-
bour method, where edges connect the equipment to the k-nearest units. The number
of neighbours, k, is a user parameter, so this approach does not remove this limitation,
and k-nearest would not take the different densities of the geographical area into account
because each piece of equipment would have a given number of edges. Another method
that could potentially consider the density is creating a set of Minimum Spanning Trees
and then connecting the missing edges to create a graph.
Methods inspired by mesh generation for FEM (Finite Element Method) analysis could
provide good results. The mesh in FEM usually varies in density and is smaller at the
points of interest [41]. One automatic mesh generation algorithm is Delaunay Triangula-
tion, which takes a set of points and creates non-overlapping triangles [42] This method
is tested and a small comparison is provided in Appendix C.
8.2
Managerial Insights
Accountability in management decisions was stressed in the introduction, and explain-
ability was lifted as a means for increased trust and accountability in machine-assisted
decisions. Therefore, one of the guiding principles of the study is to use methods which
are grounded on problem-specific principles and logical intuition. At the same time, the
methods leverage well-known principles and algorithms to solve large-scale optimisation
problems, which produce on average 90 % improvements in the objective compared to
the initial solution. It has also become clear that unplanned maintenance activity is a con-
siderable part, on average, a third of the total workload and should be considered in the
assignment problem.
Algorithmic design decisions facilitate explainability, for instance, through the modular
modelling principles used, where the workload types are discretised to planned and un-
planned actions and travel time. The modular structure enables the decision-maker to
validate the outcome of the procedure against other data and thereby gain trust in the
suggested assignment. Additionally, the described variables are of the same units, which
makes comparing the variables easier and reduce the need to correct using weighting fac-
tors, which usually reduces the explainability. If other variables of different units, e.g. util-
isation of technicians or ratios between workload types, should be integrated, then some
conversion factor, for instance, monetary measurements, should be integrated. Deter-
mining some monetary value of abstract concepts is not straightforward. Still, decision
support tools like multi-criteria decision analysis can assist when dealing with qualitative
abstract variables [43].
Modularity is also implemented in the algorithms, as shown in Section 6.1, which makes
the procedure easy to modify by exchanging blocks in the procedure. So, if more well-
suited methods are found, these could be easily plugged into the procedure. For example,
the current distribution-based uncertainty modelling could be exchanged for more sophis-
ticated methods using, for instance, predictive maintenance models and their confidence
intervals. So modifying the procedure requires fewer resources, and changes are easier
to implement.
The assumption of the capability of the technicians assumes that the current plan rep-
resents the complete capabilities of the techniciansÕĺ however, this might not always be
the case. For example, when a technician has hidden capabilities and is not assigned to

Much of the presented literature divide units into so-called locations, which contain multi-
ple units close to each other. For instance, Zhong et al. 2007 call these operational cells,
and when generating the route, these are used instead of individual visits [15]. In their
case, all visits are equal, and executing certain visits does not require specific capabilities.
This is not the case in this study, where locations can have multiple equipment of differ-
ent types. Aggregating the units to locations would decrease the solution space, as the
entire location will have more requirements on capabilities, and furthermore, assigning
technicians to locations reduces the possibility of sharing the workload. Suppose a loca-
tion has ten units of three different equipment types, and the total workload of the location
exceeds the allowed capacity. The technician must have the capabilities to maintain all
these equipment types, and it must be accepted that they will be at overcapacity. By not
creating these subsets by location and assigning them to technicians, the workload can
be more easily shared and the equipment more flexibly assigned.

was compared with the Google-generated travel time in Section 7.6. The results showed
that the two datasets are highly correlated, and the algorithm produced similar results us-
ing both datasets. A factor just increased the values in travel time. This is likely due to
the lack of natural barriers in the area, and thereby the difference between the two data
is merely a linear factor.
Using more exact data will reduce the user assumptions and create more trustÕĺ however,
acquiring the data is also a resource question, as using commercial APIs can be costly.

deem whether using Euclidean approximations is an incorrect assumption and whether
more exact data should be used, and whether the use of accurate data is financially moti-
vated. But based on this study for this geographical area, using the approximation using
Euclidean approximations is fair considering the possible cost of more accurate data. Of
course, if the cost of acquiring more exact data is considered marginal, then it is preferable
to use more accurate data.
In Sections 7.3, 7.4, and 7.5, the different recourse functions were tested and com-
pared. Even though the computation time of the Route Approximation is longer, the results

as occurs using the Sum of distances metric. Therefore, considering the overall work-
load, it is more beneficial to try to incorporate more accurate modelling of travel time as
it balances better the workload types. Additionally, using the solutions using the route
approximation recourse produced in general smaller district areas which would indicate
overall shorter travel times, which could positively affect the overall utilisation.
Finally, the cost function of the procedure only used travel time imbalance and overtime
to evaluate the quality of the solutions. Still, other cost functions could be explored to
see how the solutions change‚ÄĒ for instance, minimisation of travel time, maximisation of
utilisation etc.

Conclusion
The assignment problem of equipment to technicians is studied as a tactical decision-
making problem under operational uncertainties. The assignment quality is evaluated
on the capacity, capability and balance in workload for the maintenance fleet. Because
the workload depends on uncertain parameters, such as whether unplanned events occur
and the duration of the events, the problem was modelled as a Stochastic Program, where
two different recourse functions were introduced. Depending on the recourse function, the
total problem is either described as a Linear Stochastic Program (LSP) or as a Stochastic
Program with Recourse (SPR). The recourse action in the SPR is modelled as a multiple
Travelling Salesman Problem.
The model is implemented and solved using a metaheuristic combining Local Search
(LS) and Large Neighbourhood Search (LNS). The model was tested in a real company
case on instances of varying size and geographical structure, all of the instances can be
considered to be large optimisation instances. The results for the particular case study
showed that the generated solutions minimised the objective of overtime, and workload
imbalance, by an average of 90 % for both recourse functions. The combination of the two
search methods provided good results as they facilitated principles of diversification and
intensification, which have been shown in previous research to provide good solutions
for Stochastic Combinatorial Optimisation Problems. The search in the metaheuristic is
based on the generated Adjacency Graphs, which describe the geographical area and
the relation between the units. By generating these Adjacency Graphs, problem-specific
knowledge can be used to reduce the solution space to a subset of potential solutions,
which supports the empirical knowledge. So, in other words, the Adjacency Graph en-
ables quantifying the qualitative intuition of a good assignment and thus creates high-
quality solution candidates.
The simpler recourse function, which was described as the LSP, used a Sample Average
Approximation for the unplanned visits and summed the distances between the assigned
equipment and the technician‚Äôs depot. This was identified as an overestimation, and in
the metaheuristic, the approximation overpowers the other workload types leading for the
optimisation to focus on the travel time. Additionally, the LSP tended to create larger ser-
vice districts for the technicians compared to the SPR. The SPR model used Simulation
Optimisation to model the recourse of the assignment decision by generating numerous
scenarios and simulating the nearest neighbour heuristic to simulate the tour of the tech-
nician. The computational load due to the simulation is large as the number of samples
has to be significant to stabilise the Sample Average Approximation. However, the model
approximates the travel time to be smaller, which allows other workload types to be bal-
anced, and the negative effects on technician utilisation are smaller than for the LSP
model.
The utilisation of the technicians is identified to be an important indicator of productivity.
When reassigning the equipment there is an underlying trade-off between utilisation and
a balanced workload due to the reassignments. So even though the provided solutions
did not significantly increase the utilisation, they could minimise the workload imbalance
without really affecting the utilisation. So the imbalance is practically removed, while the
utilisation is almost the same as initially. Implicitly the same applies to the environmental
impact of the operations, as the trade-off between workload types applies to travel time.
Most of the results used the Euclidean distance approximation for travel time durations.

But tests on Google route-optimised data showed that using route-optimised data is simply
a linear factor for this particular geographical area. The tested area does not have any
natural barriers, so for a different instance, using more exact data could be motivated.
The modularity in the model enables the decision-maker to validate the proposed as-
signment against some other data as the workload is discretised by type, and since the
search algorithms start from the current assignment and use problem-specific knowledge,
the explainability of the procedure increases. The variables are of the same unit when

use of scaling and corrective parameters is minimised.
Future research efforts should focus on describing the uncertainty, generation of the Ad-

uncertainty more reliably could be valuable, as some of the assumptions needed to be
made were not supported in the technicians‚Äô tours, which would give falsified values for
travel time and could mislead the search. The remedy suggested for this is equipment-
specific models. The user parameters and case sensitivity of the generated Adjacency
Graphs could be removed by using other algorithms to generate the result, and one such
method was studied briefly in Appendix C.
The described problem is a common problem in organisations which use subgrouping of
units to support the operational planning of activities. The case study is an assignment
problem of an infrastructure equipment manufacturer offering maintenance services over
a geographic area. However, any organisation with the same workload structure could
utilise the principles presented in this report to optimise their assignment. Examples of
such sectors could be the maintenance of commercial cooling equipment, real estate ser-
vices or the assignment of nurses for a home care unit. The applicability of the problem
statement to various sectors increases the motivation to model and solve such problems.
The report summarises the modelling and solution methods found in literature whilst con-
tributing to the research field by formalising the problem and solving a particular case
study using relevant methods and principles to generate solutions for large problem in-
stances. The formulations and designed solver take two sources of uncertainty into ac-
countÕĺ frequency and duration of unplanned events. Based on the literature research, the
complexity of similar problems usually limits the number of uncertain parameters to a max-
imum of one or two parameters. Additionally, studies on VRP problems with stochastic
visits are sparse compared to other forms of stochastic VRPs. The report also discusses
the industrial domain of the case study, and based on the literature review, there are only
a few studies made of stochastic VRP or TSP problems in a maintenance management
setting. Thereby the work summarised in this report will hopefully provide a contribution
to the specific research field.
Finally, the selected methods succeeded in balancing the workload for the subgroups and
the selected methods facilitated principles of explainability, where the decision-maker can
validate and analyse the suggested solutions. These approaches will hopefully increase
trust and traceability in automated decision-making procedures. These procedures will
hopefully be a valuable addition to the decision-makers‚Äô toolkit as they can simulate the
mechanisms and uncertainties in the operations and thereby provide new insights.
These automated decision-making algorithms should be considered as decision-support
tools to enable the decision-maker to manage larger entities and use quantified data-
based procedures to support intuition-based decisions. Thereby creating more balanced
maintenance fleets, which still create value for the organisation and the customers.

Appendix: Distribution Analysis
Among the alternatives of uncertainty modelling is the approach of modelling through
probability distributions. To determine which theoretical distribution would best fit the data
in hand, the Python package distfit is used. Distfit tests 89 theoretical distributions from
scipy library using different fitting methods [44].
The distributions are tested by parametric fitting, where the distribution parameters are
fitted so that the distribution and the actual data fit each other as well as possible. This
goodness-of-fit is measured through the residual sum of squares (RSS) between the data
and the distribution. Additionally, bootstrapping is done to run Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS)
tests to determine whether the bootstrapped sample and original data belong to the same
distribution. This is repeated multiple times to get a score for the goodness-of-fit [44]
The identified three uncertain parameters in the problem are: How often and when the
unplanned events occur? And when these events occur, how long do they last? The data
used to answer the questions was introduced in previous sections. So the data available
are the time windows of unplanned events for one planning period and aggregated data
over three consecutive planning periods of the number of unplanned events and the av-
erage duration of the events.

Appendix: Delaunay Triangulation
The discussions in the thesis proposed FEM-analysis-related methods to generate the
adjacency matrix. These Geomathematical and Graph Theory methods generate non-
overlapping geometrical shapes, which are then used to calculate, e.g. differential equa-
tions over continuous surfaces, by discretising the area to these smaller surfaces. One
such method is Delaunay Triangulation, which takes a set of given points, or vertices
V (n), in a space and creates a set of non-overlapping triangles in the space. A Delaunay
triangulation has to fulfil the following properties [42]:
‚ÄĘ Local empty circles: A circumcircle covering all vertices in the triangle shall not
include other vertices from the set of vertices (V (n)).
‚ÄĘ Maximize the minimum angles: The triangulation should aim to maximize the
smallest angle. A quadrilateral should be divided into two triangles to maximise
the smallest angles.
‚ÄĘ Uniqueness: The Delaunay Triangulation for the given set of vertices is unique.
‚ÄĘ Boundary: The borders of the triangulation are the convex hull of the given vertices
V (n).
Delaunay triangulation is done for the set of points in each instance using Python Package
SciPy Delaunay [46], which creates a Delaunay Triangulation from a given set of points.
Similar visual tests are conducted for the generated graphs. Additionally, it is ensured
that the graphs are symmetrical and each vertice has at least two edges.
