Abstract
Through the passing years, large outdoor events such as festivals, sport events etc. constitute meeting places
for many people, where the need of drinks and beverages is huge. Thus, more and more plastic cups are
used in order to satisfy that need. As a result, an increased amount of plastic cups waste is gathered to such
areas. Due to that, the wholesaler company Multiline A/S is interested in a life cycle assessment (LCA)
report, comparing different kinds of plastic cups, and determine which one is the environmentally most
preferable choice. The examined cups are, a reusable PP, and three single use (PP, RPET & PLA) cups.
The functional unit of the study is ”to serve 1,000 L of beer, water or soft drinks to outdoor events”. The
report is assessing the whole life cycle of the cups from the extraction of crude oil to the disposal of the
cups, as seen on Figure 4 and Figure 5. Those 4 cups are examined for 3 different end of life scenarios, in
order to observe how the results might differ between the different scenarios. Those end of life scenarios
are, incineration and recycling applied to all 4 cups, and composting applied only to the single use PLA
cup. In order to achieve this, a software developed by the Technical University of Denmark is used, named
EASETECH (Environmental Assessment System for Environmental TECHnologies), and the LCIA method
used in order to run the model and obtain the results is the ”ILCD recommended - 2013 Prosuit Normal
NR w/o LT”. Examining the results from the software and taking into consideration the sensitivity and
uncertainty analysis conducted through the report, several conclusions were drawn and explained in detail in
the last chapters. It seems that the single use RPET on Scenario 2 (recycling), has the lowest impact to the
environment. However, due to several limitations on that speciﬁc cup, the most scientiﬁcally robust results
belong to the reusable PP and single use PP cups. Moreover, recycling is the end of life scenario which
presents the lowest impact to the several impact categories.

Introduction
Every year, thousands of festivals, sport events and generally outdoor events gathering a large amount of
people, take place all over the world. In such events, there is an overwhelming consumption of beers, water
and soft drinks. The answer to this need is given by several kinds of plastic cups, which are commonly used
for years.
Plastics have become one of the most common replacements in many cases because they are cheaper, lighter,
more resistant, stronger, etc. than the initial material they substitute. As a result, they are used almost in
every ﬁeld such as in buildings, infrastructure, packaging, electronics, safety gear, tools, cosmetics etc. [6].
Although plastic cups are an easy and cheap solution in serving that many people beverages, they have be-
come a huge problem from the environmental perspective, as they are a great source of waste. 400 million
tones of plastic are produced every year and single use plastics are the 40% of this. Due to that, 1 out of 3
sea turtles and approximately 90% of the seabirds have eaten plastic and more than 100,000 animals in the
sea are killed [6].
In general, tonnes of plastic wastes are gathered after a festival, sport event, etc. There are already some
football stadiums trying to eliminate the use of the single use plastic cups by replacing them with reusable
or returnable ones [7]. According to [16], music festivals in the US generate around 100 tons of solid waste
everyday, while in UK the waste generation reaches the amount of 23,500 tons per year. Trying to face
this issue, Tuborg started replacing single use plastic cups with reusable plastic cups on music festivals in
Denmark [25]. However, would that be eventually a good solution?
The objective of this report is to compare the performance of reusable to single use plastic cups in out-
door events. The goal of the report is to examine and identify which would be the more sustainable and the
environmental friendlier solution between the different cups assessed, hence the following questions need to
be answered:
• Which processes contribute the most to the systems?
• Which end of life scenario gives the most suitable results?
• To which parameters, the systems seem to be most sensitive?
• Are there any potential changes which can affect positively the systems?
• Which cup performs better according to the functional unit?

In order to achieve those, a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) analysis is conducted. According to [14], the
deﬁnition of LCA is ”a tool to assess the potential environmental impacts and resources used throughout
a product’s life cycle, i.e. from raw material acquisition, via production and use stages, to waste manage-
ment”. To achieve this, a program named EASETECH is used. EASETECH is an LCA model used for
assessing environmental technologies and it is developed at the Technical University of Denmark.
The commissioner of the current report is the wholesaler company Multiline A/S. Multiline was founded
in 1977 and since then, is providing its customers with a wide range of consumer products all around Den-
mark, Norway, Sweden, Greenland, Iceland and Faroe Islands. Its clientele includes private companies,
chains and wholesalers, states, regions and municipalities. Multiline’s warehouses are located at Sorø and
Randers in Denmark storing products such as dispensers, disposable items, food packaging, table coverings,
beverage and vending products, bags, kitchen equipment, cleaning and hospital products, providing them
to their clients whenever needed [4]. The report will help Multiline to promote their products to the ideal
customers. Furthermore, the report gives some clues while people in Multiline are choosing their products
from the ﬁrst place.

Background
This chapter is giving an overview of the materials considered in the report. The theory behind the study is
described, and literature review is conducted in order to investigate the work that has already been done on
this subject.
The material of the reusable plastic cups is polypropylene (PP) and is compared with 3 different scenar-
ios of single use plastic cups, polypropylene (PP), recycled terephthalate (RPET) and polylactic acid (PLA).
Short descriptions about those different kinds of plastic are followed for the better understanding of them.
Polypropylene (PP)
Polypropylene (PP) is a semi-crystalline thermoplastic polymer with many applications in various ﬁelds.
It was discovered in 1954 and it has the lowest density among commodity plastics. The chemical resistance
of PP is outstanding and converting methods such as injection molding and extrusion can be applied. PP is
ideal for products such as trays, funnels, pails, bottles, carboys and instrument jars for clinical uses, due to
its advantage of high temperature resistance [21].
PP’s mechanical properties have been improved as it was reinforced with several ﬁbers and ﬁllers. It has
many practical applications due to the fact that its mechanical and physical properties (such as electrical
conductivity, thermal conductivity, barrier property, etc.) can be improved at the same time, and this made
PP a very attractive material [32].
Polypropylene is the petrochemical product of the oleﬁn monomer propylene and the process is called addi-
tion polymeriation. During the production process, heat, high-energy radiation and an initiator or a catalyst
are combined to produce monomers. PP is a vinyl polymer and all the carbon atoms are attached to a methyl
group as shown in Figure 1 [21].

Polyethylene terephthalate (PET)
Polyethylene terephthalate (PET) is a semi-crystalline soft transparent thermoplastic polymer, which is
widely used as packaging products water, juices, soft drinks, beer, tea and food [24]. Its high melting
point at 265 oC and the mechanical strength which is at least up to 175 oC, are because of the aromatic ring
it posseses in the polymer back-bone. PET is also resistant to heat, moisture and several chemicals [1].
The initial compounds for the commercial production of PET are, ethylene (CH2=CH2) and para-xylene
for the production of ethylene glycol and terepthalic acid respectively [17]. PET is produced by heating
those compounds together with the contribution of chemical catalysts. The initial product is a molten vis-
cous mass which is later solidiﬁed becoming a plastic. The reaction described above as well as the the ﬁnal
products and the structure of PET are shown in Figure 2 [13].
Figure 2: Reaction and structure of Polyethylene terephthalate [13].
According to [2], in 2000, 25-30 million tons of polyesters were produced, and this is increased to 55 million
tons in 2012. From these amounts, the highest percentage consists of PET. Recycling is the most economic
efﬁcient way to reduce PET, providing industry with PET lower in price [2]. Moreover, PET is the most
recycled plastic due to the fact that it can be recycled into its initial use, if it is collected in a relatively pure
condition. Bottles, cups and containers made of polyethylene terephthalate, are melted down and ﬁbers for
ﬁberﬁll or carpets are generated [13].
An LCA study from [36] shows that, the main environmental impact of PET packaging is detected on fossil
fuel and respiratory inorganics categories due to exploitation of raw materials, transportation and production
phase. On the other hand, carcinogens, climate change, ecotoxicity and acidiﬁcation/eutrophication do not
show signiﬁcant impact. Regarding the disposal of PET, landﬁlling has the highest environmental impact
followed by incineration and recycling.

Polylactic acid (PLA)
Polylactic acid (PLA) is a linear aliphatic polyester which is produced by poly-condensation of naturally
generated lactic acid. In general, the production of PLA consists of three main processes such as: i. biomass
is converted to fermentable sugars, ii. fermentation of the sugars to lactic acid, iii. puriﬁcation and polymer-
ization of lactic acid. A modiﬁcation on the PLA resin can make the ﬁnal material more water resistant and as
a result the petrochemical plastic used in packaging can be replaced [17]. Moreover, due to its high strength
and good clarity, it has become a reliable solution to replace petroleum-based polymers. It is important to be
underlined that the term polylactic acid refers also to the family of materials with speciﬁc properties because
of the chiral nature of lactic acid, where chilarity is the ability of a molecule or ion to be applied on its mirror
image [28].
PLA is used mainly for packaging and commodity materials as well as biomedical and medical use such
as drug delivery systems, sutures, blood vessels, etc. [28]. The use of PLA products is so common, as they
start to biodegrade after 11 months at 25oC in water and they fully biodegrade if composted on tempera-
tures of 60oC and above [17]. However, PLA’s main drawback is the low glass transition temperature (T g),
around 60oC compared to other polyesters [28].
Regarding the structure of PLA, it has two optically active forms named L-lactic and D-lactic, because
of an asymmetric carbon atom. Furthermore, during the production of PLA there are three possible forms:
LL-lactide, DD-lactide and LD or meso-lactide which are made by two L-lactides, two D-lactides and one L
and one D-lactide respectively. The structure of the lactides mentioned above are illustrated in Figure 3 [28].
Figure 3: Stereochemistry of lactide acid and lactide molecules [28].

increased, because of their lower environmental footprint. The most popular bio-based polymer is PLA, due

5
Interpretation of the results
Interpretation of the results is the last step of an LCA report. The results are analysed considering the sen-
sitivity of the parameters, the potential uncertainties as well as the assumptions and estimations made due
to the data preparation. As a result, all the conclusions and recommendations of the report are made here,
which should be in accordance to the goal and scope deﬁnition as well as the functional unit and system
boundaries [18]. The current chapter is split into the completeness check, consistency check, sensitivity
check, identiﬁcation of the signiﬁcant issues, a chapter where the preferable cup is described and a last part
where a comparison is done between the literature review and the conclusions of the study.
5.1
Completeness check
By applying the completeness check, it is veriﬁed that all the required data and information for the interpre-
tation of the results are available and complete. In fact, this is really difﬁcult, as it is impossible to know the
100% true conditions in order to realize the actual difference [19].
The main issue faced during the preparation of the model, is the lack of the data needed as input for the
software. This data refers to the manufacturing of the cups, packaging, mobile washing machine and trans-
portation. Multiline A/S offered a few basic information about the cups as well as the names of the manu-
facturing companies. However, it was impossible to obtain any data from those companies, so the only way
was trying to ﬁnd studies using similar cups and methods.
The precise modelling of the system can be proven difﬁcult, as there is a limited list of processes in the
library of the software and online ones (such as Ecoinvent). As a result, in several cases there are just a
few options available regarding the location, method, technology, year etc. A potential solution which might
make the system seem more realistic, could be the building of a few stages of the system from scratch. In
order to achieve this, deeper research is needed, including the danger of losing some vital processes.
5.2
Consistency check
On this section, the consistency of all the assumptions and methods used on the study, is examined, and how
well they are connected to the goal and scope deﬁnition [33].

In the completeness check (chapter 5.1), is mentioned that the data for the cups were not obtained from
the companies which produced them, but from other reports. Although, the source is not the optimal, the
values are considered reliable. The data found refer to exactly the same types of plastic having similar use,
so the differences might not be huge. However, this is not the case for the single use RPET cup, as no data
could be found for it and the data for the single use PP cup is used instead. So, for a future investigation of

improvement will help the precision of the model, and it might give more realistic outcome.
5.3
Sensitivity check
According to [14], the sensitivity check is ”the process of verifying the relevance of the information obtained
from a sensitivity analysis for reaching the conclusions and giving recommendations”. The goal of the sen-
sitivity check is, to estimate how robust the results and the conclusions are, and how they might be affected
from potential uncertainties coming from several sources such as, LCI data/modeling, LCIA methods or cal-
culating the results [19].
Perturbation and scenario analysis, and uncertainty analysis are conducted in the previous chapters of the
report (see chapters 4.2.1, 4.2.2 & 4.3). The results from the contribution analysis on chapter 4.1, are used
as baseline for those. The mobile washing process, transportation from the manufacturing companies to the
use, polymers production and the end of life scenarios, are the stages of the different systems which mostly
affect the impact categories.
The perturbation analysis shows, that the most sensitive stage of the system is the production of the poly-
mers, having high sensitivity ratios, for all the cups. Moreover, it seems that all the end of life scenarios for

Examining the results, it is noticed, that the end of life stage determines the ﬁnal outcome. Recycling

Most signiﬁcant processes/compounds

result, it is known which processes or compounds play the most signiﬁcant role on each stage. The results

”Climate change” and ”Particulate matter”.

impact on ”Climate change” in all of the cases, is the carbon dioxide produced from each process, and the
production of particulates (having diameter smaller than 2.5 µm) is the reason behind the impact on ”Partic-

recycling of the PLA (Scenario 2), the impact on ”Particulate matter” is mainly caused by carbon monoxide
produced in the process of the substitution of the PLA, while in the composting of the PLA (Scenario 3),
the ammonia plays a a signiﬁcant role on that impact category. Also in composting, the ”Climate change”
is affected by dinitrogen monoxide produced during the VS, C and N degradation and composting of air
emissions in windrows as the aforementioned ammonia. Moreover, in the stage where the polymers are pro-

5.5
Which cup ﬁnally is the most preferable?
In this section, the environmental friendlier solution for serving beer, water and soft drinks to big outdoor
events is presented. After examining the results and taking into consideration the sensitivity and uncertainty
analysis, as well as all the assumptions and estimations, the following conclusions have been drawn.
According to the results taken from the software, the cup having the lowest impact to the environment in
almost all the impact categories (11 out of 13), including those examined (”Climate change”, ”Particulate
matter”), is the single use RPET cup for the 2nd end of life scenario (recycling). Most of the impact cate-
gories have negative values, meaning that the whole system is saving, instead of burdening the environment.
The fact is, that the reliability of these results can not be deﬁned, as the initial data used in the software
for the speciﬁc cup, are assumed to be the same with the single use PP cup. That is done due to lack of
bibliography of manufacturing data for this kind of cups. It is also justiﬁed, due to the fact, that single use
RPET cup for Scenario 2 (recycling) has the highest uncertainty compared to all the other cups and scenarios
(see Figure 15 and excel ﬁle ”Uncertainty analysis anex”, tab ”Overall results”, Figure 2).
According to the source of the data used and all the assumptions made, the most concrete results seem
to belong to the reusable PP and single use PP cups for Scenario 2 (recycling). The manufacturing data used
for those cups, come from the same report, where data are used provided by cup manufacturing companies,
so it is assumed scientiﬁcally robust [15]. These are the results come from the baseline scenario. From the
scenario analysis developed on chapter 4.2.2, it is proven, that the number of uses of the reusable cup, plays
a very signiﬁcant role to this comparison, and the more washes, the less environmental impact. It is known
from the company, that the reusable cups can be used up to 25, and for the baseline scenario, 10 uses is
assumed. Usually, the actual number of uses is much lower, so this is a parameter which has to be taken into
consideration while comparing the performances of the cups.

Conclusions
In this chapter, all the conclusions, limitations and recommendations are gathered and listed from the whole
report. Thus, the main conclusions are:
• According to the results, the cup with the lowest impact to the environment is the single use RPET on
Scenario 2 (recycling).
• The most scientiﬁcally robust results belong to the reusable PP and single use PP cups.
• The number of uses, plays a decisive role to the environmental impact of the reusable cups.
• The end of life scenarios determine signiﬁcantly the ﬁnal outcome, with the recycling showing the prefer-
able results.
• Contribution analysis shows, that the stages with the highest contribution on the impact categories are:
mobile washing process, polymer production, end of life scenarios and transportation (from the manufac-
turing companies to the use).
• Sensitivity analysis shows, that the most sensitive stage of the system is the polymer production, and the
sensitive parameters from the end of life stages are the electricity substitution and the granulate production.
• Uncertainty analysis shows, that the most uncertain processes belong to the end of life scenarios, and the
most uncertain end of life scenario is recycling.
• The most critical parameters are the polymer production for all the cups and end of life scenarios (inciner-
ation, recycling, composting) and the production of RPET granulate from the recycling of RPET.
Several limitations are shown up while the report was carried out. The main ones are listed below:
• There is lack of the actual data needed for the software, so those used are based on older reports or rough
estimations.
• No data for the production of the single use RPET cup can be found, so the data for the single use PP is
used instead.
• The library of the software or the online ones have limited lists of processes.
Finally, according to the conclusions and limitations there are some recommendations in order to improve
the software or the actual systems, and they are described here:
• The actual data from the manufacturing companies are the ideal scenario for this kind of reports.
• Use the actual data for the manufacturing of the single use RPET cups.
