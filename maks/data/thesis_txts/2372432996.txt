Abstract 
The purpose of this project was to investigate the influence of residential kitchen extraction 
hood work on dwellings’ overall energy parameters and indoor climate change. To achieve 
that, a market overview of available solutions was executed, expanding knowledge about 
technical design and way of operation of hoods. A simplified model of a two-room apartment 
was simulated in IDA ICE for a period of one year. The highlighted dwelling was designed in 
correspondence to Danish Building Regulations 2015 injunctions, including mechanical 
ventilation with heat recovery. Three independent kitchen usage scenarios were developed, 
differing in amount of generated heat and moisture throughout cooking periods, as well as 
occupation and hood usage scheduling. Investigation of the kitchen extraction hood work 
included study of the effect of heat recovery from extraction flow of the hood on the annual 
total energy use of the dwelling. 
Simulation results show that without providing an inflow of warmed air to the apartment in 
accordance to hood extraction flow rate, a heat loss resulting from infiltration will negatively 
affect energy performance of a dwelling. Excessive use of range hood extraction will intensify 
the heat loss. In order to eradicate the heat loss, a heat recovery is proposed by connecting 
kitchen hood extraction duct to the air handling unit responsible for ventilating the apartment, 
equipped with a heat exchanger. Heat retrieval is only possible with mechanical ventilation 
balancing. After establishing ventilation balance, for apartment in question it is achievable to 
obtain from 20 to 34% total annual energy use saving and 31-139% increase of recovered heat 
in the heat exchanger of air handling unit, subject to particular cooking and kitchen hood use 
assumptions.

1 Introduction 
The progressing development of technologies related to design and construction of heating, 
ventilation and air conditioning allows to take more advanced steps into solving various issues 
affecting performance of installations. Nowadays there are numerous paths for improvement 
to be investigated by researchers. That includes among many others the matter of ensuring 
high quality of ventilation systems performance in the kitchens of newly built dwellings. In 
accordance to the standing document Danish Building Regulations 2015 (later marked as 
BR15) depicting the rules for construction of dwellings in Denmark, all erected buildings with 
kitchens should be constructed with taking into account addition of elements like mechanical 
ventilation with heat recovery and an extraction hood [1]. Both of them, related in a certain 
mean to overall kitchen ventilation, should be adapted and operated in a way guaranteeing 
rational energy consumption along with safety and wellbeing of inhabitants, which gives an 
opportunity to explore the functioning circumstances of those devices by scientists. One of the 
interests, inspected in this work, is operation of the extraction hood and its contribution to the 
general indoor environment conditions of a household. Range hood used as a primary kitchen 
device to get rid of fumes can be found in the households all over the world, thus determining 
factors behind adjusting inner conditions can have a worldwide benefit. Along with the 
increase of the hood extraction flow rate, drop in the pressure in the kitchen occurs resulting 
in a ventilation disturbance if no replacement air is provided. A coordination of household 
ventilation with the kitchen hood by a demand control strategy could be a solution securing 
stable and correct air exchange. 
In the times of comprehensive care for the environment one of the global objectives is striving 
for energy efficiency increase and correlated savings. Realisation of such goal can be 
accomplished on a different scale, both in the industrial and individual areas of concern. 
Every possible way of reusing energy that might be wasted should be revised. When talking 
about domestic ventilation, research show that because of the increase of thermal efficiency of 
buildings (decrease of heat loss related to building materials’ thermal conductivity 
improvement), the main reason of energy loss is linked to excessive ventilation and 
infiltration 2. With increasing airtightness of newly built households, the focus should be put 
on the heat recovery, and, desirably, demand control strategy matching the air flow rate to the 
real need. However, recently installed systems in dwellings do not include the concept of 
retrieving heat from the cooking effluents exhausted through a range hood, nor the attempt of

Benefits can be described by the means of household operation economics or ecological

2 Background 
2.1 Residential ventilation 
Ventilation is an intentional circulation of the air between indoor zones and outside space. 
The aim of a ventilation system is to provide fresh air into the indoor environment that is 
indispensable for living individuals and organisms to breathe. Proper work of various indoor 
appliances like i.e. powered by fossil fuel boilers or cooktops also require assurance of 
supplying an air containing oxygen. Except for providing oxygen-filled air, another objective 
of ventilating indoor space is to get rid of noxious impurities causing harm to occupants’ 
health, wellbeing or having negative impact on indoor furnishings, working devices, building 
materials and all kinds of present items. This is one of the main methods of elimination of 
indoor air pollutants, besides emitting materials/products selection and usage frequency 3. 
All considered, it is obligatory to design a ventilation system in a new dwelling that would 
have a fresh air supply rate of 0.3 l/s per m2 of heated floor in each habitable room and in a 
dwelling overall (BR15). 
For existing residential buildings, both infiltration and exfiltration have a big influence on 
overall ventilation design. It can be defined as an unintentional exchanging of indoor air with 
outdoors through various cracks and existing openings. The air leakage can occur through 
walls, ceiling details, ventilation system ducts, windows, doors, fireplaces, etc. 4 The 
driving mechanism behind this process is a pressure difference across the building envelope 
as an effect of wind appearance or air density inequality related to outdoor and indoor air 
temperature difference. BR15 instructs to decline the volume flow through leaks in the 
envelope to 1.0 l/s per m2 of heated floor area at a pressure differential of 50 Pa (0.3 l/s for 
high ceiling buildings). Additionally, a group of narrowed down parameters are shown in 
form of a building class 2020 which is to be mandatory for public new builds by the end of 
2018 and for other new builds by the end of 2020. For such class the value of the volume flow 
through leaks is 0.5 l/s (0.15 l/s for high ceiling buildings). The reason behind lowering the air 
leakage level is lack of control of the airflow that may cause deterioration of the indoor 
environment by, among others, introducing air at a different temperature level compared to 
indoors, or introducing moisture. A profound analysis of air leakage is relevant to meet 
expectations for well-designed ventilation.

Ventilation systems can be divided into two essential types: natural and mechanical. Natural 
ventilation is the flow of air being a result of naturally occurring pressure differentials. Air 
exchange takes place through planned air ducts in the building and through open windows, 
doors or other openings manually operated by an occupant, as well as leaks in the envelope. It 
does not require additional active appliances to provoke air movement. Mechanical 
ventilation, also called forced ventilation, is the flow of air between indoors and outdoors 
propelled by a fan and taking place through inlet and outlet vents. Therefore it requires energy 
introduction making it a source of additional dwelling maintenance cost. The amount of 
energy consumed by the mechanical system is subject to the ventilation rate, resistance to the 
airflow in the ducts, efficiency of the fan and motor, and operation time, so a high class 
design of the system can diminish the consumption 5. It can provide beneficial features 
increasing the quality of indoor climate like heat recovery, heating or cooling supplied air, 
adjusting air exchange rate in connection with occupant’s desire or level of indoor 
contamination, etc. 
A further distinction for mechanical ventilation is possible. The control of air exchange in a 
household can be executed with a centralised system or with a decentralised system. A 
centralised system forces air flow from and into the entire building through an air handling 
unit (AHU) connected with ducts to all ventilated zones, with an approximately same flow 
rate. Generated indoor flow is routed in such way that the supply air is applied in dry rooms, 
and exhausted from wet room. A wet room is an indoor zone with an increased emission of 
humidity (and usually pollutants) resulted from the purpose of utilization. Kitchens and 
bathrooms are considered to be wet rooms. Living rooms, bedrooms, etc., are defined as dry 
rooms.  
The AHU unit main parts are: exhaust and supply fans, air filters, humidifiers, and optionally 
controllers, heat exchanger, heating/cooling coils 6. An exemplary schematic diagram of an 
AHU is demonstrated in Fig. 1.

A decentralised system works on the same principle but it is responsible for ventilating just 
one, designated area such as a room, an apartment or a floor of a multi-storey building. The 
smaller the selected area, the smaller the ducting for supplying and exhausting air, resulting in 
smaller energy consumption for distribution. Decentralized system’s equipment is organized 
as a separate unit for the selected zone, not affecting the environment of the entire building. 
Those units require smaller amount of space to be mounted and can be controlled 
individually, depending on particular IAQ requirements of ventilated zone. On the other side, 
providing one air handler for the entire building is an easier to control (from the point of view 
of a building manager) and cheaper solution. Waste of fan energy, duct leakage or heat loss 
by conduction, lack of unoccupied zones isolation, accumulation of health hazards inside 
ducts and their later spread are some the drawbacks of centralised setups making it reasonable

2.2 Kitchen ventilation 
The aim of ventilating indoor space is to provide an acceptable air quality. When designing a 
ventilation system for a household, it is necessary to take the kitchen room into special 
consideration. This is due to the fact that the kitchen indoor environment is characterized by a 
number of features exclusive to the cooking space resulting in an increased concentration 
level of pollutants causing negative effects on occupant’s health and comfort. The features 
include presence of kitchen appliances, the use of which may result in emission of cooking-
based effluents and heat into the indoor air. Inhabitants’ occupation and cooking activity leads 
to contamination of the surroundings with CO2 as a product of breathing. Increased movement 
of occupants may bring a situation of getting exposed to airborne particles that can be either 
inhaled or ingested with food 10. Materials used for the building envelope and everyday 
objects specific for kitchen furnishings can cause aggravation of the air in the kitchen zone. 
The increased amount of water vapor spread during numerous cooking or cleaning actions 
may trigger mould and fungus growth, which in turn concludes into MVOCs (Microbial 
Volatile Organic Compounds) emission being a product of fungal metabolism [11. Despite 
global awareness about its unhealthy effect, it is still observable that some residents do smoke 
tobacco in their kitchens, causing by the way second-hand smoke issue for other non-smoking 
people in the zone. 
Adverse consequence of bad indoor environment is a combination of physical, chemical and 
biological factors that can be defined by different effects on occupants, starting from 
annoyance, through acute or long term health problems, down to premature death. Usually 
kitchen user can experience i.e. mucous membrane irritation (eye, nose or throat annoyance), 
==PAGE:15==
Master’s Thesis – D. KOMOR 
9 
 
neurotoxic symptoms (headaches, fatigue, irritability), asthmatic symptoms (chest tightness, 
wheezing), skin complaints (dryness, irritation) and others. Aforementioned signs and 
symptoms are related to a term called Sick Building Syndrome (SBS) [12. Improper design 
of the HVAC system may lead to draught causing dissatisfaction. In extreme cases including 
failure of combustion-based appliances performance an intoxication with carbon monoxide 
may take place. Presence of chemicals, e.g. cleaning substances, can emit dangerous vapour. 
Specific pollutants intake should be observed and adequate ventilation rate should be chosen 
for overall safety. 
Various relevant parameters identified in the indoor environment require a well thought out 
ventilation. Many factors should be taken into account, both related to occupant’s wellbeing 
and to a reasonable energy consumption. The assessment of the scale of ventilating system 
can be reached by considering particular pollutant concentration, exposure duration, temporal 
variations (time of day, heating or cooling season), associated health and comfort effects etc. 
Tightness of the building envelope is another important element to determine the ventilation 
intensity. If a tight envelope is observed, a bigger ventilation rate is required, otherwise the 
fresh air can get inside through gaps and leaks. The minimum ventilation rates providing 
acceptable indoor air quality can be specified by following codes and standards. Obligatory 
values of kitchen air flow can be found in brought up document BR15. There are other, well-
respected and referred to standard. For instance, a global organization called American 
Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers (ASHRAE) gathering over 
50 000 members including high class HVAC specialists, publishes an ASHRAE Standard 
62.1 and 62.2, which include suggested rates of ventilated air. Those standards are updated 
with the frequency of several years, alongside with expansion of available indoor environment 
knowledge and increasing level of applied technologies in HVAC industry.  
Different indoor conditions in the kitchen comparing to other rooms require a practice of task 
ventilation, being an action for specific activities. System designers focus on providing an 
adequate extraction level of contaminants and heat. Unnecessary energy consumption may 
occur with oversizing the ventilation units when exhausting indoor air in mechanical 
ventilation systems. It implies further energy usage when more complicated installations are 
concerned, simultaneously heating or cooling supply air. On the contrary, underestimation of 
the air exchange rate will contribute to unsatisfactory indoor conditions due to too long 
presence of impure air in the zone (increase of air age).

Most of the effluent and heat released while performing cooking activities is a part of a plume 
of heated air flowing above kitchen appliances. The most efficient way of getting rid of the 
effluents is to place an exhaust hood (also called range hood, kitchen hood, extractor hood) 
above the stove or the cooktop. It prevents emission of unwanted contaminants to the rest of 
the dwelling, ensuring reduction of occupants’ exposure [13]. The principle of work of a 
kitchen hood is to gather the rising gases in a duct, from where they are exhausted by using a 
fan generating a flow. All of the exhausted gases must flow through a filter to prevent the 
sedimentation of the contaminants on the duct walls. Filter placement, structure and properties 
depend on the type of ventilation system in the kitchen and on the way of handling exhausted 
air - residential hoods can be ducted (which means venting polluted air outside of the kitchen) 
or ductless (filtering polluted air and recirculating it back into the kitchen). 
There are several factors influencing the quality and the quantity of the captured fumes by the 
hood. Those elements are exhaust air rate, way of providing makeup air, hood style and 
design, construction features or the setting of appliances and furnishing surrounding kitchen 
hood. Moreover, when commissioning the design of a particular kitchen ventilation system, a 
checklist of main characteristics mention in Table 2 can be used (both for residential and 
commercial appliances):

Performance testing of range hoods for residential use is specified in the European Standard 
EN 13141-3, released in 2004. This document specifies methods for measuring the main

characteristics, the only aim is to determine the relation of the actual flow rate to the pressure 
rise across the device.

2.3 Kitchen hood market 
In the moment of designing a kitchen space, the designer encounters the question of choosing 
the kitchen hood. The variety of assortment of hoods on the market makes it possible to 
choose a variant meeting thorough expectations of a future kitchen user. When it comes to 
residential exhaust hoods, it is an often practice to focus on the aesthetics of the product on 
the same time neglecting the importance of parameters related to the effectiveness of indoor 
air improvement or to the energy usage. Depending on the customer’s preferences, there is 
number of ways for categorizing mentioned devices from various producers. 
It is challenging to execute a profound analysis of the current market situation but a 
fundamental character of products can be described.  
Classification of the available products can be fulfilled by considering: 
 Price range, 
 Hood placement, 
 Hood design, 
 Eventuality of polluted air recirculation, 
 Type of applied filter, 
 Way of operation, 
 Way of control, 
 Nominal power and energy efficiency, 
 Noise generation level, 
 Airflow rate, 
 Other additional features. 
There is a difference in the technology and the design of hoods when commercial kitchen 
ventilation is concerned. More intensive cooking processes occur and more harming 
pollutants are getting to the kitchen atmosphere. Commercial (restaurant, hotel, etc.) kitchen 
is a workplace, and therefore there is no need for designing a good looking hood. This implies 
that an emphasis should be put on the plumes capture effectiveness. The construction of the 
hood is strictly related to the type of cooking equipment installed in the kitchen. Taking 
everything into consideration, only commercial kitchens’ hood took attention of HVAC 
organizations to provide guidelines describing the operation of this device. Offered by 
ASHRAE and The American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists (ACGIH),

the operation instructions are based on the type of cooking process and the hood geometry 
[15]. Due to identified differences, application of commercial hoods in residences is not used 
in practice. 
The selection of an extractor hood guided by the way of designing ventilation system in the 
kitchen or designer’s outlook lead to creation of two kinds of installation placement: above 
the stove or downdraft setup forcing contaminated air to flow through the duct underneath the 
stove. The first one is the most bought device thanks to the fact of being simpler in the 
principle of work and having lower values of specific fan power, consequently being cheaper 
in operation compared to downdraft systems. Changing the cooking plume flow into the lower 
direction require an increase of fan power to achieve the same extraction rate, compared to 
hoods above the stove. Downdraft hoods can be located behind rear cooking rings or between 
rings in the cooktop. Due to the way of assembly influencing air circulation near the stove, 
they should be applied mainly for electric cookers. The montage can include a vertically 
movable extractor perpendicular to the cooktops or a vent located on the same level as the 
hobs.  
Above-the-range devices can be further distinguished on units with or without capture hood. 
Capturing element can increase the capture efficiency by adjusting the effluent flow over the 
cooktop. That also applies to the presence of side panels and the placement of exhaust 
opening [16]. It is worth to add that capture efficiency is also dependent on the burner used 
while cooking. Especially for the downdraft extractor, the use of burners near the outlet vent 
is more efficient. For the typical hoods, the back-burner operation is more environment-
friendly [17].

Other way of dividing the hoods into groups, more common on the market, is classifying as: 
chimney-connected hoods placed on the wall, chimney-connected hoods placed above a 
cooktop island, OTR microwave ovens with exhaust device and under-cabinet hoods [18]. 
Interior design is usually the main reason behind picking an appliance from a certain group. 
As stated before, depending on the ventilation units applied in the kitchen, the range hood can 
be attached to an exhaust duct or can filter and recirculate the absorbed polluted air back to 
the cooking space. The hood exhaust duct can be connected to an air handling unit throughout 
a bypass meaning that the cooking fumes do not flow through the heat exchanger, but still 
their movement is forced by the main unit. The benefit of this solution is no need of designing 
a special passage for cooking by-products in the ventilation system, improving the overall 
flow characteristics of the ducting system. If there is no possibility for mounting a bypass in 
the AHU, the fan of a cooker hood is mounted in the appliance itself or in the extraction vent 
placed on a roof or in the wall. Outside fan location decreases sound emission in the zone, 
especially when large air flow rates are considered. For high-end products it is possible for the 
user to determine whether the air exhausted through the range hood should be recirculated or 
exhausted directly outdoors. This feature is especially useful in winter period because it 
allows to save heat energy accumulated in the indoor air, if, for instance, low-polluting 
cooking (such as water boiling) is performed and there is no significant contamination of the 
indoor environment [19]. Control of the exhaust mode is realized by a valve placed in the end 
of the outlet duct of the hood. 
A major advantage for having recirculating hood is that it is versatile making it possible to 
mount without worrying about providing additional ducts. The lack of extraction of waste 
heat in cooking fumes makes it possible to consider such solution as a heat recovery. The 
disadvantage of a ductless installation is the necessity of using additional filters and more 
frequent cleaning or filter swap to maintain required air purification effectiveness. Another 
problem with this kind of installation is low humidity reduction efficiency causing growth of 
mould and general discomfort for the occupants. For this appliances it is possible to reduce 
the noise level with mounting exhaust fan outside the kitchen. 
Regardless of way of dealing with exhausted air, every range hood needs a grease filter. 
Cooking processes produce grease particulate matter and grease vapour accumulating in the 
hood and further in the duct due to the condensation. Long exposure of collected grease to 
exhausted air characterized by increased temperature may cause fire, however it is an issue in 
==PAGE:20==
Master’s Thesis – D. KOMOR 
14 
 
question mainly in commercial kitchen ventilation systems. Grease sticking to the surfaces 
affects the work of ventilation fans and lower their efficiency, causes an unpleasant odour, all 
of which makes it necessary to provide proper filters. In general, residential hoods are 
equipped with mesh filters made of aluminium or stainless steel. Mesh filters consist of layers 
of fine metal mesh. The cooking plume is transferred through the mesh to be screened which 
results in grease build-up. Hoods standing out because of a decorative design are sucking 
cooking contaminants through a narrow gap. In the hood’s narrow ducts the air velocity rises 
and the change of air flow direction occurs which leads to generation of centrifugal force on 
fat molecules. Grease is then gathered in a removable and cleanable collection tray, neglecting 
the necessity of using filters – the removal efficiency is claimed to be up to 97% [19]. Hood 
producers offer removable filters and advice to ensure regular cleaning (in dishwasher or 
manually with the use of detergents). If the household resident decides to use ductless hood, 
an additional activated charcoal (activated carbon) filter is unavoidable to get rid of other 
fumes causing odour and deterioration of indoor air quality. This filter needs to be replaced 
with the frequency suggested by the producer. 
For the majority of the hoods available on the market it is practiced to apply manual fan speed 
control system. The cheapest solutions only allow to select between on/off mode. For more 
advanced cases a multispeed motor supply is offered, which is the most encountered design 
when reviewing the products. Subject to user’s sensation of the indoor environment, including 
perceived air quality and the thermal conditions, suitable fan speed and directly related air 
extraction rate can be selected. With the increase of fan speed level, the capture efficiency is 
rising [18].  
Some devices incorporate sensors placed in the exhaust duct detecting specific types of 
pollution like: 
 Steam intensity, 
 Grease content (informing about the necessity of filter cleaning or removal), 
 Heat intensity, 
 Fumes existence.  
Sensors are helpful when automatic operating mode is considered. More advanced 
constructions include controllers that can operate the hood without user’s influence. Measured 
parameters’ values are analysed by the controller and the fan speed is adjusted to ensure

there are still cooking by-products in the kitchen space, even with all the appliances shut

activities. To avoid the need of staying in the kitchen to operate the hood it is possible to set 
automatic delayed switch off. The kitchen hood can be also programmed to continuously turn 
on at intervals with purpose of ventilating the zone. 
An important factor of every owner of the household should be the energy consumption of all 
of the existing equipment. For ease of choice of energy efficient device for a customer, an 
energy labelling scheme was implemented in the European Union followed by the Directive 
2010/30/EU of the European Parliament and of the Council. The aim of the labels is to display 
energy consumption and other basic features in a versatile way by allocating major technical 
attributes in a certain class. The features are: energy efficiency class, annual energy 
consumption, fluid dynamic efficiency, lighting efficiency class, grease removal efficiency 
class and the noise value at the highest setting of normal use. That allows to compare products 
of different producers thanks to a unification of data description. 
To obtain an efficient installation of the kitchen ventilation, positioning, dimensions of the 
hood and the airflow rate should be selected accordingly. The capacity of the extractor hood 
for different power level settings is depending on performance curve of the fan and pressure 
drops occurring within the appliance and ductworks. Both volume of the kitchen area and the 
size of the stove impact the required hood exhaust rate. The closer the capture vent of the 
hood to the stove, the higher the capture efficiency. The usual recommendation from 
producers is to select a hood having a bigger capture area than the area of the stove. 
Regardless, there is no clear and independent guidance developed by any specialized HVAC 
organization to determine recommended sizing of the appliance in domestic buildings. 
One of the concepts of increasing the capture efficiency is to equip the range hood with 
additional fans generating air curtain. Air curtain is a method to prevent cooking by-products 
from escaping to the zone beyond cooktop. Outdoor air is injected vertically by nozzles 
located on the edges of the face of the hood, creating an air screen that is blocking the 
contaminants flow outside extraction hood capture area. The supply air flow rate cannot, 
however, be high, because it may cause discomfort for the cook. It might occur that the 
excessive curtain absorb the cooking plume and spread it in the kitchen [20].

2.4 Technological issues 
As an effect of the process of extracting polluted air from the kitchen through the range hood, 
the quantity of gaseous molecules in the zone decreases causing a lower overall pressure 
compared to other household zones or outside of the building. The larger the extraction rate, 
the greater the pressure drop. The outcome is that due to the pressure differential an air flow 
coming from other dwelling zones takes place, or, if the building is not airtight, the fresh air is 
brought up from outside by means of infiltration. The unwanted consequences are draught 
which causes discomfort of the occupants, and unintended air circulation causing cooking by-
products to be spread around the kitchen instead of being captured by the hood. If the building 
is airtight, excessive air outflow can lead to a decrease of oxygen concentration level.  To 
prevent this from happening, an action to insert the makeup (replacement) air is needed. The 
easiest way to assure makeup air is to open the window while using the hood. It has however 
a big disadvantage in the form of heat/cool losses during air exchange when the temperature 
outside is different than the one maintained indoors. The 2015 International Residential Code, 
a code establishing minimum requirements for dwellings being built, developed by 
International Code Council, suggests applying makeup air systems when the kitchen hood is 
capable of generating extraction rate bigger than 680 m3/h. Such a system should involve a 
gravity or electrically operated damper working automatically whenever kitchen hood is used. 
The replacement air discharge should be located in the kitchen, or the kitchen area should be 
connected to another room with a supply air system through an adequate permanent opening. 
The amount of air introduced while using the hood should be approximately even to the 
extracted air. Since available market products offer extraction rate of up to 1000 m3/h, it is 
reasonable to include a detailed makeup system whenever a high flow rate device is installed. 
With the opportunity to use decentralized ventilation system optimized with controlling 
systems and sensors, a demand control strategy can be designed to increase amount of 
supplied air to the covered zone whenever the kitchen hood is in operation. 
Most of the cooking processes target is to treat food with heat. Some of the heat provided to 
or generated by the cooking appliances disperse around cooking zone with the largest amount 
being transferred above the cooking place in a thermal plume. This plume is then captured by 
an extractor hood and ventilated outside the building. All the cooking by-products transferred 
through the extraction duct contain thermal energy that is wasted when expelling to the 
outdoor environment. In the modern designs of household ventilation there is no practice at all 
related to the heat recovery from cooking fumes, putting smoke and grease settling down on 
==PAGE:23==
Master’s Thesis – D. KOMOR 
17 
 
the heat transfer surface as an argument. Without regular maintenance including cleaning 
those surfaces it is impossible to achieve reasonable thermal energy recovery. Yet, it is a 
matter of debate whether it is beneficial to install a heat recovery system from exhaust hood 
ventilation air or not. 
Despite the necessity of constructing an extractor hood as a part of the ventilation appliances, 
it is a choice of the occupant to set the extraction on for the cooking activities. Aggravation of 
the indoor air quality may not be noticed when preparing meals with the use of stoves and 
oven, however every amount of contaminants can result in health risk. Increased noise level 
of the hood fan operation is also the reason why not always this device is used [21]. Building 
home cooks’ awareness about their health threat and making them realize that the regular use 
of the range hood is beneficial could result in a great improvement of pollutants concentration 
level in the kitchen. Except from that, for more advanced appliances it might be a solution to 
equip the extraction hoods with sensors measuring the presence of a range of pollutants such 
as Volatile Organic Compounds to perform an automated mode of turning on and off the fan, 
even with the absence of the occupant after cooking process.  
2.5 Technological solutions 
The issues mentioned in the previous Subchapter caught attention of groups primarily 
interested in commercial ventilation systems such as kitchens for restaurants, hotels, schools, 
offices etc., where cooking takes place on a bigger scale. It is followed by increased energy 
consumption, ventilation flow rates, pressure differentials, indoor air contamination levels and 
others. Therefore, additional focus is put on the opportunities of managing the kitchen indoor 
environment to ensure energy savings and controlling the working conditions to provide 
acceptable workplace for the employees in terms of health and safety.  
As for the overall conditions in a building with a kitchen, air distribution paths should be 
investigated with consideration of placement of the openings such as doors, passages, 
windows etc. An underpressure in the kitchen is held to prevent all of the harmful 
contaminants from escaping into the other zones of occupancy.  
For the purpose of eliminating draught causing discomfort in the zones outside kitchen, a 
makeup air systems are introduced. One of the ideas of replacement air inlet used in practice 
is to locate the supply vents within the design of a range hood. Such concept enables steady

Decrease of capture effectiveness can occur with high velocities and turbulences of

In the highly exploited commercial kitchens there is a need for regular maintenance of filters 
in the form of cleaning them from cooking residues (smoke, grease, etc.). Those baffle filters 
are exposed to the heat generated while cooking. Since grease sedimentation is a problem for 
heat exchanging, connecting the idea of heat recovery with consistent cleaning of baffle filters 
lead to the invention of energy recovery filters [23]. Energy saving is realised by pre-heating 
the incoming cold water from the supply network before it is heated in the boiler with the 
waste heat from cooking processes. Due to the decrease in vapour temperature by extracting 
heat, it is possible to condense more of the grease on filters making it more efficient. The 
drawback of such solution is that it requires increase of the water pumps energy usage to 
ensure overcoming flow resistance in the baffle filters. 
There is no clear uniformity of technical problems to offer any universal solution. Many 
factors have influence on overall kitchen environment conditions and every case should be 
investigated individually. Insertion of makeup air, heat recovery and advanced control of 
contaminants extraction can be realized by taking different steps into general design, 
including a number of technologies available globally.

form of supply or transfer air through an integrated ventilation system. 
 Determination of heat recovery from the air exhausted by the extraction hood. 
 Evaluation of effects of different ventilation arrangements on indoor environment 
parameters. 
 Assessment of IDA ICE competency to design a distinct, local air handling unit 
having a targeted goal such as cooking side products extraction.

3 Methods 
3.1 Approach 
The procedure behind this project was based on the design of a simplified building in a 
dynamic simulation software with inclusion of occupancy behaviour patterns, determination 
of the internal heat and moisture gain of typical appliances, adaptation of the prepared model 
to the prevailing building requirements (such as those implemented in BR15) and 
arrangement of possible ventilation setups. The goal of creating sophisticated model is to 
obtain energy- and climate-related adequate object giving realistic view of the undertaking. 
Simulation were performed for various configurations of assumptions and for variable 
outdoor conditions. From the simulation results it was possible to retrieve constructive 
information used to discuss planned objectives of the project. 
3.2 IDA ICE tool 
The analysis of concerns in debate is made in a simulation software called IDA Indoor 
Climate and Energy (IDA ICE), created by Swedish company EQUA Simulation AB. This 
application allows to create a model of a building with taking into consideration many aspects 
influencing the indoor environment and execute calculations that can be used to study indoor 
air quality, energy consumption and thermal indoor climate. To obtain a useful simulation 
design, a wide range of input data should be applied, especially such as building materials 
with their physicochemical parameters, location-related climate data and surroundings 
structure, HVAC systems placement with special focus on technical characteristics and 
performance, control and operation schedules with the possibility of addition of appliances or 
building occupants having an impact on the overall indoor environment. It is therefore 
achievable to create numerous kinds of HVAC systems including custom ventilation units that 
are special for certain dwelling zones. As an effect of a simulation, variety of data in form of 
tables, diagrams, charts and plots is generated that can be compared with other models created 
in the software. All of the calculated data can be exported to an Excel spreadsheet to proceed 
with own data development. The mathematical equations behind calculations are open to 
investigate and, if necessary, adjustable. 
The program was checked by respected organizations in order to ensure the validation and 
acceptable quality of simulation results. It is approved by ASHRAE, European Committee for 
Standardization, International Energy Agency and building councils providing certification

schemes including: U.S. Green Building Council (LEED certification system), Building 
Research Establishment (BREEAM certification system) and German Sustainable Building 
Council (DGNB certification system). 
The use of the EQUA IDA ICE programme was implemented thanks to the educational 
license provided through the Department of Civil Engineering at DTU. 
3.3 Model description 
To observe a pattern of data alteration for the indoor environment parameters and ventilation 
units, several theoretical configurations of air movement were designed in an unalterable 
model. Simplified apartment is a part of a bigger building, created out of two main zones. The 
most attention is turned to the kitchen zone, as a source of excessive heat and moisture gains. 
It is a common situation that the kitchen appliances are in the same room that is also used as a 
living room, or, in other words, part of a dwelling where occupants spend the most time. In 
addition to the essential room concerned, a second zone in form of a dry room is applied. It 
can be considered as a bedroom. The idea behind making a double zone model with both dry 
and wet room, is to enable designing a mechanical ventilation solution in which supply air is 
provided to an apartment in a separate room in relation to the exhaust units. Both rooms are 
connected with each other by an inner door. Based on such model, a series of ventilation 
variations representing different, commonly seen solutions, were defined. Such structure of 
the model is sufficient enough to investigate the fundamental problems expressed in this 
project. The main emphasis is put on the parameters defining elements of ventilation system, 
heat sources in the kitchen and estimation of the occupation behaviour, such as the cooking 
scheme or occupant-lead regulation of the extraction hood device. 
IDA ICE is not an application that would allow performing very detailed modelling of indoor 
environment. By this statement it is meant that whenever applying a predefined internal gains 
in the zone, they are considered as a point in the zone space, without exact location of the 
source. It results in even dispersion of emitted heat, which is not particularly adequate when 
cooking occurs. Food treatment on a hot cooktop result in heat loss due to convection, 
radiation and vaporization of liquids (latent heat). One of the most common cooking process 
is cooking in a boiling water. Research show that most of the heat loss is related to the 
evaporation [24], ranging to over 80% dependent on the cooking appliances. Generated steam 
is then emitted horizontally, and ideally captured by a working range hood. Assuming that 
most of the contaminants (heat, vapours, smoke, etc.) are concentrated and mainly affecting

one particular part of the kitchen, an idea of designing a special zone for cooking activities 
and appliances only was implemented. Dimensions of the zone are corresponding to a 
workplace of cooking processes involving heat emission (for instance a stove with built-in 
oven underneath) and to a size of a capture area of the kitchen hood. All of the internal gains 
from cooking and extraction hood input data are set up in this zone. Defining a zone in the

set as being constantly open.

The second group of parameters that should be adjusted for the calculations is the one related 
to the performance of operating fans. A system designer is allowed to adjust performance by 
determining a value of efficiency and one of the two others: SFP (Specific Fan Power) or 
pressure rise. SFP is a measure of electric power needed to run the fans in relation to the 
achieved flow rate when at work with given power. Fan pressure rise is equal to the drop in 
total pressure through the entire ventilation system.  
Fan system efficiency is a ratio of power input in form of electricity to a useful output power 
from the fan (which can be defined as pressure rise multiplied by the nominal flow rate). It 
depends on the partial efficiencies originated from motor work, power transfer and fan 
aerodynamic properties. The efficiency decrease is observable in form of heat loss. Efficiency 
values of a fan vary widely depending on the size, construction and way of use (constant, 
multi-speed or variable speed drive). In general, when the same design is considered, with the 
rise of the nominal power value of a fan, there is an increase of the total efficiency.

The internal gains take a significant part in overall energy performance of the building. Since 
focus is on ventilation effects in relation to the system design, those objects have fixed input 
values for all cases. The most important assumptions include emitted heat per unit and 
moisture emission from cooking. Determination of the amount of spread heat and moisture 
produced from a cooking process is very difficult. It depends on many factors, such as the 
cooking appliances properties (including those generating heat and those in which the food is 
contained while the cooking occurs), type and amount of food being cooked, kitchen design, 
etc. Experiments indicate that the change of stove heat generation result in the variation of 
water vapour dispersion [34]. Predefined moisture contamination values can differ up to 
25 times for different design cases [35]. The same issue appears with grease emission rates 
assessment (as shown in commercial cooking appliances grease emission measurement [36], 
where the range of value can be between 0.12 and 8.27 mg/m3). This research also 
investigates radiant, sensible convective and latent convective heat emission rates for 
commercial cooking appliances. Sensible convective heat generation rate may vary from 85 to 
2454 W.  Therefore, there is no strict value that would be really reasonable for illustrating 
real-life conditions, especially for residential kitchens. Another way to differ the heat and 
moisture emission in a simulation tool is to change the cooking intensity by manipulating with 
the amount of appliances used and their usage schedule. Kitchen occupancy profiles 
distinction is a good alternative for modelling indoor conditions.

surface and heat intensity setting [39]. The amount of heat emitted through the usage of one 
cooktop ring is assumed to be 200 W (since most of the heat loss is due to evaporation when a 
typical process of boiling water is concerned [24]). The water vapour emission is estimated on 
a level of 400 g/h, in correlation to the range of values proposed in [35].

3.7 Dwelling ventilation modifications 
Determination of the image of indoor conditions change or effect on the local environment, 
related to different ventilation systems, is realised by designing number of ventilation 
configurations. Broad range of parameters can be analysed to obtain overall conclusion or to 
compare specific cases. Every case in question will be described by adequate flowcharts. 
3.7.1 Reference file 
Three cooking scenarios described in Chapter 3.6 are used as reference data for further 
comparison. The reference model is designed with a CAV (Constant Air Volume) mechanical 
ventilation provided by the work of main air handler fans. The supply and exhaust air flow 
rate is continuous throughout the entire working period and is kept at a level of 0.3 l/s per m2 
of floor area (10.8 l/s for each room). The effect is that whenever range hood is not in use, the 
system is balanced, but start-up of the extraction fan leads to disturbance between dwelling’s 
inflow and outflow of the air. The inner door is constantly open, with 10% of the maximum 
opening ability, and the windows are closed. Entire volume of air exhausted by the kitchen 
hood is directed straightway to the outdoor environment.

In total, exploitation aspects of a kitchen may affect the energy consumption considerably. If 
the occupation is longer, cooking activities are increased using the same appliances and the 
extraction flow rate from the hood is set accordingly, the entire delivered energy can be 
doubled.  
District heating is a sum of zone heating and domestic hot water (DHW) use. In all of the 
simulated models value of DHW is equal to 315 kWh and remains unchanged despite various 
model variations. The growth of district heating energy consumption values is then dictated 
by the fact that more air is exhausted from the inside and the makeup air is provided through 
infiltration. As a result more energy has to be used to warm the newly entered air to provide 
acceptable thermal conditions in the zone. Percentage values in Table 10 indicate that cooking 
appliances can be a primary source of heat in the dwelling if used excessively.

energy balance showing the total balance of energy for all zones in the building. The primary 
heat loss is a result of aforementioned infiltration. In relation to the heat gain from the 
equipment (as well as associated hood extraction rate), it can be seen that the principle of

5 Discussion 
The objectives and issues defined in the Subchapter 2.6 were investigated and the results in 
Chapter 4 gather the information collected about raised concerns. 
Designing the model with focus on building construction materials and their probable 
characteristics or performance in accordance to Danish Building Regulations 2015 [1], 
establishing technical specification of particular appliances including ventilation devices or 
cooking appliances, led to creation of a simulation instrument that was supposed to perform as 
a real life dwelling in terms of indoor environmental quality and energy management. 
Because of the fact that it is a theoretical model made in a dynamic simulation tool, it will not 
provide exact information about number of variables change that would be observable when 
analysing and measuring energy use or climate conditions in an existing apartment. In a real a 
household, it is expected to witness construction inaccuracy leading to thermal bridges 
occurrence and change of energy balance. Air handling unit performance would differ from 
the simulation model because of expenditure of elements and eventual effect of grease 
settlement on the heat exchange surface reducing heat recovery. Actual continuous outdoor 
climate changes may differ from those based on generalized values suggested in Design 
Reference Year. It is certain to witness irregular occupants’ presence, behaviour and actions 
related to the use of energy using appliances.  
Core data has been entered but depending on the purpose of simulation, improvements of the 
base model can be made. Values of specific fan power were assumed based on requirements 
of BR 15 and fan efficiency was postulated to comply with European Union Regulation [31], 
not actual performance specification of particular units, which have an influence of fans 
energy usage and should have a deeper consideration. Additional attention could be placed on 
even more adequate apartment design, in particular integrating more elements having an 
influence on the overall apartment performance, such as furnishings, commonly found 
residential appliances, etc. Except from those elements, more complex room layout could be 
created with incorporation of bathrooms and other specific zones. Modelling adjacent 
dwellings could also have a true effect on the residence in question. Scheduling adjustable 
elements (cooking appliances, zone heaters, occupation, inner door opening schemes, 
windows shading and opening schemes) in relation to outdoor climate conditions – 
temperature change, solar radiation change and so forth, could give a very precise model 
which could be used for verification of numerous parameters. As far as the thermal indoor 
==PAGE:80==
Master’s Thesis – D. KOMOR 
74 
 
climate is concerned, all of the assumed data should ensure that satisfactory temperature 
conditions are achieved, regardless of whether it is a summer or winter period. In the present 
project, the focus was on energy and indoor air quality, putting less attention to the occupants’ 
perceiving of the thermal environment. High temperatures in the summer period, related to 
building’s envelope ability to accumulate heat inside the apartment, would cause thermal 
dissatisfaction in an extended period of time. Modelling cooling systems like an air 
conditioner or a cooling coil in an air handling unit, or simply opening windows at the time of 
excessive indoor temperature occurrence, would prevent thermal dissatisfaction. 
Despite possible improvements, the analysed model of apartment gives the opportunity to 
observe data alteration and conclude on the simulation results. Using a simulation program is 
inexpensive and time-saving. There is no need to organize an experimental environment, 
whether it would be a particular apartment, or just an idealized facility with the minimum 
required elements (heat generating cooking appliances, kitchen hood and air handing unit) to 
proceed with measurements. Measurement process is omitted and no measuring apparatus is 
necessary. The simulation model allows to reflect a multitude of effects of intensive air 
exhaust with use of the compulsory cooker hood. Determination of the usual kitchen hood use 
schemes made it possible to look at advantages and disadvantages of its operation. Basic 
control change can result in apartment energy use change. Observed changes are possible to 
happen when a hood controlled by occupant would be placed in the kitchen – application of 
advanced control, such as demand controlled ventilation linked to specific indoor parameters 
sensors usage (as those mentioned in Subchapter 2.3 and designed precisely for a kitchen 
hood, or those used in general for entire ventilation system), has to be further investigated. 
5.1 Cooking scenarios 
The three developed scenarios vary greatly in terms of occupancy, emitted heat from cooking 
equipment and the kitchen hood use. They were created to resemble possible kitchen use 
situations. Simulation proceedings show that selecting one scenario would allow to perform 
an expanded analysis (such as sensitivity analysis of cooking heat generation of performance 
of ventilation system elements), showing impact of particular parameters on the indoor 
climate and energy use change. Yet, all of the scenarios are possible considering how 
indispensable cooking is in the modern life of all of societies around the world. They are not 
similar enough to be compared directly but they can indicate the change of energy use and 
==PAGE:81==
Master’s Thesis – D. KOMOR 
75 
 
indoor climate parameters in the same apartment, and with use of the same appliances, such as 
a stove, oven and a multi-speed extraction hood.  
If the ventilation system is not advanced and there is no heat recovery from the hood 
extraction flow, it is important to reduce the hood operation to the minimal necessary level, as 
to restrain excessive waste of heat energy in the exhausted air (in form of heat loss from 
infiltration, where a loss of 700 to 3500 kWh was observed depending on the scenario). The 
lost energy has to be then supplemented by heating up the indoor environment, resulting in a 
use of district heating energy from 600 to 2000 kWh, increasing with the scenario. For 
developed scenarios. Overall, with the use of the same appliances in the kitchen, it is possible 
to double the total annual energy use, taking into account all of the elements – lighting, 
cooking heat generation, district heating and fans use. 
Reference scenario is assuming a simple case of unbalanced ventilation system. Mechanical 
ventilation unprepared for cooperation with the use of kitchen hood is resulting in providing 
the replacement air to the kitchen through the leaks in the mounted windows, whenever 
increased exhaust is happening. The more intensive kitchen hood use, the more intense the 
heat loss from infiltration is (Fig. 11), ranging up to 3600 kWh per year for the utmost 
scenario. If the heat in extracted air is not recovered, the mechanical ventilation with heat 
exchanger would lose its recovery potential. 
5.2 Setting a separate zone for cooking processes 
To the extent possible, an attempt to design a zone where the kitchen hood operates was 
made. In terms of temperature differential between stove area and the rest of the kitchen, 
divergence of up to 13 ºC in time of intensive cooking can be noted. The uncertainty of the 
results is that it is a value of mean air temperature difference, not precise enough to determine 
temperature distribution over particular space points in the entire kitchen. Such value should 
be verified by an experiment or computational fluid dynamics including numerical analysis.  
Further investigation revealed that most of the heat generated by the cooking equipment is 
transported to the kitchen area and not exhausted directly through the hood. After setting the 
extraction mode on, it can be seen that there is a sudden decrease of mean air and operative 
temperature of 1.5 and 1 ºC, respectively. The cooking equipment heat generation in time of 
cooking is characterized by a parabola (Fig. 14), which is not necessarily truthful because 
typically, after initial heating up of the cooking appliances the heat generation rate should be 
stable. However, many ways of cooking (boiling, frying, etc.) differ in terms of heat 
==PAGE:82==
Master’s Thesis – D. KOMOR 
76 
 
management and emission from the oven and stove top. Such difference would have an 
impact on indoor temperatures trend. Validation in form of an experiment should be made and 
adjustment of heat generation characteristics should be adjusted. 
An important parameter for the cooking zone is the setting of discharge coefficient value for 
large vertical openings at a level of 0.001. In indoor environment simulations scientific 
consideration [25] this coefficient is used to analyse natural ventilation of flows through 
windows. IDA ICE allows to change the value of the parameter for each opening. The 
desirable effect was to prevent air exchange between cooking area and the kitchen whenever 
hood is not used. As an effect air buoyancy, caused by temperature and moisture differences 
was neglected. It does not affect the inflow to the cooking zone when extraction is set on, 
because the volumetric flow rate values are as expected (Fig. 19 and 21), resulting from big 
pressure difference. In spite of all, there is no certainty whether lack of air flow around the 
stove would have a verification in practice. Assumption of no air flow after cooking between 
hood space and rest of the kitchen should be authenticated through a deeper analysis, which is 
another reason supporting the necessity of performing a real life experiment. 
Mentioned facts imply that IDA ICE as a simulation tool is limited when the ability to create a 
designated ventilation unit like a kitchen hood or a cooking appliance is in question. It is not 
possible to determine how much of the heat energy generated from the appliances can actually 
be captured by a certain hood because the heat is first dispersed in the entire kitchen to obtain 
an energy balance. Expanded literature review on this topic could reveal such information.  
Moreover, it is not achievable in the program to make a hood model considering capture 
efficiency, filtering efficiency or generally determining cooking contaminants level in the 
dwelling, all of which would have impact on heat retrieving from the air extracted by the 
hood. 
5.3 Constant air flow rate 
The suggested in the Danish Building Regulations 2015 requirement of expanding hood 
extraction to at least 20 l/s is a reasonable value encouraging to mount low-energy using 
hoods and by this preserving the heated air inside the apartment but it does not include the 
necessity of providing an acceptable indoor air quality in the kitchen, in especial when the 
building is airtight and the mechanical ventilation system operates with the required minimum 
value of 0.3 l/s per floor m2. Except from that fact, the vast majority of the most basic hoods 
perform with bigger extraction rate. Selected extraction values of 47.2, 60 and 75 l/s are based 
==PAGE:83==
Master’s Thesis – D. KOMOR 
77 
 
on a modestly expensive, low range kitchen hood. Market overview revealed that it is 
common to find products capable of providing extraction flow rates over 250 l/s, sold by most 
of the manufacturers. The future kitchen user unfamiliar with the risk of overrating his needs 
for extraction rate, can end up with an economical problems linked to unnecessary energy use 
for fans operation. It would be reasonable to determine uniform guidelines about how to 
determine the extraction of a hood in relation to the actual needs of a potential user. 
In general, more intense cooking scenario results in an increased total annual apartment 
energy use because the period of time of hood usage is longer. Analysed cases for a constant 
flow rates from the kitchen hood fan highlighted that it is not the fan energy use that is a 
leading aspect of total apartment energy use change, but the main problem is the indirect 
effect of exhausting the warm air resulting in introduction of a cold outdoor air that has to be 
heated in order to ensure acceptable conditions for the occupants. Whenever kitchen hood is 
not in operation, zone heating decrease can be observed with the increase of cooking 
intensity. In terms of heat energy preservation it is a positive effect but as a side effect the 
indoor air contamination will become a rising problem. The same goes for extraction rate of 
20 l/s proving, that extraction rate low enough would be reasonable to preserve warm 
conditions without heating energy loss (zone heating energy use decrease despite increase of 
the cooking scenario – Fig. 30). From the extraction level of 47.2 l/s, the zone heating is 
getting higher with the intensification of cooking - it is too high to preserve heat in the 
kitchen. Balance should be found by determining minimum necessary extraction rate to 
achieve proper kitchen air purification and yet preventing heat loss, for example by 
application of sensors measuring the actual contamination around the hood (e.g. cheap and 
generic semiconductor VOCs sensors) that would select an appropriate minimal level of air 
exhausting. 
5.4 Window opening 
Another possible situation is when the residents open windows at the time of cooking, for 
example when the contaminants extraction rate is considered to be insufficient or when the 
user wants to achieve a change in thermal conditions. Even 10% window opening (creating a 
“hole” of 0.592 m2) will result in a vast zone heating increase. From the Fig. 37 it can be 
deducted that if the hood is turned off and windows are being opened, the heat loss is smaller 
because no warmed air is extracted by the ventilation device and the pressure differential 
between outdoor and indoor environment is not as big as it would be hood turned on. Still, a 
==PAGE:84==
Master’s Thesis – D. KOMOR 
78 
 
complete window opening would induce the stack effect and the air exchange through 
opening would be very intense. 
Without cooling units in the apartment it might be necessary to open windows in the summer 
period to lower the mean air temperature indoors. Except from such situation, it is not 
profitable to do it because of prominent heat loss. In the moment when an occupant decides 
that it is needful for him to open the windows, he should at least turn off the hood from the 
point of view of energy waste. Nevertheless, the best solution is the idea of application of a 
system allowing to change the extraction mode from complete exhaust to recirculation, which 
will result in improvement of heating energy management. If the reason behind window 
opening is thermal dissatisfaction (too high temperature), solving it could be possible by 
directing hood extraction air to a heat exchanger. 
5.5 Balanced ventilation 
As an effect of ventilation balancing, all of the supply air is provided through the air handling 
unit. Air handling units are equipped with air filters, so the incoming air quality is improved 
compared to a situation when it is coming through infiltration. Ventilation balancing means 
increasing the cost of a ventilating system because a control system is necessary to manipulate 
with AHU fans speed. On the opposite side, such solution encourages elimination of the fan in 
the hood construction and ensuring extraction with the use of AHU return fan. Another energy 
saving can be achieved with a VAV system. Having in mind that with regular cleaning of 
grease filters it could be possible to maintain high filtering efficiency, the heat exchanger 
performance should be preserved. 
The results indicate that balancing the ventilation without providing heat recovery from hood 
extraction exhaust air does not improve the waste energy retrieving but in case of low 
extraction flows, it can provide energy savings related to fan usage. Additionally, a simplified 
control system was designed that shuts off the return fan in the kitchen when the hood is 
turned on. The result is that throughout the simulation period less of the indoor air is blown to 
the heat exchanger and less heat energy is retrieved to be brought back to the apartment with 
the supply air. Therefore, if no supply air heating is designed in the system (either through 
heat recovery or by using a heating coil), ventilation balancing will not have, the overall 
improvements of the indoor parameters will not grow.

5.6 Balanced ventilation with heat recovery 
At this point it can be said that heat retain, in form of recirculation or heat recovery in an 
AHU heat exchanger, is vital to obtain an improved energy balance of a household. However, 
Danish law forbids using recirculating kitchen hoods [41]. Just by connecting the exhaust duct 
to the air handler and substituting the extraction source from the hood’s fan to the AHU return 
fan, defined by better specific fan power value (1000 J/m3 for the hood fan and 400 J/m3 for 
the AHU fan), energy saving by means of fan operation can be obtained, providing 2-3% 
decrease of total delivered energy use. Since the heat recovery in a heat exchanger is defined 
by the smaller mass flow rate of the two: supply and return air, if there is no ventilation 
balancing, the constant outdoor air inflow through an air handler will ignore the impact of the 
big hood exhaust air transfer through AHU to the heat exchanging device. Except from mass 
flow rates, the heat recovery is dependent on the specific enthalpy of the air incoming to the 
unit from outdoors and indoor environment. Improvement of AHU heat recovery at a level of 
0.2-0.4% is yet another proof that designed hood is not capturing excessive cooking heat 
before its dispersion in the kitchen. 
The ultimate energy-related benefits throughout all of the modelled cases are obtained when 
the mechanical ventilation is balanced and with exhaust air heat recovery. Compared to 
unbalanced simulation, zone heating is substantially reduced depending on cooking intensity 
(46-92% decrease). The supplied air flow rate is equivalent to the apartment outflow, so the 
heat exchange value is growing greatly – dependent on the scenario, improvement can occur 
from 31 up to 139% (Table 18). Overall, total delivered energy use improvement can be rated 
from 20 to 34% for a balanced and full heat recovery ventilation system. Another positive 
effect is the maintenance of assumed heating minimum temperature level of 21 ºC throughout 
entire simulation period, so no unwanted cooling will occur with those solutions. 
Received simulation results about total delivered energy use are relatively high and should be 
treated more as an attempt of an overall speculation than actually realistic values because the 
modelled apartment is designed without inclusion of most of the energy using appliances 
found in the households. Including those in the complete energy balance of an apartment 
would diminish the improvements. Presence of high temperatures in the indoor environment 
is also a matter of adjusting the model to comply with real life conditions in the apartment. 
Usage of IDA ICE does not allow to determine the effect of grease filtering by the kitchen 
==PAGE:86==
Master’s Thesis – D. KOMOR 
80 
 
hood filters, which would consequently affect the effectiveness of heat exchange in the air 
handler heat exchanger. 
All of the limitations pointed out in this project indicate a need of real life experiment. It 
could be useful for a deeper analysis of a single case of a particular apartment and ventilation 
system, or for recognition of parameters (like e.g. cooking heat generation rates) that would 
be used as a base for multitude of following simulations or experiments. Among others, it 
would be educating to expand knowledge about: cooking heat capture effectiveness of a hood, 
residential AHU heat exchanger performance in relation to contact with exhaust air polluted 
with cooking grease and smoke, heat recovery from hood exhaust air in a decentralized 
ventilation single room systems and similar.  
Regardless, achieved results show the significance of cooperation of the kitchen hood 
operation with the main mechanical ventilation system and suggest that it is crucial to 
continue the research because a visible potential of energy saving

6 Conclusion 
Having in mind that every case of a dwelling differs in terms of indoor environment 
parameters and energy management, it should be stated that study described in this project 
cannot distinguish any representative values that could be used as a comparison for any other 
examples of households with HVAC systems. The aim was to determine a change tendency 
for number of parameters for several true range hood operation schemes in dwellings 
equipped with such device. Objectives in question were entirely analysed in a dynamic 
simulation tool and the proceedings depicted multitude of connections between variables, 
revealing paths for supplementary studies, both theoretical and practical by means of 
experiments. 
It is certain that hood operation is very important for refreshing the indoor environment by 
outflowing cooking pollutants and excessive cooking heat. A side effect is that the warm air 
from the kitchen space is exhausted to the outside as well. Inappropriate arrangement of a 
household ventilation system or its operation will lead to unnecessary heat and electricity 
energy waste because if the hood is in operation, the replacement air is incoming through 
leaks in the envelope (such as windows), usually being cold enough to cause thermal 
dissatisfaction. Meaningfulness of this problem is more profound if the hood use is relatively 
more intense. Commonly available kitchen hoods are characterized by extraction flow rates 
significantly higher than the minimum 20 l/s imposed by Danish Building Regulations. 
Providing a constant air flow through the mechanical ventilation with heat recovery is not 
preventing heat loss resulting from replacing air exhausted by the hood with an outside air 
coming from infiltration. A heat recovery from the hood extraction flow is necessary to 
maintain energy savings due to heat preservation in the indoor environment. Since it is 
obligatory in Denmark to apply mechanical ventilation with heat recovery in a dwelling, the 
fundamental solution is to connect the hood extraction duct to the air handling unit. The heat 
recovery in a heat exchanger is only possible if amount of exhausted air from the hood is 
equalized by the same amount of air supplied to the apartment (through supply duct of air 
handling unit). Therefore, a control ventilation system that would balance inflow and outflow 
through the air handling unit, is also indispensable. In relation to how much heat can be 
generated and how big the flow through heat exchanger is, overall profits from heat recovery 
will occur on a different scale. For a simplified model in compliance with BR 15, 
improvements can be seen in form of 20-34% total annual energy use decrease or 31-139% 
heat recovery increase in the air handling unit heat exchanger.

7 Future work 
As far as future work related to this project is discussed, a sensitivity analysis of number of 
parameters can be performed. Indoor climate conditions and energy use for apartment heating 
can be examined by assessment of influence of cooking time periods, heat generation and 
moisture emission rates, ventilation system components parameters, etc. Adaption of the 
kitchen hood operation to demand control ventilation systems could be the next step in the 
research connected to this project. 
Early phase of the project required understanding of the present advancement of knowledge 
regarding residential hoods in terms of construction and operation. It can be concluded that 
the possibilities of expanding recognition about those topics are very broad. 
The most substantial way of validation of simulation performed on a theoretical level, is to 
perform analogous experiment. Under similar conditions it could give profitable information 
that cannot be observed or experienced in a simulation tool. Especially valuable, and at a 
bigger scale useful for a group of HVAC engineers, would be defining a group of uniform, 
applicable as standard or general guideline, cooking scenarios used to simulate kitchen’s 
energy use and indoor climate performance. It would be an important reference scheme 
enabling comparison of multitude of different forms of calculations, measurements and 
experiments. By a cooking scenario, a typical set of heat, moisture and contamination related 
data, as well as operation periods, should be specified. 
Heat recovery from the cooking fumes available by using the heat exchanger in an air 
handling unit is the simplest solution because of ability to adopt it for most of the mechanical 
ventilation systems. There is a field of eventual research for heat recovery performed not in 
the main ventilation heat exchanger, but in a dedicated exchanger, which is purposeful in case 
of single room decentralized ventilation units where the supply air comes directly from 
outdoors. Since technologies of grease filtering are continuously improving, there is no reason 
to ignore the significance of retrieving heat from the cooking fumes, as the more intense 
cooking, the more energy savings can be obtained.
