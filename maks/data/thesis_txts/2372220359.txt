Abstract
The signiﬁcant concern about the environmental impacts of the polluting energy production methods has
led to global actions for the transition to a more sustainable and environmental-friendly energy sector. The
integration of renewable distributed energy resources (DERs) in the distribution level is in line with recent
actions for higher fossil fuel independence in the ﬁeld of electricity production. However, the penetration of
a large number of DERs requires particular attention to avoid congestion problems and violation of voltage
limits in the distribution grid.
During the last decades, intermittent energy sources increased their share in the electricity production
sector resulting in a higher need for regulating power to even out the imbalances, namely the deviations from
the scheduled generation/demand in the spot market. In this project, the response of DERs to the regulating
power price with aggregators as middle agents is examined and optimized from a maximizing social welfare
perspective, where the interests of all market participants are taken into consideration. The model can be
used to evaluate whether the participation of aggregators in the regulating power market reduces the charging
costs of EVs and HPs satisfying the local grid constraints.
Throughout this thesis, the optimal participation of an EV and HP aggregator in the regulating market
is examined, where the transactive control (TC) scheme is applied to integrate the constraints for secure
and reliable network operation. TC is employed in the distribution grid to manage the interactions between
aggregators and DSO (Distribution System Operator), ensure minimization of aggregators’ charging cost,
satisfy speciﬁc requirements for the aggregators (energy requirements for EVs and indoor temperature within
a temperature range for HPs) and prevent from congestion problems.
The participation of aggregators in the regulating power market is achieved using two bidding strategies,
which are mentioned as Method I and Method II. A rolling horizon optimization approach is proposed to
determine the optimal regulation offer of aggregators at each time slot of the period. The difference between
Method I and Method II is that predictions of regulating power prices are also received for the time slots
outside the optimization horizon for Method II, where the minimization of expected costs from regulation
service in the whole period is desired. The response of aggregators to the regulating price using both Method
I and Method II is examined, while a cost analysis is conducted to compare the bidding strategies and
conclude whether it is beneﬁcial for aggregators to participate in the regulating market.
To ensure normal network conditions in the local grid from the participation of aggregators in the
regulating power market, the TC is integrated to the rolling horizon optimization. An independent market
operator (IMO), which ensures neutrality in the process, facilitates the TC in the distribution network. The
efﬁciency of the TC process and the effect of the congestion price on regulation offers are mainly of interest
in this thesis.
The results from the application of the aforementioned bidding strategies (Method I and Method II)

showed that the participation of the EV aggregator in the regulating power market is economically beneﬁcial.
More speciﬁcally, the charging costs dropped by 3.84%, from 64.37 DKK to 61.90 DKK, in the case of
Method I, while a 15.33% decrease, from 64.37 DKK to 54.50 DKK, was observed when Method II was
implemented. On the other hand, the participation of the HP aggregator in the regulating power market had
the opposite results increasing the costs for the examined period using either Method I or Method II. Another
case is also examined for the HP aggregator, where a preferred indoor temperature setpoint is selected at
Tpref = 22oC and a deviation more than ±10% from this temperature is penalized. According to this
modiﬁcation, the use of HPs as regulating power providers was marginally beneﬁcial, as the cost decreased
approximately by 3.3%.
A TC process was effectively integrated to the rolling horizon optimization, as the regulation offers of
aggregators were updated properly in order to comply to the local grid constraints. It was observed that
EVs are more ﬂexible than HPs to adjust their regulating energy, as the same congestion price causes a
higher decrease in their power schedule. Finally, the performance of the TC was high, as the maximum
computational time was 35 seconds and the charging costs of aggregators slightly increased by 2.51% and
0.62% for the EV and HP aggregator, respectively.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
During the last decades the climate change and its impacts on the life quality of people has raised concerns
about the environmental reaction to the polluting methods employed for energy production. To effectively
mitigate the adverse consequences of fossil fuel dependency of the energy sector, several solutions have
been recommended promoting the sustainability principles and environmental friendly technologies. The
transition from conventional generating units to renewable energy sources (RES) in the ﬁeld of electricity
production has signiﬁcantly contributed to the achievement of the aforementioned goal. In Europe, an
ambitious commitment has been made, which states a 30% emissions reduction by 2020, while in Denmark
an even more promising plan is under implementation stating that the residential heat and power consumption
must be provided completely by fossil fuel independent technologies by 2035 [2]. Moreover, an ambitious
goal of 100% renewable energy in the energy supply sector by 2050 has been set by the Danish government.
In line with the aforementioned, the majority of renewable generating units are connected to the distribution
level to promote a more decentralized power system and reduce the dependency on imports from neighboring
countries, when regulating power is needed (in the case of Denmark) [3]. A new type of load is also
introduced (ﬂexible load), which is considered a part of the distributed energy resources (DERs).
The most popular DERs are micro-gas turbines, internal combustion machines, fuel cells, photovoltaic
systems (cells), wind turbines, storage technologies (electric batteries, hydrogen etc.), electric vehicles (EVs)
and heat pumps (HPs). In distributed generation (DG), the focus is more on renewable methods, as they also
provide important beneﬁts to the environment.
The number of DERs is expected to increase even more the following years and play a signiﬁcant role in
the future power system providing additional ﬂexibility to the grid and contribute to the fast development
of decentralized energy systems. Thus, it is obvious that DERs are critical components and their optimal
operation will be of interest in the upcoming years when the smart grid framework will be even more
developed. The high penetration of RES in the existing system poses additional challenges to power system
operation. The replacement of conventional units with non-dispatchable generating units creates challenges
to the system operators to maintain the system balance and ensure reliability and security of supply at the
whole system level. Thus, the intermittent nature of RES increases the need for ancillary services, as the
reserve power from traditional sources has been decreased.
DERs are able to provide ancillary services to the system and contribute to the overall system balance.
However, the use of DERs as providers of regulation services requires an update of the existing market
framework. The existing market scheme limits the participation of small-scale DERs in the electricity
markets, as central generation units are only considered. Therefore, the ﬂexibility of DERs cannot be fully

utilized [3]. Furthermore, a minimum bidding requirement exists for the participation of players in the
markets, which is an additional barrier for small-scale DERs [4]. In addition, to leverage the ﬂexibility of
DERs, an aggregated and coordinated action is required, as it is more difﬁcult to control DERs individually.
Therefore, a new actor is introduced, which aggregates and optimizes their ﬂexibility. The new actor is
called aggregator which together with DERs can contribute positively in maintaining the system balance.
The participation of DERs through aggregator in the balancing market is required to be optimized providing
economic beneﬁts. Therefore, a bidding strategy is necessary to be created in order to motivate DERs as
regulating power providers and decrease their charging costs.
Recent studies propose the shift of energy management to the demand side, as power production has
become less controllable (more stochastic and variable) due to the high penetration of intermittent power
technologies (PV and wind power production). This is called demand response (DR) and aims to adjust
properly the power consumption of end-users for better energy usage. More speciﬁc, demand response
is used for peak shaving which smooths out the peak demand during the high-price period. This affects
signiﬁcantly the system reliability, security of supply, the achievement of the pollution targets and the
operating costs which are of major concern for the utility company. Demand response is also beneﬁcial for
end-users, as power demand is shifted from high-price to low-price period reducing consumers’ electricity
bills [5]. Controllable loads (such as EVs and HPs) can be used for demand response applications and
further as regulating power providers. Even though the ﬂexible loads increase the total energy efﬁciency of
the system, a higher electricity demand is observed which stresses even more the system in terms of grid
congestion and voltage deviations.
To address the aforementioned challenges and facilitate the control and coordination of a large number
of DERs, an efﬁcient control scheme is required to be introduced. An innovative approach is the Transactive
Control (TC) scheme. TC is a market-based method, where the balance between supply and demand is
reached by exchanging information about production, load and grid constraints through an economic signal
[6]. Thus, TC is able to handle the interactions between different actors via an incentive price signal. The
main advantage of this control scheme is that the priorities and costs of the users of DERs are also taken into
account.
In Denmark, a signiﬁcant increase of DERs is anticipated the following years. For instance, the number
of EVs is expected to increase from approximately 1,400 EVs in 2013 to 400,000 EVs in 2035, while the
predictions show that around 265,000 HPs will be installed by 2035 [7]. Therefore, it is of particular interest
to examine the opportunity to provide ancillary services from DERs taking into consideration possible future
modiﬁcations of the existing market framework. Furthermore, the reliability and security of the power
system is of great signiﬁcance taking also into account the interests of all actors, meaning that a control
scheme to handle their interactions would be a necessity. All the abovementioned provide motivation for this
thesis where the integration of DERs in the balancing market is studied incorporating the constraints for
normal network conditions in the distribution level using TC.

Scope and Limitations
The scope of the study is presented in this part of the report:
• A low voltage grid is under examination in this study, where high penetration of DERs is considered.
The grid is located in Børnholm and consists of two feeders and 33 buses. A 10/0.4kV transformer is
considered and 72 households are in total connected to the system.
• The DERs connected to the system are EVs and HPs, while the conventional load is also taken into
account. The location where DERs are connected is ﬁxed. To limit further the scope of the thesis, only
private detached homes are connected to the examined grid.
Throughout this study, there are limitations that it is signiﬁcant to be noted as well as some assumptions
that are presented in the following lines:
• Two aggregators, which cluster separately EVs and HPs, are introduced. This enables DERs to
participate in the markets, where their coordinated response to price signals is examined separately.
• Currently, as stated by the TSO of Denmark (Energinet.dk), the minimum bid size for a market player
to participate in the electricity markets is 10 MW. This would require a large number of EVs and
HPs clustered by the aggregator. In this study, the aggregators consists of a limited number of HPs
and EVs. However, assuming that their charging does not affect the electricity price, the results can be
scalable to a case where the required bid size is reached.
• It is assumed that the battery capacity is the same for all EVs connected to the network and the same
energy requirements are considered for all EV-users. Even though the daily driving patterns of the
EVs have similarities, in reality there is stochasticity in their energy requirements. Due to lack of
information and simpliﬁcation reasons, individual energy requirement and battery capacity of EVs are
not considered.

• The EVs installed in the examined system cannot discharge energy back to the grid, meaning that their

1.4
Overview of the optimization procedure and contributions
This study examines the possibility of providing regulating power from ﬂexible resources connected
to the distribution network taking into consideration the local network constraints and the balance of the
system. The negotiation between DERs’ owners and system operators is managed through aggregators,
which act as intermediaries and are able to adjust DERs’ ﬂexibility to procure regulating power in order to
maintain the system stability (number 1 in ﬁgure 1.1). Furthermore, the aggregators’ interest is to reduce the
customers’ electricity bills by optimizing the power schedules of DERs. Therefore, the optimal integration of
DERs in the TSO balancing market is mainly of interest. For this reason, a model is created to optimize the
participation of an EV and HP aggregator in the regulating power market creating a more detailed bidding
strategy based on historical data of the spot and regulating power prices.
A rolling horizon optimization approach is implemented, where the optimal up and down regulation
volumes are determined for the time slots within the optimization horizon. The optimization process ends
when the optimal regulation offers are determined for the examined period. This optimization tool is applied
in case of high penetration of DERs and a market-based control method is implemented in order to manage
the interactions between actors in the distribution level. An overview of the interactions among the actors in
this study is shown in ﬁgure 1.1.
In the distribution network, a TC scheme is applied concerning the minimization of charging costs of
DERs and the normal network condition in terms of congestion management. An IMO to facilitate the TC
process is introduced, which communicates a congestion price to the aggregators in case of violation of DSO
constraints (number 2 in ﬁgure 1.1). TC is an iterative procedure where the number of iterations is of great
signiﬁcance for the efﬁciency of the process.

2.1
Nordic Market Structure
In this section the structure of power markets in the Nordic countries is presented and described brieﬂy.
The main focus is on the regulating power market which is described, in more detail, separately from the
other markets.
The types of electricity markets in the Nordic system depending on the time scale are presented below
and are graphically depicted in ﬁgure 2.1:
• Financial Market: In this market the trade takes place with bilateral contracts and are used to hedge
the price and manage the risks, which are of great importance for the market participants. These
contracts can be traded days, weeks, months or years ahead [9].
• Day-Ahead Market: The main market where the power is traded, is the DA market. The market
participants place their bids before 12:00 at noon for delivery of power the following day. The bids of
participants consist of both power quantity and price. After the gate closure, a price is established for
each hour of the next day [10].
• Intra-day market: The participants of the DA market can trade power from the moment the spot
market closes until 45 minutes before the actual operating hour. Intra-day market is a pay-as-bid
market place, where the price for the same product may be different during the trading period. This
market was created to counteract the imbalances, occurred in the time period between the gate closure
of the DA market and the delivery hour, by trading energy close to real-time [1].
• Balancing market: This market comprises of the balancing power market and the regulating power
market. In the regulating power market, the TSO requests to approved actors to adjust their produc-
tion/consumption in the delivery hour in order to counteract the imbalance in the system. The result of
the trades is calculated just after the delivery hour. On the other hand, in the balancing power market,
TSO purchases or sells power to the market players that were unable to respect their agreed schedule,

2.1.1
Description of the Nordic regulating power market
The electricity production from intermittent sources (as solar and wind power) has increased the demand
for regulating power in order to counteract imbalances [11]. Imbalances are deﬁned as deviations between
the scheduled and actual electricity production/demand. The scheduled power generation and demand is
represented by supply offers and demand bids in the electricity spot market, respectively. In the next lines
the regulating power market in the Nordic system is described in more detail.
Figure 2.2: An example of a common merit order bidding list in Sweden (SvK) [1]
The main function of the regulating power market is to counteract the imbalances created by deviations
from the planned operation in the spot market. In order to maintain the balance in the system and ensure
the overall security of supply, the transmission system operator (TSO) is responsible for handling these
==PAGE:37==
2.1. NORDIC MARKET STRUCTURE
11
imbalances. Thus, at this market, TSO is in the demand side requesting from the balance responsible
parties (BRPs) to adapt their production/demand in order to maintain the system in balance. Therefore, it
is obvious that the regulating power can be either increased/decreased generation or increased/decreased
demand. The gate of the regulating power market closes 45 minutes before the actual hour of operation. The
BRPs place their bids for upward and downward regulation consisting of power quantity (MW) and price
(DKK/MWh) [4]. Thus, an open merit order list of offers and bids (NOIS-list) is created, an example of
which is shown in Figure 2.2.
In this ﬁgure, the spot market price is placed in the intersection of axes, while the up-regulation and
down-regulation bids are placed above and below the spot market price, respectively. The regulating price
is calculated at each hour and is deﬁned as the highest/lowest activated bid in case of up/down-regulation.
However, in case of a bottleneck, a regulating bid in a certain area is not activated. Therefore, a different
regulating price is determined for the area in question, meaning that the regulating power market will split
[12]. At each area the price is determined by the most recently activated bid on the NOIS list. However, in
the case of the area in question, the NOIS list before the bottleneck occurrence is used. If the total power
demand is higher than the total production, then upward regulation is required in the system, while if the
total power generation exceeds the total demand, then downward regulation is needed [1].
Another important point to be mentioned is how the price is settled in the Nordic regulating power market.
Two price settlement systems are mainly used: the (i) one-price and the (ii) two-price balance settlement
system. The BRPs can be divided into load balance responsible (LBRs) and production balance responsible
(PBRs) parties. In case of LBRs, the one-price settlement system is implemented, where the settlement price
is always the regulating price regardless of whether the contribution of LBRs in imbalance is positive or
negative. This means that LBRs can beneﬁt from their imbalances, which is considered permissible, as they
have limited control on the consumption of the large number of small users that they are responsible for. The
one-price settlement is summarized in table 2.1, where the price that the LBRs pay/receive in case of up and
down-regulation can be seen [4].

2.2
Optimal participation of DERs in the electricity markets
Previously, several studies propose different strategies that demand-side resources could implement in
order to play a more active role via demand bidding. The optimal participation of DERs in the regulating
and DA market through a bidding strategy has been studied in [13], [14],[15] and [16].
In [13], different charging strategies were examined for the participation of EVs in both DA and
regulating power market. It was assumed that the spot prices were known for the examined period, while
forecasts of regulating power price were used for bidding in the regulating market. It was concluded that a
stochastic optimization model based on different scenarios for the regulating prices, adding different levels
of uncertainty, leads to a higher drop of the charging costs. However, a rolling horizon approach could be
implemented that optimizes the decisions for up and down-regulation for each hour of the examined period.
The study disregards also the constraints for normal network condition in terms of congestion management
in the distribution network.
In [14], the bidding strategy included a rolling horizon planning with subhourly time intervals in the
regulating power market. A two-stage stochastic bidding strategy was proposed for the participation of an EV
aggregator in the DA energy and reserve market. Furthermore, a penalty was imposed if aggregators were
not able to provide the agreed regulation volume. In that research study, it was assumed that the regulation
offer is fully accepted, while the reliable operation of the network was not taken into account as in [13].
On the other hand, the expected probability of acceptance of bid in the DA market and probability of
deployment of bid in the real-time operation were considered in [15]. In this study, the optimal bidding
strategy of an EV aggregator was examined for participation in the DA energy and reserve markets from a
proﬁt maximization perspective. In this case, bidirectional EVs were considered and thus the degradation of
battery was included in the cost function to compensate the EV-owners for the increased cycling wear of the
EV-battery.
The study in [16] examined the possibility of providing regulation services from a microgrid aggregator
which clusters multiple DERs (DGs and ﬂexible loads). The main purpose was the overall proﬁt maximization
of the aggregator reducing the bidding risks by taking into account multiple uncertainty factors. However,
in this study, the RES technologies in the microgrid aggregator do not act as regulating power providers
meaning that they only participated in the DA market.
The optimal participation of DERs in the electricity markets intrigued the interest of many researchers
over the last decade. In the previous paragraphs only a fraction of the research studies are presented, while
the literature review focuses mostly on the demand response of aggregators consisting of ﬂexible loads.
Finally, it can be observed that there are still several cases that remain uninvestigated.
2.3
Congestion Management Methods
The congestion management methods are divided into two categories; the market-based and direct
control congestion methods. The market-based or indirect congestion management methods utilize an
economic signal which is communicated to the aggregators in order to adjust their charging schedule, unlike
==PAGE:39==
2.3. CONGESTION MANAGEMENT METHODS
13
direct methods where direct orders are used. In the following paragraphs, common market-based methods
are described brieﬂy, where it is assumed that only EVs and/or HPs are clustered by the aggregators.
The most common indirect congestion management method is the Day-Ahead Dynamic Tariff. In this
approach, the optimal dynamic tariff, which updates the charging schedule of the ﬂexible loads, is calculated
by the DSO in order to avoid grid congestion problems. This method can be expressed by a bi-level
optimization model, where the objective is to minimize the value of the dynamic tariff. However, this type of
problems are difﬁcult to be solved and therefore a modiﬁcation of this method is proposed. Thus, Distributed
Locational Marginal Prices (DLMP) are charged at each node of the examined distribution grid which result
in a more reasonable solution in terms costumers’ costs [17]. The computational burden is also reduced, as
an one-level model is required to be solved, where the DLMPs are represented by the Lagrangean multipliers
of the constraints [18]. Although this method is quite fast, signiﬁcant private information of aggregators
are published (such as objective functions), as the problem is solved centrally by DSO. A previous study in
[19] implements a distributed locational marginal pricing (DLMP) through quadratic programming (QP)
to ensure that the maximum loading of the transformer is not exceeded. Thus, a decentralized aggregators’
optimization and a centralized DSO optimization is employed to satisfy the constraints of local network.
Furthermore, QP instead of LP (linear programming) is used in order to avoid failure of the method due to
degeneracy. Finally, in this study the aggregators of the ﬂexible loads (EVs and HPs) do not participate in
the regulating power market, which is examined throughout this thesis.
Another indirect congestion management method is the Intra-day Shadow Price. It is called "Intra-day",
as it is applied tens of minutes before the actual operating hour. In the DA market, the aggregators have
determined the capacity required for their schedules in the distribution level. However, the aggregators’
demand might change close to the actual hour of operation. Thus, a proper (shadow) price should be issued
to enable the trading of their capacity. This procedure can be facilitated by the DSO, which acts as a market
operator in this case. However, the problem is better to be solved in a decentralized iterative way and not
centrally by the DSO in order to avoid leakage of aggregators’ private information [20]. The shadow price
determined at each iteration of the optimization problem is added on the top of the DA price plus the tariffs
(DLMP or dynamic tariff).
The last market-based congestion management method is the ﬂexible service market. In this method,
the aggregators decide about their charging plans without taking into account the grid constraints in the
distribution level. The responsibility has moved now to the DSO, who purchases ﬂexibility services from
DERs in order to ensure secure and reliable grid operation [21]. The aggregators participate in the ﬂexibility
service market if they are able to provide ﬂexibility services and further obtain beneﬁts in terms of cost
minimization. Finally, DSO has the option to reinforce the grid (using FACTS or additional DGs, long-term
solution) rather than buy ﬂexibility from DERs (short-term solution).
In some cases, the performance of indirect methods is not sufﬁcient, as they do not efﬁciently solve
congestion problems due to market failures or errors in the predictions. Therefore, the employment of direct
methods is necessary in order to complement them and provide a reliable solution for the system. Two direct
methods are described brieﬂy in the following paragraphs; the Reconﬁguration and the Reactive Power
Control method.
The Reconﬁguration method propose a change in the structure of the grid in order to deliver power to
the consumers in a more appropriate and effective manner avoiding congestion problems. The control of
normally-open and normally-closed switches is carried out, where their status is changed to modify the
operating radial conﬁguration and further contribute to a proper change of demand in the distribution grid
[22]. An optimization problem is created which includes the allowable voltage limits, the maximum loading
of the lines, the line losses, the equations of the power ﬂow and the voltage at each bus of the system.
==PAGE:40==
14

Normally, the optimization problem is nonlinear, but recent studies have shown that it can be converted to a
mixed integer linear problem or it can be solved by heuristic methods. The main advantage of this method is
that there is no cost charged to the costumers in order to solve the congestion problem.
In Reactive Power Control, the over-voltage and under-voltage issues in the distribution grid are alle-
viated by using FACTS devices such as static synchronous compensators (STATCOMS) and Static VAR
compensators (SVC) or ﬂexible loads that are interfaced by a converter. The cost associated with this method,
in order to avoid congestion problems, is almost zero assuming that minor losses are induced in the devices
because of the increase in the apparent power. It is further suggested that Reactive Power Control is used
along with on-load tap changers in order to improve the performance of the method [23].
It is important to note that the indirect congestion management methods are mainly of interest throughout
this thesis. Indirect methods exploit the ﬂexibility of DERs connected to the distribution level, as they are
price sensitive, which further promotes the smart-grid concept through demand-side management. However,
the aforementioned review of congestion management methods suggests the employment of direct methods
along with the market-based methods, in order to avoid problems due to forecast errors and market failures.
In this study, the TC process was implemented to avoid congestion problems, which is described in
more detail in section 2.4. It is important to note that TC method is similar to the intra-day shadow price
method when the examined aggregators participate in the regulating power market. The study in [24] applies
a network constrained TC for congestion management which further regarded losses and voltage limits. An
iterative optimization algorithm was created which updates the charging schedules of aggregators properly
through a congestion price in order to comply to the DSO constraints. However, aggregators do not act as
regulating power providers and participate only in the DA market as in [19].
2.4
Transactive Control for distribution grid congestion prevention
2.4.1
The concept of Transactive Control
Several control strategies have been developed in order to handle congestion problems in the distributed
network and ensure reliable and secure operation due to high penetration of DERs. The transactive control
(TC) scheme is an innovative market-based approach which can manage the interactions between different
actors in the distribution level (there are already applications of TC in the transmission level) and ensure
normal network conditions by performing efﬁcient congestion management.
Generally, the vision of TC is to achieve optimal allocation of resources taking into account the grid
constraints using an economic signal which is received by the intelligent devices connected to the distribution
grid. The communication is achieved through energy quantities and prices in a bidirectional negotiation.
The smart devices communicate their ﬂexibility to a control agent and their reaction to a range of prices is
estimated, as the consuming/producing assets communicate the price at which they are willing to pay/produce.
A proper price signal can be issued to the devices, which causes the balance between supply and demand,
if required. Thus, it is obvious that TC contributes to a controllable market-based reaction of demand
responsive appliances, operated by a control price signal, reducing the uncertainty of the overall response
[25]. The main advantages of the TC process is that the ﬂexibility of DERs can be fully exploited, an
efﬁcient market is established providing proper incentives to the participants and protection of the end users
privacy is achieved, as only information about price and amount of energy are disclosed.
Two common examples of the TC application in the distribution level are listed below:
•
The GridWise Olympic Peninsula project, where the TC framework is applied to coordinate and
control demand responsive devices in a so called double auction market.

2.4.2
Transactive Control as a congestion management method
TC consists of a set of control and economic mechanisms, where an equilibrium is reached after the
dynamic balance between demand and generation by exchanging information through an economic signal.
The value is considered the main operational parameter [6]. The TC is also implemented in the distribution
level where, normally, network losses, voltage limits and maximum loading of the transformer are taken
into consideration. The objective is to manage the interactions between different stakeholders considering
the priorities and costs of DERs, while reliable and secure operation of the network is ensured. The
communication between actors, in this case between Distribution System Operator (DSO) and aggregators,
is achieved through a third party called independent market operator (IMO) which manages the congestion
prices in case of constraint violation.
Figure 2.3: An example of the transactive control process in the low-voltage network showing the interactions among
different actors
Initially, the aggregators submit their optimal charging schedules with respect to charging cost minimiza-
tion according to the electricity price forecasts. These schedules are communicated to IMO. Afterwards, the
IMO evaluates the charging schedules with respect to local grid constraints and a congestion price is received
by the aggregators, which reﬂects whether constraint violation exists or not. The aggregators update their
power schedules according to the received congestion price. If network constraints are still violated, a higher
price is charged (increase by a small amount) and the charging schedules submitted by the aggregators have
to be modiﬁed properly in order to comply to the requirements of DSO [24]. The procedure ends when the
proper congestion price is communicated to the aggregators which causes the appropriate modiﬁcation of
their power schedule with respect to congestion prevention.

2.5
Summary
From the aforementioned literature review, it can be observed that there is room for additional research
in the ﬁeld of participation of DERs in the electricity markets, as there are some cases that have not
been investigated, yet. More speciﬁcally, a case where DERs participate in the regulating power market
considering the normal network operation in the distribution level would be interesting to be examined. The
majority of studies examine the optimal participation of DERs in the DA market without taking into account
the local grid constraints. Furthermore, several congestion management could be implemented to ensure
secure and reliable operation in the distribution network. However, the market-based methods are mainly of
interest in order to exploit the ﬂexibility of DERs.
Throughout this thesis, the optimal participation of an EV and HP aggregator in the regulating power
market is studied taking into consideration the local constraints for secure and reliable operation in terms of
maximum loading of the transformer. A rolling horizon optimization approach is applied for the bidding
strategies in the regulating power market, while the TC process is also integrated to the optimization
procedure to ensure normal conditions when the aggregators act as regulating power providers. Furthermore,
the probability of activation of the regulating bid is considered, in order to obtain a more risk-averse bidding
strategy.

3.1
Procedure description - Rolling horizon approach
In the following paragraphs a brief description of the procedure for the optimal DA planning and
participation of aggregators in the regulating market is given. Throughout this study the danish electricity
market structure is taken into consideration in order the aggregators to generate their optimal energy schedules
and determine the regulation offer with respect to minimization of their charging cost.
In the DA scheduling, the EV and HP aggregators receive the spot price data and determine their charging
schedule for the examined period. The spot price is used as an incentive signal which along with the speciﬁc
energy requirements (EVs) and the indoor temperature comfort zone (HPs) create the desirable charging
schedules. The minimization of the charging costs according to the projected spot price is mainly of interest.
Having deﬁned the optimal charging schedules for the examined period, the aggregators are able to
participate in the regulating power market by providing up/down-regulation services to the TSO and revise
(update) their energy schedule in the intra-day market. In this case, an optimization model is deployed to
determine the amount of regulating power that can be provided at each time slot resulting in a beneﬁcial
participation, namely a decrease in the aggregators’ costs. This optimization model enables the participation
of EV and HP aggregators as regulating power providers. The published spot prices and the upward and
downward regulation power price are used as inputs.
In order to optimize the participation of aggregators in the regulating power market, a rolling horizon
optimization is implemented from the ﬁrst time slot of the examined period. In the rolling horizon planning, a
3-hours optimization window with 15-minutes time intervals is considered. This means that the optimization
horizon consists of 12 time steps with duration of 15 minutes. The upward and downward regulating power
prices are expressed on a 15-minutes basis and are used to determine the amount of regulating energy for
each time slot for the next 3 hours. The regulating price is known for the ﬁrst time slot, while for the rest time
slots of the optimization window forecasts of the regulating power price are received using an uncertainty
rate. The farther the time slot from the current time step, the higher is the uncertainty in the regulating power
price. Forecasting of regulating prices is a demanding task, however throughout this thesis it is assumed that
quite precise forecasts (not perfect except for the ﬁrst time slot) of regulating power price are available for
the next 3 hours. Moreover, the update interval of 15 minutes works equally well with the 5 minutes interval.
However, the latter requires a faster metering data management system and increases the computational
burden [27].

charging requirement, as they are only activated when the buffer tank is not able to deliver the required
amount of energy. Although HPs are not activated so frequent if they are used along with the buffer tank,
they are responsible to maintain the temperature of water in the tank between a predeﬁned temperature range.

4.1
Framework of the Transactive Control
In this part of the thesis the TC framework is described to obtain an overview of the process used to
integrate the DSO constraints in the optimization model of aggregators’ participation in the regulating power
market. TC is a market-based coordination method which is used to handle the interactions between the
actors. The goal of TC is to reach a consensus, where the dynamic balance of demand and generation is
achieved and the interests of the actors are respected by exchanging information through an economic signal.
TC is an iterative procedure, where the accuracy and computational time is mainly of interest to achieve
efﬁcient coordination of market participants in the examined network.
Throughout this thesis, TC is applied to ensure normal network conditions in terms of congestion
management in the distribution level. The actors considered in the TC scheme are the HP aggregator, the
EV aggregator and the DSO. In the examined case, the main responsibility of DSO is to avoid congestion
problems regarding the maximum permissible loading of the transformer. The EVs and HPs are considered
an additional load required to be satisﬁed along with the existing conventional/base load of the low voltage
network.
To ensure that DSO constraints are respected in case of participation of aggregators in the regulating
power market, the TC process is required to be applied as a part of the rolling horizon optimization described
in section 3.1. The procedure used in this study is described as follows:
• The HP and EV aggregator determine the amount of regulating power for each time slot within the
optimization window (12 time slots) according to regulating power price data. The bidding strategy
presented in Method II is implemented.
• The total power from aggregators and base load in the low voltage network for the ﬁrst time slot are
summing up in order to check whether violation of local network constraints exists. IMO evaluates
the power schedule in terms of local grid constraints.
• In case of congestion, namely the total power exceeds the maximum loading of the transformer, a
shadow (congestion) price is communicated to the aggregators in order to update their regulation offer
for the ﬁrst time slot.
• At the end of the ﬁrst iteration of the TC process, it is checked whether the updated regulation offer
comply to the DSO constraints. If congestion still exists, a higher shadow price is charged to the

aggregators. The iterative procedure ends when the decision for regulating energy does not create
congestion problems to the grid satisfying a certain convergence criterion. The updated regulating
power for the ﬁrst time slot is applied, while the state variables and energy schedule of aggregators are
updated properly.
• The rolling horizon optimization is implemented for each time slot of the examined period. TC ensures
that the DSO constraints are respected for each time slot at the end of the optimization process.

At this point, it is important to underline that during the optimization process the actors have different
objectives. The main purpose of the aggregators is to minimize their charging cost by participating in the
regulating market following the bidding strategy. Additionally, the speciﬁc requirements for fully charged
EVs at the end of the charging period and indoor temperature within a predeﬁned temperature range must
be satisﬁed. Before submitting the bids to the TSO, the schedule of the regulating power is checked in
terms of DSO constraints. The main objective of the DSO is to maintain normal network operation. In the
examined case, the maximum allowable loading of the transformer is of interest, while the voltage limits are
not taken into consideration. A third party called independent market operator (IMO) is used to facilitate
the interactions between the actors and determine the shadow price at each iteration of the TC process. The
responsibility of the IMO is to manage congestion prices in a impartial and efﬁcient way.

The shadow price established by the IMO is an essential parameter for the process of TC. As afore-
mentioned, this price reﬂects whether the maximum loading of the transformer has been exceeded for the
ﬁrst time slot of the optimization window. More speciﬁc, shadow price can be interpreted as the difference
between the power schedule and the maximum allowable power. Throughout this analysis, a positive shadow
price indicates that the charging schedule exceeds the maximum allowable power from the transformer
creating congestion problems. On the other hand, a negative shadow price indicates that the allowable power
from the transformer is higher than the power charging schedule, namely there is potential for additional
charging at the time slot where a negative shadow price exists.
Throughout this study, the bidding strategy is applied prior to the TC process. Therefore, the regulating
power schedule after TC is desired to be as close as possible to the planning according to the bidding strategy.
To avoid deviation from the power schedule if there is no congestion problem in the system, only positive
prices are issued by the IMO. This means that if the allowable power from the transformer is higher than the
total power schedule (from base load, EV and HP aggregator), a zero congestion price is communicated to
the aggregators. Therefore, the aggregators do not deviate from the power schedules created before TC.
Moreover, a trade off between accuracy and computational time of the procedure is required in order to
ensure the efﬁciency of the TC process. Accuracy is of importance in order to determine the shadow price
that updates the regulating power schedule of aggregators in an optimal way. This means that in case of
congestion occurrence, the minimum deviation from the maximum allowable power is achieved after TC.
On the other hand, TC needs to be a fast process in order to be able to apply the results in real-time.
To achieve a compromise between accuracy and computational time of the TC, proper values of the step
size that updates the shadow price and threshold value of the convergence criterion are selected. The step size
indicates the rate by which the shadow price increases at each iteration of the TC algorithm. The convergence
criterion ensures that the difference between the shadow prices of the last two iterations is lower than a
certain value. The lower the threshold value of the convergence criterion, the more accurate is the resulting
congestion price. However, a very small threshold value contributes to an increase of the computational
time. The values of these parameters are explained in more detail in the mathematical formulation of the TC
process.

a positive congestion price is sent to the aggregators in order to reduce their charging power and respect
the limits for normal network operation. On the other hand, if DSO constraints are not violated, a zero
congestion price is communicated to aggregators, meaning that the power schedule remains the same as
before TC.

5.2
Optimization Tool and Solvers
Throughout this thesis the optimization toolbox YALMIP was used for the creation of the optimization
models for the participation of aggregators in the regulating power market and the TC process. YALMIP is a
high level modelling language interfaced by MATLAB which can be used along with several solvers and
provide solution to a wide range of optimization problems. The YALMIP optimization package was selected,
as the syntax is quite simple and the performance for the aforementioned problems was excellent.
Internal solvers are included to the YALMIP optimization package which are used mostly to solve convex
and non-convex optimization problems. YALMIP is able to detect the type of the problem created by the user
and choose an appropriate solver, while it converts the problem, if none of the internal solvers are applicable
[35]. Furthermore, external solvers can be added with modest effort and used properly by YALMIP to solve a
certain type of problem deﬁned by the user.
==PAGE:73==

In this project, YALMIP was used along with "linprog" and "fmincon" solvers for the optimization
problems of aggregators’ optimal energy schedule and aggregators’ participation in the regulating market,
respectively. Linear programming was applied for the DA scheduling, while non-linear programming was
applied for the participation in the regulating power market in order to avoid the possible simultaneous
upward and downward regulation service. Furthermore, the "gurobi" solver was used as an alternative to
"fmincon", which is able to solve mixed-integer problems (MIP). Therefore, binary variables were introduced
in the optimization models to prevent from simultaneous upward and downward regulation. The performance
of "gurobi" solver was similar to "fmincon", while the results obtained at the end of the optimization problem
were identical.
5.3
Results from the optimization models
In this part of the thesis the results derived from the optimization models are described and explained in
more detail. Initially, the optimal charging schedules of the HP and EV aggregator are created according to
the published spot price. Afterwards, the aggregators participate in the regulating market and the regulation
volumes are calculated for each time slot of the examined period. Two cases are considered, in which the
rolling horizon optimization method is implemented:
• Case 1: The aggregators participate in the regulating power market applying the bidding strategy
in Method I. Therefore, the amount of regulating energy at each time slot is determined by the
regulating power prices within the optimization window and the revised energy schedule according to
the published spot price.
• Case 2: The aggregators participate in the regulating power market applying a bidding strategy based
on historical data of deviation of regulating power price from the spot price from year 2016 (Method
II). A weighted average deviation is calculated for each hour of the day using as weighting factors
the probability of upward and downward regulation occurrence. This is used to predict the regulating
power price for the time slots beyond the optimization window. Thus, the regulation volumes for
each time slot of the optimization period can be calculated at each iteration of the rolling horizon
optimization. The revised energy schedule is affected by the regulating power provision outside the
optimization window.
In addition to the previous cases, a subcase is examined where a preferred indoor temperature is
introduced. Thus, the optimization model of the HP aggregator is modiﬁed to include the aforementioned
constraint. The regulation offers are calculated using the modiﬁed HP optimization model for both Case 1
and Case 2. However, the results only for the second case are displayed in this section.
To test the efﬁciency and reliability of the bidding strategies, the period of operation is extended to one
month. Therefore, the performance of the bidding strategies is assessed in the long-term. This means that
the charging costs from Case 1 and Case 2 are compared for one-month operation. Moreover, to underline
the signiﬁcance of the input data in Case 2, another case is created, where the weighted average calculation
is based on data from a single month in 2016.
It is signiﬁcant to mention that the costs associated with each of the aforementioned cases and subcase
are also presented and summarized in tables. In the last part of the results’ section, the total power schedule
of aggregators before and after the TC process is depicted. Furthermore, the effect of congestion price on the
regulation offer of each aggregator is presented separately. In the TC process, the time period where both
EVs and HPs are available for charging is selected, as there is a higher probability of congestion occurrence.

other cases, as the model in this case can perfectly determine the amount of regulating energy and the type
of regulation service it can provide at each time slot of the examined period. Therefore, the participation of
aggregators in the regulating market is optimized to the maximum extent. It is important to mention that the
HP model with a preferred indoor temperature is only examined, as in this case it is beneﬁcial for the HP
aggregator to provide regulation services according to the previous analysis. It is observed that there is still
room for improvement for creating a more optimal bidding strategy, as the cost is quite decreased, when
perfect information is received, compared to Case 2 (which performs the best). Possible improvements of
the created model are discussed in section 6.2 to mitigate the risk and uncertainty rate at each level of the
proposed optimization process.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion and Conclusions
This project performs a study for the participation of DERs (EVs and HPs) through aggregators in the
regulating power market considering the local grid constraints. Besides the response of aggregators to
the regulating price signal, a cost analysis is conducted to estimate the charging costs in the case where
aggregators act as regulating power providers. Two bidding strategies for the participation of aggregators in
the regulating market were proposed (Method I and Method II) and assessed in the long-term (one-month
operation). A TC process is integrated to the optimization procedure to ensure normal conditions in the
distribution grid. Finally, the effect of the TC on the ﬁnal decisions for up and down-regulation volumes is
examined.
6.1
Conclusions
The main conclusions of this thesis are summarized and discussed in the following lines:
• The participation of the EV aggregator in the regulating power market decreases the charging costs
either using the bidding strategy in Method I or Method II. More speciﬁcally, the bidding strategy in
Method II, which receives forecasts of regulating power prices as inputs for the whole optimization
period, favours the use of the EV aggregator as regulating power provider decreasing even more the
costs compared to Method I. The costs for the examined day reduced by 15.33% when Method II was
applied, while there was a 3.84% decrease in the case where Method I was used. As Method II is more
beneﬁcial than Method I, it means that the analysis in Method II resulted in sufﬁciently high-quality
forecasts of regulating power prices.
• The HP aggregator does not beneﬁt from the participation in the regulating power market neither
implementing the bidding strategy in Method I nor Method II. In the optimization model of HPs there
is not a speciﬁc energy requirement needed to be satisﬁed at the end of the examined period. Therefore,
the total charging power at the end of the period differs from the one in the DA scheduling. Having
selected a wide acceptable temperature range [17oC - 28oC], on the one hand the HP aggregator is
able to provide more regulating power, but on the other hand it is exposed to additional costs due to an
increase in the total charging power.
• For this reason, another case is examined where a preferred indoor temperature (22oC) with an
allowable ±10% deviation is deﬁned by the HP-users. Furthermore, this is considered a more
reasonable case, as the indoor temperature does not ﬂuctuate in a such wide range. In this case, the
results showed that it is beneﬁcial for HPs to provide regulation services, as the costs decreased
compared to the case where the HP aggregator follows the optimal energy schedule. Similarly to the

case of the EV aggregator, the performance of Method II is better than the performance of Method I in
the examined day. It was observed that, compared to the case where aggregators do not participate in
the regulating market, the costs decreased by 3.3% and 1.7% when the Method II and Method I were
employed, respectively. It can be concluded that the determination of a desired indoor temperature
decreases the amount of energy provided for regulation services, but also decreases the difference of
total charging power from the DA scheduling. Therefore, the charging costs become comparable.
• The charging costs are also calculated for the case where perfect information about the future regu-
lating power price and probability of bid activation are used in the optimization models. The cost
analysis showed that a 36.88% and 14.44% reduction is achieved for the EV and HP aggregator,
respectively, compared to the case where the aggregators do not provide regulation services. Therefore,
a minimization of the uncertainties at each level of the proposed bidding strategy could lead to a more
beneﬁcial participation of aggregators in the regulating market, decreasing the cost difference from
the case where perfect information is received.
• To evaluate the performance of the bidding strategies in the long-term, the operation period is extended
to one month. The assessment concluded that the bidding strategy created in Method II is more
beneﬁcial for the aggregators, as they decreased signiﬁcantly the charging costs compared to Method I.
However, the cost difference between Method I and Method II drops signiﬁcantly, if price data from
only one month are used for the prediction of regulating power prices. Therefore, the performance
of Method II depends on the quality of the regulating power price forecasts. More speciﬁcally, the
bidding strategy in Method II saves approximately 186 DKK and 78 DKK more than Method I, for
one-month operation for the EV and HP aggregator, respectively. On the other hand, the performance
of Method II deteriorated when price data from only one month were used, saving only 3 DKK and
16 DKK more than Method I for one-month operation for the EV and HP aggregator, respectively.
• The TC process effectively solved the congestion problem in the distribution grid, as the aggregators
modiﬁed properly their regulation offer to avoid overloading of the transformer. It is observed that both
HP and EV aggregator respond to the congestion price signal sent by the IMO. The same congestion
price is communicated to aggregators, however, it can be seen that EVs reduce more the power
schedule compared to HPs. This means that the EVs are more price-sensitive and more ﬂexible to
adjust their regulation offer. Furthermore, the HP optimization model is more complex depending
on the ambient and indoor temperature, convection losses and water temperature inside the tank.
Therefore, this may explain the fact that HPs are less ﬂexible and less responsive to the congestion
price signal.
• In addition, it is observable that the energy by which the power schedule of the EV aggregator is
reduced due to congestion price, is counteracted by an increase of the same amount of energy in the
following time slots, as EVs require a speciﬁc energy level in the battery at the end of the charging
period. This is not depicted in the power schedule of the HP aggregator, as they do not have a speciﬁc
energy requirement at the end of the examined period.
• The costs of aggregators increase after TC, as they deviate from the decisions from the optimal
bidding strategy deﬁned in Method II. However, this increase is slight (13-hours period) accounting
for approximately 1.4 DKK and 0.5 DKK for the EV and HP aggregator, respectively. Therefore, it
can be concluded that the TC process is efﬁcient for the examined day, as it prevents from congestion
problems (DSO’s interest) and has low impact on the charging costs of aggregators (aggregators’
interest).

6.2. DISCUSSION OF POSSIBLE IMPROVEMENTS OF THE CREATED MODEL
65
• Finally, in this project, the maximum computational time needed for the TC algorithm to calculate
the proper value of shadow price in case of congestion is 35 seconds. Therefore, the procedure is
quite fast and the results can be implemented in real-time application of the algorithm. An 1-minute
market is proposed, where the gate closes one minute before the actual operating time. Thus, the
aggregators can revise their regulation offer according to the update after the TC process. However,
this is only applicable for the examined case with the speciﬁc number of EVs and HPs connected
to the distribution grid. A more complete study can be conducted taking into consideration the total
number of nodes in the national transmission and distribution grid in Denmark using estimations about
the expected number of EVs and HPs connected to the system, in a future scenario.
Previous studies have shown that the participation of aggregators in the regulating power market can
be proﬁtable resulting in a substantial decrease of their charging costs. In [36], the participation of an EV
aggregator reduced the costs approximately by 20%, in a case where predictions of regulating power prices
were used as inputs for the whole optimization period. This result is close to the one obtained throughout
this project using Method II, where a 15.33% decrease is observed. Furthermore, in [31], a HP aggregator
achieved a 18% reduction of charging costs from the participation in the regulating power market for one-day
operation, employing a strategy where both spot price and regulating price predictions were used and a
desired indoor temperature was deﬁned by the HP-users. However, in the case examined in this thesis, only
a 3.3% reduction was achieved, as the costs are compared to the case where perfect information about the
spot price are received in the DA scheduling.
6.2
Discussion of possible improvements of the created model
In the following lines limitations of the current model are presented and possible modiﬁcations for further
improvement are recommended. Furthermore, ambiguous points throughout the thesis are discussed.
Firstly, throughout this thesis it was assumed that all EVs are available for charging at the same time
period (lasting for 13 hours), while EV-users have the same energy requirements. In reality, the EV
availability is characterized by stochasticity, which is not considered throughout this study for simplicity
reasons and due to lack of information.
Moreover, bidirectional EVs are not used in this project and thus the potential of increasing proﬁts from
supplying energy back to the grid is eliminated. However, bidirectional EVs increase signiﬁcantly the wear
of battery due to a larger number of charging cycles. Therefore, the cost from EV-battery degradation should
be included in the objective function of the EV aggregator to compensate the EV-owners for the increased
cycling wear of the EV-battery.
HPs are considered the only heat source inside the house, while the solar irradiation is also neglected.
Therefore, this increases the consumption of HPs resulting in lower ﬂexibility to provide regulation services
and lower sensitivity to the price signals.
A comfort temperature zone from 17oC to 28oC is assumed in this study deviating from the common
one, which ranges from 20oC to 24oC [37]. However, it was interesting to examine the participation of a
HP aggregator in the regulating power market in the case where a higher amount of regulating power can
be provided. A more realistic case is also examined, where a desired indoor temperature is deﬁned from
end-users. Furthermore, the lower acceptable water temperature inside the tank was 40oC, but recent studies
state that the lower limit should be increased at 70oC to avoid the exposure to pathogenic microorganism.
From the cost analysis in section 5.3.3, it can be observed that there is room for improvement of the
proposed bidding strategy, as there are uncertainties in the model that can be reduced and beneﬁt even more
the aggregators. The main uncertainties of the model are derived from the regulating price forecasts and
==PAGE:92==
66
CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
the probability of bid activation. The forecasting of regulating power prices is a demanding task which
often increases the risk for the participation of aggregators in the regulating power market. This means that
low-quality regulating price predictions result in faulty decisions for regulation offer, which will probably
increase the charging costs. To mitigate this risk, a stochastic optimization model is proposed which includes
different scenarios for the future regulating prices (different possible price realizations). More speciﬁcally,
a probability rate is included in the regulating price predictions instead of receiving ﬁxed values. The
creation of different scenarios for the future regulating prices contributes to a more complete study taking
into account possible deviations from the forecasts. Moreover, to reduce the uncertainty derived from the
probability of bid activation, a Monte Carlo simulation is recommended. This simulation can deﬁne multiple
randomly-generated scenarios for the wind and demand realizations for the examined period. Thus, the
optimal activation probability of the bid can be estimated which leads to a more beneﬁcial participation of
aggregators in the regulating power market. To implement the aforementioned modiﬁcations, stochastic
programming is required to be used.
Different congestion management methods can be used, instead of TC, in order to satisfy the DSO
constraints and ensure reliable and secure operation in the local grid. To reduce the computational time, the
dynamic tariff method can be applied which aims to estimate the minimum tariff that causes the aggregators
to properly update their regulation offer in order to comply to the local grid constraints. However, the
problem is solved centrally by DSO in this method and thus private information of aggregators and end-users
are being published.
Throughout this thesis, the TC is implemented only for the ﬁrst time slot of the rolling horizon approach.
However, the revised energy schedule and the decisions for the other time slots of the optimization window
may violate the local grid constraints. A TC scheme for the whole examined period could be applied in order
to prevent from congestion problems in the following time slots. This will require a higher computational
time, though.
In the case where the revised energy schedule causes violation of the local grid constraints, the aggregators
should update their regulation offer and provide up-regulation service. Throughout this study, the aggregators
do not receive payment from the up-regulation offer in case of congestion, as they would beneﬁt from
congestion occurrence (incurred by them) and further encourage strategic behavior of aggregators. This
could be avoided, if TC was applied for the whole examined period, as aforementioned in the previous
paragraph.
6.3
Future work
In this section of the thesis, proposals for future work are given, which are presented in the following
lines:
• A more detailed EV model can be created, where the EV availability patterns and the individual energy
requirements of EV-users are considered. Furthermore, bidirectional EVs would be interesting to be
used in order to examine a case where EVs participate more actively in the regulating power market
by discharging energy back to the grid.
• The thermal model of the house can be improved including also the solar irradiation in the heat balance
equations and more precise calculation of the heat capacity of the structure.
• A more complete study for congestion management is recommended for future work, which takes into
consideration the voltage constraints at each bus of the system.

• Several scenarios with different penetration levels of ﬂexible loads in the distribution network can be
