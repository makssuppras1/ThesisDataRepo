Abstract
At the end of the Muslim reign in the Iberian Peninsula, in the XIIth century, a new
religious order coming from France called Cistercian expanded. The Cistercian Archi-
tecture is best described as minimalist, sober and reflects the transition between the
Roman and Gothic architecture movements. The project’s motivation is to crystallise
the acoustics of such architecture to protect the Spanish acoustical heritage. To this
end, the goal is to verify the capacity of simulating accurately a directional source in
buildings with such architecture. The investigations took place in the ”Monasterio
de Piedra” in Zaragoza, Spain.
After the creation and calibration of the geometrical acoustics models of the stud-
ied rooms thanks to in-situ measurements, the used directional source is modelled.
Then a prior processing of the measured and simulated soundtracks to be compared is
carried out. The subsequent validation goes through assessing in subjective listening
tests the differences between simulation and measurements of various subjective pa-
rameters, known as reverberance, clarity, plausibility, distance and coloration. The
study relies on sticking as much as possible to the original use of the rooms, using
a directional source mimicking the voice directivity pattern and anechoic signals of
biblical readings as well as ancient liturgical chants.
The results show that the calibration process permits to obtain a reliable geomet-
rical acoustics model, with differences below the recommendation of the ISO 3382-1
standard. The 17 participants of the subjective listening test judged the measured
recordings very slightly more reverberant, less clear, further to the source, as plausible
and as coloured than the simulated audios.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and goal
Credible and convincing acoustic simulations are of a major importance in the room
acoustics field. Since the first fully simulated room acoustics model in the late sev-
enties, and with the continuous enhancement of computation power and acoustic
knowledge gathering, the realism has been constantly improving.
Today’s simula-
tions are getting closer to the real signals although the quality of the output widely
depends on the engineer’s rigour while creating the geometrical acoustic (GA) model.
This model allows architects and acoustic engineers to easily communicate about
sound properties thanks to a powerful tool called auralisation. Kleiner and al. [8]
define it as ”the process of rendering audible, by physical or mathematical modelling,
the sound field of a source in a space, in such a way as to simulate the binaural
listening experience at a given position in the modelled space.” This name was given
as an analogy of visualisation in a 3D model . The more accurate a GA model will
be, the more realistic will be the auralisation it will produce.
The active sources in the spaces that are usually relevant to model are for the
most part directional. For example, a mezzo soprano performing in an opera house
will have a particular directivity. Consequently, the simulation will have to take that
voice directivity into account. The same goes for numerous situations, a PA system
into a concert hall, a violin in an orchestra pit...
Although the model creation and calibration have been widely investigated during
the last decade, there is still room for improvement. While omnidirectional sources
have been successfully auralised objectively and subjectively [15], there is still no
relevant research on how directional sources auralisation are perceived by subjects.
This thesis takes advantage of having a monastery at one’s disposal composed of
rooms with contrasting acoustical parameters. In addition, anechoic recordings of
Spanish liturgical rites are available to use for the tests and measurements. Having
both building and recordings contextually matching is a real asset to evaluate the
realism of the simulation using a subjective listening test.
The motivation of the study is to have sufficient knowledge to be able to recreate
the monastery’s church acoustics currently in ruins, in further investigations. This
goes through validating the hypothesis that, in the studied rooms, using the last
calibration methods developed and, using contextual anechoic recordings to gener-
ate auralisations, the signals are subjectively judged to be similar compared with

1.2.1
Acoustic parameters
1.2.1.1
Reverberation Time
The reverberation time characterises the most known subjective aspect of a room, the
reverberance. The reverberation time T is defined as the time it takes to the sound in
the room to decrease by 60 dB after a continuous sound source has been shut off [20].
However, for the measurements the decay curve is often analysed from -5 to -35 dB
and then extrapolated to a 60 dB decay (see fig.1.1), the parameters is then called
T30. T20 is obtained in the same way extrapolating the decay between -5 and -25 dB.
Finally, it can also be obtained from a decay between 0 and 10 dB, EDT. The latter
one, EDT, is more associated with the early reverberance (”dynamic reverberance”)
while the others are related to the late reverberance.
In this study the reverberation times defined by EDT and T20 are going to be
used to characterise both the dynamic and late reverberance.

decay is, in practice, measured between -5dB and -35dB of the initial
level (from Barron [3]).

component. It is defined as the ratio in dB scale between the early energy (between
0 and 80 ms) and the later one (from 80 ms). Therefore clarity is clearly linked with
the notion of reverberance. The higher the early energy compared to the later one
the higher the clarity, the clearer the sound.

1.2.1.3
Just noticeable difference
The Just Noticeable Difference (JND) or difference limen is defined as the smallest
change that can be detected subjectively.
The ISO 3382-1 standard[20] on room
acoustic measurements proposes JNDs values of 5% for reverberation parameters and
1 dB for clarity parameters. It should be noted, however, that in a survey by Höhne
et al. [6] the difference limen for C80 was determined to have a value of about 2.5
dB.

1.2.2.1
GA method
Geometrical Acoustics take into account the geometry of a room, the distribution of
the absorption materials on each surface, the source and receiver positions. There
are two main models of sound propagation, ray tracing and image sources.
The first one relies on tracing rays from the source positions that undergo reflec-
tions until their energy has been reduced below a chosen threshold (time-based or
chosen according to the sound-to-noise ratio). The direction of the reflections follows
Snell’s law, that is the angle of reflection equals the angle of incidence. The angle
reflection will randomly slightly differ from the incident one if a scattering coefficient
is added to the surface.
The second one, based on image sources calculations, is often only used for early
reflections. Indeed, this method is computationally heavy as the number of reflections
and thus image sources increase proportionally to t3 (where t is the time [16]), because
of the three dimensional nature of rooms. The image sources are created following
the specular paths of sound rays. They can use both complex pressure data and/or
energy data[22].
1.2.2.2
ODEON’s Hybrid Method
This thesis has been carried out with the use of the room acoustics simulation software
ODEON[1]. This program combines both GA methods seen previously. For the early
reflections the software uses a mixture of the Image Source Method and Ray-Tracing
and for the late reflection a special ray tracing called Ray-radiosity. The transition
between the first and the second method is determined by a parameter chosen by the
user called Transition Order (see fig.1.2).
Furthermore, as remembered in the conclusion of the round-robin leaded by Katz
(2004)[7], some studies reported variations in RIR analysis algorithm implementa-
tions. Hence, it is important to use a single analysis tool to estimate the acoustic
parameters when comparing results from measured and simulated RIRs. Considering
this guideline, ODEON will be the only software used during this study.

1.3
Related work
This section will explore numerous breakthroughs in the field of acoustic modelling
of historic buildings and auralization for virtual acoustics. The purpose here is not
to give an extensive description of each of those investigations but rather to have an
overview of the current state of research.
First of all, it has to be noted that the majority of the investigations in this area
are based on the ISO 3382-1 standard[20]. This document describes how should be
carried out the measurements of room impulse responses (RIRs) and how the room
acoustic parameters are derived from it.
Even though some started to investigate the auralisation quality before, in his
thesis, Lokki started using some more formal methods. Voices signals in a conference
room were recorded and simulated (auralised). The audios were then to be compared
both objectively and subjectively. As the perceptual differences were quite subtle he
used the same kind of test that was usually done for evaluating audio codecs. The
chosen comparison method, recommended in ITU-R BS.1116-1[2], was double-blind
triple stimulus with hidden reference method. He concluded that it is indeed possible
to produce quality auralisations and that this ones were hardly distinguished from
the recordings made in-situ.
Then, in 2008, Wang and Vigeant[25] investigated the effect of simulated direc-
tivity on perception. Thanks to ODEON, they produced auralisations using three
directional source types with the same sound power: omnidirectional, moderately
directional and an artificial, extremely directional beaming source. Subjects did sig-
nificantly differentiate between auralisations from the omni-directional source and
the extreme beaming source. Subjective results from comparing these two sources in
terms of reverberation, clarity and realism were generally consistent with objective

2.1
Presentation of the studied building
From its inaugural religious ceremony in 1218, the walls of the monastery ”Monasterio
de Piedra”, located in Zaragoza in Spain, are hiding eight centuries of history and
evolution. The construction of the monastery took place right during the transition
between the Romanesque and Gothic architecture artistic movement. The result of
this hybrid is called Cistercian Architecture and is best described as minimalist, sober,
austere and bright.
The monastery’s church is currently in ruins as a result of years of abandonment
after expropriations carried out by the Spanish government during the nineteenth
century. It can be seen in Figure 2.1(b) and will be reconstructed in further projects
of the acoustic laboratory ArquiLAV of the Technical University of Madrid (UPM),
using the knowledge gathered in this study.
The studied rooms in this thesis will be the chapter hall (see Figure 2.1(c) and
Figure 2.1(d)) and the refectory (see Figure 2.1(a)). Both rooms overlook a central
fountain (noise source) as can be seen in Figure 2.2. The chapter hall entrance is
opened whereas refectory’s entrance is closed by a solid wooden door.
The chapter hall was used every morning by the monks to discuss religious texts
and the compliance of the rules of the monastery by the hosts. The monks were
sitting in a circle around the columns with the abbot (head of the monastery) facing
the entrance of the room. This one read a chapter of the text (hence the name of
chapter hall), and then gave the floor to the monks. The room is mainly made of
unpainted limestone/concrete, organised a square of 9 ribbed vaults and sounds quite
dry.
The second one, the refectory, was one of the most important room of the monastery.
The meal in the refectory was considered a daily ceremony that had to be carried out
with the highest solemnity, complete silence, only interrupted by a religious reading

2.2
Room Acoustics Measurements
Actual in-situ measurements have to be carried out to serve as a reference during the
model calibration process. This section describes how the measurements have been
made. As previously mentioned, the rooms have been chosen given their different
characteristics in size and reverberation. Due to these differences some parameters
will differ in the protocol, however the main process remains the same. The mea-
surements were achieved following the normative document ISO-3382:1[20]. Besides
impulse responses used for calibration, some signals recorded in anechoic chamber
were played directly in the rooms in order to be later processed and used for the
listening tests.
These signals and the reason behind these measurements will be
explained later on, in the section 3.

Measurement positions
The measurement positions were chosen in order to have a meshing dense enough
to describe the acoustic behaviour of the room. Besides, the sources positions were
decided according to the typical usage of the room.

Input/Output equipment
The signal generated by DIRAC was reproduced by two different types of speakers.
The first one was an omnidirectional speaker Brüel and Kjaer Type 4292, used to

is particularly relevant for the present study given the fact that these rooms were
almost only used for speech or singing.
As for the input equipment, the e-sweep stimuli were recorded with two different
sound cards each on plugged on a different computer unit. The first one was the Bruel
and Kjaer sZE-0948 that is also used to generate the aforesaid stimuli. Its output is
connected to one of the loudspeaker (omni or echo speech). The input is a sound level
meter Brüel and Kjaer 2250. The frequency sampling in DIRAC of the sound card is
set to 48 kHz. The software DIRAC will automatically calculate the impulse response
from the sweep input (from the sound level meter). The second sound device is a
RME Fireface UFX combined with an RME Octamic II. This one is connected to 4
omnidirectional microphones DPA ST 4006. For further investigations, a microphone
TSL Soundfield SPS200 and a Head and Torso simulator Brüel and Kjaer Type 4100-

thanks to the software REAPER on a spare computer, at a frequency sampling rate
of 44.1 kHZ.

2.3
3D model design
General guidelines on room modelling[1] are given in ODEON’s manual. The first
parameter to have in mind is the recommended size of the surfaces. Indeed, a major
approximation in the classical laws of geometrical acoustics to calculate the energy
reflected is that surfaces are considered to be infinitely large in comparison to the
wavelength. Small elements will either reflect more energy than their area permits or
conversely will be ignored by the software as their surface area is too small. Rather
than improving the accuracy of the results, the only consequence will be an increased
computation time. Accordingly, it should be avoided to use many small surfaces to
recreate the exact geometry of a room. However, small surfaces will always be needed
for complex geometries in order to close the volume.
Secondly, the curved surfaces cannot be taken into account in ODEON and, conse-
quently, should be approximated by dividing them into plane sections. It is however
important not to divide too coarsely a surface with a focusing effect, as it is the case
with the roofs studied in this thesis. A rule of thumb is to make subdivisions about
every 10° to 30°. This rule was applied for all the modelled curved surfaces in both
models.
The available plans of the monastery were sparse and not accurate enough to
actually design a 3D model of the studied rooms. Hence, the first step of the study
was to get correct geometric measurements of the refectory and the chapter hall. The
measurements were carried out using a 3D laser scanner Faro® Focus3D. The principle
relies on a laser being pointed in all directions over 360°. According to the time the
laser takes to be reflected, a point is assigned at a given distance and direction.
The ensemble of points is called a point cloud. In Figure 2.4 can be seen the so
called cloud point of the chapter hall visualised in the software Autodesk ReCap™.
It has to be remembered that this points will only serve as a basis for the 3D model.
Indeed, this cloud is only composed of a very dense point database and does not
include any defined surface.
The point cloud of each of the two studied rooms were then imported in a BIM
(Building Information Modelling) software, Autodesk Revit. The advantage of this

Geometrical acoustics model creation and
calibration
The creation of the geometrical acoustics model consists in defining properties to the
surfaces of the 3D model. These properties intend to simulate the way a surface will
behave when sound waves interact with it.
2.4.1
Definition of the absorption and scattering coefficients
The first one is the sound absorption coefficient.
It is defined as the fraction of
the total sound that is absorbed at each reflection on a surface. This parameter is
frequency dependent. ODEON possesses an embedded database of material sound ab-
sorption. Various other databases are available, the most exhaustive one is the PTB
database[21]. This is the largest collection of absorption coefficients for different fre-
quencies as found in published measurements, handouts from manufacturers and in
relevant literature.
The indexed coefficients are generally obtained from measure-
ments in reverberation chambers derived from Sabine equation and thus in random
sound incidence conditions. Some other databases or manufacturers sources provide
the coefficient obtained in normal incidence absorption condition. In this case, it is
necessary to convert them into random sound incidence condition coefficients in order
to be used in ODEON.
The second parameter is the scattering coefficient. The latter one describes the way
the incident sound is reflected. The rays can be either specularly reflected, that is
following Snell’s law, or reflected with an angle independent from the incident ray
(memory-less reflection), the sound is said to be scattered. This effect is due to the
irregularities of the surface (bumps and roughness) as well as the diffraction caused
by the limited size of the surfaces and their edges. This parameter is also frequency
dependent.
Even though ODEON is a GA algorithm based software, the scattering effects are
accounted for. Indeed, during the First International Round Robin on Room Acous-
tical Computer Simulations[23], only simulation programs which included scattering
were found to provide reliable results. The user controlled scattering is called s and
varies between 0 and 1 according to the irregularities of the surface. The scattering
by diffraction is automatically taken into account by ODEON. There is two different
ways of processing the scattering depending on the algorithm used (before or after
the transition order as seen in section.1.2.2.2).
The first model is a vector based scattering, included in the ray-tracing algorithm.
The direction of the reflected ray is found by adding the specular vector multiplied by
a factor (1-s), s being the scattering coefficient, to a scattered vector (which direction
is randomly generated according the Lambert distribution) scaled by a factor s. This
vector operation is represented in the Figure.2.7.

2.4.2
Calibration protocol
In his thesis, Postma[13] developed a calibration method consisting of a list of steps.
This method was subjectively tested for omnidirectional sources comparing the mea-
surements in-situ with the simulated auralisations and was successfully validated.
The listening test results showed no significant perceptual differences regarding rever-
berance, tonal balance, coloration and plausibility. However, some slight differences
were found in clarity and distance to the source. The calibration method adapted
and applied to ODEON is the following:
1. Room impulse responses are measured in the studied rooms (see section 2.2)
The results of these measurements are used as a reference for the calibration.
2. The 3D model is created and remains unchanged during the process of calibra-
tion (see section 2.3).
3. The rooms’ materials are defined and the absorption coefficients are found in
various databases. The dynamic range for the calibration are defined by the
variations between the percentage of differences between the diverse databases
coefficients. As seen in section 2.4.1 a special care should be given to assigning
random incidence absorption coefficients rather than normal incidence coeffi-
cients.
4. A first version of the geometrical acoustics model is created by importing the 3D
model in ODEON and assigning acoustical properties to the surfaces (absorption
and scattering).
5. The implementation method of scattering can lead to stochastic variations in
some acoustic modelling software programs as CATT. For this reason, ten
simulation repetitions are normally run of the initial iteration and taking per
acoustical parameter the average over the standard deviations per position, the
run-to-run variation of the model is established. However ODEON provides a
non-probabilistic solution thus this step will not be taken into account.
6. The absorption coefficients are modified thanks to the genetic optimiser tool
provided by ODEON. The first modified materials should be the one with the
largest surface areas. The dynamic ranges of changes in properties are defined
by the variations found in step 3. At this point the difference between simulation
and measurements should be less than 1 JND for the reverberation time.
7. In order to study the effect of scattering on the acoustic parameters T20 and
C50, two simulations are run using the absorption coefficients found in step
6 with the scattering coefficients set to 0% and 100% respectively for all the
surfaces.
8. Thanks to the scattering study carried out in step 7, the clarity parameters C50
and C80 are brought within 1 JND by changing the scattering coefficients.

extract is the recording of the loudspeaker Echo Speech Source playing some anechoic

3.3
Test designing
The test will be based on a method widely used in the nineties to judge the quality
of audio codecs.
The recommendation is known as BS.1116 and can be found in
the ”Methods for the Subjective Assessment of small Impairments in Audio Systems
including Multichannel Sound Systems”[2]. The method is called ”double-blind triple-
stimulus with hidden reference”. This method is sensitive, stable, and allows accurate
detection of small differences. The test is designed on Matlab with the embedded
App Designer.
For the first part, the subject is presented three stimuli ”A”,”B” and ”C” of
approximately 10 seconds each. One of them is the measured signal, another is the
simulated signal and the last one is either one or the other. The signals are randomly
assigned to the letters. The subject is asked to choose which one sounds different
to him. He is allowed to listen two times maximum each signal. He can either say
which one sounded differently or say that he did not perceive any difference between
the two signals. In the second part, the subject has a visual reference of which room
corresponds to the soundtracks. He is now presented two signals 1 and 2. One of them
is the measured signal and the other one the simulated signal. The subject is asked
to judge several parameters on a 7-grade scale. He has to compare the parameters
between both sounds. The fourth grade (middle one) corresponds to ”No difference”,
the fifth ”Sound 2 is slightly more”, sixth ”sound 2 is moderately more”, seventh
”sound 2 is a lot more”, and the same goes for ”sound 1” for three to one.
The
subject can listen two times to each sound for each parameter.
==PAGE:51==
3.4 Results
41
Both parts are repeated as many times as the number of different positions by
the number of different types of signal by the number of rooms, for a total of 8
repetitions. One of the most important consideration of this test was to make sure
to limit its duration between 20 and 25 minutes. This precaution is taken to have
all the attention of the subjects during the test. Considering the duration of each
extracts and the fact that the subject would use the maximum of listenings allowed,
the number of parameters to be judged was limited to 6. These parameters are:
• Plausibility: Given the visual description of where the recording was done, this
parameters corresponds the realism of the extract.
• Reverberance: Time that sound takes to decrease.
A high reverberance is
identified by a lingering sound.
• Clarity: Defines how well each sound can be distinguished from one another.
A low clarity means that sounds will merge together. A speech will be more
difficult to understand because the consonants are masked by louder lingering
sound, the vowels.
• Distance: Perceived distance from the source.
• Coloration: Amount of high frequencies present in the sound. The stimulus
sounds fake, as if it was reproduced by an old radio station.
Beforehand, the parameters were discussed with each subject and some audio
examples were given to fully understand their meaning.
However, the difference
between clarity and intelligibility proved to be a difficult concept to understand for
many of the subjects and thus they were told to only judge the clarity and both
concept definitions were combined.
There were 17 participants from 19 to 66 years old, a mean of 38.6 y.o and a
standard deviation of 16.0 y.o. The subjects were presented the stimuli at a moderate
level (same level between each extracts but no absolute calibration) with Sennheiser
HD650 headphones, using a RANE HC6S headphone amplifier in a DEMVox ECO100
sound isolation booth.
Some images from the test translated from Spanish to English can be found in
the Appendix B.

3.5
Discussion
The results demonstrate a high discrepancy in the evaluations. Out of five parameters
3 are judged with values ranging from the minimum to the maximum included. For
instance, the coloration is rated from -3 to 3 and on average judged without any
difference perceived between measured and simulated audios. This distribution of the
evaluations characterises the difficulty for the subjects to understand in a such a short
period of time the concept of coloration. Another cause might be that the participant
all reported normal hearing but were not tested with an audiometry. A solution to
these issues is to increase the number of participants as well as selecting trained ears
(as musicians) and perform an audiometry test for each individual. However, it should
also be noted that during the ”double-blind triple-stimulus with hidden reference” the
participants distinguished the measured and simulated stimulus in 83.4% of the cases,
which shows they were able to notice small differences between signals.
With this preliminary notice in mind, the results are subsequently discussed in
relation with the value of the simulated parameters after the calibration process.
It has first to be noted that given the spectral distribution of the signals (more
power in low frequencies) and the sensibility of the speakers (more power in high
frequencies) the most influencing frequencies should be between 250 Hz and 1 kHz.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
Starting from the desire of protecting the historical Spanish heritage, and more par-
ticularly the Cistercian architecture, this thesis exposed the successive steps in order
to virtually recreate the acoustics of an old historic building. Previous studies set the
foundations[9] and the procedures[13] to apply in order to design subjectively valid
simulations. However, this investigation verified that these basis could be apply for
directional sources with contextual signals. Indeed, simulations and measurements of
a directional source mimicking the human voice directivity pattern and reproducing
anechoic signals of a biblical reading and liturgical Gregorian chants were compared
thanks to a subjective listening test. The consistency between the environment where
the subjects are supposed to be listening and the actual signals is of the utmost im-
portance to carry out a truthful comparison between simulations and measurements.
First the building ”Monasterio de Piedra” was presented. The first studied room
was a chapter hall with a fairly low reverberation time (below 1.5 seconds) and the
second one a refectory with a rather high reverberation time (around 6 seconds for
low to mid-frequencies).
The measurements of the room acoustic parameters according to the ISO3382
standard was then depicted. Next, the creation and calibration of the geometrical
acoustics model was described, starting from the 3D modelling of the rooms. The
calibration was based on a method developed by Postma and Katz[14] in 8 steps.
The absorption and scattering properties of the material were successively studied as
well as the influence of the algorithm used on ODEON. At the end of this procedure
almost all the average of the objective parameters considered (EDT, T20, C50 and
C80) were brought below 1 JND compared to the prior measurements. The directivity
of the directional source (also used during the measurements) was then calculated.
Thanks to the measurements of the sensitivity of this source with the microphones
that were used to record in-situ, the source was fully characterised as well with the
frequency response of the measurement system (microphone). These results permitted
to simulate the source in ODEON as well as compensate for the measurement system
response.
The resulting simulated room impulse responses were then processed and back-
ground noise equal to the measurements was added to the simulated signal. After
the binauralisation of both signals thanks the impulse response of a Kemar Head
and Torso Simulator, the extracts were compared in a subjective listening test. Five
parameters were tested, plausibility, reverberation, clarity, distance and coloration.
==PAGE:58==
48
4 Conclusion
The results demonstrated that the simulated audios were fairly closed to the mea-
sured audios. Very slight differences were found on average. The measurements were
judged more reverberant, less clear and further to the source than the simulations.
The plausibility and coloration were found to be equal on average. However the high
discrepancy in some results indicates the need of a higher number of participants in
further studies or selecting only participants with trained ears, as musicians. If the
first solution is chosen, the parameters should be simplified as many participants did
not clearly understand every concepts.
Nonetheless, the results show that following the given calibration protocol bring-
ing the differences between simulated and measured parameters below 1 JND, taking
into account the measurements system frequency response, the HRTFs, the sound-to-
noise ratio, a directional source can be successfully simulated. Promising studies[12]
already showed that dynamic directivity voice sources were judged more plausible
than static voice sources. Further research could focus on creating a dynamic direc-
tivity source along with a database of recordings of common directivity sources as
instruments, voices and noise sources. A subsequent subjective listening test compar-
ing the measured and simulated dynamic directional sources would finish to prove
the realism of the powerful tool that is auralisation.
Finally, this thesis showed that with the actual tools of computed acoustic simula-
tions, the Spanish Cistercian heritage can thoroughly be virtually recreated. Thanks
to this study, the historic buildings can be simulated in their antique usage to protect
their old acoustical legacy.

APPENDIX C
Matlab code
This section includes the Matlab code programmed to process the simulated and
recorded soundtracks as well as the creation of the background noise (see listing C.1).
Also, Figure C.1 shows the gain in dB of the filter’s transfer function used to create
the background noise from a Gaussian white noise.
1 %% Convertion to binaural tracks
2 %Sampling frequency
3 fs=44100;
4 %Load the recorded track
5 audio_rec = audioread('recorded_signals\soundtrack.wav');
6 %Load the impulse response simulated in ODEON
7 ir_sim=audioread('SimulatedIR\IRsim.wav');
8 %Load the anechoic signal used in the recorded track
9 signal=audioread('anechoic_signals\speaking.wav');
10 % Convertion from one to two channels tracks
11 bin_audio_rec=[audio_rec audio_rec];
12 bin_audio_sim=[ir_sim ir_sim];
13 %Convolution of the recorded track and simulated RIR with the IR of Kemar
14 % at angle 0° (+convolution with the anechoic signal for the simulated RIR)
15 convoluted_rec=conv(bin_audio_rec ,kemar0);
16 convoluted_ir_sim=conv(bin_audio_sim ,kemar0);
17 convoluted_ir_sim=conv(convoluted_ir_sim ,signal);
18
19 %% Background noise
20 %Nyquist freq
21 fny=fs/2;
22 %Number of samples
23 samples=size(audio_rec ,1);
24 %Frequencies ANSI
25 freq=[0 125.89 251.19 501.19 1000 1995.3 3981.1 fny];
26 %Attenuation needed in dB for whitenoise of 40dB(per band)
27 %The attenuation depends of the room
28 att_ref=[200 0 5.1 11.4 13.5 16.1 17.5 200];
29 %Create a uniform white noise of 40 dB(per band)
30 %The value in dB has no reference
31 whiteNoise=wgn(samples ,1,47.8*2);
32 %Normalised frequencies
33 fn=freq/fny;
34 %Creation of the filter to recreate the background noise
35 coeffs=fir2(8000,fn,10.^(-att_ref/20));
36 %Filter the white noise
37 filtered_whitenoise=filtfilt(coeffs,1,whiteNoise);
