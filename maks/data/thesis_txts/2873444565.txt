Abstract
Waste management plays a crucial role in the transition toward a circular economy. As part
of the European Green Deal, the European Commission has introduced an ambitious policy
framework aimed at reducing greenhouse gas emissions, decoupling economic growth from re­
source consumption, and making Europe the first climate­neutral continent by 2050 (European
Commission, n.d.­b; European Union, 2019, 2024). Within this agenda, waste is increasingly
viewed not as a burden but as a resource that must be better managed, reused, and recy­
cled (European Union, 2024). A key prerequisite for effective policymaking is the availability of
transparent and comprehensive data, as this enables policymakers to monitor progress, prior­
itize initiatives, and evaluate the environmental and economic performance of waste systems
(Albizzati et al., 2024; Sund et al., 2025).
This thesis addresses a critical gap in Danish waste data by introducing a public methodology
to estimate generated waste quantities. The methodology is designed for application within the
Danish waste management system using annual waste data published by the Danish Environ­
mental Protection Agency (MST). The model was developed to align with the core structure
and principles of the official national model developed by the Danish Research Institute for
Economic Analysis and Modelling (DREAM), the results of which are not publicly available.
The methodology was applied to a case study for the 2022 national waste data and validated
through comparison with model outputs obtained by the DREAM group. The methodology has
been designed to be directly applicable to the national waste data system (ADS) dataset, using
input files provided in the supplementary material to automatically run and present the main
results of applying the methodology for the case study. The methodology follows six overall
steps to estimate generated waste: 1) data collection and aggregation; 2) distributing data onto
waste groups; 3) aggregating individual ADS entries into waste classification codes; 4) deter­
mining target materials and impurities within each waste group; 5) redistributing impurities into
targeted waste groups; and 6) combining all data into one final overview of generated waste.
Data processing files were designed to allow users of the methodology to easily adjust waste
classifications, and some additional data investigation files have been provided to help users
explore the waste quantities contained within the waste classification codes.
The case study revealed that approximately 12.2 million tonnes of waste were collected in Den­
mark in 2022, with industries responsible for 67% of this total. Approximately 31% of collected
waste was either missorted or collected as residual waste, leading to major differences in col­
lected and generated waste distributions. Approximately 67% of all waste redistributions per­
formed in the methodology were applied to residual waste. Thus, the composition of residual
waste used in the methodology was found to be important for the results obtained from it. The
model’s sensitivity to changes in residual waste composition was estimated, and it was found
that a 1% change in composition results in a shift of 22 thousand tonnes of generated waste.
This sensitivity is especially noticeable for waste groups containing little other waste, such as
textiles (TEX), plastics (PLA), and waste suitable for landfilling (LANI). Despite these variations,
the overall structure and outcomes of the methodology remain robust across the investigated
compositions. A range of expected values from the application of the methodology for a stan­
dard residual waste composition was established and used for verification against estimates
obtained by the DREAM group. It was found that values generally aligned and that differences
in results could be explained by data input to the system and methodological decisions in the
allocation of waste across groups.
The methodology developed in this thesis thus introduces a structured and hands­on approach
for estimating generated waste quantities in Denmark. It is the first of its kind to apply this type
of methodology to both household and industrial waste, offering a more complete overview of
national waste generation. Designed for broad applicability, the methodology can be extended

that not only strengthens Danish waste statistics but also serves as inspiration for how other
EU member states might approach a more encompassing and policy­relevant waste reporting.

Sammenfatning
Affaldshåndtering spiller en afgørende rolle i Europas overgang til en cirkulær økonomi. Som
et led i den europæiske grønne pagt har Europa­Kommissionen sat en række ambitiøse mål,
der skal reducere udledningen af drivhusgasser, afkoble økonomisk vækst fra ressourcefor­
brug og gøre Europa til et klimaneutralt kontinent inden 2050 (European Commission, n.d.­b;
European Union, 2019, 2024). I denne dagsorden ses affald i stigende grad som en værdi­
fuld ressource, der bør forvaltes ansvarligt, bland andet i form af genbrug og genanvendelse
af materialer (European Union, 2024). Et solidt og gennemsigtigt datagrundlag er helt cen­
tralt for at kunne udvikle effektive politiske tiltag og handlingsplaner – da dette både muliggør
overvågning af fremskridt, prioritering af indsatser og vurdering af affaldssystemers miljø­ og
samfundsøkonomiske præstation (Albizzati et al., 2024; Sund et al., 2025).
Denne afhandling adresserer en væsentlig mangel i Dansk affaldsstatistik ved at præsentere
en offentlig tilgængelig metode udviklet til at estimere mængden af affald genereret i Danmark.
Metoden er udviklet med henblik på anvendelse i det nationale affaldshåndteringssystem og
bygger på årlige datasæt udgivet af Miljøstyrelsen. Den tager afsæt i principperne bag den
nationale model udviklet af the Danish Research Institute for Economic Analysis and Modelling
(DREAM), hvis resultater dog ikke er offentligt tilgængelige. Metoden er afprøvet gennem et
casestudie baseret på data fra 2022 og valideret via sammenligning med DREAM­gruppens
estimater for samme år. Den udviklede metode er direkte anvendelig på data fra Affaldsdata­
systemet (ADS) og benytter en række specifikt udviklede inputfiler til automatisk at generere og
præsentere nøgleresultater. Metoden består af seks hovedtrin: 1) indsamling og aggregering
af data, 2) fordeling af affald på relevante affaldsgrupper, 3) yderligere aggregering baseret på
affaldsklassifikationskoder indberettet i ADS, 4) identifikation af målfraktioner og urenheder, 5)
omfordeling af urenheder til relevante affaldsgrupper samt 6) udarbejdelse af et samlet dataset
og en oversigt over genereret affald. Databehandlingsfilerne er opbygget med fleksibilitet for
øje, så brugere nemt kan tilpasse affaldsklassificeringer i analysen. Derudover er der udviklet
supplerende værktøjer, der gør det muligt at undersøge affaldsmængder i de enkelte koder
mere detaljeret.
Casestudiet viste, at der i 2022 blev indsamlet omkring 12,2 millioner ton affald i Danmark,
hvoraf industrien stod for 67% mens husholdninger stod for det resterende. Derudover var
cirka 31% af det indsamlede affald enten fejlsorteret eller indsamlet som restaffald, hvilket sk­
abte en betydelig forskel mellem mængden af indsamlet og genereret affald. Hele 67% af de
foretagne omfordelinger af affald udført i metoden vedrørte restaffald. Dette understregede at
restaffaldets sammensætning har betydelig indvirkning på metodens resultater. Metodens føl­
somhed over for ændringer i restaffaldssammensætningen blev derfor undersøgt og det viste
sig, at en ændring på blot 1% i restaffaldets sammensætning medførte en forskydning på 22.000
ton i de estimerede affaldsmængder for en given gruppe. Følsomheden var særligt udtalt for
affaldsgrupper indsamlet i lave mængder, såsom tekstil (TEX), plast (PLA) og deponeringseg­
net affald (LAN). På trods af disse variationer forblev metodens struktur og resultater robuste
på tværs af forskellige sammensætninger. Baseret på analysen blev der fastlagt et sæt ref­
erenceværdier for en standardrestaffaldssammensætning, som blev anvendt til at kontrollere
metodens resultater mod DREAM­gruppens estimater. Sammenligningen viste en overordnet
overensstemmelse mellem de to metoder, og afvigelser kunne forklares med forskelle i datain­
put og metodiske valg i fordelingen af affald på tværs af grupper.
Den udviklede metode, præsenteret i denne afhandling, tilbyder en systematisk og praktisk til­
gang til at estimere genererede affaldsmængder i Danmark baseret på offentligt tilgængelige
data. Det er første gang, en sådan metode anvendes særskilt på både husholdnings­ og indus­
triaffald, hvilket bidrager til et mere nuanceret billede af den samlede affaldsgenrering i Dan­
mark. Metoden kan frit anvendes på data fra andre år samt udvides til at dække alle europæiske

Preface
The MSc thesis Assessment of the Danish Waste Management System was written by Sarah
Niaraki Jensen (s194461), student in Environmental Engineering at the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU). The thesis workload was equivalent to 32.5 ECTS and constitutes the obliga­
tory thesis submission to obtain the Master’s of Science degree in Environmental Engineering
with a specialisation in Waste and Circularity. The thesis was prepared over five and a half
months at the Department of Environmental and Resource Engineering, DTU Sustain. The
project work was conducted under the supervision of Marie Kampmann Eriksen, senior re­
searcher at DTU Sustain; Josefine Hededam Sund, PhD student at DTU Sustain; and Charlotte
Scheutz, Professor and Head of Section for Waste, Climate, and Monitoring at DTU Sustain.
This MSc thesis introduces a methodology designed for application within the Danish waste
management system to calculate generated waste quantities in a consistent and transparent
manner, thereby contributing to improved data quality in Danish waste statistics. To extend the
possible usage of the result, the method will be aligned with national economic frameworks
used for evaluating the effects of policies in Denmark. It is assumed that the reader has a
basic understanding of waste management, life­cycle thinking, and the political structure of the
European Union.

Introduction
Waste management plays a crucial role in the transition towards a circular and sustainable
economy. As part of the European Green Deal, the European Commission has introduced an
ambitious policy framework aimed at reducing greenhouse gas emissions, decoupling economic
growth from resource consumption, and ultimately making Europe the first climate­neutral con­
tinent by 2050 (European Union, 2019). Within this agenda, waste is increasingly viewed not
as a burden but as a resource that must be better managed, reused, and recycled to reduce
environmental impacts and preserve raw materials (European Commission, n.d.­b; European
Union, 2020).
A key prerequisite for effective implementation of legislation and policymaking is the availability
of transparent and comprehensive data that enables policymakers to not only monitor progress
but also prioritize and evaluate intervention areas as well as the environmental and economic
performance of the systems (Albizzati et al., 2024; Sund et al., 2025; Takou et al., 2019). To
improve transparency and accuracy in waste statistics, the European Commission adapted a
revised Waste Framework Directive (2008/98/EC). This framework introduces new reporting
requirements by shifting the focus from simple waste handling to more elaborate monitoring
and management (European Union, 2006, 2024). This directive requires member states of the
European Union (EU) to monitor and report on national waste generation quantities. For years,
member states have reported on waste statistics to the EU based on collected waste quantities.
However, in recent years, the difference between waste collection and waste generation has
gained more attention. This is an important distinction as collected waste data represents the
composition of waste streams as they enter sorting and processing plants, including impurities
and omitting uncollected waste (Sund et al., 2025). In contrast, generated data reflects the
amounts of material discarded regardless of sorting accuracy (Sund et al., 2025). Understand­
ing generated waste is therefore essential to identifying inefficiencies in collection systems and
accurately tracking progress toward EU targets.
Many Member States face challenges in accurately reporting waste generation from waste data
rather than economic activities. This is due to a lack of standardised methods and to the com­
plexity of estimating generated waste from collected waste data (Albizzati et al., 2024). Ad­
ditionally, the two terms are frequently used interchangeably, which further complicates data
interpretation (Albizzati et al., 2024; Sund et al., 2025). Waste generation of products such
as packaging waste is often estimated based on economic activities like the number of prod­
ucts placed on the market in a given year. This approach is fine for products with a short
service­life, but for products with a long service­life (e.g., electronics) there is often a signifi­
cant delay between the year of marketing and the time when the product is discarded as waste
(European Environment Agency, 2020, 2023a, 2023b). Determining generated waste quanti­
ties from waste statistics requires a deep understanding and extensive knowledge of the waste
system in which the waste is generated. A prerequisite for estimating generated waste is de­
tailed knowledge on the composition of the waste streams that make up the system. Despite
ongoing efforts to determine these compositions, current waste reporting and modelling efforts
in Denmark and across the EU tend to focus on specific waste streams such as municipal solid
waste (MSW), construction and demolition waste (C&D), textiles, or plastics (Albizzati et al.,
2024; García­Gutiérrez et al., 2025; Petersen et al., 2023; Solis et al., 2024; Sund et al., 2025).
While these streams are increasingly prioritized in EU strategies, they account for only a frac­
tion of total waste. MSW, for example, represents just 9% of EU waste (Albizzati et al., 2024).
As a result, many waste streams remain insufficiently understood, limiting the ability to accu­

rately determine generated waste quantities in the system (Albizzati et al., 2024; Sund et al.,
2025). In addition to highlighting the limitations of solely relying on collected waste data, Sund
et al. (2025) and Albizzati et al. (2024) emphasise the importance of aligning national systems
with EU­wide objectives and economic models. Limited knowledge on actual waste generation
limits policymakers’ ability to set targeted priorities and design holistic strategies. Sund et al.

waste composition varies across member states, it should be quantified at the national level. A
clearer understanding of country­specific waste streams will enable national­tailored legislation
and more effective contributions to the EU waste targets and visions.
In Denmark, official estimations on waste generation are conducted using a modelling structure
developed in collaboration with the government. However, the specific model and its results
are not publicly available, which means that estimated waste quantities remain inaccessible for
public use and independent research. Consequently, researchers and other interested enti­
ties lack a transparent method for analysing Danish waste generation data. This thesis seeks
to address this knowledge gap by developing a methodological approach for estimating waste
generation in Denmark and providing a comprehensive and transparent overview of the underly­
ing data sources, assumptions, and methodological choices. This is achieved by (i) developing
a methodology for estimating generated waste quantities in Denmark, based on national waste
statistics for collected waste; (ii) extending the possible usage of results by aligning system
setups with the core structure and overarching principle of existing models developed by the
Danish Research Institute for Economic Analysis and Modelling (DREAM); (iii) demonstrating
practical applicability by implementing the method in a case study using 2022 waste data; (iv)
assessing the robustness of the method by comparing its results to those estimated by the
DREAM group; (v) emphasising potential limitations of the method and its use cases; (vi) out­
lining potential applications and opportunities for further development of the method in future
studies and practical use.
Together, these objectives should provide a solid foundation for future research and analysis by
improving data transparency and supporting more consistent waste­related studies in Denmark.

Theoretical background
The EU has several well­defined waste management strategies and policies (European Com­
mission, n.d.­b; European Union, 2024). These strategies are supported by a standardized
vocabulary that defines core concepts such as waste handling, collection, material recovery
and many more, ensuring consistency across EU policy. The vocabulary used in this thesis
follows the definitions posed by the European Waste Framework Directive (2008/98/EC) and
its current Danish transposition, Affaldsbekendtgørelsen. A list of important definitions used in
this study can be found in Table 1 of the nomenclature.
2.1
Fifty years of waste policies in the Euuropean Union
2025 marks the 50th anniversary of waste management legislation in the EU. The initial legis­
lation on waste management in the EU laid out basic principles for waste management across
Member States, aiming to prevent harm to human health and the environment through proper
waste handling and disposal (European Commission, n.d.­b; European Union, 1975). Over
time, policies have evolved to become more extensive and integrated, shifting from basic waste
handling and disposal to more elaborate management policies supporting the agenda of tran­
sitioning towards a circular economy (European Union, 2006, 2019, 2024). Today, EU waste
policies are based on the European Green Deal, which sets an agenda to transform the EU
into a modern, resource­efficient and competitive economy. The Green Deal is not limited to
the waste sector, but covers many industries and services, with the overarching goal to reduce
greenhouse gas emissions, boost clean energy and circular economy, protect biodiversity and
ecosystems, and promote sustainable agriculture and transport (European commission, n.d.;
European Union, 2019). Since its initial presentation in 2019, the strategy has had huge impacts
on the political landscape of the EU (European commission, n.d.; European Union, 2019; Sachs
et al., 2022). Influenced by the Green Deal, EU waste policy today aims to: manage waste in an
environmentally sound manner and without endangering human health; promote growth while
contributing to the circular economy by extracting high­quality resources from waste; stimulate
innovation in recycling; and limit landfilling (European Commission, n.d.­b, n.d.­c; European
Union, 2024). These objectives are based on the five­step EU waste hierarchy.
Depicted in Figure 2.1, the waste hierarchy establishes an order of preference for managing
and disposing of waste: prevention, preparing for reuse, recycling, other recovery (e.g., energy
recovery), and disposal. The hierarchy encourages efficient use of resources by keeping mate­
rials and products in use for as long as possible. It does so through prioritising waste prevention
and the recovery of materials, ultimately aiming to minimise the environmental impacts of waste
generation and treatment (European Commission, n.d.­b, n.d.­c). While the hierarchy has pro­
vided a common framework for waste handling, it overlooks key aspects of waste handling, such
as material quality, economic costs, practical implementation barriers, and life­cycle impacts of
waste handling. These are all important aspects of waste handling that must be considered
before the EU can consider itself content with its waste handling under the agenda set out in
the Green Deal (European Union, 2019; Sund et al., 2025).
As policies and frameworks continue to develop, waste policies become subject to revision.
In recent years, these revisions have led to notable changes in waste reporting requirements.
Specifically relevant for this study is the shift in policy introduced by the Waste Framework Direc­
tive (2008/98/EC), where requirements for reporting annual waste generation were introduced
(European Commission, n.d.­b; European Union, 2006, 2024). Before this directive, waste
had been monitored, but there were no official EU­wide requirements for reporting on waste

2.1.1
Waste classification in the European Union and Denmark
In an effort to standardise waste classification across the EU, the European Commission intro­
duced the European Waste Catalogue1 (EWC) in May of 2000 (European Commission, 2000).
Along with the recovery and disposal operations (R/D codes) defined in the European Waste
Framework Directive (2008), these codes establish a common terminology for determining
waste type and disposal methods across the EU. The EWC is made up of about 650 individ­
ual waste codes divided across 20 overall categories (Novus Environmental, 2017). The code
consists of six digits where the first two digits specify the overall category (e.g,. 02 ­ wastes
from agriculture, horticulture, aquaculture, forestry, hunting and fishing, food preparation and
processing), the following two specify an overall process often found within that category (e.g.,
02 01 ­ wastes from agriculture, horticulture, aquaculture, forestry, hunting and fishing), and the
last digits specify the specific waste type (e.g. 02 01 01 ­ sludges from washing and cleaning)
(European Commission, 2000; Novus Environmental, 2017). A complete list of the EWC can
be found in European Commission (2000). In addition to the EWC codes, the EU has also es­
tablished 51 EWC­stat categories for waste, each defining a specific type of waste (e.g., glass,
plastic, or wood waste)(European Commission, 2010). The 650 EWC codes are distributed
across these 51 EWC­stat categories using another set of waste classification codes called
EWC­stat codes. While the EWC­stat categories consist of 51 waste categories with specific
names, the EWC­stat codes consist of 117 specific code­numberings, each defining an aggre­
gated classification of the EWC codes (European Commission, 2010; European Union, n.d.­b;
Eurostat, 2023). Figure 2.2 provides a summatory overview of this setup with an example in­
cluded for chemical waste. The EWC codes are used operationally to classify and manage
specific waste types at the point of collection and treatment. These are the codes used and
reported by member states, while the EWC­stat codes are used statistically to aggregate the

as intermediary steps before final treatment. During a non­final treatment process, waste is not
eliminated but rather prepared for the next phase in the waste management chain. Examples
include intermediary storage (R13/D15) or mechanical treatments such as sorting, shredding,
or baling (D14), used to prepare waste for another treatment process (Cirkulær Økonomi and

2.1.2
Collected vs. generated waste
Member States of the EU have been required to report on waste statistics since the initial adop­
tion of the Waste Framework Directive (2008/98/EC). However, definitions and methodologies
for reporting were not harmonised, and for years, many Member States have reported on col­
lected waste rather than generated waste, or estimated generated waste from economic activ­
ities, as this is the data that is most readily available. In 2018, the Waste Framework Directive
was revised5, emphasising the need to improve waste management in order to transition to a
circular economy and to guarantee the Union’s long­term competitiveness (European Union,
2024). This revision ultimately led to the adoption of Commission Implementing Decision (EU)
2019/1004, in which the requirements for waste reporting were specified. The specification was
meant to improve transparency and accuracy in waste statistics. For full transparency, it is im­
portant to understand the difference between collected and generated waste. While often used
interchangeably, the terms cover two distinctly different categories. Collected waste statistics
include impurities in waste streams, and omit misallocations of materials such as plastic, metal,
and bio waste found in mixed residual waste. These materials often make up a large share of
the mixed residual waste, leading to an under­representation of specific materials in the waste
quantities and a distortion of recycling rates (Albizzati et al., 2024; Sund et al., 2025). Generated
waste statistics account for impurities and misallocations in waste (e.g., through the reallocation
of waste based on residual waste compositions), thereby providing a more accurate represen­
tation of the actual waste system. This makes for a stronger basis for monitoring and policy
development (Sund et al., 2025). Figure 2.3 illustrates the distinction between collected and
generated waste.

2.2
The Danish waste system
Denmark has a well­developed and legally anchored waste reporting system, supported by
detailed classifications and strict documentation requirements (Miljøstyrelsen, 2024c, 2025a).
All entities involved in the handling of waste are obligated to report any transaction in which
waste is transferred from one party to another in the national waste data system8 which will be
referred to as ADS in this thesis. These reports must include information on waste type, quantity,
source, and treatment method9, using both Danish and European waste classification codes.
Producers remain responsible for ensuring that waste is transferred only to authorised entities,
such as registered collection companies, treatment plants, or municipal schemes. Failure to
report in ADS, or incorrect handling of waste, may result in fines for both the waste handler
and the producer (Miljøstyrelsen, 2024c). The information reported to ADS forms the basis
for national waste statistics, EU reporting, municipal planning, and regulatory monitoring. The
MST reviews the reports and publishes an annual report on waste statistics along with a raw
data sheet containing all waste records from the given year (Miljøstyrelsen, 2024b, 2025b). The
most recent report and published data contain waste statistics from 2018 to 2022.
2.2.1
Structure of the ADS raw data
The publicly available ADS raw data file consists of 19 columns, each containing a classification
code. In broad terms, the file contains four main sections as seen in Figure 2.4. The first section
pertains to waste classification codes, determining the waste type, using both Danish I/H codes
and EWC codes. The second section pertains to waste treatment codes, specifying the fate
of the waste, again using Danish and EU codes. The third section refers to the origin of the
waste and only contains Danish classifications. The fourth section shows the amount of waste
reported in an entry.

of different policies has been developed by the DREAM group. The framework consists of a

mark for a given year. This estimation is based on ADS data and aggregates Danish I/H codes
into 17 individual waste groups. The specific modelling structure and results of the model are
not publicly available, and thus, generated waste quantities remain unquantified for public use.
As a result, current research on the Danish waste system often relies on collected waste quan­
tities for analytical purposes. However, this can lead to uncertainties, as studies have shown
that economic analyses and life cycle assessments based on collected waste data may yield
results that differ significantly from those based on generated waste estimates (Albizzati et al.,
2024; Edjabou et al., 2021; Sund et al., 2025).

3
Methodology
This study was conducted using a combination of methods, including reviews of literature, ex­
tensive data processing, methodological development, and sensitivity analysis of results. The
following chapter provides a detailed overview of these methods, explaining their relevance and
role in addressing the objectives of the study.
3.1
Review of literature and data collection
The project work was initiated with an extensive review of relevant legislative and scientific lit­
erature. This included regulations, directives, recommendations, and strategic plans from the
European Commission related to circular economy and waste management in the EU, their
transposition into Danish legislation, and scientific literature on waste management and com­
position in Denmark and the EU. The purpose of this initial review was to define the study’s legal
and strategic context and to identify relevant waste fractions and data sources. Building on this
review, a comprehensive list of waste targets and performance indicators was compiled. This
list provided a legal and current political context for the thesis, guiding the development of the
methodology described in Section 3.3. The list was used to ensure that later methodological
decisions were made in line with relevant legislation and to ensure a broad use context for the
methodology developed in this thesis. The list can be found in Supplementary Material S1.
The study revolves around a detailed mapping of the Danish waste management system. This
mapping was developed using publicly available data on waste collection derived from ADS,
published by the MST. The methodology was applied to a case study, where the 2022 ADS
dataset was used, as this offers the most up­to­date and complete overview of Danish waste
collection statistics in 2025. While it may not fully reflect current­day practices or legislation, it re­
mains the most comprehensive dataset available and was therefore deemed the most relevant
application for the case study. Additional data sources and reports were used to supplement
data from ADS and to validate the findings of the study. This includes technical reports, anal­
yses, and studies conducted by Edjabou et al. (2021), COWI et al. (2025), and Petersen et al.
(2023). The composition of residual waste was modelled using data derived from Petersen et
al. (2023), who have estimated the residual waste composition of Danish household waste. A
range of expected outcomes from the methodology was established by comparing model out­
comes using different residual waste compositions. Lastly, the model was verified by comparing
the results obtained in this study with the estimated waste generation for 2022 obtained by the
DEARM group’s GR module.

3.3.1
Scope and system boundaries
This thesis aims to provide a methodology to estimate generated waste quantities in Denmark
based on collected waste quantities retrieved from the ADS database published by the MST.
The data does not include soil waste or imports/exports to and from Denmark. Nor does it
include waste collected through the Danish deposit and return system for beverage containers
(pant)1 as this data is registered in a separate system. Lastly, the study does not account for
informal waste handling, waste dumped in nature, or other routes of waste handling outside
of the official Danish waste collection system. Thus, the study assumes that generated waste
quantities are equal to collected quantities. Figure 3.2 provides a visual representation of the
system boundaries.
Figure 3.2: System boundaries for the methodology developed in this study. Yellow boxes
indicate processes included within the system; grey boxes represent those excluded. *Pant
refers to waste beverage containers handled by the Danish deposit and return system.
The generated waste quantities estimated in this study are based on the assumption that waste
reported in ADS has undergone some form of mechanical sorting. Thus, estimates for gener­
ated waste reflect the actual material­specific composition of the waste, rather than the waste
categories predefined by the sorting scheme. For example, although a sorting scheme may
require that all C&D waste be collected as C&D waste, regardless of its content of materials
such as plastic, metal, or glass, this study accounts for those materials individually. As a result,
plastic, metal, and glass components from C&D are included in their respective material­specific
waste groups rather than being grouped under the C&D waste group. This assumption is based
on the setup of the ADS data file, where waste collected from various activities (e.g., C&D) has
been classified to a material­specific level (e.g., gypsum, concrete, glass).
To estimate the quantities of generated waste, it was sometimes necessary to use data on
waste treatment. For this reason, the methodology developed in this study provides estimated
waste treatments in addition to the generated quantities. However, the treatment of waste is
otherwise disregarded in this study. Lastly, the study seeks to provide a detailed methodology
that can provide a thorough overview of both household and industrial waste generation. These
waste streams are therefore evaluated separately. While household waste is covered in MSW,
this term covers a combination of household and industrial waste. It is important to note that

3.3.2
Model overview and structure
The GR module provides a context for meaningful waste groupings within the Danish waste
management system. This is done by allocating waste across 17 different waste groups based
on material type and appropriate waste handling. The DREAM group’s GR module is used for
national reporting purposes but is not publicly available. To accommodate a scientific commu­
nity interested in values emulating the national reporting, and to have a model on which the
results of the methodology can be verified against, the methodological structure developed in
this thesis closely aligns with the setup of the GR module. The waste groups defined in this
thesis are thus structured around the same 17 waste groups found in the GR module. The 17
waste groups used in this study are: biowaste (BIO), combustion residues (COM), electronic
waste (ELE), gardening waste (GAR), glass waste (GLW), hazardous waste (HAZ), metal waste
(MET), mineral waste (MIN), mixed waste (MIX), paper and cardboard waste (PAC), polymeric
waste (PLA), PVC and tyres (PVT), sludge (SLU), soil (SOI), textile waste (TEX), waste for land­
filling (LAN), and wood waste (WOO). The allocation of Danish I/H waste codes to these waste
groups is based on an interpretation of the GR waste groupings, informed by internal working
documents from the DREAM group. It should be made clear, however, that the waste groups
defined in this study are not identical to the actual classification used in the GR module. One
significant difference in the group formation is the allocation of hazardous waste. A detailed
description of each waste group, and the type of waste included or excluded in each group,
can be found in Appendix D. Additionally, the data processing file Overview of fractions and
sorting, found in Supplementary Material S2, provides the setup and all information needed to
perform the allocation of collected waste onto the 17 waste groups. Group names defined by
the DREAM group were translated into English following the definitions established by Sund
et al. (2025); however, many of the waste groups are not defined identically to these, and di­
rect comparisons should therefore be made with caution. A comparison of the similarities and
differences between the waste groups used in this study and those in Sund et al. (2025) is also
provided in Appendix D.
The methodology developed in this study is based on the methods developed by Sund et al.
(2025) and Albizzati et al. (2024). The methods have been customised to the Danish con­
text and specifically designed to align with the waste groups defined in the GR module. The
methodology consists of several Python scripts and two spreadsheets, all set up to run on data
extracted from ADS. The methodology can be applied to all ADS data stored in the waste col­
lected in DK excluding soil sheet of the datafile The sheet is named: Affald indsamlet i DK
ex. jord, in the file., and can be run for any given year with minor adjustments in the coding
structure. The method consists of the following six overarching steps: 1) data collection and
aggregation; 2) distributing data onto the 17 waste groups; 3) aggregating individual ADS en­
tries onto self­defined waste classification codes based on reported EWC codes; 4) determining
target materials and impurities within each waste group; 5) redistributing missortings and resid­
ual waste based classification codes and a predefined composition for residual waste; and 6)
combining all data into one final overview of generated waste. While these steps are largely
inspired by methodologies developed in Sund et al. (2025) and Albizzati et al. (2024), the order
and content of the steps are not directly comparable as the methodologies have been devel­
oped for different data systems. Where the methodologies described in Sund et al. (2025) and
Albizzati et al. (2024) pertain to EU waste data provided by Eurostat, the methodology devel­
oped in this study is based on the official annual raw data file on waste statistics reported in
ADS. The structure of the methodology and all associated data processing files are thus built
on the data structure found in that system.

Numerous mass balances have been performed throughout the different steps to ensure that
no waste data is lost when applying the methodology. All extracted data is kept in separate files

sections provide a walkthrough of the methodological approach and the six steps on which the
model is built.
1) Data collection and aggregation
In the first step of the methodology, individual entries in ADS are aggregated to sum the reported
quantities of waste for all identical waste reports in the dataset, and all entries that are empty
for the year of interest (2022) are removed from the dataset. Three data files are created in this
step. One data file containing the condensed dataset and two data files containing an overview
of specific rows that were aggregated and all removed entries. The latter two are created for
data tracking purposes but are not otherwise used in the remaining methodology.
2) Distributing data onto GR waste groups
Once the dataset has been aggregated to a more manageable size, all entries with NaN­values
(i.e., entries that contain no data) for their I/H codes are removed, while the remaining waste
is divided into industry and household datasets based on their I/H code. Following this, the
remaining part of step 2 and steps 3­5 are performed on household and industrial waste indi­
vidually. The remaining part of step 2 consists of distributing waste into their respective waste
groups. In this thesis, this is done following a description provided in internal documents from
the DREAM group. The distribution is based on the specific I/H code associated with each ADS
entry (Danish Research Institute for Economic Analysis and Modelling, 2025b). Bulky waste2
(I/H27) and Other waste3 (I/H29) are distributed across numerous categories based on EWC
code. Additionally, some treatment categories are adapted to match reality as described in
these documents. All necessary inputs to perform the distribution have been provided in Sup­
plementary Material S24. I/H codes are distributed following the definitions for each waste group
as seen in Appendix D. Additional specifics on the distribution will not be further detailed since
the approach follows internal documents from the GR module, which are not publicly available.

3) Aggregating individual entries onto waste classification codes
In Step 3, the composition of waste found within each waste group is determined. This is done
by aggregating waste into specific categories based on the reported EWC codes associated
with an entry. This process was standardised through the development of two grouping levels
for waste classification: Group Level 1 (GL1) and Group Level 2 (GL2). These levels transpose
detailed EWC codes into a hierarchical structure, where GL1 remains relatively detailed, while
GL2 offers a broader, more general overview of the data. The groups were developed based on
the national waste targets found during the review of literature (see Supplementary Material S1)
while ensuring the representativeness of waste materials in the groups. Below is a summative
description of how the two categories were created.
Group Level 1 codes: The purpose of the GL1 category is to group unique EWC codes into
a more manageable overview of waste types. The codes were developed through an iterative
process where EWC codes and GL1 codes were refined several times throughout the project
period. When establishing GL1 codes, particular attention was paid to high­quantity and clearly
defined waste fractions. Any waste fractions that may be relevant for national waste targets
or further analysis were considered, as well as the distinction between hazardous and non­
hazardous waste. If a waste fraction could logically belong to more than one code, it was placed
under the most specific and representative code. As an example, off­specification compost
(EWC 19 05 03), was categorised as biodegradable even though it is a waste product from
anaerobic digestion and thus could also logically be classified under Mixed treatment plants.
Group Level 2 codes: The GL2 category was developed to provide a further aggregation of GL1
codes into broader groupings. This second grouping level serves to further reduce data com­
plexity and highlight only those waste fractions deemed specifically relevant for the subsequent
analysis in this report. The GL2 codes also serve to determine target materials and impurities
in all of the 17 waste groups. This process also distinguishes hazardous and non­hazardous
waste.
A total of 400 EWC codes were reported for the 2022 waste data. These were individually
distributed onto a GL1 and GL2 code. Once allocated into GL1 codes, the entries were further
aggregated into GL2. Table 3.1 provides an overview of the 67 GL1 codes and 35 GL2 codes
created and how they align with the 17 waste groups defined in this study. It should be noted
that one GL1 group, Mixed liquids, is missing from the overview. This is because this group
consists of waste that did not seem to fit into any of the 17 waste groups. The group makes
up 0.2% of all waste collected in Denmark in 2022. It was therefore deemed negligible for the
outcome of the study. Additionally, 0.5‰ of waste was removed from the dataset during the
initial data treatment, as this data had only NaN code values (i.e., no data) connected to its
entries in ADS. Lastly, exactly one tonne of waste disappeared from the data when bringing
redistributed waste back into the main dataset. The code performing this action was examined
thoroughly, and the reason for the loss of data could not be explained. The data loss represents
a few millionths of a percent of the total data, and it was thus disregarded for the evaluation
made in this thesis. Approximately 20 thousand tonnes (0.2%) of collected waste were thus
excluded from the study.
Waste classified as electronic waste (I/H codes 40 to 50) was categorised slightly differently from
the remaining groups. This is because all Danish waste reduction targets are based directly on
the Danish categorisation of electronic waste, rather than EWC codes. It was therefore deemed
more useful in a Danish context to instead categorise electronic waste based on Danish I/H
codes. All waste reported with I/H codes 40 to 50 is instead assigned to the ELE waste group
independently of its EWC hazard classification. Some of the waste found in other categories has
also been classified as ELE waste based on their EWC codes. In these instances, hazardous

waste was assigned to the HAZ waste group, while non­hazardous waste was assigned to ELE.
4) Defining target materials
In step 3, waste is assigned to a GL2 code describing the overall material qualities of the waste.
In step 4, waste is tagged as targets or impurities within each waste group. Target materials
were established by determining the waste group that best describes the characteristics of the
GL2 group. Thus, all GL2 listed under a waste group in Table 3.1 are target materials in this
group. Impurities were defined as GL2 waste found in a waste group that was not a target of this
group. As an example, the GL2 code Bio waste is assigned as a target material within the BIO
waste group. Thus, all waste tagged as GL2 Bio waste found in BIO is classified as a target,
while any Bio waste found in any other waste group is tagged as an impurity in that group.
During this distribution, almost all hazardous waste is assigned to HAZ, independently of the
waste composition. This distribution is purely based on EWC codes and means that materials
such as hazardous C&D waste are categorised as HAZ rather than MIN or LAN. This approach
ensures a realistic overview of the amount of hazardous waste represented in the results derived
from using this methodology. Appendix D describes all instances where hazardous waste is not
distributed into the HAZ group.
5) Redistributing impurities
In this step, all target materials are extracted from the data. The remaining data in each group
is seen as impurities in the group and is redistributed onto their targeted groups. Thus, any
waste found outside of its targeted group is removed from its original group and reallocated into
its targeted group (e.g., waste classified as GL2 plastic found in GAR would be removed from
GAR and instead placed into PLA). During this reallocation, waste is tagged based on its original
waste group, ensuring traceability of all waste movements across groups. In cases where an
appropriate waste group, GL1, GL2, or target group was ambiguous, waste was classified based
on the waste management of Copenhagen Municipality.
The MIX waste group is handled slightly differently: Here, impurities consist of materials tagged
as targets in other waste groups and are thus handled as described above. The target materials
of the MIX waste group, however, consist of mixed residual waste. As the name suggests, mixed
residual waste consists of several different materials. Thus, the materials tagged as ’targets’ in
this waste group are made up of many varying materials. This could be metals, plastics, textiles,
etc., which are targets in another waste group. Following steps 5 and 6 of Albizzati et al. (2024)
and Sund et al. (2025) and Albizzati et al. (2024), mixed residual waste should be reallocated to
their targeted waste groups (see Figure 2.3 for concept visualisation). The quantities of specific
waste to redistribute from the residual waste were calculated using the established residual
waste composition, multiplying this by the total amount of residual waste (targets) collected in
the MIX group. In doing so, all information on I/H code, origin, and designated treatment is lost;
however, the waste gets redistributed onto GL1 and GL2 codes, which can then be allocated
into their targeted waste group. This reallocation is tracked and all designated treatments are
set to incineration, as this is the designated treatment of all waste collected in the MIX waste
group.
6) Combining data and creating an overview
The last step of the methodology consists of combining the extracted target materials with the
re­allocated impurities from step 5. While doing so, it is ensured that all original placements
of data are tracked, and a matrix for the redistribution is formed to establish an overview of
the collected and generated waste. Appendix C provides an overview of the many output files
achieved when applying the methodology. The data processing files have been set up in a way
that allows for easy reallocation of EWC codes onto GL1 and GL2 codes and GL2 codes to
target groups. The method thus allows for quick alterations in the waste grouping decisions

3.4
Sensitivity of residual waste composition
Several studies have shown that the composition of residual waste has important implications
for results derived from life cycle assessments of waste systems (Albizzati et al., 2024; COWI
et al., 2025; Sund et al., 2025). The composition of residual waste has large temporal and
geographical variations, even in a small country like Denmark (Edjabou et al., 2021; Petersen
et al., 2023). To address this, the impact of residual waste composition on the outcomes of
the methodology was examined. For the main results found in this study, the composition of
residual waste (which in this study is referred to as MIX) was assumed to be equal to that found
in Petersen et al. (2023). This composition is based on random samplings of household waste
from four Danish municipalities deemed representative of geographical variety in Danish waste
composition. This sampling was carried out in 2022 and covered municipalities with various
progress in implementing the standardised waste collection system used in Denmark today.5
To address the methodology’s sensitivity to residual waste composition, two additional compo­
sitions were investigated. COWI et al. (2025) does not present the source of their composition,
but the composition found in Edjabou et al. (2021) is based on sampling campaigns carried out
in 2011 and 2017. The analysis was performed to illustrate and investigate the range of gener­
ated waste that can be found using the methodology for different residual waste compositions.
To perform the analyses, compositions were translated into GL1 codes, which were then used
to perform the redistribution of residual waste as described in Section 3.3.2. The results were
saved in separate data files and the sensitivity of affected waste groups was estimated. For
the composition obtained by Petersen et al. (2023), three waste fractions in the residual waste
were noted as <1%. These were set to 0.33% each in their appropriate GL1 code. All composi­
tions were normalised to ensure consistent waste quantities throughout the analysis. Table 3.2
shows the normalised compositions used for all three compositions.

4.1.1
Collected and generated waste quantities
Approximately 12.2 million tonnes of waste were collected in Denmark in 2022. Figure 4.1
shows the estimated composition of all collected and generated waste in 2022. Here it is seen
that MIN waste is by far the most collected waste category in the system (34%), followed by MIX
waste (20%), BIO and MET waste (both 9%). PAC, WOO, GAR, and COM collectively make
up approximately 20% of the collected waste, while the remaining groups make up just 9%
collectively. From the generated waste perspective, MIN waste remains the most predominant
category (31%). However, BIO waste accounts for a substantially larger share of generated
waste (16%), while MIX waste represents only a small fraction (3%) of generated waste. At the
same time, PAC, PLA, and MET waste each account for approximately 2% more of the gener­
ated waste compared to the collected. The figure clearly shows that waste is largely reallocated
onto the SOI, PLA, HAZ, BIO, and PAC waste groups, with smaller quantities reallocated to the
MET, TEX, and ELE waste groups. Additionally, it is seen that a small fraction of the reallo­
cations stems from LAN and MIN waste, but that waste collected as MIX waste is by far the
main source of redistribution. The dataset used for this study excludes SOI waste collected and
reported as such. Despite this, approximately 4% of the generated waste is found to consist of
SOI waste. All generated SOI waste found in the system stems from waste that was missorted
into other waste groups. During steps 3 and 4 of the methodology, some EWC codes reported
in the collected waste were identified to belong in the SOI waste group rather than the waste
group it was collected in. This waste was thus reclassified into appropriate GL1 and GL2 codes
and redistributed to the SOI group. An overview of all EWC codes categorised as SOI waste in
this thesis is found in Appendix E.

4.1.2
Overview of target materials and impurities
The following will provide deeper insight into the composition (i.e., the content of target materials
and impurities in each waste group) of collected waste in Denmark in 2022. Figure 4.3 shows
the content of target materials and impurities identified in the waste groups for household and
industrial waste separately. Some waste groups were found to contain ≤1% impurities. This
was the case for the groups ELE, TEX, and PAC from both households and industries, as well
as PLA and SLU from households and GLW from industries. These have been excluded from
the overview. An overview of all waste groups can be found in Appendix G. ELE contains no
impurities at all, due to differences in methodological approach for this waste group, where it
was assumed that all waste grouped as ELE waste, as per their I/H codes, consists purely of
electronic waste (see Step 3 in Section 3.3.2). Targeted waste in the MIX group consists of
residual waste collected as such. This is the targeted waste in the group and has thus been
marked as targeted waste; however, for the purpose of this study, all residual waste collected
as this target waste in the MIX group was redistributed based on a predefined composition of
residual waste. The target waste in the MIX group is thus treated as impurities.
On Figure 4.3, it is seen that while some waste groups are quite pure and close to free of
impurities, most groups contain a noteworthy degree of impurities. These impurities stem from
missortings of waste in the groups. For both households and industries, the level of impurities in
collected GLW, PLA, PAC, and HAZ is <5%. This means that these waste groups are generally
sorted into quite clean fractions. The same is the case for BIO and GAR in household waste.
Additionally, it is seen that households are significantly less effective than industries at sorting
PVT and WOO waste. Conversely, industries perform worse when it comes to sorting MIX, BIO,
GAR, and MIN waste. When comparing sorting efficiency, it is important to note that households
generally produce much smaller quantities of waste across the waste groups, particularly in the
case of MIN, MET, BIO, SLU, and HAZ waste, as well as COM waste, which households do not
generate at all. While industrial waste appears more poorly sorted than household waste, it is
important to consider these quantities of waste. With industries collecting much larger quantities
of waste than households, it is not surprising to observe a greater absolute volume—and thus
fraction of impurities in industrial waste streams.
Among the above­mentioned groups, it is seen that MET, GLW, and HAZ tend to be better sorted.
This can potentially be attributed to well­established return systems and regulatory requirements
that ensure higher sorting quality. Metals are valuable and highly recyclable, which provides a
strong economic incentive for proper separation. Glass is often clearly distinguishable from
other waste, and hazardous waste is subject to strict legislation governing its handling and
disposal, leaving little room for improper sorting. One of the most significant differences between
households and industries is found for MIX waste, where industries are significantly worse at
sorting than households, despite the two producing similar waste quantities. This could be due
to potentially complex compositions of waste found in industries, industrial setups not allowing
for efficient separation of non­residual waste, requirements for certificates of destruction on
specific waste in specific industries (e.g., biomedical waste from medicinal industries), general
behaviour in and outside of work settings, or a combination of all these.
The LAN waste group appears to almost exclusively contain impurities. It should be noted that
this category has been challenging to categorise from the information provided by the DREAM

4.1.4
Sensitivity to residual waste composition
The methodology’s sensitivity to various residual waste compositions was investigated for two
alternative compositions. In the analysis, only the composition of residual waste was altered,
which influences the following waste groups: ELE, MIX, LAN, GLW, MET, PAC, TEX, BIO, HAZ,
and PLA. All other waste groups are unaffected by the analysis and will thus not be presented in
the following. An overview of all results derived from the analysis can be found in Appendix H.
The following will provide a summative overview of these findings.
The analysis shows that estimates of generated waste quantities are directly dependent on the
composition used when redistributing impurities within residual waste (step 5 of the method­
ology). Specifically, it is found that a 1% increase in the composition of a given waste group
results in an increase of approximately 22 thousand tonnes of generated waste for that specific
category (e.g., a 1% increase in the share of PLA in residual waste leads to an additional 22
thousand tonnes of generated PLA waste). Figure 4.6 shows the compositions of generated
household and industry waste attained when applying the methodology to different composi­
tions of residual waste.

5
Summary of results, implications,
limitations, and outlook
The methodology developed in this study was designed to be directly applicable to ADS data
and intends to provide a publicly accessible estimate of generated waste quantities in Denmark
simulating the structure used for official national reporting. Despite structural differences be­
tween the developed model and the GR module, the results show no major inconsistencies
that cannot be explained, and it is found that the model developed in this study performs well
for its intended purpose. The methodology can thus be used to enhance underlying data for
academic purposes and has the potential to support researchers and policymakers by offer­
ing a transparent and adaptable tool for understanding waste flows in Denmark. However, the
model’s strengths are closely tied to its assumptions, data limitations, and the scope of applica­
tion. The following sections summarise the key findings of the study, highlight some implications
of these results, provide a detailed description of some of the major limitations of the developed
methodology, and suggest key areas for future development.
5.1
Summary of key findings and implications of these
The practical application of the methodology developed in this study was presented through a
case study for the latest available national data. The results of this case study were presented
in Section 4.1. It was found that approximately 12.2 million tonnes of waste were collected in
Denmark in 2022. Approximately 33% of this waste was collected from households, while the
remaining 67% was collected from industries. Approximately 34% of the collected waste was
reported as MIN waste, while MIX waste made up about 20% of the collected waste. Combined
MET, BIO, and GAR waste made up about 25% of the collected waste, while PAC, WOO, and
COM made up about 13% collectively. For all of these waste groups, except for GAR and WOO,
industries were responsible for the majority of the waste collected in a group. The remaining
waste was collected in smaller fractions across ELE, LAN, PVT, GLW, TEX, SLU, HAZ, and PLA
waste, where households were responsible for the majority of waste collected as ELE, GLW,
TEX, and PLA waste.
For generated waste, the distribution looked rather different. Industries were still responsible for
the majority of waste generated as MIN, MIX, MET, PAC, SLU, BIO, and HAZ; however, the BIO
group was much closer to being evenly distributed on households and industries. Generally, the
waste was found to be more evenly distributed across groups for the generated waste. MIN,
LAN, and MIX waste all seem to have been generated in noticeably smaller quantities than
collected. Specifically collected MIX and LAN waste were found to consist almost entirely of
impurities or missortings. Of the collected waste, LAN made up just under 1.5%; however, for
the generated waste, LAN made up just 0.41%. While not drastic in the scheme of all waste,
this decrease of approximately 130 thousand tonnes of waste from collected to generated LAN
waste was caused by difficulties identifying waste categories that specifically belong in the LAN
group following its definition as seen on Appendix D.

5.1.1
Impact of residual waste
MIX waste was by far the waste group that differed most drastically between collected and
generated waste quantities. Approximately 35% of the difference in collected and generated
waste quantities stem from direct missortings of waste. The remaining 65% stems from residual
waste collected as MIX waste. This is due to the large amount of waste collected as MIX waste,
and the extensive share of residual waste found in the MIX group from both households and
industries. The redistribution of impurities in residual waste was based on data from Petersen
et al. (2023). It was found that the redistribution has a particularly large effect on LAN, GLW,
PAC, BIO, and PLA waste, where the share of redistributed residual waste is particularly high. It
was found that a 1% increase in residual waste composition leads to an increase of 22 thousand
tonnes of waste in that particular group. While some waste groups contain such large quantities
of waste that this increase has little impact on the total fraction of waste in the group, other
groups are hugely impacted by changes to residual waste composition. Examples of this are
LAN, TEX, MIX, and PLA, where generated waste quantities are small enough for this sensitivity
to have quite a noticeable effect on the shares of generated waste.
The residual waste compositions used as the basis for this study and the sensitivity analysis are
all based on Danish waste data, collectively spanning over a time period of around 10 years. In
this time period, major changes have been made to the Danish waste collection system. This is
not directly apparent from the compositions of residual waste. When applied in the methodology,
the compositions led to noticeable variation in results. Specifically, the process of estimating
a collection efficiency of waste is largely dependent on the residual waste composition. The
differences in residual waste composition could be due to methodological reasons, geographical
and temporal scope of the studies. While these should all be comparable, their data show that
the composition of residual waste is notoriously difficult to establish, and thus, it is important
to be aware of assumptions made for waste compositions, as these can significantly alter the
outcome of a study.
In studies by Sund et al. (2025) and COWI et al. (2025), the importance of waste composition
for outcomes of LCA studies has been investigated. Sund et al. (2025) have found that waste
groups most affected by the residual waste composition are also the waste groups associated
with the largest climate change impacts. The same pattern is seen in this thesis, with TEX, MIX,
and PLA being largely affected by the composition of residual waste. Albizzati et al. (2024) also
found that the relationship between impact and waste generation is not linear. Thus, an increase
of 1% in plastic (22 thousand tonnes) will have a larger climate change impact than the same
increase in, e.g., MIN waste.1 Thus, a missorting of PLA into MIX waste, which is treated in
an incineration plant, will lead to vastly different greenhouse gas emissions when incinerated
compared to a missorting of MIN. An important step on the road to a better understanding of
life cycle assessments on waste systems, and to more informed policy and decision making, is
thus not just distinguishing collected and generated waste, but also understanding the impacts
that waste compositions used for these estimates have on the modelled systems.
5.1.2
Impurities in collected waste
The results obtained in this study show that approximately 31% of waste collected in Denmark
in 2022 was either incorrectly sorted into the wrong waste bin or collected as mixed residual
waste. Approximately 20% of this waste consisted of residual waste, which consists largely
of waste that is covered by a separate collection scheme (e.g., PLA, BIO, GLW). Specifically,
food waste was found to be poorly sorted into its appropriate waste bin. The results obtained
from the methodology developed in this study can be used to map inefficiencies in the current
waste system and create plans for future improvements. These findings for food waste could,

scheme in an effort to improve the system. However, it is important to also note the 11% of
missorted waste in 2022. While the residual waste is contained in a singular waste group in the
system, these 11% are found as impurities within all the remaining waste groups. Generally,
waste is covered by separate collection schemes for human health and environmental protec­
tion (e.g., HAZ waste) or for resource recovery (e.g., recycling of MET waste). Impurities in
the waste collected for resource recovery can have a very negative effect on the quality of the
recovered resource (European Environment Agency, 2023c). As an example, the presence of
HAZ waste in a waste group will not only lead to unwanted effects on the environment or human
health; it will also, more often than not, render the entire group of collected waste useless for
high­quality material recovery. While the Waste Framework Directive (2008/98/EC) is modelled
largely around the waste hierarchy, which does not account for quality of recovered materials,
the Green Deal scientifically mentions quality of material recovery, thus this is and should be a
priority within waste management in the EU (European Commission, n.d.­b; European Union,
2019). It is important to know that waste will never be perfectly sorted, and impurities in waste
are to be expected. While focus can and should be partly focused on maximising waste collec­
tion for material recovery, it is important to structure waste systems in a way that optimises both
collection efficiency, alongside material recovery and quality. The collection of residual waste
is highly important in this effort, as it provides a place for impurities and an option for users of
the system to default to when in doubt of the appropriate disposal method. Managing residual
waste is a difficult balance, which is precisely why knowing the composition of residual waste
is interesting and necessary for effective waste management policies.
5.1.3
Effects of system boundaries
System boundaries and methodological decisions on waste classification have had a large ef­
fect on the results of this study. It was found that the results obtained from this thesis and
the GR module are comparable, and that differences largely stem from the methodological ap­
proach to waste grouping. In this thesis project waste has largely been grouped based on EWC
codes associated with waste entries in ADS. This was done to minimise subjectivity in waste
classification and has, among others, meant that the majority of waste classified as hazardous
in the EWC was placed into the HAZ waste group, and that large quantities of soil collected and
MIN waste were reclassified. As a result, large quantities of construction materials and waste
collected for landfilling have been moved to the HAZ and SOI groups. It is important to note
that this study does not consider whether waste was sorted and collected in accordance with
current regulations or guidance. Thus, it is possible that this specific waste was collected in
accordance with current protocol. The system boundaries in this system will reclassify waste
such as construction materials containing asbestos, since the EWC code clearly classifies this
waste as hazardous.
While intentional, this methodological choice does lead to potential misinterpretations of the
data. For example, the reallocation of waste can skew the collection efficiency of both the MIN
and HAZ waste groups. It is suspected that these hazardous construction materials are largely
kept in the MIN and LAN groups in the GR module, leading to the misalignment of the gener­
ated waste estimations between the two models. Differences in structure explain all immediately
noticeable differences in estimated waste quantities. Neither approach is wrong, but the two
approaches offer different overviews of waste generation in Denmark. The approach adopted
in this thesis provides a more immediate overview of hazardous waste quantities generated in
Denmark. The approach adopted in the GR module likely provides a more realistic overview of
current legislative requirements for waste collection and handling. This then allows for a more
direct data reporting point (e.g., for landfilled waste in Denmark for a given year). This specific
reporting point is desirable in a political setting, as Denmark and the EU have established spe­

This assumption was accepted as a trade­off in order to obtain more direct and detailed data

The methodology was set up in a way that allows users of the model to make changes to any

5.2
Limitations of methodology
The analysis conducted in this study relies on several assumptions and estimates that influ­
ence the study’s reliability. It should be noted that the methodology developed in this study only
accounts for waste collected in Denmark, excluding soil, informal waste handling, and all bev­
erage containers collected through the national pant system. The study does not account for
imported and/or exported waste, and the methodology effectively assumes that collected waste
is equal to generated waste. In addition to these limitations, some data were also excluded from
the ADS waste statistics due to a lack of information or methodological fit. These exclusions
account for less than 0.2% of the waste assessed in this study and are therefore considered to
have a negligible impact on the study’s results or conclusions. Users of the model must keep
in mind that the model itself does not include all waste generated in Denmark in a given year,
but simply estimated quantities for waste reported in the ADS data file.
5.2.1
Applicability and modifiability of methodology
The results presented in Section 4.1 were derived by applying the methodology developed in
this thesis to a case study of the Danish waste management system, using 2022 ADS data.
The methodology was developed to be directly applicable to ADS data and was intended to
be applicable for any given year. However, in its current form, the methodology is only directly
applicable to the year of the case study. This is because the model relies on the translation of
EWC codes into the developed GL1 and GL2 codes defined in this study. In its current form, the
files supporting the methodology have only been developed to distribute EWC codes reported
in the 2022 data into GL1 and GL2 codes. This covers 400 of the 650 EWC codes. Not all
waste covered on the EWC is generated in Denmark; however, in the years 2018 to 2022, a
total of 580 different EWC codes were reported in ADS. This means that the methodology in
its current state covers 69% of EWC codes reported in the four most recent years of waste
statistics in Denmark. It has not been investigated what percentage of waste across the four
years is not covered; however, it is assumed that the composition of the majority of Danish
waste has not changed drastically in these years. The quantity of waste generated in Denmark
has been quite consistent from 2018 to 2022 (Danmarks Statistik, n.d.), and thus, it is likely that
the waste classified in any of the 180 EWC codes not reported in 2022 do not make up large
portions of the waste reported in Denmark in those years. Thus, the methodology can be applied
to these years, as long as the user is aware of potential misclassifications in data and ensures
that the data processing files are updated if an unacceptable amount of waste is missing or not

5.2.2
Waste classification decisions
While the classification methods applied in this study were designed to be transparent and
systematic, the lack of a standardised translation between Danish and EU waste codes made
subjective decisions in waste categorisation unavoidable, particularly when allocating waste into
GL1 and GL2 codes. The allocation of waste to GL1 and GL2 codes was done to the best of
abilities, informed by knowledge gathered during the literature review; however, the EWC codes
are plentiful and at times very specific. Since the author is not an expert in waste classification,
there are likely to be instances where a different allocation of an EWC code or the establishment
of another GL1 or GL2 code would have been more sensible. Users of the model can make
changes to any classification choices or grouping of GL2 codes onto targeted waste groups,
without major insight into the coding setup. Thus, it should be possible for most users to adapt
the model to their linking without major difficulties. It should be noted that the method developed
in this thesis handles hazardous waste differently from the GR module. Though deliberate, the
difference in methodological approach means that the results obtained in the two approaches
differ and are not directly comparable. While these differences are important to note, they are
not contradictory. Rather, they offer alternative ways of presenting waste data that may be more
suitable for certain types of policy analysis or academic purposes.
5.2.3
Understanding and interpreting data
When allocating waste across waste groups, it is important to have a clear understanding of the
waste. However, it can be difficult to establish how the waste reported by ADS has been and
should be collected, as well as how and when it was reported. In this study, it was assumed
that waste is reported after an initial sorting process where individual materials in waste entries
were distinguished. This was interpreted to be the only way that the information provided in
the ADS dataset can have the level of detail it has. However, it is not unambiguous when
in the waste handling chain an entry reported in ADS is made (i.e., before or after an initial
sorting process). The R/D codes provide some insight into this; however, the usage of both
Danish and EU waste codes without a clear or standardised translation between the two leads
to uncertainties in whether the data itself is completely aligned with reality. This ambiguity leads
to uncertainties in the appropriate interpretation of data. In this thesis, it was assumed that all
waste reported as I/H40 to I/H50 was electronic waste and was thus place in the ELE waste
group, free of any impurities. When delving into the ADS data file, however, it becomes apparent
that electronic waste is comprised of many different materials such as plastics, metals (including
copper, aluminum, etc.), glass, and various hazardous substances, which should potentially
have been reallocated to the PLA, MET, GLW, etc. waste groups. If the entry in ADS is made
before a sorting step, then the interpretation of the data made in this study is not as accurate
as expected. This information is, however, not reliably available in ADS.
5.2.4
Assumed composition of residual waste
The sensitivity analysis conducted on the model shows that the residual waste composition
used for modelling can lead to substantial variations in the outcome of the analysis. It is as­
sumed that the composition of residual waste used in the study offers the most representative
composition of the available data. The composition is considered representative of the waste
system in 2022. However, it reflects a collection system that was only partially implemented at
the time and has since evolved. The findings of this study are thus not necessarily representa­
tive of the state the waste collection system found in Denmark today. Of the three compositions

compared in this study, Edjabou et al. (2021) presents the oldest and most out­of­date waste
system. Although the compositions are based on data with just 5 to 12 years of difference, the
results derived from the compositions offer quite different interpretations in terms of potential
policy recommendations. Thus, it is important to consider the reliability and representativeness
of the compositions used in the methodology. Residual waste compositions are known to be as­
sociated with significant geographical and temporal variances, which have not been specifically
accounted for in this study. Additionally, the residual waste composition used for distributing
impurities was assumed to be the same for household and industrial waste. This is a very rough
assumption, made solely due to a lack of data on industrial residual waste compositions. In re­

for industrial waste are thus associated with noteworthy uncertainties and should be critically
assessed.
Despite the above limitations, the methods applied in this study allow for a detailed and repli­
cable mapping of the Danish waste management system and provide a strong foundation for
both policy­oriented modelling and further academic research.
5.3
Future research
The study has provided an initial insight into Danish waste at a more detailed level than previ­
ously seen. Far from all results derived from the methodology have been covered in this thesis,
and there are many different interesting aspects which could be further developed upon within
the topic and area of the thesis. As an example, although this thesis focused on waste gener­
ation, it originally aimed to explore a broader segment of the Danish waste system. To support
this, an additional Python file (05 Data analysis waste to treatment) was developed as part of the
methodology to distribute both collected and generated waste across their reported treatment
types. While this functionality is not explored in this thesis, it offers a clear opportunity for future
studies to build on the existing model and expand the scope of analysis to include treatment
pathways. The following provides suggestions for future work on the topic and suggestions for
improvements to the methodology developed in this thesis
5.3.1
Composition of residual waste from industries
This study highlights that industries are responsible for the majority of waste collected in Den­
mark, and that residual waste composition has a notable effect on the generated waste quan­
tities. The study shows that the purity and composition of waste streams vary across house­
hold and industrial waste. The same tendency is expected to be found in the case of residual
waste. Yet no detailed studies have been performed on the composition of industrial residual
waste, forcing the model to assess generated waste quantities in industries based on a residual
waste composition from households. The sensitivity analysis showed that even a small change
in residual waste composition leads to a large change in generated waste quantities. More
precise estimations on residual waste compositions, both in Denmark and across the EU, will
allow for more accurate estimates for waste generation as well as collection efficiencies for both
households and industries. Sund et al. (2025) and Albizzati et al. (2024) have shown that the
composition of waste in a system has an important impact on the outcomes of life cycle assess­
ments and economic evaluations of waste systems. This area of investigation would thus not
just benefit the accountability of this model, but also of all waste activities reliant on residual
waste compositions from industries.

5.3.2
Standardising translations between waste classification in the EU and its
member states
The lack of a standardised translation between EU and Danish waste classification codes leads
to subjectivity in evaluation and different interpretations of waste categorisation across the waste
sector. The EU classifies waste into 51 waste groups, Sund et al. (2025) translates these into
16 waste groups, and this study translates the 50 Danish I/H codes into 17 waste groups. Some
of the groups are exact copies, others have minor or major differences, and some are not in­
cluded in one another. It is apparent that the terminology and classification of waste within the
EU, and between the EU and Denmark, must be standardised to optimise the two systems and
minimise potential confusion and misinterpretations in the future. Waste terminology is often
misused or used interchangeably across different waste sectors and systems. In combination
with the many different waste categorisation groups, this makes it extremely difficult to fully un­
derstand and explain waste systems across the EU. It is apparent that studies do not follow any
standardised waste grouping system or definitions when performing high­level investigations of
waste systems (Danish Research Institute for Economic Analysis and Modelling, 2025b; Sund
et al., 2025). Establishing a preferred and standard list of waste grouping to be used for studies
on waste systems within the EU would be beneficial as this would make studies much more
comparable. In general, waste management could benefit from a broader and more encom­
passing standard terminology to be used across the EU.
5.3.3
Improvements to the methodology
The current methodology is based on a number of methodological decisions and waste cat­
egorisations determined by the author of this thesis. These choices reflect existing models,
literature reviews, peer­to­peer, and supervisor discussions. One such decision, which has had
a large effect on the model outcome, is the decision to allocate hazardous waste from MIN
and LAN to the HAZ waste group. It was found that this differentiates the results of using this
model from the results obtained by the DREAM group. In its current setup, the model can eas­
ily be adapted for changes in target group allocation or in classification choices. However, in
its current setup, the model is also sensitive to potential user errors when adapting the model.
This setup could easily be automated, and the many different results of applying the methodol­
ogy could be automatically presented, where it currently requires manual walk­throughs of the
underlying data.
Another decision that is expected to have had a notable impact on the results obtained through
the methodology is the allocation of ELE waste and the assumption that the collected waste
in the group contains no impurities. When looking a the EWC codes reported in ADS, it is
apparent that the waste contains several impurities. Better ways of handling ELE waste while
still maintaining an overview of waste generation for specific EU waste goals on electronic waste
could be developed. Additionally, the current model does not account for soils, informal waste
handling, imports/exports, or waste collected through the Danish pant system. The ADS data
file contains information on both soils and imports/exports, and these could thus be directly
included in the methodology. Additionally, statistics from the pant system could be collected for
inclusion in the methodology. These inclusions would provide a more comprehensive overview
of the Danish waste management system and thus more encompassing estimations of waste
generation quantities in Denmark. Lastly, informal waste handling could be investigated. This
section would, without a doubt, be more challenging to include in the methodology/estimates,
as this sector does not have any data readily available.

6
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to develop a public methodology that can be applied to ADS data to estimate
generated waste quantities in Denmark. The model was developed to align with the core struc­
ture and principles of the official national model, the results of which are not publicly available.
The methodology was applied to a case study for the 2022 national waste data, and the robust­
ness of the methodology was evaluated by comparing its results with those attained through
the national model.
Through the application of the case study, it was found that approximately 12.6 million tonnes of
waste were collected and generated in Denmark in 2022, when excluding soil waste, informal
waste handling, and pant waste. Households were responsible for 33% of this waste, while in­
dustries were responsible for the remaining 67%. It was found that the distribution of collected
and generated waste groups had major differences. MIN and MIX waste were the most col­
lected waste groups, followed by MET and BIO. Approximately 31% of the collected waste was
redistributed across waste groups. Of these 65% stemmed from the redistribution of residual
waste collected as MIX waste. The remaining 35% stemmed from waste collected as impurities
in other waste groups. It was found that large quantities of SOI, and MIX waste were collected
as missortings in other groups. The missortings of SOI waste stem almost exclusively from
track ballast collected as MIN waste.
Industrial waste collection generally seems to be more efficiently separated than household
waste, with some room for improvement, specifically for SOI, HAZ, BIO, and PAC waste. For
both household and industry, it seems that the waste contained in residual waste has the most
significant impact on generated waste quantities. BIO waste was found to be the most missorted
waste category, independently of the residual waste composition used in the methodology. The
ranking of other categories depended on waste composition. Further investigation into the BIO
group showed that approximately half of the generated BIO waste stemmed from food waste
collected as MIX waste. Data suggest that this may be due to limited engagement or awareness
regarding food waste sorting. This was highlighted as a potential information campaign or point
of improvement for future strategic considerations within waste management.
The validity of the methodology was investigated by establishing a range of expected values
from the application of the model and comparing them with the official estimates obtained by the
DREAM group. Here, it was found that values generally aligned and that differences in results
could be explained by data input to the system and methodological approach in waste allocation
across groups.
The study underscored the importance of having a comprehensive understanding of national
waste systems as a foundation for effective and informed policy recommendations, legislative
decisions, and action plans. The Danish waste system is complex and cannot be effectively
managed without insight into both collected and generated waste quantities, as well as a thor­
ough understanding of the compositions of the individual waste streams in the system. The
study provides some suggestions for future research on the topic which may assist in contin­
ued monitoring and a deeper understanding of the waste system, enabling informed, strategic
decision­making in support of more sustainable waste management practices.

Supplementary Material
Supplementary material containing data processing files developed to run the methodology
presented in this thesis. Below is an overview of the supplementary files provided.
S1 ­ Political waste goals in the EU and Denmark
The Excel file Danish and EU waste targets contains an overview of EU and Danish waste tar­
gets relevant from 2025 onward. All EU targets are goals that the EU must achieve collectively
across all member states of the union (European Union, 2019). Generally, Danish policies aim
to at least match the EU goals (Miljøstyrelsen, 2024c, 2025a). All Danish targets are specific
to Danish legislation and policies. The targets have been grouped into waste­specific targets,
which are provided in separate sheets in the file. A separate sheet provides an overview of
references used for the overview. It should be noted that several of the Danish statutory orders
have been updated throughout the course of this thesis. This overview found in the file does
not account for any potential changes made since the date stated in the accessing time of the
reference.
S2 ­ Method application for case study
The attached zip file Assessment of the Danish Waste Management System ­ Supplementary
material zipped contains all files needed to run the methodology developed in this study. Ap­
pendices B and C provide an overview of files contained in the zip file. Each file contains a short
table of contents, but users of the methodology will have to refer back to Appendix B and C for
additional information on file structure and purpose.
