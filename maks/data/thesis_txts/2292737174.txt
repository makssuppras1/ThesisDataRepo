Abstract 
 
As a part of the study for the East tower Cour de Justice des Communautees Europeennes, a finite 
element model has been constructed to simulate observed full-scale monitored response events in 
terms of peak horizontal acceleration.  
The finite element model is to represent the dynamical response of the East tower, when applied 
wind loading from varies wind directions and mean wind velocities. The wind load data that is 
applied from the finite element model is obtained through a wind-tunnel study. The structural and 
dynamical properties are obtained through full-scale forced vibration tests. The observed events 
have been conducted by an accelerometer that is placed on the top of the tower. The wind data for 
the observed events have been transferred from recorded wind data at the international airport in 
Luxemburg to simulate similar events with which can be compared with the observed response 
events with respect to the peak horizontal acceleration and the human comfort. Additionally a 
study of mean wind speeds with a 10 years return period has been analyzed to investigate the effect 
of Tuned Liquid dampers used in the east tower. 
The comparison of the simulated and observed response events showed deviations in terms of 
magnitude and characteristics in terms of limited amount of variation of wind direction and 
wind velocities. The comfort criteria for the observed and simulated response events were not 
exceeded. For mean wind speeds with 10 years return period showed that the usage of Tuned 
liquid dampers would efficiently reduce the response to fulfill the comfort criteria.

1. Introduction 
A brief introduction will be piloted in terms of the background of this thesis, the main objectives 
regarding the problem of the Eastern tower of the ‚ÄúCour de Justice des Communautees 
Europeennes‚Äù and at last there will be a summary describing the different chapters within this 
thesis. 
1.1 Background 
This thesis considers the Eastern tower of the ‚ÄúCour de Justice des Communautees Europeennes‚Äù 
which is located in Luxemburg. The tower is a 102m high-rise building and is located in 
Kirchberg-Plateau. The tower is sensitive to wind-loading with respect to the horizontal 
acceleration response about the weak-axis at the top of the structure, and therefore it has been an 
interesting study to conduct in terms of determining the horizontal acceleration and suggesting a 
possible solution for this problem. The first study was conducted in 2002 focusing on determining 
the horizontal acceleration response about the weak axis, through wind-tunnel tests and time-
domain calculations of the dynamic response. A proposal for a solution of the response problem 
was made by using Tuned Liquid Dampers, which were studied through feasibility studies and 
shaking table tests for the proposed design. In 2010 the dynamic characteristics of the east tower 
were determined through Full-scale testing, and the Tuned Liquid damping ratio was also 
determined. An overview of the Kirchberg-Plateau can be seen in Fig (1.1a) [1], and the scaled 
model that was used for the wind-tunnel study Koss [1] can be seen in Fig (1.1b) [1]. An important 
detail, which has to be emphasized, is that the tower lies in a twin arrangement, and hereby shelters 
the tower for SW winds.

1.2 Main objective 
One of the main objectives of this thesis was to study the background/previous studies for the 
Eastern tower [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], [6], [7] in order to obtain relevant data regarding structural and 
dynamical parameters for the first and third mode, since that the horizontal acceleration for the 
weak axis is the main interest. 
Constructing an accurate Finite element model (FE-model) to describe the dynamical behavior of 
the structure. The FE-model will have to accommodate for the changes of the structural and 
dynamical variables to derive the peak horizontal acceleration response for different wind 
directions by time-domain analysis.  
In addition, a part of the objective was also to verify the effect of the T.L.D dampers in terms of 
peak horizontal accelerations for different wind directions with respect to human comfort. 
Also, sorting and analyzing relevant wind response data from full-scale monitoring, and 
transferring wind data from Luxemburg Airport to the location of the East tower. 
Lastly, a comparison of the observed events and simulated events will be conducted with 
magnitude and human comfort. 
The main contributors have to be identified to obtain a more accurate solution.

2. Literature review 
For the study of the eastern tower of the Cour de Justice des Communautees Europeennes there 
have been seen several studies made, from the preliminary phase of a wind-tunnel study to the 
actual full-scale testing and analysis phase. Therefore it is important to have a glance over what 
has already been determined and figured out from all the previous studies which have been made 
about the Eastern tower. This helps with the understanding of the Eastern tower both in terms of 
structural properties and dynamical properties. 
 
2.1 Preliminary design 
The preliminary design consisted on several researches, which investigates the wind effect on the 
buildings in the Court de Justice, and the dynamic response in order to take into account for the 
comfort by wind-tunnel testing. Furthermore there has been conducted a feasibility study and a 
detailed analysis to take external dampers as a solution for the exceeding horizontal acceleration 
about the weak axis, and several other studies due to changes in the structural properties of the 
Eastern tower. Shaking-table tests of the damper was at last investigated for the purpose of 
determining the damping level for the design of the tuned liquid dampers (T.L.Ds)

Wind-tunnel study ‚Äì (2002-12-6) [1] 
The first research, which was made upon the Kirchberg-Plateau in Luxembourg-City [1] was the 
wind-tunnel study conducted by the Danish Maritime Institute (FORCE-DMI), and the author was 
Professor Holger Koss. The study was not only done for the purpose of determining the design, 
loads for the Eastern tower and the dynamic response on the top of the Eastern tower due to the 
comfort assessment at the top level, but it was made for several other reasons as testing the design 
loads on the surrounding buildings in the Court de Justice. 
The design mean wind speeds, which were obtained from design wind speed profiles from the 
National Research Institute (RIS√ò) provided for wind-load investigation of a nearby construction 
Salle de Concert. The different directions design speeds were obtained from analysis of 19 years of 
wind data. From these there was found omnidirectional design wind mean speed with a return 
period of 50 years , and this was of course transformed to directional design wind mean speeds 
by linear scaling. Since directional winds will be more accurate, a transformation to 10 years return 
period was also made, this is what the comfort criteria for horizontal peak acceleration is based 
upon. The Salle de Concert and the Court de Justice both have the same terrain level which is

3.2 Damping 
Damping of structure is an essential part to avoid the presence of excessively high responses, and it 
can also help to obtain specific criteria such as the comfort criterion which is mentioned. The 
damping gives the structure the ability to dissipate energy and is proportional to the velocity of the 
structure, and this has been an important issue for tall buildings as they are exposed to wind and 
earthquake loads since they are made from flexible beams, which causes swaying motions of the 
structure, massive forces, accelerations and displacements and this has been a major concern due 
to serviceability considerations especially for resonant dynamic motions. The damping of a 
structure can be divided into three categories, the structural damping, the aerodynamic damping 
and the external damping also called auxiliary dampers, and these together forms the total damping 
of the structure, and the relation can be described by Eq. (3.27) which is the relation between the 
logarithmic decrement of damping.

3.2.3 External damping 
If the structural and the aerodynamic damping are not sufficient then additional damping to a 
structure can be considered. There are several external damping devices which can be taking into 
consideration such as viscoelastic dampers, tuned mass dampers (T.M.D) which is the leading 
damper among sky-scrapers and high-rise buildings, friction dampers and tuned liquid dampers 
(T.L.D) which is a new technology based on the T.M.D. The Tuned liquid dampers can be divided 
in two different categories, the Tuned liquid column dampers (T.L.C.D) and the tuned sloshing 
dampers (T.S.D), in which the second one is going to be investigated further and understand its 
features. These dampers are called passive since they rely on the motion of the structure to be 
activated, they are typically installed to operate at certain frequencies to counteract the resonant 
amplitude which is obtained by wind or earthquake induced vibrations. An important factor when 
using such passive dampers is to have made an preliminary analytical or FEM calculation of the 
structure due to the fact that the dampers has to work optimal and effective to reduce the high 
amplitudes. This requires that the dynamic properties are investigated to know which modes are 
critical for a given axis, and the natural frequencies which can produce critical resonance 
amplitudes. The background for the choice of which damper is the most optimal can depend on a 
lot of factors such as the cost of implementation, this goes especially for active and semi-active 
since these systems can often cost a lot, the long-term maintenance, complexity and flexibility and 
simplicity in tuning the dampers [2]. The tuned liquid dampers which are used is also known as the 
tuned sloshing damper, but for the rest of the thesis it will be referred to as TLD. 
T.S.D 
The tuned sloshing dampers is the simplest passive damper devices, it can be divided into two 
categories, the motion of shallow water in containers and deep water where baffles or screens is 
added to increase the damping contribution, these containers are usually on top of the structures to 
obtain the highest effectivity thus leading to smaller responses. The energy dissipation in the 
dampers for shallow water come from several factors, such as internal viscous forces, wave 
breaking and tank wall surface friction which are the three dominant contributions [13], and the 
deep-water case it is the baffles or screens which contributes mostly to the damping by the drag 
which is caused, but with addition from the wave breaking and surface friction [19]. The most 
important for the T.S.D is to tune the dampers around the natural frequency since that is where the 
damping is mostly needed and this is done by providing a specific liquid density and water level,

3.5 Wind excitation 
High-rise buildings are targets for dynamical vibrations created by wind, this is due to the low 
natural frequencies which are obtained which makes them sensitive to wind loading. The responses 
which can be obtained for a high-rise building is, along wind vibration, across wind vibrations and 
torsional vibrations. The wind velocities has a fluctuating nature which comes from vortices which 
is created within the flow, these fluctuations acts on structures and can excite the structures 
dynamically. For structures with low-natural frequencies and small damping it is easier for the 
wind to excite the structure dynamically.  The wind velocity can be treated as a stationary random 
process, where it is divided in to component, the fluctuating component and the mean component. 
While mean component also known as the time-averaged component which is dependent on the 
height, the fluctuating component is dependent on the time and its position, the mean contribution 
of the fluctuating component is also equal to zero. From Holmes [] it is considered that along-wind 
is the most dominant in the low-frequency regime, and this is also the dominant natural 
frequencies of tall like structures which are longer in relation to their width, this is also illustrated 
in Fig (3.6) [13]. This is related to that earthquake excitations has usually a lower duration but up 
to 10-50 times higher frequency than in a fully-developed windstorm [13]. This will most likely 
seem to give a high resonant response which can cause critical effects on the structure.

4.  Simulation of Structural response 
The simulation of the structural response is one of the two essential parts of this study, 
where the term response refers to the horizontal acceleration of the tower in the direction of 
the weak axis. The term simulation comes from the information obtained from the wind-
tunnel study performed in [1], where the force time histories were obtained and are essential 
for obtaining the response for the given a FE-model which is going to be reconstructed.   
In order to test whether or not the FE-model is accurate and reasonable it needs to be able to 
give approximately the same responses, which were found in [1] when using the same 
structural parameter, and by performing a ping test, and then further investigating it by the 
change of parameters, the addition of external damping and other changes in structural and 
dynamical parameters as center of rotation and modal mass and stiffness can be done. 
The response of the weak axis is coming from two different movements, a translational 
movement around the weak-axis, and a torsional movement, which contributed, to the 
movement on the weak axis. 
This therefore requires the modelling of two FE-model, which therefore gives the two 
movements that can be combined by superposition to obtain the total response. This 
superposition method is considered accurate enough to describe the total response of the 
structure. 
4.1 FE-model 
The model of the tower is going to be divided into two models as done in [1], in which a 
superposition approach is going to be used. This is done as a simplification of the model and 
to use a 2-D stick model. The calculations for the two models can be seen in Appendix 
(A.1). 
The two stick models are going to be divided in terms of the two responses, which are 
obtained for the weak axis, hence the translational acceleration, and the torsional 
acceleration. A modification is also made since that the first mode as seen in [1] and [3] 
shows that a torsional coupling is stimulated in the first mode due to offset of the center of 
the rotation position. This is taking into account by scaling the torsional stick-model by 
manipulating and either changing the modal mass or the modal stiffness so it fits the first 
mode, and also by obtaining the same natural frequency as seen in Section (3.6).

4.1.4 Damping properties 
The damping properties of the structure have been defined in the previous studies, and most 
importantly it is the critical damping ratio, which has been estimated. For the FE-modelling 
it was simplified that the damping acts are linear, and therefore it was determined that the 
stiffness and mass proportional Rayleigh damping would be the most efficient method to 
take the damping into account. For the FE-model the critical damping ratio, and the natural 
frequency of the particular mode together with the mass and stiffness matrix is what 
determines the damping matrix, as seen in Section (3.1). 
The damping of the structure varies due to the addition of different damping components. 
The main component is the structural damping because it will always exist. Another main 
contributor to the damping is the aerodynamic damping, which will also exist though it 
varies due to the wind direction Section (3.2.2), whereas the TLD damping is the addition 
due to the large response, which was obtained in [1].

5.2 Analysis of response 
For the analysis of the event response triggered by the accelerometer the program ‚ÄúData & 
Probailistic Analysis Tools‚Äùby Prof. H. Koss has been used Appendix (A.11). The program 
is a time-history analysis that analyses the time series for measured signal by such as 
accelerometer data.  The program divides the time series in sub-series to find 
maximum/minimum values, and also mean and standard deviations. It also creates a 
histogram of the time-series (Probability density). The program also calculates the power 
spectral density of the time series, this is done by using the Matlab rutine ‚ÄúTHA5‚Äù and the 
plotting is made on a double-logarithmic graph. Different types of graphs are made, such as 
the normalized spectrum. 
In the ‚ÄúTHA5‚Äù program a digital filtering option can be used, and this is due to eliminated 
contributions from certain frequency range from the signal. This option can be used in three 
manners; a cut-off frequency can be used for all contributions below the specified frequency 
(Low-pass filter), another option in which similar manner where only the contribution from 
frequency above the cut-off frequency (High-pass filter) and at last frequency contribution 
from in between to frequencies can be obtained (Band-pass filter). 
The digital filtering will have sorted out all of the unwanted signals, and new histograms or 
spectral density functions can hereby be obtained. 
The input files are organized in such a manner that it takes into account that the input data

5.9 Analysis of maximum acceleration 
The maximum acceleration for the events that were triggered can now be calculated, as 
earlier, by the two obtained methods; the peak factor method, and by the mean value of the 
peaks. This method is done separately for the three categories of events which are obtained, 
the stochastic events where the T.L.D was deactivated, the resonance events, and the 
stochastic events where the T.L.D was activated. The maximum peaks are not specifically a 
good representation for the peaks observed by the triggered events due to the fact that the 
events last 60 seconds, and the mean wind duration is approximately 10 minutes. For most 
of these cases 1 peak is triggered for one event, which can be caused by large gust winds in 
a 20 second duration. For the resonance events, the characteristic is different due to the 
sinusoidal shape, where acceleration can be felt in the intervals of more than a 1 second 
peak. Therefore it is an averaged value of these peaks, which can be compared to the FE-
model simulation.

6.  FE-simulation of observed events and 
Comparison 
In this section, there is going to be simulated the relevant events which were observed and

characteristics. By using the FE-model from Section (3.3.3) a simulation of the events will 
occur. The model which is chosen for the simulation of the events is the mass tuned model. 
Due to the fact that the events are distributed in such a manner that the wind directions for 
the events with the TLD deactivated is in the wind directions 40 to 60 degrees, and from Fig 
(4.25) it was seen that these wind-directions showed good compliance between the models. 
For the case where the TLD was activated, great compliance was achieved for all wind 
directions, so the decision was based upon these results. 
The mean wind speeds observed from the metrological center at Luxemburg Airport is also 
going to contribute in order to shape these simulated events by reducing the load vectors 
with regards to the mean design velocities. The wind directions for the events, which are 
simulated, are rounded to the nearest 10 degrees. 
The characteristics of the simulated events are not going to be changed due to the change of 
these parameters. The characteristics of the response time-series from the accelerometer are 
observed in nature, and this can vary in terms of the wind that attacks. This is also why, as 
seen in Section (6), the events when the T.L.D dampers were deactivated, a low-band 
response and stochastic response divided the characteristics of the responses. This cannot be 
taken into account in the simulated events. Therefore, this only takes the reduction of the 
pressures measured on the scaled-model in terms of magnitude into account, and this is with 
respect to the change of wind-direction and the mean wind speed. 
As seen in Section () there was proposed three different tuning possibilities, and as it could 
be seen from both Section and Section, the mass tuned models were more significant in term 
of magnitude compared with [1], [6] and [7]. The modal combination used was the SRSS 
method, due to the simplicity of the method and also the compliance with the CQC.

7. Discussion 
The FE-model showed a deviation for the two natural frequencies for the first and third 
mode compared to [1] when applying the same structural properties. This was solved by 
tuning of the mass since the mass was suspected for uncertainty. A ping test performed for 
the two FE-models showed that the model was in great compliance in terms of the natural 
frequency and the damping of the structure. 
The force time-histories, which were obtained, showed a distortion in a big amount of the 
data that was obtained from the wind-tunnel tests. These were in form of sudden drops in 
the force and electromagnetic impulses. This distortion was seen as a big issue due to the 
differentiation of the distortion for the different wind directions for the translational 
response, but for the torsional response it showed a smaller deviation. The translational 
mode response was evaluated by an extreme value analysis, and this showed that for some 
wind directions the error which was obtained was large and was spread randomly for 
different wind directions.  
For the comfort criteria diversity was obtained compared to the values which were used by 
[1] & [3] this can be investigated further if the research in [1] had an additional contribution 
to the comfort criteria due to the additional torsional contribution to the first mode. 
Melbournes comfort criteria was used throughout this project.  
The tuning of the first mode was investigated to see which method would be sufficient to 
implement when applying the new structural and dynamical properties proposed by the full-
scale test. This showed that the response form the mass tuned model, the un-tuned model 
and the response obtained from [1] showed great compliance while the stiffness/mass tuned 
model gave a huge over estimation. The peak responses which were obtained all exceeded 
the comfort criteria proposed by Melbourne for the Win directions 190 to approximately 
280 degrees for the different obtained models. A rather rare disturbance was seen for the 
case for the stiffness/mass tuned model when the T.L.D dampers were deactivated; it 
showed a huge deviation for the SRSS and CQC modal combination method. This is due to 
the fact that the CQC method depends on the damping ratio of the modes, while the SRSS 
method does not. 
The responses which were obtained from the new FE-model were obtained with respect to 
structural and dynamical properties from the full-scale testing, and showed great compliance 
for both the mass-tuned model and the stiffness/mass tuned model. The responses which

8. Conclusion 
For the amount of events, which were preserved for a rather limited number of wind-
directions and mean wind velocity variation it could not be with certainty to evaluate the 
FE-model which was obtained for the new structural properties which was found through 
forced excitation. A rather interesting finding was that for the wind-directions 40 to 50 
degrees, it was difficult to maintain any good similarity for the simulated model and the 
observed events. This is due to the fact that the East tower is sheltered by the West tower in 
the direction, which implies that the wind-tunnel study could not comprehend for the 
pressure distribution on the fa√ßade. The same behavior was seen for the wind directions 150 
to 180 degrees which is southeast-south wind and this will not be affected by the shelter of 
the west tower, though this could be the small velocities which are coherent with the events 
with these wind directions. The mean wind velocity 6.5 m/s showed small deviations for all 
comparisons and this was cryptic but for more events, this could be an interesting 
investigation. The events which were exited through the wind also showed that the 
resonance found through the power spectrum were around and close to the natural 
frequencies which were obtained by the full scale testing. 
The main contributors for the inaccuracy of the simulated events can expected to be the 
transfer function, since its effect of the velocity is major for the simulated model and has to 
be accurate and consist of more determination parameters other than the height and the 
roughness length. The force histories conducted from the wind-tunnel study is also a main 
contributor for the inaccuracy of FE simulation due to the impulses and sudden reduction in 
force, while these contributions can both have an effect of the extreme value analysis, it also 
contributed to the standard variation obtained for the distorted responses which is not 
consistent for the different wind directions.  
The observed and simulated events all showed that the comfort criteria would not be 
exceeded, while also showing that the acceleration level would not attain any sense for 
persons occupying the structure in the worst case scenario by being on the top level. 
For the mean wind speeds obtained for the 10 year return period a reduction in the peak 
response was obtained for the new structural values determined from the full-scale testing. It 
also showed that the T.L.D dampers would even reduce the horizontal peak response to be 
under Melbournes comfort criterion.
