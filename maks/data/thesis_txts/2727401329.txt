Abstract
This thesis uses experimental analysis to study the application of Experimental Modal
Analysis (EMA) on a test rig with a rotating shaft supported by a radial active gas foil
bearing (AGFB).
The test rig consists of a rotating shaft with a disc attached on one end and it is sup-
ported by one ball bearing at the opposite end and a gas foil bearing around the middle of
the shaft. EMA is carried out using an impact hammer mounted with a force transducer
producing input force and displacement sensors measuring the output.
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate and characterize a bump-foil gas bearing
experimentally for controlling lateral vibrations of rigid and flexible rotating shafts. It is
done to prove and characterize the capabilities of the radial gas injection in improving the
dynamics while providing valuable experimental results that can be used for comparison
with a numerical model.
An extensive series of EMA tests are conducted to obtain the damping and stiffness char-
acteristics of the AGFB along with its position in the bearing and frequency responses of
the rotor-bearing system.
The AGFB is tested both with and without the aerostatic injection. Comparison and dis-
cussion of the results obtained about system dynamics with and without the injection and
its impact on the performance are conducted. Also, the effect of adding shims to the
AGFB on its performance is heavily tested by changing the bearing configuration and
parameters.
Results obtained from the experimental analysis show that the radial clearance of the
Active Gas Foil Bearing (AGFB) can be decreased by inserting shims of different sizes.
Inserting multiple shims of thinner sizes gives better results than inserting single shims
with thicker sizes. When air injection pressure is increased in an AGFB, the resonant
frequencies in the vertical direction decrease and in the horizontal direction, it increases.
When radial clearance was decreased by inserting shims, the resonant frequencies in the
vertical direction were also decreasing and in the horizontal direction were found to be
increasing.
When the air injection pressure was increased, the damping of the AGFB decreased for
both vertical and horizontal impact tests.
When air injection pressure was increased, the position of the shaft moved towards a
positive vertical direction meaning the load-carrying capacity of the rotor bearing system
was also increased.
This thesis presents extensive experimental results obtained by conducting clearance
measurement experiments, locus of the shaft measurement experiments, EMA tests in
regard to bearing clearance, the position of the shaft, FRFs obtained from horizontal and
vertical impact tests, and the impact of shims on bearing properties.

1
Introduction
In any rotating machinery, bearings are widely used as a mechanical component to sup-
port the shaft & load; and also to decrease friction between the moving parts. It also helps
to absorb and dampen the vibrations when the shaft is rotating. Bearings are found and
used in several applications such as vehicles, machines, turbines, etc. Different bear-
ings are designed for specific applications based on load types, shaft speed, and working
conditions.
1.1
Motivation on using Gas Bearing and Experimental
Modal Analysis (EMA)
The most commonly available and widely used type of bearing is ball bearing. It consists
of balls enclosed in a raceway and can handle both radial and thrust loads. Other types
of bearings include: a) roller bearings which use rollers instead of balls to reduce friction
and are designed to handle heavy loads, b) thrust bearings which are designed to handle
axial loads, c) plain/sleeve bearings which are simple and affordable bearings that use
cylindrical sleeves to minimize friction between two surfaces, d) linear bearings which are
designed for linear motion as in a linear actuator or a slide, e) magnetic bearings that use
magnetic fields to support the load and mostly used in applications where high speed and
accuracy are needed like MRI machines and f) fluid bearings which use a fluid, such as
oil or water, as a means of decreasing friction and supporting the load and are used in
high-speed applications such as turbomachinery.
All these types of bearings try to minimize friction by reducing the surface contact between
the shaft and the hub in some way. There are other types of bearings being researched
called gas bearings where the friction is minimized by the formation of the gas film when
the shaft is rotating [1]. However, it doesn’t work properly while stationary or at low speeds.
Then, there are active gas bearings (AGB) where pressurized air is injected between the
shaft and hub to reduce friction and provide damping. To further improve the damping
in gas bearings, gas foil bearings (GFB) are being tested where thin foils of metal are
placed between the shaft and the hub. Then, to further improve the efficiency of GFB,
pressurized air is injected and such bearings are called active gas foil bearings (AGFB).
There are several reasons for using AGFB over the usual ball bearing with lubrication
which are given below:
• High-speed applications: In most cases, lubricated ball bearings can fail at high speeds
due to centrifugal forces but active gas foil bearings can maintain better stability and per-
formance at such high speeds.
• High-temperature applications: Major cause of system failure at higher temperatures is
caused by the degradation of the lubricant. Since lubricant is not used in AGFBs, it can
operate at higher temperatures.
• Clean room applications: Since AGFBs do not produce any debris, it is ideal for working
conditions where cleanliness is required such as semiconductor manufacturing.
• High-reliability applications: Since there is no or very less contact between surfaces in
AGFBs, they require very less maintenance over their lifetime and do not wear and tear
as much as the ball bearings.

For the investigation, the Experimental modal analysis (EMA) technique was utilized. This
technique is used in engineering by exciting a structure with a known input force and
measuring its consequent vibration response. It can be used to measure various things

predicting the performance of structures that can help in building safer, better, and more
efficient structures or system.
In this project, EMA is mostly used for examining the natural responses, estimating modal

Theoretical Foundation
2.1
Active Gas Foil Bearing and its principle
To understand AGFB, the principle behind gas bearings must first be understood. Gas
bearings work on the principle of utilizing a thin gas film to separate and support the
moving shaft. Instead of using liquid lubricants like conventional bearings, they use gas
mediums for reducing friction and stabilizing the rotating system.
2.1.1
Principle of Passive Gas Bearing
Gas bearings need some clearance between the rotating surface often called the hub
and the rotating shaft as shown in Figure 2.1. The gas can typically be air or specific gas
such as nitrogen. While stationary and at very low speeds, there is high friction and no
lubrication between the hub and the shaft.
Figure 2.1: Passive gas bearing showing 1) hub, 2) shaft and clearance between them
When the shaft starts to rotate and maintain speed, then the gas between the shaft’s outer
surface and the hub’s inside surface starts to pressurize which generates an aerodynamic
effect. As this pressure increases, it separates those two surfaces and forms a thin layer
of gas film between them. Also, when the shaft rotates, a shearing action is generated
in the gas film which generates pressure to counter the load and also helps in separating
the contacting surfaces. So, this thin gas film acts as a lubricating layer.
The pressurized gas film helps in supporting the load or forces exerted on the rotating
system, opposes them, and also prevents direct contact between the hub and shaft sur-
faces which reduces friction, wear, and tear. In addition to that, the gas film also acts as
a damping element by absorbing vibrations in the rotating system and making the system
smooth. Also, the pressurized gas film helps in making the system concentric and more
stable.
2.1.2
Principle of Active Gas Bearing
To make the pressure of the gas film more uniform and also to make the bearing operate
at no or low rotating speeds, externally pressurized gas can be injected into the bearings
from different sides as seen in Figure 2.2. This can be done by making four uniform
holes at 00, 900, 1800, and 2700 positions inside the gas bearing. This will improve the
load-bearing capability of gas bearing at low speeds.

Figure 2.2: Active gas bearing showing 1) hub, 2) shaft and 3) gas injection
2.1.3
Principle of Bump Foil Bearing
Another way of improving the gas bearing capabilities is to add bump foils between the
hub’s inner surface and the shaft’s outer surface. The bump foil consists of a folded metal
strip and a top foil as shown in Figure 2.3. There can be different combinations such as
single foil, two pads, and four pads combination. The principle is to provide more damping
by making the bump foil act as a mechanical spring. Also, the top foil can be coated with
low-friction material such as Teflon in order to make the contact between hub and shaft
smoother. Also, since the shaft now sits on the top foil, there is very less friction and more
damping. This also means that when used for a long period of time, only the top foil may
be replaced to fix wear and tear.
Figure 2.3: Bump foil bearing showing 1) hub, 2) shaft, 3) bump foil and 4) top foil
2.1.4
Principle of Active Gas Foil Bearing
In AGFBs, all the above principles are combined to form a more robust system. It can
be seen in Figure 2.4. Here, the pressurized gas film generated by injected air through
nozzles carries the load and the bump foil provides more damping and reduces friction.
In case of any instabilities in the system, the shaft will contact with the top foil and the
bump foil preventing the hub from being damaged.

2.2

When the shafts rotate in the rotating bearing system using AGFB, the location of the
shaft changes with an increase in speed, and its position over time is called as the locus
of the shaft. The shaft typically starts from the bottom position making contact with the top
foil of the bottom pad and moves towards the center of the hub following a curved path
when ideal aerodynamics is gained. However, if the gas film is too thin and the shaft is
too close to the hub’s surface, there might be too much friction which can cause severe
damage to the system. Thus, it is desired to make the system as much stable and safe
as possible by keeping the shaft locus stable.

2.3
Experimental Modal Analysis
In order to study the dynamic characteristics of the system, it is important to study its
frequency response function (FRF). FRF can be simply explained as a mathematical rep-
resentation showing the relationship between the input and output of a system in terms of
frequency. It is a complex-valued function that shows how a system (mechanical struc-
ture, electrical circuits, etc) responds to inputs at different frequencies. The magnitude
of FRF determines how the system amplifies or attenuates the input signal at different
frequencies and its phase corresponds to how the output is delayed or advanced com-
pared to its input signal. FRF can be generated experimentally using a technique called
Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA).
EMA is a widely used technique used to determine the dynamic characteristics of a sys-
tem. In this, project it is used to study how the mechanical system vibrates or responds
to different inputs. A typical EMA procedure involves exciting a system with known and
controlled input and the corresponding output is measured. In this project, impact ham-
mer is used to produce force as input and displacement sensors are used to measure
displacement in X and Y axis as outputs.
EMA can be used to determine various system properties such as estimating modal
parameters, obtaining natural frequencies, damping characteristics, mode shapes, and
more. It involves various other sub-processes from taking the input from sensors to ob-
taining the final result. First, the input (force data) and output (displacement data) are
auto-correlated and cross-correlated and then Fourier transformed into the frequency do-
main using transfer functions. This will convert the raw signals into frequency data which
can be further used to determine the power Spectral density (PSD) and the Cross Power
Spectral Density (CPSD). These are then used to calculate the two transfer functions:
H1(ω) and H2(ω). Finally, these two transfer functions are used to obtain the final FRF.
The subprocesses are explained in brief below:

2.3.6
Filtering
Usually, the raw signal obtained from the force transducer or displacement sensors is
full of noise. These noises are not ideal for the measurement and can cause unwanted
discrepancies while obtaining the result. So, the raw signal needs to be processed and
noise needs to be minimized. For this, filters are used during the data processing. The
noise can also be minimized either at the source or after it is collected. For this project,
filtering is done after the data is collected. There are different types of filters such as low
pass filter which only allows signals below a certain threshold, high pass filters which only
allow signals above certain threshold and band filters which only allow signals in a certain
range of frequencies. In this project, a Butterworth filter is used during data processing.
2.3.7
Segmentation, Averaging, and Windowing
A typical experiment includes a system excited by a hit with an impact hammer for a short
period of time and measuring the impact force and displacement. While only one set of
this procedure is enough to generate an FRF, it is definitely not ideal. It is desired to have
30 different hits and then take the average. However, it’s not ideal either to just average
out the force and displacement of the entire measurement as there is a gap between
the impacts and the impacts are not uniform. For this, only the portion of the signals
representing the hits are separated into blocks which are called segmentation then PSD

Runout Compensation
An ideal shaft has no imperfections on its outer surface which is not the case in real
life. The shaft surface has some roughness which when rotated and measured by the
displacement sensors causes some noise in the signal. The noise becomes even more
enlarged when the system has some unbalance and then the shaft is rotated at higher
speeds. The shaft will then rotate eccentrically causing more noise. This type of noise
is called runout and can be seen in Figure 2.13. This noise needs to be accommodated
through runout compensation. Firstly, when the shaft is rotating normally without any
impact is recorded and the displacement signals corresponding to the angular positions
of the shaft are measured.

previously is substracted with precise angular positions and thus the noise in minimized
as seen in Figure 2.14. This improves the quality of measured data and noise created by

5
EMA Test Procedure
In order to generate a FRF of the rotor bearing system, EMA is carried out by exciting the
shaft with an impact hammer and measuring the vertical and horizontal displacements as
output.
5.1
Methodology
The shaft was hit at the location between the disc and the AGFB with the impact hammer
for 30 consecutive times with around a 2-second gap between each impact. For the
vertical impact test, the shaft was hit from directly above and for horizontal impact tests,
it was hit from the horizontal axis.
All the sensor data are measured and recorded in dSpace ControlDesk program and
stored on the computer. During post-processing, the raw data is shifted against its mean
value. Then, it is filtered, segmented, autocorrelated, and crosscorrelated after which
PSD and CPSD are computed. Then, finally the complex functions H1(ω) and H1(ω) are
computed. Then, the coherence and its phase are also computed and plots are generated
for result extraction.
The AGFB was tested with and without shaft rotation. All the tests conducted without
any shaft rotation and with air injection are considered ’aerostatic’ tests and all the tests
conducted with shaft rotation and air injection are considered ’aerodynamic’ tests in this
project. During the aerodynamic lubrication tests, when the shaft is rotated, runout com-
pensation was also done on the measured displacement data.

7
Discussion
7.1
Clearance measurements
From the clearance measurement tests, it can be seen that the AGFB without any shims
has a radial clearance of around 180 µm which is more than ideal. This high clearance is
because the shaft surface was grinded in a previous project in order to increase clearance.
When shims were added, the clearance was reduced. However, when shims of 0.07 mm
were added the radial clearance was not reduced by the same amount but only around
0.035 mm. This was also the case when shims of (0.07+0.04) and (0.07+0.04+0.04) mm
were added. The radial clearance reduced didn’t match with the shims added. It might
be because the 0.07 mm shim is thicker and provided some resistance due to bending
when inserted into the AGFB. Also, it is possible that the 0.07 mm being thicker provided
stiffness and when bent formed a gap and resistance between the bub and the bump
foil. This explains why the radial clearance didn’t match the shims added. Also, in the
clearance measurement plot, it can be seen that the part corresponding to the gap in the
bearing is tilted more when 0.07 mm shims were added. This also proves that the 0.07
shims provided some kind of extra stiffness.
It is also possible that when different shims were stacked with one another, a small gap
of air might have been created which affected the measurement slightly.
7.2
Locus of the shaft
From the locus of the shaft, it can be seen that adding more air injection pressure to
the bottom injector pushed the shaft upwards which is expected. However, the size of
the orifice was small and wasn’t enough to support the load of the shaft when remained
static. The speed of the shaft had very little effect on the locus of the shaft. However,
this might be because the speed was very low to have any effect. The speed couldn’t be
increased because of the low resonant frequency of the shaft but if it had been increased
to higher speeds like greater than 5000 RPM, it could have a much higher effect on the
shaft position.
7.3
EMA tests with aerostatic lubrication
When EMA tests were conducted with only aerostatic lubrication for different configura-
tions, the natural frequency in the vertical direction ranged from around 46 Hz to 60 Hz,
and in the horizontal direction ranged from around 20 Hz to around 46 Hz. For most of the
configurations, adding shims did not change the natural frequency in the vertical direc-
tion by much compared to the horizontal direction. This can be because the shaft can be
supposed as being supported by two stiffness in both horizontal and vertical directions.
One stiffness comes from air pressure (ka) and another from the bump foils (kb) as shown
in Figure 7.1. Then, the equivalent stiffness (k) comes from these two. In the vertical
direction, the load of the shaft presses down the bump foil making (kb) much more signif-
icant than (ka) whereas, in the horizontal direction, the shaft doesn’t touch the foils when
stationery so (kb) is not that significant. When air pressure is increased (ka) increases but
in the vertical direction, it doesn’t affect the equivalent stiffness (k) as (kb) is dominant.
However, in the horizontal direction, k is impacted significantly by ka being increased.

7.4
EMA tests under aerodynamic lubrication
During the aerodynamic lubrication, it is seen that the speed of the shaft has very little
effect on the natural frequency. This might be because the speed is very low and it needs
to be increased to larger speeds to have a high impact. Increasing the gas pressure had a
positive effect on the performance and friction of the shaft. Among all the configurations,
the bottom and side injectors open was the best-performing configuration and only the
bottom injector open was the worst-performing one. The second best was all injectors
open and the third was both bottom and top injectors open. This might be because when
only the bottom injector was open, there was very less damping in the horizontal direction
because of the lack of gas pressure. When side injectors were also open, then there was
enough damping for the shaft and the friction was greatly reduced. It was found that when
the top injector was open, it provided extra load to the shaft, and the friction was slightly
increased. However, opening top injector along with the bottom injector helped as the air
flow was increased.
The damping for the aerodynamic lubrication is seen to decrease when air injection pres-
sure was increased for all the bearing and injection configurations.

8
Conclusion
An active gas foil bearing was successfully tested in the laboratory using a test rig con-
sisting of a shaft attached with a disc supported by AGFB and a ball bearing. A few
adjustments were done to the AGFB such as the alignment of its foils. Various tests were
performed to determine its static and dynamic characteristics.
The radial clearance of the AGFB was measured using static force transducer setup and
the effect of adding different shims on the clearance was also determined. It was found
that adding thicker shims added stiffness to the foils and so adding multiple shims of
smaller thickness such as 0.04mm was found to be more reliable. With the addition of
shims the radial clearance of the AGFB was reduced from around 192.18 µm to 57.7 µm.
The locus of the shaft was also measured and the effect of increasing gas injection pres-
sures on the position of the shaft was also tested. It was seen that when the gas injection
pressure was increased the load-carrying capacity of the shaft increased and the shaft
position was lifted towards positive vertical direction. However, the effect of increasing
speed was very low which can be because the speed was lower. The speed of the shaft
couldn’t be increased to higher than 1000 RPM because of the lower natural frequency
which had a risk of damaging the AGFB permanently.
The FRF of the rotor bearing system was generated through a series of extensive EMA
experimental tests. Many different configurations were used and different combinations of
shims were added for the tests. The natural frequency of the system generally decreased
with an increase in air injection pressures and also with the addition of shims. The natural
frequency of the rotor bearing system ranged from 50-60 Hz in the vertical direction and
from 22 to 40 Hz in the horizontal direction. The magnitude of the FRF plot seemed to
decrease with the addition of shims.
In the vertical direction, the resonant frequencies decreased when air injection pressure
was increased and in the horizontal direction, the resonant frequencies increased when
air injection pressure was increased.
Also, when shims were added, the overall resonant frequency of the system was found
to be reduced in the vertical direction and increased in the horizontal direction.
The damping was decreased when air injection pressure was increased for the differ-
ent configurations. This is quite a significant result and is different from other types of
bearings.

9
Recommendation and Future Works
If this test rig is to be sued in the future or any similar experiments are to be conducted,
there are a few recommendations suggested as given below:
9.1
Using shaft with increased diameter:
The AGFB without shims should have radial clearance of around 35 µm. The radial clear-
ance of the existing shaft and AGFB is measured to be around 192 µm without shims.
This is way too large and even though it was reduced with the addition of shims, it would
be better if the shaft had a slightly larger diameter or smaller radial clearance from the
start.
9.2
Improving the hits from impact hammer :
Hitting the shaft surface manually by hand doesn’t produce the same force every time
and thus the impact could be automated by mounting some hitting mechanism such as a
pendulum or motorized mechanism.
