Abstract
For some off design wind turbine loads and stability simulations the inflow over an airfoil
can be from angles in the region of 160-200°. Datasets exist for these angles, however,
reverse flow and the impact of add-ons like vortex generators or serrated trailing edge ex-
tensions (Dino-Tails) is not fully understood. The goal of this project is to investigate the
impact of aerodynamic add-ons on reverse flow over airfoils. With that aim, three different
airfoil geometries have been analysed in clean configuration and with add-ons in the wind
tunnel for reverse flow conditions. The data captured has been post-processed using the
current methodology of wall correction factors as explained in [1] with slight modifications.
Then these wind tunnel results have been compared against its theoretical performance
in free flow using Computetational Fluid Dynamics techniques (CFD), to assess the va-
lidity of the wall effect correction factors.
The results of this study show the presence of a local reduction in lift for every airfoil at
clean configuration happening for a very sort range in angle of attack (1° to 3° depending
on the airfoil studied). That is originated by a sudden modification in the pressure field
around the leading edge caused by the jump of the stagnation point from one side of
the airfoil to another, generating a separation bubble on the pressure side of the trailing
edge. CFD simulations using a steady state solver and a k −ω SST turbulence model
were not capable of capturing this phenomena of lift reduction. This effect in Cl is also not
present in any airfoil case using add-ons due to changes in the pressure field introduced
by the increase in turbulence generated behind these devices. The introduction of vortex
generators in reverse flow proves to increase the maximum Cl achievable before stall in
reverse flow. Dino-Tails reduce the maximum Cl and drastically increase the drag on the
airfoil.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Aerodynamic airfoils are designed considering that the flow lines move around them start-
ing from the leading edge, travel around the suction and pressure side of the airfoil, and
meet at the trailing edge. If the trailing edge is sharp, the circulation generated is enough
to generate a stagnation point around it, making the flow lines meeting at the trailing edge
to fly away from the body without jumping from one side to the other (Kutta Condition).
This scenario is typically called forward flow, and the characteristic shape of almost ev-
ery subsonic airfoil with a blunt leading edge and a sharp trailing edge, trying to obtain
the best aerodynamic performance (maximum lift to drag ratio) and stall characteristics.
Looking around to airfoil databases like the NACA Family [2], one can see that this ge-
ometry philosophy monopolises the airfoil design for subsonic speeds. Reverse flow, for
instance, is the condition where the flow lines travel around the airfoil starting from the
trailing edge to the leading edge. This situation can happen just locally in the flow field
around the airfoil, like for example in a separation bubble. Or it can be extended to the
whole field around the airfoil, for example in the case of a wind turbine idling or parked
during a storm, where the flow can change direction in a very short period of time, way
faster than the yawing system of the turbine is capable of compensate; or in operation
with a yaw error. This is one of the load cases that the wind turbine manufactures must
include as a Design Load Case (DLC) required by the IEC-61400-1 Standard [3] under
the umbrella of extreme condition called Extreme Direction Change (EDC) and Extreme
Coherent Gust (ECG).
For this reasons, 360° airfoil polar data is required for loads calculations. Which is not
easy to obtain due to the high blockage at angles of attack around 90° and 270°, which
disables the assumptions of linearity of the wall correction factors [1]. On the other hand,
for angles of attack around 180°, the airfoil is inverted and the trailing edge acts as a
leading edge, theoretically having a similar aerodynamic behaviour to a flat plate. This
thesis focuses on the reverse flow range around 170° to 190°.
With the objective of understanding better the aerodynamic performance of wind turbine
blade airfoils in the regime of reverse flow, several reverse flow measurement have been
conducted by Siemens Gamesa Rotor Performance (ROP) in multiple wind tunnels (TU-
Delft, PLCT) between the AoA envelope of 160/200°. To get good quality polar results,
it is fundamental to confirm if the wall correction factors that are used in forward flow still
apply or, for instance, a re-calibration of this parameters is required. To be able to answer
this question, a CFD simulation has been performed in one of the airfoil models in clean
configuration (no add-on fitted), to study the ideal free-stream behaviour of the profile
under the same reverse flow regime, allowing to establish a correlation to compare the
validity of the tunnel correction parameters.
Once a reliable wall correction has been established, the next step in this study will be the
post processing of different add-ons (vortex generators and Dino-Tails [4]) under the same
reverse flow conditions, with the objective of understanding how these devices affect to
the boundary layer of the profile and the loads that the blade will experiment under these
circumstances.

1.2
Prior work
The study of airfoil aerodynamic performance in reverse flow has been an important topic
of analysis for the flying dynamics of rotor aircraft (i.e. helicopters, tilt-rotor aircraft), as
in forward flight, in the retreating blade is moving in the same direction as the inflow, and
if the forward speed of the vehicle is high enough, it will neutralize the tangential speed
of the most inboard sections of the rotor, having a scenario of reverse flow, as shown in
fig. 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Reverse flow region for different values of the advance ratio µ (ratio between
the forward-flight speed to rotor tip speed) of an helicopter rotor in forward motion.
For this reasons, the aerodynamics in reverse flow of sharp and blunt trailing edge airfoils
has been researched in depth since the 1940’s, in parallel with the development of rotor
aircraft [5]. Early studies of airfoils in reverse were generated by the National Advisory
Committee for Aeronautics (NACA) [6, 7].
For the study of the physical and mathematical assumptions behind the wall correction
factors used in the wind tunnels of this study are taken with only minor modifications from
the analysis of Allen and Vincenti [1].
1.3
Research Question / Hypothesis / Thesis / Problem
statement
In the scope of this thesis there are three research goals that the experiments designed
will try to answer with respect to the realm of the research of airfoils and add-ons in reverse
flow:
• Are the wall interference correction factors universal for all possible the angles of
attack? Should these factors change for reverse flow?
• Which of the different configurations of airfoil with add-ons (vortex generators (VG),
2
==PAGE:15==
Dino-Tails (DT)), generates the maximum variability of Cl before stalling during re-
verse flow.
• What is the best CFD set up to simulate reverse flow robustly? Can we simulate real-
istically the cross-sectional phenomenons of laminar-turbulent transition, boundary
layer detachment, vortex shedding, etc; experienced at the wind tunnel at reverse
flow conditions for a case of a clean airfoil?
1.4
Research goals
• Goal 1: Determinate what are the correct values of the wall interference correction
factors that should be implemented in the post-processing algorithm to accurately
estimate the lift, drag and moment coefficients.
• Goal 2: Comprehend how the presence of different add-ons (vortex generators,
Dino-Tails) affects the aerodynamic performance on different wind turbine blade
master airfoils. This analysis will be qualitative, as a visual interpretation of the
near flow field that surrounds the airfoil; and quantitative, with the calculation of the
corresponding lift and drag polars, having clear values of stall angles of attack.

Methodology
2.1
Calculation methodology to determine the aerodynamic
coefficients by wind tunnel measurements
As data from two wind tunnels (TU-Delft and PLCT) has been analysed during this project,
it is necessary to describe what is the post-processing criteria used on each wind tunnel
to translate the pressure gradient information captured by the several channels normally
available:
• Surface pressure ports.
• Wake rake.
• Wall pressure ports.
• Force balance.
For the SGRE A3 tested at PLCT, the four mentioned types of data were used for the
analysis. SGRE A1 and A2 tested at TU-Delf Tunnel didn’t use the force balance for
measurements in reverse flow.
2.1.1
Surface pressure ports
Calculation of surface pressure coefficients Cp
The methodology for converting airfoil surface pressures to Cp is as follows. The undis-
turbed test section velocity is determined with the tunnel dynamic pressure calibration
curve, which establishes a relation between the dynamic pressure (qo) in the center of the
empty test section and the tunnel control pressure (∆pb). The tunnel control pressure is a
dynamic pressure formed by the difference between the settling chamber total pressure
and the static (wall) pressure at a station about halfway in the contraction, sufficiently
far from the model in the test section. The static pressure is an average of 4 pressures
measured at the circumference of the tunnel.
The measured pressures are reduced to pressure coefficients using eq. (2.1):
Cp = p −po
qo
(2.1)
Where the value of qo is determined from the dynamic pressure calibration curve. The
value of po (static pressure) is calculated using the eq. (2.2):
po = pto −qo
(2.2)
The total pressure pto is an average of all the wake rake total pressures outside the wake.
In the absence of a wake rake (or if the wake is too large for the rake), the total pressure
is derived from a pitot static tube located at the entrance of the test section.
5
==PAGE:18==
Once the surface pressure coefficient distribution is obtained, blocked or leaking tubes
are easily identified by varying the angle of attack. Blocked tubes do not show pressure
changes and leaking tubes lag behind. These are taken out of the integration and are not
presented.
No check is performed on the surface pressure coefficient distributions to ensure that a
value of a Cp = 1.0 is present in the leading edge vicinity. Not all pressure distributions
need to contain a Cp = 1.0 point. The stagnation point can lie between two orifices. If in
rare cases with a lot of unsteadiness the Cp exceeds 1.0, then it will be set to Cp = 1.0.

line is carried out to arrive at Cn, Ct, and Cm, and then Cl and Cd are derived from Cn and

removed, the trailing edge value is obtained as follows. The last two (available) pressures
of the suction side are extrapolated to x/c = 1.0. That extrapolated pressure value is then
used as the last pressure (at x/c = 1.0) for the suction side and for the pressure side.

2.3
CFD Simulation Set-Up
After the post-processing of the clean cases, the next step was to simulate how the real
aerodynamic performance of the airfoil will be in a free flow environment through the use
of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD). The CFD tool used to conduct the experiment
was a derivative of OpenFoam with and automatic mesher bespoked for the geometrical
particularities of wind turbine blades and airfoils. It was decided to conduct 2D steady
simulation using a steady state solver. The decision of using a 2D model and a steady
state solver and a k-Omega SST turbulence model in finding a middle point between
saving computational time and having a realistically enough approach to the physics that
is manifesting in reverse flow. After multiple trials to define a size of mesh suitable to
reach convergence, the following table defines the detailed set up of the simulation:

To better understand what is happening in the flow field around the airfoil that can make
us understand the reason behind the difference in lift coefficient behaviour between wind
tunnel and CFD for α = 178 −180° , the distribution of the pressure coefficient has been
represented for these angles of attack in fig. A.1. The Cp distribution represented with
a blue line is the data obtained from the surface pressure tabs of the model, corrected
from wall effects as explained in section 2.2. The red line represents the Cp for the CFD
simulations of the same geometry and angle of attack in free flow.
Looking at the evolution of the Cp distribution starting from 178°, it is possible to appreciate
that the Cp at the pressure side correlates really well between CFD and wind tunnel, with
a slight offset. The x/c position of boundary layer detachment at the pressure side (PS)
seems to be very similar in both cases. On the other hand, the suction side (SS) presents
a bigger offset of Cp all along the span of the airfoil, the flow accelerates more over the
SS of the CFD than the wind tunnel, having a later detachment of the boundary layer on
the SS, happening at x/c=0.06 at CFD, instead of x/c=0.1 at the wind tunnel. This good
correlation in the shape of the pressure distribution but an offset margin on the value
explains why the CFD and wind tunnel surface pressure Cl polar have the same slope in
the interval of angle of attack 176.5 to 178.5° (see fig. 3.3).
Progressing in angle of attack from 178° towards 180.5°. Concentrating our attention
into the area where the boundary layer detaches from the airfoil and forms the wake,
which can be identified because in that region the flow decelerates up to the point that
the Cp distribution flattens out, an indicative of separation. The point of boundary layer
detachment at the pressure side doesn’t vary significantly from 178° to 180°. However on
the suction side, moving from 178° towards 180.5°, the suction peak increases at the point
of maximum thickness (x/c
0.35), the boundary layer has therefore more kinetic energy
to continue attached to the surface, and the point of wake separation suffers a significant
recession towards the leading edge, to the point of almost reaching the leading edge for
α 180° figs. A.1i to A.1k. In those two figures one can observe as well that the suction
side Cp from x/c = 0−0.2 manifest certain wobbliness, this is an indication than unsteady
3D phenomena like vortex shedding is developing after the point of maximum thickness.
This translates from a wake perspective, looking from a coordinate system fixed to the
airfoil, that at 178° wake is exiting the LE bias towards the SS, it balances in both sides
between 179° and 179.5°, and after it is bias towards the PS.
To understand how this variation in the wake region affects to the distribution of lift along
the airfoil, the chord-wise normal force coefficient per unit of length was calculated and
represented in fig. A.1 by the use of the equation eq. (3.1), as described in [15], with the
exception that for reverse flow we consider that positive lift is when the lift vector points
towards the pressure side, inverting the sign of the original equation:
cn(x) = CpSS(x) −CpP S(x)
(3.1)
The SS and PS terms of the equation need to be at exactly the position in x for it to be
valid, but the pressure ports on both sides are not necessary aligned in x. Therefore to be
able to calculate this equation, the Cp measurements of the suction side where used, and
for those exact x positions, the Cp at the pressure side was obtained by linear interpolation
of the measured pressure field by the existing pressure ports.

3.4
Add-on effect to aerodynamic performance in reverse
flow
This section evaluates how the introduction of add-on devices affects the aerodynamic
performance of the studied airfoils in reverse flow conditions. Add-on devices are addi-
tional components that are glued to the blade surface to improve its aerodynamics and/or
aeroacustic performance. In this project the two types of add-on devices studied are:
• vortex generators SGRE Type VG1.
• Trailing edge serrated extensions SGRE Type DT4 (witch will be mentioned from
now onwards with their commercial name Dino-Tails [4] for simplicity purposes).
(a) SGRE Vortex Generators family
(b) SGRE Dino-Tails family
Figure 3.13: SGRE portfolio of VGs and DT that SGRE has available. fig. 3.13a Dino-Tails
from DT1 (top) to DT3 (bottom). fig. 3.13b Vortex generators from VG1 (bottom) to VG5
(top). Source: SGRE
Vortex generators are sets of small fins that can be installed in the pressure and/or suction
side of the blade with the purpose of inducing turbulence in the boundary layer, delaying its
detachment and therefore reducing the stall area for a given angle of attack, increasing the
trust and torque in the process. When vortex generators were originally introduced in the
wind turbine industry they were mainly concentrated near the root of the blade, where the
high relative thickness of the airfoils in this section made them prone to develop separation
bubbles. As the technique has progressed and the benefits of vortex generators has been
widely proven, one can find VGs installed almost though the whole blade span in some
of the latest blade designs.
Dino-Tails are serrated trailing edge extensions that are glued to the tip of the trailing
edge with the purpose of generating eddies that helps to make a more gently mixture of
the wake flow between the pressure and the suction sides. This reduces the noise level
of the rotating blade for a certain rotational speed, reducing the level of curtailment re-
quired to meet the noise regulation and, as a consequence, increasing the annual energy
production (AEP).

4
Conclusions
The analysis of the aerodynamic performance in reverse flow for three cambered airfoils
has consistently shown the presence of a dip in the lift polar around the angle of attack
range 180 ± 1° (fig. 3.7). This phenomena has been observed in all the different ways
available at the wind tunnel to measure the lift (surface pressure, wall pressure and force
balance). This depression in the lift coefficient is linked to a sudden increase in the normal
force coefficient Cn (fig. 3.10b), originated due to the appearance of a separation bubble
at the pressure side just after the trailing edge (fig. 4.2), as it can be seen in the pressure
coefficient distributions in appendix A.1. This separation bubble transitions the flow from
laminar to turbulent when the flow gets reattached. At the same time, for angles of attack
inferior to the point where the dip in lift starts, the stagnation point (U = 0m/s, Cp = 1)
of the trailing edge is located at the pressure side of the airfoil. And by the angle of
attack where the dip begins, the stagnation point jumps to the suction side of the airfoil
(this can be seen looking at the progression of the point Cp = 1 for the clean case in
appendix A.2). Now the flow traveling over the suction side suffers a higher acceleration
and the boundary layer detaches from the surface later than at the pressure side, this
generates a net suction peak at the leading edge that is the origin of the the dip in Cl at
this region. As it is possible to observe from fig. 4.1a, that represents how each section
of the surface of the airfoil contributes to Cn in an accumulative way, the big difference in
the Cn distribution is induced at the leading edge.

The lack of correlation of data between CFD and wind tunnel for a wide region of the
polars made it important to understand the reason for this deviation . That is the reason
that the intended study of the wall effect correction factors was not carried out in the way
that was intended to answer the first research question. However the good correlation in
lift between CFD at free flow and corrected wall pressure in the angle of attack range 176°
to 178.5°, gives a cautious indication that the correction factors defined by the application
of the image theory of infinite row of flow sources on both sides of the tunnel as described
in section 2.2 are still a good approach in reverse flow. At least for angles of attack that
generate the less amount of tunnel blockage.
Assessing the add-on impact to the aerodynamic performance of airfoils in reverse flow
with the objective of answering the second research question, it has been observed that
the vortex generators are effective to increase the maximum lift coefficient achieved at
high angles of attack prior to stall. However the VGs don’t help to delay the angle of
attack where the stall begins to manifest. It is important to note as well that the dip in Cl
manifested in the clean case of each airfoil is no longer observed in the cases with VGs.
On the other hand, the case with Dino-Tail doesn’t present either the local dip in Cl. The
physical mechanism that generates the dip in lift is an imbalance on the location of the
separation point of the boundary layer between the pressure a suction side, generating a
suction peak at the SS close to the leading edge that is making the lift to decrease. The
insertion of VGs and/or DT modifies the detachment point of the boundary layer, making
that suction peak to disappear. The larger amount of turbulence induced by the passing
of the flow over the serrations makes the readings of the airfoil surface pressure quite
inaccurate for most angles of attack on the cases with DT. The presence of the DT makes
the detachment of the boundary layer to happen sooner than in the clear case, making
the airfoil stall faster at lower angles of attack and, as a consequence, generate lower Cl
values than the clear case. For the cases with VG and DT together, the wake behind the
DT washes the VG at the suction side for most angles of attack, making it ineffective. Only
at very high angles of attack, when the suction side is well exposed towards the incident
current, the VG has clean flow ahead of it to be able to have an impact, slightly increasing
the maximum Cl with respect to the case with DT only.

Abstract: For some off design wind turbine loads and stability simulations the inflow over an airfoil
can be from angles in the region of 160-200°. Datasets exist for these angles, however, reverse flow
and the impact of add-ons like vortex generators or serrated trailing edge extensions (DinoTails®)
is not fully understood. The goal of this project is to investigate the impact of aerodynamic add-ons
on reverse flow over airfoils. With that aim, three different airfoil geometries have been analysed
in clean configuration and with add-ons in the wind tunnel in reverse flow conditions. The data
captured has been post-processed using the current methodology of wall correction factors. Then
these wind tunnel results have been compared against its theoretical performance in free flow using
Computational Fluid Dynamics techniques (CFD), to assess the validity of the wall effect correction
factors.
