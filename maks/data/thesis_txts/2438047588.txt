Abstract 
 
Recently paracetamol, a drug which is otherwise considered safe, was found to affect the endocrine system. 
This is an example of why it is important to investigate the unintentional endocrine disrupting potential of 
drugs, just like other kinds of safety parameters are investigated. 
In this project, the effect of eleven drugs on the steroidogenesis was investigated in the H295R assay based 
on a human adrenocortical cell line. Dose-response data were analyzed statistically and were together with 
information of Cmax at the therapeutic dose used to determine so-called ‘pseudo’ safety margin of the drugs. 
The safety margins were compared to safety margins calculated from other toxicity endpoints and were used 
to determine an overall risk score of the drugs based on endocrine disruption, antiandrogenicity, and liver 
toxicity. Furthermore, the effects of spironolactone, ketoconazole, and dexamethasone on gene expression of 
relevant enzymes and genes in the steroidogenic pathway were investigated. 
The results showed that all compounds except paracetamol and ibuprofen were able to interfere with 
steroidogenesis. However, only flutamide, enzalutamide, and ketoconazole would affect steroidogenesis at 
therapeutic concentrations. Spironolactone, ketoconazole, and dexamethasone were all able to affect the 
steroidogenic gene expression, but apparently not at therapeutic concentrations. Based on the overall risk 
score, enzalutamide was evaluated to be of highest risk according to tested endpoints, whereas paracetamol 
and ibuprofen were the least. However, the reason that paracetamol was not revealed as liver toxic is due to 
the lack of metabolic capacity of the H295R assay. Future studies in this field should analyze the effects of 
the drug metabolites as well.  
The conclusion is that many of the tested drugs showed unintentional endocrine activity, some of which may 
explain some of the known side effects. This stresses the importance of screening new drug candidates for 
these toxicities, as has recently been recommended by the FDA.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1 Hormones 
Hormones constitute a large group of compounds, which mediate various signals throughout the body by 
interacting with receptors on or inside target cells. These signals are important for many functions of the 
body, such as development and growth as well as regulation of metabolism and reproductive functions [1]. 
Depending on the distance the signal has to travel to reach the intended target, there are different terms of the 
signaling, as can be seen in Figure 1. Intracrine and autocrine signaling is when the cell itself responds to its 
own signals, paracrine signaling is signaling to cells in the same tissue as the excreting cell and endocrine 
signaling is signaling for cells in other tissues [1]. Most of the endocrine signaling is conducted by certain 
glands responsible for the production and excretion of different hormones, which will be described in later. 
Hormones can be classified into three main groups based on structure. These groups are: (I) peptide/protein 
hormones such as insulin, (II) steroid hormones such as cortisol, and (III) hormones derived from amino 
acids or fatty acids, such as melatonin and thyroxine, respectively [1]. This report is focusing on the steroid 
hormones.

signaling. This figure is modified from Nussey and Whitehead 2001 [1].

1.1.1 Endocrine glands 
As previously stated, hormones are primarily produced and excreted by specific endocrine glands. These are: 
The pineal gland, hypothalamus, the pituitary gland, the thyroid gland, the parathyroid gland, pancreas, 
adrenal glands, ovaries, and testes [2]. An overview of the location of these glands can be seen in Figure 2. 
 
Figure 2: Location of the endocrine glands. This figure shows the location of different endocrine glands. This figure is modified 
from a figure by United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) [2]. 
 
The hypothalamus is controlling the general hormone balance by secreting hormones which stimulates the 
production of other hormones in the pituitary gland. Examples of this is the growth hormone-releasing 
hormone (GHRH), which stimulates the synthesis and excretion of growth hormones in the pituitary gland. 
This co-operation between the glands is important for the regulation of endocrine systems, which will be 
described later. Furthermore, the hypothalamus serves as a connection between the endocrine system and the 
nervous system, and it is therefore also able to secrete the neurotransmitter dopamine for signaling through 
the nervous system [1,2]. 
 
The pituitary gland is made of two lobes named the anterior and posterior lobes. The anterior lobe is 
synthesizing, and secreting hormones based on signaling from the hypothalamus, as illustrated in the 
example of growth hormones. The posterior lobe is storing and releasing hormones, which have been 
synthesized by the hypothalamus [2]. 
 
The pineal gland is secreting melatonin, which is used to modulate sleep patterns in circadian and seasonal 
cycles. The production of melatonin is stimulated by darkness and inhibited by light. Furthermore, melatonin 
appears to affect the bones, the immune system and blood pressure regulation [3,4,5]. 
 
 
==PAGE:8==
Page 7 of 70 
 
 
The thyroid gland secretes non-steroidal hormones, which are important for the development and maturation 
of individuals, as well as for the regulation of metabolism. Another gland important to metabolism is the 
pancreas, which secrete glucagon and insulin in order to regulate the blood sugar concentration [2]. 
 
The adrenal gland is also consisting of two parts named the adrenal medulla and the adrenal cortex. The 
medulla is secreting the non-steroidal hormones epinephrine and norepinephrine, also known as adrenalin 
and noradrenalin, both of which are secreted as a stress response. The adrenal cortex is composed to three 
regions, each responsible for synthesizing different hormones. The zona glomerulosa (ZG) synthesized 
mineralocorticoids, zona fasciculata (ZF) synthesizes glucocorticoids, and zona reticularis (ZR) secretes 
adrenal androgens [1,2,6]. Each zone is also preferentially regulated by different factors being angiotensin II 
and potassium for ZG as well as adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) for the ZF and ZR. ZR is expected to 
be regulated by other factors as well, but they are yet to be determined [6]. 
 
The gonads are known to secrete a range of steroid hormones used for reproductive maturation and 
maintenance of the reproductive functions. Examples of these are estrogen, progesterone, and estradiol 
secreted by the ovaries as well as the androgens testosterone, Dihydrotestosterone (DHT), and 
androstenedione secreted by Leydig cells in the testes [2,7].

1.1.2 Endocrine systems 
Endocrine systems are systems in which different endocrine glands must co-operate in order to regulate the 
more complex functions in the body. Specific endocrine systems are often called “axis”, for example the 
hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, in which the hypothalamus, the pituitary gland and adrenal 
glands are co-operating to e.g. provide a rapid response to stress [8]. The HPA axis is activated when a 
stressor is sensed by the hypothalamus, making it secrete corticotrophin releasing hormone (CRH). The CRH 
stimulates the pituitary gland to release adrenocorticotropin (ACTH), which makes the adrenal gland secrete 
dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA) and cortisol. Glucocorticoid receptors of the hypothalamus and pituitary 
senses the cortisol concentration, and once a certain concentration is reached, the release of CRH and ACTH 
are suppressed [9]. Other important endocrine systems are the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis, 
which regulate growth and metabolism [10] and the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis, which 
regulate reproductive functions [11]. The regulation of these systems is similar as can be seen in the 
schematic overview in Figure 3. Cross-talk between the systems can occur [9] but is not shown in the figure.

pituitary gland, and the gonads. This figure originates from Nussey and Whitehead 2001 [1].

1.1.3 Endocrine Disruption 
The official definition of endocrine disruptors from World Health Organization is: “An endocrine disruptor 
is an exogenous substance or mixture that alters function(s) of the endocrine system and consequently causes 
adverse health effects in an intact organism, or its progeny, or (sub)populations” [12]. 
As previously stated, the endocrine system is important for e.g. growth/development, metabolism and 
reproductive functions. Therefore, endocrine disruption e.g. caused by endocrine disrupting compounds 
(EDCs) is known to cause various types of adverse effects [13]. An example of this is the EDC diethylhexyl 
phthalate (DEHP), which has been observed to decrease testosterone synthesis in the male rat fetus, resulting 
in an increased frequency of genital malformations [14]. 
The structure and mechanism of action of EDCs are highly diverse, making the definition by US 
Environmental Protection Agency rather broad: “An endocrine-disrupting compound is an agent that 
interferes with the synthesis, secretion, transport, binding, or elimination of natural hormones in the body 
that are responsible for the maintenance of homeostasis, reproduction, development, and/or behavior” [13]. 
The lack of similarity between the EDCs also makes it difficult to classify them, resulting in different 
classification systems. One classification is based on whether the EDC is naturally occurring in e.g. food, 
such as phytoestrogen or if the EDC is synthesized for use in/as a plasticizer, plastics, pesticides, fungicides 
etc. In another classification, the EDCs are grouped in four different groups based on their origin: (I) Natural 
and artificial hormones e.g. for use in contraceptive pills and thyroid medicines. (II) Drugs with hormonal 
side effects. (III) Industrial and household chemicals and lastly (IV) Side products of industrial and 
household processes [13].

1.2 Steroid hormones and their synthesis 
Steroid hormones are a group of hormones with similar structure, due to them being derived from 
cholesterol. In Figure 4 examples of steroid hormones can be seen. 
 
 
Figure 4: The structure of various steroid hormones. This figure shows the principal metabolic pathways for the conversion of 
cholesterol to steroid hormones, intermediates, and metabolites, as well as the structure of the compounds. This figure originates 
from Granick and Kappas 1967 [15]. 
 
There are five major groups of steroid hormones, namely estrogens, progestogens, androgens, corticosteroid 
and mineralocorticoids [16]. Steroid hormones are primarily synthesized in the adrenal cortex, Leydig cells, 
ovarian granulosa, theca cells and placental cells [17]. 
The steroidogenesis is regulated by steroidogenic enzymes, most commonly cytochrome P450s or 
hydroxysteroid dehydrogenases (HSDs) [18].   
Cytochrome P450 is a group of enzymes in which the members are target-specific oxidases. The genes 
encoding the cytochrome P450 enzymes have been termed CYP genes. The cytochrome P450 enzymes are 
known to be important in many conversion processes e.g. degradation of drugs in the liver or synthesizing 
steroid hormones. Examples of the later can be seen in Figure 5 [18]. 
Another important enzyme group is the HSDs. In contrast to the cytochrome P450 enzymes, they are not as 
ligand specific, nor are they able to function on their own. The HSDs require the presence of nicotine 
adenine dinucleotide (NADH/NAD1) or nicotine adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH/NADP1) as co-
activator. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:12==
Page 11 of 70 
 
The reactions involved in the conversion of cholesterol to steroid hormones have been termed 
steroidogenesis. Steroidogenic cells are able to synthesize the required cholesterol from acetate, but the 
majority of the cholesterol is derived from plasma lipoprotein, either low-density lipoproteins (LDL) or high-
density lipoproteins (HDL) [17,19,20]. HDL or LDL is taken up by receptor mediated endocytosis and, once 
inside the endosome, the cholesterol is cleaved from the lipoprotein. The cholesterol for steroidogenesis will 
then be transported to the outer mitochondrial membrane via one of three mechanisms. Firstly, the transport 
may be mediated by the endoplasmic reticulum, secondly it may be mediated by cholesterol-binding 
transport proteins, or thirdly the lipid droplet may associate with the outer mitochondrial membrane via 
SNARE proteins. Once the cholesterol reaches the outer mitochondrial membrane, certain proteins appears 
to interact with outer mitochondrial membrane proteins to trigger the rapid movement of cholesterol from the 
outer mitochondrial membrane to the inner mitochondrial membrane, though the mechanism is still unclear. 
These proteins are steroidogenic acute regulatory protein (StAR) and the peripheral benzodiazepine receptor 
(Bzrp)[18,19,21]. Once inside, the cholesterol will be converted into steroid hormones. The first step is 
always that cyp11A1 turns cholesterol into pregnenolone, but after this step, the reactions and enzymes used 
are different, as can be seen in Figure 5 [18]. The conversion of cholesterol into pregnenolone is often 
considered the enzymatic rate-limiting step, whereas the transport of cholesterol to the inner mitochondrial 
membrane is often considered the “true” rate-limiting step [21]. 
Several endocrine disrupting compounds have been found to affect the activity of some of the cyp-enzymes 
including the aromatase (cyp19), which catalyzes the conversion testosterone to estradiol. 
 
 
Figure 5: The major steroidogenic pathways. This figure shows the major steroidogenic pathways in humans. Key enzymes are 
written in bold, and the hormones are written in italic. CYP17A1 activity is indicated as a17α-hydroxylase activity and b17,20-lyase 
activity. The conversion from testosterone to dihydrotestosterone mediated by 5α-reductases is missing from the figure, even though 
it is an important reaction. The figure originates from Oskarsson et al 2016 [22].

1.2.1 Disruption of androgens 
Androgens are hormones required for maintenance of male reproductive function as well as male 
development during fetal life and puberty. Disruption of androgens results in more severe consequences at 
developmental stages in life. In animal studies, the adult male may be asymptomatic or demonstrate 
reversible symptoms when exposed to androgen disruption compounds. However, the exposure of EDCs 
may cause delayed puberty in boys or testicular dysgenesis syndrome (TDS) in male fetuses [23,24].  
The male fetus is believed to be particularly sensitive towards androgen disruption during the 
masculinization programming window, expected to be around week 8-12 of gestation in humans [25]. 
As can be seen in Figure 6, male development is completely hormone dependent, and therefore more 
sensitive towards endocrine disruption. The disruption of androgens can occur in different ways, as shown in 
Figure 7, e.g. by interfering with the androgen receptor (AR), or by affecting the synthesis or metabolism of 
androgens. 
 
 
 
Figure 6: The effect of endocrine disruption on the sexual undifferentiated fetus. This figure show that male development is 
completely hormone depended, whereas female development is mostly not hormone dependent. Due to male development being 
hormone dependent, it is sensitive towards endocrine disruption. This figure originates from Vinggaard 2018 [26].

Testicular dysgenesis syndrome 
Testicular dysgenesis syndrome (TDS) is a group of male reproductive health disorders, believed to originate 
from fetal life. Examples of disorders are genital malformations such as hypospadias and cryptorchidism, but 
also disorders emerging later in life such as low semen quality and testicular cancer [23].  
Different factors, either alone or in combination, are believed to cause TDS, e.g. genetic factors or 
environmental exposure towards EDCs. Different factors causing testicular dysgenesis syndrome and the 
consequences of testicular dysgenesis syndrome is shown in Figure 8. 
Depending on the specific factor, the fetal Sertoli cells and/or fetal Leydig cells are affected. Disturbed 
Sertoli cell function may impair germ cell differentiation, though the consequences of that are emerging later 
in life as lower semen quality and higher risk of a certain form of testicular cancer called germ cell neoplasia 
in situ (GCNIS) [28]. Disruption of fetal Leydig cells leads to a reduction in insulin-like 3 (INSL3) and 
testosterone production. Both hormones are required for masculinization of the of the external and internal 
male genitalia and testis descend, meaning that the disruption of Leydig cells may lead to genital 
malformations [28,29]. The reduced level of testosterone, especially during the masculinization 
programming window, may also lead to a reduced anogenital distance (AGD) [30]. 
Furthermore, fetal androgen exposure appears to pre-determine testosterone levels in men, meaning that low 
fetal testosterone production may result in low testosterone production as an adult [31].

1.3 The hormones investigated in the experiment 
 
Pregnenolone 
Pregnenolone is derived from the first conversion of cholesterol and is therefore a precursor to the steroid 
hormones [18]. It is also a neurosteroid, which can be converted to other compounds with neural functions.  
Because it is a precursor for several compounds, it is difficult to differentiate between the action 
pregnenolone and its metabolites. It is believed that pregnenolone is able to enhance learning and memory as 
well maintaining immune homeostasis. The mode of action for pregnenolone is still unknown, as they appear 
to not bind to nuclear receptors, nor regulate gene expression directly [32]. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:16==
Page 15 of 70 
 
Progesterone and 17α-hydroxyprogesterone 
Progesterone is one of the female sex hormones and is therefore important in the regulation of normal female 
reproductive functions. Progesterone is mediating much of its action via the progesterone receptor. 
Progesterone is e.g. important for the release of mature oocytes and suppression of milk protein synthesis 
before parturition [33]. It is primarily synthesized in the ovary and adrenal gland, but it is also produced by 
glial cells in the nervous system, meaning that progesterone is a neurosteroid. There, it has been found to 
affect different neurotransmitter receptors. It has been shown to inhibit the neural nicotinic acethylcholine 
receptor, as well as activating hypothalamic oxytocin receptors [34,35]. Studies have also shown that 
progesterone synthesized by Schwann cells (the myelinating cells in the peripheral nervous system) plays an 
important role in the myelination of the peripheral nerves. The myelination of the nerve axons is required for 
the rapid conduction of nerve impulses [36]. Progesterone also functions as a precursor of aldosterone, 
cortisol, and hydroxyprogesterone [18]. 
Hydroxyprogesterone is a derivate of progesterone. There are different types of hydroxyprogesterone, 
depending on the location of the hydroxy-group. Examples of this are 3α-hydroxyprogesterone and 11β-
hydroxyprogesterone. The one used in these experiments is 17α-hydroxyprogesterone. 17α-hydroxy-
progesterone is a precursor of cortisol [18] but is also functioning as a very weak progesterone receptor 
agonist [37].  
 
Cortisol, Corticosterone, and hydroxycortisol 
Cortisol, corticosterone, and hydroxycortisol are glucocorticoids secreted by the adrenal cortex. In humans, 
corticosterone is the weaker glucocorticoid, and is primarily used as a precursor to aldosterone [18]. 
Glucocorticoids are known to affect many kinds of tissues and organs. In regard to metabolism, they are 
known to stimulate the release of amino acids from the muscles. The released amino acids are converted into 
glucose, which stimulates the release of insulin. The glucocorticoid also inhibits the insulin-stimulated 
uptake of glucose in the muscle. Furthermore, it shows minor lipolytic effects, but it is overshadowed by the 
lipogenic action of the insulin secreted in response to the increase in glucose concentration [38]. 
Glucocorticoids are also known to affect the bones in different ways. Firstly, it is able to directly inhibit bone 
formation [39] and secondly, it is able to decrease gut calcium absorption, as well as decrease calcium 
reabsorption in the kidneys [40]. They are also known to affect the cardiovascular system, central nervous 
system, the kidney, and maturation of the fetus [38].  
 
 
 
==PAGE:17==
Page 16 of 70 
 
Dehydroepiandrosterone 
Dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA) is also known as androstenolone, and is secreted by the brain glial cells, 
the adrenal cortex, the gonads, and the gastrointestinal tract [41,42]. DHEA both function as a precursor to 
androgenic and estrogenic steroids, but also function as a hormone by itself. It is known to function as a 
weak androgen, meaning that it has a lower affinity towards the AR than testosterone. DHEA is also able to 
activate the estrogen receptors ERα and ERβ, though the concentrations of DHEA required to do so is higher 
than the physiological concentration of DHEA [43,44]. Furthermore, DHEA is able to activate the 
peroxisome proliferators-activated receptors and the pregnane X receptor [41,43]. DHEA is also known to 
affect the central nervous system (CNS), and is therefore considered a neurosteroid [41].  
 
Androstenedione 
Androstenedione is secreted by the adrenal cortex in men and women, as well as the testes in men. It is 
synthesized from DHEA. Like DHEA, it is a weak androgen, and may be converted into testosterone and 
estrone. However, the amount of testosterone produced from androstenedione is physiologically insignificant 
in men, as they are secreting androgens from the testes [38,45]. 
 
Testosterone and dihydrotestosterone 
Testosterone and dihydrotestosterone (DHT) are both androgens, meaning that they are important for the 
male development of the fetus, important for male sexual maturation, and maintenance of the male reproduc-
tive functions. Both are AR agonists, though DHT is more potent than testosterone [7].  
Testosterone is primarily being synthesized in the testes. DHT may be synthesized in the testes as well, but 
the majority of DHT is produced by the peripheral conversion of testosterone by the prostate [46]. DHT is a 
functioning as a paracrine hormone, affecting the tissue in which the conversion from testosterone to DHT 
takes place [47]. 
 
Estrogens 
Estrogens are important female sex hormones. There are different estrogens, but the ones used in this thesis 
are estrone and 17β-estradiol (estradiol for short). In premenopausal women, estradiol is synthesized in the 
ovary by conversion of testosterone [18,48]. Estradiol can reversibly be converted into estrone, a less potent 
estrogen. Estrone can also derive from the conversion of androstenedione in adipose tissue [18].  
The mayor physiological functions of estrogens are regulation of the menstrual cycle as well as breast 
development in puberty but are also known to play a role in the regulation of bone mass [48,49]. 
 
Epitestosterone 
Epitestosterone (17α-hydroxy-4-androsten-3-one) is the 17α-epimer of testosterone. It has been shown to 
function as an antiandrogen in hamsters, as well as an inhibitor of 5α-reductase in rats [50,51], but to the 
knowledge of the author of this thesis, no studies have determined if this is also the case for humans. 
Epitestosterone is secreted primarily from the gonads with only small contributions from the adrenal gland 
[52]. A study has shown that epitestosterone is possibly synthesized from DHEA [53]. Even though the 
article was published in 2005, the author of this thesis was unable to find more recent research regarding the 
synthesis of epitestosterone.

1.4 Drugs and the endocrine system 
Some drugs have been developed with the purpose of interacting with the endocrine system as a direct 
mechanism. Other drugs are interacting with the endocrine system as a side effect. Paracetamol and 
ibuprofen are examples of drugs, which only recently have been found to unintentionally disrupt various 
hormones both in-vitro and in-vivo [22,54-57]. One reason for the late discovery of the “endocrine disrupting 
potential” of paracetamol and ibuprofen could be insufficient toxicity testing of drugs. There is currently an 
increase in focus of endocrine toxicity, but the International Council for Harmonization (ICH) has yet to 
make a guideline describing a standard battery for endocrine toxicity testing [58]. Currently, ICH has only 
made guidelines for testing if the drug candidate is toxic to the liver, the central nervous system, the 
cardiovascular system, the respiratory system, the immune system, the reproductive functions or if the 
compound is carcinogenic [59]. However, the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has recently made a 
guideline for testing of endocrine toxicity, but these tests are not mandatory requirements [60]. 
 
Another reason to focus on improving the testing of endocrine toxicity is the purely economic aspect. Many 
drugs are failing in clinical trials due to safety reasons, and the company developing the drug would save a 
lot of money, if the toxicity was detected in the preclinical studies. 
 
1.4.1 Drugs used in the experiment  
Additional information about the drugs can be seen in Table 1 in “Materials and methods” and Appendix A. 
 
Bicalutamide, enzalutamide, and flutamide 
Bicalutamide, enzalutamide, and flutamide are drugs used to treat prostate cancer. They are known anti-
androgens, which are able to inhibit signaling through the (AR) by binding to it. By inhibiting the AR, the 
cells exhibit a reduced response to testosterone or dihydrotestosterone (DHT) signaling, which is otherwise 
required for the growth of prostate cancer [61]. These drugs are known to have some adverse effects on the 
hormone balance, due to the unresponsiveness towards testosterone and DHT. These adverse effects are e.g. 
gynecomastia (breast development) and impotence [62]. 
Bicalutamide and flutamide are first generation AR antagonists. They are known to function as partial 
agonists at high concentrations, meaning that they are able to activate the AR at high doses, which is not 
desirable. The second generation of AR antagonist, such as enzalutamide, have been designed so that they 
have no partial agonistic effects [63,64]. 
 
Cyproterone acetate 
Cyproterone acetate is also used to treat prostate cancer but it is used to treat other androgen related 
conditions such as acne and hirsutism as well [65]. It is also known to block the AR [65], but studies indicate 
that the antiandrogenic effect may be caused by other mechanisms too. In a study lower plasma levels of 
testosterone, as well as a higher metabolic clearance of the testosterone was observed in hirsute patients 
treated with cyproterone acetate. This led the researchers to conclude that cyproterone acetate induce the 
degradation of testosterone in the liver as well as decrease the production of testosterone by an 
antigonadotropic effect [66]. Furthermore, a decrease in 5α-reductase activity was also observed after 
cyproterone treatment [66].

Spironolactone  
Spironolactone and its active metabolite, canrenone, are aldosterone antagonists and are therefore used to 
treat conditions caused by hyperaldosterone such as hypertension and edema [67].  The mode of action for 
spironolactone is, that it reduces the number of sodium channels in kidneys, thereby inhibiting excess re-
uptake of sodium as well as loss of potassium from urine. In this way, the effects of excessive aldosterone 
levels are negated [68]. 
Furthermore, studies have shown that spironolactone may be anti-androgenic [69].  
Spironolactone shows a range of adverse effects. Tiredness, gynecomastia, impotence and disturbance of the 
menstruation cycle are considered very common adverse effects (occurring in >10% of the patients). A 
common adverse effect (occurring in 1%-10% of patients) is disturbances in the electrolyte balance such as 
hyponatremia (low sodium level in the blood) and hyperkalemia (elevated levels of potassium in the blood) 
[68]. 
The effect of spironolactone on various hormone levels has been tested. In a study they measured the plasma 
levels of testosterone, LH, FSH, and estradiol and found no significant differences between individuals who 
did or didn’t receive spironolactone. They also measured the levels of DHEA, androsterone, LH, FSH, and 
estrogen in urine, and here they observed lower levels of androsterone, and estrogen [70]. In another study, 
spironolactone appeared to reduce the transcription of StAR proteins, a key protein for transporting 
cholesterol into the mitochondria, and the rate-limiting step of steroidogenesis [71]. 
 
Finasteride 
Finasteride is a drug used to treat benign prostatic hyperplasia [72] and male pattern hair loss [73], both of 
which are conditions caused by androgens. Finasteride is inhibiting 5α-reductase, which converts 
testosterone into DHT, a more potent androgen. To date, three iso-enzymes of 5α-reductase have been 
identified, namely SRD5A1, SRD5A2, and SRD5A3. Some differences between these enzymes have been 
found. E.g. SRD5A1 shows a broad pH optimum, which range between 6.0-8.5, whereas SRD5A2 has a 
narrow pH optimum ranging from 5.0-5.5. SRD5A3 appear to have a pH optimum around 6.5-6.9. 
Furthermore, SRD5A1 has a higher turnover, but lower substrate affinity than SRD5A2 [74].  
Finasteride is known to inhibit SRD5A2, but there are conflicting data on whether finasteride also inhibits 
SRD5A3 [75]. Due to the inhibition of DHT production, there are endocrine-related adverse effects such as 
impotence and gynecomastia [76]. 
The effect of finasteride on steroidogenesis has previously been investigated. In a study by Rittmaster et al 
1994, they measured the plasma levels of various steroid hormones before and during prolonged finasteride 
administration. The tested steroid hormones were: 17OH-pregnenolone, 17OH-progesterone, 
deoxycorticosterone, corticosterone, aldosterone, cortisol, dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA), 
androstenedione, testosterone, and dihydrotestosterone (DHT). They found no evidence that finasteride 
inhibits adrenal steroidogenesis other than the inhibition of the conversion of testosterone into DHT [77]. 
Finasteride has also been found to reduce the anogenital distance in male rats exposed in utero [78].

Page 19 of 70 
 
Ketoconazole and itraconazole  
Ketoconazole and itraconazole are anti-fungal drugs, used to treat various types of infections, e.g. infections 
in nails, the skin, and in mucosal lining. They are used against a range of different fungi, both yeast and 
filamentous, e.g. Candida spp, Malassezia spp and Aspergillus spp [79-81]. Both are known to inhibit the 
synthesis of ergosterole, an important component for the integrity of the fungal cell wall [82]. The primary 
target is a specific cytochrome P450 enzyme, which catalyzes the 14α-demethylation of lanosterol (an 
ergosterole precursor) [82,83].  
Ketoconazole and itraconazole are also known to affect the steroidogenesis in humans by inhibiting other 
CYP enzymes according to in-vitro studies. Studies have shown that ketoconazole reduced the level of 
testosterone and estradiol while increasing the level of progesterone in vitro [84,85]. Furthermore, 
ketoconazole has been observed to inhibit CYP17A1, which is required for adrenal and testicular androgen 
synthesis [86]. It has also been observed that ketoconazole inhibits the transcription of CYP11B2 
(aldosterone synthase) and 3βHSD2 (3b-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase) genes [71]. The prior gene is 
encoding the enzyme synthesizing aldosterone from 18OH-corticosterone. The latter gene is encoding an 
enzyme responsible for the conversion of pregnenolone to progesterone, 17OH-pregnolone to 17α-
hydroxyprogesterone, DHEA to androstenedione, and androstenediol to testosterone [18]. In the same study, 
ketoconazole was found to increase the transcription of CYP19, CYP21, and 17βHSD1 [71]. 
Furthermore, ketoconazole has been observed to decrease the levels of insulin-like 3, as well as anti-
Müllerian hormone. The latter is secreted by Sertoli cells to induce the regression of Müllerian ducts, thereby 
preventing female development of the male fetus [87].  
Ketoconazole also appear to reduce the anogenital distance, which is a biomarker of prenatal androgen 
exposure [85]. 
Both ketoconazole and itraconazole are able to affect liver function, and in rare cases cause liver damage. 
Furthermore, ketoconazole is able to cause adrenal insufficiency in 1%-10% of patients [79,80]. 
 
 
Dexamethasone 
Dexamethasone is a glucocorticoid and is therefore an agonist of the glucocorticoid receptor. This means that 
dexamethasone is used to e.g. treat some cases of congenital adrenal hyperplasia, a group of disorders which 
is characterized by excessive or deficient production of mineralocorticoids, glucocorticoids, or sex steroids 
[88]. Furthermore, dexamethasone has an anti-inflammatory effect, making it useful for immunosuppressive 
therapy. Examples of this are inflammatory rheumatic diseases (e.g. rheumatic arthritis), allergic diseases 
(e.g. eczema). Dexamethasone also has an antiemetic effect, allowing the treatment of nausea induced by 
chemotherapy, though the mechanism for this is unknown [89]. 
Common adverse effects (1%-10% of the patients) are adrenal insufficiency, osteoporosis, and inhibition of 
growth (in children). Hypertension and disturbances in electrolyte balances (e.g. hyperkalemia) are 
uncommon adverse effects (0.1%-1% of patients) [90].  
Fetal exposure to dexamethasone in spiny mice has been shown to affect the secretion of DHEA and 
testosterone in male offspring [91].

Paracetamol and ibuprofen 
Paracetamol and ibuprofen are drugs with antipyretic and analgesic effect caused by inhibition of 
prostaglandin synthesis. 
Ibuprofen is known to inhibit the cyclooxygenases COX-1 and COX-2, which are important enzymes in the 
two pathways to synthesize prostaglandins. The COX-1 is believed to constitutively produce prostaglandins 
in order to secure a baseline level, whereas the COX-2 system is believed to produce prostaglandins through 
stimulation. Paracetamol is believed to primarily block COX-2 in the central nervous system, but not much 
in the rest of the body. Therefore, paracetamol has limited anti-inflammatory activity, and is not considered a 
nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID), like ibuprofen [92,93]. Paracetamol rarely shows any adverse 
effects, but may lead to allergic reactions in 0.01% - 0.1% of patients [94]. Ibuprofen is known to show many 
adverse effects, both in the types of adverse effects, but also the frequency of them. For example, tiredness, 
stomach pain, diarrhea and gastro-intestinal bleedings are very common (>10% of patients) [95].   
Several epidemiological studies have linked the maternal use of paracetamol during pregnancy with 
cryptorchidism [57,96,97]. In two of these studies, the maternal use of ibuprofen was investigated as well, 
but contain conflicting data on whether ibuprofen may induce cryptorchidism [57,96]. However, a recent 
study has associated the use of ibuprofen in men with “compensated hypogonadism”, a condition in which 
augmented LH levels compensate for compromised function of Leydig cells [56]. 
An ex-vivo study of the human fetal testis indicate that paracetamol appears to decrease levels INSL3 [87], 
though another study indicates otherwise using rat fetal testis [98]. 
Another ex-vivo study showed that ibuprofen suppress both testosterone and INSL3, but only during week 8-
9 of gestation [99]. That study also showed that ibuprofen suppressed anti-Müllerian hormone production, as 
well as reduced the expression of the cyp17A1, cyp11A1 and HSD17B3 genes. cyp11A1 catalyzes the 
conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone. Cyp17A1 catalyzes different reaction leading to the generation of 
glucocorticoids, estrogens, and androgens. HSD17B3 catalyzes the conversion of androstenedione to 
testosterone. 
There are conflicting data of whether paracetamol decreases the levels of testosterone in the human fetal 
testis. At least two studies indicate that paracetamol decreases the levels of testosterone in the fetal testis 
[54,98], and at least one demonstrates the testosterone level isn’t decreased [87]. The differences may be 
caused by the fact that they have used different models and exposed the cells to paracetamol over different 
time spans. 
It has also been reported that paracetamol increased the secretion of progesterone and aldosterone, as well as 
decreased secretion of 17α-OH-progesterone, cortisol, DHEA, and androstenedione in H295R assays [22]. 
Lastly, exposure to paracetamol in gestation week 8-14 has been linked to shorter anogenital distance in male 
infants [100].

2.2 H295R Steroidogenesis assay 
Cell line used in the H295R steroidogenesis assay 
For the H295R assay, the cell line H295R-S1 (also known as ATCC CRL-2128) was used. This strain has 
 been developed to be able to grow more rapidly and form better monolayer attachment than the original 
NCI-H295 cell line. The original cell line is derived from a patient diagnosed with an adrenocortical 
carcinoma and is able to produce a variety of steroid hormones [6].  
 
Cell culturing 
H295R cells were cultured in a medium consisting of DMEM/F12 supplemented with 2.5% Nu-Serum™ and 
1% ITS+ premix additive at 37°C with a humified atmosphere of 5% CO2/air.  
At about 90% confluency, cells were passaged by adding 0.05% Trypsin-EDTA to the culture flask after it 
has been washed with PBS. The cells were trypsinated at 37°C for 10-15 minutes, after which the 
trypsination was terminated by adding a volume of the previous mentioned medium corresponding to 3 times 
the volume of trypsin added. 
 
Experimental set-up 
After trypsination (described above), the cell solution was diluted in the above mentioned medium so that the 
concentration was 300,000 cells/ml. One ml cell solution was added to each well in a 24-well plate, which 
then incubated at 37°C in a humified atmosphere of 5% CO2/air over night. The medium was replaced with 
1ml new medium. For the test plates, triplicates of different concentrations of the tested drug would also be 
added, ranging from 0.8μM-50μM. For the QC plates, different concentrations of forskolin and prochloraz 
were added. The plates incubated for 48h at 37°C with a humified atmosphere of 5% CO2/air, before the 
supernatant was transferred to new 24-well plates and stored at -80°C until quantification of the hormones. 
For the gene expression assay, 500μL of PBS was then added to each well in the cell containing plates in 
order to avoid drying the cells out when preparing the gene expression assay. 
The drugs were not tested for cell toxicity in these experiments, but this has been performed in earlier studies 
[101]. 
 
Compounds used for QC in the H295R steroidogenesis assay  
Forskolin and prochloraz were chosen for the QC in the H295R assay, because the prior is a known inducer 
of testosterone and estradiol production, whereas the latter is known to inhibit the production of those 
hormones. These are the controls that are recommended in the OECD test guideline.

2.3 Quantification of steroid hormones 
The QC plates were analyzed using an immunoassay, whereas the test plates were analyzed using LC-
MS/MS. The immunoassays were conducted by Birgitte Møller Plesning, and the LC-MS/MS was conducted 
by Maud Bering Andersen and Mikael Pedersen. 
 
The QC plates were analyzed using a testosterone EIA kit and estradiol EIA kit from Cayman Chemical’s 
ACEtm EIA Kits. The experimental procedure was then performed according to the protocol supplied by the 
manufacturer of the kits. 
 
According to Mikael Pedersen, the LC-MS/MS was conducted the following way: 
Internal standards (testosterone-d2, methyltestosterone-d3, progesterone-c2, and estradiol-d3) were added to 
the cell extracts and the samples were then centrifugated at 15.000G for 10 minutes. 
The steroid hormones were separated, detected, and quantified using on-line-SPE LC-MS/MS. 
For on-line SPE an Oasis HLB column (2.1×20mm, 15µm) was used.  
For estradiol and estrone a Kinetex C18 column (2.1×100 mm, 2.6µm) was used as analytical column with 
an injection volume of 100μL in ESI- mode with methanol and 1mM ammonia in water as the mobile 
phases. Flow rate was 0.4mL/min and gradient method was used. 
For the other hormones an Ascentis Express C8 column (2.1×100 mm, 2.7µm) was used as analytical 
column with an injection volume of 100μL in ESI-/ES+ mode with acetonitrile and 0.1% formic acid in 
water as the mobile phases. Flow rate was 0.25mL/min and gradient method was used.  
The MS was an EVOQ Elite Triple Quadropole Instrument from Bruker and the UPLC system was an 
Ultimate 3000 system with a DGP-3600RS dual-gradient pump. 
For quantification, external calibration standards were run before and after the samples at levels of 0.1, 0.2, 
0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0 and 20 ng/mL (with 5.0 ng/mL internal standards).  
The limit of quantification (LOQ) can be seen in Table 2.  
 
The synthetic medium was tested for steroid hormones concentrations, though no hormones were expected in 
the media itself, due to it being synthetic. However, cortisol, progesterone, testosterone, and estradiol have 
been detected in the medium, and results regarding these hormones may be affected. In Table 2, the levels of 
background hormone in the media is shown.

2.4 Gene expression assay 
Ketoconazole, spironolactone and dexamethasone were chosen for the gene expression analysis. The cell 
culturing procedure prior to the gene expressions analysis were almost identical to the H295R assay 
previously described. The difference was that fewer concentrations of the drugs were tested in the gene 
expression assay. The concentrations used were 50μM, 12.5μM and 3.1μM, instead of seven different 
concentrations. 
 
The H295R assay was conducted as previously described. The PBS media was discarded and 350μl of RLT 
buffer + β-mecaptoethanol solution (10μl β-mecaptoethanol for every 1ml RLT buffer) was added. The cells 
were loosened from wells using a cell scraper and the supernatant with cells were then transferred to 1.5ml 
Eppendorf tubes. These tubes were stored at -80°C until the RNA isolation. 
 
RNA Isolation 
The RNA isolation was performed using a RNeasy micro kit from Qiagen according to product protocol, 
with one exception. In step 13 page 22 in the user manual, 16μl RNase-free water was added instead of 14μl 
which was specified.  
 
cDNA synthesis 
The cDNA synthesis as then performed using an Omniscript® Reverse Transcription Kit from Qiagen as 
described in the user manual. The samples were then stored at -20°C until qRT-PCR.

Quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR) 
The cDNA was diluted by adding 7.5μl of the cDNA and 142.5μl MiliQ water to each well in a Fast 
Reaction 96 well plate from ABI- LifeTech.  
The cDNA was analyzed in a 384 Reaction Plate from ABI- LifeTech using TaqMan® Gene Expression 
Assays listed in Table 3. In each well TaqMan® Gene Expression Assay (0.5 μL), TaqMan® Gene 
Expression Master Mix (5.0 μL), nuclease free water (2.5 μL) and diluted cDNA template (3.0 μL) were 
added. The qPCR was performed using QuantStudio 7 flex and QuantStudio Real-Time PCR software. The 
thermal cycling program was initiated with 95 °C for 20 seconds followed by 45 cycles of 95 °C for 1 second 
and 60 °C for 20 seconds. 
All genes were analyzed on a single plate in duplicates. 
Β-actin, 18S, and HMBS/PBGD are house keeping genes and were used for the normalization of the Ct 
values the following way. The geometric mean value for the two replicates of all house keeping genes in 
each sample was subtracted from the average Ct value for each tested gene in each sample. The power dCt 
was then found as 2-normalized Ct for each sample and used in the statistical analysis and the figures.  
 
2.5 Data and Statistical analysis 
The statistical significance was determined by a one-way ANOVA followed by a Dunnett’s post-test using 
the software GraphPrism 5. P-values below 0.05 were marked *, p-values below 0.01 were marked ** and p-
values below 0.001 were marked ***. Estimation of EC50 and IC50 were also made in GraphPrism 5. This 
was done by log-transforming the data, selecting the curve-fit “log(antagonist) vs response – variable slope”. 
Curve-fits with R2 below 0.6 were disregarded. Based on the estimated EC50 or IC50 value, a safety margin 
was calculated. The safety margin was calculated as: Estimated IC50 or EC50 value / Cmax,unbound 
The calculated safety margin of each drug was compared to other safety margins calculated in different 
ways. All the safety margins were then used to create a ToxPi chart using the free software available from 
http://toxpi.org/. All safety margins were scaled using 1/x. This is to convert the numbers, because the higher 
number in the calculated safety the safe it is, whereas the higher number in the ToxPi chart, the more toxic 
the compounds is.

3. Results 
3.1 H295R assay data 
The results from the H295R assay is shown in Figure 9-19, page 27-37. For ketoconazole, spironolactone, 
and dexamethasone, the results from the gene expression analysis are indicated as well. Data regarding 
epitestosterone and hydroxycortisol can be seen in Appendix B.  
DHT levels were always below quantification limit. 
 
Bicalutamide decreased pregnenolone, progesterone, 17-OH-progesterone, corticosterone, estrone, and 
estradiol. The androgens appeared unaffected. 
Enzalutamide decreased progesterone, 17-OH-progesterone, corticosterone, cortisol, epitestosterone, 
androstenedione, and testosterone. DHEA was increased, estrogens were not affected, and hydroxycortisol 
was not measurable. 
Flutamide increased the levels of corticosterone, and possibly progesterone, whereas it decreased the levels 
of estrone, and estradiol. 17-OH-progesterone, androstenedione, epitestosterone, and testosterone levels were 
slightly decreased. 
Cyproterone acetate increased the levels of pregnenolone, DHEA, and possibly 17-OH-progesterone. 
Corticosterone, cortisol, androstenedione, estrone, and testosterone levels were decreased. Hydroxycortisol 
appeared increased at certain test concentrations of cyproterone acetate. 
Spironolactone increased the levels of pregnenolone, and possibly progesterone. Corticosterone, cortisol, 
hydroxycortisol, 17-OH-progesterone, androstenedione, epitestosterone, and testosterone were decreased. 
DHEA appeared to increase at low concentrations of spironolactone and decrease at higher concentration. 
Estrone appeared to be slightly decreased, but it was not statistically significant.  
Finasteride appeared to increase pregnenolone, DHEA, and 17-OH-progesterone. Estrone and epitestosterone 
levels were decreased, and cortisol appeared slightly decreased as well, though it is not statistically 
significant. 
Ketoconazole decreased the levels of all hormones except progesterone and estradiol. The progesterone level 
appeared to increase as the ketoconazole concentration increased up to a certain point, at which the 
progesterone level decreased again. 
Itraconazole decreased the levels of all hormones, except the estrogens. Hydroxycortisol was not measurable. 
Paracetamol slightly increased progesterone, and possibly pregnenolone and 17-OH-pregesterone. 
Ibuprofen appeared to increase pregnenolone slightly, but otherwise had no effect on the hormone levels.  
Dexamethasone increased the levels of pregnenolone, progesterone, and corticosterone. 17-OH-progesterone, 
hydroxycortisol, DHEA, androstenedione, testosterone, epitestosterone, and estrone was decreased. Cortisol 
appeared slightly reduced and estradiol also appeared slightly reduced, though it was not statistically 
significant.

3.2 Gene expression 
It was investigated whether ketoconazole, dexamethasone or spironolactone induces changes in the gene 
expression of selected steroidogenic enzymes. 
Spironolactone decreases the transcription of cyp11A1, cyp17A1, cyp21A2, and HSD17β at 50 µM, as can 
be seen in Figure 20, page 39. Ketoconazole increases the transcription of cyp21A2 at 50 µM, whereas it 
decreases the transcription of cyp11A1, cyp17A1 and SULT2A1 at 12.5µM, and cyp19A1, HSD17β, and 
HSD3B2 at 50 µM, as can be seen in Figure 21, page 40. 
Dexamethasone increases the transcription of cyp21A1 and cyp11A1 at 3.1 µM, as can be seen in Figure 22, 
page 41. However, the increase in transcription of cyp11A1 is not statistically significant at the highest 
concentration, is most likely a chance finding. 
The gene expression data and data from the H295R assay has been combined in Figure 13, 15, and 19 (page 
31, 33, and 37, respectively).

the report. From the four-parameter logistic curve fit, estimated values of EC50 and IC50 were found and can 
be seen in Table 4 and 5. When more IC50 /EC50 values were available for one compound, the lowest value 
was used for the calculation of the “pseudo” safety margin. Cmax at the therapeutic dose of the drug was used 
as the exposure measure. These toxicity and exposure values were used to calculate a “pseudo” safety 
margin, which can be seen in Table 6. It was calculated the following way:

4. Discussion 
 
As mentioned earlier, the medium appears to contain small amounts of certain hormones. The detected 
amounts can be seen in Table 2, page 23. The presence of hormones in the media will add to the hormones 
created by the cells and will therefore have an impact on the exact concentration of the hormones, as well as 
to the overall “effect pattern” of the drugs. Written in math: Cmeasured =Cbackground +Ccell_produced 
A hypothetical example of how the pattern is affected is the following: 
The concentration of testosterone present in the media is at most 100ng/ml according to Table 2. If 
testosterone concentration is measured to be 150ng/ml at no exposure to “drug A”, then only 50ng/ml would 
be produced by the cells. When the cells then are exposed to e.g. 12.5μM “drug A”, the measured 
concentration may be 125ng/ml, meaning that the cells are only producing 25ng/ml. If the presence of 
testosterone in the raw medium is unknown, one would compare the 150ng/ml and 125ng/ml and see a 
reduction of about 17%. However, in reality, the production is reduced from 50ng/ml to 25ng/ml, a reduction 
of 50%.  
The point of this example is, that increases or decreases in the hormone levels might still be visible, but the 
magnitude of the effect may be affected. 
Since only the relative levels of the hormones are used, and not the exact values, the presence of hormones in 
the media may only affect the magnitude of changes in the hormone levels. 
The LC/MS-MS data received from Mikael Pedersen was data already extrapolated from the external 
calibration standard. It is therefore not possible to disregard the part of the cortisol, progesterone, 
testosterone, and estradiol concentration caused by the medium itself. Furthermore, the uncertainty in the 
exact value of hormone present in the medium (see Table 2, page 23) is too great to be able to use in 
calculations. 
 
Unpublished data from the research group indicate that none of the tested drugs show cyto-toxicity in the 
H295R cells [101]. Therefore, no cyto-toxicity experiments were performed in these experiments. 
 
Three house-keeping genes were chosen in order to provide more reliable normalization of the data.  
 
A problem with gene expression analysis is that it is not truly able to tell what is happening on the protein 
levels. E.g. a lowered gene expression may be due to the drug interfering directly with the transcription itself, 
or that the gene expression is changed to compensate for the changed protein expression of the drug.

Bicalutamide, enzalutamide, and flutamide 
Data referred to can be seen in Figure 9-11 (page 27-29), as well as Appendix B. 
 
It is interesting to see how different these antiandrogens are affecting the androgen levels. Flutamide 
decreased the levels of all the measured androgens, but only at a concentration of 50μM. Enzalutamide 
decreased androstenedione and testosterone at a concentration of 3.13μM, and increased DHEA already at a 
concentration of 0.78μM. The androgen levels appeared unaffected by bicalutamide. Based on these data, it 
is likely that there is another antiandrogenic mechanism of flutamide and enzalutamide in addition to the AR 
inhibition. 
 
Flutamide caused a decrease in estrogen levels, which could be explained by a decrease of androgens, 
because if the levels of androgens are decreased, the less estrogen can be synthesized. However, the 
reduction of estrogens occurred at lower flutamide concentrations than the reduction of androgens. The 
estrogens levels are also decreased, when cells are exposed to bicalutamide, without causing the androgens 
levels to drop. Enzalutamide decreases the levels of androgens without decreasing estrogen levels.  
 
For enzalutamide, the decrease in progesterone and 17-OH-progesterone levels may be correlated to the 
increase of DHEA. A reason could be that pregnenolone is increasingly turned into DHEA, instead of its 
other product (progesterone) especially considered that the pregnenolone level is stable, and not increased, 
even though the product (progesterone) is decreased.  
The decrease in the precursor (progesterone) would affect its products, specifically corticosterone and 17-
OH-progesterone, both of which are decreased as well. 
For enzalutamide, it would have been interesting to consider the levels of 17-OH-pregnenolone, but it was 
not measured. It would be interesting to see, if it was increasing like DHEA, because it would indicate that 
pregnenolone indeed was increasingly converted to 17-OH-progesterone and not progesterone. 
 
Flutamide appeared to increase the corticosterone level considerably, which is interesting because both 
bicalutamide and enzalutamide causes it to decrease. A rat study has observed higher levels of corticosterone 
when the rats were exposed to flutamide, likely because flutamide affected the HPA-axis [110]. However, 
corticosterone is the primary corticosteroid in rat, whereas cortisol is the primary in humans, so it is not 
necessarily comparable. According to the present H295R data, cortisol was unaffected when cells were 
exposed to flutamide, but was at least somewhat reduced when exposed to enzalutamide. 
The decrease in corticosteroids mediated by bicalutamide and enzalutamide may be caused by the decrease 
in progesterone and 17-OH-progesterone, because when levels of the precursors (progesterone and 17-OH-
progesterone) are reduced, less corticosteroids can be synthesized.  
 
50μM flutamide appeared to reduce the hormone levels also when it otherwise increased the hormone levels 
at lower concentrations. It is possible that 50μM flutamide is slight cytotoxic, but this was not tested for in 
this thesis, because earlier experiments had indicated that no cytotoxicity would occur for the tested drugs at 
the chosen concentrations [101].  
 
The therapeutic Cmax levels of bicalutamide, enzalutamide, and flutamide (2.8, 11 and 6 µM, respectively) is 
within the range of tested drug concentrations. At the therapeutic concentrations, flutamide only reduced 
estrone levels. Enzalutamide affected most of the hormones already at the therapeutic concentration. 
Bicalutamide showed no effect on the steroidogenesis at therapeutic concentrations.  
 
As enzalutamide is the most recently developed antiandrogenic drug for treatment of prostate cancer, it may 
on one hand benefit from its ability to lower testosterone as well (part from the AR antagonism), but on the 
other hand side effects caused by its effects on estrogens and corticosteroids can be expected.

Cyproterone acetate 
Data referred to can be seen in Figure 12 (page 30), as well as Appendix B. 
Cyproterone acetate increased the levels of pregnenolone and DHEA in the present H295R assay. The reason 
may be that the 3β-HSD enzyme is inhibited, as another study has indicated this. The authors suggested that 
it is an uncompetitive mode of inhibition [111]. This would explain the increase in pregnenolone and DHEA, 
as well as the decrease in androstenedione and testosterone. However, it should also cause progesterone and 
17-OH-progesterone to decrease or remain at low levels. Progesterone slightly decreases at lower 
concentrations of cyproterone acetate, but appears unaffected at higher concentrations. The 17-OH-
progesterone level doesn’t decrease, but increase at certain concentrations. If the 3β-HSD is uncompetitively 
inhibited, the inhibition is independent of the precursor concentration. This means that high levels of e.g. 
pregnenolone would not be able to “undo” the inhibition of progesterone and that progesterone levels should 
remain low. It is possible that the levels of progesterone and 17-OH-progesterone isn’t reduced, because their 
products are reduced. The reason is that if progesterone and 17-OH-progesterone synthesis are decreased, the 
synthesis of their products, corticosterone, cortisol, and androstenedione, may likewise decrease. If the 
reduction in synthesis of the precursors, progesterone and 17-OH-progesterone, is equal to the reduction in 
the synthesis of their products, then the levels of the substrates would appear constant. 
 
Cyproterone acetate is also known to decrease 5α-reductase activity, which means that the conversion of 
testosterone to DHT is inhibited [66]. This alone could have caused a slight increase of testosterone, but the 
lack of testosterone production from the lack of androstenedione may influence the testosterone level as well. 
The slight decrease in testosterone fits with a study, which observed decreased levels of testosterone in the 
blood after cyproterone acetate administration in humans [66]. Based on the results, the authors proposed 
that cyproterone acetate induces the degradation of testosterone as well as decreasing the production, though 
it cannot be seen in a H295R assay. 
The reduction of estrone is likely related to the reduction of androstenedione, because androstenedione is 
used to synthesize estrone. 
The decrease in cortisol may correlate to the increasing levels of hydroxycortisol, because hydroxycortisol is 
a product of cortisol. However, the decrease in cortisol levels could also be caused if less 17-OH-
progesterone was being synthesized as discussed above. 
 
The therapeutic concentration of cyproterone acetate (0.5 µM) is slightly lower than what has been tested in 
the present experiments (0.78 µM is the lowest tested concentration). It is therefore not possible to say 
whether therapeutic concentration of cyproterone acetate may reduce corticosterone levels. It will also 
depend on more complicated in vitro to in vivo extrapolations.

Spironolactone 
Data referred to can be seen in Figure 13 and 20 (page 31 and 39), as well as Appendix B. 
Pregnenolone levels was increased when the cells were exposed to spironolactone, whereas enzymes 
required to make pregnenolone were downregulated. Because the downregulation of StAR and cyp11A1 
occur at higher concentration of spironolactone, than the concentration needed to increase the pregnenolone 
concentration, it is likely that the downregulation is to compensate for the increasing levels of pregnenolone. 
The downregulation of StAR has been observed in another study [71]. Bzrp transcription appeared 
unaffected. 
 
Progesterone levels increased slightly at higher spironolactone concentrations, which may be due to the 
increasing levels of its precursor, pregnenolone. The reduction in 17-OH-progesterone, DHEA, and 
androstenedione correlates with the reduced transcription of cyp17A1, because the cyp17A1 enzymes are 
needed to synthesize these compounds. However, the spironolactone concentration to reduce the cyp17A1 
concentration is much higher than the concentration needed to affect 17-OH, DHEA, and androstenedione. 
Considering that the changes in StAR, cyp11A1, and cyp17A1 expression occur at higher concentration than 
changes in the hormones that they produce, it is likely that spironolactone may affect steroidogenesis at 
protein level, and the changes in transcription are compensatory responses. 
The transcription of 17β-HSD is decreased, but unlike the transcription of the other enzymes, the 
transcription of 17β-HSD is reduced, as is the levels of testosterone, the hormone it is synthesizing. At a 
glance it may appear as if no compensatory action takes place. However, considering that the decrease in 
testosterone may be caused by the decrease in its precursor, androstenedione, the concentration of 
testosterone appeared to decrease less than its precursor. It is therefore possible that spironolactone is 
causing testosterone concentration to increase slightly, though it doesn’t outweigh the reduction of 
testosterone production caused by the lack of precursor. It is therefore possible that 17β-HSD is 
downregulated as a compensation for the actions of spironolactone. 
 
In general, androgen levels were decreased. This fits well with the adverse effects seemingly being related to 
androgens [68]. However, a study observed no changes in plasma testosterone, when the test-person was 
administered spironolactone [70], whereas testosterone level is reduced in the current experiments. 
The estrogen levels appear unchanged despite a decrease in androgens. It could have been expected that if 
the levels of the precursors are decreased, so are the levels of the products. 
 
The changed progestogen levels may, at least partially, explain the disturbances in the menstruation cycle, 
which is another known adverse effect. 
 
The corticosteroid levels are decreased, which is corresponding to the decrease in cyp21A1 transcription.  
In general, the results from the present gene expression analysis correlates well with another study by Saito 
et at 2016 [112], in which H295R cells was used as well. The difference is that the present data showed that 
spironolactone had no effect on HSD3B2, whereas Saito et al 2016 showed that spironolactone decreased the 
transcription. 
 
It is uncertain whether spironolactone affects hormone levels at therapeutic levels because it is lower (0.1 
µM) than the lowest tested concentration (0.78 µM). Corticosterone, 17-OH-progesterone, cortisol, DHEA, 
and androstenedione are affected at 0.78μM, but it is unknown whether they are affected already at 
therapeutic concentration.

Finasteride 
Data referred to can be seen in Figure 14 (page 32), as well as Appendix B. 
According to this H295R assay, finasteride appeared to affect steroidogenesis. This is somewhat in contrast 
to another study examining the effect of finasteride on the plasma level of the hormones in humans [77]. 
It is possible that the increase in DHEA in the present experiments is correlating to the increase in 
pregnenolone, because it would be logical if the level of a product (DHEA) is increased if the precursor 
(pregnenolone) is increased. 
The levels of DHT were too low to be quantified, but are expected to be reduced due finasteride inhibiting 
the 5α-reductase enzymes required to convert testosterone into DHT. The DHT level is therefore (likely) the 
only tested androgen, which is reduced by finasteride. Considering that finasteride is able to shorten the 
anogenital distance in male rats [78], it is possible that the decrease in DHT is the reason, but it is not 
possible to determine based on the present experiments. 
Since finasteride inhibits 5α-reductase, it could be logical that testosterone levels would increase, because the 
conversion to DHT has been inhibited. This is not observed, nor an increase in the level of estradiol, which is 
the result of another conversion of testosterone. This is probably due to a very low expression of 5α-
reductase in the human adrenal. 
 
It is uncertain whether finasteride affect the hormone levels at therapeutic levels because Cmax is lower than 
the lowest tested concentration. However, all but one hormone is not affected at the lowest tested 
concentration of finasteride. Only 17-OH-progesterone is affected at 0.78μM, but it is unknown if there 
might be an effect at 0.1μM finasteride. Considering the curve for 17-OH-progesterone, it is most likely that 
the “back-ground” measure is too high, causing the lower levels of 17-OH-progesterone to appear 
significant. If this is the case, finasteride will not affect the steroidogenesis at therapeutic concentrations, 
except for DHT.

Ketoconazole and itraconazole 
Data referred to can be seen in Figure 15,16, and 21 (page 33,34, and 40), as well as Appendix B. 
Ketoconazole decreased the levels of all the tested hormones, except estradiol and progesterone, whereas 
itraconazole decreased all hormones except for estrone and estradiol. 
This is likely because ketoconazole and itraconazole are able to interfere with cyp-enzymes themselves due 
to their primary purpose is to inhibit a specific cyp-enzyme (cyp51). In the present gene expression analysis, 
ketoconazole affected all the tested cyp-enzymes. The results from this experiment differs from the one by 
Hilscherova et al 2004 [71]. We both see that ketoconazole reduces the transcription of 3β-HSD and 
increases the transcription of cyp21A1, but the results from cyp11A1, cyp17A1, cyp19A1, and HSD17β 
differ. Despite the upregulation of cyp21A1 in the present experiment, the levels of the corticosteroids were 
reduced. For cortisol it may be due to the lack of precursor (17-OH-progesterone), but the corticosterone 
level is reduced even though the level of its precursor (progesterone) is not. The reason may be that the 
transcription of cyp21A1 is increased to compensate for the decreasing levels of corticosteroids. Another 
possibility is that the cyp11β is affected, as indicated by Britton et al. 1988 and Hilscherova et al 2004 
[113,71]. However, the expression of this enzyme was not investigated in this project. 
In the ketoconazole experiment, the levels of estradiol appeared unaffected by the reduction of transcription 
of 17β-HSD and cyp19, as well as reduced level of the precursor testosterone. The reason may be that the 
transcription of SULT2A1 is reduced, meaning that there might be fever enzymes to convert the estradiol. 
SULT2A1 converts a broad range of steroids such as estrone, pregnenolone, DHEA, and testosterone [114]. 
 
Therapeutic concentration of ketoconazole would affect the majority of the tested hormones, because the 
hormones are affected at 0.78μM and the therapeutic concentration for ketoconazole is 7μM. The therapeutic 
concentration of itraconazole (0.4μM) is lower than the lowest tested concentration (0.78μM), so it is 
unknown whether it affects the steroidogenesis at therapeutic concentration.

Paracetamol and ibuprofen 
Data referred to can be seen in Figure 17 and 18 (page 35 and 36), as well as Appendix B. 
According to the present H295R data, high concentration of paracetamol may increase progesterone level, 
and high concentration of ibuprofen may increase pregnenolone level. Considering the deviations of the data 
set and that no dose-response effect or no logical pattern occurs, it is believed to be caused by chance. Other 
than that, no changes in hormone levels were detected. 
A study has reported that paracetamol increases progesterone levels, but also that paracetamol reduced 17-
OH-progesterone, cortisol, DHEA, and androstenedione which was not observed in this experiment [22]. 
One important difference in these experiments is that different concentrations of paracetamol has been used. 
In the present study, 0.78-50μM has been used, whereas Oskarsson et al 2016 [22] used 0.1-1mM. It is 
plausible that if the same concentrations of paracetamol were used, similar results would be observed.  
Likewise, a study by Kristensen et al 2018 [55] indicates that ibuprofen inhibits steroidogenesis in H295R 
cells, which is not observed in the present data. Once again, the main difference appears to be the 
concentrations of ibuprofen. Kristensen et al 2018 used 1μM-1mM of ibuprofen, whereas the present 
experiment used 0.78μM-50μM. When looking at the curve-fits of Kristensen et al 2018 in their figure 3, 
ibuprofen appeared to affect the steroidogenesis at concentrations higher than 100μM of ibuprofen, which is 
higher than what has been tested in the present experiment. Therefore, it is plausible that if the same 
concentrations of ibuprofen were used, similar results would be observed. 
 
Considering that Cmax of paracetamol is 130μM, the present study used too low concentrations of 
paracetamol to be able to indicate how therapeutic concentrations of paracetamol affect the steroidogenesis. 
It is plausible that an effect on the steroidogenesis would be seen at concentrations closer to the therapeutic 
concentration. Likewise, the therapeutic concentration of ibuprofen is considerably higher than what has 
been tested.  
Based solely on the present data, paracetamol induced cryptorchidism and shortening of the anogenital 
distance is not caused by direct disruption of the steroidogenesis. Likewise for the consequences of 
ibuprofen.

Dexamethasone 
Data referred to can be seen in Figure 19 and 22 (page 37 and 41), as well as Appendix B. 
Dexamethasone appears to increase the transcription of cyp11A1, an enzyme required to synthesize 
pregnenolone. The increase in cyp11A1 transcription may be correlated to the increase of pregnenolone 
levels. The increase in the transcription of cyp11A1 occur already at 3.1μM dexamethasone, whereas the 
level of pregnenolone is increasing at 6.25μM. it is therefore likely that the increase in pregnenolone is, at 
least partially, caused by an increase in the cyp11A1 transcription. 
The increase in progesterone may be caused by the increase in its precursor, pregnenolone. Transcription of 
cyp21A1 is increased which correlates with the increasing levels of corticosterone. The 17-OH-progesterone 
levels appear to decrease, but the reason for this may be that its precursor (progesterone) is increasingly 
converted to corticosterone instead of 17-OH-progesterone. The decrease in 17-OH-progesterone is likely the 
reason why the levels of its products (cortisol and hydroxycortisol) are decreased as well, despite an 
upregulation of cyp21A1, the enzyme mediating the conversion from 17-OH-progesterone to cortisol. 
Dexamethasone has been observed to decrease the concentration of cortisol in humans, so this correlate to 
what is observed in the H295R assay [89]. 
 
The reduction in androstenedione levels may be explained by the reduction of its precursors, DHEA and 17-
OH-progesterone. 
 
The present results are different from a similar experiment. The result from the other experiment indicated 
that dexamethasone decreases the transcription of StAR, cyp11A1, and HSD3B2 [115], whereas the present 
data indicates an increase in the cyp11A1 transcription and no changes in the StAR and HSD3B2 expression. 
An important difference between the experiments is that Koibuchi et al 2014 [115] used another cell line 
than the one used in the present report. Koibuchi et al 2014 used human glioma GI-1 cells and not H295R 
cells as in the present experiments. 
 
The therapeutic concentration of dexamethasone is lower than what has been tested. Most of the hormones 
are not affected at a concentration of 0.78μM dexamethasone, so they are likely not affected at the 
therapeutic concentration. Testosterone and androstenedione are affected at a concentration of 0.78μM 
dexamethasone, but it is unknown whether they are affected at therapeutic concentration (0.2 µM).

Lowest effect concentration for AR antagonism and activation 
Data from this research group indicate that cyproterone acetate and spironolactone show AR agonistic effect, 
whereas finasteride and ketoconazole, as well as the designed AR antagonists show AR antagonistic effect 
[101]. Though the data is not shown in this thesis, the data was used to calculate a pseudo safety margin and 
will therefore be described shortly. 
Cyproterone acetate was showing a very significant (p<0.001) agonistic effect at a concentration of 0.4μM, 
and spironolactone was showing a significant (p<0.01) effect at a concentration of 12.5μM. 
Finasteride was showing a very significant (p<0.001) antagonistic effect at a concentration of 0.4μM, and 
ketoconazole was showing a significant (p<0.05) antagonistic effect at a concentration of 0.4μM. The AR 
antagonistic drugs bicalutamide and enzalutamide showed a very significant (p<0.001) antagonistic effect at 
a concentration of 0.05μM, whereas flutamide showed a very significant effect at a concentration of 0.4μM 
[101].  
Comparing these values to the Cmax values seen in Table 6 (page 44), bicalutamide, enzalutamide, and 
flutamide will inhibit the AR at therapeutic concentrations, as expected. If cyproterone acetate function as an 
AR partial agonist at a concentration of 0.4μM, as observed by Frederiksen [101], then it will activate the 
AR at therapeutic concentrations. This is slightly unexpected because it is designed to be an anti-androgen. 
Furthermore, according to this data, ketoconazole will antagonize the AR at therapeutic concentrations too. 
The Cmax of finasteride is lower than the tested concentrations, so it is uncertain if it inhibits the AR at 
therapeutic concentrations.  
 
Lowest effect concentration for hepatoxicity 
Experiments regarding the hepatoxicity of the drugs have been performed in the group [101]. Though the 
data is not shown in this thesis, the data was used to calculate a pseudo safety margin and will therefore be 
described shortly. 
Bicalutamide, enzalutamide, flutamide, and cyproterone acetate showed significant effect after 72h of 
exposure at concentrations of 30μM. Keto- and itraconazole showed significant effect after 72h of exposure 
with a concentration of 9μM. The rest of the drugs showed no effect at a concentration of 100μM, the highest 
tested concentration. For all the anti-androgens, spironolactone, finasteride, and dexamethasone, the lowest 
effect concentration is higher than the Cmax, so limited hepatoxicity occur at therapeutic concentrations. For 
paracetamol and ibuprofen, the Cmax is considerably higher than what has been tested, meaning that it is 
untested whether hepatoxicity occur at therapeutic concentrations. However, paracetamol is known as a 
hepatoxic drug, because of its hepatoxic metabolite [104], which has not been tested in this assay. According 
to unpublished data from the group, the metabolic capacity of the H295R cells are limited, meaning that it is 
unlikely that paracetamol will be converted to its toxic metabolite in the assay. 
For ketoconazole and itraconazole, the Cmax and lowest effect level are close, so hepatoxicity may occur.

Safety margins and ToxPi 
Based on curve-fitting of the H295R data, estimated EC50 and IC50 values were found as seen in Table 4 and 
5 (page 42 and 43). These values were used to estimate safety margins as can be seen in Table 6 (page 44). It 
is important to keep in mind, that some of the estimated EC50/IC50 values are derived with a low R2-value.  
Furthermore, other safety margins based on other in vitro-assays have been indicated. The higher the safety 
margin number, the less a risk of adverse effects. The opposite is the case for the ToxPi score. The higher the 
ToxPi score, the higher the risk of adverse effects. 
 
In reality, it doesn’t make sense to use the term “safety margin” for AR effects for AR antagonists. The 
reason is that the “safety margin” in reality is a measure of efficacy, because the AR inhibition is the 
purpose, and not a side effect of the drugs. These safety margins were calculated anyway for the anti-
androgens based on AR inhibition and were as expected significantly lower than the safety margins based on 
steroid hormones inhibition and hepatotoxicity. Based on these calculated safety margins, the steroid 
hormone synthesis is more at risk than the liver, which fits somewhat with the reported adverse effects of the 
antiandrogens [62]. 
The calculated safety margin based on hormone inhibition for finasteride is very high. This means that 
according to the H295R assay, finasteride shows very little effect towards steroidogenesis. If DHT levels had 
been measurable in the H295R assay, it would likely have provided a lower IC50 -value than what has been 
used, thereby resulting in a lower safety margin. On the other hand, considering that the decrease in DHT is 
the primary effect of finasteride, an IC50 value of DHT may be closer to a measure of efficacy than risk of 
adverse effects. In this case, it makes more sense to use the second lowest IC50 value as a measurement of 
risk of adverse effects, when the lowest IC50 could be a measurement of the efficacy. 
 
Figure 23 showing the ToxPi, and Table 6 showing the safety margins may appear off at times (page 44 and 
45). For example, the hepatoxicity of flutamide appears to contribute a lot to the toxicity of flutamide despite 
the safety margin of hepatoxicity is the highest of the three calculations. The reason is that all values are 
relative to other values, even for other drugs. 
The ToxPi software also make a ToxPi score, which can be seen in Table 6. The ToxPi score is useful for 
comparing the risk of compounds but is not a score for the overall risk of the individual drug. Enzalutamide 
and ketoconazole received the highest ToxPi scores, meaning that they are the drugs with the highest risk of 
adverse effects of all the tested drugs. This correlates to a similar ToxPi score generated by Frederiksen, in 
which the safety margins of AR inhibition and hepatoxicity was used as well as chemical properties [101]. 
However, the use of enzalutamide may not be as risky as indicated by the ToxPi score. The reason is that the 
AR inhibition, is considered a risk-factor of enzalutamide in the ToxPi calculation, even though it is the 
primary effect of enzalutamide. If the AR inhibition was disregarded, the use of enzalutamide, and the other 
AR antiandrogens would likely appear safer.

5. Conclusion and future perspectives 
Ketoconazole, spironolactone, and dexamethasone appeared to induce changes in the transcription of 
steroidogenic enzymes, but will probably not do so at therapeutic concentrations. It remains unknown 
whether the change in transcription levels is caused by direct changes in the transcription or if the change is 
compensation for what the drug does at protein level. This can be determined by protein expression analysis, 
e.g. western blot. 
All the tested drugs except paracetamol and ibuprofen showed an effect on the steroidogenesis, though the 
effect was off-target for some of the drugs. However, flutamide, enzalutamide, and ketoconazole were the 
only drugs to affect steroidogenesis at therapeutic concentrations. Still, the potential for off-target activity for 
most the drugs were observable in the in-vitro assays and some could be correlated to known adverse effects. 
This correlates to the points of Bowes et al 2012 and Papoian et al 2015, who are stressing the importance of 
in-vitro pharmacological profiling [116,117]. According to both papers, it would be better if pharmaceutical 
companies perform more in-vitro tests to screen for potential adverse effects in order to discover adverse 
effects of a drug candidate earlier. Bowes et al 2012 are stating, that one obstacle in the use of in vitro-assays 
for pharmacological profiling is, that the official guidelines are mostly describing in-vivo studies. Papoian et 
al 2015 writes something similar, claiming that a major issue is the identification of specific targets, target 
categories, and biological functionalities to be used in a minimal in-vitro panel.  
It should be noted, that the medium of the H295R cells contained progesterone, cortisol, testosterone, and 
estradiol in concentrations shown in Table 2 (page 23). The presence of hormones in the media is expected to 
affect the results. It is expected to affect the magnitude of changes in hormone levels, but it is believed that it 
is still possible to see whether a drug increase or decrease the hormone levels. 
Safety margins based on three different in-vitro assays have been compared and used to evaluate the risk of 
side effects of the drugs. The different calculations for the same drug sometimes result in substantially 
different margins, so data from other types of in-vitro assays would be needed as well. The three calculated 
safety margins presented here were used to determine the ToxPi score of the drugs, in which enzalutamide 
scored the highest and paracetamol and ibuprofen scored the lowest. This means that according to the three 
calculated safety margins, enzalutamide might be the most toxic of the tested compounds, whereas 
paracetamol and ibuprofen are the least toxic compounds. It should be noted that only the parent compounds 
themselves have been investigated in this report. It is well-known that the toxicity or effect of a drug in 
reality may be caused by a metabolite of the drug and not necessarily the drug itself. This is for example the 
case with the hepatoxicity of paracetamol. Therefore, it is essential for future studies that metabolites are 
tested as well. 
Generally it was surprising to find that relatively many of these drugs showed endocrine activities and were 
either able to antagonize AR or to effect synthesis of sex steroid hormones or do both. This highlights the 
importance of screening new drug candidates for these toxicities as has been recommended by FDA [60].
