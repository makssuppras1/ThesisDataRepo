Abstract
The increasing penetration of renewable energy in energy systems has raised major con-
cerns in terms of intermittency, unpredictability and variability with the consequence
of raising pressure on the Transmission System Operator (TSO) to balance demand and
supply. Until now, TSOs have mainly relied on conventional thermal power plants to
ensure security of supply and grid stability.
For this purpose, the European-funded
project GRIDSOL [1] promotes Smart Renewable Hubs (SRHs) which provide flexible
power generation on selected critical locations, such as non-peninsular system. Based
on Concentrating Solar Power (CSP), GRIDSOL is able to combine different renew-
able and storage technologies. Such innovative solution finds purpose mainly in non-
interconnected energy systems, based on aged, fossil-fuel conventional technologies. In
this context, the island of Fuerteventura (Canary Islands) is selected as case study to
test GRIDSOL’s resilience and adaptability.
The present study examines first the integration of the GRIDSOL technology in the
energy system of Fuerteventura and Lanzarote using an energy system optimisation
model. Secondly, the analysis focuses on the private and socio-economic feasibility of
implementing GRIDSOL. In terms of energy system, the Balmorel model is chosen to
obtain the optimal configuration of GRIDSOL technologies in Fuerteventura. Together
with outputs from the optimisation model, such as electricity generation and investment
in new GRIDSOL capacity, the most relevant economic figures are further analysed via
a custom-made cash flow tool, designed for the specific case-study. In both private and
socio-economics, key indicators such as Net Present Value (NPV) and Internal Rate of
Return (IRR) are used to assess the feasibility of GRIDSOL in Fuerteventura.
Private and socio-economic evaluations have yielded negative NPVs and IRRs for each
combination of technology in GRIDSOL. Several sensitivities on subsidy schemes, such
as investment grants and feed-in premiums, have highlighted an higher profitability
potential for the CSP+PV and CSP+GT+PV combinations. From a carbon emission
perspective, the viability of GRIDSOL in Fuerteventura could pave the way towards a
decarbonised future for the island. By exploiting solar firm hybrid plants, GRIDSOL
is able to further reduce the risk of unpredictability and relieve the pressure from the
TSO, while preserving the uniqueness and fragility of the local environment.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In light of the 2020 package, each European country is subjected to binding legislations in
order to meet climate and energy targets established within the European Union (EU) [2].
Among the key goals, two relevant targets are set by the 2020 package: 20% reduction
in greenhouse gas emission (compared to 1990 levels) and 20% share of renewables in
EU’s energy consumption. Furthermore, according to the 2030 Energy Strategy, EU
members have agreed on a new climate and energy framework in order to reach a 40%
cut in greenhouse gas emissions and a 27% share of consumption from Renewable Energy
Sources (RES) [3]. These targets become particularly challenging when considering small
isolated systems such as islands. Most of European islands rely in fact on energy markets
with limited capacity, making it difficult to implement the necessary measures for the
aimed clean energy transition. In 2015, The Paris Agreement (COP21) has furthermore
highlighted the vulnerability of non-peninsular systems in relation to climate change,
being not only extremely reliant on fossil fuel generation but also dependent on energy
imports [4]. Nonetheless, it has been acknowledged that those same EU islands have the
potential in terms of renewable sources to become more sustainable.
As one of outermost European islands, Fuerteventura has emerged as interesting loca-
tion for the present study. Electrically connected to Lanzarote, Fuerteventura is highly
dependent on electricity generation from fossil fuel-based power plants. Together, the
two islands account for 95% power production from conventional thermal plants, with a
cumulative share of installed capacity of almost 90%. Despite the extensive use of fossil
fuels, Fuerteventura can count on good renewable energy sources, such as wind and solar.
On a power system perspective, the increasing penetration of renewable energy on the
island poses major concerns in terms of intermittency and unpredictability. Opposite to
conventional thermal power plants, whose dispatchable nature ensures security of sup-
ply and grid stability, a greater effort is required to integrate RES in the small energy
context of Fuerteventura.
In this context, the European-funded GRIDSOL project proposes Smart Renewable
Hubs (SRHs) designed to provide not only scheduled dispatch but also ancillary services
on tailored locations by means of a single steady output, combining different renewable
and storage technologies [1]. A first promising configuration of GRIDSOL SRH relies on
Concentrating Solar Power (CSP), which has emerged has a viable alternative thanks
to its dispatchable characteristics. For control purposes, a Dynamic Output Manager of

1.2
Aim and Methodology
Following the motivation, the present thesis aims to analyse the overall performance of
GRIDSOL technology on the island of Fuerteventura from both a technical and economic
perspective. On the one hand, the thesis examines the integration of GRIDSOL SRH
in the energy system of Fuerteventura and Lanzarote. On the other hand, the study
focuses on the private and socio-economic feasibility following a possible GRIDSOL
installation. In this regard, the main objective of the thesis is to answer the following
research question:
‘‘Is the GRIDSOL concept feasible on the island of Fuerteventura from a private and
socio-economic perspective?”
The thesis objective opens the investigation of the following aspects:
• How does GRIDSOL affect the current energy system of Fuerteventura and Lan-
zarote?
• To what extent national support policies are necessary to incentivise a GRIDSOL
investment?
• Which GRIDSOL components are the most economically beneficial?
The thesis is carried out as a feasibility study in order to assess the economic attractive-
ness of the project and provide a reasonable framework for informed decision making.
The present study will not only focus on the economical aspects but will also investigate
the technical, regulatory, and environmental feasibility; the financial feasibility has not
been considered.
To identify the optimal configuration, GRIDSOL technologies are modelled together with
the energy system of Fuerteventura and Lanzarote by means of Balmorel model1. The
modelling of GRIDSOL elements in Balmorel has been performed thanks to the contri-
bution of [5] by Alexandre Millet in collaboration with Ea Energy Analyses. The current
energy system is simulated in 2016, defined as the reference year, whereas the implemen-
tation of GRIDSOL is assessed in the short-term perspective of year 2022. Outputs of

Balmorel are expressed mainly in terms of annual electricity generation from each unit
(new and existing) and capacity investments of GRIDSOL. A secondary tool, System
Advisor Model (SAM) developed by NREL, is used to cross-validate GRIDSOL power
capacities found in Balmorel. For the validation, SAM evaluates the system design of
single GRIDSOL elements (namely PV and CSP) taking into account land restrictions.
Using the optimised results from Balmorel, a custom excel-based cash flow tool is devel-

results in Balmorel, the cash flow analysis focuses on four different combinations of
GRIDSOL components: CSP alone, CSP+GT, CSP+PV, and CSP+PV+BESS. The
economic feasibility of each configuration is assessed using Net Present Value (NPV), In-
ternal Rate of Return (IRR), and Levelised Cost of Electricity (LCOE) obtained in the
private and social evaluations. As for the private perspective, sensitivities are performed
on relevant parameters in order to highlight sensible changes in the cash flow model.
1.3
Report outline
After this brief introduction, the technical framework of GRIDSOL is presented in Chap-
ter 2 together with a cost estimation of each considered GRIDSOL technology.
Following GRIDSOL, Chapter 3 serves to contextualise the case study, namely Fuerteven-

CHAPTER 2
What is GRIDSOL?
The GRIDSOL project started in October 2016 from a collaboration of ten partners
from five different European countries: Spain, Denmark, Germany, Italy, and Greece.
The project, led by Cobra, includes, among others, the Technical University of Denmark
(DTU) and Ea Energy Analyses from Denmark. At the present moment, GRIDSOL is
funded by the European Commission under the ”Horizon 2020” framework programme.
For the project’s purpose, this European consortium aims to propose Smart Renewable
Hubs (SRH) in order to increase grid stability and relieve pressure from the Transmission
System Operator (TSO). In the last years, Variable Renewable Energies (VRE), i.e. PV
and wind, have been rapidly deployed thanks to its increased economic competitiveness
against mature conventional power plants. This rising penetration of VRE has led to
a new level of instability in the power grid, due to the non-dispatchable nature of such
technologies. As a consequence, VRE hard controllability requires counter-measures such
as synchronous generators, which have been traditionally fossil fuel-based. Thus, in order
to boost electricity generation from Renewable Energy Sources (RES), an additional
effort has to be made to guarantee the grid’s resilience and stability.
2.1
Technical framework
As per project definition [1], a Smart Renewable Hub is a power plant consisting of two
or more RES technologies, connected to the grid via a single output. The core concept
of the GRIDSOL project is that SRHs are able to incorporate both variable renewable
technologies and dispatchable synchronous generators in a single power plant. In addi-
tion to VRE and synchronous generators, Smart Renewable Hubs are integrated with
energy storage technologies, i.e. Thermal Energy Storage (TES) and Battery Energy
Storage System (BESS). As a result, the multi-hybridisation of SRH allows to provide
firm electricity generation, while maintaining a high penetration of RES.
Among the possible configurations of SRH, GRIDSOL has identified the following tech-
nologies’ portfolio:
1. Concentrating Solar Power (CSP) plant feeding a Steam Turbine (ST), i.e. the
synchronous generator, and linked to thermal energy storage (TES).
2. An additional synchronous generator, a Gas Turbine (GT), using natural gas,
equipped with a heat recovery system (HYSOL).

Concentrating Solar Power
In simple terms, a Concentrating Solar Power (CSP) plant converts solar energy into
heat through mirrors, which are able to concentrate the sun irradiation onto a receiver.
The receiver then collects and transfers this solar energy to a Heat Transfer Fluid (HTF),
allowing electricity generation via a steam turbine coupled to a synchronous generator
[8]. At the state-of-the-art developments, four CSP technology variants exist: Parabolic
Trough Collector (PTC), Linear Fresnel Reflector (LFR), Solar Power Tower (SPT) and
Parabolic Dish System (PDS), as displayed in Fig. 2.2.
Figure 2.2: CSP plant available variants [8]
All four configurations require Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI) to convert the solar
resource into heat and electricity, but differ in the mirror and receiver’s design as well
as in the heat transfer fluid used.
To give a proper definition of DNI, it is important to mention that sun rays are divided
into a direct and diffuse (indirect) component: DNI, the Direct Normal Irradiance, and
DHI, the Diffuse Horizontal Irrandiance. Together, they constitute the Global Horizontal
Irradiance (GHI):
GHI = DHI + DNI · cos(z)
(2.1)
being GHI the total sun irradiance on an horizontal surface, z the solar zenith angle [9].
Within the GRIDSOL project, two main CSP configurations have been analysed: parabolic
trough and single tower, where the PTC technology is used as benchmark being the
most commercially mature among the four configurations. However, recent tests arose
the need for a new approach and thus, a multi-tower configuration is currently under
study [6]. For this reason, the present study considers the single tower configurations
from both a technical and economical perspective.

The solar heat generates electricity by driving a thermodynamic cycle. The vast majority
of operating CSP plants uses in fact a steam turbine for the energy conversion. The rank-
ing cycle of the steam turbine can be summarised in four important steps: compressing,
boiling and superheating, expanding, and condensing. Following the first phase, where
feedwater is compressed to high pressure, the resulting steam is boiled and superheated
in a boiler in a secondary phase. The third phase is represented by the steam expansion
to lower pressure which then drives the electric generator’s shaft. At last, low pressure
steam is condensed and re-used in the cycle.

Gas Turbine
In the GRIDSOL project, two main GT technology are considered, namely an Open
Cycle Gas Turbine (OCGT) and a Combined Cycle Gas Turbine (CCGT). For the
purpose of this study, the gas turbine analysis has been focused solely on the Open
Cycle configuration, both in technical and economical terms.
The OCGT, also defined as simple-cycle gas turbine, consists of four main elements: a
compressor, a combustor, a turbine and a generator. From the compressor, compressed
air is directed into a combustion chamber (the combustor section), where fuel, typically
natural gas, is introduced and successively ignited and burned.
Coming out of the
combustion chamber, the heated gases are driven to the turbine and expanded. The
mechanical work produced in the expansion phase makes the shaft rotate, thus converting
rotational energy into electricity.
As mentioned in the beginning of Section 2.1, the gas turbine proposed in the GRIDSOL
project is coupled with a heat recovery system. Such system has been developed in a
previous project, HYSOL, which acted as a predecessor of the current GRIDSOL project.
As core of the HYSOL project, the heat recovery system allows to transfer heat from
exhaust gas produced by the turbine to the molten salts in the storage tanks. In order
to technically reproduce this feature, the OCGT includes a flue gas heat exchanger for
cogeneration, i.e. Combined Heat and Power (CHP) production. A sketch of a simple
cycle gas turbine designed for cogeneration is presented in Fig. 2.4.

Photovoltaic
In the last years, Solar Photovoltaic (PV) has been vastly deployed on a global scale,
thanks to its increasing technological maturity. Nonetheless, its declining costs have fur-
ther incited commercial attractiveness: only between 2010 and 2014, the total installed
costs of a PV plant have been decreasing by -58% in the most flourishing scenarios
[15]. Given its price advantage, this low cost RES plant has been included in the SRH
proposed configuration and its role has been investigated in this feasibility analysis.
In technical terms, solar photovoltaics are electronic devices which are able to convert
sun irradiation directly into electricity. Unlike CSP plant, PV systems can operate using
the global horizontal irradiance (GHI), thus taking advantage of both its direct (DNI)
and diffuse (DHI) components. In order to produce electricity, the PV plant consists
of solar modules, hardware features, namely inverters, electrical cabling and system
controls. A single solar module is composed by several solar cells, which convert sun
irradiance into a flow of electrons via the so-called PV-effect.
Thanks to technological advancements, photovoltaics have reached a third-generation
development; However, with their commercial maturity, first and second-generation PV
plants are the ones that currently dominate the photovoltaics market. Among the various
types of solar modules, Crystalline silicon-based modules have reached technological
consolidation, estimated to account for 89% of solar PV installed in 2014 [15].

Battery Energy Storage System
In order to achieve the flexibility level promoted by GRIDSOL project, the PV system
has been proposed in conjunction with electrical storage. Especially in isolated systems,
the introduction of a storage system may be beneficial to the transmission network by
guaranteeing grid stability and security of supply. Utility-scale storage for grid applica-
tions have been deployed since the mid-1800s as Lead-Acid (Pb-Acid) batteries. Despite
being still prevalent for certain applications, the low energy density1 and the limited life
cycles of Pb-Acid batteries justify the development of other types of utility-scale storage.
As a relative new technology, Lithium-ion (Li-ion) batteries have reached commercial
success thanks to their high energy density, round-trip efficiency, increased cycles and
low self-discharge rates. Hence, thanks to their improved technical performance, Li-ion
batteries have been proven to be ideal especially for PV-BESS applications in compari-
son to Pb-Acid storage systems [16]. For these reasons, the present feasibility analysis
considers Lithium-ion batteries as GRIDSOL’s electrical storage system.
In simple terms, the Li-ion battery generates a current flow as lithium ions move between
the anode and the cathode. Grid-tied utility-scale Li-ion BESS are divided in three main
blocks: battery, grid connection (including power electronics) and system operation
components, as presented in Fig. 2.5. Furthermore, Li-ion BESS can be used for a great
variety of applications: from ancillary services, such as frequency regulation, to grid
support, i.e. voltage support and grid reinforcement.

From a geographical perspective, the islands of Fuerteventura and Lanzarote belong to

3.2
Regulatory framework
The following section aims to describe the current regulatory framework in terms of elec-
tricity markets dynamics, as well as existing state policies and incentives for renewable
energy support. In fact, when considering RES-based plants, such as GRIDSOL, the
assessment of available energy markets becomes crucial in the estimation of potential
revenue streams. Moreover, the success of such capital intensive technologies is highly
dependent on the level as well as the type of support schemes that might be in place or
that might be implemented in the near future.
3.2.1
Electricity markets
In 2007, MIBEL, the Iberian Electricity Market, was launched following the successful
union between the Spanish and Portuguese electricity markets [32]. Within MIBEL, two
market operator roles are identified: an electricity operator and a system services oper-
ator. Focusing on the latter, Red Eléctrica de España is the sole Spanish representative
of system services, i.e. the Transmission System Operator for the Spanish electricity sys-
tem. The former is instead represented by several market operators, depending on the
settlement timing in the electricity markets. That is, power transactions with short-term
delivery, e.g. daily and intraday, occur in the spot market, whereas transactions with
long-term power delivery occur in the futures market. However, for the purpose of this
study, we will solely focus on the spot market, and particularly on daily transactions.
Spot market
OMIE, Operador del Mercado Ibérico de Energia, is responsible for managing the spot
market on the whole Iberian peninsula. In the daily market, also known as day-ahead,
the power bids are to be submitted by 12pm for the next day, where the buyer and seller
will define, hour by hour in a 24-hour span, how much he would be willing to pay for a
certain volume (energy) and how much power he would be able to sell at a certain price,
respectively. Hence, the balance between supply and demand is given by the market
price which, in other words, represents the equilibrium point at which the demand and
supply curves meet. Once the market price is settled and trades are agreed, the power
is delivered from midnight of the following day. Integrated in the European markets
context, OMIE follows the same marginal pricing model adopted by all European coun-
tries. That is, a well-functioning and competitive electricity market produces power at
==PAGE:41==

the lowest possible price for each hour of the day, by ordering the purchase and sale
bids according to their economic merit order and ensuring that technical limitations are
accounted for.
Once the day-ahead is closed, market players have the possibility to trade power in the
intraday market. In this case, the trading occurs very closely to the delivery time in
order to secure the previous trades from any unexpected deviations, e.g. power plants
failures. The Spanish intraday hosts six bidding sessions and market prices are found
with the same marginal pricing mechanism used for the daily market.
In the Canary Islands, power production activity and its dispatch is regulated by Real
Decreto 738/2015, published on the 31st of July 2015 [33]. Among different matters, the
Real Decreto 738/2015 has established a regulated power pricing scheme for the power
plants in the Canarian archipelago. In fact, due to the remoteness of the archipelago
and lack of market competition, generators located in the Canary Islands incur in higher
production costs compared to the ones in the Spanish peninsula.
Thus, the pricing
scheme aims to mitigate this effect by allowing the generators to sell electricity at an
advantageous regulated price.

Ancillary services
Among the different system services, ancillary services, or adjustment markets, are han-
dled by REE, the Spanish TSO. Those markets ensure that any slight mismatch between
production and consumption is taken care of by balance responsible parties, i.e. gen-
erating units entitled to manage frequency deviations. Hence, in order to restore any
imbalance caused by deviations, both on the demand and supply sides, to its real-time
values, balance responsible parties submit their bids (in MW) for up and down reserves
with their corresponding prices [34].
However, sources from REE1 have confirmed that adjustment markets in the peninsula’s
format are not present on the Canary Islands. In fact, according to Annex X from Real
Decreto 738/2015, ancillary services in the Canary Islands are regulated within the same
dispatch procedure for the daily and intraday schedules, as will be explained below.
3.2.2
Dispatch procedure in the Canary Islands
For the Spanish peninsula, we have seen that OMIE represents the market operator,
whereas Red Eléctrica de España is the system services operator. Differently from the
peninsula, REE is responsible for managing the dispatch procedure in non-peninsular
systems, such as the Canary Islands, by taking on the market operator role as well as
the system services operator role.
The dispatch procedure is regulated on three different scheduling horizons: weekly, daily
and intraday. Once set, the weekly generation schedule is then re-adjusted with the
daily and intraday schedules. Despite some substantial differences, it is important to
mention that, Canary Islands’ power dispatch follows the same merit order principle as
for the peninsula. Similarly to OMIE’s spot market, daily (and weekly) bids have to be
submitted one day in advance, while at least two hours in advance for intraday bids.
The dispatch scheduling consists of three steps: first, second and third dispatch pro-
cedure. The first dispatch is obtained by strictly applying the economic merit order,
where the demand bids are given exclusively by retailers and direct consumers. The
second dispatch follows both economic and security of supply criteria: taking into ac-
count the results from the first dispatch, the generators’ spinning reserves and the most
recent demand and supply forecasts, a new merit order curve is created. Lastly, starting
from the previous step, the third dispatch will take into consideration of any possible
supply-demand imbalance and technical restrictions on the transmission grid.

3.2.3
Support schemes
Incentives schemes on renewable energy have been subjected to a drastic change since
Real Decreto 413/2014 came into force [35]. With this Royal Decree, the Spanish govern-
ment introduced a new premium tariff, called Régimen Retributivo Especifico. Despite
not being formally defined as support scheme, this “specific remuneration regime” was
introduced as a complementary compensation for renewable energy plants, in order to
fairly compete with conventional, cheaper and more mature technologies on electricity
markets.
In addition to electricity market sales, renewable installations are allowed to receive
a lifetime specific retribution composed of two terms: a monetary compensation per
installed capacity, Rinv (Retribucion a la Inversion), and a retribution per operational
costs, Ro (Retribucion a la Operacion). The former, expressed in €/MW, aims to cover
the capital investment, which could not be otherwise recovered by the sole electricity sale
on the power markets. The second term, Ro, expressed in €/MWh, is instead designed
to smooth out the difference between the electricity sales and the operational costs. For
the calculation of these compensatory parameters, the Spanish government has defined
an “installation standard” for each technology type, which includes standardised values
for capital costs and operational expenditures.
The eligibility of the compensation was established by several competitive tenders. These
auctioning procedures are carried out for six years, defined as regulatory period, starting
from year 2014 to the 31st of December 2019. Throughout the six-year duration, all
retribution parameters may be completely revised. The last auction calls were launched
in Resolución de 10 de abril de 2017 [36], and in Resolución de 30 de junio de 2017 [37],
where only PV and Wind power plants located in the mainland system turned out to be
eligible for the retribution tariff.
Following the introduction of Real Decreto 413/2014, the new compensatory parameters
were also approved for the Canary Islands, and more in general, for non-peninsular terri-
tories [35]. In fact, Orden IET/1459/2014 established the retributions’ allocation mech-
anism for new photovoltaic and wind power installations in the Canarian archipelago
[38]. The Régimen Retributivo Especifico for non-peninsular systems consists of Rinv,
as previously mentioned, and Iinv, an additional economic incentive on the capital in-
vestment for the reduction of the operational costs. Similarly as for the peninsula, the
tendering process for eligibility in the Canary Islands was initially carried out for the
first half of the regulatory period, years 2014, 2015 and 2016. Following the auction,
eligible PV and wind power plants were granted the specific retribution regime if the
official authorisation for new installations was given between 2014 and 2016.
However, according to Orden ETU/130/2017, the retribution tariffs corresponding to the
second half of the regulatory period (years 2017, 2018 and 2019) have not been updated
as no auction has been held yet for renewable power plants in non-peninsular territories
[39]. Thus, at the present moment, renewable support schemes are not available in the

4.1
Optimisation model: Balmorel
Balmorel, i.e. BALtic MOdel for Regional Electricity Liberalisation, is a modelling tool
used for analysing the heat and power sectors in an international context. As its name
would suggest, Balmorel has been originally developed for the Baltic Sea Regions: Den-
mark, Estonia, Finland, Germany, Latvia, Lithuania, Norway, Poland, Russia (partially)
and Sweden, [40]. However, thanks to Balmorel’s high versatility, the model has been
applied to a numerous range of projects outside the scope of the Baltic regions, [41].
Implemented as a mainly linear programming optimisation problem, Balmorel can be
used for the analysis of future developments of a regional energy sector as well as for
shorter planning of energy-related operations, or environmental policy evaluation, among
other activities. The Balmorel model formulation is written in GAMS, General Algebraic
Modeling System, which is a modelling language that provides solutions for large math-
ematical problems. Being open-source, the Balmorel concept emphasises its purpose of
energy analyses tool by guaranteeing the transparency of the modelling details. In light
of the scope, Balmorel further supports modifications of the model code by any user.
Hence, the Balmorel version, with its related modifications, used in this study has been
provided by the Department of Management Engineering at the Technical University of
Denmark. A brief description of the mathematical formulation is presented in the next
section.
4.1.1
Theoretical background
In order to understand the most important details of the model structure, the general
model formulation is presented with a focus on generation technologies, demand, taxes
and objective function. As the complete model formulation requires a detailed explana-
tion, only the most relevant aspects are presented in this section (for a more in-depth
study, see [42]).
In broader terms, the scope of the Balmorel model is to satisfy the power and heat de-
mand of a chosen energy system. The model’s core is identified by numerous parameters
and variables which are linked by a set of equations, allowing Balmorel to determine the
system variables by satisfying both physical and economic principles. In other words,
power and heat generation technologies, which represent the bulk of parameters, are
dispatched according to an objective function, whose aim is to minimise the total sys-
tem costs. The dispatch is regulated with a series of constraints, i.e. equations, which

4.2
System Advisor Model
Developed by the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL), System Advisor
Model (SAM) is a performance and financial tool designed to model cost and perfor-
mance of renewable energy projects [44]. Each model in SAM represents a single renew-
able technology, whose input data needs to be defined to run the simulations. Thanks to
SAM’s user-friendly interface, input data, such as technology characteristics and costs,
might be manually specified or either imported from several libraries available in SAM.
When carrying out a project in SAM, a typical analysis involves three main steps:
1. definition of technology inputs: weather data at the project location, character-
istics of system components (e.g. size of collectors, number of PV modules and
inverters, etc.)
2. definition of technology-related costs inputs and other financial parameters (e.g.
tax rates, inflation, etc.)
3. once inputs are defined, running simulations and analysis of results
Concerning step 1., defining the technology characteristics allows SAM to calculate the
system design for each modelled technology before running the financial simulations.
Thus, for the purpose of this study, SAM is not fully exploited in terms of financial
structure and it is merely used in the evaluation of the system design, instead.

maximum capacity allowed taking land restrictions into consideration.
4.3
Cash flow model
When evaluating a project’s feasibility, a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) analysis rep-
resents the standard procedure for a correct economical and financial assessment. As
per definition, Cash Flow (CF) is the net amount of money moving into and out of a
business (or a project) over a finite period of time [45]. When dealing with cash flows,
two important concepts symbolise the foundations of an economic and financial analysis:
the difference between real and nominal values, and the time value of money, as following
described.
Real and nominal values:
In monetary terms, prices in a cash flow (including earn-
ings, costs, power prices, etc.) can be expressed in real and nominal values, where the
difference between the two lies within time variations to which the price is subjected to.

include any price change due to inflation.
In private-based cash flows, prices are usually stated in nominal terms. In this case, to
calculate the nominal value in year t, it is convenient to set an inflation index which
starts at 1 in the year of the real value (base year), as shown in (4.8).

Time value of money:
When assessing long-term projects, time value of money is
one of the fundamentals in DCF analyses. That is, time value of money expresses the
fact that the value of money received today is greater than the value of expectation to
receive money (in equal amount) in the future. In project terms, this means that the
value of an investment made today and the expectation of receiving monetary returns
on the investment in the future are not directly comparable as the two cash-flows occur
at two different points in time.
Thus, assuming a certain interest rate r and an x-amount of money today, the Future
Value (FV) in year t is:

investor.

Differently from Balmorel and SAM, the cash flow model has been entirely developed

Valuation date and start of operation:

Private discount rate:
in this case, the lack of real values for possible interested
firms led to the assumption of a private discount rate. For the estimation of discount
rate, it has been chosen to follow the commonly adopted approach of Weighted Average
Cost of Capital (WACC). In other words, the WACC defines a firm’s discount rates
and thus, it is equivalent to the private discount rate. WACC estimations for on-shore
wind energy projects [60] report average WACCs in 2016 between 7% and 8%2 for firms’
projects in Spain. According to [52], recent surveys from European firms have reported
a weighted average cost of capital in the range of 8% for the renewable sector. Following
these recent estimations, it was decided to assume a private discount rate of 8% for the
present project.
Corporate tax rate:
as a results of the Canary Islands Special Zone (ZEC), the
project can benefit from a low tax zone, created for promoting the islands’ economic
and social development [57]. For this purpose, generation from renewable sources falls
under the list of activities that an economic entity is authorised to set up within the ZEC,
thus benefiting from the favoured tax regime. In addition to the authorised activities,
few requirement have to be taken care of when becoming a ZEC entity:
• The company must be newly established within the geographical area of ZEC,
i.e. registered address and effective place of management must be in the Canary
Islands.
• Minimum investment of 50 000 euros and creation of at least three jobs during the
first six months upon registration.
• The reduction of the gross payable tax following the application of the lower tax
rate (4%) cannot be higher than 30% of the revenues of the company.

in addition to basic interconnection costs, a cost for substa-
tion has been included in the analysis. This choice comes as a direct consequence of the

Scenario cluster (1)
Starting from the technology mix, it has been decided to compare two different config-

• Configuration 2: CSP + GT + PV + BESS

Scenario cluster (2)
The second scenarios cluster is defined in terms of national policies, and more specifically
renewable support policies. That is, possible government policies that could concur in
the promotion of renewable energy, such as carbon taxes, CO2 emissions reduction caps,
and feed-in tariffs.
Reference Scenario: carbon taxes
As explained in Section 5.1.2, carbon taxes implemented in Spain are defined under
the EU Emission Trading System. As a policy for CO2 emission reduction currently
in force, the reference scenario for simulations in Balmorel is represented by this active
policy in supporting renewable generation. Future projections of EU ETS carbon prices
are explained in Section 5.1.3.
Emission Scenario
An emission cap was defined in accordance with Spanish 2020 national targets [65] de-
veloped within the framework of Europe 2020 climate and energy package [2]. In line
with EU targets, Tenerife and El Hierro (two of the Canary Islands) have adhered to the
Covenant of Mayors for climate and energy [66], where several islands’ municipalities
have developed and submitted climate action plans in order to reach an emission reduc-
tion target by 2020. Following these directives, it was assumed a common reduction
target for Fuerteventura and Lanzarote of -20% CO2 emission by 2020 in comparison
with 2005 levels.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
The previous chapter opens up the discussion around two macro-areas: the medium-term
integration of GRIDSOL in the energy system of Fuerteventura and Lanzarote, and the
possible effect of support schemes on the overall feasibility of the project. In addition to
these topics, further considerations will be drawn regarding results in the perspective of
carbon emission reductions.
7.1
Impact on energy system
In terms of energy system, the penetration of GRIDSOL technology portfolio is evalu-
ated on the timing dispatch of the day-ahead market. The hourly resolution of power
production highlights the role of the CSP: in every configuration, the bulk of base-load
generation, which has been historically granted by diesel power plants, is partially shifted
to the CSP plant. Thus, even in scenarios at lowest penetration (e.g. option A), the
CSP is able to singularly cover 30% of electricity demand in Fuerteventura, value that
is additionally supported by an high capacity factor (58%).
In the most flourishing
scenario (configuration D), the total GRIDSOL system reaches 68% of Fuerteventura’s
total consumption with capacity factors up to 67%.
To achieve these outcomes, thermal storage and the open cycle gas turbine play a crucial
role in increasing capacity factors. The combination of GT and TES could allow not only
to decrease the generation burden from diesel plants during nighttime but also phase-
out existing gas turbines operating on demand peaks. This latter effect is enforced in
scenarios that account for the integration of photovoltaics, which acts as a daily peaker
in the hourly generation. Despite controversial from an economic perspective (as shown
in Section 6.2), the addition of the open cycle gas turbine affects positively the energy
system contributing in the affirmation of the CSP as base-load technology and providing
back-up electricity when the TES is no longer loaded.
The overall scope of GRIDSOL is achieved to a certain extent with the concentrating
solar power: that is, the CSP with a thermal storage system allows for a steady output,
whose steadiness is accentuated in conjunction with GT. Due to modelling limitations,
this effect is restricted to the CSP although, by project definition, DOME would call for
an unique output of energy from the whole GRIDSOL SRH.

additional sensitivities were performed on different sets of parameters, such as inflation,
operational costs, capital investment, discount rates and fuel costs.

the two cases might be explained with analyses carried out in the previous chapter:
the exploitation of PV during peak hours allows to maximise the revenues as the heat
from the receiver and the heat recovered from GT can be stored in TES shifting steam
generation to later hours.
The found subsidy levels, both as investment grants as well as premiums, constitute
a relatively high financial burden on the government and thus, on tax payers. As the
subsidies depend on corporate discount rates, the project becomes more attractive for
firms lower WACCs (e.g. 6%). In light of these considerations, the newly found feed-
in premiums could reach a high level of competitiveness, being able to exceed latest
auctions in the Spanish peninsula which have set a tender premium of 43 €/MWh [68].
Despite the outcomes of this second approach, a combination of feed-in premiums and
investment grants would be preferable in order to take less optimistic discount rates into
account.
From a regulation perspective, the regulated market environment of the Canary Islands
could facilitate the integration of GRIDSOL as dispatch of renewables is prioritised over
conventional power generation. In monetary terms, this means that the daily market
would allow CSP and PV to secure the revenues making possible investors keen on
investing in GRIDSOL. This favourable condition may be used as a further motivation for
policy makers in the Canary Islands to introduce renewable support. Priority of dispatch
of GRIDSOL’s CSP and PV could in fact minimise the risk of not penetrating the daily
market schedules, and firm the revenues, making a future investment in GRIDSOL not
only merely attractive but also worthwhile on a long-term perspective.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The present study has investigated the private and socio-economic feasibility of GRID-
SOL technology in Fuerteventura. As highlighted during the COP21 Paris conference,
non-peninsular systems are particularly vulnerable in regard to climate change. Their
electrical isolation forces these energy systems to rely on fossil-fuel generation as only
means of guaranteeing grid stability and security of supply.
In response to conventional power plants, the European-funded project GRIDSOL pro-
poses Smart Renewable Hubs, which combine variable renewable energies, such as PV,
with the firmness of synchronous generators, such as steam turbine and gas turbine in-
tegrated under the frame of a unique concentrating solar power plant. For the purpose
of this study, GRIDSOL is considered as the following technology portfolio: CSP (with
thermal storage) + GT (with heat recovery system), and PV + BESS. The performance
of GRIDSOL is evaluated on the islands system of Fuerteventura, which is inserted in
the two-island electric system of Fuerteventura and Lanzarote. Two main objectives are
identified for the present study: from a power system perspective, the study focuses
firstly on analysing the impact of a future GRIDSOL installation in Fuerteventura. Sec-
ondly, the analysis verges on the economic feasibility of GRIDSOL from a private and
social perspective.
For each component (CSP, TES, GT, PV, and BESS), the optimal capacity of GRIDSOL
is found by solving an optimisation problem in Balmorel. The optimisation is carried
out in relation to two scenario clusters: a first cluster defined according to two com-
binations of GRIDSOL technologies (e.g. CSP+GT and CSP+GT+PV+BESS), and
a second cluster which follows the implementation of national policies, such as carbon
taxes, RES subsidies, and cap on emission levels. For this second scenario cluster, sce-
narios on carbon prices and emission cap have proven to be unrealistic or unfeasible in
terms of optimal capacity. For this reason, the study has further investigated additional
subsidy scenarios. Following this approach, the first scenario cluster was widen to four
configurations of GRIDSOL: (A) CSP, (B) CSP+PV, (C) CSP+GT, (D) CSP+GT+PV.
Each option underlines the role of CSP as base-load power plant, partially substituting
diesel generation, whereas, when present, PV assumes most of the load burden during
daytime. Coupled with thermal storage, the gas turbine allows to increase the CSP
capacity factors.
As for the economic evaluation, a cash flow analysis was carried out for configurations A,
B, C, and D. Both private and socio-economics of GRIDSOL were proven to be unfeasible
==PAGE:104==
88
8 Conclusion
for each option. In light of these outcomes, the cash flow evaluation focused on break-
even analyses (via sensitivities) for two different subsidy schemes, feed-in premiums
and investment grants.
Results from sensitivities indicated B and D as least costly
configurations to reach the break-even point. Lowest premiums (option D) stand at 67.3
€/MWh and 53.7 €/MWh for real and inflated revenues, respectively. For investment
grants, a minimum share of 58% of CAPEX is required, making option C the most
profitable at break-even point. Additionally, estimation with a 6% private discount rate
led to highly competitive premiums in comparison to the last subsidy auctions in Spain.
For both Fuerteventura and Lanzarote, a maximum 25% reduction in CO2 emission
draws attention as possible motivation for the implementation of renewable support
schemes in response to what might be of public interest in the Canarias communities.
8.1
Future Work
Because of time, modelling, and various constraints, possible interesting investigations
are excluded from the present analysis and are left as future work to the readers or to
the author herself. Ideas for future works will be described according to specific subjects,
as presented hereafter.
Ancillary services
Despite numerous efforts, accounting for ancillary services be-
comes particularly difficult in regulated markets, where the lack of transparency hinders
the process of allocating specific capacities for balancing purposes. As one of GRIDSOL
strength, ancillary services should be considered in future analyses not only as means
of ensuring flexible power generation but also as additional stream of revenues, which
would contribute positively to the overall profitability of GRIDSOL.
Bio-gas for open cycle gas turbine
The remoteness of the Canarian archipelago
has a great impact on the viability of the GT where gas oil imports are driven by logistics
costs on top of fuel prices. An interesting analysis could consider to switch from gas
oil to bio-gas, as pilots for bio-gas turbines are already in place in Lanzarote. As no
information on bio-gas prices has been yet gathered, a comparison between the fuels
should be performed to underline the economic competitiveness of bio-gas versus gas oil.
Modelling of DOME
As mentioned in Chapter 4, the possibility of dispatching
GRIDSOL as an unique power plant does not fall within the several modelling features
of Balmorel. Therefore, future investigations should bridge this gas by considering an
advanced software or, if possible, modifications of the Balmorel code.
Furthermore,
deeper analyses should state whether the government of the Canary Islands could possi-
bly establish a regulatory framework for the dispatch and remuneration of DOME-based
system, such as GRIDSOL.
Demand and fuel cost projections
Fuel costs and demand projections have been
based on several reasonable assumptions, which are fixed across the developed scenarios.

demand trends and fuel costs as additional scenario framework in order to achieve greater
comprehensiveness. This is particularly important when accounting for volatility of fuel
prices, whose fluctuations might impact positively or negatively the overall investment
potential of GRIDSOL.
