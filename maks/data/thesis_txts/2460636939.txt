Abstract
In this end of degree thesis, a process design for green diesel using hydrotreatment of vegetable
oil is developed. Four potential feedstocks of palm oil, palm stearin, palm fatty acid distillates
and beef tallow are presented and discussed for use in the process. Based on the economical
potential and composition, the use of palm stearin was decided. Literature is studied to gain
knowledge of the process to determine the process design structure. The process features ﬁve
sections; pretreatment, hydrotreatment, separation of excess H2, isomerization and separation
into desired products. The process design is conducted using the simulation tool PRO/II. The
process design has an annual processing capacity of 72.7 ton/hr (602080 ton/year) of palm
stearin and production rate of 49.8 ton/hr (412344 ton/year) of product.
The design was sized and the cost was estimated to be evaluated economically. The evaluation
provided a return of investment at 13 %/y and a payback period of 4.5 years. It was found that
raw material costs were a large factor in the economical potential of the process.
The design was optimized with a heat exchanger network system to lower the utility cost of
heating and cooling. The heating and cooling in the used streams was reduced with 90.87%
and 65.88%, respectively. Parameter optimization was attempted on ﬂash drums, isomerization
reactor and the distillation columns, to optimize the operating conditions of the plant increasing
the annual production of product. A sustainability analysis determined the largest impacts of
the process could be seen in CO2 formation, ozone depletion and land transformation, with
ozone depletion being the major impact.

Resumé
I denne kandidatafhandling fremlægges et process design for grøn diesel ved brug af hydrogen-
behandling på vegetabilske oiler. Fire potentialle råmaterialer, palme olie, palme stearin, palme
fedtsyrer distillater og oksetalg, er præsenteret og diskusteret for brug i processen. Baseret på
det økonomiske potentiale og råmaterialets komposition er palme stearin valgt som råmateriale.
Et litteraturstudie blev foretaget for at samle viden om processen for at bestemme designet
af processen. Processen har fem sektioner; forbehandling, hydrogenbehandling, adskillelse af
overskydende hydrogen, isomerisering og adskillelse til de ønskede produkter. Designet er lavet
i simuleringsværktøjet PRO/II. Procesdesignet har en årlig bearbejdning på 72.7 ton/time
(602080 ton/år) af palme stearin og en production på 49.8 ton/time (412344 ton/år) af produkt.
Størrelsen på designet er regnet og prisen er anslået for at blive evalueret økonomisk. Evaluaering-
en gav et afkast af investeringen på 13 %/år of en tilbagebetalingsperiode på 4.5 år. Evaluaeringen
viste yderligere at prisen på råmateriale har stor indﬂydelse på potentialet af processen.
En optimisering af designet blev foretaget med et varmeveksler netværkssystem for at mindske
udgifterne til opvarmningen og nedkøling. Opvarmningen og nedkølingen i de involverede strømme
blev nedsat med henholdvis 90.87% og 65.88%. Parameteroptimering blev forsøgt ﬂash, isomerise-
ringsreaktoren og destillationskolonnerne for at øge den årlige mængde produktion af produkt.
En bæredygtigheds- analyse blev fortaget for at anslå de miljø- og sundshedsmæssige påvirkninger
som processen ville have. Det blev fastslået at CO2 formation, ozone udtømning og jordomdannelse
havde de største påvirkninger med ozone udtømning som den største.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION

Introduction
With the entire population of the planet expanding over the last 50 years, with an increase of
over 100%, the need for fuels are expanding with it. The increasing quality of life in the Western
world equals a higher consumption of fuels. Especially in transportation and production. The
large deposits of petroleum in the underground, to produce fossil fuels, are not so never-ending
anymore. The need for renewable fuel sources emerge.
Biofuels are a general name for fuels processed from some sort of biomatter. Fossil fuels are
actually an ancient version of biofuels as they are been comprosed in the underground for million
of years. Biofuels are in a way the same except they are made from biomatter produced today
[1]. Diﬀerent types of biofuels can be categorized as ’generations’. Whereas ﬁrst generation uses
food crops. Second generations uses non-food crops, such as wood, food waste and organic waste
etc. Third generation utilizes algae for the production of biofuels. The fourth generation focuses
on biomass that captures CO2 during growth [2].
First generation biofuels have certain problems associated to them. One being the ’fuel vs. food’
debate, where the crops used for fuel could potentially also be used for food, which is in conﬂict
with the increasing population of the world. Also, there are debate that ﬁrst generation fuels
produce more CO2 than the feedstock captures during the growth. Some of these issues are ﬁxed
when utilizing the second generation biofuels, as most second generation biofuel are produced
using food crop waste or non-edible vegetable oils.
The most commonly known biofuel is biodiesel, which is produced with transesteriﬁcation of
vegetable oils with methanol resulting in Fatty Acid Methyl Esters (FAMEs). These are similar
to the composition of fossil fuels, however the performance is not quite that of petroleum fuels.
Biodiesel is not used as a ’stand alone’ fuel, but rather used for blending with petroleum fuels to
stretch the life-span of petroleum fuels. The same feedstocks that are used for biodiesel can also
be used to produce green diesel. Green diesel is produced with hydro-cracking of the vegetable
oil, also known as Hydrotreated Vegetable Oil (HVO), resulting in hydrocarbon, which yields
a fuel that has the same chemical properties as petroleum fuels [3]. Besides simply replacing
petroleum fuels, biofuels are also better for the environment, as biofuels contains less oxygen
and the greenhouse gas emissions are also greatly reduced.
The feedstocks for both biodiesel and green diesel are many. But, many of the popular feedstock
are also a food source; palm oil, rapeseed oil and soybean oil to name a few. The challenges
are to shift the production of biodiesel and green diesel from a ﬁrst generation to a second
generation approach. Neste is, with their Renewable Diesel, a front runner for second generation
green diesel, or as they call it Renewable Diesel. They use a variety of raw materials for their
diesel including residues from animal production, byproducts from oil reﬁning and waste from
the food industry [4].
The most produced edible oil is palm oil, with an annual production of 62.5M tonnes in 2015,
according to the European Palm Oil Alliance [5]. This makes palm oil an obvious candidate
for biofuel production as the amounts are high. But as palm oil also have other application, it
becomes a complication. But, the reﬁning of palm oil yields a by-product of which can not be
used for food application due to odor and bad taste. This by-product is called Palm Fatty Acid
Distillate (PFAD). The annual production of PFAD is roughly 3-5% of the palm oil production
[6]. Not only vegetable oils can be used to produce green diesel. The use of animal fats is
also being investigated. Beef tallow, an animal residue have also been proposed as an possible
feedstock for the production of green diesel. The annual production was 8.46M tonnes in 2009 [7].
There are still many challenges for biofuels. They still can not compete with petroleum fuels
costs. So, further research must be done in these ﬁelds. Bringing biofuels to a competitive level
is a must before the petroleum fuels are gone.

2.1
Petroleum vs. biofuel
Despite not being as proﬁtable as petroleum diesel, biofuels have advantages in other areas.
Besides the renewable aspect, biofuels produce way less greenhouse gases than diesel does. This is
due to diesel consisting of roughly 75% hydrocarbons and 25% aromatic compounds. The average
chemical range of carbon in found these compounds are 10-15. The aromatic compounds yields
incomplete combustion, resulting in CO. This can be noticed when black smoke sometimes comes
out of the exhaust on a vehicle. Besides the incomplete combustion, the aromatic compounds
also impose a risk to health and environment. A proposed way of removing these issues from
fuels in vehicles are by the use of biofuel. Traditional biodiesel are produced using methanol and
some kind of base catalyst to yield glycerol and methyl esters, seen in ﬁgure 2.1. Despite aiding
some of the disadvantages of petroleum fuel, the biodiesel have some disadvantages of its own.
The methyl esters are hygroscopic, which means they can absorb moisture. This is not ideal as
water contamination can lead to microbial growth, which can be damaging to the fuel [8]. Also,
FAME are not chemically similar to diesel and thus the direct replacement of diesel is harder
without changing the engine in the vehicles.
This leads to the production of green diesel. Green diesel have ﬁxed some of the problems
that FAME has, while chemically resembling the petroleum diesel. The process of green diesel
production is described in the following section.
Figure 2.1: Transesterﬁcation of triglyceride to methyl esters and glycerol.
2.2
HVO Process
Green diesel can be processed from vegetable oils in a number of ways, all using dihydrogen
in various ways. One method called ’decarboxylation’ (DCOx) have been used to produce
diesel consisting of long hydrocarbons. This produces CO2 as a byproduct. Another method
called ’decarbonylation’ (DCO) is similar to DCOx, however the byproduct is CO and H2O.
A third method called ’hydrodeoxygenation’ (HDO) use more H2 than the other methods,
however produces only H2O as a byproduct. One signiﬁcant diﬀerence is that HDO produces a
hydrocarbon with the same number of carbons as the fatty acid chain. Unlike DCOx and DCO,
which produces a hydrocarbon with one less carbon than the fatty acid [9].
Examples of the reactions can be seen below, using a palmitic triglycide as reactant.
DCOx:
C51H98O6 + 3 H2 →3 C15H32 + C3H8 + 3 CO2
(2.1)
DCO:
C51H98O6 + 6 H2 →3 C15H32 + C3H8 + 3 CO + 3 H2O
(2.2)
HDO:
C51H98O6 + 12 H2 →3 C16H34 + C3H8 + 6 H2O
(2.3)

2.3
Fuel properties
Neste Oil has presented a handbook with information on their renewable biofuel. Within it is
a list of relevant fuel properties for their fuel [10]. The list is long, however only a few of the
properties can be controlled solely on the composition of the feedstock and the process used to
reﬁne. These are:
• Oxidative stability
• Cetane number
• Cold properties
• Density
2.3.1
Oxidative stability
When used for fuel, thes tability of fatty acids is important. This is due to need for a longer
shelf life [11]. The oxidative stability of the vegetable oil and beef tallow depends on the
composition of fatty acids, the placement of the fatty acids on the glycerol and the level of
tocopherol/tocotrienols. Kamal et al [11] established that monosatured fatty acids are less likely
to oxidate than unsaturated fatty acids .
Lipid oxidation is started by hydrogen abstraction of a allylic or bis-allylic position on an
unsatured fatty acid (LH, L for lipid) to make an alkyl radical (L•) [11].
The alkyl radical reacts with oxygen and form a lipid peroxyl radical.
LH + X• →L• + XH
(2.6)
L• + O2 →LOO•
(2.7)
LOO•, due to having longer lifetime than L•, propagates the oxygen reaction by selectively
abstracting a hydrogen from LH.
LOO• + LH →L• + LOOH
(2.8)
LOO• + LOO• →non-radical products
(2.9)
LOO• + L• →non-radical products
(2.10)
L• + L• →non-radical products
(2.11)
The presence of antioxidants, in this case; tocopherols and tocotrienols, inhibits the oxidation
of the fatty acids.
LOO• + AH →A• + LOOH
(2.12)
A• + LOO• →non-radical products
(2.13)
The antioxidants are stabilized by resonance and therefore does not propagate oxidation.
According to Kamal et al, monounsaturated fatty acids (oleic) are better at withstanding
oxidation than polyunsaturated acids (linoleic and linolenic).
Obtaining the right composition of fatty acids is not always enough for the stability. The
placement of the fatty acids within the triglyceride aﬀects the stability as well [11]. According
to Mateos et. al [12], one or two saturated fatty acids can help stabilize an unsaturated one.
Especially if the unsaturated fatty is place in the sn-2 position (middle position). Oleic acids
are also found to be more stable than other unsaturated fatty acids and can acutally help
stabilize if placed in sn-1,3 positions (outer positions). This is shown by Mateos et al, as the
triglyceride LLL (trilinoleoyl glycerol) have a relative oxidation rate of 3.0. The oxidation rate,
with the replacement of a linoleic acid in sn-1 to a palmitic acid (PLL), is lowered to 2.0. It can
be further stabilized with the replacement of the outer linoleic acid to oleic acid (PLO). This
descreases the relative oxidation rate to 0.7 [12].

2.3.2
Cetane number
Cetane number (CN) is used for diesel fuels to determine the combustion speed of the diesel. It is
the corresponding value to octane number is for gasoline fuels. Combustion speed relates to the
ignition quality. High ignition quality means low time between injection and start of combustion.
This is called ignition delay time.
CN is determined using two methods described in EN 15940, DCN and EN 15195 [10]. Two
reference fuels is used to determine the cetane number.
• n-hexadecane (C16H34), which have good ignition quality; cetane number of 100.
• 1-methylnaphthalene (C11H10), which have poor ignition quality; cetane number of 0.
1-methylnaphthalene have later been replaced with 2,2,4,4,6,6,8,8-heptamethylnonane (isocetane,
C16H34), which have a cetane number of 15. Chemical structures can be seen in Appendix B,
ﬁgure 10.5a & b.
Despite being fatty acid methyl esters, table 2.1 shows that with increasing length of the carbon
chain, the higher the cetane number becomes. While at the presence of a double bond the cetane
number is greatly reduced. Palmitic and stearic chains also resembles n-hexadecane in structure,
which explains the higher cetane number.
Long saturated chains are therefore desired when looking at combustion in a diesel engine.

2.3.3
Cold properties
Cold properties include a low cloud point, pour point and cold ﬁlter plugging point (CFPP).
These are all related to the composition of the fuel, hence also the fatty acid composition. Good
cold properties for fuels are important, especially in colder climate and when the fuel is used
for aviation. Enhancing cold properties is a trade-oﬀbetween density and cetane number. When
enhancing cold properties, the density goes down and the cetane number are reduced [10]. The
cold properties can be enhanced by additives, which only yields minor improvements. The most
eﬀective way of enhancing the cold properties is by isomerization [14].
2.3.4
Density
Density has a positive eﬀect on the maximum output of an engine. This is due to the heating
value for diesel fuels are quite constant when inside a certain range of aromatic content. If the
density is reduced, so is the heating value, yielding less energy in a full throttle. As the density
of HVO is lower than diesel fuels, so is the heating value suspected to be. However, according to
the Neste Handbook, it is not. Hydrogen content in HVO fuels are higher than in diesel fuels.
This results in HVO fuels having roughly the same heating value as diesel fuels [10].

2.6
Feedstock selection
Palm oil can be fractioned into two fractions, palm olein and palm stearin. The ratio of the
split is commonly 85% palm olein and 15% palm stearin [19]. This makes palm olein available
feedstock, as the availability of it is high. However, palm olein is the liquid fraction of palm
oil, meaning its composition is more unsaturated than palm stearin. This makes the hydrogen
consumption of palm olein larger than palm stearin, when using hydrotreatment.
Palm olein is also, with a prize of 521.5 USD/t [25], more expensive than palm stearin. Even
the smallest diﬀerences in price is crucial when looking at a very large scale production.
PFAD have also shown clear advantages in terms of price. The fact that it mostly contains free
fatty acids, will lower the hydrogen demand due to no cracking of the triglycerides. The hydrogen
demand is compared to palm stearin, see table 10.1 and 10.2.
The uncertainties regarding composition and price of beef tallow makes an accurate process
evaluation diﬃcult.
Therefore, the continuing work in this study will focus on palm stearin and PFAD as feedstocks
and eventually compare the two to ﬁnd the feedstock that can yield the highest amount of fuel
along with the lowest costs.

3.1
Pretreatment
In order to obtain palm stearin and PFAD, the crude palm oil must be reﬁned when palm
oil is used for food applications. This is referred to as ’reﬁning’. However, for the case of fuel
production, when the two feedstocks are to be compared, this step is seen as a pretreatment
step in order to obtain the feedstocks. In the pretreatment step, the impurities in the CPO are
removed in order to obtain better fuel properties. The impurities includes phosphatides, trace
metals and oxidation products. During reﬁnement, the beneﬁcial phytonutrients, which is also
found in CPO mainly tocopherols/tocotrienols, are maintained. Pretreatment of edible oil can
be done in two methods, with physical reﬁning or chemical reﬁning, see ﬁgure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Physical reﬁning vs. chemical reﬁning of CPO
The main diﬀerence between physical and chemical reﬁning is how FFA is removed. In physical
reﬁning, the FFA is removed with steam stripping. In chemical reﬁning the FFA and phosphatides
are removed with alkali neutralization [26].
If the oil contains a high amount of FFA and low amounts of phosphatides, the physical route
is preferred. This is the case for palm oil. The physical reﬁning route have several advantages as
higher yield, use of less chemicals and better operating costs [27].

The main objective of the degumming step is to remove the gums, this includes phospholipids.
If left in the oil, these can implicate the later stages of reﬁning as they decompose at higher
temperatures and darkens the oil. Phosphatides are similar to the triglycerides in composition,

phosphatides are found in CPO. The distribution of the phospholipids can be seen in table 3.2

The composition of the phospholipids is vital when determining the type of degumming used.

PI at about 80% of its own weight. This means that if the composition of phospholipids consists
of more than 55% PC, all of them can be removed with water [27]. However, this is not the case
for CPO.

3.1.2
Deodorization and fractionation
Deodorization and fractionation of palm oil are operations used in the pretreatment of edible
oil. Deodorization removes odors and improves the taste by removing the free fatty acids using
distillation. This step produces PFAD as a by-product (about 3-5% of the crude oil) [11]. As
taste and odor is of less importance when utilizing oil for fuel production, this step can be
neglected. In fact, the expense of removing the FFA is unnecessary as FFA actually require less
hydrogen to convert into fuel than triglycerides does.
The fractionation steps yields fractions of the palm oil, palm stearin and palm olein. The
fractionation can be done in several ways. One method is the dry fractionation, which utilizes
heating and controlled cooling, ﬁltering and centrifugation. The palm stearin fraction contains
more saturated fatty acids, which yields a more solid fraction, while the palm olein fraction
contains more unsaturated fatty acids resulting in a more liquid fraction. This step are normally
also neglected in fuel production as it is an extra step which have little to no impact on the
overall product, which only makes it an extra expense.
3.2
Hydrotreatment
As previously described in section 2.2, and shown in ﬁgure 2.2, hydrotreatment of triglycerides
happens in several steps. Not all of these reaction steps have complete conversion. The saturation
and hydrocracking can reach 100% conversion with suﬃcient H2 pressure and temperature [29,
30, 31]. However, the DCO, DCOx and HDO pathways does not have a 100% conversion and it is
not possible to completely suppress certain pathways to only obtain the desired pathway. Simply,
they all three happens at the same time. Although, it is possible alter the operating conditions
and the choice of catalyst to favor one of the reaction pathways [32]. The most important
operating condition is temperature [30], while hydrogen pressure, liquid hourly space velocity
(LHSV) and H2/oil ratio is also important [29]. Multiple references, as shown in table 3.4, suggest
an operating temperature between 300 and 350oC. Temperatures above 360oC promotes cracking
of the hydrocarbons [29, 33]. Several steps from fatty acids to hydrocarbons via the HDO route
have also been observed [31, 34, 35]. Several intermediate products have been report, as can
be seen in ﬁgure 3.3. Egeberg et al. [34] along with Donnis et al. [35] found that intermediate
products remain in batch operation if temperature is not high enough.
The inﬂuence of hydrogen pressure on the deoxygenation have been studied extensively. Sources
reports that a high hydrogen pressure inhibits the decarboxylation and decarbonylation routes
[36]. This is also concluded by Boyas et al. [33], that in batch tests the rate of DCO/DCO2
routes decreased with increasing initial hydrogen pressure.
The eﬀects of LHSV is also important. LHSV is the inverse as residence time, meaning the lower
LHSV, the higher reactor volume is needed. It is reported that LHSV above 1 hr−1 promotes
the DCO/DCOx pathways. While LHSV at 0.25-1 hr−1 seems to promote the HDO pathway [29].

3.2.1
Catalyst
The hydrotreatment of VGO happens on catalysts in a reactor. The type of catalyst, amount and
the support used are not accidental. In fact, in a comprehensive study by Xin Li et al. [32] the
diﬀerent types of catalysts their support and the results are discussed. The most common catalyst
for hydrotreatment is a Ni- and/or Mo-based catalyst combined with an various supports. Pure
Ni-based and Mo- catalysts promoted decarboxylation and hydrodeoxygenation, respectively.
While a combination yielded both. However, the activity was low for the pure based catalysts,
resulting in the use of the NiMo catalyst. A conclusion supported by various other studies, which
uses the same kind of catalyst, see table 3.4. These catalysts contains sulfur which, at traces of
water, can lead to sulfur leaching and deactivation. The presence of alkali and phosphor can also
lead to deactivation, which is why the pretreatment step is important [32].
Other catalysts have been proposed as a result of this. Noble metal catalysts, mainly Pd and Pt
catalysts, have been tested. Pd and Pt catalyst have shown to be eﬀective for decarboxylation
and decarbonylation processes, respectively [32, 33].
Xin Li et al. proposed metal nitride and carbide catalysts as a replacement for metal sulphide
catalyst, however the performance of these are not at the level of metal sulphide catalyst yet.
The noble metal catalysts are not suggested since the cost is high and the abundance is limited.
The various types of support for the catalyst have also been tested. It seems that despite the
type of catalyst (sulphide, nitride or carbide), the Al2O3 and SiO2 supports prove the best.
3.2.2
Reactor type - Trickle Bed Reactor
The typical reactor type for hydrotreatment is a trickle bed reactor (TBR). [39, 40]. A TBR
is a type of packed bed reactor, where a liquid and a gas phase ﬂows downwards over the
packing material which is ﬁxed. The reactor is also equipped with a cooling/heating jacket, since
most hydrotreatment reactions are exothermic. The liquid and gas reactants undergo a chemical
reaction on the surface of the package material, which, in the case of hydrotreatment, is catalysts.
There are two ways to operate a trickle bed reactor; co-current downﬂow or countercurrent gas
ﬂow. The most applied method is the co-current downﬂow, where both the liquid and the gas
enters in the top of the reactor and ﬂows down over the catalyst [40].

Hydroisomerization is the process of converting n-paraﬃns to mono- or multibranched iso-paraﬃn

freezing point than n-paraﬃn, which is ideal for winter-grade diesel or aviation fuel. Figure

lowered when the more centered the methyl group is placed. When performing isomerization it
is impossible to force the reaction to a certain isomer product. In fact, the product is a mixture
of many diﬀerent isomers, as can be seen in ﬁgure 3.8.
Figure 3.6: Freezing point of n-paraﬃns and i-paraﬃns as a function of carbon number [44]
The n-paraﬃns releases a H2 to form n-oleﬁns (alkenes). The oleﬁn forms a monobranced

the iso-paraﬃn. The reaction pathways can be seen in ﬁgure 3.7.

Cracking
Along with the process of hydroisomerization is the process of hydrocracking. The operating
conditions are vital as they can determine the degree of cracking. There are two types of
hydrocracking; thermal and catalytic. Thermal cracking is the cracking of hydrocarbons at high
temperatures (750-900oC) [48]. Catalytic cracking utilizes a catalyst to lower the activation
energy and makes it possible to perform the cracking at a lower temperature [49]. Catalyst type
and temperature are therefore two important operating conditions to choose when hydrocracking.
Temperatures above 360oC greatly increase the cracking products when utilizing catalytic cracking
[29]. The cracking happens with β-scission type C and D mechanism [44], shown in ﬁgure 3.10.

4.1
Thermodynamic model
The thermodynamic model, or the physical property model, used for the process design is
the Non-Random Two-Liquid (NRTL) and UNIQUAC Functional-group Activity Coeﬃcient
(UNIFAC) models. NRTL is used as default, while UNIFAC is used to implement components
and in the distillation columns. The base models implemented in PRO/II are used, however
extension could potentially be used. Following the selection model from Carlsen 1996 [55], other
property models can also be used. Especially when the pressure rises above 10 bar.
4.2
Pretreatment
The oil is pretreated with a mixture of water and EDTA. The oil/water mass ratio is 20:1 [28].
The pretreatment was hard to model, since the EDTA-phosphatide complexes are unknown.
Also, Gibon et al [27] did not provide operating conditions for the centrifuge. The oil in water
equilibrium was also not calculated. It was assumed that the oil loss in the pretreatment was
10%. This was both an assumption (discussion with supervisor) as well as documented by Hilbers
et al. [47]. The pretreatment is simple compared to the traditional pretreatment, which utilizes
bleaching and deodorization. The bleaching step is reported to have a 20-30 wt-% oil loss due
to the absorption of oil in the bleaching clay [27].
The separation of the oil and water phase was done using a stream calculator, which is not ideal.
Both in terms of pure modelling purposes but also, the equipment and utility costs can not be
calculated and included for this part. The pretreatment was still included in the process design
as it is a contributor to the overall process cost due to the oil loss. The oil/water mass ratio was
kept constant with the EDTA-CNT controller, seen in ﬁgure 10.17.

• The recycle of unreacted fatty acids can react.
• If the composition of PFAD is considered, the reaction set does not need to change.
• It makes it possible to later implement a kinetic model, should one be found.

means that no energy should be added terms of heating and increased pressure in order obtain
the best operating conditions. The pressure was chosen to be the ﬁrst speciﬁcation, however
temperature could be chosen as well. In ﬂash 2, the remaining light gases along with the water
is to be removed. Utilizing the same temperature from F1 made the only variable the pressure.

for a production this size. Optimization of ﬂash 2 is to be considered heavily. Flash 3 is a large
open tank. Large amounts of propane are present in the inlet, which ideally is recycled. A lot

fatty acids. Fatty acids are removed to protect the catalyst in the isomerization reactor [47], but
also so the fatty acids can be recycled. The result of 97.8677oC is good, since the hydrocarbon
stream has to be heated up again for the isomerization reactor. The low pressure also contributes

4.6.1
Fractional distillation
The fractional distillation to separate the hydrocarbons is done with three columns, since PRO/II
does not have a fractional distillation unit. The ﬁrst column splits Liqueﬁed Petroleum Gas
(LPG) and petrol (<C9) to the top and the rest in the bottom. The second column splits
kerosene (C10-C14) and the diesel and remaining fatty acids in the bottom. The third column
has diesel (>C15) in the top and the fatty acids in the bottom to be recycled.
As speciﬁed earlier, the columns operate with the UNIFAC property model. This is done since
the isomers are inputted using this method, which means that other property model e.g. EoS
models, does not have isomer data.
The columns are designed in PRO/II by a three-step process with each step increasing the
complexity of the unit.
• The ﬁrst step is a stream calculator.
The split is 100% between the light and heavy keys. The light and heavy key is deﬁned.
The dew point is calculated for the top product and the bubble point is calculated for the
bottom product.
• The second step is a shortcut distillation column (SCD).
SCD uses the light and heavy key components, a desired reﬂux ratio, speciﬁcations for
the top and bottom streams, the product estimate streams from the stream calculator and
selected type of condenser.
• The third step is a rigorous distillation column.

CHAPTER 7. OPTIMIZATION
7
Optimization
Optimization of the basecase process design is desired when looking at the economical evaluation.
Optimization can be done in multiple ways. The chosen options are:
• Heat integration
A heat exchanger network system (HENS) that utilizes the crossing of cold and hot streams
in the systems, minimizing the added energy to the system.
• Parameter optimization (sensitivity analysis)
The goal is to ﬁnd the optimal operating condition for the given unit operation.
7.1
Heat integration
A heat integration is done in two parts; a pinch analysis and the heat exchanger network system.
The pinch analysis determines how much of the heat duty from the cold and hot streams that
can be integrated and how much excess heating/cooling is needed when the integration is done.
The HENS is the pairing of cold and hot streams. In ﬁgure 5.1, the heat duties, Q, for each
streams can be found. Not all streams have been used for the heat integration. Figure 7.1 shows
the streams used. Note that the heat exchangers previously named by a ’C’ for cooling, now
operates as heating and are therefore denoted with ’H’ and vice versa. The HENS consists of
two hot stream and three cold streams, but can theoretically consist of all the streams.
Figure 7.1: The stream used for HENS
Composite curves, illustrated in a T-Q plot, displays how much heat can be integrated. The
composite curves are found by cascading hot and cold stream heat duties in intervals. E.g. both
C1 and C2 can be used in the interval of 81.19 to 97oC. The composite curve are shown in
ﬁgure 7.2, which is based on the hot stream temperatures. The minimum temperature diﬀerence
between a hot and a cold stream was set to 20oC. However for a heat integration, the cold
streams can not be above the hot streams. The cold composite curve is shifted, as illustrated in
ﬁgure 7.3. The point where the two lines then meet are called the pinch point. In ﬁgure 7.3, the
cold composite curve is now based on the cold stream interval temperature and the temperature
diﬀerence in the pinch point is therefore 20oC. The area between the composite curves is the
amount of heat that can be integrated in a HENS. The remaining is excess heating and cooling.

The pairing of the hot and cold streams are done in two networks. One above the pinch and
one below the pinch. It is not possible to cross two streams across the pinch point (one stream
above and one below). The pinch point is found to be 326.5oC. When doing the HENS, several
rules must be followed.
• One network above and one network below the pinch point.
Therefore, no heat exchanging over the pinch point.
• Do not suggest a heat exchanger with ∆T < ∆Tmin. Match only if;
– FCp(hot) < FCp (cold) above Pinch
– FCp(hot) > FCp (cold) below Pinch
• The excess heating/cooling should match the pinch analysis. Thus;
– NH > NC above Pinch: Split cold streams
– NH < NC below Pinch: Split hot streams
Following the cited rules, the HENS have been developed and can be found in ﬁgure 7.4. Looking
at the shifted composite curves, the excess heating and cooling is matching the pinch analysis.
Some of the cold streams have been split due to a too high FCp. The HENS can be expanded,
but it is limited in number of streams used for cooling (increasing temperature). Potentially the
H3 heat exchanger could be added, while H1 only adds very little energy in a low temperature
range (ﬁgure 5.1).

Flowsheet optimization
The operating conditions of the unit operations are to be examined with a tool in PRO/II called
’Case Study’. A series of cycles can be run to change the given operating condition with every
cycle, resulting in the eﬀect of the changes displayed graphically. E.g. changing the pressure and

the optimization is implemented and the results of the new product compositions and ﬂowrates

All optimization contributes to less waste and more product. The ﬂash 1 pressure does not
signiﬁcantly increase the composition in the top stream above 6 bar, however the ﬂowrate drops
from 1100 kg/hr at 6 bar to about 1020 kg/hr at 40 bar.
The F2 optimization is crucial. Like with ﬂash 1, the composition does not signiﬁcantly increase
above a certain point (5oC). But the ﬂowrate decreases drastically above 30oC. Sticking with 5oC
also lowers the lost hydrocarbon, but also minimizes the heating done by H3. The hydrocarbons
lost in the waste water stream is minimized greatly due to the large decrease in hydrocarbons
entering ﬂash 3. The ﬂash 5 optimizations are also crucial since a lot of excess H2 are removed
at the new operating conditions. This enables more H2 for recycle, thus decreasing the raw
material costs. The optimizations also increases the recycle ﬂowrate of fatty acids, increasing
the amount of diesel produced. The top composition does not signiﬁcantly change, but the
bottom composition does with increasing pressure. However, above 100oC the top composition
begins to decrease.
The product stream of kerosene is decreased, however this is due to better separation of n-C14
and i-C15 in T2 with increasing pressure. This is also one of the reasons for the higher production
of diesel in T3. The reﬂux ratio in T3 does not signiﬁcantly change the top composition, but the
total heat duty of the condenser and reboiler increases 5 times from a reﬂux ratio of 1 to 10. The
optimal pressure is found to be 0.35 bar. This calls for a new test of reﬂux ratio. At 0.35 bar, the
bottom composition and ﬂowrate increases with increasing reﬂux ratio, however not signiﬁcantly
above a reﬂux ratio of 7. The isomerization reactor was also subject for optimization, since the
kinetic model is temperature dependent. However, no optimization of the temperature could be
found. The reason is that the temperature term in the calculation of the reaction rate equals
0.999 ≈1 in the entire interval, since the activation energy is so low.
The production of diesel increases with roughly 3320 kg/hr, yielding in a total increase of 27.5
ton/year. The petrol production is slightly increased with 0.52 ton/year. All optimizations are
presented graphically in Appendix G, ﬁgure 10.36-10.44. The product ﬂowrates can be found in

CHAPTER 9. CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
9
Conclusions and future work
Several feedstocks were selected for use in production of green diesel from hydrotreatment. The
examined feedstocks of palm oil, palm stearin, PFAD and beef tallow were compared. From
the composition and price of these, palm stearin was chosen as feedstock. A process design of
palm stearin to green diesel, utilizing the hydrotreatment path, is generated in PRO/II. Following
ﬂowsheet schematics from the industry, the process design consists of ﬁve sections; a pretreatment
of the oil, hydrotreatment, a separation phase, isomerization of generated hydrocarbons and
a separation phase to yield various products. The generated process design of palm stearin
produces 412344 ton product annually from 602085 ton palm stearin, whereas 397416.9 ton is
diesel. The remaining products are LPG, petrol and kerosene. The product purity, within the
speciﬁed carbon range, is 81.6, 90.1, 59.1 and 97.2 wt% for LPG, petrol, kerosene and diesel,
respectively. The petrol stream contains 14.5% pentane and the kerosene product contains about
29.8% C15 and 10% C16, thus explaining the lower yields.
Following the process design, equipment sizing and cost estimation were done. The sizing
required several assumptions, which may have a large impact on sizing in reality, both positively
and negatively. Assumptions about catalysts, vacuum ﬂash drums and the distillation column
diameters have been done. However, the result of the equipment sizing is within realistic ranges.
The equipment cost is done with the CAPCOST_2017 tool with the main contributors to
equipment cost being the compressors for H2, the distillation columns and the reactors. The
equipment cost of approximately 30 million USD is considered to be realistic for a production
plant of this size. Based on this, the total capital investment is 193.727 million USD.
An economical evaluation concludes the economical feasibility of the design. The ROI, PBP,
NPW and DCFR was found to 13 %/y, 4.5 y, 50.47 million USD and 13.3 %/y, respectively.
The evaluation also revealed that the economical potential is closely dependent on the price
of the feedstock, since the price of the raw material is the main expense. This then makes the
price of green diesel more sensitive as producers have to maintain a proﬁt. Further research on
alternative feedstocks must be examined in order to lower the production costs. The suggested
PFAD is a great option to consider since the price is low compared to palm stearin [23, 24].
The process design was attempted optimized with heat integration and ﬂowsheet parameter
optimization in order to decrease the operating costs and increase yield. The heat integration
reduced with required heating in the used streams with 90.87% and the required cooling with
65.88%. Additional streams could be added. This is a reduction from 10.388 to 3.373 million
USD in heating per year. And a reduction from 4.224 to 3.298 million USD for cooling per year.
Cooling price includes condenser cooling.
The ﬂowsheet optimization yielded several optimizations, which resulted in increased green diesel
production from 47997 kg/hr to 51317 kg/hr. The utility eﬀects of the process optimization on
the economical parameters was not calculated, however temperature optimizations in F2 and F5
yields a lower duty in heat exchangers H3 and C6. The streams through these heat exchangers
are quite large, which makes the assumption of a lower utility cost highly likely. However, the
changed pressure in T2 and T3 along with the higher reﬂux ratio in T3 might cancel out the
lowered utility costs from H3 and C6.
The production plants environment impacts were analyzed with an sustainability analysis, which
determined that climate change (CO2 output), ozone depletion and land transformation were
the biggest impacts of the process with ozone depletion being the biggest. Electricity production
impacts were also examined, unfortunately parameters for the Netherlands, besides CO2 impacts,
were not found.

CHAPTER 9. CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
Several improvements can still be done to the simulation and the process in the future. More
precise modelling equals more precise economical evaluation. The largest expense in the process
design is the pretreatment where 10% of the raw material is wasted. Better modelling of the
pretreatment will, if not yield more product, give a more correct result of the process. This
requires data on the EDTA complexes, so the reaction between the phosphor and EDTA can be
modelled The operating conditions for the centrifuges must also be determined.
A kinetic model or equilibrium data of the hydrotreatment is also desired. Tirando et al [31]
presents several models, but only a few have relevance and they are not ﬁtted to the operating
temperature. The model also depends on the feedstock as the feedstock varies in fatty acid
composition, therefore the model must include as many fatty acids as possible in order to compare
feedstocks. The proposed isomerization reactions are also to be revised. Experimental data from
several sources [33, 44, 46] is more complex than has been accounted for in this design.
Another big implement is the implementation of the H2 recovery unit. The steam reforming
reaction is pretty well know [60], however the pressure-swing adsorption modelling is rather
diﬃcult. Other methods for CO2 removal is available with cryogenic distillation and acid scrubbing
[47]. The use of PRO/II makes it diﬃcult to model the distillation column as one column, which
is most ideal to replicate reality. This is however a key point to implement as it will make utility
costs for the column easier to calculate.
According to Carlsson et al. [55], an equation of state property model is ideally used when
working with the compounds in this process. At least when the pressure is above 10 bar, however
this requires the isomers to be implemented using other methods, since the UNIFAC method is
not compatible with predictive-SRK.
Future work also includes the examination of alternative feedstocks as mentioned above. PFAD
and beef tallow have been discussed as some of them. This requires a more accurate composition
of beef tallow. PFAD usage requires changes to the process design, or maybe another process
design, since PFAD consists of only fatty acids, which results in very little propane production.
Essentially changing the ﬂash operating conditions and sizes.
