Evolution of common cold coronaviruses in humans
by Amanda Gammelby Qvesel, s190171
In the light of the ongoing SARS-CoV2 pandemic, the international research commu-
nity has focused increasingly on understanding the biology of coronaviruses, including
under what circumstances they come to infect new host species such as humans.
Focus has mainly been on SARS-CoV2, but other coronaviruses have also been inves-
tigated thoroughly. This study contributes further to the research on coronaviruses
that generally only cause mild disease - common cold coronaviruses. These have been
introduced into humans from other animals, and this study tries elucidate the evolu-
tion of these pathogens with a speciﬁc focus on the evolution following cross-species
transmission events.
More speciﬁcally, I investigate recombination as a mechanism for viral evolution
and suggest an approach for handling recombination in a data set of viral sequences.
I further investigate how the immunogenicity of viral surface molecules changes over
time during the process of adaptation to the new host. This is done using bioin-
formatic methods of phylogenetic analysis, including ancestral sequence reconstruc-
tion, machine learning-based methods of immunogenicity prediction and a maximum
likelihood-based method of selection analysis.
I managed to reduce the occurrence of recombination in some of the data sets, and
I ﬁnd that the immunogenicity of the viral surface molecules appears to be decreasing
over time in selected regions. However, in some molecules, increased immunogenicity
is seen in minor regions, indicating that higher immunogenicity might sometimes
be beneﬁcial, supposedly in a trade-oﬀbetween immunogenicity and ﬁtness during
the process of adaptation to a new host. These results indicate how coronaviruses
develop over time, and together with information on the points in time where the
reconstructed ancestral viral sequences existed, we might in the future gain a more
thorough understanding of common cold coronavirus evolution. This information can
potentially help us prepare for the next cross-species transmission event.

1
Introduction
Since the emergence of severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS) coronavirus 2
(SARS-CoV2) in 2019, coronavirus evolution has been an area of increasingly intense
investigation.
One might have thought that by 2021, the threat from infectious diseases would
have vanished - at least in developed countries - due to advances in the ﬁelds of
antimicrobial agents, vaccinology and molecular biology in general combined with
improved hygiene. However, the threat persists in all parts of the world, and will
probably continue to do so in the future.
Therefore, to increase pandemic preparedness, we must continue to strive for
knowledge about agents of infectious diseases together with ways of preventing and
treating these.
This includes investigating infectious diseases and their causative
agents, and among these are coronaviruses. Important topics include what enables
these viruses to cross species barriers, and how they spread and evolve in human
populations over time.
In this thesis, the evolution of common cold coronaviruses in humans and their
relatives in other animals is investigated by a number of phylogenetic methods. This
might help us understand the origin of the viruses and how they evolve in the human
host over time.
These insights can hopefully help us in dealing with the ongoing
SARS-CoV2 pandemic and prepare us for handling emerging infectious diseases in
the future.
1.1
Aims
The overall goal of this master’s project has been to investigate human common cold
coronavirus evolution with a focus on cross-species transmission.
The speciﬁc aims have been:
• To collect relevant human coronavirus sequences from public domain and align
them using a suitable tool.
• To optimize the reconstruction of recombination-free alignments.
• To determine time-points of cross-species transmission events.
• To analyse the phylodynamics after the cross-species transmission events.
• To perform ancestral reconstructions of relevant viral proteins.
• To investigate viral surface protein immunogenicity over time.
• To analyse selective pressure in viral proteins.
By doing this, I hope to shed light on how common cold coronaviruses evolve to infect
humans and how they adapt to humans over time.

3
Background
This section introduces basic coronavirus (CoV) biology with a focus on the relevant
human coronaviruses (HCoVs). Furthermore, important methodological concepts and
considerations are introduced.
2.1
Coronaviruses
CoVs are enveloped, positive sense, single-stranded RNA (ssRNA) viruses that belong
to the family Coronaviridae in the order Nidovirales (Corman et al. 2018; ICTV 2020).
Four diﬀerent genera of CoVs have been deﬁned; alpha-, beta- gamma- and delta-CoVs.
Where alpha- and beta-CoVs are only found in mammals, gamma- and delta-CoVs
are most commonly found in birds, while some can also infect aquatic mammals and
swine (Su et al. 2016).
2.1.1
Molecular biology and evolution of coronaviruses
Compared to other viruses, CoV genomes are remarkably long, spanning between 26
and 32 kilobases (kb). This is longer than observed for any other RNA virus (Su
et al. 2016). The maintenance of this large genome by CoVs is possible partly due
to their relatively low mutation rate explained by the presence of RNA-processing
enzymes: CoVs harbour a 3’-5’ exoribonuclease and possibly also an endonuclease to
correct erroneous nucleotides incorporated by the RNA-dependent RNA polymerase
(RDRP). This proof-reading system is believed to have played a role in the expansion
of the genome of viruses in the Nidovirale order (Lauber et al. 2013). Mutation rates
of CoVs are in the order of 1 × 10−3-1 × 10−4/year/site, with variations along the
diﬀerent regions of the genome and possibly when adapting to a new host (Forni et al.
2017; Su et al. 2016; Liu, Kuo, and Shih 2020).
Apart from gradual accumulation of mutations along the genome contributing
to CoV evolution, viral replication gives rise to subgenomic RNA molecules during
synthesis of structural and accessory proteins.
Template switching between these
RNA molecules via homologous recombination may give rise to recombinant viruses if
a cell has been infected with two or more diﬀerent isolates (Su et al. 2016; Fung and
Liu 2019). By breaking linkage disequilibrium, this process can confer an evolutionary
advantage for the virus (Barton 1995). These recombination breakpoints appear to
be distributed randomly along the genome initially, but the process of selection gives
rise to so-called ’recombination hot spots’ where recombination breakpoints are found
more commonly than expected (Banner and Mc Lai 1991).
Apart from recombination, CoV genomes evolve via gene acquisition and loss, and
redundancy in the function of diﬀerent proteins has allowed for exploration of novel
molecular interactions as explained for some individual viruses below. All these fea-
tures give the group of viruses a unique ability to diversify; new CoV genotypes arise
- and once in a while, also new species (Woo et al. 2009; Su et al. 2016). By investi-
gating viruses from non-human hosts and comparing these viruses with those isolated

The organization of the CoV genome and the CoV particle
The CoV genome is similarly organized in the diﬀerent viruses. In all cases, it is
capped, polyadenylated and unsegmented, and two large open reading frames (ORFs),
ORF1a and ORF1b take up approximately two thirds of the genome from the 5’ end
(refer to ﬁgure 2.1) (Fung and Liu 2019). Together, these ORFs give rise to the viral
replicase complex. ORF1a encodes to the polyprotein (pp) pp1a, while ORF1a and
ORF1b together give rise to pp1ab via a -1 ribosomal frameshift. These polyproteins
are post-translationally processed to 16 non-structural proteins (nsps), nsp1-nsp16
(Forni et al. 2017).
Figure 2.1: Schematic overview of a HCoV genome, here SARS-CoV2. The ORF1a gives rise
to nsp1-nsp10, and continued translation of ORF1b gives rise to the additional nsps. Outside of
ORF1ab are the ORFs encoding the structural proteins as well as accessory proteins that vary
between the diﬀerent HCoVs. Adapted from Rastogi et al. 2020
Downstream of ORF1a and ORF1b, ORFs encoding the structural proteins of the
virus are found. Common for all CoVs are ORFs encoding the spike protein (S), the
envelope protein (E), the nucleocapsid protein (N) and the membrane protein (M).
Together, these proteins give rise to the enveloped, spherical CoV particle measuring
between 100 and 160 nm. The S glycoproteins embedded in the viral membrane create
a crown-like structure visible during electron microscopy (refer to 2.3) (Cui, Li, and
Shi 2019; Su et al. 2016; Masters 2006). The N protein is associated with the RNA
strand, and the E protein is found in the envelope, albeit in low amounts compared to
the M protein that constitutes the majority of the viral envelope (Fung and Liu 2019).
The S protein embedded herein is a trimeric type 1 transmembrane protein with a
large ectodomain (refer to ﬁgure 2.2). It is comprised of two diﬀerent units; S1 and S2.
S1 contains the receptor-binding domain (RBD) and S2 contains the transmembrane
region and the fusion peptide mediating entry into the host cell (Fung and Liu 2019).
Apart from these proteins, diﬀerent CoV genomes encode diﬀerent accessory pro-
teins contributing to the virulence of the viruses in the context of speciﬁc hosts, e.g.
by interaction with the host immune system (Forni et al. 2017), as explained in the
sections concerning the speciﬁc HCoVs. Receptor usage and cell-tropism, as well as
spike-receptor interactions vary greatly among CoVs, bearing witness of convergent
evolution and potential recombination events (Forni et al. 2017).

The viral replication cycle
Upon binding of the RBD of the S protein to the relevant receptor, the viral particle
undergoes receptor-mediated endocytosis. In the endosome, the viral and endosomal
membrane fuse in a process where the S protein is cleaved and thus activated. This
is catalyzed by host proteases and the contents of the viral particles are released to
the cytoplasm of the host cell (Fung and Liu 2019).
The genes encoding pp1a and pp1ab are translated via cap-dependent translation.
Nsp12 is the viral RDRP, while other nsps have autoproteolytic activities and others
again are proteins that take part in the formation of the replication transcription
complex, e.g. by inducing rearrangements of the host cell membrane (Fung and Liu
2019). Negative-sense antigenomes produced from the genomic RNA works as the
template for synthesis of genomic RNA for the viral progeny by the RDRP. Also the
aforementioned sub-genomic RNA fragments can serve as templates for synthesis of
positive-sense RNA fragments. This transcription is partly mediated by a battery of
host factors, e.g. glycogen synthase kinase 3 and RNA-binding proteins of the host
(Fung and Liu 2019).

capsid protein, and the other structural proteins are embedded in the lipid bi-layer membrane.
The transmembrane structural proteins like S, M and E are translated and folded
in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and can be modiﬁed by glycosylation. These post-
translational modiﬁcations might aﬀect the virus-host interactions and replication
(Fung and Liu 2019). When the components of the viral particle have been produced,
assembly occurs along the ER and the Golgi apparatus. The M protein assembles to
provide a scaﬀold together with the E protein that induces curvature of the membrane,
causing viral particles to bud oﬀinto a vesicle in the ER or the Golgi apparatus. These
particles are transported in vesicles via the secretory pathway and are then exocytosed,
ready to infect new host cells.
2.1.2
Human coronaviruses
Most known CoVs have been isolated from bats where they can be isolated from feces,
and similarly, some CoVs infecting livestock most commonly cause gastrointestinal
symptoms, like the porcine viruses transmissible gastroenteritis virus and swine acute
diarrhoea syndrome coronavirus. This points to the original tropism of CoVs to be
of the gastrointestinal system. However, the CoVs that have been found to infect
humans mainly cause respiratory symptoms (Corman et al. 2018; Su et al. 2016).
Seven CoVs have been found to cause disease in humans so far: HCoV-NL63, HCoV-
OC43, HCoV-HKU1, HCoV-229E, Middle East respiratory syndrome (MERS)-CoV,
SARS-CoV and SARS-CoV2 (Corman et al. 2018; Banerjee, Mossman, and Baker
2021).
Recently, however, a new CoV was found to have infected a patient who
displayed respiratory symptoms in Malaysia. The virus has not been conﬁrmed as
the cause of the respiratory symptoms as of yet, but time will show if it is to become
known as the eighth CoV to cause disease in humans (Vlasova et al. 2021). Two of
the well-established HCoVs belong to the alpha-CoV genus, namely HCoV-229E and

HCoV-229E
HCoV-229E was isolated in cultures inoculated with samples from humans in 1967,

the estimate is therefore not conclusive, but merely indicative. Other researchers have
later investigated the MRCA estimate for BCoV and HCoV-OC43, but the uncertain-

diﬀerent genotypes are observed to have evolved under diﬀerent selective pressures and
patterns of selection might therefore be diﬀerent for these (Ren et al. 2015). ORF5 of

HCoVs, but despite the diﬀerent origins, viroporins from e.g. HCoV-NL63 can rescue

genome also encodes the gene NS2a, a phosphodiesterase, and enzymes with similar
function have been found in MHV, MERS-CoV and Group A rotavirus, indicating
the importance of these enzymes that play a role in limiting the interferon response
in the host (Forni et al. 2017). The HE molecule of HCoV-OC43 shares a common
ancestor with the inﬂuenza C hemagglutinin molecule, but since HE is not critical
for HCoV-OC43 infection, the functional redundancy has been suggested to allow for
exploration of alternative HE binding sites (Zeng et al. 2008; Forni et al. 2017).
2.2
Methodological considerations
In chapter 3, the materials and methods used in this project are presented. This
section serves as an introduction to the considerations on these and related methods
to make clear why these speciﬁc methods were employed the way they were.
2.2.1
Alignment of viral sequences
Before any phylogenetic analysis of sequence data can be done, the sequences must
be aligned, so that homologous regions can be compared. From this alignment, if it
has been calculated in a suitable manner, evolutionary relationships can be inferred,
and we can make assumptions about structure and function of the genes and the
==PAGE:26==
10
Chapter 2. Background
proteins they encode. Often, the alignment is seen as a data set - something that
is representative of the truth. However, the choice of alignment algorithm is critical
and might change dramatically the conclusions drawn from the actual data set - the
sequences.
When aligning sequences, homologous nucleotides must be identiﬁed correctly, and
gaps, representing insertions and deletions (indels), must be placed wisely (Golubchik
et al. 2007). When choosing an alignment algorithm, one must therefore consider
parameters such as gap opening and gap extension penalties. Conventional alignment
methods like MAFFT, MUSCLE and T-COFFEE are either based on a progressive
approach (MAFFT, MUSCLE) or a consistency-based approach (T-COFFEE) (Scossa
and Fernie 2021).
These approaches require relatively low computational eﬀort, but they might not
be able to accurately capture sequence evolution as well as evolution-based alignment
approaches that are more computationally intensive (Scossa and Fernie 2021). For
example, in the case of HIV evolution, MAFFT, MUSCLE and T-COFFEE suggest
that the region V2 has been subject to several independent but overlapping deletions
(Löytynoja and Goldman 2008).
However, it is well-established that this variable
region evolves through small indels to escape neutralizing antibodies, not just through
gradual deletions of long sequences (Wood et al. 2009; Beretta et al. 2020).
If alignment methods considering viral phylogenies and explicitly diﬀerentiating
between insertions and deletions are employed instead, this is exactly what we see.
Instead of independent events in each sequence, the introduction of phylogenies into
the equation allows for reconstruction of the evolutionary history along each individual
branch (Löytynoja and Goldman 2008). Instead of over-aligning the sequences, tools
like PRANK’s phylogeny-aware algorithm uses a guide tree and a progressive approach
to make inference about indels based on outgroup information from the following
iteration (Löytynoja 2013).
For protein-coding sequences, performing a standard nucleotide alignment is not
optimal: Here, we disregard the information we have about which sites are most
likely to mutate (3rd position) or be conserved (2nd position) (Bofkin and Goldman
2006). Therefore, it is tempting to translate the nucleotide sequences into amino acid
sequences as part of the alignment process. These sequences evolve more slowly and
might therefore be easier to align, but by doing that, we disregard information found
at nucleotide level, due to the redundancy of the genetic code, where multiple codons
encode the same amino acid. The optimal way of aligning protein-coding sequences
is therefore to use a codon model that can discriminate between codons that encode
synonymous mutations and those that do not (Seo and Kishino 2009). Codon-based
substitution models are computationally expensive, but they allow for other types
of analyses, e.g. for analysis of selective pressure (Seo and Kishino 2009; Shapiro,
Rambaut, and Drummond 2005).
2.2.2
Recombination detection
When viruses evolve by recombination, their phylogenies are no longer strictly bifur-
cating: The evolution of diﬀerent regions of the genome can be described by diﬀerent
phylogenies. This is problematic when we want to e.g. estimate ancestral sequences
and times for zoonotic spill-overs: The ancestors we might try to date or determine the
sequence of might not be ancestral to all regions of the genome (Arenas and Posada
2010; Schierup and Hein 2000).
This is sometimes disregarded in whole-genome analyses, e.g. by Vijgen et al.
2005, but a number of tools have been developed to try to handle this challenge. These
==PAGE:27==
2.2. Methodological considerations
11
are generally either concerned with analysing small groups of sequences, looking for
similarities between pairs of these (e.g. 3SEQ (Boni, Posada, and Feldman 2007))
or analysing trees of diﬀerent regions of the genome by employing a sliding window
approach, for instance via Akaike Information Criterion(AIC)-based methods (e.g.
GARD (Kosakovsky Pond et al. 2006)).
However, also approaches using network
graphs have been made available. Most recently a Bayesian framework allowing for
analysis of pathogens like CoVs was developed by Müller, Kistler, and Bedford 2021.
A number of methods have been implemented in the tool RDP4, allowing for the
use of multiple methods in one go, but the program relies on a graphical user inter-
face and thus does not support an automatized workﬂow (Martin et al. 2015). While
GARD does not perform well on large data sets, 3SEQ can rapidly analyse several
hundreds of sequences by computing p values for potential breakpoints by means of
hypergeometric random walks, hereby quantifying the probability that a sequence, the
potential ’child’, is the result of a recombination event between two potential ’parent’
sequences (Boni, Posada, and Feldman 2007; Lam, Ratmann, and Boni 2017). 3SEQ
is operated via command-line and thus allows for automation of the workﬂow, result-
ing in a the less cumbersome analysis of the genomes. However, 3SEQ merely outputs
sites of breakpoints and the corresponding recombining and recombinant sequences -
it is up to the user to process this output and determine how the downstream analysis
should be performed.
Recently, CoV genomes were analysed for recombination by Boni et al. 2020. Here,
3SEQ was used to identify potential breakpoints, and the non-recombining regions
(NRRs) of the alignment between those were subject to further analysis: Those with
a length of 500 nucleotides (nt) or more were used to infer a maximum likelihood
tree. These trees were then compared by eye for phylogenetic incongruities. Boni
et al. 2020 thus look for both sequences that are placed diﬀerently in diﬀerent trees,
as well as entire trees that look diﬀerent from others. They use this analysis to either
remove sequences or NRRs from their alignment. While this approach of visualizing
phylogenies of a relatively small number of regions and comparing their topologies
seems like a suitable way to ﬁnd the unstable or rogue taxa (the ’jumping leaves’ in a
set of trees), we consider this method to be both labour-intensive and prone to biases.
Other tools work directly to compare trees, but e.g. RogueNaRok is concerned
with bipartitions, not how much a leaf jumps (Aberer, Krompass, and Stamatakis
2012). Furthermore, the optimality criterion of RogueNaRok has been criticized for
some use-cases (Wilkinson and Crotti 2017). Automatized ways of comparing trees
include tree distances, e.g. Robinson-Foulds distances between trees, comparing AIC
weights for trees constructed for diﬀerent alignments and performing incongruence
length diﬀerence tests (Planet 2006). However, our initial investigations of these meth-
ods revealed that very small rearrangements in the trees might lead to the conclusion
that trees are actually very diﬀerent. Other researchers have used combinations of
e.g. SplitsTree and manual analysis of phylogenetic networks to identify rogue taxa
(Trautwein, Wiegmann, and Yeates 2011), again highlighting the need for automa-
tized procedures. Therefore, part of the work on this project has been concerned with
a way of comparing phylogenetic trees to construct NRRs in an automatized way.
2.2.3
Reconstruction of ancestral viral sequences
Sequences from pathogens like DNA viruses and bacteria might sometimes survive up
hundreds or thousands of years in the remains of their hosts and hereby they provide
us with important clues about pathogen evolution (see for example Mühlemann et al.
==PAGE:28==
12
Chapter 2. Background
2018; Duggan et al. 2016 and Keller et al. 2019). However, we are not very likely to
ﬁnd well-preserved genomic material from RNA viruses due to the inherent instability
of RNA molecules - although it happens occasionally (Reid et al. 1999; Patrono et al.
2021). Fortunately, we can still make qualiﬁed assumptions about past days’ viruses
through ancestral sequence reconstruction (ASR). Here, present-day sequences and
the phylogenetic tree are used to make inferences about the sequences of internal
nodes in the phylogenetic tree.
The idea of reconstructing ancestral versions of homologous proteins is not new
but was presented by Pauling and Zuckerkandl in 1963 (Pauling and Zuckerkandl
1963). Historically, ASR has been done using a consensus-based approach employing
only sequence data, but this is extremely sensitive to sampling biases. Advances to
this includes a parsimony-based approach where also phylogenetic trees are included
(Thornton 2004). The maximum parsimony approach, however, is limited from by
the bias of the Fitch algorithm where all substitution rates are perceived as being
equal and where branch lengths are not considered (Scossa and Fernie 2021). Fur-
thermore, reaching far back into the evolutionary history is not possible unless the
sequences are highly conserved. This is due to the fact that when multiple mutations
happen on the same position in a phylogeny, multiple ancestral reconstructions might
be equally parsimonious (Thornton 2004). This challenge has been overcome by the
implementation of the maximum likelihood framework. Here, both substitution prob-
abilities and branch lengths are incorporated (Scossa and Fernie 2021). In programs
like PAML, the likelihood of every possible character state at every position can be
calculated for each internal node. By integrating over this, it is possible to calculate
the probability of the observed sequences given the hypothetical ancestral sequences
and other parameters of the model. Furthermore, diﬀerent model parameters can also
by optimized for under this framework (Yang 1997; Yang 2007).
However, when working under the maximum likelihood framework, we do not
recognize that the phylogeny is uncertain. Bayesian methods (see below) have been
developed to include this uncertainty in the analysis, but comparison has shown that it
does not lead to better reconstructions, unless focus is on estimating protein stability
of ancestral sequences (Hanson-Smith, Kolaczkowski, and Thornton 2010; Williams
et al. 2006). What might instead change the outcome of ASRs is the alignment that
serves as a starting point, since these, as mentioned, might change both tree topolo-
gies and the frequency and placement of indels (Vialle, Tamuri, and Goldman 2018).
This highlights the need for using a suitable alignment tool and a well-constructed
phylogenetic tree for ASR.
Some tools reconstruct every site in the alignment, regardless of where in the tree
the sequence is found, because indels are treated as missing data. This is seen in
e.g. PAML and in the R package ape. This might cause reconstructed sequences to
increase in length when going back in time (Selberg, Gaucher, and Liberles 2021).
However, this is handled by tools like PRANK. PRANK can output not only the re-
constructed ancestral sequences but also the alignment of sampled and reconstructed
sequences, allowing the user to follow evolution along diﬀerent branches of the phy-
logeny (Löytynoja and Goldman 2008; Löytynoja 2013).
2.2.4
Phylogenetic analysis using Bayesian modeling
In the analysis of sequence data, Bayesian methods can be used for a plethora of diﬀer-
ent analyses apart from ASR: Estimation of divergence times and evolutionary rates,
phylodynamics and phylogeographic analyses are just examples (Nascimento, Reis,
and Yang 2017). Over the recent years, these types of analyses and numerous features
==PAGE:29==
2.2. Methodological considerations
13
have been made widely accessible by the BEAST suite of programs (Nascimento, Reis,
and Yang 2017; Bouckaert et al. 2019).
In these analyses, prior knowledge and data are combined to yield posterior dis-
tributions for each parameter in the speciﬁed model, given the data as speciﬁed by
Bayes’ theorem:
P(θ|D) = P(θ)P(D|θ)
P(D)
(2.1)
From this, it is evident that the posterior distribution P(θ|D) is proportional to
the prior (P(θ)) and the probability of data given the parameter θ, namely P(D|θ).
This means that the model must be speciﬁed with great care, including the prior
distributions used as a starting point for the analyses. In these phylogenetic analyses,
this includes the substitution model and the tree prior, while the data is constituted
by an alignment and additional information about the sequences, e.g. collection dates
and species data. Then a Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) process is initiated,
starting from a point in our prior distribution of each parameter. Then a change is
proposed for each parameter based on a given step length, and if the value of the
posterior is higher than the previous parameter value, this step is accepted, and the
state is added to the chain - a series of values. If not, the step is only accepted with a
given probability. This allows for the chain to also move through less favorable areas
of the parameter space and thus escape local minima (Nascimento, Reis, and Yang
2017). This process is repeated until the desired chain length has been achieved.
Divergence dating is done by estimation of the tree height from the molecular
clock rate estimated.
This is done from sequences with collection times (at least
partially) known, and the tree height is thus the time diﬀerence between the most
recent sequence and the MRCA of all the sequences in the alignment as done by
Vijgen et al. 2005 and Boni et al. 2020. Similarly, reproductive numbers over time
can be inferred using speciﬁc skyline/skygrid models (Pybus et al. 2003; Stadler et al.
2013). This can be used to model spread of viral disease over time. These posterior
distributions of parameter values can be used to give e.g. 95 % credibility intervals.
2.2.5
Homology modelling using protein structures
Homology modelling of proteins describe the process of deducing a protein structure
based on the query protein’s sequence similarity to a related protein of which the
structure has been determined, e.g. by nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) or x-ray
spectroscopy. If protein sequences diﬀer only by a few substitutions, insertions and
deletions, the structure might be largely conserved (Kolodny et al. 2013). Therefore,
high-similarity homology modelling can yield structures of the query protein that in
quality would resemble those achieved by low-resolution NMR (Andrej and Kuriyan
1999). When performing homology modelling, a template protein with a known struc-
ture is initially selected using searching machine, e.g protein Basic Local Alignment
Search Tool (BLASTp), position-speciﬁc iterated BLAST or hidden markov models
(HMMs), in combination with our biological knowledge. By using HMMs, homology
modelling can be performed, even if the sequence similarity is as low as 15 % (Söding
2004). We can then use our knowledge about measures of protein structure quality to
select the desired template, while considering the trade-oﬀbetween template quality
and sequence identity between the template and the query proteins.
Once the template has been selected, the protein sequences are aligned and the
alignment can be reﬁned with respect to how indels are placed. For example, we prefer
to avoid insertions in the core of the protein, and we prefer especially amino acids like
cysteine and proline to be conserved. Following this, the backbone of the amino acid
==PAGE:30==

chain can be modelled from the matching residues, and to this model, loops and
amino acid side chains can be added by optimizing the phi and psi angles in relation
to the backbone atoms, before the entire structure is reﬁned and validated (Hameduh
et al. 2020).
Generally, the rotamer conﬁguration of surface exposed residues are
less accurately determined than the residues in the core where the dense packing
of the atoms limit the conﬁgurations that can be taken by the side chains (Chung
and Subbiah 1995). The template identiﬁcation can be performed using servers like
HHpred that uses proﬁle-based HMM to ﬁnd the optimal template and then forwards
the alignment to MODELLER where the remaining steps of the modelling is performed
(Zimmermann et al. 2018; Webb and Sali 2016). The resulting model can then be
used for e.g. prediction of protein-protein interactions as done for the case of SARS-
CoV2 by Hark Gan et al. 2021, visualization of structural features and prediction of
discontinuous immune epitopes as explained below.
2.2.6
Immunogenicity analysis of protein sequences
After reconstructing ancestral sequences of viral surface molecules, it is possible to
go to the laboratory and synthesize these molecules and test their immunogenicity in
vitro and compare it to that of contemporary viral molecules. However, advances in
immunoinformatics allow for in silico prediction of immune epitopes - the immuno-
genic part of an antigen - of both T cells and B cells.
B cell epitope prediction
B cells are critical components of our adaptive immune system with their ability to
produce antibodies that are either released from the cell or embedded in the B cell
membrane. These antibodies can recognize antigens, e.g. proteins from pathogens
infecting the body. These antibodies might have both linear and discontinuous epi-
topes. Linear epitopes consist of stretches of amino acid residues following each other
in the secondary structure of the antigen. Discontinuous epitopes consist of residues
that might be far away from each other in the amino acid sequence but are brought
together by the folding of the antigen so that they form a conformational epitope. Lin-
ear B cell epitopes can be predicted by BepiPred-2.0 from solely the antigen sequence
(Jespersen et al. 2017).
This tool is trained on crystal structures from complexes
between antibodies and antigens and uses a random forest classiﬁer to assign a score
between 0 and 1 to each residue, based on features such as hydrophobicity, volume
and polarity. This score indicates the probability of the residue being part of a B cell
epitope (Jespersen et al. 2017).
Conformational epitopes can instead be predicted by Discotope 2.0, a tool that
takes as input a 3D model of the antigen. Each residue is assigned a score based
on a propensity score (calculated from the residue and the neighboring ones) and
the number of neighboring backbone carbon atoms within a speciﬁed range and in a
speciﬁc direction (Kringelum et al. 2012).
T cell epitope prediction
T cell epitopes are peptides presented on major histocompatibility complex (MHC)
molecules, either MHC class I molecules found on all nucleated cells or MHC class II
molecules found on professional antigen-presenting cells (Hewitt 2003; Nielsen et al.
2010).

Here, the presented peptides can be recognized by T cell receptors on CD8+ or
CD4+ T-cells, respectively. Where MHCI molecules mainly presents peptides of intra-
cellular origin with a length of 9 amino acids, MHCII molecules usually present pep-
tides of varying length derived from extracellular proteins. In both cases, this antigen
presentation happens after intracellular processing, and which epitopes are presented
in a given individual depends on their human leukocyte antigen (HLA) allele composi-
tion (Reynisson et al. 2020b). This composition varies in diﬀerent populations around
the globe, and rational vaccine design therefore includes considering the HLA alleles
common in the target population to ensure a broad coverage. NetMHCpan 4.0a and

2.2.7
Analysis of selective pressures
When we have a codon alignment, each codon can be compared across sequences
and analysed for signs of either positive or negative selection.
By ﬁtting a codon
substitution model to the data set, we can ﬁnd the ratio between non-synonymous
and synonymous substitutions (the dN/dS ratio) for each site. If this ratio is below
1, this indicates that negative or purifying selection is working on this site, and if the
ratio is above 1, this indicates positive selection on this site. A ratio of 1 indicates
neutral selection (Nei and Gojobori 1986).
Purifying selection can be a sign of a
conserved residue with an important function, e.g. an active site in an enzyme that
would lose its function if the site was mutated, while positive selection can be a sign
of pressure from the immune system acting on this site. This site must therefore be
change to escape the immune system. We can then compare the likelihood (using
the AIC) of diﬀerent site models diﬀering in their assumptions of selection, e.g. by
using CodeML that supports comparison of a model with nearly neutral evolution and
positive selection (Yang and Nielsen 2002; Yang 1997; Yang 2007).

Materials and Methods
In this thesis, I investigate the evolutionary relationships between a number of human
as well as non-human CoVs. This chapter explains the data and the methodology
involved in my investigations.
3.1
Collection and preprocessing of viral sequences
Relevant HCoVs and non-human CoVs were identiﬁed with Forni et al. 2017 serving
as a starting point. Additional viruses were identiﬁed by means of BLAST (Altschul
et al. 1990). Full-genome sequences were collected from the NCBI Virus database
(Hatcher et al. 2017) by searching for the name of the virus. Initially, the criteria for
inclusion were:
1. Mention of a collection date (as a minimum, the year of collection)
2. A sequence with a length of more than 25.000 nt and
3. Mention of the host
A few cases where the host could be inferred from the literature or the Genbank entry
were also included, whereas sequences where the literature or the Genbank entry
indicated that the virus was a result of genetic engineering were excluded. For the
HCoVs, sequences isolated from non-human primates were excluded.
Files containing information on the downloaded sequence entries were collected
together with the sequences. These were used to programmatically modify the fasta
ﬁles containing downloaded sequences to include only the accession number, the host
organism and the sampling date in the headers. For entries with only year or year
and month of collection known, the sampling date was set to be the mid-point of the
given year or month.
3.2
Alignment of full genome viral sequences
Initially, sequences were grouped by the HCoVs in relation to which they were sampled.
These groups of sequences were aligned using MAFFT v7.453 by employing the auto
mode. By visual inspection, the resulting alignments were deemed to be inaccurate
in their description of indels.
Therefore, the indel-aware alignment tool PRANK
v.270427 was employed to align the sequences. Since some data sets contained viruses
that were relatively distantly related to each other, the diﬀerent viruses were initially
aligned separately, e.g. murine CoV and HCoV-HKU1 were aligned separately. These
initial PRANK alignments were performed using a phylogenetic tree generated using
RAxML v.8.2.11 on the MAFFT alignment as guide tree, since this method is superior
to PRANK’s own neighbor joining (NJ) tree (Löytynoja Lab 2014). The guide trees
were generated by using data on the most accurate substitution models as determined
==PAGE:34==
18
Chapter 3. Materials and Methods
by jModelTest 2.1.10 v20160303, investigating 88 candidate models with an BIONJ-
JC tree as starting point (Posada 2008).
The alignments obtained were then combined using PRANK’s function for merging
alignments. All PRANK alignments were performed by employing the "-F" option to
specify that we prefer apparent insertions not to be aligned.
3.3
Detection of recombination breakpoints
A full 3SEQ screen was initially performed on each alignment to identify potential
recombination breakpoints using a p value table of size 1900x1900x1900 as done by
Boni et al. 2020.
These breakpoints were ﬁltered in an automatized way so that
only those between diﬀerent host groups were kept in the downstream analysis. This
means that e.g. recombination between murine CoV sequences was disregarded, just
like recombination between HCoV-HKU1 sequences isolated from humans was. This
is because we are primarily interested in the MRCA ancestral sequences that should
not be aﬀected by recombination within each virus species that has happened after
the time point of the zoonosis.
3.4
Construction of optimal NRRs
In between these breakpoints, the alignment should be free from recombination. From
the 3SEQ output, NRRs with a length of more than 500 nt were programmatically
extracted from the alignment, and RAxML v.8.2.11 was used to generate phylogenetic
trees for each of these by means of a rapid bootstrap analysis. The best-scoring max-
imum likelihood tree for each region was then used together with the corresponding
alignment for analysis of phylogenetic incongruities. For this, I developed a python
tool, leaf_NRRtia.py. This was done to improve the ’eye-balling’ method to iden-
tify rogue taxa employed by Boni et al. 2020.
It was done to make the selection
of relevant NRRs an automatized and unbiased process and at the same time opti-
mize for the amount of phylogenetic information in the resulting alignment, instead of
preferring simply the longest NRR. In pseudo code, the approach taken was as follows:
1. Load in trees and corresponding alignments
2. Root trees using minimum variance rooting to adjust for variance in branch
lengths (Mai, Sayyari, and Mirarab 2017)
3. Go through pairs of trees and evaluate leaf jumps. For a tree with n leaves,
the set of N=0.2·n nearest neighboring leaves were found for each leaf, and the
Jaccard similarity of these two sets was found. If this similarity was below 0.1,
the leaf was regarded as a rogue taxon between the two trees.
4. Go through the power set of alignments. For each combination of alignments,
ﬁnd the leaves that are regarded as rogue taxa for this combination of trees. All
these leaves will have to be removed from the NRRs for them to be compatible.
5. To evaluate the phylogenetic signal in the alignments, I deﬁned a measure; the
area of variable sites in the alignment. If an alignment e.g. has 20 variable sites
and 10 sequences, the area of variable sites is 10·20 = 200. This was done to
give suﬃcient penalty for removing sequences compared to adding additional
alignments, while also focusing on sites containing phylogenetic information,
==PAGE:35==
3.5. Bayesian analyses using BEAST2
19
something not considered by Boni et al. 2020.
For each combination in the
alignment power set, the total variable site area was calculated after removing
the rogue taxa from the alignment.
6. The combination in the power set with the highest score was selected as the
ﬁnal NRR, meaning that both NRRs and taxa could be potentially removed to
optimize the ﬁnal NRR.
The resulting alignment was used to construct a tree using RAxML v.8.2.11, and
the R2 value of root-to-tip distances was compared between this alignment and the
original alignment to evaluate eﬃcacy in removing recombination. This analysis was
done using TempEst v.1.5.3 (Rambaut et al. 2016).
3.5
Bayesian analyses using BEAST2
The alignments resulting from the NRRs can then be used in downstream analyses
of e.g.
estimation of TMRCA and phylodynamics using BEAST2.
Furthermore,
during the course of this project, the BEAST2 package Recombination was published,
enabling BEAST2 to explicitly handle the issue of recombination. I attempted to do so
using BEAST2 v2.3.6 and XML-ﬁles were constructed using BEAUti v2.6.4. However,
due to time constrains, I did not have time to optimize the models for convergence,
but the approach used for the initial analyses is described in the following:
Each alignment was loaded in an unpartitioned way, and the tip-dates were read
in. Packages SSM v1.1.0 and Recombination v0.2.2 were installed to access standard
substitution models and the coalescent with recombination tree prior, respectively.
The model of evolution corresponding to the alignment as determined by jModelTest
was used as site model. In all cases with gamma-distributed rates, the number of
gamma categories was set to 4, and the gamma shape parameter was estimated, just
like the proportion of invariant sites, where relevant. The nucleotide frequencies were
speciﬁed as empirical for the data sets evolving according to the general time-reversible
model. The clock model employed was a strict clock in all cases, based on previous
analyses. Diﬀerent tree priors were investigated: Birth-death skyline serial, Mascot
and coalescent constant, as well as the coalescent with recombination. Examples of
the parameters for the priors used can be found in Appendix A.
The analyses were run by employing a single chain with a length of 100 million
states in an attempt to achieve an estimated sample size (ESS) of 200 for each param-
eter. Resulting log-ﬁles were analysed using Tracer v1.7.1, discarding the ﬁrst 10% of
the data points as burn-in (Rambaut et al. 2018). In the cases where the tree prior
was the coalescent with recombination, tree heights for diﬀerent parts of the alignment
were extracted from the resulting network ﬁles by means of a java script provided by
Nicola Felix Müller, Ph.D. (personal communication).
3.6
Alignment of protein-coding sequences and ASR
For ASR of selected coding regions, an alignment of the coding sequences is needed.
To ensure accurate gene borders, the coding sequences were retrieved independently
from the full genome sequences in an automatized fashion through the Genbank acces-
sion numbers corresponding to the sequence entries. Not all entries were annotated,
so some single gene data sets contain fewer entries than the full genome data sets.
Entries that by 3SEQ were found to have recombination breakpoints in the relevant
genes were excluded. Furthermore, only sequences with the length being a multiplum
==PAGE:36==
20
Chapter 3. Materials and Methods
of 3 were included. This is due to PRANK’s codon alignment option that only allows
for sequences of this length to be aligned. The spike proteins were aligned for all
data sets, and for the HCoV-OC43 and the HCoV-HKU1 data sets, also HE genes
were aligned. The "-F" option was employed to explicitly handle indels, and maxi-
mum likelihood trees were used as guide trees. The "-showanc" option was used for
the program to output ancestral sequence reconstructions for internal nodes. The
resulting phylogenetic trees were visualized with internal node names using IcyTree
(Vaughan 2017) and used to select the relevant ancestors for downstream analysis.
However, if BppAncestor employed by PRANK fails at reconstructing ancestral se-
quences, PRANK will infer approximate ancestral sequences that might be wrongly
labelled (refer to Github 2017). Where possible, I used the alignment output from
PRANK to infer the true correct labels of the relevant ancestral nodes by visualizing
the corresponding phylogenetic tree built using PhyML v.3.3.20190909. In one case
(HCoV-229E S), this was not possible due to the topology of the phylogenetic tree.
Therefore, PAML was instead used to reconstruct the MRCA of the human sequences
and the MRCA of sequences from camelids and humans. PAML cannot reconstruct
the deepest node in a given tree, but ﬁnding the MRCA of sequences from humans and
camelids was possible since the data set also contained viral sequences from bats that
could be used as outgroup. A codon alignment and a maximum likelihood tree were
used as input. "RateAncestor = 1" was speciﬁed in the codeml.ctl-ﬁle to reconstruct
ancestral sequences, and the resulting tree was visualized in IcyTree. The output ﬁle
’rst’ was used to extract the amino acid and the nucleotide sequence from the relevant
nodes using an automatized approach.
3.7
Homology modelling
Homology modelling of reconstructed ancestral HE and S protein sequences as well as
a number of contemporary ones was accomplished by using the HHpred to ﬁnd the op-
timal homologous protein with a determined structure using HMM-HMM comparison.
The analysis was performed against the PDB_mmCIF70 database using default set-
tings with the amino acid sequence used as input. In each case, one or more templates
were selected to ensure both a high sequence similarity with the query sequence and a
high quality protein structure among the templates. Then MODELLER was used to
perform the structural prediction, and the resulting .pdb ﬁles were saved for visualiza-
tion purposes. To evaluate the quality of the homology models, the MolProbity server
was employed to generate a Ramachandran plot for each model (Williams et al. 2018)
by using the default settings for geometry analysis. This yields the proportion of the
dihedral angles of the protein backbone that are in disallowed and favored regions.
3.8
Immunogenicity analysis
3.8.1
Prediction of discontinuous B cell epitopes
These predictions were performed on the structures resulting from homology modelling
of ancestral sequences as well as homology-modelled contemporary sequences from the
diﬀerent data sets. This was done using Discotope 2.0, using the default threshold for
epitope prediction of -3.7, a threshold that represents a trade-oﬀbetween sensitivity
and speciﬁcity (Kringelum et al. 2012).
This particular threshold is equivalent to
a speciﬁcity of 75 % and a sensitivity of 47 %. The PyMOL-scripts outputted by
the program were saved and used to illustrate identiﬁed epitopes on 3D structures in

PyMOL 2.5. These scripts were programmatically modiﬁed to hide residues that were
not aligned in the homology modelling process to avoid highlighting false positives.
3.8.2
Prediction of linear B cell epitopes
Immunogenicity was analysed by using BEpiPred 2.0 on reconstructed amino acid
sequences, namely the MRCA of the sequences isolated from humans (HMRCA) and
the MRCA of these and the neighboring clade found in another host (camelids, cows,
bats), the grand MRCA (GMRCA). It was also performed on all human sequences
isolated in 2010 or more recently. Resulting .csv ﬁles were downloaded and analysed
for epitope probability in the range of the sequences. In this analysis, the results from
the diﬀerent contemporary sequences were averaged to give a representative picture
of the immunogenicity of contemporary sequences that could be compared with the
HMRCA and the GMRCA.
3.8.3
Prediction of T-cell epitopes
Prediction of T-cell epitopes was done using NetMHCpan 4.0a and NetMHCIIpan
4.0. The analysis was run on the MRCA, GMRCA and the sequences from humans
collected since 2010 as described above.
Furthermore, sequences with ambiguous
character sites were excluded. The analysis was performed on a number of speciﬁc
alleles with HLA-A, HLA-B and HLA-DRB1, namely those found to be common in
Europe by Sanchez-Mazas et al. 2017, see appendix B.
For the case of MHCI, the analysis was performed investigating the protein in a
sliding window with a length of 9 amino acids. For the MHCII, the default peptide
length of 15 amino acids was used. In both cases, the Excel ﬁle output format was
selected for downstream analysis to be performed in Python. For each peptide, a
consensus score was found by normalising the scores for the diﬀerent alleles with the
allele frequencies found in a large, German population denoted pop 8 in The Allele
Frequency Net Database (Gonzalez-Galarza et al. 2019) (refer to appendix B).
The scores of the diﬀerent contemporary sequences were averaged for them to
be compared with the scores of the GMRCA and the HMRCA. This yielded scores
assigned to the ﬁrst amino acid in the peptide investigated that were then mapped to
the corresponding position in the alignment.
3.9
Analysis of selection
Selective pressure was investigated using codeML from PAML v.4.9i. Codon align-
ments for relevant genes and trees constructed based on whole genome data were used
as input. Sites with ambiguous data were preserved, and NSsites was speciﬁed as 1
and 2, investigating models with neutral selection and with positive selection. The
models were then compared using the AIC:
AIC = −2 · lnL + 2K,
(3.1)
where K is the number of parameters of the model and lnL is the logarithm of the
likelihood of the model. For the proteins where model 2 favouring positive selection
was selected, relevant sites of selection were mapped to a structural model of the given
protein.

4.5
Analysis of selection
From the analysis of selection in PAML, it we ﬁnd that the S and HE proteins of
the beta-CoVs evolve according to a model with nearly neutral selection (model 1),
whereas the S proteins of the alpha-CoVs evolve under a model incorporating positive
selection (model 2), as seen in from the AIC weights presented in table 4.5. In all

Discussion
The aim of this master’s project was to analyze common cold HCoV sequences and
their relatives isolated from non-human animals and analyze these with a focus on the
evolution around the time of cross-species transmission. This included investigating
patterns of selection, how viral populations have developed over time, when the cross-
species transmission events took place, and how immunogenicity developed over time.
Before this could be done, it was necessary to align the sequences in a way where the
resulting alignment reﬂects the evolutionary history of the viruses; something that is
not trivial. In the process, it furthermore became clear that recombination must be
dealt with in a suitable manner, why part of this project also focused on optimizing
alignments without sequences marked by recombination.
5.1
Alignment of viral sequences and ASR
In my analysis, a MAFFT alignment was used to construct the phylogenetic tree
used as guide tree in PRANK, instead of the default NJ guide tree generated by
the program, since this is generally suboptimal (Löytynoja Lab 2014): PRANK is
sensitive to both errors in topology and large deviations in branch lengths of the
guide tree; especially the ’-F’ option employed here, where insertions are introduced
in a permanent manner, is sensitive to this (Löytynoja 2013).
However, using a
MAFFT alignment that is believed to not accurately reﬂect the evolutionary history
constitutes a bootstrapping problem that might result in alignment errors. However,
although the placement of insertions and deletions by MAFFT might not be correct,
we hope that the resulting phylogenetic tree will to a large extent represent the true
relationship between the sequences and aid PRANK in the subsequent inference of
indels.
However, while conducting the analyses of reconstructed sequences, it became clear
that the ancestors were incorrectly labelled in the output ﬁles from PRANK. This is
of course a problem in itself, but it can in most cases be corrected for. What is more
critical, however, is that this mislabelling occurs when BppAncestor, which PRANK
relies on, fails silently due to a misspeciﬁcation of a ﬁle format when reconstructing
ancestral sequences. I attempted to ﬁx this using a patch suggested by another user
(Github 2017), but the run-time for the compiled version of the program was too
high compared to the downloaded version, and I did not get it to work in the end.
Instead, PRANK in these cases approximates the ancestors, and the quality of these
inferred sequences in the output is not known. Therefore, an attempt should be made
to get this ASR inference running more smoothly, making sure the output is not just
approximate.

5.2
Detection of recombination
As described, many programmes and tools have been developed for detection of re-
combination. Here, 3SEQ was selected, since it can be run from command line and
run-time scales well with the number and length of the sequences. However, there is
a ﬂaw in the program: From an analysis of the adjacency matrix of the network of
’parents’ (recombining sequences) and ’children’ (recombinants) identiﬁed by 3SEQ, I
found that some sequences had been found by 3SEQ to be their own ’grand parents’
while numerous 3-, 4- and 5-cycles etc. were present in the network. The fact that
3SEQ cannot control for this is obviously a ﬂaw is the programme. Therefore, it was
not possible to remove all recombinant sequences, and we instead choose to remove
sequences that resulted from recombination between viruses with diﬀerent hosts, e.g.
bats and humans. We believe that this misidentiﬁcation of recombination patterns is
caused by multiple sequences or their ancestors being the result of recombination of a
sequence that has not been sampled with diﬀerent sequences in the data set (or their
ancestors), but maintaining this recombination signal in our data set might contribute
to the convergence problems in the Bayesian analysis.
5.3
Construction of optimal NRRs
Recombination between sequences in a data set makes it impossible to construct a
strictly bifurcating evolutionary tree. Not all parts of the recombinant sequences have
the same ancestor, and this might lead to distortion of the temporal signal of the
sequences. We therefore expect that the temporal signal of the data sets increase as
we remove recombinant sequences and regions; we take away some of the noise that
might have obscured this signal. In two out of three cases, the R2 value of root-to-tip
distances was increased markedly by employing leaf_NRRtia.py, the tool developed;
from 0.029 % to 31.7% in the HCoV-NL63 data set and from 14.6% to 66.2% in
the case of HCoV-OC43. For viral data sets of these sizes sampled over a relatively
short time-period, this is deﬁnitely a decent improvement. However, the tool failed
at improving the temporal signal in the case of HCoV-229E. No temporal signal was
present in the beginning in this data set, but this is not any diﬀerent from the case of
HCoV-NL63 before applying the tool. In both cases, this is surprising, since we would
expect more recently sampled sequences to be further away from the best-ﬁtting root
than older sequences.
When examining the phylogenetic trees of the diﬀerent NRRs of HCoV-229E,
however, we get a hint as to why the tool does not work: leaf_NRRtia.py is not
optimized for handling entire clades, small or large, jumping around while maintaining
their set of nearest neighbors, as is the case of HCoV-229E. This could be improved
by implementing a function in leaf_NRRtia.py that collapse taxons with a certain
topology and within a certain distance to each other into clades and then compare
the topology of the diﬀerent trees with respect to these clades. The runtime of the
programme scales with the number of NRRs that are to be compared, and since the
power set (scaling exponentially) of these is evaluated, it is best suited for data where
the number of NRRs and trees to be compared does not exceed ∼15. We only include
NRRs with a length of more than 500 nt, so with CoV genomes, there is a natural
limit to how many NRRs there can be. However, some alignments are very gappy,
giving rise to alignments that are considerably longer than the genome length itself,
and in these cases where the number of NRRs might be more than e.g. 15, one could
consider setting a higher threshold for the NRR length.

5.4
Bayesian inference
From the NRR analysis, it is clear that there is still recombination present in the data
sets; the R2 value of root-to-tip distances are still not 1, and we have only considered
recombination events between viruses isolated from diﬀerent hosts. This might be
part of the reason why the BEAST2 analyses have diﬃculties converging, regardless
of additional downsampling of the NRR data sets. When the Recombination package
was published as part of the preprint by Müller, Kistler, and Bedford 2021, however,
we then hoped that it would allow us to actually handle recombination explicitly,
instead of trying to eliminate it from the data set; after all, recombination is a large
part of CoV evolution, and we are interested in gaining a better understanding of how
it has shaped the viruses we see today, instead of trying to eliminate the signal.
However, the package displayed a strange behaviour when it comes to estimating
the clock rate; the steps taken by the MCMC algorithm appeared to be very small
and not approaching any value. Furthermore, we observed some interplay between
the parameter network.recombinationNodeCount and the prior that could not be ex-
plained: When the network node count increases above a certain threshold, the prior
would take large jumps in parameter space. This behaviour is also seen in the output
presented as part of the tutorial on the package (Müller 2021). Therefore, it seems
like there is still some optimization to do on the package for it to give completely
reliable estimates.
5.5
Homology modelling of S and HE proteins
Most of the homology models constructed had a minor proportion of residues in the
Ramachandran-disallowed regions. This indicates that the models are far from op-
timal, but contrary to what one might have expected, the homology models of the
contemporary sequences are not found to be of a higher quality than those of the
reconstructed ancestors that are generally more distantly related to the templates
used for the modelling. Part of the explanation for the Ramachandran outliers might
be attributed to the templates; although I attempted to optimize for both resolution,
coverage of the query protein and general protein structure quality scores, the number
of relevant proteins to choose from is limited. For instance, 5I08 that was one of the
proteins used to model HCoV-HKU1 S has 0.8% Ramachandran outliers, and unfor-
tunately, we cannot expect homology models to just be better than the input used
to generate them. In all cases, the query coverage was also not 100 %. This is not
surprising, considering the diﬃculty of determining an accurate structure for trans-
membrane and cytosolic regions, meaning that the template sequences were shorter
than the query sequences. These unaligned regions gave rise to C-terminal regions
that were ﬂailing around in the protein structure. These regions were due to their
conformation were found to be heavily immunogenic by Discotope, but removed for
visualization purposes as they were deemed false positives.
5.6
Immunogenicity analysis
MHC class I epitopes are heavily relying on the amino acids in the so-called anchor
positions, positions 2 and 9 (Lund et al. 2005). The amino acids here bind in pockets
of the MHC molecule’s groove and are determinants of binding. This is why we in
these plots see many low scores interspersed with occasional higher scores. MHC class
II molecules also have anchor positions, namely positions 1, 4, 6, and 9 (Nielsen et al.
==PAGE:66==
50
Chapter 5. Discussion
2010). MHC class II molecules do not have the same ’closed groove’ structure as the
MHC class I molecule. This causes the peptide to be placed in the groove of the MHC
class II molecule while potentially extending to both sides and therefore, the part of
the peptide giving rise to the signal (the binding core) is not known on beforehand
(Nielsen et al. 2010). This means that diﬀerent peptides evaluated here can still have
the same binding core, possibly explaining why the MHC class I and MHC class II
plots look diﬀerently.
It would generally be beneﬁcial for the interpretation of immunogenicity evolution
to have estimates of the time of both the GMRCA and especially the HMRCA. We
might suspect that in the cases where the immunogenicity is very similar in the HM-
RCA and the contemporary sequences that this reﬂects a sparse sampling or just a
high degree of similarity in contemporary human sequences and that the reconstructed
HMRCA is far from the true HMRCA that existed just after the cross-species trans-
mission event.
For example, the HCoV-NL63 S protein appears to be largely unchanged in T cell
immunity since the HMRCA, but larger changes took place from the GMRCA to the
HMRCA (refer to e.g. ﬁgure E.4). This is also seen in the prediction of discontinuous
B cell epitopes of the same protein (refer to table 4.4). Therefore, including sequences
sampled either at other times or locations than those included in the data set might
serve to give a more accurate estimate of the MRCA sequences.
One could hope that the ongoing pandemic prompted research communities to
dig into preserved samples that might contain common cold CoVs or just investigate
isolates for these viruses when suspecting a viral pneumonia in humans. Furthermore,
the intense sampling of bat sequences that is currently going on might also contribute
to further shed light on the evolution of CoVs.
This very high degree of similarity between the HMRCA and the contemporary
sequences is mainly seen in the plots of T cell immunogenicity. It thus seems like these
require greater changes in the sequence before immunogenicity changes are visible in
these cases than the B cell epitopes that are more sensitive to mutations. This is for
example seen in the three plots of HCoV-229E S immunogenicity. Considering how
linear B cell epitopes are predicted (i.e. based on stretches of amino acids with e.g. low
hydrophobicity, and not on protein motifs that rely on speciﬁc anchor positions), this
is not surprising; a stretch of potentially immunogenic amino acids might be abrogated
by one mutation in the case of a linear B cell epitope, whereas changes outside the
anchor positions might not have a major eﬀect on the T cell epitope probabilities.
The similarities between the HCoV-OC43 GMRCA and contemporary sequences
could be a result of a recent transmission from cows compared to some of the other
viruses, but it might also indicate that BCoV is just not that diﬀerent and more im-
munogenic, compared to the human virus.
From the immunogenicity analyses, we notice a number of diﬀerent patterns: In many
areas, immunogenicity appears to be reduced over time. This is to be expected when
the virus is under selective pressure to escape recognition by the immune system. This
is, for example, seen in the case of HCoV-OC43 HE, where changes especially take
place around alignment position 150-200 for both linear B cell epitopes and T cell
epitopes. This part of the molecule is in the RBD, and since this protein plays no
role in the entry mechanism of the virus in contemporary viruses, it is not surprising
that a we see a decrease in immunogenicity here (refer to ﬁgure 4.6 in particular):
This part of the molecule is still very exposed, but there might only be little pressure
to maintain any receptor-binding ability. In the region around position 170 and 200,
however, we can see from the alignment that PRANK has inferred indels (4 amino

5.7
Analysis of selection
In the PAML selection analysis, I found that data sets of S proteins from HCoVs
-229E and -NL63 evolve according to a model that includes positive selection. Does
this mean that there is no positive selection in the cases of the other viruses, both for
the S and the HE protein? Either that, or we fail to notice it, potentially because we
investigate the entire protein altogether: Another study (Kistler and Bedford 2021)
recently found evidence of positive selection in the HCoV-OC43 S protein, but they
did not include BCoV sequences in their data set. Instead, they split the protein into
S1 and S2 for analysis, and they further divided the data set by genotypes. This
might explain the diﬀerences seen. They further did not ﬁnd any signs of positive
selection in the human HCoV-NL63 S protein. This indicates that the selection takes
place at or around the jump between species in the case of HCoV-NL63. This might
also be the case for HCoV-229E around the species jump from bats into humans and
camelids; when excluding the bat sequences from the data set, model 1 is found to be
selected.

5.8
Adaptation to a new host species
Introduction of a virus into - and sustained transmission in - a new host species is
promoted by a number of factors, e.g. closer interactions between the original and the
new host species, mutations enabling the virus to bind to new entry receptors, and
mutations allowing for immune escape (Louz et al. 2005).
Following host switching, adaptation occurs in most cases (Parrish et al. 2008).
This is due to cross-species transmission representing a serious bottleneck for the virus.
Adaptation was seen, for example, during the course of the SARS-CoV outbreak in
2003, where orf8, a gene encoding an ion channel, evolved via larger mutations (Cui,
Li, and Shi 2019; Forni et al. 2017), and we currently see it for SARS-CoV2, where e.g.
the D614G mutation in the S protein that alters the ﬁtness of the virus has become
dominant (Plante et al. 2020).
Over time, diﬀerent mutations that themselves can be detrimental or deleterious
may play together in unpredicted ways to allow for the virus to adapt and regain a
==PAGE:69==
5.8. Adaptation to a new host species
53
level of ﬁtness that is comparable to that observed in the original host (Parrish et al.
2008).
If HCoV-OC43 indeed caused a pandemic upon introduction into humans as sug-
gested by Vijgen et al. 2005, this virus has gone from causing severe disease to causing
a common cold today. This prompts one to speculate if other epidemics/pandemics
have been caused by other common cold CoVs, before they transitioned into endemic
pathogens. Here, having TMRCA estimates would provide an important clue as to
whether this has been the case. What could have caused this transition from a virus
with pandemic potential to a harmless endemic one? The explanation might be found
in either the pathogen or the host; the virus could have become much less virulent
over time, or humans could have developed antibodies to prevent severe disease.
It might well be that the explanation can be found in changes in host immu-
nity: Lavine, Bjornstad, and Antia 2021 describe how common cold CoV immunity
to infection quickly wanes. This allows for continuous transmission of the virus that
might cause reinfections that are often mild and cleared more rapidly than primary
infections. Therefore, they argue, we would expect a virus like SARS-CoV2 to also
transition into endemicity where susceptible children experience mild courses of pri-
mary infections, while those in risk of more serious disease have been protected by
vaccinations or previous infections. This line of reasoning supports the hypothesis
that common cold CoVs over time have gone from serious diseases to harmless ones
while evolving, trying to continuously gain ﬁtness and escape immune recognition.

Conclusion and further
perspectives
The starting point for this project was the hypothesis coined by Vijgen et al. 2005
that HCoV-OC43 was introduced from cows to humans around 1889, causing a pan-
demic that had previously been believed to be caused by a strain of inﬂuenza.This
prompted us to question how diﬀerent common cold CoVs known today have evolved
in humans over time. In summary, I have investigated the four common cold CoVs
that are endemic among humans. More speciﬁcally, I have identiﬁed a suitable tool
for aligning the viral sequences and used this to perform ancestral reconstruction of
ancestors representing the virus before and after the introduction into the human
population. I have investigated how to best deal with recombination in these viral
sequences and developed a tool to minimize the occurrence of recombination and op-
timize alignments. Using this, I managed to increase the temporal signal in two out of
three data sets. I did not manage to estimate the TMRCA of the diﬀerent viruses, nor
the corresponding phylodynamics, but based on analysis of the reconstructed ances-
tral sequences and homology models, I have investigated immunogenicity over time.
I have further studied the selective forces acting on viral surface proteins and com-
pared the results from this analysis with the ones from the immunogenicity analyses.
Here, I identiﬁed residues in HCoV-NL63 and HCoV-229E S proteins that are under
positive selection, some of which are located in B or T cell epitopes. I further ﬁnd
evidence of increased immunogenicity in some HMRCAs compared to GMRCAs and
contemporary sequences, indicating that it is sometimes advantageous for the virus
to become more immunogenic in the process of adaptation to a new host.
The current pandemic has shown how basic research investigating e.g. mecha-
nisms of viral adaptation can become applied research before we know it. Further
understanding of how CoVs evolve in relation to their host might help us dealing with
the current pandemic as well as increase our overall pandemic preparedness. History
has shown how cross-species transmission events occur regularly, and we expect that
to also happen in the future.
