Abstract
This thesis presents an optimization framework for a semisubmersible floater and the IEA
Wind 15 MW reference wind turbine. The framework minimizes the total manufactoring
costs that satisfies the inequality constraint function. The inequality constraints contain a
set of geometrical, static, stability and natural frequency constraints. The approach is to
integrate the static part of the Quick Load Analysis of an floating offshore wind turbine,
and use an analytical approach to solve the hydrodynamics of the floater. The floater is
derived from the Morison equation, and therefore, is built on arbitrary straight cylinders.
The framework optimizes for the different geometric design variables for the floater, mean­
while having a linear catenary mooring design and integrated ballast system. The ballast
system is designed to be a physical parameter and influence the center of gravity.
The study shows that by adding a simplified hydrodynamic model, the optimization can
be done in a fast way. With that being said, the hydrodynamic model still needs to be
compared with more floaters, as the heave natural frequency was 20% off, compared to
results solved by an SoA software.

Introduction
The interest in offshore wind turbines is growing, as countries have set mutual sustainable
goals due to the 17 United Nation’s sustainable development goals. Within these goals,
renewable energy plays a vital role in the revolution of reducing global CO2 emissions.
The European Union sees wind energy as the primary source of electricity in Europe, and
it is expected to become a $1 trillion industry within the next two decades. The report by
(IRENA, 2019) states that offshore wind will have a capacity corresponding to approxi­
mately 1000 GW in 2050, compared to 23 GW in 2018. The predictions can be seen in
Figure 1.1, but it is necessary to state that the growth hinges on the government policies
and industry strategies (Birol, 2018).
Figure 1.1: Offshore wind power deployment would grow gradually to nearly 1000 GW of
total installed capacity by 2050. Source: (IRENA, 2019)
As stated in the World Energy Outlook report (ibid.), The European Union will use offshore
wind energy as the main renewable electricity in the future.

1.1
Types of offshore wind turbines
For offshore wind turbines, there are two main types; bottom­fixed or floating wind tur­
bines. Currently, the most common are bottom­fixed wind turbines, which means the wind
turbine has a substructure fixed to the seabed. For bottom­fixed wind turbines, there are
different substructures, such as jacket, gravity­based, tripod, tripile and the most com­
mon substructure, the monopile. The different substructures for floating wind turbines are
barge, semi­submersible, spar­buoy and tension­leg platform (TLP). Wind Europe has
made a report (Ramiréz, Fraile, and Brindley, 2020), which shows the accumulated use
cases of each substructure up to 2019. The use cases are shown in Figure 1.3.
Figure 1.3: Number of wind turbines with different substructures. Source: (Ramiréz,
Fraile, and Brindley, 2020)
When choosing between different substructures, the main factor is the price, which is
dependent on the environment where the wind turbine is installed. The different sub­
structures vary in price based on construction and manufacturing prices, but also material

1.2
The potential of floating offshore wind turbines
The reason why there are not many floating substructures in Figure 1.3 is due that the
technology has only been commercialised recently as the first floating offshore wind farm
commissioned in 2017. Offshore wind turbines are currently installed in water depths be­
low 60 m, which is the restriction for bottom­fixed offshore substructures and not the case
for floating substructures. As stated by WindEurope (2017), 80% of the wind resources
in Europe are in water depth deeper than 60 m; thus, to exploit the full potential of off­
shore wind turbines in deeper waters, it is crucial to do more research and development
in offshore floating substructures. Figure 1.4 shows the technology readiness for floating
offshore wind substructures from the report WindEurope (ibid.), which also indicates that
the technology has just become ready for spar­buoy, semi­submersible and barge, where
TLP is estimated to be ready around 2023.

Furthermore, the potential price for a floating substructure is estimated to have the lowest
levelised cost of energy, which IEA floating offshore wind experts expect to be reduced
by 50% in 2050. The forecast of the floating substructure having the largest reduction
(Figure 1.5) is due to a higher average wind speed as it is possible to harness the best
wind resources without depth constraints. Floating substructures have the advantage of
having fewer operations below water reducing costs, risks and environmental impacts.
The environmental impact might be a deciding factor in the future for mid­depths (30­50
m) as bottom­fixed substructures cause a lot of activity on the seabed during installation
(IRENA, 2019).

1.3
Offshore floating platforms
The different floating substructures have now been introduced, and the following section
will explain their key characteristics and differences.
The static stability which secures the wind turbine not to fall is induced by the pitch restor­
ing force. For a floating wind turbine, the pitch restoring moment can be achieved in
different ways, such as by the tanks, mooring system and ballast configuration. The spar­
buoy achieves stability through ballast installed in the bottom of the buoyancy tank, where
the difference between center of gravity and center of buoyancy induce a restoring pitch
restoring moment. The semi­submersible and barge floater achieves stability through the
waterplane area moment, while the wide use of buoyancy tanks and other elements make
the center of buoyancy less significant. The TLP configuration achieves stability through
the tension from the vertical taut mooring line system.

1.4
Motivation and scope of project
The design process of a floating wind turbine is highly influential of the model fidelity used
for the aerodynamics, hydrodynamics, mooring lines and structural responses. There
is a balance between computational cost and accuracy, which is an important factor in
optimising a design. In the pre­design and optimisation process, the purpose is to have
a coupled system that is fast and efficient while giving good results.
In this thesis, a semi­submersible floating platform is defined with parameterised geome­
try. The key parameters defining the floating platform and the additional parameters for
a catenary mooring system are used as design variables in the optimisation. The hydro­
static stiffness and the hydrodynamic properties (added mass, damping, stiffness, and
wave loads) are calculated by using an in­house analytical model for a cylinder of any
position and orientation. Moreover, a parameterised design of the mooring system aug­
mented into the stiffness matrix. A simplified model of the floater and IEA15MW turbine
configuration is built in the frequency domain using the QuLAF model. The objective func­
tion is the cost of the floating platform and the mooring system. The constraints include
the maximum limits of the static motions (e.g. pitch/surge), the maximum limits of the
dynamic motions (e.g. pitch, tower top acceleration), and a constraint to avoid the fail­
ure of mooring system by anchor. Analytical gradients of the objective function and the
constraints are derived and used in the optimisation
Due to their complex geometry and more complex hydrodynamics (when compared to e.g.
spar­buoy), it is still challenging to achieve a fast optimisation of such floating platforms.
The structural optimisation is hard to do in practice, as the final structure has an impact on
the response and excitation of the floating wind turbine and therefore, the optimisation will
be an iterative process. The motivation for this thesis is to derive an optimisation model
to give a more sustainable design for a semi­submersible floater and IEA15MW turbine
based on simplified models. The industry is currently using state­of­the­art numerical
models such as Ansys Aqwa or WAMIT (WAMIT, Inc., 2020) to calculate the hydrostatics
and hydrodynamics terms for the floater. Such models are computational heavy, and time
consuming, where a simulation can take half a day. Karimi, M., Hall, M., Buckham, B. et
al have been researching an alternative model based on a basis function approach but
with less success. Therefore, this thesis will use an in­house analytical model, which is to
be released sometime in the future. The response and excitation calculation have shown
to be fast and effective in the frequency by Pegalajar­Jurado, Antonio, Michael Borg, and
Henrik Bredmose. In Suguang Dou, Antonio Pegalajar­Jurado, Shaofeng Wang, Henrik
Bredmose and Mathias Stolpe a parameterised optimisation framework gave satisfying
results for a spar­buoy floater in minutes. This thesis will examine if a similar framework
can be done for a semi­submersible floater.

2.2
Quick Load Analysis of floating wind turbines
The analysis of the system will be done by using a part of the Quick Load Analysis of
Floating wind turbines (QuLAF) model, a model developed at DTU by Pegalajar­Jurado,
Borg, and Bredmose (2018). This paper is build around the QuLAF model, but only the
static analysis of the model is being used, which means the optimization framework will
not include the dynamic analysis.
State­of­the­art alternatives are the aeroelastic model such as HAWC2 and the aero­
hydro­servo­elastic model FAST. A former study developed by Madsen, Pegalajar­Jurado,
and Bredmose (2019) has been comparing QuLAF with FAST, where QuLAF gave good
results with much less CPU power. The QuLAF model saves a lot of CPU power by
analysing the dynamic terms in the frequency domain than in the time domain. This is
mainly done by rewriting the equation of motion from the time domain to the frequency
domain.
2.2.1
Equation of motion
This section will show how the general equation of motion(EoM) in the time domain can
be rewritten to the frequency domain. The EoM for a system of equations is defined as:

2.4
Slender model
The slender model is a simplified model to calculate the hydrodynamic contribution and
the floating parameters for the QuLAF model, described in Section 2.2. The model was
developed in the FloatStep research project, and is yet to be public released. For this
reason the different derivations of the hydrodynamic terms will not be covered in full detail,
but still be adequate to give an overview of the assumptions behind the model. Most of the
deviations are from Sumer and Fredsøe (2006) and Sarpkaya (2010) where the viscous
damping is derived by utilising Pegalajar­Jurado, Borg, and Bredmose (2018).
The slender model calculates the hydrostatic restoring matrix, wave excitation load, added
mass matrix, and viscous damping. This implies that the radiation damping is neglected,
which is the case for a slender cylinder compared to the wavelength. Larger bodies will
disturb the incident waves by reflecting the waves and creating wave scattering, called
diffraction. Experiments have shown that diffraction influences vertical cylinders when the
ratio between the diameter of the cylinder and wavelength becomes larger than 0.2.

Ballast
This section interprets the ballast system of the floater. The ballast system is the counter­
weight that secures equilibrium state stability by balancing the buoyancy and gravitational
forces.
The submerged volume and density of water lead to the buoyancy force, where the slender
model calculates the submerged volume. The gravitational forces are the sum of the
vertical mooring forces, steel of floater mass, and wind turbine mass. The equilibrium
equation for the ballast is considered:
mballast = mbuoy −(mmoor + mfloater,s + mwt)
(2.77)
The ballast has an influence on the mass matrix, not only in the form of the mass itself
but also the orientation and location of the ballast will contribute to the mass inertia. For
the preliminary design, the implementation of the ballast can be done in different ways.
One method is to set the ballast as a single point load in the bottom of the floater, but
this will neglect the mass inertia contribution. This model will set the ballast to only be
iron concrete, where the material will be in the form of solid cylinders, which means it is
possible to utilise the slender model.

4
Framework of Optimization
This chapter will contain the analysis and implementation of the optimization framework.
The optimization framework will use the slender model described in Chapter 3. The condi­
tion to use the slender model in an optimization framework is to use the same topography
of the floater. In this paper, the design philosophy of the UMaine VolturnUS­S floater is
being used, hence the same number of cylinders are created and the implementation of
the ballast is identical. The framework is built on two parts:
1. The objective
2. The constraints
In the first part, the objective and input variables x of the framework are defined. The
second part contains all constraints for the framework, which combines the geometric
constraints, static considerations, and constraints for the natural frequencies.
The implementation of the slender model described in Chapter 3 is refactored to a single
function with the input variables of x.The input variables are chosen base don the results
from Zhou et al. (2021). The results from the paper show how the radius of the columns
and the column spacing influence the hydrodynamics and natural frequencies. Mean­
while, the length of the draft only had a significant influence on the hydrodynamics. This
will set the foundation of the input parameters for the optimization framework. The input
variables in the framework will be based on the four submerged members of the floater
and the mooring design, which can be seen in Table 4.1.

The objective of the optimization framework is to minimize a certain value of a given func­
tion. In this paper, the objective is to minimize the cost of the floater. The objective
function takes a set of input variables x, where the optimization framework will change
the input values to get the lowest cost. The reason the cost is chosen as the objective is
due to the purpose of designing the most cost­efficient offshore wind turbine which con­
tributes directly to the LCOE. The influence of the cost in relation to the different elements

Conclusion
The thesis showed that it was possible to develop a fast constrainted optimization frame­
work for a semisubmersible offshore wind turbine. The speed came from the slender
model which calculates all hydronamic terms, but also the Quick Load Analysis of floating
wind turbines (QuLAF) model. The slender model is a simplified analytical model com­
pared to SoA models. The norm for a simplified model is the sacrifice of the accuracy for
its speed, therefore, the thesis is built around the UMaine VolturnUS­S semi­submersible
floater in extension with the new 15MW wind turbine.
The results from the comparison between the slender model, and WAMIT results for the
UMaine VolturnUS­S semi­submersible floater, showed that the slender model has some
problems defining the added mass in heave. This resulted in a 20% difference for the nat­
ural frequency for heave. For the wave excitation forces, the slender model overpredicted
the magnitude of the waves for the moderate and severe sea state. Overall the wave ex­
citation forces were in the same order of magnitude as the SoA model. The error in the
heave is a result of the limitation of the model, as the model perform better for a platform
that consists of slender cylinders. In this case, the UMaine VolturnUS­S semi­submersible
floater consists of four vertical cylinders and three rectangular pontoons. The change in
geometry changes how the fluid reacts with the body, which might be the source of error.
Furthermore, for a slender body, the body has to be slender in relation to the wavelength
which might not have been the case in this scenario. To know the limitations of the model
better, more research has to be made, where the model is compared to a different range
of floaters. Currently, it is hard to know how sensitive the model is for certain dimensions.
The objective function of the optimization framework included both the labor work and
overhead costs. This gives the objective function more variety when comparing the price.
The model did not include different form of welding technology, but it is possible to include
in the future. Moreover, from the results it could be observed that the mooring lines were
kept short, which concludes that the benefit from stiffness compared to its price is not worth
it. Instead, the optimization model used the maximum range of column spacing, which
gives larger hydrostatic stiffness. It was observed the diameter of the pontoon had a big
influence on the constraints of natural frequencies. As the optimization framework does
not include the dynamic response, there is still space for improvement. Furthermore, a
new model in a SoA software could be made to validate the performance of the new flower.
Other modifications in the framework can be a FEM­analysis, to see if the optimized values
actually are statically approved.
