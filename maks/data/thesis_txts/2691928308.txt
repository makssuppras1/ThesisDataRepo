ABSTRACT
2
Abstract
This MSc thesis focuses on the design and the implementation of a a dimming light emitting diode
(LED) driver with charge-pump-based power factor correction. In particular, this project aims to di-
rect power from the grid to dimming for lighting applications. A study about how to use a power
converter, in order to use the power from AC mains for dimming a LED, is conducted. This converter
constitutes to an existing resonant converter (charge pump-based technology) and the main tasks are
to make the optimal control resolution, testing dimming capabilities. The thesis was completed at the
Department of Electrical Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark, in collaboration with
the company ”Nordic Power Converters”.
To this purpose, the structure and the basic operating principle of the resonant converter are presented,
as well as the control design and how this can enable the dimming functionality and affect its perfor-
mance, with respect to the efﬁciency and the power factor.
Following, the theoretical analysis, simulations of the entire system are conducted, using the sim-
ulation software tools LTspice and TINA-TI, in order to validate the converter’s behavior and fully
analyze its operation in the steady state operating condition.
Additionally, the closed control loop method is examined in order to control the converter’s output
current. For that purpose, the development of the appropriate code for the CY8CKIT-043 microcon-
troller to implement this control, is conducted and the logic behind that is presented.
Finally, the converter was tested in the laboratory, at various operating conditions. The experimental
measurements from the laboratory tests are presented, for conﬁrming its operation and experimentally
verifying its efﬁciency.
Figure 1: From the AC mains to lighting applications [1].
Key Words: AC-DC converter, resonant converter, power factor correction, charge-pump based
technology, dimming functionality, closed control operation, microcontroller

INTRODUCTION
4
Introduction
Nowadays, power electronics and more speciﬁcally, the switch mode power supplies are playing a
vital role in every electronic device. Indeed, their utilization scopes to convert the AC power from the
mains to a steady state DC power, which is required by all electronic loads.
The converters being based on the pulse-width-modulation (PWM) have become the most widely
used topologies across a wide range of applications, thanks to their high efﬁciency and their low ini-
tial cost. However, the main disadvantage of them relates to the fact that, their operation is relying on
the hard switching. Consequently, they can cause switching losses, which may destroy some parts of
the converter. As a result, they can be used in low frequencies, in order to eliminate these losses.
The current trend of the electronics industry regards to the reduction of the converter’s size by de-
creasing the size of the passive components like inductors, capacitors and the transformers. This
could be achieved by increasing the switching frequencies [2]. Hence, the need of soft-switching
topologies, like the resonant converters, become vital and the main advantage that they yield refers to
their capability to operate at high frequencies and to achieve higher power densities [1].
4.1
Thesis Scope
The main objective of this thesis is to examine the operation of a resonant power converter at high
switching frequency, which will be utilized in a lighting application and more speciﬁcally, as a light
emitting diode (LED) driver. Furthermore, it is a fact that in modern lighting systems, the dimming
capability of the LED drivers, is important to achieve the savings in energy consumption and it con-
stitutes to a mandatory feature [3]. For that purpose, the dimming functionality is being examined
and discussed in detail, in this thesis.
In order to enable the aforementioned functionality, the implementation of the converters control,
with respect to its output current is being studied. Furthermore, the close control loop operation is
examined and for that reason, a microcontroller is used and its utilization constitutes to the main task
of this project.
4.2
Thesis Structure

5
Resonant Converters
In this chapter, an introduction to the resonant converters is being presented. Additionally, a study
about the operation and the theoretical background of the prototype is being conducted.
5.1
State of the art
It is a fact that the resonant converters have gained the interest of the power supply industry and consti-
tute to a popular topologies due to their ability to achieve high efﬁciency through softly commutating
the switching devices. The main advantage that these converters yield refer to the capability of being
well suited for higher power applications and also they can achieve higher power density.This means
that the overall converter size can be reduced as the converter can operate at very high switching fre-
quencies due to the soft switching techniques [4].
The main soft switching techniques are Zero Current Switching (ZCS) and the Zero Voltage
Switching (ZVS) . The switching losses of the converter can be eliminated through the turn-on and
turn-off of the various semiconductor devices because the current or the voltage turn to zero dur-
ing these switching transitions. As a result, the resonant converters can be used at higher switching
frequencies in comparison with other converters. In particular, some of the converter-generated elec-
tromagnetic interference can also be eliminated through the Zero Voltage Switching, which means
that this soft switching mechanism provides better Electromagnetic Interference performance along
with higher frequency [2].
A common resonant converter consists of three main parts: 1) the switching network, 2) the resonant
tank and 3) the rectiﬁer network. The structure’s diagram is shown in the Figure 2.
Figure 2: The structure’s diagram of a resonant converter [1]
In particular, the switching network could have a half bridge or full bridge conﬁguration and
generates a pulsating voltage, which accounts for a square wave voltage and it is fed to the resonant
tank. Resonant tank can be formed by an inductor (L) and the capacitor (C) and its usage ensures
a reduced electromagnetic interference and harmonic distortion. The impedance of both capacitance
and inductance at the resonance condition are equivalent and will produce the resonance frequency.
There are several resonant tank networks that can be designed and they are illustrated in the Figure 3
[5] .

5.2.3
Switching network
The switching network, as it is presented in the prototype’s structure is in a half bridge conﬁguration
and comprises of two gallium nitride - GaN ﬁeld effect transistors - FET QHs and QLS, the high side
and the low side switch, respectively. The reason of selecting this type of semiconductor is that the
GaN FETs yield several advantages like the reduced size for given on-resistance (RDS,on), the very
low input capacitance and fast on-off switching [8].
The driving signals of the gate for each switch constitute to two complementary pulse width modu-
lated (PWM) signals with ﬁxed switching frequency of 1 MHz. These signals are produced by the
gate driver circuit, which consists of the gate driver (Si8274 by Texas Instruments) and the voltage
controlled oscillator (LTC6990, by Analog devices), which produces the required ﬁxed switching
frequency, by adding ﬁxed resistors. The Figure 9 depicts the topology of the gate driver circuit.
Figure 9: The gate driver circuit for driving both of the FETs [1].
In addition to this, in order to avoid the hard switching of the FETs and as a consequence the
losses that might be occured, the dead time is included. Particularly, the dead time is an important
parameter to the switching of half bridge conﬁguration, because each FET has a gate charge and
discharge time, which means that a FET does not go instantly from ON to OFF or from OFF to ON.
In other words, it is possible for one FET to turn on, while the other FET is still in the process of
turning off, which may result a very low resistance for the current’s path and the total damage of the

Control
In this section, the control of the converter is being examined. More speciﬁcally, the burst mode con-
trol for the dimming capability and the closed control operation and its implementation are analyzed.
6.1
Burst mode Control
For the implementation of the dimming functionality, the operation of the converter in burst - mode
is being studied. In particular, this operation mode is widely used in the LED drivers, in order to im-
prove the efﬁciency of the converters at light load condition and is implemented by halting the control
signals of the switches [3]. In this case, an external PWM signal is applied to the gate drive circuit
for turning on and off modulation of the switching signals, where the output current ﬂowing the LED
lamp is regulated using the duty cycle of that particular PWM signal. Hence, the LED current can be
adjusted using this PWM signal through the speciﬁc input pin of the gate driver. Also, it is important
to be mentioned that the output capacitor does not discharge during this operation and the dimming
of the LED lamp.
Furthermore, for the selection of the PWM signal’s frequency there are two main limitations. Partic-
ularly, the PWM burst signal’s frequency has to be adequately lower than the switching frequency,
in order to avoid the inter modulation of the PWM signals, which may cause the contortion of the
output voltage and at the same time it should be high above the audible range as well as the 40th
harmonic of the input line current in order to avoid the contravention of the standards relating to the
PFC applications with input current harmonics. It is worth noting that the burst signal introduces low
frequency harmonics that can cause high EMI noise and for that reason a ﬁlter might be needed[1].
Lastly, for this application a burst PWM signal with frequency of 20 kHz is being applied in order to
test the dimming capability of the converter and regulate the output current in the range of 20 and 900
mA.
6.2
Closed Control operation
In order to make the converter as an ”intelligent” system, the generation of the aforementioned burst
PWM signal is achieved, with respect to the output current. Moreover, getting as feedback value,
the instantaneous output current, the duty cycle of the burst signal is adjusted in order to enable the
dimming functionality. This adjustment can be carried through a PI controller.
6.2.1
PI Control
A proportional (P) and integral (I) control -PI control- is a control loop mechanism that is relying on a
feedback, from a system and calculates the difference between the process variable, which in this case
corresponds to the output current and the setpoint, which relates to a ﬁxed value. Afterwards, after the
calculation of this error, this mechanism applies a correction to that error, based on the proportional
and the integral term. Firstly, the proportional term produces an output value that is proportional to
the current error value. The proportional response can be adjusted by multiplying the error e(t) with
a constant Kp, which is a tuning parameter and it is called proportional gain. Hence the output of the
proportional term can be expressed, by the following Equation.
Pterm = Kp ·e(t)
(36)
Secondly, the integral term takes into account the history of the error, or how long and how far the
measured process variable has been from the set point over time. Integration of the error over the time
means the sum of the instantaneous error over time and gives the accumulated offset that should have
been corrected previously. The accumulated error is then multiplied by the integral gain Ki, which is

6.2.2
Current Sense
For the provision of the output current to the PI controller, which plays the role of the process variable,
it is important to measure that. For that reason, a current sense ampliﬁer is being utilized. In particular,
a shunt resistor is being added in series with the output load and the voltage across that resistor is taken
into account and is ampliﬁed, in order to calculate the output current.
There are two ways to sense a current using a shunt resistor and a current sense ampliﬁer. The ﬁrst
one is called ” High Side measurement”, where the shunt resistor is placed before the load, or in other
words between the load and the voltage bus (Vbus). In that project, the voltage bus represents the
output voltage of the converter, which is stabilized by the output capacitor, as described above. The
main advantage of that method regards to the fact that it can detect open or short circuits, as the load
is placed directly to the system ground, but on the other hand the common mode voltage (Vcm) of the
ampliﬁer is approximately the same as the output voltage of the system, which in some cases might
be extremely high [12]. In the Figure 13 , the topology of the High Side measurement is illustrated.

This component can be used in high sample rate systems, where the user needs to sample multiple
channels without CPU intervention until all channels are sampled. It can also be used in low sample
rate designs or in designs that have just a single channel to sample. In this project, a single channel
is used to sample the output signal of the current sense ampliﬁer. In addition, in order to enable the
analog to digital conversion, the user has to conﬁgure some parameters, which are described in detail,
below.
• Input of the ADC
The input to the ADC is the positive analog signal of the current sense ampliﬁer. The operation of
the ADC is differential, by default, which means that the conversion’s result is a function of the input
signal minus the voltage reference (Vref ).
• Voltage Reference Vref
This voltage reference can be the internal voltage reference of the microcontroller, or the ground
Vss. Because of the single ended operation of the current sense ampliﬁer, the single ended format
result of the ADC conversion is selected. The internal voltage reference of the microcontroller for
this format is 1.024 V and according to the manufacturer’s datasheet, the usable input signal’s range
can be between 0 and 2·Vref . In other words, a user by selecting this option of the internal reference
can apply input signals of voltage level between 0 and 2.048 V. However, there are other options for
selecting the voltage reference, like using an external voltage. This option is recommended only for
the differential mode, where the input signal is the result of a differential measurement [18].
• Resolution
This parameter is important for the ADC operation. It can be deﬁned as the minimum imperceptible
voltage that can be conceded and it causes a change to the digital output. The resolution represents
the number of the bits output by the ADC, and it can be expressed in terms of the least signiﬁcant bit
(LSB). The default value of the resolution is 12 bits , which means that the ADC converts the analog
signal to a 12-bit digital value. Hence, the minimum voltage that can be recognized in terms of the
LSB, is derived from the Equation below.
LSB = Vre f −Vss
2n
(41)
where n is the number of the resolution bit and Vss is the ground of the microcontroller. Hence,
using 12-bit resolution and voltage reference of 1.024 V, the LSB can be calculated as 0.25 mV.
On the other hand, there are available two more options for an alternative resolution, the 8-bit and the
10-bit, respectively. It is inportant to be mentioned that in order to have high accuracy of the ADC,
and therefore get the digital output closer to the analog input, higher resolution is needed [19].
• Averaging Mode
The averaging technique is an useful method, where the ADC converter samples the analog input
several times and takes the average of the results. It helps to eliminate the effect of noise on the
analog input. The average is made on several readings that correspond to the same analog-input
voltage and this means that the analog input remains at the same voltage during the time period until
the conversion is done. As the result this method plays an important role the the control operation, as
it improves the accuracy of the conversion.
• Sample rate
The sample rate is expressed as the number of the output samples per the time unit and it is speciﬁed
as the number of samples per second. It is related to the ADC clock frequency and its value depends
on the number of the ADC channel, the resolution and also if the averaging mode is enabled or not.
At high sample rates, the ADC can generate large amounts of data and consequently the CPU clock
will need to be running fast enough to process the data.

Following this, the ADC converter uses a comparator, which receives the analog signal on one input
and a unique fraction of the reference voltage on the other. The reference voltage for this comparator
consists mainly by a resistive voltage divider, whereby the comparator is biased in voltage increments
equivalent to 1 LSB. The comparator array is clocked simultaneously. The comparators with ref-
erence voltages less than the analog input will output a digital 1. The comparators with reference
voltages greater than the analog input will output a digital 0. In the SAR ADC, the analog input
signal is compared against a series of successively smaller voltages representing each of the bits in
the digital output code. Furthermore, the ﬁrst comparison occurs between the input voltage and the
voltage expressing the most signiﬁcant bit (MSB) and if the input voltage is grater than the MSB
voltage, the value of the MSB becomes digital 1, otherwise 0. In the next comparison, the analog
input is compared with the sum of the MSB, which corresponds to the previous value plus the current
one and the new MSB is set accordingly. The procedure terminates, when the least signiﬁcant bit is
achieved [20].
Eventually, the ADC converter after the completion of one cycle of the conversion, it saves the result
in the format of a signed 16-bit integer into the speciﬁc register of the microcontroller and afterwards
it converts that to a voltage value in (mV) [18].

8.5
Limitations of the control’s system.
After the completion of the experimental tests, the main limitations of the control’s system, are sum-
marized below:
• When the input voltage changes, the setpoint, regarding to the PI control, also changes, which
means that the user has to re-set its value to the appropriate one, with respect to the new output
converter’s current.
• When the setpoint changes more than 10 % of the total output current, then a high ﬂickering to
the output power is observed. This happens because the resulting duty cycle of the burst signal
varies from 100 % to 0 % and the vice versa, quickly. As a consequence, the power factor
decreases and the total harmonic distortion increases to unacceptable values.
• Due to the internal offset errors of the ADC, the accuracy of the conversion’s results is affected
and as a result decreases.

9
Conclusion and Future Work
In this chapter, the conclusions of the work presented in this Thesis, are discussed, as well as the main
ideas for the improvement for future work.
9.1
Conclusions
This thesis presents an analysis about the operation of a resonant converter, which is used in a lighting
application and speciﬁcally in a LED driver. The theoretical aspects of that converter are investigated
and the design of the existing prototype is examined.
Lastly, simulations through out the simulation tools and the experimental tests in the laboratory are
carried out, in order to validate the converter’s performance.
After the completion of this project, the main takeaways are the following:
• In the applications, where the power of the AC mains is used, the power factor correction circuit
is essential, because the input current is composed of harmonics, which may causes high power
losses and the overheating of the components, especially the semiconductor devices. For that
reason, the main scope of this circuit is to keep the input current harmonics within the limits of
the standards.
• The soft switching converters like the resonant, show a very good performance in the range of
the high frequency, which may help to the reduction of the entire size of the prototype.
• The variation of the switching frequency is not recommended, because higher frequency may
require higher input current but also lower output voltage, due to the reduction of the resonant
tank’s gain. This may deteriorate the performance of the converter, in terms of the efﬁciency.
• In order to enable the dimming functionality, the burst mode control is an useful technique
for the converters control but the main disadvantage is the introduction of the low frequency
harmonics, which may worsen the total harmonic distortion.
• The close loop operation of the converter is possible with the usage of a microcontroller and
with a low cost electronics.
• The PI control is an useful and helpful method, which accounts as a feedback value, the output
current and calculates the difference between this and the desired value.
Additionally, this control returns the duty cycle of the burst-signal. If this difference is positive,
then the duty cycle is increasing, while in case of the negative, the duty cycle is decreasing.
Hence, the selection of the appropriate desired value in different voltage levels, plays an impor-
tant role to the trend of the duty cycle.
• The result of the simulations and the experimental test may differ. The main reason is that
in the simulations, a more ideal approach is followed. Moreover, initial conditions are taken
into consideration and also the instruments, like the power supplies do not include internal
resistances. Lastly, in the laboratory, the thermal issues of the devices, as well as many other
parameters, like the noise, which is introduced by the big current paths and the interference of
the neighboring components, may affect the accuracy of the measurements and the ﬁnal results.
• The power factor remains high at both open and closed loop operation, however the total har-
monic distortion gets worse in the closed loop due to the burst signal. Also, a slight improve-
ment in terms of the efﬁciency, occurs at the closed loop operation.
• Lastly, high ﬂickering can lead to the worsening of the converter’s performance, in terms of the
power factor and the efﬁciency.

9.2
Future work
In order to improve the operational performance of the converter, especially the close loop operation,
the following ideas are summarized and proposed.
• Better design of the printed circuit board , with shorter current paths, in which the current
ampliﬁer should be included. The effective consequence will be the reduction of the noise,
which results through the long wires.
• The increment of the conversion frequency, regarding to the ADC of the microcontroller and the
implementation of a ﬁrst order low pass ﬁlter, in order to eliminate the noise from the current
sense ampliﬁer. In addition, the increment of the resolution regarding to the averaging of the
sampling signal, should help to improve the accuracy of the measurements.
• The used current sense ampliﬁer can be removed, as the microcontroller includes operational
ampliﬁer with a comparator mode and programmable gain, which can be used for the sensing
of the output current.
• In order to reduce the ﬂicker of the output current, some boundaries on the duty cycle should
be taken into account. Also, the step of the change in the duty cycle to be decreased.
• Isolation between the converter and the microcontroller is required for system’s protection
against the over-currents.
