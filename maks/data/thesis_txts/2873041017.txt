Abstract
This thesis analyzes the production cost of green hydrogen and compares it to the bench­
mark cost of conventional gray hydrogen of 2 EUR/kg. Green hydrogen is considered a
key component of the EU decarbonization strategy, with the potential to reduce emissions
in sectors such as industry and transport. Although the EU has set ambitious targets to
scale up green hydrogen production, the industry faces significant challenges due to high
production costs compared to natural gas­based hydrogen.
To address this challenge, this thesis develops techno­economic models in Python to an­
alyze the main cost drivers of green hydrogen production. Two production strategies are
analyzed. First, a grid­connected electrolysis using hourly spot market prices in Norway,
and then a hydrogen plant operating with temporal and geographical correlation with off­
shore wind and hybrid wind–solar systems in Denmark. A levelized cost of hydrogen
model is developed and gradually expanded to incorporate dynamic input files such as
spot market prices and renewable energy profiles.
The results show that electricity price, plant CAPEX, and annual operating hours are the
most critical factors influencing the LCOH. In todays market the target cost of 2 EUR/kg
cannot be reached. Achieving this competitive hydrogen costs requires a specific CAPEX
cost below 1500 EUR/kW, electricity prices below 20 EUR/MWh, and more than 6700 full­
load annual operating hours. The results highlight the importance of location and cheap
electricity access to produce cost­competitive green hydrogen.

Introduction
Reducing global warming to mitigate its environmental consequences is one of the defin­
ing challenges of this century. To address this, the Paris Agreement sets a global goal
to limit the temperature rise to below 2 ◦C, and preferably below 1.5 ◦C [1]. Achieving
this target requires rapid decarbonization across all emitting sectors, including electricity
production, industry, transport, and heating [2]. In line with these goals, the European
Union has committed to achieve climate neutrality by 2050 and to reduce greenhouse
gas emissions by at least 55% by 2030, compared to 1990 levels [3].
To support these ambitions, the EU introduced the Renewable Energy Directive III (RED
III) [4]. The directive sets a binding target that at least 42.5% of the EU’s total energy con­
sumption must come from renewable sources by 2030, with a non­binding goal of reaching
45%. Hydrogen is considered to play an important role in supporting this transition.
Hydrogen is seen as a key component for reducing emissions in sectors that are hard
to directly electrify [5]. It can replace fossil fuels in energy­intense industries like steel
and chemical production. It also has the potential of working as a clean fuel for long­
distance transport, like shipping and aviation. In addition, hydrogen production can play
an important part in balancing the power system with high renewable energy penetration
and provides both long­term and large­scale energy storage. These features make green
hydrogen a valuable tool not just for cutting emissions, but also for improving flexibility,
stability, and energy security in future energy systems.
To accelerate hydrogen deployment, RED III includes sector targets [6]. By 2030, at
least 42% of the hydrogen used in industry must be produced from renewable sources,
increasing to 60% by 2035. In the transport sector, a minimum of 1% of fuels must come
from renewable hydrogen by 2030. The REPowerEU plan aims to produce 10 million
tonnes of renewable hydrogen in Europe each year by 2030 to support decarbonization
and improve energy security. In addition, it plans to import another 10 million tonnes of
hydrogen annually [7].
The global hydrogen industry is expanding, where the total hydrogen production reached
97 million tonnes in 2023, but only less than 1% of this was classified as low­emission
hydrogen [8]. By the end of 2023, the global installed electrolyzer capacity stood at 1.4
GW and was expected to grow to 5 GW by the end of 2024.
In Europe, many countries are planning and developing large scale hydrogen projects
and infrastructure. One major project is the European Hydrogen Backbone, which aims
to build more than 58,000 kilometers of hydrogen pipelines by 2040 [9]. In Denmark, a
hydrogen pipeline is being developed to support green hydrogen production and future
export [10] [11]. Several projects in Denmark are already in progress, where one example
is the Kassø e­methanol facility, which became operational in 2025 [12]. The power­
to­X (PtX) plant combines electrolysis with solar power to produce green hydrogen and
methanol. Another example is Skovgaard Energy, which is working on a project with 150
MW of electrolysis powered by onshore wind and solar energy [13]. These projects show
that both governments and private companies are committed to making green hydrogen
a key part of the future energy system.
However, despite strong political support and growing interest, the PtX sector is facing
serious challenges [14]. While the long­term vision for hydrogen is clear, real­world de­

ployment is falling behind. By the end of 2024, many projects faced rising costs and
uncertain regulations [14]. Although global plans suggest that electrolyzer capacity could
reach 520 GW by 2030, only about 4% of this has reached the final investment decision
or is under construction [8].
One of the main barriers is the economy of green hydrogen production, where the levelized
cost of hydrogen (LCOH) is two to six times more expensive than gray hydrogen from fossil
fuels [8]. At the same time, unclear regulations and different sustainability rules across
countries make investment planning more difficult [15]. These combined challenges limit
the scale­up of PtX and green hydrogen. Addressing both cost structures and regulatory
design will therefore be an important part of initializing the green hydrogen industry over
the next years.
To contribute to this effort, this thesis focuses on the economic side of the challenge. It
aims to analyze the cost structure of green hydrogen production and identify the key cost
drivers. The analysis seeks to clarify where the main barriers lie, and what targets are
required to make green hydrogen cost­competitive in the future.
1.1
Thesis Objectives
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate the cost structure of green hydrogen pro­
duction and identify the key barriers preventing it from becoming cost­competitive. Multi­
ple models are developed in Python to analyze how technical and economic parameters
affect the levelized cost of hydrogen.
The study aims to:
• Analyze the cost of green hydrogen compared to gray hydrogen produced via steam
methane reforming (SMR), using LCOH as the comparison metric.
• Identify main cost drivers by analyzing how parameters such as CAPEX, electricity
price, annual operating hours, and technical constraints influence the total LCOH.
• Analyze two production strategies by modeling different configurations:
– Grid­connected electrolysis using hourly spot prices based on Norwegian mar­
ket data.
– Corresponding hydrogen production with offshore wind and solar energy based
on Danish weather conditions.
• Analyze the impact of different combinations of wind, solar, and electrolyzer sizing,
on hydrogen cost.
• Establish cost targets by determining minimum CAPEX and electricity price thresh­
olds required for green hydrogen to reach competitive LCOH levels.
The goal is to provide insight into what is realistically achievable today, what is holding
green hydrogen back, and what specific cost components must improve to close the gap
with gray hydrogen.

2
Background
This chapter presents the cost structure behind the levelized cost of hydrogen. It first
introduces the key components in a green hydrogen system, explaining what drives the
capital costs (CAPEX) of green hydrogen plants. Then an introduction to the operational
expenses (OPEX) of a green hydrogen system, focusing on electricity costs. The same
type of breakdown is done for gray hydrogen produced from natural gas using steam
methane reforming. This includes a short description of the plant, the role of natural gas
prices, and the possible effect of CO2 taxes or carbon capture. Finally, the chapter ends
with a literature review of other studies on hydrogen production costs and explains how
this thesis builds on and improves existing work.
2.1
Green hydrogen production
According to the EU rules under RED III, there are some specific requirements for clas­
sifying hydrogen as green. This analysis focuses on two key production strategies. First
a grid­connected electrolysis in energy systems with a high share of renewable energy,
and then a grid­connected electrolysis that meets specific requirements for temporal and
geographical correlation with renewable energy production [16].
The first case applies if the hydrogen is produced in a bidding zone where more than 90%
of the electricity came from renewable sources in the previous calendar year. In these
energy systems, the hydrogen can be considered as renewable as long as the production
does not exceed the renewable share of the grid [16].
The second case applies if the grid is not considered fully renewable. Then, the hydrogen
plant must purchase renewable electricity through power purchase agreements (PPAs),
and meet two conditions. First, the electricity must be produced in the same bidding
zone as the electrolyzer is located, referred to as geographical correlation. Second, the
electricity must be consumed at the same time it is produced, referred to as temporal
correlation. Until 2030, the temporal correlation must be done on a monthly basis, but
from 2030 it must be hourly correlation [16]. These rules ensure that green hydrogen
production is based on renewable electricity and does not increase demand for fossil
fueled electricity production.
Only a few bidding zones in Europe currently meet the 90% renewable threshold. These
are typically regions with a large share of hydropower or other renewable sources and
relatively low electricity demand [17]. As shown in Figure 2.1, Norway qualifies as a
renewable power system due to its high share of hydropower in the energy system. In
contrast, Denmark has not yet reached the 90% level and would therefore need to follow
the second strategy, meeting the temporal and geographical correlation requirements.

2.2
Green hydrogen plants
This section describes the main components included in a green hydrogen plant. The
total CAPEX of the plant includes both the core equipment, such as the electrolyzer stack,
balance of plant (BoP) and the additional costs related to engineering, procurement, and
construction (EPC). The section provides an overview of what drives the total investment
cost of a green hydrogen plant and how these costs are typically distributed over the
components.
A green hydrogen plant includes multiple systems that work together to produce and han­
dle hydrogen. The system boundary typically starts where electricity enters the site and
ends where hydrogen is delivered at a specified pressure, usually around 60–100 bar for
pipeline injection [18]. The voltage level at the point of connection depends on the scale
of the plant. Smaller plants connect to medium­voltage grids (20–30 kV), a 100 MW plant
would typically connect to a higher voltage (110 kV), and a 1 GW plant is connected di­
rectly to the transmission grid at around 380 kV [18]. Figure 2.2 is an illustration by the
Institute of Process Sustainable Process Technology [19] illustrating what a 1 GW alkaline
electrolyzer plant could look like.

Electrolyzer stack
At the core of the system is the electrolyzer stack, where water is split into hydrogen and
oxygen presented in Equation 2.1. This is the single most expensive component in the
plant [20]. The two main commercially available technologies today are alkaline (AEL)
and proton exchange membrane (PEMEL) electrolyzers. Both require DC electricity and
a high­purity water supply. The stack typically accounts for 15–45% of the total installed
system cost, depending on the defined system boundaries, technology and scale [20], [8],
[18].
2H2O →2H2 + O2
(2.1)
The high cost of the stack primarily comes from the use of specialized materials and com­
ponents [20] [18]. In PEM systems, this includes expensive catalysts such as iridium and
platinum, as well as membrane electrode assemblies, porous transport layers, and high­
performance bipolar plates. Alkaline systems generally use less expensive materials, but
still have a high cost, especially due to the electrodes and the diaphragm. Additionally,
stacks are expensive because the technology is relatively new, causing high manufactur­
ing costs. Reductions in catalyst loading, improved material efficiency, and manufacturing
scale­up are assumed to be the key pathways to lower the stack costs in the future [20]
[18].
Surrounding the stack is the balance of plant, which includes all supporting systems re­
quired for the electrolyzer to operate safely and efficiently. These subsystems typically
account for a significant share of the total CAPEX. A cost breakdown for both an AEL and
a PEMEL plant is presented in Figure 2.3.
Power supply and conversion
This subsystem includes transformers, inverters, and rectifiers that convert incoming AC
electricity into DC. The power electronics are designed to handle high current levels and

operate across a range of voltages. This part of the system typically accounts for a sig­
nificant share of the total capital cost, often around 15–25% of overall CAPEX [20] [18].
Water treatment
Electrolyzer technologies are highly sensitive to water purity, requiring demineralized wa­
ter to reduce degradation and ensure reliable performance [18]. The water treatment sys­
tem removes impurities such as minerals, salts, and organic compounds, typically through
a combination of filtration, deionization, and reverse osmosis. Although this subsystem
represents a relatively small share of the total cost, it is essential for the safe and efficient
operation of the plant [20].
Hydrogen gas handling and purification
After the splitting of water, the hydrogen is typically saturated with water vapor and may
contain oxygen traces. A drying and purification system is used to meet purity standards.
Depending on plant design, this can involve temperature swing adsorption units or other
gas cleanup systems [18].
Compression and storage
After purification, hydrogen is compressed to the required delivery pressure, often using
multi­stage compressors. Compression is one of the most significant balance­of­plant
cost components, particularly in systems designed for high­pressure output [18].
Cooling and thermal management
Electrolyzers generate heat during operation, and an effective cooling system to maintain
stack temperatures within safe and efficient operating limits is important. Most systems
use water­based cooling systems to remove excess heat and ensure performance over
time [20].
Control and process automation systems
To ensure safe, flexible, and efficient plant operation, a control system is required. A
typical control system includes sensors, programmable logic controllers, and system­level
software for plant monitoring and diagnostics [19].
Engineering, procurement and construction
In addition to the direct equipment costs, another part of total CAPEX comes from indirect
costs such as civil works, engineering, installation, site preparation, and project manage­
ment. These costs are typically referred to as engineering, procurement and construction
(EPC) and can account for 15­50% of the total project budget, depending on location and
complexity [8] [19].

2.3
Green hydrogen operational expenses
In addition to capital costs, green hydrogen production involves several operational ex­
penses that play a significant role in the total cost of hydrogen. The largest share of
variable operational costs is the electricity consumption, which includes both the power
needed for the electrolyzer stack and the electricity used by the BoP. It is the electrolyzer
stack which is the absolute main consumer of electricity, where it accounts for around
90% of the total electricity consumption of the plant [18]. Additionally, water consumption
for electrolysis is a variable cost, though it represents a much smaller share. As a result,
water costs are typically neglected in cost analyses [8].
Other important cost elements include grid fees, transmission tariffs, and electricity taxes,
which can vary depending on the country and regulatory setup [14]. In addition to these
variable costs, hydrogen plants also face fixed annual operational expenses. These typ­
ically cover maintenance, insurance, labor, and monitoring systems, and are often esti­
mated as a fixed percentage of the total capital investment [21].
Electricity cost
Electricity is the single most important operational cost in green hydrogen production,
often accounting for the majority of the total LCOH. If the electrolyzer is supplied by a
dedicated renewable energy source, the relevant electricity price is based on a fixed power
purchase agreement (PPA) related to the levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) from that
source [22]. Typical LCOE values for renewable energy sources are, onshore wind with
45 EUR/MWh, PV with 45 EUR/MWh, and offshore wind with 70 EUR/MWh [23]. As
seen in Figure 2.4 LCOE for wind and solar power has fallen steadily in recent years
and is expected to continue declining as technologies mature and scale increases [24].
This makes renewable electricity increasingly competitive and vital for lowering hydrogen
production costs.
Figure 2.4: Historical cost declines for energy generation technologies. The figure is taken
from: [25].
Alternatively, if the electrolyzer operates using electricity from the grid, the price is deter­
mined by the spot market. The spot price reflects real­time supply and demand conditions
and can vary significantly by hour, day, and season. Prices tend to rise during periods of
high demand or limited generation such as low wind or solar output. In periods of surplus
energy, the prices drop or may even turn negative [26].

Grid fees and taxes
When connecting an electrolyzer to the power system, the operator typically pays various
grid fees and tariffs as a form of rent for using the transmission and distribution network
[14]. These costs are usually based on electricity consumption and installed capacity.
Grid tariffs can include charges for energy transport, system services, and infrastructure
maintenance. These fees can have a significant impact on the LCOH and should be
carefully considered when evaluating grid­connected hydrogen production.
2.4
Gray hydrogen production
Gray hydrogen is the color classification of hydrogen produced from fossil fuels without
carbon capture [27], where steam methane reforming is the most common production
method. This process uses high­temperature steam to convert methane from natural gas
into hydrogen and CO2. SMR is a well­established and cost­effective technology, which
is why it currently dominates the global hydrogen market [27].
To mitigate the CO2 emissions, SMR can be combined with carbon capture and storage
(CCS) technologies. Where the CO2 produced during reforming is captured and stored,
the resulting hydrogen is referred to as blue hydrogen [27]. While blue hydrogen offers
lower emissions than gray hydrogen, it still relies on fossil fuels and does not achieve full
decarbonization. Additionally, the CAPEX cost will increase by adding a carbon capture
facility.
2.4.1
Gray hydrogen plant
Similar to a green hydrogen plant, a steam methane reforming plant consists of several
key components illustrated in Figure 2.5. First, a desulfurization unit removes sulfur com­
pounds from the natural gas [28]. Then a reformer unit makes the methane react with
high­temperature steam to produce a mixture of hydrogen and carbon monoxide [29], pre­
sented in Equation 2.2. The gas mixture then enters the water­gas shift reactor, where
additional steam reacts with CO to form more hydrogen and CO2 [30], presented in Equa­
tion 2.3. Finally, a hydrogen purification unit called the pressure swing adsorption (PSA)
separates hydrogen from the remaining gases [29].
CH4 + H2O →CO + 3H2
(2.2)
CO + H2O →CO2 + H2
(2.3)
The system boundaries include the natural gas supply, water input for steam generation,
and the energy required to maintain high process temperatures. In gray hydrogen plants,
the CO2 generated is typically vented to the atmosphere. If carbon capture is applied, the
system boundary expands to include capture units, compressors, and CO2 storage [31].
2.4.2
Gray hydrogen operational expenses
The total cost of gray hydrogen production is dominated by the price of natural gas, which
serves both as the primary feedstock and the main energy source in the steam methane
reforming process [14]. Natural gas serves as the main operational expense, and its
price is highly dependent on global and regional market dynamics. This leads to the cost
of gray hydrogen being sensitive to fluctuations in natural gas prices, in a similar way
as green hydrogen is sensitive to electricity prices. In recent years, these prices have
shown increased volatility, largely driven by geopolitical tensions and supply uncertainties
in Europe.

Another operational expense is CO2 taxation. In regions with strict climate regulations,
CO2 emissions from gray hydrogen production may be taxed or included in carbon pricing
systems [14], [32]. These costs can have an important impact on the cost of gray hydrogen
and can be a key factor in reducing the economic competitiveness of gray hydrogen [27].
Other operational expenses worth noting are electricity cost and water cost. Electricity is
used for auxiliary systems and the general operation of the plant, but its contribution to the
total cost is relatively minor [14]. Water is consumed for steam generation and cooling,
and the related cost is typically low.
2.5
Literature review
A broad range of studies has analyzed the LCOH of green hydrogen electrolysis and
compared it to gray hydrogen produced from fossil fuels. These types of analyses are
reported in both large industry reports and smaller academic publications. Across the
literature, a variety of techno­economic modeling approaches are applied, each with its
own set of assumptions, data sources, and boundary conditions. As a result, the reported
LCOH values vary depending on the year of publication, regional context, and the specific
input parameters used.
2.5.1
Industry reports
Several industry reports and tools have assessed the cost of green hydrogen production,
offering a wide variety in the current and expected LCOH values. The International En­
ergy Agency (IEA) and International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) state that the
cost of green hydrogen in Europe ranges from 5.8 to 13.5 EUR/kg according to the Eu­
ropean hydrogen bank (EHB) auction. Where only Greece, Spain, and Sweden reported
average costs below 6 EUR/kg [8] [33]. The wide range of LCOH reflects the impact and
uncertainties related to geography, project scale, and renewable resource availability [33].
The European Hydrogen Observatory report slightly lower LCOH values ranging between
4.13 and 9.3 EUR/kg for projects in Europe [34]. Fraunhofer ISE and Lazards levelized
cost of energy report suggest LCOH values from PEM and alkaline technologies in the
range of 3.7 to 5.3 EUR/kg [18] [25]. The Maersk fuel calculator estimates LCOH ranges
from 3.2 to 4 EUR/kg in 2025, expecting a price drop to 2.6 ­ 3.5 EUR/kg in 2030 and 1.3
­ 1.8 EUR/kg in 2050 [35].
Across all these reports, electricity price and electrolyzer CAPEX consistently emerge as
the dominant cost drivers [20], [18],[8], where electricity typically accounts for more than
50% of the total LCOH. CAPEX costs of electrolyzer systems are also reported in a broad

2.5.2
Academic journals and literature
Multiple studies have analyzed how technical and economic parameters affect the LCOH.
A 2024 study by the International Journal of Hydrogen Energy [36] modeled a 100 MW
solar­powered hydrogen plant and found that while electrolyzer stack degradation and
lifetime had limited impact, the LCOH was highly sensitive to financing conditions. The
authors concluded that reductions in electrolyzer CAPEX, electricity price, and cost of
capital were the most effective pathways to achieving an LCOH target of 3 USD/kg.
Another recent comparative study assessed gray, blue, and green hydrogen production
methods [32]. The report highlights that green hydrogen remains the most expensive
production method, ranging between 3.50–6.00 USD/kg, with electricity cost as the dom­
inant driver. The study showed that renewable electricity prices below 20–30 USD/MWh
and electrolyzer CAPEX reductions are critical for reaching competitive production costs
for green hydrogen. The report also identifies the infrastructure as a bottleneck, with
repurposed pipelines being the most viable transport option despite challenges such as
hydrogen embrittlement and reconversion costs.
Weather data and system configuration have also been explored in more dynamic models.
A techno­economic analysis of Australian hydrogen hubs used hourly solar and wind data
and showed that only one region in Australia could meet a target LCOH of AUD 3/kg
under base­case assumptions [37] . The study highlighted the design choices like dual­
tracking PV and wind tower height, electrolyzer efficiency and overload capacity impacts
the LCOH.
Finally, a 2024 case study in Brazil [38] evaluated a 100 MW hybrid wind–solar hydrogen
plant using hourly temporal correlation and found that the system could operate on 100%
renewable power for 80.7% of the year. LCOH was lower for AEL at 5.29 USD/kg than
PEMEL at 5.92 USD/kg. Electricity price, discount rate, and balance of plant costs were
the most sensitive parameters for the LCOH.
While many studies estimate the overall LCOH, fewer studies set concrete cost targets for
several components simultaneously and visualize the trade­offs needed to reach bench­
mark price levels.
This thesis will contribute to the economic understanding of green hydrogen by analyzing
the main cost components using a techno­economic Python model. The focus is on what
can realistically be achieved under current or near­term conditions and what specific cost
barriers prevent green hydrogen from becoming competitive. The study sets concrete
cost targets for key components such as CAPEX and electricity price, and evaluates how
close the current trends are to meeting the benchmark cost of gray hydrogen.
The analysis presents a clear breakdown of each LCOH cost component, offering a better
understanding of their individual impact. The analysis also investigates the opportunities
and limitations of different production strategies. This thesis uses Norwegian spot market
data and Danish weather data to investigate the potential for cost­competitive hydrogen
production for different production strategies. Finally, it presents a comparison of the
operating hour requirements across the scenarios, helping to clarify under what conditions
green hydrogen can become cost­competitive.

3
Methodology
This chapter presents the methodological framework used to analyze the cost drivers
and performance of different green hydrogen production scenarios. The LCOH is used
as the comparison metric, capturing the cost of producing one kilogram of hydrogen. A
benchmark LCOH value of 2 EUR/kg based on the conventional SMR production method
is used as a reference point [32].
Two green hydrogen production strategies are modeled in Python, aligning with the EU
regulatory criteria [16]. One modeled scenario purchases electricity from the day­ahead
hourly spot market in Norway. The two additional scenarios use Danish weather data to
model hydrogen production with temporal and geographical correlation with an offshore
wind farm and a combined offshore wind and solar system.
The modeling approach is executed through the development of three Python models
building on the logic of an initial base case model. The modeled evolution can be sum­
marized as:
1. The base case LCOH model using fixed average input values, which serves as the
foundation for future model expansion.
2. The integration of the Norwegian hourly spot market prices.
3. The integration of the variable electricity output from an offshore wind farm off the
west­coast of Denmark.
4. An additional integration of the electricity output from a fixed PV solar farm located
on the west­coast of Denmark.
3.1
Base­case model
The base case model is based on the methodology from the European Hydrogen Obser­
vatory LCOH calculator [39] [21]. The calculator illustrates how the different cost compo­
nents influence the total LCOH. In the first part of the analysis, the calculator is replicated
into a Python model and serves as the foundation for future model development.

Spot price model
To better analyze real­world spot market conditions, the fixed averaged electricity cost
from the initial model is replaced with hourly day­ahead spot price data. This enables the
model to capture the effects of fluctuating electricity prices.
In this model, the electrolyzer is assumed to purchase electricity directly from the day­
ahead spot market and operates based on a maximum price threshold. Hydrogen pro­
duction occurs only during hours when the spot price is equal to or below this threshold.
This method allows for the analysis of how the LCOH responds to variable electricity prices
and highlights the trade­off between operating hours and electricity costs.

• Available power for hydrogen production varies hourly, based on simulated wind
output.

exceeding this threshold.

3.3.4
Capacity scaling and utilization
Offshore wind only
To analyze the effects of electrolyzer scaling on the LCOH, multiple scaling configurations
were analyzed for both of the RES models. The purpose was to identify an optimal siz­
ing ratio between the electrolyzer and the renewable energy source (RES) that enables
the project to meet predefined cost targets, while maintaining practical feasibility. While
smaller electrolyzers may reduce the CAPEX contribution to LCOH, they will also under­
utilize the available renewable energy. Therefore, the analysis aims to find an optimal
ratio for cost and utilization of the RES electricity production.
The scaling analysis is first applied to the offshore wind scenario. In this case, the wind
farm capacity is fixed at 1 GW, while a range of electrolyzer sizes is evaluated. Each elec­
trolyzer size is assessed against some target values for the CAPEX component. These
targets are used to determine the required scaling that enables sufficient operating hours
and hydrogen production cost. The objective is to understand how large the electrolyzer
can be, relative to the wind farm, in order to reduce the CAPEX cost per kilogram of
hydrogen to an acceptable level.
To evaluate how effectively each electrolyzer size uses the available renewable energy,
a parallel calculation of hydrogen utilization efficiency is performed. For each electrolyzer
capacity, the total hydrogen output over the economic lifetime is compared to the theoret­
ical maximum hydrogen output that could be produced if all available renewable energy
were utilized.
In the offshore wind scenario, this maximum is defined by an equally sized 1 GW elec­
trolyzer that can operate at full capacity during all hours of available wind power. Smaller
electrolyzer sizes will not be able to use the full wind output, leading to lower hydrogen
production. The hydrogen utilization efficiency is calculated as:

3.4
Assumptions and Simplifications
This section outlines the main assumptions and simplifications made in the modeling of
green hydrogen production. A set of general assumptions applies across all the models,
while additional model­specific assumptions are listed separately.
3.4.1
General model assumptions
The following assumptions and simplifications apply to all models.
• Generalized electrolyzer: The model is designed to represent the generalized per­
formance for PEMEL or AEL technologies. Hydrogen purity and output pressure are
not explicitly modeled.
• System boundaries: The model excludes additional revenue streams from by­
products such as oxygen or heat, and does not consider potential subsidies or green
premiums.
• Identical years: Each year of the economic lifetime is assumed to be identical in
terms of inputs such as operating hours, electricity prices or renewable availability.
The only parameters that change between years is the degradation of the elec­
trolyzer stack, and discounted hydrogen output.
22
Analysis of cost drivers in green hydrogen production
==PAGE:31==
• Constant load degradation: Stack degradation is updated on a yearly basis and
treated as a constant value for each year. Stack degradation is modeled as a linear
function of operating hours. Dynamic degradation rate between full­load and partial­
load is not considered.
• Operation dynamics: The models does not consider dynamic operational behav­
iors such as startup time and partial­load efficiencies.
• Full­load operation: For the base case and spot price scenarios, the electrolyzer
is assumed to operate at maximum capacity during all active operating hours.
• No hydrogen storage: It is assumed that all produced hydrogen is immediately
transported via pipeline. Technical or economic aspects of hydrogen storage are
not included in the model.
3.4.2
Spot Price Scenario
In addition to the general assumptions, the spot price model includes the following sim­
plifications.
• One year price data: A single year of hourly spot price data is repeated identically
across the full project lifetime. This simplification excludes any long term changes
in electricity market dynamics.
• Fixed price threshold: A fixed electricity price threshold is used as a simplified
operating strategy. It does not represent how a real hydrogen plant would operate,
but it serves as a useful tool to explore the trade­off between operating hours and
electricity costs.
3.4.3
RES scenario
For the offshore wind and hybrid models, the following additional assumptions are made.
• One year energy data: A single year of energy production data is repeated for all
years, and no annual variability in wind and solar profiles are modeled.
• Direct use of renewable energy: All available power that meets the electrolyzers
operating constraints is used for hydrogen production.
• Constant load degradation: Stack degradation is based on cumulative operating
hours, regardless of actual load level. Partial loads are not treated differently from
full­load operation in terms of degradation.
• Constant energy consumption: The energy consumption of the stack is constant
on a yearly basis, and updated based on degradation for every new year. The model
does not consider a dynamic load dependent efficiency of the stack.
• Energy allocation: Electricity input is proportionally allocated between wind and
solar for each hour, allowing for separate cost tracking per source.

In this chapter, the results are presented in the same order as the model evolves. Starting
from the base case model, following the integration of data files with variable electricity

First, an overview of the estimated LCOH range is given, based on techno­economic

The goal of this section is to identify the main cost drivers of the LCOH, establish a refer­

impacts the LCOH and the hydrogen utilization efficiency. The objective is to identify if the
right system configurations can meet cost targets while maximizing the use of available

5
Discussion
This chapter is structured into three parts. First, the methodology is discussed, high­
lighting the modeling approach and simplifications. This is followed by a discussion of
the results. In the end, broader reflections on green hydrogen production are discussed,
including its competitiveness compared to gray hydrogen, market considerations, and po­
tential directions for future research.
5.1
Methodology
Base case model
The base case model uses a simplified static approach where key input values such as
electricity price, energy consumption, degradation rate, and annual operating hours are
treated as fixed average values. While this method does not reflect the full complexity of
a real­world hydrogen production plant, it provides a transparent and practical framework
for analyzing the main cost drivers of green hydrogen.
A central simplification in the model is the assumption of a yearly fixed energy consump­
tion per kilogram of hydrogen. In practice, the electrolyzers energy consumption is not a
fixed value. It depends on various technical factors including load levels and startup con­
ditions. These dynamics are not captured with fixed averaged input values. Additionally,
it is not technically accurate to update the stack degradation on a yearly basis. In reality,
stack degradation is a dynamic behavior that continually evolves.
It is worth noting that the model assumes that all of the operating hours occur at full load.
This simplifies the realities of a hydrogen plant operating under dynamic conditions. In
practice, variable energy supply from renewable energy sources or fluctuating spot market
prices would lead to hours with partial load operation and frequent start­up cycles. These
effects are not captured in the model. As a result, the operating hours should be seen as
indicators or estimates of what is required for economic viability, rather than exact hourly
targets.
From an economic standpoint, the base­case model also simplifies certain cost elements
by assuming fixed electricity prices, grid fees, and taxes. The annual fixed OPEX is mod­
eled as a fixed percentage of CAPEX, without itemizing specific costs such as water us­
age, maintenance, or labor. While these are economic simplifications, they allow for con­
sistency when comparing different scenarios and technologies.
Despite these simplifications and limitations, the base­case model is still a useful tool
for the cost analysis. It clearly identifies the main cost drivers and illustrates under what
conditions, especially in terms of electricity price, CAPEX and operating hours a green
hydrogen plant could become cost competitive with gray hydrogen. To summarize, the
base­case model does not replicate the detailed technical operation of a hydrogen plant,
but it serves as a simplified and effective framework for estimating cost ranges and eval­
uating economic competitiveness. While it lacks technical precision, its clarity and com­
parability make it a valuable starting point for assessing hydrogen production strategies.
Spot price model
The spot price model was developed to explore aspects that the base­case model could
not capture. While the two models may produce similar outputs if the average electricity
prices and operating hours are aligned. The spot price model allows for a deeper insight
Analysis of cost drivers in green hydrogen production
39
==PAGE:48==
into the relationship between electricity prices and operating hours. The model captures
how a grid­connected electrolyzer could take advantage of low­cost electricity by operat­
ing only during hours with low spot prices, and how this would affect the LCOH.
In this model, the electrolyzer is assumed to operate only during hours when the spot price
is below a given price threshold. While this does not reflect a realistic operation strategy,
it effectively captures the trade­off between reduced electricity costs and fewer operat­
ing hours. This trade­off is critical for understanding what is required for grid­connected
hydrogen production to become cost competitive, and it highlights the potential and limi­
tations of consuming electricity from the spot market.
A key simplification for this model is the replication of one year of hourly spot price data
across the full project lifetime. In reality, electricity prices vary from year to year, and the
model does not capture long­term market volatility. Despite this simplification, the model
is still useful for illustrating the general behavior and limitations of green hydrogen plants
purchasing electricity from the day­ahead spot market.
Technical constraints such as startup time, ramping, and minimum load are not consid­
ered, which makes the model less realistic from an operational point of view. Additionally,
the model assumes that the electrolyzer can consume large amounts of electricity without
influencing the spot market price, which may not be true for large­scale plants.
Despite these simplifications, the spot price model provides valuable insights into the
economic potential and barriers of the spot market. It highlights the importance of low
CAPEX costs to allow fewer operating hours, making room for consuming cheaper elec­
tricity. While not technically precise, the method successfully reveals the opportunities
and boundaries of hydrogen production by purchasing electricity from the spot market.
RES connected models
The RES connected models do similarly to the base­case and spot price models rely on a
simplified representation of electrolyzer operation. The model assumes constant energy
consumption and does not account for startup times or part­load behavior. As a result, it
may slightly overestimate hydrogen output by neglecting operational constraints such as
cold starts and dynamic electrolyzer efficiency.
Additionally, the modeling of stack degradation is simplified. Degradation is based only on
total operating hours, without considering variations due to load changes, frequent shut­
downs, or startup conditions. This could underestimate the true wear on the electrolyzer
stack in intermittent systems.
Despite these limitations, the model captures how the variability of renewable electricity
production influences the LCOH. It enables the analysis of how proper scaling between
the renewable energy system and the electrolyzer capacity can reduce the cost of the
CAPEX component. While the model is not technically detailed, it provides a reasonable
estimate of performance and cost outcomes in RES­connected hydrogen production.
General summary of the methodology
The methodology used in this thesis does not aim to replicate the full technical and eco­
nomic complexity of real­world green hydrogen plants. It focuses on capturing the main
cost drivers that influence the LCOH of green hydrogen projects. While the models sim­
plify operational dynamics and some details in the financial structures, they provide re­
liable indicators of the cost range for different production strategies. These results form
a solid basis for identifying the main barriers and targets for competitive green hydrogen
production.

The use of LCOH as the comparison metric is based on the simplicity of the metric allowing
consistency across scenarios. It is worth noting that the LCOH does not reflect the full
economic viability of a project. For example, it does not consider revenue streams, loan
structures, or internal rate of return. For such purposes, more detailed financial tools like
discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis would be required. However, LCOH is still widely
used in techno­economic studies because it offers a clear and consistent way to compare
production costs across technologies and configurations.
It is also important to note that the analysis focuses on the cost of hydrogen at the point
of production, within the defined system boundaries. It does not include downstream el­
ements such as storage, transport, or delivery to end users. This means that the LCOH
values presented do not represent the final consumer price, but rather the cost of produc­
ing hydrogen at the plant.
Other important factors that can affect hydrogen projects, such as site­specific conditions,
local demand, or potential co­product revenues from heat or oxygen are excluded. The
purpose of the analysis is to isolate and understand the core cost structure of hydro­
gen production, without relying on optimistic assumptions or external revenues that could
lower the LCOH. This results in a clear and transparent comparison between green and
gray hydrogen based on hydrogen output and production costs.
5.2
Results
The main results from this analysis highlight the two critical cost components that currently
prevent green hydrogen from becoming cost competitive, CAPEX and electricity cost.
These two components are the main cost drivers for the LCOH across all production
scenarios. These results align well with the findings from various literature from Section
2.5.
The base case model analyzing the range of optimistic and pessimistic input values gave
the LCOH range from 4.63 to 10.00 EUR/kg. This is slightly below the reported values from
the European Hydrogen Bank auction, with values ranging from 5.8 ­ 13.5 EUR/kg, with
only four countries reporting average costs below 6 EUR/kg. This comparison indicates
that the values used for this analysis may be slightly optimistic.
Among the various scenarios analyzed, the spot market model appears to be the most
cost­effective option. This is primarily due to favorable market dynamics that allow access
to cheap electricity prices below those typically found in fixed­price PPAs. Since electricity
cost is the largest contributor to LCOH, access to low spot prices significantly reduces the
cost of hydrogen.
It is worth noting that despite the spot market being the cheapest production strategy, even
the best spot market case (NO4) results in LCOH values between 3.25 and 3.97 EUR/kg.
This is still a more than 50% higher cost than the target of 2 EUR/kg. NO4 represents
a best­case scenario and should not be considered as a standard expectation for large­
scale hydrogen production. Instead, it highlights the potential under optimal conditions,
such as those found in northern Norway where electricity is cheap and renewable.
For the more typical Norwegian price zone NO2, the total LCOH ranged from 5.45 to 6.95
EUR/kg across four years (excluding the extreme year of 2022). This is likely a better
reflection of what a grid­connected hydrogen plant might expect under spot market­based
operation.
It is also an important note that the spot market scenario does not capture the system
challenges related to the integration of a large­scale hydrogen plant. The model does not

reflect how integrating a large­scale electrolysis facility into the grid might affect market
prices and the transmission infrastructure. In areas like NO4, although the electricity is
cheap, it is unclear whether the grid can handle the impact of a large­scale electricity
consumer. According to the literature reviewed earlier, infrastructure constraints such as
limited transmission and the lack of hydrogen pipelines can significantly influence overall
project viability.
Additionally, the model does not account for the long­term sustainability of using hy­
dropower for hydrogen production. Reducing the water reservoirs to produce hydrogen
may have negative consequences for long­term energy reliability, especially in dry sea­
sons or during peak electricity demand. It is worth considering the use of dispatchable
hydropower for hydrogen production compared to intermittent wind and solar power.
For the RES­connected scenarios using electricity from wind or solar, the results show that
it is possible to achieve the required annual operating hours through appropriate system
scaling. However, these systems are challenged by high electricity costs, as they depend
on the LCOE from renewable sources. The results highlight the need for the LCOE to fall
to around 20 EUR/MWh or lower to make hydrogen production competitive. This LCOE
target still has a long way to go, especially for offshore wind.
While LCOEs for solar and wind have continuously declined over the past decade, the
LCOEs still need to fall to less than half of the current cost. This is a key barrier for green
hydrogen production, where offshore wind in particular has a high LCOE, making it difficult
to achieve a competitive LCOH.
While spot market operation can deliver the lowest LCOH under ideal conditions, it re­
quires the grid to meet the 90% renewable share threshold. Most grids today do not meet
this threshold, which means that the corresponding production with wind and solar is ex­
pected to be a more common strategy across Europe. This strategy currently lacks the
economic viability to compete without significant cost reductions in both LCOE for wind
and solar.
The analysis shows that a minimum specific CAPEX of 1500 EUR/kW is required to reach
the CAPEX component target of 1 EUR/kg. It is also worth noting that this target might
also be an insufficient target, most likely leading to a higher cost than 2 EUR/kg. Since
the 1500 EUR/kW CAPEX is considered a futuristic cost estimate, the analysis does not
explore the impact of reductions below 1500/1000 EUR/kW in detail. Based on Figure
4.4 the results show that around 6700 annual full­load hours are required to reach the
1 EUR/kg target. This sets the bar for the scaling of the electrolyzer to RES system.
To summarize the key cost targets required to reach the 2 EUR/kg cost, the following
conditions need to be met.
• CAPEX must fall below 1500 EUR/kW, ideally closer to 1000 EUR/kW.
• Electricity cost must average below 20 EUR/MWh for more than 6700 annual full
load hours.
• These targets assumes that the other cost components (other OPEX, grid fees and
taxes) must be minimized, approaching less than 0.5 EUR/kg.
These findings underscore the importance of continuing cost reductions, particularly for
electrolyzer manufacturing and renewable energy deployment, to enable green hydrogen
to become a viable and competitive solution at scale. It also highlights that the main bar­
riers lie within high costs related to CAPEX and OPEX and not for the technical limitations
of a plant.

5.3
Reflections and outlook
The results from this analysis suggest that the cost target of 2 EUR/kg for green hydrogen
is unlikely to be achieved in the near term. A more realistic target may lie in the range
of 3 to 4 EUR/kg, assuming favorable developments in CAPEX reduction and renewable
electricity costs.
Even under optimistic assumptions, the analysis indicates that green hydrogen cannot
compete with gray hydrogen on cost alone. The economic gap remains significant, pri­
marily due to high electrolyzer CAPEX and too expensive renewable electricity. These
cost barriers are not expected to close in the near future without strong policy support.
To improve the economic competitiveness of green hydrogen, policy interventions such
as higher CO2 taxes on fossil fuel­based hydrogen or subsidies for green hydrogen pro­
duction will be necessary. These mechanisms can help level the production costs and
accelerate market adoption while hopefully the costs of green hydrogen gradually fall due
to learning curves.
A deployment strategy may be to start small, deploying hydrogen production in regions
with particularly favorable conditions. For example, a small to medium­sized electrolysis
plant in the NO4 price zone in Norway could serve local industrial demand and demon­
strate feasibility. From there, the industry can expand step by step, following a systematic
deployment path rather than aiming for rapid large­scale implementation.
The EU hydrogen targets are ambitious and they may need to be adjusted to reflect eco­
nomic realities. Achieving long­term cost competitiveness for green hydrogen will require
a combination of cost reductions, targeted deployment in optimal regions, and supportive
policy frameworks.
A relevant direction for future work would be to analyze how capital costs for electrolyzer
systems are likely to decline as more projects are developed. Understanding the learning
curve and estimating the rate of CAPEX reduction per additional system installed could
help assess whether the ambitious cost targets for green hydrogen are realistically achiev­
able. CAPEX reduction is critical for the viability of green hydrogen, and as it stands
today, early­stage projects face the disadvantage of being first movers. Additionally, a
more structured deployment strategy in relation to the CAPEX learning curve should be
investigated to help manage this challenge. Projects should be initiated in regions with fa­
vorable conditions, such as Norways NO4 zone or areas with exceptional solar and wind
resources and a local demand. As deployment scales and costs decline over time, the
hydrogen economy could then expand in a more economically viable way. Referring to
the wide cost ranges reported today, an analysis on a targeted deployment strategy may
offer a more economically stable path forward.

6
Conclusion
This thesis has analyzed the cost drivers of green hydrogen production and compared
them against the target LCOH of 2 EUR/kg. The analysis compares two production strate­
gies that meet the EU regulations for green hydrogen production. A grid­connected elec­
trolysis in a renewable power system and hydrogen production with temporal and geo­
graphical correlation with renewable energy sources.
The main results from this analysis are that electricity cost, followed by the CAPEX are
the two main cost drivers preventing green hydrogen from becoming cost­competitive.
The 2 EUR/kg target appears to be out of reach under the current market conditions. If
green hydrogen will ever have the chance to reach this target, this analysis provides some
minimum cost targets. The specific CAPEX cost must fall below 1500 EUR/kW, and the
plant must operate for more than 6700 full­load hours annually with an average electricity
price below 20 EUR/MWh. Even these targets can be considered as optimistic to meet
the 2 EUR/kg, assuming small contributions from additional cost components such as grid
fees, taxes and other OPEX costs.
The comparison between production strategies revealed that the lowest LCOH of 3.25
EUR/kg is achieved in an optimal spot market production scenario, where hydrogen pro­
duction takes advantage of periods with very low electricity prices. On the other hand,
the spot market scenario also results in the highest LCOH of 6.95 EUR/kg reflecting the
risk of relying on the variable spot market. The main barrier from this strategy is related to
ensuring enough annual operating hours while maintaining low average electricity costs.
For correlating hydrogen production with offshore wind and solar energy, the LCOH is
slightly higher, ranging from 5.94 EUR/kg for the hybrid scenario to 6.33 EUR/kg for the
offshore wind only scenario. These production strategies suffer from high electricity costs
in relation to too high LCOEs for both wind and solar energy. In these hydrogen sys­
tems, the cost reduction potential comes with scaling the electrolyzer capacity right to
reach enough operating hours to lower the CAPEX component. This analysis shows if
the CAPEX cost can be reduced to 1500 EUR/kW, the required operating hours can be
reached with an electrolyzer ratio of 0.5 with an offshore wind farm. This electrolyzer scal­
ing results in a hydrogen production ratio of 0.69 which means that the system captures
69% of the total available energy produced by the offshore wind farm. This hydrogen
utilization efficiency can be increased with the addition of solar energy, resulting in a hy­
drogen output ratio of 0.78. Combining this result with the LCOH of 5.94 EUR/kg, the
hybrid system configuration stands out as a promising production strategy.
In summary, the analysis highlights how it is not the technical parameters such as energy
consumption per kg hydrogen that stand out as the main barrier preventing low produc­
tion costs for green hydrogen. For both production scenarios, the problem lies in securing
enough annual operating hours (around 6700) to reduce the CAPEX component, and se­
curing cheap enough electricity (average below 20 EUR/MWh) across all those hours.
Both of the production strategies can reach the required annual operating hours with the
right electrolyzer scaling and the willingness to purchase expensive electricity. The bar­
rier then comes to too high electricity costs over these hours. Given the state of todays
technology and market conditions, it is not possible to produce green hydrogen at a com­
petitive cost level without substantial support. If the CAPEX can be lowered, the need
Analysis of cost drivers in green hydrogen production
45
==PAGE:54==
for a large amount of operating hours reduced, opening up for the possibility of utilizing
cheaper electricity.
The future outlook for green hydrogen will likely depend on stricter CO2 policies, carbon
pricing, and targeted subsidies or incentives to help bridge the cost gap. Without such
measures, green hydrogen is unlikely to reach the necessary cost levels for broad com­
mercial adoption in the near future.
