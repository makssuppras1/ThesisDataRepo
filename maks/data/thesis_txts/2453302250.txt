ABSTRACT
The potential and application of Real-time Travelling Wave (TW) based fault locating
methods are investigated for an oﬀshore wind farm with hybrid cross-bonded export
cables. The ﬁrst half of this report focuses on modelling of transmission cables, instrument
transformers and the oﬀshore wind farm, compatible with TW theory. Whereas, the second
half focuses on the Electromagnetic Transient simulations of the developed oﬀshore wind
farm system, and then, experiments testing the reliability of the real-time current based
TW fault locator in the SEL T400L relay, for the common discontinuities in an oﬀshore
wind farm power system.
Based on testing, it was observed that the double-ended TW fault locator was very accurate
for a hybrid cross-bonded export cable when it was terminated by an equivalent grid,
representing a combination of transformers, reactors and transmission lines/cables, at
the both ends. However, due to multiple TW reﬂections from the discontinuities such as
fault, bus terminals, cross-bonding and non-homogeneous joints, the single-ended TW fault
locator could not reliably estimate the correct fault location on the export cable.
Further, when considering the single hybrid cross-bonded export cable with transformer
and reactor termination, as in some oﬀshore wind farms, the double-ended TW fault
locator failed. This was due to the inability of the current TWs to penetrate the oﬀshore
substation’s surge impedance, which is dominated by the inductance of the transformer
and shunt line reactor. This results in complete reﬂection and hence cancelling of any
current TWs at that terminal. For such scenario, the fault location could be estimated
oﬄine through the SEL T400L relay, by measuring the arrival times of the incident voltage
TWs at the line terminals, as voltage TWs are not aﬀected by the inductive transformer
and reactor windings.
The double-ended TW based fault locator was observed to be reliable for the same oﬀshore
wind farm but with two or more hybrid cross-bonded export cables. Since, here, the current
TWs do not get completely reﬂected, i.e a part of the current TWs propagate through
the additional line, and hence do not get cancelled. Also, the TW fault locator, could
accurately locate the line faults even when there was no wind power production. Further,
for tests with typical fault inception angles in a cable system, the TW fault locator could
easily pick up the TWs for fault location calculations. It was also observed that the TW
fault locator was immune to Current Transformer core saturation.
Finally, an alternative method for fault locating based on Wavelet Transform was examined,
where, the core voltage, core currents or sheath currents could be freely be chosen as input
to the developed fault location algorithm. The results from the Wavelet Transform method
were veriﬁed against the test cases from the SEL T400L relay testing. As a result of the
transform’s ability to localise transients in time, very accurate fault location on cable
systems could be observed.

INTRODUCTION
This chapter is an introduction to the report. Here, a brief background of the project
is presented, along with the problem formulation. Further, the methodology and scope
of the project are also discussed. Finally, the outline of the report describing the major
components and construction are presented.
1.1
Background
Most faults on overhead lines are caused by temporary occurrences such as lightning,
conductor swing, contact with trees, etc.
In the overhead lines, since the electrical
insulation is air, which is self-restoring, an auto re-closure mechanism can be used to
obtain the original system conﬁguration without the need for examining the fault location.
However, when a fault occurs in cables, since the insulation material being non-self-restoring,
re-energizing the cable may lead to more damage on the cable. Instead, the fault must be
located and repaired before the cable is re-energized.
Further, in the case of oﬀshore wind farms, where production units are connected through
single export cable, it gets completely disconnected from the main grid in case of line faults.
Long outage time results in ﬁnancial losses for the wind farm owner as well as bottlenecks
in the power system network. Hence, a fast and accurate fault locating method is required.
Present oﬄine fault locating methods require special equipment, trained personnel, and
the faulted cable out of service before it can be applied. Whereas the online methods (or
the real-time methods) utilize information in the current and voltage measurements at the
fault locator terminals, as a result, fault locating can be very fast.
1.2
Formulation of the problem
In general, the export cable in oﬀshore wind farms are cross-bonded and of hybrid
conﬁguration (a combination of land and submarine cables). This introduces new diﬃculties
for existing real-time fault locators, such as those based on Impedance method, as they
expect a homogeneous impedance for the entire cable run [2]. In this thesis, the analysis
and testing of a fault locator system based on travelling wave concept, in the protection
relay SEL-T400L will be carried out. Theoretically, the travelling wave method is expected
to provide both fast and accurate fault location even if the cables are cross-bonded and
hybrid. Based on this, the following questions will be addressed during the project:
• How a high-voltage oﬀshore wind farm export cable, which is both cross-bonded and
hybrid, can be accurately modelled in order to observe the travelling waves during
power system transients?

• Whether the typical inductive measurement transformers (CT and VT) can replicate
the high-frequency transients (i.e. travelling waves) in its secondary side, such that
the fault locator in SEL T400L relay can pick up the travelling waves?
• How accurate are the results from the travelling wave-based fault locator in the SEL
T400L relay, under various fault conditions on the wind farm export cable?
• Does the travelling wave-based fault locator in the SEL T400L relay, still locate if
there is no power output from the oﬀshore wind farm?
1.3
Methodology, scope and conditions
The SEL-T400L relay incorporates a travelling wave-based fault locator with two methods,
namely the Single-ended method and the Double-ended method. The single-ended method
requires only one relay on either terminal of the line. Whereas, to implement the double-
ended method, SEL-T400L relays are required on both sides with a communication channel.
It must be noted that the travelling wave-based fault locator in SEL T400L has been
developed for transmission lines, and not for pure cable conﬁguration. The main objective
of this project is to know the reliability of the SEL-T400L relay and the accuracy of the
fault location with regards to a cross-bonded hybrid oﬀshore wind farm export cable. The
following are the detailed project objectives to be carried out.
• Modelling of oﬀshore wind farm export cable along with measurement transformers
in PSCAD. Initially, an equivalent wind farm (grid) model would be considered with
emphasis on the frequency-dependent cable modelling.
• Simulating various cable fault scenarios and generating 1 MHz sampling rate
COMTRADE (Common Format for Transient Data Exchange) ﬁles in PSCAD.
• Building a reliable test setup with the two SEL-T400L protection relays to test the
inbuilt standalone travelling wave-based fault locator.
• Conﬁguring the SEL-T400L protection relays and playing back the COMTRADE
ﬁles, to check the reliability of the travelling wave-based fault locator.
• Comparing the performance of both the single-ended and double-ended travelling
wave fault locating method for the cross-bonded and hybrid export cable.
• Finally, an oﬀshore wind farm with type-4 wind turbine converter model must be
developed. Further, the reliability of the travelling wave fault locator would be tested
under various fault conditions with diﬀerent generation scenarios of the wind farm.
• Further, theoretical veriﬁcation of a test case on the SEL T400L fault locator would
be carried out, to compare the experimental and analytical results.
A fault at any point on the voltage wave launches a travelling wave that propagates in
both directions from the fault location, except when the fault happens at voltage zero.
This is a limitation of a travelling wave based fault locator. However, it is understood that
in cable systems the fault, i.e. insulation breakdown will most likely occur at maximum
voltage peak with some distribution and standard deviation.

1.4
Thesis outline
This section presents a short summary of all the forthcoming chapters and the information
that they carry. This thesis is separated into 10 chapters.
Chapter 2: Fundamentals of Traveling Waves
In this chapter, a brief description of the fundamental concepts of transmission lines and
the travelling wave theory is presented. Further, a brief discussion regarding the common
discontinuities in the power system is alos presented.
Chapter 3: Real-Time Fault Locating Methods
In this chapter, the operating principle for both the travelling wave-based fault locating
methods is presented. This is followed by an adaptation of the travelling wave methods on
hybrid export cables. Finally, a method to determine the propagation delay of the hybrid
lines is discussed.
Chapter 4: HV Export Cable Modelling

FUNDAMENTALS OF TRAVELLING WAVES
Travelling wave phenomenon in high voltage lines constitutes one of the shortest power
system transients. It occurs from microseconds to milliseconds. Travelling waves are
associated with the propagation of electromagnetic waves which results from the short
circuits, lightning or switching operations in the power system. A sudden and signiﬁcant
change in voltage within the high voltage line (ref. Fig. 2.1), leads to the initiation of an
electromagnetic wave which propagates from that point in opposite directions.
Figure 2.1: Propagation of electromagnetic wave as a result of fault.
An electromagnetic wave can be divided into a voltage wave associated with phenomena
occurring in the electric ﬁeld and a current wave associated with the magnetic ﬁeld. An
important feature of such a wave is moving the speciﬁc values of voltage and current along
the lines with ﬁnite speed [3].

2.3
Reﬂection and refraction of travelling waves
Travelling waves travel along the transmission line and encounter discontinuities, such as
buses and transformers. When travelling waves reach a discontinuity, part of it is reﬂected
back and the remaining part passes through. The magnitude of the reﬂected and refracted
waves depends on the characteristic impedance of the transmission line and the impedance
beyond the discontinuity. The amplitude of the reﬂected and refracted waves is such that
the proportionality of the voltage and current is preserved.
The phenomenon of the reﬂection and refraction of travelling waves is shown in Bewley’s
Lattice diagram, which is reproduced in Fig. 2.4. This is a convenient diagram devised by
Bewley, which shows at a glance the position and direction of motion of every incident,
reﬂected, and transmitted wave on the system at every instant of time. The diagram
overcomes the diﬃculty of otherwise keeping track of the multiplicity of successive reﬂections
at the various junctions [7].

REAL-TIME FAULT LOCATING METHODS
The SEL-T400L, time-domain line protection relay incorporates a standalone fault locator
with four fault-locating methods, (a) Single-ended TW-based method, (b) Double-ended
TW-based method, (c) Single-ended impedance-based method, and (d) Double-ended
impedance-based method. In this thesis, due to the known inaccuracy of impedance-based
fault location methods for cross-bonded hybrid cables [9], only the TW based fault locating
method is studied. This chapter presents a detailed operating principle for both the TW
based fault locating methods.
In order to implement the Traveling-wave-based fault locating methods, ﬁrst, the TWs
have to be extracted that are generated by the faults. The SEL-T400L uses an anti-aliasing
ﬁlter (1 MHz sampling rate) and ﬁnite impulse response ﬁlter to extract TWs from the
raw input signals [10]. The ﬁlter combines the operation of numerical diﬀerentiation and
low-pass ﬁltering (or smoothing) and is therefore called the diﬀerentiator-smoother.

Travelling wave method on hybrid lines
When a transmission line consists of land and underground cables, the travelling-wave
propagation velocity is diﬀerent in each section. This diﬀerence in the travelling-wave
propagation velocity makes it challenging to calculate the fault location using Eq. 3.2 or
Eq. 3.3. [11] summarizes the approach for locating faults on hybrid lines as follows:
For a transmission line with three diﬀerent sections,
• Step-1: Calculate the fault location M* with Eq. 3.2 or 3.3 as if the line were
homogeneous.
• Step-2: Calculate the propagation time t* corresponding to the fault location M*
assuming the line is homogeneous.
• Step-3: Calculate the actual fault location M corresponding to the propagation
time t* using the distance and propagation time characteristics of the actual (non-
homogeneous) line.

4.1
Cable construction
A typical single-phase, HV co-axial cable is formed by four main layers, namely; Conductor,
Insulation, Sheath, Outer Insulation and some times armour. In addition to these layers,
the cable also has semi-conductive (SC) layers, swelling tapes and metal foil. A typical
cross-section of a HV cable is presented in Fig. 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Typical cross section of a single phase, HV co-axial cable.
4.1.1
Conductor
The purpose of the core conductor is to transmit the required current with low losses. The
core conductor can be solid, hollow, stranded or segmental. Hollow conductors are usually
used for the oil or water cooling. The advantage of stranded and segmental conductors is
that they provide a reduced current displacement because of skin eﬀect. The core conductor
is either made of copper (Cu) or aluminium (Al) where Cu has a lower resistance which
leads to lesser conductor cross-sectional area. Whereas, the advantage of Al over Cu, is
that Al has a lower density which leads to much less weight for the same cable capacity.

4.1.2
Insulation and semi-conductive layers
The purpose of the insulation is to ensure that there is no electrical connection between
the two current-carrying components of the cable, the core conductor and the sheath. The
insulation between the two conducting layers resembles a very long cylindrical capacitor.
The AC loads will, therefore, draw a capacitive charging current which is superimposed on
the desired current transmitted by the conductor. There are two main types of dielectrics
for extruded HV cables, cross-linked polyethylene (XLPE) and Ethylene-propylene (EP).
The semi-conductive (SC) layers are placed between the insulation and the core conductor
and again between the insulation and the sheath. The purpose of the SC layer is to reduce
the electrical stress in the inner insulation and prevent the formation of voids, due to
bending of the cable or other mechanical stress. The permittivity of SC is very high
compared to the insulator permittivity. In PSCAD, the value of the relative permittivity
of SC is ﬁxed at 1000.
4.1.3
Sheath
The purpose of the sheath is to have a covering for electrostatic screening as well as a
return path for the cable’s charging current and a conduction path for earth fault current
in the case of faults on the cable. The sheath is constructed of two layers, a wired screen
and metallic tape, separated with a thin SC swelling tape. The wired screen and metallic
tape are normally considered as a single conducting layer in PSCAD cable model.
Under normal operation, the sheath will carry the charging current, which would result
in large steady-state losses, hence to avoid sheath over-voltages at cable terminals, the
sheath is grounded [12]. For long cables, the sheaths are cross bonded, such that the
induced sheath currents can be cancelled out, and by grounding in between, the sheath
over-voltages can be reduced. A representation of the cross bonding and grounding scheme
is presented in Fig. 4.2.

Travel time Interpolation: The FDPM model uses travelling wave theory to calculate
the terminal conditions of the cable. Therefore, the travelling time must be larger than the

the time step. Therefore it is necessary to interpolate the travel time.

range is too high, this could result in a very large shunt conductance and shunt losses.

calculations.
Total number of frequency increments: This is the total number of calculation steps
used in the frequency range for the curve ﬁtting. An increased number of steps will result
in longer simulation time.
Maximum order of ﬁtting for Ysurge and the propagation function: Usually, the
cable constants program will iterate and increase the order of curve ﬁtted waveforms until
the error is below a speciﬁed threshold. However, for some real-time applications, there is
not enough time to continuously increase the curve ﬁtted waveforms until an acceptable
accuracy is reached. Therefore, it is necessary to set a maximum order of ﬁtting for both
the surge admittance and the propagation function.
Maximum ﬁtting error for Ysurge and the propagation function: Choosing a large
number here results in a poorly ﬁtted surge admittance and propagation function. This
will then lead to an inaccurate calculation of the terminal conditions for the cable model.
On the other hand, choosing a very small ﬁtting error can lead to unstable simulations. It
is recommended by PSCAD to use 0.2 for these values.

4.4
Faults in transmission cables
Internal failures in a HV cable results from gradual deterioration of the insulation between
core and sheath [13]. Voids and impurities in the insulation material or between the
boundaries of diﬀerent material can initiate a process called treeing leading to insulation
breakdown [14]. When the insulation breaks down, an electric arc forms a low impedance
path between the cable’s core and sheath. The arc typically burns until the protection
system disconnects the cable.
All internal faults on shielded cables are shunt faults.
When the protection system
disconnects the cable, the fault can develop into a series fault or stay as a shunt fault [15].
A shunt exists if the mechanical forces ensured a connection between core and sheath, i.e.
if a carbon-metal bridge exists, it permits a low resistance path. A series fault exists when
the conductor is disconnected at one location [15], this occurs if a part of the conductor/
joint is blown apart during the fault.
Typically, the internal faults on cables are single-core to sheath faults. The ground can be
included as a return path directly from the fault location if the outer jacket is damaged by
the fault. Two or three-phase faults are most often caused by external factors or initiated
by a single-phase to sheath fault. The sheath is always involved in any fault as it encloses
the core completely.

INSTRUMENT TRANSFORMER MODELLING
In a HV power cable, both the core voltages and the core currents are of interest for
fault location purposes. However, it is important how the measurement transformers used,
represent the high-frequency fault transients in the secondary circuit. It is not important
that the fault transient is represented exactly, instead the only requirement is that the
wave is not delayed in a degree that compromises fault location and not damped to a
degree that it becomes undetectable. In this chapter, the current transformer and the
voltage transformer modelled in PSCAD is presented.
5.1
Instrument transformers for TW method
Inductive current transformers (CT) for transient representation in the secondary circuit is
quite well studied in the literature. Leakage inductance, stray capacitance and saturation
of the core, limits the bandwidth of the transformer. However, good representation of the
high-frequency components in the secondary circuit is reported in [16] [17] [18], where it is
concluded that the inductive current transformers can be used up till 200 kHz, and the
same is suitable for travelling wave-based fault location.
The use of the classical inductive VT is not very well studied for travelling wave-based
fault location purposes. The use of an inductive coupler can compromise the accuracy of
the fault location due to the band-pass features of the VT [19]. In [20], the transformer is
used for transients up till 10 kHz. However, it must be noted that a high bandwidth VT
may not be required in the present studies, but it is suggested to have one, as TWs from
the VT is needed for other functions in the relay, such as to detect polarity to determine
internal or external faults, etc [10].
5.2

The SEL T400L relay has two sets of current transformer inputs for individual current
metering and recording that can be combined for protection and fault locating [10]. The

the IEC 1 A CT nominal rating is used. Here, it must be noted that the 1 A setting has a
limit of 50 A peak current which may occur during fault conditions. Further, a multi-core
10 VA 5P20 CT is selected, which has a ratio of 1250:1 (N = 1250). Assuming a CT
secondary resistance of 2.5 mΩper turn, the total winding resistance will be 3.125 Ω( =
1025 turns · 0.0025Ω/turn).

During a fault condition, protection of the DC link capacitor is required to avoid overvoltages

A voltage hysteresis controller is used to issue the chopper ﬁring pulses. The Hysteresis
controller converts a real signal into a logic one. It provides some noise immunity by
preventing a transition to a new logic state until the input signal has moved decidedly
across the input threshold [28]. While the input signal is inside the hysteresis region, the
previous output level is maintained. Further, the size of the chopper resistance is such that
it can dissipate the full power rating of the PMSG.

Over the last few years, the Wavelet Transform (WLT) has gained a lot of attention for

signal analysis, and it has excellent ability of time-frequency localization. This proves to be
advantageous in determining the wave-front of the initial TW and the subsequent reﬂections.

of the current and voltage WLT appear accordingly.
9.1.1
Scale and frequency
The WLT decomposes the time domain signal i.e. current and/or voltage, into scales
(frequency band) using a mother wavelet. The low scales contains high frequencies, while the
high scales contain low frequencies. For a normal voltage or current signal, the fundamental
will dominate, and the lower scales which contain the high-frequency components will have
small wavelet coeﬃcients (WCs). However, when a fault wave arrives at the terminal, the
WCs at the lower scales will have higher values. It must be noted that diﬀerent faults will
have diﬀerent frequency content and will, therefore, be found in diﬀerent scales.
55
==PAGE:70==
9. Wavelet Transform and Fault Location
Several conditions must be considered before choosing an optimal scale for fault location
purposes. The lower scales enables the most accurate determination of the TW arrival, and
therefore provide the most accurate fault location. However, real-life signals may contain
high-frequency noise, which will aﬀect the low scales the most. Therefore, the choice of
the scale becomes a compromise between accuracy and the ability to pick up the TW.
9.2
Fault location strategy
To determine if a TW has arrived using the WLT is to examine the WCs at an optimal
scale and compare them to a pre-determined threshold value. As discussed previously, the
best-suited scale will depend on the frequency content and the noise level in the analyzed
signal. Further, diﬀerent mother wavelets will have diﬀerent abilities to capture the fault
TWs. The choice of mother wavelet is, therefore, often based on an engineering evaluation
for a given system. Popular mother wavelets used for fault location purposes are members
of the Dubieties (Db) family, the Morlet, the Symlet and the Haar wavelet [39].
From the above explanation, it is concluded that a unique best suited mother wavelet and
scale can be founded for each system conﬁguration, fault location, and fault type. However,
in a real-life system, since the fault location and thereby the signal frequency content is
unknown, a consolidated methodology for fault location detection is needed. In [9], for a
system conﬁguration, it is suggested to calculate the WCs with all the popular mother
wavelets at scales 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64 and 128. Thereafter, all the results can be compared to
the actual fault location, to ﬁnd the optimal scale and best mother wavelet for the system.
The advantage of WLT is that it can be applied to both the faulted current and voltage
waveforms, hence, this method can be adapted for any system conﬁguration.
Due to the limited time frame in this thesis, the fault locations are only estimated by
considering the Haar mother wavelet with varying scales. The algorithm developed is
presented in Appendix D.

a capacitive coupled voltage transformer (CCVT) is suggested for the core voltage
measurements of the HV export cable.

10CONCLUSION
The study presented in this thesis focused on the potential and application of Real-time
Travelling based fault location methods for an oﬀshore wind farm.
10.1
Results
The double-ended TW locator in the SEL T400L relay is very accurate for hybrid cross-
bonded export cables when they are terminated by an equivalent grid, representing a
combination of transformers, reactors and transmission lines/cables, at the both ends.
However, due to multiple TW reﬂections from the discontinuities such as fault, bus
terminals, cross-bonding, and non-homogeneous joints, the single-ended TW method is
not reliable for fault location on a hybrid cross-bonded cable.
When considering a single hybrid cross-bonded export cable with transformer and reactor
termination, as in some oﬀshore wind farms, the current based TW fault locator in the SEL
T400L is not suitable, since the oﬀshore substation’s surge impedance will be dominated
by the inductance of the transformer and shunt line reactor. A possible method to obtain
the accurate fault location could be through measuring the arrival times of the incident
voltage TWs at the PoC and PCC, since the voltage TWs are not aﬀected by the inductive
transformer and reactor windings. In this regard, it is necessary that a high bandwidth
voltage transformer is chosen to record the cable core voltages.
The double-ended TW based fault locator in the SEL T400L is reliable for an oﬀshore
wind farm system having two or more hybrid cross-bonded export cables, since the current
TWs can propagate through the additional line, which would result in the current TWs to
not completely reﬂect and get cancelled. The TW based fault locator, can also accurately
locate the line faults even when there is no wind power production. Further, for tests with
typical fault inception angles in a cable system, the TW locator could easily pick up the
TWs for fault location calculation. It was also, seen that the TW fault locator is immune
to current transformer core saturation.
Finally, it was observed that the alternative method for fault locating based on Wavelet
transform allows accurate fault location for any network conﬁguration.
10.2
Perspectives
The results from the TW based fault locator in the SEL T400L relay is as accurate, as the
accuracy of the line propagation delay considered in the tests. The line propagation delay
is generally recorded when the line is energized. However, the instruments to measure the
propagation delay also have some errors, this must be taken into account, when corrective
measures have to be taken on the cable, based on the estimated fault location. Further, in

the tests, the impact of communication delay on the fault location could not be carried
out. In reality, due to the large distance between the line relays, the accuracy of the TW

In the studies, the surge impedance of the substation busbars has not been taken into

The implementation of the TW based fault locator in the wind farm array cables is a
potential area of research, where the feasibility of the single-ended TW method could be
studied. Further, methods to improve the performance of the single-ended method is also
an interesting area.
Due to the advent of multi-terminal oﬀshore HVDC grids, theoretically, the application
of the TW based fault location could also be extended to DC transmission lines and its
time-domain protection.
