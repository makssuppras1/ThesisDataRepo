Abstract
In the current work, a framework based on spectral element methods is de-
veloped (ParSE - Parallel Spectral Elements). Spectral element methods are
gaining popularity in scientiп¬Ғc and engineering applications due to their high
accuracy and п¬Ӯexibility. The framework is designed to solve common station-
ary and time-dependent diп¬Җerential equations in one and two dimensions. The
core is developed in C++, taking advantage of optimized libraries for dense and
sparse matrix manipulations, and there is an extensive use of pointers to ensure
eп¬ғcient memory management. In addition, there has been an eп¬Җort to follow
object-oriented coding practices in order to ensure further extensibility, and
built-in Python wrappers are developed for the visualization and rendering of
results. The user can simply determine the type of equation to be solved along
with the boundary conditions, order of approximation, basis types and solver
type. The entire assembly, solver and visualization processes are automatically
performed. Parallel computing capabilities are embedded for distributed mem-
ory systems and there is a plan to enable GPU computations in the future. A
proof-of-concept case for the eп¬Җectiveness of parallel programming methods in
the solution of systems of equations that result from spectral element assemblies
is presented as part of this work. This research study is based on a 2D Laplace
solver for the nonlinear wave equation.

Introduction
The objective of this thesis is to develop a lightweight parallel framework for
spectral element computations. Spectral element methods have become attrac-
tive compared to more conventional п¬Ғnite diп¬Җerence or п¬Ғnite element methods
due to their п¬Ӯexibility and convergence rate properties. Due to the high order
of the piecewise basis functions, less computational nodes are needed to achieve
accuracy compared to п¬Ғnite element methods, as the convergence rate with re-
spect to the number of computational nodes reduces exponentially (spectral
convergence). A framework based on these methods would be useful for simula-
tions in many п¬Ғelds across science and engineering. Due to the nature and time
limitations of a master thesis project, there will be an eп¬Җort to set the basis for
this framework, by developing code that follows good software practices. Porta-
bility, extensibility and performance are crucial parameters for the framework
design as it is intended to run as a stand-alone application. The programming
language of choice is C/C++ as it satisп¬Ғes all the above characteristics, as well
as, smart low-level memory management. To further enhance performance, par-
allel programming techniques are utilized to extend the size and complexity of
problems the framework can handle. At this point, the framework is built to
support some of the most fundamental types of diп¬Җerential equations in one and
two dimensions. The supported equations are both stationary (Boundary Value
Problems) and time-dependent (Initial Value Problems).
Spectral element methods display a great potential in a wide range of scien-
tiп¬Ғc and engineering applications. The п¬Ӯexibility they provide by substituting
the linear basis functions with orthogonal polynomials, allows for great accuracy
improvements, without the need for an increase in mesh resolution. Additional
п¬Ӯexibility is achieved by the appropriate choice of basis functions for periodic
(Fourier polynomials) and non-periodic problems (Jacobi polynomials). Due to
the domain focus of the author and the research group, the current work revolves
around applications in hydrodynamics, despite the fact that the framework is
developed to be as п¬Ӯexible as possible, and suitable for solving various types of
diп¬Җerential equations. In the п¬Ғeld of hydrodynamics, a lot of research has been
conducted for proving the suitability of spectral element methods for nonlinear
surface wave problems [20]. Results conп¬Ғrm the expected high-order accuracy
of the models and their potential in the marine hydrodynamics п¬Ғeld.
More
studies have been carried out on the eп¬Җectivenes of high-order schemes for more
general 3D potential п¬Ӯow, п¬Ӯuid-structure interaction problems [8]. In addition,
solutions for the Navier-Stokes equations on п¬Ӯuid-structure interaction problems
have been presented, utilizing spectral element discretization and second-order
accurate time marching schemes [10]. Recent advancements have allowed the so-
lution of Reaction-Convection-Diп¬Җusion equations with stabilized forms of spec-
tral element methods [34]. The solution of these equations in simple 1D and 2D
geometries, will also be included in the framework. Another common equation
that is solved using the developed framework is the Helmholtz equation. The
Helmholtz equation is useful for time-harmonic acoustic wave problems [46].
To further improve the eп¬ғciency and п¬Ӯexibility of spectral element methods,

Mathematical Background
This section attempts to provide an overview of the Spectral Element Meth-
ods (SEM). Spectral element methods are utilized to solve Partial Diп¬Җerential
Equations (PDEs) by п¬Ғnite series of orthogonal functions. They are divided
in categories based on the type of orthogonal functions that form the basis for
solution. The two most signiп¬Ғcant categories are Fourier methods and polyno-
mial methods. Fourier methods are usually utilized for the solution of periodic
problems and polynomial methods, for non-periodic problems. In the current
work, two types of diп¬Җerential equation problems are examined, based on if
they contain time-dependent terms. Boundary Value Problems (BVPs) do not
contain time dependent terms and result in stationary solutions in space, and
Initial Value Problems (IVPs) which are solved both in time and space based
on an initial value for the solution.
2.1
Fourier Spectral Methods
A large group of spectral element methods are those that are based on orthog-
onal Fourier basis functions. These methods are suitable for periodic problems
as the complex exponentials (or trigonometric functions) that form the basis
are periodic well, on the [0, 2ПҖ] interval. As all spectral methods, they provide
a global high-order approximation for the solution of the diп¬Җerential equation.

2.4
Diп¬Җerential Equations
In this section, we will provide an overview of the problem types we try to solve
through the developed framework. Two types of problems will be approached.
The Boundary Value Problems, consist of a diп¬Җerential equation, along with
a set of boundary conditions. The solution should satisfy the equation in the
domain and the conditions at the boundaries. The progress of the phenomenon
in time is not considered for these problems. Examples include the stationary
wave equation and the Sturm-Liouville problems we encountered in previous
sections. The second group of problems are the Initial Value Problems or Cauchy
problems. They consist of an ordinary diп¬Җerential equation along with an initial
condition for t = 0. These problems, when solved, deп¬Ғne how the system will
evolve in time based on the initial condition. Examples include the diп¬Җusion and
advection equations that are frequently encountered in scientiп¬Ғc and engineering
applications.
2.4.1
Boundary Value Problems
As mentioned before, Boundary Value Problems (BVP) are time-independent
and can be solved by utilizing spectral element methods in the deп¬Ғned domain
[19]. Boundary Value Problems can either be periodic or non-periodic and that
determines the choice of basis set.
Trigonometric polynomials (Fourier) are
used to solve periodic problems and Jacobi (Legendre or Chebyshev) polynomi-

modes for modal approximations and the number of grid points (nodes) for the
nodal ones. As discussed in the previous section, there are also various numer-

equation such as the Galerkin/Tau and collocation methods. Numerical meth-
ods deп¬Ғne how the п¬Ғnal system of linear equations is formed, in order to proceed
with its solution. Besides that, it is necessary for the boundary conditions to be
imposed either after the system of equations is assembled or implicitly through
the choice of the basis functions set. As always, one should expect exponen-
tial convergence rates with respect to the number of modes (or nodes for nodal
representations), but it would be important to mention that very large values

is not scalable. Examples of boundary value problems that are included in the

3.1.2
Assembler
The assembler is the second step in the frameworkвҖҷs execution, after the input
parameters are set. The assembler contains most of the logic that generates the
system of equations Ax = b to be solved. Several steps are taken to appropri-
ately build the assembly. By taking into consideration the problem type, the
equation type, the interval values and boundary values we generate the basis
nodes x and weights w, the derivative matrices D and п¬Ғnally the system matrix
A and the right hand side vector b (Fig. 12).There are small alterations in the
logic п¬Ӯow of the assembler in 2D cases (Fig. 13), such as how to handle dif-
ferent intervals, nodes, weights and boundary types and conditions in the two
42
==PAGE:44==
directions of x and y. In addition, the process of applying tensor (Kronecker)
products to move from one to two dimensions and the translation of system
matrices to row- major sparse format (CSR) for better memory utilization are
introduced in 2D simulations. Besides that, the main concept of the assembler
remains the same in providing the solver with the system of equations to be
solved, in an eп¬ғcient and optimized way.
The boundary conditions are applied after the system matrix is formed. In
1D cases, wherever there is a Dirichlet boundary condition the п¬Ғrst or last row
of the system matrix is removed and reapplied in the solution after the solving
process. For Neumann boundary conditions, the system matrix row is swapped
for the row of the derivative matrix and the boundary value is applied in the
right hand side vector. For mixed Dirichlet-Neumann and Neumann-Boundary
conditions a hybrid initialization is performed where one matrix row is removed
and one is replaced by the derivative matrix respectively (Fig. 14)
In 2D cases, a Kronecker product is applied in order to form the system
matrix.
This procedure turns the system matrix from dense (as it is in 1D
cases) to sparse, with a sparsity pattern similar to the one shown in Fig. 15.
The initialization of the system matrices with the boundary conditions is applied
before the Kronecker product. Since the system matrix becomes sparse, Eigen
is utilized to save it in a sparse-row format (CSR). Any matrix or pointer used
after this point is expressed in the same sparse format.
3.1.3
Solver
The solver object is formed with a direct reference to the assembly object.
Almost every variable and structure in the solver object is a pointer to the
assembly.
The code is developed in this way in order to achieve maximum
memory eп¬ғciency and avoid redundant memory allocations and copies. In any
point where the system matrix or the solution vector change during the solution,
the pointer that points to their address in memory changes the values in the
original position. The logic embedded in the solver, decides which path is going
to be followed based on the problem type and if the execution should be serial
or parallel in the 2D cases. It is useful to mention that 1D cases generate dense
system matrices and are solved by a direct solving method.
In ParSE, the
selected solver is the Householder rank-revealing QR decomposition of a matrix
with column-pivoting. The logic п¬Ӯow of the 1D solver is depicted in Fig. 16. As
we can see there are two execution paths in the 1D solver п¬Ӯow, one for BVP1D
and one for the IVP1D problems. In the case of BVP1D, the direct solver is
applied, the the boundary conditions are enforced and the result is serialized in
a .dat п¬Ғle for visualization. In the IVP1D cases one has to form an equation-
dependent system matrix and then apply the selected time integration method.
A solution is obtained for each time step and boundary conditions are applied
dynamically. Then the result for each time step is serialized, in order to be used
as an input for the animation routine of the visualizer.
The solver logic п¬Ӯow for the 2D cases can be found on Fig. 17. Similar
to the 1D cases, the problem type is deп¬Ғned along with other conп¬Ғguration

properties. The extra concept in the 2D cases is the parallel execution. Parallel
iterative solvers are embedded through PETSc and can be used both in BVP2D
and IVP2D problems. In the IVP2D, cases the parallel execution feature is only
applicable to the implicit solvers (Backward Euler and Adams-Moulton) as they
involve a full linear system solution in each time-step. For the explicit solvers,
the solution is obtained by a matrix-matrix or matrix vector operations and thus
no iterative solution is needed. Due to their high-stability and computationally-
intensive characteristics, implicit methods are an attractive class of methods to
implement with high performance computing techniques.

High Performance Computing
In this section, the high performance computing component of the framework
is presented, along with the libraries that are utilized in its basis. These are the
Message Passing Interface (MPI) protocol and the Portable, Extensible Toolkit
for Scientiп¬Ғc Computation (PETSc), which is built on top of it. These tools are
embedded in the framework with a C++ to C wrapper and users can deploy
them in supporting hardware. A study case of a 2D spectral Laplace solver is
used as a proof-of-concept for the eп¬ғciency of these tools in spectral element
problems.
4.1
The Message Passing Interface (MPI) and PETSc
The Message Passing Interface (MPI) is a message passing protocol for dis-
tributed memory systems such as clusters and supercomputers with discrete
nodes [22].
The interface is portable across diп¬Җerent systems and operating
systems and supports C, C++ and Fortran programs, although wrappers exist
for C#, Java, Python and others.
Many open source implementations exist
for MPI. One of the most popular is OpenMPI, which is used in the current
work [24].
MPI supports both point-to-point and collective communications
that belong in a network in a high-performance, scalable and portable way.
When an MPI program is executed, a number of processes is spawned. These
processes will run in parallel depending on the network topology and the number
of nodes and cores the user chooses to utilize. MPI handles the communication
between the processes as well as the synchronization which is crucial when racing
conditions arise.

The Portable Extensible Toolkit for Scientiп¬Ғc Computation (PETSc) is a
software package containing data structures and routines for the solution of
partial diп¬Җerential equations in parallel. PETSc is built on top of BLAS and
MPI ( Fig. 18). The supported data structures include matrices, vectors and
index sets. A collection of linear and nonlinear solvers are available for the user,
along with time-stepping methods. The most interesting PETSc elements for
the current work are the Krylov subspace iterative methods and preconditioners.
In ParSE an interface was developed, through which, the system matrix (A.dat)
and right-hand side vector (b.dat) are passed to a separately compiled PETSc
script written in C, and then the solution is serialized to a new п¬Ғle solution.dat
and passed back to ParSE for further processing (Fig. 19). In the current form
of the framework the user can select the number of cores for execution. In next
versions, the user will also be able to select the preconditioner and solver that
are most suitable for the modeled problem. The PETSc interface in ParSE is
used for both BVP2D and IVP2D problems. In the BVP2D cases, an iterative
solver is deployed once in parallel to solve the system. In IVP2D cases, the
parallel solver is called in each time step. This shows the potential of the parallel
execution in time dependent problems as a large system of equations must be
solved hundreds or even thousands of times depending on the simulation time
and time step.

Good parallel scalability is the main goal of high performance parallel pro-
gramming.
There are two main classes of tests to assess parallel scalability.
The п¬Ғrst includes strong scaling tests. These tests, investigate the reduction in
execution time for an increasing number of computational units. For example, a
parallel MPI execution on N processors is expected to run N-times faster than a
serial execution in an ideal case. AmdahlвҖҷs law restricts the expected speedup,
based on what portion of the entire code is parallelized.
This gives a more
realistic estimation on the speedup one should expect from the code. Strong
scaling tests are suitable for computational bound problems, where the number
58
==PAGE:60==
of п¬Ӯoating point operations per second (п¬Ӯops) limits the execution speed. In the
case we examine here, the problem is memory bound as the system matrix size
results in memory issues when we exceed 2400 elements with the current setup.
For memory bound cases, weak scaling tests are more appropriate to determine
the parallel scalability of the code. In these tests, the computational load per
processing unit is kept constant. In theory, the execution time must also remain
close to constant, as for N processors and an N-times larger problem, the exe-
cution time is expected to be the same. The problem size in these simulations
is deп¬Ғned relatively to the number of nonzero elements of the system matrix,
as this is a measure of the number of operations that will need to be performed
in the optimized sparse format. GustafsonвҖҷs law gives the theoretical speedup
in the weak scaling tests. The benchmarks of the weak scaling tests can be
found in Fig. 22. The simulations are set up the same way as the algorithmic
scalability tests, and executed on the DTU cluster for up to 40 cores in parallel
(2 nodes with 20 cores each). The number of elements on the serial execution
is 60 and the number of elements for 40 cores in parallel is 2, 400 maintaining a
constant amount of nonzero elements in the system matrix.

Results show that the best scalability is achieved with the MG-precondtioned

Г—2, with the GAMG-preconditioned BiCGSTAB having the poorest behavior
with a factor of Г—4.1. It is thus concluded that with appropriate tuning, spectral
element methods are scalable through PETSc and can beneп¬Ғt from high perfor-
mance computing. Therefore, the inclusion of a parallel programming interface
in ParSE is expected to provide signiп¬Ғcant beneп¬Ғts towards more complex sim-
ulations in later stages.

6
Conclusions and Future Work
The objective of this thesis was the development of a spectral element frame-
work, with parallel computing capabilities. This goal was met with the develop-
ment of ParSE, a cross-platform C/C++ framework designed to be extensible,
eп¬ғcient and lightweight due to smart memory management. An overview of the
mathematical background of spectral element methods was provided, in order
to demonstrate their advantages in performing accurate scientiп¬Ғc simulations.
The basis methods were implemented inside the framework, along with the uti-
lization of libraries for handling matrices, parsing expressions etc. A big part
of the framework design was analyzed, as proper software architecture was one
of the most crucial objectives during development. The framework is built up
on object-oriented principles and is fully modular and extensible. This creates
a big potential for future development and the addition of new features. A re-
search study was performed to investigate the suitability of distributed parallel
programming execution in spectral element assemblies. The 2D Laplace prob-
lem showed good weak scaling properties up to 40 cores in 2 nodes, and thus a
PETSc wrapper was developed as an interface for parallel computations.
Since the framework is built to be extensible, there is a great potential for
future development. In its initial form, as presented in this thesis, it is capable
of solving speciп¬Ғc diп¬Җerential equations. These simulations are the basis and
a proof of concept that is designed to lead to more advanced simulations. In
its п¬Ғnal form the framework could support general equations and geometries,
using mesh п¬Ғles as an input. Parallel capabilities could be extended to GPU
support as the structure is already there for GPU execution through PETSc or
CUSP solvers. In addition, more complex 3D problems were not discussed and
their inclusion would be an important step towards the completion of the frame-
work. Since the framework is built to be modular, a diп¬Җerent cross platform
compilation procedure could easily generate the main components (assembler,
solver, visualizer) as C++ libraries. This way an entirely new potential emerges,
as these modules can be used independently, based on the needs of the user.
For example, spectral element assemblies could be created on the п¬Ӯy, and then
solved by another software package, or an existing assembly could be solved by
the frameworkвҖҷs solvers. That would be even more interesting in the class of
parallel solvers that are embedded. Finally, the visualizer routines and Python
scripts are available for 1D and 2D data visualizations both stationary and in
time.
