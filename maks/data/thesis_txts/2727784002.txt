Abstract
Deception detection plays a crucial role in domains such as psychology and criminol-
ogy. Traditional approaches to deception detection often rely on subjective human
judgment, which can be limited in terms of accuracy and scalability. In recent years,
deep learning approaches, have shown great potential for advancements in the field.
Despite the potential, there is a lack of high quality large deception datasets as cre-
ating such datasets is challenging due to the diﬀiculties in annotating ground truths
and the subjective nature of human perception of deception. This thesis proposes
the creation of a novel deception detection dataset derived from the photo-realistic
detective mystery video game L.A. Noire. The dataset comprises multiple modalities,
including video, audio, and text clips, consisting of a substantial collection of data-
points. Ground truth labels for deceptive and truthful behavior are assigned to each
datapoint based on the video games’ deterministic ground truth values. To explore
the dataset, we experiment with a multimodal approach to detect deceptive behavior
using state-of-the-art transformer models, effectively learning how to play the game.
To develop the dataset, video, audio, and text clips are extracted from the game,
and annotations are assigned corresponding to subject truthfulness. We then con-
duct experiments to classify deceitful behavior in the dataset by implementing deep
learning models for each individual modality separately, as well as a multimodal
model. The evaluation focuses on the balanced accuracy metric to account for any
class imbalances in the dataset. The results demonstrate performance above 70% in
all individual modalities, with the multimodal model achieving a balanced accuracy
score of 80.89%. The results highlight the potential of utilizing transformer-based
multimodal approaches in deception detection tasks and emphasize the importance
of balanced metrics to eliminate the inherent biases in datasets. Furthermore, this
research contributes to the field by providing a comprehensive deception detection
dataset.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Deception detection has been a topic of significant interest in various fields, with
applications ranging from psychology to criminology [16, 44]. Traditional approaches
to deception detection often rely on subjective human judgment, leading to limita-
tions in accuracy and scalability [2, 38, 7]. However, with the emergence of machine
learning, new possibilities have arisen for advancing our understanding of deception
detection. In recent years, deep learning models have demonstrated their ability to
capture intricate features [29]. Specifically, the combination of deep learning with
multimodal approaches, which integrate information from multiple sources such as
linguistic and non-verbal components, has shown great potential for improvement in
the field [34, 15].
Despite these advancements, the availability of high-quality datasets for deception
detection remains limited. Creating comprehensive datasets for deception detection
poses significant challenges due to the nature of deception data and the complexities
surrounding the annotation of ground truths, as human perception of deception is
inherently flawed and subject to controversy [16]. In detail, capturing and defining
deception in a natural environment is particularly challenging as high-stakes deception
is rare and observed in restricted and diﬀicult-to-access environments. Furthermore,
high-stakes deception exhibits a multitude of fallacies [5, 24] that further complicate
the annotation process. Conversely, low-stakes deception exhibits another set of de-
ception cues [24] and may not align with the applications that many studies aim to
address. Existing datasets have already extensively been studied or lack the size and
data variety to effectively capture deceptive behavior. For instance, some high-stakes
multimodal datasets suffer from limitations such as low video quality and a limited
number of videos, which can introduce imbalances and biases that hinder accurate
analysis [28].
Furthermore, the field of multimodal deception detection remains relatively un-
explored, with only a limited number of studies addressing this specific area [15, 34,
23]. Moreover, there is a research gap in investigating the application of transformer-
based architectures [42] for multimodal deception detection. Transformers, known

2.1
Transformers
The Transformer is a network architecture, based solely on attention mechanisms,
dispensing with recurrence and convolutions entirely. Introduced by Vaswani et al.
(2017)[42], transformers are a type of deep learning model distinguished by using
self-attention mechanisms to capture the relationships between words or tokens in a
sequence. Unlike recurrent neural networks (RNNs) that process sequences sequen-
tially, transformers can parallelize the computations, making them highly eﬀicient.
The self-attention mechanism allows transformers to focus on different parts of the
input sequence, effectively capturing long-range dependencies. Moreover, transform-
ers have demonstrated remarkable performance on various natural language process-
ing(NLP) tasks, including language translation, sentiment analysis, and named entity
recognition. Their success in sequential data processing has extended transformers
to other domains, such as computer vision and multi-modal deep learning.[42]
2.1.1
Architecture
Most competitive neural sequence transduction models have an encoder-decoder struc-
ture[42]. The encoder is composed of a stack of N = 6 identical layers. Each layer has
two sub-layers. The first is a multi-head self-attention mechanism, and the second
is a simple, position-wise fully connected feed-forward network. The decoder is also
composed of a stack of N = 6 identical layers[42]. In addition to the two sub-layers in
each encoder layer, the decoder inserts a third sub-layer, which performs multi-head
attention over the output of the encoder stack. In both the encoder and decoder,
residual connections are employed. To facilitate these residual connections, all sub-

2.2
Video Classification
2.2.1
ViT
In 2021, Google Research, presented an innovative approach to address image classi-
fication using transformers. Previously, the state-of-the-art models vision were using
attention applied in conjunction with convolutional networks or used to replace cer-
tain components of convolutional networks while keeping their overall structure in
place. This research demonstrated that reliance on CNNs was not necessary, and
a pure transformer applied directly to sequences of image patches can perform very
well on image classification tasks[11].
This new model architecture, known as Vi-
sion Transformer (ViT), has since become the foundation for current state-of-the-art
models such as ViViT and TimeSformer.
The idea behind ViT is to apply a standard Transformer directly to images, with
the fewest possible modifications. To achieve this goal, the researchers divided images
into patches and provided the sequence of linear embeddings of these patches as an
input to a Transformer. These image patches are treated the same way as tokens in
a natural language processing (NLP) application. Moreover, the researchers followed
the transformer architecture closely and finally used a classification head implemented
by a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) with one hidden layer at pre-training time and
a single linear layer at fine-tuning time.

2.2.2
TimeSformer
TimeSformer is a video classification model introduced in 2021 by Gedas Bertasius,
Heng Wang, and Lorenzo Torresani of the Meta research team[4].
Motivated by
the improvements Transformers have over traditional convolution neural networks
(CNNs), TimeSformer adapts the image model Vision Transformer(ViT)[11] architec-
ture to video. In detail, the model handles the video as a sequence of patches extracted
from the individual frames. Each patch is linearly mapped into an embedding and
augmented with positional information. This makes it possible to interpret the re-
sulting sequence of vectors as token embeddings that can be fed to a Transformer
encoder, analogously to the token features computed from words in NLP.[4]
To address the computational intensity associated with self-attention in conven-
tional Transformers, which involves computing similarity measures between all tokens,
the researchers propose several self-attention designs tailored for the space-time vol-
ume of videos.
These designs aim to address scalability issues and optimize the
accuracy of the model.
By evaluating the different designs of self-attention, the researchers concluded that
the best design is represented by a “divided attention” architecture which separately
applies temporal attention and spatial attention within each block of the network [4].
TimeSformer offers a compelling choice for video classification due to its distinctive
approach, which is characterized by a conceptually simple model design and low
computational cost. Despite its simplicity, the model has demonstrated state-of-the-
art performance on benchmark video datasets, making it a solid choice for video
classification.

2.3.1
CNNs
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) is a family of deep learning models specif-
ically designed for processing grid-like data, such as images, videos, or audio spec-
trograms.
CNNs are inspired by the way living organisms achieve vision, where
individual neurons respond to specific regions of the visual field. CNNs employ a
hierarchical structure that enables them to automatically learn and extract relevant
features from input data.
Figure 2.5: The figure shows the basic CNN Architecture components
CNNs take as inputs tensors with the shape: N x H x W x C where N is the
number of inputs, H is the input height, W is the input width and C is the number
of input channels.
These inputs initially pass through the core operation of the
CNNs, the convolutional layer. In this layer, a set of learnable filters or kernels is
==PAGE:21==
2.3 Audio Classification
8
applied to small regions of the input data at a time. These filters slide over the entire
input, performing element-wise multiplication and aggregation operations, producing
abstract versions of the image called feature maps or activation maps, that capture
different aspects of the input. By stacking multiple convolutional layers, CNNs can
progressively learn more complex and abstract features.
CNNs also usually include pooling layers, which reduce the dimensionality of the
feature maps by combining the outputs of neuron clusters at one layer into a single
neuron in the next layer [10]. Finally, fully connected layers connect the extracted
features to the output layer for classification or regression purposes.
2.3.2
ResNet
Introduced by Kaiming He et al. in 2015, Residual Neural Networks(ResNet) [17], is
a deep learning architecture that addresses the challenge of degradation on training
very deep neural networks. In traditional deep networks, the gradient signals diminish
as they propagate backward through numerous layers, making it diﬀicult to train
deeper models.
ResNet overcomes this challenge by introducing skip connections,
also called residual connections, that allow information to flow directly from one
layer to subsequent layers in the network. These skip connections create shortcut
connections that bypass one or more layers, enabling the network to learn residual
mappings.
Figure 2.6: In the figure, the ResNet building blocks [17] are presented
The architecture of ResNet consists of a series of residual blocks, which are the
fundamental building blocks of the network. These blocks typically contain two or
more convolutional layers along with residual connections. The core idea is that each
residual block learns the residual mapping with respect to the input, enabling the
network to focus on the residual information to be learned rather than learning the
entire transformation.

2.3.3
DenseNet
Dense Convolutional Network (DenseNet) is a deep learning architecture introduced
by Huang et al. in 2017 [19]. DenseNet addresses the vanishing gradient problem,
the challenge of information flow issue, encourages feature reuse and requires fewer
parameters as there is no need to relearn redundant feature maps.
Figure 2.7: A deep DenseNet with three dense blocks. The layers between two
adjacent blocks are referred to as transition layers and change feature map sizes via
convolution and pooling [19]
DenseNet utilizes densely connected blocks, where each block consists of multiple
layers. Within a block, the output feature maps of each layer are concatenated along
the channel dimension and serve as inputs to subsequent layers. This dense connec-
tivity pattern allows each layer to receive and reuse feature maps from all preceding
layers within the same block. Consequently, the number of feature maps within each
block remains constant, as all layers have direct access to the collective knowledge
of the block. To facilitate eﬀicient information flow and alleviate the need for excess
parameters, DenseNet employs transition layers between dense blocks.
Transition
layers contain convolutional layers followed by pooling layers to reduce dimensions
and adjust the number of channels. These transition layers aid in controlling the
growth of computational requirements while maintaining an effective flow of informa-
tion through the network.

DistilBERT
DistilBERT, short for Distilled BERT, is a compact version of the BERT model in-
troduced by Sanh et al. in 2019 [37]. It aims to provide a more lightweight and
eﬀicient alternative to the original BERT model, while still preserving the effective-
ness of its larger counterpart. DistilBERT is designed to achieve a balance between
model size and computational eﬀiciency, making it suitable for resource-constrained
environments or applications with limited computational resources.
The main process behind DistilBERT is called knowledge distillation[18]. This method
is a compression technique in which a compact model, the student (DistilBERT), is
trained to reproduce the behavior of the teacher model (BERT) or an ensemble of
models. This method allows DistilBERT to capture similar language representations
while staying significantly smaller in size and computationally lighter.

3.1
Deception Detection
From its scientific origins in 1895 with Cesare Lombroso’s hydrosphygmograph [33],
deception detection has historically been a topic of interest, debate, and controversy
thus it has become a study subject for multiple fields such as psychology, criminology,
and politics. After a few inventions and realizations about deception detection [33] a
prevalent tool was invented and quickly saw widespread use until recently and still is
widely used, widely known as the ‘lie detector’ or polygraph [40]. This machine uses
physiometric data like cardiovascular activity, respiratory activity, and electrodermal
activity combined with a series of questions conducted by the interviewee, in order
to understand deceptive behavior. This tool has been improved and its potential
analyzed, disputed, and aﬀirmed throughout the decades yet still contains a lot of
limitations and is a subject of controversy [40, 2, 38, 7].
In addition to technological advancements, further research has focused on the
psychological aspect of deception detection. Psychologists have delved into the search
for specific cues that may reveal deceptive behavior. One significant line of study
emerged from the development of the Facial Action Coding System (FACS) by Ekman
and Friesen in 1978 [12]. FACS derived from an analysis of the anatomical basis of
facial movements and is used to accurately describe facial expressions. This analysis
brought light to how microexpressions, could potentially serve as valuable cues for
deception detection.
Moreover, researchers have explored other behavioral cues, such as body language,
eye gaze, and verbal components, in reference to deception detection. The analysis of
body language involves studying gestures, and movements exhibited by individuals
during deceptive interactions.
Eye gaze patterns have also been investigated, as
potential subconscious reactions during deceptive behavior. Verbal cues, including
==PAGE:28==

speech patterns, linguistic content, and voice characteristics, have also been examined
to identify potential markers of deception [16, 44].
In the past two decades, the rise of machine learning reignited the interest in
automated approaches for deception detection. Machine learning models offer the
potential to analyze large volumes of data and extract complex patterns. Multiple
studies have achieved significant progress in the field of deception detection by creat-
ing single [29] and multimodal models [34, 15] that can leverage a variety of previously
mentioned features, such as linguistic cues [6], microexpressions [35], and physiolog-
ical signals [15], to capture indicators of deception.
While the field of deception
detection with machine learning has made significant strides, challenges still exist,
such as the availability of annotated datasets, and the generalization of models to
real-life contexts [28]. Addressing these challenges can contribute to the development
of robust and reliable deception detection systems

The basis of every multimodal deception detection study is the dataset used. In this

In the literature, two prominent datasets have been widely studied and explored:
The Real-life Trial Dataset and the Bag-of-Lies dataset. The Real-life Trial Dataset
was introduced by Perez et al. [35] in 2015. This dataset consists of 121 video clips
extracted from actual court trials and contains 61 deceptive and 60 truthful instances.
For the modalities, Perez et al. used the transcription of the videos to extract the text
data, representing the text modality. Additionally, they manually annotated the video
clips to extract relevant gestures, representing the video modality. It is noteworthy
to highlight that this dataset encompasses high-stake deception [24]. The video clips
within this dataset involve individuals engaging in deception or truthfulness before
judges, with potentially significant real-world consequences, while all other datasets
observed consist of low-stake deception scenarios.

Multimodal Deception Detection
In this section we analyze the results and the methods used in related research. In the
original study of the real-life trial data [35], the researchers identified and utilized four
different modalities, text, audio, silent video, and full video. For the implementation,
they created an automatic analysis and evaluation system which was then trained in
different modalities, excluding audio. Finally, the performance of the system was com-
pared with the evaluations of three human annotators. Notably, the results showed
that the system consistently outperformed the human annotators across all modal-
ities. The highest accuracy achieved by the system was in the full video modality,
with a score of 75.2%. An interesting observation from the study was that the human
annotators’ ability to detect deception was only slightly better than chance, which
aligns with similar findings reported in other studies [1] [30]. This suggests that de-
ception detection is a challenging task even for trained human observers.

Another noteworthy study conducted using the real-life trial dataset was intro-

in 2018 [21].
This study aimed to explore new
modalities by using modern deep-learning models. The researchers incorporated text,
audio, and video modalities into their approach. Specifically, for the text modality

the transcripts of the videos. For the audio modality, they extracted relevant fea-

The achieved results were impressive, with the textual modality achieving an accu-

In the study accompanying the Bag-of-Lies dataset [15], four modalities video,
audio, EEG, and eye gaze were utilized.
For the Video modality, the researchers
extracted Local Binary Pattern (LBP) features from the frames of the video clips and
used machine learning methods such as Support Vector Machine (SVM), Random
Forest, and Multilayer Perceptron (MLP). Considering the Audio modality, various
frequency-based properties were calculated and combined into a feature vector which
was then fed to two binary classifiers, a Random Forest and a K-Nearest Neighbor
(KNN) classifier.
In the case of EEG, the researchers employed two different ap-
proaches. The first approach involved feeding the EEG data into a Convolutional
Neural Network (CNN) for classification. In the second approach, they transformed
their data to keep 1000 points, which were ultimately fed into a Random Forest clas-
sifier. For the Eye Gaze modality, a dimensional feature vector was created by incor-
porating all the gathered data related to eye gaze. Finally, the best models from each
modality were fused together to form the multimodal approach. This fusion aimed
to leverage the strengths of each modality and improve the overall performance of
deception detection.
The researchers compared all results and were able to achieve the best accuracy
with the multimodal approach which achieved 66.17% accuracy. It is evident that by
utilizing CNNs and simple machine learning models they could improve the overall
performance of the model. However, the accuracy achieved with individual modali-
ties indicates a potential need for a more powerful model or larger dataset to further
enhance performance and improve the overall accuracy. This suggests that individual
modalities may not be suﬀicient on their own to capture the complexity of deception
detection, and leveraging multiple modalities can provide complementary information
to improve overall accuracy.

4.1
Overview
The origin of our dataset is the 2011 detective-themed video game called L.A Noire
published by Rockstar Games [46]. Set in the 1940s the game builds a realistic recre-
ation of Los Angeles where the player assumes the role of a police detective called
Cole Phelps. The game’s main objective is to solve a series of cases of different types
of crimes. The core mechanics include examining crime scenes, collecting evidence,
interviewing suspects and witnesses, and making verdicts about the suspects that
affect the outcome of each investigation. A large part of the game and our main
focus point revolves around the interrogation/interview mechanics, where the player
has to deduce by the evidence at hand and by carefully examining the interviewee’s
reactions and statements, if the suspect or witness being interrogated is being deceit-
ful or truthful.
The game employs a unique facial animation technology called MotionScan [47,
25], which captures the performances of real actors and translates their expressions
onto in-game characters. This technology enhances the realism and authenticity of the
game, making suspects’ facial cues and body language more distinguishable during
interrogations, and emphasizing these characteristics to determine if they are telling
the truth or withholding information. Microexpressions, shifts in eye contact, nervous
tics, or other telltale signs can indicate if a person is withholding information, lying,
or being truthful. Players must pay attention to these subtle cues and make decisions
based on their observations, making this video game a very suitable choice for our
multimodal deception dataset.

4.2
Game Mechanics
For the objectives of the thesis, we focus only on the interrogation aspect of the game.
The game follows a formula for all interrogations, wherein each one a series of dialogue
sequences is presented. The detective/protagonist will ask a question based on the
questions gathered from the crime scenes and clues gathered. Following the question,
the interviewee will respond with an answer to the questions and the player will
be prompted to respond between three different approaches. The three options for
their responses are: ”Truth,” ”Doubt,” and ”Lie.”, these responses reflect the player’s
judgment about the suspect’s statement as well as different ways to approach the
conversation.

The player’s approach to the conversation determines the level of pushing or ag-
gravation from the protagonist, prompting different responses from the suspects, re-

gardless of whether the player’s judgment is correct or not. Choosing the ”Truth”
option means the detective believes the statement and will usually not push them for
more information on the specific question. Then, the suspect or witness is usually
giving straightforward information. The ”Doubt” option allows players to push for
more details or challenge inconsistencies in the person’s statements. This approach
means that the player has spotted deceitful behavior by the subject yet has no con-

and a prompt will appear to the player, to present evidence contradicting the charac-

will admit to the statement’s accuracy and be more straightforward.
L.A. Noire uses a rating system that evaluates the player’s performance after
completing each case. The player is given a performance rating based on their overall
performance during the investigation. The rating is determined by how many clues the
player found, how many questions they judged correctly and what was the final verdict
on the final chapter of each case. In this aspect, the rating system is deterministic as
there is only one correct response to each question and a specific amount of clues in
each case.

Sources
Another main reason for our decision about the specific video game was the accessi-

relevant, as the extra content of the game is either exploratory or action-based and

We are accessing the game via a full walkthrough made on the YouTube platform

contains all the content of the game. More specifically it contains all the information
needed for the creation of the dataset, as it navigates throughout the whole game,

way to access the audiovisual component of the game by extracting only the correct
samples. As the game is deterministic, the correct answers and clue locations are
fixed, therefore we can extract the ground truth from various sources. For ease of
building the dataset, we utilize online walkthroughs that contain the correct responses
to each question.

4.4
Setup
The game is split into five chapters and 26 cases. We use 21 of them, as the other five
are exclusively either exploratory or action based. In each case, there is a number of
suspects and witnesses that differs depending on the case. The interrogation process
has a certain amount of written questions that the protagonist has on his agenda and
they differ per suspect. In each written question there is a dialogue with the original
(main) question, an answer from the subject, and in many cases more subsequent
questions and answers depending on the flow of conversation and judgment bestowed
by the player.
To construct the dataset, we divided the video game into its components, following
a hierarchical structure. First, we partitioned it into individual cases as the largest
indivisible part.
Within each case, we identified the subjects, where subjects are
all the witnesses and suspects, with whom the main character interacts through the
interrogation mechanic. The subsequent division involves extracting the questions,
whether they were written main questions requiring a response from the subject,
or if they were important subsequent questions. The latter, are cutscene questions
prompted by the protagonist to extract more information without the input of the
user, which still is crucial for the deception detection part of the thesis and thus
separately extracted. Finally, we extracted the answers to these questions, capturing
the corresponding responses of the subjects both from main and cutscene questions.
Splitting the game into cases, subjects, questions, and answers enables us to create a
comprehensive, structured, and easily maintainable dataset.
In each main question, there is a prompt response where the player has to de-
termine the truthfulness of the subject and select one of the three presented choices
”Truth”, ”Doubt” and ”Lie”. Our sources contain only the correct information, there-
fore, we can deduce that by selecting ”Truth” then the character is truthful, and
by selecting ”Lie” we know they are lying. These assumptions are generally correct,
although there are some complications. The ”Truth” choice is straightforward, all the
answers given by the interviewee on the main question are truthful. In the questions
where the ”Lie” option is chosen, the interviewee is initially deceitful up until the
point where the incriminating evidence is presented. At that point, the suspect will
change their approach and be truthful. Finally, when ”Doubt” is the correct choice,
the interviewee will either be outright lying or withholding information in the begin-
ning and when pressured, admit to the protagonist’s statement. If we labeled the
whole main question interaction as truth or lie, we would miss a lot of information
about the actual behavior of the suspects. For this reason, we split these answers into
more granular parts in order to encapture the different ground truths and we label
the data in the granular parts either as ”Truth” or ”Lie” to denote either truthful or
deceitful response.
To transform the video into a cohesive and structured dataset, we initiated a pre-
processing process by creating an Excel sheet to contain all the relevant data. Specif-
ically, we organized the data per case, following the split mentioned above. For each

Doubt, Lie) of each question with the aid of the online guides. Additionally, we man-

carefully watching the video walkthrough. At this stage of dataset construction, we
divided each question into multiple parts, if needed, and fragmented the answers
to each question to encapsulate the various deceitful and truthful moments in each
subject’s replies.

automatic naming process with sequential numbers for each frame. The tool allows

Audio

now-existing folders where we organize the audio clips per subject and subsequently

Similar to the video extraction mechanism, the audio extraction process requires
specific parameters. These include a path to the video input, a timestamp for the
start of the audio clip (HH:MM:SS), another timestamp for the end of the audio clip,
and finally a name for the output audio file as well as the format extension. For audio,
the tool also allows for customization in quality and audio format. For the purpose
of the thesis, we chose the default parameters and the mp3 format.

Method
In this chapter, we present in detail the methodology followed for the design, develop-
ment, and evaluation of the multimodal deception detection model based on our novel
dataset. Initially, we showcase the overview of the multimodal model. Subsequently,
we delve into the architecture of each modals model in 5.2, 5.3, and 5.4. Finally, we
present the method behind the multimodal architecture in 5.5
5.1
Overview
For the purpose of the thesis, we developed a multimodal model to detect deception
in the L.A Noire video game dataset and compared its performance against separate
models for each modality. The first design and development step was to train separate
models for each modality.
In the text modality, we designed three different approaches. Firstly, we trained
various transformer models using the answers as inputs. Secondly, to utilize more
information from the dataset, we combined the questions and answers together as
single inputs and evaluated them with the same transformer models.
Finally, we
created a new architecture where we employed two separate transformer models one
for questions and one for answers, and then concatenated their features. In all three
approaches, we designed a method to optimize each model’s hyperparameters. For
the audio modality, we trained various convolution neural networks (CNNs) by trans-
forming the audio clips into image spectrograms and inputting them directly into the
models.
Regarding the video modality, we also developed three distinct approaches. The
primary approach involved feeding the original images to a large video transformer
model and optimizing its hyperparameters. The second approach focused on utilizing
only the facial characteristics in each image to enable the model to extract information
from microexpressions and eye movements. For the third approach, we employed the
use of an action unit detector to analyze the facial expression of the subjects and
subsequently trained an MLP model with the action units extracted as sole inputs.
Finally, for the multimodal approach we developed a method to save and concate-
nate the features from the best model of each modality and feed them to an MLP
model.

5.2
Text Encoder
As shown in related work, text modality is a crucial aspect of any deception detection
method.
Consequently, we have exercised the required gravitas to approach this
modality with multiple designs in order to effectively encapsulate the underlying
information.
5.2.1
Single model architecture
In this subsection we present the first and second approaches since the key distinction
lies in the input preprocessing technique while maintaining consistent model design
and evaluation.
Input preprocessing.
The initial step was input extraction to encompass the
model’s requirements.
In the first approach, the input consisted of the answer’s
text. The second approach involved concatenating the text of the question with the
text of the respective answer. In both approaches, we mapped each input with the
corresponding label and add the mapping to a list, which became our input to the
model. Finally, we divided the data into train, validation, and test sets following
the considerations previously discussed, taking into account the dataset size and the
preservation of temporal information within each case.
Model Design.
With the aid of the Simple Transformers [20] library we employed
three widely utilized transformer model architectures BERT, DistilBERT, and RoBERTa.
Given our objective to classify the extracted inputs as deceitful or truthful, the ob-
jective was to train the models toward binary classification. The selected models are
inherently geared for classification tasks, allowing us to leverage their original archi-
tectures by only specifying the necessary parameters for training and validation.
As these models have already been fine-tuned on large-scale datasets, there are
two design decisions. We could either freeze the model parameters apart from the
classification head or retrain it with our dataset. With the first choice, we utilize the
fine-tuned capabilities of a large dataset, while with the second we could potentially
fine-tune the model on our dataset. We decided on testing both designs and compare
their outcomes.
5.2.2
Separate model architecture
In this subsection we present the third approach where we utilize a different input
and model architecture strategy.
Input preprocessing.
For the last approach, the input was split into two different
streams. Since we were using different models, we had to have the same input numbers,

and due to the larger proportion of answers, the number of data points resembled
the number of answers as the more numerous set. For this reason, the input for the
answer model was the text of the answer with the corresponding label, and the input
for the question model is the text of the question with the corresponding answer’s
label. Consequently, this meant that in the question input, there would be repetition
in the input and in a few cases some discrepancies in labels.
Model Design.
In this approach, the architecture was a two-part model design.
Two separate transformer models, each for one of the inputs. Both models shared the
same parameters and base architecture, which was the BERT model architecture.

In order to adapt to the two-part model structure, we had to modify their archi-
tecture. We removed the pooler layer and created a separate multi-layer perceptron
(MLP) classifier layer. During training, each model processed its input separately,
leveraging the power of BERT’s contextualized embeddings to capture the seman-

extracted their respective features. Finally, to combine the information from both in-
puts, we concatenated the extracted features into a single vector. This concatenated
feature representation was then passed through the MLP classifier layer.
In this approach, we also tested the architectures with frozen and unfrozen layers
of the BERT models to test which design would perform better.

6.1
Evaluation Metrics
In order to accurately represent the performance of the models in this thesis there
is a need for an impartial and solid evaluation method. For this reason, we utilize
multiple evaluation metrics. Those metrics stem from the confusion matrix and its
derivations. Those metrics range from simple ones such as accuracy and loss to more
complex ones like balanced accuracy and the F1 score.
The confusion matrix is usually depicted as a table depicting the summary of
predictions made by the model on a test dataset compared to the actual values or
ground truths. The four components (True positive (TP), True negative (TN), False
positive (FP), and False negative (FN)) of the confusion matrix are the foundations
for the evaluation metrics we utilize across all models.
The simplest metric we utilize is accuracy.
Accuracy =
TP + TN

Accuracy is the basic tool of any model’s performance evaluation and is widely
used as the main metric for many applications. Accuracy is not a suitable metric
in our case as our dataset is not balanced.
Therefore all accuracy values would
be unfairly skewed towards truthful behavior and would cause the model to predict
more truths as they would be overvalued. For this reason, the evaluation metrics
we use have to have more complexity in order to encapsulate and fairly present the
information of the dataset.

6.3
Hyperparameter Tuning
The optimization process was kept the same for all approaches and modalities in or-
der to have consistent and fair results and comparisons. To track and optimize each
model’s hyperparameters we utilized the Weights & Biases (wandb)[45] library and
interface. This library allows for an easy way to monitor hyperparameters and metrics
in the training, validation, and testing process. Moreover, the library contains a pow-
erful function called Sweep. Wandb Sweep is an automated process that eﬀiciently
explores a search space to identify the optimal combination of hyperparameters for
our models. The process begins by choosing the parameters to be explored for the
experiment. The hyperparameters we train are learning rate, batch size, and number
of epochs. Furthermore, Sweep requires the input of the hyperparameter space search
method. Between the choices of random search, grid search, or Bayesian optimiza-
tion, we experimented using the Bayesian method as we found it is the less intensive
and more effective of the choices. This method allows the user to input the metric
they want to optimize the training process for, in our case the metric was balanced
accuracy.
After configuring the parameters of the tool, we initiated a series of runs that
systematically varied the hyperparameters within different specified ranges. For each
combination of hyperparameters, the process automatically trained and validated
the model, recording the logs and visualizing the results and metrics. Finally, by
observing the results of each model, we select the better-performing hyperparameters,
make predictions with the test set, and document their performance.

process.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This thesis contained two main objectives, the creation of a novel deception detection
dataset and the exploration of multimodal approaches by implementing transformer
models based on the newly created dataset.
For the first objective, the creation of the dataset was successful.
The result-
ing dataset encompassed multiple modalities, including video, audio, and text, and
contained more than 5 hours of video clips and a large number of data points includ-
ing questions and answers in all modalities. This comprehensive dataset allowed for
further experimentation with state-of-the-art models in deception detection.
For the second objective, we developed and evaluated models in multiple modal-
ities. Initially, by creating baseline models to establish a reference for comparison.
Moreover, we implemented multiple approaches for all three modalities separately.
From those approaches the best-performing modality proved to be the text modality
which achieved the highest balanced accuracy in all of the approaches used for it.
The audio modality proved a solid addition achieving results far surpassing the base-
line models. Lastly, the approaches used for the video modality resulted in mediocre
accuracy. Even though the second approach showed promising results, it still un-
derperformed compared to the other modalities. Finally, the multimodal approach
where we utilized the features of all modalities proved to be the best performing over-
all, outperforming the text modality’s accuracy by a small margin. The ability of
the multimodal model to achieve the highest accuracy overall means the model could
leverage the strengths of each modality proving it is worth exploring further.
In conclusion, the results of individual modalities show the effectiveness of transformer-
based models in the field of deception detection. Furthermore, the ability of the mul-
timodal model to achieve the highest accuracy overall means the model could leverage
the strengths of each modality proving it is worth exploring further, yet the increase
over the text modality is marginal and more experiments are necessary to further
improve the multimodal accuracy.
In conclusion, this thesis contributes to the field of deception detection by provid-
ing a comprehensive dataset and showcasing the effectiveness of transformer-based
models as well as the effectiveness of multimodal approaches. However, it is impor-
tant to acknowledge the limitations of our study. The small size of the dataset led to
some generalization issues, especially in the video modality where it caused overfitting
issues in both approaches. Therefore, further research and expansion of the dataset
would be needed to validate and extend our results.
==PAGE:71==
CHAPTER 8
Future Work
By drawing inspiration from the conclusions section, we can envision several directions
to further enhance the performance and capabilities of our deception detection model.
A scope expansion could be a future possibility for the improvement of the dataset.
Expanding the dataset to include clues and more data points in existing modalities
such as eye tracking can help enhance the models’ generalization capabilities and
mitigate the dataset’s inherent biases.
As for the multimodal approach, the integration of eye tracking or gaze tracking
into the video modality is one idea worth exploring. Eye tracking can provide valuable
information about changes in gaze direction, and gaze aversion in crucial moments
of the interrogation process, which may serve as important indicators of deceptive
behavior. By capturing and analyzing eye movement patterns, additional cues related
to deception can be uncovered to further enhance the video modality. Another aspect
for consideration is leveraging clues utilized in the game to augment the dataset. By
incorporating information such as damning evidence, the location where the clues
were identified, and the clues’ connections to the subjects, the model’s inputs could
be enriched and subsequently, the text modality performance could be improved.
Furthermore, an expansion of the audio modality by exploring transformer-based
models specifically tailored for audio analysis could yield better performance overall.
Transformers have demonstrated remarkable success in natural language processing
tasks and could have promising results when paired with audio data. Lastly, the
multimodal approach could be further explored and refined. Multimodality has shown
the potential in improving classification performance by leveraging the strengths of
different modalities. Investigating various fusion techniques, such as different types
of attention, and optimizing the ensemble architecture could be the focus of future
work.
