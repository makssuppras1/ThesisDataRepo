Abstract - English
Jørgensen, Anders R. Mechanical Engineering, Technical University of Denmark.
Abstract of Master’s Thesis, Submitted 23 January 2015.
Direct Light Processing (DLP) in Additive Manufacturing (AM).
A stratiﬁed method within the ﬁeld of AM, wherein solid geometries are formed by
projection of image masks into a reservoir of liquid photopolymer resin, via a digital
light projector.
This thesis is about the design and development of an improved platform, for pre-
cision direct light processing in additive manufacturing. The thesis covers a review of
the main branches in AM, a theoretical analysis of the main operating principles in the
DLP process, the design considerations and implementation of a developed DLP AM
platform, and an initial study of the platforms capabilities.
Initially, the thesis covers the development of a highly modular DLP platform,
which takes basis in a theoretical review, as well as experience from previous projects
at DTU. The developed platform features a wide array of connectivity options, both
mechanically, but also electronically.
By incorporating open source software and a
LightCrafter 4500 Evaluation Module from Texas Instruments, the platform allows the
user to change and control virtually all parameters in the DLP process.
In the initial operational study of the developed platform, a part is manufactured
and compared against an equal one, made on a commercial system (EnvisionTEC Per-
facory). In these initial tests, the capabilities of the developed platform are shown,
in some instances, to rival and exceed those of the commercial system. It is achieved
to manufacture parts with a z−resolution of 58 µm, and it is shown that issues with
residual light, a problem encountered in previous projects at DTU, has been drastically
minimized.
In conclusion, the developed platform is made operational and its potential for
future research is demonstrated, even without pushing the limits of the platform. The
thesis ends with a discussion, in which the weak points of the platform are described
and suggestions for future work are given.

Problem statement
To enable research within the ﬁeld of Direct Light Processing (DLP) in precision
additive manufacturing, it is sought to develop an experimental platform, which fea-
tures a versatile range of user controllable process parameters, as well as mechanical
extensibility for future upgrades and modiﬁcations.
Further, the project seeks to implement and calibrate the required ﬁrmware for the
operation of the platform on an introductory level, as well as creating the necessary
custom proﬁle(s) in a graphical user interface, so that the platform is made operational
and ready for small production runs. In the end of the project, it is sought to do an
initial study of the capabilities of the developed system, versus that of a commercial
alternative and a previous platform developed at DTU, by a qualitative comparison of
manufactured parts.
Key focus points in the project are: the development of an improved and extensible
DLP platform, selection of appropriate equipment for optimal process control based on
a theoretical and experimental investigation of relevant process parameters, as well as
an evaluation of limitations in the developed setup and a discussion of areas relevant
for future research.

Introduction
For as long back as human history goes, the necessity to manufacture and produce
goods has been a vital element in our survival and well-being as a species. Tradition-
ally, products and tools have been made by either a subtractive or a replicative process.
The subtractive process is well known, and examples of use includes milling, turning,
electrical discharge machining and electrochemical machining. In essence, the subtrac-
tive process is the removal of material from a base shape, into a desired form, which
may be either an intermediate production step, or a ﬁnal product. In the replicative
production process, parts are made by material addition or deformation into a shape
tool of a desired geometry. Examples of replicative processes includes sand-casting, in-
jection moulding, deep drawing and sintering. The shape tool in the replicative process
has a dedicated shape, and comes in direct contact with the material being processed.
Both of these two classical processes have existed for millennia, and are yet to this day
dominant in the manufacturing industry.
In more recent times, additive manufacturing (popularly known as 3D printing), has
gained a lot of attention. Additive manufacturing is, in its broadest deﬁnition, a pro-
duction process in which parts are manufactured by the addition of material, without
the use of a shape tool. This process has several advantages over the two traditional
manufacturing processes, including no tool wear, fast adaptability to manufacturing
of varying components and creation of parts with complex internal geometries. The
ﬁrst pioneering steps within the ﬁeld were taken in the 1960s, and later followed with
commercialization by companies, such as 3D Systems and Stratasys, in the late 1980s
and early 1990s. At this time, most of the fundamental operating principles of additive
manufacturing were patented, and consequently research and development has been
fairly exclusive to those parties. Most of the original patents however, began expiring
around 2007, consequently giving rise to a renewed interest in research within the ﬁeld.
In this thesis, the design and development of an open Direct Light Processing (DLP)
platform is documented. The DLP method utilizes a light projector, which projects
an image mask of a 3 dimensional part, into a vat of liquid photopolymer resin. The
light from the projector initiates a photopolymerization process of the resin in the vat,
curing and solidifying the exposed area. The part being manufactured is then raised
slightly from the vat, after which another image mask is then projected into the newly
formed liquid region between the part and the vat surface. A part is then created, by
repetition of this procedure for all remaining cross sections of the desired geometry.
The purpose of developing a new DLP platform, as opposed to utilizing a commercial
solution, is to provide an open research platform on which all aspects of the DLP
process can be controlled and observed. The platform is intended for use in precision
additive manufacturing, and the thesis covers development of the platform, a review of
key process parameters, as well as an analysis of initial results achievable.

State of the Art
In recent years, additive manufacturing (AM) has gained a substantial amount of
attention from the media and consumer market.
The purpose of this section is to
familiarize the reader with the working principles of the major branches of AM, as well
as to summarize the main ﬁndings from previous work at DTU, within the ﬁeld of the
DLP process.
2.1
Industrial development and the major branches of ad-
ditive manufacturing
Photopolymerization was ﬁrst conceived of in the 1960s at Battelle Memorial Insti-
tute [1]. At this time, researchers attempted to solidify a photopolymer, by focusing
two intersecting laser beams at a spot in the center of a vat. The operational principle
of this experiment, can generally be thought of as the precursor of modern stereolithog-
raphy (SLA) technology. Since then, it would be almost 30 years until the ﬁrst AM
platform was made commercially available by 3D Systems in 1989[1][4]. The platform
was based on SLA technology, and for the ﬁrst time in history, provided designers and
engineers with a fast and relatively inexpensive method of rapidly realizing 3D models
of prototypes. In the following years, several more technologies would emerge, includ-
ing fused deposition modelling (FDM) and selective laser sintering (SLS), known from
companies such as Stratasys[5] and EOS[6] respectively. The working principle of these
three technologies can generally be divided into three main categories: processes based
on a liquid, solid or powder base material. Table 1 shows a non-exhaustive list of the
various sub-processes within each of the three top level working principles.

2.1.1
Fused deposition material (FDM)
Each of the AM technologies has their respective strengths and weaknesses, as well as
support for a vast variety of diﬀerent materials. From table 1, the most commonly
implemented technology in the consumer hardware market is the FDM process. The
process is a relatively simple and safe one, in which a solid plastic ﬁlament is forced
through a heated extruder nozzle via a motor drive. Given that the process does not
contain toxic or otherwise harmful substances, it is easily marketable and the simplicity
of the printing process makes it highly user friendly. A schematic of the process is shown
in ﬁgure 1.
Figure 1: Schematic of the FDM process[7].
The FDM process works by depositing a plastic ﬁlament in a geometric pattern
deﬁned by a cross section of a 3D model, with a set of extrusion nozzles. When one
layer of the 3D model is completed, the workpiece is lowered and the process is repeated
for another cross section. This process is repeated until all remaining cross sections are
completed, thus creating the desired part. At the cost of added machine complexity
and post treatment of parts, the FDM process allows for prints to be constructed using
support material, so that overhanging geometry of the workpiece can be achieved. The
intelligence of the FDM processes is located in the control of the nozzles, and the
principle of consolidation is liquid phase bonding, which is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.

2.1.2
Selective laser sintering (SLS)
In the domain of powder based AM processes, perhaps the most commonly known is
SLS. In SLS a laser is used to selectively consolidate a powder being either metallic,
ceramic or polymeric. In comparison to the FDM process, SLS is signiﬁcantly more
complex, usually requiring expensive equipment and careful material handling. Due to
costs and complexity of the required machinery in the process, it is mainly found in
industrial settings, where the process is competitive with conventional manufacturing
processes for small batch sizes, e.g.
for functional prototypes.

2.2
Direct Light Processing (DLP)
Building on the working principles of SLA technology, EnvisionTEC launched their
Perfactory line of 3D printers in 2002.
In the Perfactory line, EnvisionTEC uses a
Direct Light Processing (DLP) projector as the consolidation tool, instead of a laser as
used in the SLA process. Commonly this technology is simply referred to as the DLP
process, referencing the consolidation tool, and is also how it will be referred to in this
thesis. The most noticeable diﬀerence between the DLP process and the other AM
processes is the orientation of the workpiece during manufacturing. The DLP process
is currently the only process in which the workpiece is suspended on the build plate
instead of being posed. Suspending the workpiece under the build plate has several
advantages over posing it, including better control of layer thickness and uniformity,
as well as minimizing the required amount of photopolymer in the vat. A schematic of
the DLP process can be seen in ﬁgure 7.
The principle of consolidation in the DLP process is photo polymerization, which
can be initiated simultaneously across the entire projected cross section of the work-
piece.
This has the advantage of lower consolidation times, in comparison to SLA,
enabling faster build times. As shown in ﬁgure 7, the consolidation tool is contained
within a light projector unit containing a Digital Micromirror Device (DMD), wherein
the intelligence transfer lies in the control of the mirrors. As incident light is projected
from a source, e.g. a bulb or LED, it is directed onto the DMD and out of the op-
tical lens of the projector, before reaching the transparent vat surface. As the light
penetrates into the resin tank, the polymerization process is initiated, solidifying the
exposed resin volume between the vat surface and the workpiece, deﬁned by the layer
thickness. After the curing process is completed for a given layer, the z-axis is raised
by a predetermined distance, separating the workpiece and vat surface, while allowing

2.3
Results from previous work at DTU
In the time between autumn 2013 and spring 2014, two projects within the ﬁeld
of DLP AM were undertaken at DTU. In one project, a diploma thesis by Karl-Emil
Nielsen, the development and construction of a DLP platform, with focus on the forces
involved in the print process, is documented [2]. In the second project, a bachelors
thesis by Hansen, Rasmus S., and Franch, Joachim C., the capabilities of the platform
is studied, with a focus on light sources, vat coatings, resins and software [3]. This
project strives to build upon the ﬁndings of each of these previous studies, in order
to further develop and improve the design and capabilities of the old set-up (hereafter
referred to as version 1.0). In this section, a brief summary of the main ﬁndings from the
previous studies is given, so that it may serve as a point of origin for further research.
2.3.1
Hardware design and initial tests
The hardware platform developed by Karl-Emil [2], is based on the functional principle
of that known from the EnvisionTECTM (DLP) process. The main structural com-
ponent of platform version 1, is the chassis made from extruded aluminium proﬁles.
Attached to the chassis, is two independently controlled linear feeds which controls
movement of the build plate in the z-direction, away from the vat surface. A build
plate mount is located in between the dual z-axis, and is pin constrained at one end
and slider constrained at the other. This set-up forms a bearing which admits minor
misalignment errors between the two z-axes, as well as allowing each axis to be op-
erated independently without straining the build plate. The build plate is suspended
underneath the mount by four aluminium brackets, which are tightened by screws at
each corner. Calibration of the build plate, so that it is parallel with the vat surface, is
achieved by loosening all four screws and letting the build plate rest on top of the vat
surface, after which the four screws are tightened again, securing it in place. A CAD
model of the set-up can be seen in ﬁgure 8a, and the actual set-up is shown in ﬁgure

2.3.2
Capabilities and limitations
Testing of DLP platform version 1.0 was done by Rasmus Severing Hansen and Joachim
Christian Franch in their bachelors thesis[3], during the spring semester of 2014 at DTU.
The photopolymers investigated in the thesis are SubG+ from Maker Juice Labs[11] as
well as the FTD resin brieﬂy mentioned in section 2.3.1. The light source used varies
between three commercial DLP projectors, originally intended for AV entertainment,
but repurposed to the DLP AM process.
Initial results in the thesis showed, that the light intensity at the projected plane
had a signiﬁcant inhomogeneous distribution, with a clear gradient of intensity from
one edge of the image plane to the opposite[3, p.
11].
Due to the inhomogeneous
distribution of light across the projected plane, even curing of a cross sectional area
of the workpiece was diﬃcult to attain. The result of having a varying light intensity
is, that certain areas of the workpiece may be subject to under-curing, while other
areas are subject to over-curing. The report mentions dealing with these problems by
use of an image mask, as well as various other measures, however in conclusion it was
recommended to ﬁnd a more suitable light source all together.
The magnitude of the normal forces on the vat surface, listed in table 2, suggested
that it would be advantageous to use a combination of surface coating of the vat as
well as a skewed lift procedure of the z-axis. This lead to tests being made of multiple
surface coatings, in which certain silicone compounds as well as PTFE coatings showed
promising results.
The recommendation of the report is to further investigate the
properties of these coatings.
In the experimental work of the thesis, several process diﬃculties were encountered.
When manufacturing parts with small geometry, or features suspended in front of a
region of liquid resin, residual light leaked into unwanted areas causing the resin to
cure and leaving unintended solid geometries on the workpiece.
The residual light
eﬀect was found to be governed by exposure time and resin pigmentation, however no
conclusive settings for optimal performance of the platform was given. An illustration
of the residual light problem can be seen in ﬁgure 9.

Theory
In this section, a theoretical overview is given of the main inﬂuencing parameters
of the DLP AM process.
The section covers the advantages, of implementation of
advanced lift sequences of the build plate, an overview of the working principle of the
projection unit and its optical conﬁguration, as well as an introduction to the working
principles of the photopolymer resin and how bonding between the vat and workpiece
may be avoided chemically.
3.1
The DLP process
The forces on the vat surface originates from two factors, one factor being polymer
adhesion to the vat surface during the curing process, and the other being a pressure
diﬀerence which is created as the build plate is lifted. The pressure diﬀerence originates
from the lift sequence, which creates a cavity between the workpiece and the vat surface
that must be ﬁlled by viscous polymer ﬂow, from the surrounding resin.
3.2
Print strategies
One of the main concerns in the DLP AM process is, that the workpiece bonds not
only to the build plate, but also to the surface of the vat. This section aims to discuss
some of the mechanical strategies, which can be implemented to reduce/overcome this
issue.
When selecting a suitable material for the vat, it is important to mind both the
optical properties (for UV-transmission), its level of chemical inertness, as well as the
surface ﬁnish. Using a chemically inert vat is important, as the photopolymerization
process (discussed in section 3.4.1), produces radical species, which could potentially
react with the surface and thereby create chemical bonding between the two. Ideally,
the surface of the vat should be plane, chemically inert, non porous and have as low a
surface roughness as possible. Having a vat with low porosity and surface roughness,
prevents photopolymer material from being deposited and subsequently cured within
these areas, whereby a mechanical bond between the two could be created. Addition-
ally, maintaining a low surface roughness lowers the overall surface area of the vat,
whereby bonding by adsorption is minimized. In reality however, a material which
perfectly match all these criteria does not exist. As such, a lift strategy is needed to
aid in removing the workpiece from the vat surface. Mechanically, four main removal
principles can be thought of which is illustrated in ﬁgure 10.

projector should ideally be able to cast a large picture with a high and uniform lumi-

readily available, and its light source as well as optical elements should be conﬁgurable.
This section deals with the main selection criteria for the projection unit.

3.3.1
General projector considerations
In order to achieve an even curing level across the projected picture, a uniform light in-
tensity should be projected. In section 2.3.2, results from previous work[3] showed that
the light intensity in commercial projectors can vary greatly. Information regarding
the luminous intensity, at the plane of projection, is generally not available from man-
ufactures, and is largely inﬂuenced by the optical layout of the projection unit. Ideally,
once a projection unit has been selected, its brightness variation at the image plane
should be mapped and corrected for, e.g. by software or modiﬁcations to the optical
system. Alternatively, the build envelope of the DLP platform could be minimized and
located within an area of homogeneous light intensity at the image plane.
Another important aspect of the projection unit is, that it should be able to project
light in the UV range, ideally λ ≤400 nm. Most commercially available projection
units, such as the ones used in DLP platform version 1.0, do not project large amounts
of UV light due to internal optical ﬁltering. The UV ﬁltering in consumer projectors is
done to protect the DMD chip, as studies by Texas Instruments, have found a correla-
tion between UV exposure and decreased DMD component life[13]. Furthermore, UV
light has no value in consumer applications, such as viewing digital or motion pictures,
since it is invisible to the naked eye and can have a negative eﬀect on health in large
quantities (eye damage, sun burn, skin cancer). Table 3 lists the eﬀects on DMD life,

3.4.1
The photopolymerization process
A suitable polymer material for the DLP AM process is one, which when subjected to
light of a speciﬁc wavelength will cure and harden, but in which the polymerization
process will not propagate outside of the illuminated region. The three steps in the
photopolymerization process were shown in ﬁgure 6, and is: initiation, propagation
and termination. One example of a photopolymerization process is shown in ﬁgure 18,
where a UV-light source initiates the production of free radicals in an aromatic carbonyl
compound.

One of the advantages of the photopolymerization process is, that the reaction is
practically independent of temperature, and that it can be stopped by simply remov-
ing the light source. As an additional advantage, the polymerization process may be
accurately controlled, since the photopolymer can be engineered to only initiate decom-
position (into radical species), at a narrow bandwidth of wavelengths[20, p. 172]. This
could in future research have the potential, to allow for construction of multi-material
parts and/or the creation of chemically removable support structures, which can be of
aid during the manufacturing process of the workpiece. One can imagine a setup, in
which the polymer material is composed of diﬀerent compounds, which may be selec-
tively and independently cured by a light source, with varying wavelengths of incident
light.

the initiator. When using unpigmented photopolymers, the curing depth can be directly
controlled by varying the initiator concentration in the compound. Finding the correct

Before getting started on the actual component design of the new DLP platform,

the thoughts behind the choices made in section 4.3, which describes the mechanical

4.2.1
General approach
The overall design philosophy of the new platform was, that it should be as ﬂexible and
easy to upgrade as possible, without compromising structural integrity or functionality.
In order to achieve this goal, inspiration came from the kids toys ”Lego Technic”. In
Lego Technic, most components are mix and matchable with each other, and surfaces of
the individual parts in large assemblies have multiple connection points, so that creative
ideas can quickly be implemented into the existing system. The same general approach
has been taken when designing the new DLP platform. A result of this approach is, that
structural elements have been selected not only based on their mechanical properties,
but also on how ﬂexible they would be in future upgrades. Additionally, the variation
of bolt and nut sizes has been attempted to be kept to a minimum, so that mainly M5
and M6 bolts have been used in the set-up, according to ISO 4762/DIN 912 standards.
Additional design considerations were that the platform should be self contained,
including all hardware and software needed for operation, and that it should be movable
to ease assembly and modiﬁcations. The main points speciﬁc to the operation of the
DLP platform are given in the list below:

4.3.2
Z-stage liner guide
As UV-light is projected through the vat and onto the work piece, the curing process
tends to create an adhesive interface between the two. This adhesive interface must
be broken for each successful layer of the part, which can under certain circumstances

overcome this problem, the platform has been designed to feature two independently
controlled linear Z-stage rails. The idea is that by allowing more control over the Z-
stage lift sequence, the work piece can be peeled oﬀthe vat surface at an angle, as
opposed to simply being lifted oﬀparallel to the normal. This system was already
implemented in DLP platform version 1.0, where it proved to be a viable strategy, so

Given that DLP platform 1.0 used a consumer A/V DLP projector modiﬁed by removal

the photopolymer decreases with higher wavelengths, the blocking eﬀect of the polymer
(depth of penetration) relies at increasing levels on the pigmentation in the material.
The result is, that light penetrates deeper into the resin, and over multiple exposures,
cures material in regions of previous layers not intended to contain solid geometry.
By using a projection unit which targets the absorption peaks of the resin better, less
problems with the residual light eﬀect is expected.
In addition to the exposure tests done on the miniature DLP test stand, two non-
stick surface coatings were tested: PDMS and FEP-ﬁlm.
The working principle of
the PDMS coating was treated in section 3.4.2, and seemed to have good anti-stick
properties. Unfortunately, the coating has a habit of becoming ”cloudy” very fast when
in use, and as such would need to be replaced often. An added disadvantage is that it
is easy to damage the surface of the coating, so it must be handled very delicately. The
PDMS product that was used for all tests is Sylgard 184 from Dow Corning, which is
a two component silicone compound.
The second surface that was tested is FEP-ﬁlm, which is a ﬂuorinated ethylene
propylene copolymer which shares many of the non-stick properties of PTFE, com-
monly known by the brand name Teﬂon. In relation to the DLP platform, its high
optical transmissivity and low reactivity towards radical species made it an interesting
candidate for use as a non stick surface. In practice, the ﬁlm can be attached to a glass
surface by ﬁrst applying a droplet of premixed Sylgard 184 at its center, after which the
ﬁlm is gently placed on top and the PDMS material underneath is spread evenly with
a plastic spatula or equivalent. Hot embossing of the FEP-ﬁlm was also attempted,
unsuccessfully. Hot embossing of the ﬁlm showed potential however, but ﬁne tuning
the process was outside the scope of this project. In ﬁgure 41, two pictures from the

6.3
Stepper controller
The stepper controller installed on the DLP platform is a Printrboard Rev B. with
an Atmel DFU Bootloader. The board features 1/16 micro-stepping, which allows for
smaller increments and thereby higher resolution in the z−axis. In the ﬁrmware, the
relationship between steps per revolution and axis movement has been speciﬁed as
discussed on page 31 in section 4.3.3.
Due to the speciﬁed accuracy grade (C5) of
the ball spindles used in the developed platform, it was determined that calibration
of the lead accuracy was not an issue worth spending time on at this point. As such,
movement of the z−and y−axis was simply veriﬁed by use of a simple vernier caliper,
which proved to be suﬃcient for the initial tests that were done. As it was mentioned in
section 4.3.3, calibration by a laser interferometry system or equivalent may be desired
in future work on the platform, when the limits of z−axis resolution is pushed. The
controller board is shown in ﬁgure 52.
==PAGE:60==

The ﬁrmware is standard Marlin, as commonly used
in the RepRap community, and has been modiﬁed to suit the developed DLP AM
platform. Firmware can be edited and compiled in the Arduino environment, creating
a .HEX ﬁle which can then be uploaded to the printrboard through Atmel FLIP. On the
printrboard, the x−and y−channels control the z−axis, and the z−channel controls
the y−axis. This may seem counter intuitive, however as the platform requires two
channels to operate the z−axis, at least one of them has to be mapped to a wrongly
labelled connector. In future projects, if so desired, the channels can easily be changed
in the hardware (switch connectors) and ﬁrmware (remap the relevant channels).

Given the optical properties and mechanical durability of FEP, it was decided to

parts with small cross sectional areas followed by large ones, as discussed on page 13.

Tower as discussed in section 7.1.1.

8
Discussion
This section seeks to highlight some of the main ﬁndings in the project, and to act
as a discussion for areas of interest in future work. Generally, the developed setup has
performed as expected from the beginning of the project, however, there are still areas
which could be improved.
8.1
Mechanical setup
The mechanical implementation of DLP platform version 2.0, has generally been
performing very well during the initial tests. Areas which are advisable to improve on
in future projects, are discussed in the following subsections.
8.1.1
Build plate setup
During initial testing, the levelling system of the build plate, implemented by a ball and
socket joint as illustrated in ﬁgure 32, proved to be a more snug ﬁt than anticipated.
This does not render the mechanism unusable, however it makes it more diﬃcult to
achieve proper alignment between the vat and the build plate, as the ball part of the
joint does not move as freely as hoped. The issue could potentially be resolved by
implementing a 3-point support system instead of the lower ball joint (4, ﬁg. 32), or
alternatively by milling the concave feature of the existing part to a larger radius, and
inserting e.g. a PTFE lining.
Another point of improvement in the build plate setup, can be found in the slider and
pin joints (7, 8 ﬁg. 32). In the current implementation, the joints function mechanically
as intended, however they are not forgiving of user error, as a too large angle of the
build plate crossbar will result in strain of the z−axis linear guides (6, ﬁg. 28). A
design is proposed, where the center facing side of the slider bearing (7, ﬁg. 32) is left
open, so that the build plate crossbar is free to fall out of the groove of the bearing, if
too large angles are applied to the assembly. Alternatively, too large angles of the build
plate crossbar could be prevented, by a limit set in the ﬁrmware of the printrboard
electronics.
In order to gain additional insight into the separation forces of the workpice from
the vat, it is proposed to mount a strain gauge on the threaded rod for the build plate
(9, ﬁg. 32). By implementing a strain gauge in this location, quantitative data could
be logged e.g. during a test series of various vat coatings.
8.1.2
Vat assembly
The 3-point levelling system of the vat bottom (1, 7 ﬁg.
33) in its current imple-
mentation, does not allow independent adjustment of pitch and roll. The system was
developed in the manner that it is, in order to give maximum stability of the vat dur-
==PAGE:76==
8
DISCUSSION
67
ing lift sequences. In future iterations, if it is found that the vat will often need to be
levelled, it may be advantageous to move one of the micrometer screws (1, ﬁg. 33), so
that it is in-line with one of the two others.
Currently, the glass window of the vat (6, ﬁg. 33) is mounted onto the bottom plate
by a glue interface. This setup was suﬃcient during the initial tests of the platform, and
helped limit the amount of mechanical components, that needed to be manufactured
during the project. In future work, to allow easier testing of diﬀerent vat materials,
it is highly recommended to redesign the interface, so that it is mechanical instead of
adhesive.
During initial testing of the platform, when pouring leftover resin from the vat back
into a storage container, it was found that the walls in proximity of the nozzle on the
vat tray barrier (5, ﬁg. 33) were too low. Pouring is still possible, however one needs
to be careful not to have the resin overﬂow the sides. As the part is 3D printed, the ﬁx
of making the walls of the barrier taller is easily implemented.
While working on the project, a suggestion was made to implement an ultra sound
shaker on the vat plate, which could potentially help loosen the workpiece from the
vat surface after curing of an image mask. The introduction of an ultra sound shaker,
would in principle make it possible to use a combination of all lift strategies, shown in
ﬁgure 10. The implementation of such a shaker should be fairly straight forward in the
current setup, as long as the adhesive interfaces previously discussed are exchanged for
mechanical ones, in order not to destroy the glue interfaces between parts 5, 6 and 7
(ﬁg. 33) with the shaker.
8.1.3
Projector mount
Structurally, the designed projector mount was found to be more than suﬃciently rigid,
even though it is manufactured from 3D printed ABS parts. For the current setup, the
assembly provides adequate adjustability to support a range of build envelopes, and
was generally easy to operate. In future projects, if it is decided to keep and modify
the optics of the LightCrafter 4500 Evaluation Module, to allow for shorter focus set
points, the projector mount would need to be slightly redesigned, in order to allow the
projection unit to be moved close enough to the vat.
8.2
Electronics setup
Currently, there are no endstops installed on the y−and z−axis, meaning that the
platform is exposed to user error, such as wrongly input manual G-code. The lack of
endstops is mostly due to time constraints in the project, however mounting points have
already been designed into the platform, where e.g. Opto Endstops can be mounted.
On he z−axis, four M3 holes are provided on the Z-stage overbar mounts (2, ﬁg. 28),
two on either side of the linear guide rail.
For the Y −stage, a design for an Opto
Endstop can easily be made in CAD and 3D printed, after which it can be mounted in
any suitable location on the optical breadboard (5, ﬁg. 26).
8.2.1
Optics
As it was discussed in section 6.1, the focus and light intensity of the LightCrafter
4500 did not live up to the full expectations of the module. As a result, there is a
==PAGE:77==
68
8

distinct degradation in part quality, when evaluated at points manufactured further
towards the top of the projected image versus the bottom. In future work, it is highly
recommended to improve on the optics of the projection unit, in order to reach even
better print quality than achieved in this project.
The issues with varying light intensity did not pose a problem for the initial tests
done, although at higher LED power settings P > 100, the maximum dark ﬁeld of
the unit was suﬃciently strong, to unintentionally cure a thin ﬁlm on the vat surface,
during larger prints such as the Eiﬀel Tower model. In future work, it is recommended
to more precisely map out the intensity variation of light in the image, after which
a compensation mask can be implemented.
This will not solve the dark ﬁeld issue
previously mentioned, but will ensure that a more even curing level is achieved across
the projected mask, eliminating a potential source of error.
In addition to the previous points regarding the projector, when modifying the
optics, it is advised to do so in a way, so that the projected image can also be made
smaller.
This will give better x−and y−resolution, which will in turn aid in the
manufacturing of thin geometries, such as the truss structures of the Eiﬀel Tower model
treated in section 7.
8.3
Software
Generally, the focus of the project has not been the development of software for the
platform. As such, open source software has been used to as large an extend as possible,
with the only closed source required for the platform being Atmel FLIP for uploading
ﬁrmware to the printrboard, and the TI DLP LightCrafter 4500 Control Software for
interfacing with the projection unit.
8.3.1
Creation Workshop
In CW, custom G-code was implemented to aid in the support for advanced lift se-
quences. Much was left to be desired however, in terms of control and customizability,
as the CW interface does not allow for variations in between individual image masks.
The CW software suite is open source, written in C#, and can be downloaded from
GitHub. In future projects, it may be desirable to create a custom version of the soft-
ware, in order to gain more control of the slicing and G-code proﬁles. Modifying the
CW software was outside the scope of this project however.
An additional point, where CW lacks desired features, is in the creation of support
structures.
Currently the support structure, albeit automated, is slow, buggy and
can sometimes make the program unresponsive, accompanied by a crash to desktop.
Support could possibly be generated using other 3rd party software, such as Meshmixer,
however it would be nice to have this functionality implemented in the CW suite.
8.3.2
Firmware
It wasn’t necessary to do much adjustment in the ﬁrmware, in order to calibrate the
machine decently and make it operational for the initial tests. Largely, the ease of
calibration originated from the quality of the ball spindles used on the z−and y−axis,
which are both machined to C5 tolerances.
Having ball spindles with such precise
tolerances, means that the axis can be expected to follow the nominal travel expected

8.4
Test results
In general, the tests results discussed in section 7 were of a very satisfactory quality,
considering the short amount of time for calibration there has been in the project. It was
shown that the eﬀects of residual light had been considerably minimized in comparison
DLP platform version 1.0, and the ability of the developed platform, in manufacturing
of reasonably detailed geometries, was demonstrated with the Eiﬀel Tower model and
the Ford engine block.
When manufacturing the Eiﬀel Tower model with the water clear photopolymer
from Venus Creator, signiﬁcant problems with residual light were encountered. These
issues can be attributed to multiple factors, the most apparent one being the lack of pig-
mentation in the resin. Lack of pigmentation would allow UV light to penetrate deeper
into the resin, whereby unwanted areas in the geometry would be cured. When looking
up the resin on the company website however, several models are showcased, seemingly
manufactured with the same resin product. This could indicate, that the process pa-
rameters on DLP platform 2.0 had not been calibrated properly before manufacturing
(too long/much UV exposure), or it could be a marketing trick. Alternatively, the ab-
sorption curve for the resin in mention has distinct peaks, none of which are located at
the λ = 405 nm wavelength, where the CBT-39-UV LED installed in the LightCrafter
4500 operates.
Regarding the coatings tested, namely FEP and PDMS, the FEP coating was found
to be the most durable, while the PDMS coating seemingly provided the lowest bonding
strength between the workpiece and the vat. Even though it was not possible to manu-
facture the Eiﬀel Tower model using the FEP coating, it is still highly recommended to
keep testing with it. Using the FEP coating, several versions of the Ford engine block
were manufactured using varying light intensity (exposure time kept constant). This
indicates, that for some geometries, the FEP coating is suﬃcient to prevent bonding,
and given its higher mechanical durability, it was generally easier to work with.
Though not covered in section 7, due to lack of satisfactory results, two prints
attempting to manufacture the head of a T-800 terminator model, were done after the
completion of the Eiﬀel Tower model. In both of these prints, the part broke during
manufacturing, leaving a blob of cured resin on the surface of the vat, which was then
repeatedly exposed to all subsequent image masks in the queue. When cleaning the
PDMS covered vat in-between these test runs, it was found that the over-cured blob
adhered signiﬁcantly to the PDMS surface, and upon removal, damage was found to
have been inﬂicted on the coating, shown in ﬁgure 71.

Of the items listed above, it is believed that a combination of too low mask area Amask

In between test runs of the Eiﬀel Tower model, as well as the T-800 terminator models,
the vat tray was not emptied, thereby not allowing oxygen from the atmosphere to
diﬀuse back into the PDMS coating. This could have lead to an increasing adhesive
interface between the workpiece and the vat, which ultimately lead to structural failure
in the part, when manufacturing some of the later image masks with a higher cross
sectional area. To test this theory, a fresh PDMS vat could be installed, and the job
could be repeated. Alternatively, a scraper could be implemented in the design, as
suggested in section 3.4.2, which would expose the PDMS coating to the atmosphere
in-between each projected image mask.

Future tests
Due to time constraints in the project, it was not possible to carry out as many

In section 7, the most pressing tests were

Regarding the breakdown of image masks into smaller sub-masks, a system is conceived
of, where each layer in a model is cured during multiple exposures of smaller sections,
and where the workpiece is raised from the vat surface in-between each sub-mask, so

would require modiﬁcation of the CW software, to allow for more control of the G-code
generation. Alternatively, a custom sub-mask and G-code generator could be designed
e.g. in Matlab or equivalent, which could be used to post process the output from CW,
before re-injecting it and running the print.

9
Conclusion
In this thesis, the design and development of an improved DLP platform for pre-
cision additive manufacturing is documented. The developed platform is based on a
theoretical review, of the main inﬂuencing parameters aﬀecting the DLP process, as
well as two previous studies within the ﬁeld, conducted at DTU.
The developed platform was made user conﬁgurable and extensible, by utilizing
modular elements as structural components in the mechanical setup, and by using
open source software and highly conﬁgurable electronic hardware, to as large an extend
as possible.
As a consequence, the platform features anchor points for equipment
mounting at practically all surfaces, a design which was inspired by the kids toys ”Lego
Technic”. On the control side, the software used for G-code and image mask generation
is open source (Creation Workshop), and can thus be modiﬁed to suit the needs of
future research. To aid in the control of the platform, custom G-code proﬁles has been
created in the software, which allows users to execute small production runs.
The
ﬁrmware implemented in the platform is a version of Marlin, known from the RepRap
community, which has been slightly adjusted to allow for operation of the custom
platform developed. The ﬁrmware runs on a stepper controller, which allows for up
to four stepper motors to be connected simultaneously, and which also features a wide
selection of additional I/O ports, again allowing for the platform to be extended and
reconﬁgured easily in future projects.
To enable manufacturing of highly accurate and precise parts, a Texas Instruments
based DLP projector was acquired and subsequently modiﬁed to allow for shorter fo-
cus set points. This modiﬁcation halved the pixel size at the image, in comparison to
the stock conﬁguration, and eﬀectively quadrupled the pixel density achievable. With
the modiﬁed DLP projector, a small test series of CAD models were manufactured on
the developed platform. The results of the test series were then qualitatively bench-
marked against those achievable on a commercial platform (EnvisionTEC Perfactory)
and against those of the previous DLP platform developed at DTU (version 1.0). In the
results, it was shown that the residual light problem, previously an issue in DLP plat-
form version 1.0, had been close to eliminated by the selection of a projection unit, with
a light source illuminating at a shorter wavelength (UV). This enabled the developed
platform to manufacture parts of high quality and high levels of detail, in some areas
rivalling and exceeding the results achievable on the EnvisionTEC Perfactory system.
During the test series, the importance of a proper non-stick vat coating was also demon-
strated, by testing two diﬀerent materials for said purpose: FEP and PDMS. It was
shown that each surface coating has its respective advantages and disadvantages, with
the FEP material being more mechanically durable but also more prone to adhesion
with the workpiece, while the PDMS coating allowed for easier release of the workpiece
from the vat surface, but was also found to be more fragile.
In the end of the thesis, a discussion is given where the weak points of the developed
platform are treated, and suggestions for future work within the ﬁeld of DLP AM is
==PAGE:82==
9
CONCLUSION
73
given. In general, the performance of the developed platform was found to be satisfac-
tory, with the main areas of improvement being systems for easier material changeover
and build plate calibration, as well as an upgrade of the projection unit optics, which
would allow for even higher voxel densities to be achieved in manufactured parts. In the
very end, a list of areas relevant for further study is given, which could provide further
insight into of the inﬂuence of various process parameters, but which time unfortunately
did not permit to be carried out during this project.
