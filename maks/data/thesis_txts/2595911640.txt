Abstract
Beer production is an intricate chemical process. From the same critical ingredients (a starch source, yeast, hops
and water), a highly complex mixture of chemical species is obtained. The ﬂuctuating combinations of these
compounds, also called off- and on-ﬂavours, are responsible for the single taste of each beer [1]. Thus, the ability
to improve any stage of production will have a signiﬁcant effect on proﬁtability and the ultimate success or
failure of a brewery [2]. Therefore, it is important to carefully describe the different stages of the beer production
process, in order to optimize process conditions and beer ﬂavour. To do so, in this study, a comprehensive
fermentation model and optimization problem are formulated, extending the work of [3], [4] and [5]. This model
includes a complete yeast growth model which is related to uptake of three different sugars; the model also
includes the uptake of amino acids, production of ethanol and ﬂavour related desirable and undesirable chemical
species, including the temperature dependency of such processes as well.
Beer ﬂavour reﬂects the combination of a large number of chemical species, mostly resulting from the yeast
metabolism. Therefore, in this work, the goal is to determine how an up-to-date beer industrial production
should optimally operate. To achieve this, a the following steps are taken: (i) an extensive literature review of
the principal beer by-products and the existing beer fermentation models is conducted; (ii) a comprehensive
fermentation model is implemented, including yeast growth model, ethanol and by-products (fusel alcohols,
esters, VDKs and acetaldehyde) production; (iii) the fermentation model is carefully examined and improved, as
well as tested through a sensitivity analysis; and, (iv) a genetic algorithm optimization problem is formulated
and implemented with the objective of maximizing the conversion of substrate to ethanol, while dealing with
desired ﬁnal concentrations of the by-products and the fermentation time minimization.
The results show that the initial wort composition and the yeast strain are crucial to determine the beer type that
is being produced. Besides, the optimization study for the selected model shows that the temperature must be
kept low during the ﬁrst days of the fermentation to avoid over-production of undesired species (fusel alcohols,
VDKs and acetaldehyde); then, the temperature of the reactor should increase in order to obtain the targeted
ethanol and esters concentration in a minimum fermentation time. The Genetic Algorithms demonstrated to
be effective solving the optimization problem formulated. Finally, the challenges encountered working with a
mechanistic model suggest that other model approaches (i.e. neural networks) could be explored in future works.
A comprehensive fermentation model was implemented based upon kinetic models. Furthermore, in order to
optimize the ﬂavour proﬁle and process conditions, an optimization using genetic algorithms was formulated
and solved. Thus, this work contributes to achieving a comprehensive understanding of the impact of different
chemical species (off- and on-ﬂavours) on the optimal beer ﬂavour proﬁle, as well as in the optimization of
process conditions.

2
Introduction
2.1
Beer Production
Beer production (brewing) is one of the oldest processes where biotechnology has been used to produce alcoholic
beverages [12]. Beer is widely consumed over the world, with an average consumption of 25.7 L per capita in
2019, corresponding to 60% of the total volume of alcoholic beverages consumed worldwide [13]. The global
revenue in the beer market amounts to C545,483m in 2019, and it is expected to keep growing approximately
3.6% yearly for the next 3 years [13]. The current top 5 beer producers around the world are China, U.S., Brazil,
Mexico and Germany [14]. In these countries, the beer demand is increasing, with the consumers looking for
premium and innovative beers, willing to try a variety of ﬂavoured beers and beer mixes, as well as alcohol-free
and low alcoholic beers also increasing their popularity [15].
Beer is obtained by the yeast fermentation of malted cereal grains, with the addition of hops and water [16].
The main stages in the beer production are upstream processing (wort production); alcoholic fermentation
and maturation, and processing and stabilization [17]. During the upstream processing, the wort (a sugar rich
intermediate) is generated from a starch source, usually malted barley [16]. Afterwards, fermentation occurs as
a result of the enzymatic activity of the yeast, which leads to sugar conversion to pyruvate. Under anaerobic
conditions, the yeast converts pyruvate to ethanol and CO2 [17]. Beer ﬂavour is signiﬁcantly affected by the yeast
metabolism during fermentation and maturation due to the generation of on- and off- ﬂavour compounds [18].
Therefore, the yeast metabolism can be studied in order be able to optimize the by-products generation and thus
the beer ﬂavour. Fermentation and maturation are the longest processes in brewing; the primary fermentation
lasts from three to six days and the maturation up to two weeks [17]. Fermentation and maturation are completed
once all consumable sugars have been converted by the yeast into alcohol, following which the solution leaves
the fermenter for subsequent downstream processing (processing and stabilization) prior to sale and consumption
[16].
2.2
Motivation & Project Objectives
The beer market is a very competitive environment. Hence, breweries aim to improve any stage of the beer
production either to increase proﬁtability or to improve the ﬁnal beer product. Fermentation is an essential step
in beer production, where a large number of chemical species are produced. The ﬂuctuating combinations of
these chemical species are responsible for the single ﬂavour proﬁle of each beer.
The objective of this work is to determine how the industrial operation of the beer production can be optimized
through mathematical modelling and optimization methods. An optimal production condition should be to
reduce the total batch time (increase proﬁtability) and ensure an appropriate balance of all chemical species
(optimal ﬂavour). Some of the previous works have been focusing only on the total batch time reduction, such
as [1]. Others, use the same model to target various speciﬁc ﬂavour proﬁles, such as [19]. The objective of

the present study is to use a complete and detailed beer fermentation model to optimize simultaneously the

Literature Review
3.1
Beer Flavour Compounds
Beer is one of the most pleasant beverages in the world, the ﬁnal taste/aroma of which is the result of a highly
complex mixture of chemical species produced in the course of every step of brewing [7]. The concentration of
these compounds can be derived from: barley and hops, yeast metabolism, contamination and stability of the
compounds during product storage [20]. However, the great majority of these substances are produced during
the fermentation phase and consist of metabolic intermediates or by-products of the main living organism of
brewing, the yeast [7].
Over 800 different compounds are present in the ﬁnal beer product, that can be classiﬁed in the following chemical
classes: higher alcohols, esters, fatty acids, vicinal diketones (VDKs), aldehydes and sulphur compounds [21].
Nevertheless, not all of these compounds have the same inﬂuence in the ﬁnal beer ﬂavour [22]. Higher alcohols,
esters and VDKs are the key elements produced by yeast. While higher alcohols and esters are desirable volatile
constituents of a pleasant beer, VDKs are often considered as off-ﬂavours [7].
The concentration of all the ﬂavour-active species must be controlled; otherwise, some compounds may dominate
and destroy the ﬂavour balance. Also, it is important to consider the existing synergy between different groups
of compounds, i.e. esters can affect the beer taste below their threshold value due to the interaction with other
compounds [7]. The different beer types exhibit different ﬂavour characteristics, inﬂuenced by the yeast strain
chosen [23] as well as other factors (e.g. temperature, wort composition, fermentation time) crucial to the
fermentation stage [24]. For example, isoamyl acetate (banana aroma) is found above threshold only in lager
beers, while ethyl acetate (solvent aroma) and ethyl hexanoate (apple aroma) are the esters that are present
in concentrations above threshold in most ale beers [25]. Nevertheless, there are a few compounds that are
undesirable independently of the beer type, for example, diacetyl provides a negative buttery ﬂavour and it must
always be kept below the threshold concentration [7].

3.1.1
Esters
Esters are the most important byproducts inﬂuencing the ﬁnal beer ﬂavour. They have a very low threshold in
beer, and contribute to their ﬂoral and fruity aroma. However, if they are present in excess (using wild yeast like
yeast such as Hansenula and Pichia), they give an over fruity aroma described as unpleasant for most consumers.
The most important ﬂavour esters present in beer are: ethyl acetate (solvent-like aroma), isoamyl acetate (banana
aroma), 2-phenylethyl acetate (roses and honey aroma), isobutyl acetate (fruity aroma), ethyl hexanoate (sweet
apple aroma) and ethyl octanoate (sour apple aroma) [4, 7, 26, 33, 34, 35]. In most lager beers, only isoamyl
acetate is present above its threshold; however, the presence of different esters close to their threshold produces
a synergistic effect, contributing to the ﬁnal beer ﬂavour. The relationship between fusel alcohols and esters is a
good indicator to evaluate the beer ﬂavour: for lager beers the optimal ratio fusel alcohols/esters is between 4
and 4.7 [27, 31].
The esters present in beer are generated by the esteriﬁcation of alcohol (ethanol or fusel alcohols) and acids at
low pH. These reactions happen inside the cells during the fast yeast growth. According to their production
mechanisms, the esters can be divided in two categories: acetate esters and ethyl esters [4, 7, 26, 33, 34, 35].

3.1.1.1
Acetate Esters
Acetate esters are generated by the esteriﬁcation of acetyl-CoA and an alcohol. This group includes ethyl acetate,
isoamyl acetate, 2-phenylethyl acetate and isobutyl acetate, among others. The most common acetate ester is
ethyl acetate, because its precursor (ethanol) is the alcohol with the highest concentration in beer. The production
of these esters is strongly related to the action of enzymes called alcohol acetyltransferases [7, 26, 33]
3.1.1.2
Ethyl Esters
Ethyl esters are produced by the reaction of ethanol and a medium chain fatty acid (MCFA). This group includes
ethyl hexanoate, ethyl octanoate and ethyl butyrate, among others. In the last part of the yeast exponential growth,
MCFAs are released from the cytoplasmic fatty acid synthase complex, starting the ethyl ester production. The
MCFAs are activated by coenzyme A and they are esteriﬁed with ethanol by yeast enzymes [33].
3.1.2
Fusel alcohols
Beer contains several alcohols apart from ethanol, which are mainly produced during yeast metabolism. These
compounds, called fusel or higher alcohols, are the most abundant organoleptic compounds present in beer and
exhibit an undesired plastic, solvent-like ﬂavour. Moreover, when present in large quantities, they contribute to
the feeling of having a hangover after excessive beer consumption. The main higher alcohols present in beer are
isoamyl alcohol, n-propanol, isobutanol, 2-methyl-1-butanol (amyl alcohol), 2-phenyl ethanol and glycerol. In
general, the concentration of these compounds is about 20-100 mg/l with their thresholds being around 100 mg/l.
Their concentration usually changes in parallel with the ethanol concentration, thus, beers with higher alcohol
content present larger amounts of fusel alcohols [4, 7, 26].
Fusel alcohols can be produced via the catabolic (Ehrlich mechanism) and anabolic pathways. Both pathways
produce oxoacids, that are decarboxylated to form aldehydes, and ﬁnally reduced to the fusel alcohols. The
extent of each mechanism is inﬂuenced by the wort composition, the fermentation stage and the yeast strain
[4, 31].
The Ehrlich pathway can be divided in three steps. First, exogenous amino acids are transported into the cell and
transaminated by oxoglutarate to form their corresponding oxoacids. Second, the oxoacids are decarboxylated to
aldehydes. Finally, the aldehydes are reduced to alcohol by aldehyde reductase activity. Therefore, following
the Ehrlich mechanism, each amino acid will produce speciﬁc alcohols. The most important amino acids
for the production of fusel alcohols in beer are: leucine (producing isoamyl alcohol), isoleucine (producing
amyl alcohol) and valine (producing isobutanol) [4, 7, 28, 31, 35]. A schematic representation of the Ehrlich
mechanism is presented in Figure 6.

Although the Ehrlich mechanism has been more studied and discussed, fusel alcohols are also produced through
the synthetic pathway. The most important anabolic mechanism is the de novo synthesis. The mechanism starts
with the pyruvate intermediate generated during sugar uptake. The isoleucine-leucine-valine (ILV) pathway
synthesizes α-ketoisovalerate from the pyruvate. Then, the α-ketoisovalerate is condensed with acetyl-coA and
water to produce the oxoacid (α-isopropylmalate). The oxoacid is decarboxylated to the aldehyde and then the
aldehyde is reduced to the fusel alcohol (same as the Ehrlich mechanims) [7, 28].
Despite the general negative ﬂavour effect of this group, some phenolic alcohols (e.g. 2-phenyl ethanol) provide
a positive ﬂoral ﬂavour. These phenolic alcohols are mainly derived from the breakdown of malt and hops.
Furthermore, glycerol is another important alcohol that is produced during fermentation by the yeast sugar
metabolism. The amount of glycerol produced from carbohydrates, reduces the ethanol production. The content
of glycerol in beer is 1.2-2 g/l, and it enhances the smoothness and mouthfeel of beer [26].
3.1.3
Acids
Beer is a slightly acidic solution, with a pH between 4 and 5. The acidity of beer is coming from the presence of
organic acids and carbonic acid, creating the “tingle” in beer, responsible for the pleasurable sensation of tartness
[26]. Furthermore, every organic acid has its own characteristic ﬂavour, taste, and aroma. For example, citric
acid has a fresh acidic ﬂavour different from the ﬂavour of malic acid, whereas succinic acid has an unusual
salty, bitter ﬂavour. Organic acids are also important in beer for several other aspects apart from the ﬂavour: they
protect from microbiological attack, prolong shelf life and control the fermentation performance [40, 42].
19
==PAGE:27==

The most abundant compound of this group is acetic acid followed by lactic and succinic acids. However, in
some Belgian acid beers, the amount of lactic and acetic acids can be much higher [26]. Part of the organic
acids are derived from malt and other wort constituents, and the other part is produced during fermentation as
a result of yeast metabolism, coming from acetate, citrate, malate, pyruvate, and succinate (products of sugar
metabolism). Most citrate and malate in beer is derived from malt, whereas acetate, pyruvate, and succinate are
produced during fermentation. Most organic acids appear as a consequence of an incomplete TCA cycle, which
happens in anaerobic conditions. The organic acids precursors (pyruvate, acetate...) are produced from sugar
through the glycolytic pathway and converted into organic acids through the TCA cycle [40, 42].
The aliphatic acids and phenolic acids appear in a lower concentration than the acids discussed above, but they
exhibit important ﬂavour characteristics. The aliphatic acids with short to middle carbon chain length have
distinctive and familiar odors. These acids can be responsible for aromas described as “cheesy” or “goaty”,
but usually they are not at concentrations high enough to negatively affect the beer ﬂavour. The phenolic acids
contribute to astringency which is essential to characteristic beer ﬂavour; and the different chemical structure of
these acids leads to a range of astringency in beer. However, if the concentration of these compounds is too high
the beer taste becomes unpleasant. The phenolic acids present in beer are mainly originating from malt, with
small amounts coming from hops and adjuncts [26].
3.1.4
Acetaldehyde
Aldehydes are the compounds containing the radical -CHO and they are usually produced through the dehydro-
genation of alcohols (oxidation). Several of them are coming from yeast, others are the result of the Strecker
degradation of amino acids during the wort boiling stage and still others appear to be the result of a random
decarboxylation of organic acids [26]. They have unpleasant ﬂavours and aromas described as ”grassy”, ”fruity”,
”green leaves”, and ”cardboard" [31].
Acetaldehyde is the predominant aldehyde compound present in beer, representing approximately 60% of the
total aldehydes. It has a low ﬂavour threshold of 10-25 mg/l and it is responsible for the "green, paint" young
beer ﬂavour [18, 26]. In typical lager beers, ethanol signiﬁcantly increases acetaldehyde retention, leading to
lower perception of the worty character [31].
Acetaldehyde is secreted during the ﬁrst three days of fermentation and is later re-assimilated by the yeast.
It can reach concentrations of about 20-40 mg/l and it decreases to 2-10 mg/l in the ﬁnal product [18, 26].
The acetaldehyde is produced mainly from the partial reduction of acetate resulting from acetyl-CoA. This
acetyl-CoA is found in the fatty acid pathways and the glycolysis pathway (from sugar). However, the presence
of acetaldehyde is substantially higher in the glycolysis pathway and negligible in the fatty acid pathways [4].
After the ﬁrst three days of fermentation, the reduction of acetaldehyde to ethanol is more important than the
acetaldehyde production and the concentration decreases to 2-10 mg/l in the ﬁnal product [26].

3.1.5
Vicinal diketones
Vicinal diketones (VDKs), as aldehydes, belong to the carbonyl compounds, presenting a radical CO in their
structure. VDKs are one of the most undesired ﬂavour compounds present in beer. They are highly aromatic
and their ﬂavour is described as an unpleasant "unclean", "sweetish", ”toffee-like”, and "buttery" ﬂavour. If the
concentration of these compounds is higher, the ﬂavour becomes increasingly ”cheese-like” and sharp. The
two main VDKs present in beer are diacetyl and 2,3-pentadione. The organoleptic threshold of diacetyl is very
low (0.07-0.15 mg/l), and therefore it is crucial to keep its concentration low in the ﬁnal beer. The threshold of
2,3-pentadione is higher (1 mg/l) but it is usually considered together with diacetyl (VDKs) due to the similar
ﬂavour effect that they produce [4, 26, 31].
VDKs exhibit similar concentration dynamics to acetaldehyde, which is produced early in fermentation and
later re-assimilated. A schematic representation of the production and re-assimilation of diacetyl is presented in
Figure 7.
Figure 7: Schematic representation of diacetyl formation, re-assimilation and removal [8].
VDKs are produced as byproducts of the yeast metabolism during fermentation. Diacetyl is derived from
pyruvate through the intermediate α-acetolactate, a precursor of valine biosynthesis. α-acetolactate is leaked into
wort, where it is decarboxilated to form diacetyl (slow oxidative reaction - rate limiting step). 2,3-pentadione
follows a similar pathway with α-acetohydroxybutyrate as intermediate [8, 26, 31, 43].

Finally, when the yeast growth is stabilized, the breakdown of these VDKs happens. The diacetyl is reduced
by yeast enzymes to acetoin and 2,3-butanediol. The latter two compounds have higher ﬂavour threshold and
do not impact the beer ﬂavour. These reduction reactions are produced by the yeast enzymes inside the cell,
therefore, the yeast strain (gene expression modiﬁes the enzymes present) and the quantity of suspended cells
are important for the diacetyl reduction [8, 26, 31, 43].
The current fermentation procedures aim to reduce as much as possible the vessel residence times to optimize
production. Nevertheless, it is crucial to ensure that the ﬂavour compounds balance is appropriate at the end of
the fermentation. Therefore, the concentration of VDKs is considered as an essential criterion to decide when
to stop the fermentation process, in order to ensure that the total VDKs content is low enough (avoiding the
undesired ﬂavour effect) [8, 26, 31, 43].
3.1.6
Sulﬁdic compounds
There are many sulfur compounds present in beer, with different ﬂavour contributions. The most important
compounds are: hydrogen sulﬁde (rotten egg ﬂavour), sulfur dioxide (pungent, burnt ﬂavour) and dimethyl
sulﬁde (sweetcorn, cooked cabbage ﬂavour). These compounds may arise from malt and hops, or during the
fermentation process. If there is a microorganism (different from yeast) infection during the brewing process,
high concentrations of sulfur compounds can be generated [26, 32].
Hydrogen sulﬁde has a very low threshold (0.004 mg/l) and it is formed during the fermentation process, but
most of it is stripped off by the carbon dioxide evolution. If the intensity of the fermentation (yeast growth rate)
is not high enough the H2S may not be purged totally. Some studies found that the concentration of hydrogen
sulﬁde is strongly related to the concentration of some amino acids in the wort: cysteine increases the H2S
concentration while methionine inhibits the cysteine-induced increase [26, 32].
Sulfur dioxide may be present in malt and hops, but most of it disappears during the wort boiling stage due to its
volatility; therefore, the SO2 present in the ﬁnal beer is mainly formed during yeast fermentation. SO2 provides
an undesired burnt ﬂavour to beer, but it is usually kept well under its threshold value. The sugar concentration,
the beer type (lager or ale) and the yeast strain are the main factors inﬂuencing the sulfur dioxide presence in
beer. Usually, ale beers contain lower amounts of SO2 than lagers [26, 32].
Finally, dimethyl sulﬁde (DMS) is an essential component of lager beers if present at moderate concentrations
(30-100 µg/l), while its concentration is lower in ale beers. Higher concentrations of DMS, produce the
unpleasant sweetcorn ﬂavour (also in lager beers). The low concentration of DMS in ales could be explained
by the high temperatures of the ale beer processing that partly destroys SMM (S-methylmethionine), the DMS
precursor. There are two different mechanisms producing the DMS in beer. First, the thermal degradation of
SMM (S-methylmethionine) during the wort production. Second, the reduction of DMSO (dimethyl sulfoxide)
during yeast fermentation. Some of the DMS produced during the wort production is lost due to its volatility.

3.1.7
Ethanol
Ethanol is the most important alcohol in beer, with a concentration usually between 30 to 50 g/l. Nevertheless,
there are many varieties of beer containing less (free alcohol and light beers) or more (strong beers). Ethanol
increases the sweetness of beer and enhances the ﬂavour; it also produces a warming sensation in the mouth that
continues in the throat and stomach after swallowing. The ethanol content is generally expressed as alcohol by
volume (ABV). Ethanol has a lower speciﬁc gravity than beer and therefore the concentration in volume% is
around 20% higher than the weight% [26].

3.2.1
Research groups
In the present work, almost 30 publications have been included in the literature review. However, most of
these references are extensions of previous work by either including new model compounds (using the same
experimental conditions) or by adding an optimization study. Usually, the authors of these new publications
belong to the same research group as the original publication. Consequently, all the existing works can be
separated into the different research groups where they were developed in. There are only 8 research groups,
which are here named after the ﬁrst author of the respective publications: Gee et al. (1988) [3], Garcia et al.
(1994) [46], Enders et al. (1995) [47], Andrés-Toro et al. (1997) [50], Titica et al. (2000) [34], Hepworth (2003)
[56], Riverol et al. (2007) [57] and Vassiliev et al. (2013) [17]. Despite the relatively high number of studies
involving beer fermentation, the variety of different models available in literature is low.
3.2.2
Scale of the experiments: laboratory / pilot scale / industrial scale
The kinetic models available for the beer fermentation process are mostly determined with experiments per-
formed at laboratory or pilot scale, with a maximum reactor size of 150 liters. There are two reasons why
these experiments are not held at industrial scale: the difﬁculties to obtain off-line measurements (medium
concentrations) and to change process conditions in the industrial fermentation tank. Nevertheless, some of the
studies (e.g. [35, 38, 46]) check the applicability of their obtained models at industrial scale, stating that results
are quite similar.
3.2.3
Lager beer models
Lager accounts for more than 90% of the global beer production and therefore the interest to improve its
fermentation process is more signiﬁcant. Thus, most of the experiments are held with lager yeast strains. The
models developed for lager beer are hardly applicable to an ale beer due to the numerous differences between
ale and lager, such as: (i) ale is top fermenting and lager is bottom fermenting; (ii) different yeast strains used
(ale uses S. cerevisiae and Lager uses S. pastorianus); (iii) ale ferments around 16-25 ºC and lager ferments
around 8-15 ºC; (iv) lager yeast strain has an extra ATF1 gene that enhances acetate ester production; (v) the
DMS concentration is higher in lager beers; and (vi) lager can ferment melobiose [7, 20, 26, 27].
The only publications which include ale yeast strains are the ones from Vassilev et al. [17, 60]. The goal of this
particular study was to compare the model parameters obtained with ale/lager strains and free/immobilized cells,
and not to create an accurate temperature dependent model for the beer fermentation process (temperature is
constant at 15ºC for all the experiments).

3.2.4
Process control
Some of the publications [34, 45, 48, 47, 56, 57] are focused on the temperature control through fuzzy modelling.
They develop a model to control the fermentation progression using the CO2 evolution (the unique available
online measurement). The model is used to control the cooling requirements of the reactor according to CO2 data.
The implementation of this on-line closed loop process control allows the correction of variations or disturbances
in the fermentation process. These control models are developed using neural networks and fuzzy modelling.
The temperature control during fermentation is not the aim of the current master project; the objective of this
work is to optimize the operation conditions. However, as a future perspective, the approach for process control
developed by these authors could be a way to implement the optimal operation conditions obtained in this work.
3.2.5
Immobilized cell technologies
The increasing competitiveness of the beer market encourages the investigation of different process improvements.
Fermentation is the longest process in brewing; therefore, time savings in this part of the process can signiﬁcantly
increase proﬁtability. The use of immobilized cell technologies (ICT) is a promising alternative to reduce the
fermentation time: from 5-10 days in normal fermentation to 2-3 days using ICT. The main challenge for ICT
is to reproduce the traditional beer ﬂavour; the mass transfer limitations in ICT modify the yeast metabolism,
leading to a different chemical species balance in the ﬁnal product [17].
Some of the publications [17, 60] are focused on the study of the ICT inﬂuence using mathematical models.
However, the implementation of ICT in the brewing process is not the aim of the current project. The beer
obtained using this technology is still far from an optimal beer ﬂavour, and the optimization of the beer ﬂavour is
one of the main objectives of the current project.

3.4
Beer fermentation optimization
Some of the previously cited studies also perform model optimization [1, 5, 19, 52, 53]. In all the optimization
approaches, the temperature proﬁle and the fermentation duration (time) are the variables that are changed in order
to optimize the process. Regarding the objective function, all studies aim at minimizing time and maximizing
ethanol production. Some studies ([5, 19]) include the ﬂavour compounds in the optimization function while
others ([1, 16, 53]) just include the concentration of the undesired ﬂavour compounds as constraints (their values
must be kept below threshold).
Among the existing publications, three different methods have been used to obtain the optimal temperature
proﬁle: sequential quadratic programming (SQP), genetic algorithms (GA) and simulated annealing.
3.4.1
Sequential quadratic programming (SQP)
Sequential quadratic programming is an iterative method used for nonlinear optimization. The main drawback of
the method is that it sometimes converges to a local minimum rather than the global one. Therefore, a good
initial guess (close to the global optimum) is required in order to ensure the best possible solution is found. This
method has been used by different authors using initial temperature proﬁles known to exhibit good performance
[5, 16, 19].
In order to apply the SQP method to the fermentation models, it is required to convert the dynamic model
into a discrete one by using the collocation method. Then, the nonlinear objective function is expanded in a
Taylor Series to get an approximate quadratic objective function and the nonlinear constraints are linearized.
Subsequently, the optimization problem is ready to be solved using a standard linear programming program.
The results obtained are used as the starting point for the next approximate quadratic objective function and
linearized constraints (iterative method) [5, 19].
3.4.2
Genetic algorithms (GA)
Compared to other methods, genetic algorithms start from a population of solutions, and only the best solutions
survive to generate a new population of solutions. This method has been used in several studies (such as
[2, 50, 52, 53]). In these works, the total time is divided into equal time intervals, and the temperature can take
different values for each of these intervals. The main drawback of the method is that it does not obtain the global
optimum, just a solution that is close to the global optimum.
3.4.3
Simulated annealing (SA)
The simulated annealing (SA) algorithm is a useful technique to approximate the global optimum of a system,
and it requires less computational time than other exhaustive techniques. It does not search for the global
30
==PAGE:38==

optimum, but it is useful to obtain a good approximation. This method has been used by Rodman [1, 61] in order
to obtain promising temperature manipulation proﬁles for batch time reduction.
While GA is a population based algorithm, SA is single solution based algorithm. The algorithm generates a
new candidate solution from the current solution. In the early stages of the process, the current solution may
be replaced by a worse solution, avoiding being trapped in a local optimum. As the algorithm progresses, the
probability of it replacing the current solution by a worse solution is reduced [1].

Model selection
After thorough literature review on the existing models, it was observed that there are only 3 research groups
that focus on the development of a temperature-dependent model for beer fermentation.These are represented
by the following authors: Gee [3, 4, 5], Garcia [35, 38, 46] and Andrés-Toro [50]. The remaining publications

• More comprehensive list of compounds included in the model;and,
• the concentrations obtained match the literature research on ﬂavour compounds (for example, the con-

beer).
4.2
Model description
The model selected is developed by Gee and Ramirez in three different publications [3, 4, 5]
• Gee et al. (1988) [3]. A model including sugar uptake, yeast growth and ethanol production is presented.
The model is developed at four different temperatures (4, 8, 12 and 16ºC). The temperature variation of
the kinetic rate constants is adjusted using the Arrhenius equation.
• Gee et al. (1994) [4]. In this work, the model developed includes the kinetic rates for the production of
the following ﬂavour species: esters, alcohols, vicinal diketones and acetaldehyde. It also includes the
consumption rate of three amino acids. The model is developed at three different temperatures: 10.5, 12
and 14.5ºC. In this study, the kinetic rates are presented separately for each temperature.
• Ramirez et al. (2007) [5]. In this last publication, the model from Gee et al. (1994) [4] is used for
optimization purposes. All the yield parameters are averaged over the three temperatures, but explicit
temperature effects are included for the kinetic rate constants. By using Arrhenius equations, frequency
factors and activation energies are obtained through linear regression.
In the current work, the ﬁnal model presented in Ramirez et al. (2007) [5] will be used due to the fact that it
is temperature dependent and it includes the production of the ﬂavour compounds. All the model equations

4.2.3
Amino acids consumption
The formation of many ﬂavour compounds in beer is strongly related to the uptake of some amino acids.
Speciﬁcally the amino acids leucine, isoleucine and valine are tied to the formation of some fusel alcohols. The

4.2.8
Yield coefﬁcients: temperature dependence
In the model by Ramirez et al. (2007) [5], only the growth and uptake rates are presented as temperature
dependent. The 12 yield parameters that relate the yeast growth and nutrients uptake to the production of ﬂavour
compounds are assumed to be constant. These yields are obtained taking the average of the three values given
in Gee 1994 et al. (1994) [4]. Nevertheless, the divergence between the values at different temperatures are
important as seen in Section 5.1.2. Thus, taking the average of the three temperature values does not seem to be
the best approach.
These yield coefﬁcients relate the yeast growth rate or the uptake of sugars/amino acids to the production of
the ﬂavour compounds. Instead of taking the average of these yields, creating a 2nd order polynomial equation
is a promising solution. The solution here proposed implies that the growth rate and substrate uptake rates
change with temperature, as well as the yield for conversion of substrate to byproducts. This yield is affected

by temperature due to the fact that the enzymatic activity involved in the production mechanisms changes with
temperature. Therefore, not only the kinetic rates should include temperature dependence, but also the yield
coefﬁcients. Two examples of enzymes involved in the fermentation affected by temperature are presented as
follows.
• High fermentation temperatures increase the expression of the gene BAP2. This gene is responsible for
encoding a permease that promotes the transport of amino acids into the yeast cell. Consequently, at high
temperatures, the amount of amino acids inside the cell will be higher, favouring the alcohols production
through the Ehrlich mechanism [7].
• AATases are enzymes responsible for the the synthesis of acetate esters. These enzymes are more expressed
at higher temperatures. Thus, the acetate esters production is temperature dependent (increasing with
temperature) [7].
The 2nd order polynomial adjustment is chosen to be a good option to include these temperature effects. The
adjustment for all the yield coefﬁcients is obtained by using the polynomial regression function in Excel. The
polynomial values are presented in Appendix E, all the function adjustment plots are given in Section 5.1.2 and

4.4
Model scope
4.4.1
Fermentation parameters
Several different model parameters modify the yeast metabolism and thus the ﬁnal beer proﬁle. Among these
parameters, the temperature and time were chosen as parameters to be modiﬁed so as to optimize the fermentation.
These parameters are selected for being easily modiﬁable in an existing fermentation process and also because
the used model can distinguish changes in these two parameters.
• Temperature. The reactor temperature has a great inﬂuence on the resulting beer. As explained in Section
4.2.8, not only the growth rate and reaction rates change with temperature, but also the yield coefﬁcients.
The used model is designed to include all these temperature effects.
• Time. As explained in Section 2.2, the minimization of the fermentation time is one of the goals of the
brewing industry in order to increase proﬁtability. However, a minimum time is required to consume
all the sugars and produce enough ethanol. Moreover, the re-assimilation of VDKs requires a minimum
amount of time [43]. The present model is able to assess the trade-off between time-minimization and the
ﬁnal states of the fermentation process as predicted as predicted by the model.
Nevertheless, other parameters that will be constant in the current study also have an inﬂuence on the ﬁnal beer
product. These other parameters are out of the scope of the current study due to the following reasons.
• Reactor size / scale-up. The data for the current model is obtained with a pilot scale experimental design:
100-L non-agitated conical reactors. However, the aim of the study is to apply the model in an actual
industrial reactor.
On the one hand, increasing the reactor size may change the beer production making the model useless.
High fermenters generate a great hydrostatic pressure that increases the quantity of CO2 dissolved in beer.
The excess of dissolved CO2 inhibits yeast growth by unbalancing decarboxylation reactions, reducing the
ester production [7].
40
==PAGE:48==
Modelling and optimization of beer fermentation towards optimal ﬂavour proﬁle
Alex Matesanz Salvado
On the other hand, several experimental studies compared different reactor sizes in their experiments,
claiming that the results are similar. In the publications from Garcia et al. [46, 35, 38], models are
tested (developed with a 5 L reactor) in a 2 × 105 L industrial bioreactor, obtaining quite similar results.
Andrés-Toro [50] tests a model obtained from lab experiments (3 L) in a 100 L pilot plant reactor, also
obtaining a very good ﬁt.
Finally, the concentrations commonly obtained with the model are mostly within the ranges presented
in Figures 1,2,3,4 as will be discussed in Section 5.5. This suggests that the model may be applied to
estimate the progress of an industrial scale fermentation process.
• Yeast strain. As explained in Section 3.2.3, changes in the yeast strain lead to a completely different
fermentation process. Thus, a change in the yeast strain will require that the model parameters are
determined again, due to the fact that all the reaction rates are affected by the yeast type.
• Wort composition. The fermenting wort is the medium from which yeast absorbs all the nutrients, leading
to the different metabolic byproducts. Hence, the initial wort composition will also have a great inﬂuence
on the ﬁnal beer ﬂavour [7].
The present model is able to evaluate the effects of the wort composition, but it will not be included in the
optimization problem. The goal of the present study is to optimize the operating conditions with given
initial variables. Including the wort composition in the optimization problem may lead to an optimal
initial wort composition which is infeasible: a composition that requires unavailable raw materials or vast
changes in the upstream processing. Therefore, the initial wort composition is considered as constant
in this study and only temperature, time and pitching rate will be included in the optimization problem.
Notwithstanding, changes in the wort composition will be evaluated by performing a sensitivity analysis
on the model (please see Section 5.4).
• Pitching rate. Yeast is responsible for all the reactions that happen during the fermentation; it is then
clear that the amount of yeast introduced in the reactor will affect the fermentation process. Thus, the
model is able to evaluate changes in the pitching rate.

as explained in Section 3.1, there are other chemical species which are important for the ﬁnal beer ﬂavour. The
main ﬂavour groups missing in the current model are presented below, discussing the interest of their future
incorporation in the model.
• Phenolic alcohols: The model includes 4 different fusel alcohols derived mainly from the Ehrlich
mechanism (isobutanol, isoamyl alcohol, 2-methyl-1-butanol and n-propanol). Nevertheless, it does not

4.5
OAT Sensitivity Analysis
A sensitivity analysis on the model parameters and initial variables has been performed. The sensitivity analysis
on the model parameters will determine what could be the effects of an inaccurate parameter estimation
(uncertainty). Furthermore, by performing a sensitivity analysis on the initial variables one can test the effects of
different upstream processing conditions as well as different raw materials.
One of the simplest and most common approaches to perform sensitivity analysis is used: one-factor-at-a-time
(OAT). The OAT procedure changes only one input variable at a time, keeping the remaining parameters at their
baseline [62, 63].
One of the beneﬁts of the OAT approach is that any change observed in the output will unambiguously be due to
the input changed. Moreover, all the effects are computed with reference to the same normal values, which eases
the comparison of the results and minimizes the chances of program crashes [62]. Nevertheless, OAT does not
take into account the simultaneous variation of input variables, and is therefore unable to detect the presence of
interactions and correlation between inputs [63].
The sensitivity analysis is applied on the model presented in Section 4.2 at a ﬁxed temperature; arbitrarily, the
temperature of 10.5ºC is selected. Therefore, the parameters used have been directly taken from Gee et al. (1994)
[4], and no temperature dependence is taken into account for any of the parameters. The fermentation time is
also ﬁxed at 140 hours, which is the time used in the results presented in Gee et al. (1994) [4].
The uncertainty range is ﬁxed at 25% for all the model parameters and initial variables. This value has been
selected arbitrarily in order to enable the comparison of the effects among all the inputs. If the model is designed
properly, the uncertainty for the model parameters must be lower than this 25 %; otherwise, the conﬁdence range
for the obtained results will be very low. With the ﬁxed uncertainty, the maximum variation for all the outputs
(ﬁnal concentration) is computed; all these variations are collected and presented in Figures 28-36 in Section 5.4.
For all the species being consumed during the fermentation, the ﬁnal concentration is not a good output to
consider as a basis for comparison. Most of these compounds are completely consumed after the fermentation
and their ﬁnal concentrations are approximately zero. Therefore, no effect on these outputs can be observed if we
consider the ﬁnal concentrations. However, the consumption rate of these species can be related to the production

of many ﬂavour compounds. Thus, in order to evaluate how fast these species are consumed, the time required
to consume 50% of the initial concentration will be the output for the sugars and amino acids. The concentration
at 50% is selected once lower consumption percentages do not allow for a good comparison (shorter times
required). For higher consumption percentages than 50%, some species do not reach the consumption percentage

4.6
Temperature and time optimization towards optimal ﬂavour proﬁle
As explained in Section 4.2, the yield coefﬁcients and the sugar uptake parameters are different from the ones
used in Ramirez et al. (2007) [5]. Hence, no promising temperature proﬁles are available for the current
optimization problem. In the absence of a good initial temperature proﬁle, the use of SQP as optimization
procedure will probably lead to a local optimum. Consequently, an optimization method able to avoid local
minimums is required. Both SA and GA are capable of avoiding local optimums; however, GA is selected above
SA because it is a population based algorithm, producing a better exploration of the search space. Also, GA
demonstrated better results than SA in other types of problems [64].
4.6.1
Genetic algorithms (GA)
Genetic algorithms are optimization methods inspired by natural evolution. A genetic algorithm is based on
a population of individuals (possible problem solutions). The characteristics of each individual are expressed
using a chromosome, which represents the variables to optimize. A function known as the ﬁtness function or
optimization function is used to reward those individuals whose behaviour corresponds to better solutions in
the problem domain, by allowing those individuals to generate the next generation. Mutation and crossover
operators are used to create further generations from the best individuals [65, 10]. The basic concepts of the
Genetic Algorithm optimization are presented in Figure 13.

The GA program runs until the stopping criterion is met. Therefore, the solution obtained is not the global
optimum, it is just a solution that satisﬁes one of the criteria to stop the algorithm. If the genetic algorithm
parameters are set correctly, the solution obtained from the optimization should be very close to the actual global
optimum.
Figure 14 shows the different steps taken by genetic algorithms.
Figure 14: Schematic diagram of the GA procedure.
The explanation of each of the steps presented (Figure 14) is described below.
• Initialize Population. A random initial population of individuals is generated. These individuals are
potential problem solutions (candidate temperature proﬁles).
• Fitness Evaluation. The ﬁtness function is evaluated for all the individuals of the population, and they are
ranked according to their ﬁtness value.
45
==PAGE:53==
Modelling and optimization of beer fermentation towards optimal ﬂavour proﬁle
Alex Matesanz Salvado
• Stopping Criteria. The best value of the ﬁtness function is compared with the stopping criteria to determine
if a new generation (iteration) is required. There are different options to stop the algorithm and obtain a
solution. However, achieving only one of the stopping criteria is required to ﬁnish the optimization. Some
common stopping criteria are presented as follows.
– Time limit: The algorithm stops after running for an amount of time in seconds equal to the time
limit.
– Stall generations: The algorithm stops when the average relative change in the ﬁtness function value
over Stall generations is less than the Function tolerance.
– Stall time limit: The algorithm stops if there is no improvement in the objective function during an
interval of time in seconds equal to the Stall time limit.
– Function tolerance: The algorithm runs until the average relative change in the ﬁtness function value
over Stall generations is less than the Function tolerance.
• Reproduction: If the stopping criteria are not met, a new generation of individuals (children) is generated
using the individuals from the current generation (parents).
– Elite children: A small fraction of the best parents survive and become children (5% of the population
for the MATLAB ga function used). That ensures that the best ﬁtness value of the next generation
can only improve.
– Crossover: Apart from the elite children, the other part of the new generation is created using
crossover and mutation operators. In the MATLAB ga function, 80% of the remaining individuals
are generated through crossover and 20% through mutation. The parents are selected for the crossover
according to their ﬁtness values. Thus, parents with better ﬁtness values have more chances to be
chosen. The crossover operator creates a random binary vector and selects the genes where the
vector is a 1 from the ﬁrst parent, and the genes where the vector is a 0 from the second parent, and
combines the genes to form the child.
– Mutation: The mutation operator makes small random changes in the new population to create new
children (the fraction that is not generated using crossover). Mutation provides diversity and enables
the genetic algorithm to search in a broader space, reducing the chances of being stuck in a local
minimum zone.

Conclusions
This project aimed at determining how the industrial operation of the beer production can be optimized through
mathematical modelling and optimization methods. First, an extensive literature review of the principal beer
by-products and the existing beer fermentation models was conducted. Subsequently, a detailed fermentation
model has been implemented in MATLAB. This fermentation model has been carefully examined and improved,
as well as tested through a sensitivity analysis. Finally, an optimization has been performed in order to obtain an
optimal temperature proﬁle. To this end, the genetic algorithm method was selected, once it was concluded that
it is the most appropriate for this particular case. After analysing the results of the above-mentioned steps, the
following conclusions were gathered.
Speciﬁcity of the models. The easiest and simplest way to target speciﬁc ﬂavour proﬁles should come from the
wort and yeast selection. This is due to the fact that the initial variables are crucial to determine the ﬁnal beer
ﬂavour. This hypothesis is supported by the results obtained in the sensitivity analysis, where the initial variables
of the fermentation process are very inﬂuential in the ﬁnal species concentrations. Also, the study about the
production of sulﬁdic compounds, demonstrates that the production of these compounds is also highly related to
the wort composition. Therefore, the beer fermentation models should be designed speciﬁcally for each beer
type.
Model section. The model from Ramirez et al. (2007) [5] is used in this work to model the fermentation with
the following modiﬁcations: (i) temperature dependence is included for the yield coefﬁcients; and, (ii) the
sugar parameters (µG, µM, KM, KG and K’G) are taken from a recent publication (Rodman et al. (2020) [6]).
These changes in the model parameters aim to improve the accuracy of the model when subject to temperature
variations. Nevertheless, the model parameters and the experimental design exhibit some important challenges
which are listed and discussed in the following points.
• Arrhenius adjustment. As explained in Section 5.1.1, the Arrhenius equation does not ﬁt properly the
three parameter values for KL, KAAI and KVDK. It is assumed that these problem could arise from any
of the three isothermal experiments, and it was decided to keep using them in the absence of more data.
Either way, this problem suggests that the precision of the model to evaluate temperature changes (at least
for leucine, acetaldehyde and VDK) is not very high.
• Parameter co-dependence. A recent study carried out by Rodman et al. (2020) [6] claims that the model
exhibits correlation among some parameters. That is the reason why a new parameter estimation from
Rodman et al. (2020) [6] is taken as a starting point for this work. Likewise, the sensitivity analysis shows
that most of the parameters have nearly no inﬂuence in the models.
• Yield coefﬁcients. The yield parameters that relate the yeast growth and nutrients uptake to the production of
ﬂavour compounds are assumed to be constant over temperature in Ramirez et al. (2007) [5]. Nevertheless,

the divergence between the values at different temperatures are important; thus, instead of taking the
average of these yields, a 2nd order polynomial equation was used as a promising solution.
• Isothermal experiments. The model parameters are estimated by doing isothermal experiments. Nonethe-
less, the model is expected to distinguish between non constant temperature proﬁles and the yeast behaviour
may change when subjected to temperature variations.
These challenges cannot be solved without conducting a new experimental campaign due to the fact that data is
simply not available in order to make any further conclusions. Unfortunately, experimental work is out of the
scope of this project.
Optimization study: Despite the challenges encountered in the model, an optimization study was conducted
that aimed at obtaining an optimal ﬂavour proﬁle and within the minimum fermentation time possible. The
results obtained from the optimization study show that low temperatures keep the production of the undesired
compounds low (O4) and high temperatures are required to maximize the ethanol production (O2). When all the
species are considered in the same optimization process (O1), the temperature is minimum (283.65 K) from 15 to
60 hours and maximum (287.65 K) for the rest of the fermentation, with an optimal fermentation time of 139.97
hours. Due to the model challenges presented before, it is audacious to claim that the exact optimal proﬁle
obtained with the optimization O1 is the best temperature proﬁle for the studied beer fermentation. However, the
author is conﬁdent to afﬁrm that the temperature must be kept low during the ﬁrst days of the fermentation to
avoid over-production of off-ﬂavour species, and afterwards it must be increased to enhance the ethanol and
esters production in a minimal time.
The optimization problem was solved by using Genetic Algorithms (GA). I am convinced that this method is
very effective to ﬁnd a solution for these type of problems. Different runs of the algorithm converge to very
similar solutions. Furthermore, GA is able to obtain a solution with low computing times (less than 1 hour for
the stopping criteria used).

Future perspectives
Different alternatives aiming to extend and improve the current project are presented and discussed below.
• Immobilized cell technologies. As explained in Section 3.2.5, the use of immobilized cell technologies
(ICT) is a promising alternative to reduce signiﬁcantly the fermentation time. Up to now, ICT has not
been able to signiﬁcantly contribute to the development of an optimal ﬂavour balance. This is due to the
fact that there are mass transfer limitations that modify the yeast metabolism [17, 60]. However, further
works with this technology may succeed to obtain the desired species balance and reduce drastically the
fermentation times, increasing the proﬁt of the breweries.
• New model approaches. The model used in the current work was developed with isothermal experiments.
Notwithstanding, the model is used to distinguish between temperature proﬁles where the temperature
is not constant. Consequently, testing the applicability of the models with non-isothermal temperature
proﬁles could be interesting to ensure the efﬁcacy of the model. Additionally, another alternative could
be the use of neural networks (machine learning technique) as a replacement for the mechanistic model,
which has been used in this work. Some ﬁrst steps on the implementation of these tools have already been
suggested in publications such as (i.e. Enders et al. (1995) [47]).
Moreover, maybe by implementing different approaches in order to explore ICT and process control to
achieve optimal beer ﬂavor, one can explore methods such as: (i) neural networks (machine learning
technique); and, (ii) simpler model equation designs.
• Optimize energy requirements. As previously demonstrated, the current study focused on the optimiza-
tion of the temperature proﬁle and fermentation time regardless of the cooling requirements to achieve
it. One of the goals of the optimization was to minimize the fermentation time so as to increase the
proﬁt. Nevertheless, if the cooling requirements increase, the associated costs also increase. Therefore,
by including the minimization of the energy requirements in the objective function would allow for the
evaluation of the real economic ramiﬁcations of all candidate solutions.
• Control loop. The complex behaviour of the microorganisms (yeast) and the variability in the raw
materials, may lead to important changes in the fermentation process. Therefore, the results obtained with
a theoretical optimal temperature proﬁle might not always be the best. Therefore, in the future it would be
interesting and valuable to include the cooling requirements in the objective function. It could be linked to
the fermentation progress (measured with the CO2 evolution) using a closed loop control system. In this
way, the temperature proﬁle would be speciﬁcally adapted for each fermentation process.
In summary, by implementing different approaches in order to explore ICT and process control so as to achieve
optimal beer ﬂavor, one can explore methods such as: (i) neural networks (machine learning technique); and, (ii)
simpler model equation designs.
