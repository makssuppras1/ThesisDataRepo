Abstract
The world is facing a severe challenge of preventing climate change caused by global
warming. Climate change can to some extent be mitigated by directly targeting anthro-
pogenic CO2 emissions. A way to do this, could be to directly capture the CO2 from
heavy emitting processes in industries, by using carbon capture technologies. One nec-
essary process step in the carbon capture value chain is conditioning the captured CO2
to the requirements of transport and end-purpose. This process step was of focus in this
thesis.
Exergy and exergoeconomic analyses were made of a system that can deliver liquefied
and purified captured CO2 at a required end-pressure from a given inlet state. The cap-
tured CO2 was compressed to elevated pressure, and water impurities were removed
before the liquefaction part of the process. The system used an external refrigeration
cycle with CO2 as working fluid for liquefying the captured CO2. Optimization of the
design and operational parameters of the system was performed using an optimization
algorithm, with the objective to minimize the levelized cost of liquefied and purified CO2
over the lifetime of the plant. The exergoeconomic analysis and optimization were used
as a basis for suggesting three alternative system layouts. Similar optimizations were per-
formed for the alternative systems, and the results were compared to that of the initial
layout at different inlet and outlet conditions.
The exergy and exergoeconomic analyses indicated that the purification part of the pro-
cess was primarily dominated by thermodynamic inefficiencies through exergy losses,
while most of the exergy destruction occurred in the liquefaction part of the process.
In general, the compressors were the greatest source of costs. Therefore, the optimiza-
tion showed, that pressures throughout the system should be chosen in a way, that en-
sured optimal operation of the compressors. Through optimization of design and oper-
ational parameters, a levelized cost of liquefaction and purification of 138 DKK/t CO2
was achieved. It was found that this could be reduced by 10 % by integrating district
heat production in coolers.
It was seen that the costs were increased if the inlet pressure of captured CO2 was de-
creased, while a greater inlet pressure reduced costs. The required outlet pressure was
also found to affect the costs of the system, in a way, that a reduced pressure increased
costs. It was found that an alternative system layout with different compressor steps than
that of the initial system, would become more cost-effective at a reduced outlet pressure.
Furthermore, it was seen that optimization of the pressures throughout the captured CO2
stream was of most importance when the inlet pressure was changed, while all pressures
in the system should be optimized for a change in the outlet pressure.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation and Background
Our civilization is facing one of the largest challenges in human history, namely the com-
bat against climate change. Climate change covers a vast variety of irreversible effects
on nature, weather, and biological species, which ultimately affects societies all over the
planet [1]. Many of the known climate changes are caused by global warming, which is
expressed by an increase in the average temperature of the surface of Earth. The most
severe reason for global warming is the release of greenhouse gases (GHGs) into the at-
mosphere caused by human activities [1].
In 2016, anthropogenic CO2 accounted for 75 % of the total GHG emissions in the world
[2]. Since the industrial revolution, our civilization has substantially increased the use of
fossil fuels for industrial purposes and transport, resulting in a vast amount of CO2 emis-
sions [1]. This has increased the global average content in the atmosphere to more than
410 ppm in 2021, compared to a pre-industrial level of around 280 ppm [3]. Increased
content of CO2 in the atmosphere causes a long chain of environmental feedback effects
which all enhances global warming. A way to mitigate climate change is therefore by
directly targeting anthropogenic CO2 emissions.
The impact of CO2 emissions from human activities can conceptually be seen as a func-
tion of population size, affluence, and technology (cf. the IPAT equation1) [4]. In 2020 the
world population was 7.8 billion people and it is expected to increase to 9.7 billion by
2050. At the same time, the world economy is expected to more than double, indicating
an improved standard of living for many citizens [5]. This means (according to the con-
cept of the IPAT equation), that to reduce the environmental impact (i.e. anthropogenic
CO2 emissions) despite growth in population and wealth, the impact intensity of tech-
nology must be improved. This must be done without reducing the ability to meet the
needs of the future world population.
As seen in Fig. 1.1, the sectors with largest CO2 emissions on a global level are Elec-
tricity & Heat, Transport, and Manufacturing & Construction, contributing approx. 41 %,
21 %, and 17 % as of 2016, respectively [2]. Therefore, these sectors hold a large poten-
tial for reducing the anthropogenic CO2 emissions. The emissions are primarily caused
by the combustion of fossil fuels. These could to a large extent be mitigated by replacing
fossil-based energy with renewable sources, increasing energy efficiency, and introducing
electrification. Additionally, some industrial processes such as the production of cement,
iron, steel, and various chemicals have CO2 emissions as a direct by-product. These

emissions are difficult to mitigate, as they show technological and economic challenges
[6]. An alternative solution to reduce emissions from these processes is directly capturing
the CO2, using Carbon Capture (CC) technologies.
The CC technology is expected to account for 14 % of the emission reductions towards
2050 within the energy and industrial sectors [6]. The technology is of severe interest as
it allows for the following [5]:
• Continuous utilization of existing facilities (e.g. fossil-based power plants) and
• deep emission reductions in heavy industries which are otherwise not possible,
even with technological advances (e.g. production of cement, iron, steel, and chem-
icals).
In 2050, a total capture capacity of approx. 7.6 Gt CO2 per year is expected, with the
energy and industrial sectors accounting for a total of 60 % [5].
The technology is still on the development stage and is not mature for the expected large-
scale deployment. In 2021, the worldwide installed CC capacity in the energy and in-
dustrial sectors was just above 40 Mt CO2 per year, corresponding to only 0.5 % of the
expected capacity in 2050 [7]. This means that technology must be improved and costs
must be reduced at a fast rate for all processes in the CC chain to scale up capacity [5].
Most literature and research focus on the capture unit and the final purpose [8, 9] while
little literature is available on the connecting process in between [10, 11], that being the
conditioning of CO2 to correct pressure and purity. The pressurization and purification
process should however not be overlooked during the development of CC technologies.
When considering the chain, excluding the capture unit, it can account for up to 77 % of
the energy consumption and 43 % to 58 % of costs [8, 12].

the CC value chain. These were included to paint a complete picture of the boundary
conditions for the liquefaction and purification process, and thereby identify the require-
ments for this process step. Different possibilities for liquefaction of CO2 are presented
together with an overview of available literature on such systems. The structure of the

1.2.1
The Carbon Capture Value Chain
The CC technology consists of the process steps illustrated in Fig. 1.2. A source of CO2
is needed, from which it is separated from the remaining components using various
technologies depending on the source type and concentration of CO2. From the sepa-
ration process, a stream of concentrated CO2 leaves, which is pressurized and purified
by compression and/or liquefaction. This process step concerns the boundary condi-
tions, namely the state of the entering concentrated CO2 stream and the requirements for
transportation and end-purpose. The pressurization and purification process is of focus
in this study (marked with a green box in Fig. 1.2). Pressurized and pure CO2 is then
transported by an appropriate method to the end-purpose, which can either be storage
(referred to as Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)) or utilization (referred to as Carbon
Capture and Utilization (CCU)).
1.2.2
Separation Technologies
When considering the separation process, technologies can on an overall level be divided
into the four capture categories; pre-combustion, oxy-combustion, post-combustion, and
direct air [6, 13]. Within each of these categories, several different techniques are being
developed or demonstrated. The three first capture types mentioned, all have the pur-
pose of limiting emissions from a given process, that being e.g. a combustion or chemical
process. The capture unit should therefore be installed in direct connection to the CO2
point source. Direct air capture differs in the way, that the sole purpose is to produce a
stream of pure CO2 utilizing air as a source [13].
Pre-combustion capture separates CO2 from the combustible components in a gaseous
mixture before combustion or use. The technology is therefore applicable to gasification
and gas-reforming plants and is already today being used for the production of hydrogen
[6]. Oxy-combustion capture is used to significantly increase the concentration of CO2 in
the flue gas after combustion. It is done by providing pure oxygen rather than regular
air to the combustion process. The flue gas then mainly consists of CO2 and water [13].
This technology can be used in the power generation industry and for cement and iron
production. However, it is still on demonstration-scale [7].

The post-combustion capture process takes place after the CO2-producing process. It is
therefore well suited for retrofitting existing plants, as it does not require severe changes
to existing processes [5, 13]. For the same reason it is possible to implement at a wide
range of power-producing and industrial sites [14]. The post-combustion capture pro-
cess can be done using different techniques, which on an overall level utilize one of the
following technologies [13, 15]:
• Absorption,
• membrane, or
• adsorption.
Chemical absorption can be performed using ammonia as the sorbent, referred to as a
chilled ammonia process [16]. The technology is expected to reduce the energy require-
ment of the process but is accompanied by challenges with solid deposits. The technology
has been proven on large scale [13]. Research is also being done on CO2 selective mem-
branes, as this technology is expected to offer a more energy-efficient capture process
[15]. Currently, membranes are being used for gas separation purposes [13]. However,
one of the challenges of this technology is the required pressure difference over the mem-
brane, working as a driving force for CO2 to pass through. When utilizing the adsorp-
tion technology for post-combusiton CO2 capture, the surface of a solid sorbent is used
for binding the CO2. It is then removed by reducing the pressure (Pressure/Vacuum
Swing Adsorption (PSA/VSA)) or increasing temperatures (Temperature Swing Adsorp-
tion (TSA)) [14]. Adsorption technologies for post-combustion CC are currently on the
research level [15].
Chemical absorption using an aqueous amine solution as the sorbent is the most well-
known and proven technology [6], which is already used in the food and beverage indus-
try, for natural gas sweetening and production of hydrogen and ammonia for fertilizer
[13, 14]. The amine mostly used is monoethanolamine (MEA), since it has high selectivity
towards CO2 which is required for the low concentrations found in flue gases (< 20 %),
and it allows for capturing 90 % to 95 % of the CO2 [14]. However, the process is energy
intensive and has a thermal energy requirement of 3 GJ/t to 4 GJ/t [13]. The CO2 leaving
the capture unit, is at a temperature of 30 °C to 40 °C and a pressure of 1 bar to 3 bar. It
is in a gaseous state and saturated with water vapor. The purity of CO2 is often greater
than 95 % (mol) while most of the remaining part is water. Small amounts of pollutants
(<< 1%) can exist, primarily being O2, N2, and degradation products from the amine
solvent [13].
1.2.3
Transport
After capture and conditioning of the CO2, it is transported from the source site to the
end-purpose destination, as these are often separated by a geographical distance. Cur-
rently, two types of transport methods are discussed, namely by pipeline or ship.
To reduce the cost of transportation, it is desirable to increase the density of CO2, com-
pared to that of gaseous form. This can be achieved by making it solid, supercritical,
or liquid. CO2 in solid form is however not possible for pipeline transport for obvious
reasons, and it is not viable for ship transport because it is very complex and expensive
to manage, especially during loading and unloading procedures. Transportation in solid
form is therefore not considered a possibility at all [8, 13].
For ship transportation, the CO2 is typically in the liquid state, as this substantially in-
creases the density. Liquid state of CO2 is achieved with temperatures and pressures
ranging between the triple point and critical point, see Fig. 1.3. The triple point is found

at approx. −57 °C and 5.2 bar, while the critical point is at approx. 31 °C and 74 bar
[9, 13]. Supercritical state is preferred for pipeline transportation to avoid the two-phase
region and ensure safe operation [17]. This requires temperatures and pressures beyond
the critical point.
Transport of CO2 in pipelines is a well-established technology and is already widely used
in the North American oil industry for Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) [6]. The CO2 is in
the supercritical state, often at pressures ranging between 80 bar to 150 bar [10]. The
maximum recommended limit of water vapor is 50 ppm to 400 ppm (vol) [13]. The water
content should be limited to reduce the risks of corrosion and the formation of hydrates.
For transportation by ship, intermediate storage is often required to allow for the CO2-
producing process to operate continuously. Transportation of liquid CO2 is already uti-
lized for the food and beverage industry (on small scales), where CO2 is typically at
15 bar and −30 °C. Others suggest that liquid CO2 should be transported at 7 bar and
−50 °C, which corresponds to the conditions for transportation of liquefied petroleum
gas (LPG) [9]. Higher restrictions are set on the water content in liquid CO2 due to the
low temperatures and risks of frost damage on equipment [13].
Pipelines are currently the most feasible option for transportation of large amounts of
CO2 for shorter distances. However, ship transportation is expected to play an important
role in the future, as it is flexible, has low capital investment, and is more cost-effective
than pipelines for smaller volumes and larger distances (greater than around 500 km to
700 km) [9, 13].

a market for CO2 and associated requirements exist. The most strict classification is food
grade CO2, used in the food and beverage industry which requires purity of at least

1.2.5
Pressurization and Purification Processes
This section describes the process in focus of this study. A brief overview is given on
state-of-the-art technologies for removing impurities and pressurizing CO2 to supercrit-
ical state for pipeline transportation. It is followed by systems for liquefaction of CO2
for ship transport. Different system layouts are presented together with a brief literature
review presenting findings of studies that focus on liquefaction of CO2.
In this process step, CO2 should be pressurized by compression and/ or liquefaction.
Additionally, impurities such as water vapor, volatiles, incondensable gases, and remains
from the separation process are removed [10]. The outlet state (purity, pressure, and
temperature) of CO2 depends on the requirements of the following transportation and
end-purpose.
Impurity Removal
The compounds and concentration of CO2 in the entering stream depend mainly on the
source type and the separation method. The present impurities will change with different
==PAGE:29==
1.2. State of the Art
7
fuels in power plants, and for different industrial processes, such as cement, iron, or
chemical production [9].
Impurities in the CO2 should to some extent be avoided for safety and operational rea-
sons. Generally, the presence of water is undesirable as it induces high risks of corrosion,
frost damage, and formation of hydrates which causes plugging. Volatile gases should be
removed as their presence increases the critical point for the mixture compared to that of
pure CO2. This means that higher pressure levels are required to reach supercritical state
for pipeline transportation. Increased compounds of volatile gases also require larger
pipe diameters and thereby increase costs [17]. Additionally, volatiles increase the risk
of dry ice formation in liquid CO2 during transportation and they should therefore be
limited [10].
Water vapor can be removed by condensation. This is often done during intercooling
between compression stages with a following vapor-liquid separator installed, which
simply collects the condensed water at the bottom and gaseous mixture at the top. A
small fraction of CO2 will be contained in the water and thereby lost. Furthermore, some
volatiles will be removed together with the water [10]. Incondensable gases can also be
removed using a vapor-liquid separator combined with liquefaction of CO2. The CO2 is
liquefied at a temperature greater than the condensation temperature of the undesired
gases. These are then purged together with a small fraction of gaseous CO2 at the top of
the separator, and liquid CO2 leaves the bottom. When flashing is not sufficient to reach
the desired purity, a distillation column can be utilized [9, 17].
Supercritical State for Pipelines
The most conventional way of reaching supercritical state of CO2 for pipeline transporta-
tion, is by multi-stage compression with intercooling. It has been suggested to reduce the
steps in the compressor train and combine it with a refrigeration cycle and a subsequent
liquid pump [21]. In this way the CO2 is compressed to an intermediate pressure, liq-
uefied, and then pumped to desired transportation pressure. This can result in a power
saving of around 10 % [21].
Liquid State for Ship Transport
When the CO2 should be liquefied, it is usually compressed through several steps with
intercooling, and then liquefied using a refrigeration cycle [13, 10]. The CO2 is dried be-
fore the liquefaction. The conventional system is referred to as a closed system because an
external refrigerant is used for the liquefaction. Alternatively, an open type of system can
be used, where the CO2 is compressed, liquefied at elevated pressure and temperature,
and expanded to the desired end-pressure [12]. The concepts are illustrated in Fig. 1.4.
The open system exists in several alternative layouts, so-called Linde Hampson systems,
where the outlet CO2 on gaseous form is used as cooling media for the liquefaction pro-
cess in an internal heat exchanger and is recirculated back to the inlet gas. The simple
Linde Hampson system is illustrated in Fig. 1.5a. The system can also be integrated
with a vapor compression cycle as illustrated in Fig. 1.5b, which is referred to as a pre-
cooled Linde Hampson system. The Linde Hampson system can also be modified to a
dual-pressure type, as shown in Fig. 1.5c. Note that a multi-stage compression exists be-
fore the closed vapor compression system, Linde Hampson system, and precooled Linde
Hampson system. Gas-liquid separators should further be included after intercooling
[22].

literature and different process layouts have been evaluated in terms of energy, exergy,

sures are investigated in [9]. The system considered, uses an ammonia vapor compres-
sion cycle and an impurity removal unit. The system is optimized using cost functions
and a mathematical solver. The minimum costs are found at 40 bar to 50 bar. At lower
pressures, it is seen that the specific costs increase when impurities in the source increase.
At lower pressures costs are not affected by a purity requirement of 99 %, however, costs
increase when there is a 99.9 % purity requirement. In [10] two systems for pipeline
transport (150 bar) and one system for ship transport (6.5 bar) are developed. It is in-
vestigated how impurities in the inlet stream of CO2 affect the costs of the system. It is
found that the specific costs are reduced at low pressures when impurities are removed
and that the best-performing system varies when the inlet composition changes. How-
ever, [9] and [10] do not consider how different optimized systems would be beneficial at
different boundary conditions or consider how costs and irreversibilities are distributed
throughout the system.
In [11] an energetic, exergetic, and economic comparison is made of a precooled Linde
Hampson system and a vapor compression system using either ammonia, propane, or
r134a to deliver liquefied CO2 at 15 bar. It is found that the vapor compression system
with propane has the lowest energy consumption and highest exergy efficiency, but that
the costs are reduced by using the precooled Linde Hampson system with ammonia.
Exergy analyses are also performed in [24] on four different systems (Linde Hampson,
dual-pressure Linde Hampson, vapor compression, and an absorption refrigeration cy-
cle) for producing liquefied CO2 at 19.7 bar. They find that most exergy is destroyed in
heat exchangers throughout the system. The highest exergy efficiency is found in the ab-
sorption refrigeration cycle, and an exergoeconomic analysis of this system shows, that
the costs can be reduced by improving the design of the heat exchangers. An advanced
exergy analysis is performed in [25] on a system for pipeline transportation utilizing a va-
por compression cycle. The results are used for the optimization of the system by making
heat integration and finally by integrating a supercritical CO2 cycle. These three studies
evaluate the irreversibilities within different systems but do however not optimize the
systems from an economic point of view or consider different boundary conditions.
1.3
Scope
This project focused on the liquefaction and purification process step in the CO2 capture
value chain. The process receives captured CO2 from a post-combustion chemical ab-
sorption separation unit and should deliver liquid CO2 at a required end-pressure fit for
storage and transportation by ship. The baseline system investigated in this project was
a closed system using an external refrigeration cycle for the liquefaction. An exergoeco-
nomic analysis was made to investigate how costs were distributed between components
throughout the system, and how these costs were related to operation and investment.
The analysis was used as a basis for suggesting three alternative system layouts. Further-
more, it was investigated whether the overall costs could be minimized by changing the
design and operational parameters throughout the system. The minimization was per-
formed on all system alternatives using an optimization algorithm. A study of how the
developed systems should be optimized for different boundary conditions was included.
The project aimed to investigate the following overarching research question:

Which processes of a liquefaction and purification system for captured CO2 should
be optimized to make the overall process cost-efficient, and how is the optimization
affected when the boundary conditions are changed?
To answer the research question, the following sub-questions were addressed:
• Which processes are necessary in a liquefaction and purification system for cap-

• How is exergy destruction distributed between components in the system?

• What overall cost of liquefied and purified CO2 is achieved using the system?
• Can optimization of design and operational parameters of the system or chosen
changes to the layout reduce the overall cost of liquefied and purified CO2?
• How is the overall cost affected when boundary conditions change and how should
design and operational parameters be chosen to minimize it?

2.2
Thermodynamic Model
The model of the system was made using the software Engineering Equation Solver (EES)
[26]. In the following, a general description of the baseline system is given followed by
an overview of assumptions and modeling approach. This is followed by descriptions of
alternative system designs and the changes made compared to the baseline system.
The purpose of the system was to remove traces of H2O in the captured CO2, liquefy it
to a subcooled liquid, and deliver it at the required end-pressure. Therefore, captured
CO2 should be compressed to elevated pressure, at which the liquefaction process could
occur using an external refrigeration cycle. The liquid CO2 should afterwards be sent to a
storage tank at the desired pressure. During the compression process, the captured CO2
should be cooled to condensate traces of H2O and remove it. Since an external refriger-
ation cycle was used, and no CO2 was recirculated, the baseline system is considered a
closed system.
2.2.1
System Description
A process flow diagram of the baseline system is illustrated in Fig. 2.1. Captured CO2 en-
ters at the left in state point 1 as a superheated gas and leaves to the right in state point 9 as
a subcooled liquid. The initial compression was performed using a two-stage compressor
train (COMP1 and COMP2) with intercooling (COOL1) and a following H2O separator
(SEP1). After the second compressor, the captured CO2 was cooled again (COOL2) to
remove further H2O in a separator (SEP2), before it was liquefied in a heat exchanger by
the refrigerant (HEX1). Finally, the liquid CO2 was throttled through a valve (VAL1) to
the end-pressure.
The refrigeration cycle used in the system was a two-stage transcritical cycle with CO2 as
the refrigerant (r) and is illustrated in Fig. 2.1. The refrigerant was compressed through
two stages (COMP3 and COMP4) with inter-stage desuperheating in a cooler (COOL3)
and through a mixing process (MIX1). After compression, the refrigerant was cooled in a
gas cooler (GC) and throttled through a valve (VAL2) into a receiver (REC1) at an inter-
mediate pressure. From the receiver saturated liquid entered an internal heat exchanger
(IHEX1) in which it was subcooled before being expanded through a valve (VAL3) to the
required evaporation pressure. The refrigerant was then evaporated by heat exchange
with the captured CO2 (HEX1) and sent through the opposite side of the internal heat ex-
changer before entering the first compressor. The saturated gas leaving the receiver was

Heat Exchangers
All heat exchangers were assumed to be plate heat exchangers and were used for cooling.
The main purpose of COOL1 and COOL2 was to cool the captured CO2 to a temperature
below the saturation temperature of water to remove it from the mixture through con-
densation. COOL1 and COOL2 assisted SEP1 and SEP2, respectively, in providing a
two-phase mixture. Furthermore, the temperature reduction provided by COOL1 and
COOL2 improved the thermodynamic performance of COMP2 and HEX1, respectively.
The reduced temperature at the inlet of the compressor reduced the irreversibilities of
the process. Similarly, the desuperheating before the condensation in HEX1 allowed a
smaller temperature difference throughout the heat exchanger and improved the ther-
modynamic performance of the process. COOL3 was used as an intercooler in the refrig-
eration cycle to improve the performance of the second compression step.
In COOL1, COOL2, and COOL3, CO2 was cooled by an external water circuit which
discharged the energy to the surroundings through a dry cooler. The dry cooler was not
included in the energy and exergy analyses but was modeled to restrict the temperature
of the available cooling water by the surrounding temperature and to determine the costs
of the cooling media. The assumptions and modeling of the dry cooler are described in
Appendix A.3.
The purpose of HEX1 was to liquefy captured CO2 from a superheated gas to a subcooled
liquid. The heat was transferred to the refrigerant, which was evaporated and heated

Mixer
The mixer in the refrigeration cycle was used for cooling the hot stream of refrigerant
coming from the first compression step through COOL3 by mixing it with the colder
stream of extracted refrigerant from the receiver coming through VAL4. The reduced
temperature at the inlet of the compressor helped reduce irreversibilities occurring dur-
ing compression. The mixing process was modeled by energy and mass balances.
Valves
The purpose of the valves was different depending on where in the system they were
placed. VAL1 throttled the liquefied CO2 to the end-pressure and subcooling and en-
sured the flow into the storage tank. VAL2 worked as the high-pressure valve in the
refrigeration cycle and throttled the working fluid from high pressure to intermediate
pressure. It regulated the pressure in the gas cooler and performed part of the necessary
throttling process from the high- to low-pressure side, respectively. VAL3 performed the
throttling from the intermediate pressure level to the evaporation pressure. VAL4 re-
moved vapor from the receiver and thereby regulated the pressure in the tank. It also
reduced the temperature of the vapor and thereby reduced the temperature of the refrig-
erant entering the second compression step. All valves in the system were modeled as
isenthalpic throttling processes and conservation of mass was applied.

2.3
Exergy Analysis
The exergy of a given system is the useful work that can potentially be utilized in a pro-
cess where the system interacts and reaches equilibrium with the surroundings, while
heat transfer only occurs to the surroundings [36]. The exergy is therefore the maximum
theoretical amount of energy that can be extracted from a system at a given state dif-
ferent from the surroundings, referred to as the dead state. The difference in states can
concern pressure, temperature, composition, velocity, and elevation, each of which cor-
responds to mechanical, thermal, chemical, kinetic, and potential exergy of the system,
respectively. Performing an exergy analysis on a thermal energy system can give infor-
mation on exergy flows throughout the system, and where irreversibilities reduce the
amount of useful work in the flows. This corresponds to exergy being destroyed within
components. The thermodynamic performance of an energy system is improved if ex-
ergy destruction in the system is reduced [36]. An exergy analysis helps identify which
components show the highest exergy destruction, and therefore should be of focus when
optimizing the design of the system.
The system of focus in this thesis was at rest relative to the surroundings, and differences
in kinetic and potential exergy within the system were assumed to be negligible. There-
fore, kinetic and potential exergy were excluded. Chemical exergy was considered only
in the CO2 product stream. It was not included in the refrigeration cycle or brine streams
because the chemical composition of these streams did not change. The dead state was
denoted as state 0 and defined with a temperature, T0 = 25 ◦C and a pressure, p0 = 1 atm
[37].
The sum of thermal (T) and mechanical (M) exergy is conventionally termed the ’phys-
ical’ exergy. The theoretical maximum work that can be obtained when the given state
reaches the dead state, can theoretically be seen as two reversible processes; an isobaric
heat transfer process (corresponding to the thermal exergy) and an isothermal expan-
sion (corresponding to the mechanical exergy) [37]. The theoretical point between the
two processes is at the dead state temperature and the initial pressure. The thermal and
mechanical specific exergy (e) are given in (2.19) and (2.20), respectively.

Internal Heat Exchangers
The internal heat exchangers operated either across or below T0. The CO2 liquefaction
heat exchanger (HEX1) cooled the captured CO2 from a temperature greater than T0 to
a temperature below T0, as indicated in Fig. 2.4a. Therefore, the product exergy corre-
sponded to the exergy of the leaving captured CO2, while the exergy present in the inlet
stream was destroyed during cooling from the inlet temperature to T0. It was subtracted
in the fuel definition, which also included the exergy reduction of the refrigerant being
evaporated.
The internal heat exchanger in the refrigeration cycle was used to cool the refrigerant at
a temperature below T0. The exergy flow was therefore increased, which was defined as
the product as given in Table 2.6. The fuel was the corresponding decrease in exergy of
the refrigerant on the cold side of the heat exchanger.
Pump
The purpose of the pump was to increase the pressure and temperature of the liquid CO2
by utilizing electricity as fuel. Therefore, the product was the increase in exergy from
inlet to outlet. The pump operated below T0, meaning that the increase in temperature
was not considered a product from an exergy point of view. However, the destruction of
thermal exergy was outweighed by the increase in mechanical exergy.
DH Heat Exchangers
In the DH heat exchangers, the thermal exergy of the DH water was increased through
heat transfer with the working media. The heat transfer occurred at a temperature greater
than T0. Therefore, the increase in exergy of the DH water was considered a product from
an exergy viewpoint and had a value, as it was a useful product output from the overall
system. The fuel was the exergy decrease of the working media.
Coolers
The purpose of the coolers in the baseline system was to cool the working fluid. However,
this is not considered a product from an exergy point of view, since the cooling was done
at temperatures greater than T0 [39]. The coolers were therefore considered as dissipative
components. In the coolers, the fuel exergy was considered as the reduction in exergy of
the working fluid minus the unintended exergy increase of the cooling media. In the gas
cooler, the power needed to run the air fan was also included.
The exergy of the brine outlet streams was discharged to the environment through a
dry cooler, while the air in the gas cooler was discharged directly. These streams were

3.1.4
Optimization
Two sets of variables existed which could be used for optimizing the system; the pinch
point temperature differences of all heat exchangers and the operational parameters high-
lighted in red in Fig. 3.2. These included the two intermediate pressures in the captured
CO2 stream (p2 and p5), the intermediate and high pressure in the refrigeration cycle
(pr2 and pr5), and the subcooling and superheating in state point r9 and r11 (∆Tsc,r9 and
∆Tsh,r11), respectively. A parameter study of all design and operational variables was
made to evaluate which variables had the largest influence on the overall costs of the
system and whether any interdependency existed between variables.
The variation of pinch point temperature differences between 1 K and 10 K is seen in
Fig. 3.3. It shows the percentage change in the LCOLP compared to the initial case with
a temperature difference of 5 K in all heat exchangers. It was found that a lower tem-
perature difference generally reduced the LCOLP. The GC and HEX1 showed to have the
largest influence on LCOLP, while the 5 K increase or decrease for the remaining heat
exchangers showed to only affect LCOLP less than 1 %. These results were in agreement
with the results of the exergy and exergoeconomic analysis, indicating that the thermody-
namic performance of all heat exchangers should be improved at the expense of greater
capital investment. Furthermore, the highest exergy destruction was found in the GC
and HEX1 compared to the remaining heat exchangers, therefore it seemed reasonable
that the highest improvement potential existed in these two heat exchangers, while the
cost improvement was insignificant for remaining heat exchangers.
The operational parameters were investigated by altering them in sets since an interde-
pendency could potentially exist between some of the parameters. Furthermore, it was
investigated whether the pinch point temperature differences affected the optimal point
of some operational parameters. The most important results are shown in Fig. 3.4, and
remaining results are included in Fig. A.6 in Appendix A.6. The plots show the value of

lifetime without a significant increase in LCOLP.

3.3.2
Exergy Analysis
The sum of exergy destruction occurring in the components of the refrigeration cycle was
increased by 19.2 kW, corresponding to 33 %, in the system with alternative compressor
steps compared to the optimized baseline system. This occurred because the liquefaction
was done at a significantly lower temperature, which increased the load on the refrig-
eration cycle. The difference between the temperature and pressure of the refrigerant in
HEX1 and the GC was significantly higher, meaning that more exergy should be supplied
through compression, while more exergy was destroyed in the valves. The increased ex-
ergy destruction indicated, that three compressor steps in the refrigeration cycle were a
necessity to reduce the liquefaction pressure, as two compressor steps would have re-
sulted in even more exergy destruction. The total losses occurring in the refrigeration
cycle were increased by 4.1 kW corresponding to 34 %.
The sum of exergy destruction occurring during compression and purification of the cap-
tured CO2 was decreased by 2.7 kW or 6 %, while losses decreased by 2.3 kW correspond-
ing to 15 %. This was because the destruction in COMP2 and COOL2, and the loss in
COOL2 was eliminated.
The reduced exergy destruction and losses in the compression and purification of the
captured CO2 were outweighed by the increase in the refrigeration cycle, meaning that
the overall exergy efficiency was slightly reduced to 38.5 %. The complete exergy analysis
of the system with alternative compressor steps can be seen in Table A.13 and Table A.14
in Appendix A.9.
3.3.3
Exergoeconomic Analysis
The exergoeconomic analysis of the baseline system indicated that the investment costs of
the compression of the captured CO2 should be reduced, which was achieved by reduc-
ing the compression to only one compressor step. The capital investment and costs of op-
eration and maintenance of the initial compression and purification were reduced by 19
DKK/h. However, the capital investment and costs of operation and maintenance of the
refrigeration cycle were increased by 21.3 DKK/h, due to the extra components needed
to include a third compressor step. This was found to increase the overall capital invest-
ment and costs of operation and maintenance by just below 3 % to 108.4 DKK/h. The
increased exergy destruction increased the costs of exergy destruction by 10.6 DKK/h.

Discussion
In this chapter limitations of the model are discussed. The ability of the model to estimate
the purity of liquid CO2 is considered in Section 4.1, followed by uncertainties related to
cost functions in Section 4.2. This is followed by an evaluation of whether the optimized
operational parameters and alternative systems are realistic to implement in Section 4.3.
Finally, alternative approaches for the exergy and exergoeconomic analyses are discussed
in Section 4.4 and Section 4.5.
4.1
Purification
In the model, it was assumed that all H2O in the captured CO2 stream could be removed
in SEP2, which in reality was not the case. When instead assuming that the condensate
leaving SEP2 was saturated liquid, some H2O would be contained in the CO2 gas (this
was similar for SEP1 in the system with alternative compression). The estimated amount
of H2O remaining in the gas is summarized in Table 4.1 for three of the systems with
different liquefaction pressures.
Comparing the values in Table 4.1 to the guidelines given in Table 1.1, it appeared that
additional purification was required to remove traces of H2O. This can be done using
active coal filters, and various types of distillation columns and dryers [10]. However, it
should be noted, that the model did not include traces of incondensable gasses, which
would reduce the concentration of H2O. Furthermore, the assumption of an ideal gas
mixture is uncertain when H2O is close to saturation [28]. Therefore, the small amounts
of H2O are likely within the uncertainty and the model could not be used for estimating
the amount of H2O remaining in the CO2. However, the values in Table 4.1 indicated, that
less H2O could be removed as condensate in the system with alternative compression.
This was caused by a lower liquefaction pressure, which would also require a lower
cooling temperature if the same amount of H2O should be condensed.
The model did not include incondensable gasses, which can also to some extent be re-
moved in active coal filters, by being dissolved in the leaving condensate, or simply by

4.2
Cost Relations
Due to a lack of information, it was assumed that the sizes of the tanks were scaled
directly with the cross-sectional area while assuming a constant height. It was therefore
likely that the use of a scaling factor based on volume underestimated the increased costs
associated with increased size. It could be expected that an increase in diameter was
more expensive compared to increasing the height, which would also give an increased
volume. Therefore, it was possible that a higher capital investment of tanks would be
achieved. However, the conclusions of the analysis would not be affected, since the tanks
did not show significant importance for optimization, and because the investment costs
did not vary for the different system layouts.
Of more importance was the cost relation used for compressors. The relation between
size, efficiency, and costs was simplified in the model. The fits used for the isentropic
and volumetric efficiencies were made using several different compressors with different
efficiencies and sizes, and therefore also likely different costs. However, it was not possi-
ble to include a relation between efficiency and costs for the compressors, due to lack of
information. Therefore, the model assumed, that a certain efficiency could be achieved re-
gardless of the size of the compressor. It was possible, that the missing relation distorted
the optimization, and emphasized the need for optimal pressures to a greater extent. Op-
timal pressure ratios of the compressors would to some extent reduce both fuel costs and
capital investments. In reality, a trade-off between these costs would be expected. How-
ever, it was expected that the results of the initial exergoeconomic analysis would not
differ, with a different cost relation for compressors, as the associated costs were of sig-
nificantly greater importance for the system, compared to the remaining components. It
was unlikely that a more robust cost relation would reduce the costs of compressors to
an extent, where they became insignificant on a system level.
4.3
Alternative Systems and Operation
The lowest LCOLP was seen for the system with DH integration. However, it required
that DH could be produced in all operational hours, which is unlikely, assuming 8,000
hours of operation annually. It would be expected that DH could be sold only during
the cold season. It was found in the sensitivity analysis, that the LCOLP of the system
with DH integration increased to 175 DKK/t CO2 when the annual operating hours were
reduced to 4,000 h. This indicated that it would not be economically attractive to only
operate the system in hours, where DH could be sold. Therefore, a cooling circuit would
be necessary for the remaining operating hours. This would increase the fuel costs of
the system, and increase the LCOLP because of the reduced DH production. However,
it was expected that the LCOLP of the system with DH integration would still be lower
compared to the baseline system, even when DH was not produced in all operational
hours.
The optimization of the baseline system, the system with a liquid CO2 pump, and DH
integration showed that the intermediate pressure in the refrigeration cycle should be
increased. This was also seen for the CO2 liquefaction pressure for the baseline system
and system with DH integration for an increased outlet pressure of 40 bar. It could be ex-
pected that the components experiencing these pressures would become more expensive,

Chapter 5
Conclusion
A thermodynamic model was made of a system used for liquefaction and purification of
captured CO2. The captured CO2 entered the system at 1.7 bar and 40 °C and should be
delivered in liquid state at 15 bar. The CO2 was initially compressed in two steps to a
pressure greater than the triple point, while water was removed through condensation in
coolers after each compressor. The pure CO2 was then liquefied using an external two-
stage refrigeration cycle with CO2 as working media and throttled through a valve to the
end-state.
Exergy and exergoeconomic analyses were made which showed that the greatest share
of exergy losses occurred during purification (56 %) while the greatest share of exergy
destruction occurred during liquefaction (67 %). It was found that the greatest cost flows
throughout the system were associated with compressors. Significantly high investment
costs were seen for the two initial compressors.
A parameter study was made on the system in which it was found, that the pressure
states had the greatest influence on the levelized cost of liquefaction and purification
(LCOLP). It was found that the design of the liquefaction heat exchanger and the gas
cooler and internal heat exchanger in the refrigeration cycle was also to some extent im-
portant. The parameters were used for minimizing the costs with an optimization algo-
rithm, achieving an LCOLP of 138 DKK/t CO2.
The results of the exergoeconomic analysis were used as a basis for suggesting three
alternative system layouts. It was found that the same LCOLP (137 DKK/t CO2) could
be achieved when using a pump for reaching the required end-state of liquid CO2, while
a higher LCOLP (148 DKK/t CO2) was found for an alternative system layout with only
one initial compressor and three compressor steps in the refrigeration cycle. The LCOLP
could be reduced to 124 DKK/t CO2 by integrating district heat production in the coolers
throughout the system.
A study was made, where the inlet pressure was increased to 2.1 bar and decreased to
1 bar. The increase resulted in a lower cost of the system of 127 DKK/t CO2, while the
lower inlet pressure increased costs to 177 DKK/t CO2. Similar effects were seen for the
alternative system layouts. It was seen that optimization of the pressures throughout the
captured CO2 stream was important when the inlet pressure was altered. Furthermore, it
was investigated how a required outlet pressure of 40 bar and 7 bar would affect the costs
of the system. It was found that an increase in outlet pressure had insignificant effects
on the LCOLP. At lower outlet pressure, the LCOLP was increased to 159 DKK/t CO2.
However, no change in costs was seen for the alternative system layout having only one
initial compressor and a refrigeration cycle with three compressor steps, which therefore
seemed to be more cost-effective at an outlet pressure of 7 bar. The optimization showed,
that all pressures throughout the system should be optimized when the required outlet
pressure was changed.

Chapter 5. Conclusion
A sensitivity analysis was performed of uncertain input parameters. It was found that
the LCOLP was most sensitive to the electricity price and annual operational hours. If the
electricity price was reduced to the lowest estimated value, the LCOLP would be reduced
to 107 DKK/t CO2, while a reduction in annual operational hours would increase it to
198 DKK/t CO2.

Appendix A.
A.3
Modeling of Dry Cooler
A dry cooler (DC) was used for discharging heat from the cooling water from COOL1,
COOL2, and COOL3 to the surroundings. The configuration is shown in Fig. A.3. It was
assumed that the air was heated to 48 °C. The DC was modeled to determine the costs of
the cooling water entering the coolers.
Figure A.3: PI-diagram showing the brine circuit used as cooling media.
An energy balance was made for the component, including the power consumption used
for ensuring the flow of air, in a similar way as for the gas cooler. The specific power
consumption was assumed to be 0.16 kJ/kg air at nominal conditions, and an overall
heat transfer coefficient of 0.03 kW/m2K was assumed [34].
The costs of the dry cooler was determined for the baseline system at basecase boundary
conditions. A reference component with a heat transfer area of 5,178 m2 and a purchased
equipment cost of 626,005 DKK was used [34]. The scaling factor used for the cost func-
tion was 0.39 [43].
To determine the average cost per exergy unit of the brine streams entering the control
volume, a cost balance was made. The entering air contained no exergy, and therefore
had no cost. No costs were assigned to losses (the leaving air). According to the SPECO
approach for dissipative components used for the coolers, the brine entering the DC had
no costs [39]. This is also illustrated in Fig. A.3. Therefore, the capital investment and
costs of operation and maintenance of the DC and electricity consumption were assigned
to the cooled brine streams. This resulted in a an average cost of 0.014 DKK/kJ for the
brine entering the control volume.
