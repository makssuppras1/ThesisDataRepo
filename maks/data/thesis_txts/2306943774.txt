ABSTRACT 
 
An important increase in the population number during the last decades has led to a 
raise in the food demand, especially in the third world countries. This situation has 
aggravated the human and animal malnutrition problems that are present in these 
regions, creating the necessity to look for alternative food sources and supplements. 
Single cell proteins (SCPs), which are proteins extracted from microbial biomass, are a 
significant option to achieve this goal. Moreover, a concept like biorefinery can be 
applied in their production to make it as more sustainable as possible. 
This thesis evaluated the viability of a novel biorefinery approach which combines a 
process of carbon dioxide removal from biogas using microalgae (Chlorella 
sorokiniana) with the production of Methylococcus capsulatus biomass (currently used 
as a SCP source). Since this microorganism requires methane and oxygen for growing, 
the output gas generated in the first stage is likely to be a valuable mixture for the 
second stage. 
Firstly, experiments consisting in an autotrophic cultivation of C. sorokiniana in biogas 
(60% CH4, 40% CO2) were carried out. Low CO2 removals (22.5%) were achieved 
compared with the literature. However, the selection of the culture medium was 
considered a decisive factor for these poor results. 
Secondly, growth of M. capsulatus in a fermenter was performed. Three different gas 
compositions (CH4 / CO2 (v/v) of 60/40, 80/20 and 95/5) were tested. A significantly 
higher growth rate was found in the 80/20 case (μmax = 0.31 h-1), whilst a greater final 
biomass concentration was reached in the 60/40 experiment (176 mg/L). 
Thirdly, both processes were combined to study the feasibility of the integrated model. 
Approximately the same values obtained in the single experiments for bacterial growth 
rate and final biomass were found. 
Finally, composition analyses in respect to proteins, sugars and pigments were 
performed in both microorganisms to determine their nutritional value and potential use 
for other purposes. No influence of the initial gas concentration was observed in the M. 
capsulatus experiments. On the other hand, unexpected high values for sugars were 
found in C. sorokiniana, with low contents in proteins and pigments compared to the 
literature. 
The different experiments carried out during the thesis could prove the validity of the 
overall process, making this approach an interesting alternative for SCP sustainable 
production. However, it needs further studies and optimizations to be applied in the 
industry.

1. INTRODUCTION 
 
Today, in the 21st century, the world is facing with huge changes. A combination of 
population growth with energy and materials consumption increase is leading to an 
unsustainable scenario, which must be conscientiously modified in order not to reach a 
chaotic situation.  
One of the most significant consequences of this exponential increase in the population 
number, particularly in the developing countries of the world, is the malnutrition. 
Deficiencies of proteins and other nutrients have been detected in animal and human 
diets. The absence of sufficient protein-rich food has given rise to the search for 
innovative alternatives. Among these novel sources, single cell protein (SCP) from 
microbial biomass has been considered a promising option due to several interesting 
features such as high productivity, high efficiency in substrate conversion and 
independence of seasonal factors [1]. 
The term single cell protein refers to edible dried cells of unicellular organisms such as 
algae, fungi or bacteria, which can be used as protein supplement in human food or 
animal nutrition. Bacteria present some advantages such as high protein content (50 to 
80%) and fast growth rate [1]. For these reasons, a few companies are already selling 
bacteria derived SCP, especially from Methylococcus capsulatus. 
M. capsulatus is an aerobic bacterium that needs methane (CH4) and oxygen (O2) for 
growing, so natural gas (which is a non-renewable resource) is normally used as the 
methane source. Nevertheless, recent studies are paying more attention to the 
utilization of biogas because of the necessity to look for more sustainable methods. 
Biogas is a renewable energy source mostly composed of CH4 and CO2, and small 
amounts of other gases such as H2S, N2, NH3 and H2O. 
Since M. capsulatus requires O2 for its cultivation, this gas must be externally supplied 
besides CH4. However, if biogas is used instead of natural gas, the presence of CO2 in 
the biogas can become an advantage. There are some organisms that are able to 
uptake carbon dioxide and emit oxygen, by using a process called photosynthesis. 
Among them, microalgae are especially interesting for these purposes, since they can 
be utilized at the same time to generate a wide range of commercially viable products 
like biofuels, pigments and pharmaceuticals. 
The combination of the two mentioned processes is thought to be an original, 
interesting and promising strategy, which can be integrated in a biorefinery approach. A 
biorefinery is a facility that develops sustainable processes by utilizing renewable 
feedstocks and biological residues in order to produce energy, food, high added value 
products (HAVPs) and bulk chemicals. However, most of them are not profitable from 
an economic point of view. In order to compete with the current industry, appropriate 
methodologies need to be studied and optimized. Lowering the production costs, 
integrating different processes and minimizing the environmental footprint are important 
factors when looking for successful developments. In this way, integration of SCP 
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
production with microalgae cultivation is considered an attractive idea to reach these 
goals. 
This project studied the feasibility of a novel biorefinery process based on the 
cultivation of the microalga Chlorella sorokiniana using biogas as a carbon source. 
Then, the resulting gas, which contained CH4, a reduced amount of CO2 and O2 
generated through photosynthesis, was employed to grow M. capsulatus. The 
composition of the obtained bacterial and algal biomass was characterized in order to 
know its nutritional value, in the case of Methylococcus, and its potential for other 
purposes, in the case of Chlorella.

2. OVERALL APPROACH 
 
The process concept is schematized in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1: Overview of the biorefinery process proposed in this project. 
The first stage consists of a microalgae culture that grows autotrophically and uses 
biogas as a carbon source. Chlorella sorokiniana was the microalga species selected 
for this project. This species has been chosen because of its availability in the lab (it 
was used in previous studies in the department), its ability to grow in flue or exhaust 
gases and remove CO2 [2], [3] and its high growth rate. Moreover, it is suitable for 
cultivation in wastewater or other waste feedstocks, since it is able to adapt during 
abiotic stress [4]. At the same time, C. sorokiniana has a big potential for the 
production of biofuels such as biodiesel [5]. Indeed, C. sorokiniana has a typical oil 
content that ranges from 22 to 44% of the dry weight, but it can hyperaccumulate lipids 
up to 52% of DW [6]. 
The microalga consumes the carbon dioxide contained in the biogas, with the 
concomitant oxygen production. The output gas composition of this first step is 
methane, oxygen and the carbon dioxide that has not been utilized. 
The second stage comprises a methanotrophs culture. Methylococcus capsulatus was 
chosen due to its composition rich in proteins. For this reason, it has been extensively 
used in the large scale production of single-cell proteins [7]. 
The gas provided from the algae culture is fed in the second culture, since M. 
capsulatus is an aerobic microorganism (needs oxygen) that employs methane as a 
==PAGE:14==
4 
 
carbon source. The carbon dioxide generated during this cultivation is recirculated to 
the microalgae culture to make the process as much self-sufficient as possible. 
This novel strategy comprises two different microbiological processes that are likely to 
be integrated. In order to make industrial biochemical processes profitable, diverse 
pathways must be combined to generate a wide range of valuable products. At the 
same time, residues have to be minimized and reutilized if possible. In this case, the 
gas that is generated in the first step contains primarily methane and oxygen. This 
mixture, besides being relatively dangerous, cannot be injected into the natural gas grid 
due to its high oxygen content. However, it is valuable for other processes such as 
methanotrophs cultivation.

3. BACKGROUND 
 
3.1.  The Biorefinery Concept 
A growing world population needs to be able to deal with the increasingly scarce 
natural resources and with the wastes that is inexorably generating, in order to reach a 
reasonable sustainable development. To assess the impact of this expanding 
population on the environment, an equation can be used [8]: 
                                                         EC = P x C x B                                                     (1) 
EC is the Earth’s capacity and depends on P (the total population exploiting it), C (the 
consumption per person) and B (a conversion factor between consumption and 
environmental load). To get closer to sustainability, technology can act on the last 
factor by reducing it through two different pathways: dematerialisation and 
transmaterialisation [8]. 
The first term refers to the efficiency in using the available resources, i.e. utilizing less 
resources to produce a unit of activity. One example would be lowering the waste 
generated in the industries by recycling and reusing it [8]. 
On the other hand, transmaterialisation means substitution of the current raw materials 
(fossil fuels) to those that are renewable on a short period (such as plant-based 
feedstocks). Biorefineries represent a key factor to carry out this decisive process [8], 
[9]. 
According to the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL), a biorefinery can be 
defined as “a facility that integrates conversion processes and equipments to produce 
fuels, power and chemicals from biomass” [10]. Therefore, these refineries employ 
biomass instead of non-renewable resources in order to obtain different outputs (Figure 
2).

3.1.1. Types of Biorefinery 
Biorefineries are classified in three different types according to the literature [8]–[10]: 
 
Phase I biorefineries: They are based on a single feedstock, have fixed 
processing capabilities and generate mostly one product. Companies that are 
currently producing biodiesel in Europe are considered “phase I biorefineries”. 
They normally use rapeseed oil to obtain biodiesel (and glycerine, as a co-
product) through transesterification. 
 
 
Phase II biorefineries: Although they use only one feedstock as the previous 
generation, they can produce different products employing distinct methods. 
Therefore, these biorefineries are more flexible, since they can respond in a 
better way to the market demands. One example could be a wet mill plant, 
which generates ethanol, starch, high-fructose corn syrup and corn oil from 
grain. 
 
 
Phase III biorefineries: They are the most advanced biorefineries due to their 
capability to use a wide range of different feedstocks, producing many products 
that the society requires. Besides the advantage of market adaptability (like 
phase II biorefineries), the fact of utilizing several feedstocks secures 
availability of the raw materials. If a kind of biomass is limited in a certain 
moment, the company can change to another biomass that is more feasible to 
employ. At present, because of their complexity, there are no commercial phase 
III biorefineries. However, many efforts have been made in research and 
development. 
Phase III biorefineries can be subdivided in whole crop, lignocellulosic, green 
and two-platform concept biorefineries [8]. 
 
3.1.2. Algae-based Biorefineries 
Currently, some food crops such as wheat, corn and rice are used to produce 
bioethanol and biodiesel, which are known as first-generation biofuels [8], [11]. In 2007, 
70% of the bioethanol produced worldwide was generated from sugarcane and corn in 
Brazil and USA, respectively. Moreover, the production of biodiesel come primarily 
from food sources like palm oil or soybean [12]. This creates an important dilemma 
since the use of these crops for biofuel production induces an increase of the food 
prices [11]. In 2006, global stocks of important grains such as rice, corn or wheat were 
at their lowest level for the past 20 years [12]. In order to find a better option, other 
feedstocks that are not used for food should be considered. 
To overcome these obstacles, attempts for using marine algae as a biorefinery 
feedstock have dramatically increased. Algae have a high potential, since they do not 
need land and freshwater for growing, and are able to generate more biomass than 
terrestrial crops. In addition, they can transform solar energy into chemical energy with 
better efficiency (6-8%) than terrestrial biomass (1.8-2.2%) [12]. Due to this high 
photosynthetic ability, algae have a great potential to produce and store sufficient 
resources necessary for biorefinery processes. In this way, algae may become a key 
==PAGE:17==
7 
 
element in the future chemical and biochemical industries, in a wide range of sectors 
such as food, feed and pharmaceuticals [13]. 
3.2. Microalgae 
Algae are photosynthetic organisms, being able to transform sunlight, carbon dioxide 
and water into biomass [6]. They can be divided in two different groups depending on 
their size and morphology: microalgae and macroalgae. Macroalgae, so-called 
seaweeds, consist of multiple cells that are organized into diverse structures. On the 
other hand, microalgae are microscopic organisms, commonly unicellular [14]. 
Microalgae comprise a heterogeneous group that includes prokaryotic (cyanobacteria) 
and eukaryotic organisms (such as diatoms). The most significant microalgae sp. from 
a commercial point of view belong to Chlorella, Spirulina, Dunaliella and 
Haematococcus genera [15]. 
Many research efforts are focused on this group of microorganisms due to their high 
photosynthetic activity and ability to generate a considerable amount of lipids, which 
can be used as biodiesel feedstock. Moreover, they are also interesting for extraction 
of HAVPs, such as proteins and pigments [15]. 
3.2.1. Chlorella 
Chlorella corresponds to a genus of unicellular green algae. Its cells size range from 2 
to 15 μm in diameter with a round or ellipsoidal shape normally. They can be found in 
different locations such as freshwater, marine water and soil [16]. Some of the species 
that belong to this genus are Chlorella vulgaris, Chlorella kessleri, Chlorella lobophora 
and Chlorella sorokiniana. 
Their cultures are commonly grown autotrophically in flasks, bottles or photobioreactors 
in laboratories or in open ponds or raceways outdoors [17]. However, they can also be 
cultivated in heterotrophic (without light) or mixotrophic (combination of light and dark 
periods) conditions. For instance, C. sorokiniana cultured mixotrophically produce a 
higher amount of lipids [17]. Moreover, the composition of the algae is strongly related 
to the carbon source content in the medium. It has been demonstrated that, at glucose 
concentrations higher than 10 g/L, lipids productivity decreases [17]. 
Besides the light regime, other factors influence microalgae growth, such as light 
intensity, nutrient supply, pH, temperature, salinity and carbon dioxide concentration. 
[18] showed an increase in the biomass productivity of C. protothecoides when grown 
under 12/12 light/dark cycle and 5% CO2 compared to the value that was observed 
under supplementation of air.

Chlorella sp. have a wide range of current and potential applications [16]. Chlorella is 
rich in proteins, containing all the essential amino acids, thus enabling the production of 
a suitable quality single-cell protein, which can be used as a supplement in human food 
[2]. It is also abundant in fatty acids, fibres, vitamins, minerals and carotenoids. 
Carotenoids that are normally found in Chlorella comprise alpha and beta-carotenes, 
lutein, zeaxanthin, violaxanthin and neoxanthin. Astaxanthin is also detected in some 
species [16]. Carotenoids have become an important product in the pharmaceutical 
industry due to their strong antioxidant activity, making them beneficial for the human 
health. 
Due to the high oil content, Chlorella has become an interesting feedstock for biodiesel 
production. In addition, its rapid growth compared to traditional oil crops supposes 
another advantage [16]. 
==PAGE:19==
9 
 
Moreover, Chlorella, having a high growth rate and the ability to grow under severe and 
high oxidizing environmental conditions, can be utilized for biological carbon dioxide 
fixation and wastewater treatment [2], [16]. 
Chlorella has another interesting potential application. Recently, the biotechnological 
industry is paying attention to the use of microalgae as cell factories. Since they are 
eukaryotic organisms, they can synthesize proteins with proper modifications to be 
functional in humans. Furthermore, microalgae cultivation is more cost effective than 
developing mammalian cell cultures, since they are easy to grow and maintain [16]. 
3.2.2. Biogas upgrading with Chlorella 
In order to use biogas for energy purposes, a complex conditioning is required. 
Methane content, which is normally 55-60% in raw biogas, needs to be enhanced by 
removing other gases that are present in the mixture, especially CO2 and H2S [20]. 
Currently, physical and chemical methods like membrane separation and chemical 
scrubbing are employed in the industry. Although these technologies are able to get rid 
of both contaminants, they are environmentally unfriendly [21]. An alternative that is 
considered a greener and low-cost method is the application of microalgae [20]. 
An interesting point about algae biogas upgrading is the possibility to couple the 
process 
with 
valuable 
compounds 
production, 
such 
as 
carotenoids 
and 
pharmaceuticals [20]. High CO2 concentration may cause a physiological response in 
algae, which will produce several metabolites and pigments. Some of these 
compounds can be commercially attractive, such as astaxanthin, a carotenoid with a 
powerful antioxidant activity [2]. 
Besides carbon dioxide, hydrogen sulphide is another biogas pollutant that needs to be 
removed in order to achieve a high quality methane. Conde et al. [22] showed a 
reduction of CO2 and H2S employing microalgae. Cultivation of C. vulgaris with biogas 
as a carbon source produced a final gas with a composition of 97% CH4, 2.5% CO2 
and less than 0.5% H2S. The hydrogen sulphide removal could be explained by the 
need of this compound for the microalgae in order to generate essential sulphured 
amino acids. No information about oxygen generation during the process is provided in 
this paper. 
Mann et al. [20] reported CO2 and H2S reductions from 41% to 1.2% and from 438 to 0 
ppm, respectively. However, the percentage of methane in the purified biogas was 
lowered from 57.5% to 53.3-50.1% and the oxygen increased until 23.4%. Because of 
this, the gas obtained at the end of this process is not suitable for energy purposes. 
Algal-bacterial symbiosis seems to be another interesting methodology to get rid of 
both contaminants. Microalgae take the solar energy for carbon dioxide fixation, with 
oxygen generation. On the other hand, sulphur oxidizing bacteria utilizes this oxygen to 
oxidize hydrogen sulphide to sulphate. Bahr et al. [21] demonstrated an efficient biogas 
upgrade using this method in a high rate algal pond (HRAP) connected to a CO2-H2S 
absorption column. 90% of the carbon dioxide and 100% of the hydrogen sulphide 
were removed, and only 0.2% of oxygen was detected in the output gas.

3.3. Methylococcus capsulatus 
Methylotrophs are a physiological group of aerobic bacteria that utilize reduced C1 
compounds such as methane, methanol, halomethanes, methylated amines and 
methylated compounds containing sulphur for their growth [23]. 
Methanotrophic bacteria (methanotrophs) are a group of methylotrophic bacteria. 
Methanotrophs are able to use methane as their sole carbon and energy source. They 
are normally classified as Type I (gamma-proteobacteria) and Type II (alpha-
proteobacteria). One of their main differences is the metabolic pathway utilized to 
assimilate the methane-derived formaldehyde. While Type I use the ribulose 
monophosphate pathway, Type II employ the serine pathway [7] (see Figure 4). Type X 
are a Type I subdivision that express enzymes related to the serine pathway, 
characteristic of Type II methanotrophs. Methylococcus capsulatus is part of this 
subdivision [7]. 
 
Figure 4: Metabolic pathways for methane oxidation in methanotrophs (from [23]). 
Although methanotrophs were traditionally grouped into several genera within 
Proteobacteria [23], three recent and independent studies reported methanotrophs in 
the Verrumicrobia philum [24]–[26]. These findings suggest that Verrumicrobia 
methanotrophs may play an important role in global carbon cycling, since these 
microbes constitute up to 10% of all soil bacteria [27]. 
3.3.1. Methane consumption in methanotrophs 
Methane (CH4) is a colourless, odourless gas, representing the most abundant organic 
gas in the atmosphere. Its concentration has incremented from 0.75 to 1.75 ppm in the 
last three centuries [28]. Considering that it is approximately 25 times more potent as a 
greenhouse gas than CO2 [28], it supposes a relevant concern in perturbing the Earth’s 
energy balance.  
Methane sources include natural gas and biogas. Natural gas (99-100% CH4) is a non-
renewable resource, whose reserves have increased from 113 trillion m3 in 1990 to 194 
trillion m3 in 2012 [29]. On the other hand, biogas (60-70% CH4) is produced from 
anaerobic digestion of organic matter. Whereas natural gas was formed millions of 
==PAGE:21==
11 
 
years ago [30], biogas can be generated in a relatively short period of time from 
renewable feedstocks. Therefore, the second one is considered a carbon-neutral fuel. 
Methanotrophs are able to consume methane due to the presence of an enzyme 
known as methane mono-oxygenase (MMO). This enzyme may be found in two 
different forms, a particulate one (pMMO) that is membrane-associated and a soluble 
one (sMMO) within the cytoplasm [28]. pMMO contains copper at its active site [28]. 
The synthesis of one or another form depends on the copper availability in the medium. 
When the growth of methanotrophs is limited by NAD(P)H supply, the use of nitrate in 
the medium worsens this situation, since an extra NADH is required for nitrate 
reduction [31]. Moving from low to high copper concentration in the medium causes an 
improvement in the biomass production [31]. This is related to the increasing activity of 
pMMO when addition of extra copper is carried out [32]. Formation of pMMO is 
favoured by high copper/cell ratios, while sMMO synthesis occurs at low copper/cell 
ratios. Since an increase in pMMO activity causes an improvement in the biomass 
generation [31], relatively high copper/cell ratios is preferred for single-cell protein 
production. 
3.3.2.  Production of Single-cell Protein from Methylococcus capsulatus 
(Bath) 
The worrying population growth rate during the last century has considerably increased 
the food production demand, causing huge malnourishment problems especially in the 
third world. The United Nations Food and Agricultural Organization (FAO) reported an 
important decrease on the food production per capita since 1984, despite all the new 
technologies. Because of this, more than 3 billion people in the world have still nutrition 
deficiencies [33]. 
One of the several alternatives that has been developed is the use of microbes as a 
food supplement, being single cell proteins (SCPs) a considerable representative [34]. 
SCP is the name used nowadays when referring to protein extracted from microbial 
biomass, such as bacteria, algae or fungi. This term was introduced in 1968 at the 
Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) to replace others that were previously 
used, such as “microbial protein” or “petroprotein”, but not used anymore due to their 
lack of attraction [34], [35]. 
Norfem Danmark A/S developed BioProtein, a SCP obtained from a M. capsulatus 
(Bath) culture, which was approved by European Union in 1995 for use in animal and 
fish feed [36], [37]. Nowadays, other companies are also producing M. capsulatus 
derived SCP, such as Calysta (FeedKind) in USA, UniBio (UniProtein) in Denmark and 
Gaprin in Russia [38]. For the growth of the bacteria, the production process employs 
natural gas as carbon source, ammonia to provide N, phosphoric acid as source of P, 
oxygen and minerals. Temperature is set to 45ºC and pH is controlled at 6.5. After the 
fermentation, sterilization of the concentrated biomass is carried out by fast heating at 
140ºC followed by a slow cooling in order to break down the cell membrane and 
release the proteins [7]. 
One of the main disadvantages of single-cell protein production from methanotrophs is 
the poor mass transfer rate of methane from the gas phase into the cells due to the low 
==PAGE:22==
12 
 
solubility of methane in the medium, which limits their growth. However, some studies 
have shown that this problem can be reduced by the addition of paraffin oil [39].  
Moreover, another factor must be considered. Several studies found that Norferm 
Danmark A/S reactor was frequently invaded by three other cultures, a Brevibacillus 
agri strain, an Aneurinibacillus species and a Ralstonia species. It seems that these 
microbes consume some of the methanotrophic metabolites generated during the 
fermentation, which otherwise would inhibit Methylococcus growth. For example, 
acetate and other carboxylic acids are produced by the methane oxidation pathway 
and accumulate in the bioreactor, being a cause of toxicity [36]. Therefore, the 
presence of these external microbes appears to be beneficial for the process since a 
stable bacterial consortium is established [36]. 
Single-cell protein derived from M. capsulatus has been considered as a supplement in 
animal feed for dogs, minks, pigs, chickens and salmons [40], [41]. It is not employed 
as human food due to its nucleic acid content, which ranges from 8 g to 25 g/100 g cell 
protein. However, some methods to reduce RNA and DNA have been reported [42], so 
there is a big potential to use this food in humans in the near future.

5. MATERIALS AND METHODS 
 
5.1. Microorganisms 
5.1.1. Microalgal strain 
Chlorella sorokiniana was provided by DTU Environment Bioenergy Group. A pre-
culture was prepared in a 500 mL flask containing Modified WC medium + Selenium 
(Appendix A) and maintained on a platform shaker at 100 rpm under continuous 
illumination of approximately 75 μmol photons m-2 s-1 in a room controlled at 20ºC. 
Light intensity was measured by using a light meter (LI-COR LI-250 A). The same pre-
culture was used as inoculum for all the experiments. 
5.1.2. Bacterial strain 
M. capsulatus (Bath) NCIMB11132 was provided by NCIMB Culture Collection 
(GenBank accession number AE017282). The freeze-dried culture was properly 
opened and subcultured twice in nitrate mineral salts (NMS) medium (Appendix A). 
From the second subculture, several samples were taken in order to prepare glycerol 
stocks. These stocks were made by mixing 0.75 mL of a 50% glycerol solution and 
0.75 mL of the bacterial sample. Then, they were stored at -80ºC. 
A pre-culture of M. capsulatus was cultivated in a 100 mL Crimp Neck vial in 20 mL 
NMS medium with a head-space of 45% CH4, 45% air and 10% CO2.One glycerol 
stock was added into the vial. Then, the vial was closed with a rubber stopper and a 
metal ring. Vacuum was manually generated inside it by using a 100 mL syringe with a 
needle. Afterwards, gas was inserted with the same system. The pre-culture was left in 
the incubator overnight (ON). The temperature was set to 45ºC with orbital shaking at 
300 rpm. 
The next day, two vials of a second pre-culture were prepared by using the previous 
one as inoculum. In this case, 45 mL of NMS medium and 5 mL of inoculum were 
added into each vial. The rest of the procedure and the conditions were the same as 
before. 
Subsequent pre-cultures were made using inoculum from the fermenter, i.e. when the 
experiment was finished, 5 mL of the culture broth were taken with a syringe and 
injected to a vial with 45 mL of new medium.

5.3. Gas analysis 
Gas samples were analysed by using a gas cromatograph (Thermoscientific Trace 
1310 GC), with two different columns for different gases. A HP-Plot/Q column (Agilent 
Technologies, USA, length 15 m, diameter 0.32 mm, film 20 μm) was used to detect 
CH4 and CO2, and a HP-Molesieve column (Agilent Technologies, USA, length 30 m, 
diameter 0.53 mm, film 50 μm) was for O2 and CH4 analyses. 
Calibration was done manually with 3 different gases for each method (CH4 / CO2 (v/v) 
of 60/40, 40/30 and 5/5 for the first method; Air / CH4 (v/v) of 100/0 and 50/50 for the 
second), which were injected into the same 5 mL vials as the samples. Linear 
regression was performed in Microsoft Excel 2016. In order to discard the value of the 
residual gas that was inside the vials from the final result, two additional vials with pure 
N2 and CH4 were tested. In the N2 case, O2, CO2 and CH4 values were subtracted for 
all the sample vials. In the CH4, only O2 and CO2 were taken into account. 
 
5.4. Composition analysis 
5.4.1. Freeze-drying of the samples 
In the methylotrophs case, culture broth was centrifuged (Heraeus MultifugeX3R 
Centrifuge) using 50 mL Falcon tubes at 5000 rpm and 4ºC for 10 minutes. Regarding 
the microalgae, the centrifugation was carried out using the same tubes, but at 10000 
rpm and 4ºC for 10 min.  
Supernatant was discarded and pellet was kept in the freezer at -20ºC. Samples were 
lyophilised for two days at a T between -99 and -101ºC and at a pressure of 0.013-1.5 
mbar, by using a ScanVac CoolSafe freeze dryer (LaboGene ApS). The dried samples 
were then ground to fine powder using a metallic spoon before further chemical 
analyses. 
 
==PAGE:29==
19 
 
5.4.2. Total sugar 
Total sugar determination was carried out on 25 mg of the freeze dried microalgae or 
bacteria samples. Hydrolysis was performed by adding 0.25 mL of 99% H2SO4 for 30 
minutes. Then, 2.25 mL of distilled water were added and the sample was autoclaved 
for 30 minutes at 120ºC. Supernatant was collected and filtered with a 0.2 μm pore size 
syringe filter. The resulting liquid was 10 (microalgae) or 20 (bacteria) times diluted 
using MilliQ water and transferred to 2 mL ion chromatograph (IC) vials. Vials were 
analysed through IC (Thermo Scientific, Dionex ICS-5000, USA). 
5.4.3. Amino acids 
Determination of the amino acids composition was performed on 50 mg of the freeze 
dried M. capsulatus or C. sorokiniana samples. This amount was placed into a 
microwave tube and 6 mL of 6N HCL were added. The tube was flushed with nitrogen 
to prevent oxidative degradation and closed with a cap afterwards. Hydrolysis of the 
sample took place in a microwave oven (Anton Paar Multiwave 3000, AT) at 500W and 
150ºC for 30 min. After that time, HCl was removed from the tube by evaporation using 
the same microwave, at 600W for 20 min. Then, 5 mL of distilled water were added to 
the pellet and mixed. Samples were filtered by using a 0.2 μm pore size syringe filter 
and then analysed through HPLC.  
For amino acids quantification, a HPLC (Thermo Scientific Dionex UltiMate 3000 
UHPLC, USA), a c18 reversed phase column (Agilent Technologies USA Eclipse Plus 
C18) and an in-line guard column (Macherey-Nagel Germany EC 4/2 Universal RP) 
were used. Calibration was done with a commercial amino acid mix (AAS18, Sigma-
Aldrich) containing L-Alanine, L-Arginine, L-Aspartic acid, L-Cystine, L-Glutamic acid, 
Glycine, L-Histidine, L-Isoleucine, L-Leucine, L-Lysine, L-Methionine, L-Phenylalanine, 
L-Proline, L-Serine, L-Threonine, L-Tyrosine and L-Valine. 
5.4.4. Total protein 
Total protein concentration was determined by summing up all the amino acid masses 
retrieved after acid hydrolysis, minus the water mass (18 g H2O mol-1 of amino acid) 
incorporated into each amino acid after the disruption of the peptide bonds. 
5.4.5. Pigments 
Pigments analysis was performed on 2 mg of the freeze-dried algae samples. 3 mL of 
a 90:10 methanol:water (v/v) solution were added for the substrate hydrolysis prior to a 
10-minute sonication with ice. After that time, the samples were centrifuged at 14000 
rpm for 10 minutes. Supernatants were filtered by using a 0.2 μm pore size syringe 
filter and analysed through HPLC.

7. DISCUSSION 
 
7.1. Chlorella experiments 
7.1.1. Microalgal growth 
In this project, C. sorokiniana was growth using a synthetic biogas (60% CH4 and 40% 
CO2). Max. growth rate and final biomass concentration were calculated and are shown 
in Table 1. μmax was found to be 3.03 day-1, consistent with the numbers provided by 
other studies [43]. Layer et al. [44], by using the same system and microalgae species, 
but with wastewater instead of standard medium, showed a μmax of approx. 3.5 day-1. 
On the other hand, final biomass concentration (178.3 mg/L) was similar to the value 
provided by [3]. However, it was apparently really low compared to other studies ([2], 
[43], [44]). The possible reasons for these differences are probably the non-proper 
growth conditions, since the experiment carried out in this project was done in a glass 
bottle with a diffuser, without pH control, etc. Moreover, most of the mentioned studies 
were dealing with wastewater treatment. This is a really important factor, since 
wastewater may contain more nutrients than the microalgae medium (which normally 
has the minimum concentration of each nutrient), making it a better alternative for 
algae growth [3]. 
7.1.2. Gas analysis 
Gas analysis constitutes the principal element to determine the feasibility of a biogas 
upgrading process. In particular, CO2 removal is the key parameter to assess its 
efficiency. In this case, only 22.45% of the initial CO2 could be consumed from biogas. 
Removals of 90% or more have been reported by several studies using microalgae 
([21], [22], [45], [46]). However, other systems were used in those cases, such as 
continuous processes, columns, use of photobioreactors, different microalgae species, 
etc. Moreover, wastewater was used instead of medium in most of those studies. As 
mentioned before, wastewater normally provides more nutrients than the microalgae 
medium, thus allowing a higher growth. 
Layer et al. [44] obtained a CO2 removal efficiency higher than 90%. The main 
differences between this project and that study were the utilization of wastewater and a 
higher culture / headspace proportion. As said above, wastewater can be a decisive 
parameter for microalgae growth. Additionally, a reasonable balance between culture 
and headspace volumes should be established. Layer et al. [44] had a working volume 
of 400 mL (300 mL in this study) and a headspace of approx. 1.3 L (2.5 L in the 
experiments carried out here). It seems consistent that a much bigger decrease in the 
CO2 concentration was achieved, since microalgae had less gas to uptake. In order to 
have better process efficiencies, this ratio has to be changed. 
To conclude, it is important to remark that no biogas upgrading was achieved. Methane 
concentration did not have a significant change throughout the experiment. Indeed, the 
content was slightly lower at the end. In this case, CO2 consumed was converted into 
O2 through photosynthesis, so it was not a good system to upgrade biogas. However, 
==PAGE:37==
27 
 
the main purpose of these experiments was not to increase CH4 concentration, but to 
generate a proper gas for the next step (methylotrophs cultivation). 
 
7.2. Methylococcus experiments 
7.2.1. Bacterial growth 
Regarding Methyloccocus experiments, some interesting remarks can be extracted 
from the results. In this report, three different gas compositions were tested. Max. 
growth rate was significantly higher in the case where bacteria were grown in 80% CH4 
and 20% O2 (Figure 12). In that conditions, μmax nearly doubled the value obtained in 
the 60/40 and in the 95/5 experiments. This appears to be reasonable, since the 
oxygen concentration used was similar to the air. With a higher amount of O2 (40%), 
this gas seemed to cause a small growth inhibition, lowering the maximum rate. On the 
other hand, 5% O2 was relatively low, producing deficient growth conditions that were 
translated into an inadequate growth curve (see Appendix B).  
Growth rates were consistent with the ones reported by some studies, such as [47] 
(0.19 h-1) and [42] (0.15 h-1). In fact, the value provided by Larsen et al. [42] referred to 
the first single-cell protein production process (in a loop fermenter, patented by 
Jorgensen et al. [48]. Rates found in another methylotrophs genus (Methylocystis) are 
also very similar to the ones observed in this project (Wendlandt et al. [49] showed a 
value of 0.37 h-1). It is relevant to remark that all that experiments were performed with 
O2 concentrations around 15-20%. Additionally, the concentration of CH4 was in 
general much lower than the one used here (from 20 to 50%). 
When looking at final biomass concentration values, it can be seen that much more 
biomass was produced in the 60/40 and 80/20 experiments compared to the 95/5 one 
(Figure 13). As mentioned previously, the growth conditions in the last case were not 
appropriate, so a really low final biomass concentration was reached. On the other 
hand, 60/40 results should be viewed with caution. When the experiment was 
performed, vacuum was spontaneously generated inside the pump tube of one of the 
duplicates, approx. 10 hours after the start. This was translated into a lower gas 
recirculation compared to the other duplicate. As shown in Figure 18 (Appendix B), the 
SD for the last three samples of this experiment are much higher than the rest. Final 
biomass concentrations were calculated from the last sample, so a high deviation in 
this parameter is consistent. The reliable 60/40 duplicate reached a final concentration 
of 237 mg/L. This number, then, should be more conclusive than the mean value. It is 
significantly higher than the one obtained in the 80/20 experiment (132 mg/L). Although 
the growth rate was larger in the 80/20 case due to a possible O2 inhibition at the 
beginning of the 60/40, this last experiment had much more O2 available, producing 
more biomass. 
7.2.2. Gas analysis 
Oxygen was the limiting factor in the experiments carried out during this project. This 
gas was totally consumed in the 80/20 and 95/5 cases (Table 2). However, approx. 
10% of O2 still remained inside the fermenter when a gas of 60/40 was used. A 
==PAGE:38==
28 
 
possible reason for not removing all the oxygen could be the minerals depletion in the 
medium. In that situation, the stationary phase was reached slightly later than in the 
other experiments. Moreover, the final biomass concentration was higher, thus more 
cells were consuming the nutrients. When the minerals were exhausted, the bacteria 
could not continue to grow although some oxygen was still present in the headspace. 
A proper way for checking the reliability of the gas data is to look at the molar ratio 
between different gases. In the results section, O2 / CH4 and CO2 / CH4 are shown 
(Table 3). As seen, the values for the 60/40 and 80/20 experiments are quite similar in 
both parameters. On the other hand, the 95/5 numbers are really far from the rest. The 
initial O2 concentration was especially low in that case, so a small variation in the 
amount of CH4 during the experiment translates into significantly odd ratio values. 
Moreover, only one duplicate is taken into account, giving more inaccuracy. 
The oxidation of CH4 by M. capsulatus can be expressed with the following 
stoichiometry [50]: 
                𝐶𝐻4 + 0.104𝑁𝐻3 + 1.45𝑂2 →0.52𝐶𝐻1.8𝑂0.5𝑁0.2 + 0.48𝐶𝑂2 + 1.69𝐻2𝑂           (4) 
According to this equation, O2 / CH4 and CO2 / CH4 molar ratios are 1.45 and 0.48, 
respectively. When comparing them to the results obtained in this project, a notable 
similarity is found. Oxygen / methane ratios from 60/40 and 80/20 cases are very close 
to the theoretical one, meaning that the gases variation during these experiments was 
coherent. They also resemble the values obtained by Leak et al. [31], which range from 
1.4 to 1.5.  
On the contrary, the production of CO2 was lower than the expected in all three 
experiments. The numbers determined in this study here were half of the theoretical 
one (0.48, from eq. (4)). A possible explanation could be the ability of methanotrophs to 
consume carbon dioxide. Indeed, γ-proteobacteria like Methylococcus can uptake up to 
15% of their biomass from this gas [7]. A partial consumption of the CO2 generated 
during the experiment from M. capsulatus could justify these low values, since less CO2 
at the end could be plausible. 
 
7.3. Coupled experiment 
7.3.1. Growth 
The ultimate objective of the project was to combine Chlorella and Methylococcus 
experiments in a single process to assess its viability. Microalgal and bacterial ODs are 
shown in Figure 20 (in Appendix B). As mentioned previously, initially only the 
microalgae cultivation was carried out in order to generate a sufficient amount of O2, 
required for the methylotrophs growth. C. sorokiniana growth was quite similar to the 
ones observed in the individual Chlorella experiments. Max. growth rate in this case 
(3.97 day-1, from Table 4) was slightly, but not significantly, higher. 
Comparing M. capsulatus to the previous experiment, a relevant lag phase could be 
appreciated. A possible explanation for this unusual long adaptation to the medium 
could be the non-ideal conditions of the inoculum. As explained in Materials and 
==PAGE:39==
29 
 
Methods section, single Methylococcus experiments were performed by taking the 
inoculum from the previous experiments. In this way, the bacteria were at the end of 
the exponential phase and well-adapted to the system. In the couple experiment, 
however, the inoculum was prepared by making two pre-cultures in small bottles due to 
time constraints. Therefore, a more extended lag phase could be expected. Although 
this extra period at the start of the bacteria cultivation, μmax (0.154 h-1) was consistent 
with the values found previously. 
When looking at final biomass concentrations (Table 4), M. capsulatus value was found 
to be close to the previous experiments. On the other hand, microalgal biomass at the 
end was lower than expected. As seen in Figure 20 (in Appendix B), microalgae 
cultivation was left much more time after the stationary phase was reached. A 
significant death phase can be appreciated, thus obtaining less biomass is reasonable. 
7.3.2. Gas analysis 
The variation of the different gases during the experiment is shown in Figure 14. CO2 
removal during the first stage of the process was higher than the number achieved in 
the Chlorella experiments. Max. growth rate in this case was also higher, so this could 
be a possible reason for a bigger CO2 consumption. However, since no duplicate was 
performed, this value should be viewed with caution. 
It can be seen that O2 concentration at the start of the methylotrophs growth was 
approx. 10%. When the experiment was concluded, oxygen was not totally consumed 
although the bacteria had reached the stationary phase. As observed before 
(Methylococcus experiments), initial O2 concentrations of 20 and 5% led to a complete 
removal of this gas in the system. The main difference of the coupled experiment 
compared to the previous ones in terms of gas concentrations was the presence of a 
considerable amount of CO2 (almost 30%) when M. capsulatus was inoculated. This 
high CO2 concentration could somehow inhibit its growth, thus not allowing to uptake all 
the possible O2 present in the headspace. No studies were found in the literature using 
this initial concentration of carbon dioxide. However, Al Taweel et al. [50] suggested 
that CO2 produced during a M. capsulatus cultivation can affect the mass transfer rate 
of CH4 and O2 from the gas to the liquid phase. In this way, the presence of these two 
gases would be reduced in the medium if CO2 is also present, thus decreasing their 
availability to the cells. With a high amount of carbon dioxide, as the one in the 
experiment carried out here, this negative effect could be aggravated. 
 
7.4. Composition analysis 
Characterization of the biomass obtained during the experiments is an important 
parameter to determine the potential use of this biomass. 
In the case of C. sorokiniana, sugars, proteins (Table 5) and pigments (Figure 32 in 
Appendix D) were tested. Total sugars accounted for more than 40% of DW, being 
glucose the most important sugar by far, with up to 40g / 100g DW (Figure 26 in 
Appendix D). This is a consistent number compared to other studies (Jazzar et al. [51] 
==PAGE:40==
30 
 
reported values ranging from 30 to 40% of DW on a newly isolated C. sorokiniana 
strain). 
When looking at the total protein content, it represents approx. 10% of DW both in the 
individual and the coupled experiments. This value is lower than the one showed by 
Matsukawa et al. [52] and the one reported by Layer et al. [44]. Jazzar et al. [51], on 
the other hand, found a more similar number (approx. 20% of DW). 
Regarding the pigments content, unusual low values were found (Figure 32 in Appendix 
D) compared to Layer et al. [44]. However, as explained, wastewater was used in those 
experiments. The pigments content is highly determined by the microalgae growth 
conditions. Some factors such as high illumination, high salt content, N deficiency and 
high CO2 concentration can induce the cell to produce more carotenoids and 
antioxidants [52]. Therefore, the use of wastewater instead of medium can change 
significantly the pigments composition in microalgae. 
On the other hand, sugars and proteins were tested in M. capsulatus as well. 
Concerning the sugars, a slightly higher value could be found in the 80/20 experiment 
(Table 5). These numbers are consistent with the sugars composition of UniProtein 
(approx. 5% of DW) [53]. 
Proteins content in M. capsulatus is the most important parameter, since determine its 
potential as SCP. No significant differences were found between the 60/40 and the 
80/20 experiments. The number obtained in the 95/5 case was extremely low due to 
the poor quality biomass obtained after the experiment. The values showed in this 
report are much lower to the ones reported in other studies [41], as well as to the 
protein composition of UniProtein (72.9%) [53]. It is important to remark that not all 20 
amino acids were quantified in this project (Gln, Asn and Trp were not included), so the 
real values for total protein should be slightly higher than the ones reported. However, 
these values would be not sufficiently high. As commented above, total protein content 
for microalgae was found to be unusually low as well. Therefore, a possible reason for 
these poor values could be a non-proper optimization of the protein analysis performed 
in the project. 
 
7.5. Overall biorefinery process 
Figure 15 shows the biorefinery process scheme with the values obtained from the 
coupled experiment.  
C. sorokiniana showed an interesting capability for removing CO2 from synthetic biogas 
(removal of 41.8%). The gas generated in that step contained approx. 10% O2, which 
was enough to allow M. capsulatus growth. 
With 2.5 L of synthetic biogas as input, final microalgal and bacterial biomass 
concentrations were 130 mg/L (11.5% protein) and 183.3 mg/L (35.7% protein), 
respectively. Moreover, pigments content of microalgae was also tested, with low 
values compared with the literature. 
==PAGE:41==
31 
 
To sum up, the products obtained in the overall process were microalgal and bacterial 
biomass, and a biogas with a reduced amount of CO2. Microalgae have a wide range of 
potential applications, from feedstock for biodiesel production to cell factories for 
pharmaceuticals generation. On the other hand, methylotrophs can be further treated 
and used as SCPs. 
This approach is thought to be an interesting process for a more sustainable SCP 
production. However, it needs to be optimized in order to obtain higher amounts of 
biomass and less residual gas, since the CO2 could be totally consumed by the 
microalgae.

8. CONCLUSIONS 
 
In general, the experiments performed during the thesis were considered successful. In 
particular, the coupled experiment showed a potential biorefinery strategy by 
integrating two different microbial processes. 
The first step involved the autotrophic cultivation of the microalga C. sorokiniana in 
medium, with synthetic biogas as a carbon source. Individual experiments showed a 
relatively low CO2 removal efficiency, final biomass concentration and proteins and 
pigments content. As commented previously, the selection of the cultivation medium is 
a decisive factor for microalgae growth, highly influencing the values obtained. 
The second stage consisted of a M. capsulatus culture. Three different initial gas 
compositions were tested in the individual experiments. Results showed a significantly 
higher growth efficiency when O2 concentration was 20%. However, the highest 
biomass concentration at the end of the experiment was obtained at 40% O2. A 
composition of 95% CH4 and 5% O2 resulted in a poor quality biomass recovered from 
the fermenter.  
When looking at bacterial composition profile, sugar contents were consistent with the 
values found in the literature. However, total proteins were lower compared to 
commercial SCPs. 
Finally, a coupled experiment was carried out as an initial, lab-scale attempt to see the 
viability of the integrated process. It was demonstrated that the output gas coming from 
the microalgae step could be fed into the methylotrophs system to produce bacterial 
biomass. Therefore, this novel strategy seems to be a feasible and more sustainable 
alternative for SCP production, with a potential to be integrated within a biorefinery 
approach. In order to further study and optimize the process, some suggestions are 
proposed: 
 
Use of wastewater for microalgae cultivation, since it is a more efficient medium 
according to the literature. 
 
Testing of other microalgae species. 
 
Selection of a proper microbial cultures / headspace ratio. Ratios between the 
working volumes (microalgae and bacteria) and the gas phase should be 
optimized to obtain the highest possible biomass amount in both processes. 
 
Finding of the appropriate time for methylotrophs inoculation. Once the 
microalgae cultivation has started, gas composition in the system should be 
monitored to know which is the correct moment to add the bacteria into the 
fermenter. 
 
Testing of real biogas. In this project, synthetic biogas was used instead of the 
real one. However, some compounds such as hydrogen sulphide (H2S) may 
have inhibitory effects in microalgae and methylotrophs growth. 
 
Study of the economic feasibility of the process. While M. capsulatus is 
primarily used as SCP, microalgae have a wide range of potential applications. 
==PAGE:43==
33 
 
Therefore, the utilization of the microalgae produced is a key factor to 
determine if the overall process is economically viable.
