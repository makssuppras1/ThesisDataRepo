Abstract
The use of robotics and automation facilitates high-throughput cell engineering,
enabling the heterologous expression of proteins at scale. Building upon the work
done by Ph.D. Aske Unger, in which a split-luciferase based, high-throughput
protein expression pipeline was developed for E. coli, this work contributed by
modifying the pipeline to function with S. cerevisiae. This was done by design-
ing and assembling yeast-optimized expression vectors by utilising USER cloning,
and introducing a selection of proteins using Golden Gate cloning and restriction
enzyme based techniques. The expression cassette enabled the quantification of
target protein expression from S. cerevisiae lysates, measuring the relative lu-
minescent output caused by the degradation of coelenterazine by a NLuc/TLuc
split-luciferase complex, emitting luminescent light in the 460nm emission range.
The results showed that the expression vectors were functional for the expression
of both yeGFP and TrxA in a low-throughput setting. However, the results for
the truncated protein library CALM1 suggested that cloning strategy has to be
optimized further in order for these constructs to work at a high-throughput ca-
pacity. This was evident from a low CFU for S. cerevisiae CEN.PK113-7D as well
as a low luminescent output. Different targets for improvements include codon
optimization of the inserts and the entire expression cassette and its components,
as well as testing protein fusion linkers that are optimized for yeast.
With continued research and optimisation, the pipeline has potential applications
in a wide range of areas including drug discovery and biomedical research, with the
objective of establishing a low-cost and robust protein expression data-generating
pipeline that enables the analysis of hundreds of thousands of samples from differ-
ent organisms.

Introduction
With recent advancements in computing technology and increased utilization of
machine learning, scientists have been enabled to interpret and modify biological
data at an ever-increasing scale. It started with the advent of sequencing tech-
nology and since has evolved into the fields of bioinformatics and systems biology,
where modelling and design of complex biological systems has been made possible
[1]. A recent example of a breakthrough technology that is centered around com-
putational biology is AlphaFold developed by Google DeepMind, which is able to
predict the primary, secondary and tertiary structures of proteins based solely off
of their amino acid sequence [2]. This technology has been made possible due to
the vast amounts of shared protein data that laboratories around the world sub-
mit to centralized networks like NCBI, UniProt and others. There is strength in
numbers and quantity, but one of the primary challenges with protein expression
and structure data is that laboratories may have vastly different methodologies
and equipment that all flavour the resulting datasets in different ways. There is
a need for a unified, standardized method for creating protein expression data
that can reach a high enough volume to be universally used and tested against
computational tools like AlphaFold [3].
This is the vision that Ph.D. Aske Unger and his research group at the Techni-
cal University of Denmark had in mind when developing a split-luciferase based,
high-throughput truncation library pipeline that will be presented and utilised
throughout this work. The overarching goal is to establish a low-cost and robust
protein expression data generating pipeline that allows for scientists to sample
hundreds of thousands of samples from a single laboratory. Aske Unger developed
this pipeline for Escherichia coli (E. coli), and this work is concerned with the
expansion of this concept into Saccharomyces cerevisiae (S. cerevisiae).
1
==PAGE:12==
2
Aim of study
The aim of this work is to aid in the development of split-luciferase based high-
throughput (HTP) protein expression libraries.
Specifically, the HTP split-luciferase pipeline developed by Ph.D. Aske Unger in
E. coli is modified for protein expression in S. cerevisiae. This is achieved by
first designing and assembling suitable expression vectors for the split-luciferase
cassette, followed by the insertion of various genes into the cassette to assess the
feasibility of the design as a proof of concept. Finally, a truncated protein library
is inserted into the cassette and screened in semi-high throughput.
3
Experimental strategy
Expression vector and cassette design
Using in silico molecular biology tools, the expression vectors are designed to first
function in E. coli for plasmid propagation, and later in S. cerevisiae for protein
expression. This is controlled from host-specific promoters. The expression vec-
tors are designed from various vector backbone parts, and long Polymerase chain
reaction (PCR) primers bridge the gaps and add additional functionality. Within
each vector is the split-luciferase expression cassette, which is designed to only
express proteins in S. cerevisiae.
Expression vector assembly
The expression vectors are designed to assemble from individual PCR fragments
with uracil-specific excision reagent (USER) cloning tails. The fragments are PCR
amplified from various backbone parts and long primers, each harboring a single
uracil residue. Thus with USER treatment, the fragments fuse together scarlessly
2
==PAGE:13==
and can be transformed into E. coli for propagation.
Low-throughput proof of concept in yeast
To test the feasibility of the design, a yeast-optimized green fluorescent protein
(yeGFP) is Golden Gate cloned into the expression cassette and fused to the small
subunit of the split-luciferase.
The protein expression is quantified as relative
fluorescent units (RFU) and relative luminescent units (RLU), measured on a
spectrophotometer. This is repeated for a second gene of interest (GOI), thiore-
doxin A (TrxA), quantified only as RLU.
Semi high-throughput in yeast using liquid handling robots
In order to test expression vectors in S. cerevisiae in a semi high-throughput en-
vironment, a truncated Calmodulin-1 (CALM1) protein library is Golden Gate
cloned into the expression cassette. The Opentrons II (OT-2) and Tecan robots
are used for liquid handling and protein expression is quantified in RLU, each sam-
ple harboring a unique and randomized truncated part of the protein of interest.

Background
4.1
Microbial cell factories
Microbial cell factories are genetically engineered microorganisms cultivated to
produce valuable, biobased compounds such as pharmaceuticals, biofuels, small
molecules and more [4]. Scientists are now harnessing the natural capabilities of
microorganisms by manipulating their genetics and metabolism, creating highly
efficient and sustainable microbial factories that offer numerous advantages over
traditional chemical synthesis [4].
Microbial cell factories also offer significant
environmental benefits, and by utilizing renewable feedstocks and implementing
bioprocesses, the production of compounds in cell factories can have a lower carbon
footprint compared to traditional manufacturing methods [4]. This holds a great
promise for a greener future and aligns with the Sustainable Development Goals
(SDG) of achieving more sustainable industrial production practices [5]. There
is an ever-increasing selection of species that are being explored in research as
potential chassis for microbial cell factories, and a few of the model species include
Bacillus subtilis and Aspergillus oryzae. However, the two most commonly used
cell factories to this day remain E. coli and S. cerevisiae.
4.1.1
E. coli
E. coli is a versatile Gram-negative bacterium that has been a stable in research
and bioproduction for decades. With its well-characterized genetics and robust
growth capabilities, E. coli has been extensively engineered to produce a wide
range of valuable compounds, including pharmaceuticals, biofuels, as well as in-
dustrial chemicals and small molecules [6].
The advantages of using E. coli as a cell factory is its ability to exhibit high
4
==PAGE:15==
production yields and fast growth kinetics even at high cell density cultures [6].
Furthermore, its metabolic pathways can be precisely manipulated to optimize the
production efficiency of target compounds through a wide array of genetic tools
and techniques, and a vast catalogue of expression vectors are available [6]. E.
coli is very commonly used for plasmid propagation due to it’s fast doubling time
(20 minutes under optimal conditions), its ability to quickly reach high cell densi-
ties in cheap complex media. Additionally, E. coli is relatively quick and easy to
transform, with as low as 5 minutes needed [6].
E. coli DH5α
One of the most commonly used strains in molecular biology and genetic engi-
neering research is E. coli DH5α. It is derived from E. coli K12 and has several
genetic modifications that make it a convenient host for cloning deoxyribonucleic
acid (DNA) and plasmid propagation. E. coli DH5α was developed by Douglas
Hanahan in the 1980’s and is still to this day a stable in many laboratories around
the world ([7], [8]).
A few important genetic features of E. coli DH5α are deficiencies in the endonu-
clease genes recA1 and endA1. The mutation in recA1 eliminates homologous re-
combination in the strain, and the mutation in endA1 inactivates an extracellular
nuclease that would otherwise degrade plasmid DNA after cell lysis. Collectively,
their absence in DH5α reduces the frequency of unwanted recombination events
during cloning procedures, and increases insert stability and transformation effi-
ciency [7]. Furthermore, E. coli DH5α carries a mutation in hsdR17, eliminating
the degradation of certain types of unmethylated DNA, which is favourable when
dealing with different types of expression vectors [9].
5
==PAGE:16==
E. coli BL21(DE3)
Another commonly used strain in molecular biology, and especially for protein
expression studies, is E. coli BL21(DE3). It is derived from E. coli B and has
specific genetic modifications that make it suitable for the inducible expression of
recombinant proteins. One key genetic features of BL21(DE3) is the presence of
the T7 RNA polymerase gene (encoded by the DE3 lysogen) under the control of
the lacUV5 promoter. This allows for the tightly regulated expression of target
genes when induced with lactose or isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG
([6],[10]). The T7 RNA polymerase specifically recognizes and transcribes DNA
sequences containing a T7 promoter, enabling efficient production of recombinant
proteins. Another feature is that BL21(DE3) is Lon and OmpT protease deficient,
which ensures that extracellular proteins are not degraded after cell lysis [6]. The
E. coli B lineage, which includes BL21(DE3), also carries a mutation in ∆(ara-
leu)7697, which prevents the digestion of L-arabinose and L-leucine. This mutation
simplifies the growth medium composition and facilitates the use of minimal media
for protein expression studies ([6], [11]). BL21(DE3) is inducible using the Studier
autoinduction medium ZYP-5052, which is a balanced mix of glucose and lactose.
BL21(DE3) cells first consume glucose and then switch over to lactose inducing
expression the lacUV5 promoter, and thus, enabling the production of recombinant
proteins from the T7 promoter [10].
4.1.2
S. cerevisiae
Yeast is a diverse group of unicellular organisms that belongs to the fungal king-
dom. However, when referring to ”yeast,” it often specifically means S. cerevisiae,
commonly known as baker’s yeast. This particular yeast species holds immense
significance and is widely utilized due to its various and diverse applications in
society [4].
6
==PAGE:17==
For centuries, S. cerevisiae has played a crucial role in the production of consumer
products like alcoholic beverages such as wine and beer, as well as being a key
ingredient in bread-making, functioning as a leavening agent. It also contributes
to the taste, texture, and nutritional value of various fermented foods [4].
In more recent times, S. cerevisiae has gone beyond its traditional uses in food
products, and has emerged as a laboratory model organism and an industrial
workhorse. It plays a significant role in the production of biofuels, bioethanol, in-
dustrial chemicals, enzymes, and biopharmaceuticals. The extensive history of safe
use of S. cerevisiae has facilitated obtaining governmental approvals, as processes
involving this yeast often receive Generally Regarded As Safe (GRAS) status [4].
S. cerevisiae is highly valued as a cell factory due to its rapid growth, with a
doubling time of only 90 minutes, making it an efficient producer. Furthermore, S.
cerevisiae has relatively low-cost media requirements and demonstrates resilience
in harsh fermentation conditions. These characteristics make it a preferred choice
for industrial applications, still to this day [4].
The study of S. cerevisiae has led to significant advancements in our understand-
ing of fundamental biological processes. For example, research in S. cerevisiae has
contributed to elucidating the mechanisms of cell cycle control, DNA replication,
and cellular aging. It has also provided insights into the regulation of gene expres-
sion and the cellular response to environmental stress. Moreover, scientists have
developed an extensive genetic toolbox and advanced techniques to manipulate
and modify the yeast’s genetic material for heterologous expression of valuable
compounds [4].
7
==PAGE:18==
S. cerevisiae CEN.PK113-7D
S. cerevisiae CEN.PK113-7D is widely used in research due to its close genetic
resemblance to both laboratory strains like S288C, but also to widely used indus-
trial strains. This gives it unique genetic characteristics that makes it suitable
for both laboratory studies, but also geared towards industrial applications [12].
CEN.PK113-7D also has a stable genetic background, making it easy to manipu-
late and minimizing genetic mutations during experiments. This stability ensures
reproducibility and reliability in strain development and engineering efforts ([12],
[13]).
4.2
Genetic engineering
Molecular cloning techniques have made significant advancements since the dis-
covery and early stage application of the first restriction endonucleases back in the
1970s. Recombinant DNA technologies and heterologous expression is now com-
monly practiced, and standard procedures in this field include DNA isolation and
amplification, PCR, molecular cloning, and more recently, genome editing using
methods such as CRISPR/Cas9 and its related technologies [14]. This combined
with the ability to affordably synthesize and sequence large DNA fragments has
kickstarted an era of accessible bioengineering and synthetic biology, which to-
day encompasses a wide range of applications including the production of small
molecules, biopharmaceuticals and agricultural products. It continues to evolve
and expand as scientists develop new tools and techniques for designing and build-
ing biological systems, and find new applications for its use. The advancements
in molecular cloning techniques have played a crucial role in this development,
enabling scientists to manipulate DNA and construct new genetic sequences with
precision and efficiency [15].
8
==PAGE:19==
When it comes to targeted molecular engineering of cell factories like E. coli and
S. cerevisiae, recombinant plasmids are widely utilised and remain a crucial tool.
Plasmids are small, circular, double-stranded DNA molecules separate from the
chromosomal DNA in a cell. They replicate independently from the chromosome,
carrying additional genes that provide advantages to the host organism. Engi-
neers can manipulate these vectors by the introduction of expression cassettes.
Expression cassettes harbor one or more genes of interest, which are expressed
from open reading frames (ORF) under expressional control from promoters and
terminators. This mechanism is what makes plasmids suitable as vectors for the
transfer of foreign DNA into a host cell, enabling the production of recombinant
proteins [16]. There is a variety of cloning strategies to accomplish this, but this
work focuses on restriction and ligation-based cloning techniques such as restric-
tion enzyme digestion and Golden Gate (GG) cloning, as well as USER cloning
[14].
4.2.1
Restriction enzymes
Restriction enzymes, also known as restriction endonucleases, are enzymes that
recognize specific DNA motifs and cleave it. They are naturally occurring proteins
found in bacteria and yeasts, and they function as a defense mechanism against in-
fections [16]. Restriction enzymes are essential tools in molecular biology research
and genetic engineering, as they are used to cut DNA molecules at specific loca-
tions, creating ”sticky ends” or ”blunt ends”. These ends can then be joined with
other DNA fragments through the process of ligation, requiring a ligase enzyme,
or through homologous recombination by the cell iteself. This mechanism allows
scientists to manipulate and rearrange DNA sequences, facilitating the cloning of
genes, construction of recombinant DNA molecules, and analysis of DNA struc-
9
==PAGE:20==
tures [16].
There are three types of restriction enzymes: type I, type II and type III and they
vary slightly in their mechanism of action. Type II restriction enzymes are the
most commonly used restriction enzymes in molecular biology, as they generally
cleave DNA within or in close proximity to their recognition sequences. These
recognition sites are usually palindromic, meaning they read the same forward and
backward on complementary DNA strands. For example, the restriction enzyme
BsaI recognizes the DNA sequence 5’-GGTCTC(N) 1-5’-GAGACC-3’ and cleaves
between the G and the A, producing sticky ends with overhanging DNA sequences
[16]. Restriction enzymes serve as the basis for some of the most effective and
precise cloning techniques, for instance GG cloning [17].
4.2.2
USER cloning
USER Cloning is a molecular cloning technique that allows for the seamless as-
sembly of one or more DNA fragments through the use of uracil DNA glyco-
sylase (UDG) and endonuclease VIII (EndoVIII) enzymes [18]. This method is
particularly useful for the construction of recombinant DNA molecules without
the need for restriction enzymes or DNA ligase.
The basic principle of USER
cloning involves the incorporation of uracil residues into DNA fragments during
PCR amplification. This is achieved by using primers that contain a deoxyuridine
triphosphate (dUTP) instead of deoxythymidine triphosphate (dTTP). The re-
sulting PCR products contain uracil residues in place of thymine. Next, the PCR
products are treated with UDG, which specifically recognizes and removes uracil
residues from DNA. This creates abasic sites (apurinic/apyrimidinic sites) in the
DNA fragments. The abasic sites are then recognized and cleaved by the EndoVIII
enzyme, generating single-stranded overhangs. The DNA fragments with comple-
10
==PAGE:21==
mentary overhangs can now be annealed and ligated by the cell itself to form a
recombinant DNA molecule. The resulting construct can be further amplified or
used for downstream applications ([19],[20]). The general mechanism of USER
cloning is illustrated in figure 1.

USER cloning offers several advantages over traditional restriction enzyme-based
cloning methods. It allows for the seamless assembly of DNA fragments without
the need for compatible restriction sites. It also enables the precise and scarless
assembly of DNA fragments, as the uracil residues are efficiently removed and re-
placed with the correct nucleotides during DNA replication. Additionally, USER
cloning is highly efficient and can be easily scaled up for high-throughput applica-
tions.
This technique was first introduced by Bitinaite et al. in 2007. Since then, it has
been widely adopted in molecular biology research and has been successfully ap-
plied in various fields, including synthetic biology, genetic engineering, and protein
expression studies [18].
4.2.3
Golden Gate cloning
GG cloning is a widely used molecular cloning technique that allows for the efficient
assembly of multiple DNA fragments in a single reaction. This method is based on
the use of Type IIS restriction enzymes, such as BsaI or BsmBI, which cut DNA
sequences outside of their recognition sites [17].
In Golden Gate cloning, each DNA fragment to be assembled is flanked by specific
recognition sequences for the Type IIS restriction enzyme. These recognition se-
quences contain the cleavage sites for the enzyme. The fragments are then mixed
together with a DNA ligase enzyme and the Type IIS restriction enzyme. The
Type IIS restriction enzyme recognizes and cuts the DNA at its specific recog-
nition sequences, generating compatible overhangs on each fragment. The DNA
ligase enzyme then catalyzes the ligation of the fragments, resulting in the as-
sembly of a larger DNA construct [17]. The general mechanism of GG cloning is
illustrated in figure 2.

This technique offers several advantages. First, it allows for the seamless assembly
of multiple DNA fragments without the need for traditional restriction enzyme-

based cloning, which often requires compatible restriction sites on the fragments.
Second, GG cloning enables the precise and scarless assembly of DNA fragments,
as the Type IIS restriction enzymes cut outside of their recognition sites [17].
Third, it is a highly efficient and versatile method, suitable for the construction
of complex genetic circuits, synthetic biology applications, and gene expression
studies [21].
GG cloning has been widely used in synthetic biology and molecular biology re-
search. It has been applied in the construction of genetic circuits, creation of syn-
thetic biological systems, and engineering of novel genetic pathways. The method
was first introduced by Engler et al. in 2008 [17]. Since then, it has been further
optimized and expanded upon, with various protocols and variants developed to
suit different experimental needs ([21],[22]).
4.3
High-throughput cell engineering
The field of biotechnology has been revolutionized by the emergence of high-
throughput engineering.
Scientists have been enabled to analyze, manipulate,
and cultivate cells at a larger scale which, coupled with advancements in computer
science and informatics, has led to the development of novel strategies in molecular
biology. The interdisciplinary field of systems biology has emerged from this, inte-
grating information from genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, and other related
fields to analyze complex interactions within biological systems [1].
High-throughput engineering has also facilitated exploration in cell genetics and
genomics, allowing simultaneous analysis of DNA or RNA from numerous cells
to identify genetic mutations and variations. This comprehensive exploration has
deepened the understanding of cell mechanisms and unlocked their potential for
diverse applications. By leveraging automated screening techniques, scientists can
15
==PAGE:26==
examine thousands of cell strains concurrently, identifying optimal conditions for
cell growth and metabolism to enhance efficiency and productivity. The standard-
ization of parameters and advancements in sequencing, computational power, and
automation hardware have further improved success rates and made automation
more feasible in fields like biotechnology [1].
4.3.1
Robotics and automation
Automation has transformed the entire industrial landscape by replacing manual,
human labour with machines and computational tools. This in turn has resulted in
an enhancement of production rates, efficiency, quality and safety for companies
in various technological sectors that have embraced these tools [23]. Industrial
biotechnology is no exception, where large-scale fermentation and purification is
operated with a great degree of automation through the use of sensors and com-
putational tools like digital twins [24].
And while ”automated” tools that are
considered ”high-throughput” like PCR thermal cyclers, spectrophotometers and
cell sorters are routinely used in the research environments and academic institu-
tions, they are typically highly specialised to perform one or very few tasks, and
generally do not connect into a larger automated workflow. Furthermore, many of
the most basic experimental procedures are still largely carried out manually or
could be done more efficiently with robotics [23]. This tendency is exacerbated in
a literature analysis which concluded that more than 89% of published research
articles described manual protocols that had automated alternatives [25].
However, the reality is that while the use of robots offers many advantages like
reducing variation, contamination risks and increasing output, it still requires hu-
man creativity and ingenuity to establish robotic workflows. Researchers must
possess knowledge about both hardware, programming and life science, making
16
==PAGE:27==
the adoption of robotics in academic research a challenging endeavour. Coupled
with the fact that fully automated solutions in life science are generally upwards
of hundredths of thousands of euros in purchase price plus cost of maintenance, re-
pairs or custom features, it explains why many processes remain reliant on manual
labour [23].
Nevertheless, those who are able to integrate automation and robotics into their
workflows can reap the benefits of increased output and reproducibility through a
wide array of available technologies [26]. This project focuses on a small selection
of liquid handling robots and their utility.
Liquid handling robots
Liquid handling robots are ideal for automating laboratory work. Two of the most
well-known and state-of-the-art brands are Tecan and Hamilton, both of which are
advanced robotic systems designed for automated liquid handling tasks [26]. These
robots utilize precision pipetting technology to accurately dispense and transfer
liquids in various scientific applications. They have multipipettes for easy pipet-
ting between different types of plates and reservoirs. They also have a variety of
sensors for measuring collisions and liquid properties, ensuring precise measure-
ments. Both of these robots require programming through their own proprietary
software, and are not programmable using open-source programming languages
[26].
For simpler liquid handling needs, the Opentrons II (OT-2) robot is an ideal choice.
It is programmed in Python, making it possible for the user to create their own
scripts [26]. OT-2 does not have attached sensors but relies on defined coordinates
on the plate for reliable operation with minimal preparation and easy error han-
dling. This robot is suitable for smaller automation setups like microtiter plate
17
==PAGE:28==
filling and high throughput PCR preparations [26].
Another multipurpose robot is the Qpix 400-series colony picker (Molecular De-
vices), and it is primarily used for colony picking and cell transfer between plates.
Using needles and a camera, it can analyze agar plates, providing information
about cell shape and color. The Qpix colony picker utilizes 96 air pressure-based
needles to transfer colonies to a microtiter plate. It also offers honeycomb-shaped
needles for plastic-free inoculation between microtiter plates [26].
Combined, these robots offer most of the utility needed to run an automated,
self-contained mini-laboratory.
4.4
Expression platform
4.4.1
Split-luciferase system
Bioluminescence is a phenomenon that has intrigued scientists for decades, and
in nature, it is commonly found in organisms with varying functions such as self-
defense, communication or attracting prey, among others [27]. The mechanism
behind bioluminescence is the conversion of a substrate by a luciferase enzyme,
causing an emission of light. Some of the most well-known examples of this in
nature includes the firefly luciferase, Gaussia luciferase and Renilla luciferase, but a
recent example, and the most effective version yet, is the invention of the NanoLuc
Luciferase (NLuc) system. NLuc catalyzes the conversion of Coelenterazine (CTZ)
as well as its closely related derivative Furimazine in the presence of molecular
oxygen, producing visible light at an emission wavelength of 460nm [27]. NLuc is
smaller than its counterparts, 19 kDa, and requires no co-factors to work which
makes it especially useful in protein research.
A reporter system has been created using the NLuc luciferase, coined ’split-luciferase’,
18
==PAGE:29==
and it works by splitting the luciferase into a large and small fragment: NLuc and
TurboLuc luciferase (TLuc). The small fragment is then fused to a protein of inter-
est (POI), and the large fragment is supplemented along with a substrate such as
CTZ, producing a luminescent output relative to the amount of POI present [28].
In order to fuse the small fragment of NLuc to a POI, a fusion linker is necessary.
Fusion linkers are small peptides which link two or more protein moeities together,
and there are three general types of linkers found in nature: flexible linkers, rigid
linkers and in vivo cleavable linkers. They each have their own characteristics and
mechanism of action, which becomes important when designing recombinant pro-
tein fusions [29]. For the split-luciferase reporter system, a rigid proline linker was
determined by Aske Unger to be the best fit among four different tested linkers,
as it conferred a low amount of signal bias and had a relatively simple design [3].
4.4.2
Expression cassette and vectors
An expression cassette was designed by Aske Unger in order to quantify POI
expression using the split-luciferase system. The small NLuc fragment was placed
in front of a proline fusion linker which was placed directly in front of a Golden
Gate site. This ensured that after Golden Gate cloning, the proline linker would
connect the POI and the NLuc small fragment. Finally, the cassette was placed
between two his-tags. Upon expression of the cassette, the expressed GOI is fused
to the linker and NLuc, and with the addition of TLuc from a buffer buffer solution
as well as substrate (CTZ), degradation of CTZ is catalyzed by the NLuc/TLuc
complex, causing a luminescent output (Figure 3).

4.4.3
ORF ESPRIT truncation libraries
Open reading frame expression of soluble proteins by random truncation (ORF-
ESPRIT) is an enzymatic protein library generation method [37]. The method
works by enzymatically truncating a GOI using a Mung Bean nuclease and ex-
onuclease III bidirectionally, thus randomly cleaving the gene fragment at both
termini. The exonuclease II cuts the enzyme first, and the Mung Bean nuclease
creates blunt ends that can be ligated together. To select for truncated genes only
harboring an ORF, inteins are fused to either side of the cassette. An intein is a
part of a protein that is able to splice itself out, and it is commonly used to excise
an insert during protein fusions. Furthermore, a fragmented β-lactamase is placed
outside of the inteins, ensuring that uninterrupted translations of the cassette are
excised, causing the formation of the β-lactamase. This ultimately means that
only cells harboring truncated cassettes with an ORF are ampicillin resistant [37].
The ORF-ESPRIT method was further modified by Aske Unger for high-throughput
applications by incorporating a GG site in the ORF-ESPRIT cassette [3]. This
ensures that the truncated gene can be excised and cloned directly into an ex-
pression vector harboring the split-luciferase system mentioned in section 4.4.1.
This means that during high-throughput library screenings, the luminescent out-
put infers about the amount of expression of a given truncated protein (Figure

Engineering targets
4.5.1
Green Fluorescent Protein (yeGFP)
One of the most widely used reporter proteins is the Green Fluorescent Protein
(GFP), originally isolated from jellyfish [38]. It is a relatively large protein com-
prised of 238 amino acid residues (27 kDa), and it emits green fluorescence during
excitation in the blue light spectrum ranging from 395-489nm. It is commonly
used for expression studies as a proof of concept due it its traceability even in
22
==PAGE:33==
the absence of other reporters, such as luminescence, and a yeast codon-optimized
version exists for expression studies in yeast called yeGFP which has a relatively
high emission intensity [38].
4.5.2
Thioredoxin (TrxA)
Thioredoxin (Trx) is a class of redox proteins that play a central part of the an-
tioxidant systems in virtually all living organisms, and it is a crucial component
of antioxidative defense and regulation of intra- and extracellular signalling path-
ways [39]. Its function is to catalyze electron flux from Nicotinamide Adenine
Dinucleotide Phosphate (NADPH) through Trx Reductase, effectively reducing
reactive oxygen species and maintaining a reducing environment [40]. The mecha-
nism of Trx comes from highly conserved dithiol active site motif with two cysteine
residues, each of which cause the reduction of a disulfide bond on the substrate.
Trx itself is then reduced by Trx Reductase which utilises NADPH as an electron
donor [40]. In E. coli, Thioredoxin 1 (Trx1) is a cytosolic isoform expressed from
the gene trxA, and it can be translocated and secreted.
The Trx system has many diverse therapeutic and clinical applications ranging
from cancer treatments, cardiovascular disease and and infectious diseases [39].
TrxA was chosen for the overall project due to its relevance in clinical settings,
but also for its small size (12 kDa) and expression in high quantities as well as
efficient his-tag extractions.
4.5.3
Calmodulin-1 (CALM1)
Calmodulin 1 (CALM1) is a protein in humans that is encoded by the CALM1
gene. It plays a role in calcium signal transduction pathways by regulating control
of ion channels, enzymes, aquaporins, and other proteins. CALM1 functions as a
23
==PAGE:34==
calcium-binding protein and it acts as a calcium detector within cells and stimu-
lates the activation of over twenty amino acids, controlling various physiological
functions. It is also involved in regulatory roles in cell proliferation and through-
out the cell cycle. Calmodulin undergoes a conformational change upon binding
to calcium, allowing it to interact with phosphatases, ion channels, and kinases.
This change is associated with muscle contraction, release of neurotransmitters,
and gene expression. Calmodulin is highly evolutionarily conserved in humans,
and there are three isoforms of the protein encoded by three independent genes in
the human chromosome (CALM-1, CALM-2, CALM-3) [41].
CALM1 has shown potential therapeutic applications in various medical fields.
Due to its role in calcium signal transduction pathways, it has been studied for its
involvement in neurological disorders such as Alzheimer’s disease and Parkinson’s
disease.
Additionally, CALM1 has been implicated in cardiovascular diseases,
cancer, and diabetes, and researchers are exploring the use of CALM1 as a target
for drug development to modulate its functions and potentially alleviate associated
pathological conditions. [41].

Cultivation media

S. cerevisiae lysis

USER cloning
USER cloning was carried out using 1 µL of each purified PCR product, 1 µL 10x
CutSmart buffer (New England Biolabs (NEB)), 1 µL USER enzyme (NEB) and
MilliQ water to a total volume of 10 µL. The USER mixture was incubated at 37°C
for 25-45 minutes in a plate incubator followed by incubation at room temperature
for 25 minutes. Finally, the USER mixture was put on ice prior to transformation
into E. coli.

Gel eletrophoresis
All PCR products in this project were run on 1% agarose gels with SYBR Safe

a 1kb DNA ladder was used with the following ten bands: 0.5kb, 1kb, 1.5kb, 2kb,
3kb, 4kb, 5kb, 6kb, 8kb, 9kb. The gels were imaged in a Gel Doc (Bio-Rad) and
bands were analysed using the Image Lab Software (Bio-Rad).

The colony PCR amplicon was run in a gel electrophoresis (see section 5.5.4).

7
Discussion
In this work, it was attempted to expand on the HTP split-luciferase toolbox by
designing and assembling two functional expression vectors for the heterologous
expression of proteins from a split-luciferase cassette in yeast. The aim was to
establish the system in low-throughput first by manually cloning two genes of
interest into the expression vectors and assessing their output. Next, the system
would be tested in semi high-throughput using liquid handling robots by inserting
truncated protein libraries into the cassette, making it possible to run hundreds of
samples in tangent.
The vast majority of the time was spent in the low-throughput part trying to make
the cloning strategy work, and this was due to several challenges throughout the
project. I will elaborate a little bit in the following sections as I discuss the results.
7.1
S. cerevisiae CEN.PK113-7D lysis with E. coli opti-
mised lysis buffer
The first step in the process was to verify whether the lysis buffer that Aske Unger
was using for E. coli could be utilised for S. cerevisiae. It was tested on a yeGFP
harboring strain in order to quantify the lysis efficiency with spectrophotometry,
measured in RFU. Attempting to use the E. coli optimized lysis buffer was decided
for two reasons. Firstly, the buffer composition is relatively inexpensive and easy
to make, making it suitable for high-throughput applications and secondly, using
the same buffer solution would make the pipeline universal for multiple organisms.
There are several effective ways to lyse S. cerevisiae including sonication, glass
bead lysis and mechanical lysis, and also detergent based methods have been shown
to be effective [44].
58
==PAGE:69==
The results from the lysis experiment showed that the unmodified lysis buffer for
E. coli was effective at lysing the S. cerevisiae cells, as there was an increasing
fluorescent output over time, with no net change in fluorescent output after 1 hour,
and the WT control showed no fluorescence.
7.2
pDIV022-GG and pDIV025-GG expression vector prop-
agation
The expression vectors designed to harbor the split-luciferase cassette were assem-
bled with USER cloning from long primer pairs and vector backbones listed in
appendix A and appendix C. The vectors were assembled seamlessly, and were
successfully propagated in E. coli DH5α. For each vector assembly, there were
hundreds of colonies on LB Amp Agar suggesting that the AmpR promoter was
highly active and constitutive. After culturing single colonies in liquid LB Amp
and purifying the DNA, there was a high concentration of DNA (see appendix E).
7.3
S. cerevisiae CEN.PK113-7D-yeGFP
As previously mentioned, the yeGFP insert that was available at the institute
unfortunately harbored a BsaI cut site in the ORF, and it was surprisingly not
annotated in the DNA file that was provided. This was a big problem, as BsaI
is what was used for the Golden Gate cloning technique. Effectively this meant
that there was a 1/3 chance of getting the full yeGFP insert into the cassette,
and for whatever reason, the first many attempts to clone the yeGFP into the
cassette resulted in incomplete inserts. These could only be truly verified through
Sanger sequencing, as the band size alone was not sufficient to verify the full
size.
When it was finally discovered that there was a BsaI cut site, a colony
59
==PAGE:70==
screening was performed to look for correctly sized bands on a gel electrophoresis.
Fortunately, after two screening attempts there were a few potential bands and
they were investigated further (figure 10). After transformation into S. cerevisiae,
DNA purification and subsequent Sanger sequencing, only 1 colony had the correct
insert and was able to grow, which was pDIV022-GG-yeGFP (LCB7).
7.3.1
Growth on YPD NTC Agar & LB Amp Agar
From the YPD NTC plates in figure 9 we can see that there were a decent amount
of colonies suggesting that the TEF1 promoter was actively expressing NAT. There
was a similar amount of colonies with the insert in S. cerevisiae compared to E.
coli, suggesting that the TEF1 and AmpR promoters had similar output with the
insert. As yeGFP is a large fragment, it makes sense that there was a reduction
in the amount of clones with yeGFP inserted compared to the backbone without
insert, due to the added metabolic burden from the yeGFP [45].
7.3.2
Fluorescent and luminescent output
The LCB7 clone showed a decently high GFP output for the full cell lysate, almost
exactly double the amount compared to the control with YPD+TLuc buffer. YPD
is known to exert autofluorescence in the 530nm range which explains why the
controls were as high as they were [46]. The autofluorescence is not a problem for
the pipeline in general as YPD does not interfere with luminescence measurements,
which is the primary focus of this work. However, to minimize autofluorescence, a
minimal media could have been used such as synthetic complete media (SC media).
Effectively, no yeGFP output was present in neither the GFP supernatant or lysed
cell supernatant, suggesting that the yeGFP was not secreted by the cells but was
accumulated in the cytosol.
60
==PAGE:71==
For the luminescence measurements, we see only an output from the full cell lysate,
which is in agreement with the fluorescence measurements. However, the lumines-
cence output is remarkably low compared to Aske Unger’s results for E. coli [3].
We can infer from these results that the TDH3p promoter was actively and con-
stitutively expressed, as suggested by the presence of both yeGFP output and
luciferase output, but that perhaps the proline linker was weakly fused to the in-
sert, or that the linker and NLuc was not strongly expressed in this construct.
Since yeGFP is a large protein, it could be that the linker could not properly
fuse with it. Additionally, neither the proline linker nor the NLuc were codon
optimized for yeast, which could affect their translation by the cells ribosomes.
yeGFP itself was tailored and codon optimized for S. cerevisiae, and it could po-
tentially interfere with the E. coli optimized linker and luciferase. Had there been
more time, it would have been interesting to investigate whether the entire fused
protein was present and in what amounts, and if somehow the linker and NLuc
was lost in the translation of the protein. First step could using SDS page to look
for the right size protein bands, or to look analyze the full amino acid sequence,
mass spectrometry could be used.
Finally, it could also have been interesting to investigate different fusion linkers
for expression in S. cerevisiae, as the cells behave quite differently from E. coli.
Flexible linkers have been shown to be efficient in S. cerevisiae, since yeast are
capable of secreting heterologous proteins [29].

file was incorrect causing me to try to excise the insert from its vector using the
wrong restriction enzymes. This meant that the first many attempts to clone the

7.4.1
Growth on YPD NTC Agar & LB Amp Agar
From the YPD NTC plates in figure 15 we can see that there low amount of colonies
compared to E. coli in figure 13. We can assume TEF1 promoter was actively
expressing NAT, as there were colonies growing on NTC, but somehow by inserting
TrxA the colony forming units drastically reduced. This was the case every time
I plated the TrxA clones on YPD NTC agar. The only difference between the
TrxA insert and the yeGFP was the fact that yeGFP was codon optimized for
yeast whereas TrxA came directly from an E. coli strain, meaning it was likely
also methylated. Although the TEF1 promoter should be unaffected by this, since
each part had their own transcription terminators (CYC1t and TEF1 terminators),
that was the only difference between the two constructs.
Since the expression
cassette sat directly in front of the TEF1 promoter, perhaps there could have been
interference with read-through of the RNA polymerase when transcribing the NAT
sequence when the whole expression cassette was optimized for E. coli. It could
have been interesting to DpnI treat the TrxA insert before cloning into the vector
to see if it would make a difference to demethylate the DNA. Once more, it would
also be an idea to codon optimize the whole cassette for expression in S. cerevisiae.
Luminescent output For the luminescence measurements, we see a high output of
luminescence from the high copy number plasmid, full cell lysate, and a small
output from the low copy number, full cell lysate. However this time there is also
small output of luminescence from both pure supernatants indicating that there

may be some secretion happening, contrasted to the yeGFP results. It could be
the case that since the TrxA protein is small, it could be secreted whereas the
yeGFP is very large and thus could not be secreted. We do see a relatively high
output from the full cell lysate of the HCC construct, indicating that the cassette
was highly and constitutively expressed from the TDH3 promoter, and that TrxA
was succesfully linked to the NLuc. This could again be explained by the fact that
TrxA is a small protein, and perhaps was better connected to the proline linker
than yeGFP. The luminescence is still low compared to Aske Unger’s results for E.
coli, and this could again be explained by the fact that the cassette, and in this case
also TrxA, was not codon optimized for yeast causing translation efficiency to be
lower. Once more, it would be worth investigating the protein concentration with
SDS page or the amino acid sequence to check if the proteins were fully translated
as intended, as well as trialing different fusion linker options.

S. cerevisiae CEN.PK113-7D-CALM1
For the CALM1 truncation library, it was possible to use Golden Gate cloning

gel eletrophoresis results are not precise. Even if though the bands look similar in
size, there could still be small differences in sequences that are not visible on the

7.5.1
Growth on YPD NTC Agar & LB Amp Agar
It was actually first attempted to Golden Gate clone the insert from pET30A(+)-
CALM1 and directly transform into S. cerevisiae CEN.PK113-7D, but there was
no visible colonies from any of the attempts (results not included in this report).
Therefore, it was decided to propagate the plasmids in E. coli DH5α first to
increase the DNA concentration. The problem with propagating protein libraries
like this is that there will concurrently be an enrichment of certain inserts that pose
the least amount of metabolic burden on the cells. This means that the variety of
constructs will be lower, and that larger fragments are likely not present. I will
reflect more on this when discussing the luminescence results.
We see from figures 19 and 20 that there was a relatively low CFU on all plates
both for pDIV022-GG-CALM1 and pDIV025-GG-CALM1. Similarly to the TrxA
insert, the CALM1 protein library was not codon optimized for yeast which could
explain the issues with CFU. Once again, it should not be the case as the cassette
should be terminated from CYC1t and NAT is expressed from its own promoter,
but there could be some interference from the non-codon optimized inserts.
7.5.2
Robotic pipeline
The robotic pipeline was not fully utilised in this project. The entire pipeline
would have included plate filling of 5-8 96-well plates per experiment with OT-2,
colony picking and inoculation with QPix, culture transfers into TLuc buffer using
the Tecan and automated measurements using the Hamilton robot. The whole
point was using robots to perform all of the laborious liquid handling tasks in
bulk, and for this project, many steps were still done manually.
64
==PAGE:75==
The main problem in this regard was low amount of CFU for the CALM1 clones
on YPD NTC. Generally for Aske Unger’s project, there were hundreds of colonies
on each plate, meaning that the QPix could pick and inoculate 5-8 96-well plates
at once. This also means that he can run the pipeline in tangent, yielding many
more results to do any meaningful statistics on. With this amount of colonies it
makes sense to utilise a high-throughput robotic pipeline, but with maximum 8
colonies, which was the case for CALM1 in this project, it simply is not feasible.
To even have enough colonies to run half a 96-well plate, I had to grow the CALM1
library in duplicates on YPD NTC agar as seen in figures 19 and 20. Effectively
this means that for the robotic pipeline to be sensible for S. cerevisiae, the cloning
strategy and inserts need to be further optimized to yield a higher CFU.
7.5.3
Luminescent output
The luciferase assay was first run with cultures incubated for 12 hours, which
yielded no results. It was decided to let the cells incubate for longer, which did
yield results, suggesting that S. cerevisiae needed significantly longer incubation
time before expressing the cassette, thus in late exponential phase or stationary
phase.
The results in figure 21 a subtle luminescent output for all clones. The RLU was
significantly lower for the CALM-1 insert compared to both yeGFP and TrxA,
but nonetheless present.
This is evident from the fact that the samples with
CTZ substrate had an output, whereas samples without substrate had virtually
no output. The range of output for the samples with CTZ was however small,
suggesting that there could be an enrichment of certain CALM-1 fragments from
the E. coli propagation. The low RLU could also perhaps suggest that CTZ has a
background luminescence in the presence of TLuc. I could not find any evidence
65
==PAGE:76==
that this is the case, but it is worth considering.
Another point to make is that no controls were run for this assay, as there was no
space on the one 96-well plate that I managed to run. However, compared with
results from yeGFP and TrxA, their controls had higher RLU than the samples
with CTZ run in this assay. Effectively, we cannot safely say that there was any
NLuc present.
More optimization of the cloning strategy is needed, and more
repetitions should be made. Once again, it would have been useful to sequence a
selection of the colonies to check if the insert was present in any of the clones.

8
Conclusion
The aim of the work presented here was to expand on HTP split-luciferase based
protein expression using ORF-ESPRIT truncation libraries. The promise of the
pipeline is a low cost, highly reproducible and modifiable solution to running large
scale protein expression studies, combining computers and wetlab work and robots
into a single workflow. The methods Aske Unger has conceived in his work holds
great promise, as it is easy to follow and requires little to no programming expertise
to operate.
The transfer of the expression cassette into yeast as well as the cloning strategy
turned out to be more challenging than anticipated. Some of the components of
the cassette were designed for E. coli, and it has likely contributed to a lower
output from the expression vectors.
The overall expression vector designs showed a good promise, as the yeast opti-
mized components like promoters, transcription terminators and selection markers
worked as intended. The vector assembly was seamless and the vectors were fully
functional.
As suggested from the yeGFP assay, the codon optimized cassette
showed a higher CFU on YPD NTC media than did the other, E. coli-optimized
inserts. This begs the question how the vector performance would look had there
been a codon optimization done, and the components had been fully tailored for
expression in yeast. This includes not only the inserts, but also the fusion linker
and the NLuc luciferase. On this note, it could even be a possibility to produce
the TLuc subunit from a yeast chassis and plasmid with a fully codon optimized
TLuc subunit. However, this takes away from the original idea of this work, which
was to create one, universal pipeline that could function both in E. coli and other
organisms like S. cerevisiae. But as it is often the case in biology, cells don’t always
behave the way we want them to.
67
==PAGE:78==
Although the robotics pipeline was not fully utilised, the components that were
used were still seamless and efficient, and I believe that with future optimisation
of the cloning strategy and inserts, this pipeline will be fully functional for many
expression hosts.

9
Future perspectives
While this work only resulted in a modest contribution to the high-throughput
pipeline, the promise of a universally standardized protein expression engine still
stands. With much more emphasis on getting the basics right in constructing a
robust expression platform for split-luciferase linked truncation libraries in yeast
and other organisms, I believe there is still hope of being able to run large-scale
protein expression studies like this.
There is a pipe dream associated with a
universal protein expression engine like this, and that is the ability to accurately
predict which organisms and vectors to use for a given protein of interest - an
AlphaFold-like algorithm that can predict the best host for heterologous expression
of a protein, based off of millions of datapoints that are sampled and recorded in
a streamlined fashion.
As shown by Aske Unger, this method is also useful for the development of subunit
vaccines and other subunit therapeutics.
By running hundredths of thousands
of samples of truncated libraries of a protein of interest, the expression levels
measured by this pipeline can reveal which parts of a protein is expressed in which
amounts, and which are not. This can help in filtering out all a lot of noise, and
double down on fragments that are highly expressed, which can be investigated in
much more detail.
Safe to say that we are in the infancy of these kinds of technologies, but I believe
that the future is promising.
