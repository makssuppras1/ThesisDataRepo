Abstract 
Seabird bycatch in commercial gillnet fisheries have been a recognized concern since the 1980’s, though 
almost no information is known about this issue in Danish waters. Remote eletronic monitoring (REM) 
systems have been used to register bycatches of other animals, e.g. harbour porpoises. However, REM-
systems have never been used to register seabird bycatches.  
The aim of this study was to estimate the total number of seabirds bycaught by the Danish gillnet fleet. One 
gillnet fishing vessel in the northern part of the Sound was equipped with a REM-system. The vessel was 
monitored from Maj 2010 to December 2015. Every seabird registration was noted with information about 
species, GPS-position and date. A total of 494 seabirds were observed during the 6 years. From the Danish 
logbook database information about the vessel’s fishing activity was extracted, and showed 1152 fishing 
days. This was used to calculate a daily bycatch rate per year, ranging between 0.15 and 1.30 seabirds per 
day. 
The seabird registration clustered and caused for a more area specific based bycatch rate to be calculated. 
The northern Sound was thus divided into four smaller subareas. To increase the information about the 
fishing activity in each subarea 7 fishermen were interviewed about their fishing areas. GPS-information 
from the hauls of an 8th vessel was also used. This group of 8 vessels spend all together 5688 days at sea. 
A total of 12,119 days were used at sea in the northern Sound by the Danish gillnet vessel fleet during the 
years. This information enabled a calculation of the total bycatch in the northern Sound to result at 5446 
seabirds. 
Three species of seabirds were registered frequently as bycatch, 270 Common Eiders, 71 Cormorants and 
46 Common Guillemots. Some seabird registrations were not identifiable and created a fourth group of 107 
Unidentified Birds. The 3 identified species correlates with other registrations of seabird bycatches in 
Denmark, though Common Scoters have been registered by others. The calculated bycatch rates from this 
study correlates with results from another study from the west coast of Sweden and a study from northern 
Norway, but not with another Danish study. This seems to indicate that unidentified factors seem to play 
an important role in the differences between the seabird bycatch rates of different areas. Further research 
is needed if these factors are to be identified.

Resumé 
Bifangst af havfugle fra industrielt fiskeri har været et anderkendt problem sinde 1980’erne. På trods af 
dette, er omfanget af information fra danske farvande meget begrænset. Remote electronic monitoring 
systemer har været benyttet til at registrere bifangster af andre dyr, bl.a. marsvin, men aldrig til at 
registrere bifangst af havfugle. 
Formålet med dette studie var at bestemme det totale antal bifangster af havfugle fra den danske 
garnflåde. Et enkelt fartøj der fiskede i det nordlige Øresund blev udstyret med et REM-system, og blev 
overvåget fra maj 2010 indtil december 2015. Alle havfugle bifangst registreringer blev noteret med art, 
GPS-position og dato informationer. I alt blev 494 havfugle observeret i løbet af årene. Informationer om 
fartøjets fiskeriaktiviteter blev hentet i den danske logbogsdatabase, og fremviste 1152 fiskedage. Disse 
informationer blev brugt til at beregne en daglig bifangstrate for hvert år, og der rangerer mellem 1,15 og 
1,30 havfugle per dag. 
På baggrund af klyngedannelse af havfugle registreringerne, var en mere områdespecifik bifangstrate 
nødvendig. Det nordlige Øresund blev derfor inddelt i fire mindre områder. For at øge informationen om 
fiskeriaktiviteten i hvert af de fire områder, blev 7 fiskere interviewet omkring deres fiskeriområder. GPS-
informationer fra et 8. fartøj blev også anvendt. I alt tilbragte disse 8 fartøjer 5688 dage på havet, mens 
hele den danske garnfiskeri flåde fra det nordlige Øresund i alt brugte 12.119 dage på havet i løbet af 
årene. På baggrund af dette blev det samlede antal bifangster i det nordlige Øresund beregnet til 5446 
havfugle. 
Tre arter af havfugle blev registreret regelmæssigt som bifangst, 270 ederfugle, 71 skarver og 46 lomvier. 
En del havfugle kunne ikke identificeres, og dannede en gruppe på 107 uidentificerede fugle. De 3 
identificerede arter korrelerer med resultaterne fra andre danske bifangst studier, dog har sortænder også 
været registreret regelmæssigt andre steder. De beregnede bifangstrater fra dette studie korrelerer med 
resultaterne af et studie fra Sveriges vestkyst og et fra nordvestkysten af Norge, og samtidig korrelerer 
resultatet ikke med et dansk studie. Dette indikerer at uidentificerede faktorer virker til at spiller en vigtig 
rolle i forskellene mellem de forskellige bifangst rater fra forskellige områder. Yderligere information er 
nødvendig for at kunne påvise hvilke faktorer der påvirker raterne for bifangst af havfugle.

1 Introduction 
The term seabirds is not a scientific definition of a group of birds, but is used when grouping birds linked to 
water throughout most of their life. In this study the term seabird has been used to cover all birds linked to 
water, also the birds normally called coastal-birds. The reason for this is that the northern Sound is all 
coastal water and true seabirds rarely occur here. Seabird are birds that spend a large part of their daily life 
on or close to the water, where they hunt or find their food, many of them rest on water and the majority 
have waterproof plumages.  
The World Conservation Union, formerly known as the International Union for Conservation of Natural 
Resources (IUCN), was created in 1948. In 1964 the IUCN’s Red List of Threatened Species was created 
(IUCN 2015), and the newest Danish Red List has assessed 195 bird species (Wind et al. 2004). 46 of the 
assessed species fall within my definition of seabird and 16 of these are listed as near threatened, 
vulnerable, endangered or extinct.  
Where humans and seabirds interact to catch the resources of the sea there is a risk of seabird bycatch. 
Bycatch of seabirds by industrial fishery has been a focus of studies since the late 1980’s (Lewison et al. 
2005), but knowledge from Danish waters is limited, though some does exist (Durinck et al. 1993, ICES 
1995, Lyngs & Kampp 1996, Bregnballe & Frederiksen 2010, Degel et al. 2010). In the Sound all gear types 
except gillnets are banned, as a result seabird bycatches will only happen in gillnet.  
This introduction will start with an outline of the known bycatches from trawl, longlines and gillnet fisheries 
to enlighten the size of the bycatch problem. As information from Danish waters is so limited and scattered, 
the outline is at global level. A short description of several directives and conventions Denmark have 
ratified follows, but the focus of this introduction is on the northern Sound and the current gillnet fishery, 
as well as the biology of the expected bycatch seabird species based on previous Danish studies; Common 
Eiders, Cormorants and Common Guillemots (ICES 1995, Bregnballe & Frederiksen 2006, Degel et al. 2010). 
 
1.2 
Trawl 
Trawling is used worldwide either at the bottom of the ocean, benthic trawling, just above the bottom, 
demersal trawling, or in the water column, pelagic trawling. The mesh size varies depending on the size of 
the target species, as well as the vertical positon in the water column also is chosen depending on the 
target species. Trawling works by a net being towed behind a vessel, the mouth of the net is kept open 
horizontally and vertically in different ways and catches everything in its path larger than the mesh size. 
No global overview of the seabird bycatch rate by trawlers exists. Though Croxall (2008) estimated a global 
annual bycatch of 18,000 birds based on a single study. I have, though, reviewed several papers from

1.3 
Longline 
Longlines are used worldwide as a passive fishing method, both as demersal longlines in relatively shallow 
areas and pelagic longlines in deep water. Bait is always used, and the type and size of this, as well as the 
position of the lines, makes longlines accepted as a species - and size-selective fishing technique. Even 
though longlines are widely used, many areas do not have any or very little information about seabird 
bycatch. 
A review based on 68 longline fisheries (Anderson et al. 2011), resulted in an estimate of at least 160.000 
(up to 320.000) seabirds bycatches each year from longline fishery worldwide. The authors did however 
emphasise that current levels of bycatch must be higher, up to 50 %, due to unobserved and under-
reported bycatches, poor data from many of the 68 fisheries the review was based on as well as the fact 
that not all longline fisheries were included.

Many endangered, threatened and vulnerable species have been recorded as bycatch. Although they were 
not the major bycatch species, even small numbers have been shown to threaten the population further 
and be non-sustainable, examples being the Short-Tailed Albatrosses of the west coast of the US and the 
Spectacled Petrels of Brazil (Tasker et al. 2000). 
In Denmark longline fishery is very limited and only used to catch salmon in the Baltic Sea, with a quota of 
125 tonnes yearly. There is no study of this fishery and the possible seabird bycatch effect. 
 
1.4 
Gillnet 
Gillnet is a passive fishing method, acting like a vertical wall in the water, with either anchors or weights at 
the bottom and buoys at the top. Different types of gillnets exist, including set gillnets, driftnets, encircling 
gillnets, fixed gillnets, trammel nets and combined gillnets-trammel nets. A gillnet catches everything large 
enough to be entangled, with no selection other than position and mesh size. They are used everywhere in 
the water column, depending on the target species. Today high-sea large-scale driftnets are banned by the 
United Nation (U.N. Resolution 46/215 of 1991); one of the reasons was the impact the fishery had on non-
target species. In 1990 the year before the ban, more than 500,000 birds were caught as bycatch in the 
North-Pacific alone (DeGange et al. 1993). Small-scale driftnets are still allowed within a State’s Exclusive 
Economic Zone (EEZ), but were stopped in the Baltic Sea in 2004 by EU directive 812. Excluding driftnets, 
gillnet fishing is used in nearshore waters worldwide, except Antarctica (Žydelis et al. 2009). 
A review from 2013 reported 104 species of seabirds as having been bycaught in gillnets, with cormorants, 
auks, shearwaters, penguins and sea ducks being the most vulnerable groups (Žydelis et al. 2013). Of the 
104 species 5 are classified as critically endangered, 14 as endangered, 29 as threatened and 15 as near 
threatened.  
A global review from 2013 concluded that knowledge of seabird bycatch in gillnets was highly fragmented, 
but a simple accumulation of the most recent known data indicated that nearly 400,000 seabirds were 
caught in gillnets globally each year (Žydelis et al. 2013).

The knowledge of seabird bycatch from Danish waters is very limited, but some species are known to be 
caught in gillnets. In 1995 an ICES report showed that in 1990 seabirds bycatch happened at a rate of 0.51 
birds per 100 nets, mainly Common Guillemot (ICES 1995). In 2006 Great Cormorants were investigated 
and between 24-66 % of all ringed birds were found to have been caught in nets between 1972 and 2002 
(Bregnballe & Frederiksen 2010). In 2010 Common Eider was the most common species to be entangled in 
gillnets around Ærø, South of Fyn (Degel et al. 2010). 
 
1.5 
Bird directives and conventions 
During the last 4 decades several directives and conventions have been created, with the purpose of 
protecting the increasingly threatened bird population of Europe, including Denmark. 
In 1971 the Ramsar Convention, the Convention on Wetlands of International Importance, was adopted, 
Denmark ratified the convention in 1977. The focus is on wetlands. The original concern behind this focus 
was a decrease in the number of ducks, geese and other aquatic birds. Today the focus is not only birds, but 
the diversity generally supported by wetland areas (Skov- og Naturstyrelsen 2004). 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
In 1979 the first European bird directive was agreed upon, the Council Directive 79/409/EEC (EU 1979). 
During the years the directive has been edited several times, and was codified and replaced in 2009 with 
Directive 2009/147/EC (EuropeanCommission 2010). The aim of the directives, known as The Bird 
Directives, are to protect, manage and control all wild birds of Europe, including their eggs, nests and 
habitats. The Bird Directive has 5 annexes, including 2 which are lists of vulnerable and threatened bird 
species. Annex 1 lists 194 bird species and sub-species that require special protection, and as a result, the 
member states assigned Special Protected Areas (SPA’s) based on scientific conditions, to ensure the 
survival of these species. 
In 1979 the Bern Convention, the Conservation of European Wildlife and Natural Habitat, was created, and 
adopted in 1983 in Denmark. Today 50 countries as well as the European Union have adopted the 
convention. The 3 main focus areas are, 1) to protect the natural flora and fauna of the European 
continent, 2) in particular endangered and vulnerable stationary as well as migratory species, and 3) 
support of the cooperation between the member states (Council of Europe 1979).  
In 1985 the Bonn Convention of 1979, also known as the Convention on the Conservation of Migratory 
Species of Wild Animals was adopted. The Convention was ratified in Denmark in 1983. The aim of the 
Bonn Convention is to conserve and protect migratory species, terrestrial, marine and avian within their 
geographical range and reaching a “favourable conservation status” (UNEP 1979). 
In 1992 the Habitat Directive, Council Directive 92/43/EEC, was adopted, and together with the Bird 
Directive, they are the basis of the Natura2000 areas, which today covers more than 18 % of European land 
area and 6 % of the marine territory and also protect the most vulnerable and threatened species of 
Europe. 
 
1.6 
Focus area 
The International Council for the Exploration of the Sea (ICES) has divided the European waters into 
ecoregions (figure 1). The waters around Denmark are the Baltic Sea and the Greater North Sea. A small 
part of the western Baltic Sea is called the Sound. Its acts as a connection between Kattegat and the Baltic 
Sea, and is responsible for 25% of the water exchange between these. The Sound stretches over 118 km 
from North to South and between 4 to 28 km from East to West, with a maximum depth of 50 m and many 
shallow areas, of only a few meters (The Sound Water Cooperation).

Throughout the years, more than 76 % of the yearly weight (> 81 % in five years), was caught between July 
and January (figure 3). 2010 is biased, as January to April was not included.

1.8 
Bird biology 
 
Common Eider 
1.8.1
Three extant species of eider occur in the Northern hemisphere, with the Common Eider (Somateria 
mollissima) being the one commonly found in Denmark.  
The winter population in 1989-1992 was counted to be 800,000 individuals (Bønløkke et al. 2006, Dybbro 
2008), which was estimated to represent 29 % of the entire European population (Durinck et al. 1994). This 
number decreased to 370,000 in 2000 but has since increased to 630,000 in 2004 (Desholm et al. 2002, 
Dybbro 2008). The number of breeding pairs increased from 3,000 pairs in the 1950´s and 1960´s to 25,000 
pairs in 2008 (Dybbro 2008). Four major breeding grounds exist in Denmark, one being on the island 
Saltholm in the Sound. The number of pairs have decreased here from 7,000 in 1993 to approximately 
4,200 in 2000 (Desholm et al. 2002). 
Most Danish breeding pairs stay in Denmark to moult in the late summer and feed during the winter. Many 
individuals from neighbouring countries come to moult and feed in Danish waters from August, with the 
peak in October to February (Bønløkke et al. 2006). In 2008 the largest numbers of the winter population 
was counted in Lillebælt and the Western Sound, and 67 % of the non-flying observations (69 % in 2004) 
were observed in waters shallower than 10 meters (Petersen et al. 2010). 
Several studies (Cantin et al. 1974, Player 1974, Patursson & Jensen 2011, Krasnov et al. 2014) have shown 
that bivalves, especially blue mussels is the preferred food object of Common Eider. A study from Faroese

Fjords showed Common Eider to be one of the two largest consumers of mussels (Patursson & Jensen

Common Eiders dive up to 20 meters to reach the mussels bed at the bottom and spend no time searching 
for food on the way down or up (Guillemette et al. 2004, Dybbro 2008). Studies have shown that they dive 
from the surface at an angle around 60 degrees, and once they are at the bottom they use their feet to 
move around and search by sight for mostly small mussels (<20 mm) which they swallow on the spot 
(Guillemette et al. 2004, Patursson & Jensen 2011).  
 
 
Cormorant 
1.8.2
Two subspecies of Cormorants are present in Denmark, Phalacrocoracidae carbo sinensis and 
Phalacrocoracidae carbo carbo. The main difference is their geographic distribution, P.c. sinensis is 
continental across Europe and Asia often connected to freshwater habitats, whereas P.c.carbo lives along 
the Atlantic coast (The Environment Directorate-General of the European Commission 2016). 
The breeding population of P.c.sinensis in Denmark is part of the largest population in Europe, spread over 
Denmark, Holland and around the Baltic Sea. The number of colonies in Denmark increased from 50 in 
2000-2003, to 59 in 2004 and 73 in 2015 (Bønløkke et al. 2006, Dybbro 2008, Bregnballe & Therkildsen 
2015). The number of breeding pairs have been between 36,400 and 42,500 from 1993-2006, whereas the 
last five years have shown an increase from 25,542 to 31,076 in 2015 (Bregnballe & Therkildsen 2015). 
Before 1971 only 200 pairs were tolerated (Dybbro 2008). 
The winter population in Danish waters is mainly P.c.carbo, as most P.c. sinensis migrate to the 
Mediterranean and Central Europe, less so in mild winters. The winter population were estimated to be 
24,254 birds in 2008 (Petersen et al. 2010) and 29,500 in 2013 (pers. com. Thomas Bregnballe, June 1st 
2016). In 2008 16 % of the population was counted in the Sound and 10 % in Southern Kattegat, and 83 % 
of the non-flying observations (81 % in 2004) was observed in waters less than 8 meters deep (Petersen et 
al. 2010). 
Cormorants are opportunistic feeders, and forage by pursuit-diving targeting fish almost exclusively. They 
catch their prey underwater, but have to surface in order to swallow it. Small descent angles are more 
energetically beneficial when diving in shallow water than vertical dives (Ribak et al. 2007). 
The daily intake of fish per individual is about 500 g, with an increase when feeding chicks (The 
Environment Directorate-General of the European Commission 2016). The length of the dive depends on 
the depth of the dive as well as the amount of previous dives. Daily bimodal patterns have been shown 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
with regards to great Cormorants foraging behaviour. North of the Arctic Circle however, where conditions 
often are bad with poor or no light, snow and strong winds, the roosting period in the middle of the day is 
non-existing (Johansen et al. 2001). A study analysing faeces sample showed that during winter Cormorants 
eat significantly fewer but larger fish, than during the summer (Johansen et al. 2001). 
 
 
Common Guillemot 
1.8.3
Uria aalge called Common Guillemot (or Common Murre) are one of several auks, widespread over the 
northern parts of the Atlantic and Pacific Ocean. A population of 12,000-17,000 breeding pairs live in large 
colonies in the Baltic Sea (Österblom et al. 2002). The only Danish breeding colony of Common Guillemot 
was in 2008 on Græsholm with 2,000-3,000 breeding pairs (Dybbro 2008). The migratory and winter guest 
in Danish waters reach as high as 300,000 in late autumn, the majority visiting the North Sea, Skagerrak and 
Kattegat (Lyngs & Kampp 1996, Dybbro 2008). Here 89 % of the non-flying population were observed in 
waters between 18-36 meters in 2008 (Petersen et al. 2010). 
Ring studies have shown that 85 % of the winter population in Danish waters are from the British Isles, 
especially Scotland, while the Baltic population only represents 7 %. The mature birds begin their migration 
back to the breeding colonies after they have moulted in October. The number of British Common 
Guillemot in Skagerrak increases through the autumn peaking in October-November, as many immature 
individuals stay during the winter (Lyngs & Kampp 1996). 
Common Guillemots are pursuit-divers hunting for fish using their wings to swim (Swennen & Duiven 1991). 
Cod, Capelin, Sand Eel and Herring have been found in the stomachs of many adult Common Guillemots. 
Most prey of Common Guillemots is pelagic fish, and therefore Guillemots hunt in the water column. The 
most common diving depths are between 20 and 50 meters, but they have been observed diving to 180 
meter (Piatt et al. 1985).

2 Methods 
2.1 
Logbook data  
All Danish fishing vessels above 8 meters are by law required to fill in logbooks about their catches and all 
logbook data are collected in a database, smaller vessel can do it by choice. Data about the fishing activity 
in ICES area 23, the Sound, from May 2010 to December 2015 were extracted from this logbook database. 
The result was a detailed list of vessels with regards to years, fishing days, departure and arrival harbour, 
ICES square, gear type, mesh sizes, species and weight of species. In order to get the relevant information, 
only data from ICES square 40G2 and trips with either gillnets, set gillnets, trammel nets or combined 
gillnets-trammel nets were used. 
 
2.2 
REM systems 
Two fishing vessels (vessel 1: 11.05 m and 9.8 BT, vessel 2: 10.3 m and 10.7 BT) were equipped with REM 
systems (Remote Electronic Monitoring). The REM-systems contained 3 on-deck waterproof armoured 
dome closed circuit television (CCTV) cameras, a GPS-sensor and a computer for storing data. The cameras 
overlooked the sorting board, part of the deck and a close-up view of the area where the nets appeared 
from the water surface during hauling (see Appendix 1). This produced videos that enabled the detection of 
bycatch, whether the seabirds were hauled on-board or fell out of the net when hauled from the water. 
Two different REM-systems were used; camera vessel 1 had both REM-systems on board whereas camera 
vessel 2 only had the first version of the REM-system on board. Both systems recorded video and GPS 
positions from when the systems were turned on until they were turned off, with a picture speed of 2-9 
pictures pr. second and a GPS-position every 10 second, but differed in data storage. The first system EMI, 
made by Archipelago Marine Research Ltd, Canada used a replaceable 500 GB hard drive installed in the 
wheelhouse. This hard drive had to be manually changed when it was approximately 80 % full. The second 
system Blackbox (made by Anchor Lab, Denmark,) stored data on a computer in the wheelhouse and when 
connected to a 4G internet, the data was automatically uploaded directly to servers at DTU Aqua. 
Positions, date and time from all hauls were registered from both vessels and both systems and extracted 
directly from the programs into excel files. Due to time and founding limitations, not all videos from camera 
vessel 2 have been reviewed for seabird bycatch, resulting in only one adequate continuous registration of 
seabird bycatch, i.e. camera vessel 1. Cameral vessel 2 has therefore not been used to register seabird 
bycatch, but have been added to the interviewed vessels (explained in section 2.3). 
Seabird bycatch registrations were done by analysing the video material using playback which showed 
video- and GPS-data simultaneously, enabling the viewers to note date, time and position for all bycatch. 
The speed of the vessel was used to identify the periods of hauling, since the vessels slowed down during 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
every haul, and only these periods were analysed. Depending on the picture quality (reflection of the sun, 
darkness during night fishing, blackout of one or two cameras and blurred quality due to water on the lens) 
the videos were analysed with a speed 3-8 times faster than real time. All seabird registrations were saved 
in an excel file, with information about date, time, GPS position and species.  
 
2.3 
Interview data 
A number of fishermen were interviewed to get additional information about where their fisheries in the 
northern Sound have taken place. The purpose of the interviews was to get the fishermen to draw their key 
fishing areas on a map, with regards to species and months of the year based on their fishery the last few 
years. The aim of this approach was to create a larger and more detailed basis with regards to fishing days 
and fishing areas, and use this information to make a more complete calculation of seabird bycatch rates. 
The fishermen were chosen and contacted with help of the captain from camera vessel 1 on with the 
criteria that they used gillnets, fish in the Sound north of Copenhagen and had base on the east coast of 
Zealand. 
8 fishermen were interviewed, but only information from 7 of these was used, as one of the vessels did 
most of the fishing outside area 40G2. Because of the REM-system, GPS positions from every haul of 
camera vessel 2 was collected and added to the fishing grounds of the interviewed fishermen. 
The fishing grounds used by the fishermen were drawn by hand on a map of the northern Sound, and 
information about their fishery with regards to fishing days and species were extracted from the logbook 
database. Two of the 7 vessels were shorter than 8 meters, and therefore they were not required to fill in 
logbooks, instead information about year, date port of arrival, species and weight of species were extracted 
from a database based on the Danish fisheries sales slips. 
 
2.4 
Visual display of seabirds 
The REM data from the camera vessels were used to create a number of maps in ArcMAP 10.0.  
The first map plotted the seabird bycatches from camera vessel 1 with regards to years, to create a visual 
display of where the seabird bycatch happened. When the hauls from camera vessel 1 were added in a 
second map, a clustering of the fishing activity and the seabird bycatch became clear, and was used when a 
division of the northern Sound was created (section 2.6). 
The GPS-positions from the hauls of camera vessel 2 were also plotted in a third map.

2.6 
Area based bycatch rates 
With the aim of determining a more detailed fishing activity and seabird bycatch rate, Area 40G2 was 
divided into four smaller subareas based on the clustering of the hauls and seabirds, and used to calculate a 
more accurate seabird bycatch number from the northern Sound. The subareas were created mainly on the 
seabird clustering but the hauls from camera vessel 1 were also considered. 
By using ArcMAP to create the maps and the subareas, it was possible to both visualise and extract the 
fishing activity and seabirds from each subarea, as well as information about species and year.  
The fishing activity of all other gillnet vessels were registered as days and not hauls, and meant that the 
fishing activity from camera vessel 1 had to be extracted as days and not hauls from ArcMAP. The number 
of days extracted from ArcMap was not equal to the number of fishing days extracted from the logbook 
database. Information about the fishing activity of all other vessel were only available from the logbook 
data, and there for this was the days from camera vessel 1 that was used. The difference between the 
fishing days from camera vessel 1 from the two systems was calculated, and the difference was used to 
determine the number of fishing days in each subarea. 
From the number of fishing days and bycatches in the separate subarea the 6 years, a bycatch rate per year 
was calculated for each subarea. 
The 4 subareas were drawn by hand onto the maps drawn by the interviewed fishermen, and the 
information from the maps were combined with information about their individual fishing activity from the 
two databases, and resulted in the number of fishing day in each subarea. 
To simplify the manual counting, the most recent year with sufficient data (fishing days close to the average 
of all the years) for each of the 8 vessels were used to estimate the number in each subarea by each 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
fishermen (2015 for 6 vessels, 2013 for two vessels), as all fishermen claimed that they had fished in the 
same areas throughout the years. The percentage of days in each subarea that year, were used to define 
the days in each subarea for the other 5 years. 
Although the fishermen were not shown the subareas when asked to illustrate their fishing grounds, many 
of the fishing areas fell within a single subarea, but in the situations where there was an overlap between 
two or more subareas, the number of fishing days was divided between the subareas from a visual 
decision. A few fishing days for some of the vessels, could not be placed in a specific subarea, and were 
divided equally between all four subareas. 
Camera vessel 2 had both video data and logbook data, and as seen with camera vessel 1, the number of 
fishing days did not match, consequently the fishing days from the logbook were used. Almost all fishing 
activity from camera vessel 2 took place within subarea1. A few days had some hauls in subarea 2, but they 
were counted as being within subarea 1 as more hauls happen here. 
The seabird bycatches in each subarea for each year was calculated by using the bycatch rate from camera 
vessel 1 and the fishing days from the interviewed fishermen and camera vessel 2. 
To extrapolate this number to the whole fleet, the fraction the group of camera vessels and interviewed 
fishermen made up of the whole fleet was calculated, and the total seabird bycatch amount was 
determined. 
 
2.7 
Species specific bycatches 
The difference between the bycatch of the four seabird groups have been established previously, but the 
difference between years and subareas within the groups was also determined. Four maps were created to 
visualise the four groups’ specific bycatch according to the 6 years, and to extract the number of bird 
groups from each subarea according to years. 
By combining the fishing effort of the interviewed fishermen and camera vessel 2 and the bycatch rate 
from camera vessel 1 for each species, the calculation of seabird bycatch for the whole fleet was done. 
The GPS-position from each seabird, made it possible to determine the depth of where the each where 
caught, and the water depth average for each seabird group was determined and visualised in graphs.

3.1 
Camera vessel 1 data 
Camera vessel 1 had a total catch of 402,161 kg fish during the 6 years and a total of 21 species. 69.6 % of 
the total catch were cod, being the main catch 44 month out of 62 (7 month during the years, the vessel

flounder and plaice made up more than 25 % during the years. The mesh sizes 120 and 110 were used to 
catch more than 79 % cod throughout all the years, whereas a mesh size of 28 mm were used primarily in 
2010 to catch first and foremost herring.

2015 (2010 lower because of the first 3,5 months that were not included. The CPUE of camera vessel 1

4 Discussion 
The aim of this study was to calculate the bycatch rate and the total number of bycaught seabirds in the 
northern part of the Sound. REM data was collected from one fishing vessel. The data included information 
on fishing activity and video footage of seabird bycatch. Furthermore interviewed fishermen produced 
maps of fishing grounds, and combined with the REM data and the fishing activity of the gillnet fleet in the 
northern Sound; I was able to calculate the amount of seabirds bycaught in the northern Sound. 
The collection of data in this study is unique. It is the first study about seabird bycatch which has collected 
REM data for more than 5.5 years, almost continuously. The use of REM-systems to register bycatch from 
fishing vessels is a new approach, but has been used to observe harbour porpoise bycatch with a higher 
coverage percentage and a lower cost than the tradition method, on-board observers (Kindt-Larsen et al. 
2013). Not only is it possible to monitor all days of fishing for bycatches, the REM-system also makes it 
possible to extract the exact position, date and time for every bycatch simultaneously with the same data 
for every gillnet haul. 
 
The calculated seabird bycatch number for the northern Sound rests on a number of assumptions, the first 
is that the registered number of seabird bycatch from camera vessel 1 represents the actual number of 
seabird caught. Seabirds could be difficult to register on video, even when the video quality was good. To 
standardise the data process, I trained 6 of the 7 students viewing the videos, so almost all videos have 
been processed the same way. For all viewers seabirds can be difficult to register for several reasons; 1) 
they can be entangled in the net; 2) they can have the same colour as seaweed and be camouflaged; 3) 
they can be missed when it is dark and be difficult to distinguish because everything becomes grey; 4) they 
can by mistake be identified as cod due to their common size and 5) lastly the video quality can be so poor 
that they are simply difficult to see (Appendix 10). To prevent missing any, two precautions were taken. 
First, whenever the videos were of poor quality it was looked through at a slower speed (0.5 - 2 times real 
speed) than good quality videos (3 - 8 times real speed), second, everything that looked just a little like a 
seabird was registered by the students and double checked by me. The difficulty of identifying the seabirds 
varied between the species (Appendix 12). Male Common Eiders were under all conditions reasonably 
simple to identify, because of their distinctive colouring: white head and upper body, green neck, reddish-
orange throat and black back and wings. Female Common Eiders were difficult to identify as they could be 
difficult to distinguish from Cormorants in many conditions, due to their similar colouring, dark brown or 
black. If a female Common Eiders however was in the right angle to the camera, they could be identified by 
the head shape, beak and neck length. The yellow spot under the Cormorants beaks and the length of the 
neck could sometimes be seen, when these distinguishing features were not clearly visible they were 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
almost impossible to separate. Some Common Guillemots were easily identified; whenever their whole 
head and beak were visible, as this separates them clearly from other auks, but from a wrong angle or 
when only the body was visible, they could not be identified. The number of unidentified seabirds indicates 
the difficulty of identification, but also shows that bycatches still could be registered, without identifying 
the species. A number of difficult seabirds were identified with the help of two bird experts. Only 
registration which was positive to be seabirds was used, clear sign of head, beak or feet, so no false positive 
registrations occurred. The number of registered seabirds will consequently be a minimum of bycaught 
seabirds by camera vessel 1, as some could have been missed. 
The second assumption is that camera vessel 1 is representative for the rest of the fleet in relation to 
fishing areas and fishing activity during the years. Camera vessel 1’s detailed information of fishing areas 
was determined from the REM-data. A recognised way to track vessel’s fishing areas is by using Vessel 
Monitoring Systems (VMS) (e.g. Witt & Godley 2007; Gerritsen & Lordan 2011), this was not possible for 
this study, as Danish laws only require VMS systems on-board vessels larger than 12 meters. The majority 
of the gillnet vessels in the northern Sound were smaller than 12 meters. A total of 8 fishermen contributed 
with information to the fishing areas. When comparing the fishing area of camera vessel 1 and the 8 
fishermen, camera vessel 1 fished in almost all areas mentioned by the other fishermen.  
The fishing activity of the fleet fishing in the northern Sound was extracted with regards to fish species and 
fishing days. This made it possible to compare the fishing activity of the whole fleet, and determine that 
camera vessel 1 caught almost 12 % of the total catch during the years. The four most abundant species 
camera vessel 1 caught, were the same as the whole fleet. The CPUE from camera vessel 1 was also similar 
to the average of the fleet, (around 300 kg/fishing day). Together these factors indicate that camera vessel 
1, was a good representative for the whole fleet.  
In 7 of the 68 months that were covered, camera vessel 1 did not fish in the northern Sound, and this could 
have potentially affected the overall bycatch number as other vessels fished in the northern Sound during 
these months. All months were covered at least 3 times during the years by camera vessel 1. The least 
covered months were the summer months; April to August, all months with low seabird bycatch. This 
potential skewing of data could have decreased the number of registered seabird bycaught marginally. 
During the years, two different REM-systems were used, and the third assumption is that the detection rate 
of seabirds was equal under the two systems. The first system was based on analog footage whereas the 
second system used digital footage (Appendix 12), resulting in a difference in the quality of the footage. 
The lower quality in the analog system can thus have made it difficult to detect the birds in difficult 
situations. When looking at the registration of seabirds, it is however not possible to detect the shift 
between the systems that happened in the beginning of 2014. 
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
Regarding the interviewed fishermen one assumptions was made; that the fishermen provided the correct 
information, as their answers where both unsubstantiated and anecdotal. To prevent too many biased 
answers, I interviewed all the fishermen myself, asked the same questions and confirmed with them that 
the maps they drew covered their fishing grounds from the last few years. I also talked with the fishermen 
about the other fishermen’s fishing grounds, as they knew each other and could confirm the fishing 
grounds for each other. Interviews have been used in other studies going back more than 20 years 
(Støttrup et al. 2014), which makes it plausible that the interviews in this study contain the correct 
information. 
 
Two assumptions were associated to the use of logbook data. Firstly I assumed that by extracting 
information from the logbook database it would result in data from the whole fishing fleet of Danish 
gillnetters in the northern Sound. But not all gillnet vessels fill in logbooks, and a potential and unidentified 
bias is connected to using only the logbook data, as it is not known how many vessel from the northern 
Sound do not fill in logbooks. Two of the interviewed fishermen did not fill in logbooks, and this confirms 
the problem. Estimating the correct number of gillnet vessels from the northern Sound and their fishing 
activity was not possible, as data from the Danish Sales slips database could only be accessed with specific 
vessel ID’s, and other vessels ID’s than the ones from the logbooks database and the fishermen I 
interviewed are being protected.  
The information extracted from the logbook database was filtered to the smallest unit, ICES squares. ICES 
square 40G2, however covers waters from Helsingør in the North to Køge in the South (Figure 2) which is 
larger than the area covered by camera vessels and the interviewed fishermen (Figure 8 and Appendix3). I 
assumed that the fishing activity registered in 40G2 took place in the study area. This is likely biased, as it is 
not known if the rest of the fleet did use fishing grounds in the southern part of area 40G2. From the 
interviews I argue that the southern area was not or only inconsistently used, and the potential bias is 
small.  
 
The results of this study were produced with different approaches. The first two bycatch rates and number 
of seabirds were calculated based on the hypothesis that camera vessel 1’s data was directly transferrable 
to the whole gillnet fishing feet of the northern Sound. I argue that camera vessel 1 was a good 
representative for the seabird bycatch rates, due to the high fishing activity and large fishing ground. When 
comparing the calculation of bycatch rates from the weight of fish and the fishing days (4135 seabirds and 
5197 seabirds), they were in the same order of magnitude. However, as the seabird bycatch was clustered, 
a more area based calculation was tested. 
==PAGE:33==
33 
 
Two common ways to calculate seabird bycatch rates is in relation to either net length or time (days or 
years) (DeGange et al. 1993, Žydelis et al. 2009, 2013, Degel et al. 2010). Time was chosen as the relative 
factor, as no information about net lengths was available in this dataset. The map of the seabird bycaught 
by camera vessel 1 showed a clustering trend and to reflect this, the subareas were created. A bias by using 
the subarea was that they were not created based on any scientific or biological background, but purely on 
a visual choice. I did try to follow the most common North-South waterway used by large ships in the Sound 
(see Appendix 13), as fishing vessels stay away from the most used path of the large ships and ferries. If the 
borders had been elsewhere the seabird bycatch rate would have been different. All seabird bycatch 
registration, however, is away from the borders. The area based calculation resulted in a larger number of 
seabird bycatches (5446 seabirds) than both simple calculations, suggesting that they were both 
underestimates due to a lack of information. 
As the subareas were not based on biological borders, it is rational that no clear pattern was seen. 
Nonetheless when looking at information about Common Eiders’ preferred food object, bivalves, a pattern 
regarding where Common Eiders were caught can be seen. A paper from 2011 mapped the bottom types of 
the northern Sound, including different bivalve beds (Olesen et al. 2011) (appendix 14). Almost every single 
Common Eider was bycaught in areas with some kind of bivalve bed at the bottom, making it highly 
plausible that they were caught while they were hunting. The average water depth they were bycatch at, 
7.4 meter correlates to the observed results from Petersen et al. (2010), where most non-flying birds were 
observed at depths shallower than 10 meters. Both Cormorants and Common Guillemots hunt for pelagic 
fish, thus no specific bottom types would be expected to match their bycatch positions and none can be 
seen clearly. Both groups were on average bycaught at deeper waters than the Common Eider, which 
would be expected with their prey type. 
In relation to the unidentified seabirds they were distributed all over the study area, and Appendix 15 
shows the overlap with the other three seabird groups. In 2013 and 2014 subarea 1 had high numbers of 
Common Eider bycatches, but in 2015 the bycatch was considerable lower. With several unidentified 
bycatches in 2015 some of them are thus likely to be Common Eiders. Subarea 2 and 3 had low numbers of 
identified seabird bycatches which were almost equally distributed between the three seabird groups. The 
unidentified could thus not be linked to any identified seabird group. In subarea 4 Common Guillemots 
were caught in the northern part in both 2010 and 2015. Several unidentified birds were registered here in 
2012, and are thus likely to be Common Guillemots. A few Cormorants were caught in the same area, 
though in low numbers. The unidentified seabirds in the southern part of subarea 4 overlap with all three 
groups, and thus nothing can be said about their possible species.  
 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
Žydelis et al. (2013) argues that pursuit-diving ducks are more susceptible to gillnet bycatch than 
benthivorous ducks, e.g. Common Eider. The result of this study does not confirm this theory, rather the 
opposite. The hunting technic of Common Eiders, however, makes them vulnerable to the gillnet fishery, as 
they hunt bivalves near or at the bottom.  
Common Eider had the highest number of calculated bycatch (3330 birds). This correlates with the only 
other specific study of seabird bycatches from Denmark (Degel et al. 2010). Degel et al. calculated 598 
Common Eiders as bycaught by the Danish gillnet vessels (approximately 20 vessels) in the waters around 
Ærø. This study registered most Common Eiders as bycaught between September and March, which 
correlates with large wintering populations.  
Close to ¾ of the Common Eider population caught by camera vessel 1, was caught in November 2014 (187 
individuals). No other single month had a bycatch that large, making this a unique event. An increase in 
Common Eider bycatch was though observed through the years, even without the many bycatches in 
November 2014.  
Cormorants and Common Guillemots were likewise frequently bycaught during the years. Both species 
have in past decades been bycaught in high numbers in the Baltic Sea from the Swedish and German gillnet 
fleets (Žydelis et al. 2009). Degel et al. (2010) bycaught Cormorants frequently, but Common Guillemots 
were only bycaught twice. They also found Common Scoter and Long-tailed Ducks to be frequently 
bycaught. However, Common Scoter was not registered in this REM study but could be hidden in the 
unidentified. Long-tailed Ducks were also not identified, but they have mostly been registered further east 
in the Baltic Sea (Poland and the Baltic states) (Žydelis et al. 2009), and often far from shore, which for 
natural reasons is difficult in the northern Sound. 
A Swedish study identified Cormorants as the most common bycatch species, from interviews of 191 
fishermen (Lunneryd et al. 2004). They estimates 3004 seabirds (Cormorant, Common Eider and Common 
Guillemot) to have been bycaught in gillnets and fykes in the Sound and western coast of Sweden in 2001. 
Even though the study area is larger than the study in this study, bycatch rates can be concatenated, when 
looked at as annual bycatch per vessel per year. (Lynnerup et al. (2004) estimated 3004 birds/191 vessels = 
15.7 birds per vessel. Where I estimated (5446 birds/5.5 years)/64 vessels = 15.5 birds per vessel. Degel et 
al. registered 841 seabird bycatches (827 was Common Eider, Common Scoter, Cormorant and Long-tailed 
Duck) in the waters around Ærø between December 2001 and May 2002 from approximately 20 vessels, 
and thus calculated a seabird bycatch rate of 7.0 seabirds per vessel per month (841 seabirds/6 months/20 
vessels). I calculated 1.25 seabirds per vessel per month (5446 seabirds/68 months/64 vessels). 
The seabird bycatch rate I calculated is comparable with the seabird bycatch rate Lynnerup et al. (2004) 
estimated. Whereas the seabird bycatch rate Degel et al. (2010) found, was more than 5 times larger than 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
mine. The most possible explanation for this difference is that some factors between these areas are 
different, e.g bird abundance, fishing methods or annual changes. 
As revealed in the introduction most studies on seabird bycatch have been done with regards to longline 
fisheries (Bakken & Falk 1998, Brothers et al. 1999, Tasker et al. 2000, Weimerskirch et al. 2000, Belda & 
Sanchez 2001, Barry Baker & Wise 2005, Anderson et al. 2011, Berne 2014) and trawls (Weimerskirch et al. 
2000, Sullivan et al. 2006, Croxall 2008, Bartle 2010). However, recently Fangel et al. (2015) published a 
Norwegian paper on seabird bycatch in gillnet fisheries along the Norwegian west coast. They found auks to 
be the most common species bycaught (Fangel et al. 2015), and estimated 6400 seabirds to be bycaught in 
2009 by different kinds of gillnet vessels. Fangel et al. (2015) calculated bycatch rates per trip ranging 
between 0.10 and 1.58 birds per trip. I calculated bycatch rates per day, and annually they were between 
0.15 and 1.30 birds per day. Despite the area differences Fangel et al. (2015) calculated a seabird bycatch 
rate similar to what I found. This result confirms that though areas can geographically different, they can 
have similar seabird bycatch rates, thus some factors must be more important than area. Bycatch rates 
since 1977 have been estimated to range between; 250 birds/vessel/year; 8 - 186 birds/vessel/winter; and 
2.4 - 5.2 birds/day (Kirchhoff 1982, Kowalski & Manikowski 1982, Kieś & Tomek 1990, Stempniewicz 1994, 
Schirmeister 2003), which correlates with the larger seabird population in the 1970’s and 1980’s and 
following decrease in the 1990’s (Dybbro 2008, Ekroos et al. 2012, Žydelis et al. 2013, Laursen & Møller 
2014). These results emphasises the importance of the annual changes in seabird populations sizes 
regarding differences in seabird bycatch rates. Not at a yearly basis, but possible on a larger scale such as 
decades.  
Fangel et al. (2015) had high numbers of bycaught auks, whereas Degel et al. (2010), Lynnerup et al. (2004) 
and I had high number of Common Eider, Cormorants and Common Guillemots. This seems to undermine 
the importance of specific seabird species with regards to explaining differences in seabird bycatch 
between areas.  
These results show that no single known factor can explain differences in seabird bycatch rates. Seabird 
abundances do seem to have an impact, but more information is needed to clarify if other factors also 
influence the seabird bycatch rates.

5 Conclusion 
The aim of this study was to estimate the number of seabird bycaught by the Danish gillnet fleet, fishing in 
the northern Sound. By using a REM-system on-board a vessel, 5.5 years were continuously covered. This 
approach had never been used to register seabird bycatch before. As no other studies had covered the 
seabird bycatch of Danish gillnet fleet in the Sound before, nothing can be concluded about the accuracy of 
the total number. Seabird bycatch rates from other studies’ are though comparable, indicating that this 
approach is similar to other approaches.  
The three most abundant seabird species registered in this study, Common Eider, Cormorant and Common 
Guillemot, correlated with seabird previously registered as bycaught in Danish and neighbouring waters. 
The fact the Common Scoter was not registered at all, indicate that they were impossible to identify from 
the footage. This is a likely bias of using camera; the difficulty of distinguishing between similar species. It 
can though easily be avoided in future studies if the fishermen clearly show each bird to the camera. This 
will likely increase future seabird bycatch numbers, as fishermen handle every seabird also the ones that 
would otherwise not have be registered from the video material.  
No clear patterns were found in the differences of seabird bycatch rates when comparing areas or species, 
seabird abundances did though seem to be an important factor. In order to prevent possible future seabird 
bycatches it is not enough to know how many and which species that get bycaught, estimates of how to 
avoid them are also need. This includes potential changes in gillnet gear types, but also an understanding of 
why the seabirds are bycaught.
