Abstract (English)
The notable transition from geostationary to low-earth orbit satellite systems, initiated several years
ago, has had a profound impact on the satellite communication industry. To tackle the challenges
arising from this shift, companies are actively developing new antenna solutions.
The purpose of this thesis project was to design and investigate the planar phased array antenna
as a solution for satellite communication operation with one of the low-earth-orbit constellation
systems. The work was carried out in collaboration with Cobham SATCOM company. The an-
tenna solution put forward was designed using microstrip patch technology and included a complete
design process, covering the creation of a broadband single antenna element, which served as the
foundation for the subsequent development of a phased array system. The project’s primary focus
was to conduct an investigation into the performance of the array through the manipulation of its
features such as lattice configuration, inter-element spacing, or modification of individual antenna
element. The antenna elements and phased array antennas were validated using comprehensive,
full-wave 3D simulation software, accompanied by numerical verification facilitated by a Python-
based script rooted in antenna theory principles.
The simulation and numerical outcomes of the designed planar phased array antennas were dis-
cussed and assessed using a comparative analysis framework. While the final design of the planar
phased array antenna was not achieved, the undertaken work provides certain guidance on the design
process, illuminates potential challenges that may arise and offers insights for future iterations.

Introduction
In the last decade, the satellite communication is experiencing a paradigm shift from the use of
primarily geostationary satellites (GEO) to several large constellations located in Low-Earth-Orbit
(LEO). This shift has led to a growing number of companies developing their own LEO satellite
constellations and seeking an antenna solution for user terminal (UT) that would ensure effective
operation. The transition to LEO operation means that the satellites are no longer stationary from
the viewpoint of the user and therefore UT antennas must be able to track the satellites in the real
time as well as be capable for frequent handovers from one satellite to the next one. This is neces-
sary due to satellite’s short time presence in the certain region field of view (FOV). Because GEO
satellites are constantly seen from the same region of the earth, the communication can be achieved
using a single fixed installation of, for example, mechanically steered antenna. On the other hand,
LEO satellites require at least two tracking antennas or a single antenna with a complex mechanical
design to support scanning. This setup is essential for adjusting the beam to accommodate satel-
lites as they appear and depart. Consequently, companies specializing in satellite communications
are leading the development of new technologies to replace traditionally used mechanically steered
antennas with integrated single-antenna systems that do not require complex mechanical structures
for operation. This endeavor is driven by the dual objectives of achieving cost reductions and tack-
ling the challenges posed by the limitations of mechanically steered antennas, notably slower beam
pointing speeds.
With a focus on the demands of LEO operations, the objective is to tackle current challenges as-
sociated with dual antenna operation while simultaneously pursuing cost reduction. Consequently,
electronically steered antennas are emerging as an appealing alternative for the existing product.
Their various advantages include the ability to efficiently communicate with Low Earth Orbit (LEO)
satellites using a single array antenna, enabling swift beam switching for seamless connections with
multiple satellites, and the integration of technologies capable of substantially reducing costs and
antenna dimensions. Considering the aforementioned factors, the goal of this project is to design
and, if time permits, fabricate an antenna element of planar construction that will be suitable for use
in a planar phased array antenna, which belongs to the category of electronically steered antennas.
The design shall include the choice of the materials, physical layout, simulation results and, if pos-
sible, prototype measurements of the element in isolation, element in array and array performance.
The design of the antenna solution is to be conducted for the selected LEO constellation system.
The project is conducted in the collaboration with Cobham SATCOM, a prominent player in the
satellite communication sector who has opted to spearhead the development of an electronically
steered antenna solution. This initiative aims to offer a comprehensive solution to address these
needs and enrich their portfolio with such innovative solutions, marking a departure from their
previous offerings.
This report presents the compiled and detailed work efforts, organized as follows::
• Chapter 2 presents the overall background, relevant related work and the solution pursued in
this project.
• Chapter 3 outlines the antenna theory fundamentals with regards to microstrip patch antenna
technology and phased array antenna technique.

2
Background and motivation
2.1
Low-earth and geostationary orbits
For many years, the GEO has served for various forms of telecommunication. Its primary advantage
lies in the synchronization of satellite speed with the Earth’s rotation, ensuring that the satellite
remains stationary over a particular region at all times. Additionally, due to being positioned at
high altitude of approximately 35790km, GEO satellites offer extensive coverage of the Earth’s sur-
face requiring only a few satellites for comprehensive coverage. However, despite these advantages,
there are significant drawbacks that have prompted the transition towards Low-Earth-Orbit.
Firstly, in comparison to GEO, the LEO spans altitudes between 160 km to 1000 km according
to the European Space Agency (ESA) or below 2000 km according to the National Aeronautics
and Space Administration (NASA) [1] [2]. Consequently, the signal path between the satellite and
user terminal is significantly shorter compared to the path from the Earth to GEO. As a result,
the round-trip signal latency is approximately 100 ms for communication links with LEO satellites,
while it amounts to about 500 ms for GEO satellites. This fivefold reduction in latency achieved
with LEO satellites is a crucial factor for real-time missions and critical applications, such as mil-
itary operations. Secondly, owing to the shorter distance between the user terminal and satellite,
the resulting path loss is greatly reduced for communication with LEO satellites. As a result, the
requirements for user terminals and antenna structures are simplified, leading to less stringent con-
straints on dimensions and minimum parameter values, such as gain. This benefits both antenna
designers and companies by potentially saving costs and enabling the use of the same antenna
product across various environments with more stringent requirements. It is worth mentioning that
because of the lower altitude of LEO satellites, the overall cost of launching 1kg of payload is sig-
nificantly lower compared to launching the same payload to GEO. Additionally, LEO satellites are
much smaller structures operating in a less hazardous environment compared to those in GEO. An-
other noteworthy advantage is that the utilization of LEO enables extended coverage of the Earth’s
surface by deploying multiple satellites. This facilitates satellite communication links in areas not
served by GEO systems. Additionally, in LEO constellations, the failure of a single satellite does
not result in communication loss or significant problems, unlike in GEO setups.[3].
On the other hand, operating with LEO satellites experience certain drawbacks. To achieve com-
plete global coverage, a vast array of satellites is necessary. This is followed by need for more ground
stations that are tracking LEO satellites. Another issue stems from fact that individual satellites
are seen only for a short period of time from a certain region of the earth. This is because LEO
satellites move rapidly and thus are present for a short time in a field of view of a certain region
[1]. This implies that user terminal antennas need the ability to switch between individual antennas
to establish a connection with incoming satellites before losing connection with those exiting the
field of view. Additionally, these antennas must cover high scan angles to maintain a continuous
communication link [3]. Another challenge may arise from the operational environment, such as the
pitching and rolling of vessels. This phenomenon complicates the task of antennas in maintaining a
robust connection to Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites, which move swiftly and spend only a brief
time within the field of view.

2.2
Constellation choice
Despite significant investments being allocated to the development of LEO constellations, the ma-
jority of them are still in the process of being constructed. There are several constellations that are
currently in the advanced development stages where the operation is partially available. These are,
among others, Starlink, Telesat, OneWeb and Kuiper. Each constellation is characterised by the
number of crucial parameters such as number of satellites in full constellation, altitude, user link
frequencies, channel bandwidth, maximum antenna gain and EIRP as well as number of user beams
[3]. Due to the limited data availability regarding the communication technology adopted on the
board satellites, the Telesat Lightspeed constellation was chosen as the target for the antenna design
carried out in this project. This decision was made because the constellation allows third-party user
terminals, providing access to certain specifications.
Telesat Lightspeed is the satellite constellation dedicated for aeronautical, maritime, enterprise,
telecom and government networks. According to available data from [3] and [4], the number of
current satellites in the constellation is equal to 298 where the full constellation should consist of
1671 satellites. This means that as compared to the other constellations, the Telesat Lightspeed
is not a Mega-Constellations. Following that, the requirement for scan angle will be large. The
altitude of operation is between 1015 km to 1324 km above the sea level. The Telesat Lightspeed
system can support over 16 user beams, operating within the K-band frequency range. Specifically,
downlink frequencies range from 17.8 GHz to 20.1 GHz, while uplink frequencies range from 27.5
GHz to 30 GHz. It is important to highlight that all the mentioned satellite constellations utilize
the K-band for communication. This choice is driven by the broader bandwidth available in this
frequency range, enabling greater capacity, higher potential data transfer rates, or the possibility of
smaller user terminal antennas compared to lower-frequency bands. Although there might be higher
attenuation experiences as compared to the C-band or X-band, the utilization of K-band for com-
munication with LEO satellites does not lead to significant signal’s power degradation. Moreover,
it is generally less congested than the bands mentioned earlier [3].
2.3
Antenna solutions in K-Band
There are many different antenna systems that can be used for satellite scanning application in the
K-band. The main classification of such antennas is based on the scanning mechanism: mechanical
scanned antennas and electronically scanned antennas.
In principle, mechanically scanned antennas are the group of antennas where mechanical struc-
tures are utilized to enable scanning in different directions. There are four main classes of such
antennas that can be distinguished[3]:
• gimbals based antennas - these antennas allow to perform scanning in every direction. Their
structure consists of advanced mechanical gimbals that enable coupling the azimuthal rotation
with movement in elevation.
The gimbal structure usually utilizes several servomotors to
control scanning in different directions. The typical scanning gimbals based antennas utilize
reflector antennas that are quite bulky and heavy. While gimbal-based antennas are widely
used in the industry as full direction pointing systems, their mechanical complexity, reliance on
rotation mechanisms and non low-profile structure are prompting a shift towards alternative
technologies.
5
==PAGE:11==
• hybrid systems - these are antenna systems where the azimuthal rotation is achieved using
mechanical systems so that structure complexity - as compared to the gimbals based antennas
- is reduced.
The scanning in the elevation angle is thus obtained through utilization of
phase shifters, quasi-optical beamformers or multifeed and movable feed systems depending
on the applications. Some of the examples are lens-based concepts in parallel plate waveguide
technology or arrays based on long slot fed by multimode parallel plate waveguide technology,
however the limited scanning in elevation angle for such systems was achieved - up to 45◦-
50◦
• movable feeds and multifeed systems antennas - these antennas are based on volumetric lenses
array structures where movable feeds and multifeed systems are implemented. The current
solutions are based on modifications of Luneburg lenses and half Maxwell fish eye lenses so that
better efficiency, small aperture and low profile of the antenna can be obtained. The scanning
in such systems is obtained through the displacement of the feed. In such systems, scanning
occurs through feed displacement. It should be noticed that Luneburg lenses provide complete
azimuthal coverage, and ongoing research is currently exploring this antenna technology.
• rotatable surfaces antennas - this antenna classification comprise of systems that are low-
profile and enable scanning in every direction. One of the example could be VICTS antennas
designed by ThinKom company that consist of lightweight platters layers such as polarized
platter, aperture platter and feed platter that can rotate a center axist to steer the beam and
control the polarization of the placed antenna. As compared to gimbal solution, the motion
is performed within the antenna. These antennas enable low elevation performance as well
as full azimuthal coverage. Similarly to other mechanical solutions, it does not posses the
functionality of multi-beam operation. [5]. The other examples of rotatable surface antenna
systems could be antennas having rotatable graded index lenses or translating lenses where
the main challenges are control over the grating lobes during scanning or beam steering above
50° .
To sum up, the mechanically scanned antennas can offer full coverage of scanning angles. Depend-
ing on the technology, the scanning in elevation angle can be limited - as for some hybrid and
rotatable surfaces systems - or extended towards full scanning capabilities as it is in case of gimbals
based antennas. The primary drawbacks of this antenna category include dependence on mechani-
cal structures for scanning, leading to heightened system complexity, increased power consumption,
limited speed and beam repositioning capabilities, lower reliability, and typically, the absence of
multiple-beam operation. Additionally, certain antenna systems, such as reflector antennas, may
not be suitable for operation on aircraft or certain vehicles due to their size and dimensions.
The second category of antenna systems, which could serve as a viable alternative to mechani-
cally steered antennas, encompasses electronically scanned antenna systems. These systems achieve
beam steering without relying on mechanical structures and motors. They stand out for their ex-
tensive scanning capabilities, achieving a scan range of ±70◦degrees [3], supporting multiple-beam
operation, radiation pattern shaping, operating with dual polarization, and minimizing maximum
scan loss. With electronic scanning, these antennas are particularly appealing for satellite communi-
cation applications, especially in scenarios requiring low-profile antennas capable of swiftly altering
scanning directions—crucial for installations on aircraft or ships, where limited space, continuous
movement, and potential rolling are challenges to be addressed. Furthermore, when operating with
6
==PAGE:12==
LEO satellite constellations, such antenna systems offer quicker beam repointing and necessitate
only one antenna for such operation. In contrast, mechanical scanned antennas may require multiple
antennas to maintain connection stability as satellites move in and out of the field of view. The fully
electronic scanning array antennas performs the beam steering through utilization of signal phase
shift that is delivered to each of the antennas creating the array and due to that such antennas are
called phased array antennas. Despite numerous advantages, phased array antennas have certain
drawbacks. One issue arises from scanning to high elevation angles, leading to deterioration of
power performance. Additionally, there is a reduction in aperture projection, resulting in decreased
gain. Moreover, phased array antennas are characterized by narrow bandwidth, necessitating the
use of multiple antennas for multi-band operation [3].
2.4
Summary - design requirements
After thoroughly assessing market demands and considering various solutions, the joint decision
with Cobham SATCOM was made to design and investigate a low-profile, phased array antenna
that would be suitable for operation within the Telesat Lightspeed constellation. This decision was
driven by market requirements necessitating wide-angle scanning capability, rapid beam switching,
and the ability to apply the antenna across land, maritime, and aircraft domains that is possible
to achieve through utilization of electronically scanned array antenna. The following design criteria
and features are defined:
• Antenna type: Planar phased array
• Antenna technology: Microstrip patch technology
• Operational frequency and bandwidth: Designed for the reception band of Telesat Light-
speed, operating within the frequency range of 17.8 GHz to 20.1 GHz, providing a bandwidth
of 2.3 GHz. This bandwidth is defined by the criterion S11 > −10 dB.
• Scanning capabilities goal: Designed to achieve scanning in elevation angles up to 70◦
while ensuring complete azimuthal coverage.
It was decided to limit the number of requirements to the most fundamental ones, given the inves-
tigative nature of the project.
For simulation verification purposes, the Dassault CST Studio Suite, a 3D full-wave electromag-
netic simulation software, was chosen. It was agreed that in addition to simulation design, further
verification will be conducted using Python-based scripts. These scripts will employ antenna theory
to calculate specific antenna parameters and the purpose of implementing a numerical approach is
to investigate and confirm its compatibility with simulation results obtained from full-wave soft-
ware solvers. This is crucial because, if proven accurate, the numerical approach could serve as a
viable approximation for antenna design. Such validation could potentially save considerable time
otherwise spent on conducting numerous simulations.

Antenna theory
The following chapter contains the necessary theory in the field of microstrip patch antennas and
phased array antennas. At the beginning, the knowledge of microstrip antenna technology is pre-
sented where principle of operation, substrate materials and feeding techniques are described. Af-
terwards, the broadbanding techniques together with chosen solution and design procedure are
described. At the end the fundamental theory of phased array antennas is presented.
3.1
Patch antennas
3.1.1
Structure and operation
Figure 1 shows the microstrip patch antenna together with microstrip feed line. The fundamental
configuration of the microstrip patch antenna consists of three layers:
• ground plane - the conductive surface located at the bottom of the patch antenna.
The
conducting materials such as copper or aluminium are used for ground plane due to their high
conductivity and ease of fabrication.
• substrate material - the middle layer of the patch antenna. This layer serves as a mechanical
support for the antenna and separates the patch from the ground plane, and is characterised by
parameters such as dielectric constant and height (also called thickness) that have significant
impact on the patch antenna performance.
• microstrip patch - top layer of the patch antenna. Microstrip patch acts as the radiating
element of the antenna and similarly to the ground plane it is made of well conducting material
such as copper or aluminium.
The microstrip patch antenna presented in Figure 1 is described by the following parameters:
• length of the patch, L
• width of the patch, W
• height/thicnkess of the substrate material, h
• dielectric constant of the substrate material, εr
• metal thickness, t

==PAGE:14==
Figure 1: Microstrip antenna - top and side view [6]
.
Radiation mechanism of patch antennas can be explained using rectangular patch antenna example
and cavity model. The microstrip patch antenna is excited by applying radio frequency signal from
the source connected to the feed line. Under the presence of this signal, the charge distribution
within the antenna is established so that positive and negative charges are present on the surfaces of
the radiating patch and ground plane. These charges are distributed over the patch so that positive
and negative charges are present on the two edges of the patch - see Figure 2 [6].
Figure 2: Charge distribution and current density on microstrip patch. [6]
.
The charge distribution is associated with two mechanisms. The first one is the repulsive mechanism
where charges, that are displaced on the bottom of the radiating patch, tend to push charges at
the bottom surface to the top surface through the patch edges. With this phenomenon there are
two associated current densities - ⃗Jb representing the current density on the bottom surface of the
patch and ⃗Jt representing the current density on the top surface of the patch. However, due to
the substrate’s small thickness as compared to the patch dimensions, there is a second force - the
attractive mechanism - that dominates and thus the charge concentration together with the current
flow remain on the bottom surface of the patch. Because of that, negligibly small tangential mag-
netic field is present, which is usually approximated to be zero, and therefore there are no tangential
magnetic field components at the edge of the patch. Following this, the cavity model of microstrip
patch antenna consists of four side walls which are modelled as perfect magnetic conducing surfaces
[6].
9
==PAGE:15==
The two remaining cavity walls are associated with the top and the bottom of the microstrip
patch antenna. They are modelled as electric walls because the substrate height is very small as
compared to the wavelength guided in the substrate, which leads to the fact that electric field vari-
ations along the height can be considered constant and normal to the surface of the patch. The two
remaining cavity walls are associated with the top and bottom of the microstrip patch antenna, and
are modeled as electric walls. For such cavity model of the microstrip patch antenna the following
currents can be defined:
• ⃗Jb - equivalent current representing current density at the bottom of the radiating patch,
however it is not used in this model explanation.
• ⃗Jt - equivalent current representing current density on the top of the radiating patch. This
current can be set to zero due to being very small as compared to ⃗Jb.
That means that
radiation coming from the current on the top surface of the patch is negligible.
• ⃗Js - equivalent current associated with magnetic field in the side slot of the cavity. Because
it was shown that tangential magnetic fields are negligible, the ⃗Js can be set to zero.
• ⃗Ms - equivalent current associated with the electric field in the side slot of the cavity. This
is the only non-zero current density in the cavity model. By exploiting image theory it can
be shown that this current will be doubled if the presence of the ground plane is taken into
consideration. The current representation in the cavity model is represented in Figure 3
Figure 3: Cavity model representation with only equivalent magnetic current density present on
the side walls of the cavity model [6].
The conclusion is that the radiation of the microstrip patch antenna can be modeled as radiation
from four magnetic currents on the four side walls of the cavity model. Further investigation shows
that magnetic current densities along the two slots separated by length L are of the same magnitude
and phase, thus these two sources add in a direction normal to the patch and ground plane forming
a broadside pattern. The other two slots that are separated by width W are also of the same
magnitude, but opposite direction, so they cancel each out in both the E- and H-planes [6].

3.1.2
Substrate material
The substrate material is essential part of the microstrip patch antenna structure. Substrate mate-
rials are characterised by, among others, two important parameters,:
• dielectric constant εr
• substrate height h
Dielectric constant of the material describes the energy storage capabilities of a material as com-
pared to the storage capabilities of vacuum. This means that the higher the dielectric constant
value, the grater the material’s capacity for energy storage. From the microstrip patch antenna
point of view it means that the binding of the electric field withing the antenna will be stronger,
leading to worse efficiency or limited bandwidth. However on the other side, the antenna dimen-
sions are inversely proportional to the dielectric constant resulting in smaller structure that can be
important, depending on the application. Therefore, a compromise must be reached to optimize
performance. However the most desirable substrate materials for the microstirp patch antennas are
characterised by low dielectric constant value [6][7].
Figure 4 shows the simulated bandwidth as a function of the substrate permittivity. It can be
seen that lower dielectric constant substrates allow to obtain higher bandwidth. It is important to
point out that the achieved fractional bandwidth is in the best case scenario - for dielectric constant
equal to 2.2 - around 1.6% which indicates a very narrow-band antenna. The antenna design for

Radiating patch shape
The are numerous shapes of the microstrip patch antennas. The simplest and most common con-
figurations are square, rectangular, circular or circular ring patch due to their ease of analysis and
fabrication. Every shape of radiating patch may improve certain antenna parameters such as band-
width or modify the radiation characteristics, whereas the other may enable operation with some
additional features such as circular polarization. What is more, there are numerous works where
various shapes modifications are performed in order to obtain certain characteristics or improvement
[6].

3.1.4
Feeding techniques
There are various feeding techniques that are applied in microstrip patch antenna design. According
to [6], the two most common are microstrip feed line and coaxial probe that are shown in Figure 6:
• microstrip line feeding - in this configuration it is the additional microstrip line that serves as
path for applying signal into the antenna. The microstrip lines are common due to their ease
of fabrication and possibility of achieving good matching to the antenna input impedance.
On the contrary, microstrip feed line requires substrates with higher dielectric constant to
minimize the undesired radiation and coupling. Moreover, the microstrip feed lines should
be of low thickness so that excitation of surface waves and spurious radiation is minimzed.
Having the requirement for substrate material described in the previous section in mind, a
compromise for substrate material parameters must be established.
• coaxial probe feeding - in this configuration it is the coaxial probe inner wire that is soldered
to the radiating patch of the antenna and outer wire that is connected to the ground plane.
As compared to the microstrip line technique, the coaxial feeding provides very good isolation
between the feed network and the radiating patch without comprising the substrate parameters
[7].
The two other common feeding techniques are aperture coupling and proximity coupling. However
due to focus on the microstrip line feed and coaxial probe feed techniques in this project they are
not discussed in a more detailed way.

3.2
Broadband techniques
There are different techniques used for achieving broadband operation for microstrip patch anten-
nas. Some of the techniques are based on antenna structure modifications, whereas the other ones
consider minor changes [8].
13
==PAGE:19==
3.2.1
Stacked patch antennas
One of the common techniques used for broadbanding is stacking patch antenna elements. The
stacked patch antenna configuration utilizes additional substrate layers and radiating patches in
order to realize broadbanding - Figure 7 shows the simple stacked patch antenna structure that
consists of two substrate materials, two radiating patches and coaxial probe feeding. The radiating
patch located on the bottom substrate to which coaxial probe is connected, is called a driven patch,
whereas the patch above is called a parasitic patch. While a stacked patch antenna can incorpo-
rate multiple dielectric layers and radiating patches, the enhancement in bandwidth compared to a
well-optimized double-layer stacked patch antenna design is relatively marginal [8].
Stacked patch antenna configuration is popular due to several advantages[8]:
• It possess significant tuning degrees due to multiple design variables associated with substrates
dielectric constants and thicknesses, patch dimensions, freedom of utilization different feeding
techniques together with feed positioning
• It is possible to obtain operational bandwidth ranging from 10% up to 67%
• Stacking patches does not require increase in the single antenna surface area which is a crucial
feature for array antennas
• Symmetry of radiation pattern and phase centre is preserved
Figure 7: Side view of exemplary stacked patch antenna configuration with coaxial probe feed.
3.2.2
Stacked patch antennas with coplanar parasitic elements
Another broadbanding technique, that can be considered as a modification of stacked patch antenna
method, utilizes coplanar parasitic elements. Similarly to the stacked patch antenna, this configu-
14
==PAGE:20==
ration consists of additional substrate layers, however the main feature are the coplanar resonators
in a form of gap coupled patches that are located in the close proximity to the radiating patch -
Figure 8 shows the radiating patch together with two parasitic elements. Depending on the shape
and location of the resonators, a different bandwidth improvement can be achieved - for coplanar
parasitic patches located along the radiating edge a better bandwidth improvement was obtained
according to [8]. For this antenna structure additional modifications were implemented such as
introduction of quarter-wave short circuited coplanar parasitic patches that reduced the mutual
coupling between the two radiating edges of rectangular patch.
Patch antennas with coplanar parasitic elements share some of the advantages of stacked patch
antennas.
Due to additional substrate layers and parasitic patches they also give a significant
degree for design tuning. Moreover, the implementation of coplanar parasitic patches enrich this
advantage and allows to influence on different phenomena such as mutual coupling. The main dis-
advantages of this configuration is enlargement of the surface area as well as change in the radiation
pattern and phase center while broadband operation of the antenna is desired. These aforemen-
tioned drawbacks have to be taken into consideration while implementing such antennas into the
array structures.
Figure 8: Top view of the exemplary patch antenna with coplanar parasitic elements.
3.2.3
Resistive loading and impedance matching
The exemplary methods utilizing minor adjustment to the antenna structure that may result in
bandwidth enhancement are resistive loading and impedance matching.
The resistive loading is based on the fact that impedance bandwidth can be improved by intro-
ducing losses to the structure. This can be achieved by either changing the substrate material for
the one with higher dissipation factor or by implementing a chip resistor to the radiating patch.
Together with bandwidth enhancement it is possible to achieve antenna size reduction [8], however
it should be noted that the trade-off of such approach is decrease in the radiation efficiency and
thus antenna gain.
15
==PAGE:21==
The impedance matching approach utilizes the tuning of matching network instead of stacked patch
antenna as itself.
In principle, the feeding network should be located as close to the radiating
patch as possible. One of the microstrip feed line configurations that can be used is multi-section
impedance transformer. In such configuration the process of matching impedance from the genera-
tor impedance to the antenna input impedance involves several impedance transformation sections.
Due to that, bandwidth reduction that occurs due to a high-step impedance transformation is min-
imized. The more impedance sections there are, the smaller the bandwidth reduction. However,
this comes at the cost of increasing the size of individual antenna elements. Another example of
impedance matching technique might be introduction of conducting strip inclined from the radiation
patch down to the ground plane. Although this approach may lead to bandwidth improvements,
the deterioration of the performance at the higher frequencies can be observed due to higher order
modes excitation [8].
3.2.4
Chosen broadband technique - design procedure
The stacked patch antenna configuration presented in the Section 3.2.1 was chosen as the broad-
banding method. The broadband operation of the antenna can be illustrated using the antenna
input impedance representation on the Smith chart. Figure 9 Shows the simulated antenna input
impedance of single patch and stacked patch antenna designs. For the single antenna design the
impedance plot is located in the close proximity to the centre of Smith chart for limited frequency
range. This means that good matching is achieved for limited band of operation. On the contrary,
for the stacked patch antenna design the so-called resonant loop is present in the close proximity
to the centre of Smith chart. This resonant loop creation means that for larger frequency range
the antenna input impedance is located in the close proximity of the smith chart. Due to that for
larger bandwidth good matching is obtained. This resonant loop creation – being the graphical
representation of broadband operation – is the design goal.
16
==PAGE:22==
Figure 9: The antenna input impedance presented on Smith chart for single antenna element and
stacked patch antenna designs.
According to [7], the largest bandwidth is obtained when the magnitude of the first-order mode on
the bottom patch is bigger as compared to the magnitude of obtained for the top patch. In order
to achieve this behaviour, the top patch should not be coupled too strongly as compared to the
bottom patch. Following the above, the bottom substrate material should be of higher dielectric
constant and the top one of the lower dielectric constant value.
The thickness of material also plays an important role - following the outcomes of conducted in-
vestigation on single patch antenna in Section 3.1.2 it would be natural to choose thicker material
for the bottom substrate. However, for the stacked patch configuration it is important to achieve
strongly coupled behaviour to obtain large bandwidth. Due to that it is the thin material on the
bottom that would be more desired. Moreover, if the coaxial cable is used as a feeding method,
its inductive behaviour has to be taken into consideration. Additionally, the patch adjustment will
have impact on impedance change and thus resonant loop shift on the impedance chart, so it will
be mainly utilized for frequency tuning [7].
Taking the above aspects into consideration, the following design strategy is formed [7]:
1. Choose the substrate materials for the antenna - bottom substrate should be of lower thickness
and higher dielectric constant as compared to the top susbtrate.
17
==PAGE:23==
2. Design the single patch antenna consisting of bottom substrate material and bottom patch
with resonance frequency around the lower edge of the frequency range, that is 17.8 GHz, so
as a results strongly capacitive behaviour of the antenna input impedance can be observed
across the bandwidth of interest.
3. Add the top substrate and tune the design so that capacitive behaviour described in step 2 is
preserved.
4. Add the parasitic patch on the top of the top substrate. Tune the antenna in order to place
the obtained resonant loop close to the centre of the smith chart so that largest possible
bandwidth is achieved.
5. If the probe fed method is implement, tune the coaxial probe position so that better matching
is obtained.
3.3
Planar phased array antennas
Planar phased array antennas are low-profile structures that consists of multiple single antenna
elements and utilize electronical beam steering.
The beamforming mechanism is based on the
constructive and destructive interference of the fields being radiated from the array elements. It
is desired to achieve constructive interference in the direction of scanning, whereas the destructive
interference should occur for all other directions. This constructive and destructive interference
may be achieved through feeding individual array antenna elements with signals that are shifted in
phase with respect to each other. If the amplitude of excitation signal is equal then the excitation is
called to be uniform, whereas amplitude varying, known as amplitude tapering, is utilized to achieve
certain pattern shape modifications [9]. According to [6], there are at least five mechanisms that
can be used to modify the pattern of the antenna (including phase and amplitude adjustments):
1. the lattice/geometry of array configuration
2. the displacement of element in the array
3. the excitation phase of the individual elements
4. the excitation amplitude of the individual elements
5. the relative pattern of the individual elements
3.3.1
Beamforming mechanism
The far-field radiation pattern of phased array is a product of element pattern and array factor -
is also known as pattern multiplication. The element pattern is the far-field E-field pattern of the
array’s individual element obtained through either simulation or measurement when all other array
elements are terminated to the matched loads. The array factor is the function of array number
of elements, their geometrical arrangement, relative magnitudes, phases and relative spacing [6]. It
does not depend on the directional characteristic of the radiating element so it can be concluded that
it includes information regarding location and excitation of each individual array antenna element.

3.3.2
Mutual coupling
Single antenna elements present in the array are capable of interchanging a certain amount of energy
during transmission or reception. This undesired phenomenon is called mutual coupling and have
significant impact on the array performance [6]. Mutual coupling for the receiving array can be
explained using Figure 14. There are two antennas m and n that are located next to each other in
the array. When the incident plane wave reaches the first antenna, antenna m, before reaching the
second one, two things are happening. First of all, a part of incident wave causes the current flow
in the antenna m, whereas the other part of incident wave is scattered back. This scattered wave
can either travel into the space or into the direction of antenna n resulting in additional, undesired
current flow in this antenna. What is more, in the antenna m there is a possibility that some part
of current wave will be reflected back to the antenna. This undesired current flow excitation in the
second antenna as well as current wave reflection in the first antenna will influence both antennas
self-impedance by introducing mutual impedances.
From the circuit point of view the presence of mutual coupling can be illustrated with voltage,
current and impedance matrices. In ideal and unreal situation where mutual coupling phenomenon
does not exist, the antenna input impedance would be equal to antenna self-impedance. In case
of mutual coupling presence, the additional current flow in the antenna is associated with voltage
and impedance change of this particular element. Thus the antenna input impedance is not only
dependent on the antenna self impedance but also it contains the mutual impedances between this
antenna and other elements present in the array. It should be noted that mutual impedances are
in principle calculated between the two elements where the other are open-circuited. The scale of
mutual coupling between two elements depends on several factors among which antenna element
type and design parameters, array geometry and spacing between the elements have important role.
[6] [10]
22
==PAGE:28==
The presence of mutual coupling can significantly impact the performance of an array. Initially, it
alters the impedance of individual antenna elements. If this impedance deviates significantly from
the generator/load impedance, it deteriorates the antenna’s input reflection coefficient, potentially
reducing its operational bandwidth. Moreover, mutual coupling can further degrade the overall
bandwidth of the array. Additionally, changes in the antenna’s input impedance can increase power
loss in individual elements, leading to a decline in the array’s power performance. Another crucial
aspect is that mutual impedances between array elements vary with the scan angle, leading to varied
antenna performance when scanning to different directions. [6] [10].
Figure 14: Coupling paths in the receiving mode between two antennas m and n [6]
Depending on the antenna type there might be additional factors that have to be taken into con-
sideration while designing the full array. For the microstrip patch antennas, the mutual coupling is
influenced by the presence of surface waves. In principle, the stronger the surface waves, the higher
impact through mutual coupling effects they have on individual array performance in the array.
Due to that the control over surface waves should be performed - this can be done by for example
ensuring the operation with substrates of small thickness [6] [10].
3.3.3
Array parameters and characteristics
This section outlines key array characteristics along with distinctive ones associated with array and
phased array antennas. The fundamental array parameters are:
23
==PAGE:29==
• Directivity: It is the ratio of radiation intensity in a given direction to the radiation intensity
averaged over all directions.
It can be seen as a parameter that describes the ability to
concentrate the energy in a certain direction. The directivity of the array is dependent on
factors such as the number of elements, relative spacing between the elements, and frequency
of operation.
• Gain: It is the ratio of the radiation intensity in a given direction to the radiation intensity
that would be obtained if the power accepted by the antenna were radiated isotropically. Gain
takes into account mismatch losses. It is dependent on the same factors as directivity as well
as the matching quality between the antenna and circuitry.
• Realized Gain: It is the gain of the antenna reduced by the additional losses related to
mismatches, transmission lines, connectors, and other factors that affect the antenna’s per-
formance in real-world conditions. Realized gain provides a more accurate measure of the
antenna’s performance in practical scenarios.
• Beamwidth: It represents the angle between certain power points in the main lobe of the
antenna’s radiation pattern. Typically, it is related to the half-power point of the main lobe
and thus is called half-power beamwidth (HPBW). In the phased array antennas it is desired
to achieve as narrowest beamwidth as possible. The beamwidth can be controlled by utilizing
amplitude tapering in the array. In this report beamwidth results always refer to half-power
beamwidth.
• Bandwidth: The range of frequencies within which the performance of the antenna, with
respect to some characteristic, conforms to a specified standard. It can also be specified as
fractional bandwidth where normalization to the center frequency is performed. Bandwidth
is strongly influenced by the mutual coupling effects.
• First Sidelobe Level: It represents the level of radiation in the first sidelobe compared to
the main lobe of the antenna pattern. It is desired to have first side lobe level as low as
possible. The side lobe lebels can be controlled by utilizing amplitude tapering. In this report
side lobe level results always refer to difference between main lobe level and first side lobe
level.
• Aperture efficiency: it indicates how efficiently the physical area of the array is utilized.
During reception it gives understanding how well antenna captures and converts the incident
waves into the electrical signals.
The aperture efficiency is inversely proportional to the
physical area of the antenna and directly proportional to the wavelength and directivity.
Phased array antennas possess additional parameters such as:
• Scan accuracy: it tells how closely the actual scan angle matches the desired scan angle. The
higher the scan accuracy the better is the array performance regarding scanning capabilities.
• Scan loss: represents the degradation of realized gain over the scan angle [9]. Scan loss can
be represented in a form of some power of the (cos θ)n where n is equal or higher than 1. The
best case scenario where scan loss can be represented by cos θ can be achieved in only one
plane of scan. To ensure that the degradation remains within acceptable limits, it must be
verified along the array design and due to that obtaining the (cos θ)1.5 curve for both planes is
24
==PAGE:30==
a common practice. It should be noted that if design is not performed carefully, it is possible
that so-called scan blindness will occur. The scan blindness is the phenomenon where for
certain scan direction the full radiation cancellation is obtained [10] [9].
• Grating lobes: In the visible range, defined by the elevation angle θ spanning from -90° to
+90° , it is conceivable for the array factor to exhibit maximums at multiple scanning angles.
Consequently, due to pattern multiplication, the array’s radiation pattern may feature two
lobes of equal magnitude. The primary lobe points towards the desired scan direction, while
the secondary lobe, known as a grating lobe, points towards an unintended direction. The
occurrence of grating lobes hinges on the spacing between the elements within the array. If
this spacing is too large, grating lobes will manifest in both the array factor and the radiation
pattern. For a rectangular array, avoiding grating lobes necessitates ensuring that the spacing
between elements is either equal to or less than half the wavelength (λ/2). It is important to
note that the specific limitation on inter-element spacing might vary depending on the array
lattice.
• Beam squint: for the arrays where scanning is performed with fixed phase shifts, the beam
direction may change with frequency.

Coordinate system
The coordinate system shown in Figure 15 is utilized for all antenna designs and considerations. In
this system, the elevation angle θ spans from the z-axis to the xy-plane, while the azimuth angle ϕ
starts from the x-axis and remains within the xy-plane. Point ST represents a sample scan target,

4.2
CST model
The Dassault Systems CST Studio Suite, a high-performance 3D electromagnetic analysis simula-
tion software, was utilized to design and validate both single antenna elements and phased array
antennas. Specifically, the Time Domain Solver, based on the Finite Integration Technique, was
26
==PAGE:32==
selected for its efficiency in high-frequency simulations, particularly in antenna design applications.
This solver allows for two simulation types: simultaneous, where all ports are simulated concur-
rently, and sequential, where only one port is simulated at a time with the others terminated into
matched loads. This capability enables the extraction of S-parameters and Z-parameters matrices,
particularly useful when the phased array is fed through multiple sources.
Concerning specific model properties, hexahedral meshing was selected with a defined meshing
density of either 15 or 10 cells per wavelength. Initially, the intention was to streamline the design
process by incorporating infinite substrate and ground layers, achieved by setting the boundary
condition to ”open,” except for the antenna’s field of view direction, which was set to ”open (add
space)”. While this approach proved effective for a single antenna, it encountered problems when
applied to array designs. As a result, the decision was made to standardize the simulation process by
employing boundary conditions set to ”open (add space)” for all directions in all designs, ensuring
consistency throughout.
The array designs were generated with the assistance of a feature known as the array task. This
functionality streamlines antenna array design by utilizing uploaded TSV files to implement appro-
priate geometry and signal definitions based on the single antenna element design.
It is worth noting that during the initial three months of the project, the Multilayer Solver was
utilized. While the manufacturer describes the solver as a 3D planar electromagnetic solver capable
of simulating multilayer geometries and featuring automatic layer stackup and operation with infi-
nite planes, however, several challenges arose during the design process, including difficulties with
implementing probe feeding. Fortunately, an employee with expertise in CST was encountered at
Cobham SATCOM, enabling access to CST support which is unfortunately not available for private
users, students or academic institutions. This accidental encounter proved pivotal, as it revealed
that the multilayer solver is actually a 2.5D solver, does not support out of plane ports, the multiple
layers function is not recommended for infinite plane creation and that solver is rarely used nowa-
days. The CST representative therefore suggested using alternative solvers like Frequency Domain
or Time Domain.

Phased Array - design and analysis
The forthcoming chapter outlines the design and parametric investigation of planar phased array
antenna. The antennas have been designed and verified using the Dassault CST Studio Suite sim-
ulation software. Furthermore, the simulation results have been preceded by verification through
numerical computations based on antenna array theory. The numerical approach has been imple-
mented to verify the simulation results, but primarily to ensure coherence.
All simulation results were achieved through simultaneous simulation, unless otherwise specified.
Additionaly, exceptions include cases where the input reflection coefficient, directivity pattern of
center and edge array elements, as well as the scattering matrix of a uniform linear array, were
obtained through sequential simulation. During simultaneous simulation, all antenna elements were
excited simultaneously with specific phase shifts to enable beam steering to the desired angle. No-
tably, amplitude tapering was not employed, indicating uniform amplitude excitation.
5.1
Uniform linear array design
The phased array antenna investigation has started with the brief evaluation of Uniform Linear
Array (ULA) design that consists of three stacked patch antennas placed along x-axis. For the array
creation the initial stacked patch antenna design with probe feeding has been chosen. Figure 26
shows the uniform linear array design in CST studio where adjacent antenna element are positioned
with inter-element spacing of λ/2 = 8.015mm. The frequency corresponding to a wavelength is
18.7 GHz, which serves as the midpoint frequency within the range where the initial stacked patch
antenna input reflection coefficient result is better than -10 dB.
Figure 26: Uniform linear array antenna design in CST studio.
The array’s linear configuration enables scanning in a single direction, featuring a range of elevation
angles denoted by θ, while the azimuth angle ϕ remains constant at 0°. Elevation scanning was
conducted across angles ranging from 0° to 70° with increments of 10°.
Figure 27 shows simulation results of the input reflection coefficient for each antenna composing the
uniform linear array, as well as the performance of an isolated antenna element. Simulation results
for the array antenna have been obtained through simultaneous excitation of all of the the array
38
==PAGE:44==
elements. It is worth noting that the S11 results for both left and right antennas are identical. This
similarity arises from the uniform strength of mutual coupling effects, the utilization of identical
antenna elements within the Uniform Linear Array (ULA), and the symmetry of these elements
relative to the central antenna. Upon analysis, it becomes apparent that integrating antennas in
the array causes a reduction in bandwidth for each element compared to their isolated counterpart.
This reduction stems from the phenomenon of mutual coupling. With additional antennas present
in the array, there are corresponding mutual impedances that influence the self-input impedance of
each antenna. These mutual impedances can either augment or diminish impedance performance,
leading to deviations from the original values. As a result, the S11 performance deteriorates, with
the most significant impact observed on the middle antenna element within the array. This occurs
because the power transfer between this element and the adjacent left and right elements remains
consistent, owing to the identical strength of mutual impedance. Conversely, in the case of the right
antenna element, the power transfer between it and the middle element remains unchanged. How-
ever, the power transfer between the right element and the left antenna element decreases due to
the larger spacing between these two elements - to that the mutual impedance is reduced and more
bandwidth is preserved. The above is confirmed and by analysing the scattering matrix transmission
coefficients between the left antenna element and the other ones illustrated in Figure 28.

The S11 parameter deterioration is caused by the lattice change - in contrast to the rectangular

5.3
Summary
The comparative analysis between rectangular array antennas and equilateral triangular phased
array antennas, with an inter-element spacing of 0.5λ, indicates that the rectangular array is prefer-
able when prioritizing S11 performance. However, neither array meets the bandwidth requirements
due to input reflection coefficients falling below -10 dB across the frequency range. Conversely, the
triangular lattice proves superior, exhibiting lower sidelobe levels, highly reduced risk from grating
lobes, improved aperture efficiency at high elevation scan angles, and slightly better scanning accu-
racy. Additionally, this lattice exhibits minimal sensitivity to changes in operational frequency with
respect to the directivity pattern. This characteristic is especially vital in addressing the appearance
of grating lobes and low side lobe levels.
The obtained results from conducted investigation of triangular phased array antennas with varied
inter-element spacing are grouped and presented as follows:
• Bandwidth - lattice configuration with the widest inter-element spacing 0.75λ achieved an
S11 greater than -10 dB within part of required operational bandwidth, specifically 2.09 GHz
band.
In contrast, mutual coupling effects were too strong in other array configurations,
resulting in significant power loss that failed to meet bandwidth requirements.
• Scanning accuracy - the array with the widest inter-element spacing 0.75λ demonstrated
the highest scanning accuracy for elevation angles, while the array with the narrowest spacing
0.4λ yielded the poorest results. At the highest elevation scan angle, the greatest differences
between the main lobe direction and the desired scan angle were observed, achieving 10◦, 7◦,

and 5◦for arrays with inter-element spacings of 0.4λ, 0.5λ, and 0.75λ, respectively. However,
it should be noted that for all arrays the difference between desired elevation scan angle theta
and main lobe direction were acceptable for elevation angles up to 50◦. For azimuth angle
results, the scanning accuracy is excellent. From performed investigation it should be also
noted that scanning in azimuth angles is characterised by very small scan accuracy variations
for elevation angles and no variation for azimuth angles. However, it should be noted that
70◦elevation scan requirement was not fulfilled for any of the arrays with chosen uniform
illumination and phase shifts definitions.
• Directivity - the array with the widest inter-element spacing 0.75λ attained the highest
directivity values, up to 25.9 dBi, particularly for scanning in elevation angles below 50◦.
However, due to the steep directivity pattern of this array element, directivity deterioration
becomes more pronounced when scanning to higher elevation angles, resulting in lower direc-
tivity compared to other arrays - 9.7 dBi directivity drop in highest, examined elevation angle.
For array with inter-element spacing of 0.4λ and 0.5λ the highest directivity values were 20.7
dBi and 22.7 dBi respectively. It’s worth noting that as the inter-element spacing decreases,
the deterioration in directivity during scanning to high elevation angles also decreases. As a
consequence, for angles above 50◦, the array with the widest inter-element spacing no longer
exhibited the highest directivity. During scanning across various azimuth angles, the varia-

maintaining directivity consistency when scanning across different elevation angles.
• Scan loss - the array with an inter-element spacing of 0.5λ showcased superior scan loss

spacing displayed the weakest performance, lacking any resemblance to (cos θ)N behavior.
This discrepancy arises from the pronounced deterioration in realized gain experienced by the
latter array when scanning to higher elevation angles. However, it’s important to mention
that the evaluation of scan loss performance was conducted for a specific plane of interest.
• Beamwidth - the array with the widest inter-element spacing 0.75λ attained the narrowest

6
Discussion
This chapter entails a brief discussion of the obtained results along with suggestions for future
endeavors.
6.1
Results
6.1.1
Single antenna element design
The successful application of the stacked patch broadbanding technique has been exemplified
through the development of two stacked patch antennas employing coaxial probe feeding and meet-
ing the requisite operational bandwidth. The primary disparity between the two designs lies in their
respective achieved bandwidth and input reflection coefficient performance.
In the first stacked patch antenna design, a broader bandwidth of 3.61 GHz, equivalent to a 19.05%
fractional bandwidth, has been attained. Conversely, the second design has achieved a narrower
bandwidth of 2.98 GHz, accounting for a 15.72% fractional bandwidth. Despite the narrower band-
width of the second design, this antenna element is distinguished by an input reflection coefficient
superior to -15 dB across nearly the entire band of interest—precisely from 17.88 GHz to 20.2 GHz.
Within the frequency range from 18.2 GHz to 20.2 GHz, the S11 is better than -20 dB. In compar-
ison, the first stacked patch antenna design demonstrates an average input reflection coefficient of
-13 dB within the frequency range of 17.8 GHz to 19.45 GHz, and maintains a reflection coefficient
above -15 dB for frequencies spanning from 19.45 GHz to 20.1 GHz.
The comparative analysis of simulation results for equilateral triangular array antennas, using either
initial stacked patch antennas or optimized stacked patch antennas as single elements, indicates that
enhancing the input reflection coefficient of individual antenna designs has a minor to moderate
impact on the overall performance of the array. While the S11 improvement of a single antenna
element reached as high as 10 dB to 12 dB for certain frequencies, the enhancement in array S11,
comprised of optimized stacked patch antennas, peaked at a maximum of only 1.8 dB. This em-
phasizes the necessity to focus on parameter enhancements through mitigating the mutual coupling
phenomenon instead of investing efforts solely in designing exceptional single antenna elements.
However, it’s important to highlight that the performance in terms of scan loss was improved, espe-
cially at high scan elevation angles, without encountering any trade-offs. As a result, single antenna
elements should prioritize achieving a higher input reflection coefficient within the band of interest
rather than solely focusing on achieving a wide operational bandwidth.
6.1.2
Planar phased array antenna design
Through the examination of array performance across diverse lattice configurations, inter-element
spacings, and various single antenna elements, valuable insights emerged from comparative analy-
sis. While the detailed considerations and summary are delineated in the preceding section of this
report, it is important to underscore that the obtained results and conducted comparisons offer an
understanding of how the array responds to specific alterations and the magnitude of this response.
These findings are related to numerous important antenna parameters such as bandwidth, direc-
tivity, beamwidth, side lobe level, maximum aperture efficiency, scanning accuracy, realized gain or
grating lobes.
85
==PAGE:91==
While the complete final design of the planar phased array antenna was not achieved in this project,
it is worth noting that investigative approach and analysis laid solid foundations for future project
development. Additionally, valuable insights and guidance were obtained, which might be useful
for informing future project advancement.
6.1.3
Numerical and simulation approach
Two Python scripts were developed to apply a numerical approach, integrating antenna and circuit
theory, for the calculation of antenna parameters and their validation against simulation results.
One script computed the directivity pattern using pattern multiplication, while the other assessed
power loss in the uniform linear array by leveraging scattering matrix simulation outcomes and
incident wave definitions. Through a comprehensive comparison between numerical and simulation
results across various scan angles, it was observed that they exhibited significant similarity. This
underscores the efficacy of the numerical approach in approximating directivity patterns and power
loss in the array. Consequently, employing numerical methodologies can yield reliable estimations,
thereby optimizing time and resources by obviating the necessity for multiple simulations.
6.2
Future work
Drawing from the conducted work and analysis of results, several recommendations emerge for fu-
ture endeavors.
Initially, it is advisable to fabricate the single antenna element and conduct measurements in a
radio anechoic chamber to validate its performance on a physical device. This step will provide in-
sights into the accuracy of the simulation model and highlight any potential requirements for tuning.
It is advised to continue exploring parametrically with a triangular lattice antenna featuring 0.5λ
inter-element spacing, using the initial stacked patch antenna as a single element. Initially, focus-
ing on implementing amplitude tapering is recommended. This approach could potentially reduce
side lobes, thereby enhancing antenna efficiency, although it’s crucial to balance this with potential
beamwidth broadening. Additionally, amplitude tapering might offer control over nulls placement,
useful for mitigating certain grating lobes or side lobes. Subsequent investigation can involve the fur-
ther increasing number of elements. This expansion can enhance directivity, narrow the beamwidth,
reduce the first side lobe level, and facilitate scanning to higher angles. Exploring subarray struc-
tures is another option. Grouping elements into subarrays with larger spacing between them could
improve scan coverage while suppressing grating lobes, however this may complicate the feeding
mechanism. These recommendations can be applied across arrays with different inter-element spac-
ings to assess the scale of their impact. By conducting such investigations and gaining a thorough
understanding, one can define the optimal lattice and array spacing. Further work may address
additional mutual coupling negative effects reduction methods, whether through modifications to
individual antenna element or by implementing changes at the array level.
The final recommendation involves further development of a Python-based code used for numerical
calculations of additional antenna parameters. It is particularly crucial to incorporate functionality
for calculating power loss, utilizing scattering matrix, for planar phased arrays or visualizing either
86

the 3D radiation pattern or, preferably, the 2D uv plane. The latter would facilitate the identifica-
tion of high side lobes and grating lobes in the pattern, enhancing the analysis process while both
options would assist in reducing time spent on performing multiple simulations.

7
Conclusions
Inspired by the notable upswing in interest and significant movement towards Low-Earth-Orbit
operation, advanced development of the LEO satellite constellation systems and the need for de-
velopment of novel antenna systems being capable of effectively operating with LEO satellites, the
development and parametric investigation of broadband, planar, phased array antenna has been
conducted.
Starting with the design of single antenna element, the application of the stacked patch antenna
broadbanding technique has enabled the successful achievement of broadband operation of isolated
element, satisfying the prescribed criteria for bandwidth and operating frequency. Following that,
the design of planar phased array antenna has been conducted utilizing the designed single antenna
elements. A parametric investigation and analysis of phased array antenna performance have been
performed by varying features of the array, including lattice structure, inter-element spacing, and
individual antenna element properties. Designed antennas performance have been verified using a
full-wave 3D simulation software - the CST Studio Suite from Dassault Systems - as well as with
numerical approach based on antenna theory. The demonstrated high consistency between simu-
lation and numerical results for investigated planar phased array antennas indicates that utilizing
numerical approaches can replace the resource-intensive process of simulation for extensive antenna
design. At the culmination, the analysis of results has yielded recommendations for future work,
laying a foundation for ongoing advancement.
Although the full planar phased array antenna meeting specified requirements and including feeding
system was not achieved, the conclusions drawn provide valuable insights and serve as useful guid-
ance for planr phased array antenna development. It is important to highlight that the development
of a comprehensive final design, meeting all requirements and utilizing additional techniques, would
require the full-time dedication of a team of engineers.
