Abstract  
The early breakthrough and gravity override of CO2 due to its low viscosity and low density, 
respectively, are the primary challenges of displacing the resident fluids of reservoirs with CO2. 
These hurdles can be mitigated by incorporating small quantities of surfactants to produce CO2 in 
water macroemulsions. Surfactants with a hydrophilic CO2-philic balance exceeding unity are 
preferred. However, the range of suitable surfactants for high-temperature CO2 flooding/storage in 
carbonate reservoirs is limited, and most surfactant floodings have been executed in sandstone 
reservoirs due to challenges like low injectivity, high heterogeneity, and surface adsorption. 
CO2/water foams are employed to enhance sweep efficiency and control CO2 mobility in high-
temperature, high-salinity conditions. The surfactants stabilizing these foams must exhibit high 
solubility in the CO2 phase, a requirement often unmet by anionic surfactants. Conversely, while 
nonionic surfactants offer high solubility in the CO2 phase through tail design, they suffer from 
low cloud points in high-temperature, high-salinity reservoirs. Switchable surfactants hold promise 
in tackling these challenges, especially in carbonate reservoirs at elevated temperatures, as they 
can transition between nonionic and cationic states. Thus, CO2-switchable surfactants possess 
selective surface activity, which can be activated or deactivated by introducing or removing CO2 
from their solution environment. Their surface activity is triggered by CO2, and when CO2 is 
removed (e.g., by adding Nitrogen), they can be easily eliminated from the solution. Consequently, 
they not only enhance the mobility control of injected CO2 but also offer a straightforward reversal 
of their surface activity in the face of operational challenges. 
The properties of switchable surfactants can be fine-tuned through the design of their head and tail 
groups. The structure of the head group influences basicity, the rate and extent of switchability, 
solubility, and the heat of protonation. This study aims to assess the foamability, switchability, and 
interfacial properties of Cetyltrimethylammonium Bromide (CTAB) and Benzalkonium Chloride 
(BZK) demonstrating their capability to generate viscous CO2/brine foams under high-
temperature, high-pressure (HPHT) conditions, amidst high salinity and the presence of divalent 
ions. To achieve this, an interfacial tension (IFT) measurement instrument (pendant drop method) 
by Kurss and FoamScan™ by Teclis Scientific are used. IFT experiments are carried out under 
conditions mimicking those of a depleted oil reservoir in the Danish sector of the North Sea, with 
a pressure of 100 bar and a temperature of 70 °C. Additionally, foamability, foam stability, bubble 
size distribution, and interfacial interaction experiments are conducted to characterize the foam, 
its stability, and switchability.

similar surface activity even at elevated temperatures and divalent ions levels, effectively reducing

1. Introduction  
1.1 Background of the Study 
Colloidal particles that exhibit changes in their properties or behaviors in response to external 
stimuli are called responsive colloids (RS). RS systems transformation can be reversible or 
irreversible based on the design and effect of their stimuli triggers. This transformation can include 
alterations in size, shape, or other properties of the colloidal particles. Responsive colloids are 
often composed of polymeric hydrogels that can react to various stimuli such as temperature, pH, 
or pressure. The responsiveness of these colloids allows them to be utilized in a wide range of 
applications, including actuator design, soft sensors, drug delivery systems, and catalysis [1]. 
Several studies have addressed different types of colloidal triggers such as temperature, p-H, light, 
electric-field, and chemical reaction. Temperature-responsive colloids are typically composed of 
polymers or microgels that undergo reversible changes in their properties in response to variations 
in temperature. pH-responsive colloids exhibit changes in their properties, such as swelling, 
aggregation, or surface charge, in response to variations in pH levels. Light-responsive colloids 
undergo changes in their structure, shape, or aggregation state upon exposure to light of specific 
wavelengths. Electric field-responsive colloids can be composed of materials such as liquid 
crystals, metallic nanoparticles, or dielectric particles. Chemical-responsive colloids react to 
specific chemical stimuli by undergoing structural changes or chemical transformations [2] [3] [4] 
[5] [5] [6] [7]. 
Surfactants are molecules that can stabilize emulsions during different processes such as cleaning, 
manufacturing, and oil recovery. They are designed to lower the interfacial tension between two 
phases, such as oil and water, and play significant role in applications where emulsions need to be 
stabilized temporarily. Switchable surfactants are a class of amphiphilic molecules that can 
undergo reversible interconversions between active and inactive forms. These surfactants can be 
switched on or off, allowing for the controlled stabilization or destabilization of emulsions at 
specific desired stages of a process. Switchable surfactants offer advantages such as delayed 
activity until needed, reusability, and facilitated removal from the product stream by switching to 
the form least soluble in the relevant medium. The transformation of switchable surfactants is 
triggered by benign gases like carbon dioxide and air [8]. CO2 is an advantageous trigger that is 
inexpensive, nonhazardous, non-accumulating in the system, easily removed, and it does not 
require the material to be transparent [9]. Typically, CO2 switchable surfactants contain functional

groups that can be reversibly protonated or deprotonated in the presence of CO2, leading to 
alterations in their molecular structure and surface affinity. When CO2 is present, these surfactants 
undergo a chemical transformation, causing a shift in their hydrophilic-hydrophobic balance. In 
their activated state, CO2-triggered switchable surfactants exhibit enhanced hydrophobicity, 
reducing their solubility in aqueous environments and diminishing their surface activity. 
Conversely, in the absence of CO2, they revert to their original hydrophilic state, thereby restoring 
their surface activity and solubility. This reversible switching mechanism enables precise control 
over the interfacial properties of the system, making CO2-triggered switchable surfactants valuable 
for applications such as CO2 capture and utilization, emulsion stabilization, and controlled release 
systems, where dynamic modulation of surfactant behavior is desired [10]. 
CO2 switchable surfactants play a critical role in underground CO2 storage by facilitating the 
capture, transportation, and injection of CO2 into geological formations for long-term 
sequestration. These surfactants are utilized in the initial step of CO2 capture from industrial 
sources, where they aid in the separation of CO2 from flue gas streams by forming CO2-rich 
micelles or emulsions in aqueous solutions. Once captured, CO2 is compressed and transported to 
storage sites via pipelines, where CO2 switchable surfactants serve as key components in enhanced 
oil recovery (EOR) techniques [11]. Hence, foam flooding is an EOR technique used in the 
petroleum industry to increase the production of oil from reservoirs by improving the displacement 
efficiency of injected fluids. In foam flooding, a stable foam consisting of gas bubbles dispersed 
in a liquid phase is generated and injected into an oil reservoir through injection wells [12]. The 
foam acts as a mobility control agent, reducing the mobility of injected fluids, such as water or 
CO2, and diverting them into unswept regions of the reservoir. This results in improved sweep 
efficiency and a more uniform displacement of oil towards production wells. The foam also helps 
to reduce the mobility of oil by increasing its apparent viscosity, enhancing its ability to flow 
through porous reservoir rocks. Foam flooding has been widely utilized in mature oil fields to 
recover additional oil reserves that are otherwise difficult to access using conventional production 
methods. When surfactants are injected into underground reservoirs along with CO2 foam, they 
help to reduce the interfacial tension between CO2 and oil, thus improving the mobility and 
displacement efficiency of CO2 in the reservoir, leading to increased oil recovery. Besides, CO2 
switchable surfactants can aid in the prevention of CO2 leakage by forming stable foams or gels in 
the caprock layer, effectively sealing off potential pathways for CO2 migration. Overall, the use of 
CO2 switchable surfactants enhances the efficiency and safety of underground CO2 storage

operations, contributing to the mitigation of greenhouse gas emissions and the transition towards 
a more sustainable energy future [13]. 
1.2 Problem Description  
The challenges associated with early breakthrough and gravity override of CO2 in displacing 
resident fluids within reservoirs are multifaceted and intricate. Early breakthrough of CO2 occurs 
when injected CO2 reaches production wells sooner than anticipated, bypassing significant 
portions of the reservoir. This phenomenon is primarily attributed to the preferential flow of CO2 
through high-permeability pathways, such as fractures or highly permeable zones within the 
reservoir rock especially in carbonate reservoirs. These pathways act as streams, allowing CO2 to 
rapidly migrate towards production wells without effectively displacing resident fluids from the 
low-permeability matrix [14]. As a result, the sweep efficiency of the CO2 flood is compromised, 
leading to reduced recovery of resident reservoir fluids, and leaving behind significant unrecovered 
oil. In fact, gravity override exacerbates the challenges associated with CO2 displacement by 
promoting the downward migration of floating CO2 within the reservoir. This gravitational 
segregation occurs due to the density difference between CO2 and the resident fluids such as oil 
and water. In reservoirs with heterogeneous permeability distributions or inclined geological 
formations, gravity override causes CO2 to preferentially accumulate in the lower regions of the 
reservoir, leaving upper regions insufficiently contacted by the injected fluid. Consequently, these 
upper regions may experience limited displacement and remain saturated with resident fluids, 
impeding effective oil recovery [15]. 
To mitigate CO2 migration and enhance its dissolution within water, the strategic incorporation of 
surfactants to generate CO2-in-water macroemulsions stands as a superior solution. By 
encapsulating CO2 within water droplets dispersed in a continuous aqueous phase, surfactants 
serve to stabilize the resulting macroemulsions, effectively trapping CO2 and impeding its rapid 
escape or migration within the reservoir. The pivotal criterion for surfactant selection in this case 
lies in achieving a hydrophilic/ CO2-philic balance surpassing unity, as surfactants possessing this 
attribute exhibit a pronounced predisposition towards CO2 binding, facilitating the formation of 
robust CO2-in-water macroemulsions. Such surfactants, provided with a superior affinity for CO2 
over water, generate a conducive environment to the encapsulation of CO2 molecules within 
aqueous droplets, thus sustaining macroemulsion stability and resilience. Consequently, the 
sustained presence of these macroemulsions throughout the reservoir environment serves as a

protection against CO2 migration and premature breakthrough, causing a shift towards enhanced 
sweep efficiency and elevated oil recovery yields. In essence, by navigating the intricate interplay 
between surfactant chemistry and CO2 encapsulation dynamics, reservoir engineering activities 
arise to new levels of sophistication [16] [17]. 
Limited surfactants can withstand high-temperature and high-salinity conditions for CO2 
applications within carbonate reservoirs. Surfactant molecules resilient to high temperatures and 
salinities are engineered with robust chemical structures, characterized by stable functional groups 
and molecular architectures capable of maintaining integrity under harsh conditions. While foam 
stabilizing surfactants should have high solubility in the CO2 phase, anionic surfactants are 
generally not favored because they are negatively charged hydrophilic head groups so they 
inherent limitations in their interfacial behavior and stability. They have low stability and high 
susceptibility to coalescence and foam collapse because of electrostatic repulsion between like-
charged head groups [18] [10] [19]. Anionic surfactants are prone to interactions with divalent ions 
commonly present in formation water, leading to precipitation that can destabilize foam structures. 
Also, they may be absorbed at reservoir surfaces which limits their availability at the fluid interface 
for foam stabilization, diminishing their effectiveness. Compatibility issues with components of 
reservoir fluids, such as crude oil or brine, further undermine their ability to stabilize foam, often 
resulting in phase separation or emulsion destabilization [20]. On the other hand, nonionic 
surfactants, despite their general stability and versatility, may encounter difficulties in stabilizing 
CO2 foam under conditions of low cloud points in high-temperature, high-salinity reservoirs. One 
obvious challenge is the potential for thermal degradation, as nonionic surfactants may lose 
efficacy or undergo degradation at elevated temperatures characteristic of such reservoirs, leading 
to unstable foam [21]. Nonionic surfactant may also suffer from phase separation at low cloud 
points which exhibit reduced solubility in aqueous solutions and reduce their availability at the 
foam interface. High salinity further aggravates these challenges by altering surfactant solubility 
and micellar properties and potentially leading to surfactant precipitation. Further, some nonionic 
surfactants may exhibit limited compatibility with CO2, resulting in weak interfacial activity and 
reduced foam stability [22].

1.3 Aim of the Study  
This research aims to evaluate selected CO2-switchable surfactants in high salinity and high 
temperature carbonate reservoirs. The study will evaluate foam generation and stability as well as 
the interfacial tension under different levels for temperatures and salinity. Salinity evaluation will 
be on three main salts: namely, Na2SO4, CaCl2.2H2O, and MgCl2.6H2O. CO2-switchable 
surfactants offer several distinct advantages over traditional surfactants, stemming from their 
unique ability to reversibly alter their properties in response to changes in carbon dioxide (CO2) 
concentration. One of the primary advantages lies in their dynamic responsiveness, wherein they 
can switch between hydrophilic and hydrophobic states upon exposure to CO2, facilitating precise 
control over interfacial behavior and solubility properties. This responsiveness enables CO2-
switchable surfactants to adapt to high temperature and salinity environment. Besides, the 
reversible nature of CO2-triggered switching ensures sustainability and reusability, as surfactant 
molecules can undergo multiple cycles of activation and deactivation without loss of efficacy. CO2-
switchable surfactants also often exhibit enhanced selectivity and specificity towards CO2, 
minimizing interference interactions, and improving overall efficiency.

2. Literature Review  
2.1 Surfactants and CO2 Switchable Surfactants  
Amphiphilic compounds have lipophilic, typically hydrocarbon, structures and hydrophilic polar 
functional groups that can be either ionic or uncharged. These compounds with a hydrophilic part 
and hydrophobic part are known as surface active substances or surfactants. Surfactants' tendency 
to concentrate at surfaces and aggregate in solutions into different supramolecular structures, like 
micelles and bilayers, is caused by their amphiphilic structure. According to the type of the polar 
group, surfactants can be categorized into nonionic and ionic, which may be of anionic, cationic, 
and amphoteric or zwitterionic nature. First, nonionic surfactants are amphiphilic substances with 
a hydrophilic, lyophilic portion that does not split into ions and is hence chargeless. The main 
active range of hydrogen ion concentration is uncharged for non-ionic surfactants (pH). Esters of 
fatty acids, or sorbitan acylates, are a common type of moderate and low-toxic emulsifier used in 
food, pharmaceutical, personal care, and industrial applications. Second, anionic surfactants are 
amphiphilic compounds that have an anionic group bonded to a lengthy hydrocarbon chain either 
directly or via intermediaries as a necessary component. Third, a molecule is said to be cationic if 
it can split into a typical anion and a surface-active cation in solution. The four primary kinds of 
cationic surfactants are quaternaries, alkyl imidazolines, ethoxylated amines, and alkyl amines. 
Approximately 90% of the entire cationic synthesis is made up of quaternary compounds. A few 
cationic surfactants are used as starting points to produce additional surfactants. Known as 
"quaternaries" or "quats," quaternary ammonium compounds are thought to be the most varied 
class of cationic surfactants. They have four alkyl or aryl substituents covalently bound to at least 
one nitrogen atom. Quaternaries can be classified structurally into the following categories: 
heterocyclic compounds, bisquaternary ammonium compounds, or polyquaterniums in a broad 
sense; two types of tetra alkyl derivatives (containing one long-chain alkyl and two ones); 
compounds with mixed short-chain N-substituents (methyl, ethyl, hydroxyethyl, benzyl, halide-
substituted benzyl, and so forth). Lastly, if the functional group of an amphiphilic molecule can 
transport both cationic and anionic charges, it is referred to as an amphoteric surfactant. 
Compounds exhibiting pH-dependent amphoteric characteristics are often referred to as 
"amphoterics" or "amphoteric surfactants." A quaternary ammonium group and a sulphonic group 
are examples of amphiphiles that contain both very basic and strongly acidic groups. These 
amphiphiles, which were identified as zwitter-ionic surfactants, exhibit both anionic and cationic 
characteristics regardless of pH. Amphoteric and zwitterionic surfactants provide a lot of potential

for synthesis, even if their share of the global surfactant industry is less than 2%. To put it simply, 
this class of surfactants is known as "amphoterics." Most of the beneficial amphoterics fall into

Surface-active and surface-inactive states can be switched on and off for CO2-switchable 
surfactants, and their surface activity can be changed by varying the amount of CO2 present. This 
switching ability originates from CO2 solubility in water as it produces free hydrogen ions. 
Hydrogen ions then protonate the nitrogen atoms in acetamidine group which activates the surface 
of the long-chain alkyl acetamidinium bicarbonates. Nitrogen is introduced to turn off the surface 
activity by removing CO2 from the solution. CO2 switchability of surfactant can be evaluated by 
electrical conductivity as the electrical conductivity of the liquid phase increases as surfactants are 
protonated. The switchability can be confirmed by cycling the bubbling of CO2 and N2 as the 
surfactants switch surface activity on and off respectively. Moreover, CO2 switchable surfactant 
consists of two parts: head group and tail group. The head group, hydrophilic, determines rate and 
extent of switchability, solubility, and protonation heat. SCS or switchable cationic surfactants are 
the most utilized and have Nitrogen atom in the head group that is protonated to produce the 
switchability property. Tail group, hydrophobic, controls solubility, partitioning, and ecotoxicity. 
Critical micelle concentration decreases with long chain tails and increases their water partition 
coefficient [10]. 
2.2 CO2 Switchable Foam and CO2 Flooding 
Aqueous foams made of carbon dioxide (CO2) are colloidal dispersions made of CO2 bubbles 
scattered across an ongoing aqueous phase [24]. The gaseous phase is dispersed within the interior 
phase, while the liquid phase constitutes the external phase. Illustrated in Figure 1 is the 
progression of foam formation, with the thin fluid sheet denoted as a lamella. A lamella can be 
segmented into internal gas bubbles within the foam. The "Plateau boundary" designates the region

gas bubbles ascending to the surface due to density discrepancies between the medium and the 
bubbles. Pure liquid cannot produce froth; however, with the introduction of a surface-active 
substance, foaming occurs. Upon injection beneath the liquid's surface, a gas bubble invariably 
ruptures instantaneously. The utilization of a diluted surfactant solution engenders a restoring 
force, facilitating equilibrium restoration. As the air/liquid interface expands, alterations in surface

2.3 Carbon Capture and Storage  
Carbon capture and storage is a complete value chain of CCUS that comprises technologies used 
to capture, convert or utilize and store CO2 for the purposes of reducing and removing CO2 from 
the atmosphere. According to findings and research from the International Energy Agency (IEA) 
and the Inter-Inter-governmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), it would be necessary to reduce 
green-house emissions and limit the global warming increasing temperature to below 2°C and 
transition to net-zero emission target by 2050 [32]. 
Various net-zero emission targets have been set by the European Union to achieve an interim target 
of 55% by 20230 and a net-zero target by 2050. CCUS is seen as a necessary step to combat climate 
change and emissions from hard-to-bate sectors such as cement and steel manufacturing and to 
achieve the net-zero target set by the Paris Agreement. However, other various carbon abatement 
technologies such as renewable energy, carbon neutral fuels and improvement in energy efficiency 
are needed to supplement CCUS to achieve this target. Carbon capture and storage is recognized 
as an effective mitigation mechanism where carbon dioxide is captured either from point sources 
or through direct air capture and transported through pipelines to be stored in geological formations

2.4 CO2 Storage in Carbonate Reservoirs  
An impermeable layer usually in the form of shale or chalk of the caprock serves as a seal to 
prevent the penetration and migration of hydrocarbons and the risk of leakage of CO2. In 
heterogenous reservoirs, the storage capacity may be reduced as a result of the bypassing of some 
pores and carbon dioxide (CO2) is most likely to pass through only the permeable layers. 
Proposals by the Intergovernmental Panel on the mitigation pathways on Climate Change to limit 
the temperature to the desired target and CCS was recommended to play an instrumental role in  
combating climate change and achieve carbon neutrality by 2050 [34]. 
Among the storage options currently being implemented across the world, geological storage such 
as storage in depleted oil and gas reservoirs is considered the most mature method because of 
existing knowledge about reservoirs. A typical example is the project Greensand which has 
received funding from the Danish government to capture and sequester CO2 in depleted Danish

oilfields.  However, this storage option is small compared to deep saline aquifers but has faced

Adequate knowledge of geological formations is necessary to choose the most suitable geological 
sites for CO2 storage where it can be contained by the following trapping mechanisms.  
I. 
Structural trapping – The efficiency of the seal cap rock is controlled by a mechanism 
known as hydrodynamic or structural trapping which prevents the migration of the injected 
CO2 plume either vertically or laterally. It is usually referred to as a primary trapping 
mechanism. 
II. 
Solution trapping is a secondary dissolution trapping mechanism where CO2 dissolves in

2.7 The Importance of Interfacial Tension (IFT) Reduction for Foam Generation 
Foaming agents, like surfactants, help to reduce interfacial tension (IFT) between gaseous and 
liquid phases. When IFT is lowered, liquids mixtures have a higher probability to generate more 
stable bubbles. For example, in ambient conditions, the IFT of water is as high as 72 mN/m, 
though, conventional surfactant reduces it to less than halve which generates successful foam [55]. 
The challenge becomes more difficult with generating and stabilizing foam using CO2 as large 
amounts of it dissolve quicker in oil than water. This is because CO2 molecules can spread out over 
a larger area within the oil phase which influences the distribution and movement of CO2 gas 
within the foam structure. The challenge causes Ostwald Ripening effect which is a phenomenon 
when smaller bubbles shrink and disappear while larger bubbles keep expanding, Figure 8 gives a 
representation. This process negatively affects foam stability and ultimately causes the foam to 
collapse [56].

equilibrium and balanced between surface forces and gravitational forces. The method can be used

3.1.3 Foam Generation and Stability  
Foam generation and stability has been of interest to the scientific community, specifically within 
the physics community. The community emphasizes the behavior and characteristics of foam 
transition from liquid foam to dry foam. The focus is put on mechanical behavior of foam under 
different conditions, how substance passes through thin liquid phase of foam, deformation, 
elasticity, and arrangement of foam structure. However, these efforts are focused on ordered foam 
whereas foam is more disordered and has different shapes and sizes. Physical chemists on the other 
hand, are focused on the role of interfacial activities on stabilization and surfactants in liquid 
foams. Surfactants foams are mainly stable by huge amounts absorbed at the interface which 
reduce interfacial tension. Yet, factors like adoption and surfactants type and concentration affect 
foam stability and generation. Greater emphasis is placed on the viscoelasticity properties and 
repulsive interaction among the film lamellae in determining stability [65].  
Foam stability is defined as the drainage ratio of foam to liquid after preset time interval elapses 
[66]. Initially, during foam generation, there is a linear increase in foam volume as gas is 
introduced into the liquid phase. Next, foam undergoes decay and ultimately leads to its collapse. 
The feature of stable foam lies in its tendency to endure beyond its generation phase, maintaining 
its structural integrity over an extended period. Contrastingly, poorer foam exhibits a swift collapse

Micelle structure occurs when amphiphilic molecules are aggregated, see Figure 14. This 
aggregation depends on characteristics of surfactant and dissolving liquid. The concentration of 
surfactant in a solution at which micelles start to form is known as critical micelle concentration

(CMC). CMC is not absolute and can be qualitatively measured through experimental analysis of 
surfactant characteristics. For this reason, many attempts were made to experimentally define 
CMC such as the concentration at which a small and consistent quantity of the surfactant molecules 
has come together to form micelles. Another CMC definition is the concentration of surfactant in 
a solution where the concentration of micelles would drop to zero if their concentration followed 
the same trend as it does at slightly higher concentrations. Others define it at the point where both 
monomer and micelles concentration are equal [23].

Surfactant concentration in a solution exhibits different physical or chemical behavior if it is above 
or below CMC such as conductivity, surface tension, and several others. Tracking the rate of 
change of some of these properties assists in determining CMC for the surfactant of interest. In 
fact, CMC would lay on the breaking point of changing the concentration and response of these 
properties. Hence, several techniques are applied to determine CMC such as electrical conductivity 
and surface tension measurements. First, the theory behind measuring CMC by electrical 
conductivity relies on the behavior of surfactant molecules in solution. Micelles contain charged 
groups that change with the change in the number of aggregates thereby the conductivity of the 
solution change. The conductivity of the solution is relatively low before the CMC because 
surfactant contribution is low. Yet, the conductivity proportionally increases with surfactant 
concentration. The increase in charged micelles increase the conductivity, which enhance the 
movement of ions in the solution. There is a point of sharp increase in conductivity that 
corresponds to CMC, indicating the concentration at which micelles begin to form spontaneously, 
see Figure 15. Unlike nonionic surfactants, ionic surfactants and cationic surfactants contribute to 
changes in solution conductivity due to the presence of charged groups, negative and positive 
charge respectively.

Second, the theory of determining CMC by Interfacial Tension (IFT) is rooted in the observing 
surfactant behavior in the solution. IFT refers to the force per unit length acting perpendicular to 
an interface between two immiscible phases. When surfactants are added to such a system, they 
orient themselves at the interface in a way that the hydrophobic tails are in the non-polar phase, 
such as CO2, while the hydrophilic heads are in the polar phase, such as brine. This orientation 
reduces the interfacial tension between the two phases, making the surfactant molecules effectively 
absorbed at the interface. Surfactant at low concentration has a low influence on lowering the 
interfacial tension. When surfactant approaches CMC, though, interfacial tension drops 
significantly as micelles begin to form and adsorb at the interface, i.e. CMC. A logarithmic scale 
is usually used to determine CMC to overlook small changes in IFT values and increase the

3.2.1 Preparation of Synthetic Brine 
Synthetic brine consists of a solution of different salts and concentrations, synthetically prepared 
to depict the natural brine found in saline aquifers and hydrocarbon reservoirs. 
Salinity is a very important parameter due to its influence on IFT of CO2 brine systems which we 
will later discuss. The salts are made up of monovalent (NaCl) and divalent salts and NaCl with 
the highest concentration. Most previous studies and research focused mainly on the IFT between 
monovalent ions system such as aqueous sodium chloride and CO2 system since most of the ions 
in deep underground reservoirs and saline aquifers are predominantly chloride ions and NaCl salt. 
However, the trend has begun to change with focus being placed on different salt species and the 
IFT increases linearly with increasing salinity or salt concentration [51].

concentrations were obtained from Gorm formation water. The different salt species were 
estimated to mimic the same conditions of deep saline aquifers and reservoirs made of different 
salt species with NaCl as the dominant salt species.

3.2.3 Carbon Dioxide (CO2)  
Carbon dioxide is a major component of green-house gases made up of 43% coal, 27% oil and 
20% natural gas. It is colourless and odourless gas which can also exist as a solid, liquid, and 
supercritical states depending on the temperature and pressure of the surrounding medium.  
Some characteristics such as interfacial properties, compressibility and solubility are responsible 
for determining the phase state of CO2. At temperature below -78°C, CO2 exists as a solid and it 
is a low-pressure gas at temperatures between − 56.5°C and 31.1 ◦C [71]. According to research, 
the IFT OF CO2 -brine system is influenced by reservoir pressure, temperature, purity of the gas 
phase and salinity. Thus, at thermodynamic conditions of temperature and pressure, CO2 exists as 
a supercritical fluid at 31.1°C and 7.38 bar (1070 psi) respectively. At the supercritical state, CO2 
has the diffusivity of a gas and the density of liquid thereby reducing the geological storage space. 
Several extensive laboratory research have shown that, the IFT of the CO2/brine system is affected 
by the reservoir pressure, temperature, brine salinity and purity of the gas phase.

a very useful product including enhanced oil recovery applications (CO2 flooding), fire 
extinguishers, refrigeration and among other uses.

3.3 Design of Experiment  
There are five factors of interest in this experimental work; namely, surfactant type, temperature 
of the solution, salinity, and content of three divalent ions; Calcium, Magnesium, and Sulphate. As 
discussed before about the significance of these factors, let us investigate the experimental design. 
To start, the number of experiments needed to investigate the effect of main factors and all levels 
of interactions combinations goes beyond the available resources. To overcome this issue, many 
procedures are taken to reduce the number of needed experiments. First, surfactant concentration 
is kept at CMC level which is the minimum limit of surfactant surface activity. Second, salinity 
levels are limited by changing levels of the mentioned divalent ions and salinity calculated at each 
of their level combinations. Third, factors are set to be continuous variables between high and low 
preset values to avoid discrete values that dictate more experiments. Fourth, factor screening is 
done through fractional factorial design to test second degree level interaction to evaluate the 
interactions surface and locate potential areas for further screening. According to Douglas in his 
Design of Experiments textbook, it is possible to reduce the number of experiments through 
fractional factorial design where higher order interactions between factors are neglected and the 
design is reduced to lower order interactions [72]. This design is based on creating subgroups of 
experiments, projection of higher order interactions by investigating lower order, and sequential 
experimentation. Finally, three center runs are introduced to set threshold for variations. Adopting 
this design reduced the number of experimentations to 14 randomized runs of different levels 
combinations, see Table 4. This shows three levels temperatures, calculated salinity for each 
experiment, and three levels divalent ions concentrations, note that the percentage [%] means out

3.5 Experimental Setup 
3.5.1 Interfacial Tension Set-up  
There is the possibility of immiscible phases to co-exist in a porous medium and this is influenced 
by different surface energy and gravity which can have an impact on the displacement of different 
phases, fluid saturations as well as distributions in reservoirs.   IFT is a short form for Interfacial 
tension (σ) and is defined as the force that exists between two immiscible fluids or between a liquid 
and a solid (σ). It can be quantified and shows the relationship between the force acting on a surface 
to the distance per unit length (F/L) where the force is acting normal to the surface of the liquid. It 
has units of dynes/cm, J/m2 or mN/m. 
The IFT experimental set-up is designed to work up to a maximum reservoir temperature of 100°C 
and a pressure of 500 bar. This makes it possible to measure interfacial tension for two phase 
systems (immiscible) using the pendant drop method.  The experimental set-up primarily consists 
of the following main components. 
• High-pressure visualization cell - equipped with a needle of diameter 1.588mm. 
• A fluid circuit and feeding system such as pumps and high-pressure cylinders for the fluids and  
• Data acquisition and imaging system with camera makes it possible to monitor and take images 
of drops and the IFT is measured using Advance system software.  
The IFT set-up is used for measuring the interfacial tension between two immiscible fluids using 
the pendant drop method.  An equilibrated mixture of brine and CO2 of different salinities is 
obtained, and the brine was synthesized using the recipe for Gorm formation water described in 
table 2 under section 3 and the CO2 obtained from DTU Offshore lab serves as the bulk phase 
liquid. The gas is injected into the IFT cell to pressurize the cell with supercritical CO2 from the 
high-pressure cylinder containing CO2 using an ISCO pump.  
Three levels of temperature are set at different stages (from ambient, 45°C and 70°C max) and the 
pressure was maintained at max 100 bar throughout the experiment. More information about a 
complete design of the experiment is attached and described in the DOE in section 3.3 above.   
The drop liquid injected generates a bubble at the tip of the needle and the camera is used to capture 
the images of the drop in a few seconds [61] . This method of measuring IFT is called pendant 
drop method and a step by step of the experimental process of generating drops using the pendant 
drop is described below and comprises the following experimental setup and procedure.

3.6 Error Propagation  
Experimental errors can arise from various sources, intricately woven into the fabric of scientific 
inquiry. These errors, though often perceived as unwelcome intruders, serve as critical gateways 
==PAGE:45==
DTU Chemistry  
March 2024 
Master Thesis 
 
45   
 
to understanding and refining experimental methodologies. Understanding the sources of these 
errors is paramount in strengthening the reliability of the findings.  
In this experimental work, there are four predominant potential sources of errors; namely, 
mechanical factors affecting IFT measurements, chemical mixing, temperature control, and 
illumination, light calibration, and pressure control. Each of these factors will be explained next 
as well as the precautionary measures taken to reduce the impact of them on the results.  
First, contamination is one of the most important sources of error. The presence of impurities such 
as salt precipitates adsorbed on the surface of the view cell and inside the high-pressure gas 
cylinders can affect precision and measurement outcomes. The brine and surfactant mixtures 
contain salts of both monovalent and divalent charges with different concentrations. The 
experiments aimed to investigate several different concentrations so any salt residues left on the 
surface or inside the cylinder before a new experiment would change the target concentration.  If 
water droplets and foreign particles are left on the surface due to improper cleaning and drying, 
they can possibly affect measurement results.  Surface and equipment contamination due to the 
presence of both impurities and contaminants during sample handling and preparation was 
minimized. This is done by apply cleaning after each experiment for IFT cell, brine cylinder, 
connection tubes, and lenses. The cleaning procedure starts with water rinsing, Ethanol rinse, and 
air drying.  
Second, chemical mixing presents a huge challenge when it comes to experimental errors. One 
common source of chemical mixing errors stems from the fact that even minor discrepancies in 
the volume or concentration of substances can lead to significant deviations from expected 
outcomes and product properties. Also, human factor plays a significant role in errors regardless 
of training precautionary measures as experimentalists may still introduce contamination and 
impurities. Insufficient knowledge about chemical compatibility and reactivity poses additional 
challenges in chemical mixing. Instruments failures and error margins also considered a challenge. 
In this work, chemicals were measured in a highly sensitive scale, minimizing air weight, and the 
chemical mixes are let to stir on the magnetic stirrer overnight. Another measure is taking multiple 
measurements for the sample to ensure accuracy. All liquid measurements, water for example, are 
done by weight instead of volume. Finally, all calibration dates are checked on the instruments 
before using them to ensure accuracy.  
==PAGE:46==
DTU Chemistry  
March 2024 
Master Thesis 
 
46   
 
Temperature control is one of the factors that posed a great challenge in the IFT measurements.  
One common source of error associated with IFT heating jackets is temperature uniformity. While 
these jackets excel in rapidly heating reaction vessels to target temperatures, variations in heat 
distribution across the vessel's surface can occur. A temperature controller equipped with sensor 
was used to heat-up the heating jacket, increase and regulate the overall temperature. However, 
the challenge faced was maintaining the temperature for a certain period had to be increased to a 
few more values (usually 3-5 more) to get the required preset temperature. Also, despite 
sophisticated temperature regulation mechanisms, fluctuations in power supply, ambient 
temperature, or environmental conditions can perturb the heating process, leading to deviations 
from the desired setpoint. FoamScan™ measurements, on the other hand, at high temperature were 
done by increasing the temperature to the pre-set value using a chiller and this allowed 
thermodynamic equilibrium to be achieved with the surfactant. However, there was a likely case 
of either heat loss to the environment or heat transfer when a sample was transferred for 
measurement.  
In some instances, measurements were taken at 1°C less or more in some experiments but these 
challenging factors were accommodated for by repeating some runs to ensure consistency and 
accuracy.  Moreover, the ambient temperature influenced both measurements taken at ambient 
conditions. Fluctuation in ambient temperature inside the lab meant a constant temperature wasn’t 
achieved for some experiments carried out at ambient temperature of 22°C. Nonetheless, the 
fluctuated ambient temperature fell within ambient temperature and should not have an impact on 
the measurements.  
Since both IFT and FoamScan™ depend on image analysis, illumination serves as a critical factor 
that can significantly influence the accuracy, reliability, and interpretability of results. One of the 
primary roles of illumination in image analysis is to provide sufficient contrast between objects of 
interest and their background. Adequate illumination enhances the visibility of features within 
images, allowing for more precise segmentation, detection, and measurement of bubbles/droplets. 
Insufficient brightness or improper positioning of light sources, can result in underexposed or 
poorly illuminated regions within droplet images, compromising the visibility of relevant features. 
Conversely, excessive illumination can lead to overexposure, saturation, or photobleaching effects, 
altering the appearance of objects and impeding accurate analysis. To overcome any potential 
discrepancies, illumination levels were fixed for both instruments to obtain consistent results and 
data points that are relevant to each other.  
==PAGE:47==

Lastly, pressure was almost maintained and ensured at a constant and maximum value of 99 bar 
throughout the entire experiment to pressurize the cell and establish an equilibrium pressure. Only 
a very small deviation of 0.5 – 1 bar due to reduction or increment was encountered and should 
have a negligible or no impact on our results. Depending on the stability of the drop, a minimum 
of four runs are made for each experiment and the average IFT obtained from the average of all 
the runs carried out. The equilibrium of the system is very important to obtain IFT measurements 
and once the drop is generated and enlarged, it is allowed to stabilize or maintained in the viewing

or their interactions. In this report, the goal is to look for strong statistical evidence of the effect of

4.4 Discussion  
This section presents a comprehensive analysis of the findings obtained from conducting IFT and 
foam scan experiments on both surfactants: CTAB and BZK. IFT’s pendant drop method provided 
interfacial properties of these surfactants while Foam scan provided characteristics of foam 
generation and stability. The fundamental purpose of these experiments is to have a deep 
understanding of these CO2 switchable surfactants properties and to clarify implications of the 
observed responses.  
Benzalkonium Chloride shows consistent performance under all different combinations of factors. 
BZK seems resilient when it comes to surface activities as it kept activated under high temperatures 
and salinity combinations. The IFT values remained in the range of 2.5 to 5.5 [mN/m] with an 
average of 3.47 [mN/m] for all tests. The projected error associated with these experiments is based 
on the 3 center runs which have an average of 2.8 [mN/m] and standard deviation of ± 0.29 
[mN/m]. It is reasonable to accept the range of errors and conclude by the superiority of BZK over 
CTAB. Cetyltrimethylammonium Bromide (CTAB), however, showed less consistent interfacial 
activities with considerable discrepancies. IFT values exhibited a broad distribution spanning from 
5.6 to 18.3 [mN/m] which indicates that CTAB is susceptible to the influence of experimental 
factors and their combinations. The average of the center runs is 6.8 [mN/m] with standard 
deviation of ± 0.24 [mN/m] which is closer to BZK’s. This consistency enhances the reliability of 
the comparative analysis, allowing for more robust conclusions to be drawn regarding their 
performance and efficacy. 
Furthermore, the relationship between interfacial activities and foaming, as elucidated in section 
2.7, is significant. Decreasing IFT through the addition of surfactant enhances foaming, as 
highlighted in previous literature. The results affirm this relationship, particularly evident in the 
behavior of BZK foams. The mean bubble radius variation of ±0.003, which accounts for 6.5% of 
the average radius across all experiments, underscores a clear correlation between bubble radius

This part of the discussion section focuses on the effect of temperature and salinities of the different 
salt species and divalent cations Ca2+, Mg2+ in MgCl2.6H2O and CaCl2.2H2O respectively as well 
the anion from Na2SO4 and their effect on IFT and the mean bubble radius. The significance of 
these factors is analyzed and discussed starting with benzalkonium chloride (BZK) and later 
cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB). 
The effect of temperature on the IFT of BZK is inconsistent and fluctuated in the data that was 
obtained for the various experiments. The data obtained from the statistical modelling JMP™ 
software shows the influence of the main factors as can be seen in the summary table in figure 36 
above and temperature was ranked the highest alongside the salinity of MgCl2.6H2O and Na2SO4   
with a probability of 0.03 (30%) which is less than the set significance level of 0.05.  Moreover, 
the interaction profiler for temperature in figure 36 from JMP™ model shows a general decreasing 
trend and fluctuation of temperature with IFT for benzalkonium chloride. For instance, at ambient 
temperature of 22°C, high IFT values were obtained and the IFT tends to decrease when it was at 
a medium level (45°) and decreased further when it was raised to 70°C showing a better IFT 
reduction performance at high temperature.  
A few research showed that the IFT of CO2-brine system fluctuated with temperature under 
pressures below 15 MPa and gradually decreased with temperature when the pressure exceeded 
30 MPa [75], whereas in this research, pressure was kept constant. Many other studies have 
revealed a positive impact of high temperature on IFT due to a decreased solubility of CO2 in brine 
at low temperature which contradicts the IFT results obtained for Benzalkonium chloride. Further 
findings from literature shows that temperature has no effect or has a negative effect on IFT [76] 
and this shows the complexity of temperature and a combination of factors such as salinity of 
cations, sulfate ions,  surfactants  and CO2 phase which makes it difficult to predict their effects.  
Secondly, the influence of salt types such as MgCl2, CaCl2 and Na2SO4 for BZK can be seen in the 
summary and interaction profiles in figure 36 above where MgCl2 had the same effect as Na2SO4 
on the IFT of BZK with a probability value of 0.03 each. The effect is significant by the significant 
target level that we set for this model even though it isn’t too pronounced.  
Under ambient conditions, Mg2+ is expected to have a bigger effect than CaCl2 due to its small 
atomic size and radius. Cations are considered structure making ions and depending on their size 
can be strongly hydrated and repelled due to their ability to enhance hydrogen bond in water 
molecules. The valency of cations in aqueous mixtures have a considerable impact on IFT and the

a certain salinity due to higher hydration and bigger repulsion from the interface.  However, from 
our experiment backed by the model data for BZK, high IFT values were obtained at low level of 
MgCl2 and as the salinity of Mg2+ increases to a high level, the IFT decreases and the other divalent

Sodium sulfate (Na2SO4) as an anion exhibited the opposite effect where high IFT values were

In ascending order →Cs+<Rb+<NH4+<K+<Na+<Li+<Ca2+<Mg2+ [77].

the influence of temperature on IFT shows the same trend for the mean bubble radius as in the IFT.

significant effect was observed for any of these ions including Na2SO4 with respective p-values of

interesting but unexpected as a similar trend was expected as in the previous surfactant, only to be 
affected by temperature based on this data.

5. Conclusion  
In conclusion, this experimental work was undertaken with the objective of evaluating the 
performance of two cationic CO2/ N2 switchable surfactants. The experimental design aimed to 
evaluate the effects of temperature variations and the presence of three divalent ions: calcium 
(Ca2+), magnesium (Mg2+), and sulfate (SO42-). Employing two instrumental techniques, namely 
the interfacial tension measurement using the pendant drop method and foam scanning, the study 
focused on assessing two primary properties: interfacial tension and foam generation and stability. 
Below, we briefly outline the key findings collected from this investigation. 
• Benzalkonium Chloride, BZK, exhibits remarkable consistency across various 
experimental conditions. This surfactant demonstrates exceptional resilience, retaining its 
surface activity even under elevated temperatures and high salinity levels, effectively 
reducing surface tension. Though, a limitation arises in its inability to sustain foam 
structure once CO2 injection ceases. Nonetheless, in experiments involving nitrogen gas 
foam generation, BZK has showcased its impressive CO2/ N2 switchability property, 
further highlighting its versatility and potential in diverse applications. 
• Cetyltrimethylammonium Bromide, abbreviated as CTAB, exhibits limitations in 
maintaining foam structure at elevated temperatures once CO2 generation terminates. 
Despite this drawback, CTAB demonstrates effectiveness in lowering interfacial tension 
(IFT) under high temperature conditions, as it is activated by heat. However, despite its 
capability in reducing IFT, CTAB falls short compared to BZK, as the latter consistently 
achieves significantly lower IFT values. On a positive note, CTAB's ability to sustain foam 
structure post-generation cessation stands out as one of its advantageous features. 
• A notable correlation exists between IFT and foaming behavior, wherein lower IFT values 
correspond to smaller bubble radii. Both surfactants display parallel trends regarding their 
impact on interfacial tension and bubble size. This observation validates the notion that 
active surfactants play a crucial role in diminishing the susceptibility to the Ostwald 
Ripening effect, thereby contributing to the enhancement of foam structure integrity.   
• The study unveiled the sensitivity of both surfactants to temperature variations, particularly 
at elevated levels. While the impact on BZK was relatively minor, discernible differences 
were evident in the case of CTAB. This suggests that CTAB's performance is significantly 
==PAGE:61==
DTU Chemistry  
March 2024 
Master Thesis 
 
61   
 
influenced by temperature fluctuations, underscoring the need for careful consideration of 
thermal conditions in applications involving this surfactant. 
• Salinity was found to exert minimal influence on the performance of the surfactants. Even 
so, subtle variations were observed, particularly in response to changes in magnesium and 
sulfate levels. These findings imply that while salinity may induce slight effects, its impact 
is more pronounced on CMC rather than directly on surfactant performance. 
• Future research endeavors could explore various avenues, including the evaluation of CMC 
under the conditions adopted in this study. Additionally, investigating non-ionic CO2 
switchable surfactants to contrast their performance against cationic counterparts could 
provide valuable insights. Another promising direction involves assessing the maximum 
foam volume generated by each surfactant and comparing it with their stability profiles. 
Moreover, extending this study might involve redesigning experiments to incorporate 
three-level interactions or varying concentrations to evaluate higher-order interactions 
comprehensively. Such advancements could deepen our understanding of surfactant 
behavior and optimize their application in diverse industrial processes.
