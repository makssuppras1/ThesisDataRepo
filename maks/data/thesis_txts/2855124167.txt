Abstract
This project focuses on the research of Structural Variants (SVs) in genes associated
with Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young (MODY), aiming to optimise the identifi-
cation of these variants and contribute to precision medicine. MODY is a monogenic
form of diabetes which often results from mutations in genes like GCK, HNF1A,
HNF4A, and HNF1B. Despite advancements in diabetes diagnostics, MODY patients
are frequently misdiagnosed with other diabetes types, leading to inappropriate treat-
ment. While current diagnostic strategies focus predominantly on the identification
of single nucleotide variants (SNVs) associated with the disease, SVs in those genes
can lead to the same phenotype, and therefore it is worth to include them in the
analyses.
This project seeks to identify SVs in MODY-associated genes by leveraging Whole-
Genome Sequencing (WGS) data from diabetes patients that are part of the TRANS-
LATE project. This project is in collaboration with TRANSLATE and the Danish
National Genome Center, and it focuses on developing a comprehensive SV report.
The report includes visual representations of the identified SVs as well as their anno-
tations and predicted functional effects, with a focus on high-impact variants which
can result in a pathogenic phenotype. The study addresses the challenges in SV de-
tection by utilizing a well-established variant calling and annotation pipeline, as it is
nf-core/sarek.
A total of 1719 samples were processed following this approach, and the results were
analyzed with a focus on the MODY genes, revealing that at least one high-impact
SV had been identified by at least one tool in 57 patients. The work developed during
this project not only allows the analyses of the already processed samples, but could
also be implemented in TRANSLATE production, providing an informative PDF
report that can be used by geneticist and clinicians to diagnose the patients. This
project aligns with the purpose of precision medicine, enabling tailored treatments
and reducing the burden of misdiagnosis on both the healthcare system and patients.

Introduction
1.1
Diabetes
Diabetes Mellitus (DM) is a group of conditions associated with the endocrine system,
which are characterized by unusually high blood glucose levels, what is known as
hyperglycemia. Diabetes can appear from an inability of the body to produce insulin,
an inability of the body’s tissues to respond to insulin (known as insulin resistance)
or a combination of both. Diabetes has traditionally been divided into 2 main types:
type 1 diabetes (T1D) and type 2 diabetes (T2D). In addition to T1D and T2D,
there are other clinically relevant sub-types, such as gestational diabetes (GD) and
maturity-onset diabetes of young (MODY), which vary in their origin and clinical
approaches to treatment[1][2].
Diabetes is a chronic disease which by 2021 affected 536.6 million adults (20-79 years)
in the world. It has been estimated that by 2045, 783.2 million adults in the world will
have diabetes [3]. Diabetes is a disease associated with multiple health complications,
among them macro and microvascular complications which can lead to blindness or
kidney failure. These complications are usually life-endangering, incapacitating as
well as expensive, and contribute to an increase mortality [1][3].
T1D is an autoimmune disease which results in the autoimmune destruction of the
β-cells, which are located in the pancreas and are responsible of insulin production.
It is an autoimmune response mediated by T-cells, which recognize and interact with
autoantigens, proteins from the β-cells [2]. T1D is a complex disease where genet-
ics plays a key role, being the primary genetic association with Human Leukocyte
Antigen (HLA) genes. Environmental factors such as viral infections, have also been
probed to increase the susceptibility to T1D, nevertheless the interplay between ge-
netic predisposition and environmental triggers remains unclear [4]. T1D constitutes
between 5 and 10% of the diagnosed cases [2].
T2D is characterized by insulin resistance, which is a decreased response to insulin
by the target tissues. Initially, this effect is faced by an increased insulin production,
however over the time it results in a decreased insulin production. It is the most
common diabetes type, representing around 90% of diagnosed cases [2].
MODY is a special type of DM characterized by abnormal function of β-cells which
results in reduced insulin secretion. It is manifested usually before 25 years old, and
has a genetic basis. It is inherited with an autosomal dominant pattern, which means
that having a single defective copy leads to disease. It accounts for less than 5% of the
diagnoses. It is a monogenic disease, therefore it is caused by the mutation of a single
gene. There have already been identified some of the genes associated with MODY,
being most of the cases due to mutations in glucokinase (GCK), hepatocyte nuclear
factor 1 homeobox A (HNF1A), hepatocyte nuclear factor 4 homeobox A (HNF4A),
and hepatocyte nuclear factor 1 homeobox B (HNF1B) [2][5].
1
==PAGE:12==
All of these DM types have a genetic component, and traditionally there has been a
division between monogenic and polygenic diabetes. Polygenic diabetes is a result of
the contribution of more than one genetic variant that collectively increase disease
risk, while monogenic results from one penetrant variant in one gene.
As stated
before, MODY is part of the monogenic category, while T1D and T2D are part of
the polygenic category. Having a correct diabetes diagnosis is essential to receive
the most appropriate treatment for the disease, and to get more accurate prognosis
when it comes to the future complications that may derive from diabetes. Precisely,
tailoring disease prevention and treatment to align with the characteristics of each
individual is known as “Precision Medicine” [6][7].
Nevertheless, MODY patients are usually misdiagnosed with T1D or T2D, which
results in patients receiving inappropriate treatment. This is the case of patients
with mutations in the GCK gene, which leads to mild hyperglycemia, and usually
does not require treatment. However, when misdiagnosed they receive unnecessary
glucose lowering treatment. HNF1A-MODY and HNF4A-MODY can be treated with
sulphonylureas rather than with insulin. Therefore, an accurate diagnosis is needed to
avoid overtreatment, which results in a burden for both the patient and the healthcare
sector. Genetic analysis, such as Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS), arise as tools
which facilitate an accurate diagnosis and tailored treatment strategies [6].
1.2
Whole Genome Sequencing
Since the Human Genome Project was completed in 2003, DNA sequencing has con-
siderably advanced. One of the biggest breakthroughs in the DNA sequencing field
happened with the development of Next Generation Sequencing (NGS). NGS plat-
forms have allowed not only to sequence whole genomes in a considerably smaller
amount of time, but also to make it affordable. Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS)
refers to the sequencing of the complete genome of a person; this includes coding and
non-coding sequences, structural rearrangements, and might also include the sequenc-
ing of the mitochondrial genome depending on the protocol [8]. Short-read sequencing
technologies (Illumina or BGI) are commonly used for WGS given their high accuracy
and eﬀiciency, but in the recent years the emerging long-read sequencing technolo-
gies (Oxford Nanopore or PacBio) offer improved capabilities, especially for resolving
Structural Variants (SVs) and repetitive regions [9].
WGS has emerged as a diagnosis tool for different types of diseases, mainly because
it offers a series of advantages over other NGS analyses or molecular diagnosis tests.
One of said advantages is that WGS can detect different genetic variants in a single
analysis, such as Single Nucleotide Variaants (SNVs) or SVs [10]. Additionally since
it does not require the capture of any sequence, it includes sequencing data of introns,
intergenic, and regulatory regions, which can also shed some light when it comes to
the comprehension of how diseases work [10].
Nevertheless, it must be taken into account that WGS generates a large amount of
2
==PAGE:13==
data, that must be stored, processed and interpreted. In this context, the bioinformat-
ics efforts have been focused lately in developing pipelines that facilitate processing
and interpreting WGS data. A data analysis pipeline for WGS usually includes the
following steps: mapping, calling and interpretation. In most of the cases the turnover
time may not be critical, however under specific circumstances such as patients in
unstable conditions, or pediatrics’s patients it is essential that the bioinformatics anal-
ysis can be carried out in a relatively short amount of time [8]. Therefore, it becomes
a priority to keep improving the available tools for data processing and interpretation,
as well as the computational infrastructure where these analyses are carried out.
WGS can be used in the diagnosis of rare genetic diseases, but also in the diagnosis of
polygenic diseases, as WGS can identify common and rare variants that can be used to
define the Polygenic Risk Score (PRS). This can be particularly useful in diseases such
as some cancers, but also T2D; diseases which are associated with the combinations
of common genetic variants, each of which slightly increases the risk of the disease.
[8]. Therefore the clinical application of WGS can help in disease diagnosis, but it is
essential to focus our efforts in improving annotation and interpretation of sequencing
data.
1.3
Genetic Variants: Structural Variants
When talking about genetic variants, there are two main concepts that arise, germline
variants, which are genetic variations between individuals, and somatic mutations,
which are the genetic variations within individuals. Germline variants are inherited
and present in all cells of a person whereas somatic mutations appear on a single
cell (any cell except a germline cell) and then spread by cell division, therefore they
appear in a mosaic manner.
Both kind of variants play a role in human disease;
germline variants are behind inherited diseases, whereas some somatic mutations can
affect essential cellular processes resulting in diseases such as cancer [11].
There are 4 main classes of genetic variants:
• Substitutions: mainly SNVs [11]
• Indels: short insertions and deletations, less than 50 bp (base pairs) [11]
• Structural Variants (SVs): this includes duplications, deletions, insertions, in-
versions and translocations of a large number of bp, anywhere from 50bp to
megabases [11].
• Large chromosomal abnormalities, like the presence or absence of whole chro-
mosomes [11][12].
Among them, this project focuses on the identification of SVs, given that while SNVs
haven been largely studied in the context of MODY diabetes, SVs can also lead to
3
==PAGE:14==
the disease and have not been so largely explored. Identifying and understanding the
role of SVs is essential as they affect a wide range of cellular and molecular processes,
which in some cases can be associated with disease [12]. SVs can be broadly classified
into 2 categories, balanced and unbalanced SVs.
Balanced SVs are chromosomal
rearrangements that do not imply net gain or loss of genetic material (inversions and
translocations), whereas unbalanced SVs are variants that imply net gain or loss of
genetic material (deletions, duplications and insertions). An overview of the different
types of SVs can be seen in Figure 1.

interspersed), insertion (novel sequence and mobile-element), inversion and translocation.
Image extracted from Alkan et al. [13]
SVs constitute a remarkable mutational force, partly due to the fact that they often
result in disrupting genes that code for proteins and the cis regulatory architecture of
said genes [14]. Nevertheless, SVs have been largely understudied compared to other
variations due to the limitations on their identification, mainly as a consequence of
their diversity and proximity to repetitive regions [12]. Overall, benchmarking SVs is
more challenging, first of all because it is more diﬀicult to resolve this kind of variants
using short-read sequencing data and second because it is more diﬀicult to define the
precise breakends of the variants, adding an additional diﬀiculty when comparing
between call sets [15].
1.4
Variant Calling
Variant calling refers to the process by which variants are identified using sequencing
data. In fact, one of the most relevant applications of NGS is the discovery of variation
by comparing to a reference. When discussing variant calling is important to consider
4
==PAGE:15==
2 different types of variant calling: somatic variant calling and germline variant calling
[15].
• Germline Variant Calling: identification of variants in germline cells which will
be inherited.
• Somatic Variant Calling: identification of variants in somatic cells which are
not inherited. It is often associated with diseases such as cancer.
A typical variant calling workflow involves different steps:
1. Sequencing: generating the reads from the sample using some sequencing plat-
form.
2. Assembly or mapping: the generated reads are either de novo assembled, or
most likely mapped against a reference genome.
3. Variant Calling: variants are called using specialized tools and algorithms that
can identify the difference between the reference and the sequencing data.
4. Filtering: the identified variants undergo some filtering to make the results more
reliable.
5. Annotation: the identified variants are annotated and their effects are predicted
In a germline variant calling workflow, the samples can be compared against a reg-
ular reference genome, however when doing somatic variant calling is it needed a
healthy/non-healthy pair of samples to be able to properly distinguish between noise,
and the actual mutations that lead to the disease in those cells [15][16] .
1.5
Sequencing Data for Diabetes Diagnosis
With the advent of NGS techniques, genome sequencing has become an accessible and
affordable tool for disease diagnosis. More importantly, in the context of diabetes,
it is particularly useful for the diagnosis of MODY diabetes, given that it is due to
single gene defects that can only be identified by genetic testing [17].
As patients with MODY diabetes are frequently misdiagnosed with other types of
diabetes when genetic testing is not involved, the correct diagnosis using NGS tech-
niques represents an example of precision medicine. MODY patients when properly
diagnosed can have a tailored treatment more appropriate to the specific type of di-
abetes they have, considering the routes that are impaired as a consequence of their
gene defects [18].
As of today, several projects have utilized NGS techniques for the diagnosis of patients
with MODY diabetes. Some of these involve sequencing 7 of the most important genes
5
==PAGE:16==
associated with monogenic diabetes using a targeted approach [18], while others opt
for a broader set, including up to a total of 52 genes associated with pancreatic cell
dysfunction [17]. Nevertheless, these conventional methods are sometimes costly and
limited when considering multi genic disorders, making WGS a key player, that given
the reduction of its cost in the last years, can improve the diagnosis, but at the same
time contribute to increase the amount of data available for research, which could
potentially lead to the identification of new variants associated with the disease [19].
1.6
TRANSLATE Project
The Danish TRANSLATE Project is an innitiative that aims at improving diagno-
sis and treatment of diabetes, more specifically of non-autoimmune diabetes. This
project is framed in the concept of personalized medicine, aimed at implementing
genetic information for both diagnosis and treatment of diseases. TRANSLATE uses
WGS data as a standard diagnostic method, but focuses on the study of the genes
associated with MODY diabetes. The additional advantage of using WGS data rather
than targeted data, is the possibility of studying non-coding regions as well as other
variants such as SVs, that otherwise would no be caught [20].
The WGS data is processed according to nf-core/sarek pipeline (See Section 2.3.2.1).
The encountered variants are classified as immediately actionable, potentially action-
able or non-actionable, depending on the severity of their effects. A more detailed
description of all the variants that are looked for in the data can be found here. Over-
all the variants that are currently under study are SNPs, nevertheless having available
WGS data opens the door to the study of other kind of variants, such as SVs, that
can have the same disruptive effect[21].
To date, the TRANSLATE Project has mainly focused on the identification of SNPs
on sequencing data, overlooking SVs that may equally disrupt gene function and lead
to MODY diabetes.
This study aims to bridge this gap by identifying structural
variants using the available WGS data.
1.7
Graph genomes
The creation of the human reference genome was a milestone achieved within the scope
of human genetics. A reference genome acts as a scaffold, allowing the mapping and
assembly of short sequencing reads into longer contigs. This mapping and assembly
process is essential for the the subsequent steps of the analyses, however it must be
considered that genomes reconstructed using a reference genome, can end up looking
more similar to the reference than they truly are [22] [23].
Over the last years, with the increased popularity of WGS, a number of new unique
sequences not present in the current reference genome have been revealed, as well
as population specific differences in genetic variants which are considered common;
6
==PAGE:17==
altogether, this could result in some populations being left out. The genetic divergence
can lead to mismapping of the reads, which can result in variants not being detected
(false negatives) or the erroneous identification of variants (false positives).
The
deficiencies of the linear reference that have come to light in recent years have led
to the search for new paradigms that better reflect the complexity and variation of
human genomes, and then is when the concept of a graph reference genome arises
[22] [23].
Graphs have been used in bioinformatics for tasks like assembly (de Bruijn graphs)
[24] and they can be useful to represent reference genomes. One of the advantages that
graph reference genomes have is that they may encode a larger number of variants.
Nevertheless, it must be taken into account the increased computational cost that
this approach has, given that the number of graphs will increase as new sequences
are included in the analysis [25].
More specifically, within the context of this project, graph genomes can outperform
traditional reference genome approaches when it comes to the detection of SVs. This
is due precisely to the intrinsic complex nature of SVs, which can be better represented
using graphs.[25]. As a consequence, graph genomes can constitute a powerful tool
to detect SVs in MODY genes, and therefore improve the diagnosis of MODY.
1.8
Objectives
To date, the WGS data generated as part of the TRANSLATE Project, has only
been used to identify SNVs. The goal of this project is to use the available data from
TRANSLATE to optimize the identification of SVs in genes known to cause MODY,
with the purpose of improving MODY diagnostics.
This involves identifying SVs using different SV calling approaches and integrating
the findings to generate personalized clinical reports, which include the identified
variants, their annotations and predicted effects and graphical representations. This
clinical report would help clinicians and geneticists to make more accurate diagnoses
and treatment decisions, therefore establishing a framework that could be use for
clinical practice.

2.2
Sequencing Data
The data used for this project is part of the Danish TRANSLATE Project, a research
project aimed at improving the diagnosis and treatment of non-autoimmune diabetes,
using WGS data as a standard diagnostic method.
The TRANSLATE projects includes data from patients above 18 years of age once
signed the informed consent form. Patients that decide to join the project get their
blood drawn at either the Steno Diabetes Center or at the obstetric clinics (women
with gestational diabetes). The samples are sent to BGI where the DNA extraction
and sequencing is performed.
The sample has to include more than 5 ml of whole blood, more than 1µg of DNA,
and a DNA concentration higher than 12.5ng/µl.
Once DNA is purified WGS is
performed using the DNBSEQ-T7 sequencer using paired end (PE) sequencing with
a read length of 150 bp [21]. DNBSEQ is a sequencing technology based on DNA
nanoballs (DNBs) which are created during the library preparation. The process is
divided in the following steps:
1. Single-Strand DNA Circularization [29] [30] [31].
11
==PAGE:22==
2. DNB Making: DNBs are created using Rolling Circle Replication from the single
stranded circular DNA [29] [30] [31].
3. DNB Loading: each individual DNB is attached to a unique binding site in the
chip thanks to the electrostatic interactions between the chip and the DNBs
[29] [30] [31].
4. cPAS: sequencing primers are hybridized to the DNBs’ adapter regions, followed
by addition of fluorescently labelled dNTP probes. dNTPs that do not bind are
washed away, and the Flow Cell image is taken. Fluorescent signal is converted
to digital, and bases are called using BGI’s software. Once the image is taken,
the fluorescent dye is removed so that DNBs are ready for the next cycle [29]
[30] [31].
5. 2nd Strand Preparation: once the 1st strand has been sequenced, it begins the
sequencing of the 2nd strand using a polymerase with strand displacement. The
2nd strand contains more copies of the insert DNA, resulting in a stronger signal
and consequently a better accuracy [29] [30] [31].
6. Base Calling Algorithm: the signal intensities are used for base calling and base
call quality. Quality scores follow Phred-33 [29] [30][31].
Once the sequencing data has been generated, the next step is to process it using a
Nextflow pipeline Sarek.
2.3
Nextflow
2.3.1
Brief Introduction to Nextflow
Nextflow is a workflow manager system that allows the creation and use of scalable
and reproducible computational pipelines.It is inspired by UNIX pipes, allowing the
streaming of one operation’s output into other operation, chaining simple command
line tools into more complex tasks. Nextflow scripts are mainly based on a modular
design, based on processes. Each process can execute a given scripting language or
tool, providing a great versatility and enabling the reuse of code. Nextflow is based
on a Domain Specific Language (DSL) making possible its integration with High
Performance Computing (HPC) Clusters as well as cloud platforms. Additionally,
one of the key features of Nextflow is its support of a variety of container runtimes,
such as Docker and Singularity, contributing to the reproducibility of the pipelines. Its
is also remarkable its integration with some of the most common software repositories
such as BitBucket or GitHub. The present project has made use of the version 23.10.0
[32].

2.4
Variant Callers
The detection of SVs constitutes a significant challenge as SVs are inherently really
complex and heterogeneus, ranging from small insertions or deletions, to extremely
complex rearrangements. There are different variant calling tools that follow different
algorithmic approaches to address the challenge of SV detection.
2.4.1
Manta
Manta is a SV detection tool developed by Illumina, specifically designed for PE
sequencing data. Manta can identify complex rearrangements, such as deletions or
inversions with a high precision. It works in two phases.
1. Creation of a genome-wide graph with all the breakend associations. In this
graph each node represents a region where there is evidence of the existence of
15
==PAGE:26==
a breakend, while edges symbolize the evidence of a junction between breakends
[35].
2. Once the graph is completed, different sets of graph edges are studied as in-
dependent SV discovery problems; the sets are analyzed in paralel. The edges
of the graph are analyzed to create SV candidates. Each of the candidates is
assembled and scored according to either somatic or diploid quality models, and
finally they are VCF reported [35].
Before the start of the first phase, Manta estimates fragment size and read depth
information.
The estimated fragment size distribution is used to understand the
typical range in the data it works with [35].
For this project the version of Manta used was Manta v1.6.0, and it was run as part
of the nf-core/sarek pipeline [35], .
2.4.2
TIDDIT
TIDDIT is a SV variant caller capable of identifying a wide variate of SVs, includ-
ing insertions, deletions, inversions and tandem duplications. Overall TIDDIT finds
discordant pairs and split reads to find the location of SVs, followed by classification
and quality assessment of the variants using the read depth information [36]. More
specifically TIDDIT’s algorithm for SV detection is divided in 2 main phases:
1. Calculation of coerage and insert size distribution [36].
2. Identification of discordant pairs and split reads. This information will be used
to detect and further on classify SVs [36].
Once the candidate variations are identified, to determinate which variant type it
might be, TIDDIT analyses the orientation and coverage across the region of the first
and second reads, as well as the region in between. Finally, for each SV that is called,
there are some statistics calculated with the main goal of providing more information
and filtering out noise. TIDDIT uses 4 different filters:
• Expected_links: to filter variants caused by random events like contaminations.
• Few_links: to filter variants caused by reference errors and alignment errors.
• Smear: remove variants that have been called just for being in large repetitive
regions.
• Unexpected_coverage: with the same purpose as Few_links but using coverage
information.
For this project the version of TIDDIT used was TIDDIT v3.6.1, and it was run as
part of the nf-core/sark.pipeline. [36]
16
==PAGE:27==
2.4.3
CNVkit
Finally, CNVkit is a tool focused on the identification of Copy Number Variants
(CNV). CNVkit uses the on-target and off-target reads to create some genomic bins
where the read depth is calculated.
The on- and off-target locations are used to
calculate the depth in each interval, and further on combined and normalized against a
reference dataset to result in a final table of log2 copy ratios. Finally the segmentation
algorithm will use the log2 ratios to infer copy number segments [37].
Overall CNVkit is a powerful tool to estimate copy number from high-throughput
sequencing data.
In this project the CNVkit version that was used was CNVkit
v0.9.10 and it was run as part of the nf-core/sarek pipeline [37].
2.5
Variant Annotation
Variant annotation is essential to transform genetic variation data into biological
insights that can be used for other purposes, such as diagnosis or research.
The
importance of annotation lies in its ability to provide some understanding to the
genetic variations. Annotation tools categorize and predict the effect of the variants,
leading to their potential consequences, which is essential in the context of this project,
as the final goal is to determine if a patient has or not any form of MODY diabetes.
There have been developed several tools for Variant Annotation, and in the context of
this project 2 of them have been used: VEP and SnpEff. These tools integrate gene
annotation databases and predictive models to predict how the detected variants can
affect gene regulation or the function of the protein [38] [39].
2.5.1
VEP
VEP stands for Variant Effect Predictor, and it is a widely used tool developed by
Ensembl which allows the analysis, annotation and prioritization of variants. VEP
provides effect annotation for proteins, transcripts and non-coding regions. VEP can
annotate two types of genomic variants:
1. Sequence variants precise and well characterized changes. This includes SNVs,
tandem repeats, insertions and deletions.
2. Larger structural variants (>50bp).
VEP works by taking the VCF files with the variants as input, and mapping the
variants to a reference genome. For each variant it identifies the transcripts that over-
lap, and analyzes the effect of the NDA changes introduced by the variants. Finally
VEP retrieves information from different genomic databases, and can apply some
prediction algorithms (PolyPhen) to assess the functional consequences of amino acid
changes [38][40] .
17
==PAGE:28==
In this project the VEP version that was used was VEP v110.0, and it was run as
part of the nf-core/sarek pipeline [38]
2.5.2
SnpEff
SnpEff is a tool which facilitates variant annotation and variant effect prediction.
SnpEff can annotate variants in a variety of genomic locations, including exons, in-
trons, untranslated regions or intergenic regions, and the coding effects it can predict
range from amino acid replacement, to frame shifts. SnpEff workflow is divided in 2
main parts:
1. Database build: the databases are built using the provided annotation file and
the reference genome [39].
2. Effect calculations: once the database is built, a structure called interval forest
is created to facilitate interval search. Each variant queries the data structures
looking for the genomic annotations. All the genomic regions affected are re-
ported, and if any of those regions includes an exon, the effect of the variant is
predicted [39].
In this project it was used SnpEff in its version v5.1, and it was run as part of the
nf-core/sarek pipeline. It is important to highlight, that since v4.3 SnpEff allows the
annotation of large SVs (contrary to what its name might indicate) [39].
2.6
Identification of variants in MODY genes
This part of the analysis is carried out for every sample by 07_del_dup.py. This
script looks for SVs using a multi-modal approach across three primary computational
platforms: Mosdepth, VCF-based tools (Manta and TIDDIT), and CNVkit, each with
its unique algorithm for identifying structural variations in MODY genes.
2.6.1
Mosdepth Approach
The nf-core/sarek pipeline provides quality control and coverage assessment as part
of its workflow. This includes Mosdepth, a fast an eﬀicient tool that can calculate the
genome coverage using as input alignment files, such as as BAM (Binary Alignment
Map) or CRAM. The version of Mosdepth used in the TRANSLATE setup is v0.3.3
[41]. More specifically it runs on window sizes of 500 bp, therefore providing a depth
value for intervals of 500 bp. Analyzing the output file (BED) in the gene region can
provide important information regarding the detection of genomic alterations. Under
normal conditions the depth of coverage is expected to be relatively uniform across
a genomic region, therefore deviations may suggest duplications or deletions. In this
analysis, a sliding window is implemented to detect genomic alterations.
18
==PAGE:29==
The method first calculates the analysis region, by considering the chromosome, start
and end position of every gene, and extending 200,000 base pairs (bp) around the
target gene (up- and downstream). This approach enables to get a better intuition
of the coverage trends in the gene and its surroundings.
A critical step involves
smoothing the coverage data using a rolling mean with a window of 80 data points,
which helps mitigate local noise and highlight broader genomic trends. This rolling
mean is calculated over overlapping intervals, minimizing the effect of short-term
fluctuations.

2.6.2
VCF-based approach
The VCF-based analysis, makes use of the Manta and TIDDIT output. The script
parses VCF files to extract structural variations, identifying deletions and duplications
based on several key attributes:
• SVTYPE field in the INFO column (DEL for deletions and DUP for duplica-
tions)
• Direct comparison of reference and alternate allele lengths to identify small
indels.
• Precise genomic start and end positions, as well as chromosome, to ensure only
the variants in the MODY genes are captured. For this purpose the variant
must fulfil the following conditions:
– The chromosome where the variant is located must be the same chromo-
some where the gene is located.
– The start of the variant must be before the end of the gene.
– The end of the variant must be after the start of the gene.
This VCF-approach is both performed in the annotated and non-annotated VCF
files, and afterwards the results are compared to see if any of the variants was not
annotated.
2.6.3
CNVkit approach
CNVkit analysis focuses on log2 ratio calculations. The script extracts genomic re-
gions from the cnr output of CNVkit. It focuses on the region corresponding to the
target gene and calculates the mean log2 ratio. Variant classification is determined
by predefined thresholds, and each segement withing the gene region is evaluated:
• Log2 ratio ≥0.5 indicates a duplication
• Log2 ratio ≤-0.5 indicates a deletion.
Segments with similar log2 values and close to each other (≤800 bp apart) are merged
to ensure accurate representation of the SV. As the threshold for a variant to be SV
is 50 bp, only segments larger than 50 bp are retained.
2.6.4
Integration of Results
To integrate these different analytical approaches, the script implements an unification
approach, that consolidates results from Mosdepth, VCF analysis, and CNVkit into
a single comprehensive DataFrame. With this output it is possible to:
20
==PAGE:31==
• Validate the SV calls for a clinical application.
• Analyze the notations and the potential impact that the variants may have.
• Report in detail the SVs.
The integration process prioritizes annotated results over non-annotated ones and
removes duplicate entries, ensuring a clean and informative final output. For each
gene, each variant detected with each of the methods is reported including the name
of the gene where it was found, the chromosomal location (chromosome, start and
end), its size, which type of variant it is, the source (which approach has detected it)
and if there are annotations, those are also reported.
To complement the numerical analysis, the script generates two visualizations:
1. A coverage plot displaying genomic coverage, smoothed coverage, the gene area,
and highlighting regions above or below average coverage. This is generated for
all of the samples.
2. A gene structure plot showing structural variations mapped to the gene’s exonic
structure. This is displayed only for the samples where at least one of the tools
has identified one structural variant.
This multi-modal, integrative approach allows the detection of SVs in MODY genes
given the information obtained from other bioinformatic tools. It provides a more
robust and clinically relevant approach than relying in a single tool. It additionally
provides some graphical representations that help to understand the results. This
approach represents a significant advancement, offering a tool that based on data
obtained through nf-core/sarek pipeline, can provide detailed information for the
SVs detected in the MODY genes. Therefore this script could be implemented in
TRANSLATE’s production, enabling the report of SVs which can be associated with
MODY diabetes.

Report generation
A custom script was developed to automatically generate a PDF report by processing
the data generated in the identification of variants in MODY genes (See Section 2.6).
This approach integrates tabular information extracted from CSV files, visual plots
(coverage and gene plots), and additionally parses the annotations which were ex-
tracted from the annotation tools (VEP and SnpEff).
The report is structured so there is a section dedicated to every gene, highlighting rel-
evant deletions and duplications as well as the annotations, in an easily interpretable
format.
21
==PAGE:32==
It is important to highlight how the parsing of the annotations was perfoemd in this
step of the process. The annotations from both VEP and SnpEff (when available)
are stored in a column named annotations, and they are in a JSON-like format.
The script retrieves the annotations field from the specified row.
If the field is a
string (not NaN or empty), it is parsed using the module ast, to convert the JSON
like annotations to a dictionary.
As the annotation was performed in all of the samples with both VEP and SnpEff, it
is expected to find always as keys vep and snpeff. Each of these keys is mapped to a
list of annotation records which are converted to dataframes for easier manipulation
and visualization. Then for each of the tables (one for VEP and another one for
SnpEff), missing values are looked for, so that if any column only has missing values
it is dropped, as it is consider it will not provide any useful information.
Finally, in order to narrow down more the results to be displayed, the entries are
filtered so only the rows that correspond to the gene under study are retained. This
ensures that the annotations are contextually relevant to the report.
Automating the report generation by means of a script offer significant advantages in
the context of genomic analysis, mainly when it comes to the time it would take to
process manually all the needed information for all of the samples. The automated
approach ensures consistency and reproducibility, which is critical in both research
and clinical settings. Furthermore including the graphical representations, as well as
tables with the most important results into a single report facilitates data interpreta-
tion.
Overall, a consolidated PDF report aids clinicians and researchers by providing a
summary of detected variants and their annotations, therefore facilitating both inter-
pretation, and making informed decisions.
2.8
Graph Genomes
Graph genomes have the potential to represent more accurately the complexity of
genomes, and therefore they could improve the detection of SVs in MODY genes.
The tool chosen for the creation of a graph reference genome was vg, more specifi-
cally v1.32.0 [42]. Vg is a toolkit of different methods that enables the creation and
manipulation of variation graphs. A genome graph consists of nodes, which represent
DNA sequences, and edges, which represent the connections between the sequences.
The different paths that can be taken through the graph represent specific haplo-
types, and the variations (deletions, insertions, SNPs) are alternative paths through
the graph[42].
The construction of graph reference genome by the method vg contruct requires
a reference FASTA file and a VCF file.
In this case the GRCh38 version of the
reference genome was used as reference. Regarding the VCF file, VCF outputs from
22
==PAGE:33==
both TIDDIT and Manta for 2 samples of the project were used. The merging was
performed using bcftools v1.20 ([28]). Vg does not naturally support all types of SV
annotations, specially those that involve mobile elements, therefore the VCF file was
cleaned to remove this entries that otherwise raise errors when building the graph.
The resulting VCF file was indexed using tabix v1.2.1 [43]. A graph was constructed
for each chromosome. Working with human genomes, it is advisable to prune the
graphs before indexing them.
The calling step of vg does not directly use the graphs, but some indexes that must be
generated instead. These indexes are XG and GCSA2. XG is defined a space-eﬀicient
immutable graph, whereas GCSA2 is an index of a pruned de Bruijn graph, which
allows locating in the graph a specific sequence in a fast way. Finally the reads of the
samples of interest were mapped, in this case using both read 1 and 2 as the data is
PE sequencing.
The reads have to be mapped against each of the graphs generated (1 per chromosome)
and given the time the mapping takes for each chromosome, it was decided to map
only against the chromosomes where the MODY genes are (7, 12, 17 and 20). The
output of the mapping is a GAM (Graph Alignment Map) file, that must be sorted.
The variants from the GAM files can be called using vg call, which relies on samtools
pileup, being the output a VCF file.

Given the information extracted from the initial overview, and the datasets generated,
Seqera platform was set up for performing the variant calling of the samples. As it
will be used to run the Sarek pipeline but starting in the variant-calling step, the

the set up in TRANSLATE production only stores CRAM and CRAI files, as they
have a smaller size.
Nevertheless, tools such as TIDDIT or CNVKIT only accept BAM as input, not
CRAM, which is why Sarek has a module named CRAM_TO_BAM. During the first
attempt of running the pipeline, it continued failing in some samples, and it was due
to the reference genome which was being used. One of the main differences between
CRAM and BAM files is that the compression of CRAM is driven by the reference
data which was used during the alignment.
The reference genome which is used for the whole pipeline can be set when launching
it through the pipeline parameters.
However, it must be the same for the whole
dataset, and that is why the overall pipeline was failing. Even if it succeeds for some
samples, if it does not succeed for all of them, the overall exit status of the pipeline
will be failure.
This problem can be easily solved by splitting the datasets according to the reference
genome which was used for its creation. In total 3 different reference genomes were
found to have been used in the samples that needed to be processed.

Pipeline Setup and Execution
The datasets were divided according to both the reference genome and the callers

a different Sarek run using different pipeline parameters. This is what happens with
the directories where the temporary results are stored, which differ between datasets
to avoid crashes, as well as with the iGenome settings, which need to be different
between the datasets to match the appropriate reference genome.
The iGenomes
configuration from Sarek points at the path where the different files are stored. In
this case, the iGenome file in the Sarek’s repository that is run in TRANSLATE
Production had the information for all of them except Homo. As a consequence, it
was decided to create a branch, to avoid affecting the production flow, where the
paths for corresponding files were included.
Seqera platform enables parallel execution of the Sarek workflow, therefore it can
distribute the tasks across multiple nodes and CPUs, so that each task can be exe-
cuted independently. In this case the processing of the samples is distributed though
different nodes, optimizing the HPC resources and in a reasonable turnaround time.
Using Seqera platform has allowed that tasks that executed sequentially would have
taken weeks could be completed in a matter of hours, or at most days. For an insight
of the resources used in the runs see Table 2 .
It is important to highlight that these runs were started in the variant-calling step,
and only TIDDIT and/or CNVKIT were set as tools, which meant that the annotation
step had not taken place. However, Seqera and Sarek implementation allow to easily
solve this problem, by simply setting a new dataset with the sample name and VCF
files. The sarek pipeline can be run from the annotation step, and by setting VEP
and SnpEff as tools, both will be used to annotate the files. This shows the great
advantage of using Seqera and Nextflow to process large datasets using pipelines such
as Sarek.

4.1
E100079731_L01_77 Presents a Partial
Deletion of the Gene HNF1A
The results displayed in Section 3.2, show how all of the approaches that have been
followed identify a partial deletion in the gene HNF1A. Although it is true that
the breakends identified by the tools differ, the breakends identified by Manta and
TIDDIT differ in just a couple of base pairs, at least when it comes to the beginning
of the deletion.
However, the variant has passed the filters of both TIDDIT and Manta, and the other
two approaches also identified a deletion. These results provide strong evidence of a
partial deletion of the gene HNF1A in the sample E100079731_L01_77. Even though
the breakends differ for the different approaches, both TIDDIT and Manta identify
a deletion of the exons 2 to 10. The annotations from both VEP and SnpEff predict
this variant to be of high impact. A high impact variant for SnpEff and VEP is a
variant that is assumed to have a disruptive impact on the protein (loss of function
or protein truncation), which indicates that the identified changes may be critical for
the normal biological function of the gene. Therefore, high impact variants are key
targets for clinical evaluation and genetic counselling.
The consequence that VEP predicts is the loss of a stop codon, meaning that the
translation process would not stop as it usually does, which is in concordance with
the fact that the deletion covers until the end of the gene (according to Manta and
TIDDIT). It affects the coding region, as causes the deletion of exons 2 to 10, but also
the non-coding region, as it causes the deletion of introns 1 to 9. To highlight that it
also predicts feature truncation, which would lead to the truncation of a functional
feature of HNF1A.
When it comes to the annotations from SnpEff, it is also annotated a feature ablation,
as well as the loss of exons. To note that SnpEff for both TIDDIT and Manta includes
an entry that involves 2 other genes affected by the deletion C12orf43 and OASL, once
again with a high impact.
The protein coded by HNF1A is a Transcription Factor part of the HNF1 homeobox
family, and it codes for the hepatocyte nuclear factor 1 alpha. HNF1A protein is
expressed in liver, pancreas, gut and kidneys. Regulation of gene activity by the
HNF1A protein plays a key role in several biological process, being one of them the
development of β-cells in pancreas, as well as liver development. When it comes to
41
==PAGE:52==
its protein domains, the dimerization domain is of high importance as it allows the
formation of a dimer with other protein unit, so the dimer can work as transcription
factor. In transcription factors such as HNF1A it is also of high importance the DNA
binding domain, which as its name indicates is in charge of binding DNA to regulate
gene activity [44].
The deletion that has been identified in this project, specially taking into account
Manta and TIDDIT results, would result in the deletion of exons 2 to 10. Exon 1
codes for the 109 first amino acids (aa) of the final protein. Regarding the functional
domains of HNF1A protein, the dimerization domain ranges from 1 to 33 aa, the
DNA binding domain from 100-184 aa and the transactivation domain from 282 to
631 aa. Therefore the DNA-binding domain and the transactivation domain would be
affected by this deletion, resulting most likely in an impaired function of the protein
[45].
HNF1A deletions have already been reported in the literature, more specifically of
exons 2 to 10, which is in agreement with the results found, and furthermore, this
deletion has been associated with MODY [46].
In summary, all of the approaches (Manta, TIDDIT, Mosdepth and CNVkit) identi-
fied a partial deletion in gene HNF1A which according to the annotations is of high
impact, suggesting a clinically relevant variant that aligns with deletions that have
previously been reported to cause MODY.[46].
While the detection of a clinically relevant deletion in the gene HNF1A in one sample
exemplifies how the presented set up can be used to detect clinically relevant SVs in
MODY genes, broader analyses reveal the limited concordance between TIDDIT and
Manta results across the sample set.
4.2
TIDDIT and Manta do not agree in Structural
Variant Identification
TIDDIT and Manta are Structural Variant calling tools that differ on their approaches
when it comes to SV identification; Manta uses a complex graph-based approach
that includes split reads and paired-end alignments, whereas TIDDIT relies more in
discordant read pairs and read-depth analysis [35][36]. This reflects on the variants
that the tools identify. Considering that both tools present their own filters which
allow to determine whether the variant might be an artifact or not, it is important to
highlight the need of only considering filtered variants. Taking that into consideration,
it can be seen how when it comes to duplications there is only one sample for which
both Manta and TIDDIT agree in predicting duplications, more specifically in the
gene HNF1B. There seems to be more agreement when it comes to deletions, as both
tools have identified deletions in 27 samples for the gene HNF4A, in 1 sample for the
geneGCK, and in 1 sample for the gene HNF1A.
42
==PAGE:53==
Regarding HNF1B duplication, the identified duplication was not predicted to be of
HIGH impact, and although there have been identified duplications that affect the
gene HNF1B, they have not been linked to MODY [47].
Although the results point at HNF1B as one of the genes where there could be
deletions in more samples, there is not concordance between TIDDIT and CNVKkit
when it comes to the identification of said deletion, which means that most of the
deletions where identified only by one of the tools, in this case Manta, and might be
false positives. The sample which presents a deletion in HNF1A has already been
adressed in section Section 4.1. Finally, regarding deletions in the gene HNF4A, it
has been reported that 1.9% of the mutations in the gene are caused by partial or
whole gene deletions, and they are rare events in familial MODY [48][49]. However,
the ones detected here by both TIDDIT and Manta are not of high impact, therefore
they are less likely to be clinically relevant.
Overall, the results highlight how Manta identifies more SVs than TIDDIT, but spe-
cially when it comes to duplications. Previous benchmark studies have shown how
Manta has a low precision in validated data sets, specially when identifying dupli-
cations.
This indicates that its output includes a large number of false positives,
which are sometimes repeated along unrelated samples, and therefore could be easily
removed [50].
The disagreement between Manta and TIDDIT results highlights the complexity that
clinicians face when relying on the output of a single tool for SV calling, and supports
the need for careful filtering and cross validation using multiple algorithms.
4.3
CNVkit, Mosdepth and other Variant Callers
can Validate Structural Variant Detection
Other approaches have been followed in this project to try to identify structural
variants. From the results seen in Section 3.2, it can be seen how they do not agree
with more sophisticated algorithms such as those implemented by Manta and TIDDIT.
It is important to highlight, that the approach followed in this study to make use of
the CNVkit data, is not the standard approach that one would use, given that CNVkit
is designed for studying Copy Number Variations. This means that CNVkit output
is useful when identifying whole gene variation, but more imprecise for studying
partial gene deletions or duplications. Other option would have been modifying the
parameters of CNVkit call to export copy number call into a VCF format, and setting
as target for the tools the MODY genes that are under study, nevertheless that
would imply modifying the current set up for the production pipeline and could have
consequences on processes that are part of the pipeline.
On the other hand, when it comes to Mosdepth, it is important to remember that
simply provides values of the depth of coverage of the regions it considers, in this case
regions that are 500 bp. Therefore, it is not the most reliable approach to identify
43
==PAGE:54==
SVs, specially when it comes to determine the breakends. Visualizing the depth of
coverage across the gene and adjacent regions can be useful to reassure if the identified
variant can actually be a variant, or if it just an artifact.
Other SV callers could be implemented in the production pipeline to generate some
more results which could be useful to identify deletions, duplications, or other type
of structural variants. Other widely used SV callers are Delly or Lumpy, and it could
be argued that they could be included in Sarek, nevertheless this would imply an
additional computational cost which may be unnecessary given tat overall, sort-read
sequencing data is more prone to result in False Positive calls from SVs. Although
there are many SV detection algorithms, up to date there is not a single one of them
which can call every SV type with both high recall and precision [51]. Additionally,
different benchmarking efforts show variability in sensitivity and precision among
tools when applied to different datasets. [52]
Taking this information into account, a promising tool could be Varlocirator, which
claims to control better the False Discovery Rate than other available tools. Var-
lociraptor would take the variants called from a previous discovery step, as could be
the variants identified by Manta or TIDDIT and classifies them providing addition-
ally a posterior probability for each of them, which can be used to control the False
Discovery Rate [53].
4.4
Resolution of Structural Variants is a Challenge
for Clinical Diagnosis
Structural Variants can range from large deletions and duplications, which has been
the main focus of this project, to even more complex genomic rearrangements. In a
clinical diagnosis context, it is certainly a challenge to provide a diagnose based on
the SV calls done on WGS data, given that there is not a single SV caller which has
a good performance for all of the variants, but also given the diﬀiculty of accurately
identifying the breakends of the Structural Variants [51]. Different tools will predict
the same variant with different breakends (as it was seen in Section 3.2), and it adds
a dimension of SV analysis, which is determining which is the threshold to determine
if two SVs are the same SV. The lack of a gold standard for SV identification in
sequencing data presents a major limitation when trying to use said data for clinical
diagnosis.
One of the approaches that can be taken with this regard, is using SVDB to create
a SV database with the outputs from Manta and TIDDIT. This structural variant
frequency databases can be queried for some of the already described SVs associated
with MODY outcome. This allows to compare the variants found in a sample with the
variants that are part of the database and annotate the VCF files using the frequency
with which that variant is present in the database. Following this approach, high
frequency variants can be removed, allowing the detection of rarer variants, as it
44
==PAGE:55==
seems to be the case with SVs in MODY genes [36].
Ideally the available SV callers would be tested in a validated data set where the SVs
that have already been identified associated with MODY diabetes would be available,
altogether with samples that do not present said SVs. The results would reveal if any
of the tools outperforms the other, and which one presents a better sensitivity and
recall when identifying this specific type of variants. The lack of a robust, publicly
available and well-characterized dataset with the conformed MODY-associated SVs
limits the ability to evaluate and compare SV callers. False positives pose a risk in
clinical diagnosis, leading to patients receiving the incorrect treatment, as for now
and given the lack of a gold standard in SV calling on short-sequencing data, the
diagnosis may need to rely on a panel of experts that given the results evaluate if the
patient has the disease or not.
The lack of a standardized SV calling approach and a validated reference dataset,
can lead clinicians to base treatment decisions on uncertain SV calls. This highlights
the need for better SV callers, reliable validation sets, as well as expert oversight in
clinical genomics.
4.5
Graph Reference Genomes are not a Feasible
Approach for Structural Variant Detection
The use of graph genomes for detecting SVs has great potential, as graph genomes are
more accurate representation of the genomic variation. However, the utilization of
graph genomes comes with a set of challenges that must be taken into account, partic-
ularly when it comes to usage of computational resources. The mapping process is a
big bottleneck which considerably limits the scalability of this approach, additionally
the large storage requirements, at least when working with human genomes, can be
problematic when working in environments with limited disk space.
Even though there is literature stating that variation graphs created with vg toolkit
can enhance the accuracy of SV detection compared to traditional methods [54], the
computational resources that it requires, specially when it comes to working with big
genomes as human genomes make out of it an unfeasible solution to implement and
address SV detection.
Additionally, one of the main advantages that this approach could have, would have
been the possibility of augmenting the graph, which means that the variation from the
GAM files can be embedded back in the graph. Nevertheless using vg augment when
the purpose is calling variants remains experimental, and it is strongly contraindicated
for calling SVs [42].
Future developments within vg toolkit and the general graph genome landscape may
make this approach more feasible in the future.
Improvements in the algorithm
eﬀiciency could reduce the computational time and make graph genomes a viable
45
==PAGE:56==
tool for an improved SV detection in clinical settings.
Nevertheless, as it stands,
the computational and storage demands, as well as the considerable turnaround time
constitute a barrier to its widespread adoption.

5
Conclusions
The main conclusions from this study are outlined bellow:
• Partial deletion of HNF1A gene in the sample E100079731_L01_77:
this study has identified a partial deletion in the HNF1A gene, involving the
deletion of exons 2 to 10. This deletion has already been reported before, and
is associated with MODY [46]. It affects both coding and non-coding regions,
and is likely associated with an impaired function of the HNF1A protein, which
results in a MODY outcome.
• TIDDIT and Manta disagree in SV identification: TIDDIT and Manta
show results that differ when it comes to SV identification, especially with dupli-
cations. While some tools agree on certain deletions, the discrepancies highlight
the need of a careful filtering of variants in order to avoid false positives.
• CNVkit and Mosdepth alone are insuﬀicient for precise SV detection:
approaches involving CNVkit and Mosdepth were used to identify SVs, but
given the nature of the data that these tools generate they are not the most
appropriate for SV detection.
Nevertheless their results, specially the plots
generated with the Mosdepth approach could be useful to validate the presence
of a deletion or a duplication.
• Additional SV callers and improved methods could shed some light
in the identification of SVs: including additional SV callers could help with
the validation of the results, which is highly important in a clinical setting.
However there is not a gold standard for SV calling in short-read sequencing
data, and including several SV callers would imply an additional cost not neces-
sarily providing a more reliable outcome. Developing a benchmark dataset and
standardized evaluation protocols could lead to the establishment of a reference
standard that would improve clinical reliability.
• False Positives present a challenge for clinical diagnosis: the potential
for false positives in SV identification implies a risk for its clinical usage, as it
may result in patients receiving inappropriate treatment. Including tools such
as Varlociraptor, or setting up a database with SVDB can help to overcome this
issue by filtering out false positives.
• Graph genomes are not a feasible approach for structural variant de-
tection: using graph genomes for structural variant detection involves high
computational demands, specially in the mapping process, which limit its scal-
ability and practical application in clinical settings.
These findings address the thesis goal of using WGS data to detect SV variants for
MODY diagnosis, emphasizing the inherent complexity of SV detection using short-
read sequencing data.
Particularly in the context of clinical diagnosis, where the
erroneous detection of a variant can have a direct impact on the patients’ health.
47
==PAGE:58==
Although this project focused on two specific SV types, the complexity of the problem
is broader given the great variety and diversity of SVs. Future research should focus
on refining the output of SV callers by identifying and removing false positives, as
well as on the establishment of benchmark datasets which will meet the demands of
clinical diagnosis. As methodologies improve, reliable and accurate SV detection may
become a reality, enabling reliable clinical diagnoses and personalized treatments.
