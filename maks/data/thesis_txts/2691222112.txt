Abstract
The integration of additive manufacturing into the metal injection molding process by the
3DIMS technology, serve as a potential solution to overcome some of the current limita-
tions in the technology, when a high degree of geometrical complexity at low production
volume. This paper investigate the capabilities and limitations in technology, based on
diﬀerent powder particle size distributions eﬀect on dimensional capability and quality
of the powder by use of monosized and bimodal distributions. The project in general
showed improved dimensional stability, less surface defects and higher achievable density
with increased concentration of ﬁne powder. However, many limitations were found in
current technology state, where high shear sensitivity to powder-binder separation was a
main cause of distortions and failures.
The project demonstrate the eﬀect of PPSD on feedstock characteristics in terms of rheo-
logical properties, homogeneity and initial density. Further, a main limitations in current
technology were concluded to be due to mold fabrication step, that transferred defect
further into the process chain and caused failure in the case of small dimensions and com-
plex structures. Its aimed to use as low process parameters as possible in order to avoid
deformations of the sacriﬁcial polymer mold. Its observed an increase in viscosity by the
increase in concentration of small powder due to increased inter-particle forces, however
the optimal feedstock composition should exhibit a large sacking eﬃciency, therefor the
concentration of small powder is directly linked to the quality of ﬁnal part as demonstrated
in the main ﬁnding of this project

Introduction
Injection molding of plastics is a widely used and of great impotence, within the area of
processing thermoplastic and thermosetting engineering products and consumer goods.
In order to improve the mechanical, physical and electrical properties of the products, the
process developed into ﬁlling the polymers with dispersed metallic or ceramic powders.
Through maximizing the content of powders, refereed to as the solid loading, this injection
molding technique evolved into the early stages of powder injection molding [51].
Powder Injection Molding (PIM) is a powder moulding technique that combines the shap-
ing capability of polymer injection moulding with the material ﬂexibility of powder met-
allurgy, to produce net-shaped or near net-shaped components in one process step. In
general the manufacturing technique is well suited for producing small, extremely complex
shaped parts with high accuracy [13]. The conventional PIM process typically consist of
four steps, where initially the powder of interest is mixed with a suitable organic multi-
component binder. The injectable mixture of powders and binder is termed the feedstock
and usually composed of a polymer to support and maintain the shape, and a wax for
rheological properties during the processing. during molding the feedstock is injected into
the mould cavity under heat and pressure, to form the green part with the desired shape.
Subsequently the moulded part undergoes debinding steps, where the binder-system is
extracted out, and the powder is sintered to get a near full density [66].
Figure 1.1: The traditional metal injection molding process chain [13].
One main subdivision of PIM is Metal Injection Moulding (MIM), that are an manu-
facturing process that has emerged during the 90’s as an alternative process route to
manufacture small-to-medium precision components. These components were previously
manufactured by other methods with higher cost or requirement for multiple process tech-
niques. Today MIM is an recognized manufacturing technique with a high capability for
producing cost-eﬀective, complex shaped parts of large volumes, using almost all types of
metals and inter-metallic compounds in a single process step, that are not possible with

where capable of high performance application, the technology was too complicated and
proven diﬃcult as an industrial tool. After 1990 the technology started to develop and

large potential [18].
Today MIM has started to replace a large part of traditional molding technologies and
serve as a competitor to small precision components. According to recent market forecast,

1.3
3D Integrating Manufacturing System
Additive Manufacturing (AM) refers to a family of manufacturing technologies that se-
quentially add units of standard input materials to enable fabrication of discrete physical
products. By this deﬁnition applied in three dimensions, results in the commonly applied
terminology of 3D printing.
The incredible freedom of additive manufacturing allows
you to make highly optimized components, that would be prohibitively expensive or even
impossible with other process routes.
Integrating AM into the MIM process chain can help overcome some of the mentioned
limitation of the MIM process, that the design and fabrication of the mold and tools itself,
typically at low volume demands of intricate parts are too costly. More complex geometries
and requirements for the molds like blind holes, overhangs, undercuts and helical features
are common, and therefor machining of the mold can be challenging and once the design
change, the rather expensive mold is useless. In the case of highly customized parts with
low demand as in the biomedical industry, a single or standardized transitional metal
mold would not be a cost eﬃcient solution, and the conventional MIM process is here not
applicable [53].
The combination of the two technologies has lead to a new innovative soft tooling sub-
division called 3D-Printing Integrated Manufacturing System (3DIMS) also refereed to as
free-form injection molding. This technology serves as a potential innovative process for
powder injection molding to overcome some of the current limitations. The AM technology
is used to fabricate a sacriﬁcial polymer mold containing an internal cavity for the MIM
process, allowing an even higher degree of freedom in terms of complexity. The 3DIMS
process is under investigation for using the technology, instead of milled metal molds, in
the case of highly customized parts and complex geometries that often need an overall low
production volume. A sacriﬁcial mold is typically produced by vat-photopolymerization
and placed inside the cavity in an injection molding plate, where feedstock containing
metal powder is injected and subsequently ejected together with the mold. The part is
released by dissolving the mold is an aqueous solution, where it subsequently is debinded
and sintered [24]. Figure 1.4 shows the principle of the 3DIMS-MIM technology.

Scope of the Project
The project is based on the danish company Addifab’s patent pending Sacriﬁcial Thermo-
plastic Injection Molding technology (STIM), ﬁrst time used for metal injection molding
in the innovative 3DIMS process chain [38], in order to improve the commercial readiness

due to the lower thermo-mechanical properties, in order to avoid deformations of the
cavity and ensure precision and a high replication. Therefor new metal feedstocks needs
to be produced that exhibit low viscosity in order to be injected at low pressure and
temperature.
This master thesis investigate the eﬀect of mixing diﬀerent powder particle size distribu-
tion of SS 316 L powder to obtain a monosized and bimodal distribution, for seeing the
eﬀect on the rheological properties and the resulting quality of the part through the whole
process chain, in terms of geometrical variation, defects, crack formation and achievable
physical properties. Especially, this project aims to investigate the dimensional limitations
and achievable complexity of the printed cavity and metal injected part. Furthermore,
the project focus on optimize the process by determining the quality of the feedstock
based on particle distribution and powder size eﬀect on dimensional stability and achiev-
able density, while demonstrating optimal measurement techniques to evaluate internal
cavities and complex structures.

2.1
Soft Tooling
Soft tooling is a sub category within Rapid Tooling (RP) and a technology using additive
manufacturing to produce tools, by a reference 3D model ﬁrst designed by a Computer-
Aided Design (CAD) system. Polymer resin molds produced by vat photopolymerization
is an example of soft tooling for creating temporary molds, suitable for a single production
run or small batch production, in contrast to hard tooling usually made of steel for larger
volume production [23]. The beneﬁts of using direct soft tooling in the injection molding
process is well documented in the literature for its much shorter turnaround time and
lower production costs in the initial development stages, where a signiﬁcant number of
prototype parts may be required to conduct design evaluations. Demonstrated in the work
of G. Tosello [12], shifting to injection molding based om AM fabrication of soft tools for
new molded product development, gave a reduction in production cost and lead time of
80-90 % and 60-70 % respectively. Figure 2.1 illustrate the principle in using direct soft
tooling for mold inserts in polymer injection molding.

VAT Photopolymerization
Photo polymerization is an additive manufacturing technology that makes use use of
radiation curable resins, called photo-polymers, to print 3D parts. Upon UV radiation the

photopolymer undergo a chemical transformation where the monomer molecules start to
form polymer chains, that subsequently start to cross link by creation of strong covalent
bonds. With the development of the Stereolithography (SL) technology discovered by
Charles Hull in the mid 1980’s, solid polymer patterns could be fabricated by exposing
them to a a light within the ultraviolet wave length, and curing one layer over the previous
one. This 3D system was shortly after marketed as as rapid prototyping machines, and a

Two of the most prominent and popular additive manufacturing technologies within pho-
topolymerization, that today is extensively used worldwide is called Stereo-lithography
(SLA) and Digital Light Processing (DLP). The principle of SLA and DLP processing
is illustrated schematically by the diagram in ﬁgure 2.2. In SLA a light source is used
to selectively cure a liquid surface in a resin vat containing the photopolymer monomer,
often with a few additives. The light activated polymerization proceeds point-to-line,
line-to-layer, than layer-to-layer until one layer is ﬁnished and the building platform or
resin tank moves one thickness of the layer, to start the polymerization of the next layer.
However, in the DLP process an integral image is transferred to the photopolymerisable
liquid surface, by exposing the light source through a patterned mask once only. The
ultra-fast light switching and integral projection allow the DLP printing time to be dra-
matically reduced. The key diﬀerence between DLP and SLA is therefor the type of curing
technology, where SLA use an UV laser to cure in a point by point fashion, and the DLP
uses a projector and a single layer is cured at the same time [67].
Both the SLA and DLP technology is well documented in the literature for their ability to
produce complex and high precision parts, with a resolution down to a few micrometer.

2.1.2
Freeform Injection Molding
Even though the integration of AM into injection molding serve as a time and cost eﬃcient
solution for low volume production, at the current technology state there still is some
limitations. Printed soft tools are still designed as a split mold, meaning the part will
be ejected mechanically and leaves some geometrical design constraints, where example
undercuts and inner channels should be avoided [10]. By using Freeform Injection Molding
(FIM) or 3DIMS as a sub process for conventional soft tooling to produce a sigle-use
closed-cavity mold insert, a new freedom is given when very complex parts are needed
in low volume. In general, the approach makes use of a block of solidiﬁed water soluble
resin with the internal cavity having the desired shape of the part. The entire mold will
be than be ejected after the injection process, and subsequently dissolved to give access
to the molded part [16].

2.1.3
Thermal & Mechanical Constraints
Some constrains related to the processing by soft tooling technology, is the limited proper-
ties of the resin mold compared to hard tooling, where the strength of a cured photopoly-
mer is up to many magnitude lower than that of conventional tool steel. At the current
technology state, much attention has been given to investigate how the limited mechanical
and thermal properties of the resin mold, may aﬀect the ﬁnal quality and replication of
the parts produced. In metal injection molding, normally operating at high pressure and
temperature, can cause deformation of the mold during the injection process, resulting
in defects and deviation of the nominal geometry for the ﬁnal part. Demonstrated in
the work Y. Zhang [22], deviation from the nominal geometry can occur due to thermo
mechanical stresses, leading to crack initiation and deformations of the mold. Deviation
in geometry can also happen from the printing process itself, therefor an important fac-
tor in obtaining high quality parts with a good surface ﬁnish is proper choice is additive
manufacturing process and resin.
Mold deformation due to high process parameters is further observed by S. Krizsma
[63], measuring the strain of SLA printed epoxy based mold inserts. In agreement with
Y. Zhang, he concluded that the deformation was caused mainly by thermal expansion
during the injection, and mechanical deformation from the cavity pressure. He further
concluded that the strain developed with each cycle until the inserts broke, creating a
larger deviation from the nominal geometry with lower replication from each cycle. The
results of strain, temperature and cavity pressure and diﬀerent cycles is illustrated in
ﬁgure 2.4.
Figure 2.4: Mold insert strain development caused by thermal and mechanical stresses during
the injection [63].
This means that even though the free form molding approach only consist of one cycle
pr. mold, deformation may occur when using too high injection parameters. In order
to produce high precision part with a good replication in metal injection molding, the
injected metal feedstock needs to poses characteristics that enables it to be injected at
relatively low pressure and temperature, in order to avoid distortions of the sacriﬁcial
mold that may be transferred to the internal part and further in the process chain.

2.2
The Process Chain of 3DIMS Metal Injection Molding
The integration of additive manufacturing into metal injection molding contains of six
fundamental steps composed of (1) 3D printing of the mold, (2) injection molding, (3)
demolding, (4) debinding, (5) sintering and (6) if necessary additional machining [10]. The
technology is labeled 3D Integrating Manufacturing system for Metal Injection Molding
(3DIMS-MIM), and the process chain is illustrated in the ﬂow chart of ﬁgure 2.5 below.
The individual process steps will be explained in depth in the following subsections.
Figure 2.5: The process chain for 3D Integrating Manufacturing System (3DIMS) [24].
The key diﬀerence from the conventional metal injection molding is the integration of
additive manufacturing for the production of the sacriﬁcial mold, that requires the ad-
ditional steps of mold fabrication and demolding in the process chain. The mold insert
has an internal cavity with the desired shape of the part and is mounted in the injection
molding machine, where feedstock containing metal powder is injected by a piston or
screw. Subsequently the water soluble mold is ejected from the machine, containing the
green part, that is released by dissolving the mold in an aqueous solution [24].
As mentioned, by using the principle of sacriﬁcial free-form molding, the geometrical
constrains of using a split mold is overcome to achieve a high degree of freedom for very
complex geometries. At the same time, the cost and time beneﬁts of small production
runs for customized parts is achieved, with non to little additional machining required, to
overcome some of the current limitations in the MIM technology.
2.2.1

an aqueous solution, without damaging or causing disturbances of the green part. Hence,
the material used for mold production is limited to water soluble resins.

heating from the screw, until the feedstock reach a molten state. Material equivalent to
one shot size is conveyed by the screw to the shot zone between the nozzle and screw tip.
One shot size should be enough to completely ﬁll the runner system and cavity with an
extra amount in front of the screw for cushion to prevent an incomplete ﬁll or voids. When
the injection temperature is reach and plastiﬁcation is completed, pressure is applied to

2.2.4
Debinding
After the injection molding and demolding step, the binder system becomes a disposable
product and therefor it must be removed from the green part before sintering. The method
used for debinding has a strong inﬂuence on the shape retention, uniformity of shrinkage
and ﬁnal mechanical properties of the samples, hence its important to understand the
parameters aﬀecting the debinding. In MIM, solvent debinding where the primary binder
is removed by a solvent dissolution, with additional thermal debinding for removing the
backbone polymer and surfactants without cracking or degrading the molded part, is the
most widely used technique in the industry and the one used in this particular project
[52].
The demolded part is submerged into an organic solvent that dissolves the primary binder
material. As the binder is removed, no diﬀusion bonding takes place between the powder
particles, as the solvent debinding usually is carried out far below the activation energy
for diﬀusion of stainless steels. Therefor the application of this method required that the
binder consist of materials that can be dissolved in the liquid at relatively low temper-
atures, such that the network of interconnected porosity of the powder is formed while
immersed in the solvent. The part is submerged in the solvent for a duration long enough
to dissolve all the primary binder, than subsequently removed and dried [13].
Thermal debinding is used as the second debinding step, where the polymer that are the
backbone, together with interparticle friction holds the particle together and maintain
the shape. The polymer is removed thermally at moderate-to-high temperatures where
it evaporates. At this temperature interparticle diﬀusion starting to occur, just enough
to hold the metal powder particles together prior to the sintering [52]. After this step
most of the binder should be removed, and only the inter-particle friction and some bond
formation hold whats called the brown part together, meaning that volume shrinkage and
large porosity is formed by the interconnected channels, that once were occupied by the
binder matrix as observed by A. Basso [70].
Figure 2.10: Scanning electron micrographs show the development of interconnected pores

done to improve and accelerate the removal of binder, and to investigate the parameters

2.2.5
Sintering
Sintering is a heating process carried out below the melting temperature of the metal in a
protective atmosphere, where the brown part that consist of separate powder particles is
consolidated to provide the necessary strength of the ﬁnished product. Sintering rely on
the driving force of thermodynamics, where the metal powder will lower its surface free
energy by the formation of interparticle bonds for reducing the surface area. The growth of
the bonding signiﬁcantly change the porous structure resulting in improvement in many
properties such as strength, ductility, corrosion resistance, conductivity and magnetic
permeability.

center of spheres begin to get closer and shrinkage is observed. Here bulk transport mech-

2.2.6
Interrelation Between Process Steps
As described, the 3DIMS-MIM process chain is complicated with many parameters that
are interrelated across the whole process chain in a smaller or larger degree. As it will be
clear from the results of this thesis, defects or errors at any stage may aﬀect the whole
process chain, but might only be visible as late as after sintering. As mentioned, A. Basso
[70] saw how the parameters from demolding, debinding and sintering strengthened or
counter-acted each other, aﬀecting dimensions and the degree of replication, but many
other authors has done similar experiments. As an example, in the work of A. Askari
[4] he observed how insuﬃcient feedstock composition lead to defect during the injection
that causing swelling during debinding and inhomogenity in the sintered part. He further
concluded that parameters such as debinding and sintering temperature and time had a
large inﬂuence on one another for the obtainable physical and mechanical properties of
the ﬁnal part. However, all parameters are highly aﬀected by geometry of the part and
material characteristics of the feedstock, therefor general guidelines is diﬃcult to obtain.
In the literature wee see a large interest in obtaining knowledge of the eﬀect of each
process parameters across the whole process chain, and this project aims to contribute to
this knowledge.

2.3
Feedstock Characterization
Feedstocks for MIM are fundamentally composed of a ﬁne metal powder and a complex
binder system. The shape, size, composition and distribution of the selected metal powder
is of great interest and plays a crucial role in controlling defects and distortions of the
ﬁnal component. The binder is designed to provide ﬂow characteristics during the injec-
tion, and structural rigidity to the resulting solid part during the subsequent demolding,
debinding and sintering processes. Although there are many similarities between ﬁlling
and ﬂow of the polymer injection and metal injection process, unique diﬀerences due to
the ﬁlling of metal powder exist, because of diﬀerences in physical, thermal and rheolog-
ical properties of the feedstock, being closer to that of the metal rather than binder for
modern MIM feedstocks [52].
For a complicated soft tooling process as 3DIMS, where a sacriﬁcial mold is used with
poorer material properties compared to tool steels, the feedstock characteristics is of
signiﬁcant impotence. The strength of the photopolymer produced by AM is up to 4
degree lower than the one used for tool steels, and have poorer thermodynamic properties.
The feedstock for MIM has higher viscosity compared to common material for injection
molding, thus the increased pressure applied during the injection can cause defects and
deformation due to the shear stresses on the mold walls, because of the lower stiﬀness of
the polymeric materials. Therefor even though the resin is able to withstand a certain
level of pressure, the feedstock characteristics must be tailored in a way to produce high
quality parts with the lowest possible pressure applied, hence have a low viscosity [3]. The
next subsections aims to explain how diﬀerent important feedstock parameters aﬀect the
characteristics of the feedstock, with a perspective to the whole process chain.
2.3.1
Solid Loading
The Solid Loading (SL) also referred to as the powder loading is the volume fraction
of solid powder, to the total volume fraction of powder-binder in the feedstock. In the
literature you usually see a solid loading ranges between 50 to 75 % depending on the
process and feedstock composition, although commercial formulations of more than 80
% has been reported [25].In general for MIM the solid loading is expected to be high,
since this is a critical parameter aﬀecting the achievable density, mechanical properties
and quality of the ﬁnal part. A feedstock composition of too low solid loading may result
in an unacceptable excess of binder, that will aﬀect the additional steps in the process
chain. The excess of binder can separate from the powder during the injection process,
leading to ﬂashing or inhomogeneity in the green part. More importantly, a large excess
of binder can cause slumping during the debinding, as the particles are not held probably
in place as the binder is removed [31].
A high solid loading results in smaller compact volume shrinkage and easier dimensional
tolerance control, important for producing delicate complex parts. This was demonstrated
by M. Dropman [40] using ultra high solid loading of coarse spherical Ni-base alloy powder.
The minimum content of binder resulted in a minimum density gradient along the axis of
ﬂow in the green part, with the result of reduced shrinkage, less distortions, reduced level

Binder Systems
Binders are usually multi-component polymer systems, responsible for improving ﬂow-
ability of the component during the injection process and provide the necessary strength
to retain the shape after molding. The binder-system has an inﬂuence on the particle
distribution, shaping process, dimensional tolerances prior to the sintering, therefor proper
choice of binder and additives is of huge important to obtain high quality parts [25].
Many diﬀerent binder system exist today, all tailored to speciﬁc metal powder character-
istics and classiﬁed based on the main component. However, the 3DIMS-MIM process is
limited by the demolding process in terms of binder system, due to that the binder cannot
be water soluble in order to ensure the green body is retained during the process. For
this particular project a wax based binder system is used and will be elaborated. The use
of Paraﬃn wax (PW) is the most commonly used binder system in MIM responsible for
the ﬂowability of the feedstock, due to their low molecular weight, thermoplastic nature
and low melting point [52]. Paraﬃn wax is a proven binder and mentioned extensively
in literature for diﬀerent metal feedstocks, as in the work of M. Zaky [41] using diﬀerent
compositions of paraﬃn wax based binder systems to create homogeneous low-viscosity
high-speed steel feedstocks below 10 Pa-s, also eﬃcient for subsequently chemical extrac-
tion prior to the molding.

Mixing and Homogeneity
The MIM cycle start with mixing to ensure each particle is coated with the selected
binder system, in order to break down agglomeration and yield a uniform feedstock with
no powder-binder segregation. Homogeneous mixing of the feedstock is crucial for the
quality of the ﬁnal part, since any inhomogenity such as bubbles, binder pockets and
powder segregation will be carried over to the additional process steps. An heterogeneous
feedstock can result in density gradients within the molded part, that may cause distortion.
Various factors such as particle size distribution, shape and binder properties, along with
mixing temperature and time aﬀect the homogeneity [52].
Today various types of shear mixers are available, that break down the clusters of particle
under the inﬂuence of shear action, and binder is dispersed among the interparticle pores.
The mixing process is carried out at elevated temperatures according to the melting point
of the binder-system and the metal powder is gradually added, since the temperature
decreases sharply due to high heat capacity of the powder.

Phase Separation
Phase-separation also referred to as powder-binder segregation is a result of the high
shear stress gradient, equally producing an high shear rate gradient according to equation
5 and 6. This gradient will create a separation interface illustrated in ﬁgure 2.29 where
the particles will undergo local rotations and get pushed towards the wall or central region
respectively. As a result, since the maximum shear rate in reality is located asymmet-
rically within the runner and cavity, the powder concentration may have a non-uniform
distribution, having a lower concentration near the mold wall and higher in the center or
the opposite way around.
Figure 2.29: Schematic illustration of shear induced migration of powder [14]
Powder-binder segregation is a well known phenomenon and a common defect in MIM
mainly generated by improper mixing, gravity and a high pressure gradient, due to the
diﬀerent densities associated with metallic powders and thermoplastic binder. In order to
realize homogeneous parts, these must be minimized to prevent distortions, cracks, voids,
warping and anisotropic shrinkage during sintering. In the work of J. Gaplin [28], he
observed a density gradient across the produced sample that lead to an uneven shrinkage
and distortion after sintering. However, he concluded that numerical models is an eﬃcient
way to predict the shrinkage, mechanical properties and density of the ﬁnal part in order
to optimize the parameters across the whole process chain. Since the process parameters
is highly aﬀected by the geometry of cavity and feedstock characteristics, the use of
numerical simulations for predicting defects will be addressed in section 3.

2.6
Processing
Injection molding of plastic and the eﬀect of diﬀerent process condition is well described in
the literature with a huge amount of research. On the contrary for metal injection molding,
only a few companies are specialised, resulting in general know how is not well spread,
combined with limited knowledge in the literature. The process parameters depends on
the characteristics of the feedstock, constraints and geometry of the cavity, therefor the
reader should be aware the following sections will present generalized correlations and
guidelines observed from the literature. Note that all process parameters is interrelates in
a smaller or larger degree across the whole process chain. Controlling and optimization
of parameters is important to realize a high quality part with a minimum of porosity and
geometrical deviations.
Based on the work of A. Amin [5] aiming to map the correlation by varying diﬀerent
process parameters for optimization of the green body density of a stainless steel feedstock,
he concluded that the injection pressure to be one of the parameters having the largest
inﬂuence on the quality of the green part , while the injection temperature to have the
smallest eﬀect, based on Tanguichi methods. On the contrary, A. Islam [6] concluded
that either by increasing process temperature or holding pressure, the density and tensile
strength of both the green part and the sintered increased signiﬁcantly. Based on these
observation it was decided to investigate the eﬀect of both temperature and injection
pressure for this project.

Numerical Simulation
A computer simulation can be described as a numerical computation of multiple solutions
to physical and mathematical models with their related boundary condition where any
analytical solution is not available or too complex [42]. Many simulation tools are com-
mercially available today for simulate and model the injection molding process, and their
application is extensively documented in the literature and industry. However, most of the
software target groups are the polymer industry, and only limited articles are published
validating the use of simulation tool for the low-pressure PIM process and non were found
using a soft tooling approach combined with the PIM.
Nevertheless, a correlation between the ﬁlling and packing stages in the numerical simu-
lation and what was observed experimentally was recently demonstrated by M. Azzouni
[49]. Simulating the mold ﬁlling stage for a 17-4PH stainless steel feedstock, he concluded
a good agreement between injected length, velocity of the ﬂow front, ﬁlling time and the
powder segregation eﬀect for simple geometries. Further he observed that the pressure
proﬁle having a relative diﬀerence of less than 8.3 % compared to what was experimental
observed during the ﬁlling.
For this particular project simulations was carried out using a combination of Autodesk
Moldﬂow Synergy 2019 (AMS) and Autodesk Moldﬂow Data Fitting (AMDF). The AMS
software simulation process exploits the Finite Diﬀerence Method (FDM) and the Final
Element Method (FEM) for numerically solve a system of partial diﬀerential equations
widely used in the engineering disciplines. This method divide the model into small ele-
ments of ﬁnite size usually refereed to ass the mesh, where the partial diﬀerential equations
can be solved in these small domains, giving the sum of a numerical approximation for a
solution of the entire model [42]
The simulation was carried out in order to investigate the mold ﬁlling and packing stage
of the MIM process prior to the experimental work to predict the ﬂow behavior of the
feedstock during the ﬁlling, but also to give an indication of the quality of the part,
here powder segregation, weld lines, air traps and density distribution. Moreover the the
simulation should serve as a guideline for the experimental set-up in terms of recommended
process parameters used for the injection of the diﬀerent parts, in order to save time and
material, while validate the correlation between the simulation and the actual injection
process in the 3DIMS-MIM process chain. However, the reader should note that this work
is not simulation based, and therefor most attention is given to the experimental part of
the project.

3.6
Discussion and Limitations
The powder injection molding process tap was ﬁrst introduced into the moldﬂow software
in 2017 and it is clear that it needs further development, since it do not come with the
same options of tailoring boundary conditions as the thermo plastic process, therefor also
becomes more prone to deviations.
This was pointed out in the work of M. Marhöfer [44] experiencing deviations of pressure
diﬀerences and injection time up to 20 % and 45 % respectively. In general the simulation
predicts lower values and was belived to be due the limited suitability of the powder
injection molding since moldﬂow is oﬃcially made for plastic. In general its observed that
the software underestimate ﬁlling time and pressure, also supported by this study.
A further limitation is that the software do not oﬀer tailored mold material, but only
possible to use default tool steel. Therefor in order to import the thermal and mechanical
properties of the polymer molds, it was necessary to ﬁrst change to the thermoplastic
conﬁguration, than switch back to powder conﬁguration. There were a diﬀerence in the
result, however not as signiﬁcant as what would be expected, and from the analysis log
it was clear that errors uncounted during the diﬀerent time steps. This might also be an
explanation of the deviations observed for this particular project.
Further it should be noted that many material properties imported to the software, here
PVT behavior and thermodynamics are based on feedstocks in the literature, not having
the same composition, but evaluated more accurate than the default value.
Therefor
thermo mechanical properties and shrinkage observed may not be accurate, but gives an
indication on the behavior, and is used to explain some of the results encountered.
In general the use of simulation was of great signiﬁcant to explain behavior observed
through the whole process chain, and was of great signiﬁcant to the discussion of this
particular project.
The deviation from the experiment is generally higher than what
was observed in numerical experiment regarding metal injection molding, but within an
acceptable range.

The density of each feedstock composition was measured by a Satorius YDK 01 measure-

method complies with the ASTM B311-17 standard test method for density measurement
in powder metallurgy. The Archimedes principle is applied, where the feedstock is im-
mersed in liquid and exposed to the force of buoyancy. The value of this force is the
same as the weight of the liquid displaced by the volume of the feedstock. The density
of distilled water at a temperature of 24 ◦C was acquired from a standard table given in
the documentation. The weight of diﬀerent random selected pierces of solidiﬁed feedstock
across each batch were selected and the weight in air and water measured. The density

4.4
Viscosity Measurements
The rheological investigations of the samples was done using a Anton Paar Modular
Compact Rheometer of the type H-PTD200. The feedstock was placed between a plate
and rotational disc, where frequency test was carried out between 0.1 to 100 Hz to calculate
the complex viscosity at a non-destructive environment. To ensure the non-destructive
environment, a constant shear of approximately 0.1 % was determined from previously
running an amplitude sweep test, where the feedstock is oscillating at constant frequency
while increasing the shear systematically. The amplitude sweep test is done to ensure
the selected constant shear is within the limit of the linear viscoelastic region (LVE) [3].
The viscosity test was ﬁrst carried out at a constant temperature of 120 ◦C equal to the
approximated injection temperature, than for investigating the eﬀect of temperature on
the rheological properties at a interval of 100 to 140 ◦C.

Mold Dissolution
The molds containing the injected feedstock were dissolved in an alkaline solution of pH
14, containing 40 grams of sodium hydroxide per 1 liter of distilled water. The molds were
submerged in the solution at 50 ◦C with an magnetic stirrer circulating at 120 rpm. The
process were tried replicated, but due to the time frame sometimes exceeding 36 hours,
constant supervision was not possible. The molds were in the solution until it was possible
to access the injected parts. Based on the work of A. Basso [70] the cavity should be well
replicated due to the non-soluble binder system and not damaged during the dissolution.
However, expansion of the mold may have occur [10] as described in section 2.2.3.
4.8
Debinding
The green body was debinded in two steps using chemical and thermal debinding. The
chemical debinding was carried out by submerging the part in n-heptane for 5 hours at
50 ◦C, with a magnetic stirrer rotating at 100 RPM to remove the paraﬃn wax. Even
though it is well described in literature how the debinding rate is increased by increasing
temperature, 50 ◦was chosen based on the experience of A. Basso [70], observing crack
formation at 60 ◦C possibly due to water molecules diﬀusing into the LDPE causing
expansion, also described in the work of M. Omar [50]. It was further determined that
the curve for the weight loss is more or less horizontal after 5 hours indicating the process
is completed.

Sintering was done in a tube furnace at 1300 ◦C for 4 hours. The isothermal sintering
process was chosen based based on J. Choi experiment [34], achieving a higher density
compared to the heat-up sintering process. However, it was not possible to control air
pressure or atmosphere during the sintering, and the furnace available were not able to
stabilize at more than 1240 ◦C, that may be the reason for an incomplete process with
server oxidation that will be discussed further in 5. In general many uncertainties raised
during the sintering, since the limited equipment available were not designed to operate
at the wanted parameters and a stable and controlled process were not possible.
In prior projects the sintering was done by the danish company Sintax, specialized in
the sintering process and used in the work of A. Basso [70] for a complete sintering,
where dense samples were achieved. However, this cooperation was not possible for this
particular project.

5.1.4
Summary of Feedstock Characteristics
This section aims to give a brief summary of the main ﬁndings and major point made to
the investigation regarding material characteristics based on diﬀerent PPSD.
• Smaller powder size is more diﬃcult to mix due to the larger surface area and
inter-particle interaction, based on the deviation in density of samples with a high
concentration of ﬁne powder. Longer mixing time or higher temperature may be
required to obtain the same homogeneity as for larger values of PPSD.
• White residual becomes visible on the surface upon cooling, and is believed to be
binder diﬀusing. It is assumed based on the density measurement to have little
inﬂuence on the homogeneity.
• The addition of PPSD 5 µm to 50 µm powder increased the distribution slope
parameter and therefor implies a narrower PPSD. The addition increases a new
peak towards the smaller particles causing a typical bimodal PPSD.
• The addition of PPSD 5 µpowder increases the viscosity due to the increased surface
area and present of collidal particles. All feedstock exhibit pseudoplastic behavior,
but improved viscosity is not obtained by a bimodal PPSD.
• The ﬂow behavior index decreases with the addition of 5 µm powder, meaning the
feedstock is more shear sensitive, and the viscosity will decrease faster with increased
shear.
• The viscosity will decrease with increasing temperature. However the ﬂow behavior
index seems to decrease making it less shear sensitive. It should be noted that an
increase in viscosity is observed at high shear, possibly due to agglomeration and
powder-binder separation.
• In general the feedstocks seems to be very sensitive to powder-binder separation
and agglomeration even at relatively low shear-rates, possibly due to the very low
viscosity. The eﬀect of powder-binder separation seems to be reinforced by increased
temperatures.

Summary of Main ﬁndings
This section aims to give a brief summary of the main ﬁndings and major point made to
the investigation regarding the complexity, replication and accuracy of process based on
pillar-spiral structure.
• A pattern with increased injection pressure with increased viscosity of the feedstock
is observed from the tracked process parameters.
• Only small a small pressure variance is observed between high and low viscosity
feedstock due to the high shear rate based on the simulation.
• An increased fragility with increasing concentration of small powder is observed. It
is believed to be cost by combination increased shear and powder-binder separation
and stacking eﬃciency resulting in a higher actual solid loading, making the binder
matrix suﬃcient to from a strong absorbed layer.
• The spiral broke due to a combination of phase-separation, heterogeneous shrinkage
and pressure gradients in the cavity. The major eﬀect is being evaluated to be the
initial deformed cavity that strengthened these eﬀects.
• Mold deformation were observed for the high viscosity SS 0 feedstock composition
due to shear stresses and increased pressure that deformed the mold during the
injection.
• All pillars broke after debinding, and is beloved to be caused mainly by phase-
separation. Released residual stresses from the shrinkage may also have eﬀected the
failure.
• The process were not able to produce delicate or complex structure not attached to
a main base for any of the feedstock compositions. Larger diameters has to be tested
for the pillar and spiral structure to test the dimensional and complex capabilities.
• All feedstocks seems to be very sensitive to powder-binder agglomeration at high
shear rates. The main cause of failure might be linked to insuﬃcient feedstock com-
position. Other material composition has to be tested in order to ﬁnally conclude
on dimensional limitations.

Experiment on Increased Processing Parameters
Based on the work of S. Yang [69], A. Islam [6] and K. Jamalunin [57] the moldability of
the feedstock can be improved, at the same time reducing the number of defect and achieve
higher ﬁnal density by increasing temperature, speed, injection and packing pressure. This
experiment therefor aims to increase the injection temperature and pressure to 140 ◦C
and 150 bars respectively, in order to compare the results with the previous experiment
in terms of replication, defects, dimensional stability and surface defects.
5.5.1
Injection with High Parameters
during the injection is was noticed how all mold and mold inserts were surviving fewer
injection cycles before failure.
It was discussed whether this could be a result of the
increased mechanical end thermal stresses that decreased the service life of the soft tools.
However, it should be noted that due to the increased pressure the inserts and mold were
more attached to the mold base after the injection, therefor was not possible to determine
if the failure was caused by rough handling when removing the mold inserts or due to the
increased parameters.

6
Conclusion
Based on the project scope of powder particle distributions eﬀect on dimensions and
quality across the 3DIMS process chain, the following main conclusion has been.
1. The mixing of 50 µm and 5 µPPSD exhibited a typical bimodal distribution. How-
ever, there were not obtained any rheological beneﬁts and the increase in viscos-
ity were only dependent on concentration of ﬁne powder. All feedstock exhibited
pseudoplastic behavior, but the shear sensitivity of increased with increasing con-
centration of ﬁne powder. The required pressure in order to inject the feedstocks
increased with increasing viscosity. The feedstock in general seemed to be sensitive
to powder-binder separation and agglomeration at relatively low shear, giving an
early conclusion of insuﬃcient feedstock composition.
2. Based on the ease of use, time and cost, the approach of using silicone replicate
to imitate the internal structure of the cavity, combined with optical DeMeet mea-
surement, were evaluated the optimal measurement technique for mold validation
in the 3DIMS process chain. The DLP printer showed a low precision and repli-
cation in printing helix structures, possibly due to embedded resin caused by an
insuﬃcient cleaning process. The mold fabrication were concluded a limitation in
the 3DIMS process chain for helix structures, since the deviation will be transferred
to the additional process steps.
3. The process were not able to produce complex helix structure or delicate pillar
structures. There were formed a pattern with increasing fragility with increasing
concentration of small powder, that was concluded to be due to the need of adjusting
the solid loading. The main cause of failure were, evaluated to be powder-binder
separation due to high shear rates, combined with an initially deformed cavity. It
was evaluated that the technology is not able to produce parts down too a few
millimeter, not attached to a main base, due to the resulting high shear rates and
required pressure in narrow channels.
4. Large distortions occurred doing debinding of the cylinders. There were formed
a pattern with increasingly dimensional stability with increase in concentration of
small powder, due to the improved stacking eﬃciency. In general there were formed
a pattern with higher density and dimensional stability with increased concentration
of small powder. The highest density was achieved with the SS 0 composition, also
exhibited the highest dimensional stability with less shrinkage, however exhibited
molding diﬃculty due to high viscosity. The SS 50 and SS 75 showed sign of bi-
modal beneﬁts in terms of density, but had less dimensional stability. The highest
performing feedstock was concluded to be the monosized SS 0 composition.
5. Increasing the injection temperature and pressure worsened the surface defect and
distortions doing debinding, with larger deviations in shrinkage giving less repli-
cation through the process chain. A lower density was observed for the green and
brown body, but improve density of the sintered. As sintering was eﬀected by exces-
sive oxidation, the increased process parameters is not concluded an improvement.
