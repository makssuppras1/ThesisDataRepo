ABSTRACT  
Co-digestion of cattle manure with various co-substrates is one of the most popular and 
outstanding approaches for biogas production especially in countries like Denmark due to the 
abundant availability of manure as a primary substrate. Characteristics of co-substrate plays a 
prime role in any biogas plant operation as it is one of the crucial factors that would lead to 
process inhibition. Knowing the effect of co-substrate well before inducing it in the process 
becomes extremely important for investors of full scale biogas plants. Mathematical modeling 
comes into picture at this very stage which helps in predicting the results and develops 
preparedness for handling unanticipated circumstances thereby maintaining process stability. 
With that background, this study uses the Comprehensive Anaerobic Digestion Model 
developed for biogas process in manure digesters to predict the behavior and effect of various 
co-substrates such as Gelatine, glucose, GTO (glycerol tri-oleate) and wheat straw in 
counteracting ammonia inhibition. Predicted results from the modeling were implemented in 5 
lab scale CSTRs to check their compliances.  
CSTR experiments were carried out at thermophilic conditions (53±1 degree) with mono-
digestion from Day 1 to Day 45 (Period 1 or P1) and co-digestion from Day 46 to Day 70 
(Period 2 or P2) with R1 (Gelatine alone with OLR 2.88gVS/L-feed), R2 and R3 
(Gelatine+glucose with OLR 3.88gVS/L, and 4.88gVS/L respectively), and R4 and R5 
(Gelatine+GTO with OLR 3.88gVS/L-feed & 4.88gVS/L-feed respectively). HRT was 
maintained at 15 days. An average CH4 yield of 166mL-CH4/(gVS-added) was observed during 
P1 which showed a significant improvement yields during P2 (152, 204, 184, 336, 352mL-
CH4/gVS-added) in R1 to R5 respectively. Organic loading rate played a prime role in 
depicting the performance of co-digestion with respect to the type of co-substrate added. 
Reactor operating with high OLR depicted a better CH4 yield in case of GTO addition, whereas 
the one with low OLR resulted in better CH4 yield in case of glucose addition. Co-substrate 
utilization efficiency was better in R2 and R4 (66.7% & 62.7%) than in R3 and R5 (56.5% & 
50.8%); whereas only 11.8% was observed in R1. Thus, contradicting behavior was observed 
when the performance assessment was done with respect to the type substrate overloading and 
CH4 yield. However, co-digestion with single co-substrate did not seem efficient throughout 
the operational period. VFA concentrations (propionate) and total ammonia nitrogen in all 
reactors showed increase in trend until the end of Day 69 showing probable inhibition threats. 
However, model results predicted entirely opposite trend by depicting increased acetate 
concentration over propionate which is contradicting to the obtained results. Ultimately, 
addition of secondary co-substrate successfully resulted in improved CH4 yields in R2 to R5 
compared to R1, thus illustrating synergistic effects.

Executive Summary 
Main aim of this experiment was to assess various co-substrate compositions and examine their 
effect on ammonia inhibition in manure based digesters through co-digestion. The study also 
attempted to find the synergy in manure co-digestion process. This was carried out in two main 
pathways. First way used the comprehensive anaerobic digestion model developed by DTU, 
especially for manure co-digestion process to predict the results which would be further 
implemented in the laboratory. The second way was to carry out laboratory CSTR experiments 
to test the predicted model results in reality, mainly to check whether the model results and 
experimental results complement each other.   
Results from the first scenario were predicted by performing simulations of manure co-
digestion with co-substrates such as Gelatine, GTO, glucose and wheat straw by fixing the total 
amount of added co-substrate to 40gVS/L by increasing at constant regular interval. Simulation 
results predicted that highest methane yield was achieved when manure was co-digested with 
GTO alone when compared to glucose, Gelatine and wheat straw respectively. Significant high 
VFA concentration was observed in manure-Gelatine combination from which it was 
concluded that protein rich substrates have a higher tendency to cause inhibition due to VFA 
accumulation at increased OLRs, whereas substrates with higher lipid content such as GTO 
will result in better methane yield.  
Results from the second scenario were predicted by performing co-digestion of manure with 
Gelatine as a primary co-substrate, GTO, glucose and wheat straw as secondary co-substrates 
at both low and high amounts of total co-substrate addition respectively. Gelatine-GTO 
combination in both cases resulted in better yield compared to other combinations. However, 
increase in propionate concentration was observed that exceeded acetate concentration in all 
co-substrates combination with Gelatine. From this it was evident that, substrate overloading 
resulted in VFA accumulation that subsequently increased propionate concentrations 
predicting threats of inhibition.  
In the third scenario, results of simulation showed the behavior of cattle manure co-digested 
with fixed concentration of Gelatine i.e. 15, 20 & 30 g/L-feed and varying the concentrations 
of other co-substrates up to 60g/L. With the increase in Gelatine concentration, increased 
ammonia and VFA levels in the system was observed in spite of achieving a higher CH4 yield. 
Therefore it was sensible enough to select the combinations with lower Gelatine addition yet 
rendering best results. Addition of secondary co-substrates imparted a lower total ammonia 
levels (<3g-N/L) in the system than co-digestion with Gelatine alone; thus proving that 
secondary co-substrates have a clear tendency to attenuate prospective inhibition from Gelatine 
addition. Based on this prediction, various combinations were selected with having added 
glucose and GTO concentrations both at 1:1 and 1:2 proportions with Gelatine (15g/L) to be 
implemented in lab scale CSTRs.  
Fourth scenario tested the predicted model results in the laboratory through CSTR experiments 
conducted for 70 days in 2 periods. First period underwent a mono-digestion process where

only cattle manure was fed until the end of Day 45 An average CH4 yield of 166mL- CH4/(gVS-
added) with 68.6% CH4 in biogas and an average biogas yield of 7L-Biogas/(L-feed) were 
achieved throughout the period that was within the normal range of production expected from 
cattle manure. Stable ammonia levels and declining VFA levels in the system ensured a stable 
AD process. During co-digestion (Day 46 – Day 70), overall methane production was improved 
considerably in all reactors due to the co-digestion effect. However, OLR and co-substrate 
addition certainly had an impact on the obtained results. Addition of Gelatine alone did not 
show a noticeable improvement in performance during co-digestion compared to mono-
digestion due to very less efficiency in co-substrate utilization (11.8%). In addition to this, R1 
resulted in increased concentration of propionate towards the end of this period.  
Among the two reactors operated with Gelatine-glucose combination, the one with lower OLR 
resulted in better methane yield than the other with 74% CH4 in biogas. But R3 resulted in only 
64% CH4 in biogas. This was estimated to be due to the large share of VS in carbohydrates 
would get converted into 50% CH4, and 50% CO2 (Angelidaki and Bastone, 2010) in spite of 
higher amounts of glucose addition. Co-substrates utilization efficiency was highest in R2 
(66.7%) which is so far the best among all 5 reactors in operation. However, increased 
concentration of propionate levels in R2 are imposing inhibition threats with further operation.  
In the case of reactors operated with Gelatine-GTO combination better methane yield and 
production was observed in R5 with higher OLR unlike the previous case. But, co-substrates 
utilization efficiency is very less (50.8%) when compared with R4 (62.7%). If we compare the 
performances based on co-substrate utilization efficiency and obtained CH4 yield, 
contradicting opinion will arise while inferring about factors causing inhibition to a stable 
digestion process. Considering all parameters that were examined during that study, it can be 
concluded that R2 and R4 operating at lower OLR have depicted stable digestion process.  
By the end of sampling period, persistent signs of increased total ammonia and the total VFA 
in the system is observed with extreme high concentrations of propionate over acetates in all 
reactors. Yet, the process did not cease and gas production was consistent. Even though this 
predicts a sign of inhibition, lower total ammonia levels (<2.8g-N/L) in the system might be 
allowing the process to continue in spite of having increased propionate levels.  Even if we 
assume that a steady state has been achieved in gas production from Day 53 until the end of 
Day 70, increase in VFA and ammonia levels clearly indicated that, reactors have not reached 
the steady state yet. Due to time limitations, data collection for this project was stopped. At this 
stage, we observe a clear indication of improvement in performances achieved in each 
individual reactor during P2 compared to P1. It is anticipated that, providing more time for 
acclimatization of substrates in each reactor would allow them to depict clear results about their 
respective performances. Further examination would probably lead to a better conclusion 
during performance evaluations.  
Ultimately when the model predictions were compared with experimental results, a clear 
contradicting trend was observed only in the case of VFA concentration. Increased propionate 
levels over acetates in all reactors as resulted from the experiments showed a complete opposite

remarks either about predicted results from the model or while considering additional data for

1. BACKGROUND AND INTRODUCTION 
Optimistic energy targets have been set by the Danish government in February 2011 in order 
to create a fossil free energy system in Denmark by 2050 (The Danish Energy Agency). With 
prior consideration of reaching more than 35% energy share from the renewables within the 
year 2020, Denmark is willing to achieve more than half of this share from Biomass alone. A 
major share in this is expected to come from centralized biogas plants in Denmark. The 
production of biogas from biomass resources is estimated to be approximately 30PJ/year, 80% 
of which is comprised of manure (Irini Angelidaki and Ellegaard 2002), leading to the 
valorisation of various types of biomass including organic wastes thus depicting promising 
roles that bioenergy would play in the near future.  
Livestock manure has been deployed as one of the consistent substrates in large scale AD for 
biogas production in Danish scenario due to its multiple advantages such as energy generation 
in the form of Biogas. This is achieved by treating approximately 1.5 million tonne/year of 
manure and other biomass, producing biogas equivalent of approximately 39,000,000 m3 of 
methane/year, as well as production of fertilizers and other value added products (I. Angelidaki, 
Ellegaard, and Ahring 1993; Irini Angelidaki and Ellegaard 2003). Even when considered from 
environmental perspective a recent life cycle assessment shows that, biogas production from 
manure alone is having much higher reduction in Green House Gas (GHG) emissions when 
compared to the other types of bioenergy production pathways (Thyø and Wenzel, 2007).     
However, agricultural biogas production from manure alone is not economically viable due to 
relatively low gas yields. Contribution of co-digestion comes into picture at this very stage 
where addition of co-substrates with high methane potential such as carbohydrates, lipids and 
proteins etc., to manure can increase the gas yield thereby competing with the current oil prices. 
Thus, co-digestion possesses a significant advantage of handling a major available substrate 
mixed with minor amounts of variable co-substrates. Possibilities and opportunities of 
Anaerobic Digestion (AD) were better explored with co-digestion thus turning it into one of 
the standard technologies to produce Biogas (Braun and Wellinger).  
Moreover, manure is rich in nutrients that are necessary for optimal microbial growth, 
possesses a strong buffering capacity to maintain a stable pH in case of temporary VFA 
accumulation and acts as a good substrate for dilution of inhibitory wastes, thus depicting 
synergistic effect when co-digested with other organic co-substrates (Irini Angelidaki and 
Ellegaard 2003).  
Nevertheless, excess concentrations of nutrient present in the system in the form of ammonia 
will inhibit the AD process, though it is useful for the bacterial growth. The risk of inhibition 
is even higher especially while dealing with complex organic or inorganic substrates (McCarty 
1964). There are several other factors affecting process stability, such as pH, temperature, type 
of inoculum, reactor configuration, etc. Apart from this, specific characteristic features of 
substrate and total ammonia nitrogen concentration (Ammonium ion + Free NH4) can also lead 
to inhibition of AD process (Yenigün and Demirel 2013). 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
Despite being effective, co-digestion faces several challenges while being implemented in large 
scale biogas plants. For instance, physical, chemical and biological constituents present in the 
system, sizing of reactors and related equipment, co-substrate characteristics etc., become 
questionable while dealing with big numbers. Also, it becomes important to predict outcomes 
while mixing new substrates. These ambiguities were best answered by development of 
mathematical model that can withstand variety of such variations and predict possible 
improvement strategies while addressing issues like variation in pH, process inhibition due to 
free NH3, increased VFA concentration in the system etc., thereby giving justice to the 
experimental trials  (I. Angelidaki, Ellegaard, and Ahring 1993; Zaher et al., 2010).  
Various mathematical models have been developed and have provided guidelines for operation 
and optimization of anaerobic reactors in addition to providing an understanding of process 
inhibition patterns. However, very few models attempted the simulations of co-digestion of 
different wastes which did not describe interactions occurring during degradation of the 
intermediates that are very important for the outcome of the biogas process.  
A comprehensive anaerobic digestion model (BioModel) is a mathematical model primarily 
developed by the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), for the simulations of anaerobic 
fermentations such as digestion of cattle manure in the biogas process in one or several 
interconnected CSTRs operated at atmospheric gas pressure (I. Angelidaki, Ellegaard, and 
Ahring 1993).  This is the first model calibrated and validated with respect to co-digestion of 
complex wastes with varying characteristics and composition. BioModel provides an 
understanding of processes and interactions occurring inside the biogas system. Various issues 
that arose in previous models were handled successfully by the current model with convincing 
results.   
Also, there is an increased demand to find more economical and suitable co-substrates to use 
in the AD system. Cellulosic residues such as grass, straws, cast seaweed, etc., have gained 
increased interest in Denmark in recent years (Ellegaard and Angelidaki 2009).  
Keeping in mind the aforementioned factors, this project will utilise the existing DTU 
comprehensive biogas model for investigating the new co-digestion scenario, in order to 
improve the synergistic effect of the co-digestion process. Two of the important factors i.e. 
ammonia inhibition in relation with co-substrate compositions will be dealt in this particular 
case. Different co-substrates combinations will be modelled to investigate the process 
efficiency and stability.  Outputs from model simulations will be compared with data from lab-
scale continuous reactor experiments to validate the model prediction.

1.1 Aims and Purpose of the Project 
Primary objective of this project is to find the effect of various co-substrate compositions on 
ammonia inhibition in manure based digesters, attempting to improve the synergy of manure 
co-digestion process, thus increasing process efficiency and stability.  
Two main tools were used in order to achieve the project goals and objectives.    
1. Biogas process model (BioModel) developed at DTU, specialised for manure co-digestion 
process.  
2. Continuous stirred tank reactor experimental set up, which will be used to compare with the 
model results under different co-digestion scenarios.  
More specifically, this project aims to: 
1. Collect necessary data for model input and set-up co-digestion model: Preliminary data 
collection was done by referring to previous literatures which included calibration and 
validation of BioModel during its developmental stages.  
2. Generate simulation results from the BioModel and select interesting combinations to test 
in the laboratory reactor experiments: BioModel operation manual was referred with 
necessary operational specifications such as temperature, HRT, type of co-substrate to be fed 
and the respective OLR etc.   
3. Operating continuous CSTR reactor experiment and comparing model results with 
experimental results: Based on the model results, suggested operation strategy was tested in 
five lab-scale CSTRs and results were compared with model simulation to evaluate the 
synergistic effect of co-digestion under the chosen scenario.

2. LITERATURE REVIEW  
2.1 Anaerobic Digestion  
AD is a biological process catalyzed by wide range of microorganisms where organic carbon 
is converted into its most oxidized state (CO2) and most reduced state (CH4) by subsequent 
oxidation and reduction reactions in the absence of oxygen. Major products of AD are methane 
and carbon dioxide but minor amounts of nitrogen, hydrogen, ammonia, hydrogen sulphide are 
also generated (less than 1% of the total gas volume) which in total, termed as Biogas (Irini 
Angelidaki, Ellegaard, and Ahring 2003).  
Microbiological reactions are autocatalytic. At steady state, for any biologically mediated 
reaction, the catalyst will be produced to an extent where amount of catalyst present does not 
have a significant effect on the rate of reaction but the rate limiting factors are substrate or 
nutrient concentration (Gujer and Zehnder,). Anaerobic degradation of organic material 
(polymeric substances such as carbohydrates, lipids and proteins) involves several groups of 
bacteria and archaea which together convert organic matter into methane and carbon dioxide 
through the following steps.  
1. Hydrolysis of biopolymers (proteins, lipids and carbohydrates) 
2. Acidogenesis 
3. Acetogenesis  
4. Methanogenesis   
2.1.1 Hydrolysis 
In hydrolysis, polymers of macro molecules such as proteins, fats and polysaccharides are 
hydrolysed into monomers: amino acids, monosaccharides and fatty acids. Exo-enzymes 
secreted by the fermentative bacteria is the main catalyst for this process. In anaerobic digestion 
of complex organic substrates, hydrolysis becomes a rate-limiting step in the degradation chain 
(Christensen 2011).  
Proteins  Amino Acids (catalyzed by protease enzymes), proteolytic bacteria.  
Cellulose and Xylan  Glucose and Xylose (catalyzed by cellulase and xylanase enzymes), 
cellulytic and xylanolytic bacteria. 
Lipids  Glycerol and Long Chain Fatty Acids (LCFA) (catalyzed by lipases enzyme 
produced by lipolytic bacteria).

2.1.2 Acidogenesis  
Products of hydrolysis are absorbed by fermentative bacteria for their growth. This step is also 
called fermentation or acidification that converts hydrolyzed products into aldehydes, alcohols, 
volatile fatty acids (e.g. acetic, propionic and butyric acid), and gases like CO2, H2 and NH3. 
Ammonia will be generated as a by-product of fermentation of amino acids. 
2.1.3 Acetogenesis 
Products of acidogenesis such as VFA and alcohols are further oxidized to acetate (coupled to 
a reduction of proton to hydrogen) and carbon dioxide. This reaction is catalysed by a special 
group of bacteria called obligatory hydrogen producing, acetogenic bacteria. They only follow 
the metabolic pathway of reduction of H+ ions to produce H2 and will not grow at high 
hydrogen partial pressure. Rather, a low H2 partial pressure is strongly required to make the

acetogenesis reaction possible. The hydrogen producing bacteria will produce more hydrogen 
than required by the hydrogen consuming bacteria thereby showing a syntrophic relationship 
between them. Energy yield in this process is very limited at standard conditions which is a 
characteristic feature of this step. In order to increase the energy yield of the oxidation, the 
partial pressure of hydrogen must be low. In that case, equilibrium of the reaction will shift 
towards the degradation of substrate.  
2.1.4 Methanogenesis 
This is the final step of AD where in the primary product of acid fermentation i.e. acetic acid 
will be converted into CO2 and CH4. Acetate is the important methane precursor (70%), while 
the remaining (30%) is formed from CO2/H2.  
Methanogesis step is mediated by methanogens, which belong to the kingdom of Euryarchaeota 
in the domain of Archaea that differs from bacteria in many aspects (Lange and Ahring 2001).  
Following sequence of reactions mentioned (Anegelidaki and Schmidt) shows various 
degradation steps in which conversion of acetic acid to methane is the least favourable reaction 
and carbon monoxide to methane is most favourable reaction according to lower Gibbs free 
energy.  
 
Methane gas produced will be released into the gas phase, which means that the organic matters 
in the liquid phase are removed. Growth of respective microorganisms in the entire process of 
AD happens through catabolic and anabolic reactions. In case of former, substrates are 
consumed to release energy and by-products; whereas latter consumes energy released from 
the catabolic reactions for cell synthesis.  
Methanogens are highly sensitive to adverse environmental conditions. Therefore, it is very 
important to keep favourable environmental conditions such as optimum pH, temperature, 
absence of toxic materials and availability of nutrients, in order to ease the metabolism to its 
highest level.

2.1.5 Advantages of Anaerobic Digestion  
Anaerobic digestion is the most sustainable way to treat concentrated organic wastes, as it 
produces energy that can be utilised in the waste treatment system. Major share of pollutant 
load from the industrial and agricultural activities can be successfully reduced by AD. The 
process in turn offsets the burden on fossil fuels (Chen, Cheng, and Creamer 2008).  
In addition to this, AD has effectively paved its way in producing one of most promising and 
approachable sources of renewable energy, not only used in the form of Biogas but also as 
liquid fuel.  
2.2 Co-digestion 
Anaerobic co-digestion is a process where more than one type of organic substrates are digested 
together (Astals et al. 2014). This section takes you through the advantages and limitations of 
co-digestion, synergistic effects, co-substrate composition and ammonia inhibition while 
dealing with CSTRs.   
2.2.1 Benefits of co-digestion 
Co-digestion offers several techno-economic advantages (Yen and Brune 2007; Braun and 
Wellinger; Callaghan et al. 2002).  
 Besides energy production which is one of the primary factors of consideration of AD, high 
OLR and less sludge production has proven anaerobic co-digestion as an efficient and 
robust technology.  
 Allows sharing of machinery, equipment and facility leading to an economic process. 
 Improves nutrient balance and thereby helps in maintaining a reliable and stable digestion 
performance. 
 Results in good quality fertilizer from the digestate. 
 Optimizes the rheological quality by homogenizing different wastes of varying physical 
and chemical characteristics with dilute substrate such as sewage sludge or liquid manure.  
 Increased attention to energy crops which can successfully compensate the limited 
availability of industrial waste as co-substrates.  
2.2.2 C/N Ratio  
Livestock manure in co-digestion has been an outstanding substrate as manure provides wide 
range of nutrients, has good buffering capacity etc. However, manure alone has not been very 
effective in producing good biogas yield due to its low C/N ratio. Addition of co-substrates 
with good content of carbon could improve the C/N ratio of the feedstock, leading to reduced 
risk of ammonia inhibition (Wu et al. 2010). Studies also predict that low C/N ratio in feedstock 
could result in higher VFA accumulation and high TAN in the digester. On the other hand, C/N 
ratio at optimum levels in digester feed could increase CH4 yield in AD process  (Yen and 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
Brune 2007; Hills 1979) (Wu et al. 2010). Thus, C/N ratio in the substrate is an important 
indicator for controlling the biological treatment systems that allows blending of co-substrates 
with both high and low C/N ratio. This improves the digester performance by creating synergy 
(X. Wang et al. 2012).   
2.2.3 Limitations of co-digestion 
Besides having plenty of advantages, co-digestion does have some limitations or in other words 
challenges to be addressed (Kangle et al. 2015; X. Li et al. 2009; Y. Li, Park, and Zhu 2011).  
 Manure digestion facilities are usually constructed according to wet digestion. Addition 
of straw, grass and other fibrous matter might lead to risk of clogging of pipes and 
pumps; might result in crust formation at in the digestion tank, (X. Li et al. 2009).  
 Co-substrate collection, pre-treatment and transportation requirements might increase 
the demand for additional equipment.   
 Increased mixing requirements in order to homogenize the substrate leading to 
additional power consumption. 
 Digestate application on land could be problematic if the introduced co-substrate 
possesses certain toxic characteristics.  
 Increased demand for crop residues might result in alterations in food chain. 
 
2.3 Co-digestion and its Synergistic effects 
In an experiment conducted by (Macias-Corral et al. 2008), AD of cow manure with OFMSW 
and cotton gin waste was investigated with two phase pilot scale AD system. Conclusions were 
drawn by comparing single waste digestion with co-digestion of combined wastes, which 
proved that synergistic effect was achieved in the co-digestion of cow manure with OFMSW 
with highest methane yield, higher mass conversion, lower weight and volume of digestate 
residue. 
In another study conducted by (Wagner et al. 2013), nine complex organic substrates falling 
into three major categories i.e. protein, lipid and glucose were co-digested in various 
combinations. The aim was to evaluate each substrate’s potential and utilization of its own in 
Biogas production. Highest methane production was observed in protein rich substrates, while 
problems arose in lipid, lactose and cellulose fermentation. The study also showed that similar 
substrates produced similar microbial community and different substrates produced different 
ones. Substrate overloading and/or excessive portion of easily available carbohydrates resulted 
in drop in pH, release of more H2 into the system, impeding methane production. Methanogens 
in general, particularly archaeal community showed stability towards adverse environmental 
conditions i.e. during decreasing or failing of fermenter performance, for an incubation period 
of 55 days. Authors have come to a conclusion that, despite facing an adverse condition, 
microbial community can be successfully re-established by adding appropriate substrates rather 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
than by adding new inoculum from another digester. However, no specific conclusions were 
drawn in terms of achieving synergy between the selected co-substrates.    
2.4 Co-digestion Modelling 
Anaerobic digestion is comprised of complex biological processes involving numerous 
microorganisms whose start-up and operation indeed is a delicate issue. In order to have better 
understanding of process dynamics and operations, development of mathematical model is of 
prime importance. Without this, applicability of co-digestion when experimenting with a new 
co-substrate or while implementing in a full scale biogas plant etc., will be limited. These 
limitations are either due to lack of reference data about the behaviour of new co-substrate 
during operating or lack of information/knowledge about design considerations while 
establishing a full scale plant. Misjudging them would lead to risk of failure and huge loss of 
investment as the capital cost for preliminary installation is exorbitant. Thirst for knowledge 
from industrial sector still persists that necessitates the development of a mathematical model 
for anaerobic co-digestion (Batstone et al. 2002; I. Angelidaki, Ellegaard, and Ahring 1993; 
Mosey 2011).  
2.4.1 Advantages of a Mathematical Model  
 Model allows the user to understand the complexity and dynamics involved at each 
step.  
 Sets a common basis for mandatory operational considerations with flexibility to vary 
them as per requirement.  
 Prediction of possible outcomes before implementing in reality.  
 Emergency preparedness with alternative solutions.  
 Optimization of process operations.  
 Technology transfer and increased research opportunities.  
 Credibility in technology and easy decision making.  
 Validation of model with additional parameters with which results could be more 
comparable and compatible.  
Several mathematical models for AD have developed and the selection of a specific model to 
test our hypothesis demands a pragmatic approach. A review done by (Donoso-Bravo et al. 
2011) comparing various models draw following conclusions.  
One of the first models was developed in early 70’s to fulfil the need for efficient AD system 
operation. First generation models concluded that, any one slow step of AD would be the rate 
limiting step that reduces overall rate of reaction. Since AD is a multistep process, various 
studies predicted different steps (such as hydrolysis, methanogenesis) as rate limiting. There 
was no overall certainty drawn that could not predict overall process performance.  
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
Models developed in the second generation considered VFA production in the system as a key 
parameter of consideration as well as redox potential as a function of partial pressure of 
hydrogen.  
Models of next generation focused mainly on the microbiological studies incorporating 
additional species and processes. Important considerations were type of substrates, describing 
kinetics in details including factors of inhibition.  
In spite of having several models, there was no generic model developed comprised of all 
parameters that were individually considered in different models. Therefore, a generic model 
called Anaerobic Digestion Model No.1. ADM1 describes 24 species including nine 
bioconversion processes was developed. However, it neglected some processes and species 
with respect to more specific application in order to avoid complexity. Many critics arose 
highlighting the drawbacks of ADM1.  
2.4.2 Development of BioModel 
In 1993, a comprehensive biogas process model for simulation of manure co-digestion system 
named as BioModel was developed by the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), for AD 
of complex organic substrates. This comprises of an enzymatic hydrolytic step and four 
bacterial steps involves 12 compounds. First and second generation models had a rapid failure 
when VFA concentrations increased above threshold limit or pH fell below a certain level. 
Details of pH and temperature characteristics are described in BioModel in order to accurately 
simulate the free ammonia concentration which is one of the crucial factors for inhibiting the 
process (I. Angelidaki, Ellegaard, and Ahring 1993). Protein rich substrates in AD results in 
risk of causing inhibition due to increased ammonia levels in the system especially while 
operating with thermophilic temperatures. But, previous studies have also depicted a stable AD 
process with improved biogas yields with the addition of proteins, lipids and carbohydrates 
(Irini Angelidaki, Ellegaard, and Ahring 1998). This is one of the main reasons for choosing 
BioModel in the present study over ADM1 or any other already developed models.  
2.5 Ammonia Inhibition  
Biological degradation of nitrogenous matter produces ammonia that exists in two principal 
forms: Ammonium ion (NH4+) and free ammonia (NH3). Even though it is an essential nutrient 
stimulating bacterial growth, excess concentration (above their threshold limit) of ammonia, 
particularly free ammonia can cause AD process inhibition during the breakdown of proteins 
available in the substrate, particularly while dealing with complex substrates such as cattle 
manure or OFMSW (Yenigün and Demirel 2013).   
AD of animal products in batch and semi-continuously fed reactors at thermophilic and 
mesophilic temperatures were conducted by (Hejnfelt and Angelidaki 2009). High 
concentration ammonia over 7 g Nm-3 in byproducts resulted in process inhibition.  
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
However, (McCarty 1964) indicated that, concentration of ammonia nitrogen in the range 1.5-
3g-N/L is inhibitory at high pH levels above 7.4 to 7.6  and above. When ammonia 
concentration in the system is above 3g-N/L, inhibition is caused at any obtained pH. His study 
also says that inhibition free concentration of free ammonia in the reactor ranges between 50-
200g-N/L.  
Investigation of ammonia inhibition levels was also tested by (I. Angelidaki and Ahring 1993) 
in thermophilic digestion of cattle manure. The study was aimed at adding different ammonia 
concentrations and possibility of adaptation during AD. Results showed that, ammonia 
concentration of 4g-N/L and above caused process inhibition in continuously fed biogas 
reactors with 25% reduction in methane yield. However, when ammonia was introduced 
gradually, up to 3g-N/L the process was unaffected and was slightly affected at ammonia levels 
of 4g-N/L. The performance was severely affected with ammonia concentration of 5g-N/L and 
reached the same level when the reactors were fed with 6g-N/L as in the beginning of 
experiment. But, the authors also say that, even if ammonia concentration is above 4g-N/L, 
after an initial adaptation period, it is still possible to achieve stable digestion environment with 
slight reduction in the yield (25%). It is also reported that, biogas reactors fed with livestock 
waste often have high pH level around 8 and while operating at thermophilic temperature, free 
ammonia concentration will be ten folds higher than inhibitory levels.  
Another study was conducted by (Zeeman et al. 1985) with thermophilic digestion (50°C) of 
cow manure. Authors say that, it is not the volatile solid concentration of cow manure, but total 
ammonia concentration that is responsible for poor performance of AD. Inhibition 
concentration in this study was reported to be 1.7g-N/L.  
In the present study, we examine the effect of co-substrate concentration on ammonia inhibition 
when reactors were fed with cattle manure as a main substrate and combination of co substrates 
such as Gelatine, glucose, and GTO with various OLRs, operated at thermophilic conditions in 
five lab scale CSTRs. Maximum Biogas yield among the five different selected combinations 
are reported. Also, the maximum extent to which free ammonia concentration in all five 
combinations will allow an uninhibited process are reported.

releasing of gas for every 100mL of Biogas produced. OLR and gas production was monitored 
on a daily basis, whereas gas composition, pH measurement, VFA, ammonia nitrogen was

4.1.1 Scenario 1: Co-digestion of Cattle manure with single co-substrate 
In the first step, co-digestion of cattle manure with pure substrates such as Gelatine, GTO, 
Glucose and Wheat Straw was performed at different OLR by increasing the amount of added 
co-substrate from 0 to 40g VS/L-feed. Primary intention behind this trial was to investigate the 
effect of co-substrate types and their concentrations on the co-digestion performance.   
After the first simulation, response of the model with respect to the parameters of vital 
importance depicting the performance and stability of AD system such as CH4 yield, CH4 
production rate, Total Ammonia, free ammonia, total VFA, pH and OLR were plotted as a 
function of added amount of co-substrate and the same is depicted in Figure 3. Detailed 
performance of individual co-substrates can be referred in Appendix (B).  
From the graphs it can be clearly seen that, highest CH4 production is achieved when manure 
was co-digested with GTO alone than Glucose, Wheat Straw and Gelatine respectively. Total 
Ammonia Nitrogen in the system is very less (<2g-N/L) when compared to other combinations 
with a stable pH ranging from 8 to 8.2.  
This may be due to the fact that, GTO (lipids in general) contain larger number of C and H 
atoms in their molecule that implies a high theoretical methane yield when compared to the 
other co-substrates involved; whereas carbohydrates, proteins show a faster conversion process 
but lower yield (Esposito 2012).  
Only in case of Gelatine, there is an increase in CH4 yield up to 2g/VSL Gelatine addition; after 
which, the trend goes down with further addition of co-substrate. Consequently, total ammonia 
in the system increased along with increase in total VFA concentration. Bacterial count 
decreased when Gelatine is added above 10gVS/L-feed. However, other co-substrates depict a 
complete opposite trend. This is due to the fact that protein contains nitrogen in the molecule 
which is converted to ammonia that adds up to the total ammonia levels in the system, 
increasing the probability of ammonia inhibition. Increase in organic loading has led to shorter

residence time for the particles in the reactor resulting in incomplete hydrolysis. Elevated levels 
of ammonia has shifted the microbial community affecting their performance in the system (I. 
Angelidaki 2005; Niu et al. 2013).

4.1.2 Scenario 2: Co-digestion with combination of 2 co-substrates 
In a second scenario, the response of co-digestion when adding a mixture of two co-substrates 
was investigated at total co-substrates concentration of 10 and 30gVS/L-feed, corresponding 
to the OLR of 6.16 and 7.49 gVS/(L-reactor.d), respectively. The results from co-digestion at 
total co-substrates concentration of 10 and 30 gVS/L-feed are shown in shown in Appendix 
5 and 6.  
At co-substrate concentration of 10 gVS/L-feed, GTO showed the most positive effect to the 
process, followed by glucose, and wheat straw respectively. In contrast, Gelatine showed 
negative effect to the co-digestion process. As shown in Figure 4, the decrease in Gelatine 
concentration, while increasing concentration of GTO, Glucose, or Wheat Straw, resulted in 
increased methane production, lower VFA concentration, lower ammonia concentration and 
higher biomass cell production. 
Despite all co-substrate combinations resulted in lower total VFA in the system compared to 
Scenario 1, significant variations were observed in Gelatine combinations when their 
individual VFA were examined.  
When co-digested manure with Gelatine alone, concentration of propionic acid increased over 
acetic acid at Gelatine concentration above 19gVS/L (Figure 4A).  However, when co-
digested manure with both Gelatine and Glucose, propionic acid exceeded acetic acid at 
Gelatine concentration of 26gVS/L (i.e. with Glucose concentration 4 gVS/L) (Figure 4B). 
For co-digestion of manure with Gelatine and GTO, the Gelatine concentration above 
22gVS/L-feed resulted in increase of propionic acid concentration above acetic acid (Figure 
4C). Since the ratio of propionic acid to acetic acid has been suggested to be correlated to the 
stress level of the process (Marchaim and Krause 1993), thus, the simulation results indicated 
that manure co-digested with Gelatine and other co-substrate could tolerate higher Gelatine 
concentration than manure co-digested with Gelatine alone.  
Investigations have shown that, VFA to methane conversion rates follow that order of HAc> 
ethanol (HEt)> butyric acid (HBu) > propionic acid (HPa) which means, all VFAs are initially 
converted to acetic acid (HAc) and then follow the above trend before being degraded to 
methane (N et al. 2003). Effect of individual VFA concentrations on methane yield and 
methanogenic bacteria was examined in another study by using various concentrations of 
HAc, HPa, HBu and HEt as substrates. There was no significant inhibition of AD even with 
the highest concentrations of HEt, HAc, and HBu being 2400, 2400 and 1800 mg/L 
respectively. But, inhibition was noticed when HPa concentration was 900 mg/L, which 
resulted in reduction in bacterial concentration and lower CH4 yield. Similarly, there is an 
accumulation of ethanol and VFAs in the system with significant inhibitory effect on the 
methanogenic bacteria. This was caused mainly due to propionic acid accumulation resulting 
in reduced CH4 yield (Y. Wang et al. 2009).

Model results in the present scenario show a good CH4 yield and reduction in total ammonia 
concentration below 3.5gN/L in the system with increase in OLR due to the co-digestion 
effect. However, noticeable changes in propionic acid trend depicts that, this could be one of 
the important factors causing inhibition to AD process.

4.1.3 Scenario 3: Co-digestion with fixed concentration of Gelatine and varying 
concentration of other co-substrates 
In this scenario, response for increased yet fixed total OLR of co-substrates to tolerate ammonia 
inhibition was examined by mixing 15gVS/L, 20gVS/L and 30gVS/L-feed of Gelatine with 
varied amounts of GTO, Glucose and Wheat Straw respectively to manure. Results of 
simulation are shown in Figure 7.  
In Case 1 (Figure 7) except for Ge-GTO combination, all other combinations including 
Gelatine alone resulted in higher total ammonia in the system ranging from 3.3 g-N/L to 3.5 g-
N/L especially when the Gelatine concentration was increased to 20g/L and 30g/L respectively. 
Despite showing higher CH4 yield, addition of higher concentrations of Glucose and GTO to 
Gelatine increased total ammonia levels resulting in high levels of VFA and reduced bacterial 
count. This is an indication of inhibition due to organic overloading. This could be due to the 
fact that, acetate consuming methanogens are less tolerable to increased total ammonia 
concentration than hydrogen consuming methanogens, resulting in inefficient hydrolysis and 
increased VFA levels in the system (Zeeman et al. 1985).  
Only Ge-Gl and Ge-GTO (at 15g/L Gelatine addition) combinations at both low and high OLRs 
resulted in less accumulation of Total VFA in the system indicating that easily degradable 
substrates such as glucose and GTO will undergo efficient hydrolysis, thus resulting in better 
CH4 yield. However, protein rich substrates tend to increase the total ammonia concentration 
in the system, which beyond certain levels can cause inhibitory effects. Model results shows a 
linear relationship between substrate loading rate and CH4 yield at low OLRs and the yield has 
reduced considerably as the loading increases, which might be the result of increased VFA 
accumulation in the system (I. Angelidaki 2005; Alvarez and Lidén 2008). 
There seen an evident improvement in overall performance of AD process when manure was 
co-digested with combinations of co-substrates than with Gelatine alone. Previous studies have 
concluded that GTO can result in better gas production capacity when compared to proteins 
and carbohydrates due to the presence of higher C and H atoms making it an energetic substrate 
(Sialve, Bernet, and Bernard 2009). With the above benefits, issues arising in co-digestion 
using single co-substrate can be better handled and digestion performance can be improved by 
the addition of lipid rich materials as secondary co-substrates. In general, secondary co-
substrate will not only introduce additional nutrients into the system but also 
attenuates/neutralizes the inhibitory effects that would otherwise be caused by the primary co-
substrate; thus creating a synergistic effect (Park and Li 2012). But lower alkalinity and 
buffering capacity of lipids could make it more susceptible for the process inhibition due to 
intermediates like LCFA and VFA. This situation could be balanced by the cattle manure and 
Gelatine as they have proven to have a high buffering capacity (Georgacakis, Sievers, and 
Iannotti 1982). 
Results indicate a very good CH4 production even at OLR of 7gVS/(L-reactor.d), which might 
not be tolerable by the system in reality. Keeping in mind the negative effects that could 
==PAGE:36==

possibly be caused by having a very high OLR in the AD process, following combinations 
highlighted in the Table 6 were chosen from simulation results to be tested in the laboratory. 
In this case, the model did not seem sensitive to very high OLRs.

4.2 CSTR Experiment Results 
In this scenario, selected combinations from the model results were implemented in lab scale 
CSTRs to test their performance in practice. By the end of sampling period, the CSTRs were 
operated for 70 days at 53±10 C temperature with a HRT of 15 days. Feeding period 1 lasted 
for 45 days where all reactors were fed with manure alone with an OLR of 1.88gVS/(L-
reactor.d) and entered feeding period 2 on Day 46 when they started with co-digestion. HRT 
was maintained same with varying OLRs of 2.88gVS/(L-reactor.d) in R1 fed with Gelatine, 
3.88gVS/(L-reactor.d) in (R2 and R4) and 4.88gVS/(L-reactor.d) in (R3 and R5) fed with 
(Gelatine+glucose) and (Gelatine+GTO) at both high and low OLRs respectively.

As seen in the Figure 8, during P1 an average CH4 yield of all five reactors was 166mL-
CH4/(gVS-added) with 68.6% CH4 in biogas. An average biogas yield of 7L-Biogas/(L-feed) 
was achieved with a steady state concentration from Day 18 and Day 45. CH4 yield and CH4 
production rate from each individual reactor was different in spite of maintaining similar feed 
flow and other operational specifications. To compare the overall performance between P1 and 
P2, total average CH4 yield from P1 is considered in our calculations. However, there seen a 
variation in performance from each individual reactor in spite of similar feed and operating 
conditions. This might be the effect of uneven in mixing that was observed in different reactors 
on different days. Effect of mixing on the result of AD process was observed by (Lindmark et 
al. 2014) in which they conclude that, uneven mixing might be beneficial for the bacterial 
population to adapt to the given environment during the start-up phase. If continued in later 
stages, it might have an effect on solid retention time as well as steady state gas measurements.  
Figure 9 & 10 shows the variation in pH, total nitrogen, ammonia nitrogen concentrations, 
percentage methane in biogas, organic nitrogen, and total and individual VFA concentrations 
during P1. pH values during this period is ranging between 8.33 to 7.42 in all reactors except 
for R5 with very high variation. This was due to a technical difficulty because of which R5 was 
re-inoculated after Day 34. Significant reduction in ammonia nitrogen in the system from an 
average of 1.86 to 1.29g-N/L until the end of Day 45 resulted no threats of inhibition (McCarty 
1964). Since the solid retention time is maintained constant throughout the experimental 
period, the Total Kjeldahl Nitrogen (Ammonia Nitrogen + Organic Nitrogen) concentration is 
expected to be constant either. Previous studies have shown an increase in ammonia nitrogen 
concentration with simultaneous reduction in organic nitrogen thereby maintaining a constant 
total nitrogen in the system (Rivero and Andres 2005) is expected for a stable process without 
causing inhibition. Similar effect is observed in P1 thereby causing no threats of inhibition.  
VFA levels in all reactors have increased during the initial stage of P1 which is assumed to be 
an adaptation period for the microorganisms. Highest total VFA concentration of 2.2 g/L was 
seen on Day 14 after which it has declined in all reactors until the end of P1. Among all 
individual VFAs, Acetate and Propionate are dominating during P1. Maximum concentration 
of acetate (1.67g/L) is observed on day 14 (adaptation period) which has subsequently taken 
over by a gradual increase in propionate concentration until Day 42. Even though excess 
concentrations of propionate over acetate has previously proven to be an element of causing 
process inhibition (Y. Wang et al. 2009), total VFA in this system has not reached the level of 
causing inhibition.   
From the above observations it can be concluded that, mono-digestion of cattle manure has 
resulted in reduction in ammonia levels consequently showing reduced VFA accumulation and 
stable digestion process. This is evident from the increased CH4 production rates during the 
end of P1.

4.2.2 Period 2  
Co-digestion began in P2 from Day 46 and continued until Day 70. During this period, R1 was 
fed with Gelatine alone with a total OLR of 2.88gVS/(L-reactor.d), R2 & R3 with co-substrates 
(Gelatine+Glucose), and R4 & R5 with co-substrates (Gelatine+GTO) at total OLR of 
3.88gVS/(L-reactor.d) and 4.88gVS/(L-reactor.d) respectively. HRT and other operational 
specifications were maintained as same as P1 
CH4 production in all reactors has improved from P1 to P2 whereas CH4 yield has improved 
only in R2-R5, but R1. Also, CH4 production from individual reactors was compared with the 
theoretical expected values and the results shows only 11.8% utilization of co-substrate is 
achieved in R1 and 66.7, 56.5, 62.7, 50.8% has resulted in R2, R3, R4 and R5 respectively 
from P1 to P2.  
Co-digestion performance of all five reactors with the addition of their respective co-substrates 
was also examined. Even though R1 showed a slight improvement in CH4 production, average 
CH4 yield has declined from 206mL-CH4/(gVS-added) in P1 to 152mL-CH4/gVS in P2. 
Whereas R2, R3, R4 and R5 showed improvements both in CH4 yield and production from P1 
to P2 as shown in Table 7 and Figure 8(D).  
When looked at the overall scenario of P1 and P2, addition of co-substrates have improved the 
methane production rate of AD. In spite of that, decreased CH4 yield in R1 shows that, protein 
rich substrates have a higher tendency to cause ammonia inhibition and the very effect was 
nullified with the addition of secondary co-substrates such as glucose and GTO, resulting in 
better yield and performance. Thus synergistic effect can be seen in overall co-digestion 
process with addition of secondary co-substrates (Park and Li 2012).   
CH4 yield in each reactor during P2 assessed with respect to the effect of increase in OLR has 
resulted in an interesting trend. When added glucose as a secondary co-substrate, increase in 
OLR from 3.88gVS/(L-reactor.d) to 4.88gVS/(L-reactor.d) reduced the CH4 yield from 
204mL-CH4/gVS-added to 184mL-CH4/gVS-added; whereas in case of GTO as a secondary 
co-substrate has improved the CH4 yield from 336mL-CH4/gVS-added to 352mL-CH4/gVS-
added (Figure (8D)). Many studies have resulted in a fact that lipid rich substrates have better 
CH4 potential compared to carbohydrates and proteins. However, increase in lipid 
concentration also possesses a stronger inhibition due to the accumulation of LCFAs with 
subsequent reduction in CH4 yields (Cirne et al. 2007). Similar trend has been followed in case 
of addition of GTO in the present study but with improved CH4 yields.  
But, increase in loading rate might be a reason for reduction in CH4 yield in R3. When looked 
at the percentage utilization of co-substrate, both R3 and R5 have poor performance compared 
to R2 and R4. From this it can be concluded that organic overloading is the reason for R3 and 
R5 to impart poor performance.   
 
==PAGE:41==
41 
 
 
Previous studies have shown an increase in ammonia nitrogen concentration with a 
simultaneous reduction in organic nitrogen thereby maintaining a constant total nitrogen in the 
system (Rivero and Andres 2005) is expected for a stable digestion process without causing 
inhibition. Similar effect is observed in P1 thereby causing no threats of inhibition.  
Overall biogas yield has showed an increasing trend from R1 to R5 during P2 as seen in Figure 
8. Even when compared with the results from P1, there is a significant increase in total biogas 
yield with a steady state concentration observed between Day 53 to 70 in all reactors. An 
average of 70-71% CH4 in biogas is achieved in all reactors except R3 which has only 64%. 
An expected reduction in CH4 content was observed here as a major portion of VS originated 
from carbohydrates will theoretically get converted into 50% CH4 and 50% CO2 (Angelidaki 
and Bastone, 2010). Even though R2 was fed with glucose, total percentage of VS has been 
equally divided between Gelatine and glucose whereas in R3, major share of VS is contributed 
from carbohydrates. Addition of Gelatine alone has caused no significant improvement from 
P1 to P2 like in other reactors. 
Overall behavior is slightly deviating to what was referred in literatures with the very fact that, 
organic overload and type of co-substrates in the present study have shown contradicting 
performances in case of R2 to R5 deviating from the previous results. If we only consider the 
OLR, R2 with lower loading rate resulted in better CH4 yield than R5 with higher loading rate. 
But, if we consider the type of co-substrate added in relation to OLR, R5 operated at higher 
OLR with GTO has a better CH4 yield than R2 operated with glucose at lower OLR.

4.3 Comparison of CSTR results with model predictions   
One of the main intentions of this study was to compare the co-digestion performance with 
predicted model results of similar combination of substrates in both cases. Here, comparisons 
can be made in two ways.  
1. Comparison of simulation results of real manure data with CSTR results. 
2. Comparison of simulation results from scenario 3 with CSTR results. 
In the first case, raw input data for manure that was used in scenario 1, 2 and 3 (Angelidaki et 
al. 1998) were supplemented with real manure data that was used in the current CSTR 
experiments and model simulations were performed. Real manure data with co-substrates used 
in the model can be seen in the Table 8 in Appendix C and comparison of model results and 
experimental results are plotted in Figure 11.  
In addition to the predictions from previous literatures, both model and CSTR experiments 
depicted that co-digestion with addition of secondary co-substrates delivers a better 
performance than using Gelatine alone as co-substrate. However, in spite having improvement 
in performance, as seen in Figure11 (C) and 11(D) experimental results have given lower CH4 
yield and production rates compared to model predictions. Total VFA levels (Figure 11 (E)) in 
all reactors are higher than the predicted values with R5 being the highest.   
==PAGE:47==
47 
 
 
Extremely high levels of propionate concentrations that was not predicted by the model is 
observed in real scenario. Model results predicted to have increased acetate concentration in 
all reactors. But results from the experiments showed opposite trend contradicting the predicted 
model results. This is one of the major aspects of consideration as increased concentration of 
propionate has clearly concluded as a factor causing inhibition to AD process (Y. Wang et al. 
2009). In this respect, there exists a difference in opinion which have to be further investigated 
to make clear conclusions about the reliability of results from model predictions.   
In case 2, model predictions still holds good and can be complimented with the CSTR 
experimental results. Even though a higher OLR predicted better performance and yield in 
scenario 3, we anticipated the risk of substrate overloading and higher ammonia levels in the 
reactor that could affect CH4 yield. Besides this, model results with higher concentration of 
Gelatine predicted a very high ammonia levels in the reactor thereby possessing a higher 
tendency to causing inhibition. If implemented in real scenario, increased concentrations of 
Gelatine addition might result in unforeseen situations. Therefore real experiments were 
designed to test the effect of addition of secondary co-substrate beginning with a lower 
concentrations of Gelatine.     
Both from case 1 and 2, Gelatine-GTO combination with higher OLR was predicted to have a 
better methane yield which is complemented by the CSTR experimental results indicating that 
organic overloading has not reduced the activity of methanogens. But, the same reactor has not 
succeeded in depicting better performance in spite of having a better yield.

5. CONCLUSIONS  
Behavior of different co-substrates in co-digestion and their responses to various organic 
loading rates was successfully understood from the present study. This project also gave an 
opportunity to explore the applicability mathematical modelling in anaerobic digestion, 
exclusively in manure based biogas digesters.  
 BioModel used in the current study predicted the behavior of co-substrates such as 
Gelatine, Glucose, GTO and Wheat Straw and their response towards tolerating 
ammonia inhibition when co-digested with cattle manure.  
 Predicted results from the model, when implemented in the laboratory CSTRs resulted 
that co-digestion of manure with single co-substrate (Gelatine alone) exhibited a poor 
performance than with addition of Glucose and GTO to Gelatine as a secondary co-
substrate.  
 Reactors operated with lower organic loading rate achieved better co-substrate 
utilization and better CH4 yields.  
 Type of added co-substrate combination made a clear impact on CH4 yield.  
 Even though predicted results from modelling were in line with experimental results in 
most aspects, a dispute arose in case of VFA levels when CSTR experiments resulted 
higher propionate concentration in all reactors that was predicted to be acetate 
according to model results.

6. FUTURE WORK 
In the present study, experimental results of co-digestion of cattle manure with co-substrates 
were tested only with a lower concentration of Gelatine addition (15g/L-feed). This was mainly 
due to a higher range of expected ammonia levels in the real scenario than the model 
predictions. From the results, it is clearly seen that total ammonia levels in all reactors are lower 
than the model prediction and are still operating without any inhibition. Therefore it becomes 
interesting to test different possibilities with a higher concentration of Gelatine (30g/L-feed) to 
examine the extent up to which, increase in Gelatine concentration or subsequent overloading 
of other co-substrate could tolerate ammonia inhibition. Also, it will be an interesting study to 
exclusively examine the behavior of co-substrates in neutralizing ammonia inhibition caused 
by protein rich substrates.  
Lignocellulosic biomass is gaining increasing importance in recent years due to their large 
availability as well as their property to produce biogas (Petersson et al. 2007). Another study 
shall be conducted to examine the behavior with the addition of lingo-cellulosic biomass as 
one of the secondary co-substrates and compare its performance with proteins, lipids and 
carbohydrates.   
To make the results viable, these shall be tested in a pilot scale biogas reactor as well as at an 
industry level to have real performance evaluations.  
Further investigations could also incorporate real industrial effluent rich in proteins, 
carbohydrates and lipids which could subsequently replace pure co-substrates used in the 
present study both for modeling and CSTR experiments.   
With successful examination of above cases in the real experimental scenario, additional data 
for co-substrate can be further added to the model with model calibration and validation.

II. Total sugars 
Sugar composition of raw cow manure was analysed using High Performance Liquid 
Chromatography (HPLC) analysis, in order to get the real characteristics of manure to be 
implemented in the model simulations.  
III. pH 
The pH of raw cattle and digested manure was measured by a PHM 92 LAB pH-meter (Figure 
13). The pH-meter was always calibrated before use with standards buffer solutions and the 
electrode was kept inside a KCl 3 M storage solution.  
 
Figure 13: PHM 92 LAB pH-meter 
IV. Volatile Fatty Acid:  
Volatile fatty acids were determined by gas chromatography using GC Shimadzu GC-2010 
(Figure 14). Samples were taken from each reactor and 3mL of sample was diluted with 3mL 
of DM water in a plastic bottle. The same was acidified with 400 μL of 34% Ortho Phosphoric 
Acid (H3PO4) solution for being the VFAs in their acidic form and to eliminate microbial 
activity.  
This acidified solution was then centrifuged at 13400 rpm for 10 minutes. Then, 1.0 mL 
supernatant from the sample was stored in a GC glass vial, along with the addition of 100.0 μL 
internal injection standard solution (1.1 mM 4-Methyl valeric acid). Samples were taken in 
duplicates and were stored in a freezer at -18° C until analysed by Gas Chromatography.

V. Total Kjeldahl Nitrogen and Ammonia Nitrogen  
The total nitrogen and ammonia of the samples were determined according to the Standard 
Methods for the Examination of Water and Wastewater (APHA, 1998). 2 mL of sample was 
taken in a digester tube into which a small spatula of glass beads, 2 Kjeldahl catalyst tablets 
and 10 mL of concentrated H2SO4 were added. Then, the tubes were digested for 60 minutes 
at 180° C and at 350° C for another 60 minutes. The tubes were allowed to cool down.   
A 250 ml flask containing 4 mL boric acid (4% w/v), 50 mL milli-Q water and 2-3 drops of 
Kjeldahl indicator and digester tubes of respective samples were placed in the distillation unit 
(Figure 15). Then the sample was titrated against HCL (0.1 M) solution.  
Total Kjeldahl Nitrogen is calculated by the following equation:

VI. Biogas Composition 
Methane content in the produced biogas was determined by gas chromatography using GC 
Shimadzu, equipped with a packed column for compound separation and a thermal-
conductivity detector (TCD) (Figure 16). The injector and detector temperature of GC machine 
was set to 50° C. The oven temperature was set to 50° C. Hydrogen was used as the carrier gas. 
The sample injection volume was 0.5 mL. The linear calibration curve was obtained by 
standard gas, which contained 30% N2, 30% CO2 and 40% CH4, in samples of 0.2 mL, 0.5 mL 
and 1.0 mL. This calibration curve was used to determine the percentage of methane and carbon 
dioxide in the samples.

VII. Biogas Production 
Daily Biogas production was measured using gas meters connected to each reactor by water 
displacement method. Biogas produced enters the gas meter column through an effluent tube 
fitted to the effluent bottle, displacing equal amount of water for every 100mL of Biogas 
produced. Gas meters were equipped with an electro-optic sensor which sends signal to the gas 
counter. The displaced water was reaching the sensor and the entrapped biogas was released.
