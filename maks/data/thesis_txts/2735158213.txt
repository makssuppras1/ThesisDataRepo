Abstract
As Denmark is increasingly moving towards intermittent energy and looking to build
large energy islands, the need to meet energy demands efficiently and cost-effectively
at all times becomes more challenging. This thesis explores this problem by developing
a Mixed Integer Linear Programming model to maximize the economic potential of
Power-to-X storage solutions. It uses data from the Danish energy zone DK2 and his-
torical electricity prices, integrating it with the potential wind generation of a proposed
energy island.
Hydrogen production and thermal energy storage were the technologies chosen for the
energy island due to their technological maturity and the geographical constraints of
Bornholm. Three overall storage cases are considered: one with a smaller capacity of
100 MW and 10 hrs of storage, one with a larger capacity of 1 GW and 24 hrs of storage
and finally an increased wind scenario with 1 GW and 24 hrs of storage. These cases
were further broken up into scenarios where hydrogen was sold at different prices. The
simulation results indicate a strong need for large installations of storage technologies
to stabilize the grid during periods of low wind and avoid unnecessary curtailment of
wind power during periods of high wind. Energy pricing plays the deciding role in
the financial viability of storage projects. In scenarios where hydrogen pricing follows
projections for price per kg, where the selling price declines over time due to techno-
logical advancement, it is not competitive with energy sold at the spot market price
for electricity.
The study concludes that policymaking will decide the way to go for the energy market,
as both technologies appear competitive with each other. Their performance metrics
are similar, with their choice of the pricing route for the energy sold. Hydrogen had a
significant disadvantage due to clean water consumption, where further research would
be needed to affirm the real-life viability of the project at a large scale. Similarly,
additional profits can be leveraged from thermal energy, which could assist the large
Danish district heating system.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
As climate change increasingly affects the globe, climate disasters worldwide have been
increasing in frequency and scale. In 2023, a new record for category five storms was
achieved, the first time ever with a recorded category five storm in each southern
ocean[1]. To combat these extreme changes, reaching Net Zero Emissions sooner rather
than later becomes ever more important. The U.S., European Union and China, the
biggest polluters, along with 70 other countries, have made an agreement for NZE by
2050, as defined by the Paris Agreement[2]. Reaching this target will require extensive
rethinking of how humans interact with the planet. All of our production and con-
sumption must be well balanced to achieve this goal.
In 2004, the UN released a report titled ’Who Cares Wins’[3]. The report focused on
recommendations by the financial industry on how to analyze environmental, social and
governmental issues for asset management and securities brokerage. Historically, finan-
cial investment has been made on the basis of expected returns, where investor profit
has been the focal point. The report discusses how for investment opportunities to be
viable, there must be a healthy market to invest into, which ultimately can only come
from a sustainable planet where resources are used appropriately. New technologies in
the energy sector are big opportunities for this kind of investment. Future energy is-
lands promise to deliver clean energy at amounts needed to overhaul the worlds energy
dependencies, while being competitive on prices with conventional energy.
The energy sector is major contributor to pollution, as today most of our energy
production comes from non-renewable sources, accounting for about 80% of total con-
sumption[4] worldwide. Denmark is slightly ahead here, with 57% of energy coming
from non-renewable sources, improving rapidly yearly[5]. However, to reach the targets
set by the Paris Agreement, more needs to be done. Electrification of the energy sector
will be paramount[6], and therefore, new technologies and solutions need to be explored.
Between 2010 and 2022, there has been a 60% increase in wind power capacity, in large
part fueled by offshore wind power plants[7]. This large increase in renewable energy
production can help Denmark achieve its climate targets, with further projects planned
in the North Sea with a capacity of 10 GW and the Baltic Sea as used here as a refer-
ence with a capacity of 3 GW[8]. The plan for the energy islands is to produce many
gigawatts of renewable energy primarily through wind energy, strengthening the green
transition significantly, with upwards of 20 GW of OWPP planned in the Baltic Sea
by 2030[9]. The islands will also incorporate storage options, to further enhance their
flexibility and utility. This increased power will be able to power millions of homes
in Denmark and abroad, but only if the rest of the power grid is upgraded to match
this increase in generation. In 2023, nine percent of offshore wind power produced in
Germany was curtailed[10]. Similarly, Denmark could previously not export as much
energy as it was generating, which led to windmills being stopped often, to lower gener-
ation. In 2023 this problem was largely solved by Germany upgrading their electricity
grid[11]. As intermittent energy generation is set to increase, simply transporting en-
ergy longer distances will not be a viable solution, therefore necessitating large scale
storage.

Danish energy security is very high.
In the past 5 years, the average time danish
residents are without electricity, is 20 minutes. The majority of these failures come
from local distribution issues, and not the generation itself[12]. This is possible due
to sufficient capacity in the grid, and well integrated supply options from neighbour-
ing countries. To maintain this energy security in an increasingly intermittent supply
chain, as renewable penetration increases, large scale energy storage becomes necessary.
1.2
Background
The energy island at Bornholm is still in a tendering stage, with potential bidders being
invited for further dialogue. The project is likely to be centered around constructing
3 GW of offshore wind power near Rønne, Bornholm, with a potential for a further 3
GW planted east of Bornholm in the future.
The focus of this project is best evaluate how Power-to-X can support large scale off-
shore wind power projects meeting electricity demand. The Bornholm Energy Island
project is used as the case study, as the projected wind generation capacity of 3-6 GW
may at times exceed the electricity demand of the DK2 zone alone, meaning storage
options and distribution will be important to maximise the energy supply potential of
the energy island.
The focus of the project will be on securing medium and long term grid stability,
therefore the technologies chosen must support this.
Figure 1: Capacity and Storage Duration of Technologies, cite: [13]
Figure 1 compares different technologies on their efficiency, capacity and storage time.
As the project is located near and at Bornholm geographically, the technologies eval-
uated will also have to make physical sense for this area. This means pumped hydro
storage will not be evaluated. The focus will be on hydrogen production and molten

multiple GWh size. Alkaline electrolyzer hydrogen production is considered a mature

costs in the technology, though investments will drive future costs down massively.
1.3
Methodology
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate whether energy storage can be cost-
effective and technically efficient to support the offshore energy Bornholm island. To
answer this question, the primary research question of this thesis can be outlined as
follows:
What is the optimal Power-to-X storage solution to best support Energy island Born-
holm, to meet climate goals and maintain reasonable energy costs?
How will the Power-to-X technologies proposed facilitate these achievements?
To best answer these questions, a MILP model of Energy island Bornholm with off
shore wind generation, demand from the DK2 zone and potential storage solutions will
be developed, with the goal of optimizing revenue from the storage solutions. The mod-
elling focus will be operational constraints and expenses, to compare results between
scenarios, to best predict what investments are needed to support a secure transition
to renewable, intermittent energy sources.

The main contributions of the thesis can be outlined as follows:
1. Storage Technologies: Comparison between different technologies available for
large scale energy storage.
2. Modelling of System: Building the mathematical model.
3. System Optimal Problem Formulation: Objective to maximize net present
value of assets, through optimal sizing parameters of potential storage technolo-
gies.
4. Optimisation Solver: Implementation of mathematical model in Python with
Gurobi Optimizer 10.0 used as solver.
5. Case Study of Energy Island: Integrate constructed model with real-life
scenarios of the Bornholm energy islands for case study.
6. Sensitivity Analysis: Analyze technical and economical performance of the
energy island and storage means.
For this thesis, reports from the Danish Energy Agency, International Energy Agency
and other sources have been analyzed to gain accurate understanding of development
and deployment of energy islands as well as supporting technology. Extensive research
has been performed on means of power generation and storage, to obtain parameters on
state of the art technology, with proven maturity. In the modelling of the offshore wind
power plant and storage assets, some simplifications are applied to the technologies, to
ensure linearity of the model. As the main focus of the thesis is economic and technical
planning the energy storage assets for Energy Island Bornholm, some aspects of the
system’s dynamic stability study will not be investigated. Python was used to develop
and simulate the model, due to the availability of open source software and solvers
specifically for linear problems.

As wind power is a vary varied power source, the use of historical data is required, to
predict potential power extracted at given locations. The website ’Renewables.ninja’[20]
allows us to access the MERRA-2 historical wind dataset from all over the world, and

2.1.2
Hydrogen Production and Storage
Hydrogen production is a growing industry, with huge potential as a means of storing
energy. The large interest in hydrogen comes from the high energy density of hydrogen
Figure 3: Energy Density of Various Fuels, cite: [22]
In figure 3, it is shown hydrogen has a very high gravimetric density, more than any
other fuel widely used. This is especially useful when storing the hydrogen, as a large
amount of energy can be stored at a comparably small area.
Electrolysis
For this project the focus will be on hydrogen produced by electrolysis of water, using a
renewable energy source, and therefore called green hydrogen. Electrolysis is a process
of splitting water particles using electricity, to get separate hydrogen and oxygen atoms:
2H2O(l) →2H2(g) + O2(g)
(1)
Equation 1 shows mole requirement for complete electrolysis. There is a requirement
of two moles of water, to produce two moles of hydrogen and one mole of oxygen. The
mole weight of water is 18 grams, with hydrogen weighing two grams per mole. This
means to produce a kilo of hydrogen, 18 kilos, or 18 liters of water is needed.
Electrolysis is a very power intensive way of producing hydrogen, and therefore well
suited to connection with large scale renewable energy production. As hydrogen is a
portable fuel, it has many uses:

Therefore, investment in electrolyzer technology and storage may still be of interest,

Electrolysis can be done using different methods, however for this project the focus

As seen in figure 5 using a cathode and anode, water is split using electricity. Dur-

and two moles of hydroxyl ions. The hydroxyl ions are then transferred through the

Fuel Cell
In order to use the produced hydrogen to stabilise the electricity grid, incorporating
a way of burning the produced hydrogen must also be considered [26]. Fuel cells are
used to access the energy stored in hydrogen Fuel cells can essentially be described as
functioning oppositely of an electrolyzer. Like an electrolyzer, it is an electrochemical
energy conversion device, where an input of hydrogen is converted to electricity, heat
and water[27].
Figure 6: Overview of a Fuel Cell, cite: [27]
Fuel cell are explained by figure 6. First hydrogen atoms flow into an anode, where
they are stripped of their electrons. The positively charged protons then pass through
the membrane while the negatively charged electrons are passed through a circuit, gen-
erating electricity. After passing through the circuit, the electrons combine with the
protons and oxygen, creating water and heat. As the whole process is a reaction, not
a combustion, there are no byproducts besides the electricity, water and heat. This
makes fuel cells able to produce very clean electricity. Operational aspects also make
fuel cells very beneficial. They are quiet, fast responding and scalable, making them

Storage
The ability to efficiently and cheaply storage large amounts of hydrogen will be impor-
tant for overall viability. As the focus is on large scale compressed hydrogen storage,
there are three categories of storage[28]:
• Storage vessels
• Geological storage
• Other underground technologies.
Due to Bornholm’s geography not being suitable for geological storage, the focus will
be on storage vessels. Storage vessels can be broken up into types:
• Type I - These pressure vessels are constructed from carbon steel and low alloy
steel, used mostly for industrial and commercial applications. Hydrogen is stored
at a pressure of up to 500 bar.
• Type II - These containers are made with a thicker load-bearing metal liner, steel
or aluminium. This liner is then wrapped with a fiber resin composite, allowing
for storing hydrogen at higher pressures, up to 1000 bar. This greatly increases
the
• Type III - These containers contain a thin metal liner, but with a thicker fiber
resin composite. In this type, the metal line only supports around 20 % of the
pressure load, with the composite handling the remaining 80 %. This makes it
much lighter than previous types. Type III can withstand pressures of up to 450
bar.
• Type IV - These vessels are typically constructed with a fully polymer liner,
wrapped with a fiber resin composite. These containers are very light, and can
handle storage pressures of up to 1000 bar.
The storage needed for this project is stationary storage, therefore the lightness of type
III and IV is not needed, and they would add needless cost. Gravimetric density is also
not a big consideration, as there are no size constraints. Therefore the chosen storage
type will be the cheapest for large scale applications, being the type I storage. The
lower storage pressure comes with many advantages compared to storing the hydro-
gen at higher pressures. Safety and lifespan of components are typically increased, as
the system stresses are lower. Hydrogen takes a tremendous amount of energy to pres-
surize, so lower pressures decrease overall system losses from having to store the energy.
2.1.3
Thermal Energy Generation and Storage
Thermal storage means storing energy as heat. Traditionally thermal storage has been
coupled with concentrated solar power, due to the intermittent nature of the power
source, just like wind generation. The technology can broadly be split up into four
different categories:
10
==PAGE:19==
Mads Thorsen, s160132
DTU Institute of Wind and Energy Systems
• Sensible heat storage - tank thermal storage, molten salts, underground thermal
storage.
• Latent heat storage - ice thermal storage, sub-zero temperature phase-chasing
materials.
• Thermochemical heat storage - chemical looping, salt hydration.
• Mechanical-thermal coupled systems - compressed air energy storage, liquid air
energy storage.
For this project the focus will be on sensible heat storage, using molten salts. This
type of thermal storage was chosen on the basis of its proven track record for cheap
and efficient energy storage, such as the Andasol solar and thermal combined power
plant in Grenada, Spain[29].
The basic principle for thermal energy storage is as follows:
Q = m · cp · ∆T = m · cp · (Thigh −Tlow)
(2)
Where m is the mass of the medium, cp is the energy density and ∆t is the temperature
range between the hot and cold storage tanks.
Formula 2 shows improvement is possible for future generations of thermal storage.
Improvement in materials and heat management techniques will increase thermal ca-
pacities and heat differences, allowing for a higher storage of energy per physical space,
which will become useful as the technology is expanded upon, and reaching storage ca-
pacities in the GWhs in the future[13].
The overall cycle of molten salt thermal storage is shown in the following figure:
Figure 7: General Energy Cycle of Molten Salt Thermal Storage, cite: [30]
Figure 7 shows how energy is collected from a source, is transferred into an electrical
heater or heat exchanger of some type. This is then used to heat a tank filled with
an heat storage medium, here a type of salt. Then when energy demand is high, the
stored thermal energy is used to heat water, generating steam to drive a turbine, pow-
ering a generator. The cooled medium is then transferred back through the initial heat
exchanger, restarting the process.

==PAGE:20==

Choosing a good medium for storing heat is vital for the reliability, low maintenance
and high efficiency[31]. The following aspects are important when choosing a suitable
salt:
• Thermal stability limit - The higher the thermal stability limit, the higher tem-
peratures the molten salt power plant can be run at. A high temperature increases
efficiency, while not breaking down.
• Specific heat capacity - A high heat capacity allows for the storage of more energy
per volume of material.
• Thermal conductivity - Higher thermal conductivity ensures better heat transfer,
meaning higher heat exchanger efficiencies.
• Density - The amount of energy that can be carried by the molten salt directly
relates to the density of the salt, where higher is better.
• Viscosity - Viscosity determines how difficult it is to pump the medium around the
system. Lower viscosities typically display lower thermal conductivities, therefore
the value is a compromise.
Suitable salts for the storage could be sodium hydroxide NaOH, sodium nitrate NaNO3
or potassium nitrate KNO3[32].
Technical Properties
The modelled molten salt storage system will be based on MAN Energies specifications
of their molten salt storage system[33]. The daily thermal loss rate will be set at 0.3%

3.1
Mathematical model
To evaluate the storage technologies, a model will be constructed. This model must
take different aspects of each technology into account when evaluating them against
each other. Overall the goal is to maximize the economic potential of a storage tech-
nology. This will be done by storing energy when there is a surplus of wind energy
generated, and discharging at periods the wind power is insufficient to meet demand.
The program can buy electricity from the grid at wholesale prices during periods where
neither wind energy nor stored energy is enough to make up a deficit. As the storage
technologies have a large upfront investment costs and yearly maintenance costs, the
total storage capacity installed must be carefully matched to forecast demand, elec-
tricity prices and wind generation.

present value is used to evaluate investments over their lifetime, using today’s money.
As a potential investment, its value today is more useful to investors than its value in
ten years money[37]. Using the net present value of a project also allows investors to
compare different project, thereby allowing prioritization for stakeholders. An advan-
tage of NPV is the ability to adjust for risk based on the discount rate. Even if the
discount rate is high for a project, a high NPV means it may still be financially viable,
due its resilience to future uncertainties.
Net present value can have three different results:
• Positive NPV - Present value of cash inflow is higher than present value of cash
outflow, and the asset is considered a good investment.
• Zero NPV - Present value of cash inflow is equal to present value of cash outflow,
and the asset is considered a good investment.
• Negative NPV - Present value of cash inflow is less than present value of cash
outflow, and the asset is considered a bad investment.
The maximum economic potential of the power plant will be defined as the highest
possible net present value over its lifetime of the storage options described. This evalu-
ates the income stream of the asset, while subtracting the capital costs and operational
costs. The capital costs are the initial investment costs, while the operational costs are
the necessary costs for continuing maintenance of assets.

other operating costs, and vice verse. This means the financial feasibility of a project

4.2
Results and Discussion
From the results of table 9 and 10, the largest determining factor for the net present
value is the selling price for discharged electricity. In the scenarios where stored hy-
drogen is sold at set prices, declining over time, the net present value is always much
smaller than the comparable thermal energy scenario. When sold at equal prices, hy-
drogen is the preferred option due to its ability to be stored without loss over time,
meaning it can be sold at maximum quantity when prices are high, which is optimal
when a program is optimizing for economical viability.
From equation 1, it was established for every mole of hydrogen produced, a mole of
water must be consumed. Therefore, for every kg of hydrogen produced, nine kgs of
water will be consumed by the electrolysis process. In the scenario where hydrogen
is sold at a variable electricity price with a capacity of 1 GW charge time and up to
24 hr storage time, a total of 28,913 GWhs of hydrogen is produced. The electrolyzer
produced hydrogen at the higher heating value at 54.9 kWh per kg, therefore:

From equation 8, it is established that over the 20 year period, the amount of water
consumed in the highest possible scenario is 4.73 billion liters, or an approximate aver-
age of 230 million liters a year. Bornholm has a total water production of 3 million m3
of water yearly, which would mean the electrolysis process would consume up to around
8 % of Bornholm’s yearly production. This could prove problematic for Bornholm’s
water security, as this is quite a large amount of the reserves allocated. This could
potentially be alleviated through investment, though this would increase the costs of
hydrogen production for energy storage. As Bornholm is an island, there is easy access
to unlimited amounts of seawater, which could solve any water resource constraints.
However, there are still many constraints still present here, and the technology will not
mature for commercial applications for a number of years[40].
The results showed a greater grid contribution when the installed power is larger. DK2
demand is regularly over 2 GW, so grid stabilizing assets must have high power capac-
ity. From table 3 and 2, thermal power equipment scales drops far more in price than
total hydrogen power equipment costs do. While storage prices are cheaper for hydro-
gen in large capacity scenarios, overall thermal storage had the lowest capex, meaning
it may be easier for investors to stomach the large upfront costs. Thermal power was
25% cheaper comparing the technologies in their largest, most effective scenarios, as
seen in table 10.

In all cases and scenarios, the levelized cost of energy is far lower than the average

also allowing for a lower electricity price for the consumer.
Due to computational limitations, some real world aspects were simplified in order to
get an answer. One aspect was the operation of a hydrogen electrolyzer. Electrolyz-
ers can not simply turn on and off, or ramp immediately.
This requires extensive
planning of operation, as otherwise lifetime would be severely shortened, and output
decreased[41]. Lifetime of an electrolyzer is typically 80,000 hrs, with the hydrogen
preferring scenarios having a usage time between 40,000 to 50,000 hrs. From this in-
formation, it can be estimated that in a real world application of an electrolyzer of
this scale over 20 years, there would need to be a full electrolyzer replacement, perhaps
multiple. This is the most expensive part of the hydrogen production system, and
would therefore significantly increase the operational costs of the asset[23].

5
Conclusion
5.1
Summation of Thesis
The summation of the thesis is based on the question posed in section, which was:
What is the optimal Power-to-X storage solution to best support Energy island Born-
holm, to meet climate goals and maintain reasonable energy costs?
The offshore wind facility will greatly support our green energy systems. Energy stor-
age plays a crucial role in maximizing renewable energy integration by enabling a steady
and reliable power supply. For Energy Island Bornholm, two potential storage tech-
nologies (Power-to-X and salt thermal storage) are being investigated. Evaluating the
data from the MILP program shows both technologies can be viable but are sensitive
to their circumstances. In the initial small capacity case, both technologies are invested
in fully but deliver little results to overall grid stability due to their limitations in ca-
pacity. In the second case, there is a bigger impact on the grid due to the increased
capacity of the storage options. At charge and discharge levels of 1 GW, there is a
noticeable benefit to the DK2 zone, greatly supporting the energy island. In the third
case, there is a lot of excess wind, allowing for maximizing both storage technologies,
no matter their price profile.
Using the wind generation and demand data, there does not appear to be a need for
seasonal storage, in the same way there would be if the energy island relied on an in-
termittent energy source like solar energy. As the wind power generation often exceeds
electricity demand, the potential storage time is less critical. Therefore, thermal energy
would overall be the more competitive storage option, due to a lower need of resources
and investment costs. At a 0.3 % daily decline, thermal storage holds onto energy
sufficiently for the purposes of stabilizing the energy grid in normal circumstances.
Therefore, overall energy security is maintainable using energy islands and Power-to-X
storage options, which will be necessary for a transition to renewable energy sources.
Hydrogen energy is a more stable energy source, but it requires too many resources
from local availability to be reasonable to produce large amounts. Overall, it is slightly
better for energy security as it does not decline over time and can support the grid for
longer periods. However, the results from the study cases do not indicate a huge dif-
ference here compared to thermal storage. Compared to demand and storage options,
the sizing of the renewable energy source dramatically influences the results.
As wind generation capacity and storage capacity increase, so does the possibility of
selling hydrogen at projected prices. Large-scale energy islands will also have a sig-
nificant effect on energy prices. In the study cases, there were some periods when
electricity prices were negative, as production was far higher than demand. This sce-
nario will likely increase in the future, as seen from the graphs in this thesis.
To
accommodate this, Denmark will establish a DK3 zone to handle this problem. Ro-
bust power purchase agreement contracts will be necessary, as negative prices greatly
influence the financial viability of large-scale Power-to-X storage facilities.

5.2
Limitations and Future Work
Having more computational power for future work would have been advantageous.
Running the program at a higher time resolution could mean having a better under-
standing of model behaviour, and its influence on system stability. A more realistic
modelling of storage technology behaviour may have made for a more detailed result.
System stability would have been possible to evaluate if the reaction time of the tech-
nologies had been considered. Furthermore, maximum charge and discharge rates of
Power-to-X is usually connected to the size of the storage capacity, while in the model
the maximum charge and discharge rate is set to a fixed value, no matter the chosen
capacity.
