Abstract
From the last decades of the 20th century, global warming and the effects that humans
have on the environment have drawn great attention from people, governments and
associations. International organizations such as the UN, are looking for compromises
from the international community to face the climate emergency, promoting actions such
as the Kyoto Protocol [1] or the Paris Agreement [2].
The focus lies on the industry, on transportation and in the way the energy is
produced, which are the main sources of greenhouse gas emissions [3].
Due to this
concern, one of the strategies that is followed is the generation of energy in a cleaner
and more responsible way, trying to reduce the amount of energy coming from fossils
resources, and increasing renewable energies, such as photovoltaic (PV) panels or wind
turbines. However, a change in the way of producing energy implies a change in the
way the grid is controlled. The reasons for this change are due to the fact, first, that
solar and wind energy have an associated uncertainty due to the intermittency of the
resource; and second, because if the generation of power at a node is higher than the
consumption, the power would flow in the opposite direction, and over-voltages may,
hence, occur.
This thesis studies the effects that a large penetration of PV panels have on the
distribution grid, and what actions the Distribution System Operator (DSO) can take
to guarantee security, trying to avoid solar energy curtailment. It models the behaviour
of a distribution grid, which could be from a city or town, assuming that a large number
of PV panels are distributed through different points of the electrical system.
The methodology followed in this thesis is first, the comparison of three different
optimization tools: AC Optimal Power Flow (AC-OPF), Second Order Cone (SOC)
relaxation, and LinDistFlow approximation, and following this, the choice of the most
optimal one for the scenario of this thesis. After the method is selected, the model
needs to include the uncertainty associated to PV panels, otherwise, the security of the
grid could be compromised. This problem is addressed in two different ways: a Chance
Constrained Optimization, and a Distributionally Robust Optimization, which are later
compared. The algorithms throughout the thesis are applied to 3 different grid models,
and for the analysis with uncertainty, real data from NREL between the years 2008 and
2017 is used.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
From the industrial revolution, the development of society is associated to an increment
in the energy that is utilized. It improved the average life quality, but it also involved a
raise in the resource consumption. Historically, the energy came from the combustion of
fossil fuels, such as coal or gas. However, the climate emergency related to the increment
of CO2 levels, as well as the limit of the reserves have been pushing the international
community to find cleaner and renewable alternatives.
The inclusion of sustainable energies in the grid is nowadays a main policy for most
of the governments, as seen in Figure 1.1. In particular, the number of photovoltaic
(PV) and wind plants that are being installed is growing due to the decrements of prices
and improvement of the technology. This, however, creates new challenges and issues
that must be solved in order to ensure the security and power quality of the grid.
In particular, this thesis studies how a great penetration of PV panels affects the
distribution energy system, and the tools that can be used in order to analyse the system,
and the actions to confront the problems that arise from it. It could potentially be used
to operate the system by the Distribution System Operator (DSO).
Figure 1.1: Renewable energy in the world. [4].
The world is being equipped with an increasing number of solar power plants. In
the European Union, by the end of 2018, the installed capacity was 114 GW, and it is
growing every year [5]. As a result to the decrement of the price of technology, more

owners are installing panels at their roofs, creating a grid of distributed photovoltaic
generation throughout cities. Although this is positive regarding the amount of energy

changes in PV generation during cloud transients. This transients can produce extreme
variations in the voltage levels, and imply a greater risk of failure in the grid. As an

compensated by other plant. Afterwards, when the value of the irradiance rises again,
the plant that provided with the additional power that was not generated by the panels
must be reduced, so the the excess of power is avoided.

A secondary effect of high penetration of PV is the reversal of the power flow. In
the hypothetical case that the PV production was larger than the demand, the power
would not flow from the substation to the load as it has traditionally been, but in the
opposite direction, like in Figure 1.4. This could imply over voltages at certain buses,
or over currents in the lines, which may have not been considered in the original design
of the system.
One of the proposed solutions is the implementation of fast-reacting, VAR-capable
PV inverters that can balance the reactive power, providing security and stability [7].
The ability to control the reactive power injected at each node provides an alternative

1.1
Literature review
The Optimal Power Flow (OPF) problem is a tool used to solve the power system
operation and planning. It solves the system operation regarding a specific goal, like
minimizing the total costs for grid operation. Three approaches are taken to obtain the
status of the grid: AC Optimal Power Flow (AC-OPF) [8], Second Order Cone (SOC)
relaxation [9] and LinDistFlow approximation [10]. Each of them gives a solution to the
problem in a different way that is studied throughout the thesis.
The inclusion of PV panels has an associated uncertainty.
In [11], the Chance
Constrained (CC) formulation assumes that a certain amount of constraints violations
will take place. However, it is considered acceptable if the number is below a tolerance
value. Otherwise, the operation of the system would become excessively expensive or
it would need to curtail renewable energies that contain uncertainty.
The Chance
Constrained Optimization assumes that the statistical distribution of the uncertainty
follows a normal distribution, and in consequence it is possible to calculate new limits
for each of the variables to operate safely with a confidence level.
However, the approach of Mieth and Dvorkin in [10] disagrees that the normal
distribution approach is convenient for real data and recalculates the limits of the
variables applying the Distributionally Robust (DR) Optimization. This method needs
read data to use it as forecast, which is taken from NREL [6].

In this thesis, first, the physical distribution grid is explained, and three different grids
that have been used are introduced, as well as the approximations to convert the grid
into a model. Then, three suitable formulations of optimization tools are identified. In
particular, the non-convex non-linear method called AC-OPF is studied, as well as a
convex reformulation of it known as Second Order Cone relaxation, and a final linear
approximation, the LinDistFlow formulation. The three methodologies are compared
and the conclusion drawn obtains the most suitable tool.
The next step includes the implementation of uncertainty in the grid, the changes
experienced in the nodes and how to act to minimize the effects. Two different methods
address this problem:
the Chance Constrained (CC) Optimization, which assumes
that the error follows a normal distribution, and the Distributionally Robust (DR)
Optimization, that responds to a collection of real data forecast.
Lastly, simulations are run in order to obtain the status of the grid if both approaches
are applied, and a comparison is carried to draw the final conclusions.

CHAPTER 2
Distribution grids
In this chapter, an overview of distribution grids is given. The three grids that have
been used throughout the thesis are presented and explained, as well as the short line
approximation made to compute the calculations.
2.1
Grid structure
The distribution grid is the stage of the electrical system that provides the energy at
customer-usable levels to homes and industry, reducing the voltage compared to the
transmission grid. It includes the substations, lines and protections to operate safely. In
these substations, the voltage is reduced in different stages, from ranges of 26-69 kV, to
2.4-33 kV [12]. The topology is radial, it draws power from one point of the transmission
grid, a substation also called the root node, and distributes it using diverse branches
and sub-branches. The specific feature of this type of grid is that the branches are not
connected between them, so no loops are formed. There is only one possible direct path
from the root node to the load. This implies that the number of lines are N-1, where N
is the number of nodes of the system.
In transmission lines the energy is transmitted at very high voltages (400 kV), so
the losses associated to the currents are small, around 2%-3% [13].
However, the
transformation of the power implies a decrease in the voltage and an increase in the
current, as it happens in distribution grids.
Therefore, if the current increases, the
losses increase too, rising at approximately 5%-7% [14]. In order to reduce such losses,
one of the solutions is the installation of distributed generators throughout the line, like
PV panels, so the power is consumed in close proximity to where it is being produced.
However, these benefits are not seen if the energy has to travel long distances, or if the
power is being fed into the transmission grid. Using an optimization tool, the resulting
operation could potentially promote the use of renewable and cheaper energies close
to the nodes, compared to the energy coming from combustion of fossil fuels from the
substation.
Figure 2.1 and Figure 2.2 show a diagram of two radial grids developed specifically
for this project.
They are used to confirm the correct performance of the different
algorithms applied in the thesis. Bus 1 is referred as the root node, and it is connected
to other nodes through lines, which have a certain impedance.
Once the algorithms are tested and the behaviour is the one expected in each of the
cases, a larger grid is used. It is shown in Figure 2.3, and it corresponds to a modified
version of IEEE123 grid, that only has 114 buses. A bigger modified version can be seen

Short line approximation
Lines that are shorter than 80 km are considered short lines, so to ease and reduce the
computation of variables, an approximation is done. It is widely used in the industry,

The circuit in Figure 2.4 is the model of a distribution line, which assumes that the

the end where they are: the sending end (VS, IS) or the receiving end (VR, IR).

CHAPTER 3
Grid analysis
A variety of ways to analyze the state of the grid are presented. Each of them have
advantages and disadvantages that lead to a comparison of the features of each of them.
In principle, the balance generation-demand can be solved by an AC Power Flow. This
method ensures that the generation matches the demand while keeping the variables
within the limits. However, the optimal solution is only obtained using optimization
tools. The solution is considered to be optimal when it minimizes the objective function,
which could be the minimization of the losses or the minimization of the price. The
tools that are studied and compared are first the AC Optimal Power Flow; second the
implementation of a Second Order Cone relaxation to obtain convex constraints; and
last using a linear approximation known as LinDistFlow approximation.
3.1
AC Power Flow
The power flow is a tool used to obtain grid parameters such as the voltage, voltage angles
and the power flows through a network in which the loads and the power injections
are known [16].
It shows the status of the nodes in steady state, no dynamics are
considered. To achieve this it takes into consideration physical, operational and technical
constraints, like Ohm’s and Kirchhoff’s laws, operational limits of generators, loading
limits of transmission lines, etc, and use numerical analysis techniques [8]. This analysis
can vary depending on the author, and it could be done using the currents instead of
the powers, but those are dependent on the voltage angle, whereas power injections are
completely independent. Besides, if power is integrated respect to the time, it allows a
simple computation of the energy consumed.
The AC Power Flow problem is a feasibility problem: it concludes if the system
demand can be satisfied by the supply, ensuring Equation 3.1, and taking into account
the physical limitations of the components and electrical laws.
Si = SG,i −SL,i
(3.1)
Where Si corresponds to the apparent power at each of the nodes, G is the generation at
the specific node, and L is the load. They can be decoupled in active (P) and reactive
(Q) power as Equation 3.2 and Equation 3.3.
Pi = PG,i −PL,i
(3.2)
Qi = QG,i −QL,i
(3.3)

The problem has four variables for each of the nodes: active power P, reactive power

Based on the elements that are connected to the node, it is possible to distinguish
three different types of nodes to determine the variables that are fixed [17].
• PQ buses, usually used for load nodes, in which active and reactive power are
known.
• PV buses, which are determined by active power and voltage are used for generation
buses. The generators typically impose a voltage of 1 p.u. at the node where they
are connected.
• Slack bus, which is usually the reference bus, so the voltage angle and magnitude
are fixed. They typically have a generator attached to them or correspond to the
point from which power is drawn, like the substation in distribution systems.
This problem is the base for the AC-OPF problem that is later solved in section 3.2,
including the objective of minimizing the total price of the operation of the distribution
grid.
3.2
AC Optimal Power Flow
The power flows show the state of the grid at a certain time, it contains prefixed values
of generation power to supply the loads. These states are countless, as many different
combinations of power generation may be possible. However, there is one solution that
is optimal regarding each specific configuration. The analysis applied is called Optimal
Power Flow, and obtains the optimal result, if it exists, in terms of an objective function.
This problem is widely used in the industry to solve the system operation.
The OPF is a non-linear, non-convex problem whose goal is to minimize the objective
function f(x), where x is the active power generation PG [8] . The active power has an
associated price c, so in conclusion the objective is to minimize the operation cost of

The AC-OPF is a mathematical problem that is non-convex and non-linear, similar
to the one shown at the right side in Figure 3.1. Non-convexity and non-linearity are
further explained:
• Linearity in the problem comes from the linear relationships between the variables.

• Regarding convexity, a function is convex if the segment that links two different
points of the graph of the function is above or contained in the graph [18]. An
interesting property associated to this is that the minimum point from the function
will always be a global minimum.
In the optimization problem, the desired solution is the global minimum, and no
local minimum should be selected as the optimal solution. Therefore, if the solver that
is being used is not non-convex, there is no guarantee that the solution found is going
to be the global optimum. This is the source of time consuming algorithms that try
to guarantee the optimal solution of the problem, and the reason behind the search of
faster alternatives.

3.3
Second Order Cone Relaxation (SOC)
As discussed before, the AC-OPF problem is non-convex, which could lead to a non
global optimal solution. Other strategies are presented as alternatives to look for the
global optimal solution [9].
• Use an approximation on the power flow equations.
• Search the local optimum.
• Relax the constraints to make it convex.
The first approach, the approximation of the equations, neglects conditions of the
problem to make it simpler. The assumptions of the simplifications typically eliminates
quadratic terms if the outcome does not differ significantly. Therefore, by definition
the solution is not going to match the solution from the AC-OPF. However, depending
on the approximation, it can be close enough to it to be valid for certain applications.
Two examples of this method are the DC Optimal Power Flow and the LinDistFlow
approximation, which is studied in section 3.4.
In comparison, the third option, the relaxation of the constraints converts the non-convex
problem into a convex one, so the solution that is found can potentially be the same as
in the AC-OPF.
In this section, the third approach is taken, and the constraints are changed in order
to convexify the problem. This implies that the local minimum that is obtained in the

convexified problem is also the global minimum of the problem. Figure 3.2 shows a
graphical representation of how the original function f(x) has the same global minimum
as the convexified ˜f(x).
Figure 3.2: Example of convex relaxation. Credits [8].
Relaxing the constraints to convexify and solve the OPF problem provides several
advantages. First of all, if the solution exists, it is possible to check if it is globally
optimal. This check is done by using the solution from the convexified problem in the
non-convex, and evaluate if both results are the same. Secondly, if there is a solution,
but it is not globally optimal, it will provide a lower bound for the optimal solution.
Finally, if the solution of the problem is infeasible, it certifies that the original problem
is infeasible too. In conclusion, the Second Order Cone relaxation can be used not only
to find a solution in a faster way, but also to ensure that the solution from a non-convex
solver has achieved the global optimum, or to classify complex problems as infeasible
taking less computational resources.

Analytical comparison
The three analysis have common elements. For instance, the three of them have the
same objective function, the objective is to reduce the final price of the operation of the
grid. The lines are limited by the same electrical limits like Equation 3.7, Equation 3.8
and Equation 3.35. The generators have the same maximum and minimum values, and
the root node enforces a voltage of 1 p.u.
The differences between them are focused on linearity and convexity, as a result of
reformulating constraints and making approximations.
The AC-OPF problem is clearly non-linear and non-convex as seen in Equation 3.6,
Equation 3.7 and Equation 3.8.
These equations impose equality constraints that
must be fulfilled in order for the problem to be feasible, but due to the way in which
variables are multiplied, non-linearity and non-convexity are implied. Compared to SOC
relaxation, the non-convexity is given by Equation 3.23. However, the relaxation of the
constraint in Equation 3.27 eliminates the equality constraint, obtaining a second order
cone relaxation. In this case, the minimum that is obtained, if it exists, is both local and
global due to the convexity of the problem. If it matches Equation 3.23, the solution is
therefore optimal for the original problem, and otherwise it provides with a minimum
limit. An infeasible result also involves that the original problem is infeasible. Finally,
the LinDistFlow approximation obtains linearity by neglecting the quadratic terms, in
this case the losses of the lines. However, if it is compared to the DC-OPF which also
neglects losses, the LinDistFlow approximation calculates active and reactive flows, as
well as voltages, due to the great variations of these terms in distribution lines. The
solution obtained will be a lower value than the AC-OPF for not taking losses into
consideration, but it is acceptable while the final value is found within tolerance limits.

CHAPTER 4
Uncertainty inclusion
In every system, parameters are usually inaccurate, or may be affected by perturbations.
This implies that the parameters have a certain degree of uncertainty by default. However,
this fact does not change the objective of trying to obtain the optimal point of any system,
and in consequence, uncertainty must be modelled [22].
The strategies that can be followed are multiple. As an example, one option would be
to design the system to be prepared for any possible scenario. In reality, this would mean
an infinite number of possibilities, which could turn into an expensive system, or one
in which no resources that contain uncertainty (PV and wind) are used. On the other
hand, not being prepared for any inconvenience means to let the system collapse, which
results in a useless system. In conclusion, there must be a balance between the possible
cases that could affect a system, and the cost of them.
For this purpose, multiple
approaches are available, like stochastic methods.
However, the strategies that are
implemented are the Chance Constrained Optimal Power Flow and the Distributionally
Robust Optimization.
4.1
Chance Constrained Optimal Power Flow
The Chance Constrained Optimal Power Flow (CC-OPF) is a model that considers
the probability of constraints being violated with a certain probability, that can be
summarized in Equation 4.1.
P(F(x, ξ) ≤0) ≥1 −ϵ
(4.1)
This equation expresses that the probability of a certain function F, that includes the
uncertainty modelled by ξ not being violated, must be above 1-ϵ, where 1-ϵ corresponds
to the confidence level that is assumed for that system. To do so, the limits of the
system in which it can operate are tightened, so when the unexpected process occurs,
the resulting state in contained within the limits with the desired confidence level. Due
to the tightening of the limits, the optimized values obtained for this problem are not
going to be the same as the ones obtained previously, as the solution space is smaller.
Therefore, the implementation of strategies to increase security of operation have an
increment in the cost of the system.
If this methodology is applied to the radial grid, the solution of the OPF takes into
consideration the satisfaction of physical limits of the distribution system, the principle
of balance between supply and demand, as well as it allows a certain number of violations.
The sources of uncertainty in the system are the result of many random processes: the

6.1
Conclusion
This thesis provided an overview of some optimization tools that are available to analyze
the status of the electrical distribution grid and to operate it in a safe manner when it
has a great penetration of PV panels.
First it introduced three different methods to solve the optimization problem. The
first one was the AC-OPF, a non-convex and non-linear problem. A non-convex solver
was used to obtain the result of the optimal point of operation the system. The results
were satisfactory but the computational time that took was longer compared to the other
two methods.
The second method was a convex relaxation of the AC-OPF problem, the Second
Order Cone relaxation. In this case the problem was reformulated into a convex one,
so the solution, if found, was the absolute and local minimum of the problem, since by
definition there is only one. Then, if the solution of the problem was satisfactory for
the non-convex scenario, the solution was optimal. If it was not the same, it provided
a lower bound. If the problem was, however, infeasible, then it guaranteed that the
non-convex problem was infeasible too. The results showed that for this specific problem,
the solutions matched the ones obtained in the AC-OPF problem in a faster way.
The third approach was to transform the problem into a linear one. For this purpose,
the losses were not taken into consideration, although it was necessary to compute the
voltages and the reactive power flows, so the DC-OPF was not satisfactory for this
case. Therefore, the applied tool was the LinDistFlow approximation. As it was an
approximation, the result would never match the one obtained form the AC-OPF, but
due to the better performance regarding the computational time and the consideration
that the obtained solutions were within the range of tolerance, it was selected as the
tool to continue the implementation of the next steps.
The main point though, is the analysis of the grid that contains a high percentage
of distributed solar panels.
As it was discussed, the PV panels have an associated
uncertainty regarding the power production due to the dependency of stochastic variables
such as the appearance of non-forecast clouds, which has an impact on the power
production, and therefore on the voltage levels at the node.
To be sure that the

the Chance Constrained optimization is applied. It took into consideration the random
errors assuming they were following a normal distribution.
However, as seen from the data from NREL, the solar irradiance does not follow a
normal distribution, so the results could be unexpected. For this case, the Data Robust
Optimization is selected, which used the available information about the irradiance of
the past 10 years from June to August.
These cases were compared and tested with real data and with the normal distributed
error.
The first conclusion that is drawn from this is that the models can have a
satisfactory performance for very specific cases, like the one in this thesis, in which
most of the power that was introduced in the PV panels was included in the normal
distributed model. That is considered as the reason for the good results in the Chance
Constrained Optimization.
However, the solution from the Distributionally Robust
Optimization applied to the real data is more exact, as it is precisely calculated for
it. The importance of the precision is related to the final price of the solution. It is
true that for the optimization problem, only the active power had a price to calculate
the total operation. However this is not true in reality, and the limits imposed by the
Chance Constrained Optimization required more reactive power from the distributed
generator, which would increase the final price.
As a second conclusion taken from the study of the CC and DR approaches, in
general the DR optimization tool is more flexible to take into consideration very different
values, as it is not fixed to the normal distribution.
If the data from NREL had a
second collection of values close to zero, the normal model would not be able to respond
sufficiently well to this circumstance due to the poor similarity, and the DR solution
would be vital to keep the violations within the limits.
The last conclusion of the thesis is the importance of planning correctly the amount
of energy that is being produced in new scenarios. The installed capacity of solar panels
and wind turbines is introducing huge amounts of uncertainty, and it is increasing each
day. If no actions are taken to satisfy a safe operation, the quality of the grid could
be compromised, and most probably renewable sources would be curtailed to ensure
the correct functioning of the system. This case is not desired, as it would mean the
generation of power by other sources like coal or gas, implying an increment in pollution
and price. All things considered, it is definitely a mayor and interesting challenge for the
present and future DSO, that will certainly shape the future of how energy is produced
and consumed.
6.2
Future Work
The different approaches to the analysis of the grid and the forecast of uncertainty, the
numerous possible scenarios, and the development of new technologies present more
paths for research and raised more questions for an optimal operation of the grid under
different circumstances. Some steps that could be taken in the future are presented.
==PAGE:65==
6.2 Future Work
53
As a first step, models could approach to a more realistic case. For example, shunt
capacitance could be included in the problem. For this thesis, as a simplification method,
it was considered that the distribution system would only be formed by overhead lines,
so they could be approximated thanks to the short line approximation. However, in
Denmark most of the distribution system is through cables, where the shunt capacitance
must be taken into consideration.
As a way to study the grid under different conditions, the data collected from NREL
could be the one corresponding to the spring season, in which rainy weather is more
common, to study and show how the Distributionally Robust Optimization adapts to
the situation and provides with safe limits that the Chance Constrained Optimization
is not able to give. This was not done during this thesis due to the necessary time to
collect and process new data.
It has been shown how the substation is in charge of regulating the power misalignment
produced by the uncertainty. This implies that every line and node in the path from
the node that contains the extra power, to the substation, is affected, which has a direct
effect on the new calculated limits. It would be interesting to use the inverters from
the PV panels to be the ones that regulate the power, changing the ratio of active
and reactive power that they can provide.
This implies the appearance of different
operation points for the panels, because they would need to have freedom to provide
or curtail power generation in order to compensate the system. This would of course
widen the voltages at which the nodes can operate compared to the ones in which only
the substation was compensating, because the power would not cross the whole grid to
reach the specific node.
Related to the previous point, the development of residential battery energy storage
systems (BESS) is stimulating self-consumption, and it is going to be a powerful tool to
regulate the system and to help to avoid the curtailment of solar energy. If the grid has
BESS distributed in some nodes, it could provide security against uncertainty, as well
as a less costly solution thanks to the storage of cheap energy.
Finally, it would be interesting to study the applicability of the formulations and
the results to meshed grids, in which the nodes are connected forming loops. In this
scenario, the power can flow through more than one path, so most probably the problem
should be reformulated.
