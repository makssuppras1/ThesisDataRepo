Abstract
A new school reform from 2014, which aims at implementing more activities such as
playing in an orchestra into the school day, leads to a greater demand of rehearsal spaces.
Possible rooms for this could be school gym halls. Within the current study, acoustic
conditions of gym halls used for practicing are investigated and possible improvements
provided. Three halls were visited, where rehearsals took place. The musicians’ impression
in the respective halls was obtained by means of a questionnaire. Measurements were
carried out in all three halls. The gym hall in Hanssted Skole served as calibrating the
simulations, which are carried out in ODEON. A standardized gym hall (l:20 m, w:10 m,
h:5 m) was formed and investigated using ODEON according to the dimensions of the
visited gym halls. A size study was conducted to test to which extent the dimensions
of the halls could be altered, so that a change in acoustic is not noticed by the listener,
indicated by the just noticeable difference (JND). This should imply that the later applied
improvements in the standardized gym hall will also provide better acoustic conditions
for different dimensions. A change of the dimensions to +5 m or -1 m in length and width
with respect to the reference dimensions can not be perceived by the listener. Changing the
height had much more inﬂuence on the perceived acoustics. Three different improvements
were implemented in the simulation. Closed curtains, a carpet and absorper panels were
applied and their acoustic investigated by carrying out simulations in ODEON. It was
found out that curtains provided the best reverberation time according to the desired values.
A small listening test was conducted, presenting the sound ﬁles of the standardized gym
hall with and without the applied acoustic treatment, obtained by auralisations, to ten
people with musical background in order to obtain their preferences. Unfortunately, no real
trend could be obtained. For future studies the acoustic conditions of more gym halls used
as rehearsal spaces should be obtained and measurements implementing the suggested
improvement carried out. Rehearsals with the applied acoustic treatment should take place
and the musicians’ opinions collected.

Preface
This project is the documentation of a Master Thesis Project in Enginnering Acoustics
at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The project durated from January 2015
to September 2015 and was supervised by Cheol-Ho Jeong (Assoc. Professor), Mads
Bolberg (NIRAS) and Sune Wedam (Professional Musician).
The Master Thesis project was motivated by the arising demand of rehearsal spaces due to
the new school reform in 2014, which aims to include leisure activities (such as orchestra
playing) into the school day. Possible rooms could be gym halls, which might not obtain
the ideal acoustic for rehearsal purposes. Halls are investigated during measurements and
simulations are carried out in order to provide solutions.
I would like to thank Cheol-Ho Jeong and Mads Bolberg for their supervision during this
project and for their support when questions arised. Furthermore, I would like to thank
Sune Wedam, who helped me ﬁnding the three investigated halls. Thirdly, thanks to the
people that helped me carrying the measurement equipment to the different places.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In 2014, a new school reform was introduced to the Danish public school system. Longer
school days arised including more time for academic development and new learning
strategies [4]. The concept of an open school was introduced, which called for more collab-
oration with local sports clubs, cultural centres and other associations. Furthermore, with
the new concept schools were expected to include these leisure activities into the school
day. The aim is to strengthen local cohesion and to present as well as familiarise pupils
with the various offers of the associations and opportunities they have. The Folkeskole and
the municipal music and arts school have to agree to collaborate and ﬁnd a way to realize
the collaboration. [5].
Due to the new reform, a greater demand of rehearsal and performance spaces arises.
Orchestras and classical bands have to draw on the school facilities. Since orchestras and
classical bands hardly ﬁt in a normal classroom, it is most likely that the rehearsals and
performances will take place in the gym or the assembly hall of the school. The current
study will focus on the acoustic conditions of gym halls and potential solutions to improve
them. The reverberation in gym halls is mainly fairly ok for classical music, whereas other
rooms in the school, like classrooms provide often a far lower reverberation time.
Nevertheless, the acoustic in gym halls is not made for an orchestra to rehearse or perform
in. Flutter echoes and other negative acoustic behaviour may occur and make it difﬁcult to
enjoy playing in the orchestra.
Within this Master Thesis the acoustic in gym halls is investigated. Potential solutions
are provided to improve the acoustics in a gym hall. Once the measurements are done, a
simulation model is carried out in ODEON to calibrate the simulation. A standardized
gym hall model is made according to the visited gym halls. The potential solutions are
applied in this hall and the resulting change in the acoustics studied.
The second chapter provides the description of the measured acoustic parameters. Fur-
thermore, a small literature research related to the topic is given. Since the gym hall is

converted to a rehearsal or performance space, the literature mainly concerns improve-
ments in rehearsal and performance spaces. Some reference values for obtaining a good
acoustic condition in a rehearsal room or a performance space are given. It has to be
noted that these reference values mainly account for special volumes and different types
of musical uses, like ensemble play, rehearsing, performing or a certain type and size of
the orchestra. The volume of a gym hall can neither be adjusted for the use of rehearsing
or performing and the type and size of the orchestra. Therefore, the reference values will
only give an approximation for optimal acoustic conditions.
For the measurements, different halls are obtained. The main idea is to measure three
different gym halls, which are also used as rehearsal or performance spaces. A professional
musician was consulted to help ﬁnding these halls. Unfortunately, the search of respective
halls was difﬁcult. Only one typical gym hall could be obtained. The other two halls
are an assembly hall and a small rehearsal space. The assembly hall is regulary used for
rehearsing. The small rehearsal space is rarely used for yoga and dancing classes as well.
A description of these halls can be found in the third chapter. For this study, only the gym
hall is considered. The other two halls are seen as examples for rehearsal or performance
spaces, corresponding to their main use. Furthermore, a questionnaire is handed over to
the musicians and the results are presented as well in this chapter.
The measurement setup and procedure are described in the fourth chapter. The results of
the investigations are discussed.
The investigations of the simulated gym halls are presented in chapter ﬁve. This includes
the calibration of the gym hall in Hanssted Skole, to examine how well the simulation
ﬁts the measurement. Furthermore, the forming process of a model that represents the

2.1
Room Acoustic Parameters
To obtain the acoustic conditions in the measured halls, different room acoustic parameters
have to be chosen in order to investigate the halls properly and to improve them according
to their use. As mentioned before, the purpose of this Master Thesis is to provide some
ideas for improving school gym halls while using them as a rehearsal space for orchestras
or classical bands.
Therefore, also parameters describing the musicians’ needs are considered next to the clas-
sical parameters like reverberation and early decay time. These parameters are described
in the following.
2.1.1
Reverberation Time, RT
The reverberation time is the standard parameter when it comes to describing the acoustic
in a building or room. According to the ISO 3382-1:2009 "‘Acoustics - Measurement of
room acoustic parameters -Part 1: Performance spaces"’ [6] the reverberation time T30 is
chosen for further use. This value describes the time it takes for the sound level to decrease
by 30 dB after a continuous sound source has been switched off. The interval from which
T30 is calculated is -5 dB to -35 dB below the start value of the decay curve. [7]
The suggested way to present the T30- value according to the ISO 3382-1:2009 [6] is to
average this value over the mid-frequencies, which are the frequencies of 500 Hz and
1 kHz.
The reverberation time is an important objective parameter for describing the acoustics
for the use of rehearsing or performing in a space. A very short reverberation time would
lead to a too dry and dead sound of the music. This is due to the early decay of the sound,
3
==PAGE:22==
4
CHAPTER 2. THEORY
which could be obtained for example in rooms with very absorptive materials included.
However, if a room is large and the materials of the walls, ceiling and ﬂoor are reﬂective,
the sound is travelling long distances before it actually gets absorbed. Therefore, the sound
decays much slower than in smaller, more absorptive spaces, which could result in an
interference with the following direct sound. The sound would be masked and, as a result,
the music would often sound very blurry and muddy. [8]
The desired reverberation time depends on the purpose of the room and varies with the vol-
ume of the space, as well as the music group and the purpose of rehearsing or performing
in the room. Some reference values will be stated later in Section 2.3.
2.1.2
Early Decay Time, EDT
The early decay time, EDT, is also a measure of the rate of decay. Unlike the reverberation
time, it evaluates only the ﬁrst part of the decay rate and is therefore affected by the levels
of the early reﬂections. This is the reason why the EDT value varies a lot over the different
measured positions. The interval from which the EDT- value is calculated is 0 dB to
-10 dB. The early decay time is then the time it takes for the sound level to decrease by
10 dB after a continuous sound source has been switched off multiplied by a factor six.[7]
Even though the T20- and T30- values are the most common values for estimating the
reverberation time, the early decay time (EDT) obtains a better correlation with the rever-
berance perceived during speech and music. This is due to the fact that running speech and
music contains short breaks. These breaks might be masked due the longer rate of decay
in the reverberation time parameters T20 and T30. The fact that the reverberation time is
still the basic and most important objective parameter is due to the general relationship to
other room acoustic parameters. [7]

In this section some literature related to the Master Thesis topic will be introduced. It
has to be noted that literature according to the acoustic of a gym hall for the purpose of
rehearsing or performing for classical music was not easy to ﬁnd. Therefore, literature
about rehearsal rooms for classical music, performance spaces like concert halls, and the
improvement of these spaces for the orchestra was considered.
Gígja Gunnlaugsdóttir investigated within a Master Thesis Project the "‘Optimization of
Acoustic Conditions in Music Practice Rooms"’. She stated that the acoustic conditions in a
music practice room were of great importance and that the perception of optimal conditions
varied from musician to musician. Furthermore, the conditions also depend on the type
of instrument the musicians play. To optimize the acoustic conditions in a music practice
room, Gígja Gunnlaugsdóttir simulated several music practice rooms in an anechoic room.
In order to have a reference for the simulations, measurements were carried out at the
Royal Danish Academy of Music and the simulated rooms were tried to behave as close
as possible to the measured reference practice rooms. To evaluate the different simulated
rooms, ten musicians were asked to play in the different setups of the practice rooms and to
fullﬁll a survey according to their impression and preferences of these music room setups.
Finally, the values of the objective and the subjective measures were compared in order to
==PAGE:25==

investigate if a relation between them exists. The optimal acoustic conditions for a music
practice room were then withdrawn from the comparisons. It was found that musicians’
preferences of the acoustic conditions correlated less than Gígja Gunnlaugsdóttir had
expected them to. Therefore, she tried to compare the musicians’ opinions of the different
instrument groups. However, it was still difﬁcult to obtain overall preferences for optimal
acoustic conditions in music practice rooms after grouping the musicians by instruments.
It is therefore advised that more musicians should take part in the experiment and that
different instrument groups are studied more carefully. This way, the corresponding prefer-
ences and needs of a music practice room for the different instruments can be found out. [8]
Within the Master Thesis Project "Acoustic Conditions in Small Musical Venues" by
Hallur Johanessen, acoustic properties of a group of small venues for rhythmic music
performances, more precisely, the music genres Jazz and Rock, were investigated using the
room acoustic simulation software ODEON. Furthermore, measurements were obtained
in three different venues (volume: 490 m3, 690 m3 and 1050 m3) in order to get fairly
accurate simulation models.
Listening tests were executed to judge the acoustic conditions for the simulated situations.
Within the listening test, the subjects had to rate "Clearness", "Sharpness", "Loudness",
"Spaciousness" and "Nearness" of the room. The tests should help to point out the physical
properties of importance in these venues.
The acoustic parameters studied in the measurements, as well as in the simulated models,
were the reverberation time T30, the early decay time EDT, clarity C80, Deutlichkeit D50
and the central time Ts.
Hallur Johanessen found the subjective parameter "clearness" to be the most important
one in small venues for rhythmic music performances. Since no correlation between the
subjective parameter "clearness" and the objective parameter "clarity" C80 was found, C30
was introduced. With C30 a good correlation with "clearness" was obtained. [9]
Since Johannessen’s Master Thesis dealt with the music genres Rock and Jazz and this
Master Thesis focuses more on classical music, the results are not of prime importance.
Marc Aretz and Ralf Orlowski studied within a cooperation between the Institute of Tech-
nical Acoustics, RWTH Aachen and Arup Acoustics the sound strength and reverberation
time in small concert halls. The investigations are presented in a Journal Article in Applied
Acoustics named "‘Sound strength and reverberation time in small concert halls"’.
Measurements in six different concert halls in Cambridge, UK were carried out and the
acoustic characteristics of the halls investigated. In particular, the reverberation time
as well as the sound strength were measured. Besides, some of the concert halls were
equipped with variable acoustics and their effect of these systems on reverberation time

and sound strength were studied.
Especially bigger ensembles produce a high sound level. Therefore, the sound level in a
small concert hall should be reduced, which can be done by decreasing the reverberation
time in a hall with ﬁxed volume. Since the different types of music groups need differ-
ent reverberation times, the reverberation time can not be reduced randomly. A balance
between the reverberation time and sound strength should be kept, so that a sufﬁcient
reverberation time arises, whereas excessive loudness should be avoided.
From the measurement results, it was conﬁrmed that the sound strength and reverberation
time are clearly correlated. With the use of variable acoustics like curtains or absorbing
panels, the sound strength as well as the reverberation time can be adjusted and reasonable
means are provided. Improving both reverberation time and sound strength independently
is not possible using the variable acoustic features. This is due to the fact that an increase
of absorbing surfaces in the hall reduces both parameters. From the presented results
it could be concluded that additional design parameters besides the reverberation time
and volume can have an impact on the total sound level. The location and distribution of
absorbing materials and features to improve the diffusion might be an option. This, as well
as their relation to the objective room acoustic parameters, should be studied more. [10]
The study of Magne Skålevik ("‘Rehearsal room acoustics for the orchestra musician"’)
dealt with the difference of the acoustic between the rehearsing and performance spaces
of orchestra musicians. Skålevik investigated the proper acoustic of a good rehearsal
room for the musicians. As mentioned in the article above [10] proper reverberance as
well as proper loudness, related to the sound strength, should be obtained. This causes a
conﬂict between the rehearsal and the performance spaces. If the reverberation time of
the rehearsal room is the same as in the performance space, the rehearsal room would be
too loud, whereas a sound strength G or the support ST considered to be good for the
performance space would be too dry for the rehearsal room.
Magne Skålevik stated that stage acoustics, rehearsal room acoustics, performers percep-
tion and the perceived reverberation are all related. Furthermore, he stated that it is more
important for the orchestra musician to hear its own instrument rather than the others. This
may be a new key to investigate the mechanisms, which have long-term effects on the
development of sound levels, the style of playing and the noise exposure of an orchestra.
Within some simulations with models of rooms and ensembles it could be obtained that a
reverberant sound, which had less impact on the sound pressure level at the musicians’ ear,
could prompt the musician to play louder.
The Foreground Level (FGL), described as the sound level that is perceived at one’s own
ear from the direct sound of the own instrument, which is determined by inherent instru-
ment properties like directivity and source-to-ear distance, is investigated. Furthermore,

the Background Level (BGL), the level at the own ear consisting out of the direct and
reverberant sound of the other instruments and the reverberant sound of the own instrument,
which is determined by the instruments nearby and the reverberant sound from the whole
orchestra, is studied. The Foreground- Background Balance FBB is the result of the
substraction of FGL minus BGL.
Magne Skålevik found out that the foreground-to-background balance is sensitive to room
acoustical conditions. If the background was too strong, the musician tended to play louder.
Another observation was that the smaller the ensemble, the more reverberant was the
sound, which has an impact on the background.
Furthermore, a distinction between the direct and the indirect effect of the room response
has to be made. The direct effect of the room response leads the musician to play louder
when the room response is weak. A strong room response causes the musician to play
more quiet. The indirect effect leads to stronger reverberant sound from an ensemble and
therefore making the individual play louder in order to improve the FBB. [11]
Another study dealing with the acoustic in music rehearsal rooms is described in the article
"‘Music rehearsal rooms: loudness levels and quantity of absorption as a function of use"’.
It states that loudness is probably even more of concern for music rehearsal rooms than
reverberation. This leads to the study of the quantity, as well as the location, of the acoustic
absorption in order to get a sufﬁcient acoustic for music rehearsal rooms.
A case study of various rehearsal spaces was executed. Within this case study it was
observed that walls leaning forward increase the sound level signiﬁcantly in the lower part
of the room. One rehearsal space was equipped with a leaning backward reﬂecting panel,
which led the room to sound bigger, since the sound is sent to the upper part of the room.
Moreover, the effect of an increase of the ceiling height was studied. It was found out that
the height can have various multiple positive inﬂuences on the acoustic qualities. These
inﬂuences are for example an increased acoustic volume, which leads to a potentially
decreased loudness. The increased ceiling height is preventing the strong reﬂections and
less absorption on the ceiling is needed, since the distance between the musicians’ ear
height and the ceiling is increased. With a higher ceiling it is also possible to get a more
reverberant volume above the musicians’ ear height. This leads to an increase in the
loudness level at the musicians’ ear due to directed reﬂections towards the upper part of
the room.
Smaller rehearsal spaces need to be more damped than bigger ones. It is also more effective
to place the absorption close to the source of sound, which is mainly in the lower part of
the room. A distinction between the acoustical needs of loud and softer instruments should
be made, since more damping is needed for louder instruments and less for the softer ones.
With a decrease of the sound level in the lower part of the room and an increase of the

Anders Christian Gade underlines the statement that sound levels are of high importance
when the acoustic of a music rehearsal or teaching room is evaluated. This is due to the fact

giving concerts. This means that the main noise dose is consisting of the practicing and

In the article "‘Experiences in acoustic design of rooms for music rehearsal and teach-

investigated that the predicition of the preferences of the musicians in the speciﬁc rooms is
very difﬁcult. In larger rehearsal rooms, the focus on the sound exposure level showed a
preference towards lower stage support parameters, mainly for musicians playing in loud
ensembles. To improve the acoustic design of rehearsal spaces, close involvement of the
musicians in the designing process, whenever it is possible, is suggested. [13]
In "‘Music Rehearsal Rooms Compromising between Quality and Investment"’, Peter H.
Heringa gives acceptable compromises between quality and investment of smaller and
bigger rehearsal spaces to achieve a good quality.
To get a good compromise in a smaller rehearsal room, Peter H. Heringa suggests a
sufﬁcient height and a compact volume to achieve good ensemble conditions. Also the
dimensions of the room are of great importance and dimensions of 3 m x 4 m x 5 m are
preferred compared to similar dimensions, since they lead to the same eigenmodes in the
different directions. The size of the rehearsal rooms is limited, which leads to an increased
loudness, therefore absorption has to be added. If the room is small, a shorter reverberance
is desired. Still it has to be kept in mind that a too short reverberation also leads to an
insufﬁcient acoustic quality.
The absorption material has to be chosen carefully within the lower frequencies. If the
absorption in the low frequency part is too low, distortion can be caused as a result of
eigenmodes.
Some rehearsal spaces have only a limited space for absorption. In these cases, a special
absorber was designed, which is effective in the lower and higher frequencies and has a
limited depth of 10 cm. This absorber consists of a low frequency panel absorber made
of a foil on a void of 0.1 m covered with mineral wool, which is effective in the higher
frequencies. The absorption should be well distributed alongside the walls and ceilings,

NS 8178:2014 Acoustic criteria for rooms and spaces for music
rehearsal and performance
In 2014, the new norwegian standard NS 8178:2014 was formed. This standard deals
with acoustic criteria for rooms and spaces for music rehearsal and performances. The
norm gives advices on how to obtain good acoustic conditions in music rehearsal and
performance spaces according to the type of music and room. The type of music is divided
into acoustic loud music, acoustic quiet music and ampliﬁed music. Brass bands (25-35
musicians), wind ensembles (20-80 musicians) and big bands (15-25 musicians), but also
symphony orchestras including brass and woodwind, opera singing and percussion fall in
the category of acoustic loud music.
Music, which is primarily performed by quieter instruments or singing is described as
acoustic quiet music. Choirs (30-50 singers), vocal ensembles, performing/show groups,
string orchestras (20-40 musicians), stringed/plucked instrument groups (guitar without
ampliﬁcation) are considered as acoustic quiet music.
Ampliﬁed music includes every type of music, which is ampliﬁed, like pop- or rockbands,
jazzbands, vocal groups, big bands and musicals, etc., every type of music, where the
sound is spread primarily by the ampliﬁer.
Furthermore, the different types of rooms are divided into a small practicing room (for
practicing alone or teaching), an ensemble room divided into a small, middle (three to 12
musicians) and large ensemble room (even more musicians), a showroom or performance
hall and a multi purpose hall.
According to the type of music and room the norwegian standard provides various criteria
to obtain a sactisfactory acoustic in these rooms according to their usage.

within the category acoustic loud music in a big ensemble room. Therefore, the stated
acoustic criteria for this category are considered as the reference values. The following

4.1
Measurement Procedure
The measurement procedure was examined according to the ISO Standard 3382 [6] and
will be explained later.
The used equipment is listed below:
• Omnidirectional Source (Dodecahedron)
• Omnidirectional Microphone (Brüel & Kjær)
• Microphone Pre-ampliﬁer (Nexus Brüel & Kjær)
• Loudspeaker ampliﬁer
• Measurement Laptop including DIRAC Software
• Brüel & Kjær 2250 Sound Level Meter
The microphone was calibrated before each measurement using a calibrator. The back-
ground noise level was measured with the sound level meter before starting the measure-
ment. The signal played back over the loudspeaker had to be at least 40 dB higher than the

halls using ODEON
ODEON is a room acoustics software which is using hybrid methods for calculating. It
consists out of a ray tracing algorithm, combined with the image source method. Later, the
secondary source method and the vector based scattering were implemented in order to
improve the software and its calculations.
The basic of the software is formed by the ray tracing method and detects relevant im-
age sources for certain source positions for early reﬂections. The image source method
constructs specular reﬂections geometrically and describes the early directional energy
in the simulated room with nearly no scattering on the surfaces. This method alone is
geometrically too precise and needs a high calculation time. Combined with the ray tracing,
unsigniﬁcant sources are not considered, which leads to a decreased calculation time. To
get more precise calculation of the late reﬂections, the secondary source method was
implemented. Within this method, the rays are handled as carriers of patches of acoustic
energy. After each reﬂection of the ray, the energy is reduced according to the applied
absorption coefﬁcients of the surfaces. The location of the secondary sources during the
ray tracing process is within each reﬂection point. The data of the energy of the observed
secondary sources will then be taken from each reﬂection point. With the vector based
scattering method the scattered and specular directions of the reﬂected rays during the
ray tracing are combined. With this method it is possible to obtain reliable results, if the
surfaces are assigned with a typical moderate scattering coefﬁcient. [16]

To get reliable results within the simulation the same source and receiver positions taken in
the measurements were adopted. The simulation results were analyzed like the measured
results using MATLAB and the average of each source for all receiver points were taken.
This was very time consuming due to running the point- responses after each change,
saving the new calculated data and analyzing it via MATLAB before comparing it with
the measured results. Therefore, the ﬁrst step in the calibration process was to use the
"‘Global Estimate"’ function. With this function, the reverberation times T20 and T30 are
estimated globally. A source is randomly sending out particles in different directions.
These particles are then reﬂected using the "‘Vector based scattering metod"’. The energy
loss in each particle is recorded as a function arising due to the absorption of the surfaces
and the air. The global energy decay function consists of the sum over many particles. By
using the "‘Global Estimate"’ function it is possible to get a reliable value for the overall
reverberation time. Still, the local behaviour of the room can not be considered using this
function. [17]
After getting a good ﬁt with the measured results using the "‘Global Estimate"’ function,
the local behaviour of the room is taken into account using the responses from the sources
to receivers. As explained at the beginning of the chapter ODEON uses a hybrid calcu-
lation method in order to get the calculations from the point sources. Different acoustic
parameters can be displayed from this calculations.

Variable acoustic systems could be used to improve the acoustic conditions in gym halls. A
model in ODEON was made in order to test potential improvements. The new norwegian
standard NS 8178:2014 [1] provides solutions to obtain a better acoustic condition for
the respective volume and type of music. For a volume of the standardized gym hall and
orchestras and classical bands, which fall in the category of acoustic loud music, ceiling,
wall and bass absorbers, as well as diffusors, if wanted, are suggested for improving the
acoustic. The variable acoustic systems are chosen having in mind that the main purpose
of the gym hall is doing sports activities. Therefore, the treatment has to be easy to apply
and to remove. It was decided to implement closed curtains, a carpet and a special absorber
as improvements into the simulated hall.
The simulated standardized hall, introduced in the previous Section 5.3.1, represents an
average gym hall. Three types of variable acoustic systems (curtains, carpet and absorber
panels) are applied in this model. Since the main focus was to reduce the reverberation
time, the effect of applying a reﬂector into the hall was not studied. As suggested by
the norwegian standard NS 8178:2014 [1], a diffuser could be used for obtaining better
acoustics. It is geometrically very difﬁcult to implement a diffusor, which is used in real
life, into the simulations. Therefore, this improvement was not taken into account.
The main focus is to improve the acoustic condition for the musicians playing in a gym
hall. Gym halls will be used mainly as rehearsal spaces and a better acoustic should be
provided to adapt the criteria a rehearsal room needs. The source-receiver positions in

6.1.2
Discussion of the results
According to the norwegian standard NS 8178:2014 the suggested reverberation time value
for a volume of 1000m3 should be within 0.9 s to 1.3 s. The sound strength G should obtain
a value around 15 dB. The reference values given in Section 2.3 account for chamber
music performed in a volume of 2500 m3 with 300 seats. Therefore, these values are not
appropriate for the volume, the type of music and the use of the gym hall as a rehearsal
space. The norwegian standard provides the reference values for reverberation time and
==PAGE:79==
6.1. APPLIED VARIABLE ACOUSTIC SYSTEMS
61
sound strength in unoccupied conditions according to rehearsal purposes. Therefore, the
focus lies mainly at the reverberation time T30 and the sound strength G. For rehearsals a
clarity value of +1 dB to +5 dB is preferred by most conductors [15]. The conditions with
the orchestra area applied will not be compared to the reference values in the following,
since these values account for unoccupied conditions.
The standardized gym hall obtains a T30- value of 1.78 s (see Table 6.6), which is too high
for rehearsing purposes. The sound strength G with 14.57 dB is very close to the suggested
one, which is 15 dB. The main complain of the musicians rehearsing in a gym hall was the
too reverberant sound. Therefore, the focus lies on lowering the reverberation time with
the applied acoustic systems.
The clarity C80 of the average gym hall obtains a value of 3.48 dB, which lies in the range
of the preferred clarity during rehearsing. With a value of -11.18 dB the STEarly represents
a good value.
Looking at the results in Table 6.6 shows that applying the absorption area of an orches-
tra already reduces the reverberation in the hall. For the average gym hall without any
improvements the T30-value can be reduced by around 0.23 s, which results in a value of
1.50 s. Due to the decrease in reverberation time the clarity C80 and the sound strength
G are reduced. The C80-value of 4.37 dB presents a satisfactory value for rehearsing. A
value of 13.39 dB can be obtained for the sound strength G. A STEarly-value of -12.33 dB
is achieved.
Closing the curtains in the gym hall has a huge effect on the reverberation time. The
T30-value is lowered to 1.08 s, which is within the suggested range of the reverberation
time for rehearsal spaces. The resulting sound strength G with a value of 13.87 dB is still
close to the reference value of 15 dB. Also a satisfactory value of -10.78 dB for the STEarly
is obtained. The clarity C80 increases with the reduced reverberation to a value of 6.80 dB,
which may already be perceived as a a too sharp sound.
If the absorption area of the orchestra is included in the model with the closed curtains, the
reverberation time decreases even further. The sound strength G is reduced to 12.80 dB.
The clarity increases to a value of 7.03 dB, which might lead to a too sharp sound. With a
value of -10.78 dB, the STEarly obtains a satisfactory value.
Covering most of the ﬂoor area with a carpet, leads to a decrease in the reverberation time
of 0.3 s compared to the standard value without improvements and applied orchestra area.
A T30-value of 1.48 s is obtained, which does not lie in the suggested range for rehearsing.
The clarity with a value of 4.49 dB lies within the preferred range for rehearsing. The
sound strength G with a value of 13.79 dB is lower than the suggested value of 15 dB, but
it is still close. The STEarly with -12.39 dB obtains a satisfactory result.

Auralisations of the applied

It is possible to make auralisations for single point responses in the different models using
the room acoustic software ODEON. Binaural room impulse responses (BRIR’s) are two
channel ﬁlters and used in ODEON for auralisation purposes. A mono signal goes through
the two channel ﬁlter from the sound source(s) to the left and the right ear entry of the
receiver, which represents the listener. The mono signal is convolved with the BRIR and
results in a binaural signal. A listening experience similar to the real room can be achieved,
if the binaural signal is played back over headphones. Multi-channel simulations can be
obtained via mixing the binaural signals of different source positions and signals. For the
multi-channel simulations only one receiver position and orientation can be simulated.
[17].
Head related transfer functions (HRTF’s) are needed to generate binaural simulations.
Each person has different HRTF’s. With ODEON it is possible to measure the individ-
ual HRTF’s and import them into ODEON. For the auralisation purposes in the current
study, the supplied HRTF’s are used. For the headphone ﬁlter a generic ﬁlter provided by
ODEON is chosen [17].
Multi-channel simulations were executed using the three source positions as shown in
Figure 6.1(a). All three sources are omnidirectional, in order to take into account as much
as possible of the sound of the room. Another explanation for using an omnidirectional
source was that ODEON only provides very few sources with a directivity pattern of
special instruments.
One receiver position can only be chosen for the multi-channel simulations, so receiver
number four according to Figure 6.1(a) was used. This receiver should represent the
conductor.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion and Future Studies
This Master Thesis Project seeked to examine acoustic conditions of gym halls used for
rehearsals and performances. A new school reform from 2014 aims at implementing more
activities such as playing in an orchestra or classical band into the school day, which leads
to a greater demand of rehearsal spaces.
Three halls were visited, of which only one ﬁt the criterion of being a gym hall, where
rehearsals took place. Nevertheless, the musicians’ impressions of the acoustic in the
respective halls were obtained by means of a questionnaire. Possibly due to language
problems or misinterpretations respecting the questions, no clear results with regard to
potential improvements could be achieved. For future studies it is suggested to improve
the questionnaire by providing the musicians with a danish version, which also explains
technical terms more clearly.
However, the conditions of the three halls were measured. Even though the assembly
hall in Dronninggårdskolen and the rehearsal space in Kulturzonen were often used by
orchestras for rehearsal purposes, the acoustic was not ideal for this usage. For example,
the reverberation was far too high in the assembly hall, whereas the reverberation in
Kulturzonen seemed to be too dry. Compared to these two halls, the reverberation obtained
in the gym hall was within the reference values. Even if this is just one example, this may
indicate that gym halls could be a good option of providing the necessary rehearsal space.
Still, small changes with variable acoustic systems may improve the acoustic. Therefore,
different gym halls were visited to obtain the dimensions and the type of material of the
surfaces. Simulations in ODEON were carried out in a previously formed standardized
gym hall (l:20 m, w:10 m, h:5 m). Since the later applied improvements should not only
address the dimensions of the standardized gym hall, a size study was conducted. It was
tested to which extent the dimensions of the halls could be altered, so that a change in
acoustic would not be perceived by the listener, having the just noticeable difference (JND)
as an indicator. It was found that the dimensions could be increased by 5 m or reduced by
73
==PAGE:92==
74
CHAPTER 8. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE STUDIES
1 m in length and width with respect to the reference dimension of 20 m x 10 m. However, a
change of height had much more inﬂuence on the perceived acoustics. This investigation’s
result has to be taken with caution, since the reverberation did not always increment with
increasing volume as it was expected. Future studies should look at the different source
and receiver combinations to investigate the local behaviour and provide more detailed
explanations.
The new norwegian standard NS 8178:2014 [1] suggests acoustic treatment to the respec-
tive volume and type of music. For a volume of the standardized gym hall and orchestras
and classical bands, which fall in the category of acoustic loud music, ceiling, wall and
bass absorbers, as well as diffusors, if wanted, can improve the acoustic conditions in
the respective hall. Since the main purpose of the gym hall is doing sports activities, the
treatment has to be easy to apply and to remove. Therefore, it was decided to implement
closed curtains, a carpet and a special absorber as improvements into the simulated hall.
The applied curtains achieved the best result for the reverberation time, which were within
the range of the desired values. With the other implementations, the desired range of
reverberation time could not be reached. Still, the acoustic was improved. It has to be
noted that the results of these simulations only account for the speciﬁc type of curtains,
carpet and absorber panels, so they only give an approximation of how these implemen-
tations provide better conditions for rehearsing. Applying a diffuser as it is used in real
life was hard to implement in the standardized gym hall and thus not investigated. Also,
the change of the acoustic using a reﬂector was not studied. For future studies, these
two implementations could be taken into account for providing even more solutions for
gym halls used as rehearsal spaces. Furthermore, the absorption area of an orchestra was
applied in the standardized gym hall without the improvements included, as well as for
the conditions using the closed curtains and the absorber panels. It was examined that
including the orchestra area already reduced the reverberation a lot. In the current study,
only the size of an orchestra with 35 musicians was studied according to the orchestra
size, which was rehearsing in Hanssted Skole. The change of acoustic with the amount
of musicians playing in an orchestra might be investigated further in future studies. Still,
ODEON suggests speciﬁc absorption coefﬁcients for the orchestra area, therefore the
results also only give an approximation.
The improvements should help to provide the musicians with a better acoustic environment
in the gym halls. Therefore, auralisations of the sound of the hall with the respective
treatment and no treatment applied were made in ODEON. The resulting sound ﬁles were
presented to ten participants, which have musical experience, within a small listening test
in order to ﬁnd out their preferences of the sound in rehearsal spaces. Unfortunately, the
opinions differed a lot and no real preference of the acoustic condition with and without
the respective improvements applied was found. However, the rating of the different sound
==PAGE:93==
75
ﬁles is very subjective. Participants might have rated the different tracks according to their
preferred sound, but might not have in mind, that the criteria for the acoustic in a rehearsal
space differ from the acoustic in a performance space.
Within this Master Thesis Project the improvements of gym halls as rehearsal spaces
were only carried out with calculating simulations in a standardized gym hall. For future
studies it would be interesting to ﬁnd more gym halls used for rehearsing and investigate
their acoustic. Measurements with and without the suggested treatment should be done.
Furthermore, rehearsals including the treatment should take place and a questionnaire
handed over to the musicians to get their impression of the acoustic condition using the
different treatments to obtain their preferences respecting the improvements.
