Abstract
This master report continues the work in the ﬁeld of large scale topology optimization of
beam structures, e.g. wind turbine blades. The structural model presented uses a ﬁnite
element beam cross section analysis software to capture the physics of advanced beam
cross sections of various materials. Beam ﬁnite elements are integrated from the cross
sectional properties and assembled into a global system that is subjected to analysis. The
topology optimization design variables enter through the 2D cross section ﬁnite elements.
The report provides contributions to the existing framework: a method for structured
meshing of 2D wind turbine blade cross sections, implementation and validation of a two-
noded beam element including design variable sensitivities along with a benchmarking
of it and the existing four-noded element conﬁrming that it is a faster alternative, three
proposed ﬁlters for design variable ﬁltering that takes 3D eﬀects into account, and a range
of functions for use as objectives and/or constraints in topology optimization on the beam
level (compliance, displacements, eigenvalues, and static moment) that are validated by
comparison of the design variable sensitivities to approximated central ﬁnite diﬀerence
values.
A small scale example demonstrates the capabilities of the implemented beam element
and the functions on both structured and unstructured meshes. A prismatic, cantilever
beam with rectangular cross section is optimized for minimum compliance, minimum tip
displacement, and maximization of the fundamental eigenvalue subjected to a range of
constraints. The results are mainly well-known structures such as variations of I- and
C-beams.
The ﬁlters are compared through optimization of cross sectional properties of airfoil-shaped
meshes. A reference code is used to solve similar problems in order to establish a base
for comparison. The ﬁlters behave almost identically, and the results are very similar to
those produced by the reference code.
Finally, the size of the problems is increased signiﬁcantly, and the implementations are used
to optimize parts of the DTU 10MW reference wind turbine blade. The largest presented
design is optimized with 263200 design variables. Because of issues with convergence, the
designs are solved mainly for minimum compliance subjected to a range of constraints. The
optimized designs resemble shell-structures. They do not have shear webs like traditional
wind turbine blade structures, and the reason is not completely clear, but various sources
are suggested.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This thesis extends the work made at DTU Department of Wind Energy on structural
blade design. More speciﬁcally, it proposes an alternative method for wind turbine blade
design using topology optimization.
This chapter introduces the concept of topology
optimization, explains the motivation for using it in the wind energy industry, outlines
the project, and gives an overview of the thesis structure with resumes for each chapter.
1.1
The wind energy sector
Wind energy is spreading across the globe and more wind turbines are installed every year.
The global, annual wind energy capacity installed in the period year 2000-2015 is shown
in Figure 1.1. It shows that every year in the period, more capacity was installed than the
year before (with the exception of 2013). In other words the market is huge and increasing
almost exponentially. With the adoption of the Paris Agreement in the United Nations
[53] in December 2015 that sets targets for limiting global warming by introducing more
renewable energy, this development is not looking to change, although other sources, such
as solar energy, are also gaining recognition. Essentially, renewable energy sources need
to compliment each other in the event of outages (low wind, variation in daylight, etc.),
hence it is unlikely to see one source becoming dominant [29].
The increase in market size means increased competition, and wind turbine manufactur-
ers are constantly searching for new approaches for the design of the wind turbines of
tomorrow. Generally, all research in the wind energy ﬁeld is conducted with the purpose
of lowering the levelized cost of energy (LCoE), which is a metric used to compare the
energy prices across diﬀerent sources. Recent research examples includes ﬂoating wind
turbines [17], as the deep sea is a huge opportunity because of great wind conditions and
limited eﬀect on populated areas, and the Vestas multi-rotor wind turbine [55] that chal-
lenges the traditional one-rotor wind turbine design. Another aspect is the upscaling of
wind turbines. Wind turbines have been seen to increase in size, and one of the challenges
is how the gravitational loads scales with the size of the turbines. Although the aero-
dynamics scale neatly with increasing size, gravitational loads scale unfavourably, which
consequently requires the load-carrying structures to become lighter and stronger [26].
This creates a demand for alternative design processes, such as designing wind turbine
components using topology optimization.

1.2
Topology optimization
Topology optimization is a mathematical method that aims at optimizing the distribution
of material within a given design space to reach the best performance while subjected
to one or more constraints. The method can be applied for various purposes such as to
increase structural stiﬀness, reduce mass, or similar. A design can take any shape within
the design space, as long as the shape complies with the imposed constraints. The method
was ﬁrst presented in 1988 by Bendsøe and Kikuchi [3].
Topology optimization is often coupled with a ﬁnite element model that mimics the physics
of the problem. The design space is meshed with ﬁnite elements, and one or more so-called
design variables are assigned to each ﬁnite element. The structure then takes its shape
through the values of the design variables, where typically zeros indicate void and ones
indicate material.
The method can also handle orthotropic materials [11], where the design variables can
represent the amount of material or the ﬁber angle in a composite. It is also extended to
handle multiple materials. This opens for the possibility of using the method for designing
advanced composite structures, such as wind turbine blades.
Typically, the value of a design variable is directly proportional to the stiﬀness through
the material properties, the layer thickness, or the ﬁber angle [10]. But some variations,
such as Discrete Material Optimization (DMO) [37], use the design variables to represent
weights of predeﬁned candidate materials, and thus the material composition.
The optimization methods can be divided into two branches: evolutionary methods (also
known as heuristic) and gradient-based methods. Generally, evolutionary methods opti-
mize by gradually testing and subsequently removing designs that do not perform as well as
others [57], and gradient-based methods use the gradients of the objective and constraint
functions to search for better performing designs [3]. This thesis uses a gradient-based
method.

1.2.1
Proceedings in the industry
Since the concept of topology optimization was presented in 1988, the method has been
adopted by the industry. Examples of optimized members are numerous: a wind turbine
mainframe [19], an engine and gearbox mount in a personal vehicle [28], or wing ribs of
an airplane [34], and the list goes on.
Designs from topology optimization can be diﬃcult to realize, because they are not nec-
essarily optimized for manufacturability. That is, the method choose the optimal design
strictly based on the formulated objective and constraints. This means that designs can
turn out with advanced layouts that are not manufacturable by use of traditional methods.
Such a structure could be hollow in a way, where joining sub-components would compro-
mise the performance, or could simply have small complex details placed inconveniently.
Luckily, the increased technological capability of additive manufacturing techniques has
lowered the barrier for realizable designs signiﬁcantly [13], hence the pairing of topology
optimization with additive manufacturing has huge potential.
1.3
Motivation
This leads to the motivation for this project. Why is it a good idea to match topology
optimization with wind turbine design? The more the LCoE for wind energy is lowered,
the more competitive the technology becomes. This competitiveness is crucial in driving
the energy transition and can create many jobs. The LCoE is aﬀected by extensive and
continuous research. Zooming in on the wind turbine blades, they account for up to a ﬁfth
of the cost of a wind turbine, but there is potential to lower the cost [30]. Additionally,
they constitute the main challenge with scaling.
Regular 3D topology optimization is computationally expensive, which is an apparent
bad match with wind turbine design that, because of safety concerns, is bound to a lot
of standards (often conservative). This means that a proposed wind turbine has to be
modelled in a large variety of situations (diﬀerent wind conditions, start-up, emergency
stop etc.), which makes heavy topology optimization even heavier. The method used in
this project presents an alternative that combines the essentials of the two ﬁelds. The
method is based on 2D cross sections that, with a low computational cost beam model
and clever ﬁltering, handles 3D-eﬀects, and thus reduces the size of the problem to a scale
that is more manageable.

But more speciﬁcally, the goals for this project are
1. Lower the computational cost of the proposed method by implementation of a new
beam element.
2. Implement new objective and constraint functions for topology optimization with
the current beam ﬁnite element model.

Chapter 2
Structural model
A structural analysis model predicts the behaviour of a mechanical system. The accuracy
of the prediction depends on the simplicity of the model and how it is applied. Every
model has its limitations and it is important to know these when applying it to a speciﬁc
problem. This chapter covers the essential theory of the structural model applied in this
project.
This structural model utilizes beam model, which is advantageous because it allows for
relatively complex structures to be described through few equations if they simply comply
with a range of assumptions. Assumptions for the structural model are:
• Structures are long and slender.
• There are no abrupt variations in the cross section geometry, materials, and loads
along the beam axis.
• Displacements and rotations are small.
• Gradients of resulting strains and displacements are small along the beam axis.
Starting from the most general level, a continuous structure is discretized into smaller
structures known as elements that interact with each other. This discretization is illus-
trated in Figure 2.1, where a wind turbine blade is discretized into beam ﬁnite elements.
Elements are connected via shared nodes and this can be described mathematically by a
set of coupled equations. The equations describe the behaviour of the system at the nodes
and the behaviour in the rest of the structure is interpolated from the nodal values.

on the theory ﬁrst developed by Giavotto et al.

2.6
Cross section properties
The optimized designs presented in this report will be plotted with a range of extra
information that relates to the structural properties of the cross section.
Reference center: Origin of the cross section coordinate system.
Elastic center: The point in a cross section at which a force normal to the cross section
plane would not induce bending curvatures [8].
Shear center: The point in a cross section at which an applied load parallel to the
cross section plane does not produce torsion [8].
Mass center: The point in the cross section that balances the mass in both x- and
y-direction.
Principal axes: The rotated coordinate system in which shear stress in the plane is zero.
This information is plotted using the symbols presented in Figure 2.3.
In addition to those, the applied loads are indicated as well. This is done with a red
arrow for forces and a circular, blue arrow for moments. The forces and moments are
scaled relative to themselves. Thus the size of the moments and the size of the forces can
not be compared in plots, and the reader is referred to what is stated in the text for the
magnitudes. They simple serve as an aid for interpreting the designs. This means that
forces and moments should not be compared between plots either.

Chapter 3
Meshing of wind turbine blades
This chapter explains the method that have been developed for meshing of wind turbine
blades for use with BECAS and the process that have gone ahead of reaching it. The
speciﬁc blade that have been used is the DTU 10MW RWT blade [2].
There are several approaches to meshing the RWT blade, or wind turbine blades in general,
for use for optimal design with BECAS. There are advantages and disadvantages for each
approach.
It is important to consider key properties of a mesh for both analysis and
optimal design. Some properties are general for all ﬁnite element analyses, others are
simply desired for this project. The main goals for the mesh(es) can be summarized as
follows:
1. The mesh should be ﬁne enough to resolve the geometry and physics accurately.
2. The mesh should be as coarse as possible to lower computation time.
3. The mesh should be of a quality that keeps the solution independent of the mesh.
4. Each section should have the same number of elements.
The top three items are general to ﬁnite element analysis. Item 1 and 2 are opposites,
and thus drive the meshing in opposing directions. Which one of them is most important
depends on the speciﬁc problem and the available computation resources. If the goal is to
be as precise as possible and computation power is abundant, then the mesh can include a
lot of details. But it should be mentioned that many small elements are not necessarily the
most important aspect of getting high precision analysis solutions. It is most important to
capture the physics of the problem, and there might be geometrical aspects that are not
important to the solution and thus can be omitted in the meshing. Most often time and
computation resources are limited, hence it is important to make simplifying assumptions
and keep the mesh as coarse as possible.
Item 3 states that a solution should be independent of the mesh. This accounts both
for optimal design problems and ﬁnite element analysis in general. This means that if a
physical interpretation of a solution is dependent on a speciﬁc mesh, then it is most likely
wrong.
The last item on the list (that each section should have the same number of elements)
is not a criteria of ﬁnite element analysis in general nor for BECAS itself. But it makes

3.1
Simpliﬁcation of geometry
As stated above, clever simpliﬁcations in the geometry can make meshing easier and result
in solutions of higher quality. A few steps are taken in that direction. The Gurney ﬂap of
the blade is neglected. A Gurney ﬂap is an extension of an airfoil mounted at the trailing
edge with the purpose of improving the aerodynamics of the wing. In other words, it is
not important when considering design of the load-carrying structure. Also, it is most
often glued on the structural wing later in the production. More details and references
can be found in for example Mcgugan [39].
The blade is cut at the leading edge and at the trailing edge, on the assumption that these
parts are not essential to the main load-carrying part of the structure either. The cuts are
illustrated in Figure 3.1 for three diﬀerent cross sections. The trailing edge is very thin
compared to the rest of the shape, which makes it more diﬃcult to mesh. By automating
the procedure based on the airfoil data, the cuts appear more severe toward the root of the
blade, while it hardly seems to aﬀect the blade toward the tip. The cuts could be reﬁned
for each section separately, but that is deemed unnecessary. The assumption that the cuts
does not aﬀect the solution could be challenged by doing diﬀerent cuts and comparing the
results, but that is not within the scope of this project.

A 3D mesh can consist of tetrahedal elements, hexahedral elements (also known as bricks),

The element types are illustrated in Figure 3.2 for diﬀerent number of nodes. The number

and a more ﬂexible solution, since each element has more degrees of freedom. Hence the
order of the element inﬂuences the balance between accuracy and computation time.

are plane. Thus the 3D model has to be meshed with hexahedral elements as opposed
to tetrahedral elements that are usually easier to mesh with. For a structured mesh with
hexahedral elements it is automatically enforced that every section has the same number
of elements.

was not possible to use it. A ﬁnite element shell model exists in the repository, too, whose
geometry is modiﬁable. The shear webs were removed and the wing was closed at the two
ends, creating a closed surface model. The surface model could then be converted into a
solid model. All this was done in ANSYS Workbench (version 17.1). Then came the

3.2.2
Meshing with Abaqus
Abaqus was tested when it became available. This gave the advantage of a higher quality
3D model (because no ﬁle conversion was needed). For hexahedral elements, Abaqus has
two meshing algorithms for this type of task: Sweep and Bottom-up. A swept mesh is
generated by choosing a source side, a target side, and a sweep direction. For the blade,
the source side can either be the tip or the root, and the target side vice versa. The sweep
direction is naturally along the length of the blade, from source to target. The algorithm
is helped by placement of mesh seeds along edges. This can include partitioning the model
to create more edges for more seeds. An example of a swept mesh made with Abaqus is
shown in Figure 3.3. The mesh is made very coarse for demonstration purpose. In the
illustration, the tip is chosen as source side. While the tip mesh looks decent, the mesh
at the root is very distorted. This is obviously a result of the change in geometry from an
airfoil-shape to a cylindrical shape. The level of distortion can be lowered by partitioning
the blade, but it is very diﬃcult to reach a satisfactory result. Lowering the element size
can create even more distorted elements. The bottom-up meshing algorithm works in a
similar way, only it relaxes the geometrical constraints imposed by the 3D model. In that
way the dimensions of a bottom-up mesh may not correspond precisely to those of the
3D model. Unfortunately, this meshing algorithm failed to mesh the wing. Abaqus also
has a structured meshing algorithm that ﬁts a generic shape, i.e. a rectangle/square or a
brick/cube mesh, inside a slightly more advanced shape. Unfortunately, a wing shape is
seemingly too complex for this algorithm.
From a geometrical point of view, it is possible to create a full 3D mesh with brick elements,
but it is very diﬃcult. There are ways of repositioning the nodes for a higher quality mesh
as a post-meshing method.
This is called mesh smoothing.
A separate optimization
problem is formulated to do this. Thus an objective could be based on some quantitative
evaluation of the mesh quality, for example the quality of the worst element, and the
problem would be constrained in various ways, for example how much a node is allowed
to move. This is similar to what have been done in Zavattieri [58]. All in all, a complete
master thesis could be written on this speciﬁc topic. Ultimately, the 3D mesh approach
was deemed too time consuming for this project and it was scrapped.

3.3.2
Meshing process
The meshing process can be divided into ﬁve steps:
1. The outer geometry is loaded in Matlab as section-wise (airfoil) data.
2. The data is processed and the points along the wing shape are redistributed.
3. An input ﬁle for Gmsh is generated.
4. Gmsh is called from Matlab with the input ﬁle and a mesh-ﬁle is generated.
5. The mesh-ﬁle is opened in Matlab, converted to BECAS-format, and saved.
For standard, Gmsh uses an unstructured mesh, where the element size is based on a
length parameter that is somewhere between global and local. This makes it very diﬃcult
to control the number of elements per section. But Gmsh can also produce structured
meshes. For this, a transﬁnite mapping is used. Details about the method can be found
in Gordon [24]. Essentially, the transﬁnite mapping ﬁts a rectangular mesh with even-
sized, square elements inside the airfoil boundary by using four reference points. The four
corners of the rectangle are mapped into four points on the section shape: leading and
trailing edges on suction and pressure sides, and the rest is interpolated between them. In
that way the number of elements along the lines between the reference points is enforced
to be what is was in the rectangular mesh, which naturally enforces the total number of
elements of the mesh to be the same before and after mapping. The mapping is illustrated

two sub-sets make out two parallel sides of the mapped rectangle, they are assigned the
same number of elements. This is done on a smaller scale. Each sub-set consists of many
line segments. A line segment is the straight line from one point to another. Each line
segment is assigned a number of nodes and the total number of nodes for each sub-set of
line segments has to match. The last two sides of the mapped rectangle are made out by
the two line segments that connect suction and pressure sides at the trailing and leading
edges. These two line segments also has to be assigned an identical number of nodes.
Obviously, this method creates distorted elements and steps are taken in order to minimize
this. Cleverly choosing the aspect ratio of the unmapped rectangle means a lot. In the
end the choice is a compromise between mesh quality in the circular sections and in the
airfoil-shaped sections. Additionally, it is possible to control which regions along the edges
that are stretched (or squeezed) more than others by not assigning the same number of
nodes to every line segment, or use settings that gradually decrease (or increase) the nodal
spacing in a line segment. Similarly, the end result will be a compromise. Appendix A

Optimization model
The aim of doing topology optimization is to ﬁnd the distribution of material(s) that
creates the best possible solution to the design problem. More speciﬁcally, this means
minimizing (or maximizing) some objective while satisfying the imposed constraints, which
more generally is known as the problem formulation. The solutions to the problems are
represented by the values of the design variables, where (in this project) 0’s indicate
void and 1’s indicate material. In essence, it is a discrete problem, but due to lack of
availability of solvers capable of solving large scale problems, design variables are allowed
to vary continuously.
The speciﬁc problem formulations for this project are covered in Chapter 5. This section
covers the underlying theory and its implementation.

4.4.1
Filter types
There are certain diﬃculties related to ﬁltering of turbine blades. For prismatic beams, it
makes sense to space the 2D sections evenly, whereas for the blade it makes sense to space
the sections based on changes in geometry. Regions with large changes, i.e. the shoulder of
the blade, should be assigned more sections than the cylindrical root or the approximate
prismatic region from the shoulder towards the tip. Not respacing the sections evenly
would with a traditional ﬁlter result in more correlation between sections in the regions
with many sections, which would limit the design freedom heavily.
Three ﬁlters are proposed:
F1: Density ﬁlter based on mapped meshes (true distance)
F2: Density ﬁlter based on unmapped meshes
F3: Density ﬁlter with correlation matrix based on unmapped meshes and weighting
based on mapped meshes
They are here described in more detail.
Filter based on true distance
The ﬁrst proposed ﬁlter is a traditional ﬁlter, in the sense that the ﬁlter radius is a global
measure. Thus everywhere in the model, the ﬁlter radius is the same. Sections are spaced
evenly when the correlation matrix is evaluated to secure ﬁltering between sections. The
distance between sections can for example be the square root of the largest element area.
This works very well for parts of the blade at a time, where sections are approximately
of the same size and the elements within the sections are all of approximately the same
size. But for the full blade, it poses challenges. If the ﬁlter radius at the tip is the same
as the ﬁlter radius at the root, it is either necessary to have a coarse mesh at the tip or
a very ﬁne mesh at the root to avoid regions that are ﬁltered too much or too little. The
former is a problem because it heavily limits the design freedom at the tip. The latter is a
problem because a small, global element size would result in a lot of elements per section
closer to the root, and thus make the model computationally very expensive.

Filter based on unmapped mesh
The second proposed mesh is based on the unmapped rectangular mesh (cf. Chapter 3).
This ﬁlter can be interpreted as having a relative radius, where the actual ﬁlter radius
varies between sections and across each section based on the changes in geometry.
This implies the condition that all sections have the same number of elements, which
naturally results in diﬀerently sized elements at the root versus at the tip. This is not
necessarily a problem for the structural model, it is in fact common to reﬁne the mesh
in regions that demand a higher level of detail. Moreover, it makes the ﬁlter correlation
matrix very simple and keeps the memory usage to a minimum. It also ensures that the
elements along the edge of two neighbouring sections are ﬁltered with respect to each
other, which is good for getting a continuous design. This would not be enforced with
the traditional ﬁlter if the sections had diﬀerent sizes. Another argument in favour of this
ﬁlter, although it can and should be discussed, is that smallest geometrical elements in
the largest sections are likely to be larger than those of the smallest sections anyway.

The controversial aspect of this ﬁlter is that the eﬀective ﬁlter radius seen by the wing

4.5
Scaling the problems
By scaling a function, what is meant is that a constant, c, is multiplied the function so
that y = cx. The scaling of a problem impacts the optimization in terms of convergence,
number of iterations, and local minima. From the experience gained with this project it
should be noted that scaling the problems is in no way straightforward.
First of all, the size and diﬃculty of the problem aﬀects the importance of scaling. Hence
for smaller problems, the scaling is not as important as for larger problems.
According to W¨achter [56], the non-zero gradients of all objectives and constraints should
be of the same magnitude. Kawajir et. al [31] suggest that the gradients are scaled to
be in the order of 0.1 - 10. The challenge is how to obtain gradients of that value, as
the values of the gradients will vary for each design variable and will vary continuously
through the optimization. Several approaches have been attempted
• Scale based on the maximum of gradients
• Scale based on the mean of the gradients
• Rescale during optimization for every X iterations
In the end, what has worked seems more based on a trial and error than actually checking
the values of the gradients. This is because the magnitude of the gradients can vary a
lot during the optimization, and thus it should be considered at which point during the
optimization the gradients should be scaled for. Basing the the scaling on the initial guess
may not be the best solution.
The order of magnitude of the gradients are determined as the rounded down, 10-based
logarithm of a reference value representing the values of the gradient. Knowing the order
of magnitude, the scaling factor can be determined with the power-ten integer that results
in the gradients becoming a target value. Thus if the reference value is b × 10−3 and the
target is the order of 0.1 = 10−1, the scaling factor is determined as 10−1−(−3) = 102.
A diﬃcult part of the scaling is ﬁnding this reference value, as it has to capture the
magnitude of all non-zero gradients with one number. Experiments have been made using
the maximum value of the absolute gradients and the mean value of the absolute gradients
to determine a reference value. Both methods work, but the target order of magnitude
has to be set accordingly. For the most, the mean of the absolute values has been used.
Rescaling every X number of iterations can be seen as a variation of continuation [44].
Continuation is solving a sequence of problems (typically varying the penalization factor
for each problem), where the initial guess to the next problem is the solution of the last
problem. Doing the rescaling is essentially starting a new problem and it should keep the
gradients at the same order of magnitude through the whole optimization process. With
this approach, Ipopt was not restarted after rescaling, and although it worked successfully
==PAGE:53==

in some cases, it caused Ipopt to fail in others. Because of that, the idea was abandoned.
It was never attempted to restart Ipopt before every rescaling, as this would slow down
the optimization a lot, because Ipopt does not handle continuation well.

Chapter 6
Wind turbine blade loads
This chapter covers the basic theory on how loads are generated on an airfoil, the origin
of the loads used in this project, and how they are applied to the beam model.
6.1
Aerodynamic loads
The loads on a wind turbine blade are generated by the diﬀerence in pressure on each
side of the airfoil. Figure 6.1 shows a blade in an airﬂow. The ﬂow aﬀects the airfoil
(blade) and a force is created. This is because the streamlines of the ﬂow are altered by
the shape of the airfoil, which by deﬁnition requires a pressure gradient [25]. The force
can be decomposed into contributions that are perpendicular and parallel to the ﬂow: lift
and drag, respectively. A moment is also generated from the pressure distribution, and
it is necessary to know the value of this moment somewhere along the chord. Usually
the moment refers to the aerodynamic center. The aerodynamic center is deﬁned as the
point along the chord, where the moment is independent of the angle of attack (the angle
between the chord-line and the free ﬂow), and is typically located at quarter-chord distance
from the leading edge.
The torque contribution from the blade can be found by integration of the loads along the
length of the blade. The sum of torque from all blades of a wind turbine is what drives
the generator.
The airfoil may be angled as a function of the radial coordinate. This is known as the
twist. Because of this angling, it is not always convenient to decompose the resultant force
into lift and drag contributions, but rather use a coordinate system that does not vary
with the spanwise coordinate. Such a coordinate system could be the one used in Figure
3.7.

Application to beam model
The way the loads are applied to each beam element is quite crude, and it should ideally
be reﬁned. But in light of other simpliﬁcations, it was not deemed important, and it still
matches the load diversity experienced by a wind turbine blade.
From the values of Figures 6.2 and 6.4, each load component is interpolated at the beam
element centres. This value is multiplied the length of the beam elements to get resultant
forces and torsion. The forces and torsion on an element are then divided by the number
of nodes per element and distributed to each node.
For the cases where the extreme loads are used and torsion is included, the torsion of
Figure 6.2 is used.

8.2.1
On the convergence
Overall, all topologies are “black and white”, feasible solutions, thus they have converged
to a satisfactory degree, despite what might be listed in the Status-column of Table 8.2.
In the table, a pattern is observed. For the results of the project code, minimizing Fs,33,
Fs,44, Fs,55, and Fs,66 and maximizing Ks,22, Ks,33, Ks,44, Ks,55, and Ks,66, generally result
in a converged solution, while the rest does not. For the reference code maximizing the
diagonal terms of Ks results in convergence, while minimizing diagonal terms of Fs does
not converge within 1000 iterations, but reaches log(µ) = −11.0, which is practically a
converged solution.
It is not known why this behaviour is observed, and an explanation can only be guessed at.
Most likely, the converged designs are easier problems. For example comparing maximizing
Ks,11 to maximizing Ks,22. It is obvious that these two design should be resistant to shear
in x- and y-direction, respectively. Looking at the design space, the available space in the
y-direction is much narrower than in the x-direction, limiting the design freedom, hence
making the problem easier.
8.2.2
On the resulting topologies
Qualitatively comparing the results of the three ﬁlters leaves the image that the designs
are overall the same. The diﬀerences are mainly seen in the vertical lines, for example in
the resulting design of maximizing Ks,55 (converged for all three ﬁlters). Small bumps are
seen in diﬀerent places for the three designs. This might be an eﬀect of the ﬁlter, but it
might also simply be diﬀerent local minima, could be an eﬀect of the volume constraint.
Obviously, the volume fraction does not necesarrily ﬁt with a complete vertical line of
elements with a = 1. To avoid a loss from penalization, the remaining material will be
added as a small bump rather than being divided between a whole line of elements. The
same thing is observed for maximizing Ks,33.
The results between the two codes are similar with the exception of maximizing Ks,33 (and
minimizing Fs,33 to some extent). The reason for the design diﬀerence is unknown. The
diﬀerence in stiﬀness between the codes from Table 8.4 most likely relates to the increased
ﬁlter size or the discretization.
Overall, based on this analysis, the conﬁdence in the results and the code is strong.

• Varying the scaling in accordance with Section 4.5. What was found to work best
was scaling the gradients to the order of 1 using the mean of the absolute values of
the gradients for the initial guess.
• Experimenting more extensively with diﬀerent volume fractions. Low volume frac-
tions are harder to generate converged results for, while shear webs tend to not show
up in results for high volume fractions. The volume fraction used was a compromise.
• Using a diﬀerent method for the parametrization (explained in Appendix B). This
was used initially (and in the majority part of the project) but was abandoned
because it resulted in singular stiﬀness matrices occasionally.
• Including more cross sections (beam elements). This has been a trade-oﬀbetween
getting time on the cluster (there are fewer nodes with large amount of RAM), using
a ﬁne cross section discretization, generating results fast, and being able to store
information in the RAM (if everything is computed on the go, the optimization
slows down signiﬁcantly).
• Using an aerodynamic shell (enforcing material in the elements on the boarder of
the mesh). The results are fairly similar, and therefore not interesting to include.
• Varying the ﬁlter radius. The ﬁlter radius is kept as small as possible in order to
give the highest level of freedom in the designs.

Generally, the material has been distributed to the outermost parts of the design space,

The internal forces and moments are plotted in Figure 9.2 for the two cases. Naturally, the
last plots (Mz) diﬀer between the two. As mentioned in Chapter 6, countering moments

Figure 9.2 shows how torsion is generated from the oﬀ-set between the shear center and

hard to interpret how it aﬀects the placement of the shear center and the design in general.
The design with torsion applied has its shear center of the last cross section placed just
slightly more to the left, shortening the moment arm. But the diﬀerence is only 0.07 cm,
which is so small that it could almost be coincidental.
9.3.2
Varying discretization
Figure 9.3 presents the results of minimizing compliance for three diﬀerent cross section
discretizations: 14080, 35520, and 52640 elements. This means that the eﬀective ﬁlter
radii are also diﬀerent (the ﬁnest mesh has the smallest ﬁlter radius). The designs for the
two coarsest meshes converged in 580, 589, iterations, and the design for the ﬁne mesh
did not converge in 600 iterations (log(µ) = −1.7, dual infeasibility 9.47 × 10−1 at exit1).
The loads applied are the extreme loads without torsion. Qualitatively, the designs with
the two ﬁnest discretizations are very similar, although the position of the shear center
is seen to vary slightly. The coarse mesh stands out more, as seen by the position of the
shear center.
The thickness of the shell at the root of the coarse mesh varies more clearly than for the
two ﬁner meshes. This seems to be an eﬀect of the ﬁlter. The solver pushes to make the
structure thin, but the ﬁlter makes it weak because there are too few elements.

9.3.4
Load application method
Applying all loads as one load case leads to a diﬀerent design. This is shown in Figure

This aﬀects mainly the shear center, but also the elastic center, and the mass center.
The big question is why the designs are so diﬀerent and why one converges much more
slowly. This is touched in Chapter 10.
9.3.5

the eﬀect of varying the stiﬀness of the void is investigated. The void is made more stiﬀ

design seen in Figure 8.2l. Second of all, from the look of cross section 2, 3, and 4, more

ﬁltering between sections. Third of all, the shear webs are closest to the center for volume
fraction 0.1. There are two possible explanations for this. Either is the design simply more
converged, or the discretization in the interior of the airfoil is too coarse, making it really
hard for the solver to create a web closer to the center. The design has a bending to shear
ratio of 15 m at the root, pointing in the direction that the ratio might be inﬂuential.

and log(µ) = −2.5 for volume fraction 0.05. It is safe to say that these designs, too,
are far from optimal. The structure at the trailing edge has disappeared, which serves
to show that it was a way of transitioning from the cylinder-shape to the airfoil-shape.
Again the shear web toward the back is closest to the center for the high volume fraction,
and unfortunately this can not be explained completely from these designs either, because
they have not converged. For volume fraction 0.05, the top and bottom of the outermost
cross section are disappearing, this could potentially be due to the discretization as well,
but the tendency is observed for the higher volume fraction, too, indicating that it is more
likely a better solution to the problem. If bending was to be included, this would probably
not be the case.

Chapter 10
Discussion
This chapter discusses various aspects of the project in terms of what could have been
done diﬀerently, what should be improved, and the nature of the results.
10.1
Speeding up simulations
Several changes could be done to the code in order to speed up simulations. As they were
not speciﬁc goals for the project, they have not been attempted. But in the light of the
outcomes of the project, they are changes that could have been helpful, as they would
have allowed for faster result generation.
10.1.1
New code structure
There are things in the structure of the code that could be changed in order to gain a
speed-up. These changes relates mostly to not having to compute the same thing more
than once per iteration. As of now, Fs (and Ks, Ms ) are updated every time a call is made
to evaluate the objective function or a constraint, which obviously leads to calculating it
without a reason other than the value(s) has not been stored properly. Therefore a check
should be made in order to determine whether these matrices should be updated or not.
Only if a change is registered are the matrices updated. The same thing is true for the
gradients of these matrices, and the same solution should be pursued.
This happens again on the beam model level. The stiﬀness (and mass matrix) is evaluated
for every call to objective and constraint functions (if they relate to the beam model),
which in many cases is unnecessary. For the eigenvalue gradients, often constraints would
be made on more than one eigenvalue, and therefore, of course, it would make sense to
only compute these once per proposed design (iteration).
These changes would mean that many of these computations are moved forward in the code
and stored before calls are made to evaluate objective, constraints, or the sensitivities of
these. Functions would then extract the values needed to compute objective, constraints,
etc. The framework for this approach is already set up in the reference code that was
used in the project, but unfortunately it was introduced at a point where transferring
the needed parts would take up a signiﬁcant amount of time, and therefore the idea was
abandoned.

10.1.2
Alternative programming language
An obvious speed-up can be achieved by changing the programming language. Matlab is
very convenient for small projects, because of its ease of use and plenty of useful built-in
functions that would take a lot of time to program from scratch in another language. Just
the plotting/visualization parts of Matlab are very valuable, especially for projects that
have limited time and resources available. Another valuable aspect of Matlab is how
easy it is to de-bug, which saves a lot of time. But the perks come at a price. Matlab
is signiﬁcantly slower than lower-level languages such as C++ or Fortran. Converting the
code to one of these languages would most likely result in a huge speed-up.
For a code the size of BECAS with hundreds of functions, it makes even more sense. Unless
it is sub-functions (that can only be called from the script in which they are written) it
is not possible to store more than one function per ﬁle. A small collection of functions
is not a problem, but when considering the size of BECAS, it would make sense to start
bundling functions together in fewer ﬁles, which would require an alternative programming
language.
10.1.3
Improved parallelization
Another programming language would also oﬀer new possibilities for parallelization. The
only paralellization possibilities in Matlab relates to the parfor-loop that speeds up for-
loops, by distributing each loop to diﬀerent cores. As of now, the cross sections are looped,
and thus distributed to separate cores. But when the number of design variables go up
for each cross section, the computation time is still long. Therefore it would make sense
to start distributing parts of cross sections to diﬀerent cores, which is very cumbersome
with Matlab (would require another instance of Matlab).
On top of that, Matlab
can only use one node on the HPC (20 cores on the cluster of choice), which means the
bottleneck becomes the amount of RAM. The only way around this problem is to use
Matlab Distributed Computing Server, which requires a separate license, hence
it is added cost. It was attempted to acquire a license for this project, but it was not
possible.
10.2
Missing shear webs for blade designs
The optimized blade designs of Chapter 9 raise the question of where the traditional shear
web designs are to be found. Only when it was solely ﬂapwise loads, did they appear (in
a weak form). In the validation cases of Section 7.4, shear webs showed for both types of
meshes without any eﬀort.
Many approaches at generating them with the full set of loads have been attempted: for
example lowering the ratio of bending to torsion and varying the stiﬀness of the void, but
nothing has really helped. In an early stage of the process, some shear webs were observed,
but after correcting a bug that enhanced the shear moduli of the material by a factor of
ten, the traditional shear webs have not shown. Therefore it can be questioned whether
the material properties plays a role in generating them.
Another source could be the aspect ratios of the elements (and therefore the discretization).
The elements of the unstructured mesh in Section 7.4 have varying aspect ratio, but the
==PAGE:129==

average aspect ratio is close to one. With the current meshing technique for the RWT
blade, the elements in the cylinder-shaped sections are stretched vertically, while the
elements in the airfoil-shaped sections are stretched horizontally. A similar eﬀect is seen
within the cross sections: the aspect ratios vary between trailing edge and the central
region. If the shear webs were to be very thin, this could be a problem. Especially, if
the load is not aligned with the thinnest dimension of the elements, as it would require
more material to make the shear webs. The shear web generated at the trailing edge of
the root-designs are aligned with the wide dimension, but this might not be a problem
because the vertical distance is short.
Another thing that diﬀers between the validation studies and the RWT results is the
change in shape of the design space between sections. If the position of the (could-be)
shear webs is very sensitive, it might not be attractive for the solver to make them at all,
because the 3D ﬁlter would ﬁlter them with nothing in the neighbouring sections. Taking
into account the spanwise depth of the sections, this can be very expensive in terms of
added compliance. Adding more cross sections could be a way to test this. The study of
the prismatic blade showed that they can deﬁnitely be generated. The diﬀerent volume
fractions indicated that the problem could be due to to the coarseness of the mesh in the
internal regions, although diﬀerent discretizations have been attempted.
But before studying these eﬀects, the ﬁrst thing that should be studied is why the con-
vergence is so diﬀerent between the designs for all loads and for ﬂapwise loads (in other
words designs with and without shear webs). Converged results are crucial in order to
draw precise conclusions.
10.3
Load application
It is quite interesting that loads applied as separate load cases converge easier than applied
all at once. From the principle of superposition, the sum of displacements is the same,
but obviously the value of compliance changes, because by using load cases (and sum
of compliances) some cross terms are avoided. An explanation is suggested using a small
example: If a beam is subjected to a moment and a force at the tip (force in y-direction and
bending around the x-axis, for example, creating displacements in the same direction), the
total displacements will be a superposition of the individual solutions to the two problems
(loads). From the total displacements, it might be diﬃcult to diﬀer between contributions
from the force and from the moment. This confusion would show up in the gradients
as well, unless separate load cases are used.
The gradient of a design variable (in an
element) would receive contributions from the two load cases. If one load case creates
much larger displacements than the other, the contribution will be higher. Naturally, this
is a second order eﬀect as the displacements are already linearly proportional to the loads.
But deﬁnitely there are designs that are relatively better in bending than in shear, and it
could be this eﬀect that is captured better when applying the loads as separate load cases.
10.4
Eigenvalue problems
One of the major issues in this project has been achieving large scale results with eigenvalue
(or eigenfrequency) constraints. The convergence issues have been most pronounced for
==PAGE:130==
116

these problems, which is naturally reﬂected in their share of this thesis.
Maximization of the fundamental eigenvalue has been attempted for the root-region of
the blade, but the barrier parameter did not move through 1000 iterations: exit values of
log(µ) = −1.0 and dual infeasibility >10, even though a constraint is added to the second
eigenvalue, specifying it to be slightly higher than the ﬁrst one. One of the issues with the
maximization of the lowest eigenvalue is that the solver attempts to eﬀectively shorten the
beam by moving material from the tip- to the root-region. This can even cause cases of
local modes, as described in Pedersen [42], where parts of the eigenmodes are so ﬂexible
that they practically behave as separate modes. The behaviour can be mitigated in various
ways: applying a load and assigning a displacement constraint to the tip, or adding a local
volume constraint specifying a minimum volume fraction for the cross section at the tip.
10.5
Aim of topology optimization with BECAS
One of the great advantages of BECAS (in combination with FRANS or another beam
model) is that complicated geometry is simpliﬁed and made easy to handle. Therefore
advanced beam-like structures such as wind turbine blades can be modelled using few
equations, and a change in the design does not mean that a complete 3D model has
to be redone. The implicated cross sections are just updated, and the beam model is
recalculated.
Then comes topology optimization, which ideally should have a ﬁne discretization in the
beam-direction to capture geometrical changes without violating assumptions. This results
in a situation where the beam-model needs relatively few elements to mimic the physics
correctly, but the topology optimization needs more cross sections in order to handle
changes in geometry between sections. The two sides pull in separate directions, which
leaves the question whether there exist a compromise.
This falls back on the aim of using the topology module of BECAS: if the aim is to design
blades that essentially are ready for production, BECAS might not be the best tool. But
if the aim relates much more to observing trends and study designs in a much more crude
way, then it might actually be a decent alternative to full 3D topology optimization.
Especially when combined with the 2D meshing techniques of Gmsh (and a more solid
approach for 3D visualization).

Chapter 11
Concluding remarks
A lot has been achieved through this project, but many problems have also been identi-
ﬁed. This chapter summarizes the work that have been done, the results that have been
achieved, and the problems that have been identiﬁed through the course of this project.
11.1
On the extension of BECAS
The capabilities of BECAS (in cooperation with FRANS) have been extended in a variety
of ways. These extensions will continue to generate value as long as BECAS is used. The
capabilities are
• A new, two-noded beam element has been implemented for analysis and for use in
topology optimization. Thus the design sensitivities for the stiﬀness (and ﬂexibility)
and mass matrix has been implemented, too. The element has been validated by
comparison to an analytical solution to the deﬂection of a cantilever beam (subject to
a tip-load) and a previously implemented element. By using the two-noded element
instead of the existing four-noded element an improvement in computation time have
been shown for both analysis and gradient evaluation in topology optimization.
• A meshing routine has been developed for structured meshing of advanced geome-
tries such as airfoils for use with BECAS (mainly topology optimization). 2D cross
sectional geometries are meshed in Gmsh using a transﬁnite mapping, resulting in a
high level of control of the number of elements in the meshes. This is an important
aspect of ﬁltering of design variables using the proposed methods. 3D-meshing of the
RWT blade has been attempted, but eventually abandoned because of mesh quality
issues.
• The topology optimization module of BECAS has been adjusted to handle meshes
with varying element sizes. This makes it possible to use unstructured meshes and
structured meshes with varying element size, such as those produced with the trans-
ﬁnite mapping.
• Three 3D ﬁlters for use in topology optimization with BECAS has been proposed
and tested. The ﬁlters showed only small diﬀerences when applied in optimization
of cross sectional properties of airfoils.

• Several functions have been implemented and validated for use as objectives or con-
straints in topology optimization on the beam model level. The design sensitivi-
ties are validated by comparison to central ﬁnite diﬀerence approximations. The
functions are subsequently tested on small validation examples, showing promising
results. The implemented functions are
– Compliance
– Displacements
– Eigenvalues (without the use of an envelope function)
– Static moment
• An automated scaling method for the objective and constraints have been proposed
(and used). This involves evaluating the magnitude of the gradients of the functions
involved in the problem formulation (using the mean of the absolute values is pro-
posed) and scaling each function to a common target (a target order of magnitude of
1 is proposed) using powers of ten. Rescaling the problem during the optimization
has been attempted, but found to cause problems.
11.2
On the generated results
During the course of this project, a lot of results have been generated, the majority part
has not made it in the report (due to bugs and other causes). The results that made it in
the report are
• A set of external loads that mimics the extreme internal loads given in the DTU
10MW RWT report [2]. The loads are made from integration of the distribution of
internal moments given in [2]. They do not correspond to a speciﬁc point in time or
a speciﬁc event, but express an artiﬁcial event, where many extreme events would
occur at once.
• Validation studies on small scale problems (around 3000 design variables) showing
that topology optimization with BECAS (and FRANS) on the beam level is indeed
viable. Designs are achieved for diﬀerent types of meshes, and a variety of problem
formulations.
• 2D airfoil designs for minimizing entries of the cross section compliance matrix and
maximizing entries of the cross section stiﬀness matrix. They are also examples of
how the implemented ﬁlters aﬀect the 2D solutions.
• Designs for minimizing compliance of the root-region of the RWT blade. Various
eﬀects have been studied from sources such as discretization, loads, and load ap-
plication). The problem has been solved with a volume constraint and with both
a volume and an eigenvalue constraint. The largest problem solved to optimality
counts 263200 design variables1.

11.3
On the identiﬁed problems
In general, using topology optimization with a beam model on large scale problems with
the advanced geometry of a wind turbine blade is not straightforward. The validation
in Section 7.4 showed promising results, but the upscaling of the problems gave trouble.
Both in terms of convergence, but also in terms of the resulting designs. Therefore it is
relevant to point out some of the bottlenecks. Problems that have been identiﬁed through
the project include
• Even-sized element gives the advantage that the matrices Me (2.5), Ee (2.6), Ce
(2.4), and more are identical for every element. This saves a large amount of RAM
as only one set needs to be stored (for each material). When moving to uneven-sized
elements, the matrices have to be calculated for each element, and storing becomes
very expensive. This puts a cap on the size of the problem (limited by the amount
of memory).
• The code is relatively slow. Beam equations should be evaluated once per itera-
tion, not per constraint. As of now it means that constraints are computationally
expensive.
• Achieving convergence is not straightforward for large scale problems. Dividing loads
into separate load cases gives faster convergence in some cases. But even in cases
with only vertical loads, convergence can still be an issue, such as for the RWT
blade results of Section 9.5. Convergence seems to be somehow easier for bending-
dominated problems.
• Shear webs are hard to generate with the current code.
This problem can have
various sources: ﬁlter, discretization, loads, and even scaling.

Chapter 12
Future work
This project could easily go on, but it’s constrained by a deadline. There are still plenty
of work to do on the code and many experiments that can be conducted. Therefore a list
of suggestions for future work is included in the hope that this project can constitute the
foundation of a new project. The list goes:
• Change the programming language to achieve speed-up and to be able to combine
HPC nodes for more RAM and better parallelization. The amount of available RAM
is essential, both for using even- and uneven-sized elements. One simply becomes a
problem before the other.
• Increase the number of iterations for the large problems if needed, as this has been
stopped by politics on the HPC, limiting the maximum time a job can run.
• Do a study on the three proposed ﬁlters in 3D. Although Figure 4.2 showed that the
position of an element with number e does not vary a lot between two neighbouring
cross sections, the eﬀect should be quantiﬁed in more detail.
• Increase the cross section mesh resolution and study how it aﬀects the shear web
generation. This can also be related to the ﬁlter study. Thus it would be interesting
to study the eﬀect of the 3D ﬁlters when the number of cross sections vary.
• Solve minimize compliance for the root region of the blade with multiple materials
(materials that are used in production of wind turbine blades). That would show
whether the choice of material (or the material composition) has an eﬀect on the
design.
• Solve minimize compliance for the full blade (20 cross sections) with the highest
discretization possible on the nodes of the HPC with the largest amount of RAM.
This require an exemption from the current HPC policy on simulation times.
• Solve minimize compliance for the full blade piece-wise with high resolution, starting
at the root, and moving forward to the tip, each time imposing the previously de-
termined design variable values to the root-most cross section (to enforce continuity
between pieces).
