Abstract
The decarbonization of the heating sector is a critical challenge in achieving Europe’s
2050 climate targets. District heating (DH) offers a scalable, low-carbon solution, yet its
adoption remains uneven across EU countries. While technical and economics factors in
DH are well studied, less is known about how financial and regulatory frameworks interact
to shape investment outcomes. This thesis addresses that gap by comparing policy and
financial configurations in France, Denmark, Lithuania, and Bulgaria, mapped to three
policy archetypes. Using a TIMES energy system model and a discounted cash flow
analysis, the study evaluates how subsidies, carbon pricing, and regulatory constraints
influence DH penetration, investor appeal, and cost-efficiency.
The findings show that high subsidies combined with moderate regulatory restrictions and
stable carbon pricing are most effective at driving DH penetration. Overly strict regula-
tion can reduce economic feasibility, while well-balanced frameworks enhance financial
resilience and lower the Levelized Cost of Heat (LCOH). These insights are applied to
Bilbao, Spain, highlighting how tailored financial and regulatory tools could support their
DH deployment. The study contributes a policy typology linking financial and regulatory
frameworks to investment outcomes and offers practical recommendations for expanding
DH in Southern European contexts.
The thesis is organized as follows: Chapter 1 and Chapter 2 provide the introduction and
literature review, respectively. Chapter 3 outlines the methodology and is complemented
by a Detailed methodology in the Appendix A.2. Chapter 4 presents the results from both
the TIMES and DCF simulations. Chapter 5 offers a discussion of the key findings, and
Chapter 6 concludes the thesis.

Introduction
1.1
Background and context of district heating systems
District heating systems are key infrastructures for achieving climate targets in the heating
sector [1]. Particularly given that heating and cooling (H&C) account for approximately
50% of Europe’s total energy consumption and 35% of its carbon footprint [2], [3]. District
heating networks (DHN) offer a highly effective solution for providing centralized heat
to buildings while minimizing greenhouse gas emissions, particularly when utilizing low-
emission heat sources such as waste heat (WH) and renewable energy (RE) [4].
Access to affordable and clean energy, as emphasized in UN Sustainable Development
Goal 7, is pivotal for the green energy transition and climate-neutral development [5].
Target 7.b highlights the need for infrastructure expansion and technological upgrades,
aligning with the deployment of DH systems as a key solution for enhancing energy se-
curity, affordability, and sustainability in line with global climate goals [1].
Aligning with the Net Zero Emissions by 2050 scenario requires significant efforts to
rapidly increase the DH penetration rate by modernizing existing networks, transitioning
to RE heat generation, and building new high-efficiency infrastructure [6], [7]. Especially
knowing that RE at present supplies only 8% of DH worldwide, a share that would need
to rise to 77% by 2050 to achieve a climate-neutral economy [8].
However, sustaining and accelerating the growth of highly efficient DHNs presents a sig-
nificant challenge. Indeed, their deployment is highly capital-intensive, requiring long-term
visibility, strong financial structures and support policies for investors [7], [9]. Additionally,
fluctuation in energy prices, long payback periods, and the lack of long-term politically
binding frameworks constitute the main uncertainties around the profitability of DH in-
vestments, which prevents companies from committing to innovation [10]. Historically,
DH development has relied on public funding through grants, loans, and guarantees [11].
Public funding helps reduce financial risks and bridge initial funding gaps, making projects
more attractive to private investors later. However, securing private financing remains
challenging due to long payback periods and high risks. The European Commission esti-
mates that large-scale DH network expansion will require hundreds of billions of euros in
investment [12], [3]. Despite the availability of EU funding mechanisms (Cohesion Fund,
Innovation Fund, etc.), current funding levels remain insufficient to meet the scale of re-
quired investment [13]. Bridging this gap will require greater mobilization of private capital
and financial markets [3].
Rethinking support frameworks for DH is especially true in Southern European contexts,
where DH is often perceived as an nonviable business case, largely due to the region’s
mild climate [14]. Compared to Scandinavia or Eastern Europe, where these systems
fulfill a higher share of the heat demand [15]. This perception has historically led to DH
being overlooked in national energy planning, especially in Spain, where no comprehen-
sive regulatory framework currently exists to support its deployment [14], [16]. As a result,
investment and innovation in the sector have remained limited. However, this assumption
is increasingly outdated. Rising energy demand, urban density, and strong decarboniza-
tion commitments challenge the traditional narrative. Spain, particularly Bilbao, has com-
mitted to ambitious national and EU climate targets, aiming for full decarbonization and
electrification of the heating sector by 2050, supported by the Basque Energy Law and

the Decarb City Pipes project [17]. These objectives are operationalized through the Sus-

the phase-out of fossil fuels in DHN. Establishing enabling financial and regulatory mech-

1.2
Hypotheses and key contributions
Effective measures to improve private investors confidence, meaning their willingness
to engage in investment opportunities based on their perception of risk and return, are
needed [19]. They should target financing tools, stable economic incentives, and regula-
tory frameworks that establish mandatory connection zones, ensuring load stability and
economies of scale [8]. However, academic literature remains limited in offering robust
financial and regulatory frameworks that address the challenges and enable the large-
scale adoption of sustainable DH networks [2],[3],[20]. This gap highlights the need for
deeper research of the financial and policy aspects crucial to scaling DH systems across
Europe.
This thesis investigates how financial and regulatory frameworks shape the deployment
of DH in the context of Europe’s heat transition. Focusing on subsidies, carbon pricing,
and regulation, it analyzes their impact on DH penetration, financial viability, and investor
appeal. Through comparative case studies of France, Denmark, Lithuania, and Bulgaria
against policy archetypes the research identifies key conditions for effective investment
environments. Insights aim to inform DH development in Bilbao, Spain, supporting scal-
able, cost-efficient strategies and guiding local decision-making.
The research question is the following: How do interactions between various financial and
regulatory frameworks influence investments in district heating systems? A comparative
analysis of different European schemes to inform the city of Bilbao, Spain.
This thesis aims to address the gaps identified in the literature described below and is
structured around the following sub-research questions:
1. What key financial and regulatory factors effectively increase district heating pene-
tration rate?
2. How do different combinations of financial and regulatory approaches affect DH
projects feasibility and attractiveness to private investors?
3. How do financial and regulatory tools interact to shape system configurations that in-
fluence the cost-efficiency of DHN, as measured by Levelized Cost of Heat (LCOH)?
This thesis adopts a base case approach, focusing on a DHN in selected European coun-
tries. The aim is to build upon an existing model developed for Bilbao, Spain, adapting it
to a different geographical and political context. The analysis of financial and regulatory
frameworks across selected countries serves to identify best practices that can inform the
optimal framework for DH development in Bilbao. The study employs the TIMES model,
a widely used energy system optimization model, to analyze investment dynamics under
varying policies and financial conditions. Building on the outputs of the TIMES model, a
Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) analysis is applied to assess the financial viability of district
heating investments. Ultimately, this master thesis aims to provide actionable insights
for investors and stakeholders in the renewable energy sector, contributing to the decar-
bonization of the heat distribution sector.

Literature review
This section reviews the current financial and regulatory frameworks affecting district heat-
ing investments.
A well-structured financial and regulatory framework is essential to attract private capital
and ensure the scalability of DH systems [3]. Munksgaard et al. (2005) highlight the im-
portance of economic incentives in promoting DH expansion while underscoring the need
for improved financial mechanisms [21]. Similarly, Wu et al. (2019) stress that regulatory
support play a crucial role in shaping investment conditions, influencing risk-return profiles
through pricing mechanisms and policy instruments [2], [22]. A clear distinction between
financial mechanisms and regulatory frameworks is essential to understanding their re-
spective roles in enabling DH investment. This relationship is examined in greater detail
in the following sections. By analyzing the key terms summarized in Table 2.1, this re-
view provides a comprehensive understanding of the financial landscape surrounding DH
investments in Europe, forming the foundation for the subsequent analysis in this thesis.

2.1
Financial framework
A financial framework consists of a set of financial mechanisms, which, in the context of
DH, refers to the methods employed to secure financial resources essential for its oper-
ation [23]. First the concepts of support schemes, like subsidies and incentives, will be
investigated, followed by an overview of taxes and carbon pricing.
2.1.1
Subsidies and incentives
To encourage investment in DH projects, various subsidies and incentives, also called
support schemes, are implemented at local, national and European levels [7]. These tools
are crucial for financing DH projects, as they can significantly reduce the financial barriers
associated with high upfront costs and long payback periods [10]. By lowering costs,
they make DH projects more attractive to investors, thereby accelerating the transition
to sustainable and efficient heating solutions. This section first categorizes the financial
tools, then focuses on the funding sources they rely on. These mechanisms are organized
in Table 2.1 according to their respective investment profiles.
A first category regroups the subsidies, funding/grants, and concessional interest loans,
which are designed to lower the upfront costs of large-scale infrastructure projects tar-
geting capital-intensive investments [10]. Grants, particularly those provided by national
organizations (“Fond de chaleur” in France) or development banks, often prioritize projects
in underdeveloped regions, aiming to bridge energy access gaps [21]. A second category

The effectiveness and availability of these financial tools often depend on their source of
funding, whether public, private, or a combination of both (blended finance). Understand-
ing the alignment between financial instruments and funding sources is crucial for tailoring
investment strategies in DH projects. Moreover, developing and expanding DH networks
necessitates a variety of funding to ensure investment feasibility and risk mitigation [22],
[24]. These mechanisms can be broadly categorized into public funding, private financ-
ing, and blended financial instruments, each playing a crucial role in the development and
operation of DH infrastructure.
Public funding and subsidies are essential for supporting the initial capital-intensive na-
ture of DH projects, particularly in regions where DH adoption is still developing [7],[2].
Government grants and subsidies provide direct financial support to reduce upfront capital
costs and promote low-carbon DH infrastructure. For example, the European Union’s In-
novation Fund has been instrumental in financing large-scale DH modernization projects
[7]. Additionally, state-owned investment funds, such as the Danish District Heating Pool
(Fjernvarmepuljen), support local DH investments, particularly in municipality-led systems
[25]. Tax incentives, including tax credits, exemptions, or deductions, are also employed
to encourage DH investment and renewable heat integration; France, for instance, offers
a reduced VAT rate when the supplied heat is produced from at least 50% RE sources
[26]. Overall, public funding is primarily assessed by its effectiveness in achieving car-
bon emissions reduction and increasing the penetration rate of district heating networks,
reflecting its role in driving decarbonisation and market expansion [7], [2].
Private investment and commercial financing involve the participation of private-sector en-
tities in DH systems [27]. Equity investments allow private investors or energy companies
to acquire ownership stakes in DH infrastructure. A notable example is Partners Group,
a global private markets investment firm, acquired one of the largest DH platforms in the
Baltics. This platform operates across Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania, encompassing 74
generation assets [28]. Traditional financing methods, such as bank loans and commer-
cial debt, are also utilized for new or expanding DH projects; in Germany, KfW Bank offers
preferential loans for green DH investments [29]. Therefore, private investment decisions
are primarily guided by the feasibility of DH projects, which refers to their technical, eco-
nomic, and policy viability [30]. Financial performance is typically assessed using key
indicators such as the Net Present Value (NPV), Internal Rate of Return (IRR), and pay-
back period, which respectively capture long-term value creation, expected profitability,
and the timeframe for capital recovery [31].
Blended finance approaches, which combine public and private funding through public-
private partnership (PPPs), have emerged as effective strategies to mitigate risks and en-
hance the bankability of DH projects. PPPs represent long-term collaborations between
public authorities and private companies to co-finance and operate DH networks. Popovic
et al. (2024) discuss how blended finance can catalyze accelerating the European heat
transition by de-risking investments and improving the risk-return profile for private in-
vestors [3]. Countries like the Netherlands have successfully implemented PPP models to
accelerate DH deployment, leveraging government-backed guarantees and co-financing
arrangements to attract private capital [32]. Concessional or low-interest loans, provided
by development banks and climate funds, are a key component of blended finance in DH.

Institutions like the European Investment Bank and the Green Climate Fund offer these

This study addresses a critical gap by investigating how subsidies and incentives influence
the attraction of investors through the calculation of financial metrics such as NPV, IRR,
and the Levelized Cost of Heat (LCOH) in DH across Europe. By conducting a quantitative
comparative analysis, the research provides actionable insights that connect theoretical
understanding with practical investment decisions in order to promote DH deployment.
2.1.2
Taxation and carbon pricing
Another aspect of financial frameworks involves taxation through carbon pricing mech-
anisms, such as carbon taxes, non-renewable energy source (non-RES) energy taxes,
tax exemptions, and emissions trading systems (ETS). These mechanisms serve as eco-
nomic incentives to reduce greenhouse gas emissions while generating revenue that can
be reinvested into green infrastructure [34].
Carbon pricing captures the external costs of greenhouse gas emissions the costs that the
public pays for, such as damage to crops, healthcare costs from heat waves and droughts,
and property loss from flooding and sea level rise and ties them to their sources through
a price. This financial tool puts a price on pollution, enforcing the “polluter pays” principle
and pushing the market toward cleaner energy [35]. Depending on their design, carbon
taxes promote the transition to low-carbon energy systems, as fossil fuel heat generation
usually becomes more expensive therefore, less competitive with non-RES energy taxes.
Non-RES energy taxes can also include tax exemptions for users who primarily consume
renewable energy sources.
Emissions trading systems, such as the EU ETS, introduce additional flexibility by allowing
companies to trade emissions allowances, helping lower overall costs while maintaining
climate ambition [34].
Despite ambitious European targets for decarbonizing H&C, securing adequate financing
for DH projects remains a critical challenge [2], [3], [20]. Extensive research has ex-
amined the technical feasibility of DH networks [36], [20], [37], [38], particularly 4th and
5th generation systems, which are characterized by lower distribution temperatures, in-
creased energy efficiency, and greater integration of renewable and waste heat sources
[39]. However, the financial viability and sustainability of such DHN has received compar-
atively less attention [2]. This gap limits the usefulness of existing models for policymak-
ers and investors, who must assess both technical and economic factors when making
investment decisions.
2.2
Regulatory framework
The regulatory framework in the context of DH refers to the legal and institutional regula-
tions and tools that govern market structure, pricing, competition, and access to networks
[7]. These regulations define the roles of public and private entities, establish operational
and investment guidelines, and ensure compliance with sustainability and consumer pro-
tection standards [2], [7]. A robust regulatory framework is essential to attract investment
in DH systems, as regulations influence financial viability, risk perception, and investor
confidence [40], [41]. First, key findings from the literature on the importance of regula-

tory frameworks will be reviewed, followed by an overview of Fuel Bans and the impact
of Third-Party-Access on DH investments.
Several studies offer insights into the regulatory frameworks across European countries,
identifying best practices that foster DH expansion [2], [20], [42]. Billerbeck et al. (2023)
provide a comprehensive overview of DH regulations and support schemes across 23
European countries while identifying key thresholds, such as the share of renewable and
waste heat in DH and the share of citizens served by DH [2], [42]. These thresholds are
used to compare policy effectiveness. For instance, this research highlights that Den-
mark, a leader in DH adoption, has regulations mandating high shares of renewable heat
and strong municipal involvement, resulting in widespread network expansion [2], [42].
Conversely, countries with less stringent regulations, such as the UK, experience lower
DH penetration rates [2]. However, countries like Finland, despite adopting relatively low
levels of regulation, have seen substantial growth in district heating, potentially driven
by urbanization trends [2]. These comparisons underscore how regulatory frameworks,
along with other factors like urbanization, directly influence investment trends in district
heating systems [2], [42].
2.2.1
Fuel bans
Regulatory support plays a critical role in the long-term decarbonisation of DH systems
[43]. Key policy instruments include fossil fuel bans and incentives for renewable energy
integration [7]. Across Europe, many countries have introduced national regulations re-
stricting the use of fossil fuels in DH generation, thereby accelerating the shift towards
cleaner energy sources. However, the design and implementation of these regulations
vary significantly, shaping both the pace and effectiveness of the transition. National
policies determine whether such bans create a coherent regulatory framework or lead to
fragmented approaches affecting investment stability[20].
Denmark exemplifies a highly developed regulatory model, with the Heat Supply Act ex-
plicitly promising the integration of RES by prohibiting fossil fuel use in new DH systems
and targeting a fully renewable DH sector by 2030 [44]. France has adopted a more grad-
ual transition through the Energy Transition and Green Growth Act (La Loi de transition
énergétique pour la croissance verte), setting targets of 80% renewable energy in DH
by 2030 and 100% by 2035 [45]. Lithuania, alongside other EU member states without
national bans, remains bound by broader EU targets mandating full decarbonisation of
DH systems by 2050 [43]. Although the European Union does not impose a direct, EU-
wide ban on fossil fuels in DH, several directives and legislative packages contribute to
this trajectory. These include the Fit for 55 package and the Renewable Energy Directive
(RED II), both of which establish binding targets for increasing the share of renewable
energy in heating and cooling networks [7]. The effectiveness of these regulatory instru-
ments will depend on national implementation, coordination across governance levels,
and alignment with financial and technical support mechanisms.
2.2.2
Third-Party Access regulation
Another regulatory dimension influencing DH investment is Third-Party Access (TPA),
which allows independent producers to supply heat through existing DH networks [1].
TPA can enhance market competition, reduce consumer heat prices, and facilitate the
integration of decentralized renewable sources such as waste heat, geothermal, and solar
thermal [41], [2]. It also supports diversification and energy security, particularly in gas-
dependent countries aiming to reduce fossil fuel reliance [29].
While TPA has the potential to improve efficiency and sustainability, its effectiveness de-
pends on supportive regulatory frameworks, infrastructure readiness, and careful risk-

ment if not balanced with stable revenue structures [2].
Although TPA is not explicitly modeled in this study, its implications for heat price compe-

While existing studies provide useful insights into specific regulatory tools, such as third-
party access and renewable energy incentives, they often examine these elements sep-
arately or focus on individual countries [2], [3], [47], [48]. What remains underexplored is
how these regulatory elements interact and influence each other, particularly in shaping
investment decisions under different financial conditions. Moreover, few studies integrate
these dynamics into quantitative modelling approaches that can assess the effectiveness
of regulatory support across diverse political and market environments. This thesis aims
to address this gap by analyzing how a policy instrument, such as a fuel ban, in different
national contexts affects district heating investments.

Methodology
This chapter outlines the methodology used to examine how interactions between finan-
cial (FF) and regulatory frameworks (RF) influence investments in DHN. Subsequent sec-
tions detail the TIMES model set up, the scenario development, and the financial analysis.
Due to space constraints, only an outline is included. The reader is referred to the De-
tailed Methodology in the Appendix A.2 for a complete picture of the approaches taken in

3.1
Methodological approach
The analysis compares four country case scenarios (France, Denmark, Lithuania, and
Bulgaria) showcasing their current financial and regulatory frameworks, to three policy
archetypes, Low, Moderate, and High, which reflect weak, average, and strong levels of
support and regulatory pressure for DH investments. By comparing real-world examples
with these archetypes, the analysis provides insight into how different financial and reg-
ulatory frameworks impact the feasibility, attractiveness and cost-efficiency of DHN. To
explore this, the analysis is conducted in two stages, using TIMES software and financial
analysis as shown in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Methodology overview of inputs and outputs of the modeling process through
the stages of TIMES Model and Financial Analysis.
The first stage uses the TIMES model, which simulates the Bilbao DH system. The key

regional scales and allows for detailed representation of technological, economic, and
environmental parameters.[51].

3.2.3
Main assumptions
The following assumptions define the key parameters shaping the DH system model,
ensuring consistency in demand estimation and economic factors:
Heat demand projection
Regarding the demand assumption from 2022 till 2030, the residential heat demand is
derived from projected population growth of Bilbao, while commercial heat demand is
based on Bilbao’s GDP growth projections. This assumption was already included in the
original Bilbao base case.
Simulation years
The simulation years 2025 and 2030 were selected to reduce computational load and
manage time constraints given the high number of scenarios (seven). The base year
2022 was already defined in the original Bilbao base case.
Modeling low interest loan
Due to the limitations of the TIMES model in representing detailed loan structures (such as
interest rates, principal repayment, and payment periods), an indirect approach was used
by applying a discount rate. This rate reflects the time value of money is used to annualize
capital costs over the technical economic lifetime of technologies, providing a simplified
but realistic representation of investment costs. In this study, a slightly higher discount
rate than the actual loan interest rate is used to conservatively account for financial risk,
uncertainties, and the inability to model detailed cash flows directly.
Implementation of fuel ban
The fuel ban is implemented in the model through a strict constraint that prohibits fossil
fuel use in DH across residential, commercial, and heat generation sectors. For simplicity,
it was assumed that the same restriction is applied uniformly. However, a more refined
approach would differentiate between sectors and introduce the ban progressively, better
reflecting real-world policy timelines and varying regulatory strictness.

Scenario development
3.3.1
Empirical data based on country benchmark
This subsection outlines the main financial and regulatory features of four European coun-
tries, France, Denmark Lithuania and Bulgaria, used as benchmarks to build policy sce-
narios (defined further in subsection 3.3.2). These countries were selected to reflect a
wide span of financial and regulatory conditions across European DH markets. France
exemplifies an emerging market, with increasing levels of incentives and progressive re-
newable energy targets. Denmark a mature Nordic market, characterized by strong regu-
lations, generous financial support, and the implementation of fossil fuel bans. Lithuania
represents a transitioning market in the Baltic region with rapid DH modernization, under
partial policy support. Bulgaria offers a case of limited regulatory pressure and minimal
public funding, reflecting a more constrained policy context in Southeastern Europe.
Together, these benchmark cases provide the empirical foundation for constructing the
policy intensity scale. The following paragraphs summarize the main types of financial
and regulatory instruments assessed across the benchmark countries, which form the
basis of the policy scenarios. These instruments serve as inputs in the TIMES model and
are summarized in Figure 3.2. Detailed figures (see Figure A.5 and A.6) and a description
of how these elements were translated into scenario inputs is provided in the Appendix
A.2 (see Figure A.4).
Subsidies and Incentives
Capital grants and low-interest loans were characterized based on investment coverage,
interest rates, and repayment periods. High-support scenarios, such as those in France
and Denmark, assumed grant coverage of up to 60% , with Denmark also offering low-
interest loans at 2% over a 25-year term [53], [54], [55], [56]. Moderate-support scenarios
reflected partial subsidies, such as Lithuania’s 30% grant coverage, and loans with a 20-
year repayment period at a 3% interest rate from the European Investment Bank [53],
[57]. Low-support cases, such as Bulgaria, assumed minimal or no public funding, with
loans featuring shorter repayment periods (10 years) and higher interest rates (4%) [53],
[58]. The percentage of grants that can be offered in every scenario applies to all new
investment processes on TIMES with renewable sources, which includes heat pumps,
electric boilers, CHP plants, biogas heating plants, solar ground heating plants [53], [59].
Taxation and Carbon Pricing
Carbon pricing in the model follows two distinct mechanisms: carbon taxation and the EU
ETS. DH suppliers pay a fixed carbon tax per tonne of CO2 emitted, typically charged upon
fuel purchase or through monthly accounting. In contrast, the EU ETS operates on a cap-
and-trade basis, where emitters receive or purchase annual emission allowances, each
corresponding to one tonne of CO2. ETS coverage is applied to DH generation units ex-
ceeding 20 MW of thermal capacity, reflecting EU regulatory thresholds. Emissions from
smaller units fall under national carbon tax schemes. This division mirrors real-world im-
plementation, where most large DH systems are subject to ETS, while smaller fossil-fuel-
based boilers are taxed instead. The ETS allows trading of unused allowances, creating
financial incentives for emission reduction. Entities emitting less than their allowance
may sell excess permits, while those exceeding their quota must purchase additional
EU allowances or reduce emissions. Scenarios ranged from high (e.g., Sweden, 140–
180 €/tCO2), moderate (e.g., Denmark, 80 €/tCO2), to low (e.g., Bulgaria, <5 €/tCO2)
[60, 61]. Both fixed and market-based mechanisms were considered to reflect varied na-
tional practices. These mechanisms constrain investments toward RES heat generation,
assessing their impact on cost structures and financial incentives for DH [62], [3].

Lifetime of the project
In this study, technical lifetime is set equal to economic lifetime of the project. This con-
servative approach avoids overestimating returns, reflects the uncertainty of long-term
economic viability and is consistent with common practice in cost-benefit analysis [66].

years, 2025 and 2030, because this is the output of TIMES. Each investment lasts for its

invested in during both years. Investments are made at the beginning of each year, so they
are not discounted for that year. For simplification in the model, the investment appears
one year earlier. Additionally, production starts in the same year as the investment.
Inflation rate
The inflation rate is set at 2% from 2025 onward, consistently across all years and sce-

Decommissioning cost
Reliable data on the decommissioning costs of DH systems specifically is scarce. To ap-
proximate a realistic value for DECEX, an average cost per MW was derived from two
sources: (i) the decommissioning cost of a DH network in France, known for its extensive
infrastructure removal requirements, and (ii) data from a summary of 28 different decom-
missioning reports on gas and coal-fired heat generation plants, which typically incur high
dismantling and remediation expenses [68, 69]. These two references were selected to
reflect a conservative yet representative estimate across infrastructure types. The final
DECEX value used in the model corresponds to the mid-range average estimate.
Scrap value
To estimate the scrap value or salvage value of decommissioned assets, the same ap-
proach as for DECEX was used. The first reference point was a U.S.-based infrastruc-
ture project, from which scrap value per MW was derived using the same cost basis as
for decommissioning, ensuring consistency across input assumptions [69]. The second
estimate was sourced from industry literature indicating that renewable energy systems
typically retain a residual value between 5–10% of their initial investment [70]. A central
value of 7.5% was assumed for this component. The final scrap value per MW was com-
puted as the average of the two estimates, then applied to each scenario according to
installed capacity.
3.4.4
Sensitivity Analysis
This section outlines the sensitivity analysis (SA) conducted to assess how variations in
upfront investment cost and the heat prices affect the financial viability of DH projects.
The analysis uses the IRR as the primary metric, as introduced in subsection 3.4.1.
Private investors typically seek IRRs above 10% to justify investing in capital-intensive
projects like DH, reflecting the need to offset risks and long payback periods. This makes
IRR a key benchmark for assessing financial viability from the private sector’s perspective
[31].
First, the SA examines how reduced upfront investment cost influence the IRR. By ad-
justing the amount of upfront investment cost, it identifies the minimum additional subsidy
required for DH investments to reach a double-digit IRR, thereby defining when projects
become attractive to private investors.The second SA assesses how heat price uncer-
tainty, driven by energy crises, policy shifts, or TPA, affects IRR and NPV. Therfore testing
the robustness of returns under market fluctuations. Unlike the TIMES model, which incor-
porates predefined subsidy levels and optimizes investment decisions based on system-
wide cost minimization, this SAs are conducted independently using the DCF model. The
aim is not to model system-wide optimization but to evaluate investor-oriented feasibility
under varying financial and regulatory scenarios. Together, these SAs provide a clear un-
derstanding of the financial thresholds that influence the investment in DH systems, while
isolating the effects of policy levers such as subsidies and TPA introduction.

Discussion
This section presents the main findings of the comparative scenario analysis. It highlights
financial and regulatory drivers of DH penetration, and examines how their combinations
affect project feasibility and investor appeal. It also explores how these factors shape sys-
tem configurations and cost-efficiency, using LCOH as a benchmark. Finally, it discusses
modeling limitations and proposes directions for future research.
5.1
Financial and regulatory factors driving DH penetration
Under Bilbao’s physical conditions, achieving high DH market share requires a policy mix
centered on high subsidies, strong carbon pricing, and moderate regulatory restrictions,
such as fossil fuel bans in 2035 similar to the French country-case. Indeed, the com-
parative scenario analysis indicates that France’s combination of FF and RF yields the
highest DH penetration of 46.49% in 2030, driven by substantial subsidies and compre-
hensive policy support. High financial incentives on green heat generation effectively
lower investment barriers and promote renewable technologies, including heat pumps
and biomass CHP. Carbon pricing further enhances the investment of clean DH by dis-
incentivizing fossil-based alternatives. The difference between the French and Danish
policy framework is in the DH penetration rate evident through the moderate vs high fuel
ban. A strict fuel ban by 2030 coupled with high carbon taxes limited the investment in
DH technologies in the danish scenario as it make the DH non economically viable. To
maximize district heating penetration and advance Bilbao’s sustainability objectives, pol-
icymakers should prioritize high subsidy schemes combined with a moderate fuel ban
by 2035. For Bilbao, adopting this combination could significantly accelerate the city’s
transition to a low-carbon, integrated heating system.
District heating penetration is not simply an outcome metric but a key indicator of policy
effectiveness [13]. Low uptake, despite financial support, may signal misaligned or in-
sufficient regulation. As such, DH penetration helps identify gaps between ambition and
implementation and guides future policy adjustments to support integrated, low-carbon
systems. It can shape future policies aimed at wider market growth and stronger energy
system integration.
5.2
DH project feasibility and attractiveness to private
investors
The scenario analysis confirms that all modeled DH investments are financially feasible,
as demonstrated by positive NPV. However, investor attractiveness varies across scenar-
ios, due to differences in technical lifetimes. The ‘High’ and ‘Denmark’ scenarios, charac-
terized by long technical lifetimes and strong public support, achieve the highest NPVs,
but only moderate IRRs (8%). They fail to reach the 10–15% thresholds typically used
by institutional investors and infrastructure funds to assess project viability, particularly in
the absence of risk-mitigating mechanisms such as guarantees or subsidies [3], [31].
In contrast, ‘Low’ or ’Bulgaria’ scenario, despite lacking subsidies, achieves the highest
IRR (19%), due to shorter asset lifetimes and faster capital recovery. This underscores a
key trade-off: while subsidies enhance long-term value, they may not deliver the capital
efficiency required to meet private investor expectations. Thus, aligning district heating
investments with private-sector benchmarks may require not only financial incentives, but
also adjustments in system design to enable quicker returns.

5.3
Financial and regulatory tools influencing cost-efficiency
of DHN
The scenario analysis demonstrates that interactions between financial and regulatory
tools influence the LCOH in DH systems. The ”High” scenario, characterized by strict fuel
bans, high carbon taxes, and strong support, results in the highest LCOH at 34.88 e/MWh
due to large-scale investments in centralized green technologies. In contrast, ”Denmark”
scenario, representing a mature market and reflecting the ”High” scenario with a shift from
high to moderate carbon taxation, achieves a lower LCOH of 26.5 e/MWh. In this case,
the TIMES model reallocates investment from centralized district heating technologies to
individual heat sources, optimizing total system cost by balancing capital investment with
operational efficiency.
Further reduction in regulation, combined with moderate policy support, as seen in ”Lithua-
nia” scenario, representative of a transitioning market, yields the lowest LCOH at 18.14
e/MWh. This outcome is driven by reduced investment costs, efficient technology de-
ployment, and lower operational expenditures. Conversely, an emerging market such
as France scenario, despite higher subsidies and a moderate fuel ban, reflects the high-
investment cost trend due to less targeted investment behavior and relatively inefficient
system performance, resulting in a high LCOH of 34.39 e/MWh.
These findings indicate that extreme policy interventions, while promoting decarboniza-
tion, may lead to overinvestment and reduced cost-effectiveness. In contrast, moderate
policy and financial frameworks allow for more flexible and economically efficient invest-
ment decisions, thereby lowering LCOH. A balanced combination of incentives and car-
bon pricing thus appears most effective in enhancing the cost-competitiveness of district
heating systems. In this context, Bilbao, as an emerging district heating market, could
benefit from adopting moderate policy support and regulation to promote cost-effective
system development.
5.4
Limitations and recommendations for future studies
Despite its analytical strengths, the Bilbao Base Case (TIMES model) presents several
limitations when applied to district heating systems using Spain’s infrastructure as a ref-
erence. Spain’s relatively warm climate leads to lower heat demand, limiting the model’s
comparability with colder European countries where district heating is more established.
Additionally, heat demand projections based solely on Bilbao’s population and economic

growth may not reflect broader regional dynamics. The assumption of similar infrastruc-
ture baselines across contexts risks underestimating challenges in areas with less devel-
oped or significantly different district heating networks. Furthermore, while TIMES opti-
mizes technology adoption based on cost, it may overlook critical local constraints such
as grid compatibility, retrofitting barriers, or consumer acceptance which can significantly
shape the viability of district heating systems. Further research should focus on develop-
ing scenarios that align physical infrastructure with corresponding financial and regulatory
frameworks to create more realistic and context-specific analyses. This would improve
the relevance and applicability of the findings for district heating systems across different
European countries, as performed in [36].
While this study provides a detailed assessment of district heating deployment under var-
ious policy frameworks, several areas offer promising directions for future research. One
such area is the role of ownership structures in shaping investment dynamics and long-
term financial sustainability. Exploring how public, private, or hybrid ownership models in-
fluence risk allocation, financing strategies, and system scalability could offer valuable in-
sights into optimal governance configurations (as explored in the appendix section A.1).As
highlighted in the SAs, heat prices are highly sensitive. Therefore, an important research
dimension would be the openness of district heating networks to third-party suppliers.
Studying how third-party access influences market competition, integration of renewable
heat sources, and system efficiency would enhance understanding of optimal system de-
sign. Additionally, it would be interesting to investigate on supports schemes that are
focused on reducing operational expenditure (OPEX). These include feed-in tariffs (FiTs),
premiums and power purchase agreements (PPAs), which ensure cheaper recurring rev-
enue streams for energy producers by setting long-term pricing agreements [2]. Such
mechanisms could reduce upfront investment and lead to higher IRRs in the DCF, there-
fore making the DH projects more attractive to private investors.
Lastly, the integration of green finance instruments such as green bonds and sustain-
ability taxonomies or represents a growing field of interest. Studying how these financial
mechanisms interact with regulatory and market conditions can help identify strategies to
attract private capital for low-carbon infrastructure.
Together, these recommendations offer a more holistic view of the financial, and regula-
tory environments that shape DH development across Europe. Most of the recommenda-
tions for future research are categorized in the extended literature review in the Appendix

Conclusion
This thesis has examined how financial and regulatory frameworks interact to influence
investments in district heating systems, by comparing four country-specific policy scenar-
ios with three policy archetypes using the TIMES energy model to inform decision-making
for Bilbao, Spain.
The analysis shows that investment outcomes in district heating are not the result of iso-
lated policy tools, but of their combined effect. While strong public support and moderate
regulation enhance DH penetration, investor attractiveness does not necessarily follow
the same pattern. Trade-offs between long-term public value and short-term return on in-
vestment highlight the complexity of aligning policy goals with private sector expectations.
Furthermore, cost-efficiency is influenced not only by the strength of the policy, whether
it is strong or weak, but also by the degree of flexibility it allows for making optimized
investment decisions. The most balanced frameworks, neither overly restrictive nor too
lenient, resulted in the lowest overall system costs.
For Bilbao, the findings suggest that a carefully calibrated mix of financial incentives,
phased regulatory measures, and clear carbon pricing signals can support both decar-
bonization goals and economic feasibility. Effective policy design will depend on man-
aging these interactions to ensure that district heating development is both economically
sustainable and attractive to investors.

Ownership
Ownership structure plays a key role in determining the financial sustainability and invest-
ment dynamics of DH systems, as it influences financial frameworks, risk allocation, and
scalability [71]. Ownership models in the DH sector can be categorized into public, pri-
vate, and public-private partnerships (PPPs), each presenting distinct opportunities and
challenges in financing DH infrastructure [72], [73].
In public ownership models, DH networks are owned and operated by municipalities or
state entities [3]. This approach has been prevalent in countries with a strong tradition
of public control over energy infrastructure, such as Denmark and Sweden (in Göteborg,
Västerås, Linköping, Eskilstuna, and Växjö) [74], [6]. Public models benefit from long-
term stability and access to low-cost capital, but they often face constraints in mobilizing
large-scale private funds [3].
Private ownership involves DH networks being financed, constructed, and operated by
private entities [42]. This model is often found in liberalized energy markets, where com-
petition fosters efficiency, innovation, and cost-effectiveness [2]. Nevertheless, private
ownership is more sensitive to market risks, regulatory uncertainties, and financing chal-
lenges, as investors require clear revenue streams and risk mitigation strategies to commit
capital [2]. Examples of private-sector-led DH investments can be observed in Sweden
(Uppsala) and Germany (Berlin, Hamburg), where Vattenfall has acquired and operates
district heating networks [74].
Public-private partnerships (PPPs) have emerged as a hybrid model, combining public-
sector stability with private-sector innovation, thus improving scalability and risk-sharing
[74], [75]. This arrangement combines public-sector stability and regulatory oversight with
private-sector expertise and investment capacity [76]. PPPs have been successfully im-
plemented in various European cities to scale up DH investments while ensuring financial
viability, such as in Borås, Sweden and in Tallinn, Estonia [74]. However, challenges
persist in regulating ownership, operational responsibilities, and long-term profit-sharing
mechanisms [73].
Based on the European Commission’s overview of DH&CN, one critical aspect is regu-
lation of ownership of DH systems [7]. In Denmark, the Heating Supply Act promotes
environmentally friendly heating forms and ensures municipal control over DH, providing
a stable regulatory environment that reduces risks and fosters long-term investment confi-
dence [44], [77]. The non-profit principle governing DH companies helps maintain afford-
ability but may limit returns, potentially discouraging private investors seeking higher prof-
its [77]. Clear regulations balancing public and private roles are essential to ensure that
DH systems remain both financially sustainable and accessible to consumers, avoiding
investment barriers caused by uncertainty around profit margins and regulatory changes
[78], [79], [80].
There is significant research on the individual effects of ownership structures and regu-
latory frameworks on DH systems, particularly concerning heat prices, risk distribution,
access to capital, and long-term financial stability [2], [71], [72]. There are also reviews

Ownership structures directly influence TPA, as it determines who can supply heat into

implementation of TPA varies significantly across Europe due to differences in national
frameworks, infrastructure readiness, and market structures [2].
Pricing mechanism regulation
Price reregulation constitutes a key aspect of the regulatory framework, as it ensures that
DH systems remain accessible to consumers while providing adequate returns for opera-
tors [7]. Heat prices in DH systems refer to the cost at which heat is sold to end-users and
are typically determined through pricing mechanisms that reflect the costs of heat produc-
tion, distribution, and system maintenance [41]. These prices can depend on factors like
the type of energy used for heat generation, system efficiency, ownership structure and
regulatory frameworks [82], [83], [72]. Price control mechanisms vary across Europe;
some countries, such as Denmark and Sweden, have adopted more liberalized pricing
with regulatory oversight, while others, like Bulgaria and Poland, use cost-plus models to
set prices [7], [71], [84]. While price regulation protects consumers, it can pose risks for
investors if not calibrated to account for operational costs and system maintenance [41].
The balance between affordability for consumers and financial sustainability for operators
is a delicate one, and overly stringent price controls can limit the attractiveness of DH&C
systems to investors [71].

Green Finance
Green finance refers to financial instruments and investments that support sustainable
environmental projects, particularly those focused on renewable energy, climate change
mitigation, and ecological preservation [85], [86]. Green financing metrics, such as share
of renewable energy and carbon reduction, are quantitative and qualitative indicators used
to assess the impact, effectiveness, and sustainability of financial investments directed
towards environmentally beneficial projects [86].
Recent EU sustainable finance policies are reshaping the financing landscape for DH
[86]. Legislative initiatives aimed at regulating the green finance sector present a signif-
icant opportunity to attract private capital for DH investments [87]. Two key regulations
stand out: the EU Taxonomy and the Green Bond Standard. These frameworks provide
a common language for sustainable investments and help direct capital towards projects
aligned with the EU’s decarbonization goals [7].

Third-Party Access
Another aspect that influences DH investment is the regulation regarding grid access and
usage [7]. Effective grid access regulations enable third-party suppliers to contribute to
the system, fostering competition and potentially lowering prices [41]. Third-Party Access
(TPA) refers to the regulatory mechanism enabling independent heat producers to access
DH networks [1]. TPA plays a crucial role in shaping the financial sustainability and in-
vestment dynamics of DH systems, as it determines the conditions under which external
heat producers and suppliers can access the network [46]. By influencing market com-
petition, tariff structures, and risk distribution, TPA impacts the scalability and long-term
viability of DH infrastructure [2]. TPA models in the DH sector can be categorized into reg-
ulated, negotiated mandatory, and negotiated voluntary access, each presenting distinct
opportunities and challenges [1], [7].
Regulated TPA involves government or regulatory authorities defining conditions, tariffs,
and terms for independent suppliers to access the DHN [1], [2]. Meaning that the network
owner has a legal obligation to allow access to the DH network. This model is typically
observed in countries aiming for significant market liberalization, such as Sweden and
Germany [7]. Regulated TPA promotes market transparency, predictability in pricing, and
fair competition [1]. However, it introduces regulatory complexity and may deter invest-
ments by operators concerned about reduced profitability and higher regulatory risk. This
may lead to higher transaction costs and operational complexity, potentially impacting
long-term investment decisions [1].
Negotiated mandatory TPA allows independent heat producers to individually negotiate
access terms, prices, and conditions directly with network operators. This type is com-
monly observed in markets with liberal regulation or where the market is still developing,
such as in Central and Eastern Europe, including Romania [76]. It provides flexibility in
pricing and terms, reducing regulatory burdens and fostering tailored agreements ben-
eficial for both network owners and independent suppliers [41]. It can quickly adapt to
market changes and specific local conditions. However, negotiated TPA may lead to
transparency issues, market entry barriers, and potential discrimination against smaller
producers lacking negotiating power [76].
Negotiated voluntary TPA models involve both the DH operator and the heat supplier
requesting grid access and deciding, on a voluntary basis, how to arrange the heat dis-
patch order for the network [1]. This setup is mostly used in DH systems that incorporate
WH from industry or CHP plants. Such models have been considered in countries seek-
ing balanced approaches to market liberalization, including Austria, Spain, and France
[7]. Negotiated voluntary TPA models balance transparency, market fairness, and opera-
tional flexibility while maintaining regulatory safeguards against market abuses, ensuring
fair pricing [92]. Nevertheless, they also induce an increased administrative complexity
and uncertainty, potentially discouraging investments [93].
TPAs have many benefits, such as fostering competition between heat producers and,
therefore, lowering costs and improving price transparency. Additionally, TPA allows de-
centralized RE sources (WH, geothermal, solar thermal) to be integrated into existing DH
networks [1]. By opening DH networks to multiple suppliers, TPA reduces dependence
on single providers and enhances energy security. This is especially relevant for coun-
tries like Germany, which still heavily rely on natural gas for heating and, following the
Ukraine conflict, are seeking to reduce their dependence on fossil fuels [29]. However,
the success of TPA depends on strong regulatory frameworks, infrastructure adaptability,
and balancing competition with long-term investment incentives [2]. While it can drive
42

down consumer prices and support sustainability, it also adds regulatory complexity and,
on its own, may not be enough to fully incentivize renewable investments [1], [2]. Thus,
TPA plays a critical role in shaping the financial and policy conditions that influence DH

Subsidies and incentives
Figure A.4: Empirical data based on country benchmark
The development of the scenarios for DH investment incorporates subsidies and incen-
tives, such as grants and low interest loans. These instruments significantly influence the
economic viability and attractiveness of DH projects [10].
Detailed data about subsidies and incentives were collected from various sources to align
these parameters with the scales described above. These included European innovation
funds, country-specific grants focused on decarbonization efforts, and low-interest loans
designed for large-scale sustainability projects. All this gathered data was then combined
to determine, for each scale (High, Moderate, Low), the typical percentage of total in-
vestment cost that could be covered by grants, or, for low-interest loans, to define the
characteristic interest rate and the expected economic lifetime of the investment.
Grants
Grants are designed to reduce risk by lowering the upfront costs of large-scale infrastruc-
ture projects targeting capital-intensive investments, thereby making the projects more
appealing to investors [10]. For example, European funds, such as the Innovation Fund
are available for all European countries that want to invest in large-scale projects to reduce
GHG emissions and can cover up to 60% of the additional cost, especially for industrial
heat pumps, adding renewables and mixed systems [59], [53]. Similarly, France’s Fonds
Chaleur provides high support, up to 65%, for solar thermal, geothermal, DH networks,
and using biomass or waste heat [21], [53]. In Denmark the Executive Order on District
Heating Heat Pump Subsidization (Energieffektiviseringer) grant can cover up to 50% of
electric heat pumps, and the District Heating Pool grant, which is a fixed amount of up
to DKK 20,000 per converted oil and gas boiler for expansion to fossil-free heating [54],
[55]. Based on these examples of support, where a significant portion of project costs
are covered, the high level for grants was generalized to represent 60% of the additional
investment cost. For Moderate level grants Lithuania’s financial support for replacing fos-
sil fuels can go up to €200,000 (20-30%) for air or ground source heat pumps [53]. The
UK’s Industrial Energy Transformation Fund (IETF) looks at each project individually and

coverage than High support examples, the moderate level for grants was determined to
be 30% of the additional investment cost. At the Low support level, grant support is lim-

Low-interest loans
Besides grants, low-interest loans are another important financial tool for DH investments.
They significantly lower the overall cost of borrowing money, since they offer lower interest
rates and longer repayment periods, than commercial loans, making projects more possi-
ble [33]. In the High support group, Denmark’s KommuneKredit is an important example.
It offers a 2% interest rate and loans that last for 25 years. These loans can lead to 1-2%
lower interest rates and payment periods of more than 20 years, which makes them very
attractive to investors [56]. Therefore, very low interest rates and extended repayment
periods define the high level of loan support. For Moderate support, Lithuania (Kaunas)
receives financing from the EIB, which offers a reasonable 1.5-3% interest rate and a 20-
year payback period. These terms, while still beneficial, are slightly less advantageous
than the High scale scenario, thus defining the moderate level of loan support. The Low
group for low-interest loans includes Bulgaria, which offers low-interest loans with a pay-
back period of up to 10 years and 4-6% interest. These terms represent more standard
loan conditions, still better than typical commercial rates but with a shorter payback period
and higher interest, characterizing the low level of loan support [58].

Taxation and carbon pricing
The model incorporates taxation and carbon pricing through carbon taxes and EU ETS
credits. Carbon pricing mechanisms were covering 22% of GHG emissions in 2020, a
percentage that grow considerably to acheive net zero emissions by 2050 [95].
Carbon taxes
As outlined in the Literature review 2, carbon taxes increase the cost of fossil fuel use
by taxing heat suppliers based on CO2 emissions from fuel combustion. This approach
promotes a shift toward low-carbon heat sources, such as DH [35]. Carbon taxes are
non-market-based instruments established by national governments as fixed charges per
tonne of CO2, calculated according to the carbon content of fuels. Tax rates vary sig-
nificantly across European countries, and tax trajectories for the period 2022–2030 were
compiled from national reports and central bank forecasts. For instance, Switzerland and
Sweden apply some of the highest carbon tax rates in Europe, averaging over 140 €/tCO2
in 2025 with projections reaching 183 €/tCO2 by 2030 [60]. These countries define the
high scenario, representing the upper bound of carbon pricing observed in Europe. In
contrast, countries such as Bulgaria and Estonia maintain negligible or no carbon taxa-
tion, with rates remaining near 2 €/tCO2, forming the basis of the low scenario [60]. The
moderate scenario is representing the European average, approximating Danish values,
with rates of 49.2 €/tCO2 in 2025 and 80 €/tCO2 in 2030. This trajectory aligns with a

long-term projection of 200 €/tCO2 by 2050 taxe rate on fossil fuels in Europe [60, 61].
EU Emissions Trading System
Unlike carbon taxes, the EU ETS is a market-based mechanism. It establishes a carbon
price through the trading of emission allowances, thereby influencing investment deci-
sions according to the cost of emission reductions [34]. In Europe, ETS typically applies
to large-scale emitters, including DH generation units exceeding a capacity threshold of
20 MW and using fossil fuels as described in the Annex I of the EU ETS Directive [96].
Smaller generators are generally subject to national carbon taxes instead, to avoid dou-
ble carbon pricing. ETS and carbon taxes are usually applied to different segments of the
generation side. Allowance prices under the EU ETS are shaped by market dynamics.
The Enerdata Carbon Price Forecast (2030–2050) highlights increased price volatility and
rising allowance costs, driven by stricter caps and declining free allocations [61]. These
dynamics justify a progressive rise in ETS prices and support the use of high-price as-
sumptions in scenario modeling. The high scenario adopts a price of 150 €/tCO2 by 2030,
consistent with the baseline trajectory presented in Chapter 3 of the IMF’s October 2022
World Economic Outlook [61]. The moderate scenario reflects current EU ETS trends,
with a projected price of approximately 75 €/tCO2 by 2030 [97], [95]. The low scenario
assumes a more conservative estimate of 50 €/tCO2 by 2030, corresponding to periods
of market oversupply or delayed policy tightening [61].

Regulatory policies
The model incorporates fuel bans as regulatory constraints, forcing fossil fuel phase-out.
Fuel bans restrict the use of fossil fuels in DH generation, accelerating the transition
to cleaner energy sources. Their implementation varies across Europe, influencing the
speed and effectiveness of decarbonization efforts. National policies determine whether
such bans create a coherent regulatory framework or lead to fragmented approaches af-
fecting investment stability. Data sourced from reports and national regulations is detailed
below.
Fuel Ban
Denmark has one of the most advanced regulatory frameworks, prohibiting fossil fuel
utilization in new DH networks and aiming for a fully renewable DH sector by 2030 via the
Heat Supply Act [44]. The Danish framework serves as a benchmark for the high scenario.
The moderate scenario represents France, with balanced restrictions fostering gradual
transitions of 80% in 2030 to 100% RES in DH by 2035 via the Energy Transition and
Green Growth Act (“La Loi de transition énergétique pour la croissance verte”) [45]. The
low scenario corresponds to Lithuania and other countries without national bans but still
submitted to the European target of a fossil fuel ban in DH by 2050. The EU does not have
a single law explicitly banning fossil fuels in DH, but several directives and regulations
contribute to the transition away from fossil-fuel-based heat generation, such as the Fit
for 55 packages, along with the Renewable Energy Directive (RED II) setting specific
targets for increasing the share of renewable energy in DH systems [7].
