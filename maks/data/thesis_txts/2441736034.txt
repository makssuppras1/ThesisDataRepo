Summary
This thesis enthitled ”Optimised planning of train cleaning” was created to analyse a
complex decision problem for the Danish State Railways (DSB), which takes the basis
of a thesis presented one year before. Therefore, it can be considered an extension of
this prior project.
This new thesis project consists of a mathematical model that, from the rolling stock
schedule and based in different decision trees, will determine which, when and where
the different cleaning options need to be performed to maximise the amount of clean-
ings with a determinate budget. In order to determine the time and type of each
cleaning, the model has to consider the cleaning standards of DSB , the time con-
straints and which makes the problem difficult, the coupling and decoupling of differ-
ent train units.
The model was fully developed in Python 3 with a three step decision tree model.
The first step of this model solves the problem for each of the train units. Then, the
second step will find a solution to violations on the train units coupling constraint
before running the last step where the price of the selected budget will be reached.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The aim of this thesis is to optimise the Danish State Railways (DSB) cleaning plan-
ning to ensure the biggest amount of cleanings with a previously selected budget and
cleaning standards. This project, is a rolling stock roster planning problem based on
a Master Thesis presented one year before, which will be further explained in more
detail.
1.1
Motivation
Every day thousands of people uses the public transport in Denmark to travel to
different points into and outside the country, in the year 2017 DSB moved 188,2 mil-
lions of customers [2]. This transit of people makes the train units have to be cleaned
frequently. The problem with the cleanings is the short time left for this operations,
with trains operating in tight schedules and that any small delay will affect on pas-
sengers opinion on the services. In addition, cleaning standards are very important
and not complying with them will have a direct impact in passengers opinion. In this
aspect, cleanings represented not only the highest expenses paired with maintenance
and repairing but also, ”Cleaning and Safety” is one of the five most important areas
in order to achieve costumer satisfaction [2].
DSB, company that will be presented with more detail in the following point, sched-
ules the cleanings of the trains on a daily basis. These schedule models are produced
for a four month period. Once the quarter is concluded, an evaluation of the cleanings
selected is performed and a new schedule is agreed with the new information.
Usually, train companies outsource the cleanings of their trains and the subcontracted
company takes care of scheduling the cleanings of these trains. However, in this case
the train company directly provided us the data, with the intention of finding a solu-
tion that could benefit both DSB and the cleaning company.
The motivation of this project is based on the previous work done by the Department
of Management Engineering with DSB. This previous work ended last year with the
Master Thesis entitled ”Optimised planning of train cleaning” [5], that will be men-
tioned several times during this work. Giovanni’s approach to give a solution to the
problem that DSB proposed, set a very good basis to keep working to a more realistic

the periodical customer satisfaction surveys.

different train units can be merged together to form an unique train during

2.1
Previous model
In the thesis of Giovanni Fanin [5], the main objective was to create a model which,
taking in consideration all the constraints, could minimise the cost. This is an im-
portant issue as in my thesis is been developed in order to maximise the number of
cleanings.
The objective of the model in both cases is to schedule the entire cleaning operations
for the fleet of train units of DSB. Assigning a type of cleaning (or not cleaning) for
all these train units, through the stations, that are planned by the rolling stock plan,
for each type of train. In Giovanni’s thesis three different types of cleanings were also
provided and the before explained merging constraint, making his project the basis
of this new one.
In Optimising the scheduling of train unit cleaning [5], a graph representation was
implemented in order to represent the decision taking at each station. In the graph,
the different decisions with the corresponding attributes were represented by nodes.
With this graph the problem was transformed to a minimum cost path problem for
each of the trains and each train unit. It was known that this approach was not com-
pletely correct as it could violate the restriction that makes each train have the same
type of cleaning in all their coaches, when a cleaning is performed in a station. This
problem was tried to be solved adding a second step to the resolution of the problem,
but due to infeasibilities found a fourth cleaning was introduced, the ”magic cleaning”.
In order to resolve the problem, in the first step for each train unit, some assump-
tions were made. The distance travelled between stations was considered as amount
of accumulated dirtiness. It can be considered that each kilometre travelled by a
train will accumulate dirtiness. This way quantify the amount of dirtiness that each
train unit had, was calculated based on the amount of kilometres travelled since the

the resolution of their rolling stock planing. On the other hand, it is not exactly a
substitute for a TR. When no other solution is available DSB performs an ”cleaning
in motion” where there cleaning personal cleans the train during a journey between
two stations. The big difference is that this type of cleanings are more similar to an
OR than a TR.

2.2
Other literature
In Coach cleaning operations planning in railway management [1], Adenso-Diaz, John
D. Lamb and Temprano made a study, similar to ours, for the Spanish National Rail-
way Company (Renfe).
In their case, Renfe was having their cleanings planned on a daily basis schedule
that the outsourced company, that was in charge to do the cleanings, was performing.
This outsourced company had to decide the cleanings that will be executed each day
considering the following questions:
• Which units where more dirty?
• When would the units be able to be cleaned?
• When was the last time that the unit was cleaned?
• What was the type of cleaning that was performed the last time?
• What were the resources available to perform the cleaning?
In [1] it was also considered that, due to the appearance of unforeseeable events,
the most optimum data will not be accurately obtained until the the working day.
However, Renfe decided that they will preferred to have a daily planning, that might
be changed due to different casualties, rather than having the outsourced company
planning all the cleanings. This way, Renfe will not have to share all the internal
data with an external company.
In [1] they also talk about cleaning families. This will give our model a more realistic
way to approach cleanings of the train units than the percentages used by Giovanni’s
[5] in his model. A cleaning family consist in ”a group of different cleaning opera-
tions with the characteristic that one operation contains the next one (a determined
cleaning is obtained by adding some operations to another one)” [1].

To avoid this situation, one of the firsts things in the new model was to eliminate
the effectiveness rates. These are just a simplification and are making the model
to actuate in a none optimal way. In order to eliminate those rates, we will set a
maximum of kilometres that a train unit can performed without a cleaning to each

are part of the same family of cleanings as TR is an extended LR and this, is also an
extension of OR. This means, that once a TR will be performed, a LR and OR will

3.4
Decision Tree
In order to maximise the amount of cleanings, instead of using the Resource Con-
strained Shortest Path (RCSP), a decision tree will be used. The RCSP was a very
useful tool when minimising the total cost of the cleaning planing, as it was looking
for the shortest path or in this case, the minimum cost for each train unit. However,
in our new approach a decision tree will be much more beneficial.

The new objective is to increase the amount of cleanings as much as possible, however,
the total cost is something that has to be taken in consideration when we are looking
for the minimum mandatory cleanings. Accordingly, the decision tree combined with
the maximum kilometre allowed constants (Mkm), will avoid the selection of unnec-
essary cleanings in this first step.
Cleanings will be performed only when needed and there is enough time to be execute.
In case there is no need, the option ”None” will be selected. The necessity will be set
by the Mkm of each cleaning and k type of train. If the Mkm it is not going to be
reached on the next journey, the cleanings will not be performed, unless there is no
chance to execute the cleaning again before the Mkm is reached. This will be used to
solve the problem with the non-linear constraints in point 3.2. There might be other
feasible solutions for this problem but we will only use the ones that are needed when
are needed. This can be easily understood with the decision tree in Figure 3.3, also
found in appendix B.

and LR where the most selected option. This way the model was able to keep the
MDDT just under the maximum allowed. Now, with the concept of cleaning families
OR can be seen as a subset of LR and this, in turn, a subset of TR. In addition, TR
is the most time consuming cleaning, thus, is the less likely to have enough time to

For each station, the first thing the model checks is if there is enough time for perform
a TR. If this answer is positive it will check how much kilometres the train unit has
to travel before arriving to the next station with enough time for a TR. In case this
distance is bigger than the MkmT Rk, of the k type of train unit that is considering
in that moment, a TR will be performed in this station, even if the MkmT Rk is not
reached in the next journey. Lastly, the model will check how long the train unit
has travelled since its last TR cleaning. It has to be mentioned that this step is
difficult to be ended as a yes, as train units cannot arrive with more kilometres than
the MkmT Rk. The only occasion that this will end with a TR being performed with
this last check, is if the train unit arrives with the exact number of kilometres that
it is allowed to do with the restriction of the MkmT Rk. If the amount of kilometres
is smaller than the restricted by the constant, the model will start to check the same

The decision tree will also skip a lot of no possible options. In case there is only time
for a OR, both TR and LR loops will be skipped and start directly with the only
possible solution.
The biggest advantage that the decision tree has against the RCSP is that if there is
time for a TR it will only consider the type of cleaning that is needed as stipulated
by the Mkm constants. At the same point, the RCSP was considering all the feasi-
ble options, creating 4 different extension possibilities and in the end taking the less
expensive one. This was taking a lot of computational time that has been cut down
with the decision tree. It can be better understood with an example.
Assuming that there is enough time for a k type unit to perform a TR in the station
that we are taking as example, but the train is needing a OR, because of the restric-
tions of MDDT in the old model and Mkm in the new one. The RCSP at this point
will add 3 different paths to the possible solution. If this case happens again in a
following station for each of the 3 paths it will make another 3 new options having
9 possible solutions. In the end, it will check which of this 9 feasible solutions will
cost the less and that one will be selected as the optimum one. In addition, it might
happen that more than one solution are feasible with the same cost.
If the same situation it is faced with the decision tree, the new model will take only
one possible solution: ”an OR needs to be performed in this station” and will continue
to the next station. At the end of all the stations that the train need to travel, the
new model will have only one solution, making the computational load much simple
and finding a solution much faster.
However, this part of the model is finding right the opposite of what our program
was supposed to calculate.
To this point, the output would be where, when and
which type of cleanings would be necessary, at least, to achieve the cleaning quality
standard set by the Mkm. Therefore, once these minimum cleanings are places, the
program will change cleanings in the stations in where the selected cleaning was set as
”None”, meaning it was not necessary to perform any cleaning, and replaced by OR
cleanings. This will be done until the budget value is reached, starting from stations
after the longest journeys without a cleaning to the shortest ones.
With this last step the program will return a planning of train cleaning schedule,
optimised for the quality standards that were set with the Mkm. Furthermore, this
standards will be improved with the rest of the budget that will clean the more dirty
train units that were not selected in the first approach placing the more of the most
cheapest cleaning. This cheap cleaning are the ones removing newspapers, emptying

CHAPTER 5
Solution methods and
Results
In this chapter, firstly, the solution methods that were used to solve the problem and
how this methods evolved during the thesis will be explained. Lastly, different results
obtained will be showed and compared with last year ones.
Making a difference with the way ”Optimising the scheduling of train unit cleaning”
[5] completed the first solution to the planning of train cleanings problem of DSB,
out decision tree model will change from a heuristic to a model that computes step
by step some actions. Even if this method seems slower than an heuristic, it helps
to avoid multiple feasible solutions, that might work for the problem, and go straight
forward for what is needed when is needed, ending up with a faster method than the
heuristic method proposed the year before.
The problem of train units merging and splitting, is solved with a second iteration,
as in [5]. This way we will be able to solve the difficulties in having all the train units
cleaned the same way. However, in [5], once the solution for the train units was com-
pleted, the model was taking the trains violating the before mentioned constraint and
merging the solutions. If two train units, sharing the same station but with different
cleanings planned, the model was changing the planning of both train units in that
station, selecting the most extensive cleaning, of the two already selected ones. This
method ended with the need of creating the ”magic cleanings” to fix the infeasibilities
encountered.
When going step by step, the model not only merges the solution for the train units
that are violating the constraint of having to perform the same cleaning, but also
reformulates the rest of the journeys for the train units sharing that station. This
way, if a more extensive cleaning, that the one that was selected in the first step, is
enforced to a train unit, because of sharing station with another one, the rest of the
cleanings for this train unit will be rechecked and changed to the new needs. This
changes may result in new violations of the shared station cleaning constraint. There-
fore, this second step will be held on a loop to check differences in shared stations
and reformulating the rest of the journey for each train unit until all the violations
==PAGE:42==
30
5 Solution methods and Results
are solved.
Lastly, once all the minimum mandatory cleanings are placed, the program will place
as much OR cleanings as possible until covering the budget introduced as input. This
additional OR cleanings will be placed from the longest journeys without a cleaning,
to the shortest one.
Before starting with the development of the solution methods, the following starting
assumptions were made:
• At the beginning of the week, all the train units are considered to be totally
clean in their starting station,
• train units can be left in the last station of their week dirty without exceeding
the limits fixed by the Mkm,
• the dirtiness of the train units are directly proportional to the kilometres trav-
elled,
• all the stations in the model are able to perform cleanings,
• big stations will be able to perform the complete train unit cleaning in the
time mentioned in chapter 4, while small stations will need this time times the
number of coaches in the train unit,
• trains sharing station at the same exact time, coupled train units, must have
the same cleaning type,
• the cost of each cleaning is proportional to the time consumed to perform it.
These assumptions will evolve in additional ones during the explanations of how the
final model was developed.
All the models and tests, including runs of [5] to make comparisons, were made on a
personal MacBook Pro with Intel Core i5 processor, 3.1 GHz and 8GB of RAM.
5.1
Step by step method
The model that was used to solve the problem will be now presented and explained
to detail. This model that will be used to solve the problem is divided in five steps.
Pre-processing, graph building, individual decision tree, looped merged decision tree
and solution processing as showed in Fifure 5.1. Firstly all the data is uploaded, once
this is done the pre-processing step will take care of all the data and to reduce its
size to decrease the amount of time needed to solve the problem. Later the graph
building will create a graph to allocate all the possible solutions and make it easier to
==PAGE:43==
5.1 Step by step method
31
visualise the problem. Then, the model will start the calculations with the decision
tree for the individual train units and will send a first simple solution to the loop.
Here, the loop will look for the violations of the merging train units constraint and
will resolve the problem with the train units not fulfilling this constraint until all the
violations are neutralised. The loop will end with the final merged solution that will

5.1.3
Individual decision tree
Now that all the data has been significantly reduced, the decision tree model will
start to work, just for train units. Firstly, the train units will be separated in litra

decision tree, then continue with the looped merged decision tree and once a solution
is found for this type, will go back and start again from the individual decision tree
with the next litra type train units.
Once the data of one train unit enters into the decision tree, a graph will also come
with it, for example the graph of the Figure 5.2 For the first station, the model will
set all the data to 0, included the node number which will allow the model to locate
itself at the first of the train unit journeys. Once this is done, the model will add the
distance of the first trip to the amount of km that the train has performed. With
this kilometres the model will know in which station the train unit is. Firstly, it will
check if the counter of kilometres since last of each cleaning has exceeded the Mkm
and if this happen it will stop the model and show: ERROR NOT ENOUGH KM
BETWEEN and the name of the cleaning that caused the error. This way, the model
is ensuring that no train will be running with an exceed of dirtiness. Once the model
checked that there is no error, it will check if there is enough time to complete any
of the cleanings. Because of the pre-process that was performed before, there will
not be any stops with time for no cleaning but still might happen that there is no
time for TR and/or LR and the part concerning this cleanings of the decision tree
will be skipped. Also, it will create three variables (kmwouttimeT R, kmwouttimeLR
and kmwouttimeOR) and search for how many kilometres will the train unit need to
travel before arriving to the next station with time enough for each of the cleaning
types. Then, if there is enough time for a cleaning to be completed, it will firstly
compare the amount of kilometres that the train unit travelled without having the
possible cleaning plus the corresponding kmwouttime cleaning variable, to the Mkm
allowed for this cleaning type. In the case that the sum of this two variables over-
pass the Mkm, the cleaning will be selected; even if in this moment the kilometres
travelled without this type of cleaning are much below than the allowed by Mkm. It
will also check if the train arrived with the exact amount of kilometres of Mkm and
in this case the cleaning will also be selected. If none of the restrictions to select the
cleaning is fulfilled, the model will move forward for the next cleaning type in the
range of cleaning families, with the same decision tree structure. If none cleaning is
selected, the option ”None” will be chosen.
Once one of the four cleaning nodes options (including the ”None” cleaning) is se-
lected, the model will check in which node the train unit is at this moment, and
where it has to go to arrive to the selected cleaning node. After the starting node,
node 0, in order to arrive to the next node, the model will add an specific number de-
pending on the cleaning selected to the ”node counter”, an specific variable pointing
to the nodes that the train unit will go through. It is important to know, as it was
explained in Chapter 3, that each of the columns in the graph represent an specific
cleaning, in all the graphs and in whole length of them, as explained before. If the
model is in the starting node 0 and after the first journey it selects to perform an
OR, it will add 2 to the ”node counter” to arrive there. Now if the model selects to

5.1.4
Looped merged decision tree
Once the model finishes finding the solution to the train units, with the decision tree,
a list of train units violating the merging constraint will be sent to the next step.
This list shows the train units that have a shared station and are not cleaned in those
stations as an unique train, with the same cleaning for all the train units in the shared
station. Now, this train units will go back again through a decision tree, that will be
slightly different from the fist one, as it can be seen in Figure 5.4 or Appendix D.
This time, when facing the new decision tree, the first question will be if the station
that is being checked in that moment is a shared station. In case of not been one
of them, the lopped merged decision tree will send this station to the normal deci-
sion tree and the needed cleaning will be selected as it was selected in the previous
point. However, if the station is a shared station, the looped merged decision tree
will firstly check the value of the most expensive cleaning in the shared station list,
that was created in the pre-process and filled in the decision tree step. With the cost

of the most restrictive cleaning and the station, the model will know which type of
cleaning is the most restrictive one and needs to be applied in all the train. If this
restrictive cleaning is a TR, the cleaning in the studied shared station for the train
unit, that is going through the looped merged decision tree, will be changed to a TR.
However, if the most restrictive cleaning is an LR and due to changes that might

Solution processing
Lastly in the model, once all the mandatory cleanings are set, the OR cleanings will
be added to the data obtained. In order to do this, firstly the model will generate
a list with all the stations that were set to ”None” changed to OR. Remember, that
all the stations that are in the model have enough capacity to at least perform an
OR because all the other ones were taken out in the pre-process. Now this list will
be shorten by kilometres of the last journey, independently of the litra types, having
the shortest trips on top of the list. Then, starting from the top of the list, all the
cleanings that where listed as ”None” in the first time, will be moved back to ”None”
until the total cost fits into the budget fixed. Lastly, all the data will be saved in an
excel, with all the cleanings type listed on it.
5.2
General Mkm and none abroad cleanings
In the first models of the solution method there was only one Mkm per cleaning, for
all the train types. We will be focused on finding the minimum cleaning solutions for
the input cleaning standard, against what it will be the final objective of the model.
This first attempt was done in order to find a feasible solution without ”magic clean-
ings” and the new model, to ensure that it was possible to find this solution without
the cleanings performed on moving train units during their journeys. In addition,
finding the minimum cleaning solution will provide us with the mandatory cleanings
to reach the requested cleaning quality with the Mkm to later improve it with addi-
tional OR cleanings.
The first difficulty was to set the minimum Mkm that will work for each of the
cleaning types, as our model needs these inputs to start the optimisation. In the
first attempt to set these values it was considered that the minimum value, for the
maximum kilometre without an OR cleaning, MkmOR could not be shorter than the
shortest journey, the 3,1km that separates the København H (KH) and Østerport
(KK) stations. If this constraint would not be taken into account and the MkmOR
would be set to, for example, 2km there will not be a feasible solution. All the trains
will need an OR cleaning before any of their journeys is finished.
Same thing was also checked for the MkmT R, as it was thought that it could not be
shorter than the longest trip. The longest trip is of 1131,4km that separates the dis-
tance covered by the train unit 1029_9, travelling from København Lufthaven (CPH)
and back to it without having time to have a cleaning. Meaning, that this train unit
needs at least 16 minutes to have an OR and, even if is stopping in big stations as

København H (KH), it is not stopping in any station for that long. Therefore, the
MkmT R should be set to at least 1131,4 km to ensure that no train is travelling more
kilometres than the maximum allowed without a TR cleaning.
Lastly, the value that we assign to MkmLR will determine how many LR there will
be in the model. As MkmOR is set to the minimum allowed, the higher the value of
MkmLR, the less LR will be. We set an arbitrary value of 400km.
When running this configuration an error showed up: ”Not enough km between OR”.
This is due to having the MkmOR set to the shortest journey completed by the train

Now, with this information if we set the MkmOR to 1131,4, it will cover all the train

Now the issue is that the maximum distance allowed without a cleaning is more than
360 times the shortest travelled journey. This will be discussed in following sections.
With this new configuration the model will run for the ERF and ICA types but will
stop in ETS. Once again showing that the problem was not having enough kilometres
between OR cleanings. This time, the program is able to have enough kilometres

• MkmOR = 1385km
• MkmLR = 1968km
• MkmT R = 2913km

Differences on cleaning prices are significant, with less cleanings in the planning of
the new model, it needs much more time to perform them. First explanation for this
difference is the objective of the model. While Giovanni’s model is trying to find
the cheapest (shortest) way to clean train units, our model, at this point is trying
to minimise the amount of cleanings, performing them just when they are needed.
This can lead in not choosing the most optimum locations to perform those cleanings,
small stations might be selected before big stations to perform a cleaning, because
there is enough time and a need of performing a cleaning at that moment. Giovanni’s
model may have chosen one station before to perform this cleaning, when there was
not a need of doing it but it was cheaper to perform it.
In an exchange of results with DSB, the second explanation was presented. Giovanni’s
model was also assuming, because DSB approved it, that trains going abroad were
taken back totally cleaned and without an additional cost for DSB. This assumption
was not only making the program to select less cleanings but also a cheaper solution.

mean that no trash cans, of the train units, will be emptied until performing 1385km
or that no windows will be cleaned until 2913km. These constants mean, that at least
one of the train units of DSB fleet, with the restrictions given, needs this amount of
kilometres to find a station with enough man power to be cleaned. As it can be seen
in Table 5.6 most of the trains are cleaned earlier than this kilometres fixed by Mkm.
Keeping with general Mkm constants, changing the values of these ones, it can be
appreciated the effect that each of them has on the final cleaning planning. As it
can be seen in the Table 5.7 if the MkmOR is increased, in the example by a 10%,
and the other constants are kept equal, the amount of stations without performing
this cleaning will decrease, making the model use more LR cleanings to maintain the
amount of TR. In the opposite way, if the MkmLR constant is increased; when run-
ning the program, train units will arrive more frequently to the MkmOR value before
reaching the MkmLR and therefore, more OR cleanings will be performed. Lastly, if
MkmT R is relaxed by 10% we will see more LR cleanings and in consequence, OR
cleanings will be shortly decreased.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis project addressed the problem of Optimised planning of train cleaning.
The scheduling of the cleanings for the fleet of train units of the Danish National
Railway Company (DSB). This is not an easy task for two main reasons: the amount
of data that means to work with all the planned train schedule, of a whole country,
and the particular constraint gave by DSB that all train units coupled together as
one train, have to be cleaned with the same type of cleaning when they are sharing a
station. DSB, right now creates this planning with the help of a MILP model but the
assignment of the cleanings is done manually, always taking care of setting a planning
that ensures the highest service quality in all their trains.
This project started as an improvement or extension of a first solution to this problem,
that was created one year before by Giovanni Fannin [5]. The aim of his project was
to find the cheapest solution to the problem and ensure that a minimum standard
of cleanings is accomplished during the whole week of each of the train units. DSB
did not find completely accurate the solution that was given to the problem, specially
because the need of performing some of the cleanings while trains were travelling from
one station to another, something that actually DSB uses but only in extraordinary
cases. In addition, giving a very short cleaning plan, was not beneficial enough for
the outsourced company that cleans the trains, as it might not accept working for
DSB for less than a certain amount of cleanings. Therefore, this time from DSB they
asked for a new project with the main focus of eliminate the before mentioned on
journey cleanings and maximise the amount of cleanings under a fixed budget.
These two new demands have been completed with a Python program that runs a
step by step model, completed by two decision trees. As a result, we are presenting
an optimised cleaning planning, similar to the one that DSB is working with, which
does not need any cleaning during journeys and fits into the budget input. Also, an
improvement on how the cleanings affect to the train unit has been made, to ensure
that trains are cleaned with all the type of cleanings before a certain amount of kilo-
metres and not having the same cleaning repeated once and again, leaving some parts
of the train units without a cleaning. It can be said that the result is positive and
no things have been left without been solved. In addition, changes have been imple-
mented after an exchange of results with the Danish Railway Company, to improve
the final solution. Cleanings are placed efficiently and the ones that are set to fill the
==PAGE:64==

budget, are also improving the quality of the service of DSB. Meaning that once all
the minimum cleanings are set, quality of the service is ensured and additional ceiling
cleaning will, probably, have no effect on passenger opinion about the condition of the
train. However, cleaning toilets, emptying the trash cans and removing news papers
more frequently will have a higher impact on the passenger impression.
However, in case the budget is set with a very high value, the program will not be able
to reach that price and will stop when all the stations that not need a cleaning, and
can have it, are placed with one. An improvement of the model could also consider
that once arrived to this point, it could start placing more expensive type of cleanings.
As it was mentioned before, to increase the amount of more extensive cleanings might
not have the same impact on the costumer, but it will reach the budget requirements.
A second improvement on the provided program would be to find a way to combine
both Giovanni’s [5] and the new model. This way, the program will be allowed to
place a few cleanings on journey, to find the cheapest way to find a solution, but will
use all the type of cleanings, maintaining highly cleaning standards and fitting in a
selected budget.
