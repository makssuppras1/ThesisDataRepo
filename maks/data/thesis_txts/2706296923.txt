Local Energy Market Design
Abstract
With the need for renewable energy generation becoming more and more prevalent, the need for ﬂexibility
in the power system to counteract the volatility of these production source is also increasing. One way to
increase the ﬂexibility in the system is to harness the ﬂexiblity of assets of demand side consumers and to
reward them for their ﬂexibility.
Energy communities have the capability to do this and in a recent EU
directive were introduced as something that all EU member countries need to work towards implementing
into the regulatory framework of their electric power systems. Therefore this thesis looks at diﬀerent market
mechanisms in energy communities, and how eﬀective they are in creating a desirable market in diﬀerent
case studies.
Three central research questions are postulated surrounding the behaviour of the allocation
mechanisms in the modelled energy community with a focus on the beneﬁts for energy community and its
members, what market properties are present with the diﬀerent schemes, and computational applicability.
Three case studies are used to answer these research questions; an inﬂexible case where no ﬂexibility is present
in the energy community, a fully ﬂexible case in which all loads can be fully shifted as is optimal, and a
complex case in which a combination of diﬀerent ﬂexible assets is modelled to be as realistic as possible. In
each case the costs are allocated using three diﬀerent mechanisms; a competitive setup in which a uniform
price is established for the energy community in each time period, and two cooperative setups where costs are
allocated using the Shapley value and nucleolus. The results show that the payment allocation is very similar
for all three mechanisms and that no large diﬀerences occur between the mechanisms. However, in the fully
ﬂexible case, it is observed that two desirable market properties are broken by the uniform pricing mechanism
when congestion occurs. It also turns out that the Shapley value has a clear trend of favoring community
members with solar production, which leads to unstable grand coalitions as some community members can
gain additional proﬁt by acting in a smaller coalition. Lastly, the nucleolus is able to guarantee the most
desirable market properties across all market setups, but has the trade-oﬀof high computational complexity
compared to uniform pricing, which could be problematic in larger scale energy communities.

Introduction
1.1
Context
One of the foremost challenges of our time is to avoid climate disaster caused by man-made carbon emissions by
making sure a global transition to Net Zero Emissions (NZE) occurs by 2050 and hereby stop global warming
at 1.5 °C. One of the main drivers in achieving this is the green energy transition, as power generation currently
accounts for over 40% of global emissions [1]. This entails increased integration of renewable energy generation
to push out carbon intensive generation methods such as coal and gas. However, with an increased share of
renewable energy sources, there is also increased volatility in the energy production sector due to the stochastic
nature of production coming from sources like wind and solar. Additionally, with increased electriﬁcation in
sectors such as transport, heating, and industry, the electricity demand will be reshaped with larger peaks and
increased variability throughout the day. Due to these factors, there will be an increased need for ﬂexibility
in the electricity system. In fact, to reach NZE, the global average ﬂexibility needs to quadruple by 2050
[2]. There are many diﬀerent types of ﬂexibility that can contribute to the required system ﬂexibility such as
hydro power, batteries, hydrogen, and demand response. In the context of this thesis, the focus will be on
demand response. According to [2], demand response will have to make up around 25% of electricity system
ﬂexibility in advanced economies to achieve any of the current stated policies or a net zero emissions scenario
[2]. Currently, the percentage of demand response in the ﬂexibility sector lies at less than 5% [2]. Therefore, a
central focus of developed economies, such as Denmark, should be on how to increase the amount of demand
response present in the system.
1.2
Why Energy Communities?
One attempt to harness more demand response is the inclusion of energy communities.
In 2019, the EU
released a directive on common rules for the internal market for electricity, in which the idea of citizen energy
communities was ﬁrst introduced in the EU context [3]. In [3], a citizen energy community is deﬁned as a legal
entity that "has for its primary purpose to provide environmental, economic or social community beneﬁts to its
members or shareholders or to the local areas where it operates rather than to generate ﬁnancial proﬁts" [3].
The directive declares that member states must create an enabling framework for energy communities such that
participation is open and voluntary, as is leaving [3] [4]. The directive also states that the energy community
should be subject to appropriate network charges at interconnection points between the energy community and
the distribution network [3]. Furthermore, the directive states that these communities should be able to access
all electricity markets either directly or through aggregation [3]. There are many more stipulations regarding
energy communities and their integration into the electricity grid, but the above mentioned criteria are the
ones worth highlighting in the context of this thesis.
In accordance with the EU directive as well the general considerations for ﬂexibility in the electricity system,
the Danish Energy Agency recently released their Market Model 3.0 [4]. The main aim of this market model
is stated to be the development of a ﬂexible electricity market [4]. One core focus of the market model is to
enable all actors to contribute to the ﬂexible electricity market of the future. This includes allowing new actors
to contribute to the ﬂexibility via ﬂexible production and demand response. This is represented in ﬁgure 1.

1.3
Literature Review
One common method with which a market with many diﬀerent interacting members can be modelled is through
the use of game theory. Game theory aims to model the decisions of players in an interactive situation between
multiple diﬀerent actors. An energy community is a situation where this is a very relevant application. An
example of this is [6], which looks at the the application of game-theoretical market mechanisms for heat
and electricity provision in an energy community. In [6] there is also a focus on the market properties of the
developed mechanisms. Meanwhile [7] looks at a sharing economy for storage assets in an energy community,
again using game theory principles to design a variety of market principles.
Additionally, game-theoretic
principles have also been applied to simulate real-life situations in countries in the EU, as done in [8], where
the creation of an energy community in northern Italy is the focus.
Another focus of the literature review was on how to best implement demand ﬂexibility in an optimization
model. One paper that looks at model development for ﬂexibility and pricing ﬂexibility is [9], which successfully
creates a demand model that prices ﬂexibility in a consumer-centric manner, where the implemented model
has a very naive approach at ﬂexibility modelling. However, demand within an energy community will not be
simple as there will be multiple diﬀerent consumption patterns and asset types.
Therefore we aim to ﬁnd a more complex method for the modelling of demand. One type of demand that
needs to be modelled in the context of an energy community is the growing technology of electric vehicles.
The use of an electric vehicle to maximize the self-consumption of solar energy in an energy community was
modelled in [10]. Additionally, [11] looks at how electric vehicles can be aggregated into their own electric
vehicle community and make proﬁt while plugged in. This approach does not look at the implementation

1.4
Research Questions
After the literature review, 3 central research questions have been developed. These are given below with a
brief explanation of why focus on these questions is essential.
How much does the presence of ﬂexibility decrease the daily cost of the energy community?
The main goal of this research question is to determine how much the community as a whole beneﬁts from
including ﬂexibility in it’s market. If so, how large is the beneﬁt compared to a fully inﬂexible case, and a
perfectly ﬂexible case. This could prove to be a key motivator for citizens to join an energy community.
How do diﬀerent payment allocations impact the payments by diﬀerent community members?
This research question focuses on how the diﬀerent game theory principles allocate the payments among the
community members. Diﬀerent allocation mechanisms may put more emphasis on diﬀerent ﬂexibility types,
meaning that diﬀerent community members may pay more or less, depending on what ﬂexibility types they
are able to oﬀer to the community. What allocation favors which assets will also be investigated.
What are the market properties of the diﬀerent allocation methods and how does the computa-
tional time of the local market change with diﬀerent allocation mechanisms?
An investigation of the diﬀerent market properties is essential to understanding if a designed market mechanism
fulﬁlls the desired purpose.
If certain mechanisms do not fulﬁll certain properties they will not be worth
implementing in a real-life situation, as community members may not be satisﬁed when participating in this
market. One last aspect that is worth investigating is the computational time needed for the diﬀerent payment
allocation mechanisms. Due to the nature of calculation, some of the game-theoretic principles lead to large
computation times (more on this in section 2.4). Looking at how large these computational times are is worth
investigating, as it may be a deciding factor in their application in an energy community.
1.5
Case Studies
To investigate the research questions, three diﬀerent case studies will be performed. The ﬁrst looks at a case in
which all community members have only inﬂexible loads. This case study acts as a baseline of what operation
without any ﬂexibility looks like. The next case study considers an energy community where the maximum
amount of ﬂexibility is present in the community. This means the case has only loads that are fully shiftable,
meaning that the entirety of the load can be pushed to any period throughout the day. This is an the ideal
scenario that can only be reached in optimal circumstances.
The last case looks to model a realistic and
complex case for an energy community. In this case novel demand modelling approaches will be used to model
a variation of diﬀerent ﬂexible technologies.

1.6
Hypotheses
The ﬁrst hypothesis is that the ﬂexibility in an energy community will signiﬁcantly decrease the daily cost
of an energy community. A fully ﬂexible case will create the lowest cost, while a more complex modelling of
ﬂexibility will create a more optimal solution than an inﬂexible case, but a worse solution than the fully ﬂexible
case.
Furthermore, it is expected that the allocation mechanisms will result in substantially diﬀerent cost allocations.
It is expected that a competitive allocation approach will not favor a speciﬁc technology, while the cooperative
mechanisms will favor the most ﬂexible community members the most and hence allocate them the least cost
and the most beneﬁt.
Lastly, it is hypothesized that the market properties for the cooperative approaches will be favorable as the
cooperative approach is built with the welfare of the community as it’s central objective. Additionally, the
expectation is that the computational time for the cooperative allocation mechanisms will be signiﬁcantly
greater than for the competitive mechanism.
This is due to the nature of calculation of the cooperative
allocation mechanisms that will be explained in section 2.4.
1.7
Novel Research Aspects
This thesis contribues with a novel design of market mechanisms for energy communities. Although game-
theoretic mechanisms have been developed before, this is the ﬁrst mechanism to the author’s knowledge that
implements asymmetric block oﬀers in an energy community, and by extension the ﬁrst time the market
properties of a mechanism including asymmetric block oﬀers will be investigated. If successful, this thesis can
act as a building block for future researchers that look to incorporate ﬂexible assets into energy community
market mechanisms. Additionally, this thesis looks to gain further insight into the asset types that generate
the most proﬁt in the diﬀerent allocation mechanisms. This will also be used to delve into what allocation
mechanisms seem most desirable in the diﬀerent setups and the properties of each.

Background & Methodology
This section of the text will focus on gathering and explaining the relevant theory to allow the reader to better
engage with and understand the work done in this thesis.
2.1
Energy Communities
First, some general terminology within energy communities needs to be deﬁned.
Additionally, as energy
communities are a novel concept, there is no established regulatory structure for them at this point in time.
There are a multitude of ways that energy communities could be established within the current regulatory
framework, but in [14], two main structural frameworks are established.
These are also described in this
section. Lastly, we look at the modelling constraints that need to be included for an energy community model.
2.1.1
Terminology
Community Member: Any contributing member of the energy community. This can be any single actor
in the energy community who is either consuming or producing within the energy community. This does not
include the community manager.
Community Manager: The entity that is in charge of gathering all required information and subsequently
optimizing the market within the energy community. This is by deﬁnition of the EU directive a non-proﬁt
seeking entity [3].
Prosumer: A member of the community that has the ability to produce energy themselves. This production
can cover either part or the entirety of their consumption.
Distribution System Operator (DSO): This is the entity that is in charge of making sure that power is
eﬃciently and securely delivered to individual consumers and owns the larger distribution network. This entity
is not part of the energy community, but rather the body that controls the network outside of the connection
point of the energy community. The DSO collects tariﬀs for the usage of it’s grid.
2.1.2
Structure
Communities with Grid Ownership
One potential structural framework for an energy community is a community with ownership of its own
distribution network. This means that all community members are located behind one common meter with
respect to the larger grid. All shared energy in the community is distributed via this community owned grid
and therefore no DSO tariﬀs are levied on this shared energy. This however also means that the community is
responsible for maintenance of this grid themselves, which may incur other costs. Additionally, it complicates
the ability of community members to freely join or leave, due to the ﬁxed location of the metering point.
Distributed Communities
The other structural framework proposed is a so-called distributed community.
In this setup, an energy
community no longer operates behind a single meter. Rather this community shares it’s energy via the common
grid.
This complicates the idea of energy sharing, as using the DSO-owned grid could lead to conﬂict of
interest between parties and tariﬀs between energy community members. To achieve this kind of structure,

2.2
Market Properties
Throughout this thesis there are a few main market properties that will be used to evaluate if a speciﬁc market
design is desirable or not. These are described below.
2.2.1
Market eﬃciency
Market eﬃciency means that the decided outcome is also the best outcome possible for each individual player.
If market eﬃciency is achieved, no single player aims to deviate away from the market outcomes that were
established. This means that their best possible outcome is achieved under the greater market mechanism.

2.2.2
Incentive Compatibility
In an incentive compatible market, a single market actor maximizes their individual proﬁts only by revealing
their true preferences to the market. This means that there is no desire for actors to misrepresent their actual
costs or constraints to further maximize their beneﬁts derived from this market.
2.2.3
Individual Rationality
Individual rationality refers to the fact that no individual player should have a negative operational proﬁt
when operating on this market. This means that all operational costs need to be recovered while acting in the
market. Therefore this is also referred to as cost recovery.
2.2.4
Revenue Adequacy
Revenue Adequacy refers to the fact that the market operator shall not be left at a ﬁnancial deﬁcit when
operating the market i.e. the payments made by the market operator should not be less than the payments
received. A special case of this is budget balance, in which the payments made to the market operator are
exactly equal to the payments made by the market operator.
According to [15], it is impossible to achieve all of the above listed properties at the same time in a real-life
situation. Therefore one must strive for a balance between these properties and decide which are most relevant
for the market at hand. This will be addressed in the discussion of the various designed market mechanisms
later in this thesis.
2.3
Competitive Game Theory
One area of game theory is competitive game theory. In this setup agents aim to maximize their own proﬁt
while interacting with other agents in the game. The goal of competitive game theory is to determine how to
best organize a game so that an equilibrium state is achieved and no individual player aims to deviate from
the outcomes that have been determined. This kind of solution is called a nash equilibrium.

Asymmetric block oﬀers is a modelling technique that allows for pre-speciﬁed amounts of demand response

providing demand response. An example of loads that can be modelled using this approach is thermo-electric
loads, more speciﬁcally thermostatically controlled loads (TCLs), such as building heating, water heating, and
refrigeration. This group of loads can operate within a range of acceptable temperatures and thereby up or
down regulate their consumption for a set time period. However, with this initial regulation, there is also

The model is implemented in python using the help of two vital software elements.

sets of hardware used throughout this solving process as well. The simple models are executed on a Intel(R)

solved using the DTU HPC. In this process, 128 cores of the HPC are used. What cores exactly were used
cannot be determined but they range from 2.20 GHz to 2.90 GHz.
2.8
Assumptions
Lastly, it is important to highlight the assumptions that are made while creating and working with the models
in this thesis. These are important to keep in mind going forward as changing these may alter the conclusions
made at the end of this thesis.
• All production within the energy community is zero-cost as it comes from renewable sources.
• A non-proﬁt seeking central entity is in charge of securely operating the energy community after receiving
all required information from all actors in the energy community.
• The market operates under perfect competition when operating under a competitive setup
• Only true information is revealed by all actors in the energy community when operating under a coop-
erative setup.
• There is only a single interconnection point with the grid at which tariﬀs are charged.
• There are no power ﬂow considerations necessary within the energy community.
• Demand response for a given community member cannot exceed the minimum demand of that communitz
member at any time.
One thing worth noting is that depending on what regulation is decided upon by the Danish state down the
line, some of these assumptions may no longer be accurate.

Furthermore, the stability of the mechanisms is investigated to see if any of the community members have
incentive to form a smaller coalition. The uniform pricing allocation is indeed stable as no positive excesses are
created. Next, we investigate the Shapley allocation for stability. The excesses for all coalitions are calculated

by operating in a smaller coalition. The largest excess is 0.01577 € for the coalition {1, 4, 5, 7}. Meanwhile
there is only 1 other coalition with an excess of more than 0.01€, which is coalition {1, 3, 4, 5, 7} at a value of

{4, 5, 7}. We can see that the greatest excess value and smallest coalitions with a positive excess is created
when a solar producer is combined with multiple consumers who do not have self-generation. This could be due
to the fact that the Shapley mechanism allocates more cost to the community members with no PV generation,
and therefore combining a single PV which can oﬀer cheap generation with consumers that are allocated more
costs creates a positive excess. Focusing on the nucleolus, the largest generated excess is exactly 0. This is for
the coalitions {3} and {1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8}. This is logical given that the value of cooperation is determined to
be 0 for community member 3. It is therefore also logical that the complimentary coalition does not beneﬁt
from the addition or subtraction of community member 3 from the grand coalition. However, as this is the
largest excess in the coalition, it can safely be stated that the nucleolus is stable. Looking at the allocated
prices, we can see that compared to the Shapley value, the nucleolus generally ﬁnds the middle ground between
the Shapley allocation and the uniform pricing. This counteracts the previously mentioned ﬂaw that leads to
instability in the Shapley allocation, namely that the PV producers are allocated too little cost compared

The hypothesis stated regarding the costs of the diﬀerent case studies is conﬁrmed when looking at the results.
The inﬂexible case has the highest cost, while the fully ﬂexible case has the lowest total cost. As hypothesized,
the complex case has an objective value between the ideal and the base case. In fact, the complex case is 2.07€
cheaper than the base case, and 1.24€ more expensive than the ideal case. This indicates that the complex
solution actually does a very good job of decreasing costs, as it covers more than 60% of the cost gap between
the base case and the ideal case. This thesis therefore clearly demonstrates that joining an energy community
can provide an economic beneﬁt for citizens in Denmark that are in an energy community. Therefore energy
communities can beneﬁt not only the grid by providing ﬂexibility, but also the citizens by reducing energy
prices. It is worth keeping in mind that this model does not consider capital costs required to invest in the
assets necessary to provide this ﬂexibility and contribute to an energy community. However, the basis for a
proﬁtable operation as a citizen within the framework of an energy community has been set and seems to be
a real possibility.
With regards to the payment allocations, a few interesting conclusions can be drawn. The diﬀerence in payments
for community members was not found to be signiﬁcant in any of the cases. Therefore the hypothesis stated
in section 1.6 is found to be incorrect. This result diﬀers from the results found in [6], and to a lesser extent
from the results shown in [7], where large diﬀerences in payment allocation are observed. The largest diﬀerence
in payment allocation found in the case studies conducted here was 0.03€ for community member 6 in the
complex case, which cannot be considered signiﬁcant. There are a few possible explanations for this. Firstly,
[6] uses a model which includes heat energy in its modelling.
Perhaps the additional ﬂexibility added by
cross-carrier ﬂexibility changes the way in which the allocation mechanisms allocate the cost.
This could
be an interesting extension to the market mechanisms presented in this thesis and would allow for a more
direct comparison with the results presented in [6]. Additionally, [6] uses a larger variety of case studies with
diﬀerent combinations of ﬂexible asset types. This could also be an interesting extension to this thesis, as a
larger variety of case studies could provide better insight into which ﬂexible assets are most beneﬁcial for the
community and how the allocation mechanisms change their allocations with these diﬀerent combinations of
ﬂexible assets. Meanwhile, in [7], uncertainty in PV production has been included to create a stochastic model.
With uncertainty in the solar production, it seems reasonable that ﬂexible assets would gain in importance
and be allocated more beneﬁt by the allocation mechanisms. Modelling the uncertainty of solar production in
this thesis would turn the deterministic model used into a stochastic model. This would allow for more insight
into how PV production uncertainty aﬀects payment allocation and enable more direct comparison with the
results of [7].

Looking at the diﬀerent market properties, there is only one that is achieved under all scenarios. This is
revenue adequacy. This means that the community manager at no point is looking at a monetary deﬁcit within
the community. In fact, in all cases except one, the revenue and grid costs were exactly budget balanced.
The exception to this was the uniform pricing schemes in the fully ﬂexible case. In this case the community
manager has more revenue from the community members than has to be paid to the grid. This diﬀerence was
negligible in this case study as the diﬀerence was less than a cent, but this discovery highlights a larger problem
with the uniform pricing mechanism. This problem relates to congestion and the resulting price increase in
the energy community price. As was discussed in section 4.2, congestion at any given time causes a budget
imbalance which leads to a proﬁt for the community manager. This was also suggested in [6], however it
does not come into play in any of the case studies in said paper. In the case study done in this project, it
was proven that congestion breaks two of the market properties in the uniform pricing allocation mechanism,
namely the individual rationality as well as the market eﬃciency. This implies that uniform pricing would
not be a suitable allocation mechanism in communities where the infrastructure is susceptible to congestion,
as this could lead to ineﬃcient markets as well as communities that will dissolve as they are not individually
rational. An interesting further research question here could be to consider power ﬂow limitations within the
community. How does this impact the community energy price for diﬀerent members within the community,
namely if congestion occurs within the energy community, does the community manager have to set diﬀerent
prices for diﬀerent parts and members of the energy community? Furthermore, is an energy community even
able to oﬀer any beneﬁts to its members when congestion occurs within the community?
Moving on, it seems that a community with prosumers who are all fully ﬂexible is not beneﬁcial for any
individual party, as was evidence by only negative values of cooperation for each individual community member

in the fully ﬂexible case. This implies that an energy community with too much internal ﬂexibility provides
no added beneﬁt to the community members as they are already able to operate optimally by themselves.
A possible further extension here is to look at how these large amounts of ﬂexibility could be used to create
additional revenue streams. In this project no markets other than the day ahead market are considered, but
large amounts of ﬂexibility could be aggregated to serve as ancillary services such as FRR, FCR-N, FCR-D,
aFRR and mFRR. By aggregating ﬂexibility and using it for these purposes there is potential to tap into a new
revenue stream that was previously unavailable for consumers in the residential sector. Of course, there are
many questions that would need to be answered when considering this. For example, how would the market
mechanisms have to be changed to account for the diﬀering time horizons of the ancillary service provision and
day-ahead market? Additionally, what types of ﬂexible assets are suitable for what kind of ancillary service
i.e. what technical capabilities does a ﬂexible asset need to have to be able to beneﬁt from a revenue stream
through the ancillary service markets?
The ﬁnal hypothesis to evaluate relates to the computational time of the diﬀerent allocation mechanisms.
As was demonstrated in ﬁgure 13, 17, and 25 the computational time for the cooperative mechanisms was
indeed signiﬁcantly larger than for the competitive setup, hereby conﬁrming the hypothesis. Even for just
an 8 member energy community the processing time came close to an hour. With the intractable nature of
the Shapley value and the nucleolus, even just adding a single community member will greatly increase the
computational burden of this problem.
This makes the application of such cooperative methods in larger
energy communities seem very infeasible. However, due to the desirable properties displayed by the nucleolus,
it could be very beneﬁcial to ﬁnd a way to apply this allocation mechanism to a larger energy community.
One approach that could allow for this is the notion of grouping community members into prosumer types and
treat all community members with similar traits as one entity while determining their payouts. As suggested
in [6], this would greatly decrease the computational burden of such a problem. However, how to best apply
this aggregated nucleolus calculation in an energy community needs to be investigated and could be a very
fruitful future research direction in the ﬁeld of cooperative energy community operating schemes.
It is also worth revisiting the assumptions made in section 2.8 and determining the impacts that breaking these
assumptions could have and what future research could allow for these assumptions to be loosened in the future.
As mentioned, perfect competition is necessary to ensure an eﬃcient market in a competitive setup. However,
in the market created in this study, only 8 market participants are present. As this is a very small number of
participants it is unlikely that a perfectly competitive market will be created. Rather, it is more likely that as
community members learn the habits of their other community members, they begin to use said information to
maximize their own proﬁt. Therefore, it could be beneﬁcial to investigate how to keep the market information
of market participants private from competitors so that market power cannot be established in the scope of an
energy community. In fact, this will be a focus in the recently established research project titled FLEX-CEC,
which will look at how to best create privacy proof energy community operating schemes and how to share
both energy and privacy costs among the community members.
The last assumption worth visiting is the assumption that there is only one grid interconnection point. The
energy community modelled within this thesis was assumed to be behind one central meter.
However, as
stated in [14], the idea of distributed energy communities that do not share one common meter also needs to
be investigated. One central theme in this would be how to structure the tariﬀs within the energy community.
If all shared energy in the community is subject to tariﬀs, the entire economic beneﬁt of sharing energy
within an energy community crumbles. Therefore a solution to this tariﬀstructure problem must be found
that allows for both regulatory compliance and economic savings within the energy community. Could the
energy community manager act as a retailer that supplies the households of the energy community with tariﬀ-

between all community member households tariﬀ-free or at a cheaper tariﬀ?

determining which structure would be best will be to model the potential mechanisms and see what market
properties are present with this diﬀerent tariﬀstructure. This opens a very interesting ﬁeld of research in the

schemes as well as the results. Lastly, many questions remain open for research, but the fact remains that
energy communities are a promising tool to incentivize community participation in the green transition and
unlocking the ﬂexible energy markets of the future.
