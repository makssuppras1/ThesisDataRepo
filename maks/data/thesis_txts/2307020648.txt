ABSTRACT
Vaccine design relies greatly on identifying pathogen specific antibodies’
cognate epitopes. As identifying epitopes within the laboratory is an ex-
pensive and time consuming matter, being able to computationally predict
epitopes is of great interest.
In this study, a sequence based epitope prediction pipeline was constructed,
showing that utilising the cognate antibody sequence significantly enhances
epitope prediction.
Results showed, that using a multiple layered ap-
proach introducing global sequence information, significantly improved per-
formance and potentielly allowing prediction of discontinous epitopes. The
present prediction pipeline was benchmarked towards state of the art predic-
tion tools, and proved to significantly outcompete state of the art epitope
prediction tools.

DANSK RESUMÉ
Vaccine design er i høj grad afhængig af at identificere patogen specifikke
antistoffers epitoper. Idet identificering af epitoper i laboratoriet er dyrt og
tidskrævende, er det af stor interesse at kunne beregningsmæssigt forudsige
epitoper.
I dette studie er en sekvens baseret epitop forudsigelses metode konstrueret,
hvilket viser, at anvendelse af den tilhørende antistof sekvens forøger epitop
forudsigelse betydeligt. Resultaterne viste, at ved hjælp af en flere lags
tilgang, som anvender global sekvens information, kunne man væsentligt
forbedre forudsigelsen og potentielt forudsige diskontinuerte epitoper. Den
nuværende forudsigelses metode blev benchmarket mod state of the art
forudsigelsesværktøjer, og viste sig at væsentligt udkonkurrere state of the
art epitop forudsigelsesværktøjer.

1 INTRODUCTION
B-cells are considered to be the core of the humoral immune response, by
neutralising pathogens using their secreted receptors called antibodies and
providing a long term protection towards the invading pathogen. Vaccines
mainly take advantage of this mechanism to provide a long term protection
towards desired pathogens, using different methods, such as attenuated and
subunits vaccines (Putnak et al. (2005)1).
The antibody recognises specific foreign molecules called antigens by bind-
ing a unique region on their surface called epitope. Some epitopes, called
linear epitopes, are comprised of a linear stretch within the protein sequence.
However, the majority of epitopes are the so called discontinuous epitopes,
which contain sequence gaps in the epitope region, though often being com-
posed of one or more linear stretches.
Modern vaccine design relies greatly on identifying epitopes for specific an-
tibodies. This is mainly done in the laboratory using various techniques,
such as X-ray crystallography (Sela-Culang et al. (2014)2), and is therefore
a difficult and expensive task. Being able to computationally predict epi-
topes simply based on sequences could potentially reduce the time and cost
of vaccine development by providing qualified guesses of which proteins to
investigate in the laboratory.
Nevertheless, predicting epitopes based on sequence have proven to be a
difficult task, since little improvements within the field have occurred since
2006, where the linear B-cell epitope predictor, BepiPred, was published
(Larsen et al. (2006)3).
As antibodies are known to bind to their own
unique epitope, the antibody sequence could provide valuable information
to identify its respective epitope.
1.1 Project Overview
In this study, a sequence based epitope prediction pipeline is constructed,
utilising both antibody and antigen sequences to predict epitopes.

2 BACKGROUND
2.1 The Immune System
The immune system is a defence mechanism for a given host to prevent dis-
eases induced by foreign invading organisms known as pathogens. However,
this is a difficult task as pathogens attempt numerous strategies to breach
and avoid this defence mechanism, which has lead to an increase in the
complexity of the immune system. The mammalian immune system can
be divided into three different lines of defences; the mechanical, the innate
immune system and the adaptive immune system (Hoebe et al. (2004)4).
The mechanical defence is simply mechanical and chemical barriers, like the
skin, which the pathogens need to penetrate before the innate and adaptive
systems are used.
The innate immune system is the second line of defence, which is fast re-
sponding towards invading pathogens. The innate immune system relies on
two systems, the complement and the phagocytic system, which each eradi-
cate pathogens and alert the rest of the immune system in various ways (B
et al. (2002)5).
The Adaptive Immune System
The third line of defence, is the adaptive immune system. A major fall-
back of the innate immune system is the lack of flexibility and therefore
pathogens have developed multiple ways to avoid this system. This is where
the adaptive immune system steps in, having the ability to adapt to new
invading pathogens and neutralising them. The adaptive immune system
is a lot more pathogen specific and its main forces are the B and T lym-
phocytes (B and T cells). Lymphocytes carry receptors which recognise
specific molecules of pathogens called antigens. These antigen recognitions
are based on small unique patterns on the antigens, called epitopes.
T cells can be split into two different categories; cytotoxic (Tc cell) and
helper (Th cell) T cells.
As mentioned earlier these lymphocytes also
identify specific epitope patterns, however, they do not interact with the
pathogen, but with receptors on the host’s cells called Major Histocompat-
ibility Complex Class I and II (MHC I/II). MHC I interacts with Tc cells,
which work as a window for the host’s cell presenting all proteins within
3
==PAGE:14==
the cell. If a given cell is infected by an invading pathogen, proteins frag-
ments of the pathogen will be presented on its MHC I and be identified by
a Tc cell which then kills the host cell (Parkin and Cohen (2001)6). On the
contrary, Th cells binds to so called antigen presenting cells (APC) which
contain MHC II. The APCs digest pathogens and present them fragments
of their proteins on MHC II to further induce the immune response.
Unlike T cells, B cells interact directly with pathogens by their B cell re-
ceptor (BCR). These receptors are highly specific and can be secreted, then
called antibodies. A B cell is also a kind of APC as it contains MHC II,
which it presents after digesting the bound antigen.
For a B cell to be
activated and start proliferating, three signals are needed: antigen binding
to BCR, Th cell binding to its MHC II presenting the digested antigen and
receptors stimulated by cytokines produced by the Th cell as illustrated in
fig. 2.1. After activation a B cell moves to the lymph nodes where they pro-
liferate and go through a process called affinity maturation. During affinity
maturation the BCRs increase the affinity towards the antigen, by introduc-
ing mutations and selecting the most efficient binder (Sompayrac (2013)7).
After this process the B cells can either become antibody factories known
as plasma cells, or long life spanned B cells called memory B cells.

2.4 Cross Validation
An important part of machine-learning is to assess the method’s accuracy
in a reliable way. This can be done using a so called k-fold cross validation
7
==PAGE:18==
(CV). In a k-fold CV the data set is split into k partitions of similar sizes.
Each k partition is then tested individually by leaving it out of the training,
thereby avoiding overfitting (Kohavi et al. (1995)12).
A common used CV is the 5-fold CV, which is shown in fig. 2.5. This shows
the initial data set (light gray) is split into 5 partitions of equal size followed
by 5 different models, leaving out each their own partition for testing (dark
gray) and training on the other partitions. These left out testing partitions
then add up to the same size as the initial data set, in this way allowing an
entire data set to be tested without overfitting.

2.5 Random Forest
The first version of Random Forest (RF) was developed in 1995 by Tin Kam
Ho, who wanted to improve the Decision Trees algorithm (DT), which of-
ten performed poorly when applied to data not present in the training
(Ho (1995)13). In 2001, Leo Breinman and his group created a new ver-
sion of the RF introducing bagging, also known as bootstrap aggregating
(Breiman (2001)14).

of multiple prediction models in order to boost the performance. The name
Random Forest originates from using numerous of DTs, which each con-

The Ensemble of Decision Trees
The first step to creating a RF consists in creating multiple decision trees
using different subsets of the training data constructed with the bagging
method. The concept of bagging is basically a specific use of the bootstrap-
ping algorithm to train models individually on different subsets of a given
data set (Breiman (1996)17). The bootstrapping algorithm is a sampling
method, creating multiple random subsets of data, which for example can
be used to assess the performance of a model, neglecting potential outliers
(Mooney et al. (1993)18).
Additionally, bagging uses resampling in the
bootstrap allowing the selector to pick the same data point multiple times
within a data point.
Multiple random DT are then created using this bagging technique, result-
ing in a wide variety of DTs as none are trained on the same data. These
DTs are then grown recursively by splitting the node into child nodes by the
best splitting feature of m randomly selected features (Breiman (2001)14).
All the DTs’ outputs then contribute to the final output of the RF by aver-
aging all. This is illustrated in fig. 2.7.

2.6 Monte Carlo
For optimising entire systems (global information), Monte Carlo simula-
tions (MC) can be performed. A MC simulation is a stochastic method in
which new states of a given system are generated and assessed (Earl and
Deem (2008)19). The main strength of MC methods are to find the best
global state, and that they are computationally quicker than greedy search-
ing all states in a system through (Li and Scheraga (1987)20). However,
there are also disadvantages of the method, such as in systems where small
changes creates large energy differences, so that the proposed moves are
rarely accepted.
In some MC methods, a so called temperature is used to slowly decrease the
possibility of accepting moves which worsen the energy. This temperature
is often decreased each cycle by multiplying with a given value below 1,
called the exponential decay (ED) (Hukushima and Nemoto (1996)21). This
is done to slowly relax the system, allowing to exit local minimas in the

3 RESULTS & DISCUSSION
This study presents a sequence based epitope, paratope and Ab-Ag interac-
tions prediction pipeline. To predict epitope residues better than state of
the art epitope prediction tools, this study uses the interacting antibodies’
sequence to guide the epitope predictions using multiple layers of different
methods to harness the strength of the individual models.
The training set used in this study comprised of interactions between an
antibody and an antigen, as shown in fig. 3.1 A. Layer 1 (see fig. 1.1) is
a Random Forest (RF) that predicts pairwise potentials between antibody
and antigen residues, as shown in fig. 3.1 B where the green dashed lines
are predicted as interacting pairs and red as non-interacting pairs. Layer
1 is followed by a refinement step which focuses on reducing false positive
interactions, as shown in fig. 3.1 C. These refined predictions are then used to
further optimise the performance in layer 2, using global information of the
Ab-Ag complexes to select the most probable combination of interactions,
which is illustrated in fig. 3.1 D to E.

3.1 Data set
The positive data set used for this work consists of a collection of molec-
ular interactions between antigen and antibody residues extracted from
solved structures of 665 Ab-Ag complexes present in the protein data bank
database (PDB). Removing redundancies resulted in 160 Ab-Ag complexes,
where 5 were left out completely for final validation. Together with this
positive data set, for each PDB structure we have created several nega-
tive dataset by pairing antibody and antigen residues that do not interact.
These negative data sets differ in size, composition, and sterical properties
of the selected residues. More details about the data set creation pipeline
can be found in chapter 5.
The data set is composed by 6117 positive interactions and 2503031 neg-
ative interactions. As this imbalanced positive:negative ratio can disturb
the learning (Chen et al. (2004)26), a sampling technique is used, down-
sampling the negative interactions. However, in order to evaluate the per-
formance of a given structure, all interactions between an antibody and an
antigen were used for testing. As only few antibody residues frequently bind
to the antigen, the antibody residues within the data set are trimmed down
to only consist of antibody residues interacting with the antigen in more
than 3% of the 155 structures. This leaves a total of79 antibody residues,
which from now on are referred as CDR residues and are shown in fig. 3.2
for the heavy chain’s residues and in fig. 3.3 for the light chain’s residues.
The 155 structures are partitioned into 5 equally sized partitions to use for
5-fold cross validation.

3.2 Analysis of Surface Accessibility
Both paratope and epitopes are located on the surfaces in order to interact
with each other. It is therefore expected that the RSA is important for
predicting these interactions. To analyse how well NetsurfP predicts RSA
on the 155 antigens, a pearson correlation coefficient (PCC) comparing it
to the real RSAs is calculated resulting in a correlation of 0.63.
The RSA of the antibody residues in cases where no structure is available are
calculated using DSSP on modelled antibodies created by Lymphocyte Re-
ceptor Automated Modelling server (LYRA) (Klausen et al. (2015)27). All
modelled antibodies had their similar antibodies blacklisted when modelled.

Comparing the CDR residues’ RSA of the modelled to the real resulted in

3.3 Layer 1 - Pairwise Potential Model
To identify the most probable Ab-Ag pairs to interact, a RF model is trained
to differentiate between positive and negative interactions, by including
various local antibody and antigen sequence information.
The RF is trained separately on all training sets within a 5-fold cross vali-
dation to avoid overfitting. Multiple analyses are performed in order to test
which data set is the most robust and efficient for training, which features
are important, and how the RF model behaves. The RFs are trained on the
155 non redundant Ab-Ag complexes with 1000 trees, using a 5-fold cross
validation. As the RF model does not handle the raw sequence, each fea-
ture including characters are encoded based on their type, which is further
detailed in chapter 5.
A data point consists of the Ab-Ag pair and their four flanking neighbour-
ing residues in sequence. Each residue on antigen side is additionally repre-
sented with their RSA and SS, whereas only RSA of the antibody residue
pairing the antigen is included. A few global features were also included
in each data point such as the volume of the antigen, all 6 CDR loops’
canonical structures predicted by LYRA, and multiple features to describe
the pairing antibody residue’s position in the antibody and CDR. These
are further described in detail in section 5.1
To evaluate the performance of the pipeline on predicting the correct inter-
actions (ITA), paratope residues (AB) and epitope residues (AG), four
performance measurements are calculated per structure and then aver-
aged. These four measurements consist of the area under the curve (AUC),
AUC10, the positive predictive value (PPV) and the true positive rate
(TPR). For both AB and AG, the interaction profile of each residue are
condensed into a single residue score (RS), using their highest predicted
interaction. As the average size of an epitope is calculated to be 17 and
the average size of a paratope is 19, the threshold for PPV and TPR of AG
and AB are set to these values, whereas the ITA PPV and TPR threshold
is set to 50, due to a high standard deviation (10 and mean being 40).

Marginal and Joint Probabilities
The output of layer 1 are values in the [0,1] interval, that can be inter-
preted as the occurrence probability for a given antigen-antibody interac-
tion.
These predictions are referred as interaction (ITA) scores or joint
probabilities. From these predicted probabilities, two other scores are de-
rived: the antibody (AB) and the antigen (AG) marginals (more details
can be found in section section 5.3). These scores can be interpreted as the
predicted probabilities that a specific residue in the antibody and in the
antigen is part of the paratope and the epitope respectively.
The joint probabilities between antibody and antigen is the main drive
of this study and it is therefore important to validate that the prediction
pipeline gains information to further improve epitope prediction by adding
the antibody features. This was tested by performing the three following
experiments.
First, several different RFs were trained separately with only antigen fea-
tures and only antibody features.
In table 3.3, the combined model
(AB+AG) and the single model (AB or AG) for both DSSP and NSP are
compared on AB and AG performance. This shows the performance only
differs significantly in DSSP’s antibody AUC performance, indicating when
condensing the interactions to the marginals there is little gain in the joint
probabilities.
Second, all the top scoring predictions for layer 1 were analysed and it
was found that 80% of the highest interaction towards the antigen residues
were paired to a tyrosine on the antibody side. Tyrosines are kind of uni-
versal binders, relatively large and additionally they are over-represented in
paratopes (Kringelum et al. (2013)29), which means interactions towards
paratope tyrosines are very redundant (25%) in this study’s positive data
set. This indicates that the layer 1 favors these interactions too much, as
these interactions are predicted most probable pair in more than three times
the real case scenario.
As previous results showed little performance gain was achieved when con-
densing the marginals, the third analysis was conducted to investigate
whether all CDR residues followed the same prediction pattern towards
the antigen residues. The average PCC for each structure is calculated be-
tween all CDR profiles and averaged. This results in an average PCC of
0.917 for NSP and 0.912 for DSSP, which indicates that a close relationship
in prediction pattern between CDR residues, further validating that the
marginals have little relationship.

3.5 Layer 2 - Global Optimisation
Epitopes are mainly known to be discontinuous with the addition of a few
linear stretches, however, residues which are far away in sequence can easily
be close together in structure and therefore epitope residues can be some-
what scattered over an entire protein sequence (Larsen et al. (2006)3). As
layer 1 is limited to contain local information, there is a great possibility
that this global information is not obtained. Therefore a global approach
could prove to be promising in order to improve the overall performance of
the prediction pipeline.
In this second layer (layer 2, see fig. 1.1 and fig. 3.1), a Monte Carlo simula-
tion (MC) is constructed to optimise on global features on both the antigen
and antibody side. The main purpose of the MC is to obtain the most
probable combination of predicted interactions within a given structure,
based on these global features. An important step is therefore to do various
analyses, identifying which global relationships potentially could be used
to optimise the performance.
Due to the fairly long computational time, it was chosen to only consider
NSP version and use it as a proof of concept study to validate that these
energy functions potentially could further improve our predictions in the
future.

To discover energy terms which could help improve the precision of the
MC, multiple correlation analyses are made on both paratope, epitope and
paratope-epitope relationships within the 155 non redundant structures.
The first analyses the correlation between the surface area of the paratope
and epitope. In fig. 3.9 A and B shows the results of the first analysis for
DSSP and NSP, respectively. This high correlation indicates that this could
potentially be a good energy term for the MC.
fig. 3.9 C and D, compares antigen volume to epitope size and epitope mean
polarity and paratope mean polarity.
Both show very little correlation,
indicating this would be a poor energy term.

4 CONCLUSIONS AND PERSPECTIVES
In this study a prediction pipeline was constructed, which showed that using
the antibody to guide the epitope predictions instead of predicting general
epitopes, significantly increased the performance. Additionally, this predic-
tion tool used global information, whereas many sequence based epitope
prediction tools are based on local information where only segments of the
sequence are dealt with and not the global sequence. Results showed that
the antibody sequence in fact contribute with important information, by
almost doubling the performance on AG’s PPV17 and TPR17 in both the
DSSP and NSP model.
The layer 2 and refinement step indicated that using some global informa-
tion terms could improve the performance significantly, however this needs
further validation in future studies. A benchmark was performed by com-
paring the DSSP and NSP model to both of the state of the art prediction
tools BepiPred and DiscoTope 2.0.
This showed that using the layered
approach and the real calculated relative surface accessibility (RSA) signif-
icantly outperformed both prediction tools. The NSP which uses predicted
RSAs, outperformed BepiPred significantly and was better or at least com-
parable in performance to the structural epitope predictor, DiscoTope. It
should be kept in mind that these comparisons were made on the test set
and not on the external validation set as a decision was made to save this,
to enable further improvements in future studies.
It still remains unclear exactly why a reduction of the marginals signal is
needed to gain the proper mutual information signal, however this could
potentially be improved by defining the training set. The positive data set
could be modified so that only specific interactions (hydrophobic, charged,
hydrogen bonds) are included. As tyrosines are relatively large amino acids,
these could potentially contribute to more unspecific (van der waals) inter-
actions.
The joint Ab-Ag information gain could be further evaluated by testing
whether a given antibody is in fact the best antibody to identify the epitope
of its paired antigen.

5 METHODS
5.1 Data generation
Database
To create a robust dataset, a comprehensive search for Ab-Ag complexes
was performed. All entries within PDB with a resolution below 3 Ångström
(Å) were scanned by five different hidden markov models (HMM) obtained
from LYRA. Each HMM was specific towards a type of lymphocyte receptor
chains; heavy, lambda, kappa, alpha and beta. The T-cell receptor’s HMMs
(alpha and beta) were used to sort away T-cell receptors that were close in
homology of antibodies.
Entries containing one or more pairs of heavy and light chains, which had at
least one CDR residue within 4Å of a non-receptor chain were considered as
antibody-antigen complexes. A potential antigen chain was considered as
longer than 60 amino acids with no ambiguous residues. In the occurrence
of multiple heavy and light chains in an entry, distances between all anti-
body chain’s conserved cysteines, at position 92 in heavy chains and 88 in
the light chains, were used to pair them correctly (Marcatili et al. (2014)32).
The antibodies were renumbered to Kabat-Chothia numbering scheme and
split into separate PDB files, with their respective antigen chain(s) (Abhi-
nandan and Martin (2008)33). Additionally, LYRA was used to model each
antibody candidate, where 15 structures failed to find a canonical structure
for a given loop. This resulted in 650 antibody-antigen complexes in the
final database.
To avoid overfitting, the data was clustered on both the antibody and anti-
gen side. The antibodies were clustered by appending the HMM aligned
heavy and light chains and running CD-HIT with a 90% sequence identity
threshold (Fu et al. (2012)34), whereas a threshold of 70% was used for
the antigens. These two clusterings were then merged so each cluster had
no relationship towards each other on both sides, resulting in 160 clusters
where 5 were taken out as a validation set, leaving 155 clusters for training
and testing.

with the shortest distance above 6Å were considered as a negative residues.
All in between could potentially be considered as weak positives or negative
residues, therefore these were left out (CMC (2015)35). A positive residue
on either antibody or antigen side included at least one positive interaction.

5.2 Analysis of Surface Accessibility
The correlation between calculated RSA (DSSP model’s RSA) and pre-
dicted RSA (NSP model’s RSA) was constructed by comparing them using
the PCC. On the antibody residues a similar analysis was done, however
instead of predicting using NetsurfP, the “predicted” RSA was calculated
using DSSP from the modelled structures done by LYRA. Each antibody
LYRA model had all antibody templates which belonged to the same cluster
as the given LYRA model blacklisted to avoid overfitting.
5.3 Layer 1 - Pairwise Prediction Model
The interaction prediction model was based on a random forest regressor
(RF) made by a python package, sklearn. As RFs are generally very robust
against overfitting, the model was set to use 1000 trees and within each split
to use the square root of features used (Breiman (2001)14). The RF within
sklearn provides an easy way to obtain values of feature importances, given
by the gini coefficient of each feature in the RF. This could be visualised
per partition within the 5-fold cross validation, which could give a valuable
insight of how robust the RF model is. Additionally the sklearn’s RF can
handle weighted learning, however this was neglected as computation time
understandably increased significantly and the results were not significantly
different.
Encoding
As sklearns RF’s numeric features, each feature containing characters should
be encoded into numbers. This was done in several ways depending on the
features’ type, as some encoding types might make more biological sense
for a specific feature type.
Amino Acids
For encoding amino acids, three different types of encodings were evaluated;
sparse encoding, BLOSUM encoding and molecular encoding.
Sparse encoding, is a binary string of the length of possible characters.
Each position within this binary string, would in our case, represent an
amino acid type. If the given amino acid is a histidine, then the position
belonging to the histidine would be ”1” and the rest would be ”0”s.
42
==PAGE:53==
BLOSUM encoding, each amino acid was encoded by its entire row
within the BLOSUM50 matrix obtained from NCBI (NCBI (2015)37).
Molecular encoding each amino acid was encoded by using its polar-
ity (Radzicka and Wolfenden (1988)38), hydrophobicity (Sweet and Eisen-
berg (1983)39) and volume (Bigelow (1967)40).
Molecular encoding ended being used in the training, as there was no signifi-
cantly difference in performance between the three encodings, and molecular
encoding only increases the feature space 3 values per amino acid where the
other gave 20 per amino acids increasing the computation time significantly.
Secondary Structure
Sparse encoding was used to encode the secondary structures which with
NetsurfP had 3 classes; α-Helix (H), β sheet (B) and coil (C). However,
DSSP returns up to 7 classes of secondary structures; α-helix (H), residue
in isolated β-bridge (B), extended strand, participates in beta ladder (E),
3-helix (G), 5 helix (I), hydrogen bonded turn (T) and bend (S). To make
it more comparable B, G, I, T and S were condensed into a coil (C).
Antibody Chain
As above, sparse encoding was used to tell which type of antibody chain
the CDR residue of a given interaction belongs to, specifying either heavy
chain(H) or light chain(L).
CDR Orientation
To encode which side of the CDR, N- (L) or C-termi (R), a given CDR
residue is closest to, sparse encoding was used again to specify L, R or O,
where O is if the given CDR residue is not part of normal CDR given by
the Kabat-Chotia scheme.
Performance
To avoid overfitting a 5-fold cross validation scheme was used. This was
done by randomly partitioning all 155 clusters into 5 partitions. The test
set of a given partition was as mentioned earlier the data set with all inter-
actions included, whereas the training for the remaining 4 partitions were
with a specific positive:negative ratio.
To calculate the performance of a model, four main types of performance
measurements were calculated per structure and averaged after each full

cross validation. The first performance measure was AUC, which often can
be biased towards easy negatives, which is why the second (AUC10) was
included. AUC10 only includes the first 10% of the false positive rate (FPR),

is biased towards the size of the testing (the larger, the more negatives can
be included before a 10% FPR is reached).

positives did we actually collect within our positive predictions. The PPV
and TPR measurements were calculated at a given threshold, which depend

5.4 Reﬁnement
To sort away the marginal signal, a refinement step was constructed to find
more mutual information within our predictions from layer 1. This was
done in three steps; marginal reduction, linear patching and a combination
of these steps (ensemble).

Marginal Signal Reduction
As this study focuses on the joint probabilities between antibody and anti-
gen residues and not just prediction the marginals, a marginal reduction
using the background probability of each marginal, antibody (Pab) and anti-
gen (Pag), was performed.
For each structure, all of its predictions were combined into an interaction
matrix, ita_matrixi,j where i is the CDR residues and j is the antigen
residues. Each prediction was supplemented with a pseudo score of 0.05
in the case of prediction scores at 0. The ita_matrixi,j matrix was then
fractionalised into three matrices (U, s, V) using SVD. The U and V
matrices contains the eigenvectors antibody and antigen side respectively,
whereas the s matrix is a diagonal matrix containing the square roots of
the eigenvalues linked to U and V (Golub and Reinsch (1970)41).
As the eigenvalues with highest value explain the most variance in data, the
highest eigenvalue of s and the first eigenvector of both U and V were used
to create the background probability vectors Pab and Pag (Jolliffe (2002)42).
This was done by multiplying both eigenvectors with the square roots of
the eigenvalue. A given structure’s ita_matrixi,j was then updated using
the equation 5.3, which removes the background probability of a specific
antibody position interacting with a specific antigen position. This was
done on all 155 structures separately.
ita_matrixi,j = ita_matrixi,j −Pab,i ⋅Pag,j

5.5 Layer 2 - Global Optimisation
Investigations of Paratope and Epitope Relationships
An important part of layer 2 was to perform several analyses to identify
global features which could optimise layer 1’s predictions. The analyses
were calculated on the 155 non-redundant structures and mainly focused on
relationships between paratope and epitopes, where Pearson and Spearman
correlations or probabilities were calculated to determine the strength of
their relationship. These analyses would improve our knowledge of how to
construct layer 2.
Five different analyses were performed focusing on; paratope-epitope size,
paratope-epitope surface area, paratope-epitope hydrophobicity, antigen
volume and CDR residues relationships.
The first was a simple analysis to test whether paratope size and epitope
size correlates.
46
==PAGE:57==
The second of the analyses were based on the relationship between paratope
surface accessibility and epitope surface accessibility. The hypothesis was
that the paratopes surface area and the epitopes surface area should be
similar.
The third was to test whether the summed hydrophobicity of the paratope
was similar to the summed hydrophobicity of the epitope. As hydrophobic
residues mainly bind to each other, it could be hypothesised that this could
correlate between paratope and epitope.
The fourth was the correlation between paratope/epitope size and antigen
volume.
The fifth was an analysis to obtain knowledge of CDR residues which often
bind to same or nearby residues in sequence of the antigen. For each CDR
residue the conditional probability was calculated towards all other CDR
residues. This was done for multiple window sizes; 1, 3, 5, 7, 9 and 10+.
The size 1 window, shows how often two CDR residues binds towards the
same antigen residue, whereas the size 3 specifies how often a CDR residue
binds antigen residue (ri) while another CDR residue binds to ri+−j, where

5.6 Monte Carlo Optimisation
To optimise globally on both antibody and antigen, a Monte Carlo optimi-
sation algorithm was created. Various energy terms aided in making either
epitope, paratope or the interactions look more like the real thing calcu-
lated from the 155 structures. This layer was not implemented in the cross
validation, due to lack of time, which means it could slightly overfit.
A pool was optimised by this MC, called the positive pool and the rest of
the interactions is called the negative pool. The initial positive pool was
the 50 highest scoring interactions from layer 1.
Mover Functions
A very big part of a MC is to find suitable mover functions which do not
screw your detailed balance (Swendsen and Wang (1987)43). Three mover
functions were made for this MC, mainly focusing on antigen side.
47
==PAGE:58==
Add and Remove Interaction
Adding and removing interactions is are a simplistic mover function, where
it either adds or removes an interaction from the positive pool.
Switch Interaction
This mover function switches an interaction from the positive pool with one
from the negative pool. In addition, it has fifty-fifty chance of switching to
an interaction with same antigen residue or same CDR residue.
Switch Antigen Residue
To avoid landing in local minimas of residues, this mover function was added
to switch antigen residues out, thereby taking all its interactions out of the
positive pool. Then adding a new antigen residue with interactions towards
same antibody residues as the one antigen which was taken out. This allows
it to not interfere with too many energy terms and thereby focuses on the
importance of the newly introduced antigen residue.
