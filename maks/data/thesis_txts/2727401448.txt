Abstract
Quantum technologies are predicted to revolutionize the state of the art of both computa-
tion and information technology. Such technologies require the development of appropriate
hardware, such as quantum processors. This project proposes a photonic quantum processor
which transforms coherent light into a single photon emission, useful in quantum computa-
tion, as well as a pulse of excess light. A single-photon emitter has many applications in
quantum computation and communication. The considered photon processor is implemented
in an integrated optical circuit consisting of nonlinear cavities with dynamic coupling rates.
The primary focus of this project is a circuit composed of a single nonlinear dynamically
coupled cavity. A single-photon state is created inside the nonlinear cavity and emitted after
a given time. This time scale is designed to avoid interference between the excess of the in-
coming light source and the single photon. Separating the two light pulses is possible due to
the dynamical coupling rate. The system is optimized to increase the probability of a single
photon state inside the nonlinear cavity by considering the coupling rate, the expectation
value of the number of input photons as well as the nonlinearity. The optimized simulations
yield a single-photon efficiency of 0.96 and a purity of g2 ≈10−6. The optimized value of
the dimensionless nonlinearity is χ/κ0 = 5, and the expectation value of the photon number
is |α|2 = 16. The results are obtained assuming no losses in the cavity.
The single-photon efficiency of the considered system was compared to other similar single-
cavity systems. In the linear cavity system, isolating the single photon state is impossible,
and only an optical attenuator can be made. For a static coupled nonlinear cavity, it is possi-
ble to consider a single photon state. However, its efficiency is lower than for the dynamically
coupled cavity. Thus, the integrated optical circuit consisting of a single dynamically cou-
pled nonlinear cavity yields the highest maximum single photon efficiency. This makes it the
best candidate for applications in quantum technologies.
Comparisons with expanded circuits show that a simple single cavity system is a good
candidate. This expansion includes a system with multiple cavities and a closed system with
a linear and a nonlinear cavity. When compared, no improvement is determined. Thus,
the single-cavity system is chosen as the ideal single-photon emitter due to its simplicity.
A system including a beam splitter is also investigated. This will enable the emitted single
photon to interfere with coherent light. The output intensity shows oscillations corresponding
to the self-phase modulation from the nonlinearity of a single photon.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Interest and investments in developing quantum technologies have continued to increase over
the last couple of decades[1]. Quantum technologies offer new groundbreaking strides within,
e.g., computing, information, and metrology[2]. Quantum technologies exploit quantum ef-
fects such as superposition and entanglement[2]. The properties enable the possibility of
measuring features even smaller than the wavelength of the light[2] and assist in secure com-
munication technologies by implementing a quantum key distribution. For the quantum key
distribution, one uses the state’s collapse at measurement to ensure that no third party has
intercepted the message[3].
The development of quantum algorithms can decrease the calculation speed for some classical
algorithms commonly used today[4]. An example is Shor’s algorithm,[5], which substantially
reduces the time for integer factorization. The implementation of this algorithm will impact
information security, since integer factorization is used to create encryption keys[6]. When
the quantum implementation works faster than any classical implementation, it is called
quantum supremacy. Quantum supremacy has already been achieved for measurement sam-
pling of probability distributions[7].
There are multiple ways to implement quantum technologies, i.e., trapped ions, supercon-
ducting qubits, or photonics[8]. Photons are advantageous due to their rapid propagation,
their ability to carry information, and easy manipulation[9]. The usefulness and potential of
photonic implementation have led to the development of photonic quantum processors. A
photonic quantum processor is a device that manipulates light for computations[10]. How-
ever, not all photonic processors have yet been implemented; thus, it is crucial to understand
and optimize different photonic quantum systems.
One approach of using photonics to quantum computing is creating a quantum photonic
neural network. Steinbrecher
et al. have proposed a quantum photonic neural network
implementation scheme[11]. The quantum photonic neural network will potentially have the
capability to act as a quantum simulator and speed up tasks usually performed by classical
neural networks[12]. The proposed implementation uses third-order Kerr nonlinearities in
their hidden layers. Their definition of the unitary from the nonlinearities assumes that the
==PAGE:6==

system is single-moded[11]. However, it does not necessarily hold due to the incoming light,
which might not be single-moded. Thus, another method of implementing Kerr nonlinearity
should be investigated. This could be using dynamically coupled nonlinear single-moded
cavities. The dynamically coupled cavity allows for loading the entire light pulse into a
single cavity mode. This project considers a quantum photonic processor that uses multiple
single-moded dynamically coupled nonlinear cavities.
Dynamically coupling rates enable control of the interaction between the waveguide and the
cavity. The dynamic coupling rate makes it possible to completely stop the light. The stop
of light allows storage of the light, which is useful in quantum information[13]. An imple-
mentation of a dynamical coupling cavity rate has been proposed by Tanaka et al..[14]. They
used interference between light pulses to change the Q-factor on a time scale of picoseconds.
This project proposes a photonic quantum processor that creates a single photon from a
coherent light source. The proposed device is created using dynamically coupled nonlinear
cavities. Single photons are used in quantum applications such as quantum key distribution
and photonic qubit-based quantum computing and networking[2]. A single-photon device
requires the device to create pure single photons with high efficiency, purity, and indistin-
guishability. The nonlinearities are used to alter the photon distribution. A third-order
nonlinearity is considered in this project as it exits naturally in some materials, such as
silicon[15]. The nonlinearity also results in a photon blockage, leading to a change in the
resonance of the cavity once the photons enter[15].
Outline of the thesis
The project aims to describe complex quantum phenomena through theoretical considera-
tion and simulation. To be able to describe the Hamiltonian coming from the nonlinearity
and the light inside the cavity, the quantized electromagnetic field is introduced in chapter
two. In chapter three, a description of the photon dynamics inside the considered cavities is
derived and presented using the SLH formalism[16]. The SLH formalism is chosen as it can
describe complicated integrated photonic systems without describing the dynamics in the
interconnecting waveguides leading to and from the cavities. Chapter three also compares
the equations of motion obtained with the SLH formalism with those obtained by the clas-
sical coupled-mode theory to ensure continuity.
Chapter four discusses how the dynamics of the integrated photonic system can be simu-
lated, including necessary assumptions, characteristic time, and errors induced by limiting
the simulation to have a finite Hilbert space.
The optimization schemes and results of the numerical simulation are shown in chapter five.
The photonic quantum processor is optimized by considering the maximum single-photon
probability inside a single dynamically coupled nonlinear cavity. The optimal configuration of
the parameters of the physical system is also presented and discussed. Chapter six expands

In this project, the dynamics of an integrated photonic cavity are described using input-
output theory.
Input-output networks take a given set of inputs and convert them into
individual outputs. While networks are typically considered a black box, this project will
elucidate the dynamics of the network as well. This section investigates the equations of
motion for cavity systems in the classical and the quantum domain. The two equations of
motion are compared to see whether they agree.

3.3
The SLH formalism
The SLH formalism is introduced to make the description of more cavity systems more
manageable. This section’s introduction of the SLH formalism is based on the work of J.
Combes et al.[16]. In the SLH formalism, each element is described as a triple G, consisting
of three elements: the scattering of the light on the element, S, the coupling between the
element and the waveguide, L, and the Hamiltonian of the element, H. Each object in the
described system is defined with the triple G = (S, L, H). The SLH formalism works as
an input-output theory, collecting all the element triples into a single triple. Different rules
are applied depending on the coupling between the elements to create a total triple GT.
This project uses three rules: one for connecting elements in series (cascade), one to put the
elements in parallel, and one which relates the inputs and outputs. One of the significant
advantages of using the SLH formalism is that the quantum states of the waveguides are not
included in the calculations. Thus, the considered Hilbert space can be reduced. It will make
it computationally easier to find the dynamics of the system, especially when considering

systems containing more photons.
3.3.1
Assumption necessary for the SLH formalism
The SLH formalism enables a relatively simple description of a complicated system. How-
ever, the formalism also requires assumptions regarding the system beyond those initially
proposed: the rotating wave and the Markov approximation.
Assumption 1
The calculation assumes that all operations happen simultaneously. Thus, the travel time in
the waveguides is not included. It is possible to include the phase difference that can arise
for longer distances by adding a phase scattering:
Gϕ = (eiϕ, 0, 0), ϕ = ∆cL/v.
(3.17)
∆c is the detuning from the carrier frequency, L is the length of the waveguide, and v is the
medium’s light speed. This implementation of the phase change only holds for delays that
are small compared to the timescale of the system dynamics.
Assumption 2
The SLH formalism is limited to only describe one-way propagation. Thus, the light can only
propagate in one direction when implementing a connection between two cavities. The one-
way propagation directly appears in the concatenation rules where G2 ◁G1 ̸= G1 ◁G2. The
notation ◁indicates the direction of the light and is further introduced in the next section.
Two-way propagation can be implemented by considering the right and left propagations
individually and, afterward, putting them in parallel as in Figure 3.3
Assumption 3
The SLH formalism assumes the input field is in a vacuum state. An additional cavity is
introduced to expand the input field to a non-vacuum state. Introducing a virtual cavity to
create a non-vacuum input state is proposed by Kiilerich and Mølmer in[24]. The virtual
cavity changes the input vacuum state to the desired input state. For example, the following

4.2
The Hilbert space of the cavities
In this project, the dynamics of the cavity systems are simulated in Julia.
The size of
its Hilbert space and belonging operators are defined in the initialization.
The photon
distribution of the coherent input state follows a Poissonian distribution equation 2.9, and
thus an error of a finite Hilbert space will be induced. The error arises as some photon number
states are excluded due to higher photon numbers. After investigating the introduced error,
a further Hilbert space reduction is introduced.
Finite Hilbert space
The error introduced by the finite size of the Hilbert space is investigated in figure 4.1. The
error is determined by observing the difference in the simulated results depending on the
size of the Hilbert space. The finite size of the Hilbert space results in a maximum num-
ber of photons in each cavity, Ncutoff. The error is found by comparing the result of the
simulations of a given Ncutoff with those of a simulation made with a large Hilbert space.
A large finite Hilbert space will approximate an infinite Hilbert space and is thus set as
the comparable "true" value. A large Hilbert space refers here to Ncutoff = 25 for a system
initially in the state |α = 2⟩shown in figure 4.1a) and Ncutoff = 40 for |α = 3⟩in figure 4.1b).
There are different errors for the photon number expectation values in figure 4.1. The dif-
ference is due to different photon expectation values inside the cavity. The relevant errors
to observe are the zero or one photon state expectation value, as those states are primarily
considered in the project.
The comparison between different Hilbert spaces and the reference Hilbert space confirms
that increasing the Hilbert space will generally decrease the error of the expectation values.
It is confirmed that a higher average input photon number requires a larger Hilbert space
to obtain the same error threshold. Increasing the Hilbert space is more computationally
expensive; thus, simulating a nα will be computationally slower. In the project, the Hilbert
space is chosen such that the maximum error is in the order of ∼10−3. The maximum

expected photon value will not hold for a system with a finite Hilbert space. For the Hilbert
space with a cutoff photon number of ten, the expected value is 3.96 instead. Thus even
though the errors on probabilities inside the cavity are in the order of ∼10−3, the error of
the total average photon number of the incoming light is in the order of ∼10−2. However,
as the relevant part of this project is the photon distribution inside the nonlinear cavity, the
error on the total light is neglected.
Reducing the Hilbert space
A larger Hilbert space leads to longer computational times as it includes the dynamics of
more photon states. Therefore, reducing the size of the considered Hilbert space in the calcu-
lations would be beneficial. In the simulation, the Hilbert space of each cavity is assumed to
be identical. The maximum photon number of the cavities is similar to ensure all transitions
are possible. Nevertheless, not all photon number combinations are possible, as no photons
are added during the simulation. For example, if the nonlinear cavity is initially assumed to
be empty, obtaining Ncutoff photons in each cavity at later times will not be possible.

Nonlinearity
The nonlinearity creates a cavity detuning for all higher photon numbers, equation 4.4. The

inside the nonlinear cavity is maximized by varying the nondimensional nonlinearity χ/κ0,

will not be strong enough to affect the shape of the photon distribution. For intermediate
nonlinearities 0.1 < χ/κ0 < 1, the probability of obtaining a single photon increases mono-

5.2.1
Dynamically coupled linear cavity system
If no nonlinearity is included in the cavity, generating a photon distribution inside the cavity

Even though the cavity has a dynamic coupling rate, the photon distribution inside the

from fitting the photon distribution. Compared to the single nonlinear cavity system, it is

Thus, using the linear cavity to create a single-photon emitter is not preferred. Instead, the
linear dynamically coupled cavity practically works as an optical attenuator.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This study investigated the possibility of creating a photonic quantum processor using non-
linear cavities with dynamic coupling rates. The photonic quantum processor aims to create
a single-photon pulse from an incoming coherent light. The primary system considered to
create a single-photon emitter was a single nonlinear one-sided cavity with a dynamic cou-
pling to its connecting waveguide.
The cavity system was described using the SLH formalism, which creates a single triple
describing the total system. The equations of motion found with the SLH description were
used to describe the photon dynamics inside the cavities and give an expression for the
outgoing light. The SLH enables simulations of higher photon numbers as it neglects the
state of the waveguides between the cavity. While the SLH formalism assumes an incoming
vacuum state, a non-vacuum incoming state was introduced by including a virtual cavity in
the system.
It is computationally costly to simulate systems with an infinite Hilbert space. Thus a finite
Hilbert space was chosen such that the order of magnitude of the error on the output results
was below a threshold of ∼10−3. An additional reduced Hilbert space algorithm was defined,
further reducing the computational time by a factor of two.
An optimization scheme was defined to maximize the probability of a single photon inside
the nonlinear cavity working as a quantum photonic processor. The scheme describes the
dynamic coupling rate by interpolating an optimized vector. The vector was optimized to
obtain the highest single-photon probability inside the nonlinear cavity. Different interpola-
tion types were investigated, showing that the maximum single-photon probability achieved
by all tested types was almost identical. The length of the optimization vector was shown
to have no gain when increasing the number of optimization values beyond five.
The simulation showed that the nonlinearity significantly influenced the possibility of making
a single-photon emitter. The nonlinearity value had to exceed a value of χκ0 ≥5 to obtain
usable single-photon probabilities. However, a lower nonlinearity, χ, can be compensated
for by increasing the time scale and lowering the coupling rates, κ0. However, obtaining the
==PAGE:52==
Chapter 7. Conclusion
single-photon probability was only possible if the loss was not included in the simulations.
The best single-photon probability found was 0.96 for a single nonlinear cavity with a dimen-
sionless nonlinearity of χ0/κ0 = 5 and an input average photon number of 16. It was shown
that the outgoing pulse from the nonlinear cavity was pure as the second-order correlation
function was on the order of g2 = 10−6. These values make the proposed single-photon
emitter useful for different quantum technological implementations.
The dynamically coupled nonlinear cavity was compared to a dynamically coupled linear
cavity and a static coupled nonlinear cavity. Simulations showed that the linear cavities
could not change the type of photon distribution, resulting in a maintained Poissonian dis-
tribution of the light inside the cavity. The only change after optimization was the average
photon number. Thus the linear cavity only worked as an optical attenuator. The nonlin-
ear stationary coupled cavity changed the photon distribution like the dynamically coupled
cavity. However, the probability of a single photon was significantly reduced. It is also
more challenging to distinguish the excess of the incoming light from the light from the
static coupled cavity. The statically coupled cavity had an efficiency of 0.63 compared to
the 0.84 obtained by a similar cavity with a dynamic coupling. The efficiency refers to the
probability of a single photon in the outgoing light. The larger values cause the dynamically
coupled nonlinear cavity to be the best choice for a single-photon emitter. At the end of the
investigation of the single cavity, the loss was included in the nonlinear cavity. It was shown
that the numerically optimal nonlinearity was too high for the current proposed experiential
implementation. However, the single-photon probability could be obtained by combining the
best-obtained nonlinearity with one of the highest obtained Q-factors.
The single cavity system was expanded to determine whether the efficiency could improve
further. A system with multiple cavities in a series was simulated with the same optimization
protocol as the single photon. The simulation was made in order to improve the single-photon
probability found for a single cavity. The single-photon probability was not improved for the
chosen parameters due to the lack of excess photons after passing through the first cavity.
A closed system, including a linear and a nonlinear cavity, was also investigated to avoid
lacking photons. However, the optimal single-photon probability was not improved. Thus
the best single-photon emitter is the single dynamically coupled nonlinear cavity, as it is
both simple and efficient.
The last expansion considered the nonlinear cavity interfering with a coherent light on a beam
splitter. The introduction of interference made it possible to confirm the other advance of
the nonlinearity in the cavity, the self-phase modulation. By changing the time inside the
cavity, it was possible to show the single photon existence by looking at the output’s first-
order correlation function, which measures the intensity.
Outlook
The demonstrated use of nonlinear dynamically coupled cavities as a photonic quantum pro-
cessor has many possibilities. Concretely, other theoretical investigations can be made to

obtain a better single-photon emitter. For the single nonlinear cavity system, it would be
interesting to investigate whether an even higher input photon value would lead to a higher
efficiency. Another exciting change in physical parameters would be to change the type of
nonlinearity. Another nonlinearity could be to include an emitter in a dynamically coupled
cavity.
In this project, the expanded cavity system was investigated using the optimization of input
state and nonlinearity from the single cavity investigation. Thus, a full investigation of the
system might improve the single-photon probability. The efficiency could come from includ-
ing several light pulses, reducing the probability of a vacuum state. Investigating the result
of interfering with different quantum states would be interesting as this might improve the
single-photon probability.
For all the considered systems, only Gaussian mode amplitude was considered. However,
another shape might lead to different results. Thus, it would be interesting to try and re-
peat the single cavity investigation for another mode amplitude. This extension would be
implemented in the outcoupling of the virtual cavity and the nonlinear cavities.
This project showed one of the advantages of using dynamically coupled cavities compared
to stationary coupled cavities. Using the dynamically coupled nonlinear cavity might make
implementing a more extensive cavity system more straightforward. A more extensive opti-
cally integrated cavity system could apply to the quantum photonic neural network proposed
in the introduction. A further investigation could include making different gate operations
using several cavities and beam splitters.
