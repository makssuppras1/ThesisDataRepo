Abstract
The increasing penetration of distributed energy resources (DERs) in electricity distribu-
tion grids poses challenges to grid stability and reliability. This study explores how De-
mand Response (DR) strategies, leveraging the thermal flexibility inherent in residential
building heating systems, can mitigate grid congestion and safety violations that arise,
particularly, from the impact of HP penetration. Drawing from a flexibility envelope frame-
work proposed in previous research, this study assesses households’ available flexibility
and develops a methodology for cost-effective flexibility dispatching by the Distribution
System Operator (DSO). Two strategies are proposed and compared: Strategy A, which
employs flexibility dispatching based on households’ intended operation, energy bound-
aries, and cost of flexibility; and Strategy B, based on simpler current regulatory practices.
For HP penetration of levels up to 30-40%, results indicate that Strategy A effectively im-
proves grid performance, reducing violations and minimizing flexibility costs compared to
Strategy B and grid reinforcement alternatives. However, challenges such as forecast
errors and the need for a holistic approach to include other DERs remain. This study
highlights the potential of unlocking the inherent thermal flexibility of residential buildings
through quantification of flexibility methods, and DSO redispatching strategies.
Keywords: Building flexibility, Thermal flexibility, Flexibility envelope, Demand response,
Distribution Networks, Congestion Management, Voltage Violations, Flexibility markets,
Integration of DERs.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The electricity system relies on a continuous balance between supply and demand, tra-
ditionally achieved through supply-side flexibility, which involves power plants with varied
response times. However, with the integration of fluctuating renewable energy sources
like wind and solar, and in the progress towards decarbonization, substitution and addi-
tion of flexibility resources are needed. Among them are several emerging demand-side
measures as highlighted in [1]. Not only for balance between supply and demand, flexibil-
ity is also crucial in congestion management of power systems to prevent overloads and
ensure the grid’s reliability. Various congestion management techniques are presented
in [2], including generators rescheduling, load shedding, optimal placement of distributed
generation, and advanced nodal pricing schemes.
On the low voltage grid, the rapid deployment of solar photovoltaic, heat pumps and elec-
tric vehicles makes a positive contribution towards decarbonization and decentralization
of the energy system. At the same time, massive integration of these technologies can im-
pose challenges in distribution grids. Enough PV can create reverse power fulls, voltage
violations and line congestions [3], [4], [5]. At the same time, HPs and EVs can signifi-
cantly increase demand [6], resulting in voltage violations (especially low voltages at the
end of the feeder) and line congestions.
In Switzerland, the authors in [7] investigate the impact of widespread adoption of solar
photovoltaics (PVs), electric heat pumps (HPs), and electric vehicles (EVs) on a Swiss
low-voltage distribution grid, serving 170,000 households. They project that total grid
reinforcement expenses could reach up to 11 billion CHF by 2050. The costs of grid
reinforcement depend on the technology and urban settings, rural areas incurring the
higher cost.
Currently in distribution grids, the hosting capacity has often not been yet reached and
while most DSOs have not been forced to take measures to guarantee the grid’s stability,
research is being carried out to investigate the status of grids hosting capacity [8]. In some
countries with more advanced deployment of DER and favorable weather conditions like
Australia, DSOs have imposed a fixed limit at the customer’s connection point [9] to avoid
safety violations in their grids. If the hosting capacity is reached, DSOs can, alternatively
to grid reinforcement, safely plan the integration of DERs in the grid and consider smart
solutions that can facilitate the massive integration of DER without compromising distri-
bution grid security.
Among the strategies that DSOs can rely on to safely increase the DER penetration in
distribution grids is Demand Side Management (DSM), which refers to strategies that
influence the patterns and intensity of end-user electricity consumption. DSM, particularly
through load shifting offers a unique advantage, DSM can achieve 100% efficiency since
there’s no transition to an intermediary storage form, unlike energy storage technologies.
DSM can help in harmonizing electricity generation with consumption, especially when
incorporating variable renewable energy (VRE) sources. Furthermore, it can react across
different time scales, addressing the most substantial variability in VRE generation within
1-12 hours, where the bulk of DSM potential exists [10].

While once primarily used to manage peak power demand, it’s now also exploited to bal-
ance renewable energy sources. There are several advantages of Demand Side Manage-
ment, it contributes to: moderating price surges, lowering average spot prices, transferring
market influence from producers to consumers, delaying or substituting infrastructure ex-
pansion, minimizing the use of expensive peak power, and cutting down transmission and
distribution losses. Moreover, DSM can acts as a catalyst for energy efficiency implemen-
tations.
DSM faces several challenges, such as lacking the right technological infrastructure, fund-
ing problems, timely access to energy and pricing data, stakeholder engagement, the
need for automation, limited savings, and regulatory hurdles [1].
In line with the present work, residential buildings offer notable potential in demand side
management due to their large thermal capacity. Employing a holistic perspective of the
energy system, integrating both electrical and thermal loads can reveal substantial op-
portunities for renewable energy integration. In [1], the high flexibility potential of different
residential loads is presented at a national level in Germany based on [11], [12], [13], [14].
Demand Side Management (DSM) encompasses wide series of strategies to influence
electricity consumption patterns. Demand Response (DR), understood as a subset of
DSM, specifically targets immediate adjustments in electricity demand during peak pe-
riods or grid emergencies. This project centers on exploring the mechanisms of DR,
particularly in leveraging household flexibility to alleviate grid congestions
One of the key aspects to unlock the households available flexibility is finding the right
method to quantify and express such flexibility. Several methods have been proposed in
literature, as well as the need for standardization [15] in the methodologies. One line of
research is the concept of flexibility envelopes, initially proposed in [16], where, for a set
a of time indexes, a set of constant power levels that can be sustained while maintaining
comfort are provided. If communicated to a system operator, it provides information on
the total available flexibility in terms of power levels and energy. More relevant for the
present study is the work in [17], where a methodology for energy scheduling and real-
time flexibility quantification of electrical loads is proposed for a typical Swiss residential
building.
1.2
Motivation
This project aims to contribute in the investigation of the potential for DR to alleviate con-
gestions in distribution grids, relevant to the mass integration of DERs. Specifically, it
investigates how a DSO could best utilize the flexibility available in heating systems of
individual households due to their large thermal inertia. This potential has been investi-
gated in [17] [18], where the available flexibility of individual buildings was assessed and
an energy management system that self-exports a flexibility envelope was designed. By
means of a simplified version of that flexibility envelope, this project aims at investigating
how a DSO with access to such flexibility envelopes can avoid transformer thermal limits
and local voltage violations in distribution grids.
On top of the simplified version of the flexibility envelope, a module for estimation of cost
of flexibility based on [19] is included. This project proposes a framework for flexibility
dispatching where households provide their intended operation, energy boundaries and
costs of flexibility. Designing a framework that incentivizes load shifting and resorts to
load curtailment if necessary or economically beneficial, the DSO finds the most cost-
efficient solution to avoid forecasted violations while respecting the households energy
boundaries.

In addition, the proposed framework for flexibility dispatch, hereafter Strategy A, will be
compared in terms of grid performance to a Base Case scenario with no redispatching
framework. It will also be compared to a second framework, Strategy B, based on the
latest directions from the Federal Network Agency of Germany (Bundesnetzagentur) on
dealing with massive deployment of DER. By doing so, this project aims to be compared
with one of the most up to date initiatives from energy regulators. In addition, the module
for estimation of households cost of flexibility in Strategy A allows the proposed method-
ology to be economically compared with Strategy B, and the alternative estimated costs
of grid reinforcement.

Thesis structure

Literature Review
In the context of congestion management in distribution networks, [20] reviews the main
methods to deal with high penetration of DER, distinguishing between direct control meth-
ods and market methods. Direct control methods such as network configuration, reactive
power control and active power control, which are left outside the scope of this work, are
considered by the authors as back-up alternatives for when the market methods can only
partly solve the congestions. The market methods reviewed are: Dynamic Tariff [21], Dis-
tribution Capacity Market [21] [22], Intra-Day Shadow Price [23], and Flexibility Service
Market [24]. In [24], a simplified but rigid approach is proposed, which offers several but
fixed flexibility activation agreements. This study will aim to contribute in the flexibility
service market line of research, the only one of the previous methods where consumers
decide their own intended operation and DSOs must purchase the flexibility.
In this line, it is relevant to explore one of the most advanced research areas in managing
DNs congestion. In Australia, the widespread adoption of solar PV and high solar irradi-
ance led DSOs to set fixed export limits for households to prevent voltage violations and
congestion. The Dynamic Operating Envelopes (DOEs) concept [25] [26] [27], emerged
as a solution, allowing for dynamic adjustment of export and import limits to enhance dis-
tribution grid efficiency and resource use. The EDGE project saw collaboration among
customers, DER owners, aggregators, distributors, and researchers to test DOEs imple-
mentation .
This line of research has been significantly studied recently by other authors: [28] con-
siders uncertainty and fairness when calculating DOEs; [29] introduces robust-DOEs to
deal with uncertainty and variability; [30] proposed decentralized and partly-decentralized
DOEs for the cases where the knowledge of the network is limited; and [31] develops a
control methodology for Demand Response Aggregators (DRA) to track a load set-point
signal while taking into account the DOEs assigned by the DSO.
Early DOE implementations focused on consumption forecasts and conservative export/im-
port limits, applying uniform DOEs to all consumers. The EDGE project introduced more
optimal, location-specific DOEs, and in [32] prosumers send their intended operation to
the DSO, which then sets individual operational limits through OPF, incorporating fairness
and network constraints, to prevent potential violations. By means of a framework for a
flexibility market, this project aims at bringing the optimality of the congestion management
again a further step, by having the households local engine not only obtain their intended
operation, but also calculate their available region of flexibility and their estimated cost of
flexibility.
From the household modelling side, this project is based on the works of [17] [18] in
flexibility envelopes. In their work a household including solar rooftop PV, SHP, TES, DHW
and EV are modelled. Three energy management systems based on MPC and a module
to quantify flexibility via flexibility envelopes for such households are proposed. One of
the main contributions of their work is to provide a real-time quantification of a flexibility
envelope that can be sent to the system operator. This project aims at investigating the
potential for a DSO to use simplified versions of such flexibility envelopes in order to avoid
violations in distribution grids.
Bringing both sides together, this project differentiates itself from the previous studies
by intending to provide further knowledge on the potential use of flexibility envelope to

flexibility market that aims to find a cost-efficient solution to avoid system violations while

Mathematical models and proposed
methodology
This chapter introduces the methodologies and mathematical models of this work. Firstly,
the thermal model of the households will be described, following with the methods for
obtaining the flexibility envelope and cost of flexibility of the household. Secondly, on the
DSO side, the methodology for obtaining the sensitivity matrix is presented, as well as
Strategy A and B that correspond to the two compared frameworks for flexibility dispatch-
ing of this work.
3.1
Household modelling
The model of the buildings that will be providing flexibility in this work are described in this
section. They are assumed to have a heat distribution water cycle where a Space Heat
Pump heats the water flow circuit, transferring heat to the floor and lastly to the rooms of
the building, as seen in Figure 3.1. The thermal models of the building and heat pump in
this project are based on [18], where a Swiss household is modelled with HP, TES, PV,
DHW storage, and EV. This work focuses on the effect of HP penetration and its potential
for flexibility. The households considered hereafter include the same circuit for the HP of
their work, and rooftop solar PV is considered as uncontrollable load.
Figure 3.1: Typical Swiss Household as modelled in [17]. The present work only includes
the underground HP circuit and rooftop PV.

DSO Framework for dispatching flexibility
In this section, the methodology used to define the strategies for flexibility dispatching
proposed in this work are presented. First, the use of a sensitivity matrix to predict nodal
voltages based on active power consumption is introduced. Second, Strategy A, the main
proposed methodology of this work is presented. Lastly, Strategy B, is defined with the
purpose of comparing Strategy A with a simpler approach in line with current DSO regu-
lations.
3.2.1
Active Power-Voltage sensitivity matrix
In the context of flexibility markets in distribution grids, utilizing optimal power flow allows
to solve the relevant optimization problem while reliably representing the network’s safety
constraints. But at the same time, it poses several challenges. Just to run a power flow
analysis, full knowledge of the grid is normally necessary, which is infrequent in distribution
grids. Furthermore, in order to run an optimal power flow algorithm, not only knowledge of
the grid is needed, but also a method to deal with the non-convex nature of the problem.
In distribution grids, a commonly used method to deal with both lack of grid knowledge
data and the non-convex nature of optimal power flow algorithms, is to obtain a so-called
sensitivity matrix, which provides a linear relation between the active power consumed
per node and the nodal voltage values. The methodology utilized on this work is based
on [36].
Given a set of nodal time series voltage and active power consumption measurements
without full knowledge of the grid, such sensitivity matrix can be obtained by solving the
following problem:

3.2.2
Flexibility dispatching Strategy A
In section 3.1, the methodology to quantify and express the available flexibility of house-
holds has been introduced. This section, proposes a methodology in which a Distribution
System Operator exploits that available flexibility to deal with voltage violations and con-
gestions in distribution network. During periods of peak consumption when the network
is predicted to suffer violations, knowledge of the households upper and lower energy
bounds can provide a means for load shifting and/or load curtailment in order to avoid the
predicted violations.
A simplified block diagram of the present strategy is shown in Figure 3.6. Regarding the
modules corresponding to the household local engine: Module 1 substitutes the real-life
measurements by emulating the building behaviour to obtain the initial conditions of the
household at the beginning of the optimization period; Modules 2 to 4 correspond to the
previously defined in section 3.1; Module 5 predicts the nodal voltages using the sensitivity
matrix as presented in subsection 3.2.1; lastly Module 6 corresponds to the optimization
problem solved by the DSO that will be introduced in this section.
The presented block diagram can be seen as one iteration of the proposed flexibility mar-
ket, where households obtain their intended operation, energy boundaries, and cost of
flexibility to then send them to the DSO. The DSO,together with the inflexible consump-
tion, rooftop PV production, and using the obtained sensitivity matrix, predicts if any viola-
tions are expected to occur and if there are, solves an optimization problem to determine
the most cost-efficient alternative to solve the grid’s violations. Lastly, the DSO sends a
redispatch signal, if necessary, back to the households.

3.2.3
Flexibility dispatching Strategy B
The subsection 3.2.2 describes a flexibility framework that allows the DSO to strive for
an optimal use of the households available flexibility while respecting their energy con-
straints. Strategy A has a level of complexity and data intensiveness significantly higher
than currently implemented DSO strategies to deal with voltage violations in distribution
grids.
For the purpose of comparing the proposed Strategy A, this section describes a simpler
flexibility strategy more in line with current DSO implementations. The present methodol-
ogy is based on the latest regulation from German Bundesnetzagentur [38] . The German
Federal Network Agency has established a common strategy among DSOs for grid con-
necting flexible consumption such as EV and Heat pumps in distribution networks while
providing means of flexibility dispatch when necessary. Network operators will be obliged
to facilitate grid connection of new heat pumps and electric vehicle charging facilities,
without being able to delay their connection due to potential network overload. At the
same time, they will be able to curtail controllable consumption devices when network
overload is expected. Consumers will be guaranteed a minimum consumption during the
overload events: 4.2 kW for heat pumps and charging for 50 kilometres in 2 hours for
electric vehicles.

Description of the Case Study
In this chapter, the Case Study utilized to compare, at different levels of HP penetration,
the Base Case scenario with the described Strategy A and Strategy B of chapter 3 is
introduced. First, the synthetic grid is presented together with the method used to populate
it with households, where households may include rooftop PV and HP, as well as the rest
of their inflexible consumption. Secondly, the parameters relevant for the modelling of the
households and their local engines are introduced.
4.1
Distribution grid
This section will introduce the distribution grid of the present Case Study, as well as the
technique used to populate the grid with households and rooftop solar PVs. Then, it will
explain how the presented grid is used as an emulator for generation of voltage mea-
surements to obtain the sensitivity matrix according to subsection 3.2.1. The sensitivity
matrix is considered the only tool for the DSO to predict nodal voltages. This work aims
to create a Case Study that allows to investigate the problems that arise with increased
HP penetration and, at the same time, investigate their own flexibility potential to alleviate
such issues.
To analyze and validate the proposed methodology a synthetic grid developed by the CI-
GRE Task Force C6.04.02 is utilized [40], for which a python panda-power implementation
can be readily implemented [41]. Figure 4.1 shows the Low Voltage network utilized in
this work, considering that only the Residential sub-network is the focus of this study and
not the Industrial and Commercial subnetworks originally present in [40].
The network’s transformer is connected to two main feeders. First, the residential sub-
network seen in Figure 4.1 and second, another feeder represented as a load in Bus 2.
According to the standard load values of [41], they are both feeding almost equivalent load
sizes (190kW in B2, and 193kW aggregated from B12 to B19). As there is no grid knowl-
edge on the second feeder, it is represented as an aggregated load of households, and
the present study is focused on the local voltage violations of the residential subnetwork
represented in Figure 4.1.
In order to determine the amount of households that the described grid will host, the
transformers capacity of 500kV A is used for the sizing of the grid. It is done based on the
data processing work and grid’s sizing criteria carried out in [42] with anonymous Swiss
households active power consumption smarter meter data for 2021 and 2022 with 15
minute resolution [43]. The same dataset, but only using the year 2022, is used in the
present project as the households inflexible consumption. Based on [42], the amount of
households chosen ensures that: the maximum historical consumption is below the trans-
formers capacity, and the 99th percentile of consumption is below 50% of the transformer
capacity.
The grid is sized to the maximum amount of households that meet both of the previous
criteria. In addition, based on [42], the anonymous smart meter data is filtered out of
households with solar production, electric heating, or EV. Solar production and HPs will
be separately included as explained in this section. In addition, EVs are left out of this
work that aims to focus, in terms of flexible demand, on the influence of HP penetration.

to populate the grid. The households are then distributed proportionally according to the

Results
This chapter will present and analyze the results of the Case Study. First, the results
for the sensitivity matrix relating active power consumption and voltage are presented.
Secondly, in section 5.2, the results of the redispatching strategies of the DSO during the
day with the highest inflexible consumption is analyzed. Lastly, both strategies and the
Base Case are compared through a series of KPIs to analyze their performance over a
year.
The described analysis will be carried out in three different levels of HP penetration al-
lowing to further analyze the behaviour of such strategies depending on the level of elec-
trification of the grid’s consumers. The percentage of HP penetration utilized, refers to
the percentage of HPs in the households connected from Bus12 to Bus 19 (object of the
present analysis). For each HP penetrations case, all the HPs will be assumed to par-
ticipate in the corresponding redispatching strategy. The Base Case corresponds to no
framework of flexibility by the DSO, and Strategies A and B refer to those presented in
the methodology. The safety limits on the local voltage is defined as ±10% of the optimal,
therefore local voltages under 0.9 p.u. will be considered under-voltages and those above
1.1 p.u. will be considered over-voltages.
Lastly, note that the concept of curtailment in this work is used typically referring to load
curtailment of household consumption. There is no curtailment of production in the current
analysis, and the use of the curtailment concept is here-after applied as reduction of the
households consumption.

5.2

This section intends to improve the understanding of the behaviour of the two strategies
presented in subsection 3.2.2 and subsection 3.2.3. More precisely, it investigates the

inflexible consumption in the dataset, with the purpose of analyzing one of the most chal-

compared in the 40% HP penetration case, and Strategy A, being the main contribution
of this work, will be further analyze in the cases of 30%, 40% and 50% HP penetration.
This section focuses on local voltage violations as it was found to be the bottleneck in the
grids performance and not the thermal violations of the transformer as it will be further
analyzed in section 5.3.

In all presented cases, the cost of flexibility represent close to a 0% increase in the house-
hold costs. Two main counter effects influence this result: household energy savings be-
cause of less energy consumption if curtailed enough, and the load shifting effect that can
make households consume more energy during high tariff times of the day than they were
intending to. In the case of the true cost of flexibility in terms of discomfort, one would
expect that as households are being redispatched outside of their intended operation their
discomfort would increase. That is what can be seen in Strategy B in the 40% and 50%
HP penetration cases, where households do not only significantly increase their discom-
fort, they are also pushed outside of their thermal limits as seen by the penalty values.
Nevertheless in the 30% HP penetration case Strategy B sees an improvement in both
energy saving and reduction of discomfort of -29% simultaneously. This is an interesting
but challenging result, as it shows that, in aggregated terms, the intended operation of
the households is somehow improved by the curtailment of DSO.
Several factors might play a role to result in such behaviour. Firstly ,as described in sub-
section 3.1.2, there are times where households will not incur in any discomfort costs
when it is not occupied. Secondly, also as described in subsection 3.1.2, households
have a temperature band of ±0.1C around the setpoint where the household might oscil-
late without incurring in any discomfort costs. Thirdly, households decide on their intended
operation every 4 hours by looking into the next 24 hours. Nevertheless, they are simu-
lated over an entire week, and therefore, their intended operation profile over the week
decided by their local engine will not necessarily match the actual week’s optimal oper-
ation. Lastly, the nature of the households local engine tends to unfairly prioritize the
beginning of the decision horizon due to the large time constants of the system. When a
households assess when to consume electricity in the next 24 hours, it will have a gen-
eral preference to consume at the beginning of the optimization period since it will have
a longer effect on the building’s temperature rather than if it consumed that energy at the
very end of that same period.
Moreover, it is worth comparing the values obtained for the true cost of energy and discom-
fort combined with the estimated costs of flexibility as presented in the KPIs of Table 5.2.
From the 30% to 50% HP penetration case, the cost of flexibility incurred by the DSO
is of 1.5, 26.2, and 116.2 CHF/day. In contrast, the obtained true costs of flexibility are:
3.7, 1.9, and 13.2 CHF/day respectively. Together with the previous factors already dis-
cussed, which also influence the behaviour of Strategy A, the following are relevant for this
mismatch in the flexibility cost estimate. First, households always calculate their cost of
flexibility up and down as positive since it is obtain as an opportunity cost of deviating from
the intended operation. Nevertheless, load shifting from the DSO might lead to trajecto-
ries with equal or very similar incurred true costs of energy and discomfort. Second, the
costs of flexibility as explained in subsection 3.1.3, are obtained through a conservative
estimate. By assessing the cost of flexibility of deviating from the intended operation to
the upper and lower energy boundaries, households estimate linearly the cost of smaller
deviations from the intended operation. This leads to overpriced flexibility costs incurred
by the DSO when sending redispatching signals to households with small deviation from
the intended operation, which will not affect their true cost as much as their estimated
flexibility cost implies, effect that was introduced in Figure 3.5.
They are several the factors influencing the obtained results for the true costs of flexibility,
and they should be further investigated as stated in chapter 6.

Conclusion
Supporting massive integration of DERs in distribution grids requires measures to avoid
the accompanying violations of the grid’s safety limits. This study focuses on the influence
of increasing HP penetration in grid’s performance and the potential to utilize households
thermal flexibility to avoid such violations. The households local engines utilize a simpli-
fied version of the flexibility envelope framework, together with a module for estimation of
costs of flexibility. The households then provides information to the DSO on their intended
operation, energy boundaries and cost of redispatching. To complete the proposed Strat-
egy A, a communication framework and optimization problem to redispatch households in
the most cost-efficient alternative to avoid violations, while respecting households energy
limits, is proposed. The proposed methodology is compared in terms of grid performance
and costs of flexibility to the Base Case scenario, Strategy B (based on the latest regula-
tion in Germany), and the alternative costs of grid reinforcement.
In the Base Case scenario, increasing HP penetration induced more often and more se-
vere local voltage violations, which appeared before violations of the thermal limits of the
transformer. Also, as expected, end of the feeder nodes where the most affected and
were the bottlenecks to completely avoid all violations. The proposed Strategy A proved
to solve or significantly improve the grids performance in all HP penetration levels, but not
been able to fully avoid the grid’s violations on the higher HP penetration levels. When
the violations were small enough, it incentivized load shifting of the residential house-
holds, while more frequent load curtailment was needed for high HP penetration levels
and prolonged periods of expected violations. In addition, Strategy A did not surpass the
households energy boundaries, contrary to Strategy B.
Strategy B proved to improve the grids performance at all levels of HP penetration when
compared to the Base Case, but obtained significantly worse performance than Strategy
A due to its inherent limitation of guaranteeing a minimum of 4.2kW of HP consumption. In
addition, it proved to be more conservative because of not considering the effectiveness of
redispatching based on the households nodal position nor focusing on solving the violation
in the critical nodes. The simplicity and fairness of Strategy B, came at the cost of worse
grid performance, surpassing the households energy boundaries, and over-priced cost of
flexibility when compared to Strategy A.
Both Strategies were compared to grid reinforcement costs. In the 30% HP penetration
scenario, Strategy A represented the lowest cost alternative compared to estimates of
grid reinforcement in all areas (rural, suburban, and urban). In contrast, Strategy B, with
costs higher than grid reinforcement in urban and suburban areas but in the range of
rural areas, even provided worse grid performance than Strategy A. In the case of 40%
HP penetration, Strategy A became a more expensive alternative than grid reinforcement
in urban and suburban areas, but it remained cost competitive for rural areas. On the
50% case, the cost of flexibility severely increased in Strategy A, becoming higher than
all of the estimated grid reinforcement costs, in addition the grids performance was far
from enough and further measures would be needed to complement Strategy A. In the
case of Strategy B in the 40% and 50% HP penetration cases, even if the flexibility costs
only increased linearly compared to the 30% case, the grid performance indexes showed
too frequent and severe violations to be considered a feasible alternative without further
measures.

==PAGE:51==
Lastly, the limitations of the present work are now highlighted. Firstly, the sensitivity ma-
trix used historical data for inflexible consumption and solar production, showing smaller
than ±0.002p.u. errors in voltage predictions, but didn’t account for heat pumps (HPs)
consumption, whose effect on the sensitivity matrix should be investigated. Secondly,
the methodology also does not address line congestions, suggesting a need for further
research to investigate if there is need to include them as constraints in the DSO’s redis-
patching problem. Thirdly, this study emphasizes the impact of heat pump penetration
and thermal household flexibility but acknowledges the need for a holistic approach incor-
porating electric vehicles, as well as studying over-voltage issues due to different levels of
rooftop PV. Moreover, analysis reveals counter-intuitive improvements in discomfort costs
for households in lower heat pump penetration scenarios, highlighting the need to inves-
tigate factors influencing household behavior and discomfort costs further. Lastly, both
the DSO’s redispatching optimization problem and the households local engines assume
perfect foresight, a stochastic approach would be needed to deal with forecast errors.
