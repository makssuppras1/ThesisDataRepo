Abstract 
This study delves into the speciﬁc impact of load shedding on tech-startups in Cape 
Town, South Africa. The research employs a qualitative approach, focusing on in-person 
interviews with various stakeholders in the tech start-up ecosystem. The study reveals 
that load shedding poses signiﬁcant operational and productivity challenges for these 
startups. However, it also uncovers the resilience and adaptability of these entities, as 
they pivot their business models and operations to mitigate the impact of the crisis. The 
ﬁndings underscore the potential of tech-startups as catalysts for change during crises, 
highlighting their role in the digital transformation of the region. The study also identiﬁes 
a gap in the literature, indicating a need for further research to understand the long-term 
eBects of load shedding on tech-startups in Cape Town.

1. Introduction 
In the heart of South Africa, nestled between the azure waves of the Atlantic and the 
majestic slopes of Table Mountain, lies a city that pulses with an energy as vibrant as its 
landscape. Cape Town, often referred to as the Silicon Cape (Anonymous, 2019), is a 
beacon of innovation and entrepreneurial spirit, a city that has carved out a unique 
identity in the African tech landscape. The city’s tech-startup ecosystem, a kaleidoscope 
of ﬁntech, edtech, energytech, and more, is a testament to Cape Town’s innovative spirit 
and adaptability (The Startup Ecosystem of Cape Town, s.f.). This diversity is not just a 
characteristic but the lifeblood that fuels the city’s tech ecosystem. 
The cityscape of Cape Town is dotted with numerous tech hubs, accelerators, and co-
working spaces, each a hive of activity and innovation that provide essential support to 
budding entrepreneurs. Among these, LaunchLab and UVU Bio stand out, providing a 
nurturing environment for startups to grow and thrive (Jackson, 2019). The city’s tech 
ecosystem is also fueled by a rich talent pool, thanks to the presence of top universities 
like the University of Cape Town and Stellenbosch University (OCallaghan, 2024). These 
institutions, renowned for their academic excellence, provide a steady stream of talented 
graduates, eager to make their mark in the tech industry (Modise, 2023). 
In the midst of this vibrant ecosystem, Cape Town is home to some of Africa’s most 
successful startups. Companies like Plentify, Obox Themes, and Switch Energy, among 
others, are not just businesses, but visionaries that are redeﬁning the future of 
technology in Africa. Their stories, their struggles, and their triumphs form an integral part 
of this research, providing an intriguing perspective on the topic of load shedding in their 
businesses.  
Venture capital ﬁrms, the ﬁnancial lifeblood of the startup ecosystem, play a crucial role 
in shaping the city’s tech landscape (Wakeﬁeld, 2024). Firms like Holocene Venture 
Capital and Innovate UK, along with representatives from the European Union, provide 
much-needed funding to Cape Town’s startups, enabling them to transform their 
innovative ideas into reality. 
However, beneath this vibrant facade, the tech-startup ecosystem in Cape Town is 
grappling with a series of crises and challenges. The socio-political landscape of Cape 
Town, marked by corruption, political instability, and policy uncertainty, presents a 
unique set of challenges for startups. The COVID-19 pandemic has further complicated 
the situation, leading to a severe economic downturn, high levels of unemployment, and 
increased poverty (State of Cape Town Report 2020, 2020). Amidst these challenges, 
another signiﬁcant hurdle that startups face is the ongoing issue of load shedding. 
In the heart of South Africa, a crisis has been unfolding that has signiﬁcantly impacted 
the rhythm of daily life and the pulse of its burgeoning tech industry. This crisis, known as 
load shedding, is a measure employed by the state-owned power utility, Eskom, to

prevent a total collapse of the power system when demand exceeds supply (Eskom, 
2019). Originating in 2007 as a response to escalating electricity demand and insuBicient 
generation capacity, load shedding involves deliberately switching oB parts of the 
electricity supply. This is done to maintain the balance of the electricity grid and prevent 
a nationwide blackout, a scenario that would plunge the country into darkness and bring 
economic activity to a standstill (Anonymous, What is laod shedding?, s.f.). 
The roots of load shedding are entangled in a web of issues. South Africa’s power 
stations, many of which have been in operation for decades, are showing signs of age and 
lack of maintenance, leading to frequent breakdowns, 2021 had an averaging 2,3 failures 
a year from the targeted 1 per year, being worsen in 2022 (Stacey, 2022). New power 
stations, intended to bolster the grid’s capacity, have been plagued by delays and cost 
overruns. The result is a power system that is struggling to meet the country’s energy 
needs.

The duration and intensity of load shedding can vary greatly, depending on the extent of 
the imbalance between supply and demand. It can last for a few hours a day during peak 
demand periods, or in severe cases, it can last for most of the day. The process is typically 
divided into stages, with each stage corresponding to a certain amount of power that 
needs to be shed. The higher the stage, the more severe the load shedding (Eskom, 2019). 
To have a better picture, stage 1-4 implements a 2 hour blocks which are experienced 3 
adittional times per stage on a four-day period. This has an incrementla impact as stages 
get higher to the point of having stage 8, expecting 12 hours of the day being shed 
(Anonymous, FINDING AND INTERPRETING SCHEDULES , s.f.).

For the residents and businesses of Cape Town, load shedding has become a part of life. 
Power is cut in a rotational manner, ensuring that no area is without power for too long. 
However, this can lead to signiﬁcant disruptions, especially for tech startups that rely on 
a steady power supply (Olajuyin & Mago, 2022). Servers may go down, equipment may 
malfunction, and work may be lost due to sudden power outages. This can lead to delays, 
increased costs, and loss of revenue. 
Yet, in the face of these challenges, the tech-startup ecosystem in Cape Town has shown 
remarkable resilience and adaptability (M. Kuijpers & L. Eijdenberg, 2020). Startups are 
ﬁnding innovative ways to navigate these power outages, such as using alternative 
sources of energy, implementing energy-eBicient practices, and scheduling work around 
the load shedding timetable. This adaptability in the face of adversity is a testament to 
the innovative spirit that characterizes Cape Town’s tech-startup ecosystem. This thesis, 
therefore, seeks to unravel the story of resilience and adaptability, as it attempts to 
answer the question:  
How does the tech-startup ecosystem in Cape Town, South Africa, 
adapt during times of energy crisis?  
The ﬁndings are based on grounded theory, gathered through interviews conducted 
during a visit to Cape Town. The aim is to provide a comprehensive understanding of how 
these startups navigate and adapt to the challenges posed by load shedding and other

2. Literature Review 
The current energy crisis in Cape Town presents a unique challenge to the local start-up 
ecosystem. As businesses are inherently dependent on energy, the crisis necessitates a 
signiﬁcant pivot in start-up operations and strategies. This study aims to understand the 
current crisis, evaluate the pivoting of startups due to the energy crisis, and create 
comparable data on the changes across industries. The goal is to analyze this data in 
relation with literature on previous crises in the area and potential cases elsewhere in the 
world, answering the main research question of this study is:  
How does the tech-startup ecosystem in Cape Town, South Africa, 
adapt during times of energy crisis?  
To answer this question, several sub-questions will be addressed: 
1. How has the load shedding crisis speciﬁcally impacted tech-startups in Cape 
Town? 
2. What are the common strategies employed by tech-startups across various 
industries during energy crises? 
3. What speciﬁc challenges do tech-startups face during energy crises in Cape 
Town? 
4. What opportunities have emerged for tech-startups during energy crises? 
By investigating these sub-questions, this study aims to contribute to the existing 
literature on the actions of tech startups during times of crisis, particularly in developing 
countries. The focus on Cape Town as a case study is particularly relevant due to its 
previous experience with the “Day Zero” severe drought crisis, which provides valuable 
insights into the challenges faced by startups in a crisis context.  
Grounded theory (Delve, 2021) will be utilized to analyze qualitative data and derive 
meaningful insights from the experiences of tech-startups in Cape Town during the 
energy crisis. This approach will allow for a comprehensive understanding of the eBects 
of the crisis on the tech-startup ecosystem across various industries. 
The literature review plays a crucial role in achieving these objectives. It provides a 
broader perspective on entrepreneurship and crises around the world, with a special 
focus on the Financial Crisis of 2007-2008 and the Covid-19 pandemic, due to the interest 
of scholars. These global events oBer valuable insights into how entrepreneurs respond 
to crises and adapt their business models.

2.1 Understanding the dynamics of entrepreneurship during times of 
crisis. 
Lee, Kim, Mah, and Karr’s comprehensive review delves into the dynamics of 
entrepreneurship during various crises, both human-induced and natural. This research 
serves as a benchmark for comparison with this study’s ﬁndings. The authors map the 
development and research over a variety of crises and their eBect on the 
entrepreneurship journey, which has become the new norm for society (Bendell, M. 
Sullivan, & Ornstein, 2020). 
The authors’ exploration of the co-presence of both challenges and opportunities during 
crises is particularly relevant. They provide a detailed review of papers focusing on the 
Financial Crisis of 2007-2008 and the recent COVID-19 pandemic, in addition to 
considering the Asian ﬁnancial crisis of 1997, local economic collapses, environmental 
disasters, political conﬂicts, and a variety of other crises (Yoowoo, Jayoung, Sunghyuck, 
& Karr, 2022). 
From the Financial Crisis of 2007-2008, the authors discuss theories behind the 
inﬂuence of economic crises on entrepreneurship, highlighting both pro- and counter-
cyclical viewpoints. The pro-cyclical viewpoint explains how crises aBect demand for 
products and services, thereby impacting entrepreneurial opportunities (Abdesselam, 
2017). Conversely, the counter-cyclical viewpoint suggests that economic crises 
encourage people to seek alternative employment opportunities, such as 
entrepreneurship, as conventional organizations decline (González-Pernía, 2018).  
The authors also discuss the concept of “desperate entrepreneurship,” deﬁned by 
Mühlböck et al. (2017) as entrepreneurs starting ventures when the perception of 
==PAGE:10==

opportunity is low, which likely increases during a crisis. This phenomenon has a 
signiﬁcant geographical impact in nations where economic crises have led to high 
unemployment rates.  
From the Asian Financial Crisis 1997, Vial (2011) emphasizes the inﬂuence on entry and 
survival of operations in developing countries, linking geo-ﬁnancial characteristics with 
necessity entrepreneurship. Those wealthier focuse on informal and low-technology-
based enterprises that serve as a safety net, where low-skilled workers engage in service 
and trade businesses due to the lack of alternative source of income. 
The COVID-19 pandemic, like the Financial Crisis of 2007-2008, has prompted 
entrepreneurial actors to cooperate and share knowledge across communities to 
recognize and capitalize on opportunities for sustainable recovery (Korsgaard, 2020). 
However, the ﬁndings do not present suBicient results towards a community-based 
perspective. 
The pandemic shares similarities with other crises in terms of the impact on income 
options. Similarly, necessity-based entrepreneurs emerge due to the reduction of 
alternatives. Liñan and Jaén (2022) elucidate how the number of necessity-entrepreneurs 
increases following an initial decline triggered by the shock of the crisis. Kurckertz (2021) 
discovered that serial entrepreneurs persistently seek opportunities in the current 
environment. They possess a heightened perception to initiate innovative ventures to 
combat the Covid-19 Pandemic. Their awareness of potential threats contributes 
positively to the future success of their ventures.  
The pandemic has also highlighted the vulnerability of small and young ﬁrms, with major 
obstacles in supply chain management and capital acquisition. Afbad et al. (2021) found 
that small-medium enterprises (SMEs) in Pakistan suBered signiﬁcantly from the 
lockdown, with a subsequent decline in sales, proﬁts, and the capacity to acquire 
funding.  
Despite these challenges, the crisis has also led to the development of innovative 
products tailored to the crisis, particularly in non-contact industries such as 
telemedicine, automation, and commerce (Negrutiu, 2021). The realignment of 
imbalanced resources following the shock of a crisis has allowed entrepreneurs to 
prepare their ventures for the next phase of growth.  
Many ﬁrms’ success during the COVID-19 pandemic can be attributed to their ability to 
transform and digitalize their operations. This resilience has created a new business 
environment for eBective cooperation, with digital technology enabling entrepreneurs to 
connect with necessary support, ecosystems, and resources.

2.1 Startups’ business model changes during the COVID-19 
pandemic: Counteracting adversities and pursuing opportunities 
In this study, Guckenbiehl and de Zubielqui (2022) explore how startups have adjusted 
their business models in response to the COVID-19 pandemic. They categorize startups 
into six types based on their responses: digital adjusters, stable beneﬁciaries, business-
as-usual continuers, opportunity graspers, lemonade makers, and adversity survivors. 
This source provides a framework for analyzing how tech startups in Cape Town have 
responded to the load shedding crisis. It can help on how these startups have adjusted 
their business models, whether they have perceived the crisis as an opportunity or an 
adversity, and what strategies they have used to survive and thrive. 
Digital adjusters are startups that have made signiﬁcant changes to their business 
models to adapt to the digital environment necessitated by the pandemic. Stable 
beneﬁciaries are those that have beneﬁted from the pandemic due to the nature of their 
business and have not needed to make signiﬁcant changes. Business-as-usual 
continuers are startups that have continued with their existing business models, despite 
the challenges posed by the pandemic. Opportunity graspers are startups that have 
identiﬁed and capitalized on new opportunities presented by the pandemic, making 
changes to their business models to do so. Lemonade makers are startups that have 
turned the adversity of the pandemic into an opportunity, innovating and pivoting their 
business models to thrive. Adversity survivors are startups that have managed to survive 
the pandemic despite signiﬁcant challenges, often through making minor adjustments to 
their business models (Guckenbiehl & Corral de Zubielqui, 2021). 
The authors found that the perception of opportunities and adversities during the 
pandemic varied among startups in Australia. Some startups saw the pandemic as an 
opportunity and made signiﬁcant changes or innovations to their business models, while 
others viewed it as an adversity and made only minor adjustments. 
2.2 Showcasing Entrepreneurs’ Responses to Severe Drought: 
Qualitative Findings From Cape Town, South Africa  
In this study, Florine M. Kuijpers and Emiel L. Eijdenberg apply institutional theory to 
investigate entrepreneurship in Cape Town in relation to “Day Zero,” a severe water crisis. 
This research is particularly relevant to this paper as it provides insights into how 
entrepreneurs in Cape Town respond to crises. 
M. Kuijpers and Eijdenberg’s research is grounded in social, economic, and political 
dynamics. They delve into the operational institutional context, which is considered to 
shape entrepreneurs’ behaviors. Legal structures, which determine market entry ease 
and inﬂuence bankruptcy laws, are also examined. This research highlights the market

and government failure aspects of the country, thereby providing room for diBerent types 
of entrepreneurs to operate. 
The study also explores the ecological aspect of the crisis and its impact on 
entrepreneurs. It highlights that eBective drought management can be hindered by 
complicated bureaucratic procedures, static-centric institutions, slow top-down 
management, corruption, lack of transparency and accountability of the institutions, and 
poor infrastructure. The societal consequences of the drought were dramatic, including 
destroyed farmlands and malnutrition (M. Kuijpers & L. Eijdenberg, 2020). 
Interestingly, the authors note that drought management has only been implemented 
after times of drought, not during or as a precautionary measure. This crisis could have 
been avoided, and it has led to new legislation on water management. The study also 
identiﬁes challenges such as lack of resources, unemployment, price sensitivity, and 
lack of ﬁnancial safety nets; and inadequate government support, psychological stress, 
and high levels of stock theft.  
2.3 EMects Of Load-Shedding On The Performance Of Small, 
Medium And Micro Enterprises In Gqeberha, South Africa 
This study investigates the eBects of load-shedding on the performance of Small, 
Medium, and Micro Enterprises (SMMEs) in the food industry in South Africa. The 
research was conducted by collecting data from SMME owners and managers in the food 
industry in Gqeberha. The ﬁndings reveal that load-shedding aBects the activities, 
productivity, and performance of SMMEs, aligning with the economics of power system 
reliability and planning theory, which states that electricity is essential for the 
productivity of businesses (Olajuyin & Mago, 2022). 
The ﬁndings reveal that load-shedding aBects the activities, productivity, and 
performance of SMMEs. When load-shedding is active, the quality and capacity of 
services rendered by SMMEs are reduced, leading to customer dissatisfaction. SMMEs 
can use backup generators to mitigate such adverse eBects and adjust business 
operations to cater to load-shedding (Olajuyin & Mago, 2022). However, while planned 
load-shedding enables SMMEs to strategize, the cost of mitigating the eBect of load-
shedding is substantially high. 
The study concludes that the solution to the eBects of load-shedding primarily lies with 
Eskom, the national utility responsible for electricity generation, and the adjustment of 
tenancy agreements by shopping centers to take cognizance of the impact of load-
shedding. The ﬁndings of this study are not generalizable to other business contexts, 
indicating a need for quantitative research that can produce generalizable results.

2.4 Making Sense of Africa’s Emerging Digital Transformation and its 
Many Futures 
In this paper by Ndemo and Weiss (2017) they discuss how the digital transformation in 
Africa is characterized by the rapid growth of digital technologies, transforming all facets 
of life. This transformation is not just about the adoption of technology, but also about 
the creation and dispersion of technological innovations across societies. This point is 
particularly relevant to this paper as it provides a broader context for understanding the 
digital transformation in Cape Town and its potential impacts on tech-startups during the 
energy crisis. 
The document also discusses the potential for change that comes with the adoption of 
digital technologies. Through particular actions, digital technologies can realize their 
potential in a given society by becoming a catalyst for change and blending into the 
altered social structure. This point can inform the thesis study by highlighting the 
potential role of tech-startups in catalyzing change during the energy crisis. 
The document further highlights the opportunities and challenges presented by the 
digital transformation in Africa. While digital technologies can unlock development 
eBects, they can also risk seeding false hope if the possession of smartphones alone 
becomes the symbol of societal progress. This point can be used for this study to discuss 
the opportunities and challenges faced by tech-startups in Cape Town during the energy 
crisis, and seeing the opportunities that can emerge from rural areas across Africa. 
Finally, this article highlights the importance of establishing entrepreneurial hubs across 
the African continent and the investment activities surrounding them. This will aid market 
transactions in African societies, which could beneﬁt rural areas by improving their 
opportunities to access markets and services.

endevures of entrepreneurs, the is a gap on understanding the speciﬁc impact of load

3. Methodology 
The sampling strategy involved a combination of purposive and snowball sampling. After 
identifying potential participants, the researcher expanded the network through LinkedIn 
and local events. This approach allowed for a diverse range of perspectives from 
entrepreneurs, accelerators/incubators, investors, and other relevant stakeholders. The 
researcher also asked for referrals from each meeting, which signiﬁcantly expanded the 
network of contacts. 
Data collection involved a mix of online and face-to-face interviews, casual 
conversations, and observations from discussion panels and events. All formal 
interviews were recorded and transcribed, with notes taken during the meetings to 
capture key points and guide follow-up questions. Casual conversations and 
observations were documented through note-taking. The interviews typically lasted 
between 15-30 minutes, with longer interviews conducted with those closely involved in 
the crisis. 
When scheduling a formal meeting, attendees were introduced to the interviewer, the 
nature of the project, and the time required. Once the meeting was scheduled, the 
following interview guide was used as the roadmap for the interview. Please note that this 
guide was tweaked and improved during the course of the study: 
 
Interview Guide 
Introduction 
Brieﬂy introduce yourself and explain how you came to meet this person (through 
referrals, posts, articles, etc.). Ensure that the participant agrees to be recorded and that 
the information gathered can be used for this study. Explain the purpose of the study and 
its evolution from an interest in Day Zero to a focus on Load Shedding.

The data collected was analyzed using a grounded theory approach (Delve, 2021). This 
involved categorizing the data to identify patterns, themes and ﬁnal concepts when data 
processing is saturated. This approach was particularly suitable given the exploratory 
nature of the study and the lack of existing research on the speciﬁc impact of load 
shedding on tech startups in Cape Town. The researcher went through all the transcripts 
to identify themes that were relevant to each case and then ran through them again once 
some themes were identiﬁed in the majority of the transcripts. The notes taken were used 
to support some of the most apparent themes and potentially ﬁnd more concrete details 
per industry or type of venture. 
Ethical considerations were addressed by obtaining informed consent from participants 
for recording interviews and using the information for this study.  
The limitations of the study include potential selection bias due to the reliance on 
personal networks for sampling, and the possibility of missing in-depth data from casual 
conversations. The interpretation of data in grounded theory also carries inherent 
subjectivity. However, these limitations were acknowledged and mitigated where 
possible. 
To enhance the validity of the study, triangulation was used (Bhandari, 2023). This 
involved comparing interview data with LinkedIn posts, newspaper articles, and other 
relevant documents. The literature review also served as a form of triangulation, providing 
a broader context for the ﬁndings and allowing for comparisons with other crises. This 
comprehensive approach to data collection and analysis ensured a nuanced 
understanding of the impact of load shedding on tech startups in Cape Town.

4.1 Adaptive Startegies 
This core concept refers to how startups are adapting or pivoting their business models 
or operations to mitigate the impact of load shedding, while some of the strategies can 
be related to other categories. It includes themes like ‘Adaptation and Pivoting Strategies’, 
‘Alternative Energy Solutions’, ‘Update Hardware’, and ‘Low Price to Combat 
Loadshedding’.  
Start-ups in Cape Town are demonstrating remarkable adaptability in response to the 
load shedding crisis, echoing the resilience observed in entrepreneurs during other 
crises (Lee, Kim, Mah, and Karr). An example of the adaptation of their business models 
is from Andrew Murray, from Switch Energy, and Brian Gadisi from AriOn Power, two 
EnergyTech companies. They discuss how their companies have pivoted to service-
based models:  
“What we did was how we diQerentiate our business, was mainly our transition 
from product base to service base. So by service-based, it’s one after-sale... the 
customizability of the products... So it’s ﬁne that it’s pre-developed in that way. 
And we can just share that information.”  (Brian Gadisi) 
This new strategy helped AriOn grow and have a better oBering in a competitive market

 “We have tried to pivot and diQerentiate by creating unique functionality that 
helps buildings become more eQicient and manage their diQerent energy

Andrew also showed how the new oBering could highly beneﬁt the current situation in the

“We want to essentially roll the software out on a national scale, municipalities

independent, essentially create smart grids that will incorporate distributed 
renewable energy technologies.” (Andrew Murray) 
These EnergyTech companies are creating competitive prices in addition to their

“We moved away from a product-based business to a service-based business. 
We develop solutions for our clients who come to us with a solution, we’ll ﬁgure

4.6.2 How SMMEs can Participate in the JUST Energy Transition 
From the Global Entrepreneurship Congress + Africa 2024 event, attending the “SMMEs 
in the Just energy transition event”, provides a comprehensive understanding of the 
current energy crisis in South Africa, particularly the phenomenon of load shedding, and 
its impact on entrepreneurs and Small, Medium, and Micro Enterprises (SMMEs).  
The energy transition in South Africa is not a new phenomenon. Over centuries, societies 
have moved from traditional biomass to coal, oil, gas, hydro, and nuclear energy. The 
current transition, however, is driven by the need to address climate change while also 
considering social and economic objectives. As noted from the panel:   
==PAGE:41==
Alberto Benito Pontes – MSc Technology Entrepreneurship 
39 
“It’s not purely about energy, but it’s about how the energy space interacts with 
other sectors.”  
This transition involves a shift from one technology to another, and it’s not just about 
solving the ecological side of the equation, but also about meeting social economic 
objectives.  
Load shedding, the controlled power outages implemented by Eskom, the national 
electricity utility, to manage the demand and supply of electricity, has signiﬁcant 
implications for entrepreneurs. It disrupts operations, increases costs, and necessitates 
a shift in business strategies. However, it also presents opportunities for innovation and 
growth in the clean energy sector. Policymakers, investors, and entrepreneurs need to 
work together to navigate these challenges and seize the opportunities presented by the 
energy transition. As one panelist stated:  
“For those who are looking for opportunities, obviously it’s about being 
innovative, right? So meaning there are opportunities across the board, 
opportunities in new technologies, renewable energy and associated 
technologies.” 
The discussions at the event highlighted the need for supportive policies and plans from 
the Department of Energy and Eskom that reinforce the industrial capacity of small 
businesses. Entrepreneurs need funding and investment to grow in the energy sector. 
Partnerships between the public and private sectors can drive investment and provide 
ﬁnancial support to small enterprises. As one panelist pointed out:  
“…there’s been a lot of policy pronouncements that have actually made it 
possible or are making it possible for entrepreneurs or small entities to be able to 
play into this space.” 
Capacity building, particularly in rural areas, is crucial. Rural communities often lack the 
necessary skills, energy access, and capital to participate fully in the clean energy sector, 
as gathered in the ﬁndings. EBorts are being made to provide training and support to 
empower these communities. As another panelist said:  
“…it’s really important for SMMEs to be able to do that. And then the other one is 
in terms of the energy transition in the urban and peri-urban.” 
The energy transition presents opportunities for entrepreneurs. These include the 
development of new technologies, renewable energy solutions, and abatement 
technologies to reduce the negative impacts of fossil fuels. The convergence of 
technologies and industries also presents opportunities for entrepreneurs to innovate 
and contribute to the clean energy sector 
“There are opportunities across the board, opportunities in new technologies, 
renewable energy and associated technologies.”

Entrepreneurs need to access markets and collaborate with local municipalities and 
other businesses. Partnerships and collaborations can help SMMEs secure a continuous 
ﬂow of work and overcome the challenges of isolation and distance from main centers, 
pointing out from the ecent: 
“…there’s been a lot of policy pronouncements that have actually made it 
possible or are making it possible for entrepreneurs or small entities to be able to 
play into this space.” 
In conclusion, while load shedding poses signiﬁcant challenges for entrepreneurs in

5.0.1 Context 
Cape Town, known as the Silicon Cape (Anonymous, Silicon Cape: Pushing for Diversity 
in Cape Town, 2019), is a dynamic hub of technological innovation in South Africa. The 
city’s tech ecosystem is diverse and vibrant, supported by numerous tech hubs, 
accelerators, and co-working spaces. It also beneﬁts from a rich talent pool provided by 
top local universities (OCallaghan, 2024). However, this thriving ecosystem faces 
signiﬁcant challenges, including socio-political issues, economic downturn due to the 
COVID-19 pandemic, and notably, the ongoing issue of load shedding. 
Load shedding is a measure employed by the state-owned power utility to prevent a total 
collapse of the power system when demand exceeds supply (Eskom, 2019). This involves 
deliberately switching oB parts of the electricity supply, a practice that has become a part 
of life for residents and businesses in Cape Town. The duration and intensity of load 
shedding can vary greatly, leading to signiﬁcant disruptions, especially for tech startups 
that rely on a steady power supply.

5.1.2 Adpating from Resources 
The concept of ‘adapting by avoiding’ is an interesting strategy that emerged from the 
interviews. This strategy involves structuring the business in a way that minimizes the 
impact of load shedding, essentially avoiding the problem rather than directly addressing 
it. This approach is exempliﬁed by Marc Perel from Obox Themes, who structured his 
business to be less dependent on a constant power supply. He explains: 
“I speciﬁcally set up my company that we don’t work with like deliverables that 
are hourly, everything is on the day. So because if you work too close into like 
delivering hourly now, you’re not going to be able to do that with this problem.” 
(Marc Perel) 
This approach allowed Obox Themes to continue operating during load shedding without 
signiﬁcant disruptions. By not tying their deliverables to speciﬁc hours, they were able to 
work around the power outages and maintain their productivity. This strategy aligns with 
the pro-cyclical viewpoint, where entrepreneurs adapt their business models to the 
changing economic conditions during a crisis. 
On the other hand, Joe Philip from Embedded Employees did not initially structure his 
business to avoid the impact of load shedding. Instead, he adapted his business as the 
crisis unfolded, investing in backup power solutions for his employees and adjusting their 
work schedules to accommodate the power outages. He explains: 
“…we essentially waited for problems to arise to then resolve them instead of 
proactively resolving it prior to a problem arising, which is never a good 
experience for a customer.” (Joe Philip) 
This approach aligns with the counter-cyclical viewpoint (Abdesselam, 2017), where 
entrepreneurs respond to the crisis as it unfolds, often resulting in a reactive rather than 
proactive approach. While this strategy allowed Embedded Employees to navigate the 
crisis, it also resulted in disruptions and additional costs. 
These contrasting strategies highlight the diversity of responses to the load shedding 
crisis among tech startups in Cape Town. They underscore the importance of adaptability 
and strategic planning in navigating crises (Olajuyin & Mago, 2022). As discussed in the 
literature review, entrepreneurs’ responses to crises can vary greatly, ranging from pro-

cyclical strategies that react to the crisis as it unfolds. These ﬁndings provide a nuanced

linked to the notion of ‘entrepreneurial bricolage’, which involves making do with the

This adaptation can be seen as a form of ‘temporal bricolage’ (Baker & E. Nelson, 2005),

around the load shedding timetable, working during oB-peak hours when power supply 
is more reliable, or using backup power solutions to maintain operations during power 
outages. 
These strategies align with time pacing  (Charlotta Siréna, 2018), which involves 
synchronizing business activities with external time schedules or rhythms. In the context 
of load shedding, this could involve aligning work schedules with Eskom’s load shedding 
timetable to minimize disruptions.

Conlcusion answering: What speciﬁc challenges do tech-startups face during energy

Tech-startups in Cape Town face several speciﬁc challenges during energy crises, 
particularly due to load shedding. The unpredictability of power outages, dictated by 
Eskom’s load shedding schedules, adds complexity to business operations and planning, 
disrupting work routines and productivity. Connectivity issues pose another signiﬁcant 
challenge, with sudden disruptions in internet and network connections leading to 
communication breakdowns, delays in project delivery, and potential data loss. These 
connectivity issues are particularly problematic for tech startups that heavily rely on 
digital technologies and online communication. Additionally, while the shift towards 
remote work oBers ﬂexibility, it also presents challenges in hiring and managing remote

load shedding. These challenges underscore the need for adaptability, strategic 
planning, and innovative solutions in navigating energy crises.

5.6 International Implications of Energy Crises 
Energy crises, such as the load shedding experienced in Cape Town, are not conﬁned to 
developing countries. Recent events in various parts of the world, including Texas in the 
United States and Kiev in Ukraine, have shown that even developed regions are not 
immune to such crises. These events underscore the global nature of energy crises and 
highlight the need for innovative solutions that can be applied across diBerent contexts. 
In Texas, a severe winter storm resulted in massive power outages, leading to loss of life 
and highlighting the vulnerabilities of the state’s isolated power grid (Cohen, 2024). 
According to a Forbes article titled “Massive shift for load shedding in South Africa”, this 
crisis prompted a surge in private investments in the energy sector, as companies sought 
ways to mitigate the impact of the power outages.  
Similarly, the ongoing conﬂict in Kiev has led to extended blackouts, underscoring the 
vulnerability of energy infrastructure in times of war (Mackenzie, 2024). This crisis has 
highlighted the need for resilient energy systems that can withstand external shocks, 
whether they be natural disasters or man-made conﬂicts. 
These international events have important implications for tech startups in Cape Town 
and elsewhere. They highlight the potential for these startups to develop and implement 
sustainable energy solutions, not just in their local markets, but on a global scale. This

5.8 Limitations 
While this study provides valuable insights into the impact of load shedding on tech 
startups in Cape Town, it is not without limitations. The study relies on a qualitative 
research approach, focusing on in-depth interviews with a limited number of 
participants. While this approach allows for a rich understanding of the participants’ 
experiences, it limits the generalizability of the ﬁndings.

Conclusion 
The central research question of this study was: 
How does the tech-startup ecosystem in Cape Town, South Africa, 
adapt during times of energy crisis? 
The ﬁndings reveal that, despite the operational and productivity challenges posed by the 
load shedding crisis, tech startups in Cape Town have demonstrated signiﬁcant 
resilience and adaptability. They have innovated and adjusted their business models to 
navigate through the crisis. Additionally, they have moved with the transition towards 
digitalization and remote work, beneﬁting from the ﬂexibility that this brings. 
These ﬁndings, gathered through qualitative interviews and processed according to 
grounded theory, contribute to the existing body of knowledge by providing a unique 
perspective on the resilience of tech startups during energy crises, particularly in the 
context of a developing country like South Africa. The study also highlights the role of tech 
startups as catalysts for societal change during crises, underscoring their potential to 
drive digital transformation in the face of adversity. 
However, the study is not without limitations. The ﬁndings may not fully capture the 
dynamic nature of the crisis and the ongoing adaptations of the startups, as well as the 
lack of additional interviewees to add more substance to the study. Additionally, 
potential selection bias due to the reliance on personal networks for sampling may have 
inﬂuenced the results. 
Based on these ﬁndings and limitations, the study recommends future research to 
employ a mixed-methods approach and conduct longitudinal studies to capture the 
dynamic nature of the crisis and the startups' adaptations over time. Comparative 
studies could also be beneﬁcial to understand the experiences of tech startups in other 
regions aBected by similar energy crises. 
In conclusion, the study underscores the potential of tech startups as catalysts for 
change during crises, highlighting their role in the digital transformation of the region. 
Despite the challenges posed by energy crises, tech startups in Cape Town have 
demonstrated resilience and adaptability, providing valuable insights for other regions 
facing similar challenges.
