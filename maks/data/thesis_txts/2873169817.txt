Abstract
Coastal sites often vary in pCO2, acting as sources or sinks to the atmosphere.
Little is
known about the contribution of submarine groundwater discharge (SGD) to CO2 dynamics
within coastal lagoons, that are often characterised by high anthropogenic nutrient loads and
long water residence times due to their semi-enclosed nature. This study presents a high-
resolution timeseries observations in seawater from Nissum Fjord in Denmark over seven tidal
cycles including four closing periods of flood gates. We assess CO2 drivers by coupling surface
water measurements to the groundwater tracer 222Rn. Ocean inflows enhanced CO2 to 422
µatm on average, compared to 271 µatm when the lagoon was draining to the ocean. Simple
mixing between high CO2 seawater and low CO2 lagoon waters explained much of the CO2
distribution. The lower pCO2 inside the lagoon was likely related to enhanced nutrient release
from SGD and subsequent primary production. Dissolved oxygen implies primary production
and aerobic respiration. Persistent low CO2 imply lagoon autotrophy, while O2 undersaturation
imply net heterotrophy. On average, Nissum Fjord was a net CO2 sink over the measured time
period with an average flux of -41 mmol m2 d−1. Variability in CO2 water-air exchange was
majorly governed by water mass mixing, wind speed and SGD contribution.
Plain language summary
Carbon dioxide (CO2) flux from aquatic systems are highly variable, including coastal lagoons.
These systems lack of understanding how groundwater influence the CO2 inside the lagoon.
In this study, we monitored the water conditions in Nissum Fjord, a Danish lagoon, over
four days, with both tidal and gate opening influences.
We measured CO2 and a natural
groundwater tracer (222Rn) in surface waters to understand where the CO2 was coming from.
We found that when ocean water flowed into the lagoon, CO2 levels were higher while they
were lower when the lagoon drained out to the ocean. This difference could mostly be explained
by mixing between ocean and lagoon waters. The groundwater might lower CO2 inside the
lagoon by nutrient supply, supporting photosynthesis. The consistently low CO2 suggests that
the lagoon produces more organic matter than it uses, while low oxygen suggests the opposite.
On average The lagoon absorbed CO2. Changes in CO2 uptake were mainly driven by the
mixing of different water sources, the influence of groundwater and winds.

Introduction
Carbon dioxide (CO2) is a major greenhouse gas (GHG) of the natural carbon cycle, facilitating
carbon exchange among the atmo-, bio-, and hydrosphere (Falkowski, 2012). Human activities
have significantly disrupted the natural cycle through the intense fossil fuel burning leading to
increasing global CO2 emissions (Falkowski, 2012).
The oceans play a significant role in the global carbon cycle, containing 60 times the amount
of carbon compared to the atmosphere (DeVries, 2022). Since 1850, the global oceans have
absorbed approximately 26 percent of the anthropogenically emitted CO2 (Friedlingstein et al.,
2022). However, estimates on carbon budgets often focus on the ocean and land separately,
even though the land-ocean continuum contributes disproportionately large amount to the to-
tal carbon (Regnier et al., 2022).
On the water-air interface, oceans exchange gases with the atmosphere, including CO2. This
process is driven by solubility, gas exchange rate and the difference in gas concentration be-
tween the water and the atmosphere (Takahashi et al., 2002; Wanninkhof, 2014; Raymond
et al., 2001). Estuaries (semi enclosed coastal bodies between the river mouth and tidewa-
ter) act as sources, whereas coastal shelves (from shoreline to the shelf break) are CO2 sinks
(Rosentreter et al., 2023; Cai, 2011). The dissolved CO2 concentration depends on the type of
marine ecosystem driven by prevailing physical and biogeochemical processes (Falkowski et al.,
2000). Remarkable differences in the order of magnitude in CO2 oversaturation were found
in different coastal ecosystem types (Tomer et al., 2025). Turbulent mixing, gas exchange,
stratification, changes in salinity or temperature, can drive the solubility of the gas, whereas
the biological pump explains biogeochemical processes, such as photosynthesis, respiration,
calcification and dissolution (Falkowski et al., 2000). All of these are significantly affected by
the proximity to the coast, with carbon and nutrient inputs transported by freshwater (Regnier
et al., 2022).
Many CO2 studies only focused on water-air interactions (Borges et al., 2004) or biogeo-
chemical processes within the water (Gattuso, 1998; Laas et al., 2012). Another significant
CO2 contribution can be attributed to submarine groundwater discharge (SGD), that includes
fresh terrestrial water as well as, through sediments, recirculated seawater such as porewater
(Moore, 2010). Often, studies considered rivers and streams as the only land to ocean con-
tinuum pathway, but recent findings suggest SGD as a major pathway for both nutrients and
CO2 (Wilson et al., 2024; Tomer et al., 2025). Large gaps remain in the understanding of
SGD contribution to CO2 dynamics in coastal areas due to its multiple different ecosystems
(Taniguchi, 2002; Tomer et al 2025).
1
==PAGE:8==
The natural SGD tracer ²²²Rn can be coupled to pCO2 measurements to assess the contribu-
tion of SGD on local CO2 variations (Santos et al., 2012b; Swarzenski et al., 2006). ²²²Rn
decays from uranium-238, that is found globally in nearly all sediments. The decay rate of
²²²Rn of 3.82 days is comparable to the time scale of many coastal processes, such as tides,
rain events and algal bloom cycles, or altered water-air fluxes (Wilson et al., 2024; Maher
et al., 2019; Santos et al., 2012b; Tomer et al., 2025). In comparison to surface water, SGD
contains 2-4 orders of magnitude higher concentrations of ²²²Rn, revealing SGD intrusion into
the water body (Cook et al., 2006; Peterson et al., 2010). Along the coast, the shallow wa-
ter and vertical mixing permits groundwater to alter the surface CO2 concentration (Santos
et al., 2012b). Several advantages are attributed to the use of ²²²Rn as a natural tracer: It
is constantly produced by the natural decay from uranium (half-life of billions of years), only
found in waters that were in contact with sediments, relatively easy to measure and the signal
is integrated from multiple groundwater and porewater pathways, smoothing out smaller-scale
variations (Santos et al., 2009; Cable et al., 1996).
Although several studies have now linked SGD intrusion to pCO2 water-air dynamics, coastal
lagoons remain underrepresented (Tomer et al., 2025). None of the studies has so far addressed
this interaction within a temperate Danish estuary. This study presents the first assessment
of pCO2 dynamics coupled to SGD over seven tidal cycles including four gate closing periods
within Nissum Fjord, a temperate Danish coastal lagoon on the westcoast of Denmark in
Jutland.
Based on continuous high-resolution pCO2 field-measurements, governed by key
auxiliary parameters, temporal trends and primary drivers in aquatic pCO2 and water-air fluxes
are identified and discussed, with a major focus on SGD.

2
Submarine groundwater discharge (SGD)
SGD is defined as ”as any and all flow of water on continental margins from the seabed to the
coastal ocean, regardless of fluid composition or driver” (Burnett et al., 2003b). It can orig-
inate from terrestrial fresh groundwater or recirculated seawater that entered sediment pores
and is released back into the coastal water (Burnett et al., 2003b). Any waters, freshwater
originated from land or recirculated seawater, can mix within the sediments and be released
as an integrated source of SGD. Single sources are, thus, complicated to disentangle and SGD
is often a combination of many different drivers (Santos et al., 2012a).
The recognition of SGD as an important variable in the hydrological and biogeochemical cycles
of the ocean has increased in the last two decades (Moore et al., 2008). The quantity of SGD
is controlled by hydraulic gradients (Moore et al., 2008), mainly influenced by tides (tidal
pumping) or wave set-up (wave pumping) along coast (Moore et al., 2008; Li et al., 1999),
and groundwater levels on land, driven by seasonality, water movement or topography (San-
tos et al., 2012a). In comparison to riverine inputs, the magnitude of SGD can vary greatly
depending on local conditions and can range from less than 1% of rivers (Du et al., 2024) up
to 60-fold the river discharge (Correa et al., 2020).
Due to high concentrations of solutes, total SGD inputs may exceed river inputs (Moore et al.,
2008; Crotwell et al., 2003). Many studies found SGD to be a significant source of nutrients
(Wang et al., 2021; Bishop et al., 2017; Maher et al., 2019), metals (Wang et al., 2019;
Xue et al., 2023) or carbon (Tomer et al., 2025; Cabral et al., 2023) into coastal areas, often
exceeding the concentrations and fluxes of rivers. The chemical composition can be affected
by many factors, such as domestic or industrial sewage influenced by seasonality, tourism or
urbanization (Alorda-Kleinglass et al., 2024; Kwon et al., 2014; Rufí-Salís et al., 2019).

Methods
3.1
Study site
Nissum Fjord is a shallow, large lagoon-like Danish estuary on the west coast of Jutland,
Denmark. Coastal lagoons are defined as “a shallow coastal water body separated from the
ocean by a barrier, connected at least intermittently to the ocean by one or more restricted
inlets, and usually oriented shore-parallel” (Kjerfve, 1994). They are typically very shallow
but can vary greatly in size (Kjerfve, 1994).
Often, they are influenced by anthropogenic
and natural forcings and the salinity ranges between freshwater until hypersaline conditions
(Kjerfve, 1994).
Coastal lagoons act as sinks for inorganic sediment and organic matter,
support primary production, and are often valuable sites for fisheries (Kjerfve, 1994).
Towards the west, the lagoon is connected to the North Sea via a narrow sluice opening at
Thorsminde (Figure 1). The in- and outflow between the salty ocean water and the fresh lagoon
water is controlled by a local government authority (Thorsminde Havn, Appendix section A.1)
which regulates the flow of water in and out the lagoon. The opening and closing follows
distinct parameters within the Nissum Fjord, primarily, controlled by keeping the water level
change minimal (0.3 m), secondly, regulate salinity in the lagoon (12-20 g kg−1, Miljø- og
Ligestillingsministeriet, section A.1). The freshwater inflow originates from several streams
flow into Nissum fjord, of which River Storå is among the largest Danish rivers (1.1 km³ yr−1,
Conley et al., 2000). Despite their small sizes, their fresh-water discharges play a significant
role in Danish costal ecosystems (Conley et al., 2000).
The lagoon is 70 km2 large with an average depth of 1 m and a maximum depth of 3 m
(Geopark Vestjylland). Areas surrounding Nissum Fjord are diverse, but mainly compromise
plain flat landscapes, characterised by well reserved land (Geopark Vestjylland). Nitrogen (N)
inputs into Danish coastal waters were among the highest N loads in Europe (Conley et al.,
2000) with a concentration of 2,400 kg N km² yr−1, 1.7 times higher than northern Europe
averages (Conley et al., 2000; Howarth et al., 1996). The annual load of 2,400 kg N km2 yr−1
was primarily driven by non-point sources, linked to extensive agriculture, and was found to
be almost 2-fold higher during wet years compared to dry years (Conley et al., 2000). P loads
were majorly point-sources, with an averaged annual Danish Phosphorus (P) load of 112 kg P
km2 yr−1, equivalent to P loads from Northern Europe (Conley et al., 2000; Howarth et al.,
1996). Total Nitrogen (TN) and total phosphorus (TP) loads follow seasonal trends and are
linked to freshwater runoff peaking during winter and being minor during summer (Conley et
al., 2000). Total N concentrations align with this trend, whereas Total P concentration show
a reversed pattern, probably through fewer dilution of point sources during summer (Conley et
al., 2000). Further variations are related to geographical characteristics of each Danish estuary
(Conley et al., 2000). Dissolved Inorganic Nitrogen (DIN) and Dissolved Inorganic Phosphorus
(DIP) concentrations follow seasonal patterns in Danish estuaries and are explained by the
coupling of load variations and biological activity (Conley et al., 2000). Compared to other

Danish estuaries, DIN and TN concentrations were relatively high in Nissum Fjord (Conley
et al., 2000). Light penetration in Danish estuaries is largely reduced due to wind-induced
sediment resuspension (Olesen, 1996). However, the shallow nature of Danish estuaries allows
light penetration reaching the bottom in almost all Danish estuaries, including Nissum Fjord,
allowing benthic primary production (Conley et al., 2000). With
50 µg l−1, mean chlorophyll
concentrations in Nissum Fjord during grow season (March to October) were extremely high
in comparison to most other Danish estuaries (< 20 µg l−1) (Conley et al., 2000). Chlorophyll

et al., 2000; Jørgensen et al., 1980; Conley et al., 2007; Fallesen et al., 2000).
Anoxia
was enhanced during summer, when temperature stratification and primary production were
dominant (Jørgensen et al., 1980). Benthic communities and organic matter respiration in the
sediments are altered by repeated anoxic conditions (Conley et al., 2007). Future projections
suggest enhanced eutrophication and more frequent anoxic conditions (Conley et al., 2007).
Pelagic respiration can be important in Danish estuaries; in extreme cases, even exceed benthic
respiration (Jensen et al., 1990).

3.2
Time series observations
High-resolution continuous timeseries sensors were installed from the 14th of March 2025 until
the 18th of March 2025 in the harbour at Thorsminde inside the lagoon ∼80 m upstream
of the sluice.
Water was pumped from ∼1.5 m (total depth ∼3m) above seafloor into
two separate showerhead water gas exchangers (RAD AQUA) (Figure 2, Santos et al., 2012b;
Henriksson et al., 2024). Each water gas exchanger was connected to a desiccant unit including
Drierite, one followed by a GHG analyser (LI‐COR LI‐7810 for pCO2 and CH4) via a closed
loop, while the other connected to a RAD7 for 222Rn.
Dissolved greenhouse gases were
measured every second; 222Rn every 30 min. The set-up order was changed in the evening of
the 16th of March. Auxiliary parameters were measured with automatic loggers deployed next
to the pump, including the water salinity, temperature and depth (Solinst 3001 Leveloggers 5),
dissolved oxygen (DO, MiniDOT oxygen loggers PME), and the pH (HOBO MX2501) each 1
minute.
Windspeed and -direction, and water level on the ocean side of the sluice gate, were provided
by the local authority (Thorsminde Havn). The wind speed was recalculated to 10 m above
surface level, assuming an initial height of 5 m (Wanninkhof, 1992).
Flow periods were
defined following the flowrate calculations from the Danish Coastal Authority, provided upon
email request.
Figure 2: Schematic setup based on the graphic from Linnea Henriksson, GU; Barefoot mate-
rial.
3.3
Discrete samples
Discrete water surface samples with triplicates were taken around and inside the lagoon for
end-member analysis at seven different locations, representing the timeseries location twice
when open and closed (Figure 1). Samples were filtered through 0.2 µm filters into 12 ml
exetainers (DIC) and 50 ml falcon tubes (TA) and stored at ∼4°C. Samples were analysed for
DIC (AS-C5 DIC Analyzer) and TA (potentiometric Gran titration with 0.01 M HCl with an
888 Titrando) along a CRM (batch 189 provided by A. Dickson) in the laboratory thereafter.
Additionally, water temperature (°C), salinity, pH, DO and specific conductivity (SPC) were
measured with a handheld probe (YSI) when waters were sampled.

Results
4.1
Continous timeseries
Seven tidal cycles were captured including four gate closing periods that led to major changes
in chemical conditions. When open, the system follows the natural tidal variation (Figure 4)
and water masses are exchanged between the ocean and lagoon-dominated brackish water.
When closed, the system is isolated from any ocean-lagoon water exchange. Wind speed was
independent of the flow direction and averaged 6.5 ±2.7 m s−1. Wind direction varied between
onshore and offshore direction. Weather conditions during the sampling were overall dry and
sunny with minimal cloud coverage.
Resulting water level variations from the water mass exchange were ∼10 cm with low water
level during outflows (Table 2). Water mass exchange was mainly governed by a strong salinity
shift between brackish lagoon water (6 g kg−1 on average) and ocean water (26 g kg−1 on
average), accompanied by lower pH during inflows (mean 8.1) and higher pH during outflows
(mean 8.3). Temperature changes were minor with ∼5°C on average during all flow periods.
Water mass shifts governed strong changes in CO2 and ²²²Rn. The pCO2 varied greatly by
1.5-fold on average between 271 µatm during outflows to 422 µatm during inflows. ²²²Rn
concentration was 1.5 - fold higher during outflows with 835.5 dpm m−3 compared to 543.4
dpm m−3 during inflows, associated to water level variations. A notable shift in ²²²Rn mag-
nitude occurred in the morning of the 17th by a sharp increase in ²²²Rn concentrations from
<1000 dpm m−3 to ∼2000 dpm m−3, reflecting great variations of ²²²Rn during all flow
periods.

4.2
pCO2 correlations
In- and outflows drive variations in CO2, DO, water level and pH (Figure 6).
The main
correlation was a positive relationship between pCO2 and salinity (R2 = 0.74), suggesting a
main source from oceanic saltwater intrusion (Figure 6). This reveals inflow waters from the
ocean as a CO2 source to the lagoon with a 1.5-higher concentration on average (Figure 5).

was minor (R=0.0). pCO2 and DO (%) showed an inverse relationship, while constantly being

4.3
CO2 fluxes
On average the lagoon was a CO2 sink over the total measured period, regardless of the
model applied (Figure 7). Flux calculations based on a Danish gas transfer velocity (Mørk
et al., 2014) is on average 1,5-fold higher than applying the gas transfer velocity from an
integrative comparison of estuarine gas transfer velocities (Raymond et al., 2001) and varies
between - 26.17 mmol m−2 d−1 and - 40.98 mmol m−2 d−1 in this study (Figure 7). Both
flux calculations (varying gas transfer velocity) follow a similar pattern. Inflow waters are near
equilibrium or minor CO2 sources, and outflows as CO2 sinks (Figure A2). Further discussion
about the flux is based on the calculation using the approach from Mørk et al., 2014. The
contribution of groundwater, traced by ²²²Rn, on the CO2 flux (Figure 8), imply stronger
CO2 sinks with increased SGD. Overall, outflows showed a larger variability in pCO2, DO,
temperature and pH.
Inflow periods act as weaker sinks by two reasons: A reduced air–water CO2 gradient through
elevated pCO2 levels and salinity-driven reduced solubility. Winds further alter the flux by
changing the gas transfer velocity. Stronger winds are associated with larger fluxes compared to
low wind conditions at the same pCO2 (Figure 8). Elevated wind speeds and larger SGD tend to
enhance CO2 uptake under non-equilibrium conditions, while under equilibrium CO2 conditions
(or CO2 supersaturation), wind and SGD promote greater CO2 release to the atmosphere.
The similar pattern of ²²²Rn and wind speed further suggests an interaction between these two
parameters.

5
Discussion
5.1
CO2 variation and drivers
The salinity vs. pCO2 scatter reveals the ocean as a CO2 source to the lagoon (Figure 9)
in contrast to often observed in most coastal systems (Frankignoulle et al., 2001; Polsenaere
et al., 2023; Akhand et al., 2022). In other systems, weak to absent relationships with salinity
were found, indicating recirculated seawater as the main driver for CO2 supersaturation (Tomer
et al., 2025). Mixing of salty ocean and brackish lagoon waters along the salinity gradient
often results in a non-linear behaviour of pCO2 through changes in the carbonate systems
and altered buffering capacity. The resulting typical bell-shape of pCO2 was first described
by Mook et al., 1975 and later observed in different field studies (Cotovicz et al., 2020; Abril
et al., 2021; Joesoef et al., 2015). The apparent conservative mixing along the salinity gradient
could result from absent changes in the carbonate system or primary production masking the
typical bell shape behaviour.
Hysteresis loops between pCO2 and the water level represent laged water movement to/from
the timeseries position. The concept of hysteresis loops - relationships between two variables
following a cyclical path rather than a linear correlation (Malutta et al., 2020) - was previously
found in other ecosystems in the context of gas concentration, such as methane (CH4) in
dependency to temperature/oxygen saturation/light availability (Yau et al., 2024).
River endmember concentrations were ∼10-times greater than continuous measurements dur-
ing outflows (3111 µatm vs 217 µatm on average). The high river pCO2 found ∼6 to 12
km away from the time series observation reflect strong respiration of organic matter in fresh-
water endmembers that are later modified once freshwaters remain in the lagoon for a longer
time. A similar spatial pCO2 pattern was found in other coastal lagoons (Maher et al., 2019;
Stokowski et al., 2020). A reasonable endmember could instead be centred lagoon water, being
homogeneously mixed by multiple runoffs. This is not represented by this project but could be
accounted for in an upcoming study within the same area. High pCO2 variability among out-
flow waters could result from high pCO2 river endmember coupled to non-homogenous mixing
inside the lagoon, a change in buffering capacity through mixing or high biological activity.
An inverse relation between DO and pCO2 is also seen in many other coastal systems, reflecting
primary production and aerobic respiration (Tomer et al., 2025; Zhai et al., 2009; Cabral et
al., 2024). In this study, DO shows a consistent undersaturated state, suggesting one or more
processes removing O2 from the water, decoupled from a CO2 variation.
A constant DO
undersaturation is found in many coastal areas where respiration removes DO and releases
CO2 (Tomer et al., 2025; Cabral et al., 2024; Ljungberg et al., 2024). High biological activity
could result in this net heterotrophic balance (respiration > primary production), supported
by sunny weather conditions combined with the eutrophic state of the system (section 3.1)
and enhanced biological activity. A greater pCO2 and DO variation within lagoon-dominated
17
==PAGE:23==
waters could be the result of a complex interplay between biogeochemistry and the variation
from high pCO2 river endmembers. Nutrient and phytoplankton observations are unavailable,
but would help to explain the combined DO and CO2 undersaturation.

5.2
CO2 fluxes
On average, the lagoon was a CO2 sink from the atmosphere (- 40.98 mmol m−2 d−1).
Contrastingly, many estuaries are CO2 sources to the atmosphere (Tomer et al., 2025; Chen
et al., 2009) with pCO2 ranging from ∼400–10,000 ￿atm (Cai, 2011). The variation in surface
water pCO2 is mainly driven by the ecosystem type with a significant oversaturation in tidal
rivers ( ∼1900%), salt marshes (∼580%), and mangroves (∼450%), and lower oversaturation
in highly productive coral reefs and seagrass meadows (∼110%) (Tomer et al., 2025).
Coastal lagoons were found to be CO2 sources or sinks to the atmosphere, depending on the
season and location of study (Stokowski et al., 2020; Polsenaere et al., 2023; Maher et al.,
2019). Surface water pCO2 of a temperate coastal lagoon in France had a minor oversaturation
of 496 ± 36 µatm on average over two years, whereas tidal and diurnal pCO2 variations (∼50
µatm in winter/spring and 150–200 µatm during summer/autumn) exceeded seasonal ranges
(461 ± 14 µatm up to 530 ± 39 µatm Polsenaere et al., 2023). Resulting fluxes were 1.4 up to
40.1 mmol m−2 d−1, slightly lower to the magnitude found in this study. Nutrient enrichment
can shift coastal lagoon waters from being a source to a sink (Peixoto et al., 2013). This could
be an important factor in Nissum Fjord, due to its high nutrient supply (section 3.1). The
importance of nutrient loads was demonstrated at an eutrophic coastal lagoon in Australia
shifting from a CO2 source during pre- and post-bloom (1717 µatm and 3056 µatm) to a CO2
sink during a phytoplankton bloom (137 µatm, Maher et al., 2019). Respective fluxes changed
from 66 mmol m−2 d−1 and 274 mmol m−2 d−1 during pre- and post-bloom to an uptake of
-13 mmol m−2 d−1 during bloom.
The observed pCO2 values (on average ∼314.28 µatm) align with undersaturated results from
other studies in similar temperate climates in the region. For instance a typical Baltic Sea pCO2
is in the order of ∼200 µatm to ∼275 (Thomas et al., 1999) and ∼200 µatm for Roskilde Fjord
(Mørk et al., 2014), aligning well with Nissum Lagoon observations even after corrections to
an annual oceanic pCO2 increase of 1.5 µatm (Takahashi et al., 2009) since observations were
recorded. Observed CO2 fluxes in this study (- 26 to - 41 mmol m−2 d−1) are of comparable
magnitude of those found in in Roskilde Fjord, using the same gas transfer velocity (- 60,48
mmol m−2 d−1; Mørk et al., 2014). Differences in those fluxes can be explained by different
prevailing wind speeds, pCO2, salinity, temperature or the averaged time periods (four days in
this compared to March-July by Mørk et al., 2014). Despite the quantitative difference, the
CO2 uptake trend in spring is consistent with geographically comparable locations (Algesten
et al., 2004; Mørk et al., 2014; Thomas et al., 1999).
A large uncertainty in CO2 flux estimates is the gas transfer velocity (Borges et al., 2004).
Several studies have numerically or observationally tried to estimate k, but uncertainties re-
main high, especially for rivers and estuaries (Raymond et al., 2001), due to the variety in
different ecosystems. The here chosen k-values reflect a broad comparison of k among estuar-
ies (Raymond et al., 2001) and a Danish coastal k (Mørk et al., 2014). Depending on models
applied, the magnitude of the flux varies between -26 mmol m−2 d−1 and -41 mmol m−2 d−1.
Wind stress was found to dominate bottom turbulences in deeper ecosystems (> 10m), or in
19
==PAGE:25==
conditions influenced by high wind speeds ( > 8 m s−1, Raymond et al., 2001). Due to the
shallow nature of Nissum Fjord and wind speeds < 8 m s−1 on average, bottom stress could
be an important driver in the CO2 fluxes in Nissum Fjord.
5.3
SGD contribution
222Rn concentrations in this study (742.9 ± 505.8 dpm m−3) are comparably low to many
other ecosystems. A comprehensive summary of observations in 40 coastal ecosystems globally
revealed 222Rn varying between near 0 to ∼15 000 dpm m−3 (Tomer et al., 2025). Outliers
were up to almost 150 000 dpm m−3 and showed a large variation depended on the ecosystem
type (Tomer et al., 2025).
In nearly all groundwater studies, SGD was considered a source of pCO2 and nutrients into the
system (Santos et al., 2012b; Tomer et al., 2025; Porubsky et al., 2014; Maher et al., 2019). In
this study at Nissum Fjord we found opposing trends. Negative correlations between ²²²Rn and
CO2 suggest that groundwater discharge lowers CO2 levels through physical or biogeochemical
processes. Temporal stable ²²²Rn concentrations can be found during bloom and pre-bloom
conditions, when high nutrient supply from groundwater and subsequent algae bloom masked
a positive pCO2-²²²Rn relationship, as previously observed in an Australian lagoon (Maher
et al., 2019). Considering the eutrophic nature of Danish estuaries, coupled with good light
availability, and high productivity (section 3.1), it is likely that a high nutrient supply through
SGD enhanced primary production that removed CO2 while increasing radon. The subsequent
biological uptake of CO2, fueled by nutrient supply, could mask the CO2 inputs via SGD.
The two different ²²²Rn regression patterns could indicate a change in ecosystem response of
SGD, possibly linked to residence time within sediments and related accumulation of ²²²Rn.
Previous studies have found a dependency of ²²²Rn concentrations during neap vs. spring tides,
explained by longer/shorter time periods within the sediments and providing more/less time
for ²²²Rn regeneration and CO2 production (Santos et al., 2009). The concept could hold here
as one possible explanation for elevated ²²²Rn concentrations after prolonged residence time
within sediments. Water is accumulated within the lagoon, reflected by maximum water level
heights, potentially allowing longer time for ²²²Rn accumulation (Figure 4).

==PAGE:26==
6
Conclusion
This study was among the first linking SGD to CO2 fluxes in a temperate coastal lagoon.
High-resolution data from Nissum Fjord revealed the salty oceanic inflows as a CO2 source
to the lagoon with a 1.5- higher pCO2 compared to brackish outflows. Primary production
counteracted the trend by removing CO2 inside the lagoon, depicted by a negative pCO2 – DO
relationship. Surprisingly, this study is the first reporting a negative correlation between ²²²Rn
and pCO2, indicating SGD reducing surface water CO2 inside the lagoon. Nutrient-enriched
SGD could stimulate primary production, thereby lowering surface pCO2 and modulating CO2
fluxes. Contrastingly to low pCO2, undersaturated DO implies net heterotrophic conditions.
The high pCO2 variability of river endmembers, as well as lagoon-dominated waters, highlights
the biogeochemical complexity of the system and challenges in disentangling CO2 drivers. On
average, this Danish coastal lagoon was a weak CO2 sink, against the trend of many other
coastal systems that act as CO2 sources.

A.1
Sluice practices
This table is translated from the website of the Danish Coast Authority (Miljø- og Ligestill-
ingsministeriet).
• The water level in the fjord does not exceed +0.20 m DVR90 and is not less than -0.1
m DVR90.
• Minimum water level is kept in the period 15 March - 1 July in the range 0 to +0.10 m
DVR90.
• The water level is controlled according to a calculated simple average of the water levels
at the three water level meters in the fjord.
• Salinity in the Yder Fjord is held in the period May-September in the range of 12-20 per
mille.
• The rest of the year, salinity is kept below 20 and preferably over 6 per mille.
• Salinity is controlled according to a weighted average of measurements from Yder Fjord.
• Until the target salinity has been achieved, priority must be given to allowing salt water
into the fjord, even at low wind speeds. When the target is achieved, salinity is main-
tained at the installation preferably at the prospect of wind speeds that ensure quick
mixing.
• In situations where it is necessary to prioritize between meeting the water level and
salinity requirements, the water level is prioritized over salinity.
• An attraction flow is maintained through the lock to accommodate the migratory fish
passage through the flood in and out of the fjord.
• Navigation and cleaning of the entrance to the harbor requires that the lock be kept
closed for periods, just as the lock is closed during storms and high tides.
Additional practice
• For turning current (± 10 cm in relation to the fjord), all ports (including the transit
gate) are opened as far as possible at least once a day to accommodate fish passage.
