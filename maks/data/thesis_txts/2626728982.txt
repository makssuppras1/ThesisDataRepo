Abstract
The objective of this thesis was to examine cracks and porosities found in continuously cast
ductile iron produced at Tasso A/S. Light optical microscopy was employed to examine
the microstructure at the defects. The chemical composition at defects was quantiﬁed
using scanning electron microscopy. Image analysis was performed to quantify changes
in nodule size. Thermal analysis was used to ﬁnd variations in the solidiﬁcation during
production. Two types of defects were identiﬁed. This thesis’s conclusion suggests the
defect types are caused by failure at the solidiﬁcation front due to changes in cooling rate
and the formation of chunky graphite caused by improper inoculation.

Introduction
Cracks and porosities are commonly found in rectangular continuously cast ductile iron
strands produced at Tasso A/S. These defects lead to costumer complaints or scrapping
of strands.
The objective of this thesis was to examine these defects to ﬁnd possible causes. The
defects and surrounding microstructure were examined using light optical microscopy,
scanning electron microscopy and image analysis. Furthermore, thermal analysis were
employed for qualitatively investigate trends and possible correlation between solidiﬁca-
tion parameters and occurrence of defects. Graphite dies were 3D scanned to visualise
wear of the dies.
The thesis starts with introduction to the defects found at Tasso and examining literature
for possible causes of the defects. Introduction to relevant theory is summarized. The
results of the microstructural microscopy and image analysis along with thermal analy-
sis of cooling curves are then presented and examined. Possible causes of the defects are
given and the limitations of the methods and models employed are discussed. Recommen-
dations for future work and possible strategies for elimination of defects are then given.
Finally a conclusion is reached.
3.1
Defects in strands produced at Tasso
According to the experience of Tasso the defects are commonly occurring at the end of
a production and in strands with rectangular cross section. Furthermore, the defects are
often located at the bottom of a strand. These experiences suggests the defects are caused
by:
• Change in production parameters, due to strands at the end of a production being
defect prone
• Cross section, as the batches with circular cross section does not commonly exhibit
these defects
• Gravitational eﬀects, such as ﬂotation of elements/particles, as the bottom of strands
are more prone to defects
The strands produced at Tasso are investigated for defects when the surface skin is ma-
chined. The information on defects are documented along with other relevant information.

3.2
Defects in continuous casting
Multiple studies have studied cracks, defects and the formation thereof in continuous cast
steel slabs. Limited research has been dedicated to the study of transverse crack forma-
tion in continuous cast ductile iron. Therefore, this literature study will mainly rely on
defects in continuous cast steel slabs.
One should note the diﬀerences between the processes in a standard steel foundry and
Tasso’s production line. First, foundries producing steel slabs usually uses a vertical cast-
ing technique, where the melt is guided into a tundish (or a receiver which guides into the
tundish) which feeds the mould. To insulate, limit oxidation and lubricate, mould powder
is poured on top of the melt. The mould oscillates to increase the production rate. When
the melt has solidiﬁed suﬃciently it exits the mould and are usually straightened and

3.2.1
Transverse cracks in continuously cast steel
Research has shown the most common causes for transverse cracks in continuously cast
steel are[2]:
• Thermal stresses in the shell and turbulent ﬂow at the meniscus
• Variation of the meniscus level
• Depth of oscillation mark, as deep marks causes slower solidiﬁcation resulting in
weakened austenite grain boundaries
• Friction in the mould
Transverse cracks in continuously cast steel are most commonly found at oscillation marks.
Diﬀerent types of oscillation marks can be seen in Figure 4[3].
Oscillation marks in
continuously cast steel are due to the oscillating movement of the mould. At oscillation
marks the ”meniscus”(the thin region of solid shell between the melt and the ﬂux) often
breaks down resulting in overﬂow and entrapment of the meniscus. This is seen in Figure
4a-b. This leads to segregation at the entrapped meniscus. A study has shown elevated
phosphorous content at the meniscus and oscillation marks[4]. It has been hypothesised
to be one of the causes of the crack formation at oscillation marks.
Crack formation of both facial and corner cracks have been linked to increased ”sticking
in the mould due to friction”[5]. Furthermore, these types of cracks also occurs due to
”too severe cooling in mould or sprays”[5]. Other studies have found that high content of
manganese and concentrations of 0.03 wt. % niobium or vanadium promotes transverse
cracks[6].

4.1
Nucleation
Nucleation is the initiation of phase transformations. In the case of hypoeutectic cast iron
the phase transformation occurs between the liquid melt to austenite and graphite. The
transformation requires the formation and growth of nuclei. These nuclei can form either
by homogeneous nucleation or heterogeneous nucleation. In general, the nuclei forms due
to the atoms rearranging into a more favourable thermodynamic state.

4.2
Dendrite formation
For non-eutectic alloys one phase solidiﬁes ﬁrst. This phase has a diﬀerent composition
from the remaining melt due to the higher melting of that phase at the speciﬁc compo-
sition. The phase solidifying initially will have diﬀerent chemical composition than the
melt due. This results in rejection of elements at the solid/liquid interface. If solidiﬁca-
tion occurs at a high rate, the rejected elements are unable to completely diﬀuse into the
melt. This gives concentration diﬀerences in the melt. These concentration diﬀerences
changes the melting point. The lowered melting point at some points at the solid/liq-
uid interface gives a preferential solidiﬁcation direction. If this is exacerbated a planar
solid/liquid interface breaks down and dendrites starts forming.
This phenomenon is
known as constitutional supercooling.
4.3
Redistribution of solute
The rejection of elements from the solid/liquid interface results in segregation of elements
in unidirectional casting and between dendrites. The type of segregation is dependant on
the chemical composition and the cooling conditions. Three cases of solute redistribution
is considered:
• Some diﬀusion in solid and some mixing in liquid
• Complete mixing in solid and liquid
• No diﬀusion in solid and complete mixing in liquid
It should be noted that these cases are derived for unidirectional solidiﬁcation and assumes
a planar solid/liquid interface. Though it can be extended to and observed in dendritic
castings between the secondary dendrite arms[13].

4.5
Nucleation and growth of spheroidal graphite
The spheroidal morphology of graphite in ductile iron is achieved by adding nodulising
elements, especially Mg is used. Multiple theories for the nucleation of spheroidal graphite
has been proposed. These theories includes nucleation due to gas bubbles, salt-like car-
bides, silicon carbides and sulﬁdes/oxides[16]. Research[16] showed MgS compounds as
the main nucleation sites for graphite. Furthermore, research[17] has shown Ti as an im-
portant nucleation point for spheroidal graphite. In general, research shows many possible
nucleation sites for spheroidal graphite. The nucleation sites for graphite are aﬀected by
Ostwald ripening and chemical reactions of particles which are functioning as nucleation
sites. The nodule count taking Ostwald ripening into account can be estimated by[18]:

4.6
Graphite degradation
In ductile cast iron the growth of spherical graphite nodules can be inhibited. This results
in degradation of the graphite, changing the properties of the iron. Graphite degrada-
tion is related to chemical composition, inoculation and cooling rate. Multiple types of
graphite degradation have been observed ductile iron, though only ”chunky graphite”
and ”exploded graphite” are introduced as these are the relevant types for this project.

==PAGE:19==
Master Thesis
Technical University of Denmark
January 24, 2021
The focus is mainly on ”chunky graphite” as the causes are in general similar to those of
”exploded graphite”.
4.6.1
Chunky graphite
Chunky graphite is generally found to be a result of excess rare earths(RE), high Si
content, and slow cooling[19]. Elements found to inhibit the formation of chunky graphite
includes; Bi, Pb, Sb, Sn, Cu and As[19][20][21]. Elements found to assist formation of
chunky graphite includes; Ce, Ca, Cu, Ni, Si, Al and RE[19][20]. One should note that
the chunky graphite inhibiting elements also promotes ﬂake graphite. Especially excessive
Ce has been found to increase the risk of chunky graphite[22]. Statistical analysis found
pearlitic irons were less likely to contain chunky graphite compared to ferritic irons[23].
Predictive indexes for the occurrence of chunky graphite has been proposed[24], though
they need to be further developed to include more elements and the cooling rate. While
chunky graphite in general are found at heavy section irons due to the low cooling rate,
research has shown Ce to promote chunky graphite even during high cooling rate[25].
Research[26] has shown that solely metallurgical modiﬁcation is insuﬃcient in preventing
chunky graphite and changes to the solidiﬁcation is needed. Furthermore, research[27]
has hypothesized Pb to reduce the risk of Ce forming chunky graphite. In Figure 13 and
Figure 14 proposed growth of spheroidal graphite and chunky graphite are presented.

Exploded graphite
Exploded graphite is, like chunky graphite, found to be a result of excess rare earth
elements[20]. The carbon equivalent and the cooling rate has a higher inﬂuence on ex-
ploded graphite[28]. Therefore, exploded graphite is more often found in heavier sections
of the casting than chunky graphite.

4.8
Thermal Analysis
Thermal analysis is used for examining phase transformations and is widely used for the
solidiﬁcation of cast iron[12][30]. Thermal analysis is performed on cooling curves ob-
tained during solidiﬁcation. Thermal analysis gives insight in the solidiﬁcation, chemical
composition and the resulting microstructure. This is achieved by using knowledge of
both the solidiﬁcation process and the phase diagrams. Cooling curves are obtained by
solidifying melt in test cups with thermocouples. Generally two test cups are used, one
with added Tellurium and one without. The Tellurium suppresses stable eutectic solid-
iﬁcation resulting in a cup with white iron. Combined with a Tellurium-free cup it is
possible to acquire information on the chemical composition of the melt and measure the
start of eutectic solidiﬁcation.
Figure 16: Sketched cooling curves for (a) grey iron, (b) mottled iron and (c) white
iron[30].
In Figure 16 the start and end of solidiﬁcation is marked. If the start and end of solidiﬁ-
cation happens above the metastable line the resulting graphite is lamellar or spheroidal

if nodulising elements such as Mg has been added. If the start is above the metastable
line and the end is below mottled iron is achieved. White iron is achieved if all of the
solidiﬁcation takes place below the metastable line.
One should note that the stable
and metastable lines converges as carbide forming elements are rejected in front of the
solid/liquid interface, increasing the risk of carbide formation at the ned of solidiﬁca-
tion. This knowledge is used to predict the type of iron produced. Often the 1st and
2nd derivatives of the cooling curve are calculated and plotted as these indicates the start
and end of the release of latent heat, e.g. start and end of solidiﬁcation.. The start and
end of solidiﬁcation is found by ﬁnding the local maximum and global minimum of the
2nd derivative, respectively. This is illustrated in Figure 17[30]. The onset of eutectic
solidiﬁcation, TeStart, is found using the tellurium rich cup, as the eutectic undercooling
is inhibited. The eutectic undercooling, TeMin, is the ﬁrst local minimum on the cooling
curve, where TeMax is the second. TeMin and TeMax can easily be found by using the
1st derivative. Furthermore, as other important points on the cooling curve often are
inﬂection points or related to signiﬁcant changes in the release of heat, the derivatives
can be used to ﬁnd these points and temperatures.

7
Discussion
In this section possible explanations for the examined defects are presented. Then the re-
sults are discussed. Finally, corrective actions for Tasso to prevent/reduce the occurrence
of defects are proposed based on the possible causes.
7.1
Possible causes
It is hypothesised the morphology change in sample 1 and sample 4 is due to change in
cooling rate due to the abrupt change. Furthermore, the SEM examination showed no
clear diﬀerences in chemical composition for the diﬀerent matrices. The formation of the
carbide rich morphology is hypothesised to be due to cracking of the shell during pulling
of the strand. If cracking of the shell occurs melt can ﬂow into the newly formed crack.
Heat is then transferred through both the die and the sides of the crack, resulting in a
high cooling rate giving rise to carbide rich morphology. Porosities can then form during
the second solidiﬁcation as feeding of melt into the crack is insuﬃcient. The SEM ex-
amination indicated Na- or Zn-oxides as possible nucleation points for cracks. Another
possible mechanism is cracks forming after movement of the strand, as the melt is unable
to ”weld” onto the solidiﬁed shell. These defects has been observed to appear primarily
at the bottom of strands.
Mean graphite nodule area changes in a single sample are possibly due to change in
cooling rate caused by movement of the strand. Prior to movement of the strand the
solidiﬁcation front are closer to the receiver and therefore experiences low heat extraction.
After movement of the strand, the solidiﬁcation front experiences higher heat extraction
from the die. This increased heat extraction results in greater undercooling, coupling
this with the rate of nucleation in Equation 15 the nucleation rate is increased leading
to more and smaller nodules. This may explain the cracks in Figure 39 and Figure 40 as
the temperature of the melt may be insuﬃcient to weld onto the solidiﬁcation front. This
was also the hypothesises for the crack formation in the above paragraph.
Changes in mean graphite nodule area during production may be explained by the tem-
perature changes in the die in Figure 79. At the start of production the temperature of
the die is elevated leading to lowered cooling rate compared to the end of the production.
This results in larger nodules at the start compared to the end.
The diﬀerences in mean graphite nodule area between top and bottom of a strand is
hypothesised to be caused by either diﬀerences in temperature of the liquid or higher
cooling rate at the bottom due to higher pressure. Flotation of graphite from bottom of
the strand may also be a cause of the diﬀerences.
The cracks in Figure 59 - Figure 71 are believed to be caused by the chunky graphite
found at the cracks. The chunky graphite weakens the solidiﬁed shell and serves as nu-
46
==PAGE:53==

cleation points for cracks. The tension in the strand allow the cracks to propagate. The
cause of the chunky graphite may be due to improper inoculation as explained in Section
4.6 as the inoculation used by Tasso contains both Ce and RE as seen in Table 2 and
Table 3. These defects appears to appear primarily at the top of a strand. This ap-
pears to substantiate the hypothesis as Ce is lighter then Fe, 6.55g/cm3 and 6.98g/cm3,
respectively[36]. Therefore, Ce would tend cluster at the top of strands due to ﬂoating in
the melt. Furthermore, Ce is often found as oxides or sulﬁdes and the compound therefore
even lighter. Another plausible explanation for the cracks being situated at the top is due
ﬂotation of oxide ﬁlms and free graphite. To determine the validity of each hypothesis
SEM examinations close to the cracks should be performed.
The graphite abnormalities seen in Figure 77 and Figure 78 are thought to be graphite
nodules transitioning to compacted graphite or dendrites restricting graphite growth.
Though no defects were found in close proximity to these abnormalities.
7.2
Discussion
In general, the literature reviewed were of limited relevance due to the signiﬁcant diﬀer-
ences between current literature and the setup at Tasso. While the defects were diﬀerent
some of the causes may be related, such as the friction of the die increasing the tension
in the solidiﬁed shell and changes in cooling rate.
As the nature of this project was to examine multiple possible causes for defects the exam-
inations has been mainly qualitative. The qualitative nature of the examinations should
be taken into consideration when making conclusions. Furthermore, the industrial scale
and continuous nature of the process limited the possibility for tightly controlled process
parameters and reproduction of samples and defects.
Material removal from milling and grinding/polishing a sample could be a source of er-
rors or result in data being misleading. Especially as the defects are located near the
edge, material removal could potentially cut out the source of the defect. This could
also aﬀect the mean nodule area as the depth of the investigated plane would diﬀer and
thus inﬂuence the cooling rate at the investigated plane. Therefore, the comparison be-
tween samples may diﬀer if this is taken into consideration. Moreover, the comparison
to the temperature measurements is precarious as the samples are from diﬀerent batches
than the measurements. This problem becomes evident when comparing the temperature
measurements between Figure 79 and Figure 80. Therefore, temperature measurements
should be recorded for the production of the samples examined if they are to be compared.
Furthermore, temperature measurements showing consistently similar temperature curves
should be produced to increase the understanding of the temperature evolution during
production.

The mean nodule area could be inﬂuenced by the constraints imposed during image
analysis. The circularity constraint and the limits for area nodule size are likely to change
the mean. Though the diﬀerences in a single sample are relatively large as the diﬀerences
are between 21%-86%, it appears unlikely to change signiﬁcantly.
The hypothesis for the top containing larger nodules being due to ﬂotation of graphite
from the bottom of the strand, are relatively unlikely with the current information. If
this was the case and no diﬀerence in heat extraction between bottom and top of a strand
existed, one would expect there to be small nodules at the bottom and the top containing
both small and large nodules. Because the small nodules would solidify simultaneously,
while larger nodules at the top of the would be caused by ﬂotation of graphite nodules
from from the middle and bottom. This would result in a small deviation of nodule size
at the bottom and a larger deviation at the top. This does not appear to be the case from
Figure 44 - Figure 53, thus making this hypothesis less likely than the diﬀerence being
due to diﬀerences in cooling rate.
In general, the results of the SEM examination are possibly erroneous to some degree
as seen from the reported carbon concentration. This is especially evident in Table 55
where the carbon concentration in point 10 and 11 changes from 0 wt.% to 32 wt.%. This
should be considered when concluding using information from the SEM. Future SEM
examination may beneﬁt from changing the acceleration voltage, as this may allow for
better detection of carbon.
Though as there were no consistent diﬀerences in the matrices investigated point scans,
it indicates no signiﬁcant amount of solute are rejected to the regions with cracks. This
eliminates another usual crack formation in continuously cast steel[4], namely weakening
due to segregation of P. To exclude the possibility of segregation new SEM measurements
should performed.
The hypothesis of the occurrence of chunky graphite is based solely on the LOM exam-
ination. Therefore, the hypothesis needs to be further substantiated by SEM or other
material characterisations methods. The compacted graphite are unlikely to be the cause
of defects, as no defects were found in close proximity to this graphite morphology. Both
chunky graphite and compacted graphite has been observed to be caused by improper
inoculation[28], suggesting inoculation as the cause of these defects.
Experiments[37]
have shown reduced nodule count to reduce amount of chunky graphite. This could to
some degree explain Tasso’s experience of strands becoming defect prone during produc-
tion due to the nodule count increasing production as seen in Table 7. Though this is
somewhat in contrast to the general notion that increased cooling rate reduces the risk of
chunky graphite, as increased cooling rate would increase the number of graphite nodules
if inoculated properly.
As mentioned in Section 4.6.1 pearlite appears to inhibit the formation of chunky graphite[23],
and thus Tasso’s predictor of pearlite, ”Sumtal”, may thus possibly help predict chunky

The compacted graphite is likely due to over inoculation of spheroidising elements such
as Mg[38]. Furthermore, new research indicates great importance of Ti on compacted
graphite[39].
Restriction of graphite growth could potentially be the cause for the graphite abnormali-
ties seen in Figure 77 and Figure 78, but it does not explain the changes in nodule size and
shape in a single sample. If future examination shows the large nodules are in fact porosi-
ties, this hypothesis becomes more likely. The porosites would be explained by dendrite
arms restricting melt to ﬂow between dendrites. Both the formation of chunky graphite
and compacted graphite could possible be inﬂuenced by ﬂotation of elements, particles
or oxide ﬁlms. This could have deleterious eﬀects on the microstructure if concentration
of carbide, compacted graphite or chunky graphite promoting elements were increased.
This appears as a somewhat likely explanation for the graphite abnormalities in Figure
77 and Figure 78 as there are large diﬀerences in nodule size.
In general, the temperature measurements of the dies are missing information or contain-
ing sudden drops in temperature. The sudden drop in Figure 80 can possibly be due to
loss of contact between the thermocouple and die or due to mishandling of the equipment
and thus more temperature measurements should be performed to establish clear insight
into the temperature changes during production. There are clear diﬀerences between the
two productions as in Figure 79 where the top temperature is recorded lower at the start
whereas in Figure 80 the bottom temperature is the lowest. This is unexpected but may
be explained due to change in the cross section of the die and thus changes to the heat
transfer. It can also be due the placement of the thermocouples. If there are large temper-
ature gradients in the die, small misplacements of the thermocouples can have signiﬁcant
inﬂuence on the temperature measurements.
The diﬀerences in solidiﬁcation time could to some degree be explained by changes in
pouring temperature as seen in Table 12. Further diﬀerences in solidiﬁcation time may
be explained by variations in the chemical composition and amount of melt poured into
the measuring cups. In general, there are multiple issue when attempting to correlate
occurrence of defects to parameters in the production. Firstly, the delay between measur-
ing the liquid and the solidiﬁcation. Attempting to correlate thermal analysis and defects
assumes that the liquid has the same properties when measured and when solidifying.
This assumption may be erroneous due to insuﬃcient mixing between new melt from the
ladle and current melt in the receiver, loss of nucleation sites and loss spheroidisation
ability[25]. Secondly, the defect inspection of strands are limited, resulting in information
is loss as the uninspected strands are unable to be correlated to process parameters. Fi-
nally, as the frequency of measuring varies, changes may be unrecorded and may produce
abrupt changes in the data while in reality the changes are gradual. Furthermore, the
comparison of variations in thermal analysis parameters between multiple batches may
be diﬃcult, as the production time of each batch varies and because chemical changes are

Thermal analysis has been applied in studies to predict the formation of chunky graphite[40].
While some success has been achieved in studies it is unclear if thermal analysis could
be used for predicting chunky graphite formation at Tasso. This is because of the dif-
ferences in cooling rate between the thermal analysis test cups and the cooling in the
die. Furthermore, as mentioned above, the delay between measuring cooling curves and
the solidiﬁcation of the strand and the possible loss nucleation sites and spheroidisation
ability complicates this even further[41]. Therefore, thermal analysis appears unsuitable
to predict these defects in continuous casting.
The wearing of the dies may inﬂuence both cooling rate and friction in the dies. Thus
wear of dies could help explain strands becoming increasingly prone to defects during a
production.
7.3
Corrective action
As there appears to be two distinct defect types diﬀerent corrective measures should be
employed depending on the type. Below the defect types, their cause and possible cor-
rective measure are described.
Failure at solidiﬁcation front
This is likely due to the liquid being unable to weld onto the solidiﬁcation front after
movement of strands. This defect has mainly been located the bottom of a strand and at
drawal marks as seen in Figure 1. This results in weakening of solidiﬁed shell and com-
bined with the tension from moving the strand, cracks can propagate and possible give
rise to porosities. To eliminate or reduce the defect, reduced cooling rate of the strand
and decreased moved distance of the strand, possibly with increased frequency, should be
implemented.
Chunky graphite
This is most likely due to improper inoculation. This defect has mainly been located at
the top of strands. This results in the formation of chunky graphite weakening the shell,
and combined the tension from moving the strand cracks can propagate. Reduction of
added Ce and RE or the addition of ﬂake promoting elements can possibly eliminate this
defect.

8
Conclusion
Defects occurring in continuously cast ductile iron has been examined. The defects has
been examined using light optical microscopy, scanning electron microscopy and image
analysis. Two types of defects were identiﬁed:
1. Failure at solidiﬁcation front, caused by:
• Local cooling rate changes
• Cracking of shell
• Insuﬃcient feeding of melt in crack
2. Chunky graphite, caused by:
• Improper inoculation
• Formation of chunky graphite leading to weakening of the shell
• Crack formation due to tensions in the strands
The thermal analysis techniques were found to be unsuitable for predicting defects due
to delay in measuring and solidiﬁcation of strands. Graphite dies were shown to be worn
during production.
8.1
Future Work
In general, future work should aim at quantifying and substantiating the ﬁndings of this
thesis by using an array of material characterization techniques. Other possible projects
could include:
• Measuring the force required for pulling the strand and the wear of the dies. This
would give indirect information on the friction and forces in the solidiﬁed shell
• Modelling of the ﬂow and temperature of the liquid the receiver to improve the
general understanding of the process and would ease future projects. This could be
extended by modelling the heat extraction of the worn dies
• Modelling of the solidiﬁcation front with pulling of the strand included, this would
assist in ﬁnding and substantiating cause for both current and future defects. This
project would rely on information achieved in the above recommendations
• Investigating slag produced during production and attempt to relate the chemical
composition of the slag to crack initiation zones
• Performing experiments attempting to promote and limit the occurrence of defects
to validate or invalidate the suggested corrective measures
These recommendations will improve the understanding of the interaction between vari-
ations in the production with the ﬁnal product.
