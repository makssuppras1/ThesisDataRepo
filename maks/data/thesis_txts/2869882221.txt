Abstract 
As sustainability gains prominence in the investment landscape, integrating artificial intelli-
gence (AI) into the venture capital (VC) decision-making process presents a promising oppor-
tunity to enhance decision efficiency and optimize investment outcomes. This study investi-
gates the role of AI in supporting VC decision-making specifically in exit strategies for sustain-
able investments. Employing a mixed-methods approach, this research integrates a quantitative 
machine learning (ML) model, trained on Crunchbase sustainable startup data, with qualitative 
insights from expert interviews with venture capitalists (VCs). The ML XGBoost model pre-
dicts exit outcomes, Initial Public Offering (IPO), Merger and Acquisition (M&A), or No Exit, 
based on general startup, financial, intellectual property, sustainability, and computed metrics. 
By comparing the developed ML model’s outcomes with actual decisions made by human VCs, 
the research identifies opportunities for bridging the gap between traditional heuristics and AI-
enhanced exit decision-making. The findings offer actionable insights into how AI can comple-
ment VC expertise, reduce biases, and improve exit decision processes for sustainable startups.

Introduction 
 
In recent years, the landscape of venture capital (VC) has experienced a notable shift toward 
sustainable investments, reflecting a broader societal and economic commitment to environ-
mental responsibility. Between 2017 and 2022, environmental, social, and governance (ESG)1 
investing has grown rapidly (PwC, 2022). Despite market fluctuations, the global ESG assets 
are projected to constitute 21,5% of total global assets under management by 2026 (PwC, 2022), 
assembling to as much as 30 to 40 trillion USD in ESG assets by 2030 (Bloomberg Intelligence, 
2024; Broadridge, 2020). Notably, in 2023, sustainable funds outperformed their traditional 
counterparts, delivering a median return of 12,6% compared to 8,6% for conventional funds 
(IEEFA, 2024). This growth underscores the increasing integration of ESG considerations into 
investment strategies and signals a broader shift toward sustainable finance (Bai et al., 2024; 
Bansal et al., 2024; Dimson et al., 2015).2  
Furthermore, major financial institutions report that sustainable investment demand continues 
to rise, with institutional investors prioritizing ESG-compliant portfolios (Johnson & Lee, 2013; 
Morgan Stanley, 2024).  
Nevertheless, the sustainable investment landscape has faced challenges. In 2022, ESG mutual 
funds and exchange-traded products experienced a 76% decline in net inflows.3 This was driven 
by underperformance relative to traditional funds, rising concerns over greenwashing, political 
and regulatory backlash, and a broader investor shift toward lower-cost passive investment 
strategies (Alaudhli, 2024; Antoncic, 2021; Meziani, 2020; Sinha, 2024). These global and po-
litical shifts have introduced new uncertainties for the sustainability sector. With the return of 
the Trump administration in 2025 concerns raise about the stability of green investments in the 
U.S., as potential rollbacks of key policies such as the Inflation Reduction Act threaten financial 
incentives for renewable energy projects (Chu & Smyth, 2025). As a result, investor confidence 
in the U.S. sustainable market has weakened, prompting some firms to pause or reassess large-

of sustainable investment strategies. As AI increasingly influences VC decision-making, its

Literature Review 
 
This literature review delves into the intersection of VC decision-making and AI, with a specific 
focus on how AI can enhance VC processes, particularly in sustainable investments. Section 
2.1 outlines the foundational principles of VC, exploring its traditional decision-making criteria 
and the evolution of sustainable VC (SVC) in addressing global challenges such as climate 
change. Section 2.2 examines the transformative potential of AI, highlighting ML as a critical 
tool for improving predictive accuracy, minimizing biases, and streamlining decision-making 
processes. Finally, Section 2.3 connects these two domains by analyzing how AI, and ML in 
particular, can transform the VC investment lifecycle, from deal sourcing to exit strategies, by 
integrating financial performance with sustainability metrics into exit choice decision. Collec-
tively, these sections provide a structured framework for understanding how AI integration can 
reshape VC practices in an increasingly data-driven and sustainability-focused investment land-
scape. 
 
2.1  Venture Capital 
This chapter introduces VC as a distinct investment model and highlights its role in fostering 
innovative startups. A detailed definition of VC is provided and its traditional decision-making 
processes explained by examining core evaluation criteria. VC’s innovation and disruptiveness 
are also analyzed in hindsight of sustainable startups. Thus, this chapter sets the foundation for 
understanding how VC combines financial and non-financial metrics for decision-making. 
 
2.1.1  Definition and Background 
Venture Capital (VC). VC refers to the financing of a non-publicly traded company with equity 
capital (Achleitner, 2001). These companies are high-risk innovative firms with high growth 
potential (Gompers & Lerner, 2001; Sahlman, 2022). VC not only bridges the financial gap for 
entrepreneurs but also offers strategic guidance and operational expertise (ibid.; Sapienza, 
1992; Zider, 1998).  
Venture-funded firms are typically young and small, often referred to as innovative startups. 
These businesses face significant uncertainty and a pronounced information asymmetry be-
tween entrepreneurs and their investors (Berger & Udell, 1998; Gompers & Lerner, 2001). VCs 
==PAGE:14==
  5 
 
often serve on boards of directors, take on critical corporate roles within the venture-backed 
companies, or offer essential governance and advisory assistance (Romain & Pottelsberghe, 
2004). 
 
Unlike conventional institutions, VCs provide funding in exchange for equity stakes thereby 
accepting the high risks and uncertainties of early-stage ventures (Hellmann & Puri, 2002; Sahl-
man, 2022). The objective is focused on achieving high returns within specified time horizons. 
They prioritize investments in startups with strong exit potential, aiming for liquidity through 
Mergers and Acquisition (M&A) or Initial Public Offering (IPO) within five to ten years by 
selling the VC’s shares in a venture (Forbes Finance Council, 2024; Gompers & Lerner, 2001; 
MacMillan et al., 2022; Marcus et al.,2013; Robinson, 1987). The likelihood of any single in-
vestment yielding substantial returns is low, leading VCs to adopt a portfolio approach, spread-
ing risk across numerous ventures (J.P. Morgan, 2023). A VC portfolio consists of multiple 
investments spread across startups, industries, and growth stages to manage risk and optimize 
returns. Effective portfolio management is key to maximizing performance, as strategic capital 
allocation, risk diversification, and balancing opportunities contribute to long-term success (Hu, 
2024; Yadav et al., 2024). To meet their return objectives, VCs emphasize strategic selection, 
value creation post-investment, and effective scaling strategies (Li, 2023). These targets typi-
cally range between 25% and 35% over the investment lifecycle when expecting a 10x 
(+1.000%) return on capital within five years for high-performing ventures (Harvard Business 
Review, 1998), This focus on fostering scalable business models highlights the inherent pa-
tience and calculated risk integral to VC investing, with its dual emphasis on achieving financial 
returns and securing equity positions (Gompers & Lerner, 2004). 
 
In the investment context, partnerships are classified as general and limited partnerships. VC 
funds are structured as limited partnerships. General partners manage the funds, while outside 
investors, known as limited partners, provide the capital (Metrick & Yasuda, 2021; Sahlman, 
2022). The alignment of interests is underpinned by equity stakes, which ensure that returns are 
intrinsically tied to the startup's success (Kaplan & Strömberg, 2004). VCs not only provide 
financial support but also offer strategic resources, including mentorship and access to exten-
sive networks, to facilitate the growth of startups (Gompers & Lerner, 2004; Zider, 1998).

2.1.2  Traditional Investment Decision-Making Process and Criteria 
The investment decision-making process of VCs has been extensively studied in entrepreneur-
ship research due to its pivotal role in scaling startups (e.g. Cavallo et al., 2019; Hall & Hofer, 
1993; Roberts, 1991; Wells, 1974; Zacharakis & Meyer, 1998). Therefore, VC decision-making 
revolves around structured, multi-stage processes designed to manage risk and optimize returns 
(Fried & Hisrich, 1994).  
 
The VC decision-making process is inherently multifaceted, consisting of several sequential 
stages: investment deal origination and sourcing, screening, evaluation, structuring, and post-
investment activities, define the activities of a VC (Fried & Hisrich, 1994; Tyebjee & Bruno, 
1984). Each stage integrates specific criteria to optimize outcomes while managing inherent 
risks (Fried & Hisrich, 1994).

increasing maturity after each step.

For every selected investment, VCs typically review hundreds to thousands of submissions 
(Goldfarb et al., 2005; Metrick, 2007). In such environments, decision-makers rely on 
==PAGE:16==
  7 
 
heuristics, intuition, and incomplete data (Baum & Walley, 2003; Eisenhardt, 1989; Zacharakis 
& Meyer, 1998) and investments are often made under conditions of uncertainty and time pres-
sure with limited information (Zacharakis & Shepherd, 2001; Zacharakis & Shepherd, 2007). 
Research highlights that the early stages of VC decision-making, particularly Deal Sourcing 
and Screening, are pivotal to overall fund performance, with these phases collectively contrib-
uting to up to 60% of fund returns (Sørensen, 2007). During these stages, potential investments 
are evaluated against both firm-specific and generic criteria to ensure consistency with the in-
vestment thesis and strategic goals (Retterath, 2020).  
 
Deal Sourcing involves identifying potential investment opportunities through networks, refer-
rals, and industry events (Sorenson & Stuart, 2001; Teten & Farmer, 2010). As Glupker et al. 
(2019) note, strong investor networks are instrumental in sourcing high-quality deals, while 
syndication enhances resource sharing and expertise (Brander et al., 2002). The geographical 
proximity of VCs to startups further influences origination success, fostering stronger relation-
ships and deeper insights into local ecosystems (Sorenson & Stuart, 2001). Additionally, digital 
platforms and AI-driven tools have further enhanced this stage, enabling VCs to streamline their 
search for high-potential ventures (Giannantonio et al., 2022; Rasouli et al., 2023; Röhm et al., 
2022). 
At the Screening stage, VCs narrow down the pool of potential investments by evaluating policy 
variables and startup attributes (Kaplan and Strömberg 2001). This involves assessing the 
startup’s team, product, market potential, financials, and alignment with the VC’s investment 
strategy (Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984).  
During Due Diligence, VCs rigorously assess startups by evaluating their business models, fi-
nancial health, market potential, and team dynamics (Kaplan & Strömberg, 2004). While finan-
cial metrics provide a foundational analysis, qualitative factors such as leadership capabilities 
and team cohesion often play a decisive role (Gompers et al., 2020; Shepherd & Zacharakis, 
2001). These assessments are complemented by criteria used to evaluate new venture proposals, 
such as innovation potential and market scalability (MacMillan et al., 1985). Zacharakis & 
Meyer (2000) highlight that experience and intuition frequently guide VCs in this phase, un-
derscoring the heuristic nature of decision-making.  
Investment Decision involves applying criteria such as market size, scalability, and product dif-
ferentiation. Team quality emerges as a critical factor, with Kaplan et al. (2009) asserting that 
human capital significantly influences venture success. Despite the structured framework, 
==PAGE:17==
  8 
 
biases can affect judgments, as noted by Shepherd & Zacharakis (1999), reflecting the inher-
ently subjective nature of this stage.  
In the Post-Investment Decision Phase, VCs actively engage with portfolio companies (Gom-
pers et al., 2020; Mason & Harrison, 1996). By offering monitoring, mentoring, and strategic 
advisory for the selected startups, VCs help enhance their operational efficiency and increase 
exit probabilities (Brander et al., 2002; Hellmann & Puri, 2002).  
Deal Structuring involves negotiations regarding equity allocation, valuation, and risk mitiga-
tion measures. The proximity of VCs to startups facilitates better structuring outcomes, as close 
interaction fosters trust and transparency (Sorenson & Stuart, 2001).  
Finally, Exit decision marks a defining moment in the VC cycle, which shapes financial returns 
and serves as a key validation of the initial investment thesis (Fried & Hisrich, 1994; Tyebjee 
& Bruno, 1984). Whether through an IPO or a M&A, the outcome is contingent on a set of 
interrelated factors: market conditions, startup maturity, partner selection, and financial struc-
turing. Gompers (1996) highlights that strategic timing and execution during this phase are 
critical to maximizing returns. In periods of strong public market performance, IPOs offer a 
compelling liquidity event, driven by high investor appetite and favorable valuation multiples 
(Kundnani et al., 2024; Shkodina & Kondratenko, 2024). Conversely, economic downturns 
shift focus towards M&A transactions, where strategic buyers or private equity firms provide 
an alternative route to liquidity (Aktas et al., 2018). Moreover, startup maturity further defines 
the exit trajectory. Revenue growth, scalability, and profitability act as key indicators of exit 
readiness (Deias & Magrini, 2023; Golombek & Raknerud, 2012; Holstein, 2018). Startups 
with strong financial fundamentals and predictable cash flows are more likely to succeed in 
public markets, while those demonstrating strategic synergies often become prime acquisition 
targets (Idris, 2024; Smith et al., 2011). Financial buyers, on the other hand, typically pursue 
short- to mid-term value extraction, emphasizing return maximization over operational align-
ment. Lastly, financial structuring completes the equation. Valuation multiples, earn-out mech-
anisms, and secondary transactions determine the final payout (Axelson et al., 2009; Levin, 
2001; Smith, 2005). A well-executed exit strengthens the VC firm’s reputation, reinforcing its 
ability to attract new investors and sustain deal flow (Fedele, 2015; Giannangeli, 2010).  
 
The exit phase marks the final and most decisive stage in the VC investment cycle and directly 
influences fund performance and shaping future investment strategies (Gupta & Arora, 2023; 
Chung et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). A well-executed exit, be it via an IPO or M&A, validates 
the initial investment thesis and maximizes financial returns (Cochrane, 2005; Cotei & Farhat, 
==PAGE:18==
  9 
 
2020; Plagborg-Møller & Holm, 2017). Conversely, a miscalculated exit can lead to suboptimal 
outcomes, limiting potential gains and impacting overall portfolio success (Cochhrane, 2005). 
Given that VC firms operate on a high-risk, high-reward model, exit performance plays a crucial 
role in fund profitability, as a small fraction of successful investments typically offsets broader 
portfolio losses (Gompers & Lerner, 2001; Metrick & Yasuda, 2021). 
Despite its significance, exit decision-making remains one of the most complex and uncertain 
aspects of VC investing (Zacharakis & Shepherd, 2001). Traditional approaches often rely on 
investor experience, qualitative assessments, and incomplete data, introducing inconsistencies 
and biases into the decision-making process (ibid.).  
While extensive research has explored exit strategies in conventional VC investments (e.g. 
Clark et al., 2015; Cohen & Winn, 2007; Kirkwood & Walton, 2014), the role of AI in optimiz-
ing exit decisions remains underexplored, especially in the context of sustainable startups. 
Given the extended investment timelines, regulatory dependencies, and distinct risk profiles 
associated with sustainability-focused ventures (Geobey et al., 2012; Shepherd & Patzelt, 
2011), refining exit strategies is essential for ensuring both financial returns and long-term im-
pact. This gap in existing research serves as the foundation for this study. 
 
Decision-Making Criteria 
The VC decision-making process, spanning from deal sourcing to exit planning, is fundamen-
tally driven by a structured set of evaluation criteria (Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984; Kaplan & 
Strömberg, 2001; Gompers et al., 2020). During Deal Sourcing and Screening, potential invest-
ments are evaluated against both firm-specific and generic criteria to ensure consistency with 
the investment thesis and strategic goals (Retterath, 2020). These criteria form the backbone of 
assessing startups across diverse dimensions.  
 
Researchers have identified several key factors that influence how VCs evaluate startups (e.g. 
Hall & Hofer, 1993; Robinson, 1987; Wells, 1974). These were established through various 
methodologies aimed at uncovering the attributes that characterize startups as promising invest-
ment opportunities.

Despite its effectiveness, the traditional VC model is increasingly scrutinized for its reliance on 
subjective decision-making and limited scalability (Bottazzi & Hellmann, 2008; Zacharakis & 
Shepherd, 2016). Even within these structured frameworks, cognitive biases frequently influ-
ence decision-making (Franke et al., 2006; Ewens & Rhodes-Kropf, 2018; Zacharakis & Meyer, 
1998). Biases such as similarity bias, which leads investors to favor startups resembling previ-
ously successful ones, and availability bias, which overemphasizes readily accessible infor-
mation, often skew evaluations and hinder objectivity (ibid.; Huang & Pearce, 2015). The rise 
of data-driven methodologies, including AI , challenges this paradigm by introducing predictive 
analytics to enhance efficiency and reduce bias (Arroyo et al., 2019; Ross et al., 2021; Vieira, 
2024). 
The Integration of AI in VC will be further explored in chapter 2.3. 
 
2.1.3  The Emergence of Sustainable Venture Capital 
Building on the traditional VC model, Sustainable Venture Capital (SVC) adapts these princi-
ples to prioritize sustainable outcomes to address global challenges such as climate change and 
social inequality (Bocken, 2015). SVC is often referred to as Green VC and represents a signif-
icant shift in the investment landscape (Dhayal et al., 2023).  
While convectional VC prioritizes rapid scalability and financial returns, SVC incorporates 
ESG criteria into its decision-making process for addressing global challenges like climate 
change, resource scarcity, and social inequality (Clark et al., 2015; Cohen & Winn, 2007; Ge-
obey et al., 2012; Shepherd & Patzelt, 2011). SVC aligns closely with Elkington’s (1998) triple 
==PAGE:21==
  12 
 
bottom line framework5. This necessitates the inclusion of complex, non-financial considera-
tions in investment decisions, such as environmental sustainability and social equity (Geobey 
et al., 2012). While the triple bottom line framework provides a comprehensive approach to 
evaluating sustainability, its application in VC remains inconsistent due to the lack of standard-
ized metrics (Lange & Banadaki, 2024). 
 
Sustainable investors employ a variety of strategies to achieve this balance (Bocken, 2015). 
Regulatory initiatives, such as the European Union’s Green Deal, incentivize investments in 
renewable energy, circular economy innovations, and carbon-neutral technologies (Kolk & Van 
Tulder, 2010). Critics argue that the pressure to deliver financial returns within limited time 
horizons often conflicts with the long-term nature of sustainability goals (Bocken, 2015). Sim-
ultaneously, institutional investors are increasingly favoring ESG-compliant portfolios, reflect-
ing a broader societal shift toward sustainable investing (Dimson et al., 2015). In addition, 
growing market demand for sustainable solutions in sectors such as clean technology and sus-
tainable agriculture has further cemented the relevance of SVC (Ghosh & Nanda, 2010). 
 
Despite its potential, SVC faces significant barriers. Startups in sustainable sectors often depend 
heavily on supportive regulatory frameworks and require long gestation periods to achieve prof-
itability, which are factors that clash with the traditional VC model of rapid returns (Bocken, 
2015; Bürer & Wüstenhagen, 2009).  
However, increasing market demand and advancements in sustainable technologies are begin-
ning to alleviate these challenges, creating new opportunities for clean energy startups to attract 
VC (Van den Heuvel & Popp, 2023). By addressing critical global issues through innovative 
products and processes, SVC positions itself as a transformative force in the investment eco-
system (Bocken, 2015). This dual focus on sustainability and financial performance solidifies 
SVC’s role as a vital model for bridging the gap between profit and purpose (Kirkwood & 
Walton, 2014). 
 
SVC itself has evolved through phases such as socially responsible investing, cleantech invest-
ing, and more recently, impact investing (Bocken, 2015). Impact investing emphasizes measur-
able environmental and social benefits alongside financial returns (Johnson & Lee, 2013) and

Commission, 2024a; Kolk & Van Tulder, 2010). Additionally, carbon pricing mechanisms, in-
cluding the Emissions Trading System, enhance the economic feasibility of cleantech startups 
by penalizing high-emission industries while rewarding sustainable innovators (O'Rourke, 
2016). This policy-driven market shift ensures that VC firms integrating sustainability consid-
erations are not only aligning with regulatory frameworks but also capitalizing on high-growth 
sectors with long-term resilience (Bürer & Wüstenhagen, 2009). 
The alignment of SVC with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals has further 
solidified a global benchmark for impact measurement (United Nations, 2015). Additionally,

the Task Force on Climate-Related Financial Disclosures are increasingly adopted by VC 
firms to quantify and standardize the sustainability performance of their investment portfolios 
(Cohen & Winn, 2007). 
 
Regardless of regulatory advancements and growing investor interest, barriers to widespread 
SVC adoption persist. Many early-stage sustainable startups face long return cycles, high cap-
ital intensity, and policy-dependent profitability, making them less attractive to traditional VC 
models that prioritize high-growth and rapid-exit trajectories (Bocken, 2015; Van den Heuvel 
& Popp, 2023). Furthermore, the absence of standardized ESG reporting complicates invest-
ment assessments, leading to difficulties in differentiating authentic impact-driven firms from 
those engaging in greenwashing (Dimson et al., 2015). However, as regulations 
tighten and market demand for sustainability-driven innovation accelerates, SVC is increas-
ingly important to bridging financial performance with long-term impact and reshaping the fu-
ture of VC strategies.  
Despite the growing popularity of sustainable finance, SVC remains underexplored in academic 
literature (Bocken, 2015). Much of the existing literature focuses on broader categories like 
impact investing, overlooking the unique challenges faced by early-stage sustainable startups, 
such as long return cycles and high capital intensity (Bocken, 2015; Van den Heuvel & Popp, 
2023). SVC’s potential as an impactful investment model remains a relatively untapped area of 
study (Juravle & Lewis, 2009). Additionally, the integration of ESG criteria into VC lacks 
standardization, further complicating investment strategies (Van den Heuvel & Popp, 2023).  
 
This research is determined to aid in overcoming this research gap as well as obstacles for 
innovative SVC tools and frameworks by exploring how AI can enhance SVC decision-making.  
 
2.2  Artificial Intelligence 
The progress in computational capabilities, coupled with the growing accessibility of extensive 
datasets and the evolution of advanced algorithms, has positioned AI as a pivotal force driving 
technological innovation across diverse industries (Allam & Rodwal, 2023; Chaudhary et al., 
2024). This chapter introduces AI as a transformative tool in decision-making. It defines AI, 
outlines its evolution into areas like ML, and highlights its impact on industries. As the financial

2.2.1  Definition and Background 
Artificial Intelligence. Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to computational systems designed to 
execute tasks that typically require human intelligence like learning and reasoning (Shapiro, 
2003; Winston, 1992).  
 
The term AI was first coined by John McCarthy in 1956 during the Dartmouth Conference, 
which also laid the foundation for AI research (Cordeschi, 2007). AI's evolution has been 
marked by key milestones, including the rise of expert systems in the 1970s, the resurgence of 
neural networks in the 1980s, and groundbreaking deep learning advancements in the 2010s, 
shaping its current capabilities (Brock & Grad, 2022; Padmaja et al., 2024; Radanliev, 2024). 
Prominent applications of AI include image recognition and natural language processing, which 
underpins artificial assistants like Apple’s Siri and autonomous vehicles, where AI plays a crit-
ical role (Chinnaiyan et al., 2024; Nain et al., 2024). This includes decision-making, pattern 
recognition, and problem-solving (Keding & Meissner, 2021; Dellermann et al., 2017). 
Its adaptability and sophistication in navigating uncertainty distinguishes AI from traditional 
computational approaches (Russell & Norvig, 2016). It is a multidisciplinary field that origi-
nated from efforts to replicate human cognitive processes in machines, combining insights from 
computer science, mathematics, and neuroscience (Shapiro, 2003; Winston, 1992).  
The AI landscape experienced a profound transformation in the early 2020s, driven by the rapid 
advancements in generative AI and large-scale language models, for instance with OpenAI's 
ChatGPT or Alphabet’s BERT (Agrawal et al., 2024; Brynjolfsson et al., 2023; Padmaja et al., 
2024; Radanliev, 2024). Such developments marked a shift from AI as a specialized tool for 
niche applications to a widely integrated technology reshaping entire industries, from finance 
and healthcare to legal services. The ability of AI to automate complex cognitive tasks, enhance 
decision-making, and generate human-like text, code, and designs has led to both enthusiasm 
and scrutiny (Wilson & Daugherty, 2018). Businesses have increasingly adopted AI-driven au-
tomation, leveraging ML models to optimize strategies, streamline operations, and improve 
predictive analytics (Alsheibani et al., 2018; Enholm et al., 2022).  
However, this accelerated adoption has also highlighted critical challenges, including algorith-
mic bias, ethical concerns, regulatory gaps, and the displacement of human expertise (Bender 
==PAGE:25==
  16 
 
et al., 2021). As AI systems become more embedded in decision-making processes, concerns 
regarding transparency, accountability, and reliability grow, particularly in high-stakes areas 
like hiring and lending (Cath, 2024).  
 
In the financial domain, AI’s adaptability and computational capabilities have enabled break-
throughs in risk assessment, fraud detection, and algorithmic trading (Oğuz, 2024; Patil, 2025), 
setting the stage for its transformative potential in VC. Building on this foundational under-
standing of AI, the following section explores its specific applications in decision-making. 
 
2.2.2  Artificial Intelligence for Decision Support 
In the context of decision-making, AI systems are designed to analyze large datasets, identify 
patterns, and derive insights to support or automate decisions (Badmus, 2024; Sarker, 2022). 
They leverage algorithms and computational power to simulate cognitive processes, enabling 
quicker and more accurate analyses than traditional methods (Arroyo et al. 2019; Haefner & 
Morf (2021), Trunk et al. (2020), and Davenport & Ronanki (2018) highlight AI’s growing 
adoption across sectors, where its capabilities automate structured processes and improve deci-
sion quality in less structured environments. By bridging computational analysis with strategic 
insights, AI has become a crucial asset for organizations navigating data-rich decision land-
scapes (Ali, 2024; Mangal et al., 2024). 
 
For example, predictive analytics powered by ML enables VCs to assess startup trajectories 
with precision, offering data-driven clarity where human intuition may fail (Dhochak et al., 
2024; Jain, 2018). Hybrid decision-making, which integrates human judgment with AI-driven 
insights, represents a promising advancement in decision support systems (ibid.; Colson & 
Gross, 2024). Colson (2019) suggests that combining the precision of AI with human intuition 
yields superior outcomes compared to standalone approaches. However, challenges persist in 
achieving widespread adoption. Trust issues, such as algorithm aversion following perceived 
errors, and skepticism about AI's transparency and reliability, remain significant barriers 
(Dietvorst et al., 2015; Logg et al., 2018).  
 
The financial sector, defined by its reliance on data-intensive processes and high-stakes deci-
sion-making, has emerged as a prime domain for AI integration, leveraging predictive models 
and data-driven insights to refine investment strategies and improve operational efficiency

(Morande et al., 2023; Ross et al., 2021; Schroepel, 2022; Schmidt, 2019). By integrating ad-
vanced analytical techniques and diverse data sources, VCs can align their investment strategies 
with both market realities and entrepreneurial potential (Schroepel, 2022; Schmidt, 2019. As 
the investment landscape continues to evolve, AI is set to play a central role in driving innova-
tion and efficiency in VC practices. 
 
2.2.3  Machine Learning and Decision-Making Frameworks 
ML, as a subset of AI, focuses on the development of algorithms that enable systems to learn 
from data and improve their performance without explicit programming (Alpaydin, 2021; 
Shinde & Shah, 2018; Zhou, 2021).  
What distinguishes ML as a subset of AI is its emphasis on learning and adaptation (Wang et 
al., 2023; Zohuri, 2024). While AI broadly encompasses systems designed to mimic human 
cognitive abilities, such as reasoning, problem-solving, and decision-making, ML specifically 
deals with training models to recognize patterns and make data-driven predictions or decisions 
based on those patterns (Marsland, 2014; Goodfellow et al., 2016). In this sense, ML provides 
the foundational methods and techniques that power many of AI’s advanced capabilities, in-
cluding adaptive systems, predictive analytics, and real-time decision support.

vised, and reinforcement learning, and evolutionary learning. Each of them is suited for differ-
ent analytical tasks (Collins et al., 2021; Marsland, 2014; Hastie at al., 2009). Given the targeted 
use of ML in VC decision-making, this paper concentrates on the initial three types of ML.

2.3  Integrating Artificial Intelligence in Venture Capital 
Decision-Making 
Building on the foundational understanding of VC and AI, this chapter explores particularly 
how AI aids in streamlining processes, improving predictive accuracy, and advancing sustain-
ability-focused exit decision-making. It sheds light upon how AI systems analyze financial and 
non-financial metrics to support these decisions.  
 
2.3.1  Background 
Historically, VC decision-making has not only worked with data-based analysis but rather heav-
ily relied on subjective methods such as intuition, personal networks, and heuristic strategies 
(Moritz, 2021; Petty, 2023). While effective in certain instances, these approaches are limited 
by biases, inefficiencies, and the potential to overlook promising startups (Arroyo et al., 2019; 
==PAGE:29==
  20 
 
Jain, 2018; Schmidt, 2019). The increasing complexity of the investment landscape, with its 
growing volume of data and opportunities, necessitates a shift toward more data-driven meth-
odologies (Cao, 2020). 
 
By automating labor-intensive tasks such as initial screening and due diligence, AI frees up VCs 
to focus on strategic activities like fostering relationships and negotiating deals (Davenport & 
Ronanki, 2018). This shift reflects a broader trend of digitalization in financial services, where 
data-driven insights augment intuition as the cornerstone of decision-making (McAfee & 
Brynjolfsson, 2017). It also represents a pivotal advancement in optimizing investment strate-
gies by analyzing historical and real-time data (Badmus et al., 2024; Huang, 2024; Smith et al., 
2019). Dellermann et al. (2017) describe this as a move toward hybrid intelligence, where AI 
augments human decision-making by providing comprehensive analytics and identifying pat-
terns that may be overlooked. The proliferation of startups and emerging markets has signifi-
cantly increased the volume and intricacy of investment opportunities, which surpasses the an-
alytical capacity of traditional methods (Huang & Pearce, 2015).  
 
Recent research underscores the transformative role of AI in VC decision-making, particularly 
in enhancing investment evaluations, optimizing decision processes, and improving predictive 
accuracy (e.g. Morande et al., 2023; Ross et al., 2021; Schroepel, 2022; Schmidt, 2019). ML 
models have shown promise in assessing startup viability and forecasting future performance 
(Domingos, 2015). Similarly, network analysis is gaining traction as a tool for identifying high-
performing investors by mapping investment patterns and collaboration networks to predict 
those most likely to achieve strong returns (Hochberg et al., 2007; Ross et al., 2021) 
Beyond ML, graph theory has emerged as a valuable framework for modeling VC ecosystems. 
It captures the relationships between investors, startups, and funding rounds to identify patterns 
of success and failure (Ross et al., 2021). Expanding on this, predictive models have been ap-
plied to assess investor performance, with Glupker et al. (2019) demonstrating that an investor’s 
position within a funding network significantly correlates with their probability of sustained 
success. Studies by Bubna et al. (2020) and Gupta et al. (2015) further highlight the role of 
proximity to top-tier investors such as super-angels and serial collaborators as a critical deter-
minant of investment outcomes. These models not only track historical performance but also 
pinpoint emerging high-potential investors, offering data-driven insights to refine investment 
strategies (Ross et al., 2021).

Despite these advancements, AI applications in VC remain underutilized, particularly in the 
context of exit decisions. While AI has demonstrated effectiveness in startup evaluation and 
investor performance tracking, its role in forecasting and guiding exits such as IPOs and M&A 
remains largely unexplored. This gap presents an opportunity for this research to determine 
whether AI-driven models can enhance exit predictions and inform long-term investment strat-
egies within the VC landscape. 
 
2.3.2  Investment Process Stages for Integrating Artificial Intelligence  
AI technologies are revolutionizing key stages of the VC decision-making process, ranging 
from deal sourcing and screening to due diligence and post-investment monitoring. Platforms 
like SignalFire’s Beacon exemplify this trend, utilizing AI to analyze millions of data points in 
real time and reducing manual effort (SignalFire, 2025; Trocha, 2019).  In the VC context, AI 
has the potential to optimize key decision-making stages (Jain, 2018; Schroepel, 2022), which 
is explored below. 
 
Firstly, in the process of Deal Sourcing, AI tools act as web scrapers6 and Natural Language 
Processing (NLP)7 algorithms analyze data from online sources, patent databases, and academic 
publications to identify emerging trends and startups (John & Moser, 2024). Platforms like 
Crunchbase, a widely used database for startup funding, investor activity, and market trends 
and USPTO-integrated systems enable VCs to maintain a pipeline of high-potential leads based 
on predefined criteria (Schroepel, 2022; Schmidt, 2019). VC firm using NLP tools can identify 
startups working on climate-tech solutions by analyzing patent filings and media coverage 
(SOURCE). 
 
Furthermore, in the stage of startup evaluation during Screening and Due Diligence, AI algo-
rithms assess startups by analyzing a variety of metrics ranging from financial indicators to 
team dynamics and market positioning. (Morande et al., 2023; Simões et al., 2020).

Supervised learning models predict investment outcomes based on historical data (Zhu, 2024). 
They use labeled datasets, meaning for instance past funding successes and failures are used as 
training examples. Common models include logistic regression, random forests, and support

2.3.4  Enhancing Predictive Accuracy in Startup Evaluation 
AI allows VCs to allocate resources more effectively to high-potential ventures (Huang & 
Pearce, 2015). The utility of these models extends to both startups and VCs. For startups, pre-
dictive insights can guide strategic decisions, streamline resource allocation, and enhance in-
vestor communication. For VCs, AI mitigates risk by identifying high-growth opportunities and 
avoiding ventures with limited scalability (ibid.). This dual benefit underscores the transform-
ative potential of AI in reshaping traditional evaluation methods. 
 
AI’s predictive capabilities enable VCs to allocate resources more effectively. Metrics such as 
financial performance, market traction, and team composition play a pivotal role in evaluating 
startups (Kaplan & Strömberg, 2003). While traditional approaches often emphasize qualitative 
insights, AI introduces quantitative techniques like SMV, gradient-boosted decision trees and 
recurrent neural networks to predict revenue growth and user retention (Goodfellow et al., 
2016). As highlighted by Biswas et al. (2023), ML’s capacity to analyze financial, operational, 
and market-related data has significantly enhanced the accuracy of predictions in VC decisions. 
Metrics like online legitimacy (Antretter et al., 2019), growth potential ranking (Gupta et al., 
==PAGE:35==

2015) and hybrid intelligence models combine ML with human judgment (Dellermann et al., 
2017).  
 
ML models, including random forests and Bayesian approaches, have demonstrated potential 
in predicting startup success (Bhat & Zaelit, 2011; Dixon & Chong, 2014). Advanced predictive 
tools, such as gradient-boosted decision trees, hone efficacy in identifying promising startups 
based on historical data e.g. funding rounds (Sharchilev et al., 2018). Moreover, models such 
as neural networks, SVM, and XGBoost have emerged as MLs for startup evaluation.  
Neural networks excel in identifying non-linear relationships within data, offering insights into 
complex factors such as market scalability and customer growth (Goodfellow et al., 2016). 
SVMs are effective for classification tasks, helping categorize startups into high- and low-po-
tential groups based on predefined benchmarks (Cortes & Vapnik, 1995).  
XGBoost is particularly suited for generating high predictive accuracy, which makes it a pre-
ferred choice for evaluating startups in competitive markets (Chen & Guestrin, 2016).  
 
While ML continues to hold promise for advancing VC decision-making, most of the mentioned 
studies rely on small, specialized datasets, which limit generalizability. Scalable data aggrega-
tion platforms like Crunchbase offer a broader foundation for research, enabling the analysis of 
thousands of startups, though often at the expense of granular detail (Antretter et al., 2019). 
 
Research into these AI tools has demonstrated their capacity to analyze complex datasets, iden-
tifying patterns that inform investment decisions more effectively than traditional methods 
(Trocha, 2019). However, the success of these models relies heavily on the selection of relevant 
variables.  
By incorporating both quantitative and qualitative metrics, AI models can capture the multifac-

2.3.5  Leveraging Machine Learning for Sustainable Startups 
ML enhances the ability of investors to identify early-stage ventures that align with ESG criteria 
(Liang, 2023). by analyzing large datasets and identifying patterns beyond human cognitive 
capacity (Badmus, 2024; Sarker, 2022). SVC aligned MLs demand a nuanced balance between 
financial viability and sustainability metrics (Dellermann et al., 2017; Ross et al., 2021).  
 
Applications of ML in Sustainable VC 
Supervised learning models, including random forests and XGBoost, have proven effective in 
evaluating the potential of startups by analyzing structured data such as team composition, mar-
ket opportunities, and funding histories (Chen & Guestrin, 2016; Ross et al., 2021). These mod-
els not only improve predictive accuracy but also manage imbalanced datasets, a recurring issue 
in evaluating niche markets like sustainable investments. XGBoost, for example, integrates fi-
nancial and non-financial metrics to help prioritize startups in sectors such as renewable energy 
and circular economies (Sharchilev et al., 2018). 
Reinforcement learning further extends ML’s capabilities by optimizing sequential decision-
making. In the context of startup sourcing, these models continuously refine strategies based 
on real-time data, enabling a dynamic and adaptive approach to identifying high-potential ven-
tures (Sutton & Barto, 2018). This adaptability is especially critical in volatile markets where 
regulatory frameworks and consumer preferences frequently evolve (Van den Heuvel & Popp, 
2023). 
Additionally, NLP algorithms expand ML’s utility by extracting insights from unstructured data 
sources, such as news articles, social media posts, and patent filings. Röhm et al. (2022) illus-
trate how ML-enabled tools utilize web scraping and NLP techniques to identify emerging 
trends and innovative startups by analyzing platforms like Crunchbase and LinkedIn (e.g. Xiang 
et al.,2012). By automating labor-intensive tasks, these tools significantly enhance sourcing 
efficiency while providing a broader, data-driven perspective.

Challenges and Gaps 
Despite its promise, the application of ML in VC for sustainable startups, encounters several 
limitations in previous literature.  
 
1. Lack of Standardized ESG Metrics: One significant challenge lies in the lack of standardized 
ESG metrics, which complicates the integration of sustainability considerations into ML mod-
els (Corea, 2019). Without clear, universally accepted ESG criteria, training ML models to pri-
oritize sustainability remains inconsistent and highly dependent on data sources with varying 
degrees of credibility (ibid.) 
 
2. Limited Adoption of XAI for ESG Evaluation: While XAI tools, such as SHAP, offer insights 
into feature importance, their adoption in sustainability-focused ML models remains limited. 
This highlights the need for further research into interpretable AI tailored to ESG fac-
tors (Lundberg & Lee, 2017).  
 
3. Data Sparsity and Incompleteness: Many sustainable startups, particularly in emerging mar-
kets, lack robust financial or operational datasets due to weak regulatory frameworks and in-
consistent reporting standards (Bocken, 2015). Unlike large corporations, early-stage sustaina-
ble startups often do not disclose detailed performance metrics, making it challenging to train 
ML models effectively. Ensemble methods, like random forests, attempt to address this by ag-
gregating diverse data sources, but prediction accuracy remains heavily dependent on data 
quality (Hastie et al., 2009). 
 
4. Overreliance on Traditional Financial Metrics: Existing research on ML applications in 
VC largely focuses on traditional financial indicators, such as revenue growth and funding his-
tory, while neglecting the multidimensional nature of sustainability-oriented investments (An-
tretter et al., 2019; Arroyo et al., 2019). 
 
5. Bias in Training Data: ML models in VC often rely on historical investment data, which 
may reflect biases in past VC decisions. If traditional investors have historically underfunded 
sustainability-focused startups, ML models trained on these datasets may unintentionally rein-
force existing biases, limiting the ability to fairly assess green startups (Cowgill & Tucker, 
2019).  
 
==PAGE:41==
  32 
 
6. Challenges in Real-Time Adaptation: Regulatory frameworks and consumer preferences in 
sustainable industries are constantly evolving (Van den Heuvel & Popp, 2023). ML models, 
especially those based on supervised learning, struggle with adapting to rapid changes, as they 
are trained on historical data that may quickly become outdated. Reinforcement learn-
ing can partially address this challenge by continuously updating its strategies, but its adoption 
in VC is still in its infancy. 
 
7. Computational and Cost Barriers: Training ML models on sustainability-focused investment 
data requires extensive computational resources and high-quality labeled datasets, which are 
often costly and limited in availability. Many VC firms and sustainable investment funds lack 
access to proprietary databases like Crunchbase, which makes data acquisition a signifi-
cant barrier (Schroepel, 2022). Additionally, high-performance computing resources required 
for deep learning or reinforcement learning models may be out of reach for smaller investment 
firms. 
 
8. Scalability and Generalizability Issues: The few studies exploring SVC rely on small, spe-
cialized datasets, which limit the scalability and generalizability of findings (Bocken, 2015; Ju-
ravle & Lewis, 2009). Many ML models trained on a specific region, industry, or investment 
type struggle when applied to new datasets with different characteristics. This creates a re-
search gap in how to develop ML models that generalize across different industries and invest-
ment environments without sacrificing predictive accuracy (Corea, 2019; Dellermann et al., 
2017; Ross et al., 2021). 
 
2.3.6  Exit Decision Modeling with Machine Learning 
Exit decisions play a pivotal role in the VC investment process, marking the culmination of 
value creation through successful exits such as IPOs, acquisitions, or secondary sales (Arroyo 
et al., 2019; Golombek & Raknerud, 2012). These events signify a startup’s ability to meet 
market demands, achieve scalability, and deliver returns to investors (Deias & Magrini, 2023; 
Holstein, 2018). The literature highlights the critical importance of timing and method in deter-
mining the financial outcomes and strategic trajectories for both startups and their investors 
(Gompers, 1996). While substantial research has explored factors driving startup success, the 
role of AI in formulating exit decisions remains underexplored (Huang & Pearce, 2015).

Traditionally, exit strategies in VC have been shaped by experience, prevailing market trends, 
and subjective judgments (MacMillan et al., 1985). However, these methods often suffer from 
cognitive biases and incomplete information, leading to suboptimal decision-making (Zacha-
rakis & Meyer, 2000). As explored in the previous chapter, ML has the potential to transform 
decision-making by analyzing historical data, market conditions, and performance metrics to 
identify patterns and predict optimal outcomes. For example, Ragothaman et al. (2002) utilized 
rule induction in a ML model to predict corporate acquisitions, which showcases how ML can 
support investment decisions by evaluating a company’s stability and growth prospects. Xiang 
et al. (2012) demonstrate the utility of Bayesian networks in predicting acquisitions by combin-
ing 22 company-specific descriptors with external factors such as media coverage. Prepro-
cessing addressed data sparsity and class imbalance using oversampling, increasing the repre-
sentation of successful companies (Antretter et al., 2019). 
Gradient boosting techniques, such as XGBoost, have proven particularly effective in managing 
large, complex datasets, making them suitable for modeling the multifaceted variables associ-
ated with exits (Chen & Guestrin, 2016).  
Similarly, reinforcement learning offers a dynamic solution by continuously adapting to real-
time feedback and market shifts, providing actionable recommendations for optimal exit timing 
and strategy (Sutton & Barto, 2018).  This approach is particularly advantageous for sustainable 
startups, which often operate in fast-changing markets shaped by regulatory developments and 
sustainability trends. Huang et al. (2020) emphasize that reinforcement learning is well-suited 
for investments prioritizing long-term social and environmental returns alongside financial per-
formance. 
 
Overall, these studies highlight the potential of using ML with Crunchbase data for predicting 
early-stage company success. Despite these advancements, the integration of AI into VC exit 
decision processes remains in its infancy (Antretter et al., 2019). Limited access to high-quality, 
comprehensive datasets and skepticism among decision-makers regarding algorithmic reliance 
have constrained its adoption (Dietvorst et al., 2015). This resistance underscores the need for 
more transparent and interpretable AI systems that align with the nuanced and relational nature 
of VC decision-making. 
In this research, ML methodologies are advanced by building a model and addressing the lim-
itations of AI-driven analysis in SVC. As ML is emphasized as key addition for improving 
predictive accuracy in VC decision-making (Arroyo et al, 2019; Choi & Choi, 2012), this will

not only improve the efficiency and accuracy of startup exit choice prediction but also promote 
a more sustainable investment landscape.

Research Methodology  
 
This study employs a mixed-methods approach, integrating qualitative insights from expert in-
terviews with quantitative data analysis to examine AI's role in VC exit decision-making for 
sustainable startups. The research develops and tests an ML model designed to predict the exit 
status of sustainable startups, categorizing outcomes into IPO, M&A, or No Exit (Remain Pri-
vate and Active). 
In addition to the quantitative model, qualitative insights were gathered through standardized 
interviews with eight VCs, providing a comparative perspective on AI-generated exit recom-
mendations versus actual human decisions. The goal was to assess the model’s real-world ap-
plicability in predicting startup exit outcomes, validate key decision criteria, and explore how 
AI can enhance VC decision-making, particularly for sustainable investments. By bridging tra-
ditional heuristics with AI-enhanced decision-making, this study identifies key opportunities 
for improving VC exit decision processes in sustainable startups. The findings offer actionable 
insights into how AI can complement human judgment, refine decision frameworks, and en-
hance predictive accuracy in VC. 
 
3.1  Machine Learning Model Development 
Given the lack of comprehensive and publicly available data on startups, the dataset was man-
ually sourced and curated from Crunchbase. Crunchbase is widely recognized database that 
provides extensive information on startups, high-tech companies and their investors worldwide 
(Ferrati, 2020 Pisoni & Onetti 2018; Żbikowski & Antosiuk 2021). This process involved an 
initial analysis of 950 startups, from which 300 were manually extracted based on predefined 
criteria to ensure relevance and alignment with the study’s focus.  
 
3.1.1 Data Collection 
To maintain the study’s emphasis on sustainability, the filtering process targeted startups 
founded within the past decade and explicitly identified as part of the sustainable industry 
realm. This was achieved by applying a filter on Crunchbase (Crunchbase, 2025) that restricted 
results to startups classified under sustainability-focused categories. These categories were fur-
ther subcategorized into 23 specific areas, including renewable energy, green technology, and 
environmental engineering, among others. The subcategories not only guided the filtering 
==PAGE:45==
  36 
 
process but were later incorporated as input variables for the ML model. This approach ensured 
alignment between the data collection and analytical framework and that the dataset accurately 
reflected the characteristics and priorities of sustainability-oriented startups.  
A 10-year time horizon is commonly used in research to balance the need for recent, relevant 
data with the need to capture sufficient developments and trends (Forbes Finance Council, 
2024; Gompers & Lerner, 2001; Jeng & Wells 2000). This timeframe often aligns with the 
lifecycle of startups, including formation, growth, and financial outcomes, while accommodat-
ing significant progress in sustainability metrics (Busenitz 2005, Gompers & Lerner 2004, 
Popescu & Benetto 2021). This approach tailors the research to the growing prioritization of 
sustainable investments and subsequent exit decision-making, ensuring that both financial per-
formance and environmental impact are thoughtfully considered (Jain et al., 2019, Popescu, 
Hitaj, & Benetto 2021).  
 
The manual extraction process underscored the complexity of gathering holistic data on 
startups, requiring careful attention to detail to preserve the dataset's integrity while meeting 
the objectives of the research. Startups were subsequently categorized based on their exit out-
comes, inspired by a startup-success study conducted by Ross et al. (2021). For this Crunchbase 
classifications were created, which resulted in startups categorized as IPO, M&A, and No Exit. 
Since data availability on Crunchbase was scarce for exit scenarios, this prohibited to further 
differentiate No Exit into categories such as secondary market sales, long-term holding, or piv-
oting and selling shares. In addition, the dataset exhibited significant class imbalance and in-
stances of data incompleteness, which posed challenges for the ML model’s predictive accuracy 
(Emmanuel et al. 2021; Maghsoodi et al., 2023).  
 
To address class imbalance and ensure the dataset's suitability for predictive modeling, an initial 
rebalancing procedure was implemented (Tantithamthavorn et al., 2018). The dataset originally 
exhibited unequal representation across the three exit categories, No Exit, Acquisition, and IPO, 
which posed a risk of biased model predictions. To mitigate this, all variables were reviewed to 
confirm they reflected pre-exit information. Thus, post-exit data points were excluded to main-
tain the study’s focus on predicting outcomes based solely on observable conditions before the 
exit event. Data points with significant missing or incomplete information were also removed 
to preserve dataset integrity.  
Random sampling was then applied to select startups from the remaining pool to ensure equal 
representation across the three categories without skewing feature distributions (Olken,& 
==PAGE:46==
  37 
 
Rotem, 1986). Balancing the dataset was essential to improve the model's generalizability and 
ensure equitable performance across all classes (Chattopadhyay et al., 2020).  
Excluding post-exit variables prevented data leakage, aligning the dataset with the practical 
decision-making context of VCs. By focusing on high-quality, complete data points, the re-
balancing process enhanced the reliability and interpretability of the model’s outcomes. 
 
Eventually, the dataset was restructured to include an even distribution of 100 startups across 
the exit categories, which resulted in a balanced dataset comprising 300 entries that were in 
alignment with the study’s objective of exploring AI-driven exit decision-making. This balanc-
ing step was critical for ensuring that the ML model could effectively learn from and generalize 
across the three exit scenarios (Ross et al., 2021), thereby enhancing the robustness of the 
study’s predictions (Songpan & Kijkasiwat, 2023). 
 
By integrating sustainability considerations into the data sourcing and filtering process, this 
study not only addresses a pressing need for sustainable investment insights but also provides 
a balanced foundation for evaluating the financial and environmental dimensions of startup exit 
decision (Trautwein, 2021).

A significant focus during data collection was placed on maintaining uniformity across varia-
bles, a necessary step to facilitate efficient preprocessing and subsequent analysis (Li et al., 
2023). To ensure the dataset’s quality and reliability, comprehensive data cleaning procedures 
were undertaken. Such preprocessing steps are critical for ML models, as inconsistencies can 
significantly affect predictive accuracy (Han et al., 2011; Bento, 2017). This process involved 
the translation of textual data into numeric formats to enhance compatibility with ML algo-
rithms (ibid.). Numeric columns initially stored as strings were systematically converted, ad-
dressing formatting inconsistencies that could otherwise impede model training. Missing values 
were managed using imputation techniques suited to the characteristics of each variable, which 
ensures the completeness and coherence of the dataset (Lee & Geum, 2023).  
 
For discrete variables such as the number of founders, mode imputation was used. This ap-
proach replaces missing values with the most common value in the column, reflecting the nat-
ural clustering tendency of this variable. By doing so, the integrity of the data was preserved 
without introducing distortions. 
 
For numerical variables with skewed distributions, median imputation was applied (Donders et 
al., 2006; Zhang, 2016). This method was particularly effective for variables like the number 
of employees, number of investors, and the number of funding rounds. Employee counts, for 
instance, are often influenced by outliers, and the median provides a robust central measure that 
minimizes the impact of extreme values (Reiss & Reiss 1997). Similarly, for investor counts 
and funding rounds, the ordinal nature and potential skew of the data made the median a reliable 
choice for maintaining consistency (Han et al., 2011).  
 
Continuous variables with broad variability, such as last funding amount and total funding in 
USD, were addressed using mean imputation (Donders et al., 2006). These variables often ex-
hibit significant spread, and the mean offered a balanced representation of their central tendency 
(Donders et al., 2006). Here, mean imputation was chosen for its ability to approximate the 
overall distribution while maintaining analytical coherence (Hain & Jurowetzki, 2020; Li et al., 
2023). However, for exit valuation during IPOs, where the data was highly skewed with many 
missing or zero values, median imputation was deemed more appropriate. This method captured 
the central tendency more accurately under these conditions, avoiding the distortion that might 
arise from using the mean (Li et al., 2023).

Secondly, the data was standardized using z-scores to ensure uniform scaling, a critical step for 
improving model performance (Templeton, 2011). Additionally, data was reviewed for incon-
sistencies, ensuring all variables were compatible with selected ML models (Hastie et al., 2009). 
This cleaning process significantly mitigated data-related biases, which ensures robust input 
quality for subsequent modeling (Li et al. 2021).  
Moreover, feature engineering enhanced the predictive power of the dataset. Categorical varia-

3.1.3 Machine Learning Framework 
This chapter explores six ML models called, CatBoost, Gradient Boosting, LightGBM, Random 
Forest, XGBoost, and Stacking Ensemble to identify the most effective approach for predicting 
exit outcomes. Thus, these ML models were compared for predictive performance. 
 
CatBoost: As the name implies, CatBoost is characterized by two key features, which are its 
ability to handle categorical data (“Cat") and its use of gradient boosting ("Boost"). It is a gra-
dient boosting algorithm uniquely designed to process categorical variables efficiently, without 
requiring extensive preprocessing (Ross et al., 2021). Its key advantage lies in minimizing over-
fitting while expediting computations further enhances its utility in analyzing large datasets 
(Dorogush et al., 2018).  
 
Gradient Boosting: This ensemble algorithm is building predictive models by correcting the 
residual errors of previous iterations. Gradient Boosting can seamlessly integrate quantitative 
metrics with qualitative assessments, which provides a comprehensive evaluation framework 
(Friedman, 2001).  
 
LightGBM: This is a gradient boosting framework optimized for speed and memory efficiency, 
making it ideal for high-dimensional datasets. LightGBM stands for both "Light" in terms of 
==PAGE:53==
  44 
 
memory usage and computational speed while utilizing a Gradient Boosting Machine 
(“GBM”). Its ability to handle categorical features and missing values efficiently supports rapid 
decision-making tasks. However, its sensitivity to imbalanced datasets may require additional 
preprocessing steps to ensure robust performance (Ke et al., 2017; Ross et al., 2021). 
 
Random Forest: Random Forest is an ensemble-based algorithm that constructs multiple deci-
sion trees and aggregates their outputs to enhance predictive accuracy (Breiman, 2001). It is 
popular for handling high-dimensional data but is susceptible to overfitting (Ho, 1995). 
 
XGBoost: XGBoost is a high-performance gradient boosting algorithm that stands out for its 
speed, scalability, and accuracy. Hence, its name stands for Extreme Gradient Boosting. 
Through its regularization techniques, XGBoost effectively mitigates overfitting while main-
taining predictive robustness (Chen & Guestrin, 2016). It also creates multiple decision tress, 
similar to Random Forests, but uses gradient boosting for fitting new tree models as a correction 
of errors in previous tree models (Friedman, 2001; Ross et al., 2021).  
 
Stacking Ensemble: Stacking is a meta-modeling technique that combines predictions from 
multiple base models, such as Random Forest and Gradient Boosting, to achieve improved ac-
curacy and generalization. By leveraging a meta-model to learn from the outputs of these base 
models, stacking integrates their strengths to deliver a balanced and robust prediction frame-
work (Wolpert, 1992). 
 
Cross-Validation: Although not a model itself, cross-validation is a statistical evaluation 
method crucial for ensuring model robustness. By dividing the dataset into multiple training 
and testing subsets, cross-validation assesses a model’s ability to generalize across unseen data 
(Kohavi, 1995).

ensured that the class distribution was preserved within each fold, resulting in a robust assess-
ment of the model’s generalizability while reducing the risk of overfitting. Following 50 trials,

deep to model complex interactions while maintaining a level of regularization to mitigate over-

domness that helps to reduce variance and overfitting. By ensuring that each tree sees a slightly 
different subset of the data, the model is less likely to rely too heavily on specific data points.  
Finally, the colsample_bytree parameter specifies the fraction of features randomly selected for

5.1  Theoretical Contributions 
In the XGBoost model feature importance the employee size, geographic location, and sustain-
ability focus emerge as critical predictors of exit, which is reinforcing existing literature on VC 
investment strategies. The findings also confirm the importance of late-stage funding and fi-
nancial performance metrics while raising questions about potential biases in AI-driven predic-
tive models. 
 
Firstly, the analysis indicated that employee size, particularly the employee_11_to_50 category, 
is the most influential predictor in the exit decision model. This aligns with previous research 
on startup lifecycle stages (Gompers & Lerner, 2001), which suggests that firms in this size 
bracket are often in the transition phase from early-stage innovation to operational scaling. This 
phase is characterized by an increase in resource allocation, improved financial stability, and 
market expansion, all of which are critical factors for VC investors seeking promising exit op-
portunities (Kaplan & Strömberg, 2004). Hence, a startup that has 11-50 employees is like to 
decide for exit as it has reached a suitable startup stage and size. Interestingly, the employee 
size ranges are not assorted in linear order. This could indicate that the variable 51-100 employ-
ees ranks lowest due to the fact that 11-50 employees has the highest importance. Meaning that 
most startups exit at that point, which creates a vacuum effect so that 51-100 employee compa-
nies are more likely to exit after scaling more and hiring more staff, eventually reaching more 
than 100 employees. 
However, the prominence of employee size raises questions about potential biases in the deci-
sion-making process. While larger teams signal operational maturity, they do not necessarily 
correlate with higher innovation potential (Colombo & Grilli, 2005) and exit decision. This 
==PAGE:79==

correlates with findings from Zacharakis & Shepherd (2001), who highlight that smaller, highly 
skilled teams can outperform larger ones due to agility and specialized expertise and explains 
why the first two size ranges end up being the most important ones. Moreover, traditional VC 
decision frameworks, as outlined by Tyebjee & Bruno (1984), emphasize qualitative aspects 
such as founder experience and adaptability, which are not directly captured in employee count 
metrics. Future research could explore how team composition, rather than absolute size, influ-
ences exit outcomes, addressing gaps in the predictive power of size-based models. 
 
Secondly, in terms of geographic headquarters regions hq_region_5 is potentially ranking so 
high as it is strongly associated with "No Exit" scenarios, indicating a negative correlation and

emerging markets offer untapped growth potential (Bruton et al., 2005), the dataset indicates 
that such potential has yet to translate into frequent exit events. This underscores the need for 
tailored VC strategies that account for the specific risks and opportunities present in these re-
gions. Further research could investigate whether alternative financing mechanisms, such as 
blended finance or government-backed investment initiatives, could mitigate the barriers to 
successful exits in emerging markets. 
On the other hand, hq_region_2 and hq_region_3 potentially demonstrate positive correlations

literature suggesting that regulatory support can significantly impact the availability of funding 
and investment outcomes (Bocken, 2015), thus impacting decision-making leaning toward exit.

& Hisrich, 1994; Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984; Levin, 2001; Smith, 2005) and newer research on 
exit indicators (e.g. Deias & Magrini, 2023; Golombek & Raknerud, 2012; Holstein, 2018). VC 
C suggested that AI-driven exit models should incorporate growth proxies like full-time em-

developments influence the VC world, which was highlighted in the introduction (e.g. An-
toncic, 2021; Meziani, 2020; Chu & Smyth, 2025). 
Secondly, literature suggests that IPOs are generally preferred due to their potential for higher 
valuations (Dellermann et al., 2017). However, this study finds that M&A exits are more com-
mon in practice of early-stage VCs. This shows that market volatility and liquidity constraints 
often make IPOs riskier and less predictable, pushing investors toward acquisition-based exits 
(Aktas et al., 2018; Brau et al., 2024). This discrepancy highlights the gap between theoretical 
preferences and the realities of VC investment strategies. 
While AI is frequently positioned as a transformative tool in financial decision-making (Wei et 
al., 2009), its adoption within VC remains limited. AI is recognized for its strengths in pattern 
recognition and data analysis, yet investors continue to rely heavily on experiential judgment, 
which VC A, E, and D pointed out during the interview. This suggests that AI-driven decision 
models must become more interpretable and aligned with investor heuristics to gain broader 
industry acceptance.

5.3  Limitations 
While the integration of AI into VC decision-making presents significant potential for exit pre-
dictions, this research is subject to several limitations. These challenges stem from constraints 
in a) data availability, b) ML model: scope, interpretability, and generalization, c) interview: 
sample size, cognitive bias, and time horizon, d) ethical and regulatory challenges as well as e) 
limited consideration of market volatility and external factors. Addressing these obstacles is 
crucial to refining AI models and ensuring their practical applicability in SVC exit decision-
making. 
 
a) Data Availability 
One of the primary limitations in AI-driven VC exit modeling is the restricted access to high-
quality, granular investment data. The VC industry operates within a private ecosystem, where 
crucial information on funding rounds, financial data, investor participation, and post-exit fi-
nancial performance is often locked within proprietary databases (e.g. Kaplan & Lernen, 2016; 
Retterath & Braun, 2020). Attempts to obtain historical founding and exit data directly from 
VC firms proved largely unsuccessful, as firms were unwilling to disclose proprietary infor-
mation. Additionally, academic institutional access to relevant databases was also unavailable, 
which restricted the research to publicly accessible records obtained from Crunchbase. How-
ever, even within this platform, key financial metrics were often incomplete, limiting the inclu-
sion of relevant predictors in the model. As a result, this study faced challenges in securing 
enough well-documented startups across exit types. Despite efforts to extend the research 
timeframe, startups with available data remained heavily skewed toward those that were still 
actively fundraising.  
The inherent imbalance in VC exit outcomes presents a critical challenge for ML models. Suc-
cessful exits, such as IPO and M&A, occur significantly less frequently than failed ventures 
(e.g. Eisenmann, 2021; Krishna et al., 2016), leading to an overrepresentation of "No Exit" 
cases. Standard ML models tend to favor majority classes, increasing the risk of misclassifying 
high-potential exits. To address this, the dataset was manually balanced to include equal repre-
sentations of 100 cases per exit type (IPO, M&A, and No Exit) before training the model. How-
ever, discrepancies remained, as startups without exits generally had more extensive data 
==PAGE:88==
  79 
 
available, reflecting their ongoing efforts to attract investment. In contrast, post-exit companies 
had fewer publicly accessible metrics, reducing the ability to capture key financial and opera-
tional trends.  
Additionally, historical investment trends embedded in training data may introduce biases, re-
inforcing past investor preferences toward certain sectors, geographies, or founder de-
mographics (e.g. Franke et al., 2006; Ewens & Rhodes-Kropf, 2018). If AI models rely solely 
on these historical patterns, they risk perpetuating systemic exit decision biases rather than cor-
recting them. Future research should incorporate more aware ML methodologies, such as bias 
detection and correction mechanisms, to ensure equitable exit decision-making. 
 
b) ML Model: Scope, Interpretability, and Generalization  
Scope: While a more comprehensive AI-driven approach was initially considered, limitations 
in the mentioned data availability and resources necessitated a shift towards a ML model as a 
subset of AI. Unavailability of API keys and frameworks such as LangGraph10 further drove 
the implementation of a self-coded ML model. Further, the iterative nature of model develop-
ment required multiple adjustments, reducing the available timeframe for expansion. As a re-
sult, the final model focuses on exit decision-making rather than encompassing both initial in-
vestment decisions and exit predictions. To extend the model’s applicability, an additional layer 
analyzing initial investment decision-making and its correlation with exit likelihood would need 
to be incorporated. This integration would create a more holistic decision-support framework 
for VC investments. However, the required time and computational resources exceeded the 
scope of this study, making such an expansion infeasible within the given research constraints. 
Future work should explore the integration of multiple ML models or Graph Agents11 to capture 
the full VC decision-making process to ultimately reflect a more robust and comprehensive 
analysis. 
Interpretability: The opacity of the ML model remains a significant barrier to adoption in VC. 
While models such as XGBoost provide strong predictive accuracy, their decision processes

are inherently complex, making it difficult for investors to trust AI-generated recommendations 
without clear justification. Although this research incorporates SHAP to enhance interpretabil-
ity, AI-driven VC decisions remain challenging to fully explain in traditional investment frame-
works. Explainability is particularly critical in financial exit decision-making, where transpar-
ency is essential for investor confidence and regulatory compliance (e.g. Schmidt, 2019; 
Schroepel, 2022). AI models must offer clear justifications for why specific exit pathways are 
favored over others. The integration of XAI frameworks into VC applications could provide 
more interpretable decision pathways, aligning AI recommendations with industry expectations

Generalization: AI models trained on specific VC datasets may not generalize effectively 
across different investment stages or geographic markets. VC ecosystems vary significantly 
across regions due to differences in regulatory environments, economic structures, and investor 
behaviors. AI models trained on European or North American datasets may not be directly 
transferable to emerging markets, where exit strategies are shaped by unique economic condi-

hubs, while M&A may dominate in regions with limited public capital markets. 
Future research should explore transfer learning techniques that allow AI models to adapt to 
regional variations, fine-tuning investment predictions for different geographies. Expanding 
training datasets to include diverse VC markets could further enhance the global applicability 
of AI-driven investment models. 
 
c) Interview: Sample Size, Cognitive Bias, and Time Horizon 
Sample Size: Beyond the challenges posed by data constraints, the qualitative component of 
this study, expert interviews with VCs, introduced additional limitations. The interview sample 
was relatively small, consisting of eight VC professionals primarily focused on early-stage, 
European, and sustainable VCs. This limited the diversity of perspectives across geographies, 
firm sizes, and sector specializations. Additionally, many interviewees had prior exposure to 
AI applications in investment decision-making, potentially skewing responses toward a more 
optimistic outlook, while AI skeptics and non-technical investors were underrepresented. Given 
that VCs operate with distinct investment theses, risk tolerances, and exit strategies, their per-
spectives on AI’s role in decision-making varied accordingly. Notably, the overrepresentation 
of early-stage investors, who assess AI differently than late-stage investors, may have led to 
inconsistent perceptions regarding AI’s effectiveness in exit decision-making.

Cognitive Bias: Another limitation stems from the reliance on self-reported experiences, which 
introduces cognitive biases such as hindsight and selection bias (e.g. Ewens & Rhodes-Kropf, 
2018; Zacharakis & Meyer, 1998). Interviewees may have unconsciously emphasized success-
ful exits while overlooking failures, shaping a narrative that favors AI’s role in optimizing de-
cision-making. Moreover, some VCs may have overestimated their ability to make rational (e.g. 
Logg et al., 2018; Zacharakis & Shepherd, 2001), data-driven investment choices, underreport-
ing the influence of intuition and heuristics in real-world decision-making. These biases com-
plicate the comparison between AI-generated exit predictions and actual investor behavior, lim-
iting the study’s ability to assess AI’s practical impact on exit strategies for sustainable startups. 
Since AI-driven exit decision-making remains an emerging field, many interviewees lacked 
direct experience with such models, meaning discussions were largely theoretical rather than 
grounded in real-world applications. 
Time Horizon: This study does not capture long-term insights into AI adoption in VC. Since 
the research reflects a single moment in time, it does not account for the evolving nature of AI 
technologies and their integration into investment decision-making. As AI capabilities, partic-
ularly in XAI and predictive analytics continue to advance, shifts in dataset availability, regu-
latory frameworks, and real-time market analysis could reshape investor perspectives. A longi-
tudinal study tracking how VCs’ attitudes toward AI evolve over time would offer deeper in-
sights into its adoption trajectory and practical impact on VC decision-making. 
 
d) Ethical and Regulatory Challenges 
The integration of AI in VC decision-making raises ethical and regulatory concerns that remain 
largely unresolved. Algorithmic biases, potential discrimination in funding allocation, and the 
lack of transparency in AI-generated decisions present significant risks. Investors must navigate 
evolving compliance landscapes, particularly in jurisdictions with stringent AI governance 
frameworks.  
Moreover, the increasing reliance on AI-driven decision-making raises questions about ac-
countability and fiduciary responsibility in investment strategies. Without clear oversight, AI 
models could introduce unintended biases that disadvantage underrepresented founders or in-
dustries. Regulators are beginning to scrutinize automated financial decision-making, under-
scoring the need for governance structures that ensure ethical AI deployment in VC. Future 
research should explore AI governance models that incorporate regulatory feedback loops to 
align AI adoption with industry best practices and legal requirements.

e) Limited Consideration of Market Volatility and External Factors 
While AI models can effectively analyze historical trends, they struggle to capture the impact 
of macroeconomic shifts, geopolitical risks, and industry-specific disruptions that influence VC 
exit decisions. Market conditions such as interest rate fluctuations, inflation trends, and global 
economic downturns play a crucial role in shaping exit decision factors that traditional ML 
models cannot dynamically adjust for without continuous real-time data integration. 
AI-driven VC models also operate under the assumption of relative market stability, yet venture 
investments are highly susceptible to sudden external shocks. The unpredictability of techno-
logical disruptions, regulatory changes, and economic crises can significantly alter exit proba-
bilities, limiting the reliability of static AI exit models. 
A potential solution lies in reinforcement learning techniques, where AI systems continuously 
update predictions based on real-time market signals. This approach could enhance model 
adaptability, allowing AI-driven VC decision-making to better account for economic volatility 
and evolving industry dynamics. Future research should explore the integration of dynamic AI 
modeling frameworks that incorporate external market indicators to refine exit predictions. 
 
Despite the potential of AI to enhance VC decision-making, several limitations must be ad-
dressed to ensure reliable, ethical, and scalable applications. Data constraints, model interpret-
ability, class imbalances, and external market dependencies present ongoing challenges. Nev-
ertheless, advancements in fairness-aware AI, explainable ML frameworks, and adaptive mod-
eling techniques can help mitigate these issues, paving the way for AI-driven VC strategies that 
combine data-driven insights with human expertise. Future research should focus on refining 
AI methodologies to overcome these barriers, ensuring AI's role as a transformative force in 
VC exit decision-making. 
 
5.4  Future Perspectives  
The practical contributions discussed here not only extend the scope of existing literature but 
also open new avenues for future research. The integration of AI in VC decision-making chal-
lenges traditional paradigms, shifting the focus more data-driven strategies. This shift necessi-
tates further exploration of hybrid intelligence models, where AI complements human exper-
tise.  
Additionally, the findings highlight the importance of incorporating financial metrics into in-
vestment evaluations as well as more qualitative information. Future studies could build upon 
==PAGE:92==

this research by exploring the interplay between AI, sustainability, and financial performance 
in greater depth. Such studies would contribute to a more nuanced understanding of how tech-
nology can drive systemic change in the exit decision-making landscape. In that sense, ML-
driven decision models could intertwine sustainability and finance more effectively so that not 
only exit decision, but also initial investment research could benefit from AI models in decision-
making. For instance, predictive analytics could be used to quantify the long-term financial 
benefits of sustainable investments and thereby address the current disconnect between short-
term exit pressures and long-term impact considerations (e.g. Bocken, 2015; Bürer & Wüsten-
hagen, 2009). 
The insights from VCs provide a strong foundation for refining AI-driven decision models in 
VC. Expanding exit classifications, incorporating financial and qualitative variables, and inte-
grating macroeconomic data will enhance predictive precision. Developing interactive, sce-
nario-based decision tools could further bridge the gap between data-driven insights and the 
intuitive decision-making processes that dominate the VC industry. Moreover, the integration 
of an ML model like the coded XGBoost into exit decision-making introduces a new dimension 
of risk management. For instance, by simulating multiple exit scenarios, ML models could help 
investors assess the viability of different strategies, including timing for exits, reinvestment, or 
strategic pivots based on shifting market conditions. This ability to adapt dynamically to evolv-
ing investment landscapes is particularly relevant for sustainable startups, which often operate 
in changing regulatory environments. Such practical contributions not only position AI as a 
pivotal tool in modern exit strategies but also underscore the evolving role of VC in driving 
innovation and impact. Rather than replacing human expertise, AI should function as an aiding 
or augmentation tool, which offers data-backed insights that align with investor strategies. 
These refinements will ensure AI-driven investment models remain practical, adaptable, and 
aligned with real-world VC dynamics to ultimately strengthen exit decision-making in both 
traditional and sustainable investment contexts. 
 
While AI-driven decision models offer substantial advantages for VCs, their potential extends 
beyond investors to entrepreneurs and acquirers. For entrepreneurs, AI can serve as a strategic 
tool, providing real-time feedback on funding probabilities, optimizing business decisions, and 
identifying the most viable exit pathways. By analyzing market trends, funding dynamics, and 
competitor trajectories (e.g Morande et al., 2023; Kaplan & Strömberg, 2004; Simões et al., 
2020), AI models can help startups align their strategies with investor expectations, enhancing 
their attractiveness for funding. For acquirers such as private equity firms and corporate

investors AI-powered models offer a structured approach to evaluating potential acquisition 
targets. ML algorithms can assess sustainability impact, financial health, and market position-
ing and help in streamlining due diligence to uncover undervalued companies with strong 
growth potential. Furthermore, AI simulations of exit scenarios can refine acquisition strategies 
by determining optimal deal timing, evaluating financial implications, and identifying risks as-
sociated with market volatility. This is particularly relevant in sustainable investment markets, 
where regulatory shifts and ESG compliance heavily influence acquisition decisions. 
By extending AI’s role beyond VC firms, its insights can contribute to a more transparent and 
data-driven investment ecosystem, where startups, investors, and acquirers collectively benefit 
from enhanced strategic foresight and decision-making capabilities.  
 
These insights highlight the potential for AI to serve as both a decision-enhancing and a deci-
sion-challenging tool within VC. This points toward a future where VC exit decision-making is 
increasingly characterized by AI-human collaboration. Rather than simply automating existing 
frameworks, AI offers the opportunity to refine and recalibrate traditional heuristics, ultimately 
leading to a more data-driven, transparent, and sustainability-conscious investment ecosystem. 
This not only improves risk-adjusted returns for investors but also empowers entrepreneurs with 
data-backed pathways for scaling sustainable startups. Future research should explore how AI-
driven decision models can be refined to support all stakeholders in the investment lifecycle to 
foster a more resilient and impact-oriented financial landscape.

Conclusion 
 
This research highlighted the transformative role of AI in VC for improving exit decision-mak-
ing for sustainable startups. The ML model demonstrated strong alignment with established VC 
practices, reinforcing its validity in supporting startup exit decisions.  
 
Notably, its predictive accuracy surpassed conventional human-driven approaches, which un-
derscores AI’s potential to enhance exit decision-making with data-driven insights. 
Beyond predictive accuracy, this research also underscores AI’s ability to enhance decision-
making transparency by providing structured, data-driven insights for exit choices. Unlike tra-
ditional VC methods, which often rely on intuition, network effects, and experience-driven heu-
ristics, AI offers a systematic approach to decide about exit. The use of SHAP values in this 
study illustrated how ML models can quantify and visualize the importance of individual deci-
sion criteria. Thereby increasing interpretability and allowing VCs to better understand the ra-
tionale behind AI-generated exit decision suggestions.  
This transparency is crucial in sustainable investing, where ESG metrics are building a key 
component of investment lifecycles. As ESG metrics become more standardized, especially 
with evolving EU regulatory requirements, this study provided a foundation for incorporating 
sustainability considerations into AI-supported exit decisions. It serves as a steppingstone for 
future studies into bridging traditional as well as impact-related exit criteria and ML models. 
Therefore, upcoming research should deepen this exploration by integrating advanced financial 
and ESG indicators into AI to ultimately enhance predictive capabilities and boosting investor 
confidence in cleantech startups and SVC. 
Additionally, the findings revealed discrepancies between AI-generated rankings of decision 
factors and those traditionally emphasized by VCs. While financial metrics such as funding 
stage, amount, and velocity remained significant, they did not dominate the model’s predictions 
as expected by VCs. Instead, factors such as employees, headquarters region, and sustainable 
industry played a larger role in AI-driven recommendations. This divergence suggests that AI 
either requires more financial data to assort criteria more accurately or has the potential to un-
cover alternative exit choice indicators that may be undervalued in conventional VC exit frame-
works. By incorporating these insights, VCs could refine their investment selection and time 
exit strategies to maximize both financial returns and long-term sustainability impact. Also, 
==PAGE:95==
  86 
 
entrepreneurs and acquirers can benefit from AI simulations of exit scenarios or general AI 
application to refine their exit strategies. 
 
Ultimately, the integration of AI into VC decision-making is not just an opportunity but a ne-
cessity to keep up with rapid SVC growth and technological advancements. Rather than replac-
ing human expertise, AI serves as a catalyst for more strategic, scalable, and responsible invest-
ment practices. Aligning AI-powered models with global sustainability goals, such as net-zero 
targets and climate innovation, will reshape the investment landscape and accelerate the transi-
tion toward a more efficient, data-driven, and impact-focused VC ecosystem. The future of VC 
is not solely human or artificial, but a powerful fusion of both by driving innovation and sus-
tainability hand in hand.
