Abstract
3
Abstract
Globally, more than 45 million people suﬀer from dementia, and this number is expected
to reach 130 million by 2050 [Guo et al., 2017]. Parkinson’s disease (PD), Multiple system
atrophy (MSA), and Alzheimer’s disease (AD) are movement disorders that involve the
accumulation and aggregation of proteins such as α-synuclein (α-syn), Tau and Amyloid-
β(Aβ) in neurons. PD and MSA are characterized by the aggregation of α-syn, whereas the
aggregation of Aβ and Tau characterizes AD patients. Furthermore, is likely that speciﬁc
protein aggregation contributes to the symptomatology of the diseases, in some cases leading
to balance, movement and autonomic function impairments. It is of current research interest
to understand α-syn, Tau, and Aβ’s pathological development. An immunoassay, such as
mesoscale discovery assay (MSD), its often required to detect natural autoantibody (nAb) in
plasma. A nAb is a component of the human immunoglobulin repertoire that recognizes self-
or altered self-antigens. These antibodies have the housekeeping function of phagocytosing
pathological proteins and inhibiting their aggregation.
Assays are most valuable in the research environment when they yield reliable results for
low costs in a short amount of time.
The current work outlines a protocol to develop
MSD based immunoassay for nAbs using mouse anti-human antibodies to achieve this goal.
The guidelines for MSD based immunoassay development guidelines include choosing the
correct plates, blocking buﬀers, and detection systems as well as the correct concentrations
of antigens, primary antibodies, and secondary antibodies.
Additionally, the following text contains a rapid MSD protocol that allows the measurement
of nAbs in a reduced amount of time by using a MSD instrument. Following the guidelines
presented, a cost-eﬀective nAb MSD that detect a broad spectrum of antibody concentrations
can be developed with minimal background interference, allowing it to be used for research.

4.1
Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay versus Meso-Scale Dis-
covery assay
Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) is a quantitative immunological assay, de-
tecting and quantifying the presence of speciﬁc components, mostly protein components in
biological samples. ELISA assays are usually performed in polystyrene multi-well microtiter
plates in 96-well or 384-well so that dilution of serums can be easily prepared and tested.
There are three common types of ELISAs: direct ELISA, indirect ELISA, and sandwich
ELISA. This report focuses on indirect ELISA
4.1.1
Principle of indirect Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay
Indirect ELISA is the most widely used ELISA format.
It is used for the detection of
speciﬁc antibodies in a serum sample. A known antigen is immobilized on the surface of the
microtiter wells, followed by a rinse, to remove any unbound antigens. A speciﬁc blocking
buﬀer is later added to saturate the unbound sites in the wells, before adding the serum
sample or primary antibodies. The primary antibodies in the serum sample will bind to the
speciﬁc antigen present in the wells. Enzyme-linked secondary antibodies, which are speciﬁc
for the Fc region of the primary antibody, are then added to each well. The secondary
antibody is typically conjugated with the horseradish peroxidase (HRP) enzyme. Finally,
a substrate speciﬁc to the enzyme conjugated to the secondary antibody is then added to
each well. The enzyme acts on the substrate producing a colorimetric signal. The color
development corresponds proportionally to the amount of antigen and primary antibody
complexes in the well [Kim et al., 2012] [Thermo Fisher Scientiﬁc Logo, ].
Figure 1: Schematic overview of the principle of indirect ELISA. Modiﬁed [Kim et al., 2012]
However, ELISAs can be replaced with a more sensitive detection method known as the
Meso-Scale Discovery assay (MSD).

4.1.2
The principle of Meso-Scale Discovery assay
MSD assays are a technology similar to the well-known ELISA assay, the major diﬀer-
ence between these two techniques is that the ELISA use a HRP-conjugated detection an-
tibody, whereas in MSD the detection reagents and or antibodies are ruthenium labeled,
such as with a SULFO-Tag, where the readout is due to electrochemiluminescence (ECL)
[Marchese et al., 2009] (ﬁgure 2).
Figure 2: Overview of the MSD procedure. Modiﬁed [Mesoscale Discovery, 2014]
The MSD assay employs a disposable multi-array-96-well plate, with high binding screen-
printed carbon electrodes in the bottom of the wells, allowing for a greater aﬃnity between
the protein reagents and the carbon electrodes [Marchese et al., 2009] [Mesoscale Discovery, 2014].
First, the protein/antigen is coated on the plates and then incubated on the carbon sur-
face overnight. The plates are then treated with buﬀers in a step-wise manner: ﬁrst the
plasma serum, then the secondary antibody, then the ECL-labelled detection system, and
lastly the MSD reading buﬀer. The reading buﬀer provides a great sensitivity, with low
background and high luminescent signal.
The SULFO-Tag emits a luminescent signal
in the form of light through electrochemical reactions such as oxidations and reductions

The MSD assay is more versatile and robust, with a higher sensitivity for neurodegenerative
disease detection, in comparison to the ELISA [Reindl et al., 2019]. MSD has a broader
dynamic range of detection of the analyte. This means that the matrix does not need to be
as dilute as in the ELISA. Furthermore, MSD has a signiﬁcantly lower background noise and

4.2
An amyloidogenic protein; α-synuclein
α-synuclein (α-syn) is an amyloidogenic neuropathological protein, contributing to many
neurodegenerative disorders referred to as α-synucleinopathies [Wales et al., 2013]. α-syn
is a small 14.5 kDa soluble and heat-resistant protein consisting of 140 aminoacid residues
[Bertoncini et al., 2005]. It consists of three sub-domains (Figure 4): the N-terminal region,
the central region composed of hydrophobic (NAC) domain, and the C-terminus.

The human α-syn is encoded by the SNCA gene located on the 10q23 chromosome. α-syn is
expressed in the human brain, where it plays an important role in synaptic neurotransmission
[Taguchi et al., 2016]. It is present in especially high levels in the neocortex, hippocampus,
substantia nigra, thalamus, and cerebellum [Kim et al., 2014], α-syn is thought to play a
role in both the generation and maintenance of synapses [Taguchi et al., 2016], as it is ex-
pressed earlier than synaptophysin, a major synaptic vesicle glycoprotein produced in the
development of the central nervous system [Wiedenmann et al., 1986]. However, the vesicu-
lar glutamate transporter-1 (vGT-1), an excitatory presynaptic marker, mainly accompanies
the expression of α-syn. [Taguchi et al., 2016]. The normal functions of α-syn are currently
unknown, but studies indicate that the protein is likely multifunctional due to its conforma-
tional ﬂexibility, which allows the protein to adapt or take diﬀerent conformations that allow
it to bind and interact with other proteins or membranes [Lashuel et al., 2013]. Some of the
possible functions of α-syn are the interaction with synaptic proteins that allow for vesicle
exocytosis control, the traﬃcking of synaptic vesicles, as well as being a chaperone protein
for the presynaptic vesicle NSF attachment protein receptor (v-SNARE) complex (ﬁgure 5)
[Burré et al., 2010].
As ﬁgure 5 indicates, α-syn controls the regulation, maintenance, and distribution of the
v-SNARE complex as well as release of diﬀerent neurotransmitters such as dopamine. Fur-
thermore, α-syn regulates vesicle exocytosis as seen in ﬁgure 5, which may indicate that
the protein also contributes to the regulation of synaptic homeostasis-associated proteins
[Lashuel et al., 2013].

4.2.1
Pathology of α-synuclein
Evidence suggests that there are two major reasons for α-syn aggregation: over-expression
of α-syn protein and post-translational modiﬁcation of α-syn [Taguchi et al., 2016]. An in-
tracellular over-expression of α-syn aﬀects the regulation of α-syn in synaptic vesicles (ﬁgure
5) [Lashuel et al., 2013], which further decreases the intersynaptic traﬃcking of vesicles re-
sulting in a smaller reserved pool vesicle, leading to reduced neurotransmitter re-uptake and
neurotransmitter release, such as dopamine [Lashuel et al., 2013].
There are three main post-translational protein modiﬁcations: phosphorylation, ubiquitina-
tion, and nitration [Kim et al., 2014]. The most common post-translational modiﬁcation of
α-syn is phosphorylation [Kim et al., 2014]. Phosphorylation normally contributes to the
structural and functional properties of several proteins in healthy individuals as well as

4.3
A microtubule-associated protein; Tau
Tau is an unfolded, highly soluble protein which in normal conditions belongs to the microtubule-
associated protein (MAP) class of neurons, contributing to cell signaling, regulation of ge-
nomic stability and microtubule dynamics [Guo et al., 2017]. The human Tau protein is
encoded by the MAPT gene located in chromosome 17 at the 17p21 band position. It is
comprised of 16 exons, where exons 2, 3, and 10 are alternatively spliced and present in the
neurons of human adults [Kolarova et al., 2012]. The pre-mRNA expressed by the MAPT
gene is spliced according to the type of neuron and stage of maturation [Guo et al., 2017].
Tau is predominantly expressed in the human neurons of the central nervous system (CNS),
where Tau is translated from a 6 kb long mRNA, and further transcribed into six tau protein
isoforms of 37 - 46 kDa [Guo et al., 2017]. 441-residue Tau is the longest isoform found in the
CNS [Guo et al., 2017][Kolarova et al., 2012]. The isoforms are diﬀerentiated by the pres-
ence of, null, one or two N-terminals, and by consisting of either three microtubule-binding
repeats or four microtubule-binding repeats in their C-terminal [Guo et al., 2017].
Tau plays an essential role in tubulin assembly and microtubule stability in the formation for
the neuronal cytoskeleton [Chong et al., 2018]. Microtubules are cellular structures pivotal
for transporting organelles in the plus-end-directed motor kinesin and the minus-end-directed
motor dynein [Chong et al., 2018] [Domise and Vingtdeux, 2018]. The protein is essential
for axonal transport regulation, by interacting with microtubules to promote microtubule
bundles, involved in organelle traﬃcking and vesicles b transports between diﬀerent cell com-
partments. Furthermore, Tau protects microtubules from depolymerization by decreasing
the tubulin dissociation of the microtubule [Domise and Vingtdeux, 2018]. However, sev-
eral studies using Tau-knockout mice have shown diﬀerent functions of the protein, such
as neurogenesis [Hong et al., 2010], iron export [Lei et al., 2012] and long-term depression
[Kimura et al., 2014].

4.3.1
Pathology of Tau protein
The pathological hallmark of AD patients is the formation of intracellular neuroﬁbrillary
tangles (NFT) [Domise and Vingtdeux, 2018]. One of the essential factors of the disease,
are several post-translational modiﬁcations that lead to Tau aggregation. Some of the post-
translational modiﬁcations that we know of include hyperphosphorylation, truncation, glyco-
sylation, glycation, nitration, and ubiquitination. It is believed that the several phosphatases
and kinases that normally help regulate phosphorylation of Tau when they experience im-
balance and impairment result in highly phosphorylated Tau [Noble et al., 2013]. Tau hy-
perphosphorylation plays a key role in inducing tau aggregation. It is believed that tau un-
dergoes a conformational change in which it transforms from tau monomer to tau oligomer.
In some cases, the tau oligomer is transformed into pair helical ﬁlaments, which results in
the formation of NFT [Chong et al., 2018], abundant in the brain of AD patients. However,
a study shows that NFT itself may not possess neurotoxic activity, but, the intermediate
oligomer structure has shown high neurotoxicity which results in synaptic dysfunction in AD
patients [Fá et al., 2016].
4.4
Amyloid beta
Amyloid beta (Aβ) is one of the main two hallmarks of AD [Ritter et al., 2005].
Aβ is
peptide derived from the proteolysis of the much larger Amyloid precursor protein (APP),
an integral membrane protein expressed in the synapses of neurons with an essential role
in AD pathogenesis. APP is likely important for the neuronal growth and repair system.
APP can be broken down into two major pathways, the non-amyloidogenic pathway, and
the amyloidogenic pathway. The amyloidogenic pathway is the pathway that produces Aβ.
The enzymes beta-secretase and gamma-secretase sequentially cleave APP into fragments,
resulting in the production of Aβ monomers which are small peptides made up of 37 to 49
amino acid residues [Chen et al., 2017]. Aβ is normally broken down in the brain through
phagocytosis by microglial cells and by receptor-mediated internalization from Astrocytes.
However, in AD there are elevated amounts of Aβ due to its elevated production of Aβ
and decreased breakdown of the protein.
In the neural synaptic junction, the excess of
Aβ monomers aggregate into oligomers, which eventually form large characteristic proteina-
ceous deposits known as Aβ plaques, seen in AD patients. The Aβ plaques can block the
neurotransmitters in the synapse, impeding neuronal communication [Chen et al., 2017].
4.5
Parkinson’s Disease
PD is a neurodegenerative disorder that aﬀects movement. It is characterized by neuronal
loss in speciﬁc areas of the brain as well as by the intracellular accumulation of α-syn. PD is
the second-most common neurological disorder after AD [Poewe et al., 2017]. α-syn aggre-
gation at nerve endings in the heart, digestive ract and skin [Brudek et al., 2017] indicate
that PD is a systemic disease. A study showed that countries in Europe have an estimated
PD prevalence of 5 to 346 cases per 100 000 person/year in all age groups. However, age is an
important risk factor for PD [Balestrine and Schapira, 2019], the prevalence in people older

At least 90 % of all PD cases are idopathic or caused by environmental risk factors such as
pesticides [Wales et al., 2013] [Antony et al., 2013]. Some speciﬁc factors can be linked to
the development of PD, such as head trauma. The remaining approximately 10 % PD cases
are associated to familial traits, indicating rare hereditary forms of PD [Folke et al., 2019].
Pathologically, PD is characterized by the degeneration of nerve cells aﬀecting mainly dopamin-
ergic neurons in the substantia nigra (SN) parscompacta (SNpc), resulting in the decrease of
dopaminergic innervation of the striatum [Kang et al., 2018]. Aging is considered the most
prominent PD development risk factor. A stereological study of human SN showed that
there is a link between the reduction of dopaminergic neurons and aging in healthy controls
[Cabello et al., 2002]. However, another unbiased stereological study showed that patient
with PD have 66 % less dopaminergic neurons in their SN compared to healthy controls
[Pakkenberg et al., 1991]. PD reduces the biosynthesis of dopamine in the regions of sub-
stantia nigra and striatum [Park et al., 2015].
PD is an sporadic disease, with unknown etiology. However, there are some familial forms
of PD which result in several missense mutations, including duplicate and triplicates of the
SNCA gene [Taguchi et al., 2016], and formation of lewy bodies (LB) (ﬁgure 6). LB are ag-
gregated intracellular vesicles and proteins in spherical shapes, with α-syn being the major
protein component. LB are formed in SNpc neurons by mutation or aggregation of α-syn
protein encoded by the SNCA gene. The aggregation of α-syn proteins is seen in neuro-
logical diseases/pathways known as α-synucleinopathies [Power et al., 2017]. Hence α-syn
is considered to be one of the main hallmarks of PD [Taguchi et al., 2016]. Thus, there is
evidence suggesting that aggregation of α-syn is responsible for its toxicity, but how this
aﬀects human SN neurodegeneration is still not known [Power et al., 2017].

4.6
Multiple System Atrophy
MSA is a rare progressive neurodegenerative movement disorder that aﬀects nerve cells
in speciﬁc areas of the brain with a prevalence of 1.9 to 4.6 per 100 000 people and an
annual incidence of about 3 / 100.000 inhabitants above 50 years of age [Jellinger, 2014].
However, MSA progresses more rapidly than PD, and it responds more poorly responds to
treatment, which results in more deaths less than ten years after the onset of symptoms
[Brudek et al., 2017]. In comparison, PD is rarely fatal [Gilman et al., 2008].
MSA is an atypical disease of uncertain etiology, that can be further divided into sub-groups
based their symptomatology: MSA-P (Parkinsonian type) and MSA-C (cerebellar type).
MSA-P is the most common type of MSA, where almost all patient suﬀer from predom-
inantly parkinsonian features due to reduced dopaminergic neuron signaling in the SN as

Immune system
The immune system is a coherent system of organs, tissues, cells and cell products that
protect the body through the diﬀerentiation of self from non-self and eliminate pathogens
from the body, order to obtain a healthy microenvironment. The immune system is divided
into two lines of defense: the ﬁrst line of defense is called the innate immune system and
the second is known as the adaptive immune system. The adaptive branch can be further
subdivived into two subgroups: the humoral immune response with antibodies and the cell-
mediated immune response.
The innate immune system is the short-lived ﬁrst line of immunity that responds to imme-
diate pathogen invasion in the body. The innate immune system key players are phagocytic
cells, antigen-presenting cells (APC), natural killer cells and the complement system (ﬁgure
9). The innate system has no memory, and thus does not diﬀerentiate in its subsequent
reactions to at same pathogen infection.

4.9
Immunoglobulins
Immunoglobulins are highly speciﬁc proteins produced by plasma cells of the immune system
in response to pathogenic antigens. The primary function of immunoglobulin is to bind to
its speciﬁc antigen, which activates it for destruction by the immune system.
An antibody has a Y-shaped heterodimer molecule that provides it with a great ﬂexibil-
ity, so that it can bind to antigens and destroy pathogens. They consist of two diﬀerent
types of amino acid chains: the heavy chain and the light chain (ﬁgure 11). The molecule
contains two identical copies of each amino acid chain held together by disulﬁde bonds
[Liu and May, 2012]. The Ig fraction containing the heavy chains, the constant region, and
is called the crystallization fragment, Fc region. It interacts with cell surface receptors called
Fc receptors and some proteins of the complement system. On the opposite end of the Ig
molecule, there are two antigen-binding sites known as the Fab region. Each Fab region is a
combination of a heavy chain and a light chain that forms a highly variable region that can
adapt depending on the speciﬁc pathogen and antigen. Furthermore, there are two types of
light chains in the variable region: the Lambda light chain and Kappa light chain. There are
ﬁve diﬀerent isotypes of Ig: IgA, IgD, IgE, IgG and IgM. Some isotypes are further divided
into subclasses as IgG1-IgG4 and IgA1-IgA2. The isotypes diﬀer in the heterogeneity of
their Fc-region [Dondelinger et al., 2018][Moser and Leo, 2010].
IgG is the most abundant Ig in human plasma and is said to account for approximately
80% of the total antibody pool. The subclassses of IgG can be arranged according to their
serum concentration, from highest to lowest (IgG1 (â70%) > IgG2 (â20%) > IgG3 (â7%) >
IgG4 (â3%). This antibody is mainly responsible for humoral immunity and acts through
opsonization [Elkuch et al., 2017].
Igs destroy pathogens by diﬀerent methods: neutralization, antibodies form a complex with
the antigen, so the antigen is not free, but masked by antibodies: in opsonization, antibodies
coat the pathogen, which facilitates the engulfment by phagocytic cells. For opsonization to
occur, the antibody’s paratope needs to interact with the pathogen’s antigen. The paratope
of the Fab-region will form an antigen-antibody complex through non-covalent bonds such
as hydrogen bonds, electrostatic forces, van der Waals interactions and hydrophobic forces.
The speciﬁcity and diversity of the paratope depends on a mechanism known as somatic
recombination of germline DNA which encodes the variable, diversity and joining (V(D)J)
genes [Weaver and Murphy, 2017]. Genetic recombination occurs in the heavy - and light
chaind of Ig, where the quantity of gene segments varies antibodies have 51 v gene segment,
27 D segments and 6 J gene segments, The heavy chain is formed by undergoing a D-J
recomination and a further V-D-J recombination leads to the creation of diﬀerent transcript,
speciﬁc to a particular antigen. The light chain does not have the D segment, it is formed
by a V-J recombination.

4.9.1
Naturally Occurring Antibodies
Naturally occurring antibodies (nABs) are the body’s ﬁrst line of defense against infec-
tions, and are induced before exposure to antigens and T cell assistance, nABs eliminate
pathogens invading the body before immunity by signaling that establishes an innate - and
adaptive immunity [Palma et al., 2018]. In contrast to antibodies from the adaptive im-
mune system, nABs are spontaneously formed from the recombined immunoglobulin genes
in germ-free conditions, resulting in less hypermutation and matured antibodies with lower
aﬃnities [Dodel, 2018]. NABs are present from birth [Palma et al., 2018] and are mainly
produced from B1 lymphocytes, a sub-population of B-lymphocytes, as well as B lym-
phocytes from the marginal zone. In humans nABs mainly consist of the IgM and, IgG
(isoypes IgG1, IgG2, IgG3 and IgG4 and IgGA). 80-90% of IgMs are produced by B1 lym-
phocytes [Palma et al., 2018], however, the amount of nABs decreases with age. As people
grow older their number of nABs decreases, resultsing in a decrease of their immunolog-
ical potential [Palma et al., 2018].
NABs circulate through body ﬂuids conﬀering them
auto-reactivity towards various self-antigens which allows them to maintain physiological
homeostasis [Sauerborn and Schellekens, 2009], prevent pathogens invasions and patholog-
ical states like aggregated proteins [Palma et al., 2018], as well as clear distinct secreted
proteins and apoptotic cell cites [Dodel, 2018]. Furthermore, nABs are said to contain mod-
est anti-microbial aﬃnity, are autoreactive, and polyreactve, ensuringe their heterology for
one speciﬁc antibody [Palma et al., 2018].
However, the role of nABs in neurodegenerative diseases is still under research. A study
showed that the level of IgG nAbs in human serum of patients with neurodegenerative
diseases, was lower than healthy serum samples of people from the same age group.
In
several other studies, nAbs against pathological neurodegenerative proteins, such as Aβ,
α-syn and Tau, haves also been investigated. A study showed the presence of nABs against
pathological neurodegenerative proteins, such as Aβ, α-syn and Tau in the blood serum
of PD, MSA and AD patients [Papachroni et al., 2007].
For the most part patient with
these diseases had reduced nAb levels than healthy control group. Due to, reduced aﬃni-
ty/avidity between the nAb and the pathological antigen. According to a study published
in 2018, nAbs inhibit the accumulation of pathological proteins in neurodegenerative dis-
eases [Albus et al., 2018].
However, a number of studies have demonstrated altered lev-
els of nAbs with aﬃnities for pathological neuronal proteins in neurodegenerative disease
patients[Liu et al., 2021][Kronimus et al., 2016]. In another recent study from 2021, levels
of nAbs-Aβ were measured in plasma from two cross-sectional and longitudinal cohorts of
AD patients. The researcher assessed the levels of nAbs-Aβ in plasma based on their epi-
topes and their study revealed that nAbs-Aβ altered diﬀerently in epitopes of Aβ. According
to these ﬁndings, nAbs-Aβ may play a role in the pathogenesis of AD [Liu et al., 2021].
According to recent studies from our laboratory (n-lab), there is a reduction in anti-α-syn
nAbs in plasma of PD patients compared to healthy controls, and they are virtually absent
in plasma of MSA patients. According to the data from our laboratory, nAbs are less likely
to bind to α-syn in PD, and the immune system is not as eﬀective in neutralizing protein
aggregates [Brudek et al., 2021]. Another study found that IgG nAbs from serum of pa-

6.3
Optimization protocol of Nabs towards α-syn, Tau, and Aβ in
NC plasma samples using MSD
Day 1:
96-well microtiter plates (standard, high bind or small-spot MSD plate) were coated with
50 µL of the desired concentration of either α-syn, Aβ and Tau. Then, they were diluted in
cold carbonate buﬀer and incubated overnight (>12h) at 4°C.
Day 2:
The plates were washed ﬁve times with 150 µL of washing buﬀer (PBS + 0.05 % Tween-20)
and then blocked for one hour on a shaker at 800 rpm at room temperature (RT). The plates
were washed ﬁve times with 150 µL of washing buﬀer and 50 µL of plasma in dilution buﬀer.
Later they were transferred to the MSD plate and incubated for one hour on a shaker at
800 rpm at RT. The plate were washed ﬁve times with 150 µL washing buﬀer. 25 µL of
secondary antibody (diluted with dilution buﬀer) were then added to the wells and left to
incubate for one hour on a shaker at 800 rpm at RT. The plates were washed ﬁve times
with 150 µL washing buﬀer, and 25 µL detection antibody diluted in dilution buﬀer were
added to each well and further incubated for one hour on a shaker at 800 rpm at RT. The
plates were washed ﬁve times with 150 µL washing buﬀer and lastly treated with 150 µL
MSD Read Buﬀer T and surfactant (1:1 diluted with distilled water). The plates were read
immediately using an MSD Sector Imager S600 instrument.

Analyses
The relative binding of α-syn, Tau, and Aβ with the antibody, correlated to the intensity
of the emitted light, providing quantitative values that allowed for samples analysis. All
tests were carried out using Microsoft Excel. Each experiment was performed on two control
groups to ensure there was no non-speciﬁc binding. One control group consisted of plasma
with the detection antibody, but without the secondary antibody. This ensured that the de-
tection antibody would not bind non-speciﬁcally to the surface of the well. The other control
group, did not include plasma. It consisted of only secondary and detection antibodies.

8
Discussion
In the past, traditional ELISA was the standard for measuring of neurodegenerative dis-
ease biomarkers.
However, new assays such as MSD have emerged.
As opposed to the
traditional ELISA, MSD, uses electrochemiluminescence. This plate-based method binds
speciﬁc capture antigens coated on a microplate well to the plasma. Plasma attached to
the plate is detected by a secondary antibody and the detection system emits light when
excited by a voltage on carbon-coated electrodes woven into the plate’s base. The MSD
assay has emerged as a more ﬂexible and robust technique than the ELISA when detecting
neurodegenerative diseases. Moreover, it has signiﬁcantly less background reactivity, which
minimizes the number of samples required (ﬁgure 3). However, the lack of reliable, faster,
and easier immunoassays is a major obstacle in accurately diagnosing PD, MSA, and AD as
well as monitoring their progression.
It is essential to optimize a MSD based immunoassay to achieve its goal. Since MSD is
a multistep process, each component can be individually tested before an experiment is
conducted. In this thesis, diﬀerent protocols have been presented to optimize MSD using
matched secondary antibody pairs to detect plasma Ig in NCs. The protocols also present
an optimized, step-wise approach to maximize the performance of MSD. MSD procedures
consist of: selecting an appropriate MSD plate, antigen coating, blocking buﬀer, detection
with appropriate antibodies, primary or secondary, and signal detection.
Each step fol-
lowed by a wash of the plate. The optimization process involves the establishment of ideal
concentrations of each assay reagent as well as ideal conditions for each step.
8.1
MSD plate and antigen coating
The ﬁrst step in plated based immunoassay is choosing the plate and the coating wells
with antigen. The coating applies the antigen solution in carbonate buﬀer to microtiter
plate wells. The carbonate buﬀer increases pH by absorbing hydrogen from the antigens’
surfaces, allowing antigens to bind hydrophobically to the plate. Microtiter plates for antigen
coating have modiﬁed surfaces comprised of carbon electrodes.
Carbon electrode plates
have a 10x higher binding capacity than polystyrene, the material used for ELISA maxisorp
plates [Mesoscale Discovery, 2014]. Among MSD plates, there are two types of surfaces:
hydrophilic High Bind plates and Standard hydrophobic plates. As a result, a combination
of the size, the electrode type, and the surface type determines the ability to capture protein
on the plate. For α-syn the initial optimization setups, a standard plate was used. Which
as ﬁgure 13 indicates showed high signals.
The reason for α-syn to immobilize on the
hydrophobic plates is because α-syn consists of a hydrophobic core known to directly bind
to the negative surface charge of the hydrophobic plate [van Rooijen et al., 2009]. However,
compared to the standard plate, the High bind plate captured Tau better (ﬁgure 14). Due
to the overall hydrophilic nature of Tau molecules, it immobilizes better on the High-bind
plate [Mandelkow and Mandelkow, 2012]. For Aβ, the standard small spot plate was used,
based on a former study. Irrespective of the type of antigen, the whole surface of the working
electrode on the bottom must be covered. If the whole surface is not covered, the signal read
will be lower. If excess antibody/antigen is present, layers of antibody/antigen may form

==PAGE:44==

and wash away in subsequent steps, leading to a lower signal and unreliable results.
8.2
Blocking buﬀer
One of the most important steps is selecting an appropriate buﬀer for the initial blocking
of nonspeciﬁc binding sites. A blocking buﬀer prevents nonspeciﬁc binding by saturating
free binding sites at the bottom of the wells, signiﬁcantly increasing the signal-to-noise ratio
and speciﬁcity. Without adequate blocking, the detection antibody could bind to the plate
nonspeciﬁcally, resulting in low sensitivity and high background signal. A blocking buﬀer
can be conﬁgured in various ways, not all are suitable for every situation. They usually
contain a blocking component such as BSA, nonfat-dried milk, or a comercially avaliable
Intercept (TBS) Blocking Buﬀer from Li-Cor, UK. A blocking buﬀer optimizes the signal
quality while reducing the background noise.
We used a blocking buﬀer preparation of PBS+3%BSA, which for all the antibodies showed
low background indicating blocking of non-speciﬁc binding.
However, when coated with
α-syn for mouse anti-human IgG1 antibodies, we observed a non-speciﬁc binding of the the
anti-IgG1 to the plate (ﬁgure 15). As ﬁgure 15A indicates, when creating an antibody serial
dilution, the blocking buﬀer seems to be functioning properly. However, as 15B indicates,
when using 1:200 mouse anti-human IgG1 with a plasma serial dilution, we observed a high
background. This might be due to the non-speciﬁc binding of the secondary antibody with
the coating antigen, which was not visible due to the high concentration of plasma antibodies.
This was solved by blocking mouse anti-human IgG1 coated with α-syn with Intercept (TBS)
Blocking Buﬀer - Licor, showing the highest signal-to-ratio with a low background (ﬁgure
16).
8.3
Sample preparation: Dilution buﬀer
MSD tests require dilution of samples, therefore, selecting the proper diluent is critical.
Generally, the dilution buﬀer should be in as similar a manner as possible to the sample
matrix. A suitable serum replacement in MSD based immunoassay is PBS with 0.5 % of
BSA, and that solution is usually used for biological samples. For the washing buﬀer, same
serum replacement was used followed by a non-ionic detergent diluent component, Tween
20, preventing hydrophobic protein-protein interactions when used in low concentrations.
Speciﬁc bindings are usually more resistant to detergents. In one step, detergents do not
provide a permanent barrier to a biomolecule’s non-speciﬁc attachment because water can
wash them away. This is why detergents are present in all the washing buﬀers.
8.4
Primary and secondary antibody
The primary antibody used in this thesis was the plasma from healthy controls individuals.
The secondary antibody was based on the target plasma antibody type. We used mouse
anti-human IgG, IgG1-IgG4, and IgM.
Aﬃnity and avidity describe the interactions between antibodies and their antigens.
Page 39 of 41
==PAGE:45==
Bisma Khan

Discussion
Aﬃnity is a measure of how strongly an antibody binds to an antigen. Binding occurs re-
versibly, and aﬃnity determines the amount of antigen bound by an antibody at any given
time, determined by the bonds speed and the interaction’s duration. For all types of im-
munoassays, high-aﬃnity antibodies should be used since they provide the highest degree
of sensitivity and rapidly generate a large number of stable interactions. While aﬃnity is
a more intuitive term than avidity, its signiﬁcance depends signiﬁcantly on the antigen’s
number of binding sites or valency, the overall stability of the antibody-antigen interaction
is determined by this factor. Therefore, avidity depends on the isotype and whether the
antibody is fragmented or intact. In addition to the antibody structure, the hinge regions
length and motility and the gap between the Fab fragments determine avidity.
We have in this thesis used monoclonal mouse anti-human antibodies. Monoclonal antibodies
recognize a single epitope, typically a small area on the surface of the antigen/antibody. An
immunoassay is less likely to generate nonspeciﬁc signals when using monoclonal antibodies
because they are less likely to interact with related proteins. Polyclonal antibodies have
increased speciﬁcity to the antigen. Thus they bind to diﬀerent epitopes. After selecting
the secondary antibodies for proper binding with the plasma antibodies, a series of dilutions
of primary and secondary antibodies was prepared for proper antibody concentration titra-
tion of primary and secondary antibody, which was performed according to detecting the
maximum amount of binding, after which there is no further signal increase. The ideal con-
centration chosen is the one providing a high signal and lowest noise, with as low antibody
concentration as possible. In the case of many assays, using a lower antibody concentration
can reduce costs signiﬁcantly. The selected concentrations for each primary and secondary
antibody can be seen in ﬁgure 26.
8.5
Detection system
Electrochemiluminescent labels are used in MSD based immunoassay as a detection system.
This type of label allows for ultra-sensitive detection and is called Sulfo-TAG. Detection is
highly sensitive, which is suitable for use at low concentrations, and has low non-speciﬁc
binding. In MSD based immunoassays, Sulfo-TAG conjugated proteins serve as detection
reagents. The two electrochemiluminescent labels used were the SST and AM antibody to
unify the detection systems. The mouse anti-human IgG uses SST as a detection system.
Whereas, the mouse anti-human IgG1-IgG4 and IgM used AM antibody as the detection
system. As ﬁgure 25 shows, there was no signal when we used AM antibody as the de-
tection system for mouse anti-human IgG. The absence of a signal could be due to the
biotinylation of anti-IgG, and the aﬃnity of Streptavidin for biotin is extraordinarily high,
becauce a non-covalent interaction between biotin and Streptavidin is one of the strongest
[Chivers et al., 2011]. The SULFO-TAG labels emit light when electricity is applied to the
plates by an MSD instrument. Quantifying analytes in a sample is then done by measuring
light intensity.
MSD is similar to ELISA, except MSD uses electrochemiluminescence instead of the col-
orimetric reaction employed by ELISA as the detection method. Electrochemiluminescence
systems have several advantages over traditional ELISA systems, such as higher sensitivity
and dynamic range can be achieved by using the MSD platform, reduced matrix eﬀects and

sample requirements, better eﬃciency, and the ability to multiplex and scale-up.
9
Conclusion
There are several advantages of using MSD to quantify nAbs in the laboratory over using a
traditional ELISA. This thesis outlines protocols that can lead to more remarkable analyses
with a lower signal-to-noise ratio. These protocols will yield a reliable, faster, and easier
MSD based immunoassay providing optimal results.
The optimal conditions of MSD where for Tau the High-bind, α-syn used the Standard plate,
and Aβ was coated on a small-spot standard plate. The other steps, as the blocking buﬀer,
the concentration of coating antigen, the concentration of plasma and secondary antibody,
and the detection system, were optimized depending on the particular coating antigen and
the secondary antibody used. All the ﬁnal optimization can be seen in ﬁgure 26.

Future Perspective
It would be interesting to use the optimized MSD assays to measure the levels of IgG, IgG1-
IgG4 and IgMs further and their speciﬁcity against monomeric, oligomeric, and ﬁbrillary
neuropathological proteins as α-syn, Tau, and Aβ from PD, MSA, and AD patients as well
as healthy volunteers.
Furthermore, it would be interesting to run an ELISA parallel to the MSD plate to observe
if there are any signal diﬀerences between both methods.
