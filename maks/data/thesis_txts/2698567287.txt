Abstract
It has been discovered that acrylamide is present in high amount in carbohydrate-rich food
products. Acrylamide has been discovered to be genotoxic and carcinogenic and therefore
have several authors investigated diﬀerent mitigation strategies to reduce the amount of
acrylamide. This master thesis has researched the eﬀect of diﬀerent mitigation strategies
which can be applied at home to reduce the acrylamide content of potato and sweet potato
fries after baking. The two mitigation strategies chosen in this study is soaking in water
and citric acid.
The potatoes and sweet potatoes were soaked in water and citric acid
solution of 0.5 and 1.5% for 15 and 30 minutes. The potatoes and sweet potatoes were
baked at temperatures of 175 and 200 °C resulting in a moisture content of 40 g water/100
g. The acrylamide content was measured by using LC-MS/MS, with a method developed
and validated by DTU Food Institute.
Results showed that high amount of acrylamide was found in the potatoes in relation to
sweet potatoes. Soaking in citric acid in a solution of 1.5 % for 30 minutes had the best
eﬀect reducing the acrylamide content. Furthermore, it was proven that the mitigation
strategies applied had a better eﬀect of reducing the acrylamide content in sweet potato
than potatoes. It was found that when baking potatoes at 200 °C more acrylamide was
formed compared to when the potatoes was baked at 175 °C. A risk assessment was carried
out which showed a high health concern for consumers with values below 10.000 expect for
some samples for sweet potato soaked in citric acid. Finally, was a taste test conducted for
the fries, which showed that the fries soaked in 1.5 % citric acid solution had a sour taste.

Introduction
Potatoes (Solanum tuberosum L.) is a major food crop eaten all over the world. Potatoes are
grown in approximately 80 % in the world and has a worldwide production of which excess
300 million tons/year [1]. Another important food crop which has become more popular over
the years is sweet potato (Ipomea batata). The largest, sweet potato production occurs in
Asia and the Paciﬁc Islands (93 %) and china (80 %) [2]. In recent years has the sweet potato
been eaten as an alternative to potatoes as it is considered healthier for many consumers.
In addition, it has been conﬁrmed that acrylamide can be formed in a wide range of foods
[3], especially potato products, as high amount of acrylamide has been found in potatoes,
however, is the knowledge of acrylamide concentrations in sweet potatoes limited compared
to regular potatoes. Acrylamide has been discovered by Swedish researchers in 2002 and
has been classiﬁed as probably carcinogenic in humans, therefore has no tolerable daily
intake (TDI) been set.
EFSA instead estimated a BMDL10 that has been selected to
be 0.17 mg/kg bw/day for tumors [4]. Another potentially toxic compound occurring in
starchy foods are HMF, although it is still not conﬁrmed if HMF is a health risk to humans.
Both compounds, acrylamide and HMF are products generated from the maillard reaction
at high temperatures (> 120◦C) however, are the maillard reaction also responsible for
the development of attractive attributes like the color, texture and ﬂavor. The maillard
reaction in generated by the precursors reducing sugars and asparagine of the potato whose
amount depends on several factors as the variety of potato, the harvesting period and post-
harvest storage conditions such as temperature and time. The reducing sugars of interest
in potatoes are glucose and fructose, as the content accelerate the formation of acrylamide
[3]. Therefore, has the reduction of these precursors been studied by applying mitigation
strategies. Several authors have for example reported that mitigation strategies as soaking
in water or citric acid has reduced the content of acrylamide eﬀectively [5][6].

4.1
Aim and scope of the project
The purpose is to study the eﬀect of diﬀerent mitigation strategies which can be applied at
home to reduce the acrylamide content of potato and sweet potato fries after baking. Addi-
tionally, will the content of HMF be investigated in the potato and sweet potato fries after
baking. Finally, will the sugar and the asparagine content be determined on raw potato and
sweet potato. The analytical method used in this project is LC-MS/MS.
The objective of this project are therefore to:
• Describe and present the formation mechanism of acrylamide with relevant analytical
methods (e.g. LC-MS/MS) of the chosen vegetable fries.
• Evaluate the formation of acrylamide in vegetables fries based on own results. In-
cludes acrylamide formation without processing, and with applied strategies to reduce
acrylamide in the selected vegetable fries.
• Discuss processing strategies to reduce the concentrations of acrylamide and the im-
portance for consumer risks

5.1
Composition of potatoes and sweet potatoes
The white potato (Solanum tuberosum) is a root vegetable, which comes in multiple varieties
[7]. The content of dry matter in raw potatoes can range from 19 – 23 % [8], which means
that the water content is around 80 % [9]. The dry matter in potatoes contains starch (15 %
ww), sugars, nitrogen compounds, lipids, organic acids, phenolic compounds, mineral sub-
stances and non-starch polysaccharides [8]. The relevant components of interest are the two
components asparagine and sugars (fructose and glucose), as they are the main precursors
in the mechanism of the Maillard reaction which forms acrylamide [7]. The asparagine and
sugar content for potato can be seen in table 1.
Sweet potatoes (Ipomea batatas) are a root vegetable which has a yellow to orange color
due to the high amount of carotenoids, especially B-carotene [10]. The water content is
80.1 g /100 g, which means the dry matter is approx. 20 g/ 100 g. In comparison to white
potatoes, does sweet potatoes contain higher amounts of the sugars fructose, glucose and
sucrose and lower asparagine as seen in table 1.

5.2
Acrylamide
Swedish researchers proved the presence of acrylamide (CH2 = CH −CO −NH2) in food in
April 2002 [7]. The formation of acrylamide is caused by an important chemical process
known as the Maillard reaction. Several diﬀerent molecules are formed in this reaction, and
is responsible for the taste and structure of the food. One of them is melanoidins which
are dark colored compounds that provide the bitter taste. The big challenge is to create a
balance between achieving the desired properties in heated food with an acceptable level of
acrylamide [12]. Acrylamide also known as prop-2-enamide is a white odorless crystalline
solid with a molecular weight of 71.08 g/mol [13]. The chemical substance acrylamide is
formed in food when prepared at temperatures above 120°C by roasting, deep frying and
baking in carbohydrate-rich products, for example, chips, bread, coﬀee and potato products
[14].The highest intake of acrylamide from food is from coﬀee and potato products [15].
Heating is an important process for the shelf life of food as it destroys microorganisms,
reduces the water content and change the texture.
An illustration of the Maillard reaction can be seen in ﬁg. 1. In the ﬁrst stage, a con-
densation reaction takes place between free amino acid asparagine and a carbonyl group of
reducing sugar e.g glucose or fructose which leads to the formation of a schiﬀbase. After,
the Schiﬀbase rearranges to form Amadori rearrangement products which are unstable and
further decarboxylate to form oxazolidine-5-one. The decarboxylated product can easily
decarboxylate to form azomethine ylide (decarboxylated Schiﬀbase). Azomethine ylide can
then decompose directly to form acrylamide or degrade by the elimination of imine or after
hydrolyzation to form 3-aminopropionamide (3-APA) or 3-oxopropanamide which decom-
poses to form acrylamide [7] [16]. The dietary acrylamide exposure across surveys and age
was set by EFSA to be between 0.6 to 3.4 ug/kg b.w. per day [15]. Moreover, has the Joint
Expert Committee on Food Additives (JECFA) of the World Health Organization (WHO)
evaluated the adverse non-carcinogenic eﬀect in nerves of rats with a no-observed-adverde-
eﬀect level (NOAEL) of 200µkg−1
bw d−1 [17]. It has been assessed from animal experiments
that acrylamide has genotoxic and carcinogenic properties and in high doses also have neu-
rotoxic properties [18]. Therefore, has no tolerable daily intake (TDI) been set. Instead, has
EFSA estimated a BMDL10 that has been selected to be 0.17 mg/kg bw/day for tumers [4].

5.3
Hydroxymethylfurfural
Another compound formed in the Maillard reaction is 5-hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF)
(HO −CH2 −C4H2O −CHO).
HMF is a furanic compound and can be formed in sev-
eral food items, especially caramel and coﬀee. Like acrylamide, it contributes to the desired
color, taste and aroma of heated foods [20]. Besides that, it has been discovered in recent
studies that HMF participate and accelerate the formation of acrylamide during heating
since HMF works as an acrylamide precursor. The content of acrylamide should therefore
not only be based on the content of the precursors of acrylamide and reducing sugars but
also by HMF generated during heating [3]. The compound forms as an intermediate in
the maillard reaction and can be formed under thermal treatments of sugars under acidic
conditions. A proposed reaction scheme for the formation of HMF can be seen at ﬁg. 2.
The formation of HMF comes from 1,2 enolisation and dehydration of glucose or fructose
which forms the key intermediate 3-deoxyosone. Further dehydration and cyclization of
3-deoxyosone yields to HMF. HMF can be formed in acidic conditions even at low temper-
atures, although the concentration of HMF increases drastically as temperature of thermal
treatments and storage increase. It has been shown that HMF is genotoxic under certain
experimental conditions and in some cases can have some carcinogenic potential. In an ex-
periment HMF showed carcinogenic activity on rodents and mutagenic activity on bacteria
such as Salmonella when sulfotransferases was present. These enzymes can convert HMF
into sulfoxymethylfurfural (SMF) which is a more mutagenic compound [21]. However, It
is still not clear if the exposure of HMF is a potential health concern to humans, since
data reported in the literature is not clear [18]. Janzowski et al (2000) reported that HMF
does not pose a serious health risk, even at food items containing high concentrations of
HMF [20]. The Scientiﬁc Panel on food additivves, ﬂavourings, processing aids and mate-
rials in contact with foods (AFC) estimated a dietary intake of HMF at 1.6 mg/person per
day based on a mTAMDI (modiﬁed Theoretical Added Maximum Daily intake). From a
large database containing data on subchronic and chronic animal studies JEFCA estimated
a TTC (Threshold of Toxicological concern) to be 540 ug/person/day by. The estimated
dietary intake is far above the value of concern [20].

Acrylamide mitigation strategies
Several factors have been shown to be able to reduce the acrylamide content in foods with-
out aﬀecting its ﬁnal quality. The mitigation strategies for reducing acrylamide in food
include selecting varieties with low content of acrylamide precursors (amino acid and re-
ducing sugar), adding acidifying agents and natural antioxidants, and changing the process
time and heating temperature for baking or frying [12]. In this section various mitigation
strategies are summarised.
6.1
Soaking in water
In a study by Pedreschi et al. (2004) [6], the acrylamide concentration was investigated
by various mitigation strategies in potato slices during frying. One of the strategies was
soaking in water. In this study, the potato slices were cut and then rinsed with distilled
water for 1 min to remove starch on the surface. Subsequently, 60 potato slices were soaked
in 1 L of distilled water for 40 and 90 minutes, while some potato slices were not treated
(controls). Reducing sugar, amino acid and acrylamide content were analyzed in the control
and soaked potato slices. From ﬁg. 3 it can be seen that the concentration of acrylamide
is higher in the control than in the soaked samples. Comparing the control and the soaked
samples has shown that the fructose, glucose and sucrose content decreased slightly as the
soaking time increased (results for fructose and sucrose are not shown). The glucose content
decreased with 32 % in potato slices soaked in water for 90 min. The amino acid asparagine
remained almost constant in the control and in the soaked samples. This suggests that the
soaking process leads to lower content of reducing sugar which ultimately results in lower
acrylamide concentration. The formation of acrylamide is decreased by an average of 27 %,
38 % and 20 % at 150, 170 and 190 °C [6].
Another study by Jung et al. (2003) has been conducted to reduce acrylamide in french
fries. The french fries were soaked in water for 60 min and then fried at 190 °C. This resulted
in a 25 % reduction of acrylamide formation [22]. Kita et al. (2004) have investigated the
acrylamide reduction in potato crisps when soaking in water at 20 °C for 1 min and 60 min.
The results showed a reduction of 10 % and 24 % respectively [1].

6.2
Soaking in citric acid
In the study by Pedreschi et al. (2007), potato slices soaked in citric acid solutions were
performed as a possible mitigation strategy.
The purpose is to lower pH to investigate
whether the acrylamide concentration is reduced. The experiment was performed so that
30 raw potato slices were soaked in 1 L of 10 g/L of citric acid solution (1%) for 60 minutes.
This showed a signiﬁcant reduction (P>0.05) of acrylamide formation by 86, 47 and 28%
compared to control for frying temperatures of 150, 170 and 190°C. The formation of acry-
lamide increased at higher frying temperature [5]. In another study performed by Pedreschi
et al. (2004), potato slices were soaked in 1 and 2% citric acid solution for 30 min. The
pH of both citric acid solution was measured to be 2.45 and 2.25. It is stated that potato
slices deep fried at 150 °C showed a signiﬁcant reduction of 70% for those soaked in 1 and
2 % citric acid solution. Percentages for the reduction are not given at 170 and 190 °C but
from ﬁg. 4 (left) a decrease in acrylamide formation is seen when soaking in 2 % citric acid
solution. Fig. 4 (right) shows a small increase in glucose when soaking the potato slices at
1 % citric acid solution and then decreased for 2 % citric acid solution. As no percentage is
given for the reduction, it is calculated to be approx. 14 % for 2 % citric acid solution [6].

6.3
Antioxidants - Rosemary
In a study by Becalski et al. (2003), it was investigated whether the formation of acrylamide
could be reduced by adding rosemary to the frying oil for frying the potato slices [23]. In
October 2010, rosemary extract was classiﬁed as an additive, more precisely an antioxidant,
according to European Commission Directive 2010/69/EU [24]. The addition of rosemary
showed a 25% reduction in the formation of acrylamide in the potato slices [23].
Another study by Urbancic et al. (2013), where rosemary extract was added to the frying

Risk assessment for acrylamide
7.1
Hazard identiﬁcation
7.1.1
Toxicokinetic - ADME
Acrylamide has been investigated thoroughly by toxicokinetic studies in humans and animal
studies as rats and mice. Acrylamide can be absorbed orally, through the skin and inhala-
tion. It is ingested orally through food and is rapidly absorbed and distributed in animals
and humans throughout the whole body. In several studies’ acrylamide has been found in
many organs like the thymus, liver, heart, brain and kidney. Moreover, has acrylamide been
found in the human placenta and in breast milk, which means it is easily transferred to
fetus and newborn infants. The metabolism of acrylamide in the human body is proposed
in ﬁg. 8. Acrylamide can be oxidized by either cytochrome P450 2E1 into the epoxide glyci-
damide or conjugate with GSH. The two compounds acrylamide and glycidamide can bind
in vivo hemoglobin, DNA and enzymes, and it has been discovered that glycidamide is more
reactive toward DNA and proteins than acrylamide. Glycidamide can then be hydrolised
by an epoxide hydrolase to 2,3-dihydroxypropionamide. GSH-conjugates of acrylamide and
glycidamide are then further converted to mercapturic acids which are excreted with urines
[20].

7.2
Hazard characterization
7.2.1
Toxicodynamic
Acrylamide has since 1994 been classiﬁed by the International Agency for Research on Can-
cer (IARC) to be probably carcinogenic to humans. Two lifetime carcinogenicity studies
performed in F344 rats and B6C3f1 mice have shown that acrylamide can cause cancer in
multiple organs such as the lung, uterus, skin, mammalian gland and brain. The carcino-
genicity of acrylamide happens when acrylamide is metabolized to glycidamide which can
form DNA adducts [20]. Glycidamide has been shown to be mutagenic and genotoxic in
both in vitro and in vivo test studies. A study by National Center for Toxicological Research
(NCTR) performed a two-year drinking water study with rats and mice which strongly sup-
ported that glycidamide is directly mutagenizing causing mutation of DNA which causes
tumors.
Moreover, has acrylamide been shown to cause neurotoxicity and reproductive

impaired nerve function have been found in workers where a gel containing acrylamide was

Liquid-Chromatography with Tandem Mass Spec-
trometry
The LC-MS/MS method were used for detection and quantiﬁcation of acrylamide and HMF
in the vegetable fries. Liquid chromatography is an analytical technique used for separating
molecules or ions in a mixture. The mixture is distributed between two phases, the mobile
phase (eluent) which is a liquid and the stationary phase which is a porous solid (column).
An illustration of HPLC instrumentation can be seen in ﬁg. 10. The instrument consists
of a reservoir where the eluent is located. The eluent is passed through a pump containing
ﬁlters which prevent particles from entering the column. The samples are introduced into the
injector and pumped through a column. The analytes are separated in the chromatographic
column depending on the polarity of the analytes and ﬁnally detected. [26].
Figure 10: Illustration of HPLC system [20]
After separation in HPLC, the analytes enter the detector, a MS/MS system. Tandem
mass spectrometry (MS) is coupled to liquid chromatography and has been used in this
experiment to detect and quantify acrylamide in potato- and sweet potato fries. In the MS
system, the analytes are ionized by electrospray ionization (ESI). By ESI, the liquid from
the chromatography column is passed through a capillary needle with some nitrogen at high
voltage. A ﬁne mist of aerosols is then formed consisting of liquid droplets which are highly
charged. The used solvents are evaporated and the analytes are released. Subsequently, a
explosion of the parent droplets occurs and are divided into smaller stable droplets. They
are useful for the destruction of robust molecules. This mechanism is called coulomb ﬁssion.
24
==PAGE:26==
These particles are then transferred into the ion analyzer (quadrupole) by an electric ﬁeld. In
the ﬁrst quadrupole, the precursor ions are separated depending on their m/z, e.g m/z = 72
for acrylamide. These collide with a gas in the second quadrupole also called the collision
cell. In the third quadrupole, ion monitoring of product ions speciﬁc to the molecule of
interest are collected and generated in a spectrum of the resulting product ions. In ﬁg. 11
an illustration of triple quadrupole mass spectrometer can be seen [26].

Statistical analysis
A T-test of 95% conﬁdence interval was performed to test whether there was a signiﬁcant
eﬀect between the control sample and samples treated with mitigation strategies. A 95%
conﬁdence interval indicates a relatively reliable estimate of the mean value of the measured
parameters. Before the T-tests were performed, it was a requirement to make a F-test to
compare variances. F-test is a statistical test used to determine whether two samples have
the same variance or not. Depending on whether it is the same variance or diﬀerent, the
T-tests were performed. A p-value was obtained from a T-test, and is a term determining
the signiﬁcance of the results. The p-value (P>0.05) indicates the probability that the null
hypothesis is true. A statistically signiﬁcant result (P <0.05) indicates that the hypothesis
test is false or should be rejected. The deﬁnition for p-value is explained in table 8 [32].

result of interest was the potatoes fried at 170 and 190 C° as these were closets to our

in our study bought in the month of may which possibly means that the potatoes has
been harvested in the winter and therefore old which also contributes to higher sugar levels.

potatoes.

HMF
The calibration curve for HMF can be observed in ﬁg. 35. The R2 value was 0.04 which
indicate a poor linearity indicating that the method does not work possibly or that the
sample preparation has not been performed accurately. The HMF chromatogram was also
examined (seen in ﬁg. 36). Here noise was observed for all the HMF curves which means that
no area was possible to be integrated and thus no results for the HMF could be calculated. In
this study, was the same method for acrylamide used to detect HMF which has been shown
not to be eﬀective. It could maybe be possible to detect HMF by running HMF separately
from acrylamide and use another fragmentation, eluent or another gradient.
Moreover,
was it discovered after this experiment that a HMF internal standard was available which
could probably also be a possibility to detect HMF. Another group (Mads and Nadia) have
conducted an experiment in connection with their master thesis, where it was possible to

Conclusion
The acrylamide concentration in homemade potato was too high, and even with applied
mitigation strategies they have not been reduced enough to be within the benchmarck level
of 750 µg/kg. This also result in genotoxic and carcinogenic risk for consumers. The potatoes
were harvested last winter and bought this summer and therefore gave high concentrations
of acrylamide. Acrylamide concentrations for sweet potato fries were low compared to the
concentrations for potato fries. The results of the comparison of the applied mitigation
strategies showed that soaking in citric acid at 1.5% for 30 min was most eﬀective but a
sour taste was noticed. So even if this mitigation strategy was most eﬀective, this would
not be implemented at home. Baking at 175 °C showed lower concentrations of acrylamid
in potatoes and sweet potatoes than baking at 200 °C. No correlation between fructose and
glucose and the formation of acrylamide was found in this study.
