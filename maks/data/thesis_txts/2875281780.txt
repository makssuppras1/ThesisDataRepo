Abstract
This master’s thesis presents a detailed geometric analysis and aerodynamic perfor­
mance optimization of a wind turbine blade equipped with a winglet, originally designed
for the International Small Wind Turbine Contest (ISWTC). The study was motivated
by the fact that the blade’s measured power coefficient (Cp) during wind tunnel tests fell
below expected values.
The core of the analysis and simulation is conducted using Computational Fluid Dynamics
(CFD).
This study employs a systematic methodology for the aerodynamic analysis and optimiza­
tion of the wind turbine blade.
The process begins with the geometric reconstruction and parameterization of the original
blade, transforming its geometry into a set of adjustable polynomial coefficients for efficient
numerical analysis and subsequent optimization.
The simulation fidelity is established by configuring appropriate CFD models and con­
ducting a rigorous grid independence test to ensure accuracy. Following validation, the
blade’s optimal operating state is identified.
The core optimization involved a sensitivity analysis to target the most critical segments,
followed by testing various combinations of Twist and Chord to find the best configuration
and evaluate the efficacy of the proposed optimization method.
Finally, a non­winglet blade is also analyzed to both assess the fundamental aerodynamic
role of the winglet and validate the general applicability of the optimization methodology.

Introduction
1.1
What is Winglet
Winglets are small, near­vertical extensions located at the tips of an aircraft’s or a wind
turbine blade’s main lifting surface. Their primary function is to improve the aerodynamic
efficiency of the lifting surface by mitigating the energy loss associated with tip vortices.
Figure 1.1: Line drawing of wingtip vortices behind a conventional wingtip (on the left) and
a blended winglet (on the right) [1]
Winglets work by effectively increasing the aspect ratio of the lifting surface without signif­
icantly extending the physical span. For wind turbine blade, winglet is applied to enhance
the performance coefficient (Cp) by reducing energy otherwise lost in the wake, thereby
improving the turbine’s overall energy capture efficiency.
1.2
Motivation
This thesis is inspired by the International Small Wind Turbine Contest (ISWTC). The com­
petition’s rules require designing and manufacturing build the most efficient wind turbine
with the highest energy yield under the condition that the projected rotor area is restricted.

During wind tunnel test, the wind turbine’s Cp measured did not achieve the projected per­

Consequently, a thorough analysis of the blade geometry and an optimization to

to time constraints, modification to the blade’s airfoil profile itself is not considered
within the scope of this optimization study.
1.3
Composition of Power for a Turbine Blade
In order to analyze and optimize the blade, it is essential to first determine the source of
the blade’s power losses.
The analysis presented here follows the framework established by Kenneth Lønbæk [4]
and others, which decomposes the ideal power into various loss mechanisms.
This analysis utilizes the Blade Element Momentum (BEM) theory as its framework.
The net power output (Cp) is derived from the ideal 1D power, which is subsequently
reduced by several loss mechanisms.
As shown in Figure 1.3, from an aerodynamic perspective, the main energy losses for a
wind turbine typically include: wake rotation loss, tip loss, and viscous loss.

For the Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) analysis, the investigation range for TSR is
roughly from 3 to 8.

selected because, beyond this point, the proportion of Tip Loss in the total losses becomes
negligible.
Observing the first three subplots, both the Wake Rotation Loss and Viscous Loss are
seen to increase with increasing TSR, while the Tip Loss decreases as TSR increases.
As this study focuses primarily on the blade with a winglet, the main effort will be concen­
trated on minimizing tip loss. So it is necessary to do this within this TSR range.

2
Geometry Reconstruct and
Parameterization
After determined the approximate operational range for the simulation, the next critical
step is the Reconstruction and Parameterization of the blade geometry.
This approach—reconstructing the geometry from a parametric description rather than
directly importing the original Computer Aided Design (CAD) model into STAR­CCM+ is
necessary for two primary reasons:
1. Suitability for Analysis: The original CAD model contains subtle geometric mod­
ifications introduced specifically for manufacturing requirements (e.g., tooling or
molds). These details can negatively affect the quality and convergence of the CFD
mesh and are often unsuitable for precise aerodynamic analysis. The reconstructed,
smoothed geometry is optimized for numerical simulation.
2. Efficiency in Optimization: Parameterization is essential for the subsequent op­
timization efforts. Directly adjusting every detail of the blade geometry without a
parametric framework would be prohibitively time­consuming and require an unac­
ceptably large number of simulations, given the constraints on time and computa­
tional cost. Parameterization allows for systematic variation of key geometric fea­
tures (like twist and chord) using a small set of coefficients, making the optimization
process efficient and feasible.
This process effectively transforms the raw geometry into a blade entirely defined by a
set of mathematical functions, adjustable coefficients, and airfoil profiles, facilitating sub­
sequent numerical analysis and optimization.

2.2
Methodology for Parameterization and Error Analysis
With the precise twist and chord data established for every section of the blade, the next
critical step, Parameterization, becomes feasible.
The primary objective of Parameterization is to reduce the number of adjustable variables
required in the subsequent optimization process, thereby minimizing the total number
of simulations needed. Consequently, parameterization necessitates a crucial trade­off
between the number of parameters and the geometric precision that can be maintained.
The accuracy of each fit is quantified using the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) [5]. The MAE
provides a straightforward and robust measure of the average magnitude of the errors be­
tween the fitted curve and the actual data points. Unlike other metrics that penalize larger
errors more heavily, MAE gives equal weight to all errors, making it a reliable indicator of
the model’s overall predictive performance. The MAE is calculated as the average of the
absolute differences between the fitted values (ˆyi) and the origin values (yi), as shown in
the following equation:
MAE = 1

Simulation Setup
With the blade geometry confirmed to be fully parameterized and readily modifiable within
STAR­CCM+ for optimization purposes, the next step is to establish the comprehensive
settings for the computational fluid dynamics simulation.
3.1
Models Section
The simulation is set up using Simcenter STAR­CCM+ to analyze the flow field around
the wind turbine blade.
To achieve high­fidelity aerodynamic analysis and systematic optimization of the blade
with its complex winglet geometry, this study employed the CFD approach based on the
Reynolds­Averaged Navier­Stokes (RANS) equations. Unlike 2D method Blade Element
Momentum (BEM) theory, CFD allows for the direct solution of the three­dimensional flow
field. This capability is crucial for accurately capturing the complex phenomena of tip
vortices and flow separation in the winglet region, which is key to reliably assessing the
winglet’s true aerodynamic benefit.
In selecting the specific turbulent models, in order to criteria that balance computational
cost with boundary layer prediction accuracy, choosing a combination of models well­
suited for wind turbine and airfoil flow analysis to ensure both the reliability of the results
and the feasibility of the optimization iterations.
• Enabled Models:
– Steady: The simulation assumes that the flow conditions do not change over
time. Although the operating state of a wind turbine cannot truly achieve a
steady state, the steady model is still employed due to factors such as reduced
computational cost and the need for efficient iterative design.
– Solution Interpolation: This model ensures that data is smoothly and ac­
curately transferred between different parts of the mesh, which is particularly
important in complex geometries or with moving parts.
– Low y+ Wall Treatment: This is a method used to accurately model the fluid
flow directly adjacent to a solid surface, ensuring precise calculations within
the thin boundary layer.
A low y+ value (typically y+ <1) indicates that the first mesh cell is placed deep
within the viscous sub­layer of the boundary layer. This fine resolution is es­
sential for accurately capturing the steep velocity and pressure gradients that
occur very close to the wall. This approach provides a high­fidelity solution
for shear stress, drag, making it ideal for simulations where these wall­related
phenomena are crucial to the result.

3.2.1
Directed mesh
Figure 3.3: Directed Mesh (Mesh without Blade Area)
Directed mesh is a type of mesh partitioning with a clear directional orientation. The lines
or elements of the mesh ”point” or ”align” with specific directions of the geometry or the
main direction of the flow field. Compared to a regular mesh, the elements in a directed
mesh are arranged more systematically, with the direction of the elements corresponding
to the geometry and flow features. It offers the following advantages:
1. Improved computational accuracy: Because the mesh direction aligns with the
flow direction, it can better capture the velocity distribution and pressure gradients
of the fluid.
2. Reduced numerical dissipation: Compared to unstructured meshes, the flow­
aligned elements reduce numerical errors.
3. Faster mesh generation: The systematic and regular nature of a directed mesh
allows for more efficient and rapid mesh generation, particularly for geometries with
a clear and consistent flow direction.
In this simulation, except for the L2R1 area, all other areas use directed mesh.
According to the airflow patterns during the operation of this wind turbine in wind tunnel,
different regions are divided into different sizes.
Mesh Partitioning
The computational domain is partitioned both radially and axially to efficiently manage the
cell count while maintaining high resolution in critical flow regions.
Radial Partitioning
• Region R1 (Near­Wake Influence): This region, situated close to the turbine, is
significantly influenced by the wake. Consequently, it is discretized using finer grids,
with the width of each cell set to no more than 0.035 m.
24
==PAGE:28==
• Region R2 (Far from Blade): This outer region, being less affected by the immediate
wake area, is divided into coarser grids. The cell width in this region is set to 0.125 m.
Axial Partitioning
• Region L1 (Incoming Flow): This area is in the direction of the incoming flow where
the air is largely undisturbed. Therefore, it is divided into the coarsest grids, with a
cell length of 0.067 m.
• Regions L2 and L3 (Within Wake): These regions fall within the wake area, neces­
sitating finer resolution. They are divided into finer grids, with cell lengths around
0.033 m or 0.04 m.
• Region L4 (Far Downstream): This region is located far from the blade and uses
coarser grids.

Optimization
Based on the preceding analysis, we can conclude that the main wing is the primary factor
influencing Cp and Ct, while the winglet has almost no contribution to the overall values.

achieve this, constants Twist Coefficient 2 and Chord Coefficient 1 in the functions

to primarily influence the geometric curves in the outer region, while ensuring the changes

4.4
Unsteady State
The previous sections show that the improvements after optimization are not significant.
While this may indicate that the potential for improvement is genuinely small, it is possible
that the results are imprecise due to the use of a steady­state model.
• The obtained thrust coefficient (CT ) is found to be greater than 1. According to the
1D momentum theory, the maximum theoretical CT is 8/9 ≈0.889. Exceeding this
range means that the flow is operating in a state of heavy loading where the simple
momentum theory breaks down. The flow field is characterized by a highly turbulent
wake state and significant flow separation.
• Furthermore, Figure 4.3 shows clear undulations in the axial velocity profile in the
region downstream of the blade root, which suggests that the local flow is in a tur­
bulent state.
These results suggest that the steady­state model has certain limitations in capturing the
unsteady flow characteristics around the blades, and therefore cannot accurately reflect
the dynamic behavior of the actual flow field.
Based on all the reasons above, it is necessary to perform an Unsteady State simulation
at TSR = 4 and a Pitch Angle of 0◦to determine the accuracy of the preceding Steady
State model results.
Running under this condition can also compare the values in the design report[3], and
verify whether similar results can be obtained using different methods.
4.4.1
Introduction of Transient Model and CFL Condition
For this reason, a transient model is further introduced in this study, in order to better
describe the evolution of vortical structures and the influence of unsteady effects in the
flow field.
At the same time, to ensure the stability and accuracy of the transient numerical simula­
tion, it is necessary to control the relationship between the time step size and the spatial
grid resolution. This leads to the discussion of the Courant–Friedrichs–Lewy (CFL) con­
dition and its critical role in the computational process.

4.4.3
Physical Significance of the CFL Condition
Physically, the CFL number represents the fraction of a cell that a fluid particle traverses
during one time step.
• If the CFL number is too large, the information will cross multiple cells in a single
time step, which may cause numerical instability or divergence.
• If the CFL number is very small, the calculation is stable but inefficient, since the
time step is unnecessarily restrictive.
For explicit schemes, it is generally required that CFL ≤1 to guarantee stability. For
implicit schemes, the CFL condition can be relaxed to values larger than unity, although
accuracy and convergence behavior are still influenced.

4.6
Chapter Summary
In this chapter, utilizing the simulation setup from chapter 3, we first determined the blade’s
optimal operational state by searching across various TSR and Pitch Angles, guided by
the simplified one­dimensional (1D) analysis from Section 1.4.
Subsequently, we employed a methodology of sequentially adjusting individual coeffi­
cients to identify the most sensitive segments for optimization, followed by multi­parameter
combination simulations.
• Winglet Blade Optimization: Initial optimization efforts on the winglet blade showed
poor results. While this is initially hypothesized to be due to an imprecise Steady
State model, the subsequent Unsteady State analysis in Section 4.4 ruled out this
possibility. This indicated an inherent flaw in the optimization method, potentially
stemming from the difficulty in accurately fitting the complex winglet geometry.
51
==PAGE:55==
• Non­Winglet Blade Validation: To validate the methodology, the same analysis
and optimization process are applied to the non­winglet blade in Section 4.5. The
results proved that the parameterization method is effective, successfully identifying
a design point with increased Cp. However, the final performance gain is modest
because the original non­winglet geometry is already very close to the theoretical
optimum, compounded by the limitations of the simplification inherent in the method
itself.

5
Discussion
Based on the preceding analysis, we conclude that the employed parameterization and
optimization methodology is effective, yet yielded only a marginal improvement in the
blade’s performance
This finding, combined with the constraints of the study, raises several critical questions
regarding the overall design and analysis approach.
• Does Winglet Works?
The blade analyzed in this thesis is the one used in the ISWTC competition. The
CFD showed only a minimal performance increase with the winglet. This contrasts
sharply with the substantial optimization effect claimed in the design report [3], which
cited a 10% increase in Cp (from 0.468 to 0.518).
• Is it worthy to do CFD analyze?
Is it possible to use more convenient and quicker method like 2D Lifting­Line based
design method to accurately calculate blade performance for iterative design and
optimization since CFD is time­intensive?
• Is the optimization way works on other wind turbine?
Is the parameterization and coefficient adjustment methodology employed in this
paper generally effective and transferable for optimization studies on other wind
turbine blade designs?
5.1
Does the winglet works?
Table 5.1 and 5.2 list the performance of Winglet and Non Winglet blade before and after
optimization.
By consolidating the outer segments of the winglet blade (specifically the Outer, Transition,
and Winglet sections from Table 2.1) and comparing them to the corresponding sections
of the non­winglet blade, a direct performance comparison can be drawn:
• Prior to optimization, the difference in Cp between the two blade types in these
combined regions is approximately 1.3%.
• After the parameterization and optimization process, the Cp difference narrowed
to less than 1%, suggesting that the method drove both designs toward a similar
performance boundary.
A sectional analysis reveals that the Cp of the Outer part of the winglet blade is signifi­
cantly higher than that of the non­winglet blade initially. This result effectively validates
the core aerodynamic function of the winglet: reducing tip vortices and locally enhancing
efficiency. However, the final optimization results show that the optimized winglet blade
offers virtually no overall advantage over the optimized non­winglet blade. This limited
improvement suggests that, if a more sophisticated and less constrained optimization
method are used, the winglet blade might still possess substantial untapped potential for
further performance enhancement.

5.3
Optimization Potential
Thomas Hansen’s paper, ”Aerodynamic Optimisation of Airfoils and Winglets for Wind
Turbine Application” [7], offers a crucial comparative study.
Hansen performed a winglet optimization on an existing non­winglet blade, demonstrating
that the addition of a winglet could increase the Cp by at least 7%. One key reason
our optimization did not yield such a large increase in Cp is that our blade was already
designed and optimized with a winglet considered at the design stage, placing it in an
already highly efficient state.
The parameterization and optimization methodology developed in this thesis can be read­
ily extended to the design and refinement of full­scale wind turbine blades.
If a manufacturer considers retrofitting or integrating a winglet onto an existing blade, they
can simultaneously leverage this method to optimize the tip’s Twist and Chord distribu­
tions. This approach offers the benefit of achieving greater Cp gains without necessitating
extensive modifications to the entire blade geometry.

6
Conclusion
The main motivation for this work was to analyze and optimize the blade geometry used
by DTU in the ISWTC competition, specifically addressing the measured power coefficient
(Cp) being below the design target and evaluating the actual aerodynamic benefit of the
integrated winglet.
The primary method of analysis and optimization was executed through a structured,
multi­step approach: first, by completing the geometric reconstruction and parameteri­
zation of the blade, which simplified the design space to a small number of adjustable
polynomial coefficients for the Twist and Chord distributions. This was followed by uti­
lizing Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) to identify the blade segments most critical
for optimization. The process then involved adjusting individual parameters to define an
appropriate optimization range, and finally, combining the Twist and Chord coefficients to
achieve the maximum possible Cp enhancement.
The methodology proved effective. By identifying an optimal combination of Twist and
Chord, the Cp of the winglet blade and non winglet blade increased. This successful
optimization of an already highly efficient blade validated the effectiveness of the param­
eterization method. However, the method exhibited certain deficiencies when dealing with
complex geometries, such as the transition segment of the winglet blade.
The main findings are as follows:
• Winglet Benefit Disagreement: The optimization showed that the Cp gain from the
winglet was far less than the 10% increase claimed in the original design report [3].
This suggests that the blade is well­optimized for the winglet during its initial design,
leaving minimal room for further improvement through simple parametric changes.
• Recommendation for the Competition: The non­winglet blade, when operating
at its optimal Tip Speed Ratio (TSR), demonstrated a performance that significantly
exceeded the Cp reported for the original design in the final report [3]. Its optimal
operating TSR is higher than that of the winglet blade. Therefore, if the chosen
generator exhibits higher efficiency at higher rotational speeds, considering the non­
winglet blade as a viable alternative for the competition is also a choice.
Ideas for further work include:
1. Refined Parameterization: Introducing a greater number of parameters or adopt­
ing more advanced fitting techniques to increase the geometric fidelity, especially in
the complex winglet section.
2. Airfoil Optimization: Incorporating changes to the airfoil’s geometric shape (e.g.,
maximum thickness and camber) into the optimization parameter set to explore a
larger design space for performance enhancement.
