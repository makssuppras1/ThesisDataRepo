Abstract
This project describes the theory behind three methods for characterizing mechanical
sources. The methods are ”Blocked forces”, ”Free velocity” and ”In-situ”. These methods
are traditionally utilized in industrial contexts, and not on dynamic loudspeakers. The aim
of the project was to apply these methods to characterize a loudspeaker as a mechanical
source. To the best of my knowledge this has not been attempted before. The required
measurement setup was designed and fabricated for each of the methods. Details of the
individual setups were described comprehensively for ease of replication. The estimates
and measurements from the methods were evaluated based on their accuracy and their
frequency range. The method were further evaluated based on the practicality of their
respective measurement setups. The findings in this project will serve as a solid foundation
for future work in the topic of characterizing the loudspeaker as a mechanical source.
Resumé
Dette projekt beskriver teorien bag tre metoder til karakterisering af mekaniske kilder.
Metoderne er ”Blocked forces”, ”Free velocity” og ”In-situ”. Disse metoder bruges tradi-
tionelt i industrielle sammenhænge og ikke på dynamiske højttalere. Formålet med projek-
tet var at anvende disse metoder til at karakterisere en højttaler som en mekanisk kilde. Så
vidt jeg ved, er dette ikke blevet forsøgt før. Den nødvendige måleopsætning blev designet
og fremstillet til hver af metoderne. Detaljerne i de enkelte opstillinger blev beskrevet
grundigt, så de nemt kunne gentages. Estimaterne og målingerne fra metoderne blev eval-
ueret baseret på deres nøjagtighed og deres frekvensområde. Metoderne blev yderligere
evalueret ud fra, hvor praktiske deres respektive måleopsætninger var. Resultaterne i dette
projekt vil være et solidt fundament for fremtidigt arbejde med at karakterisere højttaleren
som en mekanisk kilde.

Introduction
You are an engineer tasked with designing the next speakerphone. A loudspeaker was
chosen from a supplier, based on a set of parameters decided before the project began.
You the engineer now have to design the chassis for the speakerphone. One of the big
challenges you face is; the microphones are also placed in the speakerphone chassis. This is
a challenge because vibrations from the loudspeaker can easily travel trough the chassis and
into the microphones. If a lot vibrations from the loudspeaker reaches the microphones,
the vibrations will be audible in a call in form of echo and other unwanted artifacts. In
worst case vibrations from the loudspeaker can be heard as rattling in the meeting room
where the speakerphone is placed. The task is to design a speakerphone that has a chassis
that sets the best conditions for the signal processing to work.
The very first steps in the design process is virtual prototyping and simulations. The
simulation of the speakerphone as a whole, will most like be done using finite element
(FE)-model[1]. Using FE-models opens the opportunity for optimization on acoustic el-
ements such as waveguides and phase plugs[2][3][4]. The loudspeaker part of the speak-
erphone simulation are most commonly modeled as lumped parameter (LP)-model[5] or
as FE-model. Both methods have their advantages and drawbacks, and depending on the
application one method is chosen over the other. Keeping in mind that the loudspeaker is
provided by a supplier and its characteristics are mostly unknown. The advantages of the
LP-model is that the loudspeaker can be characterized fast using commercial equipment
such as the Klippel system[6]. The disadvantage of the LP-model is that the model of the
loudspeaker becomes inaccurate above the first mechanical resonance, this is because the
LP-model assumes piston movement [5]. The advantage of using a FE-model to simulate
the loudspeaker is that they can be very accurate in a wide range of frequencies. The
loudspeaker is unfortunately a very complex component to model using FE-model. The
challenge with FE-models is to tune them to the loudspeaker that the supplier as provided.
This tuning is very cumbersome and very time-consuming, which makes it very expen-
sive. Another drawback of the FE loudspeaker model approach is that the method is very
computationally heavy compared to the LP loudspeaker model. This can be a problem if
optimization is one of intentions in the design process. This is why hybrid methods have
been developed where the electromagnetic part of the loudspeaker is a LP-model and the
rest is modeled with FE[7][8].
Recently there has been a development showing that it is possible to simulate the sound
radiation from the loudspeaker using Laser-Doppler-Vibrometer(LDV) scans[9, VII. A].
The method scans discrete points on the membrane of the loudspeaker in a wide frequency
range, then converts the velocity response to a sound pressure response.
This sound
pressure response is then imported as a boundary condition in the simulation. This LDV
approach has the advantage of being computationally light and being very precise in a wide
frequency range. This makes LDV approach almost perfect for design using optimization.
There are of course no silver bullets in engineering, and the drawback of the LDV approach
is that there is no mechanical vibration response associated with it.
The aim of the thesis is to:

• explore the possibility of making a mechanical method that can compliment the LDV
approach,
• investigate the feasibility of making a measurement setup that can characterize the

Measuring and characterizing mechanical vibrations are very common within in the au-
tomotive industry and and heavy industry[10][11][12]. These fields often have to comply
with regulations which is why there are standards for measuring and characterizing sources
of structureborne sound[13][14]. The approach in ISO:20270[13] is very closely related to
the methods used in Transfer Path Analysis (TPA). It is of course important that the
characterization of the source is universal, so it can be used in simulations. Substructur-
ing is when the components of the mechanical system is characterized separately, but then
simulated together. Some prior work show promise that it is possible to do substructuring
using characterization found with TPA[10][11][12][15][16].
The shaker was chosen as an analogue for the loudspeaker, to simplify the problem. A
loudspeaker usually have 4-6 connection points to a cabinet in form of screws, these con-
nection points would add several unnecessary diﬀiculties. One of the diﬀiculties include
more equations to solve in the in-situ method as explained in section 2.5 . More connec-
tion points would also complicate the measuring procedure, as it would take more sensors
to measure the forces and velocities. Then there is also the question whether the con-
nection points represent the actual interface between the loudspeaker and the cabinet. It
is more likely that the forces from the loudspeaker is distributed along the edge of the
loudspeaker mounting. In Figure 1.1 cross-sectional diagrams of the loudspeaker and the
shaker is illustrated. The diagrams show that there are a lot of similarities between the
two sources. Both have a permanent magnet which is formed in a way that it concentrates
the magnetic flux in the gap where the voice coil/drive coil is placed. The voice coil/drive
coil is attached to the diaphragm/table, which then moves when a voltage is applied.

Theory
The following chapter will provide the necessary theoretical background needed to read
this thesis. This thesis encompasses the topics of mechanical vibrations, acoustics, sig-
nal processing and transfer path analysis. Section 2.1 outlines the lumped loudspeaker
model for a simplified description of the loudspeakers behaviour, and the limitations of
this approach of loudspeaker modeling. Section 2.1 will also aid to highlight the compo-
nents of the loudspeaker which has a dominating influence on the vibrational response
of the loudspeaker. Section 2.2 serves to introduce the concept of vibrations in simple
systems. First a single degree of freedom system is presented, in order to explain how
the components in the system influence the response of the system. The single degree of
freedom also serves the purpose to introduce the mechanical quantities and the frequency
response functions (FRF). The single degree of freedom system is expanded to a multiple
degree of freedom system, to demonstrate how a system of multiple equations can be rep-
resented on matrix form. In section 2.3 the classical way of measuring the reactive forces
of a mechanical source is explained, and the limitations are highlighted. The reactive
forces analysis is fundamental to the component based transfer path analysis. Section 2.4
presents the main methods utilized in this thesis for characterizing the mechanical source.
The aim of section 2.4 is to explain the background for transfer path analysis (TPA) and
how component based TPA can characterize an independent mechanical source. Section
2.5.1 provides a brief overview over the calculations needed for the component based TPA
from previous section. Section 2.6 serves as a reminder of the very basic concepts of signal
processing necessary to read section 2.7 about stationary random signals, and to explain
some choices in the measurement setup in chapter 3. Finally section 2.7 presents the type
of excitation signal used in the measurement in chapter 3, the section explains the unique
methods needed when using stationary random noise.
2.1
Lumped Loudspeaker Model
One of the most common ways to model the loudspeaker, is with a lumped parameter
model. The electrical, mechanical and acoustic attributes of the loudspeaker can be mod-
eled with analogues electronic components. In Figure 2.1 the analogous circuits are drawn.
Figure 2.1a is the electrical part of the loudspeaker, where eg is the amplifier voltage con-
nected to the loudspeaker with the output resistance Rg. RE is the DC resistance of the
voice coil, with LE(ω) being the inductance of the voice coil. R′
E is a corrective resistance
that adjust for the losses caused by the eddy current in the voice coil. The displacement
of the voice coil caused by the mechanical velocity uD is inducing a voltage which is mod-
eled by the source ec = BluD, where B is the magnetic flux density in the air gap and
l is the length of the wire in the voice coil. Figure 2.1b is the analogues circuit to the
mechanical part of the loudspeaker, where the force on the diaphragm is modeled by the
source fD = Blic. The mass of the diaphragm and the voice coil assembly is modeled by
MMD, while the compliance of the diaphragm suspension is modeled by CMS and RMS is
the losses in the suspension. fa = pDSD is the acoustic force generated by the difference
in pressure on the diaphragm, where SD is the piston area of the diaphragm. ZAF and
ZAB are the acoustic impedance on the front and back respectively. Finally UD is the
radiated volume velocity from the diaphragm.

Looking at Figure 2.1 the area of interest for characterizing the loudspeaker as a mechanical

fD = Blic is the force on the diaphragm there must be an equal and opposite force which
propagates through the supporting structure/housing into the contact points between the
loudspeaker and cabinet.
The lumped loudspeaker model does have its limitations. First limitation is that the model
is a small signal assumption, so that the speaker is operating in its linear range. But more
important to this project the lumped loudspeaker model is limited in its frequency range.
The lumped speaker model assumes that the diaphragm behaves like a rigid piston, which is
only true for low frequencies. Above the first mechanical resonance the piston assumption
breaks down, due to break-up modes in the diaphragm[5]. It is possible to simulate these
break-up modes, and achieve a higher accuracy in a wider frequency range. However, it
requires a FE-model of the entire loudspeaker structure. The FE-model is not always the
best solution, because it is expensive in both cost and computations. Because of these
limiting factors of the lumped model an empirical approach is necessary to extend the
frequency range for simulations.

Linear Mechanical Systems
A linear mechanical system can be described mathematically by one or more differential
equations of motion. A linear mechanical system is a good way of describing simple vibra-
tions and some concepts carry over to more complex systems. The mechanical quantities
and frequency response functions will also be introduced.

2.4
Transfer Path Analysis
Transfer Path Analysis (TPA) is a technique that is used to characterize and analyse a
vibrating system based on a combination of FRFs, vibration responses and force measure-
ments. The most simple transfer path problem is two connected components A and B,
where A is the active component and B is the passive component. The rigidly connected
assembly is referred to as AB. The advantage over a method like reactive forces method in
section 2.3 is that, it is theoretically possible to separate the contributions from the active
component and the passive component[23]. There are two families of TPA presented in
this thesis, first a classical TPA and secondly a component based TPA. They can be clas-
sified by their approach and work flow. The classical TPA approach results in interface
forces specific for assembly AB, while component TPA results in equivalent forces specific
for subsystem A.

2.7
Stationary Random Signals
Noise signals are by definition stochastic, meaning that they cannot be described mathe-
matically nor be predicted at all points in time, contrary to deterministic signals. Noise
signals can however, be described statistically.
The Gaussian distribution is the most
common distribution for noise signals. A noise signal is considered to be stationary if the
statistical properties of the signal does not change over time. A common stationary noise
signal is the white noise signal. The main characteristic of white noise is that it has a flat
power spectral density, in other words, constant mean square value per hertz.[21] White
noise cannot be Fourier transformed like periodic signals, because it is stochastic. Instead
white noise is analysed by its power spectrum.[25]

3.1.3
Impact Hammer
The impact hammer used is a B&K Type 8202 [30]. The impact hammer can be used
in conjunction with an accelerometer to measure the frequency response function. The
impact hammer measures the force from the impact with a built-in force transducer and
the structural response is measured by the accelerometers. In that way the input force and
the output acceleration is known and the frequency response function can be derived. The
execution of a hammer strike has to be performed with a certain finesse. The frequency
bandwidth of the frequency response function depends on the duration at which the ham-
mer has contact with the structure. The width of the impulse is inversely proportional
with the frequency bandwidth. The impact hammer has a few disadvantages to be aware
of. The nature of the impact hammer is that it can have a short and narrow peak, which
means it can drive the test structures out of its linear range. This means that this method
is not suitable for inherently non linear systems. There can also be a large variation be-
tween impacts leading to inconsistencies in measurements. For this reason measurements
with an impact hammer is often repeated and averaged.

usable frequency response functions.

bad coherence. The ImpactGui allows the user to choose with a click of the mouse which
impacts to keep. When selecting and de-selecting the frequency response and the coherence

In Figure 4.1 the force
power spectrum of the measurement is shown. The force power spectrum is derived with
measurement method described in Equation 2.38. The measurement of the force in the

In Figure 4.2 a comparison of the estimated blocked force using the free velocity method is

measurement in the steel table, shows the estimate somewhat captures the overall trend
from 10 Hz to 4 kHz. While the estimate does follow the overall trend it is significantly off
target and over estimating the blocked force from 40 Hz to 3 kHz. At almost exactly 5 kHz
the estimate has a resonance while the benchmark has the resonance at 8 kHz. But the
shape of the estimate does not indicate that it is only shifted compared to the benchmark
of the blocked force. The shape of the estimate is completely different to the benchmark
over 4 kHz. The reason for the discrepancies could be many, but two likely candidates
are the method and how the method was used with the shaker. The reason the shaker
could cause these discrepancies is that the shaker is small and sensitive to the hammer
impact when measuring the frequency response function. During the frequency function
measurement the shaker would movement considerably, this could cause noise in other
directions than the ”table” of the shaker. But as previously mentioned this method is
designed and intended for a different purpose, namely structural acoustics in the industry
characterize larger machines and equipment. But doing this type of measurement on large

Discussion
5.1
Measurement Setup
A lot of valuable time was spend on the measurement setup. Some of the issues could
potentially have been solved by using the dedicated software of the instruments. One of
the big issues was the calibration of the force transducer and by extension the impact
hammer.
It took an incredible amount of time to figure out a robust and repeatable
calibration procedure for the force transducer.
The other big challenge was to make
reliable and repeatable measurements with the impact hammer. A lot of effort was put
into making a script that could process the impact hammer recordings. Fortunately the
Abravibe toolbox was recommended in the end, which played an indispensable role in
measuring reliable FRFs. The LDV was the reason why a bespoke Matlab solution was
chosen to begin with. This was because the ambition was to use the LDV in tandem with
the impact hammer, however there was an issue with B&K’s software ”Pulse”. Another
advantage of Matlab is that the data processing would be in Matlab, this meant that
tedious format conversions was avoided. Matlab also made it possible to make a sequence
for recording the FRFs and operational measurements in direct succession.
This was
very important when making 9 recording pairs of FRFs and operational measurements.
It would have been very tempting to do all the FRFs first and then all the operational
measurements afterwards. I believe that it is absolutely crucial that the accelerometer
is not moved between the FRF measurement and operational measurement. I believe in
retrospect that the benefits of using Matlab instead of Pulse, out weighted the drawbacks.
The excitation of the source should have been according to the standards, the operational
excitation from the source. Meaning that the engine or the machine is turned on and
produce the vibrations that it would under normal functioning conditions. But for the
shaker this natural operational state does not exist, it had to be defined. White noise was
chosen because it is the most common approach in structural acoustics, to characterize
structures with the frequency response function.
However, for the operational step in
the methods it was not the frequency response function that should be measured but the
absolute acceleration or force. If I were to repeat the measurements I would instead make
sure that the absolute acceleration was measured.
A more professional sound card like a RME Fireface UCX could perhaps have made a
difference to the outcome of the measurements. Mostly because it has eight inputs and
output, making it more likely reference signals were saved. The reference signals were
important because the excitation signal from the shaker was stationary random noise, and
the measurements were carried out in a sequential manner, meaning each indicator point
was measured one after the other. If the excitation noise signal was the same for every
measurement, the phases of the measurements would align. However, the measurement
Matlab script generated new noise signals every time a measurement was recorded. An
option would have been to use a periodic excitation like a sinusoidal sweep instead of
stationary random noise. With a periodic signal the excitation would be the same for
every measurement, and the phases would align between them. Another issue with the
stationary random noise is that ”Stationary random signals cannot be Fourier transformed
and have no spectrum in the same sense as transients or periodic signals have. The spectral
properties of a stationary random signal is described in terms of its power spectrum.”[25,
p. 19] As previously explained in section 2.7 the power spectra of the signals are not
complex, while the standard ISO:20270 specifically requires complex indicator velocity and
Characterization of the Loudspeaker as a Mechanical Source

==PAGE:60==
complex mobility[13, p. 4, 6]. While the impression looking at the results in Figure 4.9 and
Figure 4.10, is that the validation of the estimates very good despite not being complex.
Even though the estimate might not be influenced by not being complex, it does limit the
analysis because the phase of the measurements are unknown.
The Scarlett 2i2 Focusrite sound card is also known to be noisy and potential leakage
between the channels. A measurement for an exercise in the course ”22001 Acoustic signal

noise. The noise could have been an issue with the estimates as previously mentioned in

5.2
Method Used for Estimates
The blocked force method was presented in subsection 2.4.3, and is the most simple of
all the method explored in this project. The assumption is that the force measured be-
tween the active component and an infinitely stiff boundary, is the equivalent force that
satisfy Equation 2.34. It is impossible for me to verify that the blocked force measured
in Figure 4.1 is actually the blocked force of the shaker. However, given the similarity
to the force at the interface of the box and shaker in Figure 4.13, I believe it is a fair
assumption to make. The major difference between the two measurements is a 2 dB dif-
ference in magnitude. This difference in magnitude also suggests that the force measured
in the steel table is close to the blocked condition. The advantages of the blocked force
method is that the approach only requires one measurement, that is it. There is no need
for two different types of measurements that have to be repeated for the estimation to be
overdetermined. And because there are no calculations the result is not sensitive to noise
the same way as the estimates. The measurement setup is very simple as it only requires
a force transducer per contact point.
The free velocity method was outlined in subsection 2.4.4. The free velocity method applies
the opposite boundary condition compared to the blocked force method. The free velocity
estimates the equivalent force using the inverse input mobility and the free velocity. All
the measurements for this method has the active component uncoupled. The free velocity
method estimated the overall trend of the blocked force as seen in Figure 4.2. The free
velocity method was unable to estimate the finer details of the blocked force especially
above 4 kHz. The root cause of the discrepancy is most likely the way that the FRF of
the shaker was measured. The shaker was suspended in a bungee cord as illustrated in
Figure 3.7. This setup made the shaker very sensitive to the FRF measurement with the
impact hammer. During the measurement the shaker would move considerably in other
directions than the operational movement of the shakers ”table”. This means that the
FRF is not representative of the actual FRF. For this reason I believe the free method
would be the least suited method for characterizing a loudspeaker.
The in-situ method that was defined in section 2.5. The in-situ method assumes that the
equivalent force can be estimated in the fixture that the active component is normally
operating in. The in-situ estimate is based on a solution for an overdetermined system.
This approach makes the in-situ method the most complex and convoluted method in-
vestigated in this thesis. Two estimates of the equivalent force were calculated using the
in-situ method. The first estimate used indicator points all-over the box, where the second
estimate only used 4 indicator points on the bottom of the box. The two estimates were
so close they were almost identical as shown in Figure 4.8. The estimate using the bottom
indicator points were less noisy, but only slightly. The validation step from the ISO:20270
standard was used to validate the two equivalent force estimates. Figure 4.11 showed that
the two equivalent force estimates could accurately predict the acceleration measured at
48

validation point 8 on the box. The validation step is very similar to the approach that
would be used to predict vibrations in new assemblies. One of the most interesting out-
comes in this thesis might be how well the validation step could predict the acceleration.
This could be an indicator of how plausible it is to use the equivalent force, to predict
vibrations in new assemblies. The equivalent force estimates were compared to the force
measured between the shaker and box in Figure 4.12. The force estimates align very well
with the coupled force albeit more noisy. Both the estimates and the coupled force were
compared with the measured blocked force in Figure 4.13 and Figure 4.14. Because the

is also much wider than typical application, for instance the first eigenfrequency of a wind
turbine column is approximately 0.1 Hz.

Conclusion
In this project three potential methods for characterizing a loudspeaker has been investi-
gated. During the investigation the dedicated measurement setups for each method were
fabricated and described. In this process the potential pitfalls of the methods were high-
lighted, so they can be avoided going forward. Using empirical evidence the methods were
evaluated in their performance and limitations. The limitations of the different methods
were evaluated in terms of accuracy, frequency range and practicality. The blocked forces
method and the in-situ method were the most promising methods explored. Depending
on practical considerations one method can be preferred over the other. This project lays
a foundation for characterization of loudspeakers as mechanical sources, that can develop
into simulations of vibrations in speakerphone chassis and the like.
There is still much work to do in this field of characterizing the loudspeaker as a mechanical
source. The following is suggestions for future work.
6.1
Further Work
For future work there are two paths that are yet to be explored. The first is adapting the
methods for a loudspeaker. In this project the assumption has been that if a method works
for the B&K Mini-shaker it would work for a loudspeaker. The second path is applying a
characterization of a loudspeaker in a simulation to predict vibrations in chassis.
6.1.1
Setup for Loudspeakers
The loudspeaker usually have 4-6 mounting points, contrary to the shakers one contact
point. For the blocked method this would mean 4-6 force transducers needed for the mea-
surement. This is a potential issues due to the cost of professional grade force transducers.
Perhaps an assumption of equal force across all mounting points would be suﬀicient, and
reduce the number of force transducers to one. Another consideration for the loudspeaker
is that the contact point is not necessarily the mounting points, as for the shaker. The con-
tact point is usually a larger area on the outer perimeter loudspeaker housing. This could
perhaps be solved by interpolating the force response between the mounting points. Re-
garding the blocked method; another subject for consideration is a dedicated test mounting
to make it suﬀiciently stiff.
For the in-situ method the number of measurements scales with the number of contact
points. The suﬀicient number of indicator points to contact points have to be explored.
In ISO:20270 [13, Annex A] an example is shown where 8 indicator points are used for 4
contact points. Again the question is, what is the contact points for a traditional loud-
speaker, and how will that translate to this method. The benefit of the in-situ method is,
there is no need for specialized test mounting. The in-situ method can in theory be used
in a mockup or prototype, but how well does that work for a loudspeaker.

6.1.2
Simulation Using Loudspeaker Characterization
The initial ambition of the project was to also do simulations using the measured/estimated
force response. The plan was to simply import the force response as a boundary condition
in the FE simulation. The idea was to verify a simulation of the acceleration in the box
used in this project. A COMSOL model of the box was made and setup for this approach,
see Appendix A.4. This is the same approach which is used for the LDV method for the
sound pressure radiation. However, it might take more steps to do something similar with
the force response. In ISO:20270 [13, Annex E] guidelines for using the estimated force to
predict vibrations in a new assembly are presented. Although it states clearly that it is
not the intended purpose of the standard, it still gives some useful information. The FRFs
of the new assembly including the source/active component is necessary. The ISO:20270
have three suggestions on how to derive these new FRFs. Direct measurements is the first
suggestion, meaning that the new assembly is created and measured. This is assumed to be
the most reliable according to the standard. However, it is not a very practical approach for
virtual prototyping and simulations. The standard also suggests calculations, but without
any further details than that.
Lastly the suggestion is to use ”experimental dynamic
substructuring”, which seems to be the most applicable for simulations. Substructuring
means that the FRFs of the individual components are measured separately and combined
afterwards. Getting the FRF of the passive component could be trivial using FE software.
But as the free velocity method showed in section 4.1, measuring the FRF of the source was
very diﬀicult. There are still plenty of obstacles before the loudspeaker characterization
can be used in simulations, but there are a lot of potential in these methods.
