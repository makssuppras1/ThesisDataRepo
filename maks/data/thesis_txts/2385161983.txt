Abstract
Electricity markets are designed in such a way that renewable producers need to use the
best possible forecasts to place their day-ahead bids. The aim of the thesis is to investigate
the potential improvement of using time-adaptive and regime-switching approaches com-
pared to state of the art forecasting models. Other features like wind direction dependency
or spatio-temporal relationships between wind farms are also explored. For this purpose,
several predictive models are developed for horizons between 0 and 47 hours ahead in 15
minute-resolution steps. A distinction is made between short term (0-6 hours ahead) and
long term models (6-47). All models are evaluated within a deterministic framework aiming
to reduce Root Mean Square Error (RMSE). It is found that the time-adaptive strategies
prove to have lower RMSE than state of the art models in both short and long term fore-
casts. By contrast, regime-switching approaches show a clear advantage in very short term
horizons, i.e. less than one hour ahead. Moreover, wind direction dependency is identiﬁed
as a key feature for long term forecasts while spatio-temporal relationships showed a great
improvement for short term forecasts. These results support that the use of time-adaptive
strategies are likely to improve performance of conventional forecasting models in all lead
times, while the biggest beneﬁt of regime-switching approaches is lies probably in very short
term forecast.

Chapter 1
Overview of the forecasting problem
Renewable energies have developed exponentially in the past decades, being wind power the most
popular among them. The total world-wide installed capacity at the end of 2016 reached 486 GW
out of which 254 GW were installed in 2016, [1]. Only in Germany the installed capacity at the end
of that year was 50 GW, being more than 5 GW installed in the last year. This makes Germany one
of the countries with highest density of installed wind power (143 kW/km2) and the third country
in the world (after China and United States) in absolute installed capacity. Nevertheless, in spite
of this large deployment of wind farms, the share of wind power in the German power mix was only
around 12% in 2016, [2]. Furthermore, oﬀshore wind farms are becoming increasingly important
as most of the on-shore sites are already exploited. This is happening especially in countries where
the wind penetration in the power system is more signiﬁcant, namely Denmark, Germany and Spain.
However, it is well known that one of the biggest challenges of renewable sources is their uncer-
tainty and variability in production. As the wind power producers place their bids in the day-ahead
market 12-36 hours in advance, the use of forecasts is inevitable. However, electricity markets such
as NordPool (Nordic power system) and EEX (central-Europe power system) are designed in a
way that bad forecasts are penalized because this uncertainty and variability directly aﬀects the
system imbalances. More speciﬁcally, a two-price system makes that any deviation between the
real production and the day-ahead bids (forecast based) is penalized in a later intra-day market.
What is more, in the intra-day market, short term forecasts play also an important role since the
bids are placed with an horizon of minutes to few hours ahead. The Germany electricity market is
part of the aforementioned EEX system and thus it is aﬀected by the rules of the two-price system.
For deeper understanding of the electricity markets please refer to Morales Juan M. et al. [3] and
Pinson [4].
For the above mentioned reasons there is a big incentive for wind power producers to have as
good forecast as possible. Also, from a system perspective, wind penetration in the power mix
is somehow linked to the aforementioned penalties. Thus, improving wind power forecasts in the
short-medium horizon (0 to 48 hours) is a clear way of increasing the value of wind energy within
the power system. What is more, wind power forecasting has been identiﬁed as one of the top
priorities in wind power research [5].
One of the companies in the ﬁeld of wind power forecasting is ConWX , who have collaborated
with this study providing the data. ConWX is a private company supplier of weather and power
forecasts all over the world. Among other renewable energies, they provide forecasts to a large

1.1
State of the art
Typically, wind power prediction models can be classiﬁed in two groups: those that use Numerical
Weather Predictions (NWP) as input, and those that only need historic power observations, referred
as time series models, [6]. The choice on whether it is worth the eﬀort to use NWP depends on the
forecast horizon. Usually, times series models perform much better in short forecast horizons (up
to 3-6 hours), since they are purely based on the last power observations available. On the other
hand, NWP-based predictors clearly outperform time series models in forecast horizons above 3-6
hours. In the short-term forecasts, apart from time series models another approach is to predict
wind behavior using only physical modelling approaches, i.e. ﬂuid dynamic approximations taking
into account the surrounding environment and conditions, [7]. Nonetheless, this models performs
usually very poorly and they need to be combined with statistical tools or other techniques to im-
prove the output to reduce the remaining error. This section presents the state of the art regarding
the ﬁrst two types of model: time series for short horizons and NWP-based models for long lead
times.
1.1.1
Time series models
The ﬁrst to address wind power forecasting from a time series perspective was Brown et al. [8],
using Auto-Regressive Moving Average models (ARMA) for long forecast horizons, up to some
days. Some more authors also investigated ARMA for shorter horizons, but in most cases the
autoregressive (AR) part is good enough and they can easily beat persistence, [9]. A review of
some variations of this AR models is done in Pinson [4]. A generic form of the autoregressive
models is

between regimes which are not always optimal. To avoid this, Smooth Transition autoregressive
models (STAR) are investigated so that the transitions between regimes are made gradually but
also guided by an observable variable. Lastly Markov-Switching AR (MSAR) models are investig-
ated, where the regime sequence is governed by an non-observable process, allowing to account for
some external factors, [11]. In this last model, the regime sequence is assumed to be a ﬁrst-order
Markov process, i.e. each state only depends on the previous one, and the transition probabilities
between the regimes is deﬁned by a transition matrix RxR. From the investigations in Pinson et al.
[10] it was clear that the MSAR outperformed conventional AR and both SETAR and STAR mod-
els, meaning that the Markov Switching approach can indeed account for external non-observable
complex processes of the atmosphere whereas the others can not.
Furthermore, an on-line learning version of the MSAR model was later developed in Pinson and
Madsen [12], so that the model parameters and the transition matrix can be calculated recursively
adjusting over time to possible changes in the underlying dynamics. The adaptive algorithm was
based on penalized maximum likelihood with exponential forgetting of past observations, and it
was developed for 10-minutes horizon with clear improvements respect to AR models.
A diﬀerent approach in the line of time series algorithms is to use autoregressive Artiﬁcial
Neural Networks (ANN) although the performance reached by them usually does not compensate
the eﬀort to train them. Another ﬂaw of the ANN is that they do not perform well in case of
extreme events if such events are not present in the training data, [13]. Moreover, unlike other
prediction models, once the ANN is trained it is very diﬃcult to understand the meaning of each
trained coeﬃcient, but it is more like a black box that process a set of inputs and returns outputs.
1.1.2
Predictions using external weather inputs
In contrast to time series models, this section presents the state of art of models that take forecast
of weather variables as inputs. As mentioned before they perform usually better than time series
models for long forecast horizons, i.e. above 3-6 hours ahead. The forecast of these variables is gen-
erated by Numerical Weather Prediction models (NWP) such as the one from ECMWF (European
Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts). The most inﬂuential variable in the conversion from
NWP variables to power is obviously wind speed, although this conversion is deﬁnitely not linear
but has a very characteristic shape denoted as the power curve.
An number of approaches for power curve modelling is done in Lydia et al. [14] including para-
metric and non-parametric methods. Furthermore, an extensive discussion is done in Shokrzadeh
et al. [15] about the use of local polynomial regression for power curve ﬁtting using wind speed
module as explanatory variable. Nevertheless, it is known that the power curve can be aﬀected

by the terrain complexity surrounding the wind farm: vegetation, mountains, cities... and this
complexity depends of course on the wind direction. It is therefore not surprising that a direction-

Moreover, the shape of the power curve is also subject to variations over time. This changes
can be due to variations in the vegetation, aging of the turbines, or more importantly: changes in

operations at each iteration to minimize computational cost. For further information about on-

[17]. Both directional and time dependency of power curves are addressed in Nielsen et al. [18],
where an adaptive local polynomial with exponential forgetting on two wind components is studied.
A possible approach to use regime-switching models and NWP is to use cluster-based methods
to to establish the regime sequence. A similar approach was used in Browell and Gilbert [19],
who developed an regime-switching autoregressive based on average observed wind speed module,
classifying the days into a number of regimes according to this average wind speed.

1.2.1
What is a good forecast?
The question What is a good forecast? was ﬁrst discussed in Murphy [22], where the author de-
scribed three types of goodness in forecasting and the relationships between the three of them:
consistency, quality and value.
A forecast is consistent when it reﬂects accurately the forecaster judgment. A forecaster judg-
ment always contains an element of uncertainty and therefore the forecasts must reﬂect this uncer-
tainty. This means that a probabilistic framework is always more consistent than a point forecast,
and even when a point forecast is handed, a measure of uncertainty must be given for example in
form of conﬁdence intervals. The second category, quality, refers to the degree of correspondence
between the forecast and the real values. To address this category, a set of error measures are
used to evaluate the forecasts either from a deterministic or probabilistic perspective. The third
goodness category described by the author is value. It measures the beneﬁts or expenses incurred
by the entity using the forecast in their particular decision making problem, as compared to a
situation in which the forecast is not used. It is noteworthy that the same forecast can have quite
diﬀerent value for diﬀerent decision-makers if they are dealing with diﬀerent problems. Moreover,
even for two entities dealing with the same decision-making problem the value of the forecast can
be diﬀerent if they have diﬀerent levels of information prior to the forecast. This is because the
value of the forecast refers to the incremental beneﬁt for decision-makers when using the forecast.
The relationships between the three types of goodness are also discussed in the mentioned pa-
per. The ﬁrst two relationships are quite straight forward: to achieve the highest quality and value,
the forecast must be as consistent as possible, i.e. communicating all the uncertainty in the fore-
cast. However, the relationship between quality and value is not as clear. If a suﬃciency relation
between two forecasts is proved, it means that one of them is better than the other in any possible
aspect studied. In this case, the one with highest quality will also have higher value. Nevertheless,

1.2.3
Training and test data
The data sets provided by ConWX include power observations and weather forecasts from October
1st, 2012 to March 15th, 2016. Nevertheless, the original dataset must be split in groups that serve
for diﬀerent purposes. A diﬀerentiation must be done between the time-adaptive algorithms and
the batch learning ones.
The on-line learning algorithms start with random model coeﬃcients and adapt over the entire
dataset. Thus, during the ﬁrst period they will perform very poorly until they stabilize and the
coeﬃcients reach reasonable values. For this reason, the data of 2012 and 2013 is used as burn-in
period, in which the algorithms are not evaluated. Right after that, year 2014 is used for tuning the
model meta-parameters, since it is assumed that the algorithms had time to stabilize during the
burn-in period and they are fully performing now. This yellow period allows comparison between
diﬀerent sets of meta-parameters before the ﬁnal evaluation is done in the test set. The errors
resulting in the tuning data must never be used as a performance metric since it is likely to over-ﬁt
the data, [23]. Lastly, the various algorithms are evaluated on the Test set, years 2015 and 2016.

2.3
Basic features in the data
2.3.1
Autocorrelation and partial autocorrelation in power series
The autocorrelation of a time series at lag k is deﬁned as the correlation of that series with a
delayed version of itself k time stamps. A power series is expected to have high correlation only
for small lags and may be some every 24 hours accounting for daily variations. Figure 2.5 (left
column) shows the autocorrelation plots of two sample plants.
In general, a partial correlation is deﬁned as a conditional correlation. More speciﬁcally, the
partial autocorrelation at lag k, it is deﬁned as the autocorrelation of a series at lag k given the
values between lags 1 and k −1. This is shown in the middle and right columns of ﬁg. 2.5, (the
right plot is just a zoom for small lags).
The autocorrelation plot of the power series have the characteristic shape of a non stationary
series. Some oscillations can be seen as if there were some correlation at certain lags (3 days, 6
days...) but it might be just noise. On the other hand, the partial autocorrelation reveals that
there is a strong correlation at lag 1, meaning that each value is strongly correlated with the
previous (and next) values. There is also some signiﬁcant (but much lower) correlations at lag 3.
If autoregressive models are investigated, the optimal order is likely to be 1, but something might
be gained using also 2 and 3 lags.

3.1.3
Time-adaptive autoregressive models
A time-adaptive model is a model which coeﬃcients can vary over time to adjust to new system
characteristics. As the autoregressive model is build as a linear model, an adaptive autoregressive
model can be build using a time-adaptive linear model explained below. Moreover, its application
goes from making AR models time-adaptive, to generate dynamic linear combination of model
outputs, that will be explained in latter chapters.

3.1.4
Spatial autoregressive
The spatial autoregressive model aims to make use of the spatio-temporal correlation structure
between plants. If predictions are made for the entire portfolio using all lagged power series as
input variables, we would talk about vector autoregressive model. Nevertheless, a simpler version
is developed here in which predictions are only generated for one plant. This model is baptized
spatial autoregressive, and the motivation to develop this model is to investigate if the use of several
correlated wind farms improves substantially the performance of the autoregressive models respect
to only using the own plant lagged values.
In the spatial autoregressive model, the lagged power series of a selected portfolio F are used
to predict power of a certain plant i. A 1st order spatial autoregressive uses only 1st order lags of
all plants, and therefore can be expressed as

3.2
Long term forecast
Long term models are intended to make predictions from 3-6 hours ahead up to 48 (in this report).
As predictions are too far away in the future, historic power observations are not that relevant
anymore. Instead, these models must estimate the conversion between wind speed forecast and
power. For this purpose, the simplest relationship we could draw is a simple linear model but it
is well known that power and wind speed are not linearly related, but with the so-called power
curve instead. The characteristic shape of a theoretical power curve starts with a 3rd degree poly-
nomial, and then it ﬂattens for a certain rated power before dropping down to zero at a cut oﬀspeed.
From the approaches described in section 1.1, for modelling power curves, local polynomial
regression is chosen for long term models. This section is structured as follows: ﬁrst the refer-
ence model used for long term models (climatology) is explained. Second, simple power curves
are modelled using local polynomial regression in one variable (non-direction dependant). Third,
direction dependency is included in the local polynomial regression with two input variables (wind
components). Then, the previous model is made time-adaptive in a similar fashion as the recursive
linear model of section 3.1.3. Finally, the last two subsections are dedicated to investigate the use
of three NWP in several ways.

and the distance between ﬁtting points (or resolution of the grid). It is assumed that the last one
is not correlated to the other two, and therefore its optimization can be done independently. How-
ever, it has a huge inﬂuence on the computational time, meaning that the higher resolution (lower
distance between ﬁtting points) increases exponentially the computational time. An example of
this relationship is shown in ﬁg. 3.4a for a speciﬁc wind farm keeping all the rest of the parameters

acceptable range, see ﬁg. 3.4b. The RMSE appears to be rather stable along the studied values of
the distance between ﬁtting points. This allows to use reasonable distance between ﬁtting points
to minimize computational time in the optimization of the remaining parameters. Note that this
plots are generated for one sample wind farm and that the speciﬁc values may vary signiﬁcantly

3.2.5
Model upgrades
The time-adaptive local polynomial regression in two variables is from now on referred as Local
Polynomial Model, or LPM . Taking this model as a basis, a number of possible variations (and
potential improvements) are hereafter proposed.
Dynamic forgetting factor λ
The forgetting factor regulates the weight given at any given time to the old and new observations
when updating the local constant estimates ˆφ. Small values will force fast adaptation whereas large
values will lead to smoother and slower adaptation. However, the optimization of the forgetting
factor together with the bandwidth takes too long and it can not be optimized at every lead time
in every plant. Moreover, the optimum value of λ may be diﬀerent for diﬀerent points in time.
For instance, if the model is working perfectly with the current coeﬃcients, λ should have a large
value so that the parameters are barely updated. On the other hand, if the model starts to perform
clearly worse for some time it means that the dynamics may have changed, and then parameters
must be adapted quickly to the new system characteristics, thus low values of λ are needed.

3.2.7
Principal Components Analysis (PCA)
PCA on a single wind farm
So far, the LPM uses as inputs two wind speed components from any of the three NWP. However,
the NWP forecasts are very likely to be correlated as they are predicting the same two physical
variables. Thus, it seems reasonable to try dimensionality reduction on the weather data using
Principal Components Analysis.
Each NWP generates predictions of two wind components that conform a dataset of m obser-
vations and n variables, i.e. m data points in an n-dimension space. The PCA aims to ﬁnd a new
set of orthogonal axis (principal components) that maximizes the amount of original information
represented by each component. If several variables are correlated, it is possible to reduce the
dimension of the dataset by projecting it into this new orthogonal components. The number of
components after dimensionality reduction depends on how much of the original variance needs to
be explained, but the ﬁrst component always explains more variance than the second, the second
more than the third and so on. For the problem at hand, the Python library scikit-learn [26],
was used for the implementation of the Principal Component Analysis. For a more detailed ex-
planation of the mathematics behind PCA, please refer to Jauregui [27].
Six weather variables contain 3 predictions for each of the two wind components, therefore it
is reasonable to expect that two principal components explain a big percentage of the variance of
the original data. Indeed, applying PCA on Plant 162, the ﬁrst component explained alone above
68% of the variance, whereas the second variable explained around 28%. Together they were able
to explain more than 97% of the original variance, thus it was decided to reduce the dimension to
two components. In ﬁg. 3.7 it is shown some plots of how the power is correlated to the original
wind components in one of the NWP models (ecm, upper plots) and how it is correlated to the
ﬁrst two principal components (bottom plots).
Apart from having diﬀerent scale, it is clear the relationship between power and the u compon-
ent is narrower after applying PCA (left column). The same can not be said about the v component
from PCA which looks even less correlated to power than the original v (right column). This is
however normal, since the new axis try to maximize the variance explained by the ﬁrst component.
This also suggest that in the original weather data the variance was explained more evenly between
the two wind components, while using PCA most of it is explained by the ﬁrst component, i.e. u.
Once dimensionality reduction is applied, the LPM can be run using the new two principal
components as explanatory variables. As the scale of the components has changed, the bandwidth
and distance between ﬁtting points must be adjusted to the new conditions. For having a fair
comparison, the distance between ﬁtting points were adjusted so that the same number of ﬁtting

Regime-switching
4.1
Introduction to regime-switching models
A system which behavior switch quickly between a ﬁnite number of types is suitable to be described
by regime-switching models. Regime switching models gather a ﬁnite number of number set of
parameters, that are chosen depending on the current regime of the system. In the present work,
only regime-switching models with similar structure in all regimes are addressed. Moreover, the
switching mechanism between the diﬀerent regimes can be deﬁned in two ways. On the one hand
it can be governed by observable variables like power, or weather variables. On the other hand, the
so-called Hidden Markov Models are governed by a non-observable (hidden) variable that follows a
Markov Process. In this chapter, the Hidden Markov models are ﬁrst explained, with its applications
to short and long term forecasts. Later, a couple of approaches based on observable variables are
explained for long term forecasts, including clustering of weather variables.
4.2
Hidden Markov Model
Hidden Markov models (HMM) is a type of regime-switching model in which the regime sequence
is governed by a hidden Markov process. Let us deﬁne ﬁrst what a Markov Process is. Consider a
sequence of random variables {St : t ∈T} with t = {1, 2, ..., N} being N the number of observations.
The random variable S can only take a ﬁnite number of integer numbers called states {1, 2, ..., R},
being R the maximum number of states. From now on, the states are referred also as regimes. The
realizations of the random variable {s1, s2, ...st, ..., sT } deﬁne a regime sequence where each of those
realizations can take discrete values from the set of possible states. An R-state Markov process
consists on a sequence of realizations of S in which the probability of being in a certain state at
time t depends only on the previous state t −1, i.e.

4.3.1
Estimation of MSAR parameters
The estimation of the parameters in the MSAR models has some complications since the regime
sequence is not known a priory (it is hidden).
Thus not only is it necessary to estimate the
AR coeﬃcients and transition probability matrix but to also infer the probability of being in any
regime at each time t. In order to do so, the Expectation Maximization algorithm is used, [10],
which is based on maximization of the conditional log-likelihood of the set of parameters given
the observations. Although the likelihood is not explicitly maximized in the equations, the EM
algorithm asymptotically maximizes it [29]. This iterative algorithm works in two steps: in the
Expectation step the probabilities of being in each regime are calculated with the current set of
model parameters, while in the Maximization step, some update equations are used in order to re-
calculate them. In this section, a ﬁrst part is devoted to explain the optimal inference of the regimes
(expectation step), a second part is used to explain maximization step, and a third subsection for
summarizing the EM algorithm.

Summary of the EM algorithm
First the algorithm is initialized with some random model parameters: AR coeﬃcients, variances
and transition probability matrices. The initial variances should be chosen big enough in order to
avoid numerical underﬂow. The transition matrix is initialized so that eq. (4.1) is met. Once all
parameters have been initialized, the two following steps are repeated iteratively until a certain
convergence criteria is met:
• Expectation step: given the current set of model parameters of each regime, the probability
of being in each of them is estimated using the ﬁltered probabilistic inference (or Hamilton
ﬁlter), eq. (4.10).
• Maximization step: the ﬁltered probabilities are ﬁrst smoothed using the smooth probabilistic
inference, eq. (4.13). This tells us the probability of being in each regime given the information
contained in the entire training set, ΩT . Using the smoothed probabilities as weights, we can
re-estimate the transition probability matrix with eq. (4.14), the new AR coeﬃcients with
eq. (4.16) and the variances with eq. (4.18).
The convergence criteria here is to keep iterating until no signiﬁcant improvement is seen in the
log-likelihood. Recall that the log-likelihood can be computed in each iteration using eq. (4.11). It
is noteworthy that the EM algorithm is quite sensitive to the initialization of the parameters. Bad
initialization ends up in local maximums thus the algorithm must be initialized several times with
random parameters and chose the simulation with highest ﬁnal log-likelihood.

This chapter is dedicated to evaluate and compare the diﬀerent models explained in the previous

Input selection: principal component analysis
A complete diﬀerent approach to make use of the three NWP is to directly extract features from
the three NWP before using any prediction algorithm. This is referred here as input selection and
the method used (explained in section 3.2.7) is Principal Component Analysis.
When the PCA was used on one single site, it was used to extract two representative variables
from the three NWP. This two variables (principal components) were used as input for the local
polynomial regression method. The other version of the PCA consists on extracting two represent-
ative components from all the wind components predicted in a set of correlated NWP models. It
is important to notice that in this section Plant 032 was substituted by Plant 039. The reason for
this change is that Plant 039 was the plant with highest correlations with other wind farms of the
portfolio. This makes this plant the most suitable for using the PCA on a grid of NWP.
PCA on single site
First, the performance of simple PCA on one site is compared to the weights OLS per lead time,
ﬁg. 5.27. The bottom plots of that ﬁgure show the performance of the three output predictions
using NWP at the two plants. The ﬁrst and most interesting detail to see from these plots is
the clear diﬀerence between the two plants. While the PCA in Plant 162 yields clearly a worse
performance, Plant 039 sees some improvement for certain lead times, specially for the ﬁrst 18 hours.
It is diﬃcult to argue what is the cause for such a diﬀerence between plants.
A possible

the principal components of the three NWP regardless of which one performs better, and if one
of them performs badly, the PCA are aﬀected negatively by it.
It is reasonable then that the

Chapter 6
Conclusions and future work
6.1
Conclusions
This chapter is dedicated to summarize some of the conclusions explained in chapter 5 about the
diﬀerent forecasting models developed along the project. As mentioned before, since the company
preferred not to share their forecasts, the conclusions here relate to comparisons between the
diﬀerent models, remarking some interesting facts that could be of interest to the company. The
ﬁrst part is dedicated to short term models, second to long term and a third part comprises some
general conclusions.
Conclusions about short term models
• Autoregressive models are capable of beating persistence and have very low RMSE in short
forecast horizons. However, the performance of these models decrease signiﬁcantly for longer
lead times. The maximum lead time in which the autoregressive models beat the long term
models depend on the nature and quality of both models and the particular conditions of
each wind farm (variability of production, average load factor, etc.). In this report, short
term models rarely performed better than long term above 6 hours ahead.
• Time-adaptivity features improve the performance of the autoregressive models respect to
the non-adaptive ones, with improvements in the order of 2% for the ﬁrst three hours.
Although the improvement of time-adaptivity features was expected to be higher in the plant
with changes in its nominal capacity, the biggest improvement was found in the plant with
constant nominal capacity. Nonetheless, the latter had also low average load factor, so the
eﬀect of the change in nominal capacity was limited. Moreover, the seasonality eﬀect could
be higher also in the plant with constant installed capacity, and this could be a reason why
the adaptive version reduces RMSE respect to the non-adaptive.
Moreover, the histogram of the residuals in both test plants showed that the adaptive version
of the autoregressive model was less biased than the non-adaptive one.
• The improvement achieved by the Markov Switching autoregressive (MSAR) is only clear for
very short lead times (less than one hour) where the improvement can go up to 2%, respect
to the conventional autoregressive.
It is remarkable that the most interesting feature of the MSAR model is probably its ability
to identify periods of high and low variance, translated into predicted distributions with clear
diﬀerentiation between regimes. Therefore the biggest beneﬁt achieved by MSAR is likely to

be within a probabilistic framework, not deterministic. The MSAR model was hardly able
to predict changes in regime status, so it is expected to work better in systems with high
probability of staying in each regime.
• The spatial autoregressive model (SAR) improved signiﬁcantly the results in Plant 039 re-
spect to conventional autoregressive models, for the particular framework of 15 minutes ahead.
Nevertheless, the improvement achieved stabilizes from around 15-20 wind farms in this par-
ticular case, meaning that using more plants as inputs will make no big diﬀerence. This
number however is strictly related to how many plants are correlated to Plant 039, and it can
deﬁnitely vary when predictions are made for a diﬀerent plant.
• The Markov switching spatial autoregressive (MSSAR) improved the results of the simple
spatial autoregressive.
This was done again only for 15 minutes ahead predictions.
The
optimal number of regimes was found to be 4 at the particular plant where it was tested.
However, the optimal number of regimes is likely to diﬀerent when predictions are made in a
diﬀerent plant since it is very dependant on the spatio-temporal correlation structure of each
wind farm.
Although the identiﬁcation of the regimes were expected to be related to the wind direction,
no clear correlation was found between wind direction and the regimes. It was found however
that in each regime, diﬀerent plants had higher weights, meaning that the power produced
correlated to diﬀerent surrounding plants depending on the regime. Again, the regimes were
very related to the variances, thus a probabilistic forecast would beneﬁt much more from the
Markov switching features than the deterministic one.
Conclusions about long term models
• Performance climatology varied from plant to plant but is very similar for all lead times. The
diurnal eﬀects however can be very diﬀerent from plant to plant. In plants with low average
load factor, climatology scored relatively low RMSE.
• The improvement of modelling power curves with local polynomial regression in one variable
was very remarkable respect to climatology, although this improvement was more signiﬁcant
in Plant 162 than in Plant 032, since the latter saw changes in the installed capacity. Plants
with a nice and narrow power curve see higher improvement from using power curves. The
width of the power curves depend however on the NWP used to generate wind speed forecasts,
and this also depends on the location, the surrounding terrain complexity, etc. Nevertheless,
the improvement of the simple local polynomial regression is in the order of 40% to 60%
respect to climatology.
• Adding wind direction dependency in the two-variable local polynomial regression improved
for some lead times up to 10% respect to simple power curves. Although the improvement
is more uniform in Plant 032, it is clear that including wind direction dependency yields a
better results in both plants.
• The time-adaptive version of the local polynomial regression in two variables went up to 15%
respect to the simple power curves for some lead times. Although the improvement of the
time-adaptive features was expected to be very high for plants with changes in its nominal
power (Plant 032), it came out to be also eﬀective for the other plant. The improvement here
is assumed to come from seasonal variations that the static version is not able to capture.
==PAGE:97==
6.1. CONCLUSIONS
85
Once again, the little improvement in Plant 032 is probably because changes in installed
capacity are have little eﬀect in a plant with such a low average load factor.
• A subdivision of the local polynomial models per lead times did not improved the results,
making them even worse for most lead times. This might be because of a lack of number of
points in a 3D power curve.
• From the diﬀerent methods explored to weight the output predictions of the LPM using three
NWP, the most robust option turn out to be a simple linear combination of the outputs, which
coeﬃcients were found through ordinary least squares per lead times. A time-adaptive linear
combination was not successful. As both short and long term models are time-adaptive, it is
likely that an adaptive combination bring some extra noise and diﬃculties so the conventional
OLS was chosen ﬁnally.
From the three NWP, ETA performs poorly for the ﬁrst lead times, although for longer lead
times it turns out to be the best in Plant 162. In the other plant, it is not clear which of them
is better as they perform similarly. The diﬀerentiation between lead times when calculating
the OLS weights, allows to down-weight the ETA model in short lead times.
Two situations in which the dynamic combination could outperform the static combination
are: 1) if one of the three NWP starts performing diﬀerently somewhere in the middle of
the test set, or 2) if the weights for the NWP vary from season to season. As the dynamic
weighting did not improve the results, it is reasonable to assume that these situations do not
apply in the problem at hand.
• Regime switching approaches for model weighting were explored. From the ones using ob-
servable variables, density-based clustering showed some interesting improvements in Plant
162 but not in Plant 032. This of course can be due to the changes in installed capacity or
load factor, but it was diﬃcult to ensure that this improvement was applicable to all plants.
Clusters based on pressure and density simultaneously showed similar behavior: some inter-
esting improvements in Plant 162 but not in Plant 032, so same questions are pending here.
It is thus diﬃcult to conclude that regime based clustering would help improving at portfolio
level. It is deﬁnitely a ﬁeld that should be further explored individually in each plant.
• Unfortunately, Markov switching algorithm for weighting NWP models did not reduce RMSE,
having much lower performance than the OLS linear combination for certain lead times.
Perhaps the assumption of having a Markov chain of resolution 24 is not very correct.
Nonetheless, important information was derived from the regime inference. Both using 2 and
3 regimes, in periods with high variance the ETA and ECM models had higher weights than
the GFS. On the contrary, periods with low variance gave higher weights to ECM and GFS
than ETA. This should be also further investigated.
• Regarding input selection and principal component analysis, they performed worse than the
OLS linear combination. However, two interesting facts were derived. For plants in which the
three NWP performed similarly, the PCA is likely to improve the results for some lead times
up to 4% respect to the OLS, whereas in plants where the three NWP perform diﬀerently,
the PCA is not likely to improve. A possible explanation to this is that if one model performs
clearly worse than the others, its bad performance will aﬀect the PCA outputs.
On the
contrary, if all of them perform on average similarly, the PCA is likely to extract interesting
features from it.

6.2. FUTURE INVESTIGATIONS
86
Furthermore, using a grid of PCA improved further the results in a plant with highly correl-
ated NWP. Therefore the use of a grid of NWP surrounding a plant could potentially bring
a great improvement to long term models.
Moreover, in the lead times where the three NWP of a plant perform diﬀerently, the PCA
grid achieves higher improvement respect to simple PCA. The reason for this is that if one
NWP performs badly in one plant but not in the surrounding ones, the PCA should be able
to extract the information from the other plants related to that model and overall improve
the forecasts.
General conclusions
• The same model evaluated in various sites can perform very diﬀerently. This fact can be seen
comparing the simplest models like persistence and climatology but also with more complex
models. One factor that has specially high inﬂuence on this conclusion is the average load
factor of the plants. Plants with high average load factor tend to have higher errors since the
variability of power production at high power levels is greater than at low power levels, and
high variance usually leads to higher RMSE.
In general, plants with high variance are more diﬃcult to predict, thus any factor that in-
creases variance has also a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the RMSE scored by the models.
• The initialization of the parameters in the Markov switching algorithms have a huge inﬂuence
in the performance of the models. For these reason, a deeper study on the initialization of
the MSAR could be done. Maybe, the bad performance of the MSAR can be improved by
initializing it many more times the model parameters until an outstanding performance is
found.
• Combining short and long term models in a static fashion (batch learning) gave better results
than using dynamic combination in which weights can vary over time. Both long and short
term models however were found to be better if they were time-adaptive.
6.2
Future investigations
As the time for developing the thesis was limited, the author left some interesting ﬁelds unexplored
that are proposed for future investigations:
• Establish a comparison framework with the company to test the models here developed against
theirs.
• Exhaustive optimization of the meta-parameters of the Local Polynomial algorithm in two
variables, bandwidth and forgetting factor.
• Deeper exploration in cluster based methods for model weighting.
This includes making
diﬀerentiation between lead times, exploring other weather variables, try diﬀerent clustering
algorithms, etc.
• Deeper investigations regarding which plants or under which conditions the PCA is likely to
bring some value to the forecasting problem: use of a high resolution grid of NWP, study it
in relation to the performance of the three NWP independently, etc.

• Evaluate MSAR models within a probabilistic framework and compare them with other prob-
