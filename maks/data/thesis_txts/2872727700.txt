Background 
Lignin is a major component of black liquor (BL), a waste byproduct generated in large quantities 
during the kraft pulping process in the paper industry [1]. BL primarily consists of inorganic 
compounds (NaOH, Na₂S, Na₂CO₃, Na₂SO4, and Na₂SO₃), water, and organic biomass, including 
lignin and degradation products of cellulose and hemicellulose [2]. The elemental composition of BL 
depends on the feedstock which can be either softwood (pine, spruce, etc.) or hardwood (oak, birch, 
beech, etc.) [2]. BL constitutes 90% of the waste generated in the paper industry and each year over 
260 million tons of BL solids are produced worldwide [3]. The BL is usually concentrated by 
evaporation and burned to produce energy [1].  
 
Lignin is a biopolymer which consists of a rich phenolic structure, which makes it an attractive 
feedstock for producing bio-based chemicals and materials. The different hydroxy groups can be 
either aliphatic, phenolic, or carboxylic acid groups. They are divided into the three groups syringyl 
(S), guaiacyl (G), and p-hydroxyphenyl (H). Lignin from hardwood is primally be composed of G 
and S units, where lignin from softwood primally is composed of G units [4]. 
This thesis will focus on the extraction and recovery of lignin from BL using neutralization, liquid-
liquid extraction and the antisolvent effect. The effect of using different organic acids and solvents 
will be investigated, and the obtained lignin will be characterized to assess its suitability for further 
applications, such as organic coatings. Additionally, a catalytic hydrogenolysis may be explored as a 
method to further convert and depolymerize of the lignin. 
Application 
This project aims to provide insights into the extraction and recovery of lignin from BL, focusing on 
optimizing the use of organic acids and solvents to obtain a high-purity lignin. Additionally, catalytic 
hydrogenolysis may be explored as a method to depolymerize the obtained lignin. If successful, the

E Abstract 
This thesis investigates the extraction, recovery, and catalytic depolymerization of lignin from kraft 
black liquor with the aim of producing low molecular lignin fractions suitable for bio-based organic 
coatings. Lignin was extracted via acid neutralization combined with liquid-liquid extraction using 
2-methyltetrahydorfuran (2-Me-THF), ethyl acetate (EtOAc), and benzyl alcohol (BA) as solvent. 
Recovery was achieved either through rotary evaporation or antisolvent precipitation, and selected 
samples were further subjected to catalytic hydrogenolysis using Pd/C in ethanol to obtain a 
depolymerized lignin. The influence of solvent type, extraction conditions, and precipitation method 
on yield, molecular weight (Mw) and number (Mn), polydispersity index (PDI), and functional group 
composition was evaluated using elemental analysis (EA), size exclusion chromatography (SEC), 
NMR (1H, 13C, 31P, HSQC), thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), and gas chromatography-mass 
spectrometry (GC-MS). The highest yield (15-22%) was obtained using 2-Me-THF, which was the 
most investigated solvent in this study, at 2.5 min stirring and with water dilution prior to extraction, 
and rotary evaporation as recovery method. Dilution of BL prior to extraction prevented the co-
extraction of fatty acids, which were otherwise observed in non-diluted samples. 
TGA indicated the presence of inorganic residues in the BA-extracted sample, and HSQC confirmed 
the existence of lignin-carbohydrate complexes, rendering BA unsuitable for isolating low molecular 
weight lignin for coating applications. In contrast, 2-Me-THF proved to be the most effective solvent, 
particularly when combined with rotary evaporation or antisolvent precipitation, yielding lignin with 
low molecular weight and number (Mw and Mn), narrow PDI (determined by SEC), and high aromatic 
content confirmed by 1H NMR and 13C NMR. HSQC spectra showed a generally low abundance of 
β-O-4 linkages, suggesting that these bonds were cleaved during the kraft pulping process, while β-β 
linkages were retained across all four samples analysed with HSQC. Compared to commercial 
LignoBoost kraft lignin, all extracted samples exhibited lower Mw and Mn and a narrower PDI 
confirmed by SEC, and similar hydroxy content, shown by 31P NMR. Hydrogenolysis further reduced 
sulfur content and enhanced aromatic signals, suggesting successful depolymerization. Overall, this 
work demonstrates that by tuning the extraction and recovery conditions - particularly with 2-Me-
THF - it is possible to obtain structurally favorable lignin fractions suitable for high-value, bio-based 
organic coatings.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Organic coatings 
Organic coatings serve as protective barriers that prevent corrosion by isolating the underlying metal 
from corrosive environments. They enhance the durability of structures by providing resistance to 
weather, humidity, abrasion, and chemical exposure while maintaining mechanical toughness. The 
effectiveness of organic coatings depends on factors such as the coating system’s mechanical 
properties, the type and concentration of corrosion inhibitors, surface pretreatment, and adhesion to 
the substrate [5]. The key components are solvents, resin (binder), pigment, filler, and additives, 
which together contribute to their protective and performance properties [6]. Organic coatings are 
classified according to the resin’s chemical composition, and a common resin is epoxy resin. 
Epoxy resins are a particular class of polymers which are known for their good mechanical strength 
and adhesion and their excellent chemical and heat resistance [7]. Derived primarily from petroleum-
based raw materials, these resins are commonly used in adhesives, coatings, and composite materials 
[8], [9]. Epoxy resins are thermosetting polymers that undergo curing through reactions with a diverse 
range of curing agents. The curing process of resins involves the addition of hardeners, which enable 
the formation of a crosslinked network. Different hardeners such as polyamines, polyamides, 
anhydrides, and mercaptans have been used [10]. Among various epoxy resins, diglycidyl ether 
bisphenol A (DGEBA) is the most prevalent due to its chemical and mechanical properties [8]. 
DGEBA is formed from the reaction between bisphenol A (BPA) and epichlorohydrin in the presence 
of a basic catalyst [11]. 67% of the molar mass of DGEBA consists of BPA and in 2014 the US 
production of epoxy resins was 254,465 metric tons, making it a high valued market  [12] , [13]. 
Figure 1 shows the structure of BPA. 
 
Figure 1: Chemical structure of BPA. 
BPA has come under scrutiny due to its potential health risks and environmental persistence. BPA is 
classified as an endocrine-disrupting chemical (EDC), meaning it can interfere with hormonal balance 
in humans and wildlife [14]. It is also found that exposure to BPA is associated with metabolic 
==PAGE:20==
 
2 
 
disorders like obesity and insulin resistance, and that BPA can damage DNA and interfere with many 
cancer-related signaling pathways [15]. Consequently, regulatory agencies such as the European 
Chemicals Agency (ECHA) and the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) have imposed 
restrictions on BPA usage in food packaging, baby bottles, and other consumer products [16], [17].  
Despite that ECHA and FDA have restricted the use of BPA in some industries due to its health 
concerns, BPA is still widely used in various industrial applications and industries due to its unique 
properties. For instance, BPA-based epoxy resins are used in coatings to protect vehicles from 
corrosion, where 28,000 tons of epoxy resins are used annually, just in European car manufacturing 
[18].  
There is a growing interest in exploration of an alternative, sustainable, and renewable source to 
replace BPA in epoxy resin production. A variety of bio-based aromatic compounds have been utilized 
in epoxy resin synthesis including itaconic acid, eugenol, rosins, gallic acid, vanillin, vanillic acid, 
and lignin [12]. Among these, lignin will be the focus in this research. 
1.2 Lignin 
Lignin is one of the three primary components of the cell wall in lignocellulosic biomass, alongside 
cellulose and hemicellulose [4]. It is the second most abundant natural polymer, and its 
composition which is rich in carbon and aromatic structures, enhances its value by enabling the 
development of high-value chemicals and bio-based materials [19]. Lignin adds strength and structure 
to the cell wall, where it also controls the fluid flow and protects the cell against biochemical stress 
by inhibiting enzymatic degradation of other components [4]. Since lignin is present in all woody 
plants, a huge amount is fractionated from the cellulose during the kraft pulping process, which 
converts wood into paper. However, most of the lignin does not end up in the final paper product, but 
in a byproduct known as black liquor (BL). 
1.2.1 Chemical structure of lignin 
The structure of the lignin is a large biopolymer, and the molecule consists of different hydroxy 
groups, which are either aliphatic or phenolic, and carboxylic acid groups. The phenolics are divided 
into three groups: syringyl (S), guaiacyl (G), and p-hydroxyphenyl (H). H units have no methoxy 
groups, G units possess one methoxy group and S units possess two methoxy groups attached on the 
aromatic ring, which is shown in Figure 2. Lignin from hardwood will primally be composed of G 
and S units, where lignin from softwood primally will be composed of G units [20].

1.2.2 From lignin to kraft lignin 
Lignin, which is considered the main renewable aromatic resource, is a major component of BL. 
Despite the large production of lignin in the pulp industry, only 2% is utilized for value-added 
products [23]. However, the native lignin just described undergoes significant modifications during 
the kraft pulping process, which will be described in further details later, leading to what is commonly 
referred to as kraft lignin. The bond that gets cleaved in the pulping step is primary the predominant 
alkyl-aryl ether (β-O-4) within the lignin. The molecule has a β-O-4 linkages, which connects two 
==PAGE:24==
 
6 
 
aromatic units via an ether bond at the β position. In the presence of hydroxide ions (OH-) from 
sodium hydroxide in the BL, the alcohol on the benzene ring is deprotonated, and to stabilize the 
molecule the electrons move up which cleaves the alfa-O ether bond. The hydrogen sulfide ions (HS-
) from sodium sulfide (Na2S), which is also a part of BL, is a nucleophile and attacks the beta-carbon. 
Since the thiolate anion (S2-) is more stable than SH−, the sulfide is deprotonated by the alkaline 
solution. The system now involves a nucleophile rearrangement, where the sulfur migrates from the 
beta-carbon to the alfa-carbon. The shift occurs because the alfa-carbon is right next to the aromatic 
system, which thereby is resonance stabilized, and is the preferred position for sulfur.  The alfa-carbon 
now has a positive charge, since it had 5 bonds, which makes it an unstable intermediate. The 
electrons will thereby flow in the aromatic system by forming a double bond between the alfa-carbon 
and the ring, which will rearrange the electrons in the aromatic system making a dislocated system 
forming a negative charged phenolate group. The thiolate anion reacts as a nucleophile and attacks 
the beta-carbon which disrupts the beta-aryl bonding, causing the attached aromatic ring to be 
expelled as a phenolate ion into the BL. Now the thiolignin is formed, which is soluble in the strongly 
alkaline solution. It can be precipitated and recovered by lowering the pH of the liquor by acidification 
with mineral acids [22]. A reaction mechanism is shown in Figure 5. 
==PAGE:25==
 
7 
 
 
Figure 5: Reaction mechanism for β-O-4 cleavage in lignin during kraft pulping process under alkaline conditions. 
Because the bonds in the native lignin are cleaved in the alkaline solution of BL forming the kraft 
lignin, kraft lignin has a lower molecular weight (Mw) than native lignin. From the formation of native 
lignin to kraft lignin, ether bonds are cleaved which means that the structure of kraft lignin has more 
phenolic hydroxyl than native lignin. Furthermore, the kraft lignin contains 2-3% sulfur from the 
reaction with sodium sulfide in the BL, where native lignin does not contain sulfur [24].  
In general, softwood kraft lignin (SKL) has a higher molecular weight (Mw) than hardwood kraft 
lignin (HKL). This difference is due to the variation in the compositions of H-, S-, and G-units. HKL 
primary consists of G- and S-units, resulting in higher proportion of β-O-4 ether bonds and more 
methoxy groups. The presence of two methoxy groups on S-units blocks the reactive carbon on the 
5th position, reducing the ability to from strong carbon-carbon (C-C) linkages. The β-O-4 ether bond 
is the easiest to cleave, which contributes to the low molecular weight of HKL. In contrast, SKL 
==PAGE:26==
 
8 
 
contains fewer β-O-4 ether bonds but more C-C bonds, and fewer methoxy groups. With a higher 
proportion of C-C bonds, SKL’s structure is more resistant to cleavage, which contributes to its higher 
molecular weight [25]. As kraft lignin is derived from BL, which is a byproduct from the kraft pulping 
process, understanding the kraft pulping process is essential and will be explored in the next section. 
1.3 BL from the kraft pulping process 
1.3.1 Kraft pulping process 
The kraft pulp process consists of four different stages. The first one is the wood preparation which 
is where the bark is removed from the tree by using large steel drums where the logs will spin around 
and by the friction between the logs, the bark will loosen and fall off. Water might be applied, which 
can help to loosen the bark and wash away impurities. Now the tree is ready to be cut into smaller 
pieces and then continue to the next step, which is the pulping step [26]. In this step, the smaller 
pieces, wood chips, are placed in a digester and cooked at a high temperature (160-180 °C) and under 
high pressure (6-7 bar) for 0.5-3 hours [27]. It is cooked under alkaline conditions using a mixture 
called white liquor, which consists of 15-20% sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and sodium sulfide (Na2S), 
whereby the lignin is dissolved. This step is also where the pulp is separated from the solubilized 
compounds, which is called a weak black liquor fraction [1]. The weak black liquor fraction is a by-
product and will be described in further details later in this thesis. The pulp continues to the third step 
which is the bleaching step, which is an important step to get white paper by removing more lignin, 
which colours the pulp. This step also cleans the pulp from impurities and dirt. Usually, bleaching is 
done by chlorination and oxidation [1]. The last step is the paper-making process, where the bleached 
pulp is used for printing papers, tissues, and towelling [26].  
To make sure that the process is profitable, an important step is the recovery process of the weak BL. 
The first step in the recovery process is the concentration of the BL to a solution with at least 65% 
solids – now defines as strong BL. This solution is burned, which gives energy and molten smelt, 
which is rich in sodium sulfide and sodium carbonate (Na2CO3). These two ashes are dissolved with 
water, called green liquor (eq. 1+2) [1], [27].

1.3.2 Black Liquor (BL) 
As mentioned, BL is the primary liquid byproduct from the kraft pulping process and separated from 
the pulp in stage 2. The composition and properties of the BL depends on the feedstock and the 
operational conditions during the pulping step. One thing all BL has in common is that it is a black 
alkaline aqueous solution which compromises both organic and inorganic compounds, with up to 
15% dry solids. BL primarily consists of inorganic compounds (NaOH, Na2S, Na2CO3, Na2SO4, and 
Na2SO3), water, and organic biomass, including lignin and degradation products of cellulose and 
hemicellulose [2]. Furthermore, BL consists of extractives which include resin acids and fatty acids 
[28]. The elemental composition of BL depends on the feedstock which can be either softwood (pine, 
spruce, etc.), hardwood (oak, birch, beech, etc.), mix tropical wood, straw, bagasse, or bamboo [2].

1.3.3 Acid precipitation of lignin in BL 
Lignin's solubility in BL is primarily due to the high alkalinity of BL, typically with a pH between 13 
and 14. At this elevated pH, the phenolic hydroxyl groups in lignin molecules are deprotonated, 
resulting in negatively charged phenolate ions. This ionization increases lignin's hydrophilicity and 
electrostatic repulsion between molecules, keeping it dissolved in the aqueous phase. When the pH 
of BL is lowered, these phenolic groups become protonated, reducing the negative charge and 
hydrophilicity of lignin molecules. This causes hydrophobic and van der Waals forces to dominate, 
leading to molecular aggregation and precipitation [30]. The following equilibrium shows the 
deprotonated form (soluble) on the left, and the protonated form (precipitated) on the right.

Ela et al. [30] has investigated a titration curve of BL, which further illustrates that lignin precipitation 
is pH dependent. The titration curve can be seen in Figure 10. The titration curve is divided into key 
regions showing electrostatic stability, protonation and aggregation, and electrical neutrality. A buffer 
region is observed around pH 8.5, which indicates that lignin in this region is highly soluble as 
electrostatic repulsion prevents aggregation. At pH 5.5 a steep change in the slope occurs, which 
means that lignin starts to protonate, since the pH here is so low that the H+ ions bind to the negative 
charged phenolate groups, converting them into neutral phenol groups. The electrostatic repulsion is 
decreased, which means that the molecules will start to aggregate. Below pH 4, lignin is fully 
protonated, and its solubility is further reduces, reaching electrical neutrality, which results in 
complete protonation. This titration curve confirms that pH is a dominant factor in the lignin recovery,

influenced by factors such as pH, temperature, and ionic strength, which determine whether lignin

of reduced solubility where lignin aggregates and separates from the liquid phase. Zhu et al. [31] has 
investigated the precipitation equilibrium of lignin from softwood/hardwood mixed black liquor.

temperature and ionic strength. In all experiments the lowest pH value (9.5) resulted in the highest 
yield. A temperature at 45 °C produced the highest yield, as lower temperatures enhance lignin 
precipitation, as solubility decreases with cooling.  Increasing ionic strength by adding sodium ions 
promoted precipitation of lignin leading to a higher yield [31] . Further investigation is required to 
understand how temperature, pH, and ionic strength influence lignin solubility and precipitation. 
1.4 Extraction of lignin from BL 
1.4.1 State of the Art (SoA) 
1.4.1.1 LignoBoost 
The process starts by extracting a stream of BL, containing 30-45% dry solids, from the evaporation 
plant [32]. The stream is then precipitated by lowering the pH-value to approximately 10, preferably 
with carbondioxide (CO2) [28]. The use of CO2 as an acidifying agent helps maintain the sodium-
sulfur (Na/S) balance of the kraft mill while also enabling the utilization of on-site flue gas streams. 
When the precipitation has occurred, the lignin is separated from BL by filtration using a chamber 
press filter [32]. The filtrate is recycled back to the weak black liquor system ensuring minimal losses 
and to maintain the process balance [32]. The filter cake is reslurried where the pH is further reduced 
with sulfuric acid to a pH around 1-3.5 [33]. The controlled pH adjustment prevents filter cake 
plugging, which is a problem in the traditional precipitation process, by preventing excessive pH and 
ionic strength gradients in the filter cake while washing [28]. The slurry goes to a second filtration 
step using a chamber press filter where the lignin is washed with water [32]. The product is a low ash 
==PAGE:34==
 
16 
 
lignin which typically contains 65-70% dry solids [32]. The lignin can then be further processed 
through drying, pulverization, or pelletization depending on the intended application. The advantage 
of using this method is that the volume of acidic washing water can be kept at lower values, which 
means lower investments costs. The addition of sulfuric acid can be kept at lower level, which means 
lower operational costs. The yield of lignin is higher, and it has lower ash and carbohydrate content, 
as well as a higher content of dry solids [32].  A schematic presentation of the process is shown in  
Figure 14. 
 
Figure 14: A schematic diagram of the LignoBoost process. Adapted from [28]. 
The LignoBoost process concept is developed within the R&D program Future Resource-Adapted 
pulp Mill. The process concept is verified on a scale of 4,000 tons pr year at a plant located in 
Bächhammer, Sweden. The patents and patent applications are owned by Metso [32]. 
1.4.1.2 LignoForce 
The process starts by extraction of semi-concentrated BL with 30-40% dry solids. The BL is oxidized 
with O2 at 80 C. That helps convert hydrogen sulfide and other sulfur molecules to safer compounds 
and improves the filterability of lignin during the lignin cake dewatering steps [28]. To reduce the pH 
to 9.5-10, the next step is adding CO2 which cause the lignin to precipitate at 65-75 °C [34]. When 
the temperature is decreased further it results in coagulation. The next step is the filtration using a 
filter press and washing of the lignin with sulfuric acid and water. The filtrate is recycled back to the 
weak black liquor system, and the filter cake, which now consists of 50-60 wt% solids, is dried. The

1.4.2.1 One-Pot Acid-Catalysed Liquefaction 
Quan et al. [39] developed one-pot acid catalysed liquefaction method, which allows for the direct 
recovery of kraft lignin from BL while the homogeneity and molecular weight distribution are 
improved. The solution is mixed with methanol as a solvent in a mini Parr autoclave reactor, where 
it is stirred continuously at 160 °C for 10 minutes, to ensure the lignin dissolve efficiently. Afterwards 
the solution undergoes vacuum filtration which helps to separate the soluble and insoluble fractions 
from each other. The insoluble fraction is evaporated to remove the methanol. The lignin recovery is 
around 75%. The molecular weight (Mw) of the lignin is 1600-2024 Da, and the PDI is 1.7-1.9 [39], 
which is significantly lower than what was obtained from other research groups [30]. The method is

1.5 Introduction to this project 
The aim of this thesis is to optimize the extraction of lignin from BL and to characterize the obtained 
product to assess its suitability for further applications, such as organic coatings. To achieve this, 
different techniques are used such as acid-base neutralization, liquid-liquid extraction, antisolvent 
precipitation, and hydrogenolysis. 
Neutralization is the first technique used in the process. BL has a basic pH, which is neutralized by 
using an organic acid such as formic acid, which is dropped to the solution until the pH is around 7. 
By lowering the pH lignin begins to precipitate due to a reduced solubility, which then will facilitate 
the separation from the BL. 
The next step in the process is extraction, where the aim is to extract the lignin from BL to an organic 
phase. Extraction is a separation technique which is used to separate and isolate specific components 
from a complex mixture, in this specific project it is the separation of lignin from BL. The most 
common extraction method is the liquid-liquid extraction (LLE) where a compound is transferred 
between two immiscible liquids. The extraction process relies on differences in solubility and the 
==PAGE:41==

partition coefficient (K) to selectively transfer a specific compound to a desired phase. The K is given 
by the following formula:

If the same amount of liquid is used in both the organic and aqueous phase, and the K = 6, it means 
that there are six times as many of the specific molecule in the organic phase than in the water phase. 
If the K value is lower than 1, it means that there are more molecules in the water phase, than in the 
organic phase [41]. Different organic solvents can be used, but it is important that the chosen solvent 
is immersible with water. From the literature organic solvents that can be used in the extraction of 
lignin in BL are 2-Methyltetrahydrofuran (2-Me-THF), Benzyl alcohol (BA), or ethyl acetate 
(EtOAc) [42], [43]. After extraction, the two phases (aqueous and organic) are separated, followed 
by one of three different approaches: evaporation to obtain a lignin oil, complete drying by further 
lowering the pressure to obtain a lignin powder by evaporation or by using antisolvent precipitation 
to recover the lignin as a solid. The obtained product can further be subjected to catalytic 
hydrogenolysis to depolymerize the lignin. 
Rotary evaporation is a technique used to efficiently and gently remove volatile solvents from a 
mixture by evaporation under reduced pressure. The process involves rotating a round-bottom flask 
containing the sample in a heated water bath while applying a vacuum, which lowers the boiling point 
of the solvent and facilitates its removal. In this project the technique is used to remove residual 
solvent from the lignin product. 
Antisolvent precipitation is a commonly used method which exploits the solubility differences of a 
compound in different solvents, in this case the solubility of lignin. The effectiveness of using the 
antisolvent effect depends on various parameters such as solvent type, temperature, pH, and lignin 
concentration. Holtz et al. [44] conducted a study where they tested different antisolvents to 
precipitate lignin from 2-Me-THF. The study showed that the solvents n-pentane, n-hexane, n-
heptane, and toluene were solvents which precipitated the lignin with the use of 2-Me-THF [44]. 
Furthermore, it is possible to calculate how good an antisolvent will work to precipitate a compound 
by using the Hansens Solubility Parameter (HSP). This methodology will be used in this project and 
is explained in Section 2.3.2. Figure 19 illustrates how to obtain the different products from the BL 
by rotary evaporation and the antisolvent effect.

Hydrogenolysis (also called hydrogenation) is a catalytic chemical reaction which breaks chemical 
bonds in an organic molecule, and at the same time adds hydrogen atoms to the resulting molecular 
fragments [45]. It is a method that decreases the Mw of lignin, and it is one of the most promising 
routes to produces phenolic compounds from lignin [46]. The method cleaves the ether bonds (C-O-
C) of lignin under mild temperatures (90-200 °C) using a gaseous phase hydrogen, or hydrogen-donor 
solvents, which can be decalin, formic acid, glycerol, or other molecules [47], [48]. The weakest 
ether-linkages are α-O-4 and β-O-4, which has bond dissociation energy (BDE) of 330 and 490 
kJ/mol, respectively [47]. The reaction can occur either with or without a catalyst, where a common 
catalyst used for the hydrogenolysis of lignin is palladium on carbon (Pd/C). All the substances, 
including the organic phase with the kraft lignin, will be added to the reactor which will be running 
at high temperatures for a specific amount of time.  When the reaction is done and the reactor is 
cooled down, the solution will be filtered, and the solid phase (which contains the catalyst and 
unreacted lignin) will be dried. The filtrate, which contains the viscous lignin depolymerization oil 
(LDOs), will be dried using a rotary evaporator to remove all solvent. Figure 20 shows the

1.6 Hypotheses 
Hypothesis 1: Isolation of lower molecular weight lignin from black liquor is possible through 
neutralization with an organic acid with concurrent liquid-liquid extraction using an organic 
solvent 
This approach is expected to give efficient phase separation and transfer of low molecular lignin into 
the organic phase. Lowering the pH with an organic acid reduces lignin solubility in the aqueous 
phase, making it easier for the lignin to move into the organic solvent. Obtaining a fractionated low 
molecular weight lignin is an important step for its further use in organic coating applications.   
Hypothesis 2: Extraction parameters such as choice of solvent, stirring time and black liquor 
concentration influence lignin yield and the scalability of the extraction process 
The choice of solvent affects how well the lignin dissolves into the organic phase, and the stirring 
time will influence the mass transfer efficiency and phase interaction. Diluting the BL will help to 
destabilize the emulsions formed and thereby enhance phase separation. Optimizing these parameters 
increases solvent recovery and lignin yield and supports process scale-up. 
Hypothesis 3: The method used to isolate the lignin from the organic phase after extraction, 
including rotary evaporation, the antisolvent effect, and/or hydrogenolysis, affects the purity 
and structural properties of the recovered lignin. 
Rotary evaporation removes the solvent through evaporation and results in a lignin product containing 
more impurities, as no selective separation is involved. In contrast, antisolvent precipitation enables 
more selective recovery by favoring the precipitation of lignin over other solubles. Hydrogenolysis 
involves catalytic depolymerization, breaking down the lignin structure, which will lead to changes 
in structural properties of the lignin and may also remove impurities. These differences will be 
evaluated using SEC, 1H-NMR, 13C-NMR 31P-NMR, HSQC, TGA, elemental analysis, and GC-MS. 
Hypothesis 4: Changing the ratio of antisolvent to lignin solution influences the molecular 
weight (Mw) and polydispersity index (PDI) of the precipitated lignin 
A higher antisolvent ratio reduces the lignin solubility faster, which results in a faster precipitation 
favoring lower molecular weight fractions. In comparison, lower antisolvent ratios lead to slower 
precipitation because the solubility reduces slower, yielding larger molecular weight fractions.

Water (60 g) is added to a 400 mL beaker followed by BL (30 g). The chosen solvent (60 g) was then

2.2.1 Elemental analysis (EA) 
EA is an analytical technique used to determine the elemental composition of a sample. It tests for 
the amount of carbon (C), hydrogen (H), nitrogen (N), and sulfur (S). The method is based on a 
complete thermal combustion of the sample followed by quantification of the resulting gaseous 
products via thermal conductivity detection (TCD). The analysis was performed using a high-
temperature combustion reactor operating under an inert carrier gas. A small amount of sample (2-3 
mg) was placed in a tin capsule, folded and introduced into the combustion column. Upon injection 
==PAGE:49==
 
31 
 
of pure oxygen, the sample was combusted in a catalytic bed reactor at high temperatures, which 
produces CO2, H2O, nitrogen oxides, SO2, and SO3. These gases pass through a reduction column 
where the nitrogen oxides are converted to N2, SO3 to SO2, and the excess of oxygen is removed. This 
results in the four gases N2, CO2, H2O, and SO2, which are separated in a chromatographic column 
and quantified using a TCD, whereby the elemental contents of C, H, N, and S are calculated as 
weight percentages [49]. The oxygen content is estimated by difference, calculated as 100% minus 
the sum of the measured C, H, N, and S contents. To ensure accuracy all samples are analyzed in 
triplicates, and the standard deviations are calculated. A certified standard sample (2,5-Bis(5-tert-
butyl-benzoxazol-2-yl)thiophene (BBOT)) is run prior to the sample to monitor the calibration and 
performance of the instrument. The analysis was performed at a Thermofischer Scientific Flash Smart 
V CHNS/O (+MVC) at DTU, Kongens Lyngby. 
In this project EA was used to evaluate the composition and purity of the extracted lignin samples. 
Since BL contains sulfur from the kraft pulping process, measuring the residual sulfur content 
provides an indication of the purification efficiency, where a low sulfur content in the product reflects 
a successful extraction process which is essential for producing a lignin that is suitable for use in 
organic coatings. Furthermore, by quantifying the C, H, N, and S and calculating the O content by 
difference, the atomic H/C and O/C ratios can be determined, which are used to construct a van 
Krevelen diagram, which provides information to estimate the degree of aromaticity and oxygenation 
of the lignin. 
2.2.2 Size Exclusion Chromatography (SEC) 
SEC is a method that separates molecules based on their size as they pass through a gel filtration 
medium. The gels composed of spherical beads which contains pores of a specific size distribution. 
Separation occurs as molecules are either included of excluded from these pores based on their size. 
Small molecules will diffuse in the pores, which slows the movement through the column, where 
larger molecules will bypass the pores and elute quickly in the void volume. Consequently, molecules 
are separated by size, where the larger one elutes first, followed by smaller molecules in the order of 
decreasing molecular weight (Mw) [50]. Figure 25 shows the principles of SEC as well as a 
chromatogram, where it is shown that the larger molecules elute first.

2.2.3 Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) 
NMR is a method used to characterize the molecular structure and environment of atoms within a 
chemical compound, as well as the purity of a compound. The sample is placed in a strong external 
magnetic field which causes the nuclei with non-zero spin to align either with or against the field. 
These spins can then absorb energy, which causes them to transit to a higher energy state. When the 
energy input is turned off, the nuclei return to its original state and emits energy, which is detected as 
the NMR signal [51]. The NMRs in this research include one dimensional proton 1H NMR, carbon 
13C NMR, phosphorous 31P NMR, as well as two-dimensional heteronuclear single quantum 
coherence (HSQC) NMR. Each NMR technique gives different information about the structure of the 
compound. 1H NMR gives insight into the different proton environments, particularly aromatics and 
aliphatic protons. 13C NMR gives insight into different carbon types as the aromatic carbons and side-
chain carbons of the lignin. 31P NMR gives after phosphitylation of the hydroxyl groups insight into 
the aliphatic, phenolic, and carboxylic acid OH-groups. All these three 1D NMR gives a spectrum 
with peaks. HSQC NMR, which is 2D, gives insight into the atoms bonded directly to each other, as 
it shows which hydrogens that are directly attached to which carbons in a molecule. It gives a map 
where each spot represents a carbon-hydrogen pair. 
 
NMR is a key analytical technique in this research, as it provides insight into the structural 
composition of the extracted lignin by identifying the types of building blocks present and how they 
are connected, which is an important step to characterize the lignin structure and thereby determine 
which of the obtained samples that can be applied to organic coatings.  
The NMR analyses were carried out partly at Åbo Akademi University, Turku, Finland, and partly at 
the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), Kongens Lyngby. The 31P NMR measurements were 
conducted at Åbo akademi, while 1H NMR and 13C NMR were recorded at DTU. HSQC experiments 
were conducted at both institutions. Prior to measurement, the lignin samples were freeze dried 
overnight. For the 1H NMR, 13C NMR and HSQC NMR, approximately 50 mg of lignin sample was 
dissolved in 0.5 mL deuterated dimethyl solfuxide (DMSO-d6) solvent. For the 31P NMR experiments 
various solutions were prepared to phosphitylate the lignin prior to running the samples in the NMR 
instrument, which is described in Appendix 7.2. Figure 26 illustrates the chemical reaction involved 
in phosphitylation and the five types of hydroxy groups that are determined using this method.

2.2.5 Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (GC-MS) 
Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry is an analytical technique used to separate and identify 
volatile compounds. The method combines gas chromatography (GS) for the physical separation of 
compounds, and mass spectrometry (MS) for the chemical identification of these compounds. The 
sample is dissolved in a GC-compatible solvent (in this case EtOH), vaporized and carried through a 
capillary column by an inert carrier gas. The compounds are then separated based on their boiling 
points and interactions with the stationary phase in the column. The compounds, which are now 
separated, enter the MS where they are ionized, fragmented and detected based on their mass-to-
==PAGE:55==
 
37 
 
charge (m/z) ratios, which gives a spectrum for each compound that can be analyzed to identify the 
chemical structure. 
In this project, the method was used to monitor the chemical composition of lignin during 
hydrogenolysis, where samples were taken at selected time points (15, 30, 45, 60, 90, 120 min) and 
analyzed. The samples were filtered, added to GC vials, and injected to the GC-MS instrument. 
The analysis was performed at The Technical University of Denmark (DTU) using an Agilent 
Technologies 7890B gas chromatograph system coupled with a 5977B mass selective detector. The 
separation was performed on two DB-1701 columns (Agilent, part number 122-0732) with the 
following specifications: 30 m length, 0.25 mm internal diameter, and 0.25 µm film thickness. The 
two columns were connected in series. The retention times and mass spectra were matched against 
known standards and NIST mass spectral library for identification.

2-Me-THF is a solvent that is commonly used as a greener alternative to traditional ethers due to its

3.6.1 Elemental Analysis (EA) 
The elemental composition of the lignin samples are summarized in Table 21, where each value 
represents the average of triplicate measurements. The calculated standard deviations are provided in 
Appendix 7.6. The analysis includes values of nitrogen (N), carbon (C), hydrogen (H) and sulfur (S), 
where the oxygen (O) content is calculated by difference – subtracting the sum of C, H, N and S from 
100 wt%. As expected, N was not detected or only detected in low amounts in all samples. The sulfur 
levels were generally low (<1 wt%) across the samples, except for sample 2MT-LOP-WW which 
showed a significantly higher sulfur content (8.3 wt%). The general low sulfur content indicates that 
inorganic sulfur-containing compounds in BL were effectively separated during extraction as they 
remained in the aqueous phase. The high sulfur content in 2MT-LOP-WW could be due to inefficient 
phase separation during the extraction step, where parts of the aqueous phase (rich in inorganics) may 
have been unintentionally carried over with the organic phase. As the sample is simply evaporated, 
any extra extracted impurities would have remained in the final product. This shows the importance 
of being precise during the separation of the two phases, which is done simply by using a pipette. The 
extracts lignin products do contain some organically bound sulfur, as seen in Table 21. While some 
samples report a sulfur content of 0.00 wt%, this may in certain cases be attributed to the limit of 
detection. However, for the hydrogenolysis treated samples, the absence of sulfur is consistent. All 
four hydrogenolysis samples (H-EL-150, H-EL-180, H-EL-200, H-KL-200) showed no detectable 
sulfur, where their respective precursor samples, 2MT-AS3-30 and KL, did contain sulfur. These 
results demonstrate that hydrogenolysis effectively removes the sulfur, yielding a sulfur free lignin 
product.  
Looking at the O content, most samples have an O content in the range of 24-28 wt%. The kraft lignin 
(KL) has an O content of 30.5 wt%, which is slightly higher than the majority of the samples. Sample 
EA-LO-NW, 2MT-LO-NW, and H-EL-180 show a significantly higher O content at 54.8 wt%, 59.8 
wt%, and 56.3 wt%, respectively. This could indicate that the samples consist of either carbohydrates, 
which are degradation products of cellulose and hemicellulose during the kraft pulping process, or 
fatty acids.  Two of the samples with a high O content, EA-LO-NW and 2MT-LO-NW, are the only 
sample produced without the dilution of the BL with water prior to extraction. This suggests that the 
fatty acids, which is naturally present in BL, may have partitioned into the organic phase and remained 
in the final product. However, all three samples with a high amount of O are yielded as a sticky oil, 
which indicate that the samples are volatile, and some volatile compounds may have partially 
evaporated before the elemental analysis, leading to somewhat inaccurate results. It is also important 
==PAGE:73==

to note that the O content is not directly measured but instead calculated by the difference from the 
sum of the other elemental components, which could contribute to deviations from the actual oxygen 
content.

3.6.3.1 1H NMR of selected samples 
Proton nuclear magnetic resonance (¹H NMR) spectroscopy was used to characterize the chemical 
structure of three lignin-containing samples: EA-LO-NW, 2MT-AS3-30, and 2MT-LO-WW. These 
samples represent different extraction conditions and were selected to evaluate structural features 
relevant to lignin. The recorded spectrum is shown in Figure 41, where the specific regions are marked 
for interpretation. The COOH region (δ ~10-12 ppm) corresponds to carboxylic acid protons, which 
can come from fatty acids. The Ar-OH region (δ ~ 8.5-9.5 ppm) include protons from phenolic 
hydroxyl groups, common in lignin due to guaiacyl, syringyl, and p-hydroxyphenyl units. The Ar-H 
region (δ ~6.0-8.0 ppm) corresponds to aromatic protons, which confirm the aromatic framework 
which is central to lignin’s backbone. The O-CHn region (δ ~3.0-4.5 ppm) shows signals from protons 
on carbon atoms bonded to an oxygen atom, such as methoxy groups, side-chain alcohols, and ether 
linkages, which are all a part of the lignin interunit structure. Finally, the C-CHn region (δ ~0.5-3.0 
ppm) indicates aliphatic protons not directly bond to an oxygen, where the benzylic protons will be 
in the range of δ ~2.3-2.8 ppm, which indicates intact lignin structures, and methylene groups on side 
chains or fatty acids.

recorded in DMSO-d₆. Highlighted regions correspond to characteristic proton environments: carboxylic acids (COOH, δ 10–12 
ppm), aromatic hydroxyls (Ar–OH, δ 8.5–10 ppm), aromatic protons (Ar–H, δ 6.0–8.0 ppm), oxygen-bound alkyl protons (O–CHₙ, δ 
3.0–4.5 ppm), and general aliphatic protons including benzylic methylene groups (C–CHₙ, δ 0.5–3.0 ppm, with benzylic protons δ

60 
 
The EA-LO-NW sample shows a clear signal in the carboxylic acid region (δ ~10 ppm), which is 
likely due to the presence of fatty acids, which aligns with the fact that this sample was not diluted 
with water, which means that the fatty acids might have stayed in the organic phase. In contrast, this 
signal is not shown in the other two samples which align with the fact that those two samples are 
carried out with water dilution of the BL prior to extraction. All three samples show peaks in the 
aromatic region (δ ~6-8 ppm), which is consistent with lignin derived aromatic structures. Peaks are 
also observed in the O-CHn region (δ ~3.0-4.5 ppm), which is from aliphatic hydroxyl groups and 
ether linkages, which are characteristics for lignin side chains. In the C-CHn region (δ ~1.0-2.8 ppm), 
the benzylic region (δ ~2.3-2.8 ppm) is assigned to methylene (-CH2-) groups directly attached to 
aromatic rings. This position is relevant in the content of lignin, as it is involved in interunit linkages 
such as β–O–4 and β–β structures. Especially in the 2MT-LO-WW sample, an intense peak is 
observed. In all three spectra a sharp peak at δ 2.5 ppm is shown, which corresponds to the proton 
signal from DMSO solvent. 
Overall, the spectra confirm the presence of both aromatic cores and benzylic side chains, which 
aligns with the structure of lignin. The EA-LO-NW sample also shows signals in the area of 
carboxylic acids attributed to fatty acids, which illustrates that there are differences in the composition 
based on extraction and purification conditions. 
3.6.3.2 13C NMR of selected samples 
To further investigate the structural features of the lignin-containing samples, indirect ¹³C NMR 
spectra were recorded using heteronuclear multiple bond correlation (HMBC) for EA-LO-NW, 2MT-
AS3-30, and 2MT-LO-WW. These samples represent lignin fractions obtained through different 
extraction solvents, with and without dilution with water, and different recovery methods – either 
evaporated to yield a lignin oil, or antisolvent precipitation to isolate the lignin as a powder. The 
spectra shown in Figure 42 are divided into five main regions based on chemical shift ranges 
characteristic of lignin and related compounds. The carbonyl region (δ ~160-220 ppm) includes 
signals from carboxylic acids, esters, and ketones, which is often associated with side chains or fatty 
acids. The aromatic C-O region (δ ~130-160 ppm) represents carbon in the aromatic ring bonded to 
hydroxyl or methoxy groups, which in lignin could be methoxy groups on guaiacyl and syringyl units.

which form the core of the lignin structure. In the aliphatic CO region (δ ~50-90 ppm), signals arise

3.7 Summary on effect of solvent and recovery method 
The choice of solvent had a clear impact on both yield and structure of the extracted compounds. 
Among the tested solvents, 2-Me-THF was the most efficient, providing the highest lignin yields (up 
to 15-22%). When BL was diluted with water prior to extraction, the emulsion formation was reduced 
and thereby the phase separation was improved, resulting in a higher yield. Furthermore, it also 
influenced the extraction of compounds, as water addition appeared to favor the extraction of more 
hydrophilic compounds as fatty acids. 31P NMR showed a relatively higher content of carboxylic acid 
OH groups compared to aliphatic OH groups, when BL was diluted with water. In contrast, samples 
extracted without dilution had significantly higher O/C and H/C ratios, lower molecular weights (Mw 
and Mn), and narrower PDI. 
EtOAc resulted in significantly lower yields (up to 4-6%), and the addition of water led to emulsion 
formation that prevented phase separation and made extraction impossible. The extracted lignin with 
no water dilution contained almost no carbohydrates or β-O-4 linkages. However, clear carboxylic 
acids signals were observed in the 1H NMR spectrum, correlating with a high content of carboxylic 
acid hydroxy groups seen in the 31P NMR. This suggests that the sample may contain fatty acids, as 
these can migrate to the organic phase in the absence of water dilution. 
BA as solvent resulted in lower yields than with 2-Me-THF, but higher yields than with EtOAc. It 
showed clear γ-ester signals which indicated LCCs, further supported by a high number of G-
conjugated units. Furthermore, using BA as a solvent showed inorganics residues in the sample based 
==PAGE:96==
 
78 
 
on the TGA analysis. This sample was furthermore the only one which was soluble in water,  which 
correlates with the indication that the sample contains LCCs. 
The recovery methods also affected both the yield and structure of the extracted compound. Using 2-
Me-THF as solvent together with rotary evaporation to obtain powder gave the highest yield (15-
22%), while antisolvent precipitation resulted in lowest yields (5-8%). Lignin oils obtained by 
evaporation contained fewer G- and S-type aromatic units and fewer interunit linkages (primary β-
O-4 and β-β bonds) based on the HSQC compared to powders obtained by antisolvent precipitation. 
Furthermore, the HSQC showed that the lignin oils contain more carbohydrates compared to the 
antisolvent precipitation power, which shows that using the antisolvent effect as recovery method 
excludes smaller impurities like carbohydrates, which remains in the solution and do not precipitate, 
and thereby provide a purer lignin fraction. SEC shows that the lignin oil has a higher PDI compared 
to all the four antisolvent samples using 2-Me-THF as solvent, which means that the lignin oil sample 
contains a mix of both small and large molecules, while the antisolvent effect selectively precipitates 
a more homogeneous fraction. 
However, all extracted samples had a lower Mw and Mn, as well as a narrower PDI, compared to 
commercial LignoBoost kraft lignin. Moreover, adjusting the antisolvent-to-solution ratio led to 
structural differences, with smaller molecules obtained when more antisolvent was added. Drying 
conditions also have an influence, where vacuum drying at 30 °C led to lower Mw and Mn and a higher 
content of carboxylic acid OH, compared to drying at 60 °C. 31P NMR confirmed that all samples 
contained a substantial amount of hydroxy groups, with more carboxylic acid OH observed in samples 
extracted without dilution. 1H NMR, 13C NMR, and HSQC analyses were only performed on a few 
selected samples, limiting conclusions on broader trends. Applying hydrogenolysis showed that sulfur 
was removed, and it is expected to give a lower Mw, Mn, and a narrower PDI, but further analysis 
needs to be done on those samples before any trends can be concluded. 
Overall, 2-Me-THF was the most investigated solvent in this study and yielded the cleanest product 
when combined with antisolvent precipitation. These products contained fewer carbohydrates, and a 
high number of aromatic unit interlinkages compared to the other samples. Compared to the 
commercial LignoBoost kraft lignin, the extracted and precipitated lignin fractions showed lower 
molecular weight (Mw and Mn), a narrower PDI, and equal amounts of hydroxy groups. Furthermore, 
the elemental composition – the C/H and O/C ratios - were in the same range as the LignoBoost kraft 
==PAGE:97==
 
79 
 
lignin. These characteristics make it the most promising lignin fraction extracted in this study for 
application in organic coatings.  
4 Conclusion 
This study investigated the chemical and catalytic conversion of lignin extracted from black liquor 
(BL) for potential application in organic coating, with a focus on how solvent selection, extraction 
conditions, and recovery methods affect the yield and structure of the recovered compound. Thirteen 
different samples were produced and characterized using a range of analytical techniques, including 
elemental analysis (EA), size exclusion chromatography (SEC), various NMR techniques (1H, 13C, 
31P, and HSQC), thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and gas chromatography-mass spectroscopy 
(GC-MS). 
The results confirmed that low molecular weight lignin can be extracted from BL through 
neutralization with and organic solvent followed by liquid-liquid extraction, particularly when using 
2-Me-THF as solvent. 1H NMR and 13C NMR data confirmed the presence of aromatic structures in 
samples extracted with 2-Me-THF and EtOAc, while HSQC confirmed the interunit linkages 
commonly found in lignin (β-O-4 and β-O-β). 31P NMR showed phenolic hydroxy content ranging 
from 5.1 to 7.7 mmol/g lignin across all samples, and SEC demonstrated that the molecular weights 
were consistently lower compared to commercial LignoBoost kraft lignin, confirming Hypothesis 1.  
Optimizing process parameters had an impact on lignin yield and confirmed Hypothesis 2. The 
highest lignin oil yields (12-18%) were obtained using 2.5 min of stirring combined with the dilution 
of 60 g water per 30 g BL, why these conditions were applied throughout the experimental work. 
Upscaling the process by a factor of five with 2-Me-THF preserved both solvent recovery yield and 
lignin yield, while upscaling with EtOAc obtained significantly lower yields compared to when the 
extraction is not upscaled, which confirms that upscaling acts different with different solvents applied. 
Furthermore, extraction with EtOAc showed poor performance due to emulsion formation upon water 
addition and generally resulted in lower yields (4-5%). Dilution of BL prior to extraction resulted in 
more aliphatic OH groups, compared to no dilution, which shows more carboxylic acid OH groups, 
suggesting the co-extraction of fatty acids. When recovered by rotary evaporation, 2-Me-THF 
extracts gave the highest yield obtaining a powder (15-22%) when water was added prior to 
extraction, while the yield dropped significantly (2-3%) with no water dilution prior to extraction. BA 
==PAGE:98==
 
80 
 
extracted compounds showed strong γ-ester signals, associated with LLCs, where EtOAc extracts 
contained almost no carbohydrate signals. 
Different recovery methods – rotary evaporation, antisolvent precipitation, and hydrogenolysis – 
resulted in structural differences, supporting Hypothesis 3. Lignin oils obtained through evaporation 
contained fewer G- and S-type aromatic units and fewer interunit linkages compared to powders 
obtained via antisolvent precipitation, and lignin oils contains more carbohydrates compared to the 
antisolvent precipitation samples, suggesting that the antisolvent effect precipitates a specific fraction, 
where the rotary evaporation simply evaporates the solvent. Hydrogenolysis of selected lignin 
samples removed sulfur and resulted in more intense aromatic signals at higher temperatures, 
indicating a shift towards depolymerized and more aromatic products. 
Finally, Hypothesis 4 was supported by the observation that variations in the antisolvent-to-solution 
ratio significantly affected the molecular characteristics of the precipitated lignin. SEC analysis 
showed that higher antisolvent volumes led to fractions with reduced Mn and Mw values, along with 
a narrower PDI, suggesting that a fast change in solubility favors the selective fractionation of smaller 
and more uniform lignin molecules. Conversely, lower antisolvent volumes appeared to precipitate 
larger molecular weight fractions. 
5 Future work 
This project has laid the groundwork for a deeper understanding of lignin extraction from BL. 
However, several areas remain open for further investigation. While 13 different lignin samples were 
produced, not all were subjected to the full range of analytical techniques. Extending the full 
analytical characterizations to all samples would enable more robust comparisons and help to identify 
consistent relationships between extraction conditions, recovery methods, and resulting lignin 
structure. 
The primary focus in this study was on 2-Me-THF as the extraction solvent, while only a single 
sample was produced using EtOAc and BA, respectively. To better understand how solvent choice 
affects lignin fractionation and selectivity, additional samples should be prepared using these solvents 
under varied conditions. Additionally, since most lignin samples were produced as single batches, it 
would be valuable to examine the reproducibility of the extraction methods. Producing and analyzing 
multiple batches under identical conditions would help to determine the robustness of each process 
method. 
==PAGE:99==
 
81 
 
Further optimization of the antisolvent precipitation method is also recommended. This study showed 
that using a lower antisolvent-to-solution ratio led to the recovery of lignin with lower Mw and Mn 
and a narrower PDI. Investigating even lower ratios or altering the method the solvent is introduced 
(e.g. slow dripping vs fast mixing), could help to refine the selectivity of the precipitation. Moreover, 
testing other antisolvents, especially for EtOAc, where precipitation was unsuccessful in the current 
study, could provide additional insights into solvent-antisolvent interactions. 
The role of pH during extraction deserves more attention and further investigation. Since lignin 
solubility is strongly influenced by pH, a dedicated study investigating how extraction at different pH 
levels influences the type and composition of extracted lignin fractions could give opportunities for 
selective fractionation based on functional groups and molecular weight. 
Although hydrogenolysis was shown to be a promising approach for lignin depolymerization, the 
catalytic system was not fully optimized. Future studies could investigate the influence on reaction 
parameters such as solvent, reaction temperature, time, and catalyst loading to optimize the monomer 
yield and selectivity. It was also just applied to a few samples, where applying it to a broader range 
of extracted lignin fractions would provide a clearer understanding of its method. 
Finally, from an application point of view, it is essential to move towards formulation trials, where 
the extracted lignin fractions are tested in resin and coating systems. Evaluating its parameters will 
be essential for assessing their practical potential as bio-based alternatives to fossil-based components 
in organic coatings.

7.2 31P NMR method 
Preparation of 31P NMR: 
Three solutions are prepared.  
To prepare solution A (Pyridine/CDCl3 1.6/1 (v/v)) for 10 samples: 
For 10 samples the volume required: 1 mL for IS + 1 mL for R1x + (0.4 mL x 10 samples) = total 
solution 6 mL. Mix 2.35 mL of CDCl3 and 3.76 mL of pyridine in a vial. 
To prepare solution B (internal standard IS): 
20 mg IS (endo-N-Hydroxyl-5-norbornene-2,3-dicarboximide(e-HINDI) is dissolved in 1 mL of 
solution A. Adjust the volume of solution A accordingly with exact weighted IS to achieve the exact 
concentration of IS as 20 mg/mL. 
To prepare solution C (Relaxation agent):  
11.4 mg R1x solution are dissolved in 1 mL of solution A. 
Sample preparation:  
- 
Lignin is dried in freezedryer overnight. 
- 
40 mg of sample is dissolved completely in 0.4 mL of solution A in a 5 mL glass vial. A magnet 
is added and it is stirred for 30 min.  
- 
100 uL of solution B is added to the vial. 
- 
50 uL of solution C is added to the vial. 
- 
100 uL of phosphitylation reagent (2-chloro-4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-1,3,2-dioxaphospholane) is 
added to the vial. Note: The time between the addition of PR-II and the beginning of the NMR 
analysis should not be more than 1 h. 
- 
Spin the mixture via vortex, if not completely dissolved add 100 uL of CDCL3-d and vortex 
again.  
- 
Transfer to an NMR tube and analyse via NMR.
