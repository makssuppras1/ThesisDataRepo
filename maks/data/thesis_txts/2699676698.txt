Abstract
This master thesis is a case-study of a research house known as The Test House sit-
uated in Nuuk, Greenland. The thesis elaborates on the performance of a double
facade building, consisting of a heated inner zone and an unheated middle zone. The
investigation with focus on the energy performance as the indoor climate for this
research house situated in an Arctic climate. The thesis leads to the interpretation of
parameters affecting the actual performance in contrast to the predicted performance
based on dynamic simulation of a theoretical building model.
In retrospect of the investigation there could have been performed a more detailed
and quantitative investigation of the exact influence of system parameters, if the now
obtained knowledge concerning limitations had been present in the beginning of the
field work in Greenland.
The estimate of the annual energy consumption on basis of theoretical dynamic
showed to be approx. 128 kWh/m2 and the measured energy consumption for the
first year of operation is approximately found to be 138 kWh/m2. The analyzed gap
shows to be differentiated in the results of the indoor climate linked to the actual
room temperatures are being affected by the discovery of thermal bridges and air
leakages resulting in 5-15 degrees lower temperature opposite to the prediction of
thermal environment that showed an assumption for excessively high room tempera-
tures during the summer period.
The findings lead to increasing the awareness of consistent supervision with a
building construction can lead to prevention of building defects. The investigation is
approaching for a future study with the awareness of essential installation of moni-
toring operational data in prospect of an assessment of building performance and an
energy study.

Preface
This master thesis was prepared at the department of Civil Engineering at the Tech-
nical University of Denmark in fulfillment of the requirements for acquiring a Master
of Science degree in Architectural Engineering.
The master thesis is covering the main report and an appendix. The appendix in-
cludes contains original drawings, calculations and data from the fieldwork in Nuuk,
which are not presented in the main report. As a supplement to the written thesis,
a digital appendix is uploaded containing the relevant models, simulations files, from
Sketch-Up and IDA ICE and finally the Excels file with the processed data.
The thesis is based on Laursen’s thesis template and designed to be read in a two
page view. Due to the photos, illustrations, and graphical results it is recommended
to read the thesis in colors. The photo on the front page shows the Test House in
April 2021 taken by the author of this thesis.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Before the 1800s’ the building construction in Greenland were traditional huts made
of peat, driftwood, stone and skin. Today those types of buildings are not commonly
seen anymore.
After the 19th century use of wood in Greenlandic constructions
were increasing due to importation of wood. In 1950’s the typical Greenlandic small
wooden-houses was introduced and the building from that time period and on wards,
is mainly what is left as houses in Greenland. By means the house constructions in
Greenland is fairly new, and there is still a lack of solid building practise and knowl-
edge of how to build most eﬀicient in an Arctic climate.
Up until recently most of the buildings are houses of same types or standards. Dur-
ing the past 60 years a lack in the fast development is the conduction of knowledge
sharing and learning objective for a building project. Most of the standard houses
built back in the 1960s’ and 1970s’ were constructed according to a danish building
standard. This resulted in poor insulated houses or constructions not fitted for an
Arctic climate.
Arctic Building and Construction (ABC) is a research project managed by DTU
Byg in collaboration with four universities. The Test House is an independent part of
the ABC project. The project started with the desire to acquire knowledge from an
architectural project with an visionary statement to create a ”New Arctic Building
Practise”. During the development of the project from 2012 to 2020, where it was
built and delivered, the project changed name to ”Test House” in recognition to
that a building practise is something that gradually develops over years of experience,
documentation and sharing of acquired research knowledge. Not from a single project.
ABC-project consist of 5 sub-projects as building physics and indoor climate, process,
sustainability, architecture and urban spaces and user satisfaction. These sub-projects
are investigated by other experimental studies and not highlighted in this thesis.

2.1
Building tendencies and developments of
residential in Greenland
A wide range of previous assessments of existing residential buildings in Greenland
often all show the same problems arising from houses of the period from 1950’s until
2006. [3]. The Greenlandic wooden residential houses more than half the existing
building in the Arctic Greenland have problems with draught and unreasonable low
temperatures for internal rooms due to lack of insuﬀicient thermal insulation and
air-tightness of building envelopes, as lack of managing technical systems. Cf. the
findings in 2012 of the standard wooden houses the issues were dealing with low ther-
mal comfort, poor indoor air quality and the high amount of energy consumption and
high greenhouse gas emission (GHG) [4].
The Greenlandic Standard Wooden Houses
The paper [3] gives an account on the need for energy saving in the standard fam-
ily detached and semi-detached wooden houses in Greenland. The studies revealed
a significant potential for energy savings based on comparisons of house construc-
tion against Building Regulation requirements. The investigation carried out in 2011
showed at the time a current energy consumption of up to 383 kWh/m2/year for
heating, poor air tightness, a large number of thermal bridges and high indoor tem-
peratures.
A year later is a theoretical practice developed for applying super-insulation solution
for renovation of existing standard wood houses. The wrap-up system provides a
robust building renovation method focusing on applying membrane for air tightness
with a minimum risk of leakage, expansion of insulation with consideration of risk
of thermal bridges etc. Figure 2.1 shows the types of a standard family wooden house.
The examination of the houses included visual inspections to determine thermal
bridges using a thermal camera, air tightness was measured by the blower-door test
method. Measurements of outdoor and indoor temperature, relative humidity was
performed prior to the calculation of the energy consumption and used to determine
the potential for energy saving.

2.2
Theoretical study of previous methods used for
evaluation of energy-eﬀicient buildings
The master thesis Evaluation of the energy consumption and indoor climate in a
low energy house ([25] Nikolaos Matzaflaras and Panagiotis Paliouras, 2014) de-
scribes a methodology called post-occupancy assessment, calibration, and optimiza-
tion methodology (POACOM). The study addresses the deviation between the initial
design goals and the final performance with focus on energy consumption and indoor
climate. The main aim of this methodology aims to evaluate the performance of a
building, by comparing the design goals with the actual performance of the house
when it is fully occupied. The attempt to investigate this gap is performed by match-
ing actual measured data with simulated data for later achieve a building model which
is calibrated to have a performance equal to the actual performance state.
The report discusses previous studies in this field in respect to their chosen method-
ologies. Several of the existing methods only assess this gap with respect to the energy
performance of a building. In this master thesis is the methodology developed to take
the quality of the indoor environment into account.
The results from the investigation based on the POACOM methodology proved to
determine the reasons for these irregularities between the theoretical design and the
actual operation. It was found that the occupant behavior was an important param-
eter with significant influence in both a positive and negative manner. The sensitive
analysis demonstrated that improper regulation of the mechanical ventilation system
and solar shading devices were reasons for a dissatisfying indoor environment.
Furthermore, the paper Prospective future introduction of reduction of energy
use in buildings in the Arctic regions – How might it affect the indoor climate [Pe-
tra Vladykova, Søren P. Bjarlø, 2012] review the issues in Arctic Greenland with
draughts, uncomfortable low temperatures indoor and inadequate ventilation. The
paper compares an old residential building and a low energy house in relation to en-
ergy consumption and discuss various parameter that have influence on the thermal
comfort. They conclude that for the future reductions in energy use for buildings in
the Arctic regions the essence of the potentials lies within the control of the mechan-
ical ventilation system, building materials and their properties, the behavior of the
occupants, and last the design of the building.
The paper Energy performance and Indoor Air Quality in Modern Buildings in
Greenland ([22] Martin Kotol, Carsten Rode, 2012) describes the importance of a
house installed with a complex monitoring system, which enables the researchers to
evaluate the energy performance of the building, and the indoor air quality. It is
possible to measure the performance of single components, as balanced mechanical
ventilation with heat recovery or solar collectors.
Therefore the research aims to

Climate condition in Greenland
Greenland has an arctic climate and a tundra climate on and near the coast.
In
inland areas it is common to have an ice cap climate, where the monthly mean tem-
perature never exceeds 0◦C, also called a polar climate and these areas are normally
permanently covered by layer of ice and no vegetation. The weather conditions in
Greenland varies a lot, however an arctic climate contains cool summers and cool
winters. The average temperature during summer do not exceed 10◦C even in the
warmest summer periods. In Northeast Greenland the lowest temperature has been
measured to - 66◦C at Klinck Station, whereas the highest measured temperature at
Summit Camp, in Northeast Greenland is 2.2 ◦C [30].
This thesis focus on the climate where the Test House is built, thus the focus on the
weather conditions in Nuuk.

3.1.1
Calculation procedure for energy consumption
The annual energy consumption is composed of the energy need for space heating and
cooling, ventilation, domestic hot water consumption, electric energy for lighting, fa-
cilities, and equipment. This thesis focuses on the methodology used for investigation
of a building’s energy performance, based dynamic calculations. Therefore is given
a short elaboration of the energy contributions to the energy balance for building
consumption
The energy consumption for
• Heating accounts for net heating energy need, which is determined from the
heating energy need and the energy gain from solar radiation, energy recov-
ered from exhaust air and internal heat loads. A heating system brings the
energy corresponding to the net heating energy need into spaces, supply air,
and domestic hot water. It includes taking system losses and conversions into
consideration. System losses consist of losses from generating, storing, distribu-
tion, and transfer of heating energy. Conversion takes place in heating pumps
or fuel cells.
• Cooling accounts for the net cooling energy need, covering losses from generat-
ing, storing, distribution, and transfer as well as conversions.

Thermography and the infra-red camera
This section is a brief explanation of the technology behind an infra-red camera.
Thermography is defined as a technique for portraying an object using the thermal
energy radiating from the surface of that object. The basic task of the infra-red cam-
era is the detection of radiation and converting it into an electrical signal and display

3 Theoretical background
In the Greenlandic Building Regulation version 2021 a constructed building must
be able to document air tightness test prior to habitable confirmation. The air tight-
ness pressure test must comply with the requirements shown in Chapter 7 - Energy
consumption [10]. The air change through leakage in the climate facade must not

Pa. For buildings with the room height, where the building envelope area divided by
floor area are above 3, the air change rate must not exceed 0.3 l/s per. m2 envelope

An air pressure test must be carried out in accordance to DS/EN ISO 9972 Ther-
mal performance of buildings - Determination of air permeability of buildings - Fan
pressurization method. The requirements are to be proved on the basis of Method 3
in DS/EN ISO 9972 [13].
Air leakage is air that escapes the building via uncontrolled means. The leakage
can occur at gaps, cracks or hole in the envelope which can cause draft, or as long-
term effect create condensation that can lead to mold in the construction. Extreme
air leakage can affect the energy eﬀiciency of buildings. Securing a high level of air
tightness contributes to energy savings, comfort and durability of the building.

4.1.1.1
Building thermography
Infra-red thermography is a method used to investigate the thermal properties and
performance of building envelopes or the indoor climate. From thermograms it is pos-
sible to analyse and study whether the construction is properly insulated or detect
heat loss areas in the construction. A thermal camera is using the infra-red light to
create an thermal image, described in section 3.2.2 in the theoretical background.
Building thermography can be applied to many facet of the building performance
and performance of components as listed below [19]:
• Insulation defect detection
• Air leakage detection
• Heat loss through windows
• Dampness detection
• Detection of ’hidden detail’ (subsurface pipes, flues, ducts, wall ties, etc.)
• Examination of heating systems (eﬀiciency, damage to insulation, blocked pipes,
heat distribution, failed control equipment etc.)
• Preventative maintenance
• Electrical defect detection
• Leakage from earth embankment dams
There are two approaches for carrying out thermographic inspection; the qualitative
and the quantitative.
The method used for this survey is mostly the qualitative
approach. It is the most straight forward and it can be used to find hidden detail,
missing insulation by observation without the need for measurement detail [20].
This thermal study of the Testhouse will only be focusing on insulation faults in the
construction or air infiltration. But it should be noted, however, that once a simple
approach has been carried out, it is diﬀicult to extract further information, then it
is essential to do several detailed thermal recordings presenting more accuracy of
analytical parameters [20].

4.2.3
Calibrating the building model
The method and detailed process of calibrating a mathematical building model in a
dynamic simulation tools is described in this section. After the theoretical building
model is generated the next steps in the TAC method are to analyse the actual
performance measured during building operation and compare it with the theoretical
model. The reasons for differences between these two states must be evaluated and
identified in order to modify the theoretical building. These is an on-going process of
iterations until the mathematical building model achieves a deviation within a desired
range compared to the actual performance. The detailed procedures are illustrated
in figure 4.13 [STEP 2] and 4.14 [STEP 2.1].
==PAGE:60==

4 Methodology
Figure 4.13: STEP 2: Calibrating the building model
Figure 4.13 illustrates the overview of the calibration procedure by updating the
theoretical building with data from the actual performance state.
The early stage of the TAC process aims to acquire results from the dynamic simu-
lations of the theoretical model. These results are then intended to be compared to
the predicted ”as-design” data results from the initial calculation of the engineering
team. The assessment of the two data sets of results in as-design state may support
the investigation of the differences in the choice on methodologies. The dynamic sim-
ulation results of the theoretical model can now be compared to the actual operation
of the building.
The significant divergences between the theoretical and actual performance must
be identified. The second data set based on measurement of the building is essential
for the evaluation of the actual building performance and for the findings of the pa-
rameter with most influence on the energy performance and indoor environment.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
The Testhouse in Nuuk, Greenland is the case-study in this master thesis. The re-
search house is built with a double climate-facade covering a heated inner zone and
an unheated middle zone, surrounding the whole inner construction. Poly-carbonate
forms the outer frame and is an attempt to explore new methods within the building-
and architectural tendencies in the Arctic Greenland. The motivation for this study
derives from the question of how this Test House performs, focusing on the energy
utilization and the distribution of temperature with respect to the thermal influence
of the middle zone.
Another aim with the investigation was also to explore the gap between a theoretical
performance prediction versus the actual performance once the house is fully occupied
and in operation. To answer this aim of the investigation a method for comparing
measured data with simulation data how been carried out. The evaluation of the
performance focus on the energy consumption and the indoor climate looking at tem-
peratures and relative humidity.
6.1
Discussion of Actual Performance
6.1.1
Fieldwork in Greenland
The thermal performance of the house has been investigated during the field-trip in
Nuuk. The investigation was carried out with the use of an infrared thermal camera.
The first goal was to conduct thermal images of the building for different weather con-
ditions. These examination of the house was performed during the winter in March
and April, which is the most valid period to investigate a house in terms of thermal
bridges or air leaks, especially when situated in an Arctic region.
The investigation showed that there were several places in the construction found to
be irregularities in the temperature distribution in corners. In the kitchen a thermal
bridge was detected at the floor level facing Northeast measuring a surface temper-
ature of 8.5◦C and in the same open space in living-room facing South was found a
surface temperature difference of 21.5◦C and lowest temperature at -6.6◦C. This is
a critical low surface temperature to find indoor for a heated space. These thermal
bridges could indicate wall/floor connections that are not suﬀicient insulated or not
properly secured according to air tighten the construction.
==PAGE:94==
78
6 Discussion
Furthermore, there was found a massive thermal bridge or air leakage for the entire
jump in floor level facing Northeast, right in the core of the house at the lowest level
housing the bedroom 1.1, stairwell closet and the kitchen. In bedroom 1.1 was found
a surface temperature of -16◦C in the wall/floor connection. The problematic area in
the construction in this bedroom indicates to be an air leakage, considering the char-
acteristic pattern of the cold air penetrating through the structure in the floor/wall
construction. The leak area is very critical. It is the source for a massive heat loss,
and it is a potentially high-risk point for developing mold and fungi from due to
threats of condensation. At the same time of the investigation the room temperature
in the bedroom was approx 13.5◦C. This shows that the air leakage have a really high
influence on the indoor thermal environment, but this will not only affect the thermal
perception and satisfaction but it will also conflict with the control of managing the
ventilation system leading to ineﬀicient energy performance.
For the scullery it was possible to document very cold and fluctuating thermal
conditions arising from poor air tightness, though this is less a threat since the space
is unheated. Thus, it actually shows to have an impact on the entrance adjacent to
this part of the middle zone. At the door in the entrance was detected a thermal
bridge by the intersection of these two rooms. This could be a failure in the assembly
of the door indicating poor tightness.
In the conservatory it showed that the floor damper had a surface temperature of
-14◦C compared to an average floor surface temperature of 2◦C. It was known from
interview with the residents that the floor damper was closed on purpose since they
experienced too much heat loss from this. The closed damper is then resulting in an
undesired air leakage which creates unreasonable cold conditions in the conservatory.
On the 4th of April which is a day with less extreme weather conditions as the first
examination on the 27th of March, there was found a thermal bridge in the corner of
the walls between the scullery and the stairwell in the entrance. This indicates a risk
of undesired heat loss.
Some of the uncertainties with the thermal investigation can be discussed with
respect to the Danish Standard for performing thermal investigation. Many of these
requirements for carrying out a valid thermal investigation was not known before the
thermal images were taken. This is the first time for practising and using a thermal
camera. Thus, according to the guidance the thermography examination must be
carried out when the temperature difference is 10K between inside and outdoor. This
was no problem to accommodate. Besides, it is desired to perform the measurements
on a calm weather day and windless.
This can be argued if these conditions are
adaptable for Greenland, or any other Arctic Regions where the weather conditions
are so extreme, that it is more common to have a windy winter day than to have
a windless day. Furthermore, it was realize that the camera thermal setup for the
affecting surroundings was not entirely correct, but the images were later able to
manipulate in the program FLIR Tools and modify the settings. It showed that these
==PAGE:95==

changes had a negligible impact on the measured surface temperatures.
Taking the given uncertainties into account it can still be argued that the ther-
mal images are valid since they provide the detection of extremely high differences in
surface temperatures and the chosen thermal images in the result chapter indicates
issues with thermal bridges and for some areas air leakages.
The building is fairly new (at the time of the thermal images - less than a year with
heating activated), so it is unlikely that the thermal bridges and air leakage detected
by the thermograms would show signs of moisture, but these defects have a risk of
causing condensation. If these defects in the construction are not being prevented
these areas could be prime locations for moisture and creation of mold.
In the reflection of the thermal investigation it was acknowledge that a air tight-
ness test would have been essential for the detection of small cracks. It is given in
[13] that a pressure test is sometimes a demand in the documentation of defects in
the building shell. During the fieldwork in Greenland it had been beneficial to supply
the thermal images with a ’Blower-Door’ test. The building geometry is complex and
is therefore in higher risks of potential building defects from under construction. A
Blower-Door test is a pressure test, which helps exaggerate air leaking through defects
making the leakage areas visible. It includes a calibrated fan, a door panel system,
and a device to measure fan flow and the building pressure. The fan blows air into or
out of the building and creates a small pressure difference between inside and outside
[19]. If the test had been performed it would have been possible to calculate the air
change rate for the Test House under the given circumstances.

6.3
Reflections and limitations
Considering the detection of the building defects in the construction it is reason-
able to conclude that it would have been profitable if an air tightness test had been
conducted in the final process of the construction. The building defects could have
probably been discovered in the early process.
For the theoretical building model there are many parameters of interest to do sen-
sitivity analysis. . The theoretical building model could be modified by an iterative
process aiming to get close to a calibrated building model. Thus, it is questionable
if it possible to calibrate the mathematical model when a significant defect as the
critical air leakage in bedroom 1.1 has been found. Furthermore, this is of such influ-
ence for the actual performance that to calibrate the model it would also be essential
to know the exact air flow / air change rates for the whole building. If these values
were to be conducted by performing a blower door test the model could be updated
with these and further investigated by following the calibration process from the TAC
methods described in this thesis and elaborated on in the next section 6.4 future work.
For prospect it would possible to investigate the influence the air leakage have on the
energy consumption and the thermal environment.
Another possibility is to conduct monitored data after the building defects have
been repaired and calibrate the building model to reflect the actual performance.
With this version the potentials is an optimization study of the calibrated model in
terms of a sensitivity analysis that should provide an understanding of the potential
for performance eﬀiciency, by finding the optimal settings for regulation of mechanical
ventilation system, the importance of occupant behavior and solar shading devices
etc.
Limitations
During the first months the monitoring system was not completely finished yet, there-
fore manual readings of the energy meter from district heating company were taken
in order to obtain the monthly heat consumption separately for each component.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This master thesis has been investigating the Test House in Nuuk in terms of the
energy performance and the pattern for the indoor climate. The characteristics of
this house is the double climate facade consisting of a heated- and an unheated zone,
which have been an essential factor for studying the influence of such a structure in
Greenland.
The investigation consisted both of fieldwork in Nuuk, Greenland, and a building
performance analysis of the Test House by use of a dynamic simulation software, IDA
Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE) which complies with the ASHRAE Standard
90.1 for energy eﬀiciency requirements of buildings.
The thermographic examination of the internal building construction found es-
pecially one area with a critical air leakage. This was affecting the indoor climate
and is considered to have a negative influence on the energy demand. The thermal
investigation detected furthermore several thermal bridges, which are assumed to be
due to building defects in corner joints or wall assemblies or a result of constructions
areas being poorly insulated.The lowest measured surface temperature at -16◦C was
found in the bedroom on the ground level facing North/northeast. This was at a day
with a wind-speed of 2m/s and -20 degree outside. Another thermal examination of
the house under different weather conditions found 2◦C on a regular winter day with
10m/s and -7 degrees outside.
The creation of a theoretical building model in IDA ICE has shown to be successful.
It was possible to define a building model that reflects the complex building concept
of the double climate screen. The results of the dynamic estimations based on the
theoretical building model showed that the middle zone is exposed for overheating
during summer, where the solar radiance is at it’s highest.
The middle zone are
predicted to have an negative impact on inner zone resulting in 1175-3413 hours
of overheating for temperatures above 25◦C. Based on the dynamic simulation the
annual energy demand was estimated to 128 kWh/m2.
The monitored data from the sensors show an occurrence of low temperatures for
the inner zone at 10.7◦C and high temperatures around 30-34◦C. The average room
temperature is more or less stable at [20-21.5]◦C, but the building’s inner zone is
exposed for a fluctuating temperature variation. The relative humidity are found to

be [25.3-27]% as a monthly average, which is in the low end and can be experienced
as very dry. The actual annual energy consumption for first year of operation [Oct20-
Sep20] was measured from the period of 1 October 2020 to 25 September 2021 and
resulted in a profound calculation for the annual energy demand to be 138 kWh/m2.
When the Test House was built there had not been installed a proper monitor system
causing limitation for a detailed investigation of the delivered energy and examina-
tion of energy eﬀiciency.
Part of the building performance simulations were also aiming to explore a known
problematic with a performance gap between the actual energy performance and the
theoretical performance predicted in the design phase. It is achieved with the in-
vestigation to discover parameters that conflicts with interpretation and expectation
of how a building is designed to perform. The method called TAC is combined by
previous methodologies in this field and have moderately been a suitable approach
for this investigation.
The variation in room temperatures from the theoretical results compared to the
measured occurrence showed extremely high room temperatures for the inner zone
during summer with a temperature difference of 5-15◦i contrast to the actual condi-
tion. It was found that the actual lowest temperatures were consistently way lower
than expected due to the assumption of an excessively high air change. It is assumed
that the undesired cooling of the building eliminates overheating, thus inversely causes
issues with maintaining a steady room temperature.
The building model shows to have potential for energy optimization, based on the
discovered defects and the gained knowledge of the influence of system parameters
that could be adjusted in terms of optimal control settings. Reaching the next step
in the investigation some parameters have been found essential to conduct prior to
the process of providing optimization proposals. It is important to have monitored
data from the delivered energy to the heat pump and the utilized energy, and the
information about air change in the rooms.
For the further investigation of energy optimization was found potentials for pur-
suing a calibration of the building model but on conditions of the recognition of two
important factors. An air pressure test should be completed with the aim of calculat-
ing the air change rate in the building, representing the current operation state, or it
is recommended that the detected building defects are repaired for improving the air
tightness. Thereafter it should be possible to calibrate the model with a new set of
measured data for the actual performance.
