Abstract
This thesis investigates the innovative training concept utilising eye tracking data from
human super­recognisers to enhance human performance in identifying manipulations in
facial images, which is a crucial skill in high­security biometric verification settings like
border control. The research, extending the foundational work of Nichols et al., is centred
around developing intuitive visual feedback tools that specifically aim to boost human
ability in recognising digital alterations in facial images.
The methodology of the study involves a thorough analysis and interpretation of that eye
tracking data to understand the focal points of super­recognises when examining facial
images. The study features two newly­developed innovative methods for presenting eye
tracking traces: spotlight­ and isotherm visualisations. Central to the research is an online
experiment conducted with both professional and non­professional participants. This ex­
periment was designed to test their improved ability to identify manipulated images using
a tool providing detailed visual and textual feedback after each participant’s decision. This
innovative tool, acting as a training and enhancement mechanism, allowed participants to
engage in an in­depth analysis of the images. The interactions of the participants with the
tool were carefully recorded, with findings indicating a notable improvement in detection
capabilities across all user groups. These results demonstrate the tool’s effectiveness in
amplifying human skills in image analysis.
The study concludes with a moderate success rate in manipulation detection, empha­
sising the substantial potential of merging human cognitive skills with technological ad­
vancements in biometric verification processes. Particularly noteworthy is the observed
enhancement in accuracy during interactions with the tool. The experiment commenced
with an average accuracy of 58%, which progressively rose to approximately 64% by
its conclusion. Future research is suggested to focus on optimising feedback presenta­
tion techniques, introducing varied complexities in image manipulation, and investigating
adaptive learning models. This thesis contributes crucial insights into the development
of training methodologies for enhancing the accuracy and efficiency of human document
examiners, who remain an integral component of various security frameworks.

Introduction
In an era where biometric systems are starting to play an increasingly crucial role in iden­
tity verification, the reliability and integrity of these systems is significant. Particularly in
security­sensitive domains like airport security and border control, the ability to swiftly and
accurately recognise ­ such as those altered by face morphing or retouching ­ is crucial
[1]. This thesis proposes the development of a biometric training tool that harnesses eye
tracking data from individuals known as ”super­recognisers.” These are people with an ex­
traordinary ability to recognise and recall faces, often outperforming traditional automated
systems in identifying subtle manipulations in face images [2].
The reason for this research comes from the growing reliance on biometric systems for
identity verification and the accompanying challenges. Despite advancements in auto­
mated face recognition technology, there exists a critical gap in addressing security vul­
nerabilities, particularly the creation of manipulated face images capable of deceiving
these systems. This gap underscores the enduring importance of human inspectors in
the identity verification process. Their cognitive abilities enable them to perceive intricate
visual cues that often elude automated algorithms [3].
This research builds upon a previous study Psychophysical Evaluation of Human Perfor­
mance in Detecting Digital Face Image Manipulations study by Nichols et al. [4], which
provided an evaluation of human performance in the detection of digital face image ma­
nipulations. The eye­tracking data derived from this study forms the foundation of the
current research. This study focuses on a dataset from the best manipulation detection
performers, aiming to uncover their decision­making traces. The primary aim is to utilise
this dataset to construct informative feedback that incorporates graphical representations
of eye movement behaviour. These feedback boards will highlight regions to which top
performers directed their attention, thereby providing a unique and practical approach to
training.
Recognising the potential of harnessing human expertise in this context, the proposed
research aims to leverage eye tracking data from super­recognisers to develop an effec­
tive biometric training tool. This tool aims to improve the proficiency of security personnel
in identifying manipulated face images, thereby bolstering their capacity to detect forged
documents and identity fraud.
1.1
Goals of the research
The guiding research questions are:
1. How can eye tracking data from human super­recognisers be effectively utilised
to enhance the accuracy of human observers in detecting manipulations in face
images?
2. What strategies can be employed to transform eye tracking data into attention traces
that effectively capture the visual patterns and cues used by human super­recognisers
to identify manipulated face images?

Background and Related Work
This chapter presents the foundational aspects of biometrics and the related field of face
recognition, emphasising their growing significance in identity verification and security.
Central to this discussion are the advanced techniques of face image manipulation, in­
cluding face swapping, morphing, and beautification, and their implications for security
and privacy. Additionally, the chapter presents the human aspect of recognising these
manipulations, focusing on the capabilities of super­recognisers and ways of capturing
their eye movement behaviour. This sets the background for understanding the complex
interplay between technology and human perception in the realm of digital identification
and security.
2.1
Biometrics
Biometrics refers to the automated recognition of individuals based on their behavioural
and biological characteristics [5]. Technology is fundamentally rooted in the principle that
every person is unique, with distinct physical traits and behaviours. Biometric systems
typically involve the capture and analysis of traits like fingerprints, facial structures, iris
patterns, voice, or even gait [6].
The use of biometrics has gained prominence due to its reliability in identifying and verify­
ing individuals. This technology has become integral in various sectors, including security,
law enforcement, or consumer electronics. Unlike traditional identification methods like
passwords or ID cards, biometrics provide a more secure and convenient way of authen­
tication, due to biometric traits being inherently linked to the individual and in turn, being
generally difficult to replicate or steal [7].
2.2
Face recognition
Face recognition is a branch of biometric technology that focuses on identifying or verifying
a person’s identity using their facial traits. This technology works by capturing, analysing,
and comparing patterns based on the person’s facial contours. It has widespread appli­
cations, ranging from security and law enforcement to consumer electronics and social
networks. In security, it aids in surveillance and access control, while in consumer elec­
tronics, it is used for device authentication and personalised user experiences [8].
However, face recognition also brings with it several threats and concerns. The most
notable is the potential for privacy invasion, as it allows tracking of individuals without
consent. There is also the risk of misuse or abuse of the technology, such as in unau­
thorised surveillance or identity fraud. Furthermore, concerns about accuracy and bias,
particularly regarding race and gender, have been raised, given that these systems may
not perform equally well across all demographic groups [9].
2.3
Face image manipulation
Digital alterations of facial images, a technique originally confined to professional realms
such as advertising and graphic design, have now found a more concerning role in the
propagation of misinformation through online platforms, as well as in crime activity such

2.3.1
Face swapping
Face swapping involves a process of substituting one person’s face with another’s. This
technique, rooted in advanced machine learning algorithms, maps a subject’s facial char­
acteristics onto another person’s rest of the appearance. As a result, one can get a re­
markably realistic and often indistinguishable facial exchange Figure 2.1. presents an
example of this manipulation technique. Primarily utilised for entertainment, face swap­
ping has become a staple in movies and television shows for creating special effects. But
its use is not confined to the realm of entertainment [16].
Face swapping can be a subject to potential misuse. It can be utilised to fabricate false
images or videos, causing misinformation or identity theft. This aspect raises significant
concerns, particularly regarding the integrity of information in our increasingly digital world.
The capability of these manipulations to deceive not only humans, but also sophisticated
face recognition systems underscores the urgency to address these challenges [17].

2.3.2
Face morphing
Face morphing is a sophisticated digital technique that blends two or more faces to create
a composite image, which seamlessly integrates facial traits from each contributing face
[20].
This technique begins with the selection of source images, typically front­facing pho­
tographs with neutral expressions. The process then involves aligning key facial land­
marks such as the eyes, nose, and mouth across the chosen images. This alignment is
crucial to compare the corresponding traits of each face accurately in the final morphed
image. The sophisticated algorithms employed at this stage ensure that the warping is
smooth and the facial traits blend naturally. After this warping, the images are blended
together. This blending is often achieved through advanced algorithms that ensure the
transitions between the different facial traits are smooth and realistic, creating a final im­
age that appears natural and convincing. The final stage of face morphing is refinement,
where the composite image undergoes further adjustments. These refinements are aimed
at eliminating any imperfections that might have arisen during the blending process, en­
hancing the overall realism of the morphed face [21] .The whole process is depicted in
Figure 2.2, while the example results of morphing are presented in Figure 2.3.
Face morphing has diverse applications across various fields. In the realm of entertain­
ment and art, it is widely used to create unique characters in movies and video games, as
well as in digital art projects. Researchers in the fields of psychology and facial recogni­
tion also utilise face morphing to study how humans perceive faces and the psychological
implications thereof [22]. In forensics and law enforcement, morphed images can be in­
strumental in reconstructing suspects’ appearances or identifying individuals when only
partial facial information is available [23].

2.4
Insights from psychophysics
Psychophysics, a which is a subfield of psychology, is dedicated to exploring the transfor­
mation of physical events into psychological experiences. This discipline delves into the
relationship between the quantifiable physical characteristics of stimuli and the subjec­
tive psychological perceptions of these stimuli. Psychophysics primarily focuses on three
capabilities: stimulus detection, discrimination, and intensity estimation (scaling). Each
of these capabilities is linked to foundational concepts such as absolute and differential
thresholds [32].
2.4.1
Methods of estimating absolute threshold
Sensory systems are our biological tools for detecting changes in the environment, be they
physical or chemical. One of the key goals of psychophysics is to determine the minimum
amplitude necessary for these changes to be perceptible to an individual, known as the
absolute threshold. This threshold marks the lowest level of stimulus intensity detectable
by an individual in the absence of other stimulation. However, it’s important to note that
this threshold is not a fixed point but rather a concept defined operationally for a particular
measurement method [32].
Several methods exist for estimating the absolute threshold, with the most traditional being
the method of constant stimuli, the method of limits, and the method of adjustment [32].
Method of constant stimuli
Using the constant stimuli method, a range is first established where a stimulus is almost
undetectable to almost always perceived. Several stimuli within this range are selected
and presented in random order for numerous trials. Observers report their perception,
leading to a probability­based psychometric function. This function, often an S­shaped
ogive, helps determine the absolute threshold operationally as the point where a stimulus
is detected 50% of the time [33].
Besides the constant stimuli method, other methods like the adjustment method and the
method of limits are also used for threshold estimation.

The method of adjustment
In this method, observers actively adjust a stimulus’s intensity to perceive it just at the
threshold level. This method is more suited for difference threshold determination and
provides insights into sensitivity levels through the standard deviation of equality points
[32].
The method of limits
This method involves presenting stimuli in ascending and descending orders to determine
absolute or difference thresholds. It considers transition points from perception to non­
perception (or vice versa), averaging these points to estimate the threshold [32].
2.4.2
Signal detection theory
Despite the precision of methods like the constant stimuli method, a major challenge
arises from the influence of decision­making processes on sensitivity estimates. Signal
Detection Theory (SDT), developed in the 1940s, addresses this issue by employing two
parameters: one describing sensitivity and the other decision­making strategy.
SDT is based on the concepts of signal and noise. The signal is the specific stimulus be­
ing detected, while noise represents the ever­changing background activity, both external
and internal (physiological). In SDT tasks, observers decide whether they are experienc­
ing noise alone or noise with an added signal. This decision­making process is influenced
by the observer’s criterion, measured by the index beta (ß), leading to four possible out­
comes: hit, miss, false alarm, and correct rejection. This concept is often represented in
a form of a plot with two normal probability distribution, demonstrated in Figure 2.5 [33].

2.4.3
Sensory discrimination and difference threshold
Another critical aspect of psychophysics is sensory discrimination, which involves dis­
cerning whether two stimuli are different. The minimum intensity difference required for
differentiation is termed the difference threshold or just noticeable difference (JND).
Similar to absolute threshold estimation, the method of constant stimuli can also be ap­
plied for the difference threshold. Observers are presented with a standard stimulus and a
comparison stimulus of varying magnitudes. The observer’s task is to determine which of
the two stimuli has the greater magnitude. A psychometric function is then constructed,
revealing two key variables: the point of subjective equality (PSE) and the difference

2.5
Human super-recognisers
Super­recognises are individuals with an extraordinary ability to recognise and remember
faces. This unique skill set distinguishes them from the general population. The concept
of a super­recogniser was first introduced in research studies that identified significant
variances in face recognition abilities among individuals. Unlike average people, super­
recognisers demonstrate an exceptional aptitude for both identifying familiar faces and
recalling unfamiliar ones they have seen briefly. This ability is thought to be innate and
cannot be significantly improved through training or experience [3] [34].
To be classified as a super­recogniser, individuals must meet certain criteria, primarily
evaluated through standardised testing. The most common test used is the Cambridge
Face Memory Test (CFMT), where participants are required to memorise and later recog­
nise faces. Super­recognisers score significantly higher on these tests compared to the
average population. There’s no established threshold score for being a super­recogniser,
but typically, scoring in the top 2% of the population is considered indicative of this ability
[35].
The phenomenon of super­recognisers is believed to stem from both genetic and neuro­
logical factors. Studies suggest that these abilities may be hereditary, indicating a genetic
component. Neurologically, it is hypothesised that super­recognisers might have differ­
ences in the fusiform gyrus area of the brain, which is associated with facial recognition
and processing. However, the precise mechanisms behind these exceptional abilities are
still a subject of ongoing research [36].
Super­recognisers find applications in various fields, particularly in security and law en­
forcement. Their ability to accurately identify individuals can be crucial in solving crimes
or identifying suspects in crowds. For instance, the London Metropolitan Police has em­
ployed super­recognisers in their operations, particularly during events that require high
security. Furthermore, their skills are valuable in areas like passport control and airport
security, where they help in spotting false identities or matching individuals to their iden­
tification documents [37].
2.6
Eye tracking
Eye tracking is a technology used to measure where a subject is looking (the point of
gaze) and the movement of their eyes. This technique involves detecting the presence,
attention, and focus of the subject’s eyes using either screen­based or head­mounted
devices equipped with sensors and cameras. The data gathered from eye tracking can
be used to understand various aspects of human behaviour and cognition. For example,
it can reveal how individuals read text, view images, or interact with interfaces, providing
valuable insights in fields such as psychology, marketing, web and product design, and
medical research. Eye tracking is particularly important in studying visual perception,
learning about the way in which a subject examines the presented stimuli [38].

Measuring classification performance
In the realm of biometrics, classification is a critical process where human recognises or
automated systems categorise biometric data, such as images or behavioural patterns,
into predefined classes. In the context of this research, this primarily involves distinguish­
ing between bona fide (”pristine”) and manipulated (altered) images. Classification prob­
lems in biometrics are essentially decision­making tasks where accuracy and reliability
are key [47].
Figure 2.10: An example of a probability distribution of a classification task [48]
The outcomes of a classification task are commonly categorised into four types: True
Positives (TP), True Negatives (TN), False Positives (FP), and False Negatives (FN):
1. True Positives (TP): Instances where the classifier correctly identifies a manipu­
lated image as manipulated.
2. True Negatives (TN): Instances where the classifier correctly identifies a bona fide
image as bona fide.
3. False Positives (FP): Instances where the classifier incorrectly identifies a bona
fide image as manipulated.
4. False Negatives (FN): Instances where the classifier incorrectly identifies a manip­
ulated image as bona fide.
To illustrate these classification, probability distribution plots are often used. These plots
typically represent the probability distributions of two (or more) classes ­ bona fide and
manipulated images, in case of this study. An example of such a curve is depicted in
Figure 2.10.
The x­axis of the plot represents the range of possible values (usually a score or prob­
ability) that the classifier assigns to an instance, indicating the likelihood of belonging to
a particular class. The y­axis shows the frequency or probability density of these val­
ues. By plotting the distribution of both classes on the same graph, it becomes evident
where their distributions overlap and where they are distinct. A key aspect of these plots
is the inclusion of a decision threshold, a specific value at which the classifier transitions
from labelling an instance as one class to the other. This threshold plays a key role in
determining the classification outcomes. Instances with values above the threshold are
classified into one category (e.g., manipulated), and those below are classified into the
other (e.g., bona fide). The regions under these curves correspond to TP, TN, FP, and

4.4
Abstraction and classification of eye tracking events
The process of data abstraction in the research involves breaking down the simple eye­
tracking data into more insightful categories. This step is crucial for extracting meaningful
patterns from the participants’ gaze behaviour. This abstraction not only simplifies the data
but also enriches our interpretation, allowing for better comprehension of the cognitive
processes involved in identifying manipulated images. It’s a key step in transforming raw
data into valuable insights for the research.
Analysing the translated eye­tracking data involved categorising the events into three dis­
tinct types: fixations, active comparisons, and visual searches.
4.4.1
Fixations
Firstly, the raw fixation events have been already considered significant enough. These
events often indicate that the participant found something noteworthy in that area of the
image [51], perhaps a clue as to whether the image was manipulated or authentic. This
category was the most common among the eye­tracking events in the experiment.
4.4.2
Active comparisons
The second category involved dividing saccades, which are rapid eye movements, into
two types: active comparisons and visual searches. An active comparison was identified
when a participant seemed to compare a specific facial trait or landmark in the suspected
image with the same trait in the trusted image. This comparison suggests that the par­
ticipant is checking for inconsistencies between the two images. To be classified as an
active comparison, the eye movement had to
• start in one image and end in the other (suspected­to­trusted image saccade or
trusted­to­suspected) and
• the distance between the start­ and end point of the saccade (expressed in the
absolute coordinates of respective images) had to be less than 50 pixels.

Data abstraction involved categorising eye­tracking events into fixations, active compar­
isons, and visual searches. Fixations indicated areas where participants discerned anoma­
lies or key facial traits, while active comparisons involved comparing specific traits be­
tween images. Visual searches were identified as saccades within the suspected image,
indicating general exploration.
The final stage was scoring these eye­tracking events, assigning weights to each cate­
gory based on criteria like duration for fixations and proximity for active comparisons. This
scoring was crucial for creating visual representations that accurately depict the impor­
tance of various gaze patterns in detecting image manipulations.

Eye Tracking Trace Visualisation
Having successfully extracted eye tracking data from human super­recognisers, the next
challenge was to visualise this data effectively. The goal was to develop new methods
that are both intuitive for experiment participants and insightful in conveying where they
should focus their attention when detecting manipulations in images.
To ensure that the visualisations were user­friendly, it was essential that even new, un­
trained participants could quickly understand and interpret the feedback provided. Simul­
taneously, these visualisations needed to offer valuable insights, guiding users to specific
areas of the image where manipulations might be present but not immediately obvious.
To obtain insights from a professional viewpoint, a series of interviews were conducted
with a representative from the Norwegian National Police Directorate. The knowledge
gained from these professional document examiners was used as an inspiration for the
development of innovative methods for presenting eye­tracking data.
After an extensive review of the state­of­the­art eye tracking data visualisation techniques
and the interviews with professional examiners, it was decided to focus on two specific
methods for further evaluation and comparison. These methods, which will be called
here Spotlight Visualisation and Isotherm Visualisation respectively, represent distinct ap­
proaches to trace visualisation. These methods take inspiration from the primary presen­
tation approaches featured earlier in subsection 2.6.2 of this thesis. In many aspects,
they are complementary, each offering a unique perspective on how to best highlight cru­
cial areas in an image based on eye­tracking data. Spotlight visualisation aims to direct
attention to the regions with most key focal points, while Isotherm visualisation provides
a broader, heat­map­like view of gaze distribution, each enhancing the observer’s ability
to spot potential manipulations.
5.1
Spotlight visualisation
The Spotlight visualisation method was developed utilising a region aggregation approach,
inspired by the Area of Interest (AOI) analysis methodologies. Specific regions of the face
were predefined as focal areas for analysis. Each region’s significance was determined
by calculating and aggregating its score separately, indicating the importance of that re­
gion in the detection of face manipulations. Regions with higher scores were highlighted
more prominently in the visualisation.
5.1.1

method for defining the boundaries of facial regions was necessary. A previously used
Landmark identification method would output only 68 landmarks, which was not detailed
enough to define the boundaries. Therefore, the state­of­the­art Face Landmark Detec­
tion tool by MediaPipe was selected for this task. This tool generates a mesh consisting
of 478 three­dimensional landmarks on a facial image, mapping out detailed facial traits.
An example output from the model is presented in Figure 5.1 [59].

5.2
Isotherm visualisation
Isotherm visualisation represents the second trace presentation technique developed for
this study. Unlike the spotlight method, which relies on predefined facial regions, this
method dynamically generates regions based on the density and weight of eye­tracking
data.
This method’s flexibility rejects the need for regional normalization coefficients described
in subsection 5.1.2. However, the differing frequencies of the three eye­tracking event
types —fixations, active comparisons, and visual searches — necessitated the application
of event type coefficients, also detailed in subsection 5.1.2.
The visualisation process begins with a heatmap, a technique using colour intensities in
a two­dimensional matrix to represent data patterns and variances [62]. The Heatmappy
Python module was employed to produce initial heatmaps [63], incorporating the signifi­
cance of each eye­tracking event, adjusted by the event type coefficient. An example of
such a heatmap is shown in Figure 5.6.
However, standard heatmap outputs can obscure facial details, hindering the detection of
image manipulations, which is the aim of the visual feedback in the first place. To address
this, an isotherm approach was adopted. This method displays a single ’heat’ level at a
time, outlining an isotherm — a curve connecting points of equal heat intensity across the
image.

Chapter summary
This chapter detailed the visualisation of eye­tracking data from human super­recognisers,
aimed at developing intuitive methods for experiment participants to focus their attention
when detecting image manipulations. Two distinct visualisation techniques, ’Spotlight vi­
sualisation’ and ’Isotherm visualisation’, were developed.
Spotlight visualisation employed a region aggregation approach, defining specific facial
regions as main areas for analysis. Using the MediaPipe face landmark marking tool, the
face could be mapped into detailed regions, and each region’s significance was calcu­
lated based on eye­tracking events. Modifications like Gaussian blur and alpha channel
adjustments were applied to create a user­friendly mask overlay, highlighting regions of
importance in identifying manipulations.
Isotherm visualisation, in contrast, generated regions dynamically based on eye­tracking
data density. This method utilised a heatmap to represent data patterns, which was then
transformed into an animated isotherm display. The animation showed varying heat lev­
els, starting from the most significant points and radiating outwards, providing an infor­
mative and dynamic visual feedback for participants.
Both visualisation methods offered unique perspectives on highlighting crucial areas in
images, enhancing the observer’s ability to spot potential manipulations. These tech­
niques were designed to be both insightful and intuitive, guiding users, especially new
participants, in their analysis of suspected face images.

2. Ensuring secure authentication and data handling.
3. Presenting the experiment and its procedures in an easily comprehensible format.

2. Receive clear textual feedback indicating the accuracy of their decision.
3. Access visual feedback, such as isotherm or spotlight trace visualisations, when an

4. Utilise zoom and pan functionalities for detailed examination of the images.

6.3
Experiment trials and stimuli
The experiment consists of 60 trials. In each trial (whose order is shuffled across the
experiment runs), the participant is given the same task which is in a form of a differential
trial. In such a trial, a the participant is given a pair of images ­ one reference image (a
trusted photo) and a suspected image where a manipulation might be present or not. It is
a spacial differential task with a 2­alternative forced­choice. Such a paired comparison is
one of the most popular procedure designs in the field of psychophysics. [64] The task of
the participant is to determine whether the suspected image includes the additional signal
(i.e. manipulation of some kind) or it does not.
Facial stimuli for the study were sourced from the FERET [65] and FRGC v.2.0 [66] image
databases. An in­house collection of manipulated images was also employed, featuring
various automatic alterations applied to selected individual and paired images. Each type
of facial manipulation — namely face swapping, morphing, and retouching — was ex­
ecuted with the following methods: SimpleFS [67] for face swapping, FaceFusion [68]
and UBO Morpher [69] for morphing, and FotoRus [30] and InstaBeauty [29] for retouch­
ing. This resulted in a total of six unique classes of manipulations. Table 6.1 details the
quantity of each type of stimulus utilised in the experiment.

Conclusion and Future Directions
This chapter concludes the study on the use of eye tracking data from human super­
recognisers for enhancing human performance in detection of face image alterations. It
encapsulates the research findings, evaluates the results of the experimental study, reit­
erates the research questions, and discusses potential possibilities for future work.
8.1
Summary
This thesis presents a comprehensive study on the utilisation of eye­tracking data from
human super­recognisers to improve the detection of manipulated face images in bio­
metric systems. As biometric systems are increasingly employed for identity verification,
particularly in high­stakes environments like border control and law enforcement, ensuring
their reliability and security is crucial. The rise of face recognition technology has signifi­
cantly enhanced identification processes in terms of efficiency and accuracy. However, it
also introduces substantial security challenges, particularly the threat of manipulated face
images that could potentially deceive automated systems, highlighting the gap between
physical and digital identity verification.
The research primarily focused on leveraging the unique capabilities of human super­
recognisers, individuals with exceptional skills in face recognition. Building on the foun­
dational work by Nichols et al., which identified individuals with superior abilities in face
recognition, this thesis aimed to harness and refine eye­tracking data from these indi­
viduals. The goal was to develop and implement methodologies that could potentially
enhance the detection accuracy of manipulated face images in security applications. The
thesis elaborates on the processes of interpreting, aggregating, and abstracting raw eye­
tracking data and introduces novel methods for converting these insights into visually
intuitive displays accessible to users regardless of their training level.
An updated framework for conducting visual experiments was developed, incorporating
new features and modules to facilitate an online differential trial. In this experimental
setup, participants were presented with two images — a reference and a suspected one —
and tasked with determining whether the suspected image had been manipulated. Upon
making their decision, participants received both textual and visual feedback, along with
a suite of interactive tools for further examination of the images. The study meticulously
analysed participants’ accuracy and their interaction patterns with the tool.
The thesis culminates in a thorough analysis and interpretation of the experiment data,
offering insights into the efficacy of the developed methodologies. Conclusions are drawn
regarding the impact of this approach on enhancing the detection capabilities of human
observers in identifying manipulated images in biometric systems.
8.2
Discussion on the results
The experiment, conducted as an online survey with 91 participants, including biomet­
ric professionals and non­professionals, aimed to assess the ability to classify manipu­
lated versus bona fide images. The study employed two newly­developed presentation
methods for eye tracking data: isotherm and spotlight visualisations. These methods

The experiment commenced with an initial average accuracy of 58%, which progressively
rose to more than 64% by its conclusion.
The results showed a slight advantage in the accuracy of professional over non­professional

8.3
Discussion on the research questions
This section revisits the research questions from the Introduction, examining how the
findings address each and the implications of these results.
The first question, ”How can eye tracking data from human super­recognisers be effec­
tively utilised to enhance the accuracy of human observers in detecting manipulations in
face images?”, is key to the training tool’s development. The approach capitalises on
the unique skills of super­recognisers, converting eye tracking data into visual traces for
participant feedback. When a participant makes an error, these traces provide guidance,
thereby enhancing their ability to identify subtle manipulations in facial images.
For the second question, ”What strategies can transform eye tracking data into atten­
tion traces capturing visual patterns and cues used by super­recognisers?”, the study
introduces spotlight and isotherm visualisations. Spotlight highlights aggregated areas
focused on by super­recognisers, guiding trainees’ attention. Isotherm uses heatmap­
like animation to indicate the attention regions. Both methods utilise super­recognisers’
implicit visual examination, offering effective road maps for participants. The study did not
conclusively favour one method over the other, indicating that both are equally valuable
tools in the training process.
Addressing the third question, ”How does real­time feedback from the training tool im­
prove human observers’ performance in detecting manipulated face images?”, the study
observes a notable trend in participant performance: initial improvement, then a learn­
ing plateau. This suggests that early­stage feedback, based on super­recogniser data,
significantly enhances observational skills, with a learning saturation point beyond which
further feedback has limited impact on accuracy.

high usage of these tools indicated keen interest; however, a subsequent decline in us­

8.4
Future work
The post­experiment interviews revealed valuable insights into the participants’ experi­
ence. Many expressed concerns regarding the experiment’s duration, indicating that fa­
tigue might have impacted their detection abilities. This feedback suggests a need to
rethink the general structure of the experiment to reduce fatigue and potentially enhance
performance.
Future research should explore alternative ways of presenting eye­tracking traces, con­
sidering that the developed methods showed no significant difference in effectiveness.
Implementing different interaction tools with the images could lead to higher user interac­
tion rates, potentially translating into better learning outcomes.
Further research could also examine the impact of varying the complexity of image ma­
nipulations and the duration of exposure to these images on the learning outcomes. In­
vestigating different participant demographics and professional backgrounds might offer
additional insights into the generalisation of the training tool. Additionally, exploring adap­
tive learning models where the difficulty and nature of tasks evolve based on participant
performance could enhance the effectiveness of the training.
In summary, while this research has provided an insight into leveraging human capabilities
for detecting face image manipulation, there is ample scope for further exploration. The
findings could contribute to the field of biometrics and security, offering a foundation for
future studies aimed at enhancing the resilience of identity verification processes against
evolving threats.
