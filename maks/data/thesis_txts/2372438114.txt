Summary 
 
 
This thesis focuses on DevOps as a dynamic capability, in which it will both be examined from a 
strategic view and an organisational change view. DevOps, an approach to integrate the Development 
Department and the Operations Department, has been introduced with a rapid speed in the IT world 
since its birth in 2007. In today’s rapid changing market, it is more important than ever for an 
organisation to have the ability to respond to market changes rapidly, adapt to them and be flexible 
enough to continuously reconfigure one self. This is how organisations improve their business agility 
and obtain competitive advantage. This is crucial in a volatile environment, where one’s clients can 
switch to competitors if demands are not met. Thus, this thesis will seek to investigate DevOps 
competitive advantage as a dynamic capability in DXC. It will start with a profound literature review, 
in which the most relevant literature will be examined. Thereafter, the methods that will be used 
throughout this thesis will be introduced. The methods include the Quadric Framework to analyse the 
strategy of DXC, the VRIO Framework to examine if DevOps does deliver a sustained competitive 
advantage to DXC. In connection with this, the Dynamic Capabilities View will be used to evaluate 
if the O-criteria in the VRIO framework is satisfied i.e. the “O” is the dynamic capability that 
combines the VRI-criteria. In other words, a dynamic capability must be VRI to be a source of 
competitive advantage. Within the “O”, the Force-Field Approach will be used to identify the driving 
forces and restraining forces causing resistance to change. In addition, the approach is combined with 
the Expectancy Theory, in which motivation will be discussed. This is to ensure that DevOps advance 
as a dynamic capability and thus fulfils the “O” in the VRIO Framework. Through this, it is concluded 
that DevOps indeed can act as a dynamic capability and it has proven itself in several occasions in 
DXC. However, it also creates resistance to change, which has placed DXC in a state of inertia. Thus, 
it will only continue as a dynamic capability and thus provide a sustained competitive advantage if 
resistance to change is decreased.

Chapter 1  
Introduction 
 
Only those who have the agility to change with the market and innovate 
quickly will survive.    
– Robert T. Kiyosaki 
 
In today’s rapid changing environment, it is hard to predict changes and therefore the most optimal 
solution for an organisation is to expect the unexpected. This is in particular important for 
organisations who work with software, and if you look around today- who does not do that? The 
competition has therefore increased, it is much harder to obtain and sustain a competitive advantage 
and it is even harder to deliver value to customers. Dynamic capability is one of the accelerators for           
better business agility, which in turn contributes to a competitive advantage. With customers 
demanding frequent product deliveries, much faster, cheaper and with a high quality, it is no longer 
optimal to wait a year for a release, to take time to fix an error and let it cost a lot [1]. This will create 
a competitive disadvantage rather than a competitive advantage [1]. Thus, in order for an organisation 
to expect the unexpected and improve its business agility, acquiring a dynamic capability is an 
advantage. A dynamic capability enables an organisation to be innovative, flexible, and adaptable to 
changes. It creates the ability to continuously challenge oneself in new ways to deliver customer 
value, and to embrace changes rather than avoiding them. An organisation must thrive on change, 
and this is where DevOps comes in.  
 
DevOps has emerged as a strategic move within software, which was coined back in 2007 by Patrick 
Debois and popularized in 2009 [2]. DevOps was introduced as an approach to integrate the 
Development and Operations Departments and thus break down the silos between them. Prior to 
DevOps, software was made with separate parts within the supply chain. The Development 
Department developed the software and threw it over the wall to the Operations Department, who 
then needed to figure out how to operate it and release it into production. This lead to 
miscommunication, decreased response time to market changes and other critical issues, that there is 
not enough time for in today’s market. Thus, the two departments needed to be brought closer together 
to avoid these hurdles, and as a result, DevOps was born. The benefit of DevOps is tremendous for 
any software organisation, who with DevOps can respond to market changes rapidly, adapt to them 
and be flexible enough to continuously reconfigure processes to fit the demands. In addition, it allows 
one to continuously improve through iterations and continuous innovation, deliver software 
frequently and faster and detect errors long before they happen.  
==PAGE:10==
2 of 80 
To fully excel in DevOps and prevent critical issues, Operations needs to be included in the 
development of the software to help developers to develop a software that works before it reaches 
production. This entails that e.g. Quality Assurance needs to be made during the development and 
not when it has been thrown over the wall. Automation also needs to be included to ensure continuous 
delivery and continuous integration to detect errors fast. However, the problematic area occurs when 
two different worlds collide. Developers are used to be working in a certain way, in which their job 
stops when a software is developed. On the contrary, Operations’ job starts when it has to operate the 
software. With DevOps, these two separate walls are no longer possible, and this means that e.g. 
habits needs to change. Habits are one of the most difficult things to change because it is rooted so 
profound into individuals. Consequently, organisations often invest in DevOps and are left to wonder 
why the benefits are not shining through. One of the answers to this is that people who are working 
with DevOps are resisting it. When resistance occurs, an organisation will find itself in a state of 
inertia, in which it does not reach its future state. In addition, it decreases the chances for DevOps to 
continue as a dynamic capability and thus a sustained competitive advantage.  
 
1.1 Research questions 
The research of this thesis is based on a case study of DXC Technology (DXC) in Denmark, in which 
the focus will be on the Development Department (GBS) and the Operations Department (GIS) who

departments are sought to become integrated. DXC is a result of a merger between the IT-company 
Computer Sciences Corporation (CSC) and the Enterprise Service Division of Hewlett Packard 
Enterprise (HPE). It handles around 6.000 clients in a wide spread of industries stretching from 
Consumer Industries and retail to the Public Sector. With its vision to be recognised globally as a 
force multiplier, which enables its clients to seize the opportunities presented in today’s rapidly 
changing market, a dynamic capability is crucial to possess to attain a sustained competitive 
advantage. The research questions of this case have been split into six sub-questions due to the 
complexity that lays between DXC and its clients. This is because DevOps is used in DXC but 
simultaneously by DXC’s clients, and because DXC’s success depends on clients, one cannot be 
removed from the equation.

strategic move in DXC’s operational evidence.

2.1 Strategy  
The root of strategy can be traced thousands of years back in a military context in the form of 
deploying resources to establish a strategic position on the market [3]. Throughout the years and up 
untill the present day, strategy has rooted itself in all kinds of settings but primarily in connection 
with organisations. At the same time, different scholars have researched and argued about what 
strategy is. Michael Porter focuses on stability and argues that strategy is based on deliberate choices 
and differentiation and connects that to the reason that an organisation can obtain a competitive 
advantage [4]. Conversely, Henry Mintzberg focuses on a more organic strategy development. 
Mintzberg argues that strategy is a pattern in a stream of decisions and hence tends to emerge as 
people learn new things and adapt the strategy to what is learned [5]. He adds that organisations who 
intend to pursue a purely deliberate strategy must have precise and concrete intentions before taking 
action [5], which are common intention to everyone involved in a perfectly predictable environment. 
==PAGE:14==
6 of 80 
However, it is unlikely that an organisation will possess a pure deliberate strategy. Furthermore, in 
order for a strategy to be purely emergent, it needs to have consistency in actions with the absence of 
intentions, but again this would be unlikely. Thus, he points out that organisations must pursue a mix 
of both strategies, as one suggests no learning while the other no control [5]. He states that a realized 
strategy i.e. the strategy that is actually implemented, only partly depends on the intended strategy 
but that the determinant is the emergent strategy [5]. In one of Mintzberg’s earlier publications with 
Waters, it is argued that while a deliberate strategy focuses on direction, hierarchy, and control i.e. 
getting desired things done, an emergent strategy focuses on strategic learning [6]. When an intended 
strategy has been conceived as deliberate, the attention is placed on archiving it rather than adapting 
to it and hence removing strategic learning. Mintzberg et al. backs similar view up in a later 
publication [7]. In correlation to this, Robert Grant argues that business environments have become 
more unstable and unpredictable and thus strategy has becomes less concerned with detailed plans 
and more concerned with embracing flexibility and responsiveness [8]. He states that it is in these 
conditions that strategy becomes more important. However, he points out that a clear sense of 
direction is needed to pursuit an organisation’s objectives. Grant adds that throughout all the 
companies that he is familiar with, strategic planning combines design and emergence, and refers to 
it as planned emergence [8]. Max Mckeown argues that strategy is about shaping the future and states 
that it is the quickest route of motion between the ends i.e. where you want to go and the means i.e. 
what you need to do to get there. He claims that many strategic plans created in hierarchal 
organisations, disappears when it reaches the middle management due to that e.g. events are 
unpredictable [9]. He argues that it leads to out-of-date strategy caused by e.g. lack of flexibility. In 
a similar manner to Mintzberg and Waters, Mckeown states that a strategy needs to try to plan 
deliberate actions to shape the future, but at the same time must be responsive to new events, adaptive 
to new opportunities and enable an organisation to learn from lessons [9]. He further points out that 
there is no guarantee that a plan will happen due to the complexity of the environment, but that the 
ability to plan is important [9]. Richard Rumelt argues that a good strategy is one that has coherency, 
coordinates actions, policies and resources to obtain a desired end [10]. In addition, he adds that a 
goal and a strategy are two different things. Simply thinking that strategy is goal setting will lead to 
a bad strategy. Therefore, challenges and obstacles need to be identified as well as actions to reach 
the desired goal [10]. Finally, Johnson et al. views strategy as the long-term direction of an 
organisation [4] and thus creates a broad definition, in which other scholars’ definitions are included. 
This definition will also be the one that this thesis uses when referring to strategy.  
 
2.2 Competitive Advantage  
With the right strategy in place, an organisation has the ability to achieve competitive advantage. 
Robert Grant focuses on profitability as a definition of competitive advantage, and states that it occurs 
when two or more organisations compete within the same market, in which one organisation 
possesses a competitive advantage over its rivals when it earns a higher profit [11]. He argues that 
competitive advantage can emerge through two sources [11]:  
 
==PAGE:15==
7 of 80 
• Internal sources of change: Through an organisation having greater creative and innovative 
capability than competing organisations.    
 
• External sources of change: This can be e.g. changing customer demands and how rapid 
organisation respond to the changes. The rapidness depends on the ability to anticipate 
changes in the external environment and/or the speed to do so. He further points out that in 
order for an external change to create a competitive advantage an organisation’s capability 
must be able to create a differential parameter [11].  
 
Grant separates competitive advantage into two types (i) cost advantage, which is when an 
organisation creates an identical product/service at a lower cost and (ii) differentiation advantage, 
which is when an organisation creates a product/service that customers are willing to pay a premium 
price for so that it exceeds the additional cost of the differentiation. Similar is argued by Wen-Cheng 
et al. [12] while Wang argues that competitive advantage is obtained when an organisation develops 
or acquires a set of actions that allow it to outperform its competitors [13]. Finally, Johnson et al. 
argues that an organisation’s advantage can be separated into four different types: competitive 
disadvantage, competitive parity, temporary competitive advantage and finally sustained competitive 
advantage [14]. See Table 1 for a description of each of the four types of competitive advantage.   
 
Table 1: Four Types of Competitive Advantage 
Competitive disadvantage 
When an organisation is outperformed by its competitors, this 
can result in failure 
Competitive parity 
When an organisation performs just as good as its competitors 
and thus have obtained a threshold capability 
Temporary competitive advantage 
When an organisation’s competitive advantage is at risk to be 
imitated by its competitors 
Sustained competitive advantage 
When an organisation creates VRIO capabilities and thus 
manages to prolong its competitive advantage 
 
2.3 Capabilities 
According to Johnson et al. strategic capabilities are the capabilities that contribute to an 
organisation’s long-term survival and competitive advantage [14], and are composed of the resources 
and competences within an organisation. An organisation’s resources are the assets it has or can 
obtain through e.g. its suppliers i.e. “what we have”, and competences are the ways those assets are 
deployed i.e. “what we do well” [14]. Thus, even though resources are important, competences are 
just as important. In extension, the efficiency and effectiveness of physical- financial- or human 
resources, depend not just on their existence, but on the systems and processes that they are managed 
by, the collaboration between people, adaptability, their innovative capacity, the relationship with 
customers and the continuous learning of what works well and what does not [14]. Strategic 
capabilities can further be separated into threshold capabilities and distinctive capabilities. Threshold 
capabilities are those needed to meet the necessary requirements to compete in a given market, and 
==PAGE:16==
8 of 80 
achieve parity with competitors in the market. Without these capabilities, an organisation would not 
be able to survive over time [14]. However, threshold capabilities do not themselves create 
competitive advantage and thus distinctive capabilities, which are the linked set of skills, activities 
and resources, are needed to achieve a competitive advantage [14]. This depends on that an 
organisation has distinct capabilities that competitors find hard to imitate and at the same time are 
perceived as valuable by customers. It could be distinctive resources that contribute to competitive 
advantage but that others cannot imitate or obtain e.g. a strong brand reputation. On the other hand, 
it could be distinctive competences that contribute to competitive advantage through unique ways of 
doing things [14]. In addition, Teece et al. [14] [15] introduces one last capability and calls it dynamic 
capabilities, a deeper dive into dynamic capabilities can be found in section 2.4. A summary of the 
different capabilities can be seen in Table 2.  
Table 2: Four Types of Capabilities 
Strategic Capability 
Capabilities that contributes with long-term survival and competitive 
advantage. Composed of resources and competences 
Threshold Capability 
Capabilities that are required to meet the necessary requirements to 
compete and achieve parity with competitors  
Distinct capability 
Capabilities that are required to achieve competitive advantage and are 
composed of the linked set of skills, activities and resources.  
Dynamic capability 
Capability to renew and recreate strategic capabilities to meet the needs 
of changing environments 
 
2.3.1 The VRIO-Framework  
An organisation can attain- and sustain a competitive advantage through its strategic capabilities, if 
the capabilities hold the VRIO-criteria [14]. The origin of this VRIO framework stems from The 
Resource-Based View (RBV), which started to accelerate attention in 1991 where it was introduced 
by Jay B. Barney [14] and is sometimes referred to as the focal point of Strategic Management. 
Barney suggested that the potential to generate sustained competitive advantage should be examined 
from the capabilities’ value, rareness, imitability and non-substitutability, coining this as the VRIN-
criteria [16]. He argues that a lot of strategic research directed towards an organisation’s competitive 
position has placed large emphasise on the environment that an organisation is operating in e.g. 
Porter’s Five Forces [17], and little emphasis on the impact an organisation’s individual capabilities 
have on its competitive position [16]. In connection with this, he draws two assumptions from 
previous research (i) organisations within an industry are identical in terms of the capabilities they 
control and the strategies they pursue, (ii) that an organisation’s capabilities are mobile and thus even 
if it manages to develop unique capabilities, it would be short lived [16]. On a critical point to the 
RBV, Newbert criticizes it and argues that the acceptance of it holds little empirical confirmation and 
instead it is based on logic and intuition [18]. In addition, he stresses that the actual level of empirical 
support remains uncertain and that this uncertainty might be of worry to those who support the RBV 
[18]. In 1995, Barney gives a critical view on previous research and underlines that it has primarily 
been focusing on the opportunities and threats and less on the strength and weaknesses within an 
organisation [19]. He further adds that to create a complete understanding of the sources of sustained 
==PAGE:17==
9 of 80 
competitive advantage, resources and capabilities must be valuable, rare, and imitable [19]. In 
contrary to his earlier article he adds one last attribute, which is that an organisation must also be 
organised to explore the capabilities developed [19] and hence introducing the “O” and creates the 
VRIO framework. In a similar research, Cardeal and António builds on previous research from 
scholars and states that the “O” in the VRIO are in fact the capability of an organisation, which is the 
way an organisation organises its VRI-capabilities [20].  
 
Valuable 
As discussed earlier, a capability must be valuable to enable sustained competitive advantage. 
According to Barney, an organisation should start by answering the following question [19]:  
 
Does an organisation’s capability add value by enabling it to exploit opportunities and/or neutralize 
threats?  
 
This question is supported by Johnson et al. [14]. Cardeal and António supports this statement as well 
pointing out that a capability creates value when it allows an organisation to come up with, and 
implement strategies that will improve both efficiency and effectiveness [20]. In addition to this, it is 
important to constantly evaluate whether or not the capability continues to add value regardless of 
there being none or many changes in the environment. Customer taste can enable a capability to be 
less valuable in the future, and hence an organisation cannot solely rely on past success [19]. In 
extension to this, Barney further adds that even though the value of a capability is reduced in its 
current use, the value of it in other uses can remain unchanged and hence it is necessary to look for 
other ways to utilize the strength of the capability to exploit opportunities and/or neutralize threats 
[19]. He argues that a capability of different organisations can be valuable in different ways. This 
depends on what an organisation is focusing its capability on [19]. Furthermore, capabilities need to 
be of value to customers with the emphasize on what customers perceive as valuable rather than what 
the organisation perceive as valuable. The customers are ultimately the ones who decide the value of 
a product or service. However, Johnson et al. argues that although customer’s perception is important 
the cost of developing or acquiring a capability is just as important [14]. This is due to that 
profitability needs to take place to prevent that the product or service that is being delivered will be 
perceived as a loss within an organisation. Thus, a capability is valuable when it creates a product or 
a service that is of value to customers and if, and only if, they generate higher revenue or lower costs 
or both [14].  
 
Rare 
Johnson et al. further discusses that valuable capabilities that are common among competitors, are 
not merely enough to be a source of competitive advantage. Instead, capabilities also need to be rare, 
which are those possessed uniquely by one organisation or by a few others [14]. Barney supports this 
and adds that if a particular capability is controlled by numerous competing organisations, then it is 
unlikely to be a source of competitive advantage for any one of them [19]. Instead, he perceives it as 
==PAGE:18==
10 of 80 
being a source of competitive parity due to them being common. Thus, even though the capability is 
not rare, it is still important. He states that it might even be essential for an organisation’s survival 
[19]. Cardeal and António also support the same statement and argue that if most competitors hold 
the same valuable capability, they would most likely use it the same way [20]. It will then not result 
in any of the organisations achieving competitive advantage even though the capability is valuable 
[20].  This creates the following question to be answered:  
 
How many competing organisations already possess the same valuable capability?  
 
Newbert adds that if a capability is common, then a large number of organisations will be able to 
implement the resulting strategy [18]. He stresses that it is necessary that an organisation possesses 
rare capabilities in order to obtain competitive advantage [18].  
 
Inimitable 
Johnson et al. discusses that sustained competitive advantage cannot be archived solely on based on 
of having a valuable and rare capability. There is also a need for the capability to be inimitable [14]. 
Barney argues the same, and adds that a capability that is valuable and rare can per minimum create 
a temporary competitive advantage, however if it is hard to imitate then an organisation can obtain 
sustained competitive advantage [19]. This leaves us with the following question to answer:  
 
Do organisations without a capability face a cost disadvantage in obtaining it compared to 
organisations that already possess it?  
 
Johnson et al. add that if an organisation has a competitive advantage in a particular field it can only 
be sustained if competitors cannot imitate, obtain or substitute for them or if the costs to do so would 
eliminate any gains made [14]. In extension to this, the barriers to imitate lie deeply in the link 
between activities, skills and people. Johnson et al. further argue that tangible resources cannot alone 
explain an organisation’s competitive advantage due to the high risk of being imitated with the 
exception of e.g. brands. And adds that an organisation’s advantage is more likely to be determined 
by the way in which resources are deployed and managed on the basis of an organisation’s 
competences [14]. Thus, an organisation’s competitive advantage is explained by its intangible 
resources, which include a linkage between internal and external activities, skills, knowledge and 
people. This is due to that e.g. developing an IT-system will not on its own create a competitive 
advantage, but if an organisation has the competence to manage, develop and deploy this IT-system 
to the benefit of customers, it then might be much more difficult and costly to imitate. Johnson et al. 
suggest three main reasons that support this.  
 
Complexity: The capability can be complex and involve interlinkages. This might be due to internal 
linkages e.g. linked activities and processes that deliver customer value. Johnson et al. argues that 
these linkages can be so complex that even if a competitor tried to replicate them, it would be very 
hard to succeed [14]. This is due to the many interactions between activities and decisions developed 
==PAGE:19==
11 of 80 
over years, which is rooted within that particular organisation. Furthermore, it might also be due to 
external interconnectedness, in which organisations develops activities together with customers or 
partners so that they become dependent of them, and hence preventing imitation.  
 
Causal ambiguity: It can also be difficult to recognize the causes and effects that strengthen an 
organisation’s competitive advantage. Johnson et al. argues that this can be due to that the capability 
itself is difficult to recognize or understand due to e.g. people’s experience [14]. However, it can also 
be because it is difficult for competitors to recognize which activities and processes that are dependent 
on each other.  
 
Culture and history: The last reason discussed by Johnson et al. is the aspect of competences being 
rooted within an organisation, which makes coordination occur naturally and hence it becomes a 
regular practice within the organisation [14]. They further argue that these competences have 
developed over time and in a particular way. They add that an organisation’s history is specific to the 
particular organisation and cannot be imitated.  
 
Barney argues that imitating can happen through either duplicating or substituting a capability [19]. 
In addition, he adds that the reason a capability can be imitated can be grouped into three categories: 
the importance of History in which he, just as Johnson et al., argues that a capability can be inimitable 
through an organisation’s unique personalities, experiences, and relationships that exists within it. He 
goes on to point out that if a competitor tries to imitate this capability, that same competitor will be 
at a cost disadvantage building it [19]. The second category is the importance of Socially Complex 
capabilities, which are e.g. an organisation’s reputation, trust, friendship, culture, and encouragement 
in teamwork and across divisions [19]. All these aspects make a capability hard to imitate. In contrary 
to Johnson et al. Barney adds the importance of numerous small decisions as being the third category 
[19]. He argues that big decisions are obvious and easier to describe while small decisions e.g. small 
ideas for improvement and to spot a quality problem and report it, are invisible to competitors trying 
to imitate a capability [19]. Cardeal and António [20] agrees on the question to be answered, however 
adds a few more reasons on why a capability tend to be more difficult to imitate. These reasons are 
that they are path dependent, that there is an ambiguous relationship between the capability that 
enhances competitive advantage, that they are socially complex, that there are legal property rights 
e.g. patents, and that the process of their imitation by other companies is lengthy i.e. the time needed 
to train employees or to understand the knowledge needed to use the capability [20]. 
 
Organisation  
As mentioned previously Barney argues that the potential of an organisation’s competitive advantage 
depends on the capability being valuable, rare and imitable. However, he adds that in order to fully 
take advantage of the capability an organisation must be organised to exploit the full potential of that 
capability if it is to generate a sustained competitive advantage [19]. He backs this up in his later 
research in 1997 [21] respectively supported by Newbert [18]. Knowing this, it leaves us with the 
following question to answer:  
==PAGE:20==
12 of 80 
 
Is an organisation organised to exploit the full competitive potential of the capability?  
 
Barney and Johnson et al.  argue an organisation’s structure, formal and informal management control 
systems need to support and facilitate the exploitation of the capability. Cardeal and António build 
on this and add that a capability is the “O” i.e. the organisation in the VRIO framework and thus 
needs to be VRI to achieve sustained competitive advantage [20].  
 
In general, the literature examined in this section proposes that in order for an organisation to attain 
and sustain competitive advantage it must create a strategic capability that is valuable, rare and 
imitable, and then be suitable organised to exploit this strategic capability. However, because the “O” 
seems to remains without major emphasize, this thesis will have the largest focus on the “O” as it is 
what holds the VRI-criteria together and combines them into a capability. In particular, the focus will 
be on the Dynamic Capabilities Approach as it is viewed as being the “O”.  
 
2.4 Dynamic Capabilities Approach 
Teece, Pisano and Shuen state that within strategic management the fundamental question is to 
identify how organisations achieve and sustain competitive advantage [22]. In connection with this, 
scholars have extensively researched about this matter throughout the years, in which, as mentioned 
previously, the RBV has its roots. However, Teece, Pisano and Shuen [22], Eisenhardt and Martin 
[23], Newbert [24], Cavusgil et al. [25], and Cardeal and António [20] amongst other scholars argues 
that the research has done little in assisting in the understanding of how and why organisations build 
competitive advantage in rapid and unpredictable changing markets such as those in software. Thus, 
Teece, Pisano and Shuen propose a framework that better assist this issue and calls it the Dynamic 
Capabilities View [22].  
 
The Dynamic Capabilities View emerged as a new paradigm within Strategic Management, and starts 
where the RBV has left off [25]. Cardeal and António argue that a dynamic capability can be seen as 
the “O” in VRIO i.e. the way an organisation organises itself to support these capabilities [20]. To be 
a source of competitive advantage it must be VRI [20].  
 
Eisenhardt and Martin argues that organisations competing in today’s rapid changing environment, 
such as the software industry, is in particular need of dynamic capabilities [23]. Teece, Pisano and 
Shuen concurrently add that organisations who win on the market are those who have the ability to 
demonstrate responsiveness in a timely manner, whose innovation is rapid and flexible [22]. They 
refer to dynamic as the capability to renew competences to be suitable with the changing environment, 
and capability as an organisation’s ability to adapt, integrate and reconfigure internal and external 
skills, resources and functional competences to match the requirements of the changing environment 
[22]. Teece, Pisano and Shuen add that an organisation’s competitive advantage rest in its routines, 
which are shaped by its strategic position and path [22]. Cavusgil et al. [25] supports similar view. 
However, Eisenhardt and Martin points out that dynamic capabilities consists of specific strategic 
and organisational processes e.g. product development processes, that enables an organisation to 
==PAGE:21==
13 of 80 
manipulate resources into new value creating strategies [23]. In addition, they argue that dynamic 
capabilities take different forms according to the velocity of the market. In high-velocity markets, it 
takes the form as being simple, experiential, iterative processes that rely on rapid knowledge creation 
[23]. However, these dynamic capabilities do also consist of some structure to sustain coherency. 
Simple routines in an organisation provide structure and keep management focused on issues and 
information that are important. Simultaneously, it also enables better sense making about the situation 
and hence a notion about when to act. They underline that the fact that dynamic capabilities rely on 
real-time information enables people to adjust and adapt their actions according to the early spotted 
problems or opportunities, invest in cross-functional teams and intensive communication internally 
and externally [23]. Similar is noted by Newbert who adds that organisations who operate in dynamic 
markets needs to focus on continuous innovation to be competitive [24]. Thus, dynamic capabilities 
rely much more on new knowledge that is rapidly created through e.g. experiments such as 
prototyping and early testing. Furthermore, it also enables feedback to take place and hence iteration 
and innovation.   
 
Teece, Pisano and Shuen add that processes in an organisation take three roles or in other words, it is 
what dynamic capabilities are focused on [22].   
 
Coordination/Integration (static) i.e. how effective and efficient internal coordination or integration 
of activities are achieved by managers in the organisation. In connection with this, when an 
organisation’s processes show high level of coherence through coordination or integration, it might 
be hard to copy by competitors. Thus, it can result in difficult-to-imitate capabilities.  
 
Learning (dynamic) i.e. a process of experimentation consisting of trial, feedback, and evaluation. 
Simultaneously, learning also enables the identification of opportunities. Teece, Pisano and Shuen 
argue that learning requires individual skills but also on the organisational settings [22]. They add 
that learning processes besides imitating individuals, also occurs through joint contributions to the 
understanding of complex problems [22]. Common codes of communication and coordinated search 
procedures are also required to enable learning. In conjunction, routines are present in group 
behaviour, although subroutines might be resident in individual behaviour. Routines are also 
mentioned by Eisenhardt and Martin, in which they describe routines as dynamic capabilities that are 
developed through repeated practice hence a learning mechanism [23]. In addition, they note that 
learning is created from both success and failure such as crisis and small losses [23].  
 
Reconfiguration (transformational) i.e. the ability of an organisation to sense the need to reconfigure 
its asset structure e.g. the degree of hierarchy and the level of vertical and horizontal integration, and 
to accomplish the necessary internal and external transformation. Teece, Pisano and Shuen argues 
that decentralization and local autonomy assist these processes [22]. They call this high-flex 
organisations, while Eisenhardt and Martin calls it loosely structured organisations [23], both 
referring to the same. In turn, having this can enable organisations to constantly scan market or 
technology changes. Furthermore, an organisation must facilitate continuous innovation and hence 
==PAGE:22==
14 of 80 
create dynamic capabilities for sensing, shaping and seizing new market opportunities while at the 
same time creating a new structure that match the changing environment [22].  
 
Teece, Pisano and Shuen argue that dynamic capabilities often are characterized as being unique and 
idiosyncratic processes [22]. Eisenhardt and Martin criticise this and state that dynamic capabilities 
exhibit common key features (best practice) that are associated with effective processes across 
organisations [23]. Although, they do point out that they are idiosyncratic in their details. This is due 
to that there are many paths that organisations can take to gain the same dynamic capability i.e. 
organisations can perform similar set of activities. Simultaneously, they need to have key features in 
common to be effective for example product development. Thus, according to Eisenhardt and Martin 
a dynamic capability itself cannot be a source of sustained competitive advantage [23]. They add that 
in order for an organisation to have sustained competitive advantage, it needs to use this dynamic 
capability sooner, faster, and in a better way than the competition [23]. They further suggest that an 
organisation must not dwell on its competitive advantage, it must operate as if its competitive 
advantage can end at any time [23]. 
 
According to one of Teece’s later research, which Johnson et al. [14] and Amanda Wong [26] mention 
there are three types of dynamic capabilities that an organisation needs to possess. In other words, 
these are the capacity of dynamic capabilities. Amanda Wong calls them organisational activities 
[26]. Ralf Wilden et al. adds to this and calls it evolutionary fitness [27].    
 
• Sensing: In order for an organisation to sense an opportunity, it must constantly scan, search 
and explore opportunities across various markets and technologies. This can e.g. be a key 
activity in research and development and investigating customer needs.  
 
• Seizing: When an opportunity is sensed it must be seized and addressed through e.g. new 
products or services, processes and activities.  
 
• Reconfiguring: However, seizing an opportunity may require renewal and reconfiguration of 
organisational capabilities and investments in e.g. technologies, manufacturing and markets 
to continuously transform one’s processes.  
 
While the emergent of Dynamic Capability Approach has dived into the rapid changing environment, 
it has also brought distinct definitions with it along its journey. This is argued by Pisano who states 
that distinct definitions appear even down to the basic aspects such as how dynamic capabilities are 
defined [28]. Ralf Wilden et al. agree on this and add that the Dynamic Capabilities View has been 
constrained by these different definitions [27]. To mention a few, Teece, Pisano and Shuen 
proclaimed Dynamic Capabilities as the first scholars and defines it as the organisation’s ability to 
integrate, build, and reconfigure internal and external competences to address rapidly changing 
environments [22]. Johnson et al. simplifies this and defines it as an organisation’s ability to renew 
and recreate its strategic capabilities to meet the needs of changing environments [14]. Eisenhardt 
and Martin defines it as specific strategic and organisational processes/routines e.g. product 
==PAGE:23==
15 of 80 
development, that enables organisations to manipulate resources into new value-creating strategies 
as markets emerge, collide, split, evolve, and die [23]. Finally, Teece defines dynamic capabilities as 
the capacity to (i) sense and shape opportunities and threats, (ii) seize opportunities, and (iii) to 
maintain a competitiveness through enhancing, combining, protecting, and, reconfiguring the 
business enterprise’s intangible and tangible assets [29]. However, other scholars have argued that 
the many definition generates confusion that might prevent increased effective progress within this 
field, even though the definitions are justified by e.g. the youth of the approach. Pisano adds a critical 
view on the different definitions and states that the Dynamic Capabilities View has made little 
progress theoretically and has gained even less attraction amongst practitioners, but that that a lack 
of convergent is not surprising because capabilities and strategy are two broad terms themselves [28].  
 
Considering this, this thesis will use the simplified definition by Johnson et al. [14] while 
incorporating Teece’s definition [29]. This is to create a simple understanding for the reader. 
 
2.4.1 Business Agility 
Literature also points to that a dynamic capability can improve an organisation’s business agility. The 
survival of an organisation within the IT industry depends primarily on serving customers, being 
dynamic and taking fast proactive decisions in an environment with no significant state of equilibrium 
and that entails uncertainty and ambiguity. This is done by responding to market changes- and 
adapting to the changing customer demands rapidly, while also enabling flexibility within the 
organisation. Innovation plays a focal point in driving adaptability and hence business agility [30]. In 
addition, flexibility also promotes innovation as it enables team members to be creative and think-
out-of-the-box to create new ideas.  
 
In an article with leaders behind McKinsey’s work on organisation design Aaron De Smet explains 
the key. In order for an organisation to be agile and nourish agility it should have two key things 
when it comes to structure, processes and people [31]:  
 
• A dynamic capability: This is to move, respond and act fast.  
• Stability: A platform, a backbone of things that does not change while the environment around 
it changes. 
 
Wouter Aghina supports this, and adds that that agility is when you thrive on change and get stronger 
and it becomes a source of real competitive advantage [31]. He stresses the necessity to benefit from 
change and embody it, rather than merely seeing it as an obstacle.

2.5 Force-Field Approach to Change 
As mentioned in earlier in this chapter, the “O” remains with little emphasise, even though it is 
perceived to be important in an organisation’s ability to attain and sustain competitive advantage in 
today’s rapid changing environment. As of such, it is integrated with the Force-Field Approach to 
give it more substance and emphasize on the importance of the “O”.  
 
Kurt Lewin established the Force-Field Approach in the 1940s as an approach to understand the 
process of change and human behaviour. According to Lewin, change is not an event but rather a 
dynamic balance of forces working in opposite directions [32] [33]. Lewin argues that an organisation 
constantly has forces pushing towards change and forces resisting change. When these forces are 
evenly balanced, an organisation will find itself in a state of inertia [32], and thus will not change. To 
overcome this and hence enable change, it is necessary to reduce resistance to change, increase the 
forces for change or do both simultaneously. Slocum and Hellriegel adds that resistance to change 
will never be able to cease completely, thus it is a necessity that an organisation organise itself in 
such a way that it can learn to identify and reduce change [33]. They further points to Lewin’s research 
and state that a careful analysis of a change situation, does not guarantee successful change [33]. This 
is due to that e.g. people in control over the situation have a natural tendency to increase the pressure 
for change beyond a certain point, to reach the desired future state. However, this may only result in 
short-run change and create a strong pressure on people that in turn can develop itself into conflicts. 
Thus, Lewin’s Force-Field Approach to change led him to suggest an approach to manage and guide 
change. In particular, Lewin points out that it is much easier to reduce resistance to change i.e. a pull 
strategy, instead of increase the forces towards change i.e. a push strategy [34]. As a result, he created 
the 3-Step approach to change.  
 
1. Unfreezing: This step usually involves reducing those forces that are maintaining the 
organisation’s behaviour at its present level. Unfreezing is sometimes accomplished by 
introducing information to show discrepancies between behaviours desired by employees and 
behaviours they currently exhibit.  
 
2. Moving: This step shifts the organisation’s behaviour to a new level. It involves developing 
new behaviours, values, and attitudes through changes in organisational structures and 
processes.  
 
3. Refreezing: This step stabilizes the organisation’s behaviour at a new state of equilibrium. It 
is frequently accomplished with supporting mechanisms that reinforces the new 
organisational state, such as organisational culture, norms, policies and structures.  
 
However, Lewin’s work concerning change, in particular the 3-Step Approach to change, has resulted   
in heavy criticism as being redundant and outdated. Burnes and Dawson add that the 3-step Approach 
is not relevant in today’s turbulent environment, because an organisation need to be fluid and adapt 
to the constant changes in the environment, and thus organisations can never be frozen in a certain 
state and much less refrozen [35] [36]. Four areas concerning the 3-Step Approach to change are 
==PAGE:25==
17 of 80 
brought up as a criticism (i) it only focuses on organisations that operate in a stable environment (ii) 
it is only suitable for incremental and isolated change projects and is not able to incorporate radical, 
transformational change; (iii) it does not consider organisational power and politics; and (iv) it is 
based on a top-down approach [37]. 
 
However, in a re-appraisal Burnes argues that it is indeed still relevant today and hence contradict 
both other’s and his own previous criticism. He states that the refreeze step in the 3-Step approach is 
preventing groups and individuals for reverting to their old habits, and that it can be short lasting until 
a new change occurs [37]. Furthermore, he argues that Lewin (i) does not view change as being 
predictable and planned from one stable state to another, but rather as a complex and iterative learning 
process, stability as always fluid, and the outcome cannot be predicted but emerge through trial and 
error (ii) did recognize that a whole series of incremental changes are needed to align an 
organisation’s culture and behaviour with the new structure as well as that cultural and behavioural 
changes can take place rapidly in times of crisis. In addition, incremental change can lead to radical 
transformations (iii) did not ignore the role of power and politics since he, as part of the Jewish 
community, multiple of times addressed his concern about racism and anti-Semitism and (iv) does 
not view change as a top-down process, but rather as being able to be initiated from the top, middle 
or bottom and including equal participation from all the levels to achieve effective change [37].  
 
In addition, he points out that the criticism has forgotten the foundation of Lewin’s change approach, 
which he states is that the Force-Field approach to change, 3-Step approach to change are inter-linked 
along with Group Dynamics and the Action Research approach [37]. The Force-Field approach to 
change and the Group Dynamics Approach both were developed to understand e.g. a project group 
and motivate it, while the 3-Step Approach and the Action Research Approach were developed to 
change the behaviour of this group. Together these approaches form what Lewin called the 
approaches to planned change [37]. He adds that the criticism stems from viewing these as separate 
themes rather than inter-linked themes [37]. This thesis will however only focus on the Force-Field 
Approach without considering that an exclusion of the rest of the approaches.  
 
2.6 DevOps  
To understand DevOps, it is first necessary to understand where it comes from. Thus, literature about 
agile development and lean software development is briefly introduced in this section. Next, literature 
within DevOps will be reviewed.  
 
2.6.1 Agile development  
Agile Development was introduced as a methodology within software development as a response to 
the downfall that the waterfall model was causing. The waterfall model has been known the longest 
within software development and was first introduced in 1956 by Herbert D. Benington to describe 
advanced software programming methods for digital computers [38] and in 1970 by Dr. Winston W. 
Royce [39]. However, the term waterfall model was not used until 1976 in a paper by Bell and Thayer 
[40] [41]. In 1983, the re-launch of the waterfall model took place [41]. It included a linear sequential 
==PAGE:26==
18 of 80 
flow, see Appendix 2, the support of the model grew rapidly, as it showcased a logically flow of 
everything right from the beginning through the end of the life cycle [42]. However, in a rapid 
changing environment it was evident that the waterfall model could not hold in some cases. Dr. 
Winstron W. Royce went back and criticized it arguing that it is risky and invites failure and that re-
design was bound to happen, which meant a larger cost would be expected [39]. He ends his note 
with arguing that iterations are needed [39]. Thus, in 2001 (even though it can be tracked back to the 
1950s) 17 software developers introduced the agile manifesto and hence agile development [43]. The 
agile manifesto consists of 12 principles [44] and 4 core values i.e. individuals and interactions over 
processes and tools, working software over comprehensive documentation, customer collaboration 
over contract negotiation and responding to change over following a plan [45]. Thus, agile 
development serves the purpose of promoting rapid and flexible responses to change throughout the 
development cycle of a product. This is possible through its iterative processes e.g. SCRUM, from 
which a project team works in tight cycles and re-visits plans again to implement the needed changes 
and then continue again. A more popular term for this is “failing fast and iterating to correctness” 
[46].  
 
2.6.2 Lean Software Development  
Lean software development is related to agile development. Lean was coined back in the late 1980s 
by Toyota [47] with its purpose to maximize customer value while simultaneously minimize waste 
i.e. customer value creation with zero waste [47]. The entire value stream is examined and the flow 
of products/services is optimized to achieve zero waste. To achieve this a lean way of thinking and 
conducting business is required across the entire organisation. In addition, processes are created to 
need less human efforts, less space, less capital and less time to fix defects and hence less costs [47]. 
In turn, this creates faster responds to changing customer demands and other market changes with 
fast throughput time, high quality and low cost [47]. To optimize the whole value stream, lean 
software development follows 7 principles which was derived by Mary and Tom Poppendieck [48]. 
They argue that the negative parts of the waterfall model and describe it as a failed model [48] when 
it comes to software development. In connection with this, Mary and Tom stated 7 principles that can 
be used to optimize the value stream and work with software development in a lean way after realizing 
that the Waterfall model failed when used on software development [48]. The 7 principles are (1) 
Eliminate waste i.e. eliminate that which does not bring any added value, (2) Build Quality In i.e. 
Build in quality to eliminate the process of queuing up defects that needs to be fixed, (3) Create 
Knowledge i.e. knowledge such as feedback is created through iterations, (4) Defer commitment i.e. 
enabling decisions to be made and then easily changed, (5) Deliver quickly i.e. fast delivery while 
ensuring that the software is in its best quality (6) Respect people i.e. developing an entrepreneurial 
leader, having an expert technical workforce and responsibility-based planning and control. Finally, 
the last principle is (7) Optimize the whole i.e. optimizing the whole value stream instead of sub 
optimization [48]. See Appendix 3 for a deeper description of each principle.

2.6.3 DevOps 
DevOps was coined in 2007 by Patrick Debois and builds on both agile development and lean 
development [2]. While testing a government project Debois was positioned between Development 
and Operations, which consequently meant that, he needed to switch between the two departments 
[2]. This fuelled his interest to erase this unpleasant constant switch between the two departments. In 
2008, he attended an Agile conference: Agile infrastructure, in Toronto, Canada, as the only one. 
During the conference, the problematic area of the gap between the two departments was brought up 
by Debois, which later resulted in the development of the Agile System Administration group at 
Google [2]. However, it was not before 2009 that DevOps was popularized [2]. This first took form 
by Flickr who presented and broadcasted the speech titled 10+ Deploys Per Day: Dev and Ops 
Cooperation at Flickr [49], which Debois watched. Later the same year he and other interested 
professionals organised the conference, DevOpsDays, hosted in Ghent, Belgium [2] [50]. In 
connection with this, the Twitter hashtag #DevOps evolved, which further created a rapid growth of 
practitioner gatherings, conferences and blog posts concerning DevOps [2].  
 
Today, DevOps is primarily heard within the IT-industry. Enterprises across different industries have 
implemented DevOps, in which DXC and its clients are in the process of the implementation. What 
was first simply an idea to break down departmental silos has quickly grown into involving the whole 
development lifecycle. Prior to DevOps, there were no direct connection between the Development 
Department and the Operations Department in which organisations consisted of silos between the two 
departments. When a software was developed in the development department it was just “thrown 
over the wall” to the operations department who then needed to take over the code from the 
development department to do whatever they needed to do with it so it could be released. This process 
includes high risks of error leading to inefficiency, bad quality and hence risk of losing competitive 
advantage. However, even though DevOps has planted its roots in the majority of IT organisations, 
it is still met with criticism. For example, the Application Designer in the KOMBIT account states 
“DevOps creates a lot of friction between people”. In addition, the Senior Developer in SimCorp 
argues that they do not use DevOps because they do not have the resources needed to implement it 
and adds “Even though it does provider a lot of benefits it also provides a lot of negative effects, 
especially if it is not implemented correct. I would only recommend working with DevOps to those 
who actually know what it is about”.  
 
Due to the newness of DevOps, there is no formal universally accepted definition and thus the 
definition of what DevOps actually is, depends on the particular organisation. It is claimed to be a 
philosophy, a culture, a principle, an approach or a methodology. Researching about DevOps will 
give dozens of different definitions. However, this case has chosen to look at DevOps as an approach. 
This was primary decided through interviews carried out, in which the majority of the interviewees 
saw it as an approach. The Solution Architect in DXC defines DevOps as “It is an approach on how

In a typical DevOps setting the process, see Figure 1, is to plan, code, build, test, release, deploy, 
operate and monitor. Through the monitoring, feedback is given and the process starts over again. 
This process is typically combined with SCRUM and thus, in a real-life setting DevOps is most often 
used in the SCRUM processes.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The Development Department (Dev) is composed of the “makers” e.g. software engineers. The 
Operations Department (Ops) is composed of people who take care of the product after the makers 
e.g. Operations staff, Systems engineers and Network engineers [53]. The integration happens 
through the heavy focus on automation e.g. automating infrastructure, automating workflows and 
continuously measuring application performance [52]. A DevOps environment consists of 
autonomous cross-functional teams i.e. development and operations working together in one team to 
deliver software into production, common goals and understanding between the members of the team, 
common support and maintenance, high levels of tests and continuous delivery [54]. The goal with 
DevOps is to release products more frequently, detect defects and simultaneously decrease lead-time 
it takes to fix errors, increase reliability and create better quality product in order to respond to market 
changes, decrease the time it takes a product to enter the market and hence create a competitive 
advantage. To do all of this, DevOps focus on continuous integration and continuous delivery. In 
addition, DevOps follows the C.A.L.M.S framework to ensure that its process is effective.

• Continuous Integration (CI):  
Continuous integration (CI) merges all developer’s codes of e.g. a software application that is being 
built to one shared mainline i.e. a team build server [56]. All the work from one team is then integrated 
with each other, in which an integrated build is performed to deliver the work to a cross-team build 
server where it is integrated with another team’s work. This integration and build happens 
continuously several times a day, in which the software is unit-tested every time a developer adds in 
new code [57]. Aron Cois argues that the value in CI is that it automatically merges, build and test 
every new version of code that is merged into the central and forces developers and team of 
developers to integrate their individual work with each other as early as possible [57]. Sanjeev Sharma 
adds that it also exposes and addresses any issues on a regular basis [57]. Aaron Cois agrees on this 
and states that it is a centralized server that continually pulls code changes, as software applications 
are developed and build from scratch [56]. It notifies the entire project team of any failure e.g. a 
broken code within minutes [56]. It is then the project team’s duty to focus on fixing the particular 
failure before continuing making any additional code changes in the process [56]. This is crucial, as 
code may be checked into a centralized server while it is broken resulting in a broken codebase and 
hence the team might lose control of the system’s working state. The impact this can have is e.g. a 
loss of time, possible loss in productivity of the team members, and a loss in momentum when forced 
to revert changes that have been made by various developers. Aaron Cois also points out, that it is 
not only developers who are needed to effectuate CI, Operation is also needed [56]. The process is 
performed based on automated configuration and deployment scripts, which are written in 
collaboration between developers and operation engineers. This is to ensure a common understanding 
of the script [56].  
 
• Continuous Delivery (CD) 
Alex Yates states that Continuous Delivery (CD) happens when an application is built through CI 
[57], in which it results in frequent iterations, experimentation and fast feedback loops [59]. When 
changes are made to a code and integrated as described in the previous section about CI, it is 
automatically tested in an environment i.e. deployment pipeline, similar to the production 
environment. This similar environment goes under different names, however it usually includes a 
development environment, test environment, and a staging environment. Suzie Prince states that each 
of these environments automatically tests the codes differently and the code is only moved to the next 
environment when it has passed the test of the previous environment [60]. This is to detect and fix 
any failures to ensure that it will fully work and be release-ready to be pushed into the production 
environment [60]. Sanjeev Sharma adds that CD also involves the Operations team and Quality 
Assurance team who are involved in the validation and testing process of the code [58]. However, it 
should be noted that this process is not fully automated, as the final decision to push to production is 
a business decision, thus the final deployment is manual [57]. 
 
• C.A.L.M.S framework 
The C.A.L.M.S framework describes successful implementation of DevOps. It was first coined back 
in 2010 by John Willis and Damon Edwards [61]. It was later refined by Jez Humble who 
==PAGE:30==
22 of 80 
implemented the “L” in the framework [61]. The framework includes five pillars i.e. Culture, 
Automation, Lean, Measurement and Sharing, which all needs to take place to prevent an overall 
ineffectiveness of the software [61].  
 
Culture: Changing the organisational culture from thinking in silos into a collaborative and cross-
functional way of working is one of the objectives of DevOps. It forms the hardest and most crucial 
aspect of successful adaption to DevOps. Culture itself is not something that can be changed over-
night, however as Lloyd Taylor puts it ”you cannot directly change culture. But you can change 
behaviour, and behaviour becomes culture” [62]. In order for DevOps culture to be rooted within an 
organisation silos are broken down and the Operations Department is involved in the development 
aspect of the software application while also providing the necessary infrastructure to run the software 
application on [63]. In addition, instead of the development people throwing a deployable software 
“over the wall” to the Operations Department, working together as a team diminishes this behaviour. 
In turn, operation and maintenance of a software system becomes everyone’s responsibility. 
Incidence such errors and defects are handled and resolved in a collaborative manner i.e. all the parties 
involved should work as a team not against each other. This results in that finger pointing and blaming 
each other of any error diminishes drastically when success and failure is looked upon as being a 
team effort. To further strengthen DevOps culture control needs to be reduced and autonomy needs 
to be increased within teams in terms of flexibility to make own decisions [63]. It requires 
transparency and trust between managers and the teams. Furthermore, all departments across the 
organisational chart including e.g. marketing needs to be on board embracing the DevOps culture 
along with prompting an open communication channel [63]. Finally, continuous learning, continuous 
innovation, feedback loops and continuous improvement within cross-functional teams are important 
too. This corresponds with the Lean way of thinking.  
 
Automation: Automation is what enables continuous delivery. Eric Minick argues that it is important 
to understand that it is not only culture that needs to change, but processes also need to change by 
introducing DevOps tools that can automate the workflow [64].  This can decreases e.g. queuing up 
defects and lead-time, which is what ultimately have caused problems previously through a manual 
workforce. Rapid feedback, faster detection of defects and frequent deployment are achieved while 
simultaneously cutting cost as there might be a cut in e.g. human labour. To change processes within 
an organisation, people need to foster a DevOps culture and thus a change in mind-set, as to not revert 
to old habits of working. The ultimate idea of automation is that only one single push on the button 
will deploy the software application [63].  
 
Lean: As described earlier, DevOps builds on lean software development and thus comes in as one 
of its pillars. Lean evolves around continuous improvement and embracing failure. Failure is bound 
to happen at one time or another so rather than looking at it as a punishable offense, it is looked upon 
as being an opportunity to absorb, recover and learn from it [63]. To do this, fast detection is planned 
from the start and rapid recovery is worked towards as a team [63]. Furthermore, WIP and waste is 
reduced through removing manual repetitive work with automation.  
 
==PAGE:31==
23 of 80 
Measurement: Data to measure performance depends on each organisation, but without measuring 
performance it is hard to concise if DevOps has a positive impact on the business and hence adds 
value, as an article in New Relic puts it “DevOps without measurement- or without measuring the 
right things- is a Fail” [65]. The article states that an organisation needs to link and balance the goals 
of faster (speed of delivery), better software (high performing, quality software that delivers a good 
customer experience) to goals for innovation and business success [66]. To do this, five examples are 
given that could be measured i.e. business success, customer experience, application performance, 
speed, and software quality. It is important that the metrics that are measured are visible to the entire 
team so it is made clear how the performance is. Visualizing team performance also helps with 
changing people’s behaviour [65].  
 
Sharing: The final pillar is about the idea that DevOps contributes with a shared common ground 
between the Development and Operations department. Sharing e.g. responsibilities within a team can 
bridge an understanding and reduce frictions that might be between the developers and operators. 
This is very important in order for the silos to be broken down. The idea is that developers who build 
the software should be involved in the shipping and running of it, which previously only was the 
Operations Department’s role [63]. Thus, instead of the deployment only being the Operations 
responsibility and development only the developers’ responsibility, it is now everyone’s 
responsibilities. Debois adds that celebrating successful releases together and sharing tools and 
techniques as effective sharing mechanisms [67]. Enabling sharing between the parties involved can 
lead to a understanding, less finger-pointing and waiting time. Finally, it is important to note that 
taking one of the five pillars out of the equation can cause ineffectiveness in DevOps.

This section will give an insight into the Case Study Method, which is used to build up this case,  
 
According to Yin, a case study allows researchers to focus on a case and retain a holistic and real

“a case study is an empirical inquiry that investigates a contemporary phenomenon (“the case”) in 
depth and within its real-work context, especially when the boundaries between the phenomenon and 
context may not be clearly evident“ [68].   
 
He adds that a case study is suitable when answering a “how” or “why” question about a

3.2 Quadric Framework 
The Quadric framework, see Figure 2, was established as a management tool [69]. Its purpose is to 
bridge the gap between business strategy and implementation in such a way, that an organisation can 
achieve differentiation. Simultaneously, it creates an understand of where it is positioned in the value 
chain. Quadric consists of four interaction points across an organisation’s value chain: an internal 
bias, and an external bias, a rational point of the bias and an emotional point of the bias. In the center 
of all this lays the core idea. The core idea is the positioning strategy of an organisation i.e. a clear 
purpose and the direction that it takes. When the core idea is understood, four areas are used to relate 
them to the core idea and reinforce it. This includes cultural uniqueness, operational evidence, 
aligned marketing and optimized brand portfolio [69]. Any occurring changes should continuously 
be integrated to update the framework and ensure that it is aligned. However, as Michael Sherain 
states then in order for an organisation to fully understand its differentiation strategy, Quadric 
framework needs to be backed up by other methods and theories and thus cannot stand alone [70].  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure  2: The Quadric Framework 
==PAGE:37==

Core Idea is positioned in the center of the Quadric framework with four areas of an organisation 
that needs to be aligned with it. It represents the positioning strategy, or in other words an 
organisation’s purpose i.e. what do we stand for, and direction i.e. where do we want to go. 
Concurrently, the purpose and direction should be true, relevant and of high value to everyone across 
the value chain. This is to ensure that everyone works together to achieve it and grows together [71]. 
 
Cultural uniqueness is the cultural traits that guide and reflect the employees in terms of how 
they work and hence what others know them for [72]. These cultural traits directly relate to an 
organisation’s core idea and include e.g. shared values, norms and behaviours while visualizing the 
vision and mission of the organisation. It is very important, however that an organisation constantly 
reassures that the cultural uniqueness is aligned with the core idea, otherwise the differentiation 
strategy will not be reached [72].  
 
Operational evidence is the evidence that highlights the success that an organisation has 
achieved, which in turn relates to its positioning strategy. Thus, an organisation’s differentiation and 
hence competitive advantage can only be achieved if it directly related to the core idea. In connection 
with this, capabilities play a crucial role. It is resources and competences that are combined to create 
a strategic capability. In turn, this acts as a sustained competitive advantage and hence one of the 
enablers of an organisation’s success.  
 
Aligned marketing consists of the activities that an organisation performs e.g. advertisement 
to sell and/or buy a product or service. Marketing can act as a tool to acquire customers while keeping 
the current customers. In addition, this requires heavy research on e.g. market needs and growth 
opportunities [73]. To fully excel, an organisation’s marketing needs to match its internal capabilities 
while at the same time relate to its core idea, and hence reinforce the positioning strategy. If all other 
areas of the Quadric framework are aligned, developing the aligned marketing is the easiest step in 
the Quadric framework [37].  
 
Optimized brand portfolio needs to reinforce an organisation’s positioning strategy, and 
thus needs to reflect the core idea. The brand portfolio can e.g. include the corporate brand, unit 
brands, product/service brands, and partner brands. An organisation’s brand is an important 
differentiation parameter, which along with other factors can determine the talent that an organisation 
attracts [71] [74]. Additionally, as all the other areas of the Quadric framework this area also needs 
to continuously be evaluated to incorporate any changes that occurs and constantly stay aligned with 
the core idea.  
 
Identifying what DXC’s positioning strategy is as well as what reinforces it, can clarify why DXC 
has chosen to implement DevOps. In turn, this will also create an insight into how DevOps creates a 
competitive advantage for DXC.

3.3 The VRIO Framework  
As mentioned in chapter two, an organisation can attain and sustain a competitive advantage if its 
strategic capability complies with the VRIO framework and its criteria, see Figure 3.  
 
 
Valuable: A capability is valuable if it can exploit opportunities and/or neutralize threats. In 
addition, it can also add value if (i) it improves both efficiency and effectiveness e.g. it enables an 
organisation to generate higher revenue or lower cost and (ii) it is perceived as being valuable by the 
customer. In this context, the following question must be answered: Does an organisation’s 
capability add value by enabling it to exploit opportunities and/or neutralize threats?  
 
Rare: A capability is rare when it is possessed uniquely by one organisation or by a few others. The

valuable capability?

following question must be answered: Do organisations without a capability face a cost disadvantage 
in obtaining it, compared to organisations that already possess it?

organised in such a way that it can exploit the full potential of the capability. This entitles that the 
organisation supports this capability. The following question must be answered: Is an organisation

3.4 Dynamic Capabilities View 
As described in section 3.3 the Dynamic Capability View will be used to assess whether DXC is 
organised to exploit the full competitive potential of DevOps. In connection with this, the analysis 
will utilize Johnson et al.’s definition, which is:  
 
A dynamic capability is an organisation’s ability to renew and recreate its strategic capabilities to 
meet the needs of changing environment.  
 
This is in particular important in this case because DXC are operating in a rapid changing 
environment, and thus it is necessary to determine if DXC has the ability to renew and recreate its 
resources and competences with DevOps.  
 
Additionally, the capacity of a dynamic capability, which Teece discussed, will also be used in this 
case. The capacity comes in three forms 
 
• Sensing: In order for an organisation to sense an opportunity, it must constantly scan, search

activity in research and development and investigating customer needs.  
• Seizing: When an opportunity is sensed it must be seized and addressed through e.g. new

• Reconfiguring: However, seizing an opportunity may require renewal and reconfiguration of 
organisational capabilities and investments in e.g. technologies, manufacturing and markets.

3.5 The Force-Field Approach 
As described in section 3.3 the Force-Field Approach, see Figure 4, will be used in connection with 
the “O” in the VRIO-framework, and thus will build on the Dynamic Capabilities View. This is to 
create a larger emphasize on the “O”, in which the organisation must be organised in such a way that 
it exploits the full potential of the capability and hence supports it. However, one might then question, 
what if the people in the organisation resist this dynamic capability, how is this resistance managed. 
Furthermore, it creates an understanding about that it is not merely enough to believe that competitive 
advantage occurs if the capability holds the VRI-criteria, it must also hold the “O” to sustain it.  
==PAGE:40==
32 of 80 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 4: Lewin's Force-Field Approach 
The Force-Field Approach considers that an organisation constantly has forces pushing towards 
change and forces resisting change. When these forces are evenly balanced, an organisation will find 
itself in a state of inertia, and thus will not change. To overcome this the easiest way is to reduce the 
forces resisting change rather than the forces pushing towards change.  
The method involves the following steps [34]: 
 
1. The desired future state is identified. This is perceived as the goal to achieve and is written in 
the box on the right-hand side.  
 
2. The driving forces that are pushing towards the desired future state are listed in the left-hand 
column. The restraining forces preventing the desired future state to happen are listed in the 
right-hand column.  
 
3. Both columns are reviewed and the forces that are powerful terms of resisting change are 
considered.  
a) Brainstorm all the action steps it could take to reduce the effect of each restraining 
force.  
 
4. Review all the action steps  
a) Eliminate those action steps that are not practical, however only after they are 
reviewed as not being able to be twisted or adapted to provide a useful contribution.  
b) Identify the best of the remaining action steps.  
 
It is important to note that this method has been adjusted to fit this case. Thus, the initial fifth step,

3.5.1 The Expectancy Theory  
The Expectancy Theory, see Figure 5, will be used in connection with the Force-Field approach to 
determine how DXC can overcome a restraining force. The theory points out that motivation will be 
high when employees believe that high levels of effort lead to high performance and high performance 
leads to obtaining a desired outcome [75]. The theory points out that there are three factors that 
determines an individual’s motivation. These are expectancy, instrumentality, and valence. If any of 
these factors are low, it might cause motivation to be low too. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Expectancy is an individual’s perception about the extent to which his/her effort i.e. the input will 
result in a high level of performance. If the effort will result in a high performance, an individual is 
more likely to be motivated to put in a lot of effort. In order words, the higher the expectancy is, the 
higher the motivation is.  
 
Instrumentality is an individual’s perception about the extent to which performance at a high-
level result in obtaining desired outcomes such as job security, a salary, accomplishment or interesting 
tasks [75].  
 
Valence refers to how desirable each of the outcomes available from a job or organisation is to an 
individual [75]. This is important to consider because individuals are different and thus, needs to be 
catered to differently.

4.2.1 The Structure of DXC 
DXC is divided into two divisions i.e. the Global Business Services (GBS), which respectively is 
known as the Development Department, and the Global Infrastructure Services (GIS), which 
respectively is known as the Operations Department. Together the GBS and GIS are delivering 
solutions in terms of e.g. Analytics, Applications, Business Process Services, and Cloud [88]. The 
two divisions are structured within a formal hierarchical organisational structure, almost a 
mechanistic structure, which in turn has resulted in two silos between them. Each division is build up 
with different departments named after the tasks that the employees are working with. The GBS 
division in general deals with carrying out e.g. new projects and add-ons to systems [80]. It is build 
up with four departments: Databases i.e. responsible for maintenance of existing databases (all 
relevant data to a system) and implementation of new databases. Development i.e. responsible for the 
development of software and includes System Architects, Application Developers and Designers, 
Software Testers and Project Managers. Forvaltningen is responsible for e.g. changes to the system, 
client requirements, break downs, error correction (recorded in the product backlog) and it also 
contains accounts that are responsible for client contact i.e. two managers, two testers, seven 
developers, one configuration manager and one business analyst [80]. In addition, it is Forvaltningen 
who create and update the requirement documentation for system maintenance which in turn is 
handed over to GIS [80]. Middleware department i.e. responsible for the integration, upgrading and 
implementation of databases and other systems and in particular with focus on operation of a software 
and consultancy [80]. The GIS division is build up with seven departments: Network (outsourced to 
AT&T). Operation System i.e. responsible for e.g. virtual servers and in general installing OS on 
servers, Data Center (Servers) i.e. responsible for the servers e.g. if developers need a server it is the 
Data Center’s responsibility to order, provide and power servers. Monitoring i.e. responsible for 
monitoring the system and in case of incident, resolve them or contact the main responsible. Service 
Desk i.e. responsible for keeping track and communicating to the main responsible any incident that 
occurs. Firewall i.e. responsible for opening Firewalls in servers and systems while also monitoring 
them. Threats to the system is also detected and referred to the Cyber Department. The Cyber 
Department is responsible for security of the system e.g. to prevent attacks from any cyber hackers, 
while at the same time reporting security updates to Forvaltningen [80].

If the stakeholder gives its consensus then the process moves on with managing product backlog,

management and that each activity is aligned with e.g. the product vision.  
 
Step (2.1) Release Development and Transition

iterations. Two kinds of iterations are included in the project:  
 
Step (2.1.1) Development Iterations

the process moves on to a review and the retrospective of the iteration, while the whole system is

backlog and the planning of the second iteration. It is also during this activity where the project can

4.2.4 SCRUM  
To understand how DevOps functions within the Catalyst Agile Delivery Process Framework 
described above, the Solution Architect Trainee from KOMBIT states “DevOps is implemented in 
our SCRUM processes. The way it works is that operation is involved from the start of the agile 
delivery process. We create tasks in backlog according to the priority i.e. the importance of them, 
then we start to pull the most important tasks into a sprint. Because operation is involved, we can sit 
and communicate together with developers about the importance of each task”. The solution 
Architect Trainee continues to describe some examples and explains that prior to DevOps “when 
developers figured out that they needed a server, they would write a ticket to operation, which then 
would create a serve to them. It would then tell them that the server is now created and first then 
would they be able to move the task from backlog to a sprint. In turn, this took a lot of time, and often 
miscommunication would happen”. Likewise, both Accenture and Danske Spil use DevOps in their 
SCRUM processes. Thus, DevOps acts as a reinforcement of agility.  
 
SCRUM enables processes to be iterative in order to optimize the predictability of possible risk 
factors for a project team to act upon them and control them. It is based on three pillars [91]: 
 
• Transparency: Aspects of the process must be visible to those who are responsible for the 
outcome to ensure a common understanding for everyone involved in the process. This should 
be based on a common standard e.g. common language referring to the process [91].  
• Inspection: Inspect progress toward a Sprint Goal to ensure that undesirable factors are 
detected to prevent deviations and hence an unacceptable product. However, the inspection 
should not be frequent as it might get in the way of the work performed [91].  
==PAGE:55==
47 of 80 
• Adaption: If a process deviation if detected by an inspector, the process must be adjusted as 
soon as possible to minimize further deviations.  
 
For both inspection and adaption, events are prescribed and time-boxed, and at the same time, each 
event is designed to enable transparency.  
 
The SCRUM team  
The SCRUM method includes a SCRUM team, which is self-organized and cross-functional, this is 
to ensure that the team includes all the relevant people from the different departments to carry out a 
project without dependency. It consists of a Product Owner i.e. the one who is responsible for 
maximizing the value of the product, the work of the development team and managing the product 
backlog [91]. A Development Team, note that in the case of DevOps Operation is included here as 
well. Finally, the SCRUM team also consists of a Scrum Master i.e. who is responsible for the 
overall understanding and adaption of SCRUM by ensuring that the team follows the SCRUM theory, 
practices and rules [91].  
 
The SCRUM Preparation  
In order for a project to begin using SCRUM, different plans are prepared, developed and included 
in the process [92]. These plans are the product vision, which is the product’s goal i.e. what is 
expected as an outcome in accordance with the organisational strategy. Secondly, a product roadmap 
is developed, which displays all the new functions, which comply with the vision. Finally, a release 
plan, in which each function is timed for its release.  
 
The SCRUM Artifacts  
The artifacts represent the work that needs to be developed. Note that during the ordering and 
prioritizing of the work items that needs to be developed, Operation is also included when DevOps 
is used [91].  
 
• Product Backlog 
This is an ordered list of all that might be needed in the development of the product as well as the 
requirements for changes that might be made to the product. This includes items such as the features, 
functions, requirements and potential changes that need to be done in future releases. Each item has 
a description, order, estimate and value. The product backlog is not fixed, instead it is dynamic and 
constantly changes to adjust according to the environment of the product such as market conditions, 
and the needs of the product so that it is appropriate, competitive and useful [91]. For example, when 
the client uses the product in a particular environment, feedback is provided which in turn are 
integrated in the product backlog and hence the list becomes longer. Finally, each of the items that 
the project team will work on are refined so that they can be marked as “Done” within the Sprint 
time-box and determined to be ready for the selection in the Sprint Planning [91].  
 
• Sprint Backlog 
This is the set of items from the Product Backlog that are selected to be implemented in a Sprint. The 
Sprint Backlog is very visible and transparent and just as the product backlog it is not fixed, instead 
it is modified throughout a Sprint to enable the project team to meet the Sprint Goal. Furthermore, it 
==PAGE:56==
48 of 80 
is a forecast by the development and the operation (in accordance with DevOps), of which 
functionalities that are needed in the next product increment and the work that is needed to deliver 
that functionality into a “Done” product increment. If new work is needed, it is added to the Sprint 
Backlog. A product increment is the sum of all the product backlog items completed during a Sprint. 
After each Sprint, the new increment must be done regardless of the decision to release it or not [91].  
 
The SCRUM Events 
Each event in SCRUM is prescribed and time-boxed i.e. every event has a maximum duration to 
prevent complexity and risks and enable predictability. Once a Sprint is begun the duration is fixed 
and thus cannot be shortened nor lengthened, however an event can end whenever the content is 
achieved to ensure that no waste takes place in the process or if the Sprint is cancelled [91].  
 
• The Sprint Planning  
Sprint planning is time-boxed of maximum 8-hours [91]. The actual Sprint is time-boxed as well of 
a month or less, in which “done”, useable and potentially releasable product increment is developed. 
During the actual Sprint no changes are made that could prevent the team to reach the Sprint Goal 
i.e. what is to be accomplished in the particular Sprint. When one Sprint is concluded, a new one is 
started immediately after. Note that one Sprint contains Sprint planning, Daily SCRUM meeting, the 
product backlogs and the Sprint Review and Sprint Retrospective [91].  
 
• The Daily Scrum meeting 
This event takes 15-minutes, in which the team gather and creates a plan for the next 24 hours [91]. 
When DevOps is used, Operations is included as well here as well. Clients can also request changes.  
 
• The Sprint review  
During each Sprint, a Sprint review is conducted. It is an informal meeting between the team and the 
client during 4-hours or shorter. It takes place at the end of each Sprint to inspect the Increment, adapt 
the Product Backlog, review budget, timeline, and capabilities to optimize the value [91].  
 
• Sprint Retrospective  
The last event is a 3-hour time boxed meeting. This event consists of a reflection and inspection of 
the last Sprint, and in addition, possible improvements are suggested to be implemented in the next 
Sprint. At the end of this event, the Scrum Team plans ways to increase product quality [91].  
 
Sub-conclusion 
This section focused on identifying how the work process is currently carried out in DXC, and which 
changes will happen in the process with DevOps. It can be concluded that the current organisational 
structure of DXC is very formal. However, when it comes to the projects, they can be carried out in 
cross-functional teams using the Catalyst Agile Delivery Process framework, which in turn creates 
flexibility. It was identified that DevOps is implemented in the SCRUM processes where it helps with 
reinforcing the agile development process. The changes that will happen with DevOps are primarily 
that the Operations employees are included in development of the product and thus can take decisions. 
Finally, automation will change the process as well e.g. the manual work that previously were done 
such as handling documentation manually.

4.3.2 VRIO Framework  
To analyse if indeed DevOps does provide a sustained competitive advantage in DXC, the VRIO 
framework has been utilised to examine this.  
 
Valuable 
Does an organisation’s capability add value by enabling it to exploit opportunities and/or neutralize 
threats?  
 
DevOps creates the ability to respond to market changes in the rapid changing environment that DXC 
is operating in. This means that DXC can implement changes according to market needs and/or trends 
and that clients can request changes. In addition, it enables faster error identification through 
continuous integration. Continuous delivery makes it possible to deliver software both frequently and 
with a high speed to clients. DXC also has the opportunity to cut cost in terms of e.g. labour cost 
because automation is a vivid point in DevOps. The Lead Architect in the SKAT account states “For 
us the value is that we can cut cost. We are also able to deliver faster and more frequently with less 
errors, which automatically is a benefit for the client who view for example high quality as valuable”. 
To back this up Chief of Architect and Technique in Danske Spil among others expressed that: 
“DevOps is about continuous improvement and continuous innovation and that is what makes it 
valuable (…) you always have the ability to learn from your mistakes and do much better next time”. 
Thus, DevOps is perceived as being valuable for DXC and its clients. 
 
Rare 
How many competing organisations already possess the same valuable capability?  
 
According to the Application Architect in the CPR account DevOps “is possessed by almost every 
competing organisation in the IT industry because it started out as a buzzword and a trend”. The 
System Architect in DXC backs this up by stating “There is only one business that is more fashion 
driven than women’s fashion business, and that is the IT-industry”. Indeed, competitors do possess 
DevOps such as Accenture, IBM and KMD as stated in section 4.1.2. However, because DevOps 
consists of culture and mind-set of people, it is the culture and in general, the mind-set of people that 
makes DevOps rare. As Solution Architect Trainee states “our tools are not rare, but how we think 
and work in a DevOps way is rare”. The same answer is given by Accenture and Danske Spil who 
both states, that it is people’s mind-set and culture that are rare. Thus, even though competitors do 
possess a valuable DevOps it can be perceived as being rare.  
 
Inimitable 
Do organisations without a capability face a cost disadvantage in obtaining it compared to firms that 
already possess it?

51 of 80 
 
DevOps roots itself in the organisational culture, so even through the tools e.g. Ansible, Puppet and 
Docker can be imitated by competitors, the mind-set and culture cannot be imitated. When the 
Application Architect in the CPR account was asked about DevOps ability to be inimitable he stated 
the following: “(…) from a cultural view then it is unique. If a competitor should try and imitate our 
organisational culture it is very likely that they would face a huge cost disadvantage rather than an

Organisation  
Is the organisation organised to exploit the full competitive potential of the capability?  
 
This question has different views on it, according to the Lead Architect in the SKAT account, then 
“DXC has supported DevOps”. The Application Architect in the CPR account disagrees with this and 
argues that DXC is not organised in such a way that it exploits the full competitive advantage of 
DevOps. He states that “No, it is not organised to exploit the full potential of DevOps (…) it takes a 
long time just to get the support needed (…)“. Thus, because the “O” in VRIO remains with little 
rigidness and cannot be answered out from an interview, it will be investigated if DevOps can act as 
a Dynamic Capability and through that answer if the “O” is fulfilled in the VRIO framework. 
Furthermore, the “O” will also be combined with the Force-Field Approach in section 4.5.  
 
As of such DevOps has been analysed to satisfy the VRI criteria, but the “O” remains unstable and

analysed carefully.

4.4.1 DXC’s business agility 
It is important to understand if DevOps as a dynamic capability can improve DXC’s business agility. 
The survival of an organisation within the IT industry depends primarily on serving customers, being 
dynamic and taking fast proactive decisions in an environment with no significant state of equilibrium 
is present and contains uncertainty and ambiguity. This is done by responding to market changes and 
adapting to the changing customer demands rapidly, while also enabling flexibility within the 
organisation. Innovation drives adaptability and hence business agility. Flexibility also promotes 
innovation as it enables team members to be creative and think-out-of-the-box to create new ideas.  
 
Remember that Aaron De Smet explained that in order for an organisation to embrace agility, it needs 
to have two key things: the first one being a dynamic capability. Thus, when connecting business 
agility to DevOps, there is an evident resemblance and compatibility. As described earlier DevOps is 
an approach to break down silos between the Development and Operations Department. Automation 
allows errors to be detected much faster, decreases lead time to fix errors, and reliability is increased. 
In addition, communication channels are smoother and flexibility is introduced. All of these factors, 
enables e.g. faster response to business needs and better collaboration, and hence also business agility.

in the right direction (…)”.  The System Architect in DXC adds that “I do believe that anything 
without measurement is a fail because you will then not know what the outcome is (…) everyone are 
having different views on what DevOps is it hard to create common measurements”.

4.6 Managing resistance to change 
This section is an extension of previous section, in which the focus will be on answering the question 
of how resistance to change can be managed to get the full potential of DevOps as a dynamic 
capability. This will be done through recommending how DXC can increase motivation. In addition, 
this will fulfil the O-criteria in the VRIO-framework and thus DevOps will be able to provide DXC 
with a sustained competitive advantage.  
 
 
4.6.1 Motivation 
Even if the strategy is in place, an organisation will only be effective if employees are motivated to 
perform at a high level and hence achieve organisational goals. Motivation is described as “The 
direction of a person’s behaviour in an organisation, a person’s level of effort, and a person’s level 
of persistence” [75]. This explains why people behave the way they do in an organisation, which is 
essential for DXC to understand, as analysis have shown that people in the Operations Department 
are resisting to work with DevOps. Motivation is important in any job setting and most often jobs are 
begun with motivation occurring naturally. However, when factors intervene, it can cause one to 
perform work out of fear e.g. being fired, loosing clients and not meeting the deadline. This can cause 
bad group atmosphere, miscommunication and in worst-case burn downs such as stress. Motivation 
can come from three different sources, in which a person can be motivated by all three sources, a 
combination of two of the sources or only be motivated by one of the sources [75]. 
 
 
• Intrinsic sources i.e. behaviour that is performed for its own sake, in which motivation comes 
from performing the job itself [75]. This seems to be evident in some of the developers who 
are working with DevOps. They seem to be motivated by working in an agile way with the 
tools that DevOps is offering and seems to enjoy DevOps. However, the opposite is found in 
Operations as some of them feel that their skills and abilities are not needed anymore.  
• Extrinsic sources i.e. behaviour that is performed to acquire material or social rewards or to 
avoid punishment, in which motivation comes from the consequences of the behaviour, not 
the behaviour itself [75]. This seems to be noticeable in Operations, because most of them 
perform to avoid being fired and to essentially get a high salary.  
• Prosocial sources i.e. behaviour that is performed to benefit or help others. This is important 
in DXC because members in a project team who enjoys working with DevOps hence have 
high intrinsic motivation, also needs to have a strong desire to help the client to overcome the 
challenges with DevOps. Simultaneously, continuously search and scan the market for 
opportunities that can be integrated in the system, and together with the client, adapt to new 
changes.

These three sources all contributes to people being motivated to work towards a certain desired 
outcome i.e. anything that is received by organisational members. People are hired to deliver certain 
inputs that can be a contribution to achieve organisational goals. To ensure this, managers can use 
the desired outcomes to motivate employees to deliver inputs and perform high. The result of this is 
an alignment between the interests of employees with the organisational goals, because employees

The Expectancy Theory 
According to the Expectancy Theory, see Figure 14, motivation will be high when employees believe 
that high levels of effort lead to high performance and high performance leads to obtaining a desired 
outcome [75]. The theory points out that there are three factors determines an individual’s motivation 
i.e. expectancy, instrumentality, and valence. The analyse in this case shows that expectancy and 
instrumentality seems to be at a low level in particular when it comes to the Operations employees.  
 
  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
• Expectancy 
This factor is an individual’s perception about the extent to which his/her effort i.e. the input will 
result in a high level of performance. If the effort will result in a high performance, an individual is 
more likely to be motivated to put in a lot of effort. The decreased motivation can be due to e.g. the 
fear of uncertainty. The majority of employees that were interviewed in this case believed that even 
though they do put effort into working with DevOps, they would be fired eventually. It is therefore 
important for (i) the top management in DXC to clearly communicate the positive effect of DevOps, 
as well as create a common understanding of working within the C.A.L.M.S framework. They could 
also offer training in tools used in DevOps to enable them to be effective in their work. In addition, 
training for e.g. coding could also be given to employees from the Operations Department so that 
they would be able to work together with the developers. (ii) For the manager in each project to create 
specific roles for each member of the team, that each showcases that a high effort of that specific role

communicated out, as it is a framework that indicates what to work with in DevOps. Finally, it is 
important to continuously produce desirable outcomes.

How does DXC’s strategy differentiate them in the market?  
 
This research question was answered with the use of the Quadric framework. As a result, it was 
analysed that DXC has positioned itself around its core idea, which is to “Thrive on Change”. Due to 
that, DXC is operating in a rapid changing environment it is necessary that it embraces change and 
seize the opportunities in it, rather than avoiding it or fighting against it. To develop their strong 
position on the market, DXC has created a unique culture, which focuses on the mission to help 
clients to thrive on change. At the same time, DXC are working towards being recognised globally 
as a force multiplier, which enables clients to seize the opportunities presented in todays’ rapidly 
changing environment. To accomplish all of this DXC is working within C.L.E.A.R values. Both the 
Operational evidence, Aligned marketing and brand portfolio seems to be aligned with the core idea. 
Considering that the market is changing rapidly, DXC has positioned themselves strong by 
representing change themselves through their strategy.  
 
The focus on being innovative and focusing on digitalisation have constituted to the development of 
a unique bond between DXC and its clients. This bond created out of DXC’s strategy is what primary 
differentiate them from other competitors. The client becomes depended on DXC, just as DXC 
becomes depended on the client and thus a symbiotic and almost unbreakable bond is formed. In 
addition, DXC pulls in their partners as a strategic move, which has greatly benefited them. One of 
their partners is IBM who in turn is also their competitor, using one of their competitors as a 
competitive advantage is of benefit for both DXC and its clients. DXC gets an insight into IBM, while 
their clients enjoy even larger benefit from two strong players on the market.  
 
However, it can be discussed if indeed DXC’s strategy is truly followed by its employees. It seems 
as if there is a heavy focus on clients. Indeed, clients are the source of revenue for DXC and thus 
crucial for its success. But through a deeper analysis it looks like DXC has forgotten to focus on its 
employees. Finally, it is important to note that the Quadric framework cannot stand alone, and thus it 
is important to combine it with other models for it to fully be an advantage. For example, because 
DevOps is a cultural change, it could have been beneficial to combine the cultural uniqueness of the

How is the work process currently carried out in DXC and what 
will DevOps change in the work process? 
 
To answer this research question, the structure of DXC was first analysed. The structure identified 
was a formal structure. This does contradict the strategy of DXC and has resulted in silos between 
the Development and Operations Department. However, through a deeper analysis it was concluded 
that even though DXC does work within a formal structure, it does allow them to work in cross-
functional teams and be flexible. This can indicate that they are using a hybrid structure, in which 
e.g. flexibility is introduced. To back this up, the general work process within a project team was 
analysed. As a result, the project teams in this case uses the Catalyst Agile Delivery Process 
Framework, which as it indicates includes agile development. Agile development is suitable in rapid 
changing environment because it allows a project team to implement changes through iterations and 
get feedback through its feedback loop. Within agile development, the project teams are currently 
working with the SCRUM method. The Catalyst Agile Delivery Process framework has a number of 
steps that a project team goes through. For example, the first step is a project initiation, in which the 
goal is to gain the client’s consensus, analyse the project and develop the documents that will be 
included in the development of a particular software application. One of the major highlights in this 
framework is the heavy focus on iterations. It is composed of two large iterations i.e. the development 
iteration and the transition iteration. The development iteration is composed of many short iterations 
per release. This is e.g. to ensure that changes from clients are integrated rapidly in the development. 
Each development iteration then starts with the planning of the iteration and includes the release plan 
and other documentations already carried out. During this development iteration, tasks are planned 
and documented. Each task is then analysed, designed and implemented and tested. During the 
transition iteration, the goal is to make the software ready to be released into production, which is 
done through one or more short iterations. The first sub-activity within this activity is to ensure that 
the software application is ready for production. If it is determined that it is production ready it is 
released into production and the project is closed down.   
 
Due to that DevOps is reinforcing agility there will not be major changes in the process as of now. 
DevOps will be used within the SCRUM process, which is carried out in accordance with the general 
SCRUM guideline. The major change here will be that the Operations employees are moved in with 
the Developers and thus are included in the decision making of the Catalyst Agile Delivery Process 
Framework. Furthermore, the majority of the processes will be automated to ensure continuous 
integration and continuous delivery. Furthermore, the tasks that are created in the backlog are 
prioritised according to the importance of them with Operations being involved. The tasks are then 
pulled into a sprint according the priority. The challenge here will be to make the Developers and 
Operations employees to collaborate and to create a common understanding of the process. It can be 
discussed if it is effective to pull Development and Operations together without working on the 
==PAGE:75==

collaboration and communication first. Right now, it seems that the developers are willing to work 
with DevOps, while the Operations employees are resisting it. This can cause huge errors in the 
process if people are not on the same page.  
 
How does DevOps act as a dynamic capability and can it create 
a sustained competitive advantage for DXC? 
 
To answer this research question DevOps was first looked at as a strategic capability to understand 
how it is affecting DXC’s competitive advantage. This indicated that DevOps does provide DXC a 
competitive advantage through its resource-competence combination. However, it can be argued if 
this combination is fully satisfied. With a critical view, it is hard not to touch upon that within the 
human competences DXC are not on top when it comes to working with DevOps. Both physical and 
financial resources and competences seem to be effective, but with human sacking behind it can cause 
critical implications in the implementation of DevOps such as resistance to change. However, DXC 
is structured formally, in which the strategic level has forced DevOps upon the employees. Thus, 
even though human competences seem to be ineffective, humans within the organisation are forced 
to e.g. collaborate and effectuates this competence. This is not to say that resistance to change should 
not be dealt with, because even though the change is forced upon employees, it can in the longer run 
cause severe damages to humans themselves such as increased stress, miscommunication, 
misunderstandings and even bad quality products. Furthermore, the VRIO Framework was used to 
examine if DevOps fulfils the VRIO-criteria to understand if it can provide a sustained competitive 
advantage in DXC. DevOps was analysed to hold the VRI-criteria, however the “O” remained 
uncertain. Thus, the “O” was combined with the Dynamic Capabilities View in sub-section 4.3.3.  
 
Dynamic capability was viewed as an organisation’s ability to renew and recreate its strategic 
capabilities to meet the needs of changing environments. According to for example the Solution 
Architect Trainee in the KOMBIT account, DevOps is considered a dynamic capability. This is 
because it has the ability to renew resources and competences through promoting innovation and 
reconfiguring mistakes in one project into something positive in another project. It is achieved 
through a large focus on continuous improvement with its continuous learning cycles. It enables DXC 
to build new competences such as becoming better at e.g. coding, developing new tools that can be 
used to automate work processes. In addition, DevOps also allows DXC to recreate processes and 
activities and hence not be static, to meet the market changes and adapt to them, which is possible 
because of innovation and flexibility. Furthermore, to dive even deeper into this it was examined if 
DevOps can sense opportunities, seize them and reconfigure its capabilities to fit the changing market. 
It has the ability to sense, which new features or applications are on the market, and which changes 
the clients need but is not aware of. The latter was noticeable in the KOMBIT project who wanted 
something that was agile, but did not know what it could be, in which case DXC offered DevOps. 
This turned out to be positive, because the Chief Architect in KOMBIT would have chosen it himself 
and thought about demanding it in future projects. In addition, DXC also won the contract back with 
CPR, because they promised to deliver frequently, which was only possible with the help of DevOps. 
This was possible because DevOps seized these opportunities with the use of e.g. its tools. Finally, it 
==PAGE:76==
68 of 80 
was also concluded that it is possible to reconfigure DevOps through e.g. enabling developers to 
develop their own tools, which are used to further reinforce DevOps. As of such, DevOps is indeed 
a dynamic capability and hence fulfil the O-criteria in the VRIO Framework and creates a sustained 
competitive advantage. However, it can be discussed if it will continue to be a dynamic capability. 
This is due to the heavy resistance against DevOps, especially from the Operations. Resistance is a 
fact that needs to be worked upon to enjoy these benefits in the future as well. DevOps does indeed 
act as a dynamic capability, however DXC themselves seems to be the ones who might prevent it for 
being of any benefit. Another aspect that can be discussed is if DXC actually needs a sustained 
competitive advantage due to the rapid changing market that they are operating in, or if a temporary 
competitive advantage would be enough. It can be argued that DXC might be positioned in Horizon 
1 [4], because long-term innovation is uncertain and thus it is agile, and innovates within short time 
horizons as new changes appear rapidly. Nevertheless, DevOps does bring some sort of competitive 
advantage to DXC and has already proved itself to work. However, it sparks some doubt whether or 
not it will continue to do so in the future if DXC does not deal with the resistance.  
 
How does DevOps improve DXC’s business agility and DXC’s 
client’s business agility? 
 
To answer this research question, business agility was combined with dynamic capabilities in section 
4.4, in which it was made clear that DevOps as a dynamic capability can impact DXC’s business 
agility. DevOps does promote innovation as interviewees have pointed out. The innovative part 
primarily comes through feedback, open communication and the flexibility that is allowed in a cross-
functional team. Furthermore, automating the majority of the work processes creates the ability to 
detect errors fast and the lead-time to fix these errors is decreased. This creates the ability to respond 
to market changes and adapt to the changing customer demands rapidly and hence business agility. 
However, when the Application Architect in the CPR account was asked if DXC are measuring the 
outcome of DevOps, the answer was no. The system Architect in DXC stated that he does believe 
that anything without measurement is a fail because the outcome will not be fully known. It can 
therefore be discussed if business agility is actually improved without measuring it. It might just be 
due to that DXC and its clients are working in an interconnected symbiotic way that regardless of the 
outcome, it is perceived as being an improvement to DXC’s business agility. Additionally, resistance 
to change is visible and might hinder business agility in the future. On the contrary, faster responds 
to customer needs and frequent deliveries have already been proven to hold true, which does improve 
DXC’s business agility. Furthermore, it DevOps does also improve DXC’s client’s agility through 
enabling clients to e.g. rapidly respond to their customer needs and adapt to the change on the market 
that they are operating in. As an example, the Chief Architect in KOMBIT expressed that DevOps 
does give them an opportunity to adapt to changes on the market rapidly, which for them is business 
agility. This is possible through the flexibility that the iterations create. Changes on the market can 
be implemented much more rapidly through iterations than for example through the waterfall model.

take advantage of it. Furthermore, DevOps is not measured and thus the answer to this research 
question almost turns into yes DevOps is the right approach to improve business agility, however we 
just do not know if it actually does because we have not measured it. It turns into an assumption rather 
than a fact. On the contrary, KPI’s are looked upon with a critical view, because even though they 
are important, the projects might change so rapidly that a specific KPI cannot measure performance 
before it has to be changed again.  
 
What kinds of driving forces and restraining forces can be 
observed when DevOps is used? 
 
This question was answered in section 4.5, in which the Force-Field Approach was used to identify 
which driving forces and restraining forces result in resistance to change when using DevOps. Almost 
all the interviewees who were asked if they have experienced resistance to change with DevOps 
answered yes. Thus, it was important to look into this matter, because resisting DevOps ultimately 
also means that the ability for it to be a dynamic capability might be diminished in the future and 
hence it will not add a sustained competitive advantage for DXC. Resistance to change was identified 
to be caused by the evenly balance driving forces and restraining forces. Eight driving forces were 
identified e.g. competitiveness, innovation, flexibility and adaptability. Eight restraining forces were 
also identified e.g. uncertainty and lack of trust, lack of motivation and fear of losing one’s job.  
 
However, it can be discussed if the forces actually represent the true cause of resistance to change, as 
it is based on a small sample in DXC. But with competitors such as Accenture claiming that they too 
have experienced resistance to change, it is more safe to say that indeed a lot of forces have collided 
and increased resistance to change. In addition, Danske Spil does also state that they have faced 
resistance to change with DevOps. Furthermore, it is important to note that there might be even more 
forces than those identified in this case. It is important to continuously observe and communicate

about the impact DevOps has on the employees. Furthermore, it was also noticeable that not many of 
the interviewees in DXC knew about the C.A.L.M.S framework, this makes one wonder, if they have

are important to work with i.e. Collaboration, Automation, Lean, Measurement and Sharing. It would 
be beneficial if everyone in DXC knew these areas This would also help with creating a common 
understand of what DevOps is. It seems as if DXC has introduced DevOps, but has forgotten to follow

organisations who will benefit from a full-blown DevOps. It is not to start big, but to find a balance.

way, while Operations can see it from a different perspective. This ability is important, in order for 
DXC to maintain the clients who are demanding a more traditional way of working. Finally, a lack 
of motivation in the Operations Department was chosen as the restraining force that was easiest to

How can resistance to change be managed in order to get the 
full potential of DevOps as a dynamic capability? 
 
This research question was answered in section 4.6, in which actions to increasing motivation were 
considered. To do this, the Expectancy Theory was used to motivate employees. According to this 
theory, motivation will be high when employees believe that high levels of effort leads to high 
performance and high performance leads to obtaining a desired outcome. The analysis showed that 
expectancy i.e. an individual’s perception about the extent to which his/her will result in a high level 
of performance and instrumentality i.e. an individual’s perception about the extent to which 
performance at a high level results in obtaining desired outcomes, seems to be at a low level and this 
is what has caused a lack of motivation. Expectancy seems to be low due to e.g. uncertainty about 
what DevOps will mean for the employees. Some of the interviewees expressed the fear of being 
fired eventually due to that DevOps requires a lot of automation and less human labour. This part also 
needs to be looked at critically. It is important to note that to fully advance in motivating employees, 
it is necessary to use other motivational methods such as Maslow’s hierarchy of Needs, which dives 
deeper into the area of performance. In this case, the Expectancy Theory was determined to be 
enough, as it affects both the effort, performance and outcomes in the motivational equation. 
Furthermore, even though motivation was lacking in the Operations Department it is important to 
continuously motivate developers as it will bring developers and operations closer together, in which 
they will not feel, as two separate departments. Nevertheless, using the Expectancy Theory will 
enable DXC to manage resistance to change through increasing motivation and reinforce DevOps as 
a dynamic capability.

Chapter 6  
Conclusion 
This case has investigated six research questions to understand DevOps as a dynamic capability in 
DXC. Each of the six research questions have been answered throughout this case.  
 
It can be concluded that DXC are differentiating itself through almost 60 years of experience, in 
which traits of both CSC and HPE is present. With the experience follows an interconnected and 
almost unbreakable bond between DXC and its clients, which is one of their strongest differentiation 
parameters. Indeed, diving into the positioning strategy of DXC through the Quadric framework, 
shows the intense focus on its clients. It has positioned itself with C.L.E.A.R values and introduced 
DevOps in their operational evidence, which allows them to renew and recreate its strategic capability 
through sensing opportunities, seizing them and reconfiguring capabilities. In addition, aligned 
marketing, which focuses on change and optimized brand portfolio have created the opportunity for 
DXC to position itself strong in a rapid changing environment with its core idea to thrive on change.  
 
Furthermore, it can be concluded that DXC has a formal structure, which in theory would prevent 
DevOps to be implemented. However, within its work processes it is flexible and follows the Catalyst 
Agile Delivery framework, in which the SCRUM process is used and work is done in cross-functional 
teams. DevOps is implemented in the SCRUM processes to reinforce agility, increase collaboration 
and communication and increase quality, cut cost, and automate processes. SCRUM is part of the 
agile development methodology and thus is used in the Development Department. With DevOps, the 
Operations Department is included in the development from the start, to ensure that validation is 
made during the development and errors are prevented before they happen. This will result in higher 
quality, frequent and faster product releases and hence customer satisfaction.  
 
The third research question was how DevOps acts as a dynamic capability and if DevOps it can create 
sustained competitive advantage. It can be concluded that DevOps is build up with a combination of 
resources such as tools, experience and knowledge, and competences such as flexibility and 
collaboration between the Development and the Operations Department. This resource-competence 
combination is forming a competitive advantage that leads to e.g. frequent deliveries, faster responds 
to market change and continuous innovation.  Furthermore, it can be concluded that DevOps does 
hold the VRI-criteria and thus creates a temporary competitive advantage for DXC. However, the 
“O” in the VRIO Framework was examined to hold little emphasise, thus to analyse if DevOps indeed 
does create a sustained competitive advantage, the O in the VRIO framework was combined with the 
Dynamic Capabilities View. This showed that DevOps indeed is a dynamic capability and thus a 
sustained competitive advantage. However, viewing this question with a critical stand point, it is hard 
==PAGE:80==
72 of 80 
to determine if DevOps will continue to add any benefit due to resistance. The competitive advantage 
might just be because of the complex supplier-client relationship that there is in DXC.  
 
The forth question was about how DevOps can affect DXC’s business agility and DXC’s client’s 
business agility. It was concluded that DevOps as a dynamic capability does indeed improve DXC’s 
business agility because it promotes innovation, which comes from e.g. feedback, it enables flexibility 
and open communication in a cross-functional team, increases the ability to detect errors rapidly and 
decreases lead-time to fix errors. This does indeed improve the business agility of DXC because it 
creates the ability to rapidly respond and adapt to market changes. Additionally, it does also improve 
client’s business agility who no longer has to wait on changes to be implemented, and hence their 
customers do not have to wait too. However, it was also concluded that DXC and its clients does not 
use KPI’s and thus it would be beneficial to look into that, as it is hard to determine the true 
performance and outcome of DevOps if it is not measured. On the contrary, it was discussed that 
because change is happening so rapidly, specific KPI’s might not be the right choice. In addition, 
business agility might not be affected positively if DXC does not act on the resistance there is towards 
DevOps. This lead us to the fifth research question.  
 
Through a deeper analysis, it was concluded that although DevOps encompasses crucial advantages 
for DXC, it also presents resistance to change. This was looked into through the fifth research 
question, in which the Force-Field Approach was used to identify the driving forces and restraining 
forces. Eight driving and restraining forces were identified, which as a conclusion is the reason why 
DXC are experiencing resistance to change. One restraining force i.e. lack of motivation, was chosen 
to be further examined, which lead to the last research question of how resistance to change can be 
managed to get the full potential of DevOps as a dynamic capability.  
 
It can be concluded that motivation was lacking because the expectancy and instrumentality of an 
individual was at a low level, which consequently affected motivation and prevented it from reaching 
a high level. Thus, to overcome this it was concluded that DXC needs to take several action steps. 
One of them being to train the Operations employees in coding so that they can fully be part of the 
Development team, to create DevOps communities within DXC in which employees can meet and 
talk about all-things-DevOps. Finally, increasing motivation will also remove other restraining 
forces, which will move DXC out of a state of inertia, where DevOps can continue to act as a dynamic 
capability and a sustained competitive advantage.  
 
6.1 Future research  
This case has investigated DevOps as a dynamic capability, and the ability it has on DXC’s sustained 
competitive advantage. Furthermore, the Force-Field Approach from organisational change has been 
included to give the “O” in the VRIO Framework more substance, as well as reinforcing DevOps as 
a dynamic capability. However, this case also identified several gaps that need to be researched 
further into when it comes to DevOps and dynamic capabilities.  
 
One of the things that were visible throughout this case was the complexity that compromises 
DevOps. For future research, it would therefore be beneficial to further dive into DevOps as a 
==PAGE:81==
73 of 80 
dynamic capability and combine it with theories from complexity management. This will create a 
larger rigidness to the “O”, which seems to have been placed little emphasise on throughout the years. 
The Dynamic Capabilities View does have tremendous potential in today’s rapid changing market, 
and it is therefore beneficial to further investigate aspects of it, and potentially combine it with other 
fields of study. Through this case, it is evident that the Dynamic Capabilities View can be combined 
with other theories and methods. Furthermore, this case has only focused on one method within 
organisational change, however there are a lot more that can be looked into. DevOps is an 
organisational change, which requires a cultural change. The cultural aspect along with habits is one 
of the hardest things to change. If the culture does not follow with the change, it will be hard to work 
in a DevOps way. As of now, DXC seems to be structured in a formal manner, which in theory does 
not fit DevOps. However, it seems that the culture of working flexible, innovative and being agile 
does show through in DXC’s culture, which might indicate that the formal structure is not as formal 
as it seems like. However, people in the organisation need to work within this culture. It will thus be 
beneficial to look into the cultural aspect of DevOps, which leads us to the next suggestion. 
Organisational Behaviour, which is relevant to investigate because it is both individuals and project 
teams that DevOps deals with. In this case, the Force-Field Approach and the Expectancy Theory can 
be traced back to organisational behaviour.  it will also require an investigation of e.g. group dynamic. 
This can be done through several of theories, but one of them could be the 5-stages of group 
development, in which a project team is analysed based on e.g. collaboration where both the 
Development and Operations people are present and using DevOps. Finally, the technical aspect of 
DevOps needs to be investigated. This particularly is directed towards the tools that are used in 
DevOps and how these tools can optimize the work process.  
 
The cost of DevOps is important to investigate as well. This case has not considered the financial part 
of DevOps however it is a crucial point to consider. DevOps is an investment and therefore it is 
important to know if and when it will pay back and if the benefits indeed are larger than the cost. 
Organisations who implement something from the strategic level are more reluctant to realize that it 
might not be a good investment and instead tend to look at it from a positive angle.  
 
As an ending note, it should be noted that this case is subject to limitations. First, the time of this 
research was limited, thus other results might have been present if the period was longer. Secondly, 
this case did not follow a specific team working with DevOps as of such it was not directly observed 
how DevOps work in DXC, instead it was interpreted from interviews who can hold bias. Thus, it 
would be beneficial in future research to directly observe DevOps. Thirdly, it was only a small sample 
of employees who were interviewed out of thousands of DXC employees working with DevOps, thus 
it might be possible to find project teams who has fully succeeded with DevOps in DXC. Finally, this 
research was done during a time where CSC became DXC and DevOps was not fully implemented 
yet. Thus, resistance to change might have been caused by other factors, such as a merger happening. 
It would therefore be beneficial to look into DevOps once it is fully implemented and no other major 
changes are happening. Nevertheless, dynamic capabilities do help with the understanding of 
DevOps, however it needs more empirical research to fully excel in an environment where it has 
enormous potential.
