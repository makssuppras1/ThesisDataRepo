Abstract
The increased penetration of renewable energy sources in future power systems poses a chal-
lenge to the system stability since the responsibility for providing ancillary services is shifted
from conventional generators towards wind power plants. Among these ancillary services,
frequency support from wind turbines is crucial to preserve operational stability in case of
grid disturbances. As the ability of wind power plants to provide fast frequency support
depends on their respective operating conditions, ensuring a secure and stable operation of
the power system becomes increasingly complex.
This work investigates the possibility of improving the frequency support in a system by
optimizing and coordinating the fast frequency responses of several wind power plants op-
erating both at uniform and non-uniform wind speed distributions. A genetic optimization
algorithm is employed to optimize the fast frequency response control parameters with re-
gards to the prevailing wind speeds and wind power penetration level. Furthermore, the
impact of a measurement and communication delay is investigated.
The results have shown that by coordinating the support of diﬀerent wind power plants
it is possible to improve the system frequency response both in scenarios with uniform and
non-uniform wind speed distribution. The diﬀerent operating states of the wind power plants
are taken into consideration and the control parameters tuned accordingly. In that way, sec-
ondary frequency dips are alleviated without compromising nadir improvement. Further, it
has been shown that a optimized support coordination can improve the frequency response
compared to individual frequency support from the wind power plants in the system. How-
ever, the measurement and communication delay entails response deteriorations to diﬀerent
extents depending on the operating conditions.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
In the recent years, the electric power generation in many countries has seen a signiﬁcant
increase in the amount of variable renewable generation. Among these, generation from wind
turbines harnessing the aerodynamic power from the wind plays a central role as one of the
main technologies driving renewable energy development. As the awareness for the impact
of climate change and the contribution of human-made emissions to it has grown, support
schemes of diﬀerent forms and extent have been set in place by most governments in the
European Union [1]. These measures have allowed the wind turbine technology to improve
and mature to the point where the cost of energy has become competitive with conventional
generation. As a consequence, the penetration of wind energy has increased signiﬁcantly
in countries such as Denmark, Germany or the UK, reaching almost 40 % in Denmark [2].
However, this development necessitates that ancillary grid services formerly performed by
conventional generators become a responsibility also of wind generators.
This has been
recognized by transmission system operators (TSOs) and conveyed into the respective grid
codes, to varying extents of ancillary services [3, 4]. This means that wind turbines are
required to support the grid, i.e. to be able to control the active and reactive power and to
perform frequency and voltage control. The present thesis will focus on the frequency control
and its implications for power systems with a large share of wind power generation.
1.2
Problem Formulation
The electrical frequency in a power system can be seen as a measure of the balance between
generation and load of electric power. In general, the frequency of a system is determined by
the electrical speed of the synchronous generators, which in Europe is 50 Hz. As long as the
1

2
generators produce exactly the amount of power demanded by the load, this value remains
constant.
However, if there is a sudden decrease or increase in the load this additional
power has to be provided or consumed. Considering the equation of motion of a synchronous
generator [5]:
Pmech −Pelec = Iωdω
dt
(1.1)
it can be seen that an imbalance between mechanical power Pmech and electrical power Pelec
will result in a change in rotational speed ω, as the moment of inertia I is a constant related
to the material and size of the generator. The magnitude of this change for a given power
imbalance, however, depends on the value of the moment of inertia I. The higher the inertia,
the smaller the speed change. High inertia values in a power system can thus reduce the
impact of sudden load imbalances and contribute to the frequency stability.
As inverter-based generation is increasing in modern power systems and replacing conven-
tional generators, this synchronous inertia is reduced more and more as photovoltaic and
modern wind-based generators are not synchronously coupled to the grid. Their electrical
design is instead based on partial or full converters, which are eﬀectively decoupling the
generator from the grid. As a consequence, the risk of high values of the rate-of-change-of-
frequency (ROCOF) following a loss of infeed or demand is increasing with decreasing number
of conventional generators remaining connected to the system. This in turn increases the
risk of instabilities in the system or even disconnection of generation in distribution systems
due to activation of ROCOF-relays. In case of severe disturbances and consequent system
splitting, areas with a high amount of wind generation will experience an inertia reduction
and might thus be susceptible to frequency instability.
As the increased penetration of renewable energy sources is both preferable and inevitable,
the development of countermeasures to improve system stability even with high shares of
renewables has been in the focus of academic and industrial research for a number of years.
One signiﬁcant area of research is the provision of emulated, or synthetic [4], inertia of wind
turbines, where the inertial response of synchronous generators is replicated by additional
control mechanisms of the turbine. As these additional control mechanisms often consist of
a combination of inertial and droop components, the inertial response of wind turbines is
also called fast frequency response (FFR). In the following sections, both terms will be used
interchangeably.
1.3
Objectives
Building on the ability of wind power plants to provide ancillary frequency services to the
power system [6, 7], the objective of the present thesis is to further investigate and analyze
==PAGE:15==

3
the possibilities of improving the overall frequency control support coordination between
WPPs in the power systems. Providing FFR may lead to further issues during the recovery
period if all WPPs are dispatched in the same manner regardless of their wind speed con-
ditions and the level of instantaneous wind power penetration in the electricity generation.
Furthermore, since FFR from wind turbines is not an inherent capacity but instead enabled
by additional control algorithm, the possibilities for adapting and customizing the FFR is
more ﬂexible compared to synchronous machines.
Bearing this in mind, the goal of this thesis is to improve the overall frequency response
investigating the potential and the possibilities to coordinate the frequency control support
provided by wind power plants to maintain and ensure the stability of future power system
with large penetration of wind power. A genetic algorithm optimization is used to support
the coordination strategy, which is designed and assessed through a set of simulated case
studies with diﬀerent wind power penetration level scenarios. The analysis will result in
optimum parameters for both frequency-dependent as well as pre-shaped responses in diﬀer-
ent penetration and wind speed scenarios. In that way, the genetic algorithm optimization
will be used to deﬁne and distribute the frequency control support in a coordinated manner
between the WPPs.
1.4
Assumptions and Limitations
The ﬁeld of study considered in this work is expansive and complex, apart from being in-
ﬂuenced by a number of parameters that complicate making qualitative assertions. For the
purpose of reducing complexity, the present investigation is based on the following assump-
tions and limitations.
• The wind power generation in the simulation is modeled on WPP level, so the single
turbine dynamics are not considered and the focus lies on the production of the whole
WPP. Hence, an aggregated approach is used, namely the wind farm is represented
by one equivalent lumped wind turbine with rescaled power capacity. Consequently,
local phenomena such as wake eﬀects or losses in the electrical components within the
WPP are neglected as well as diﬀerent inﬂow conditions due to obstacles or roughness
diﬀerences.
• Each aggregated WPP is operating at a constant wind speed over the simulation time.
As the considered simulation time amounts to roughly one minute, the wind speed and
thus the operating point of the WPP would in reality be subject to change. However, it
is assumed that the time period is short enough to not introduce signiﬁcant inaccuracies
to the result.
==PAGE:16==

4
• This work is focusing on the contribution of wind power generation to frequency support
of the system. Therefore, operating limits on the conventional power plants such as
the rate-of-change-of-frequency (ROCOF) and their possible violation are outside of
the scope of this study. Furthermore, the damping inﬂuence of frequency dependent
loads is neglected in part due to diﬃculties of estimating realistic values.
• Additionally, it is assumed that the structure of the turbine is generally able to provide

at each generator and load connection. Therefore, the study is of qualitative rather
than quantitative nature.

2.1
Inertia Emulation from Wind Power Plants
The capability of WPPs to provide emulated or synthetic inertia has over the past years
been thoroughly investigated in academia. Initially, the general capability of wind turbines
to provide FFR to the power system was shown and proposals for modelling and controlling
the FFR characteristics were developed [8, 9]. These models release the kinetic energy of
the rotating parts in the turbine by modifying the rotor current of the doubly-fed induction
generator (DFIG) and thereby adjusting the torque set point [10].
For many approaches developed in academic and industrial research, the control of the FFR
model is realized by employing a combination of PI controllers that relate an additional
power reference component to the system frequency deviation and the ROCOF. With two
components in the FFR control, diﬀerent PI parameters can be assigned for ﬂexibility [6, 11,
12].
Additionally to the FFR control with PI controllers, recent papers have also suggested the
use of fuzzy controllers to improve the release of kinetic energy and reduce the disturbances
following the support period over a range of wind speeds [13, 14].
With the reality of an increasing wind power penetration and the consequent adaptation
of grid codes, manufacturers have begun to implement inertial response features into their
turbines. The manufacturers could therefore also compare their simulation models with real
measurements from WPPs participating in frequency response support.[15, 16].
Once the capability of wind turbines to provide FFR has been investigated, it is valuable to
consider and examine how the power system can employ this capability from WPPs [17]. In

Wind Power Plant Coordination
While the provision of emulated inertia has been covered extensively, the response coordin-

attention.
Some researchers have suggested using Model Predictive Control to take into account the
diﬀerent dynamic states of individual wind turbines within a WPP [21]. This work did not,
however, consider the diﬀerent operational states with the goal of reducing secondary dips
and oscillations in the system frequency response.
Other works have considered the coordination of the inertial response of wind power plants
in association with energy storage units [22]. Here, an external energy storage is used to
support the inertial response from the wind turbines. In high wind regimes, the only fre-
quency support is provided by the storage system, as it is assumed that the turbines cannot
be overloaded, thus delivering no active power injection under rated conditions.

tion between wind power plants connected to a power system. This work will therefore try to

The results of each scenario will be discussed and evaluated mainly with regards to frequency
metrics important for the operation of a power system.
The ﬁrst metric is the frequency nadir fNadir, which describes the minimum point reached by
the frequency after a disturbance. This metric is important as too low values might trigger
protection devices. An improved frequency response should therefore increase the frequency
nadir, i.e. reduce the maximum frequency deviation.
The second frequency metric is the time to reach the frequency nadir TNadir, which is related
to the system inertia. The earlier the nadir is reached, the more energy was released directly
after the disturbance. Thus, it is preferable to reduce the time to frequency nadir as it can
also have an impact on primary and secondary control.

3.3
WPP and Power System Model Design
3.3.1
Wind Turbine Model
In the past years, the growth of wind power generation has been realized predominantly with
variable speed wind turbines (VSWT). In this concept, the operating points of the VSWT
can be generally categorized into two zones [25]. In the ﬁrst zone, the optimization zone,
the turbine will vary its rotational speed in order to operate under maximum aerodynamic
eﬃciency, characterized by the power coeﬃcient CP . The purpose here is to maximize the
energy yield in lower wind conditions. Once the rated rotational speed is reached, which can
furthermore be limited by external requirements such as noise regulations, it is kept constant
and the power coeﬃcient will deviate from its maximum.
In the second zone, the limitation zone, which begins once the rated power of the turbine
has been reached, the mechanical power on the rotor side needs to be limited in order to not
exceed the component ratings and avoid damages on the machine. To accomplish this, most
modern wind turbines employ a pitching system that will change the local angle of attack
at the blade, reducing the lift forces and thus the mechanical power transferred to the shaft
[26]. Finally, once the cut-out wind speed is reached, usually around 25 ms−1, the turbine is
shut down to prevent extreme loads on the structure. The described parameters as function
of wind speed are shown in Figure 3.3. The wind turbine model used in this work will now
be described in the following section. As the study is related to frequency control only, the
complexity of the model is reduced by disregarding additional features such as voltage or re-
active power control. The complete model is shown in Figure 3.4. The model includes both
physical and per-unit values. For instance, the aerodynamic model and the pitch control are
in physical units, whereas the mechanical model and the active power control are in per-unit
values. It is therefore needed to convert between them by means of base values, in this case
the power base Pbase as well as the speed bases ωrot,base and ωgen,base. As the turbine is a
variable-speed type, it is sensible to use nominal values for the speed bases, while the power
base is chosen to be the rated power. The parameter values employed in the model have
been taken from previous works [27] and are shown in Appendix A.

4.1
Genetic Algorithm
Finding the optimum parameters of the wind turbine FFR under speciﬁc conditions poses
an optimization problem that requires a suitable and fast algorithm. Among diﬀerent pos-
sibilities, the present work employs the implementation of a Genetic Algorithm in Matlab
due to considerations of both speed and its wide spread use in power system studies.
In general, a genetic algorithm imitates the selection process of biological evolution [33].
In the ﬁrst iteration, the algorithm creates a random population while taking constraints
and limits into account. Based on the performance of each member of the population, their
so-called ﬁtness value, a new population set is created. The new population, or children, is
created by either crossover, mutation or simply passing on the genes of the parent popula-
tion. In this way, the optimization process is based on the suitability of parameters instead
of randomly varying parameters until all combinations are calculated. For instance, passing
on the parents’ genes with the best ﬁtness values to the next generation secures that these
genes survive, while the crossover children are equipped with the best vector entries from
both parents to form a possibly improved vector. Additionally, randomly determined muta-
tions result in more diverse children and thus a more diverse population.

4.2
Objective Function
The genetic algorithm optimization is programmed to ﬁnd the minimum of a given objective
function with a set of parameters, as was explained previously.
In the present case, the
optimization cannot be carried out on a given function but has to be connected with the
Simulink model of the power system.
As the goal is to improve the frequency response of the system, the genetic algorithm can be
employed to reduce important power system metrics. In this work, it is chosen to reduce the
diﬀerence between nominal frequency and nadir value, while also considering possible second
dips and the return to steady state frequency. These are included to reduce detrimental
eﬀects on the frequency in case of a high FFR contribution to improve the nadir. In prin-
cipal, such an optimization problem represents a multiobjective optimization. As the genetic
algorithm minimizes a single value, the objective function is chosen a linear combination of
frequency nadir, second-dip value and time to reach steady-state as written in Figure 4.1.

4.3
Genetic Algorithm Sensitivity Analysis
As the performance of the genetic algorithm is inﬂuenced by its combination of parameters,
the following section will describe a sensitivity analysis of selected parameters.
For this
purpose, the scenario with the WPP operating at 7 ms−1 at a penetration level of 50 %
is chosen as simulation case.
With a number of combinations of wind speeds and wind
power penetrations to simulate as well as often more than four optimization parameters,
it is important to take into account the time eﬃciency of each optimization process. The
sensitivity analysis will serve to ﬁnd parameters that result in a time eﬃcient optimization
while not forfeiting accuracy. The selected genetic algorithm options are described in the
following, with a summary and comparison of the results in Section 4.3.1.
Population Size
The population size determines the number of parameter sets that are used to evaluate the
objective function. In general, a larger population size will increase the run time as more sets
have to be evaluated per generation. On the other hand, an increased population results in
more parameter combinations which increases the chance for an improved best ﬁtness value,
in particular for a large number of optimization parameters. In the sensitivity analysis, the
population size will be both reduced and increased, to Popsize = 25 and PopSize = 100
respectively.
Function Tolerance
The default stopping criterion for the genetic algorithm is the function tolerance. Once the
relative change of the best ﬁtness value between two generations is smaller than the function
tolerance, the algorithm stops and the best parameter set is returned. The function tolerance
will be varied to FuncTol = 1 × 10−3. As the optimized values are time constants and
reference power set points, a solution to the third decimal is deemed suitable. Additionally,
a reduced function tolerance is likely to reduce the run time of the optimization signiﬁcantly.
==PAGE:37==

25
Elite Count
The elite count determines the number of parent genes that are passed on to the next
generation without crossover combination or mutation, by default 5 % of the population
size. Within a population, these have the best ﬁtness values. In the sensitivity analysis, the
elite count will be increased to EliteCount = 5, which eﬀectively also reduces the mutation
children as described above.
Crossover Fraction
With the number of elite parents determined, the large part of the remaining population
is combined to yield crossover children, by default 80 %. Each crossover child is created
by combining the best entries from two members of the parent population.
By adjust-
ing the crossover fraction, is is possible to change the number of mutation children which
are the remaining individuals in the population. The crossover fraction will be reduced to
CrossFrac = 0.5, thus also increasing the random mutation children.

5.1
Optimization Procedure
In large and interconnected power systems, the online wind generation units are often oper-
ating under distinct wind speeds and thus also distinct operating points. With these diﬀerent
conditions, providing an inertial response with the same parameters has diﬀerent implications
for the recovery period, hence likely resulting in a non-optimal active power contribution fol-
lowing a contingency. As seen in Chapter 4, higher wind power penetration levels increase
the impact of the recovery periods of the turbines on the system frequency.
In order to provide an inertial contribution as high as possible while reducing the impact on
power system stability due to secondary frequency dips, the WPPs inertial response para-
meters can be coordinated according to their respective operating points, the wind speed
distribution in the system as well as the penetration level.
The approach presented in this work employs a genetic algorithm optimization to coordinate
the response of three WPPs with the objective of recreating a reference power contribution
39

to the reference shape to yield a response close to the optimum. The optimum response is in
this case determined as the contribution of all wind power plants at wind speeds above rated.

the results of the previous section used. The reference shape is therefore the power output
corresponding to the frequency response at 14 ms−1 in Figure 5.1. It has to be noted that

disturbance. Hence, with a simulation duration of TSim = 100 s, all possible recovery periods
are included.
5.1.1.1
Weighting
Employing the above described objective function uses each error value with the same weight
in the averaging process. As a consequence, there might not be any inertial response in some
scenarios as deviating from a zero additional power injection in the later parts of the response

acceptable, the weight of the error values around the nadir can be increased by a weighting
function. However, to be suitable for diﬀerent prevailing wind speed scenarios it is necessary
to adapt the weight as scenarios with small recovery periods can sustain higher improvements
of the frequency nadir.

5.5
Measurement and Communication Delay
The provision of frequency support in power systems is usually activated by measurements
of frequency deviation and ROCOF. While the frequency deviation measurements are quick
and reliable, measurements of the ROCOF are done over sliding windows of several hundred
milliseconds and are thus always aﬄicted with a delay. To improve the signal quality, the
frequency measurement might also be performed centrally at some distance from the WPPs.
In that case, there will also be a time delay linked to the communication between the central
measurement point and the control system of the wind power plant.
Consequently, the contribution of the wind power generation to the system frequency sup-
port will be also provided with a time delay. The optimized FFR support of the previous
sections will likely be less optimum if not deteriorating to the system response.
In this section, it will therefore be investigated to what extent the optimized parameters
can be applied in the presence of communication and measurement delays. Furthermore,
the optimization process employed previously is repeated including the measurement and
communication delay. To model the time delay within the power system model, a transport
delay is added to the frequency signal path.
It has to be noted that the base case for each scenario is now distinct from the previous
closed-loop optimization. As it is assumed that the FFR from the WPP is provided with
the optimum parameters found in Section 5.4 but including the measurement and commu-
nication delay, it is sensible to use the same response without delay as comparison. Hence,
the following ﬁgures and descriptions will include the optimized system frequency responses
(blue lines), which are referred to as base cases.

5.6
Discussion and Recommendations
The previous sections described the results of coordinating the frequency support actions of
diﬀerent WPPs connected to a power system. These results will now be summarized in this
section highlighting some recommendations on how the coordination of WPPs to provide
frequency support can be utilized to improve the frequency response.
Open- vs. Closed-loop Approach
The optimized coordination between the WPPs has been conducted in two diﬀerent setups,
namely once in an open-loop approach while the other used a closed-loop approach. The ﬁrst
has a pre-deﬁned frequency shape as input, while the latter uses as input the actual power
system frequency signal. The base case for comparison has been the frequency response with
individual optimum parameters. For each scenario, the comparison between base case and
==PAGE:88==
5 Fast Frequency Response Coordination of Wind Power Plants
76
optimized coordination is done on the metrics described in Section 3.2, namely the frequency
nadir fNadir, the time to reach frequency nadir TNadir as well as the time to reach steady
state frequency TSteadyState.
Six diﬀerent scenarios have been used, as described in Section 3.2. In ﬁve of them, it has been
noticed that the closed-loop approach resulted overall in an improved frequency support and
thus an improved system frequency behavior. This can be ascribed to the fact that with a
closed-loop approach, the FFR control can react to sudden changes or peaks in frequency.
For instance, frequency oscillations due to the return of a WPP to normal operation after an
event can be reacted to by the WPPs still providing FFR. When operating with an open-loop
setup, the FFR control of the WPPs is to an extent decoupled from the grid and injects act-
ive power into the system according to a pre-deﬁned shape. Hence, there is no adaptability
to real-time changes in the power system frequency. By including the actual power system
frequency signal as input to the control approach, this decoupling is consequently removed,
resulting in an improved frequency behavior for the majority of scenarios.

increases it to a larger extent. In the remaining cases where the base case performs better,

Measurement & Communication Delay
The impact of the measurement and communication delay on the optimized coordination has
been investigated through a set of simulations. The results have been collected and compared
with regards to the metrics described above. This comparison is done as relative changes
from the base case, in this case the results of the closed-loop coordination without time
delay (Section 5.4). The values of each scenario in Figures 5.41-5.43 represent the following
simulation cases. First, the parameters of the closed-loop approach without time delay of
Section 5.4 were used in a simulation including the time delay (blue bars). Secondly, the
closed-loop parameters were optimized again, now including the time delay (yellow bars).

optimum power output shape that is tried to recreate as close as possible does not take into

the frequency thus dropping more quickly. Furthermore, the majority of WPPs is providing
frequency support without further delay and injecting a signiﬁcant amount of inertial power
into the system which quickly changes the dynamics. The time to reach steady state fre-
quency is not signiﬁcantly aﬀected when using the non-optimized parameters, as the support
duration remains constant. On the other hand, using optimized parameters partly result
in signiﬁcant extension of the dynamic part of the frequency response, coincidentally in the
scenarios with reduced nadir time.
In conclusion, the impact of the measurement and communication delay on the frequency
support provision from WPPs can be signiﬁcant, as it has been noticed that in several of
the considered scenarios the frequency support was not as fast as desired. Additionally, the
speciﬁc optimization including the time delay is only partially able to improve the response.
However, this might possibly be improved by extending the objective function of the optim-
ization procedure to a more appropriate function.

Medium wind speed scenarios
From the results above it was seen that the most critical scenarios involve the majority of
wind power generation operating at medium wind speeds, namely those just below rated.
In the context of this work where three WPPs are connected to the system, a scenario can
be seen as critical with at least two WPPs operating at 11 ms−1. Frequency support pro-
visions from WPPs to the power system in these cases is characterized by larger frequency
oscillations with small nadir improvement. To mitigate some of these eﬀects, it is therefore
proposed to group the WPPs into initial and delayed support units to a grid disturbance.
In initial support WPPs, the FFR control parameters are set such that the support mechan-
ism is be employed immediately after the grid event in order to provide the initial containment
reserve. As the nadir level strongly depends on the amount of active power injected as early
as possible, it was seen in the optimization process that two out of three WPPs should
participate in this ﬁrst support phase. In critical scenarios as deﬁned above, WPPs in me-
dium wind speeds will thus also participate as initial support WPPs and cause a secondary
dip around ten seconds after the incident. The WPPs providing delayed support will then
be controlled such that their frequency support is only activated to act on the secondary
frequency dip. The injected active power is considerably lower compared to the remaining
WPPs, as the purpose is only to reduce the second dip without causing further signiﬁcant
oscillations.
==PAGE:93==

81
Applying this approach in a real power system of course requires a well-established com-
munication network between the WPPs and a central, higher level operator. The data signal
from WPPs to operator will consist of the current operating level and wind speed, while
the operator will communicate the required FFR parameters to the WPPs. Consequently,
the operator will need to determine the current optimum parameter sets depending on the
system load and the operating points of the connected WPPs. In the case that the frequency
measurement is close to the WPPs and the corresponding time delay is smaller than that
between operator and WPP, the FFR parameters can be regularly updated on global level
while the frequency support is activated locally. If the interval between two parameter up-
dates is in the range of tens of seconds to minutes, the operating status of the system will
likely be similar to the one before and the previous parameter set will still be valid. The
FFR from WPPs is thus close to optimum, while reducing the activation time following a
disturbance. In that way, it could be possible to include a coordination between WPPs and
a central operator while reducing the impact of providing the frequency support with a time
delay.

Chapter 6
Conclusion & Future Work
This work has investigated the possibilities of improving the frequency support from three
wind power plants (WPPs) by optimizing and coordinating their individual fast frequency
responses (FFR). For that purpose, a set of simulation and optimization processes have been
carried out.
In the ﬁrst step, the FFR of a single WPP has been optimized with regards to diﬀerent
wind speeds and wind power penetration levels. To consider diﬀerent operating conditions
of the WPP, low, medium and high wind speeds have been considered. The FFR of the
single WPP has been optimized using a genetic optimization algorithm. The objective func-
tion of the genetic algorithm consists of three weighted metrics, namely fNadir, f2ndDip and
TSteadyState. The goal of the optimization has been to minimize these metrics to improve
the system frequency response. The optimization algorithm provides a set of the optimized
parameters and based on these the power setpoints of WPPs for each operating scenarios are
determined.
The next step in the investigation has been to assess the frequency support coordination
of three WPPs. This assessment revolves around two main aspects. Firstly, it has been in-
vestigated whether it is possible to improve the overall frequency response in a system with
all WPPs operating at the same wind speed by employing diﬀerent control parameters and
support durations. Secondly, it has been examined if it is possible to gain an advantage of
WPPs operating at distinct operating conditions. These diﬀerent scenarios include combin-
ations of low, medium and high wind speeds to again represent a wide range of operating
conditions. In order to determine a base case for the assessment of the frequency control
coordination between WPPs, each WPP in the system has been providing frequency support
with the optimum parameter sets of their respective operating condition as determined in
the optimization with a single WPP.
82
==PAGE:95==
6 Conclusion & Future Work
83
The optimized coordination between the WPPs has then been conducted with two diﬀerent
approaches. One approach has used an open-loop approach, where the WPPs receive a pre-
deﬁned frequency signal as input to their FFR control. In the second approach, the loop has
been closed to use the system frequency as input to the FFR control. The objective function
of the genetic algorithm has in both cases been the weighted error between a reference power
shape and the total power generated by all three WPPs. For the reference power shape, the
power output corresponding to the scenario with the best frequency response improvement
of the single WPP optimization has been used.
The results have shown that with a coordination of WPPs to provide frequency support
it is possible to improve the frequency nadir in the majority of the considered scenarios.
This has been accomplished by demanding an increased support from WPPs at high wind
speeds to compensate for a reduced contribution from WPPs at low and medium wind speeds.
Furthermore, it has been shown that in scenarios with a large amount of wind generation
operating at medium wind speeds, some WPPs have been dispatched to react not on the
initial frequency drop but on the second dip caused by the frequency support of the other
WPPs. In that way, it has been possible to alleviate the severity of the second dip.
Additionally, the impact of the measurement and communication delay between the sys-
tem disturbance and the support action has been investigated. This has been studied both
with the parameters of the closed-loop approach optimized without time delay as well as with
parameters optimized including the delay. In all cases with initial parameters, the frequency
response deteriorated, both with regards to frequency nadir and time to reach steady state
frequency. Optimizing the frequency support parameters including the time delay did not
result in a general improvement, but yielded mixed performances.
In future research, the coordination of frequency support from WPPs could be extended
to include other support mechanisms such as storage solutions or spinning reserves. Expand-
ing the power system to contain other converter-based generation such as photovoltaics or
HVDC links would allow an analysis of how the concept of support coordination could be
transferred to a modern, interconnected grid.
Furthermore, the impact of the measurement and communication delay could be investig-
ated with more complex implementations than the simple transport delay used in this work.
This will allow to gain a more fundamental understanding of the consequences of having
distributed frequency support coordinated by a central controller.
