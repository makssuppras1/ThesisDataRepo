Abstract 
This study explores candidates for an inorganic/inorganic composite electrolyte based on 
promising properties, either conductivity or activation energy. The candidates are Li-β’’-Alumina 
reported to have an ionic conductivity of 2.7∙10-3 S/cm at 25°C measured on a single crystal, Li-β’’-
Gallate the sodium form is reported to have an ionic conductivity of 0.063S/cm at 25C measured 
on a single crystal and Li-Bentonite reported to have a theoretical activation energy of 0.155eV. 
The project synthesized the Na-β’’-phase for alumina and gallate using high-temperature 
calcination react. These phases were ion-exchanged using LiCl and LiNO3, varying the time and 
temperature. For alumina, it was observed that the decomposition to a spinel phase happened at 
higher temperatures over long periods. The decomposition was found to be thermodynamically 
favorable but kinetically slow at a temperature above 527C. It was possible to obtain 62.5% 
conversion without decomposition under certain conditions. 68% conversion was achieved for β’’-
Gallate, and a high standard deviation was observed due to the EDX signals overlapping. For Li-
bentonite, initial work showed that the ion exchange is possible, with limited optimization of the 
work presented. It was challenging to measure conducivities on non-sintered pellets for all 
materials investigated in the study.

Introduction 
As countries worldwide start to set goals for reducing and eliminating CO2 emissions to limit the 
future consequences of climate change.i Many different approaches are being considered 
depending on the sector and source of emissions. Electricity produced by green sources is rising, 
and with that comes new problems for the energy and transportation sector. Renewable energy 
sources are harder to control. They are linked to nature and cannot just be turned on and off. If 
the wind blows at night, the windmills must run to produce electricity. They might be stopped if 
there’s no demand, meaning the energy will be lost. To avoid this, short- and long-term electricity 
storage options must be considered. A good candidate for short-term storage is batteries. At the 
same time, the transportation sector needs to find a way to eliminate fossil fuels. In the 
automotive industry, the solution has been to develop battery-electric vehicles. Over the past 10 
years, their introduction has helped eliminate CO2 emissions. Batteries are a fantastic tool for 
storing electricity, but we have so far been limited to a number of useful battery designs and 
chemistries. In the future, we need to explore new battery designs to improve the performance 
and help the green transition. 
The designing process of the battery makes it easy to see why many different batteries exist and 
how many more battery chemistries are being considered and tested. Broken down to the bare 
minimum, a battery comprises 6 elements, two current collectors, a positive and a negative 
electrode, a separator, and an electrolyte. The current collectors need to be electrically 
conductive, chemically inert in the cell environment, and mechanically fit the battery cell, in 
lithium batteries are they often made of copper and aluminum.ii The positive electrode will have 
the largest electrochemical potential and where the ions are stored when the battery is 
discharged. The negative electrode will have the lowest electrochemical potential and will be 
where the ions are stored when the battery is charged. The separator is designed to ensure that 
the electrons do not travel through the cell but in the external circuit. The last of the components 
is the electrolyte. Its task is to transport the ions between the positive and negative electrodes 
during charging and discharging. 
Based on these six primary components, millions upon millions of options for battery designs are 
possible; this does not even include a long list of further steps and additives that can be used to 
optimize battery performance. Limiting the scope to only looking at lithium batteries, hundreds of 
thousands of options exist for creating a lithium battery. To further limit the number of options, 
one can look at the negative electrode graphite is used in lithium batteries due to a very low 
electrochemical potential and good cyclability. This leaves three components to work towards 
optimizing. Of these, the separator is also pretty set, leaving the electrolyte and positive electrode 
material as the two areas where new candidates are still broadly explored. While this looks like a 
few areas to focus on, there are still thousands of options and ideas to explore. 
While graphite is the most commonly used negative electrode material, new candidates are being 
developed, specifically silicon-based and lithium metal electrodes, which could provide the highest 
==PAGE:6==
Page 2 of 42 
theoretical capacity at 3862mAh/g for lithium-air batteries.iii This poses new problems for lithium 
batteries the current liquid electrolytes used in lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) are unstable against a 
lithium electrode and experience poor replating when charging. Because of this, lithium metal is 
only really used in primary batteries today. The electrolyte in the battery needs to be redesigned 
to use lithium metal as the negative electrode in secondary batteries. There are many different 
options for doing this. In this study, it was chosen to go with a solid-state electrolyte (SSE). 
Not only would the use of an SSE enable the use of lithium metal, but it would also increase the 
possible electrochemical window of the battery, increase mechanical stability, and it would 
eliminate the liquid electrolyte that both have quite toxic components and have problems with 
thermal stability and the need for a separator.iv Thermal runaways and spontaneous composition 
are a big concern for LIBs when used in applications such as cars or larger electronics, requiring a 
lot of monitoring and specific safety measures to control.v All this comes at a price. SSEs have a 
lower ionic conductivity than liquid electrolytes in a system where the internal resistance heavily 
influences the obtainable power, of which the ionic conductivity place a significant role. Because 
of this, despite all the advantages, LIBs are still the preferred lithium battery over an all-solid-state 
battery (ASSB). Currently, ASSBs are in the early stages of testing for use in battery-electrical 
vehicles.vi,vii  
Work for ASSBs focuses on two types of SSEs polymer and inorganic. Each type has advantages 
and disadvantages. Polymers have a higher conductivity but poor mechanical strength, while 
inorganic electrolytes are often sintered and therefore have higher mechanical strength but worse 
conductivity. Based on this knowledge, different groups have studied using an inorganic/polymer 
composite electrolyte to take advantage of both materials.viii Using a composite electrolyte also 
ensures better conductivity between grains as the ions are no longer limited to the grain 
boundaries; it also limits any dendrite formation in pores or cracks in the electrolyte. 
One of the current major candidates as an SSE in a solid-state battery (SSB) is LLZO (Li7La3Zr2O12), 
which has most of the right characteristics for a good SSE, high conductivity, wide electrochemical 
window, and stable against lithium but do form dendrites.ix LLZO has been used extensively in 
polymer/inorganic composites. If used as the only material in an electrolyte needs to be sintered 
to obtain a high density and good conductivity.x The sintering requires high temperatures, 
increases the complexity of manufacturing, and reduces the scalability of using LLZO in SSBs. 
Therefore, using a composite should mitigate these problems but come with other disadvantages. 
The major disadvantage is the introduction of a softer polymer which could increase the formation 
of dendrites and weaken the mechanical strength. It is the opinion of the author and the project 
supervisor that an inorganic/inorganic composite electrolyte can be made that would eliminate 
the need for sintering while increasing the density and improving the mechanical stability. This is 
planned to be done by filling the voids of an LLZO pellet with a softer compound that will fill the 
voids and bind to LLZO grains upon pressing.

referenced as β-alumina. In this study, β-alumina will be used when there is a mix of the two

the high price of gallium and potential problems with electric conductivity and bandgap. Overall, 
the material is interesting to explore as the known conductivities are better than Na-β’’-alumina. It 
could be of interest to an ASSB as it would further reduce internal resistance. 
Bentonite 
Bentonite is a layered clay mainly consisting of montmorillonite, made up of aluminum oxide and 
silicon oxide groups in layered blocks. Each layer has one-row octahedral aluminum oxide with a 
row of tetrahedral silicon oxide on each side. A layer of cationic counter ions separates the layers.

Due to the design, the clay is known as an absorbent swelling clay. This means that the spacing in 
the c-direction can vary over a broad range, dependent on the hydration of the clay. 
The literature has shown Bentonite to include a protonated and lithiated form. Uses have included 
applications in lithium-sulfur batteries and as part of nanocomposite anodes for LIBs. For this 
study, the interest in bentonite originates from its layered structures. Studies have found the 
energy barrier for lithium diffusion in bentonite to be 0.155eVxx. Furthermore, the clay is easy to 
ion exchange in an aqueous solution, from mixed cation form to a protonated form using sulfuric 
acid, and only requires modest temperatures in a 1M LiOH solution to ion exchange to a lithiated 
form. Bentonite is also a cheap chemical making it easy and affordable to incorporate if it is found 
to be a good lithium-ion conductor, which the low energy barrier indicates. Other factors such as 
electrochemical stability and mechanical properties are still unknown, so more testing would also 
be interesting. 
The approach for synthesizing Li-bentonite followed in this study will be based on the W. Chen et

Ion exchange 
Different effects drive the ion exchange depending on the medium and ions being exchanged. In 
the solid-state ion-exchange described in the β-phase materials, the ion exchange is driven by a 
concentration difference between the sodium and the lithium ions in the β-alumina powder and 
the lithium powder. Excess lithium salt is needed to drive the ion exchange towards the lithiated 
form. For ion exchanges in solid higher temperatures are also required to increase the conductivity 
in the solids to bring the ions to the edges.  
In the aqueous solution, the ion exchange from proton to lithium is driven by the hydroxide in the 
alkaline solution. It removes the proton in the layer, and then the lithium occupies the empty site. 
The mild heating used for this ion exchange helps increase the reaction rate and diffusion in the 
solids and the solution.  
Conductivity 
Types of conductivity 
When investigating new materials, there are a number of interesting properties that one can look 
at. One of these properties is the conductivity of a material. Conductivity is a physical property 
describing how well a material conducts. Different types of conductivities exist for this study. The 
three of interest in chemistry and battery science are presented.xxii  
Electrical conductivity measures how well a material, gas, or liquid conducts electricity. This is a 
desired property for certain battery parts, such as the current collector and the electrode. For the 
electrolyte, on the other hand, is an undesirable property to have as it can short circuit the 
battery.

Thermal conductivity is a measure of how well a material conducts heat. It is generally desired to 
have a high thermal conductivity to remove the heat from a battery. The disadvantage of high 
thermal conductivity often has high electrical conductivity. 
Ionic conductivity measures how well a material, gas, or liquid conducts an ion. This is especially 
relevant for batteries. We desire a high ionic conductivity for the electrolyte, and the electrodes 
reduce the internal resistance in the cell and boost the battery’s overall performance. 
Transport mechanisms in solids. 
In the following section, transport mechanisms in solids will be discussed, using Na-β-alumina as 
an example. 
The three solid-state transport mechanisms are 
vacancy, direct interstitial, and correlated. The vacancy 
mechanism has the lowest diffusion barrier of the three 
and is generally the dominating transport mechanism in 
LSSBs. At high doping levels, the interstitial mechanisms 
dominate, especially the correlated mechanism. This is 
due to the lack of vacancies in the material with high 
doping. Despite the difference in the energy barrier for 
the two mechanisms, examples of materials with similar 
conductivities using the two different mechanisms exist. 
Na-β-alumina with its two different phases β’ and β’’ is a 
case of this. In β’, the sodium is in the Beevers-Ross 
sites, which are coordinated to 3 oxygen in each plane, 
see Figure 2A. These sites are limited, and as the 
material gets more doped, these sites will be shared by 
sodium atoms. 15-30% of sites are shared depending on doping. Because of this, the dominant 
transport mechanism in β’-alumina is correlated movement. The β’’ structure is changed so that 
now the sodium is in a location where they are bound to one oxygen in one spinel block and three 
in the other spinel block, see Figure 2B. With these changes, the number of sites increases, and β’’ 
has a 17% excess of sites. Because of this, the dominating mechanism is vacancy movement.xii 
Measuring conductivities in solids 
There are multiple techniques to determine the conductivity of solids. Electrochemical impedance 
spectroscopy (EIS) is the primary technique, but pulse field gradient NMR is another option for 
determining diffusion coefficients and conductivities. In this study, EIS will be the only technique 
to determine conductivities.  
The technique is based on varying the potential, potentiostatic EIS, or the current, galvanostatic 
EIS, using a small sinusoidal perturbation. The corresponding value is measured, and while the 
frequency will remain constant, the amplitude and phase of the signal will change. Based on these 
==PAGE:12==
Page 8 of 42 
 
changes and ohms law, is it possible to calculate the resistance Z. Z is a complex number due to 
the observed phase change. With this change is introduced an imaginary part to the resistance. Z 
can therefore be represented as a complex number made up of Re(Z) or Z’ and Im(Z) or Z’’.xxiii 
The acquired impedance data are typically illustrated using different types of plots Nyquist and 
Bode. As the two plots are complementary, one is generally selected for all data. In this study, 
Nyquist is chosen as this is the plot commonly used in ASSB research. The advantage of the 
Nyquist plot is the easy identification of each electrical component and estimating the overall 
resistance in the cell. Each element in a cell has its properties and will, in theory, be represented 
by an equivalent circuit made of constant phase element (CPE) and resistor (R) in parallel. Each 
component and interphase in the cell will be represented by one of these elements. A CPE and R 
element in parallel will be represented as a semi-circle in a Nyquist plot. It is therefore easily 
identified how many unique semicircles can be identified. Often one semi-circle will be observed 
due to the different components and interphases having similar conductivities. The conductivity

X-ray powder diffraction 
X-rays are electromagnetic radiation with a wavelength between 10-8m and 10-12m. The most 
common way of generating X-rays in a laboratory diffractometer is by high-energy electrons that 
are accelerated toward a metal surface (anode) from a filament (Cathode). Upon hitting the metal 
plate, one of the processes taking place is an excitation leading to the emissions of x-rays upon 
relaxation. Each element emits well-defined x-rays, making them ideal for reproducibility, analysis, 
and comparison of the common laboratory powder x-ray diffractometer radiation source.  
X-rays’ wavelength makes them ideal for material characterization as they are in the same order of 
magnitude as interatomic atomic distances in solids. This is the key property that led to x-rays 
being one of the strongest and easiest to use characterization tools to use on solid samples.  
Upon interaction with the repeating crystal structures in a solid material, the x-rays are reflected if 
certain conditions are made. These conditions planes are repeated in the crystal structure, the 
interlayer distance, and the angle of the x-rays compared to the plane. A relationship was 
proposed by Lawrence Bragg and William Henry Bragg in 1913 and is today described as Braggs 
law and the Bragg condition: 
𝑛𝑛∙𝜆𝜆= 2 ∙𝑑𝑑∙𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠(𝜃𝜃) 
Where n is the diffraction order, λ is the x-ray wavelength, d is the interlayer distance, and θ is the 
reflection angle. This condition shows that if a constant wavelength is kept, each layer will only 
diffract at one angle for each diffraction order. The diffraction order can only be a whole number. 
A decimal number would represent destructive interference. Each plane in a crystal structure can 
be described by a unique set of Miller indices (hkl). The indices will describe the orientation in the 
unit cell.  
Each of the 14 Bravais lattices will have a unique compensation of miller indices that will diffract. 
This information makes it possible to determine the material’s crystal lattice with well-
representing data. Furthermore, each type of Bravais lattice has an equation for converting 
between Miller indices, unit cell parameters, and plane spacing (d). This makes it possible to 
transform known diffraction angles to plane spacings to unit cell parameters. Vice versa, a known 
crystal structure can be used as input to match an unknown pattern based on unit cell parameters 
and atomic positions.

Bragg Brentano (BB) 
Several different geometries exist for measuring 
powder diffractograms. The most commonly used 
geometry is Bragg-Brentano (BB)xxiv. The 
technique offers a high resolution at the cost of 
highly accurate alignment and careful sample 
preparation. Figure 4 shows the setup of a BB x-
ray diffractometer; some setups, including the one 
used in this study, have an apposition-sensitive 
small linear detector located at the F2 position. 
The intersection between the focusing circle and 
the sample is limited to a narrow area. This shows why BB's two key costs are alignment and 
sample preparation.  
BB can operate in two standard modes, either θ:2θ scan or θ:θ scan. In the θ:2θ scan, the X-ray 
tube is kept fixed, the sample rotates by θ, and the detector rotates with 2θ. In the θ:θ scan, the 
sample is held fixed while the X-ray tube is rotated by θ and the detector is rotated by θ. For loser 
powder samples, the θ:θ scan is preferred as the powder in this setup is kept horizontal. The 
general idea is that the measured signals will be intensity as a function of 2θ independent of the 
scan type chosen and maintaining the same focus. 
Refinement 
An acquired diffraction pattern contains, in theory, all information needed to extract a crystal 
structure for a new material. Methods have been developed to refine the structure from an 
experimental pattern based on Peak: position, intensity, and shape. The most dominant of these 
refinement techniques is the Rietveld refinement. The goal of the refinement technique is to 
minimize the difference between observed and calculated patterns.xxv In this process, a large 
number of parameters are tuned to refine the theoretical structure, and several different 
evaluation parameters are set up significantly: weighted average Bragg R-factor and reduced 
chi^2. 
In cases where the structure might be known, but the theoretical and measured patterns do not 
align. Refinement can be used to modify the unit cell parameters to obtain information about 
variation between the product and the structure in the database. This unit cell refinement can also 
be used if a new material is synthesized that is identical to an existing material. Except for one 
element, this new element gives the same structure but different unit cell parameters due to size. 
The more complex a refinement that needs to be carried out, the more detailed a diffraction 
pattern is required to ensure a successful refinement.

SEM and EDX 
Scanning electron microscopy is a robust tool for investigating the surfaces of samples with the 
ability to image at length scales from 1nm to over 1 mm. The images are recorded by accelerating 
electrons to energies between 0.1 and 30keV and focusing them through magnetic fields. Upon 
interaction with the sample, several different processes can happen depending on the energy of 
the scattered particle. Three types of electrons are scattered Auger, Secondary, and 
Backscattered. Each type is defined by its energy and its angle of scattering. Secondary electrons 
have an energy lower than 50eV, and a detector records the signals at an angle to the sample. The 
backscattering electrons have an energy larger than 50 eV and are recorded by a detector 
surrounding the beam entrance.  
The fourth product of the electron sample interaction is X-rays. The X-rays are emitted when 
electrons that have been excited by the primary electron relax back to their ground state. Each 
element emits x-rays that are unique for the element depending on the electronic configuration. 
Some elements emit peaks close to other elements, making identifying them harder. For this 
study, the signals for sodium are located between two signals for gallium, making it slightly more 
challenging to accurately predict the amount of sodium in the sample. The limitations for accurate 
EDX results are as low as 0.1W%xxvi but will depend on the calibration, settings, and equipment 
used. For this study, the EDX is not calibrated to give accurate results below 2w%, it will detect to 
0.1w,% but below 2w%, the data starts to be unreliable. 
Experimental 
The following section covers the experiments carried out in the project. For a complete list of the 
experimental setups and the acquired samples, please see appendix 1 and appendix 2. 
Na-β-Alumina 
The synthesis of Na-β-Alumina was carried out following a method proposed by S. Barison et al.xiii 
They found that by using γ-alumina, they could achieve 100% of the β’’-phase, which was set as the 
desired form for this study. 
The reactants Na2CO3, Li2CO3, and γ-Al3O2 were mixed in a molar ratio of 0.83:0.165:5.335. The 
powder mixture was wet-milled using a Fritsch Pulverisette 7 planetary mill and 99.99% ethanol as 
solvent. Three different milling times were tested, 45, 235, and 360 minutes at 350RPM, to 
investigate the influence on the product. The Milled product was dried, transferred to an alumina 
crucible, and calcinated at 1250°C for 12 hours. The product was tested using XRD and EDX.

Na-β-Gallate 
Na-β-Gallate was synthesized using a similar process to Na-β-Aluminum. The major difference is

the work by Patrick S. Nicholson,xix Na-β’’-Gallate was synthesized using Na2CO3 and Ga2O3 in a

5 hours. No treatment was carried out after calcination.  
Li-β-Gallate 
The ion exchange was carried out on the calcination product of Na-β-Gallate using a method 
proposed in the same paper as the calcination reaction. Na-β-Gallate was mixed with lithium 
nitrate and heated to 275°C for 3 hours. A sample of this product was taken for powder x-ray 
diffraction. The product was then washed and dried using the same procedure as for Li-β-Alumina. 
Bentonite 
Hydrophilic bentonite nanoclay was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. The clay was ion-exchanged in 
a 1M Lithium Hydroxide solution, following the approach presented by Wei Chen et alxx. In this 
study, the bentonite nanoclay was added to a 1M lithium hydroxide solution in a molar ratio of 
1:5. The solution was heated to 60°C under stirring for 90 minutes, and the product was washed 
three times with deionized water using a centrifuge between each wash. Two different reaction 
conditions were tested pH 7 and pH 14. To obtain pH 7, Sulfuric acid was used. 
Two different approaches for the drying of the washed product were tested. 
1. The product was set in a freeze dryer and dried for at least 48 hours, then added to a
drying oven and dried for at least 12 hours at 105°C or 200°C. Lastly, it was dried for at
least one hour in a vacuum oven at 200°C to eliminate the last traces of water.
2. The product was set directly in a drying oven at 200°C for at least 12 hours.

Method 
Chemical and structural characterization 
For structural characterization, a Panalytical Aeris Benchtop XRD was used. Chemical 
characterization and SEM imaging were done using a ZEISS EVO 10 with a mounted Oxford 
Instruments X-Max 80 Detector for EDX.  
The powder XRD samples were prepared by grinding the powder in a mortar and then suspending 
the powder in 99.9% ethanol to achieve an even layer of powder. Air-sensitive samples were 
prepared in a glovebox and covered using Kapton tape. Scans were taken from 2θ equals 5° to 
130°. 
To determine chemical ratios with EDX, the sample was pressed into a pellet using a 13mm die set 
to have a flatter surface for analysis. The pellets were mounted on carbon tape, carbon-coated, 
and then at least four unique spots on the pellet were analyzed to acquire information about the 
chemical stoichiometry. The same approach was used for imaging powder samples using the 
secondary electron detector. All EDX samples were measured using a 20keV acceleration. 
Electrochemical characterization. 
Impedance measurements were carried out using a Biological VMP300 and a Biological SP300. The 
testing was done using CR2032 coin cells mounted in appropriate holders for coin cell testing. The 
cells were tested using frequency ranges from 7MHz to 1Hz and a minimum of 11 pints per 
decade. 
The coin cells were prepared using a manual coin cell press in argon glove boxes. The test material 
was pressed to a pellet using a 13mm die press, and the cells were gathered and then sealed. 
During testing, a number of different blocking electrodes were tested. These include stainless steel 
spacers, copper foil, aluminum foil, and carbon paper. 
Finally, a test was conducted where promising candidates were mixed with PEO and LIFSO in a 
70:30 mass ratio of inorganic to polymer. These cells were heated to 70°C before testing.

Discussion 
The calcination products shown in Figure 
5 are identified to be a mix of the two Na-β-alumina phases. This is based on specific peaks 
present from both phases in the XRD patterns. The peaks in question are at ~34° and ~42.5°. Both 
these peaks belong to the β’ phase and could be identified in the XRD pattern. The closest peak for 
β’’ to the 34° peak is around 35°, where no peak is observed for the peak at 42.5°. There is a β’’ at 
42 that might give rise to the desired peak. The problem with defining the products as a mix of the 
two phases is that many peaks overlap, and only 2 of the β’ phase peaks can be clearly identified. 
Based on this, the most qualified explanations are that the products phases might have grown on 
each other and that the product has a different Al/Na-ratio than the two phases and therefore is 
an intermediate phase between the two phases. The EDX results in Table 2 indicate that 
calcination products 2 and 3 should be the β’’ phased based on the Al/Na-ratio that matches the 
calculated Al/Na-ratio for the phase.

Outlook 
Further work on the Na-β’’-Alumina/Li-β’’-Alumina system can be split into three sections the 
starting material, the ion exchange, and ionic conductivity. 
The XRD patterns for Na-β’’-Alumina show promising results for obtaining a pure β’’-phase. The 
experiments need to optimize the ratio of starting materials towards synthesizing pure β’’-phase, 
using γ-Alumina powder instead of the γ-Alumina pellets used until this point should also be 
considered. This work can also be outsourced by purchasing Na-β’’-Alumina powder from an 
external provider. For this project, it was decided to be easier to make it in-house, but future work 
might benefit from buying the powder. 
Although promising, the results for the ion exchange were limited to about 60% conversion 
without observing any decomposition. One option not explored here is the use of vapor phase ion 
==PAGE:28==
Page 24 of 42 
 
exchange as described by Koh et al., with the promise of 90% conversion. This could be a 
promising approach to explore further. Other options could be to explore using a mix of lithium 
chloride and lithium nitrate either at the same time or in consecutive steps. This would give insight 
into whether each compound ion exchanges the same sodium ions. Based on the results of single-
crystal conversion by Briant et al. indicate that another option could be to ion exchange on a 
crystal or maybe even a sintered pellet in liquid lithium chloride and then crush the pellet to 
powder. While this may seem counter-intuitive larger crystals could be used, giving material for 
multiple electrolytes.  
A complete redesign of the experiment could also be considered by exchanging the supporting 
lithium-ion in Na-β’’-Alumina with magnesium. This would show whether the supporting lithium 
plays a role in the decomposition of the β’’-phase and whether its presence limits the degree of 
ion exchange. 
The next step toward acquiring impedance data would be centered around using the tape casting 
to create a thin film and figuring out how to dry and make a coin cell that does not short circuit. 
Following the work presented by Armstrong et al. in which, they used Glassy carbon blocking 
electrodes and applied pressure in a perspex cell during impedance measurement. 
Other options for measuring the conductivity would be pulsed field gradient NMR spectroscopy to 
determine the diffusion coefficient and the conductivity. Pulsed-field gradient NMR spectroscopy 
has successfully been used by Chowdhury et al.xxxii to determine the conductivity of lithium in Li-β-
Alumina single crystals. Their diffusion coefficients were slightly higher than impedance 
measurements but within 1 order of magnitude. The activation energy was half the activation 
energy found in impedance spectroscopy.

Outlook 
The results in Table 4 show a conversion of 68.5%. This is a promising conversion for an initial 
synthesis, but with full conversion as the target, there is still a bit to go to achieve full conversion. 
The advantage is that the ion exchange was only carried out over 3 hours, so there are good 
opportunities to optimize the synthesis with varying times and temperatures. It is limited how 
much the temperature can be varied as Patrick S. Nicholson found that a spinel phase was formed 
at 300°C using lithium nitrate. The melting point of lithium nitrate is 255°C, and below this 
temperature, the conversion might decrease more drastically due to the solid-state of both 
compounds.  
Further work on optimizing the ion exchange is the most important for the continued work on Li-
β’’-Gallate. With an optimized ion-exchange reaction, it would be possible to start exploring the 
ionic conductivity of Li-β’’-Gallate. Initial work towards this could be experimented on with Na-β’’-
Gallate. Initial studies were very limited in this project and found that using stainless steel or 
carbon on aluminum foil electrodes with pressed pellets. The impedance was almost vertical, like a 
capacitor in the Nyquist plots. The next step would include testing electrodes such as glassy 
carbon under pressure as done by Armstrong et al.xxxi on powdered Na-β-Alumina.

Discussion 
It is observed that the peaks in figure 2a are non-symmetrical, with more of the peak on the left 
side of the peak. Two proposed reasons for this are. First, due to the low angles of reflection, a 
substantial amount of background had to be removed. The peaks observed in this region and the 
curved shape of the background mean that the peak shape will change significantly when 
removing the background. This is supported by the observations that the estimated peak top shifts 
to a lower angle when the background is removed. These are strong indications that it is hard to 
get good symmetrical peaks when setting and removing the background. 
Second, upon removal of the solvent between the layers, there is a lower limit for the unit cell, 
indicated by a maximum reflection angle. The opposite is not guaranteed. Upon the drying 
process, some layers might still have some solvent left. This would be indicated by the tail towards 
lower angles, as observed in figure 2a for all samples. Furthermore, the samples are exposed to air 
before measurements are taken, and moisture absorption might occur. Zhi Lang et Al.xxxiv found 
that to obtain a fully dried bentonite sample, at least one hour at 150-200°C in a vacuum oven or 
12 hours at 200°C in a regular drying oven was needed. Based on this, the samples compared here 
should be dry; therefore, the tail may be caused predominantly by the first reason or another 
reason. 
Figure 2A shows that synthesis 1 and dried bentonite have very similar d spacing, while for 
synthesis 2 and 3, the d spacing is slightly larger. To better understand this, we must understand 
the drying process and ion exchange. To do this, an XRD pattern was recorded after each drying 
step to compare the impact of the drying and to compare structural information. Figure 19

The formation of large sheets and nanosheets is a disadvantage when trying to manufacture a 
pure electrolyte. This is due to the poor conductivity perpendicular to the conductive plane. 
Unless the sheets are ordered perpendicular to the electrodes, the ions would have to travel along 
with them before moving towards the electrodes. This would drastically increase the conductive 
pathway. In a composite electrolyte, this would be less of a problem as the surface of the sheets 
would be negatively charged and, in theory, enable easier transport between other particles in the 
electrolyte. In the end, the ion transport pathway would depend on several factors, including 
conductivity of the different phases, pathway length, and the interphases between particles, to 
name a few.

Outlook 
There are many different avenues of interest to explore to further the research into Li-bentonite 
clay as a candidate for the electrolyte of an LSSB, whether as a pure electrolyte or a composite 
electrolyte. 
Firstly, more work must be done to explore and optimize the synthesis. There are reasonable 
indications that the synthesis carried have been successful. Still, there are also several indications 
of the opposite. Therefore, is it crucial that the synthesis approach is further optimized and made 
reproducible, which has not been successful in this study. This includes optimizing the reaction 
==PAGE:38==
Page 34 of 42 
 
conditions, including time and reaction ratio. Experimenting with the temperature would also be 
an option. The last key thing that should be explored is how the ion exchange would go if carried 
out on the slurry formed upon centrifuging the product instead of on powder. 
It would be interesting to explore whether a technique such as ICP-AES or similar that can detect 
lithium could determine the degree of ion exchange, as EDX cannot be used as in the other section 
for this. In this study, conductivity was measured on a composite electrolyte with PEO. The 
problem with this is that it is unknown how the bentonite contributes to the overall conductivity. 
So, to best investigate the material, a way to measure the ionic conductivity of pure Li-Bentonite 
needs to be found. This can include sintering or on pure powder. 
Li-Montmorillonite has been tested as a separator for Li-S batteries to avoid dendrite formation 
and has proven to improve the overall performance.xxxviii It is known that certain clay materials 
conduct ions better when hydrated. This is supported by the larger interlayer distance and solvent 
molecules. Based on this testing Li-Bentonite, which is in a family with Li-Montmorillonite, pellets 
with a small amount of a LIB liquid electrolyte added in might yield promising results and help with 
dendrite formation. This is outside the goal of this study to eliminate liquid electrolytes but might 
prove helpful as a steppingstone until the material is better tested. 
For further studying the drying process, Thermogravimetric analysis could be used to study the 
losses as the powder dries.

Summary 
The work in this project ended up being limited to synthesizing the three candidates chosen for 
the composite electrolyte as it proved harder to achieve a good conversion on the two β-phase 
materials and to determine the conversion for the bentonite clay. For all candidates, the work 
approach was well designed based on the available literature, and logical steps were taken to 
optimize the syntheses and ion exchange towards a clean product and an effective conversion. 
Different variables were tuned to understand how they affected the product. 
All three candidates were studied using powder XRD to understand and observe any phase 
changes during the ion exchange experiments. Finding together with EDX that no phase changes 
happen during ion exchange, but changes in the unit cells are observed as the ion size changes. 
Powder XRD was used to identify and understand the hydration of the different products, how Na-
β’’-gallate underwent a phase change, and bentonite clay underwent large unit cell changes upon 
hydration. 
Based on this study, all three candidates have potential, and there are plenty of directions for each 
material to try and improve the investigation results. Determining which is the better candidate is, 
on the one hand, difficult due to the different amount of work carried out on each candidate, but 
what is more manageable is to rank the three candidates in terms of future potential. β-Alumina is 
the most explored material both in literature and in this study. Within the original scope of this 
study, β-Alumina was fully explored. A couple of new ideas have come up, and there is still a 
couple of untested approaches from the literature. Because of this, it is ranked as the lowest 
priority. β-Gallate was the least explored material in the stud; the work is a proof of concept and 
proved relatively well that an ion exchange could be done, but there is still some work to do to 
explore its possibilities, but it is promising. β-Gallate is given the middle priority as there is 
potential, but it might also behave like β-alumina and have a limit for conversion. The highest 
priority should be given to bentonite. The material is completely untested in terms of lithium 
conductivity. Initial tests in this study have laid the groundwork for the ion exchange and showed 
the importance of the drying process. The bentonite clay is easy to work with, and several ways to 
tune the ion exchange are just waiting to be explored. 
When working with the tested materials, they were found to be relatively soft when used in a 
mortar and pestle. This is good as the idea behind using the materials is that they could work as a 
malleable glue between the LLZO grains in the composite electrolyte.

Conclusion 
The project failed to create and test an inorganic/inorganic composite electrolyte for an all-solid-
state battery. This was because none of the second candidates for the composite electrolyte could 
be synthesized with 100% conversion. For β-alumina, a conversion of 56% was obtained using 
lithium chloride and 63.7% using lithium nitrate. It was found that at higher conversions and long 
ion exchange time, the β-phase decomposed to a spinel phase. For β-gallate, a conversion of 68% 
was achieved, while a high standard deviation was found due to overlapping EDX signals. The fact 
that only one exchange was carried out this result is promising for future studies. Li-bentonite is 
also found to be a promising candidate. While the exact degree of ion exchange was not found, 
the XRD patterns indicate promising changes. Impedance measurements on pellets made from the 
β-alumina powders were found to be problematic, and no reliable results were produced, despite 
testing several different blocking electrodes and manufacturing methods.
