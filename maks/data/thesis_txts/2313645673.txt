Abstract
The possibility of improving the current design of the Izmit Bay Bridge has been investigated
with focus on sandwich structures. The currently used orthotropic steel decks involve a lot
of welding and include many critical details whereby a cheaper and more simple design is
preferable. It is chosen to study the eﬀects of replacing the current orthotropic deck design
with a Sandwich Panel System (SPS).
Inspired from its use in retroﬁtting, the Sandwich Panel System has been found promising for
bridge deck applications. The SPS consists of a thick, low strength core layer surrounded by
two thin face plates with a higher stiﬀness. A light-weight material is typically used for the
core. Hereby the distance between the face plates may be increased to obtain a larger bending
stiﬀness while the selfweight of the system only increases slightly.
Detailed information about the Izmit Bay Bridge has been received by COWI and used in a
previous master’s thesis. Initially, requirements were set to the core material. After discussing
the beneﬁts and drawbacks of the diﬀerent options, it was chosen to proceed with plywood as a
core material. A ﬁnite element model of the sandwich structure was built using continuum shells
in Abaqus to evaluate the response when the SPS is subjected to wheel loads. With reference
to the current design, larger deﬂections were present while the curvature of the structure was
reduced.
A ﬁnite element model of a SPS beam was made. The beam was tested and compared to
analytical results. The analytical results showed good agreement with the numerical model.
Yet problems were registered when calculating the distribution of shear stresses along the beam
height as no shear stresses were present in the face plates.
The experimental tests reﬂected the previously modelled SPS beams. The response of the beams
showed large diﬀerences when compared to the analytical and numerical models, respectively.
Hence the SPS beams in the experiments were found a lot more ﬂexible than desired. By
inspection of the beams it was noticed that the adhesive had not been smeared out as intended.
Using Digital Image Correlation it was seen that the layers of the SPS beam did not behave as
expected. It was therefore assessed that the adhesive procedure was to blame.
The ﬁnite element model of the SPS beam was modiﬁed and adhesive layers were implemented.
The inﬂuence of the thickness as well as the distribution of the adhesive were investigated using
three diﬀerent Young’s moduli. It was seen that the adhesive layers had a huge impact on the
stiﬀness of the model. The most promising results were found for thin bond line thicknesses
while adhesive layers distributed with a spacing led to more ﬂexible beams.

From the investigations it was demonstrated that the adhesive had not been smeared out in
the best possible way. Thin, evenly distributed adhesive layers were implemented in the ﬁnite
element model of the sandwich structure to achieve a more realistic model. A signiﬁcant increase
was seen for both the maximum found deﬂection and the curvature, respectively. Hence it is
concluded that extra care needs to be taken when bonding the layers of the sandwich structure.
However, the SPS design is not rejected. Yet a lot of additional investigations still need to be
performed in order to approve the sandwich structure for future bridge designs.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Description of Bridge Project
The Izmit Bay Bridge (IBB) is a long span bridge which uses one of the most optimized
orthotropic steel decks. The bridge is located close to Izmit in Turkey at the eastern end of the
Sea of Marmara, 50 km from Istanbul. The bridge is currently under construction. Once built
in 2017, the bridge will be one of the longest suspension bridges in the world.
The bridge project has two main concerns. The construction period lasts for approx. 3 years
only. To compare with the Yi Sun-sin Bridge, completed in 2012, had a construction period of
approx. 5 years. The Yi Sun-sin Bridge is located in South Korea and is a suspension bridge
with a main span of 1546 m which currently is the 4th longest main span in the world.
Another challenging part for the Izmit Bay Bridge is that the bridge will be built on one of the
most seismically spots in the world. The bridge will be located at the boundary between the
northern Eurasian plate and the southern Anatolian plate.
The total length of the bridge is approx. 3.3 km. The longest span, the main span, is 1550
m and can be found between its two pylons. The side spans on each side of the pylons have
a length of 566 m. Each pylon has a height of approx. 240 m. The motorway crossing the
bridge (see front page) will contain 3 traﬃc lanes in each direction. The client is the state of
Turkey, the contractor is IHI Corporation, the architect is Dissing+Weitling architecture and
the bridge designer is COWI.
1.2
Orthotropic Steel Decks
The orthotropic steel decks used for the Izmit Bay Bridge have been used for long span bridges
since the German engineer Dr. Cornelis of MAN Corporation was given patent in 1948. The
orthotropic deck is characterized by a structural steel deck plate which is stiﬀened in either
its longitudinal, transverse or both directions. As a result of the stiﬀening the bridge deck
is capable of carrying loads from vehicles while the overall load capacity of the structure is
improved. Due to the fact that the longitudinal stiﬀness vary from the transverse stiﬀness, the
bridge deck is called orthotropic.
The stiﬀening elements may contribute to several functions. The use of stiﬀeners leads to an
increased cross-sectional area which results in an increased bending and buckling resistance,
1
==PAGE:16==
s112851

respectively. Hereby the plate is able to carry larger local wheel loads while the loads are still
properly transferred to the main girders of the bridge. Identical structural eﬀects are found
using concrete slabs in composite girder bridges. Even though the concrete slabs may be cast
on site, their weight are also considerably larger compared to orthotropic steel decks. The
larger self-weight of the composite girder bridges leads to shorter spans of the bridges which in
general is more costly.
The use of orthotropic steel decks does also lead to a number of challenges which need to
be taken into account. The steel decks typically need to be prefabricated rather than assem-
bled on site.
Orthotropic steel decks also require a large amount of welding which is very
time-consuming compared to casting concrete decks. Cut-outs also have to be made in the
diaphragms. Fatigue is typically the limiting state when investigating several details in the
steel deck. Considerable local deformations are seen in the top deck plate due to wheel loads.
Detailed investigations with focus on fatigue in the steel decks have been in a previous master’s
thesis.
The fabrication cost of the orthotropic deck segments is the biggest expense when looking at
the cost of the whole construction phase of the steel decks. Therefore it may be of interest
to lower the amount of steel used in the deck. A lower amount of steel in the deck leads to a
lower dead load which aﬀects the whole bridge system. A rule of thumb says that when 2 kg
of steel are used in the deck, 1 kg of steel has to be used in the cables. A lighter design of the
orthotropic decks will therefore result in smaller cables and hangers.
1.3
Scope of Thesis
This master project deals with an innovative approach to the bridge deck design of the Izmit
Bay Bridge. Using information about the bridge geometry an alternative sandwich structure is
used as bridge deck design. The sandwich structure is initially tested using a numerical model
in the Finite Element Method (FEM) program Abaqus. The performance of the sandwich
design is then tested in the experimental hall at DTU. The experimental data is collected
and processed to check if the numerical models ﬁt the actual behaviour of the design.
In
the software Aramis Digital Image Correlation (DIC) is used to measure and evaluate the
deformations on the surface of the test specimens. Both models and experiments are veriﬁed
analytically throughout the project. Finally the potential for further analysis is evaluated and
it is assessed whether the alternative design is realistic.

2.1
Geometry of the Bridge
To perform an innovative approach to the bridge deck design, the current design of the Izmit
Bay Bridge has to be known. The geometry of the suspension bridge is therefore described and
sketched in this section.
A side-view of the Izmit Bay Bridge can be seen in Figure 2.1. The sketch shows the ﬁnal state
of the bridge where the dead load is acting. To achieve this state, hangers and cables have to
be pre-tensioned in order to compensate for the deﬂection which takes place when the dead
load acts on the bridge. The magnitudes of the pre-tensioning have to be found through an
iterative process which has been done in a previous master’s thesis [1].
The main span is 1550 m while the side spans have an equal magnitude of 566 m. The height
of each pylon is 240 m. The pylons and girders of the bridge are made of steel while the anchor
blocks and foundations are made of concrete.

around the main span. The bridge girder consists of segments. The segments have a length
of 25 m but the geometry varies. The diﬀerent geometry leads to a variation of the stiﬀness
in the longitudinal direction. At the southern end of the side span in Figure 2.2 a transition
point is located. An expansion joint is installed in this point to allow the girder of the bridge
to contract and expand freely when the temperature changes. At each expansion joint, the

Chapter 3
Alternative Design
The basis for the project is improve the current design of the top deck plate with focus on
weight reduction. In this chapter alternative solutions to the currently used orthotropic deck
design are presented. The development of the solutions are discussed to evaluate whether the
designs are feasible solutions to work with.
3.1
Alternative Solutions
As stated earlier a lighter design of the bridge deck leads to signiﬁcant cost reductions due
to the fact that less steel is used in both deck, cables and hangers. The use of continuous
ribs in the deck requires a lot of welding which is expensive. As an example, the total cost of
the main bridge deck of the Great Belt Bridge would be 1100 million DKK today, using the
price development for steel structures from Dansk Statistik [3]. The assembling of the panels
to deck segments represents 27 % of this cost, close to 300 million DKK. This huge expense
is mainly a result of the many complicated welds. Especially the deck-to-rib connections of a
closed trapezoidal is critical since these can only be welded from one side.
In Europe orthotropic decks are used to a large extent. The large use leads to decent fabri-
cation costs as a result of the great experience. In contrast to Europe, the experience with
use of orthotropic decks are much more limited in the United States. Therefore much fewer
manufacturers in the Unites States are willing to perform the complicated welds. Due to this
problem an eﬀort has been made in order to discover new manufacturing procedures which
ensures easier welds, including open ribs [4]. However, the large amount of welds needed will
still be expensive and it is therefore of interest to ﬁnd a design which reduces the amount of
welding.
The present design includes complicated detailing at the intersection between the ribs and di-
aphragms. These types of detailing are all prone to fatigue and need to have reduced stress
concentrations. The details have been investigated in a previous master’s thesis by Bjærre [5].
A system which is able to handle the above complications is a sandwich system. Honeycomb
Sandwich Panels were developed for the aircraft industry in the 1940s as a result of the strong
demand of a light-weight plate with an improved mechanical performance [6]. The idea of using
Honeycomb Sandwich Panels with a light core separating two strong face plates has been used
for a lot of purposes over the last 50 years.

The sandwich panel (SSP) has been developed from the orthotropic deck system. An example
of the SSP is shown in Figure 3.1. It is noted that the welds are made with laser and that
the core is a V-truss. The SSP is very similar to an orthotropic plate but the installation of
the bottom plate makes the SSP stiﬀer than the orthotropic plate. To maintain the same self-
weight, or even reduce it, the face plates have to be thin compared to the thickness of the core.
If the SSP principle is used for the Izmit Bay Bridge design, the rib-to-top connections will
be more critical. A possible solution to this problem would be to use laser welding techniques
since these have shown to be less vulnerable to fatigue [8]. Laser welding is not included in
the Eurocodes yet and a lot of testing still has to be done in order to rely on its performance.
Another issue using the SSP design is that the reduced cross-sectional area of the top plate
leads to a lower stiﬀness. The lower stiﬀness causes larger deﬂections between the ribs which
increases the chance of cracking of the surfacing [4].

The Sandwich Panel System (SPS) is another type of sandwich systems which is also developed
for heavier loadings. The design was originally developed for structures which demanded high

is shown in Figure 3.2. The SPS consists of two thin face plates made of steel and a continuous
core made of polyurethane elastomer. At ﬁrst, the SPS was used on ships to repair impact-
damaged cargo spaces in bulk carriers and to strengthen vehicle decks on ro-ro ferries. Later the
SPS system was also used for the design of new ships and internal research found the properties
of the system promising for bridges [11].

Since the use of polyurethane is still very new, the knowledge of its long-term structural be-

The SPS design provides a simple way of increasing the bending resistance by increasing the
thickness of the core layer. The increase of the layer thickness brings disadvantages in terms
of a larger self-weight of the system as well as larger shear deformations in the core take
place. It is therefore necessary to ﬁnd a material with enhanced characteristics compared to
the polyurethane elastomer. Using investigations made in a previous master’s thesis by Bjærre
[5], the core layer has to fulﬁl the following criteria:
 The material needs a low density, about 1000 kg/m3.
 The Young’s modulus of the core needs to be approximately 10 GPa in order to ensure
proper shear deformations.
In reality the cost of the core material needs to be taken into account as well. Since the study
is solely investigating alternative design options, the price is of no concern.
A possible core material for sandwich elements, which meets the above requirements, is alu-
minium foams. Aluminium foams have a large gas-ﬁlled volume whereby the processed material
can be categorized as a lightweight material. The foams are divided into two groups: closed-cell
and open-cell foams, respectively. The pores in the closed cell foams are sealed while the pores
in the open-celled foams form an interconnected network. The density of the foams depend on
their porosity. Typically the density lies between 400-900 kg/m3. Open-celled aluminium foams
with 70 % porosity is reported to have a Young’s modulus of 5 GPa [16] while closed-celled
foams have a Young’s modulus of 10 GPa [17, 18]. The bonding between aluminium foams
and face plates made of steel is poor compared to the polyurethane and other options should
therefore be considered.
Glass-reinforced plastic (ﬁberglass) is a composite material which has experienced a huge de-
velopment over the past decade. The beneﬁt of using composite materials is that they can
easily be modiﬁed for speciﬁc purposes. The ﬁberglass is used in many industries today, e.g.
automobiles, windmills, boats, aircraft and lately power pylons [19, 20]. The Young’s modulus
of ﬁberglass is approx. 8 GPa and its density is about 1500 kg/m3 which is slightly higher than
requested.
Natural ﬁber reinforced polypropylene composites have also been considered. This type of ma-
terial is both cheaper and lighter. Fibers such as wood, bamboo and kenaf are used. In the
coming years the demands for these natural ﬁbers are expected to grow by 50 % annually [21].
The natural ﬁber which is expected most success is kenaf which in Malaysia is regarded as an
industrial crop. Figure 4.1 shows the kenaf plantation and ﬁbre. The kenaf is compliant to a
number of soils and usually only needs some fertilizer and a single herbicide treatment to grow
eﬃciently. Composites containing kenaf ﬁbers have a Young’s modulus of 11-14.5 GPa and a
density of 350-550 kg/m3 [22, 23]. Composites with kenaf can be used for numerical models
since its characteristics match the demands for the core material. Yet the composites may be
diﬃcult to order for experimental research and a material with higher availability should be
found.

5.3
Prediction of Failure in SPS Beam
In order to conduct the experiments in the best possible way it is chosen to ﬁnd the point at
which the beam reaches its limit. In this case, the limitations of the face plates are given at the
point where the steel reaches a von Mises stress equal to its yield strength, σy. The limitations
of the core layer are given at the point where the principal stresses in the plywood reaches its
tensile strength, ft, or its compressive strength, fc.
The three strengths above depend on the material which is ordered material. By ordering steel
of type S235JR a yield strength of σy = 235 MPa is guaranteed. Yet the strength of the steel
might be higher than ordered. A test of steel S235JR has been performed by Dick-Nielsen [30].
The results are shown in Appendix M where it is noticed that the ordered steel had a yield
strength of σy = 290 MPa, 23.4 % higher than its guaranteed strength. Based on Dick-Nielsen’s
found yield strength it is decided to use steel S235JR for the preceding experiments.
The tensile and compressive strength of plywood, respectively, depend on its thickness. By
use of the Finnish Plywood Handbook [25] birch plywood with a thickness of 50 mm has an
average tensile strength ft = 37.5 MPa while its average compressive strength is fc = 26 MPa.
It is chosen to use these values for testing of the ﬁnite element model.

Chapter 6
Experimental Testing
It is now of interest to test the structural behaviour of the SPS beam design presented in
Chapter 5. The present chapter therefore deals with experimental testing of the SPS beam to
see if the analytical and numerical solutions, respectively, match the actual beam behaviour.
At ﬁrst the experimental procedure and setup is described. Then an initial test is made in
order to check that the intended experiment performs as intended. Minor modiﬁcations are
then made to achieve a better performance and three beams are tested. The results of the
tests are processed and compared using the two softwares Microsoft Excel and ARAMIS. The
results are discussed and the ﬁnite element model of the SPS beam from Chapter 5 is modiﬁed
by including adhesive layers in order to reach a response equivalent to the experimental results.
Finally adhesive layers are also included in the ﬁnite element model of the sandwich plate from
Chapter 4 to check its response when perfect bonding between the layers is no longer present.
6.1
Test Program and Steps
In order to conduct the SPS beam experiment a large amount of preparation needs to be done.
The preparation may be divided into four phases. In Phase 1 the materials are ordered. In
Phase 2 the SPS beams are assembled. Phase 3 deals with ﬁnding a test machine which suits
the experimental setup. Finally, Phase 4 deals with the mounting of measuring devices.
In Appendix N the complete procedure of the four phases is described in detail. The description
of the phases includes the work, considerations and encountered problems which have been met
in order to achieve the best possible performance of the experiment.
6.2
Initial Test of Experimental Setup
As the setup of the machine has been done, an attempt is made in order to see if the experiment
runs as expected. For the Instron 6025 the software Merlin is used to control the piston. The
direction in which the piston runs is deﬁned while stop criterias are speciﬁed. It is chosen to
deﬁne a displacement rate of the testing. The rate is of great importance since a too largely
chosen rate leads to a higher capacity of the SPS beam. Based on the fact that the beam should
deﬂect 11.03 mm before yielding occurs, see Table 5.3, a load rate of 2.2 mm/min is deﬁned in
Merlin. Hereby each beam test should last 5 minutes before stopped.

6.5
Numerical SPS Beam with Adhesive Layer
The conducted experiments led to SPS beam responses much lower than expected. It is therefore
of interest to study the inﬂuence on the load response when an adhesive is included in the
numerical model from Chapter 5.
To include the three adhesive layers of the SPS beam in the numerical model the material
properties needs to be known. As the properties of the adhesive are not dependent on the
direction, the adhesive can be categorized as an isotropic material. Hence a Young’s modulus
and Poisson’s ratio need to be found. Using the product catalogue for Montage Ekstra 292 [31]
it is seen that its hardness is approximately 60 Shore A. The hardness of a material may be
described using a Durometer scale. The ASTM D2240-00 testing standard includes 12 diﬀerent
scales with type A and D being the most commonly used scales. The type of scale depends
on the use of the material. Within each scale a value between 0 and 100 is found. Here, high
values indicate a harder material. The relation between the hardness and the Young’s modulus
of an elastomer has been described by Gent [32] as:
E = 0.0981 (56 + 7.62336 · S)
0.137505 (254 −2.54 · S)
(6.1)
In the above equation S is the ASTM D2240 type A hardness while E is the Young’s modulus
in MPa. As the adhesive used for the experiments is a type A with a hardness of 60, the
Young’s modulus of the material is found to 3.61 MPa.
The Poisson’s ratio of the adhesive is not deﬁned in the product catalogue. From Cease et al.
[33] it is found that typical construction adhesives lie in the range of 0.358 to 0.433. Hence a
Poisson’s ratio of 0.40 is used in the investigation.
The material properties of the the adhesive layers are now known.
In order to model the
adhesive layer, the thickness of each layer must be found. A caliper is therefore used on the
untouched SPS beams to measure their total thickness. After several measurements the average
total thickness of the SPS beams was found to 77.11 mm. By subtracting the two core layers
as well as the two face plate thicknesses a remaining total thickness of the adhesive layers is
found to 1.11 mm. It is assumed that each layer is uniform and has an equal thickness whereby
the average thickness of an adhesive layer is found to 0.37 mm.
Three adhesive layers are now implemented in the numerical model of the SPS beam from
Chapter 5. Abaqus only permits the use of a single element over the layer height due to the
fact that the layer thickness is so thin. Since the response of the SPS beam is of interest only,
a pressure of 400 kPa is applied to the model corresponding to a point load of 15.2 kN.
Four tests are made in total. In the ﬁrst three models uniformly distributed adhesive layers
are used. The used adhesive layer thickness of the three models is 0.37, 1.00 and 0.10 mm,
respectively.
The fourth model uses a layer thickness of 0.37 mm.
Instead of a uniformly
distributed layer, small layers with surface areas of 100×50 mm2 are spread out evenly across
the surfaces with intervals of 50 mm. Hereby half of the adhesive is removed from the model.
In each of the four models, the response is found using the following Young’s moduli: 2 MPa,
3.61 MPa and 5 MPa. The results are shown in Figure 6.15 and Figure 6.16.

Figure 6.15a shows the situation where the currently used adhesive thickness is smeared out
across the entire surfaces of the SPS beam. It is seen that this leads to a considerably larger
stiﬀness, approximately 4.5 times lower than the analytical solution. As expected, the use of
a lower Young’s modulus results in a reduced stiﬀness while a larger chosen Young’s modulus
increases the stiﬀness of the beam.
Figure 6.15b shows case where a thick adhesive layer is used. This results in a reduced stiﬀness
response of the SPS beam as the larger shear deformations in the adhesive will lead to increased
sliding between the layers. It is seen that the response is almost equal to the experiment, 7.5
times lower than the analytical solution.
Using a layer thickness of 0.10 mm the overall response is improved, see Figure 6.16a. As
much smaller shear deformations are present in the adhesive now, the sliding between the parts
is reduced. The SPS beam now has a much stiﬀer behaviour, closer to the perfectly bonded
interfaces. The stiﬀness is now only 3 times smaller than the analytical solution. It is seen
that only a slight increase of stiﬀness is present when a larger Young’s modulus is chosen. In
Figure 6.16b the case where a spaced distribution of the layers is seen. It is seen that the
response now is closer to the experimental results compared to Figure 6.15a. This makes good
sense since a smaller bonding area is used whereby the SPS beam has an increased ﬂexibility.
The stiﬀness is now more than 6.5 times smaller than the analytical solution.
Based on the numerical results it is seen that a uniformly distributed layer with a thickness of
0.10 mm provides the best response. It is noticed that the stiﬀness of the SPS beam increases
as the layer becomes thinner. It is found that the most commonly used bond line thicknesses lie
in the interval 0.002 to 0.007 inches (0.05-0.175 mm). Since Abaqus isn’t able to provide proper
results using these thicknesses it has been chosen to test the sandwich plate from Chapter 4 by
implementing adhesive layers with thicknesses of 0.10 mm.

Adhesive layers are now implemented in the sandwich plate from Chapter 4. In contrast to the
previous tests, two adhesive layers are now needed for the sandwich plate as a single layer is
used for the core. Each adhesive layer is modelled using a thickness of 0.10 mm. A Young’s
modulus of 3.61 MPa and a Poisson’s ratio of 0.40 are used as material properties. An contour
plot showing the deﬂection of the upper face plate is seen in Figure 6.17a. It is seen that
the maximum deﬂection still is located below Vehicle 5A-H which was applied between the
supports at the center of the plate. The size of the maximum deﬂection is now found to 2.75
mm. In the sandwich plate model without adhesive layers, a maximum deﬂection of 2.25 mm
was found. Hence the application of two adhesive layers in the SPS design leads to a 22.3 %
larger maximum deﬂection.
The maximum curvature of the upper face plate is found to -1.14×10−2 rad−1. In Chapter 4
the maximum found curvature was -9.6×10−3 rad−1. Hereby the curvature has increased by
18.8 %.

The modelling of an underlying truss system is not within the scope of this thesis. Preceding

the possibilities of the current SPS design.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Based on the currently used orthotropic deck of Izmit Bay Bridge, an alternative deck design
has been proposed. The alternative solution has focus on being both cheaper and lighter than
the present structure.
The FEM software Abaqus has been used to implement the idea of a Sandwich Panel System
(SPS). The choice of a suitable core material has been discussed. It was decided to use plywood
as core material as the weight and stiﬀness were found adequate. With reference to the current
parameters of Izmit Bay Bridge, a numerical model has been made where perfect bonding
between the layers of the SPS design was used. Maximum values of the deﬂection and curvature,
respectively, are found for the top face plate and compared to the current design. The maximum
curvature was found smaller than for the current design. On the other hand the maximum
deﬂection of the top face plate was found almost twice as large as seen in the current design.
These values have space for improvements as an underlying system has not been included in
the Abaqus model.
With reference to the numerical results, experiments have been conducted in order to test the
performance of the design in reality. Analytical and numerical results have been derived in
order to get an idea of the response. SPS beams have been assembled using a construction
adhesive to bond the layers. By subjecting the beams to 3-point bending the response of the
design is evaluated. Unfortunately, the capacities of the SPS beams were not reached. As a
result of the bonding procedure the beams became very ﬂexible. Since all beams were assembled
before the experiments, other bonding procedures could not be tested. During the tests data
various deﬂections were gathered from displacement devices as a function of the load signal.
Image series were also gathered using a camera. The data of the experiments was exported and
processed in order to analyse the SPS design.
The response of the tested SPS beams was determined using the midpoint displacement and
load signal. It was seen that the stiﬀness of the tested beams was approximately 10 times smaller
than the analytically and numerically found stiﬀnesses. Using Digital Image Correlation the
beams were analysed in ARAMIS. The stresses found in ARAMIS were larger compared to the
analytical solution. Large variations were seen in the strain results and it is assessed that an
additional in depth investigation has to be done in order to trust the program completely. In
ARAMIS is was also seen that the four layers of the SPS beam acted independently as a result
of the bonding procedure.

==PAGE:76==

CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
Numerical investigations were then made in Abaqus to study the inﬂuence of the adhesive
layer. Variations were made of the adhesive thickness and distribution across the surface. All
calculations were performed for three Young’s moduli. The stiﬀness of the SPS beam was found
lowest for thick adhesive layers while a spaced distribution of the adhesive also had a negative
inﬂuence on the response.
Based on the found results the sandwich deck structure was tested again. By including adhesive
layers with thicknesses of 0.10 mm, the maximum deﬂection and curvature, respectively, were
found again. It was now seen that the implementation of the adhesive layers led to larger
deﬂections and curvatures.
Based on the investigations it is concluded that the adhesive layers need to be paid huge
attention when used for the SPS design. It is believed that the SPS design is possible future
solution due to the simplicity of the design. Compared to the present design, critical details
and welding problems are avoided. The SPS design may also be standardized so that only one
connection piece is used. Yet many investigations still need to be performed before the design
may be approved for bridge design.

Chapter 8
Further Work
As the period of the master’s thesis was limited, some work has been left unanswered. Initially,
the choice of other core materials could be investigated. The demands of kenaf are expect to
grow 50 % per year and it might be interesting to test this type of ﬁber in SPS conﬁgurations.
In Chapter 4 a numerical model of the sandwich plate was made. Problems were met when
the Poisson’s ratios of the plywood were deﬁned whereby this still has to be solved in order
to attain a more accurate structural behaviour of the design. The system supporting the SPS
still hasn’t been modelled. Investigations of the underlying system could be made to study the
response of the plate when better boundary conditions are used.
The numerical distribution of shear forces along the height of the SPS beam showed that no
shear stresses were present in the face plate layers when continuum shell elements were used. A
solution to this problem has to be found in order for the numerical model to work as intended.
It is assessed that a solution will lead to a response closer to the analytical results.
Based on the load conﬁguration of the plate, it was decided to conduct the experiments as
a 3-point bending test with vertical supports. As negative moments were also present, see
Figure 5.4, future experiments might be conducted using clamped supports.
The camera problems led to a modiﬁed experimental setup. The modiﬁed setup was very time
consuming as it involved assistance from several busy staﬀmembers of the experimental hall.
Unfortunately, this led to a lot of waiting time which isn’t visible in the thesis.
In order to reduce the time spent in the experimental hall the largest test machine can be used
by changing the load cell. The machine has four feet, one in each corner, whereby the analysed
surface area is visible without having to transfer some of the load to the support.
If the experimental process was repeated, it would be interesting to study the response of the
SPS beam if the adhesive was smeared out as intended. Using a thin bond line thickness would
lead to smaller deformations in the adhesive, closer to the perfect bonding solution.
The analysis of the results in ARAMIS made room for improvements to the model.
The
deﬂections were consistent with the response of the SPS beams. Yet some of the probed values
in the model, e.g. the strains in the X-direction, was incorrect. Future investigations could
therefore be made where the use of smaller facet sizes could be studied too. It was intended
to spend more time in ARAMIS during the project period. Unfortunately, DTU only oﬀers 8
available licenses whereby the spent time was limited.

APPENDIX E. SPS DESIGN - SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS
Description of analysis
Computational analysis set large requirements to the PC memory and can also be very time
consuming when very reﬁned meshes are used. In this model it is therefore chosen to use a
small amount of elements for the support plates as well as the vehicular part. Furthermore it
is chosen to constantly use 4 elements over the height of the core layer while 3 elements are
used over the height of each face plate layer. The edges of the three SPS layers are seeded
using a double bias. Hereby the mesh becomes coarse at the edges and grows ﬁner towards
the center of the plate.
The analysis is performed using a C3D8 and a SC8R element, respectively.
The C3D8 element is a 3D stress, eight-node linear brick element. One of the main drawbacks
using this element is that it tends to be too stiﬀin bending. This behaviour is called shear
locking and is a result of the linear nature of quadrilateral elements. Due to the fact that the
element is linear, it isn’t able to model accurate curvatures under bending and shear stresses
are therefore introduced. This extra stress contribution causes the element to reach an
equilibrium state at a smaller displacement whereby the element behave stiﬀer than it
actually is. It is therefore not optimal to use C3D8 elements for slender beams and thin plates
which is the case in this model.
The SC8R element is an eight-node hexahedron continuum shell with reduced integration.
These elements are preferred for plate structures since they perform well at handling large
shear deformations. Elements with reduced integration are usually undesirable since they use
fewer Gaussian coordinates when solving integrals. This leads to a shorter calculation time
but at the cost of a reduced accuracy.
The sensitivity analysis is made by controlling the minimum size of the double bias. The
maximum size is set to 0.5 m. The vehicle is placed between the diaphragms which will result
in the largest deﬂection of the plate. The results from the sensitivity analysis are listed in
Table E.1 while the maximum deﬂection of the plate as a function of the mesh size can be
seen in Figure E.1.

APPENDIX N. TEST PROGRAM AND STEPS: PHASE 1-4
N.1
Phase 1
The materials needed for the experiment should match the geometric dimensions which were
used for the SPS beam in Chapter 5. Each beam consists of two face plates made of steel as
well as a single core made of plywood. The geometric dimensions of each face plate are
3000×100×8 mm while the geometric dimensions of the core are 3000×100×60 mm. Based on
the results in Section 6.3 it is chosen to use steel S23JR for the face plates, assuming that its
yield stress is 290 MPa.
Plywood is typically manufactured as plates where standard sizes with thicknesses in the
range of 3-40 mm. To avoid an increased delivery time it is chosen to order a birch plywood
plate with geometric dimensions of 3050×1525×30 mm. In the experimental hall this plate is
then cut into 14 beams with dimensions of 3000×100×30 mm. The plywood beams may then
be stacked on top of each other in pairs of two to achieve a core thickness of 60 mm. It is
noted that the newly used thickness of the plywood doesn’t aﬀect its average strength
according to the Finnish Plywood Handbook [25].
The 14 beams of plywood make it possible to assemble 7 SPS beams, whereby 14 face plates
are ordered as well.
The interfaces of the SPS beam must be connected properly in order to assemble the four
parts. As the beam deﬂects shear stresses will arise in the interfaces of the beam. The chosen
connection type must therefore be able to withstand these stresses to hold the SPS beam
together. Using bolts at each beam end should restrain the layers from sliding parallel to each
other. Yet some drawbacks are found using this solution since large bolts lead to the beam
losing ﬂexibility. If the bolts are chosen too small they might not be able to transfer the load.
Even though bolts might connect the four layers, they are not able to hold the entire surface
area in place.
One of the three layers connects two plywood beams. It has been considered to assemble
these layers using nails. Yet it is diﬃcult to determine the amount of nails needed while the
center of the interface is hard to reach. Also the method doesn’t apply to the remaining two
steel/plywood interfaces.
Finally the idea of using an adhesive designed for structural parts is discussed. Construction
adhesives provide a strong, elastic bond which is able to transfer loads between structures.
Using an adhesive the entire surface area is able to be covered easily. Diﬀerent construction
adhesives have been studied to determine whether these adhesives are applicable for the SPS
design. Following a discussion with a technician from the Danish adhesive company Dana
Lim, the product ”Montage Ekstra 292” has been chosen. Montage Ekstra is a neutral
1-component construction adhesive available for both indoor and outdoor use. It is able to
work in temperatures between -40 °C to +90 °C. Montage Ekstra is applicable on many
surfaces, e.g. bricks, concrete, wood, glass and most metals. The tensile strength of the
adhesive is 3 MPa, slightly larger the maximum found shear stress of 2.96 MPa. A single 290
ml cartridge is able to cover a total surface area of 2-3 m2. Since a total surface area of 6.3 m2
needs to be covered, 3 cartridges must be ordered.

N.2
Phase 2
Once the materials are delivered to the experimental hall the SPS beams can be assembled.
At ﬁrst the plywood plate is cut into proper dimensions using a circular saw machine. Since
the plate has a thickness of 30 mm, each beam is only adjusted in its length and width.
The steel beams delivered are, as for the yield strength, delivered in a guaranteed length. By
measuring the exact length of the beams it is found that more than half of beams are longer
than desired. The beams are therefore cut in proper dimensions using a bandsaw machine.
The coolant from the process are then wiped oﬀthe beams while sharp corners are cut round.
In order to let the adhesive bond in the best possible way, the steel surfaces need to be as
clean as possible. The existing dirt is therefore removed using an angle grinder. Figure N.1
shows the beams before and after the dirt was removed.

Since the software ARAMIS is going to be used for the data processing, a camera will be

is used. The camera is in 2D and will focus on one of the sides of the beam as no deﬂections

whereby the program calculate the deformations in a certain area with reference to the ﬁrst
picture in the series. The beam is assumed to have a symmetric response on each side of its
midpoint. The area between the midpoint and right support is therefore of interest. For the
Digital Image Correlation to work, a pattern is sprayed over the area using black dots on a
white background as shown in Figure N.4.
When the pictures are inserted in ARAMIS, the program searches for these dots. As the dots
are registered, the program is able to calculate the displacements in the beam. It is seen in

N.3
Phase 3
The beams are now completely prepared for the experiment. The machine at which the tests
are conducted needs to be found. A machine which is able to apply a compressive load is of
most interest. A lot of the equipment in the experimental hall is able to handle this. Yet the
beam has diﬀerent capacities. The magnitude of the applied force therefore has to be found in
order to ﬁnd a suitable machine. This was done earlier in Table 5.3. Here, the maximum
applied pressure was found to 1024.8 kPa. By multiplying with the surface area the load
magnitude is found to 38.9 kN. Based on dialogues with several staﬀmembers it was decided
to use the Instron 6025 Universal Test Machine. The machine has a capacity of 100 kN and is
able to apply either a dragging force or a compressive force.
A supporting beam is mounted to the machine at the midpoint. The supporting beam is
carried by a large threaded rod at each end to transfer the applied load and to avoid the
machine from rotating by stabilizing the system. The supporting beam is also wide enough to
allow that the vertical supports can be placed directly on top of it. The supporting beam has
a length of 3 m whereby the distance from the midpoint to each threaded rod is 1.5 m.
The camera is placed perpendicular to the surface of interest, 1800 mm from the beam, to
achieve the best resolution with the lens. Hereby the camera distance is locked. The idea
behind the test setup is shown in Figure N.5.
In the top view it is seen that one ”feet” of the machine interferes with the camera view.
Even though the covered area is small it is of great importance since this is where the critical
stresses and largest deﬂection are found. Some of the covered area may be visualized by
moving the camera closer to the beam. Doing this requires a new lens which is undesirable.
Another option is to use 3D instead. This requires two cameras focusing on the surface from
two diﬀerent angles. As 3D leads to too included angles while advanced calibration
procedures are needed, it is chosen to ﬁnd another solution to the problem.
The idea of moving the beam and supports in the longitudinal direction is now discussed. By
moving the beam in the longitudinal direction, the entire surface area is visualized which is
desired. Yet the movement of the beam leads to an asymmetrical setup. The midpoint of the
SPS beam is no longer below the head of the piston. This can be ﬁxed by mounting a small
beam to the piston. At each end point of the small beam, cylinders are ﬁxed whereby the load
from the load cell of the machine is transferred to each cylinder. One of the cylinders is then
placed on top of the loading plate while the remaining cylinder transfers its load to the
support through a connection piece.
The change to the setup is fairly advanced. The connection piece has to be made speciﬁcally
for this experiment, see Appendix O for the geometry of the connection piece. The cylinders
have to be cut and threaded to match the connection piece. As the supports are now moved,
a 3 m support beam is no longer acceptable. A 4 m support beam is therefore needed.
Finally, it has to be checked whether the capacity of the machine is exceeded as it is now only
half of the load from the piston which is transferred to the SPS beam. As the required force
was found to 38.9 kN, a total force of 77.8 kN is now required from the machine. The Instron
6025 is therefore still suitable for the experiment. The modiﬁed experimental setup is shown
in Figure N.6.

N.4
Phase 4
Using the new experimental setup the remaining parts of the test are described. Until now
the surface area of the loading plate has been deﬁned only. The thickness of the plate is found
using symmetry assumptions. When the load is applied to the loading plate it will experience
a reaction from the SPS beam which may be considered a line load. The minimum plate
thickness is then found using Navier’s formula. From the calculation, performed in
Appendix P, the loading plate thickness is found to 25 mm. Since the bending stiﬀness of a
loading plate in steel might be too large, problems might occur when the beam starts to
curve. To ensure that the loading plate follows the curvature of the beam it is chosen to place
an additional loading plate in plywood below. The thickness of this plate is 30 mm and its
bending stiﬀness is considerably lower.
Even though ARAMIS is used to determine the deﬂections along the beam length it is chosen
to use displacement devices as well to verify the numerical results. The device, shown in
Figure N.7, is able to measure the displacement at a given location through changes in their
respective voltage output. The devices have to be mounted on barriers placed independently
of the experiment as the test machine, including the support beam, will deform during the
tests.
The small beam which distributes the load from the piston blocks the top of the beam. The
needles of the device therefore need to measure the deﬂection of the beam at another location
on the SPS beam. It has been decided to fasten small angle brackets to the side of the beam
whereby the devices will not conﬂict with the small beam. In Figure N.8 the mounting of
angle brackets is seen. A small hole has been drilled in each angle bracket so that the needles
don’t slide as the SPS beam rotates and deﬂects during the test.

APPENDIX R. ARAMIS - MODEL DESCRIPTION
Modelling procedure
New project:
Initially a new project is made in ARAMIS. The 2D box is ticked since pictures are taken
with a single camera. The model is then saved in a freely chosen directory.
Facets:
When creating a new project the size of the facets must be deﬁned. A facet in Aramis works
like an element in Abaqus since small facet sizes lead to improved results and increased
calculation times. The facets must not be chosen too large as facets, e.g. larger than the face
plate height will provide insuﬃcient results. By using a standard quadrangle facets with sizes
of 19×15 pixels it is checked that the mesh ﬁts the purpose.
Stage:
The pictures are now imported to the program. This is done by selecting the following:
”Stage”, ”New stage” and ”From image series”. The directory of the image series are now
found. It is ensured that the reference picture is the ﬁrst in the series, while the images are
numbered properly, e.g. 0045, 0046, 0046, etc. In the pop-up window the ﬁrst image is
chosen. The button ”Use all” is pressed in order to use the entire series.
Mask:
The series are now shown and referred to as stages. Stage 0 is the reference picture, while
Stage 1 is the 2nd picture of the imported series. Using the image list the diﬀerent images can
be seen. A mask is now deﬁned to tell the program what part of the image the analysis has to
focus on. Since the surface in this project is rectangular, a rectangle is drawn across the
surface. Hereby the program neglects the remaining part of the pictures in the calculations.
Start point:
Start points are now added to the model. In order to calculate correct displacements in the
beam this is necessary. A start point is therefore added at the midpoint of the beam as well
as at the support. If the ARAMIS analysis had to deal with concrete fracture, start points are
needed at each side of a crack. Hence this isn’t a problem in the the current project, two start
points are suitable for the analysis.
Compute project:
The calculations are now performed by pressing the button ”Compute”. Hereby ARAMIS
scans the pattern in each picture of the image series to register the movement of the dots.
When the calculation has ﬁnished, it is possible to analyse the beam at diﬀerent stages.
Contour plots can therefore be seen showing displacement and strain components,
respectively.
Point-to-point distance:
Since the distance between the start points hasn’t been deﬁned yet, the calculated
displacements are incorrect. The ”Point-to-point distance”-function is therefore used where
the linear distance between the two added start points is speciﬁed. In order for the results to
be correct, the calculation has to be repeated.
