Abstract 
In this studied the passive sampling with activated carbon from the FACT (FLUTe Activated 
Carbon Technology) is tested against the screening method tree cores to see if activated carbon is 
better to screen or chlorinated solvents especially vinyl chloride. As a second passive sampler ATD-
was tested as well. The two location sites used for screening was Grindsted and Raadvad. 
The carbon felt results retrieved from Grindsted showed higher concentration of chlorinated 
solvents. The results however did not show higher frequency of VC in carbon felt compared with 
tree cores. In the carbon felt VC was find in one tree in and in the tree core it was found I four of 
the trees. Which could indicate that tree cores is a better screening method for VC than carbon felt 
In Grindsted no VC was found in the ATD- sampels during the sampling period and in the same 
time VC was found in the tree cores. this indicates that tree cores is a better screening method for 
VC than ATD. And when comparing ATD with carbon felt it seamed that the carbon felt was better 
than ATD to find chlorinated solvents. Furthermore the results From Raadvad did not give a clear 
usable result and therefore nothing was concluded from this.  
 
There resultes from Grindsted and Raadvad did not give an indication on a optimal timespand aldo 
it seems that 2 month is not a good time period due to no findings after 2 month. The rest of the 
results did show if 14 days or a month was better better time period. 
 
   The distribution of samplings taken against (A) and away from the stream (AS) seems to have a 
random distribution and therefore no clear conclusion could be drawn regarding higher 
concentrations in the side of the tree against or away from the tree.  
Overall the answer to the question: “Is passive sampling with activated carbon a better 
phytoscreening method than tree core sampling to locate a pollution with chlorinated solvents 
especially vinyl chloride?” the recommendation would be that more experiments are needed. The 
choice of season seems to be important and therefore investigations should be fulfilled to find out if 
carbon felt and ATD-samples are better in detecting vinyl chloride during spring and summer time.

1. Introduction  
The impact of pollution has in most countries, including Denmark, been ignored in the past. In the 
past it was not unusual, when dealing with polluted waste, to use landfills as deposits. The practise 
of digging a hole in the ground for disposals without thinking about future consequences for 
groundwater, soil and surface water has been the reason for many environmental operations further 
on. Surface water was sometimes used as a dilution pool to get rid of  pollutants from industrial 
activities (Grundvandskontoret, 2005). It was not until the last half of the 20th century, that effects 
of pollutions were recognised. The pollutions had a negative effect on the groundwater and the 
surface water, which groundwater flows into. Today, by enforcement of the European Water Frame 
Directive (CEC, 2000) groundwater, surface water and their interactions have to be  
monitored to ensure good water quality. These rules also make it easier to find future pollutions 
influencing the water quality.   
Some of the pollutants from the past that cause problems in the surface water today are the 
chlorinated solvents tetrachloroethene (PCE), trichloroethene(TCE), cis-1,2 dichloroethene(cis-
DCE) and vinyl chloride (VC). The chlorinated solvents were used by the pharmaceutical- and 
metal industries and furthermore by dry cleaners and their efficiencies means they are still in 
production today (Ojajävi et al., 2001). PCE, TCE, cis-DCE and VC are carcinogenic and have 
damaging effect on the environment. Following this they are unwanted in the environment (mst, 
2016). 
Two locations sites affected by chlorinated solvents in ground and surface water are Grindsted and 
Raadvad. In Grindsted the cause of pollution is Grindstedværket, which used chlorinated solvents in 
the production. Grinstedværket lead untreated waste directly into Grindsted stream, while 
depositing solid materials at three other locations in the area. From groundwater borings and Point 
Velocity probes (PVP's), PCE, TCE, cis-DCE and VC were found in the groundwater near 
Grindsted Å (Petersen et al., 2010). In the study of Rønne (2016) PVP's are used for measuring the 
places where the contaminated groundwater emanates into the Grindsted stream. As indicated by 
Petersen et al. (2010) the groundwater flows from the pollution site in the upper groundwater 
aquifer, which elevates to the stream being only 1-2 meter below terrain close to the brink. 
 The other contaminated site, Raadvad once had a knife factory producing stainless steel. In 2008 
and 2016 PCE, TCE, cis-DCE and VC was detected in groundwater, surface water (NIRAS, 2008)  
and in tree cores (Roost et al., 2016). The contamination of the stream was especially effected by 
the contaminated groundwater flowing into the stream (NIRAS, 2008). 
To locate and delineate a pollution phytoscreening is a cheap and a less time consuming method 
(Trapp and Algreen, 2014) and (Vroblesky et al., 1999). The phytoscreening method, called tree 
core sampling, was used in Raadvad by Roost et al. (2016) and in Grindsted by DTU students. In 
the sampled tree cores from Grindsted near the stream PCE, TCE, cis-DCE and VC were found in 
the tree cores (Ottosen, 2016).  
Due to VC’s high diffusion rate it can be difficult to find the compound in tree cores and therefore a 
new screening method to effectively find VC in trees would be preferable. A possible new 
screenings method could be passive sampling with activated carbon from the FACT (FLUTe 
==PAGE:10==
 
10
Activated Carbon Technology). FACT FLUTe has been successfully used to monitor chlorinated 
solvents in groundwater (Sørensen and Broholm , 2015) and (K. Mosthaf et al., 2014) and therefore 
Activated carbon derived from the FACT FLUTe could possibly work as a passive sampler in trees 
to screen for VC. Besides this screening method also ATD-tubes with Tenax and Carboxen 
might be an alternative. 
 
1.1 Objective of study: 
To examine the question: Is passive sampling with activated carbon a better phytoscreening method 
than tree core sampling to locate a pollution with chlorinated solvents especially vinyl chloride? 
 
The attempt is to solve the problem by answering the following questions: Can activated carbon felt 
from the FACT (FLUTe Activated Carbon Technology) sorb for vinyl chloride?  
1. Can activated carbon felt from the FACT (FLUTe Activated Carbon Technology) be 
used to absorb vinyl chloride? 
2. Is activated carbon felt better in the absorption of vinyl chloride than tree cores? 
3. Can you use ATD-tubes with Tenax TA and Carboxen 1018 as sorbent work as a 
passive sampler? 
4. Are ATD-tubes better in absorption of vinyl chloride than tree cores or carbon felt? 
5. Determination of the optimal time span for passive sampling of VC 14 days, a month 
or two months?

2.1 Grindsted  
Grindsted is a Danish city placed in the southern region of Jutland. Amongst the 43 most polluted 
sites in Denmark, Grindsted has two of them, a factory site and a landfill site (mst, 2007). Some of 
the main pollutants derived from the site are the pollutants BTEX, sulphonamides, barbiturates and 
chlorinated ethenes including PCE, TCE, cis-DCE and VC (COWI et al, 2011). 
The factory site “Fabriksgrunden” has been occupied by different chemical activities since 1914 
and from 1924 the main production at the site was pharmaceuticals and excipients for the food 
industry. Four locations in Grindsted have been severely affected by the activities of 
Grindstedværket (Figure 1). Beside the deposit called “Banegravsdepotet” the factory used the 
drainage ditch (“Afløbsgrøften”), which lead contaminated wastewater directly to the recipient, 
Grindsted stream. Wastewater outlet took place from 1924-1960, and it was not until 1951 that 
wastewater treatment took place before the outlet. Some chemical waste from the factory also ended 
up at the landfill, called “Lossepladsen” at Figure 1. However, a hydrological model of the area 
among others including Grindsted stream points out the factory's site deposits and drainage ditch as

2.2 Raadvad 
Raadvad, the second site investigated for chlorinated solvents, is located north of Copenhagen in the 
middle of a Natura 2000 area established for protecting of the stream, Mølleåen (Arealinfo, 2016) 
(Figure 4). The stream has been used to run a production mill supporting different industries for 
many years e.g. a gunpowder production 1758. The company Raadvad Knife Factory, which had 
the exclusive right to produce stainless steel in Denmark, used the factory building until the 1970’s, 
where they moved to Jutland (Raadvad, 2016). Production of metals such as kitchen tools, can 
include the use of trichloroethylene as a solvent cleaning, and for degreasing metal parts. 
Chlorinated solvents, oil and metals have been found at the site, where old crafts education 
institutions, offices and a nature school still are situated. The area is classified as V2-contaminated, 
meaning that it causes a risk for environment and health. However, the present use of the buildings 
means that the contamination does not constitute risk for the indoor environment, and furthermore 
the contamination does not cause an unacceptable threat for the recipient, Mølleåen (Niras, 2012).

3. Chlorinated solvents  
 
The pollutant in focus in this master thesis is the gas, VC. However, the mother compounds PCE, 
TCE and c-DCE play a leading role due to their presence in the area. PCE or TCE are called mother 
compounds and their degradation products are cis-DCE and VC. VC is degraded to ethene (Figure 
5)

PCE, TCE, cis-DCE and VC are all cardiogenic and furthermore PCE, TCE and cis-DCE are acute 
toxic and have damaging effects on the environment -PCE is classified as hazardous to the aquatic 
environment (mst, 2016). 
Chlorinated solvents have been used and are still used by the pharmaceutical- and metal industries 
in the production of rubber and plastic, dry cleaning, paint removal, for solvents and in laboratories 
(Ojajävi et al., 2001) and large amounts of chlorinated solvents are still produced yearly. The 
chemicals, except for VC are DNAPLS (Dense Non Aqueous Phase Liquids), which means they are 
water immiscible phase liquids and heavier than water and able to pass through the saturated zone 
and difficult to locate (Bjerg et al., 2011).

4 Phytoscreening and factors affecting phytoscreening 
The root system of plants is used to anchor them to the soil and to bring water and nutrients to the 
plants. The root systems are very extensive, often five times larger than the top 
http://www.havenyt.dk/artikler/prydhaven/traeer_og_buske/983.html, making them able to reach 
contaminants in the underground. The groundwater level is also important affecting the uptake of 
water and thereby concentration of pollutants in the tree. The advantage of using trees to screen for 
contamination is a cheap, fast and easy technique with large potential. Besides tree core sampling 
other the techniques used for passive sampling are SPME (Solid-Phase Micro Extraction) and wood 
chips (Limmer et al, 2014). Tree coring can vary between deep coring implying dendrology, where 
rings in older trees illustrate the history of contamination or the shallow coring used in this report. 
The methods in focus here are tree core sampling and passive sampling with carbon and ATD- 
tubes.  
Trees are living organisms and the ways they behave are amongst other things affected by weather 
and seasons. Trees transpire during spring and summer and here the more volatile VC can be found 
in higher concentration due to transpiration peaking in this season. In the autumn when the water 
flow levels of, the mother compounds are found in the woody tissue (Ottosen, 2016). The variation 
in weather patterns and temperature also affects the concentration levels. During a wet summer the 
trees only take up water close to the surface, unlike a dry summer, where roots seek the 
groundwater (A. Sorek et al., 2008). Trees and tree species are different and their water uptake and 
concentration of compounds makes the trees incomparable. The fact that trees also have an ability 
to store water in the stem during the morning, which can be recharged in the evening, makes it even 
harder to estimate uptake (McGraw-Hill, 2007). 
 
4.1Tree Physiology 
Besides tree size and specie, physical properties also decide where the highest concentrations are 
found. To understand how the compounds are transported through the stem of the tree it is 
important to understand the physiology of the tree. The major organs of a tree are the trunk, 
branches, leaves, roots and reproductive structures. Besides the tips of branches, leaves and roots 
only a thin layer of cells under the bark (the meristematic tissues) are alive and divide continuously. 
==PAGE:17==
 
17
As all other trees, alder, birch and maple grow in diameter by division in the single microscopic 
layer of meristematic tissue covering the tree producing both the xylem cells (wood) inside the tree 
and the phloem, the cells towards the bark (Figure 6). This vascular cambium together with the 
springwood and summerwood is where the optimal sampling point for tree cores are. Drilling 6 cm 
into the stem to take out a tree core is based on the principle that water (sapwood) is predominantly 
transported through the outermost rings.  
 
 
Figure 6. Cross-section of tree trunk illustrating the physiology (Odellengineering.com, 2016) 
The tube-like xylem in the outer layer transports nutrients, water and potential contaminants 
vertically from the root to the leaves until the accumulation of minerals and metabolics slow the 
conduction to a degree, where it becomes non-productive and the xylem gets a dark colour, seen as 
rings in the cross-section of the stem. While roots supply the shoot with water, the shoot supplies 
the roots with phytosynthate and vitamins. This conduction path takes place in the phloem, which 
transports vertically and horizontally metabolic products and dissolved carbohydrates from leaves 
through the stem to the roots. As the xylem grows and new bark is being produced, the phloem is 
slowly crushed. However, the transport from the roots to the stem is the only goal, when targeting 
VOC compounds from groundwater and therefore phloem is of no interest. Besides this, the cork 
can pose cross-contaminations from e.g. air to the tree core sample. The outer part of the tree core 
containing bark and phloem is always removed during tree core sampling (Algreen M. & Trapp S., 
2014).  
The xylem transport is able to move up to 400 litres of water per day. The root has a passive role in 
water uptake determined by the loss of water from the leaves due to transpiration. The root system 
works besides anchorage also as ramification system to ensure water to the tree. With deep roots the 
tree can endure dry periods for a longer time and therefore the tree often grows a long taproot 
reaching the water table (McGraw-Hill, 2007). Apart from the fact that trees have different varieties 
in root systems, the soil conditions often decide what is best for the root and these facts also have an 
impact on concentrations. In the case of Grindsted and Raadvad, all trees are close to the stream, 
and, therefore, water can be soaked up directly from the stream or from the saturated zone or static 
water table only 1-2 meter from the surface. The tree species also affects the results of 
phytoscreenings (A. Sorek et al., 2008). The sizes of the trees affect the uptake of chlorinated 
solvents depicted by the diffusion rate, which is negatively correlated with the diameter of the stem 
(Wittlingerova et al 2013). One of the factors contributing to the varying results in tangential 
variation is the different root system of the tree species. Tree species also plays a role in the 
==PAGE:18==
 
18
interpretation of sampling result. Apart from the fact that trees have different rates of sap transport 
i.e. birch sap plays substantial role (Holm and Rotard, 2011), weather patterns also has an impact on 
phytoscreening. 
 
4.2 Fruit tree Model: 
A mathematical model has been developed to predict the uptake of neutral organic chemicals from 
soil and air into fruits. The Fruit Tree Model can be used to understand how the physical and 
chemical properties of the chlorinated solvents affect their transportation through the stem. As 
included in the model from Trapp (2006), chemical equilibrium, advective transport in xylem and 
phloem, diffusive exchange to soil and air, and growth dilution are the main processes determining 
the fate of the compounds.  
The contaminant mass flux through the tree stem can be compared to the flux in a river. The flow is 
provided by water, while the stem similar to sediment is able to sorb compounds. Due to 
volatilisation compounds are able to escape or be taken up into air. Metabolism has not been 
investigated. A study predicts that polar, non-volatile compounds will preferably be transported 
from soil to fruits via sapwater. Parameters used to prove these facts are the river model first order 
equation (equal to Ficks 1st law). Using the model gives the exact constants indicating the destiny 
of the compounds i.e. the partition coefficient between stem and water and the half lives in the stem 
In this case the interesting parameters for the results are Kstem/water and the total rate, k. Kstem/water 
describes the concentration ratio in chemical equilibrium between stem (wood+water+air) and 
water (such as xylem sap), while the total rate, k, describes the compound loss rate from the stem 
per day. As mentioned in Larsen et al. (2008) there are 31 parameters influencing the tree stem 
concentration used in the model. “For chlorinated solvents, such as PCE, TCE and c-DCE, sensitive 
parameters include, besides bioavailable concentration in soil or groundwater, root radius and mass; 
root water, lipid and gas content; transpiration stream; growth rates; water flow velocity in xylem; 
and others. These parameters vary with species location, season, and meteorological conditions” 
(Larsen et al., 2008). 
 
4.3 Tree core sampling 
Some of the advantages of tree coring are the method being both inexpensive, easy, fast to use and 
with minimal impact on the environment (Algreen and Trapp, 2007). Tree coring is a snapshot 
illustrating the concentration in the stem at a specific point at a specific time. It is not likely that it 
gives a precise picture of the concentrations in groundwater, but can be used as a tool to prove that 
pollutants are present. Tree core sampling is described more in depth in the method section.  
 
4.4 Passive sampling  
The principle of passive sampling is to catch targeted compounds without the use of power-
requiring devices (e.g. a pump), but by the physical processes, diffusion or permeation. It is an 
effective and cheap method to monitor pollution, and the non-power requirement means, it can be 
used almost everywhere without the need of constant supervision of pollution is needed. The fact 
that trees have unpredictable flow patterns makes it impossible to find the TWA (Time Weighted 
Average). Besides, interference of the sampler with outside air and site competition between mother 
==PAGE:19==
 
19
and daughter compounds can disturb the equilibrium of VC. The significance of a strong sorbent is 
not the only important factor, even though absorbed or adsorbed is of importance, especially with 
regards to the very volatile compound, VC. Carbon has for many years been used for applications 
such as water and gas purification. The medium is a strong sorbent having small pores and a large 
surface area –activated carbon can be produced with a surface area between 500-1500 m2/g 
(Lenntech, 2016). 
However, carbon, such as charcoal, cannot be used as a sample tool since it is not a good adsorbent 
for thermal desorption meaning it would not release the compounds during thermal measurements 
e.g. GCMS (J. Brown and B. Shirey, 2001). The active carbon used as passive sampler is derived 
from another groundwater monitoring technology called FACT (FLUTe Activated Carbon 
Technology). It is the company, Flexible Liner Underground Technologies (FLUTe), which 
developed the technology. The FACT is a flexible membrane combined with a NAPL FLUTe liner 
whit activated carbon felt sewed into it. The activated carbon is protected with a layer of aluminum 
to stop diffusion from the carbon felt. The FACT FLUTe is used directly in a groundwater borehole 
to detect contaminations of  NAPL. The NAPL FLUTe will change colour when contact with 
NAPLS occurs. Furthermore, the carbon felt will absorb the NAPLS. The FLUTe will stay inserted 
in the borehole for a certain amount of  time until it is possible to see a possible colour change. 
Later the carbon felt is cut into pieces and investigated for contaminants in the laboratory (Sørensen 
and Broholm, 2014).      
In one study the FACT-FLUTe was tested in lime-stone aquifers illustrating that it was possible to 
use the technology for pollution mapping (K. Mosthaf et al., 2014). In another study, the sorption 
coefficient of the felt was estimated and the following results for Kd were found: PCE was 12000 
L/kg and of TCE 11000 L/kg while Kd of cis-DCE was 4000 L/kg (Sørensen et al., 2014). No Kd 
for VC has been found since the compound is very volatile and difficult to handle.Furthermore, the 
competition sorption amongst the compounds was estimated. PCE was not influenced by the 
presence of any of the other compounds, whereas the uptake of cis-DCE and TCE are affected, 
especially when using high concentrations.  
 
Other developed materials used for passive sampling are Tenax TA and Carboxen 1018. Tenax 
TA is a porous polymer based on 2,6-diphenyl-p-phenylene oxide. It is widely used as adsorbent in 
air and purge trap applications (Tenax, 2016). Its desorption temperature of 200-300 °C and with a 
surface area of 35 m2/g. Normally Tenax TA are not good in retaining the very volatile analytes e.g. 
VC and c-DCE, but its capabilities is broadened with Carboxen placed behind. Tenax TA’s 
desorption temperature is 200-300 °C (J. Brown and B. Shirey, 2001). 
 
Carboxen consists of carbon molecular sieves (CMS), which are hard, non-friable, and highly 
porous. It is hydrophic spheres with a surface area of 700 m2/g and composed of the carbon skeletal 
framework, which remained after pyrolysis of polymeric precursor (sigmaaldrich, 2016). 
Carboxen’s desorption temperature is in the range 200 – 330 ° (J. Brown and B. Shirey, 2001).  
 
With the combination of Tenax TA and Carboxen 1018 in an ATD (Automated Thermal 
desorption) tube, a strong passive sampler for catchment of chlorinated solvents has been 
==PAGE:20==
 
20
developed. The ability of the ATD-tube to separate the mother compounds from the volatile VC by 
use of Tenax and Carboxen means it is easier to get better representative results for VC. Methods 
for carbon and ATD sampling are described in Method.  
 
4.5 Groundwater Concentration and Groundwater Level 
Phytoscreening results are dependent on the location and concentration of chlorinated solvents in 
the groundwater. If nothing is found in the static water level the chance of finding the compounds in 
the trees is very small.  
Multi-level sampling is a way to locate and monitor groundwater pollution at different levels. The 
multi-level sampler is a long tube with filters placed in different heights so that water can be taken 
up from different levels. The water samples can be used to determine and map pollutions. If water 
samples from the same level are taken repeatedly with the same concentrations, it can be concluded 
the plume are constant over time i.e. not moving.  
 
Movement of the groundwater table causes changes in passive sampling concentrations during 
phytoscreening because the tree roots accessibility to the water table changes. The water table 
movements during the sampling period are measured using an electric sounder consisting of a 
battery connected to an insulated wire with depth markings, which is lowered into a well until the 
water is reached and the needle deflects (IEG, 2016).

5.1.2.1 Conceptual model and trees 
In 2009 chlorinated solvents and oil products exceeding the environmental groundwater criteria 
with up to a factor 28.500 were detected (Grontmij-Carl Bro A/S, 2009). The pore air and water in

to the stream. However, a layer of peat and gyttja limits the vertical spreading with a sorption 
capacity of 20-25 times the magnitude of sand and boulder clay. Contrary, the horizontal spreading 
is influenced by the fact that the upper groundwater is placed at a higher level than Mølleåen. A 
conceptual model (Figure 9) approximates the Niras (2012) situation plan for groundwater with

Mølleåen. The two streams makes the upper groundwater flow complicated since it runs in many 
different directions. Overall the stream is affected by 1.5 kg of chlorinated solvents per year (Niras, 
2012). The chlorinated solvents contamination of the upper groundwater running into Mølleåen and 
illustrated as the upper coloured spot in Figure 9, has an average concentration of 2500 ug/L, while 
the spot between the two stream contains 250 ug/L in the upper groundwater (Niras, 2012).

DCE, were also found in the surface water (Roost, 2016). In September 2016 a DTU master student

5.1.3 Tree core sampling  
1. Before going to the field, the vials including water and labels put on were weighted to be used for 
calculating the tree core weights later. An amount of 6x2 = 12 20 mL-vials are filled with 12 mL of 
water. This chosen quantity of water was exactly what was needed to cover the tree cores for 
prohibiting the diffusion of VC, when sampled from the field. The vials were weighted so that the 
tree core mass could be determined later and afterwards placed in a tray ready to be taken to the 
field. 
2. Tree core sampling is conducted according to the method optimized for VC detection by Ottosen  
(2015) with offspring in Algreen and Trapp (2007). In the field eight tree cores, according to a pair 
from each side of the tree, is taken from each tree. The tree core is taken in approximately one 
meters height. The tree cores are taken out with a tree borer and the core is approximately 5 cm in 
length and 5 mm in width. 
 
3. The tree cores are then quickly transferred into the 20 mL vials mentioned in step 1. Internal 
standard (0.5 mL) is added from a prepared cold flask brought from the lab and consisting of 
acidified chloroform solution.  
 
4. The vials are kept cool in a cooler box upside down until measured on the GCMS in the lab. 
 
5.1.4 Passive sampling (Carbon felt) 
1. Before going to the field, carbon samples are produced by attaching active carbon from FACT* 
to a dental polystyrene stick by the use of nylon thread. In detail, the procedure consists of cutting a 
small piece (approximately 0.5 x 4 cm) of activated carbon felt from the FACTe-FLUTE material. 
The activated carbon felt is fixed to the dental stick by poking the dental point through it and twist 
nylon around. It is important that the felt is easy to take off by cutting the nylon and loosening it 
from the stick. The carbon felt is stored in a rilsan bag to prevent any contamination. Figure 11 
illustrates the size of the carbon sampler, which should fit into the tree and therefore not be bigger 
than the tree core taken out.  
 
 
Figure 11. Dental stick with carbon felt compared to a tree core. 
 
2. After the tree cores are taken out of the two bore holes two carbon samples are placed. To 
prevent any escape or contamination of the samples the hole is closed with clay and rilsan is used as 
cover fastened with duct-tape and cord (Figure 12). 
==PAGE:28==
 
28
 
Figure 12. The carbon samples are protected behind clay and rilsan plast.  
 
3. After having done this away from stream the procedure is repeated against the stream to get 
samples from both sides of the tree.  
 
4. The procedure is the same for all six trees (A-F) so that 2 x12 pairs adding up to 24 of carbon 
samples are put in to the trees. After the passive samplings have been in the tree for a certain time interval 
(in this case 14, 30 or 56 days) the cord, duct tape and the rilsan bag are removed and the dry clay is easily

and internal standard added through the rubber screw cap. The vial is placed in a cooler bag with 
cooling elements and brought to the cooling room in the lab. 
 
5. New tree core holes are made underneath the old ones and new carbon felt added (repeat from 
step 1 again). 
 
5.1.5 Passive sampling (ATD) 
1. To make sure the ATD tubes are totally clean and without condensation inside, pre-treatment is 
needed before the field trip. The ATD-tubes are heated up to 300 °C in a program that lasts 36 min.

Tenax and Carboxen to catch mother compounds and VC, respectively. 
 
3. It is important that the fitting is placed in the xylem of the tree. A drilling machine is used. 
 
4. The tubes were added to both sides of the tree in three trees placed in the area where most VC has 
been found. 
 
5. To secure the ATD-tube from cows, a half-pipe-construction are added around the sample. 
6. The ATD-tubes are taken out after 7 and 14 days respectively (except for in Raadvad with 7 days 
and a month respectively).

5.2.1 Tree cores  
The lab method for analysing tree cores is very similar to the methods for passive sampling.  
1. A standard row with known concentrations of chlorinated ethenes is produced and added to a set 
of new vials together with 0.4 mL of internal standard.  
 
2. To secure an equilibrium between gas and water phase the vials are heated at 80 °C for two hours 
before the analysis. Afterwards the vials are cooled to get a representative distribution of 
chlorinated ethenes into headspace.  
 
3. At last all vials are analysed in GCMS for 30 minute each including cooling time. GCMS is 
accomplished by the use of the detector GCMS 5975C electron impact (70eV detector with a 30 m 
x 0.32 I.D. x 20.00 m film thickness HP-PLOT/Q capillary column (Olsson, 2015).  
 
5.2.2 Passive sampler (Carbon) 
1.The GCMS needs to be calibrated before running the field samples. The calibration and control 
solutions are prepared in the same containers as used for the field samples. The level of calibration 
is the same as used analysing the tree core samples i.e. low (0.02-40 ug/L) (see appendix). The 
calibration test includes spiking five sets of felt with a known concentration of PCE, TCE, cis-DCE 
and VC and by analysing these samples at 85 °C’s for 20 minutes in the GCMS, the retrieval 
percentages are derived.  
 
2.The 20 ml vials, including active carbon felt, were weighed before being analysed on the GCMS. 
The total weight of the vials (including the glass vial, fluid and carbon felt) is used to calculate the 
total weight of fluid in each sample.  
 
3.By using a standard row spiked on carbon felt the retrieval percentage is taken into account. By 
use of the low range dilution scheme, the ten-vial-standard row is produced from a PCE-TCE-DCE 
mixture and a VC/CA mixture. Three control vials include a mixture of 400 L pure PCE and 10 
mL methanol spiked on three felt samples with 0.5 mL of chloroform. The five blind tests consist of 
felt with 0.5 mL of chloroform. Figure 16 illustrates the production of the standard row, and Figure 
17 the spiked pieces of felt. The field samples and standard rows are heated to 80 °C for two hours 
in an oven to desorb compounds from felt into headspace for measurement and afterwards they are 
ready for the GCMS-analysis (Figure 18).

7.1 Grindsted and Raadvad  
The first section includes a discussion of all results from tree cores, carbon and ATD-samples from 
Grindsted with 1. Tree cores, 2. Carbon felt samplings, 3. Tree cores versus carbon felt samplings 
and at last tree core, carbon and ATD-samplings. In the second section, the results from Raadvad 
with tree core, carbon and ATD samples are represented in one bar chart. 
 
7.1.1 Grindsted  
As shown in Figure 23 the water level for the upper groundwater aquifer Grindsted has not been 
changed significantly during the sampling period and the findings in the trees can therefore be 
based on a stable basis. The multilevel results reveal that the 2016 measurements (Figure 21and 22) 
show lower concentrations of cis-DCE and VC in the first filters compared to 2014-measurements 
indicating that the concentrations in groundwater pollute the trees less compared to earlier 
measurements. The sounding measurements from well 114.1448 is close to tree A and B, while the 
other two wells, 114.2507 and 114.2508, are closer to tree C-G. The measurement of trees distance 
above sea level compared to static water levels distance above sea level is very small. Since the 
trees were not soaked in water the small difference between the measurements must be due to the 
hilly landscape of the terrain surface.  
 
7.1.1.1 Tree cores from August to October  
On August 16’t a tree core was taken out and analysed in the lab, but no chlorinated solvents were 
found. This could be due to the fact that precipitation (i.e. 13.6-15.5 mm) in two and a half week 
before the 16th triggered the tree roots to take up rainwater from the soil surface instead of 
groundwater 1.55 m.b.t.

7.1.1.2 Carbon Felt from August to October  
The passive samplings to assess chlorinated solvent concentrations were taken after the tree core 
has been taken out. The method is important to consider since the tree core has accumulated some 
compound in the past, which are compared to the concentrations of the passive sampling taken 
afterwards. This method was chosen to illustrate the difference in sorption material when taken 
from the precise same place at the tree. If the tree core was compared with passive sampling done 
prior to its out take, the tree core would have to be taken beside the passive samplings, which were 
put into an old tree core hole. The samplings would, therefore, be taken from different places. 
Comparing passive samplings taken after the tree core has been removed, also gives the opportunity 
to collect over a longer time span and may increase the sensitivity and success rate since we 
probably get both the surrounding compounds around the tree core out take and additional 
compound from the tree in the time after out take into the sample. 
In August i.e. from the 16th to the 29th cis-DCE was found in tree E against the stream. Also VC 
was found in tree E, but away from stream. The average temperature was between 15 and 19 °C and 
precipitation was in average 19 mm. These facts indicate that even as the tree has the opportunity to 
take water from the soil above the groundwater, it still takes water from the groundwater.  From the 
16th to the 13th of September cis-DCE was found in tree B and E both away from the stream. At 
the end of this period the dry weather probably forced the tress to take in more groundwater. 
However, after a month the concentration of the compounds after a month versus the concentrations 
in samples after 14 days does not show a clear picture. No clear tendencies between concentrations 
against or away from the stream were found. 
However, in September the difference between carbon samples taken at different time intervals 
becomes clearer when comparing the first and second months samples. The concentration in a 
sample taken from the 11th of September to 11th of October showed both concentrations of cis-
DCE in the range tree B(AS) > A(AS) > E (S) and furthermore PCE and TCE in tree B (S) and VC

7.1.1.3 Tree core versus carbon felt from August to October 
The bar chart reveals that the highest concentrations of chlorinated solvents per g sample are in 
carbon felt (ng/g felt) (Figure 37). The reason for this is the high sorption value for FACT-FLUTe 
compared to tree core i.e. Table 3 and 5 in result section. VC has occurred more frequent in tree 
cores than in carbon felt and also in more trees. This could maybe explained by the fact that carbon 
felt is normally used to detect chlorinated solvents in groundwater where a constant flow of water 
passes through the felt and thereby making it easier for the carbon to sorb VC. While in trees there 
is not always a constant flow of water with chlorinated solvents passing through the felt and this 
could make is more difficult to sorb VC. More findings of cis-DCE in tree cores in September and 
October could be due to the same issue. 
In carbon TCE was found in tree B (S) in September and October for B (AS). The reason why no 
TCE has been found in tree cores could be due to the lower sorption capacity compared to carbon.  
PCE occurs more frequent in carbon felt (i.e. in tree B in September and in A, B, E and F in 
October) than in tree cores (tree B in October), which could have the same explanation as for TCE.

7.3 S Kd values  
The Kd values retrieved from Sørensen and Broholm (2014) illustrate that PCE is the compound 
absorbing strongest to carbon closely followed by TCE. Since VC's Kd value was not found it was

Kd values it is seen that VC absorbs least to carbon. The absorption is still high and can still be used 
as a passive sample for VC. The calculated Kd versus the measured Kd illustrate a percentage 
variation from 6.23 to 17.1 percent, indicating that VC's sorption to carbon in the field could be 
lower or higher. The calculated variation in Kd does not change the outcome that the concentration 
in tree cores are higher than for carbon felt (Figure X) since the Kd for carbon is still approximately

7.4 Retrieval percentages: 
 The fact that site competition happens between mother -and daughter compounds means that 
results can be misleading if not taken into account. In the field analysis it was intended to use tree 
no 18, placed in the middle of a hot spot and with tree core results illustrating presence of both 
mother and daughter compounds, to find out if the site competition resulted in the findings of only 
mother compounds. Unfortunately the wrong tree was chosen and the experiment could not be 
done. However, it was possible to find the mother compounds influence on the sorption of VC by 
doing experiments in the lab instead and the hypothesis was not confirmed. From table 5 it can be 
seen that the concentration of daughter compounds retrieves after being spiked on carbon felt do not 
vary much dependent on the mothercompounds presence or not. The table illustrate that when cis-
DCE and VC are not in the same mixture as PCE and TCE, they do not have a lower but a slightly 
higher retrieval percentage, than when they are in the same mixture as PCE and TCE and spiked on 
carbon. This could indicate that PCE and TCE do not compete with cis-DCE and VC for the site on 
the carbon felt. Furthermore this should indicate that when PCE and TCE are measured in the 
carbon felt from the field they  should not have taken the place from VC and cis-DCE and thereby 
cause no measurements of VC and cis-DCE. 
 
7.5 Tree Fruit Model: 
Results from the Fruit Tree Model indicates that the sorption strength of chlorinated solvents to the 
wood stem follows the order of PCE > TCE > cis-DCE > VC meaning that PCE sorbs strongest to 
the wood (Kstem/water = 36.09 L/kg), while VC is less bound (Kstem/water = 4.6). This result is consistent 
with the physical chemical data for the two compounds, where logKow for PCE is higher than for 
VC. In detail this means that the concentration ratio in chemical equilibrium between stem 
(wood+water+air) and water (such as xylem sap) is shifted to stem for PCE while it is the opposite 
case for VC. The total rate, k, depicts the order VC > cis-DCE > TCE > PCE with VC having the 
highest loss rate per day i.e. 16.5 times higher than PCE. The fact that VC has higher Henrys 
constant and a vapour pressure 146 times the one of PCE proves the facts indicated by the model. 
The model predicts that TCE and c-DCE have higher solubility and will preferably be in the water 
 
7.6 Comparison to other findings  
This report is built on old and recent studies describing the contaminated sites in Raadvad and 
Grindsted, tree core sampling and factors affecting it. Between 2014 and 2016 Rønde (2016) 
described the groundwater emanation to Grindsted stream with an illustration of the contamination 
of the upper groundwater (Figure 7). In Larsen et al. (2008) it is indicated that tree core sampling 
can provide: "valuable semiquantitative information for plume delineation and characterization". 
Since this master report has concluded that both tree A and B frequently contains chlorinated 
solvents it might be possible that the delineation of the contamination has changed. In this master 
thesis the emphasis has been on the VC-findings, which proved to be less successful since few 
==PAGE:62==
 
62
samples (especially carbon and ATD) showed no VC concentrations. However, in a study by Larsen 
et al. (2008) it was concluded that a low logKow and a high Kaw of VC could lead to lower 
accumulation in wood based on the study of a contaminated area in Czech Republic. The area was 
not highly polluted with VC, but either way showed that the VC concentration ratio between tree 
cores and groundwater was lower than for the other chlorinated ethenes.  
The fact that trees transpire less in autumn could also be a reason for the scarce findings of VC. The 
tree cores used as comparison material for this thesis were used in another study- a study indicating 
that daughter compounds are mostly found in spring and summer, while mother compounds are 
found in autumn and winter (Ottosen, 2016). The seasons impact on chlorinates ethenes found in 
the trees could be the reason why very low concentrations of VC were found in both Grindsted and 
Raadvad. However, according to a study by Limmer et al., (2011) and Wittlingerova et al. (2012) it 
is found that: “…no statistical significant differences were found between growing and non-
growing season tree core chloroethene concentrations”. It is concluded that a larger high-frequency 
data set is needed. An article describing the diffusivities in tree tissues (Baduru et al., 2008) 
indicates that diffusivities in xylem tissues might be inversely related to molecular weight. In this 
study the mother compounds, PCE and TCE occur in late autumn. The low transpiration rate due to 
loss of leaves means that fewer chlorinated solvents are transported from the groundwater. The 
higher Kd value for PCE and TCE means that they will accumulate in the stem. This is also due to 
their higher molecular weight favouring slow transport accumulating them in the stem. However, it 
has been proved that increase in precipitation rapidly dilutes the concentration of TCE found in the 
tree stem (Vroblesky et al., 2004) contradicting that higher concentrations of TCE is found in 
autumn especially if the samples have been taken after a recent rainfall. The difference in tree 
species and the directional place in the tree stem used for tree coring have an impact on the 
concentration of compounds found in the tree stem Vroblesky et al. (1999). According to the 
concentration variations due to tree species, Krishna et al. (2001) indicates that cellulose is polar 
whereas lignin is hydrophobic and adsorb/absorbs organics and since softwood has a higher content 
of lignin than hardwood higher sorption capacity for lipophilics can occur. However, from the 
scarce amount of tree cores taken from Raadvad, nothing about concentration variations due to tree 
species variations can be concluded here. Regarding the impact of directional variations on 
concentrations found in Grindsted and Raadvad no conclusion can be drawn with regards to tree 
side against the stream and away from the stream. 
 
8. Conclusion  
Activated carbon felt from the FACT (FLUTe Activated Carbon Technology) can be used for 
sorption of vinyl chloride. The carbon felt samples retrieved from Grindsted had high 
concentrations of chlorinated solvents including VC. However, the success rate for carbon felt in 
regards to VC-finding-frequency was not good. In Figure 37, “Tree core versus carbon felt from 
August to October”, VC occurs more often in trees than in carbon felt. Carbon felt has VC in only 
tree E while tree cores have VC in tree A, at both sides of E and D and F. The very high sorption 
capacity of activated carbon does favour this screening method, but since the importance in 
==PAGE:63==
 
63
screening methods is to detect pollutants, tree coring might be a better screening tool. The high 
sorption capacity of activated carbon means that equilibrium in trees happens at a much slower rate 
than for tree cores and the picture regarding concentrations therefore becomes reversed when 
including Kd in the calculations. This again indicates that carbon is not a better screening tools than 
tree cores according to higher sorption capacity.  
 
In Grindsted no VC was found in the ATD –samples during the entire sampling period. In the same 
time VC was found in tree cores from both tree E (AS+S) and G. In this campaign tree cores are 
considered a better screening tool than both ATD and carbon felt.  
ATD-tubes with Tenax and Carboxen might work as passive sampler, but in this study the success 
rate regarding frequency of VC findings, might be even worse than for carbon felt.  
 
Comparing ATD to carbon felt samples cis-DCE would be the chosen compound since VC is not 
present in either ATD-sample. However with the results from Grindsted (Figure 40) and Raadvad 
(Figure 41) we cannot draw any clear conclusion whether ATD is better or worse than carbon felt in 
regards to concentrations of cis-DCE. Only the fact that ATD is not as effective as expected with 
fewer results and no VC found, has depicted that the cheaper carbon method may be better.  
The results do not indicate any optimal time span for passive sampling of VC although it can be 
stated that long time periods of sampling does not cause more compound accumulation. This might 
be due to the high impacts of both diffusion and equilibrium exchange. As indicated by the results 
of the Tree Fruit model VC diffuse from the tree with a k rate of 1.1 per day. And according to the 
equilibrium exchange the difference between Kd-values of carbon felt and tree tissue means that the 
carbon may not reach equilibrium in the tree. When one compares differences between two carbon 
samplings put in place September the 13th and taken out October 11th and 8th of November, 
respectively, the carbon felt with the longest sorption period did not accumulate more compounds. 
As a matter of fact the carbon felt had no compounds at all. It was even not possible to get some of 
the carbon felt out of the tree core hole indicating that the trees are living organisms, which close 
open wounds i.e. tree core holes. Furthermore the results did not indicate that there was a preferable 
timespan other than 2 month did not seam to be optimal. 
 
The distribution of samplings taken against (A) and away from the stream (AS) seems to have a 
random distribution and therefore no clear conclusion could be drawn regarding higher 
concentrations in the side of the tree against or away from the tree.  
Overall the answer to the question: “Is passive sampling with activated carbon a better 
phytoscreening method than tree core sampling to locate a pollution with chlorinated solvents 
especially vinyl chloride?” the recommendation would be that more experiments are needed. The 
choice of season seems to be important and therefore investigations should be fulfilled to find out if 
carbon felt and ATD-samples are better in detecting vinyl chloride during spring and summer time.  
The screening tool should also be improved in regards to the method used in the field and especially 
in regards to sealing and sampling method since of the volatile VC is otherwise impossible to catch.

To prove if the carbon felt and ATD are as good passive samplers as tree cores, cis-DCE, TCE and 
PCE occurring more often than VC in winter, should also be taken into consideration when 
comparing the methods.
