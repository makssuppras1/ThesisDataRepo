Abstract
Extreme weather conditions in the northern regions have caused some issues re-
garding wind and snow behaviour. Sparsely developed urban environments are
especially experiencing relatively high wind velocities and snow depositions in spe-
cic areas due to shortage of buildings and topographic layouts in specic areas.
Largely amount of accumulated snow sometimes decreases the movement of pedes-
trians by blocking roads and creates issues around their houses by aecting the
entrances.
The knowledge of snow behaviour is somehow limited in the world. There are
few publications around the topic where experiments and investigations had been
carried out. But the topic is so wide that every investigation can consist of new
parameters and new ndings.
Greenland is a perfect example within the topic whereas most of the cities and dis-
tricts have sparsely development. The extreme weather conditions in Greenland
prevent airplanes to lift o and land, delay construction sites, stop transportation
in a certain area and more. To gain more knowledge and to have a better under-
standing of snow and wind conditions, a Wind Tunnel experiment will be made
where Qinngorput in Nuuk will be the center of focus.
The thesis will include analysis of weather data from the exact area by examining
the annual values of temperature, wind velocity and precipitation. The collected
weather data will be utilized in the experiment to achieve more realistic results.
A topographic model of the Qinngorput area will be made and used in the Wind
Tunnel experiment. The model will be exposed to the same weather conditions as
Qinngorput, where three types of our will be tested as articial snow and then
further analysed on which type gives a more realistic accumulation around build-
ings on the model. The rst experiment will be Smoke Visualization to show how
the wind ows around the buildings. Two tests will be performed regarding snow
behaviour; a test with moderate wind velocity and seeding speed (snow), and an
erosion test with high wind velocity without precipitation. All of the tests will be
later scanned with a 3D scanner and stored into a computer. The scanning results
will be able to measure the height of the snow accumulation with high precision.
The results of snow accumulation will be analysed where the formations of snow
will be compared between the dierent tests. The results show that the upwind
side of the buildings can be halfway buried under realistic extreme weather condi-
tions, i.e. worst case scenario. In all the cases, the accumulated snow in front of
the entrances of high rise buildings can't be blown away, even with erosion test.

1
Introduction
1.1
Problem formulation
The predictability of weather phenomenons have been improved since the techno-
logical tools for measurements and simulations had advanced in the modern world.
The measurements become more accurate throughout the years, and the predic-
tions for precipitation, wind velocity, temperature etc. are more precise and long
sighted.
One of the more dicult topics regarding weather phenomenons is when water
becomes frozen and falls to the ground as snow. Snow is a cold and sometimes
sticky substance that can both be very light and heavy according to its density
and can be easily moved by the lowest gust of wind, whereas rain is more simple
and is easier to predict.
The big challenges and issues of snowfall and snow accumulation in the cold climate
areas has been well-known for a long time; snow accumulations on roofs resulting
in collapsing, road blocks of too much snow, snow avalanches, short sighting due
to heavy snowfall and many more. There are several solutions that prevents the
issues from happening, and methods to minimize the risks of any harm to people
and their properties or simply make it easier for people to move through snow.
There are many factors that aects snow behaviour, but one of the biggest element
is the wind. Wind is an extremely important factor when looking at how the snow
accumulates. The slightest change in wind velocity can change the depositions of
snow, but it is still dicult to determine how exactly the snow will behave or form
under a specic situation. Wind is a more predictable natural phenomenon than
snow, there have been developed several methods and tools to calculate and mea-
sure the wind properties. One of the latest inventions is computational software
where wind behaviour can be simulated with dierent obstacles and dierent speed.
It is an important tool for, i.a., wind turbine construction, high-rise buildings, eval-
uation of the wind impact on an urban landscapes, designing of vehicles with low
air friction, and much more. The increased knowledge of technology has given the
wind engineers a fast and precise method to nd a solution of a problem regarding
wind, that way, any critical scenario can be studied and dealt with. The same
technological advance has not been developed yet for the snow accumulation stud-
ies. The snow behaviour concept is still too complex to comprehend. A snowake
is characterized as a group of water vapour particles in the atmosphere with a low
temperature beneath zero Celsius degrees, that accumulates with other particles
and form a snowake. There are innite number of shapes of snow crystals, but
only a handful of dierent types of snowakes (further described in Section 2.3).
Furthermore, there are many dierent types of snowfall, which makes the topic
more complex than it is.
1
==PAGE:10==
The combination of wind and snow behaviour is making the topic itself a pretty
wide concept, where many dierent types of weather phenomenon can occur around
the world. It depends on where on the globe the snow falls, in which altitude and in
which season of the year. It is therefore dicult to compare any scientic ndings
on this subject to the real world, where anything can happen. It is a relatively
new subject with a slower increase in knowledge. The scientic discoveries between
wind and snow can't really be compared to each other; the wind velocity can be
scaled to a smaller factor as in wind tunnel experiments, whereas snow is dicult
to scale. The wind behaviour can be simulated using computation software, but
there is no software that can simulate snow behaviour.
People in northern countries use their half of their lives in a snowy day, and it
becomes a common sense for them to construct their everyday lives according to
the winter periods where it gets a little more challenging to get by. The urban
development in the Arctic regions is somewhat slower than the cities closer to the
equator.
It is due to shortage of natural resources and the diculty in trans-
portation into the cities. The population in such countries is normally low and
the spacing between buildings is large, that makes person per square meters low.
The sparse urban layout in those environments causes some issues relating wind
and snow. One of the countries with the exact same issue is Greenland. Green-
land is the largest island on earth with an area of 2 180 000 m2 with a population
of 56 000 people (Statistics (2019)), resulting in a very low population density of
0.026 person/km2. This report will have a focus on one area, Qinngorput, located
in the Greenlands capital city, Nuuk. The built-up area in Nuuk is around 18 km2
big and roughly 18 000 people are now living in the capital city of Greenland. Ac-
cording to Statistics Greenland, there are 497 000 m2 of dwellings in Nuuk alone,
which is roughly 0.5 km2, and the number is signicantly low compared to the
total area of Nuuk. Considering the big dierence in area of dwellings and the
landscape, it is safe to assume that there must be a large distance between the
buildings, and the reason why Nuuk is a good place to investigate regarding wind
and snow behaviour.
1.2
Thesis statement
The weather in Greenland is sometimes unpredictable and intense, especially in
winter periods. The extreme climate conditions have making the cities dicult to
develop. The construction of buildings and other structures take relatively long
time to nish compared to the constructions in Denmark. The constructions are
always started in the summer time, where the foundation of the buildings is easier
2
==PAGE:11==
to create without further complications, and then the speed of construction de-
creases when the weather gets cold. The construction workers work all year, six
days a week, to maintain the construction site, shovel snow, and fasten some loose
objects. It is also dicult to determine where the buildings will be build due to the
ground conditions. There is a phenomenon in the ground called Permafrost, which
is frozen soil that can dry out when heated, and can cause severe deformations
resulting in loss in bearing capacity, or even formation of cracks and collapsing.
That's why, most of the buildings are constructed on solid ground where the foun-
dations reach all the way down to solid ground. It is very common to use explosives
to blast away a steep hill or big rocks and make it easier to construct a specic
building shape. It is one of the main reasons, the buildings are placed from each
other, developing a sparse urban environment.
Qinngorput is the newest district in Nuuks urban development. There are several
high-rise buildings in the area, where some of them are located on the highest peak
of a hill. The layout of the built-in environment is causing snow to accumulate
in specic places, derived from terrain morphology, building shapes and wind con-
ditions. It is common knowledge for the locals that the wind speed sometimes is
stronger in the Qinngorput, so strong that even the bus routes in and out from
the area must be cancelled.
The safety of pedestrians and vehicles are on the
line in some occasions, and that's a problem that needs further investigation by
studying how snow behaves with experiments. The project was developed by ABC
(Arctic Building and Construction), where snow deposition is the main issue at
the entrances of the new high-rise buildings in Unaaq beneath the tall buildings in
Pingorsuaq.
There are several publications about snow and it's behaviour. There is a report
from Japan (Koboyashi and Ishida (1978)), that had studied and wrote about how
snow erodes and accumulates in dierent wind speeds, where four types of clas-
sications were described: Deposition type, erosion type, equilibrium type, and
deposition/erosion type. This report mainly describes how wind-turbulences inter-
act with deposited snow. It was discovered that a typical wavelength and height on
a newly accumulated snow are respectively 10 m and 15 to 20 cm. This information
shows that snow accumulates even on a plain eld without any obstacles with the
help of wind.
Japanese scientists had studied snow behaviour in a long time. One of the ex-
periments is how snowakes break upon collision with a snow surface (Sato et al.
(2006)). The experiment was simulated in a 14 m long, and 1 m width and high
Wind Tunnel. Articial snow of dierent types were used in the experiment, and
the air temperature was -7 ◦C in order to maintain the density of snow. The -
nal conclusion of the report was that snowakes do not break when wind speed
is less than 2 m/s and totally decomposes into snow crystals when wind speed is

1.3
Objective
The main objective of this report is to make an experiment using closed circuit
Wind Tunnel to see how the wind ow aects snow accumulation on a landscape
model. The landscape model is based on the topography of Qinngorput, where
Pingorsuaq area is in focus. Weather data from Asiaq (Greenlandic Survey Orga-
nization) collected around Nuuk will be used in as references to temperature, wind
speed and direction, and amount of precipitation. There will be several tests with
dierent types of our as snow and dierent wind directions. One test will con-
sist of two parts; the rst part will be with low wind speed and continuous seeding
speed of our to simulate the weather as realistic as possible. The next part will be
with higher wind speed with the seeding machine turned o. The second part will
be called erosion test, it is to see how snow particles will create a formation around
the landscape and buildings, and to see where the snow accumulation is highest.
Both parts will be scanned with a 3D scanner to be able to measure the height of
accumulated snow on the whole model. It is dicult to say how the scaling of the
model and objects will be dened in the experiment. Especially regarding our,
which is really dierent from snow, but formation and deposition has shown very
similar to what snow does. It is therefore assumed that the experiments will be
carried out with realistic extreme weather conditions, where a worse case scenario
is studied. The procedure of the experiment will be as follow:
 Weather data analysis
 Making of the landscape model with a correct scaling
 Preparation of the Wind Tunnel by testing if the wind prole is close to
reality
 Use smoke visualization with laser to see how the wind ows above a targeted
area
 Wind Tunnel experiment with snow
 Scanning of the model
 Analysis of the scanning results
 Discussion of technological improvements in experiments

3.1.2

The making of the actual model is the most time consuming procedure of the
whole span of thesis. The whole model will be hand made and will be as detailed
as possible. It will be 140 cm in diameter, and according to the highest peak in the
drawings, which is 80 m from sea level, the model will be 20 cm in height without

There are many requirements in selection of the right materials. It is recommended
to use foam as material, because of the lightness in general and the roughness of
the surface. The surface of the material must be close to the terrain as possible.
If chipboard or any other material with low roughness had been used in the ex-
periment, the our might just be blown away with the slightest gust of wind. It
is ideal to order foam materials that are 1 cm in thickness and are at least 140 cm

4.3.1
Experiment procedure
One test in the wind tunnel experiment is a long process. It is a routine that will
be repeated many times over, that is why the exact procedure from the beginning
is needed to be described. The whole procedure starts with only the model in place
for the experiment. The experiment procedure is divided into three steps; Wind
tunnel test, scanning procedure, erosion test. Remember to write every parameter
down that might aect the experiment, such as temperature, relative humidity,
atmospheric pressure etc.
26
==PAGE:35==
Wind tunnel test:
1. The scanner is calibrated as the procedure in Appendix A.1. The scanner
will remain in its position the whole time.
2. Cover the scanner with a plastic bag to prevent our particles to enter the
equipment.
3. Rotate the model to the right angle of wind direction.
4. Place a ramp between the model and the opening of the wind tunnel to make
the wind ow continuous.
5. Adjust the right wind velocity with an anemometer, then turn it o.
6. Select a type of our and ll the seeding machine.
7. Start the seeding machine while holding a box under it and nd the needed
frequency.
8. Turn on the wind tunnel then remove the box under the seeding machine.
The duration of the experiment will already be decided at the beginning. If the
wind velocity and frequency of the seeding machine are low, then it will take more
than an hour till the wanted amount of accumulated snow is reached. After the
end of the rst test, stop the machines and proceed to the next step of experiment.
Scanning procedure:
1. Remove the ramp from the model, so the turntable can be rotated freely.
2. Rotate the model for its initial pose.
3. Turn on the scanner and close the curtains for the model.
4. Begin scanning procedure.
Erosion test:
1. Cover the scanner with a plastic back.
2. Turn the model to its previous angle with the right wind direction.
3. Turn the wind tunnel on with high velocity for a short time. 7 m/s and 10
min were used in this experiment.

5. Optional: Measure the height of accumulated snow in one spot to comparison
with the scanning results.

6
Conclusion and "Further Work"
6.1
Conclusion
The main objective of this thesis is to perform an experiment using Wind Tunnel
in order to nd out how wind can aect snow. In total of seven tests were carried
out where each test has dierent conditions than the other.
A smoke visualization test shows that snow accumulation between the buildings
can be expected. The turbulences become more intense above the buildings that
might be aect snow behaviour.
Three dierent types of our were tested in the experiment; Natron, Baking powder
and Potato our. One of the most important ndings is that Potato our is not
suitable in this kind of experiment. The Potato our was way too heavy and sticky
to control, that it was dicult to adjust the amount that falls through the seeding
machine. The width of the impact zone was too small relatively to other ours and
the test was stopped only after 4 minutes when the model was already lled with
our. The overall result regarding the types of our is that the Baking powder
has more realistic formation around the buildings. The slightly stickiness of the
powder forms an increasing slope upwards the buildings, like it will do in nature.
The behaviour of Natron is closer to dry sand.
