Abstract
Climate changes are becoming still more evident as the average global concentration of CO2
in the atmosphere has increased from 311 ppm in year 1950 to 403 ppm in 2016 [7] and the
annual, average, global air temperature has increased 0.8 ¶C since 1950 [16].
Buildings and constructions were responsible for 39% of the energy-related CO2 emission in
2017 [27] and it is estimated that in 2040 they can be 40% more energy eﬃcient. More than
a quarter of these savings can be achieved within space cooling, which is the fastest growing
building energy consumer due to many emerging economies in subtropical and tropical climate
zones [10].
A promising approach to fulﬁll this energy saving potential is the hybrid system design. It
combines a high supply of fresh, tempered air with elevated air speed generated by ceiling fans
to ensure an excellent indoor air quality and thermal comfort. Higher supply air humidity and
temperature than for conventional air conditioning systems enables a 30-50 % energy reduction
while the cooling eﬀect of the elevated air speed ensures high thermal comfort [28]. The purpose
of this thesis is to evaluate the adaptive comfort of a case study in Singapore, which is regulated
by such a hybrid system design.
The adaptive comfort was evaluated through the PMVeas index which has proven to be the
most valid for hot and humid climate conditions. It was found that the prevailing environmental
parameters for this comfort were the mean radiant temperature, linked with the air tempera-
ture, and air speed, which is why they created the foundation for the comfort evaluation.
The evaluation was based on the adaptive comfort simulated with the software TRNSYS, which
also simulated the mean radiant temperature and air temperature. The third parameter air
speed was provided by the Ceiling Fan Tool, which was developed as a part of the thesis to
approximate the ﬂow pattern of ceiling fan conﬁgurations.
To ensure that the comfort simulation was based on reliable data the simulated environmental
parameters were compared to the measured parameters from the actual case study.
It was proved that the strategy of the hybrid system design within the case study in Singapore
worked by providing an overall good comfort. The comfort was only perceived as slightly warm
without any elevated air speed with only 0.1 PMVeas outside the comfort range in most cases,
indicating that the passive shading systems and mechanical ventilation was performing well.
Furthermore, the rapid decrease of mean radiant temperature towards air temperature away
from the facade indicated, that even in a tropical climate could windows with good optical
properties limit the impact of radiation to a relatively small distance from the facade. Operating
the ceiling fans created an excellent perceived comfort between the fans but the coverage of this
area was highly dependent on the conﬁguration of these ceiling fans.
With these ﬁndings the ability of the hybrid system design to deliver high adaptive comfort in
a low-emission building, when the usage of ceiling fans was optimized, was conﬁrmed, and with
it the potential of the system to fulﬁll the energy savings within space cooling.

Resumé
Klimaforandringer bliver stadig mere tydelige, med den gennemsnitlige globale CO2 koncen-
tration i atmosfæren forøget fra 311 ppm i 1950 til 403 ppm i 2016 [7], og den gennemsnitlige,
globale luft temperatur forøget med 0,8 ¶C siden 1950 [16].
Bygninger og konstruktioner var ansvarlige for 39 % af den energirelaterede CO2 udledning i
2017 [27] og det er estimeret, at de inden 2040 kan bliver 40 % mere energieﬀektive. Mere end
en fjerdedel af disse reduceringer kan blive opnået inden for rumkøling, hvilket er den hurtigst
voksende forbruger af bygningsenergi grundet de mange spirende økonomier i subtropiske og
tropiske klimazoner [10].
En lovende tilgang til at opfylde dette energibesparelsespotentiale er det hybride system design.
Det kombinerer en høj tilførsel af frisk, tempereret luft med forhøjet luftbevægelse skabt af lofts-
faner for at sikre en fremragende indendørs luftkvalitet samt termisk comfort. Højere fugtighed
og temperatur i den tilførte luft sammenlignet med konventionelle klimasystemer muliggør 30-
50 % energireducering imens at den kølende eﬀekt fra den øget luftbevægelse samtidig sikrer
høj termisk komfort [28]. Formålet med dette speciale er at evaluere den adaptive komfort for
et casestudie i Singapore, som er reguleret af et sådanne hybrid system design.
Den adaptive comfort blev evalueret vha. PMVeas indekset, hvilket har vist sig at være det
mest brugbare for varme og fugtige klimaforhold. Det viste sig, at de fremherskende miljømæs-
sige parametre for denne komfort var den gennemsnitlige stråletemperatur, tæt forbundet med
lufttemperaturen, og lufthastigheden, hvilket er grunden til at de udgjorde grundlaget for kom-
fortevalueringen.
Evalueringen var baseret på den simuleret adaptive komfort med softwaren TRNSYS, som
ligeledes simulerede den gennemsnitlige stråle- og lufttemperatur. Den tredje parameter, lufthastighed,
blev simuleret af Loftfane Værktøjet, hvilket blev udviklet som en del af dette speciale for at
estimere bevægelsesmønstre skabt af speciﬁkke loftfanekonﬁgurationer.
For at sikre at komfortsimuleringen var baseret på pålidelig data, blev de simulerede miljømæs-
sige parametre sammenlignet med de målte parametre fra det egentlige casestudie.
Det blev bevist at strategien for det hybride system design fra casestudiet i Singapore fun-
gerede efter hensigt ved at levere en overordnet god komfort. Komforten blev kun opfattet som
lidt varmt unden nogen forøget luftbevægelse med kun 0,1 PMVeas uden for komfortområdet i
de ﬂeste tilfælde, hvilket indikerede at den passive solafskærmning og mekaniske ventilation fun-
gerede optimalt. Endvidere indikerede den fra facaden hurtige aftagelse af den gennemsnitlige
stråletemperatur tilnærmende lufttemperaturen, at selv i et tropisk klima kunne vinduer med
gode optiske egenskaber begrænse påvirkningen fra stråling til en relativ lille afstand fra fa-
caden.
Ved at operere loftsfanerne blev fremragende komfort opnået imellem fanerne, men
udstrækningen at det påvirkede område afhang i stort udstræk af fanernes konﬁguration.
Med disse fund blev det hybride system design’s evne til at skabe høj adaptiv comfort i en lav-
emission bygning bekræftet, når brugen af loftsfaner var optimeret, og med denne potentialet
for dette system til at realisere energibesparelserne inden for rumkøling.

1 Introduction
1.1 Motivation
Climate changes are becoming more and more evident in the public debate and with good
reason. According to the European Environment Agency the average global concentration of
CO2 in the atmosphere has increased from 311 ppm in year 1950 to 403 ppm in 2016 [7]. A
similar tendency has been observed in the annual, average, global air temperature by NASA
Earth Observatory, who has measured a 0.8 ¶C increase since 1950 [16].
Even though the topic is receiving more attention than ever, the phenomenon has been dis-
cussed on global scale for more than three decades. In 1992 the United Nations created the
Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), which led to the Kyoto Protocol in
1997, with the main goal of controlling the increase in emission of Green House Gases among
the 192 involved countries to a certain percentage [25]. In 2015 the Paris protocol was formed,
which states that the 185 member countries shall do their best to keep global warming well
below 2 ¶C increase from pre-industrial times [26]. Besides the two protocols, reducing climate
changes is directly or indirectly mentioned in several of the 17 Sustainable Development Goals
created by the UN in 2015.
Looking into how the above mentioned goals and visions can be fulﬁlled, UN’s Global Status
Report 2017 [27] states that buildings and constructions were responsible for 39% of the energy-
related CO2 emission, including upstream power generation, for this year. The International
Energy Agency estimates that buildings in 2040 can be 40% more energy eﬃcient than today,
and that more than a quarter of these savings can be achieved within space cooling, which is
the fastest growing building energy consumer due to many emerging economies in subtropical
and tropical climate zones [10]. One of the possibilities for realizing this energy saving potential
is found within the deﬁnition of the word cooling and with it, thermal comfort.
The human body works like a sensor, that constantly evaluates the surroundings based on a
vast amount of inputs. The body then communicates the collected data to the brain, which
concludes whether this environment is harmful or not. Thermal comfort is one of the evaluated
parameters, and is experienced when the body is in an equilibrium where it produces the same
amount of heat that it emits to the surroundings. This heat is exchanged through the four phe-
nomenons convection, conduction, radiation and evaporation, and the balance of this exchange
depends on the surrounding environmental conditions.
The environmental parameters that inﬂuence the thermal comfort within a space are air tem-
perature, air humidity, mean radiant temperature and air speed but common practise is to use
only air temperature as an indicator for comfort. A range of 20-24 [¶C] throughout the year
[5] is often the aim in order to create an environment that according to the speciﬁcations of
thermal comfort standards is comfortable. Standards, which are based on a heat balance model
developed for air conditioned spaces in mid-latitude climates, where low air speed and relatively
low air temperatures are preferred [28]. These values have governed the design of comfort in
buildings all over the world since the 1970’ies and inﬂuenced how people consider thermal com-
fort now a days. However, this thermal approach has proven insuﬃcient for explaining perceived
comfort in naturally ventilated buildings and hot and humid climates conditions, where it tends
to underestimate the cooling eﬀect of elevated air speed. To implement this eﬀect adaptive
comfort models have been developed based on extensive ﬁeld studies across the world and as a

reason for using this plugin together with the capability of linking the Grasshopper environment
with the TRNSYS engine.

3.2 Human Physiology
As mentioned in the introduction, a thermal comfort is experienced when the body is in
equilibrium; it produces the same amount of heat that it emits to the surrounding environment,
which is around 120 [W/m2]. This heat exchange depends on the internal conditions of the
body and the external conditions of the surrounding environment, and is possible through the
before mentioned physical phenomenons. Convection is the exchange of heat between the body
and the surrounding air, conduction is the transfer of heat between the body and surfaces it
is in direct contact with and radiation is the infra red heat exchange (long wave radiation) a
body or surface emits when it has a certain temperature or the short wave radiation received
from the sunlight. Evaporation is the exchange of energy embedded in water vapour between
the skin of the body and the surrounding air and through breathing.
Convection, conduction and radiation all inﬂuence the sensible heat load oﬀa space, which
is the kind of heat that humans feel through the dry bulb temperature of air. Evaporation
inﬂuence the latent heat load of a space, which is felt through the air humidity.
In case the body is not in a thermal equilibrium it has the ability to regulate its temperature
to meet the external conditions. If it ﬁnds itself too cold it can shiver, which causes the skeletal
muscles to shake, thus creating heat by expending energy. On the other hand, if it becomes too
warm it can sweat, which means that it releases water through the pores in the skin. On the
skin surface the water is exposed to the surrounding air, and as long as the water vapour pres-
sure is below the saturated vapour pressure, evaporation will occur. When water evaporates, it
changes phase from ﬂuid to gas which requires 585 [cal/g] and this energy comes from the body
itself, thus cooling it. When the skin is exposed to elevated air the evaporative cooling eﬀect
increases with the air speed, as the "old" air with higher humidity content that surrounds the
body is exchanged quicker with fresh air, that can repeat the process.
When evaluating thermal comfort, an average value of the environmental conditions surround-
ing the ankles, the waist and the head must be used. When a person is sitting, the respective
heights are 1.1, 0.6 and 0.1 [m] above the ﬂoor, while they for a standing person are 1.7, 1.1
and 0.1 [m] [2]. It is not necessary to ensure good thermal comfort in the whole space, since
some part are considered not to be occupied by people. The area that is occupied by people is
called the occupied zone deﬁned as the volume from the ﬂoor to a height of 1.8 [m], at least 1.0
[m] from outside walls and windows and 0.3 [m] from internal walls [2].
3.3 Comfort Indices
3.3.1 Air Temperature
As stated in the introduction is air temperature the most commonly used comfort index
around the world but this is a misconception as it does not indicate perceived comfort. Never
the less, meteorological institutes utilize the ambient air temperature to communicate to the
public whether it is going to be a warm day or not. Similarly for the indoor environment,
mechanical systems relies on the air temperature of the space as an indicator for the comfort
of the space.

When used in comfort context air temperature refers to the average temperature of the air
within the occupied space at a certain time and date. For mechanically ventilated spaces, an
air temperature of 20-24 [¶C], preferably 21-22 [¶C], is recommended in winter, and 23-26 [¶C] in
summer. However, an air temperature exceeding 26 [¶C] is allowed for no more than 100 hours
a year within occupied hours, and a temperature exceeding 27 [¶C] for no more than 25 hours [5].
3.3.2 Operative Temperature
The OT (Operative Temperature) was developed by A. P. Gagge, C. E. A. Winslow and
L. P. Herrington in 1937 and is the second most utilized comfort index for evaluating indoor
comfort. It is deﬁned as the uniform temperature of an imaginary black enclosure in which
an occupant would exchange the same amount of heat by radiation and convection as in the
actual non-uniform environment. Put in a simpler way, it is the average of the air temperature
and MRT (Mean Radiant Temperature), weighted by their respective transfer coeﬃcients, as
seen in equation (1). MRT depends on the amount of radiation that an occupant receives from
surrounding surfaces and radiant sources such the sun or radiators and will be explained further
in section 3.6.1. If the air speed is below 0.2 [m/s] and the diﬀerence between air temperature
and MRT is below 4 [¶K] the OT may be estimated as the average of air temperature and MRT

The two node model for the human body considers the core of the body as one node and
the skin as the other. The core is where the heat production as a product of the metabolic
rate takes place. This heat then leaves the core either as sensible and latent heat through
respiration to the outside air or via conduction between blood circulation and the skin. From
the skin a heat exchange with the environment takes place as sensible heat through radiation
and convection, and as latent heat through evaporation.

evaporation of the body. This value occurs when the whole skin area is wet, and is proportional

is covered with sweat. The insulation value of the clothing aﬀects the sensible heat exchange
and the permeability aﬀects the evaporation rate.

the cooling eﬀect of elevated air speed in hot and humid climate conditions, which is why this
model will be the foundations for evaluating the adaptive comfort.
3.3.5 Predicted Mean Vote Elevated Air Speed
ASHRAE 55-2013 introduced the PMVeas model (Predicted Mean Vote Elevated Air Speed),
which basically is the PMV index based on the SET model, which means that it considers the
cooling eﬀect of elevated air speed. This fusion produces an outcome within the 7 step scale of
the original PMV model, but enables calculations for conditions exceeding the aforementioned
thresholds of the PMV model and increases the accuracy of comfort evaluations when air speeds
are above the 0.15 [m/s] limit for still air. The PMVeas is calculated using the three steps be-
low; First SET is calculated based on the four environmental parameter (air temperature, air
humidity, MRT) as well as the clothing level, metabolic rate and the actual air speed, ranging
within 0.1 and 3.0 [m/s]. Then the SET is recalculated using an air speed of 0.1 [m/s] instead of
the actual one, and the average air temperature and radiant temperature are adjusted accord-
ing to the cooling eﬀect. Finally the original PMV is calculated using the same air humidity,
clothing level, metabolic rate, the still air speed of 0.1 [m/s], the adjusted air temperature and
the adjusted MRT.

3.3.6 Adaptive Comfort Model
The ACM (Adaptive Comfort Model) does not provide a comfort index like the previous
mentioned models. Instead, it is based on occupant behaviour and mentality. The approach is
such, that if an event occurs which causes discomfort to the occupant, the occupant will react
to the event by trying to restore the comfort [2]. The reaction may be to open a window for
cooling the space, if air temperatures are higher inside than outside, or to take oﬀa shirt in
order to reduce the insulation value around the body. The approach is also a recognition of
the impact of occupant expectancy and psychological adaptation on perceived comfort. This
means, that someone who traveled from an arctic to a tropical climate will perceive the ambient
temperature warmer the ﬁrst days compared to after a month, because the mind adapts to the
prevailing conditions. As a result of this realization, building codes such as ASHRAE 55-2013
and EN 15251 implemented the adaptive thinking by expanding the acceptable range of OT to
a 80 [%] acceptability limit of the initial value, and increase it linearly with an increasing mean
monthly ambient air temperature, as seen in ﬁgure 2.

3.5 Hybrid System Design
The case study in Singapore is a 7600 [m2], 5 ﬂoor building designed for 890 people, where
3500 [m2] are conditioned by the innovative hybrid system design.
Where a conventional air condition system only provides comfort by cooling the air, the hybrid
system uses a combination of tempered air, elevated air speed and natural ventilation. A con-
ventional air conditioning system is sealed oﬀfrom the ambient climate and supplies fresh air
with a rate of 13 [m3/h] per person [2]. As mentioned previously, the air temperature usually
is around 20-24 [¶C] and the humidity 11 [g/kg].
The hybrid system design provides 45 [m3/h] per occupant, thus providing excellent air quality
within the space. It utilizes a supply air temperature of 18 to 20 [¶C] with a humidity ratio
of 12.9 to 14.8 [g/kg]. The air temperature of the space ranges between 26 and 29 [¶C] and
the humidity from 15 to 18 [g/kg]. The system has no return air, and instead the supplied
air creates a higher pressure within the room which then spills into surrounding spaces, thus
minimizing inﬁltration. Windows can be opened by the occupants to provide a fresh breeze
from the outside, but otherwise the system relies on the elevated air speed from ceiling fans
Sebastian Glæsel Enevoldsen

to compensate for the higher air temperature and humidity within the space. Furthermore, an
essential part of the hybrid system design is the passive shading system. This includes extruded
facades, overhangs, external and internal louvres and windows with high optical properties, all
which reduces the internal heat gain from the outside.
Compared to a conventional air conditioning system, the hybrid system saves 30-50 [%] en-

3.6.1 Mean Radiant Temperature
The importance of MRT on the adaptive comfort was determined in section 3.4, which is
why it will be investigated in the following section how the value is calculated, measured and
simulated. This knowledge is essential for the comparison of measured and simulated MRT for
the case study in Singapore, which in the end will help evaluating the comfort of the studio.
The MRT is deﬁned as the uniform surface temperature of an imaginary black enclosure in
which a person would exchange the same amount of radiation as in the actual non-uniform
space. Put in a simpliﬁed way, the MRT for an occupant can be approximated as the sum of
each surrounding surface’s temperature weighted by their respective angle-factor. The angle-
factor is basically an indication of how big a percentage a certain surface takes up of the total
visible surface area from the occupants position in the space. If there are no strong radiant
sources (such as a radiator or a window) nearby the occupant, the MRT equals the air temper-
ature [11]. This indicates that the MRT always is closely connected to the air temperature of
the space, given the constant heat exchange between air and surfaces.

calculation that only requires the surface temperatures and the dimensions of the space.

mean surface temperature value of the surrounding surfaces, weighted by their respective angle
factor. Most surface materials have a high emissivity and a low reﬂectance. Therefore, the

A new zone revealed itself when multiple fans were running within the same space. A so called
middle zone was measured through higher air speeds in the space between fan jets. When both
fans are set to the same speed setting the following behaviour was registered in the middle zone.
For conﬁguration 1, the middle zone had a turbulent downward ﬂow created by the joining of
the two fan jets. For conﬁguration 2 an upward, well-mixed ﬂow appeared right between the
two fans, and for conﬁguration 3 the same upward ﬂow appeared, but this time released smoke
indicated that the ﬂow acted like two separated, fan-related loops.
For cases with a dominant fan speed setting, the upward ﬂow disappears in all conﬁgurations
and the ﬂow direction is similar to the one of a single fan case.
Besides the new zone, two fan interaction phenomenons were documented during the tests of
the multiple fan combinations. One was by the authors named ISR (Interactional Speed Ratio)
and basically describes that the ﬂow pattern of to consecutively running fans is not just the sum
of the individual ﬂow patterns. It was noticed that when two fans operated at the same time,
the joint airﬂow penetrated further into the still air zone providing a higher air speed for a larger
percent of the space. When the two fans operated with same speed setting, the accelerated air
from each fan with direction towards the other fan seemed to achieve a balance in between the
fans. With conﬁguration 1 and 2 a signiﬁcant increase of air speed was measured in the middle
zone compared to the surroundings, while for conﬁguration 3 no noticeable increase compared
to the surroundings was seen. This suggests that there is a limitation to fan centre distance
when it comes to fan interaction in the middle zone. However, the eﬀect of the ISR was not
only seen in the middle zone but even in the fan jet zone and ﬂoor zones of each fan.
If the weaker fan’s speed setting is low, then there will be no impact on the ﬂow pattern of

The other phenomenon was the skewness that some fan jets experienced during certain con-

the other fan would be running on an equal or higher speed setting, skewness of the fan jet

diminish towards the middle zone, implying that the skewness phenomenon also creates a more
uniform ﬂow pattern. As for the ISR phenomenon, the fan centre distance at conﬁguration 3
seemed to be the above the limit for which skewness occurs.
In general, the skewness of a fan jet increases the width of the jet while decreasing the pene-
tration depth.

4.1.2 Simulation Setup
To compensate for the buﬀer of thermal mass when running simulations in TRNSYS, it is
recommended to set the initiation date of the simulation at least three weeks prior to the desired
date. This way the temperatures of the building geometry will be correct by the time of the
interest.
The geometry and conditions of the small oﬃce in Munich were deﬁned using the TRNLizard
interface and the indoor conditions were simulated by TRNSYS with the purpose of comparing
the results with the measurements within the same space. Only the dimensions of the geometry
together with the weather conditions during the week of testing were possible to measure with
certainty. The rest of the conditions, such as the structure of the boundary geometry, boundary
temperatures, internal loads and inﬁltration rate were estimated based on observations. The
thermal and optical values of the windows were know, since the windows where renewed a few
years back.
The weather ﬁle Munich-Riem.iwec was manipulated to include the measured weather data for
the respective week of testing. The measured data was edited in Excel to ﬁt the text-format
of the weather ﬁle. Since all weather ﬁles are based on a sample rate of one hour, an average
hourly value was calculated from the ﬁve-minute-measurements. Then the total radiation was
subtracted the diﬀuse radiation to get the beam radiation. The beam radiation, diﬀuse radia-
tion and ambient temperatures were rounded to one decimal, the relative humidity to zero- and
the cloud cover to two. Then the original values for the respective hours were replaced with the
new ones, and the edited weather ﬁle implemented in the simulation.

Figure 31 shows the four MRTs, calculated from the measured OT during the rectangular
arrangement, compared to the corresponding simulated values. Throughout the day the simu-
lated values are 1-2 [¶C] higher than the measured, when accounting for the time delay found
in the Munich case study.
A reason for this is the Perez sky model mentioned in appendix 9. The model considers diﬀuse
radiation to be emitted from the circumsolar region, the horizon and then uniformly across
the sky dome. The higher the latitude of the locations the more accurate is this assumption,
since the lower the sun is on the sky, the longer is the rays path through the atmosphere and
the more will they be reﬂected. However, the latitude of Singapore is almost zero with the
country being nearly at equator, meaning that the sun always is high on the sky during the
year. Thus, the diﬀuse radiation is more concentrated at high zenith angles than lower ones.
When measuring the MRT within a space with vertical windows, TRNSYS will assume that
more diﬀuse radiation enters the space than in reality, which will result in a higher MRT.
The almost vertical increases in simulated MRT are, like mentioned before, due to the impact
of direct sunlight. The globes are not as sensitive to sudden changes in radiation because of the
thermal mass, which is why the measured MRT does not show this impact. The human body
recognizes changes in radiation immediately, so the incapability of the globe is not a decision
made for better accuracy but rather a limitation in physical design.
The temperature diﬀerence seems to be higher in the mornings which may be due to two rea-
sons. Even though no measurements of air velocity around the globes were performed, increased
air velocity is known to have been present. As mentioned in section 5.1.1, an inlet of tempered
air was observed to be located in between the four globes and a draft was felt from this during
the measurements. The inlet supplies tempered air throughout the whole day, but when the
mechanical system switches on in the morning the high temperature diﬀerence between the
supply air and the room air causes the supply air to ﬂow fast towards the ﬂoor. The formulae
Sebastian Glæsel Enevoldsen
54
==PAGE:64==
Technical University of Denmark
Case Study: Singapore Studio
for converting measured OT to MRT considers both natural and forced convection around the
globe and makes up for any air speed by increasing the MRT accordingly. The displayed graphs
considers an air velocity of 0,1 [m/s] which may have been higher during the measurements.
A higher velocity was used during the data processing, but it was observed that a signiﬁcant
increase did not make up for the 3 [¶C] temperature diﬀerence between measured and simulated
MRT. In stead the action increased the extremes of the measured values rather than increasing
the values in general. This indicates that the formulae underestimates the cooling eﬀect of
elevated air speed, and therefore that the globes should not be used when this is present.
The second reason lies on the window model provided TRNSYS. As seen in appendix 9, the
model does not consider the thermal mass of the window panes, because it is irrelevant for
typical windows with one or two panes and a surface area which is relatively small. Due to
increased regulations for building’s energy consumption, modern windows contain two or more
panes and can be produced to ﬁt into a glass facade. In this case the thickness of the panes
is designed to resist the large forces aﬀecting them. These three factors are all present in the
Singapore studio, thus the thermal mass of the panes becomes signiﬁcant for the simulation.
Since the panes have no mass in TRNSYS they heat up as soon as the sun’s rays hit them
which then leads to a rapid increase in MRT. In reality, it takes a couple of hours for the panes
to heat up which is displayed by the slower increase of measured MRT.
Because Type 56, seen in appendix 9, is programmed by Transsolar GmbH, it is improved
whenever well documented error are found throughout the projects. In the case of thermal
mass for glass panes, the importance was discover previously and is now being implemented in
the TRNSYS update.

of the cooling eﬀect from elevated air speed may have been a reason for the diﬀerence in ﬁgure

To summarize; TRNSYS is currently capable of predicting MRT in reality with an acceptable
accuracy. The implementation of thermal mass of glass panes will make the accuracy even
higher, and the inaccuracy of the Perez sky model is only apparent near equator. The function
for converting measured OT to MRT should improve the cooling eﬀect of elevated air speed,
but this is not a software problem. Finally, the diﬀerence in simulated and measured MRT is
always positive, meaning that the predicted MRT by TRNSYS will overestimate the discomfort
of a space ensuring that the comfort will be better in reality.

The pole was always orientated with the sensors perpendicular to the fan center. With this
setup, the pole would not aﬀect the measured air speeds and the sensors would, with their

setting and the sensors were orientated towards one of the fans, they would measure the highest
possible speed since there can only be one prevailing ﬂow direction.
For each measurement the following procedure was followed; Once the pole was positioned at
the required position it was left for 60 seconds to ensure that no external force obstructed the
ﬂow. When the 60 seconds passed, the measurements were conducted with a sample rate of
10 seconds for 90 seconds before the pole was moved to the next position and the procedure
repeated. Air speed measurements are required to be determined as an average of 3 minute
intervals with at least 18 samples according to [2], but in this case it was prioritized to measure
more fan conﬁgurations with what was assumed still to be an acceptable measurement interval.
When post-processing the data, the last measurement from the 90 seconds was removed, in
case the pole had been moved before the time concluded. The highest and lowest values were
also deleted, since the turbulent ﬂow of the fans always seemed to create a couple of velocities
outside the range of the majority. From the remaining six values an average was calculated and
used for representing the air speed of that speciﬁc point.

6 Ceiling Fan Tool
6.1 Introduction
With the OT as the most commonly implemented comfort index in miscellaneous building
codes, it has not been necessary to know the exact air speed within a space. Besides being
below the threshold for draft which was 0.2 [m/s] according to the PMV index, there were no
requirements from these. When ASHRAE implemented the PMVeas as an alternative to the
OT, the air speed within a space became relevant, but only to the extent that the speed was
assumed with no need for simulations to prove it. Since elevated air speed normally is created
with ceiling fans, and they create a rather turbulent ﬂow, assuming the air speed for the entire
comfort zone may seem far-fetched.
To base this value on more than assumptions, CFD software has so far been the option. The
issue with this type of software is, that when it comes to turbulent ﬂows and dynamic condi-
tions, which is the case for ceiling fans, the simulation run time is extremely time demanding.
A simulation for one ceiling fan at a certain speed setting may take from a couple of hours to
several days, depending on the mesh quality and whether it is dynamic or not, which does not
only make it expensive, but also diﬃcult to implement the results in a design process.
The conﬁguration of the ceiling fans is essential when it comes to ensuring the necessary air
speed that, together with the other three environmental parameters, provides a comfortable
space for the occupants. As the study by Liu et. al. documented, parameters such as fan
centre distance and fan speed setting dictates the air speeds of a space, and testing various
combinations is a process needed before the most optimal fan grid is discovered.
It was the complication of slow simulation time and many possible conﬁgurations that aspired
the idea of a new “Ceiling Fan Tool”. By reducing the amount of calculations behind the sim-
ulation the simulation time would be reduced equally, thus creating a more valid tool for early
design phases. In order to reduce the complexity of the calculations that are behind any CFD
software, it was decided to use to parametric interface of Rhino Grasshopper and a geometric
orientated approach to ﬂow patterns.
The results explaining the eﬀect of multiple fans operating at the same time in [20] were
limited by to relatively short fan center distance and room volume compared to the studio in
Singapore. Thus, it was decided that the early development of the tool would be based on
these results together with the ones from [31], which would provide information regarding the
behavior of single fans and the interaction of multiple fans with small center distance. Once
the measurements in Singapore had been conducted, the results will be used to implement the
unobstructed ﬂow pattern of a single ceiling fan together with the interaction of multiple fans
with high center distance.

6.3 Comparison With Measured Air Velocities From Singapore Studio
This section is a comparison between the simulated results produced by the current version of
the Ceiling Fan Tool and the measurements obtained in section 5.2.2. The diﬀerences between

patterns for fans with a higher center distance in section 6.4.
As mentioned in section 6.1, two main diﬀerences between the physical layout in [20] and in the
Singapore studio were expected to aﬀect the results; the size of the two spaces and the distance
between the fans. The studio in Singapore has a ﬂoor plan and a volume much larger than
the test facility, which means that the inﬂuence of the boundary geometry on the measured air
speeds is equally smaller. Also, the fan center distance of 3.7*D in Singapore compared to the
maximum distance of 2.1*D in [20] results in a reduced interaction between the fans.
Besides the diﬀerences in physical layouts, the installed fan types were also diﬀerent. The im-
pact of this diﬀerence is not clariﬁed and will make a reliable comparison of the two experiments
limited. Due to this diﬀerence, the variations in measured air speeds for a single fan at diﬀerent
speed settings between the experiment in Singapore and [20] will not be analyzed. Instead, as

6.4 Improving Tool
Based on the analysis in section 6.3 the improvement of the Ceiling Fan Tool was continued.

From the comparison of single and multiple fan ﬂow patterns, it was understood that the
increased fan center distance and room volume resulted in an almost negligible interaction of
the fans outside of the middle zone. Thus, the approach with adding up overlapping ﬂows of two
fans was changed to instead select the highest of the multiple air speeds applicable for the spe-
ciﬁc grid point. This way, the ﬂow patterns did not aﬀect each other outside of the middle zone.
With the new knowledge regarding the middle zone, changes had to be made to the way it
was modelled. Until now, it had been deﬁned as the whole area between the fan jets with the
same ISR value applied at each height. This approach was changed into seeing the zone as a
wall, perpendicular to the fan center-to-center line and with an air speed declining from the
center of the zone and outwards, as explained in ﬁgure 64 and 65.
During the measurements, only multiple fans running at the same speed setting had been
tested, which caused the middle zone to appear exactly in between the fans. This appealed to
a simpliﬁed, geometrical approach where the zone is modelled to always appear in the middle
of the fans. However, since the Ceiling Fan Tool operates with three diﬀerent speed settings,
the location of the middle zone is assumed to change accordingly. The upward ﬂow appears
when two opposing ﬂows with equal airspeed collides, which according to Bernoulli’s principle
creates a high pressure in the collision zone. Since this is happening along the ﬂoor and the
dynamic ﬂow from the fans keeps providing an approaching ﬂows with higher airspeed from
behind, the only direction the high pressure can be relieved is upwards. When one fan changes
to a higher speed setting, the point where the air speeds of the ﬂows equal each other shifts
closer to the fan with a lower speed setting. The intention of the Ceiling Fan Tool was not to
rely on complex CFD calculations, so the location of the equal air speeds had to be deﬁned in
simpler way.
The solution was found in the surface, which z-coordinates were used for modelling the air
speeds for each grid point. With the new approach of selecting the highest air speed whenever

8 Discussion
Validation of Measurements
The foundation of this thesis lies on the conducted measurements and the reliability of their
outcome. Of the four environmental parameters included in the calculation of thermal comfort,
MRT and air speed probably are the most diﬃcult to measure, for diﬀerent reasons.
MRT is based on radiation exchange with the environment, which is why it depends on the
size, orientation and location of the occupants as well as the color of the clothes. To measure
the same amount of radiation with a sensor that a person would receive, it requires carefully
designed parameters such as total surface area, emissivity and shape, in order to imitate the
human body. For calculating the MRT in this thesis, a sensor developed by Transsolar with a
diameter of 0.07 [m] and emissivity of 0.82 was used to measure OT from which the MRT could
be calculated. The sensor has been tested thoroughly and has proven reliable for measuring the
OT but it is known to be sensitive to air movement. A globe with a larger diameter has a more
stable heat exchange through convection in elevated air speed but it tends to overestimate the
MRT. The smaller globe was chosen because the oﬃcially acknowledged formulae provided in
building codes used to calculate MRT from the OT considers both natural and forced convec-
tion, which should compensate for the sensitivity of the globe to air movement.
Due to the diﬃculty of deriving an exact MRT from measurements within a space, the case
study of the oﬃce in Munich served as preparation for the measurements in the Singapore stu-
dio. This provided the opportunity to get familiar with the globe sensors and the rest of the
equipment and to get an understanding of how the MRT changes within a space throughout
the day. The limited usage of the oﬃce and the absence of mechanical systems made it easier
to control the environment, thus limiting the amount of sources of error. This case study also
helped ﬁguring out how to store and process all the data, which sample rates to use and how
in the end to compare it all.
The studio in Singapore represented a more complex environment, with, by the time, unknown
data for the supply air from the mechanical ventilation and a more frequent usage than in
Munich. The equipment setup was observed throughout the day, but left alone in the mornings
and evenings, which means that windows could be opened and curtains closed in this period,
without being noticed. The preparations in Munich ensured conﬁdent handling of the equip-
ment and the data but it did not prepare for such unknown variables.
As seen in the results a larger diﬀerence between measured and calculated MRT, specially in
the mornings, was present for the studio than the oﬃce, which is assumed to be caused partly
by elevated air speed around the globe sensors. This diﬀerence appeared despite the usage of
the conversion formulae from the building codes, which either shows that the formulae underes-
timates the cooling eﬀect of elevated air speed on the sensor, or that the utilized sensor behaves
diﬀerently at higher air speeds than the sensor the formulae was created for.
Air speed is more straight forward to measure and plenty of equipment is available on the
market with a high measuring accuracy. The diﬃculty of measuring it is not due to the equip-
ment but because of its dynamic behavior and unpredictability, and air speed elevated by ceiling
fans further adds turbulence to the complications. For valid measurements the sampling rate

and the orientation of the sensor is essential. Because of the turbulent ﬂow from the ceiling
fans, the air speed is constantly changing, even when no external forces are aﬀecting the ﬂow.
Thus, the air speed that represents a speciﬁc point during certain conditions is not the last
measured value but the average of all the values measured within the sampling time.
When measuring an air speed representing the ﬂow of a ceiling fan for comfort studies, the
highest values are used. Because of the measuring range provided by the sensor used for this
study, it was necessary to predict the direction of the prevailing ﬂow end then orientate the
sensor towards this direction, to ensure the highest air speeds were measured. Once the direc-
tion was determined, the attachment of the wires to the pole and the positioning of the pole
was important, because they could aﬀect the ﬂow measured by the sensor.
The sampling time utilized for the measurements was 30 seconds shorter than the one speciﬁed
in [2], and the ﬁnal value was an average of only 6 measured values due to the decided proce-
dure. This could result in an air speed that does not represent the correct airﬂow of the ceiling
fans.
Unfortunately, it was not possible to practise the handling of the equipment and to investigate
the eﬀect of sensor orientation and pole setup on the measured air speed before going to Sin-
gapore. The fan type and conﬁguration present in the studio were also diﬀerent from the ones
examined in [31] and [20], which means that there was no source of reference to compare with
the measured air speeds. This basically means that until a study of the same conditions within
a more controlled environment or a detailed cfd-simulation is performed, the reliability of the
measured air speeds is unknown.
An argument for validating the measured values is, that throughout the whole experiment no
changes were made to the setup of the equipment or the data collecting procedure, meaning
that all the values are gathered under the same circumstances. Moreover, after post processing
the gathered date and visualizing it in Grasshopper, the observed behaviour of the ﬂow patterns
for the various fan conﬁgurations coincided with the theory known beforehand.
Validation of Software
No experience with TRNSYS neither TRNLizard was possessed before the beginning of this
thesis, which required a steep learning curve during the ﬁrst months. The combination of the
open source ﬁle structure and the help from experienced colleagues enabled the transparency
that was required for understanding the theory and calculations behind the simulation. TRN-
SYS is a software that has been developed over several decades with every formulae in there
tested and proven by competent people. Even though the software is in a constant process of
validation and improvement, based on the inputs from anyone within Transsolar, the purpose
of this part of the thesis was not to look for errors in the existing code. The intention was to
understand the the reason behind the performed simulations to an extend, where strange results
or diﬀerences between the measured and simulated values could be explained and corrected by
changing the user-deﬁned inputs.
From the iterative process of comparing simulated and measured results, the severity of sim-
ulating an existing, uncontrolled space became evident. The user is able to provide a huge
amount of inputs to TRNSYS which all come down to assumed numbers, and the ﬁrst lesson
was that the results of the simulation only are as good as these assumptions. Assuming the
occupant load in watts or the insulation value of a brick wall with an unknown construction
is nearly impossible, which makes it diﬃcult to run a valid simulation. The key for providing

Ceiling Fan Tool
The Ceiling Fan Tool was developed with the purpose of providing a simpliﬁed alternative to
CFD software in the early design phase. One may argue that CFD software is complex because
ﬂuid dynamics are complex, and that a simpliﬁed, geometric rather than physical, approach
deﬁes the usability. However, from what has been learned throughout this thesis, such a tool
is a valuable alternative to the current CFD software, when it comes to optimizing the fan
conﬁguration of a space. At the present stage, CFD software such as OpenFOAM and ANSYS
is capable of simulating ﬂow patterns from ceiling fans, but even here is simulation time often
prioritized over accuracy. Despite this, simulating the ﬂow of multiple fans within a space still
is rather time consuming which makes it a disadvantage to implement in a design process for
testing out various fan conﬁgurations. This eﬀect of fan arrangement and speed setting on the
air speed within a space is proven in [20], and the importance of the air speed on the perceived
comfort evaluated in this thesis. This argues that a tool capable of testing out diﬀerent fan
conﬁgurations is a valuable mean in the early process of design for providing the best comfort
within a space, before CFD software is utilized to conﬁrm the ﬁndings.
Due to the simpliﬁed approach, the tool does not consider the inﬂuence of specially walls on
the air ﬂow. This means that it is more accurate within large spaces with signiﬁcant distances
between fans and room walls and that it will underestimate the air speeds within spaces with
relatively small volumes.
It is created upon the measurements conducted by [20] together with the measurements con-
ducted in this thesis, giving it a limited basis. The data from [20] is based on 28 combinations
of fan speed settings and fan center distances but the results apply to cases with a small room
geometry and are not as applicable to larger spaces, as seen in this thesis. The experiment
in the Singapore studio provided a useful insight in the behavior of ﬂow patterns within an
unobstructed space with large fan center distance and for more than two fans. However, only
one fan center distance was investigated together with one speed setting combination for three
diﬀerent amounts of fans, meaning that simulations of ﬂow patterns from fans with a diﬀerent
speed setting than 5 and a fan center distance diﬀerent than 4.8 [m] are based on assumptions.
Moreover, the usage of diﬀerent fan types between [20] and the measurements in Singapore
makes a fusion of the gained knowledge diﬃcult. This means that the tool should either be

used for simulating fans center distances below 3.15 [m], in which case it will rely on the ob-

no experience with the equipment for measuring air speeds was acquired before conducting the
actual measurements, meaning that the utilized equipment setup and measuring process may
not have been optimal. This indicates that the current version of the tool should be used for
understanding the behavior of ﬂow patterns, such as the occurrence of the middle zone and the
limited spreading of elevated air speed outside of the fan jet above 0.1 [m] from the ﬂoor, rather
than predicting the air speed within a space. Before the tool can be used for such predictions,
it must utilize more measurements of diﬀerent speed settings and fan center distances.
The geometrical approach of tool was chosen because it is simple to model and takes advan-
tage of the visual and parametric interface in Grasshopper. However, the approach also requires
many steps within Grasshopper to model since each height and speed setting has its own air
speed function and zone diameter, which again depends on the amount of running fans and
their interaction. Furthermore, due to the planar approach no vertical interpolation of the air
speeds has been performed, which limits the result output to one of the four ﬁxed heights.
The ideal procedure from here on would be to create a mathematical representation of the air
ﬂow from a single fan; collecting all of the derived information about the behavior of the air
speeds and boiling it down to one equation, which would then be able to calculate the air speed
for any given height at any distance to the fan center. There may not be such a single equation
but the vision of a more mathematical approach rather than geometric is possible.
Evaluating Adaptive Comfort
Finally, the adaptive comfort created by the hybrid system within the studio was evaluated.
The undertaken procedure ﬁrst of ensuring that measured and simulated results coincide and
only then to analyze the values in a comfort context is considered trustworthy. The measure-
ments were performed during the wet season but as seen from the measured weather data, the
days were unusually clear, generating more radiation than expected. December is the month
where the sun is furthest south on the sky, which for the studio means the highest exposure
to direct radiation. As a result, the MRT was measured in extreme conditions which indicates
that it will be lower for the rest of the year.
The measurements took place during occupied hours but because the building was initiated
recently it was estimated that the occupancy only was 50 [%] of the maximum expected occu-
pancy, at the most. Fewer occupants creates a reduced internal heat load, which aﬀects the air
temperature of the space.

9 Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis was to evaluate the adaptive comfort of the case study in Singa-
pore, which is regulated by a hybrid system design that utilizes the cooling eﬀect of the elevated
air speed to provide high comfort for the occupants.
The adaptive comfort was evaluated through the PMVeas index which has proven to be the
most valid for hot and humid climate conditions. It was found that the prevailing environmen-
tal parameters for this comfort are MRT, linked with the air temperature, and air speed, thus
the accuracy of which these parameters are simulated with was investigated, before they were
used in the comfort evaluation.
The MRT and air temperature were simulated with the software TRNSYS and the results
were then compared with values measured within the case study by a globe sensor developed by
Transsolar among other equipment, to determine the reliability of this data. When comparing
the two values a maximum diﬀerence of 1-2 [¶C] was observed which is considered acceptable
given the uncontrolled environment. This conﬁrmed that the simulated MRT and air tempera-
ture is valid for using in a adaptive comfort evaluation of the space.
The air speed is currently not simulated in TRNSYS, thus a substantial part of the thesis
was to develop the Ceiling Fan Tool to provide TRNSYS with approximated air speeds based
on speciﬁc fan conﬁgurations. The concept of this tool was to utilize the parametric interface
in the software Rhino Grasshopper to develop a geometrical approach for modelling simpliﬁed
steady state air ﬂows from ceiling fans. The strength of the tool would be to test out various fan
conﬁgurations in order to discover the ﬂow pattern that provides the best comfort in a fraction
of the time it would take CFD software. Once the best conﬁgurations were discovered, the
exact air speeds would then be simulated by other software. Measurements from [20] were used
as foundation for the tool, and the simulated air speeds were then compared to the measured
from the case study in Singapore. Signiﬁcant diﬀerences were observed between the simulated
and measured values due to the larger distances between the fans, and the fans and surrounding
walls in the case study. These observations were then used to improve the theory behind the
tool. After this process, a deviation between simulated and measured values below 0.1 [m/s]
was seen for 100 [%] of the investigated area in a single fan case 1.1 [m] above the ﬂoor, for 80
[%] of the measured area in a two fan case, and for 60 [%] of the measured area in a four fan
case. It was then concluded that the tool was capable of simulating the air speeds for various fan
conﬁgurations satisfyingly, but that the simpliﬁed approach and the limitation of comparable
measurements limits the reliability of the tool.
With the validation of the simulated values for the environmental parameters, the adaptive
comfort could ﬁnally be evaluated. It was noticed, that the highest MRT within the investi-
gated time interval occur at the southwestern corner of the studio and along the south and
west facade with a maximum temperature between 31-32 [¶C]. Moving from the corner 3.5 [m]
towards east the temperature drops around 2.5 [¶C] and another 1 [¶C] moving 2 [m] north,
bringing it down within 1 [¶C] of air temperature. This showed that the inﬂuence of radiation
from outside was limited to a rather small area of the studio due to the high performance of
Sebastian Glæsel Enevoldsen

the windows and passive shading. The air temperature stayed within 27-28 [¶C] while the me-
chanical system operated, which was within the range of the hybrid system design. It indicated
that the mechanical system has been dimensioned correctly to handle internal and external
loads. Finally, the simulated air speeds for the fan grid installed in the studio showed values
between 0.3-1.2 [m/s] covering most of the area between the running fans, creating an excel-
lent comfort around 0.2 [PMVeas]. However, this distribution would only occur when the fans
were running in clusters of 4 and at speed setting 5. Meanwhile the comfort was excellent
between the running fans, a distance of 3.1 [m] between the fans and the west facade and 1.9
[m] between the fans and the south facade, combined with the highest MRT in the same area
created a slightly warm thermal sensation along the facades. Since this area took up only a
small percentage of the whole studio, it could be argued that the conditions created a good
overall adaptive comfort for the studio. Should the comfort along the facades be improved
while keeping a high comfort for the rest of the studio, the Ceiling Fan Tool showed, that by
changing the fan speed setting for the northern row to high and reducing the east-west aligned
center distance between the fans to 3.85 [m], an excellent comfort of 0.1 [PMVeas] could also be
achieved along the southern facade. However, these conﬁgurations were outside of the measured
conditions and would require to be conﬁrmed by either more measurements or CFD simulations.
To summarize, this thesis proved that the strategy of the hybrid system design within the stu-
dio in Singapore worked by providing an overall good comfort. The comfort was only perceived
as slightly warm without any elevated air speed with only 0.1 [PMVeas] outside the comfort
range in most cases, indicating that the passive shading systems and mechanical ventilation
was performing well. Furthermore, the rapid decrease of MRT away from the facade towards
air temperature indicated, that even in a tropical climate high performing windows could limit
the impact of radiation to a relatively small distance from the facade. Operating the ceiling
fans created an excellent perceived comfort between the fans but the coverage of this area was
highly dependent on the conﬁguration of these ceiling fans. With these ﬁndings the ability of
the hybrid system design to deliver high adaptive comfort in a low-emission building, when the
usage of ceiling fans would be optimized, was conﬁrmed. The potential of the hybrid system
design to help achieve the UN climate goals is evident, and hopefully this will be realized by
more people in the future.

Rhinoceros is a software developed by the American company Robert McNeel

The software is a 3D modelling tool for Computer-Aided Design (CAD),

Grasshopper is a visual programming language developed by David Rutten at Robert McNeel
Associates in 2007, thus it is implemented as a plug-in for the Rhino software. The tool is
programmed in Python as open-source, which means that the source code is available to the

TRNLizard

TRNSYS
TRNSYS is the software used by Transsolar to simulate daylight- and thermal conditions
within a building, performance of a mechanical system as wells as urban conditions.
The
software was originally developed by an American stationed company and intended only for
simulating the performance of mechanical systems. Transsolar became co-developers in the
90es where they created the Type 56, which is a model for detailed multizone building simu-
lations. With the introduction of type 56 TRNSYS quickly became a renowned software for
engineers to simulate indoor conditions and performance of buildings all over the world.

The Simulation Engine (kernel) performs the actual simulation. It is programmed in Fortran
and the engine is compiled into a Windows Dynamic Link Library (DLL). The kernel reads the
Deck File (*.dck) and reads which Types that are used and how they are connected. It is also
open to additional ﬁles such as weather ﬁles.
The simulation engine is called by an executable program TRNExe.exe which also implements
the online plotter. TRNBuild is the multi-zone building component which models the thermal

diﬀuse) entering through a window is immediately transformed in a convective heat ﬂow rate for
the internal air. This fraction depends on the presence of internal elements with a very low heat
capacity (furniture, etc.). The remaining direct solar radiation is distributed according to the
distribution coeﬃcient GEOSURF, which represents the fraction of total direct solar radiation
that strikes each internal surface. If the value is 0 the direct radiation is distributed the same
way as the diﬀuse radiation (by absorptance weighted area ratios) [22].
