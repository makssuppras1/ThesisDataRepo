Abstract
Motivation: For many years the goal for politicians has been to reduce greenhouse gas emis-
sions to combat climate change. The extreme electricity prices during the recent years have
given economic and political incentives to speed up the green transition and a sustainable
energy system is more important than ever. With the change in power production from the
traditional power plants to renewable energy sources some issues appear. Renewable energy
introduces instabilities to the energy system and the demand for energy storage increases. One
of the ways to produce a more stable energy production is by a hybrid power plant. The focus
of this project is on a hybrid power plant with heat production.
Method: A literature study is conducted and relevant findings concerning the energy markets,
heat pumps, and hybrid power plants are presented. From the literature study the methods
for experiments with a heat pump, for modeling of the hybrid power plant, and for modeling
of heat production are presented.
Experimental data is collected in collaboration with HOFOR from their large-scale heat pump
FlexHeat. With a basis in the dynamic and static behavior of FlexHeat a heat pump with
flexible power consumption is built in Simulink. The heat pump model is connected to a wind
farm in a hybrid power plant. The hybrid power plant with heat production will be tested on
three levels; the flexibility of the heat pump, the reference values, and the fluctuating wind.
Results: The simulated data of the heat pump is validated by the similarities and differences to
the data from FlexHeat. The first test level shows the flexibility of the heat pump. The second
level shows the response of the hybrid power plant and the heat pump at different reference
values. The last test level shows the response of the control system in the hybrid power plant
for a time series of wind speeds. It was found that the flexibility of the heat pump could deliver
active power support to the grid at wind data with average wind speeds at 7m/s and 11m/s.
The power production from the wind power plant was very stable at 14m/s so in cases of high
wind speeds the heat pump will not deliver much flexibility.
Conclusion and perspective: This project shows the possibilities of sector coupling between
the district heating and the electric energy grid by a hybrid power plant with heat production.
The benefits of the energy system to utilise the flexibility of heat pumps in the district heating
are pointed out. Hybrid power plants with heat production can possibly substitute electric
energy storage to some degree and help the energy system with stability. The benefits for
the district heating are the possible reduction in the CO2 footprint by utilization of renewable
energy and possible improvement of the business case for heat pump projects. In the tests of
==PAGE:4==
wind data, the scaling of the heat pump was too small for the largest changes in wind speed.
The size of the heat pump in a hybrid power plant is a compromise between the utilization of
the heat capacity and delivering flexibility.

Introduction
In this chapter, the motivation for the project is introduced along with related work. From this
standpoint, the problem definition of this project is described.
1.1
Motivation for hybrid power plants and heat pumps
Increasing levels of renewable energy are demanded by politicians to move towards an energy
system with zero greenhouse gas emissions. The concern about climate change has motivated
politicians for the green transition for years. In 2022 Danish politicians voted for a new climate
agreement [9]. The agreement sets to ensure better conditions for renewable energy production
and to enable four times as much energy from solar power plants and onshore wind energy.
The agreement also sets to put 4 GW of offshore wind energy areas out to tender before 2030.
The installed capacity of 2.3 GW offshore wind energy and 4.7 GW onshore wind energy cor-
responding to 43% of the Danish energy consumption was already installed by the end of 2021.
Besides the Danish climate agreement, the European Union is also working towards greener
energy production [10]. With the war and extreme energy prices, the concern of energy security
has further brought the green transition into focus. With the transition towards more sustain-
able power production, the power system is constantly changing and getting more complex as a
result of the increasing level of renewable energy sources such as wind energy and solar energy.
When the increasing renewable energy production replaces conventional power plants, some
concerns regarding the operational security and stability of the system arise.
One of the issues with wind energy and solar energy is the mismatch in timing of the power
production and the power consumption and thus the need for storage. Another significant issue
is when the synchronous machines (power plants) are cut out, the electric grid loses stabilizing
units. Some of the markets in the power grid and the future prediction for their development
are described in the scenario report from Energinet [2]. In this report, it is made clear that the
demand for power reserves and grid stabilizing units is rising.
A method to deliver stability in the power grid by renewable energy sources is to combine mul-
tiple renewable energy power plants in Hybrid power plants (HPP). HPPs show very promising
abilities to provide grid support through the complementarity between different electric units
and by a combined control system. The HPP will often include energy storage to help stabilize
power production. Wind Europe’s definition of a hybrid power plant is: ”The term HPP refers
to a power-generating facility that converts primary energy into electrical energy and which
consists of more than one power-generating module connected to a network at one connection

When it comes to power reserves, power can be provided by multiple units including energy
storage, flexible power production, and flexible energy consumption. Often the power reserve of
the hybrid power plant is a battery and usually, the stability of the power supply relies strongly
on the energy storage.
Another way to support the electric grid is to use large heat pumps as flexible loads. Heat
pumps have typically been operated as stable loads and produced heat without consideration
of the electric grid. The main goal of the heat pumps has previously been a substitution of
heat production powered by gas and coal. With the high energy prices, the flexible operation
of heat pumps has become feasible. In some time periods, the heat pumps should be turned off
due to the high energy prices while the heat pumps might even get paid to use power in other
time periods. The typical heat pumps delivering a constant heat output can be converted into
more flexible loads and future heat pumps will be built with flexibility as an essential ability.
The heat pumps’ flexibility has the additional capability to help the electrical grid. With an
increasing level of renewable energy in the grid flexible consumption will be crucial for the
energy grid.
Another important factor for the energy grid is the electrification of the district heating. In
the report ”The future district heating supply in the capital area of Copenhagen 2050 [12]” six
scenarios are set up to describe different possibilities for heat production in the future. The
scenarios have different degrees of electrification. When the typical heat production is converted
from combined heat and power plants to completely electrified heat production, the connection
between the electric grid and the district heating will change. Today the district heating has
a maximum electric production of 1,200 MW and consumption of 500MW. In fully electrified

2.1
The electric energy grid and markets in Denmark
The electric grid in Denmark includes many customers as well as many power production units.
The power producers can bid into multiple markets including the frequency support markets,
the day ahead spot market, the intra-day market, and the balancing market. Energinet is the
transmission system operator (TSO) and has the responsibility of the balance in the system
[18].
The Danish market is split into two geographical areas DK1 and DK2. DK1 is the west of
Denmark and DK2 is in the east. The areas are split by both geography and frequency market-
wise. The areas have originally been two different synchronous markets. Today power can be
transferred between the areas by a DC connection. This project will focus on the opportunities
in the greater Copenhagen area and the market in focus will be on the frequency market in
DK2.
Most of the energy is traded in the day-ahead Spotmarket. The day-ahead market closes at 12:00
the day before the operational hour. The geographic limits in the electric energy grid have to
be taken into account when all the bids are matched. When all bids are matched the spot prices
along with the power purchased from each bid will be released. When the day-ahead market
is closed the intra-day market is open. The intra-day market is open until one hour before the
operational hour. After the intra-day market has closed the responsibility of balancing power
consumption and production in the grid lies on the transmission system operator. The TSO
can activate the different power reserves from the frequency support market to keep the grid
in balance. In Figure 2.1 the time plan of deadlines to take part in the different markets is
presented for DK2.

2.2
The district heating network in Denmark
In Copenhagen, the district heating network covers 98% of the heat demand and 64% of Danish
households are supplied with district heating [20][21]. It is important for district heating in
Denmark to produce energy-efficient, low-carbon, and cost-effective heating. The Danish energy
policy aims for a completely renewable energy supply by 2050. To reach this goal the district
heating has to implement new technologies to produce heat with renewable energy and at the
same time work to reduce heat production by a reduction of the heat loss in the network. Some
of the new technologies used in district heating will be electrical heat pumps, electric boilers,
geothermal heat, and heat storage.
The district heating company in Copenhagen HOFOR has already built three large-scale heat
pumps with low-temperature energy from seawater, groundwater, and sewage water as energy
sources. The Flexheat heat pump has a capacity of 800kW heat and electric boilers of 200kW.
The Novozymes heat pump has a capacity of 3.6 MW heat and the SVAF heat pump has a
capacity of 4MW heat. Three additional heat pump projects are planned. Tietgensgade will
have a heat pump capacity of 20 MW heat [22]. Kranparken will have a total heat pump
capacity of approximately 30MW heat and HCV will have 200MW electrical boilers and a total
heat pump capacity of 30 MW heat [23][24].
In district heating the hot water is sent to the consumers by the transmission and distribution
networks. The consumer receives the heat at the forward temperature and sends it back with the
return temperature. The forward temperature is typically higher in the winter and lower during
the summer. The minimum forward temperature is normally at 60◦C to avoid bacterial growth
[25]. In the district heating the forward temperature is typically 115◦C in the transmission
network and 90◦C in the distribution network. Two other sets of forward temperatures are
defined as low-temperature networks and ultralow-temperature networks.
In the low-temperature networks, the forward temperature in the transmission network is re-
duced to 90◦C, and in the distribution network, it is reduced to 70◦C. The ultra-low tempera-
ture network has a forward temperature of 65◦C in the transmission network and 50◦C in the
distribution network.
The heat losses in the network will be reduced when the district heating may deliver heating at
lower temperatures. To lower the temperature in the district heating network different measures
can be taken such as new radiators and insulation of older buildings. The low-temperature
network is already existing in some parts of Copenhagen including Nordhavn and Vesterbro
[26]. The forward temperature in the area of outer Nordhavn is even below the definition of low-

2.3
Hybrid power plants
This section presents the concept of hybrid power plants and the benefits they can deliver to
the electrical grid. With the benefits and principles of hybrid power plants introduced, the
related work is presented. The focus of this project is on the control architecture of a hybrid
power plant described in ”Hierarchical control architecture of co-located hybrid power plants
[14]” and the Simulink model created with the hierarchical control structure from the article.
The model in this project will be build with this Simulink model as a template.
2.3.1
Benefits and concept of hybrid power plants
Hybrid power plants are power-generating units with more than one type of power generation
connected to one point in the grid. Wind Europe describes different combinations of wind
power plants, solar power plants, and batteries as electrical energy storage. The hybrid power
plants are colocated and share a grid connection. The hybrid power plant can supply a higher
number of full load hours at the connection which is also described as an increase in the yearly
capacity factor. The higher capacity factor may increase the utilisation of the transformer,
the converter, and the connection capacity due to the negative correlation of wind and solar
resources [11]. On the other hand, the over-planting of renewable energy production will lead
to some level of curtailed energy. To obtain the high capacity factor, the grid connection will
not be scaled to full production. In periods of high power generation, it may exceed the grid
connection capacity and some energy production has to be curtailed. The loss of renewable
energy production has to be taken into account when the capacity of each power-generating
unit in the hybrid power plant is optimized. The level of curtailed energy can be reduced by
energy storage.

Hybrid power plants

The concept of the hybrid power plant is described in the article ”Component sizing of a utility-
scale hybrid power plant [28]”. In the article, a hybrid power plant consisting of a solar power
plant, a wind power plant, and a battery has been optimized. The goal of the optimization is to
find the optimal capacity of each technology to increase the feasibility of the investment. The
article finds that the hybrid power plant with a battery is more feasible than the stand-alone
wind farm, solar power plant, or hybrid power plant with solar and wind power.
Wind Europe describes multiple other benefits from hybrid power plants such as reduced infras-
tructure investment costs, reduced electricity balancing costs, more stable power output, and
less curtailed renewable energy [11]. The stability of the power from the hybrid power plant is
increased with the negative correlation between wind power production and solar power produc-
tion. The stability is an advantage for the grid and if the hybrid power plant includes electric
energy storage (EES) the stability can be increased even further. Electric energy storage can
also reduce the curtailed power and increase the flexibility of the hybrid power plant. With
more stable and flexible power production, the hybrid power plant will be a better partner for
the grid and the revenues can be increased when the energy is sold at times with better energy
prices in the energy markets.
2.3.2
Control architecture of the template hybrid power plant
The hierarchical control structure of the Simulink model for the template hybrid power plant is
illustrated in Figure 2.3. The model is built up with a multi-level hierarchical control structure
consisting of 4 levels: the HPP EMS level, the HPP Control level, the Plant Control level, and
the Asset control level. Each level plays a key role to ensure good control of the HPP but when
more levels are added the system becomes more complex and the control response time for
some of the levels becomes much longer thus making the control action slower. In the following
4 subsections the individual control levels are described based on the theory presented in [14].
HPP EMS Level
The EMS level is the highest level in the control hierarchy. EMS is short for Energy Management
System, and the purpose of the EMS is to ensure that maximum revenue is generated by the
hybrid power plant. This is achieved by optimizing the energy sold by the hybrid power plant,
thus bidding into the energy markets. The optimization of the EMS algorithm has to take
the physical limitations of the plant, regulations, weather forecasts, and current operations of
the plant into consideration. The EMS can bid in all the electricity markets including the day
ahead spot market, the balancing market, the intra-day market, and the reserve market. When
the EMS bids into multiple markets it is able to more easily maximize the revenue. An example
could be that the forecast of the weather change and the EMS might have either sold too much

more precise and the EMS can sell more or less power in the markets. Since the EMS is the

HPP Control Level
The next level in the hybrid power plant is the HPP control level. The role of the HPP control
level is to determine the power dispatch of each technology in the power plant to ensure that it
fulfills the tasks, like ensuring that the reserve sold by the EMS is available to be deployed when
needed. Furthermore, it ensures that the sold power is delivered in the specified time period
taking ramping constraints into consideration as well as ensuring that it is operating within
desired power, reactive power, and voltage levels determined by the system operator. Besides
that, the HPP controller also provides the EMS with the necessary data needed to perform the
best possible optimizations. In other words, the HPP controller is the supervisor distributing
the task to the individual plant controllers, whilst answering to the director (the EMS) [14].
Plant Control Level
The third level of the hybrid power plant is the plant control level. The plant controller controls
one specific technology in the hybrid power plant such as a wind farm, a solar power plant, etc.
The controller receives a reference signal from the HPP controller, the job of the plant control
level is to ensure that the assets are generating the power determined by the HPP control level.
This is achieved by using a PI controller and information on the maximum power capacity for

Asset Control Level
The last level of the hybrid power plant is the asset control level. It is the lowest and fastest

control, and reactive power control. These controllers are the fastest as they directly control
the different technologies in the HPP. To avoid counteracting adequate communication between
each layer must be established [14].

Alongside the hierarchical control structure presented in the previous sections, the authors of
the article have also developed a HPP Simulink model based on the approach. An overview of
this model can be seen in Appendix A.
The Simulink model is built up of three systems. It includes the Power System Model (PSM),

the power system in the PSM. The PSM includes models for nuclear, hydro, steam, and wind
power plants in order to simulate a power system with multiple types of power plants.

the model when compared to the architecture.

and temperature are increased (1-2).
The heat is then released to the heat system in the
condenser (2-3). The fluid continues to the expansion valve where the temperature and pressure
are decreasing further (3-4) before the fluid is returning to the evaporator to begin the next
cycle.
The heat from the heat pump can be delivered to the heating system in different fluids and for
different purposes. The one-stage small heat pump can deliver hot water or air for residential
use. For larger heat pumps, the heat can be delivered as hot water for district heating.

In many large-scale heat pumps, the heat pump is built with multiple stages. An example of a
two-stage heat pump is FlexHeat in Nordhavn. One of the differences between the simple heat
pump and the two-stage ammonia heat pump is the two stages of throttles and compressors.
The two-stage heat pump has a low-stage compressor and a high-stage compressor. Another
difference is the three steps of condensation in the two-stage heat pump. At Figure 2.11(a) the
diagram of the two-stage ammonia heat pump is illustrated. Next to it at Figure 2.11(b) the
log(p)-h diagram is illustrated for the two-stage ammonia heat pump. In the log(p)-h diagram
the change in specific enthalpy and pressure can be seen between each stage in the refrigeration
cycle.
The refrigeration cycle in FlexHeat starts in the low-stage compressor. The pressure of the
vapor is increased (1-2) and mixed with vapor from the state (9). The mixed state (9*) is split
into vapor (3) and liquid (10) in the intercooler. The vapor (3) enters the high-stage compressor
where the pressure is increased further (3-4) before it enters the condenser. The working fluid
enters the three steps of condensation (4-8) consisting of the desuperheater, the condenser, and
the subcooler where the heat is transferred to the district heating. The subcooler only receives
liquid due to the receiver. The pressure is then decreased and the subcooled liquid enters the
intercooler.
The pressure of the liquid from the intercooler (10) is decreased in the throttle before entering
the separator. In the separator, the stream is split into liquid (12) and vapor (1). The vapor
enters the low-stage compressor and the liquid enters the evaporator where it is partly evapo-
rated (12-13) before it enters the separator. The suction line is placed between the separator
and the low-stage compressor. At a fast ramp down droplet formation in the suction line can
be a limiting factor of the speed for ramping down.
2.5.4
Flexibility of heat pumps
To deliver heat with a flexible power consumption the dynamic response time of the heat pump
is crucial. The response time and dynamic behavior of the heat pump can be changed by many
different factors such as the working fluid, the control method, and the type of mechanical
components.
The choice of the working fluid in a large-scale heat pump can possibly change the COP of
the heat pump as well as the dynamic response. The components will be built in different
ways depending on the working fluid. Some companies argue that CO2 heat pumps have better
flexibility than ammonia heat pumps due to the smaller size of the components and the smaller
amount of refrigerant [32].

2.5.5
Previous tests of FlexHeat
Previous tests to examine the COP and dynamic behavior of FlexHeat have been conducted

The steady-state test explores the average power input and heat output from the heat pump.

output were found for each steady period of 20 minutes.

to deliver FCR-N to the grid. As described in section 2.1.1 FFR and FCR-D are the frequency
reserves with the fastest response times which are not realistic for a heat pump. FCR-N has
an activation time of 2.5 minutes [8]. Therefore it is on the edge of the possible flexibility for
FlexHeat and the ability to deliver FCR-N is highly interesting for HOFOR.

3.1
Experiments with FlexHeat
The relevant knowledge for a combination between a HPP and a HP will be examined in a new

results for FlexHeat as well as the official test of pre-qualifications for frequency reserves from

The new experiments will include dynamic tests based on the previous test results and the new

3.1.1
The dynamic test
The goal of the first test is to examine the dynamic response of the heat pump when it undergoes
the official test of FCR-N from Energinet. The test from Energinet will show if the heat pump
is able to ramp up and down in order to deliver FCR-N. The heat pump has to reach new power
set points within limits from Energinet. As described in Section 2.1.1 FCR-N is a symmetrical
product. Therefore the power reserve has to be delivered as a positive and a negative ramp
from the working set point. The test has the maximum reserves at the power capacity of 45%
and 95%. The working set point is set in the middle at 70%. Furthermore, two set points of
55% and 85% take part in the test. In Table 3.1 the time intervals for each ramp and each
stable value of power input are listed. The time for all the ramps is 150 seconds which is the
maximum limit for the heat pump to reach the new power set point. The stable power value
for each time interval is the minimum time to keep a given power set point stable. At the
start of each ramp, the heat pump is set to the next power set point and the heat pump will
automatically reach the set point as fast as possible.

3.2
Modeling of a heat pump
To create a model of a heat pump the dynamic behavior can be modeled in different ways. An
example of a modeling method is the Fluids package in Simulink as mentioned in Section 1.2.
Three other examples of methods to model heat pumps are modeled in EES, Modelica, and
optimization.
EES and Matlab are used in an example of a flexible large-scale model created by Mads Pagh
Nielsen and Kim Sørensen. The paper describes an hourly steady-state model as well as a
dynamic model of a large-scale ammonia heat pump. The paper concludes that large-scale
heat pumps can be used for ancillary services. They concluded that the limiting factor for the
ramp-down of the heat pump was the chance of liquid formation in the evaporator [34].
Another type of model is developed by Wiebke Meesenburg in Modelica. This model is used to
analyse FlexHeat and the limiting factors of flexibility. In this model, the limit of the ramping
time was also found to be due to liquid formation. At fast ramping droplets would start to
appear in the suction line [6].
Other types of models are found for thermostatically controlled loads providing power reserves
to the grid. Here optimization is used to provide ancillary services to the grid by a change in
power consumption towards a flexible load. A model of the thermostatically controlled load is
modeled with a resemblance to a battery. The model includes thermal resistance and thermal
capacitance [35].

3.5
Test levels
The control system of the hybrid power plant will be tested at different levels of complexity.
To test the model 3 different testing levels are set up. The first level tests the flexibility of the
heat pump, the second level tests the reference values of the decision algorithm, and the last
test ads fluctuating wind to quantify the response time at multiple steps.
3.5.1
Level 1: The heat pump model
In the first level, the limits for the flexibility of the heat pump are researched.
After the
experimental results of FlexHeat have been presented, the heat pump model is validated against
the experimental data of FlexHeat. The dynamic response of the heat pump model includes a
validation of the power input and the heat output of the heat pump.
The heat pump will be tested further at different setpoints and forward temperatures at test
levels 2 and 3. The change of temperature in the heat pump will have a dynamic response
corresponding to the change in heat output and power input at the new setpoint in the look-up
table. The heat pump has not been tested for the dynamic response at changes in temperature.
3.5.2
Level 2: Reference values
In the second level, the hybrid power plant is examined at step changes. Different setpoints
are given to the model and the hybrid power plant is tested to see if it is controlling the heat
pump and the wind farm as expected by the decision algorithm. All the decision scenarios
of the decision algorithm are tested by two tests. The first test is of the mode with the heat
production at the highest priority and the second test is of the mode with the grid at the highest
priority. To test all possible decisions of the algorithm a time series will change the priority of
the algorithm, the EMS value for the grid, and the available wind power. The wind speed is
set to a constant value of 18m/s in all scenarios but the available wind power is set directly to
different setpoints. The EMS reference value for the heat pump is set to 0.2 p.u. related to the
hybrid power plant during all test cases.
Test of reference signals in the heat priority mode
The first test includes a time series to test all cases of reference values from the dispatch function
when heat is prioritized. This includes priority 0 where the heat will be produced all though the
available wind power is below the EMS value of the heat pump and priority 1 where the heat
production is limited to a maximum power uptake equal to the available wind power. Each set
point will be set after a time interval long enough to reach a steady state. The EMS value for

Discussion
This chapter discusses the results and findings. The results from the experiments of Flexheat
showed the improved ramping of FlexHeat since the previous experiments of the heat pump
prior to the rebuild. The heat pump has improved the response time and except from ramp 3
and 4 the heat pump reacts faster than the requirements from Energinet for FCR-N. The heat
pump was 49 seconds to slow at ramp 3 and 36 seconds to slow at ramp 4.
The number of cylinders was changed at a capacity at approximately 60%. The change hap-
pened at a higher capacity than expected. The change in the number of cylinders was found
at 45% in the previous test. The capacity can change due to operating conditions such as heat
source flows, or due to the fact that the heat pump has not been sipped since the previous
tests.
If HOFOR want to get approved to deliver FCR-N, it might be relevant to sip the heat pump to
investigate if the capacity level for change in the number of cylinders will be changed. Another
way to increase the ramping speed for the heat pump could be to compensate for the delay
of the heat pump by using the electric boilers at FlexHeat. This was not tested during this
thesis due to the fact that the control system of FlexHeat is currently controlled by manual set
points. If the software is changed it could be an interesting test to the combination of the heat
pump and the electric boilers.
After the experiments were conducted in this project, the TSOs have published new pre-
qualifications [37]. The new demands and pre-qualifications will be valid as from the 1st of
September 2023 in Denmark, Finland, and Sweden and from the 1st of January 2024 in Nor-
way [38]. HOFOR might have to complete the new test program to be approved to deliver
frequency reserves,
Since the change in the number of cylinders has not been implemented, the actual delay of the
heat pump will be longer in reality than the simulated delays when the power output passes
60% of capacity. When the heat pump is ramping from 0% to 60% the number of cylinders will
also change from 4 to 8 cylinders as described in the theory. The second change in the number
of cylinders is not included in the model or the test data as the experiments did not include a
power set point below 45% capacity. As described in the assumptions, the dynamic response of
the Simulink model for change between temperature set points and below the lowest measured
power set point is set to follow the interpolated data.
Besides the change in the number of cylinders, the dynamic behavior of the heat pump follows
the experimental data for the behavior of FlexHeat nicely as illustrated in the validation graphs
Page 55 of 94
==PAGE:68==
June 30, 2023
showing that the Simulink model receives the same set points as FlexHeat.
When the heat pump is integrated into the HPP it is integrated as an open loop. This will
result in some degree of mismatch between the reference power set point and the power uptake
of the heat pump. The mismatch appears due to the dynamic behavior of the heat pump. If
the Heat Pump is put in a closed loop in the Hybrid Power Plant, the difference could be met
by either a change in the power to the grid or a change in the wind power production over a
short period. This could especially be relevant at larger ramps for the heat pump, eg. at large
changes in the available wind speed or when the control mode is changed.
The integration of the heat pump into the dispatch function in the hybrid power plant included
multiple control modes.
At the priority of the grid, the available wind power in the hybrid power plant is in some cases
above the EMS value for the grid and the heat pump can use the power to produce heat. In
real-life the available power for heat production might not be at the same time as the heat
demand. In these situations, flexible consumption and storage will play a key role.
If the control mode is either set to prioritize power to the grid or prioritize the heat production
but limit it to available wind power, the heat pumps will be producing heat from renewable
energy. This will reduce the CO2 footprint for the district heating. When heat pumps will
produce larger parts of the heat in the future, flexibility in power consumption will become
crucial for the electric grid.
The control mode with the priority of heat production with power from the grid was also
implemented. If a heat pump is delivering heat to the district heating this ability might be
relevant in peak hours of the heat demand.
When the hybrid power plant was tested in the power priority mode, the heat pump showed
the ability to help the hybrid power plant deliver the EMS power to the grid. The heat pump
could be turned off at EMS values for the grid below the available wind power. This will be
helpful when the electric grid has a high demand for power. The heat pump could also help
the hybrid power plant avoid curtailed energy when the EMS value for the grid is below the
available wind power.
The flexibility of the heat pump could help the hybrid power plant with the curtailed energy
and meet the grid requirements of active power. The benefit of the heat pump depends on
the wind speed. At very large variations in the wind speed and at changes in the mode, the
ramping of the heat pump will give a large delay from the power reference of the heat pump to
the actual power uptake. At the largest variations in wind speed, the scale of the heat pump
Page 56 of 94
==PAGE:69==
June 30, 2023
was too small in this project. The size of the heat pump is a compromise between the optimal
help to deliver the EMS power to the grid with minimum curtailed energy and increasing the
feasibility of the heat pump by maximizing the utilization of the heat capacity of the heat
pump. At high wind speeds the heat pump can not help the grid much by flexibility but in the
case of an overplanted hybrid power plant a constant load of a heat pump might help avoid
curtailed energy.

Conclusion and further work
This chapter concludes the project and possible further work will be suggested.
6.1
Conclusion
The main objective of this thesis was to develop a control methodology to coordinate a wind
power plant and a heat pump inside a hybrid power plant to support the power system. This
objective has been accomplished and validated by the tests of the model. The tests validate
the heat pump model and the hybrid power plant at reference values and at real wind data.
To achieve the main objective of this thesis and to answer the two research questions, six tasks
were defined and accomplished.
The first two tasks included the research of the energy markets and possible coupling between
the energy sectors. The relevant findings concerning the electric energy markets, district heat-
ing, flexible heat pumps, and hybrid power plants are presented. The literature study gives a
general introduction to the limits, benefits, and possibilities of each subject. From a basis in the
literature, it was decided to perform new experiments for FlexHeat and use the data to build a
heat pump model. A deeper understanding of the control architecture of a hybrid power plant
and the foundation for the new hybrid power plant model was found in the literature study.
The third and fourth tasks were to perform the experiments of FlexHeat and to build a model
in Simulink with the analysed data from the experiment. The Experiments were constructed
by information from the previous test of FlexHeat and the pre-qualifications from Energinet.
The experiments show the dynamic behavior necessary for the thesis but they also examine the
ability to deliver FCR-N with FlexHeat for HOFOR. The new experiments for the heat pump
FlexHeat have been successfully set up and conducted. The dynamic behavior of heat pump
has been found to be on the edge of the requirements for FCR-N from Energinet. Especially
the waiting time when the number of cylinders is changed has been found to be a limiting factor
in FlexHeat. The waiting time was also found in the literature but different capacities were
measured for the point of change in the number of cylinders. The capacity for the change in the
number of cylinders is dependent on the heat pump components and the operating conditions.
It can be concluded that it will be relevant to consider the components and the scaling of the
heat pump in future large-scale heat pump projects.
The dynamic behavior of the heat pump and the response time for ramping up and ramping
down will be crucial for heat pumps delivering frequency reserves directly but also for heat
pumps included in hybrid power plants. The heat pump was modeled in Simulink with a look-

model was calibrated to the experimental data of the dynamic experiment. The heat pump

The fifth task was to integrate the dynamic heat pump model into a simplified dynamic model
and control architecture of a hybrid power plant. A new dispatch algorithm has been developed
to prioritize either heat or grid preferences and control the power input to the heat pump, the
wind power production, and power to the grid. It has been validated by tests of different set

The sixth task was to perform investigations in Simulink to illustrate the flexibility of a HPP

with wind data. The research questions of the thesis were:
Can the heat pump help to reduce curtailed energy for the hybrid power plant?
Can a heat pump help a hybrid power plant to deliver the EMS power to the grid?
It can be concluded that a heat pump can reduce the curtailed energy for a hybrid power plant
when the EMS value for the grid is below the available wind power. At the wind speed with an
average of 7m/s the heat pump can provide flexibility to avoid curtailed energy. At the tested
data with the average wind speed of 7m/s the heat pump capacity is large enough to obtain
the surplus wind power. At the tested data with the average wind speed of 11m/s the change
in wind speed is too large at one point for the capacity of the heat pump. At 14m/s the heat
pump can not provide a large difference since the wind power production is simply very stable
due to the high wind speeds and the pitch control of the wind farm providing the wind power
at the maximum capacity. The heat pump will only help obtain otherwise curtailed energy in
scenarios of low EMS values for the grid.
It can be concluded that a heat pump can help the hybrid power plant to deliver the EMS
power to the grid. When the EMS value for the grid is above the available wind energy the
heat pump can be turned off when the hybrid power plant is prioritizing power to the grid
above power to the heat pump.
The tests of the hybrid power plant focusing on the curtailed energy and on meeting the grid
references were all conducted at the priority of power to the grid above power to the heat
pump. The dispatch algorithm also included the control mode with heat production as the
