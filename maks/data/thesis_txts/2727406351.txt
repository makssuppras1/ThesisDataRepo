Abstract
The goal of this thesis is to investigate whether a low cost, portable light sensor in combination with
machine learning can compete with more costly and less portable equipment. More specifically, the
goal of this thesis is to calibrate an Integrative Lighting Sensor (ILS) through the use of simulation
and experimentation, combined with data analysis and machine learning, to achieve the best possible
results.
The experiments done to gather data for the simulation include a setup with a monochromator to
identify the spectral response of each ILS channel. Through the use of known spectral power distribu-
tions (both measured and gathered), the response of the ILS can be simulated.
A spectrally tuneable lamp was developed. It can physically simulate an SPD. Thus a large num-
ber of SPDs similar to the known SPDs have been measured with the sensor and with a spectrometer.
Thus a lot of data with actual responses was generated, while also having an SPD from a referenced
spectrometer corresponding to each ILS measurement.
Data have also been gathered from non-simulation experiments. Several light bulbs were measured
with the ILS and a referenced spectrometer, including fluorescent light bulbs. The direct measure-
ments on these are important, since they are very hard to simulate, given their relatively narrow peaks.
The acquired data have been processed in a machine learning framework that includes regularized
linear regression, clustering and classification. This was done in order to reconstruct SPDs from ILS
measurements. Based on this framework, the algorithm can identify light from different sources and
reconstruct the SPD accordingly.

2
Introduction
In this report it will be investigated whether the ILS can be a cheap alternative to other calibrated,
more expensive measurement devices. The investigation will be supported by structured collection of
data and application of machine learning methods.
2.1
Motivation
Recently part of the industry has pushed towards Human Centric Lighting. Human Centric Lighting
includes Circadian Lighting, which is lighting that has proper amounts of light in the part of spectrum
that controls our circadian rhythm. [3]
Proper exposure to this light decreases melatonin levels based on the exposure (melatonin suppres-
sion) [6]. Light has an influence on ”certain affective disorders, sleep problems, and circadian rhythm
disruption” [19].
Lower melatonin levels during the day can lead to increased melatonin during the night, which has
several health benefits [14].
When conducting studies about lighting in e.g. hospitals [27] the data collection is among other
factors limited by expensive equipment. A setup involving a large spectrometer and a computer is
often not allowed in a room during patient occupancy. A cheap and small sensor like the ILS could
facilitate a larger scope to these studies. The detector of the ILS costs less than $10 USD.
The size and cost also allows the ILS to be worn to measure lighting exposure throughout the
day for individual people instead of rooms. Thus the effects of screens and local illumination can be
analysed as well. The cost and size make the ILS useful for much broader purposes.
2.2
Basic Working Principle
The ILS detector only has 8 narrow channels in the visible spectrum. There are two other channels:
One broad channel and one in the near infrared. Thus a proper spectral power distribution (SPD) is
not provided by the sensor output. Instead counts for each of the 10 channels are provided. These val-
ues can then be used for Spectrum Reconstruction (prediction of an SPD), more about this in Chapter
3. A machine learning model will be trained on a large data set and will be able to reconstruct an
approximate SPD based on these 10 values. More about the ILS in Chapter 5.
Thus a large amount of data is needed to get the sensor to be accurate (machine learning often
relies on a lot of data to be accurate). Therefore a spectrally tuneable lamp has been developed to
physically simulate different SPDs. This lamp uses Spectral Matching to tune the output of the lamp,
to match a given SPD. More about this in Chapter 3.
Other ways to collect and simulate data will also be described in this report.
The basic way the training works is shown in Figure 1. This creates a model, which can then be
used to reconstruct an SPD, 2. Additional data can be generated through simulation, Figure 3.

2.3
State of the Art
A company called nanoλ makes small Bluetooth compatible spectrometers. The cheapest spectrometer
from nanoλ has 200 filters (compared to the 10 of the ILS) and cost over $850 USD. This device also
uses spectral reconstruction, since the filters are not so narrow, as to directly produce a proper SPD
[15].
Other companies like Gigaherz-Optik have relatively cheap grating based spectrometers, but these cost
even more than $850 USD.
Spectrum reconstruction using cameras has also been done. This allows for hyper spectral imaging[10],
but cameras have less filters and quickly get more expensive than the ILS.

3
Spectral Matching
3.1
Motivation
As mentioned previously, sometimes it is necessary to create a custom SPD. For this project it was
needed to simulate different SPDs containing daylight, LED light, incandescent light or combinations
hereof. The way a custom SPD is created in the report is called Spectral Matching. Spectral Matching
is the art of combining the SPD of several individual lightsources, to generate another lightsource.
Typically this is done with a large set of narrow LEDs. The light of these narrow LEDs can then be
combined to create a custom SPD.
As described in Chapter 4, a lamp with 16 LED channels was developed. These 16 channels cover
the visible spectrum. By varying the contribution of each of these 16 LED channels, it is possible
to simulate many different SPDs. Spectral Matching is the optimization of the settings determining
the individual LED-channel contributions. In more technical terms, spectral matching optimizes the
combinations of different SPDs to best simulate a target SPD given some constraints and metrics of
evaluation.
3.2
Theory
The SPD of the 16-channel lamp is the sum of the SPDs of individual 16 channels (assuming proper
mixing of the light and no significant temperature change). The SPD of each of these 16 channels
are known in advance, see Figure 9. Most of the spectral change caused by changing current is know
as well. The effect of temperature will not be modelled in this report, but is known to also change
the emission SPDs of LEDs [18] [21]. The lamp will mostly be used in low current conditions for this
report, minimizing these shifts. It will also be cooled with a heat sink and a fan.
Since the resulting SPD is the sum of the 16 SPDs, it can easily be calculated.
St(λ) = ΣiαiSi(λ, αi)
(1)
where St(λ) is the total SPD, αi is the weight of the i’th channel and Si(λ, αi) is the SPD of the
i’th channel.
To achieve the optimal match, one can search the 16-dimensional space of factors, αi to minimize
the difference from the resulting SPD and the target SPD according to an evaluation constraint (Eval-
uation Metric, described below).
For a space this large, a grid search is not possible. Thus optimization algorithms were used. The
two optimization algorithms used were Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) and Genetic Algorithm
(GA).
3.3
Genetic Algorithm
Genetic Algorithm (GA) [11] works by evolving ”candidate solutions”. These are a list of possible
solutions, with properties that can be varied. Each iteration, often referred to as generation, will be
generated by taking the best candidate solutions from the previous generation and applying a random
change, a ”mutation”. These are evaluated again, and the best candidates survive. Typically the best
candidates will also advance to the next generation without mutations, so a found minimum will not
be undiscovered.

3.4
Particle Swarm Optimization
Particle Swarm Optimizaiton [5](PSO) works by having ”particles”, PSO’s corresponding candidate
solutions. These particles move around in the search space, representing possible solutions. Each
iteration the position is updated, and the corresponding solution is evaluated.
The position of a
particle is updated based on three things:
• The best solution found among all the particles.
• The best solution found from the individual particle
• The previous velocity of the particle
The position is updated with the following equation:
V (i+1)
n
= 2r1wV in + c1r2(PBin −Xin) + c2r3(GBi −Xin)
(2)
Here V (i+1)
n
is the velocity, thus the change in position, relative to the last iteration, and V in is the
velocity from the previous generation. r1, r2 and r3 are random numbers between 0 and 1. c1 is a
constant to describe the importance of the best position from the individual particle, and c2 describes
the importance of the best known position among all particles. PBin is the individually best known
position. GBi is the globally best known position. Xin is the current position.
3.5
Evaluation Metrics
The optimization algorithms need a form of feedback, to be able to optimize the matched SPD to
the target SPD. Thus the fit needs some kind of evaluation. This evaluation can be a combination of
different parameters. In this section the metrics that have been used and other possible metrics are
explained.
In this section ”weights” will be mentioned. These are values between 0 and 1, both inclusive, that
describe the relative intensity of each channel.
3.5.1
Goodness of Fit Coefficient
For most of the spectral matching a Goodness of Fit Coefficient (GFC) was used as part of the
evaluation.
GFC =
| P
λ [t(λ)m(λ)] |
pP

(3)
where t(λ) is the target SPD and m(λ) is the mathced SPD.
This metric compares the shapes of the different SPDs, and returns a value in the interval [0;
1]. However this metric doesn’t penalize absolute errors, so alone it often returns a much brighter
reconstructed SPD. Thus this should be combined with another metric that penalizes the absolute
difference. If it weren’t for the relative shift in the LED emission SPD as current changes, this would
work well by just normalizing the weights based on the difference of the SPDs.

3.5.3
Color Penalties
Another way to evaluate the SPD is to calculate the resulting colors (see section 6.5.1) or CCT values
[17]. Often this is what will be calculated from the output of the ILS, so optimizing the matched SPD
so that these values will be more correct is a good idea. This can be combined with the other penalties
as well. This metric was tested in initial simulations but was not used with the spectrally tuneable
lamp, due to time restrictions.
3.5.4
Sensor Specific Optimization
Though this was not used, a possible error metric would be to use the ILS sensitivities, seen on Figure
31. Though this would make this physical simulation closer to the data simulation purely using the
sensitivities (more on this later), the output of the lamp could also be tuned so that in theory, the
sensor would see the two SPDs as the same. It would be like using the color as a parameter for the
evaluation, but instead of using the sensitivity of the human eye to evaluate the matched SPDs, the
sensitivities of the ILS could be used instead.

The spectrally tuneable lamp
4.1
Motivation
To accurately calibrate the ILS, it is necessary to use an extensive set of SPDs. To automatically
acquire a lot of data, a spectrally tuneable lamp was created.
The lamp consists of a printed circuit board (PCB) with a hole in the middle, a power supply (the
LED driver) and half of an integrating sphere. On the PCB there are 16 channels of LEDs, which can
be controlled individually through the power supply. The half integrating sphere is mounted on the
PCB, over the LEDs so the light will reflect of the integrating sphere before exiting the lamp through
the hole in the PCB. See Figure 5a for a picture of the lamp and Figure 5b for an illustration of the
internals of the lamp.

4.2
Lamp Electronics
The driver of the lamp is designed by Henning Engelbrecht Larsen, senior electronics designer, DTU
Elektro. This driver was partially designed for and during this thesis. The general working principle
of it, is that it uses a current sink to control the current going through each string of LEDs. This
is shown on Figure 7, the general working principle is, that a voltage is very easy to control with a
computer. A digital signal can be sent to a Digital to Analog Converter (DAC), and it will output
an analog voltage. A current sink uses a resistor, Field Effect Transistor (FET) and an operational
amplifier (opamp) to draw a constant current proportional to the voltage controlled by the DAC.
Initially the current of the current sink did not go to zero. Ie. the minimum output value of the
DAC was 2mV to 6mV, the the minimum current running through each channel was 2-6mA.
Two solutions were implemented to mitigate this.
Firstly, resistors (values 4.7k-10k) were placed
in parallel with each sting of LED. This allows small currents to flow at voltages smaller than the
threshold for lighting for the LEDs, thus no emission of light at these small current levels. This was
a temporary fix. A voltage divider was also placed (by Henning) on the output of the DAC. Even
though this reduced the Max voltages (from 2.6V to 1V, which is sufficient from the purposes of this

4.2.1
LED Circuit board
For the mounting of the LEDs themselves, heat was a major concern. A total of 121 LED were to
be mounted on the same printed circuit board (PCB). A circuit board with a base of 1.6mm thick
aluminum and all traces (wires on a PCB) on top of an insulator was used. The thick aluminium layer
allows for efficient heat transfer away from the LEDs and into a heat sink mounted on the back. A fan
was pointed towards the heatsink for additional cooling. A downside of this approach is that the PCB
can only have a single layer of conductors. That means that the traces cannot cross. Any crossing
would need to be soldered by hand.
Thus the LEDs were all mounted in rings as shown on Figure 8. This brought some other concerns.
With the light of different colors being emitted at different radii from the center, the light might have
some color-angle correlation. The whole reason for this setup with the half integration sphere was to
mix the light more thoroughly.
Figure 8: Here we see how the LEDs are mounted on the PCB. The hole in the middle is not to scale.
The outer ring on the PCB has a diameter of 250mm and the hole has a diameter of 50mm. The traces
that connect to the different strings come from the connector on the right side. Figure and design by
Finn Pedersen.
4.3
Lighting
The 16 different LEDs used in the lamp cover most of the visible spectrum. The SPDs of the LED
strings can be seen on Figure 9. A mix of narrow, broad and white LEDs were chosen based on the
following considerations (These criteria were found when simulating different LED combinations for
spectral matching):
• Many narrow LEDs were needed in the blue region. This is due to white LEDs being driven by
slightly different blue LEDs. Thus to match them more efficiently, a larger resolution was needed
in that region.
12
==PAGE:13==
• It was hard to find bright and narrow LEDs in the green gap. Thus several phosphor converted1
(PC) LEDs were used.
• Two white LEDs were added, one with a CCT of 2200K and one with a CCT of 4000K. Since
many of the simulated SPDs would be white LEDs, these, along with the blue LEDs would give
a good ”base line” for the fit. Thus there would be less ”wavyness”2 in the matched SPD.
• Initially the choice was to only use 8 of the ILS channels, Figure 31, since they are just sensitive
in the visible spectrum. They are sensitive up to around 740nm, thus light above 740nm would
be useless in the sensor calibration.3
As seen on Figure 9 it is seen that some of the channels overlap. This is more than was expected
from the data sheets. Ie. channel 4 and channel 11 overlap a lot. Channel 5 and channel 12 overlap
a lot as well. The peak wavelength of these, according to data sheets, varied by 40nm. In reality, as
seen in Figure 10, their relative SPDs are almost equal.

6
Reconstruction Theory
The previous sections of this report has been about acquiring data. This chapter will concern the
theory and methods for processing the data, to create a calibration algorithm for the ILS, enabling
the reconstruction of the real SPD from the 10 channel data.
This calibration will consist of three steps:
• Clustering: Grouping training data (SPDs) into clusters based on similarity of the SPDs. Figure
23).
• Classification: Training a model to classify an SPD into the appropriate cluster.
• Regression and Regularization: For each cluster, computing a calibration matrix appropriate for
light of the particular cluster.
The application of the model will consist of two steps. See Figure 24.:
• Classification: Applying the classification model to a measurement (ILS output), thereby obtain-
ing the appropriate cluster.
• Applying the calibration matrix of the cluster found in the previous step to get the SPD.
6.1
Machine Learning
Artificial intelligence is the idea of getting a computer based system to behave intelligently. Example
of this are speech recognition, ChatGPT or a rule-based chess engine. Machine learning is a subset of
artificial intelligence. For machine learning the learning is driven by data given to it. Examples of this
are speech recognition and ChatGPT.
When working with machine learning there is a high dependency on the data. E.g. outliers, dimen-
sionality5, size of the data set etc. The importance of these aspects depends on the chosen machine
learning method.
When working with a data set, typically it is split into two portions. One for training the model
and one for testing the model. These proportions could be 80/20 or 90/10 with the training data set
being the larger one.
Then the models performance can be evaluated based on the fit on the last 10% or 20% of the data.
6.1.1
Cross Validation
Cross validation is a re-sampling technique used to iteratively test and train a model by using different
parts of the data for training and testing. This helps improve the quality of the predictions of the
model.
An example of cross validation can be splitting the data into 10 different subsets. Then the whole
model is trained and tested 10 times, each time using a different data set for testing and the rest for
the training.

6.3
Regression and Regularization
Regression [4] is a method to estimate the relation between input and output of a model. For the
calibration models of this thesis the input is the ILS data and the output is an SPD.
When creating the calibration model of the ILS, least square (LSQ) regression is used. This is also
a fundamental part in Ridge Regularization and Lasso Regularization. These are used to reduce the
importance of, among others, random correlations. When Lasso and Ridge Regularization is combined,
it is called Elastic Net Regularization.
Ridge Regularization is often referred to as L2, and Lasso Regularization is often referred to as L1.
6.3.1
Least Squares Regression
LSQ is a special case of of Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) [8]. MLE is an estimator that
calculates the probability function most likely to produce the input data. MLE saturates the Cram´er-
Roa bound, thus MLE is at the information limit for any unbiased estimator [8].
LSQ Regression is, as the name implies, the fit, which has the least square error of the training
data. Now let’s consider 20 measurements taken with the ILS and a reference spectrometer. For each
measurement, the ILS returns a vector of 10 values. The spectrometer (after interpolation) returns
a vector of 401 values. Let the 20 ILS measurements be concatenated into a 10x20 matrix called R
for responses. Let the spectrometer measurements be concatenated into a 401x20 matrix called T for
ground truth.

When generating the matrices with the regression and regularization algorithms, these matrices are

specific data set.

of the specific cluster, but very poorly with other types of light.
6.4.2
Classification
The training data is clustered into different clusters. These are the unnamed classes. Each of these
will have a separate calibration matrix, that works well with light similar to that of the cluster.
Classification will take testing data, and allocate each data point (SPD or ILS counts) to a cluster,
a ”class”. Thus the data can be reconstructed with a calibration matrix made for that specific type
of light, which means that there is less chance of artifacts (fluorescent peaks on Figure 39) and the
general performance is more optimized, given the less generalization.

Melatonin Suppression
The Melanopic sensitivity on Figure 27 is the sensitivity for the intrinsically photosensitive retinal
ganglion cells (ipRGC) in the eye. These are non-image forming eye receptors, which have been tied to
melatonin suppression [6]. Melatonin is a hormone which is part of the circadian rhythm in humans.
Melatonin can be prescribed - as the active ingredient in some sleeping pills. Circadian lighting has
sufficient amount of light for the ipRGCs response during daytime. A parameter than can describe the
SPDs ability to suppress melatonin is the melanopic-Daylight efficacy ratio (melanopic-DER). This is
a ratio that describes the melatonin suppression of the SPDs compared to equally luminous 6500K
daylight. Typically this ration is below 1 for artificial light. Note that this ratio can be above 1.

The last experiment performed was by using the spectrally tuneable lamp. An Ocean Optics STS-VIS

was such, they they were each an equal distance from the center of the lamp opening (as much as
possible).
As described in Chapter 3 the lamp uses spectral matching and a know SPD to calculate its out-
put. Large data sets were fed to the spectral matching algorithm, a matched SPD was calculated
using a combination of GFC and MAE, eq. 3 and 4.
The target SPDs consited of a combination of data from the Empir data set, data from the University
of Granada, SPDs measured in the Demo Box, SPDs measured in offices at Risø with Gigaherz Spec-

It was either from the Grenada data set or from the Gigaherz measurements.

Spectrum Reconstruction
Until this point the report has been about theory and gathering ILS responses Ri and the SPDs that
they represent.
When a set of calibration matrices are created, they will be good at analyzing light similar to what
is contained in the data. Thus different use cases, can require different calibrations, thus different
training data. A use case mentioned in the beginning of the report, is analyzing light in hospital
rooms. Specifically Danish hospital rooms. For that use case, one could argue that training data
containing different LED light and daylight would be optimal, since colored light and outdated light
sources, like fluorescent and incandescent, will likely not be present.
Another use case is as a wearable sensor. Here it is more likely that an average person would encounter
outdated light sources and colored lights (i.e. computer screens). In this chapter, this will be the use
case, which the data analysis is be optimized for. The broader use case, will make it less accurate.
In this chapeter a lot of SPDs will be reconstructed. All the SPDs that are reconstructed, have not
been part of the training set used to create the respective model they are reconstructed with.
One thing to note, is that this reconstruction is based on light from the spectrally tuneable lamp.
Since this light is not a perfect match, performance outside the lab may vary. This can be mitigated
with spectral matches focused on the sensitivities of the ILS, but was not done due to lack of time.
In this chapter, the clustering algorithm used is kmeans and the classification algorithm used is RF.

8.3
The Spectrally Matched Data
The data created with the spectrally tuneable lamp will be analyzed in this section.
When reconstructing the matched data there are two ways to create the model. One is to use the
target SPDs for the reconstruction model. This will make the model good at fitting to the target,
given the input light. The model will not fit the actual light it is hit by.
The other option is to fit the light that is measured - the matched SPDs. This way, the ILS will
reconstruct the actual light that hit it. However, this light, is not real-world light. It will always follow
the LED-channels of the spectrally tuneable lamp. So if the ILS is calibrated to the measurements, it
will not be able to reproduce actual daylight and actual flourescent light. However it will be good at
fitting data measured on the lamp, with the specific peaks, which the lamp has.

Conclusion
The goal of the project was to develop a calibration model for the ILS. Along the way, the theory
and optimization of spectral matching was described, and it was applied to the spectrally tuneable
lamp. The design choices of the spectrally tuneable lamp were described, along with the reason why
the lamp was developed. The performance and the fault of the lamps were covered, along with how
the imperfections impact the calibration of the ILS.
Later the theory behind the calibration model of the ILS was explained. This includes Regression,
Regularization, Clustering and Classification, all of which contribute to the final Spectrum Recon-
struction.
A lot of code has been developed throughout the project. This includes the code for the spectrally
tuneable lamp (not for the LED driver), integration with some of the spectrometers, integration with
the ILS (not the code on the ILS, just the interface), and all the code for the data analysis in the report
(though using built in Matlab functions, the Photometry Toolbox of DTU and Lasso regularization of
[16]). All of this currently only works in Matlab on a computer.
An early version of a calibration matrix (without the clustering and classification) was handed over to
colleagues who used it in a smartphone app, that works directly with the ILS.
The performance, including flaws, of the model was shown. If the ILS and the model performs similarly
well outside the lab, these results could be adequate for using the ILS for some use cases. The ILS
could be used as e.g. a wearable sensor or a light sensor in a hospital room. This would be a less costly
and more discreet alternative to current equipment.

Future Work
The experience from the work behind this report, has revealed points that can be improved. Some of
the points regarding the spectrally tuneable lamp:
• Two pair of the lamp channels are very similar. With two more channels, the performance of
the Spectral Matching would increase. If an additional 8 or 16 more channels were added, the
performance would increase even more.
• With more channels in the blue part of the spectrum, the lamp peak of a white LED would be
less likely to be between two channels.
• With wider LED channels, like in the green part of the spectrum, the high-frequent oscillation in
the blue region, seen in Figure 43b, would be smaller. If wide LEDs are not available in the blue
part of the spectrum, this could be achieved with a string of similar non-identical blue LEDs.
• The light of the spectrally tuneable lamp could be combined with true daylight or light from
a fluorescent light source. This could increase performance, since these SPDs are difficult to
reproduce.
Some of the points regarding the ILS calibrations and uses:
• Elastic Net regularization could be implemented to improve the performance of the calibration
model, since it combines advantages of L1 and L2, at the cost of more complex implementation.
• Other ways to cluster and classify the data are available, and might also improve performance.
• Using matched SPDs from the lamp, which have different optimization metrics, i.e. using colors
or ILS-sensitivities of the matched SPD. Better matched data would reduce the difference between
laboratory experiments and the real world.
• The final model should be tested in the real world.
• The performance of the the ILS when using a linear combination of the channels to get X, Y, Z
and more should be investigated.
• Due to availability, the spectrometer used for the calibration was not the best spectrometer
owned by the department. These measurements could be run with a better spectrometer for
more accurate and precise results.
• Performance evaluation of the RF classifier
• Melanopic-DER performance evaluation
• Direct comparison of ILS to other cheaper devices, i.e. the nanoλ
