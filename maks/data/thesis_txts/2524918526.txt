Abstract
The presence of oxygen in the wellbore is a serious issue and inﬂuence the corrosion
behavior of the material used by changing the morphology and proprieties of the
formed corrosion layers. This study investigates the eﬀect of the dissolved oxygen
concentrations on the corrosion behavior of 1Cr carbon steel and 13Cr stainless steel.
Tests were conducted at atmospheric pressure in a 3.5 wt.% NaCl solution saturated
with CO2 at two diﬀerent temperatures 40 and 80 ◦C for aerated and deaerated systems.
Three diﬀerent concentrations of dissolved oxygen (230 ppb, 650 ppb, and 2000 ppb
) at 40 ◦C were studied. Linear Polarization Resistance (LPR), Electrochemical
Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS), and potentiodynamic polarization were used to study
the electrochemical behavior. X-Ray Powder Diﬀraction (XRD), Scanning Electron
Microscopy (SEM) and Energy Dispersive X-Ray Spectroscopy (EDS) were employed
to analyze the corrosion products. Results showed that the corrosion rate of 1Cr
Carbon steel decreased with increasing the concentration of dissolved oxygen ( from
230 to 2000 ppb) in the system. On the contrary, the polarization resistance of 13Cr
stainless steel decreased with increasing the concentration of dissolved oxygen in the
system, which implies a higher corrosion rate. SEM images of 13Cr stainless steel
also showed that the localized corrosion became more serious by increasing the DO
concentration in the solution. SEM images of 1Cr carbon steel showed that the
thickness of the corrosion products increased by increasing the oxygen concentration.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1 Corrosion in the Oil & Gas production
The main condition of forming an oil reservoir is preventing hydrocarbons from leaking
out of the structure which fulﬁlled by the coverage of the porous rock by the nonporous
layers; such as (salt, shale, mud or chalk). Subsequently, with the time the rock
structures become folded resulting in migrating and collecting of hydrocarbons under
the non-permeable rock constituting a diﬀerent source of the oil [1]. Figure 1.1 shows
the typical reservoir formation in Denmark, as the main source of the oil in Denmark
is the chalk structure as reported by Danish energy agency [2].
Figure 1.1: Typical rock formation [1]
Irrespective of the sources of oil, the extraction method is still the same. When the
well is drilled, the oil and gas will be retrieved using high internal pressure resulting
in free oil and gas ﬂow, which is called primary recovery. The pressure of the reservoir
decreases over time causing a decrease in the ﬂow, so to keep the pressure of the
reservoir at its optimum value, secondary recovery is required. This mechanism is
known as Enhanced oil recovery (EOR).
In EOR mechanism, gas or water is injected in the well in order to replace the recovered
oil with water. Hence, pumping the unrecovered oil to the surface as shown in Figure
1.2.
==PAGE:18==

2
Figure 1.2: Schematic of an enhanced oil recovery system
Corrosion phenomenon occurs because of physical and/or chemical interactions between
the material and its corrosive environment. The corrosion is not restricted to steel
but impacts all materials: metals, polymers, and ceramics. The corrosion losses are
estimated to represent 4% of the gross national product. In oil and gas industries,
the corrosion costs around tens of millions of dollars to repair and maintenance the
production equipment every year [3] [4].
Pipelines used in the oil and gas industry are associated with diﬀerent corrosion
mechanisms, which attributed to the fact that the oil and gas coming out of the
well is not pure. Similarly, the infrastructure in the oil and gas industry encounters
several mechanisms of corrosion such as carbon dioxide mechanism (sweet corrosion),
hydrogen sulﬁde mechanism (sour corrosion), galvanic corrosion, erosion-corrosion,
stress corrosion cracking, and microbiologically induced corrosion [4].
Typically, the ﬂow consists of various specious; water, carbon dioxide, diﬀerent kinds
of salts (sodium chloride, potassium chloride, calcium chloride, etc.), organic acid
and hydrogen sulﬁde. The assumption is that the oxygen is absent in the well, yet a
study revealed the presence of iron oxides hydroxide and iron oxide, which implies the
existence of oxygen in the well [5]. The oxygen might be introduced during processing
operation, with ﬂuids added to the ﬂow (biocides, inhibitors), well interventions, and
maintenance operations.
The harsh nature of the operating conditions of the oﬀshore and the subsea oil and gas
ﬁeld promotes the corrosion process. Knowing that high temperatures, acidic media,
high pressures, the concentration of oxidants, and water salinity are considered as key
factors that play a big role in the corrosion process [4]. In in this study, the attention
was paid to simulate the conditions in the Danish Sector of the North Sea.
The most common material used in the oil and gas sector especially for the manu-
facturing of undersea production tubing is mild steel due to its economic advantages
and its high ductility. However, the martensitic stainless steel 13% Cr is used in wells
owing to its high strength and corrosion resistance [6].

1.2 Objectives of the study
Studies reported that stainless steels show high resistance to corrosive environments,
which can be justiﬁed due to its chemical composition. It is noticed that at least
11 Wt% of chromium is required to fulﬁll high corrosion resistance. Basically, high
chromium content can grant the material high corrosion resistance by forming a ﬁlm
of chromium oxide which increases the material passivity [7] [8]. In the case of smooth
and clean surfaces, the oxide ﬁlm is rapidly constituted, while in case of a contaminated
surface with deposits or welding slag, the oxide ﬁlm is hindered and species of corrosion
such as pitting or deposit can take place [9].
A study concluded that microstructure has a direct impact on the corrosion properties
[10]. knowing that stainless steel is classiﬁed into ﬁve classes: duplex, ferritic, austenitic,
precipitation hardening, and martensitic grades [11]. Since 13 Cr martensitic stainless
steel type can be hardened and tempered, the domain usage is in applications requiring
high mechanical properties and moderate corrosion properties [11] [12].
1.2 Objectives of the study
As mentioned under section 1.1, the studies proved the presence of the oxygen in the
well. Therefore, this project aims to investigate the impact of oxygen on the corrosion
behavior of 1Cr carbon steel and 13 Cr stainless steel in NaCl solutions under three
diﬀerent systems at stimulated EOR conditions (40 and 80◦C), which are:
• Aerated solution without CO2 (Aerated).
• Saturated CO2 solution without O2 (Deaerated).
• Saturated CO2 solution with diﬀerent O2 concentration (Dissolved oxygen DO).
In this respect, Cyclic Potentiodynamic Polarization (CPP), Electrochemical Impedance
Spectroscopy (EIS), and Linear Polarization Resistance (LPR) were performed in 3.5%
NaCl solution. In addition, dissolved oxygen concentration of 230 , 650 and 2000 ppb
were conducted at 40 ◦C.
Surface analysis of corrosion products forms a large part of this study due to the
importance of the characterization of the morphology, phase identity and chemical
composition. This work is expected to back the future development of corrosion control
in O2, CO2, and CO2/O2/ brine mixtures systems, and that can be achieved by
providing useful knowledge and beneﬁcial information for future studies.

2.1.1.4 The Aﬀecting Parameters in CO2 Corrosion
It is found that the most important factors aﬀecting the rate of CO2 corrosion are; salt
concentration, surface roughness, corrosive gas (CO2, O2, H2S), temperature, agents
added to the ﬂow (inhibitors), material used (mild steel or anti-corrosive steel), pH,
organic acid, and ﬂow [21]. Since the detailed analysis of these factors is beyond the
scope of this study, therefore the most important factors will be discussed.
2.1.1.4.1 Temperature
The studies reported that the temperature and the solubility of CO2 and O2 are
inversely proportional. In other words, at high temperature the concentration of CO2
and O2 decreases in the solution. knowing that temperature and corrosion rate are
directly proportional, therefore it can be concluded that the eﬀect of the temperature
on reaction rate is more strongly than its eﬀect on the solubility [22].
It is also noticed that changes occurred in the phase identity and morphology of
corrosion products with temperature. However, at high temperatures, the formed layer
of corrosion products is likely to become more protective resulting into the drop of
corrosion rate, with respecting ﬂow, pH, and ferrous iron concentration [23].
In other words, the temperature has two conﬂicting impacts on the corrosion process,
the ﬁrst is accelerating the kinetics of CO2 corrosion by speeding up the mass transfer
process. The second is facilitating the forming process of FeCO3, where the solubility
of FeCO3 is inversely proportional to the temperature [18].
It is found that between 20 and 60 ◦C the corrosion rate increases as the temperature
increases. However, the corrosion rate decreases with the increase of the temperature

porous and less protective than at higher values of pH, which means that the corrosion
rate increases at higher temperature as the pH value decreases. The corrosion rate
shows a steep decrease from pH 4.0 to pH 6.0 with respect to the test temperature
[25].
Basically, at higher pH and temperature levels a physical barrier constituted of a
dense layer of corrosion products limits the diﬀusion of aggressive species. Thereby,
the dense layer oﬀers a kind of mitigation against the further dissolution of steel, thus
the corrosion rate will decrease [25].
2.1.1.4.3 Flow rate
Under ﬁlm-forming conditions, the ﬂow rate of the oil has two direct eﬀects on the
growth of FeCO3 at the steel surface. The ﬁrst eﬀect by accelerating the mass transport,
which in turn can accelerate the CO2 corrosion. The second eﬀect by hindering the
growth of (FeCO3), whereas the high ﬂow rate can remove the formed ﬁlm of (FeCO3)
due to the high-velocity [26].

specimens. High-resolution images of specimens can be obtained by directing a focused
beam of electrons over the surface of the specimens, whereas the resolution is around
1 nm [47].
The typical layout of an SEM is illustrated in Fig 3.9, whereas the electron source
generates and accelerates the electrons through (1−30 KeV) accelerating voltage.
The accelerated electrons pass through a series of electromagnetic lenses which are
used to control the diameter of the electron beam and focus the electrons over the
specimens. A vacuum chamber is used to ensure high-quality resolution by keeping
a low vacuum inside the chamber (0.1-10E-4 Pa). A set of detectors is available
to detect the signals resulting from the interaction between the electrons and the
atoms of the material[48]. When the electrons hit in-elastically the outer surface, the
signals generated are detected by the secondary electrons detector and the signals
are emitted to acquire topographical information about the specimen. Note that the
signals originating from elastic interactions in a deeper penetration depth are detected
by backscattered electrons to get contrast information about the surface, where areas
with higher atomic number appears brighter [47]. After that, an ampliﬁer receives the
signals to produce the images of the surface topography.
Since this study is interested in the topography and the morphology of the specimens;
Both secondary electrons (SE) and backscattered electrons (BSE) were used. In
addition, Energy Dispersive X-Ray Spectroscopy (EDS) detector was used to do
mapping for the chemical composition of the corrosion layer. Note that Quorum
Coater was used to coat the surface of the sample with gold.

5.1 13Cr Stainless Steel
5.1.1 Aerated System
The ﬁrst aim of this project was to gather the necessary data for understanding the
impact of the aerated system on the corrosion behavior of 13Cr stainless steel used in
the oil and gas industry.
By looking at the Nyquist diagrams recorded under aerated condition measured over
96 h at 40 ◦C shown in Figure 4.1, it could be seen that the highest capacitive arc
diameter was present at 1 h. The increase in the capacitance is known to be caused
by the presence of the passive ﬁlm. As the exposure carried on, a ﬁlm breakdown
occurred leading to a rapid decrease in the impedance between 1 and 24 h, due to the
high oxygen concentration and chloride content [53]. Subsequently, the impedance
increased until the end of the test, indicating that the evolution of the ﬁlm was in
progress and oﬀered good protection against the corrosion.
The previous observations were conﬁrmed by LPR measurements displayed in Figure
4.2(a), where the polarization resistance decreased rapidly between 1 and 20 h of
exposure then increased until the end of the test. Furthermore, the corrosion potential
decreased rapidly from -0.31 to −0.4 V for the ﬁrst 24 h, conﬁrming the hypothesis of
the passive ﬁlm breakdown. This event triggered the localized corrosion, as shown
in Figure 4.3, which resulted in rapid dissolution of ferrite phase into the bulk. This
observation was also supported by the magnitude format shown in Figure 4.1(c), where
a slight decrease in RS value between 1 and 24 h could be seen, indicated a slight
change in the ionic concentrations [54]. From Table 4.1, the ﬁtted values showed a
rapid decrease in Rct and Rf values between 1 and 24 h due to the aforementioned
ﬁlm breakdown. Thereafter, Rf increased with time, conﬁrming the assumption of the
reforming of the passive ﬁlm (recovery of the passive ﬁlm and the localized breakdown
sites). It was also possible to observe that Qf increased with time due to the increase
in the passive ﬁlm surface [43].
The SEM images shown in Figure 4.3(a) showed localized corrosion, which was related
to the passive ﬁlm breakdown during the experiment period of 96 h. However, the
original polishing marks could be clearly seen on the rest of the surface, suggesting that
the thickness of the passive ﬁlm was very thin as well as the material didn’t corrode
much. The EDS mapping shown in Figure 4.4 emphasized the assumption of the
formation of the passive ﬁlm, where the main detected elements on the corroded surface
==PAGE:120==

were iron, chromium and oxygen. The detected elements suggested the presence of iron

The cross section of the corroded sample shown in Figure 4.3(b) revealed no oxide
ﬁlm, which reaﬃrmed the hypothesis that the oxide ﬁlm is very thin.
The Nyquist diagram recorded at 80 ◦C displayed in Figure 4.6(a) showed that the

It is stated that the presence of the Fe3C plays a considerable role in the corrosion
process, whereas the cementite has low hydrogen evolution (low overpotential), which
in turn, facilitates the dissolution of the ferrite phase. Therefore, selective dissolution
of the ferrite phase occurs and thereby inﬂuencing the kinetics of the corrosion process
by micro galvanic eﬀect, which in turn promotes and sustains the corrosion [43] [61].
The eﬀect of the cementite phase is considered when the carbon content is greater
than 0.15% and this applies to the tested material [43].
By comparing the behavior of the material at 40 ◦C and 80 ◦C, it could be observed

Conclusion
The present study investigated the corrosion behavior of 1Cr carbon steel and 13Cr
sainless steel in three diﬀerent systems; aerated, deaerated and diﬀerent DO con-
centrations. On the basis of the results achieved, the following conclusions can be
drawn:
• 13Cr Stainless Steel
1. In the DO system, the higher the DO concentration, the lower the impedance
(capacitive arc diameter) was.
2. In the DO system, the higher the DO concentration, the lower the polariza-
tion resistance and the corrosion potential were.
3. In the DO system, the higher the DO concentration, the lower the charge
transfer was.
4. In the DO system, by increasing the DO concentration, the localized
corrosion became more serious.
5. In the DO system, the DO concentrations had no impact on the type of
the phases formed on the surface of the tested samples, as the same phases
were detected by XRD.
6. The impedance corresponding to the capacitive arc diameter at 40 and 80
◦C was higher in the case of the deaerated solution than those corresponding
values in the aerated solution.
7. In the cases of aerated and deaerated solutions, the samples experienced
more localized corrosion with increasing the temperature.
8. The temperature in both aerated and deaerated systems had no impact on
the type of the phases formed on the surface of 13Cr stainless steel, as the
same phases were detected by XRD.
• 1Cr Carbon Steel
1. In the DO system, as the concentration of DO increased, the corrosion rate
decreased as well as the thickness of the corrosion layer increased.
2. In the cases of 650 and 2000 ppb DO, a relatively thick and dense ﬁlm of
corrosion products covered the entire surface area of the tested sample.
3. The higher the DO concentration in the solution, the higher the LPR
corrosion potential was.

4. In the deaerated system at 80 ◦C, the corrosion rate decreased drastically
after 15 h of exposure, due to the nucleation and growth of the iron carbonate
on the steel surface.
5. In the aerated system, as the temperature increased, the corrosion rated
increased and the corrosion ﬁlm became thicker.
6. 1Cr carbon steel showed lower polarization resistance and corrosion potential
than 13Cr stainless steel in the cases of aerated, deaerated and dissolved
oxygen systems.

Chapter 7
Issues & Future Work
7.1 Experimental Issues
• One of the most consuming time issues faced during this study was that for an
unknown reason some electrochemical experiments had to be repeated multiple
times to succeed, causing time limitation.
• The limitation in the experimental setup, in addition to the time constraint,
were underlying reasons for conducting the DO experiments for one time.
• Since the passive ﬁlm formed on the surface of 13Cr stainless steel was at the
nanoscale, It was not possible to investigate the thickness of the passive ﬁlm
formed by SEM and identify the chromium oxide phase by XRD.
• Although the cleaning process used to remove the corrosion products from the
tested surface of 13Cr stainless steel was performed according to the Standard
ASTM G1-03 (2011), but it was observed that the solution was too aggressive
and caused a considerable impact on the substrate of the metal, causing an
inability to deﬁne the impact of the corrosion process on the cleaned samples.
7.2 Future Works
• As mentioned in Section 7.1, SEM and XRD were not able to provide suﬃcient
information about the passive ﬁlm, it would be useful to use other techniques
such as: X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) and Transmission Electron
Microscopy (TEM) .
• The given immersion time in the case of the deaerated system at 80 ◦C was not
enough to show the corrosion rate of 1Cr Carbon steel after the formation of
the iron carbonate. As it was noted that the corrosion rate decreased drastically
after 15 h of exposure, and no change in the behavior was recorded after that.
Therefore, it could be interesting to investigate the behavior of 1Cr carbon steel
for a time longer than 96 h.
• In the case of 1Cr carbon steel in the deaerated solution at 80◦C, It was noted
that the formation of the iron carbonate oﬀered good protection against corrosion.
Therefore, it could be interesting to introduce the oxygen into the system and
investigate the eﬀect of the oxygen on the corrosion behavior of 1Cr carbon steel
after the formation of the protective layer.
==PAGE:138==
7 Issues & Future Work
122
• In the case of 13Cr stainless steel, the test time in the deaerated solution at 40
and 80 ◦C was not enough to get a clear view about the behavior of the tested
stainless steel in term of the polarization resistance. As it was noted that the
polarization resistance at 80 ◦C was higher than at 40 ◦C for 82 h of exposure,
and then became slightly lower than at 80 ◦C. Therefore, it could be interesting
to investigate the behavior of the stainless steel for longer than 96 h at 40 and
80 ◦C.
