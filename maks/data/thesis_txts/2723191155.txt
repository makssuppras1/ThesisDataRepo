Abstract 
 
The aim of this work is to verify the possibility of running a liquid salt mixture near its 
solidification point trough a commercial plate heat exchanger without substantial 
deposition of solid crystals on the cold surfaces or the clogging of the pipping. By doing 
so, the latent heat of fusion of the salt would be accessed. A set up was built for this 
purpose. It was made up of a salt loop and a cooling loop containing thermal oil. A total 
of 37 test were run on this set up changing the inlet temperatures and the mass flow of 
fluids for each different run. Out of the 37 test half of them failed due to clogging. Of the 
successful tests there were only two that showed signs of solidification. The 
percentage of solidification in those tests was 1.2±0.58 % and 10.6±2.1 %, achieving 
the same amount of heat transfer but only relying on sensible heat would lead to an 
increase in mass flow of 13±6 % and 39.6±7.7 % respectively. This showed that it is 
possible to partially solidify a flow of salt inside a commercial plate heat exchanger and 
access the latent heat of fusion.

1. Introduction 
Electricity and gas prices have been setting record prices for the past year. Now more 
than ever, alternative energy sources can be of great help to society. There has been 
an increase in renewable energy installed capacity all over the world [1]. However, 
these sources of energy still have some limitations. One of the biggest limitations is 
that its electricity generation depends on various factors like the sun, wind, or rain, not 
allowing for a steady production of energy and is often intermittent and unpredictable. 
Energy storage systems can help mitigate some of those limitations by storing the 
energy during the times when is more abundant and cheaper and releasing it when it is 
more expensive and scarcer.  
The many different existing energy storage systems can be classified in the following 
categories: 
- 
Mechanical energy storage: compressed air (CAES) [2], pumped water [3], 
flywheel [4]. 
- 
Electrical energy storage: supercapacitor [5], superconducting magnetic energy 
storage (SMES) [6]. 
- 
Electrochemical energy storage: lead-acid battery [7], lithium-ion battery [8], 
sodium-sulfur battery [9]. 
- 
Thermal energy storage: molten salt storage [10], water tanks [11], ice-based 
technology [12]. 
- 
Chemical energy storage: hydrogen [13]. 
A summary of the main characteristics of some of the most common systems can be 
found in table 1.1.

This project will be focusing on thermal energy storage (TES). TES systems work by 
supplying heat to a heat storage medium such as water, paraffin, or molten salts and 
then releasing that heat when needed. TES can be subdivided in sensible TES, where 
==PAGE:14==
2                             Analysis of a novel isothermal latent heat storage based on salt systems 
there is no phase change in the heat storage medium; latent TES, where there is 
phase change; and chemical TES, which stores heat in a chemical reversible reaction. 
Sensible TES are the most widely used systems to store heat. They have been 
thoroughly studied and are commercially available [15]. Latent heat storage systems 
have also been studied and proved to have some advantages over sensible heat 
storages [10], [16]–[18] such as the high energy storage density and the ability to store 
heat at a constant temperature. The challenge with latent heat storages comes from 
the fact that during charging and discharging the material storing the heat changes 
phase, leading to problems like scaling, pressure differences or reduced heat transfer. 
There are different solutions that aim overcome those problems, many of which are 
proven to work [10], [16]–[18]. These new technologies all have some drawbacks that 
have kept them from commercialization [19]. Although chemical TES is very promising 
[20], this project will focus on latent and sensible TES.  
This project will propose a novel latent heat storage system based on molten salts. The 
molten salts run through a plate heat exchanger where they partially solidify, releasing 
the latent heat. The rate of solidification inside the heat exchanger, the mass flow of 
salt, the inlet an outlet temperature, and the type of salts will be some of the different 
parameters studied in this project. The rate of solidification will be the most important 
parameter, as a low value implies very slow heat transfer. On the other hand, too high 
values for the rate of solidification can lead to clogging inside the heat exchanger. 
The aim of the project is to check whether it is possible to access the latent heat of 
fusion of the molten salts in a set up using a plate heat exchanger to provide heat at a 
constant temperature, and, if so, what are the best settings to operate the system. The 
purpose of the experimentation will be to investigate on the heat transfer occurring 
inside the heat exchanger and to observe how the salts behave when they are close to 
the solidification temperature. 
This dissertation will be divided in four sections. The background, where the operating 
principles of the different TES technologies will be explained, including several latent 
TES. Methods and materials, where the process of building the test set up, the 
approach followed for the salt selection and the parameters applied in the COMSOL 
simulation to support the experimental results will be discussed. The last two sections 
are centered on results and discussions, and conclusion.

2. Background 
All TES work on the same principles. There is a charging phase when energy is 
supplied to the system, where it is stored for a period of time that depends on the 
needs and the characteristics of the system. Finally, the energy is released again when 
needed. The main variations from one system to another are the size and method to 
store the thermal energy. Depending on the application, different materials can be used 
to store the energy for hours, days, or even months [21]. 
The ultimate goal of a TES is to achieve an economic saving. There are mainly two 
paths to achieve that. One is to transfer the energy demand to periods when the 
energy is cheaper. An example is the Alitalia building cooling system in Rome. It uses 
electricity during the night to freeze water that will be used during the day to cool the 
building [22]. In this case the energy demand corresponding to the cooling of the 
building is transferred to the night hours when the electricity is cheaper. Another way is 
to store the energy that would be wasted otherwise and use it when there is a demand 
for it. Plataforma Solar Almeria is a solar thermal power plant in Almeria, Spain, which 
heats molten salts to produce electricity by means of a steam turbine. There are two 
storage tanks one for the cold temperature salts and another for the hot ones. With this 
set up more hot molten salts than needed can be stored when there is enough solar 
irradiance [23].

Water has a very high volumetric thermal capacity compared to the other materials, 
which is why it’s one of the most commonly used. However, it also has some 
drawbacks, such as its low boiling temperature, which makes it unsuitable for 
applications above 90 degrees. 
Water has been thoroughly studied as a storage material due to its advantageous 
characteristics, being the storage medium for many different technologies. 
Water tanks are one of the most efficient ways to store the energy required for the 
thermal needs of a household. When paired with solar collectors, 93 % of the thermal 
energy for space heating and water consumption can be covered using solar energy 
[26]. Water tanks can be made of different materials, such as steel, aluminum or even 
concrete, and they are usually well insulated [27]. The top of the tank is always the 
hottest part thanks to the thermal stratification induced by the buoyancy forces. Good 
thermal stratification and reduced mixing during charging and discharging operations is 
very important to improve the efficiency of the system [28]. The discharge is always 
done from the top part, where the water is at higher temperature, and the charge is 
done through the middle of the tank. There are systems with different inlets for 
charging depending on the temperature to favor thermal stratification [29]. There is 
usually and electric heater or a gas boiler to ensure that a minimum amount of water is 
always ready for use in case there is not enough solar irradiance available. 
Another example where water is used as storage material is water pits. In this 
technology a large water reservoir is used to store heat. The size of the reservoir is in 
the order of tens of thousands of cubic meters. The shape of the pit is usually an 
inverted truncated pyramid with a polymer liner in between the water and the ground to 
==PAGE:17==

avoid leaks. On the top, there is a floating insulated lid [30]. The working principle is 
remarkably similar to the water tanks, relying on good insulation and thermal 
stratification to obtain a satisfactory performance. Sifnaios et al. (2022) [31] analyzed 
the performance of two water pits located in Marstal and Dronninglund, Denmark. Their 
size and capacity are 75000 m3 and 6000 MWh, and 6000 m3 and 5500 MWh 
respectively for both locations. Both water pits are charged using solar collectors and 
are supplying heat to the district heating network. While the one in Marstal is a 
seasonal storage, the one located in Dronninglund also discharges at night the heat 
collected during the day. The storage in Dronninglund had 92% energy efficiency, while 
Marstal had 63% energy efficiency. Figure 2.1 shows the three ports that charge and 
discharge the pit. There is one port in the middle, one in the bottom and one in the top.

Solar pond technology also uses a large reservoir of water, but the working principle to 
store energy is different. While water pits use fresh water and rely on thermal 
stratification and an insulated cover to prevent heat losses at the top, solar ponds use 
water with dissolved salt. The salt concentration varies throughout the pond, creating a 
vertical gradient. The top layer is very thin and is mainly fresh water, followed by a 
layer with an increasing concentration of salt called the non-convective zone. The salt 
in this layer prevents convention from occurring, and taking away the heat from the 
lower layer, which is the one where the heat is stored. The lower layer, called the lower 
convective zone, is a layer with a constant concentration of salt where the solar 
radiation is absorbed and trapped. The water can then be pumped from this layer to a 
heat exchanger to extract the heat when needed. There is also a liner between the 
water and the soil to insulate and also help absorb the solar radiation [33]. The 
efficiency assuming perfect conditions calculated by Nielsen [34] is 20 %. One example 
of this technology was built in Kutch, India and consisted of a 24000 m3 pond to test 
the viability of the technology in the country. In the two years of operation, it supplied

15000 m3 of water at an average temperature of 75 °C to a dairy production plant, 
saving them $19000 a year in coal [35]. 
Aquifer thermal energy storage is a technology that strongly depends on the ground 
characteristics and cannot be implemented in any location. Two bores are drilled 
underground, a cold well and a hot well, to extract and inject the ground water [36]. The 
water is heated using, as an example solar collectors, and then injected into the hot 
reservoir where the heat is stored. It is usually used as a seasonal storage, storing the 
heat for long periods of time [37]. It has some disadvantages, the ground needs to be 
permeable and no insulation can be installed, so the working temperature is lower [38]. 
Although it is cheaper than building a water tank [39], all the disadvantages have made 
this type of TES less used and studied compared to some of the others mentioned in 
this section. 
Borehole thermal heat storage systems also store the heat underground but, in this 
case, using the soil as storage material. Multiple holes are drilled in the ground 
between 30 and 200 m in depth [40], where U shaped pipes are inserted to create the 
borehole heat exchanger. Once the pipes are installed, the empty space in the hole is 
filled with grout or water to facilitate the heat transfer between the pipes and the soil 
[41]. Water is used to transport the heat through the pipes and into the ground, where it 
will be stored until it is needed. There is no risk of spillage, and they can be installed 
almost anywhere, so they are usually preferred over aquifers. Borehole TES can be 
used in a wide variety of configurations and systems, from single family homes to large 
district heating systems and have been already used successfully in many heat storage 
systems around the world [40]. One example is in Drake Landing Solar Community in 
Okotoks, Alberta, Canada. The heat needed for 52 houses is supplied by a system 
consisting of 800 solar panels, a water tank for short-term storage and a borehole TES. 
The borehole TES consists of 144 holes 37 m deep and separated 2.25 m between 
each other [42]. The holes cover an area of 35 m in diameter and are connected in 
series in groups of 6 radiating from the center. All that area is then insulated and 
covered in soil and a park is built on top. At the end of the summer the core of 
temperature reaches 80 °C, which is almost enough to last until the next summer. The 
solar fraction was 96 % on average over the period 2012-2016 [43]. 
Thermal oils have also been studied as a storage material, but they do not offer many 
advantages over water or molten salts, so there have not been many heat storage 
systems using thermal oil as a storage material [44], [45]. Their working temperature is 
higher than water but lower than salts, they are more expensive than most salts while

Molten salts are another fluid frequently used in TES. There are different salts 
frequently used in these systems, usually chloride and nitrate salts. All of them have a 
lower heat capacity than water. However, they have some advantages that make them 
attractive. The vapor pressure is lower than that of water and the boiling point is much 
higher, allowing it to work at much higher temperatures, up to 800 °C [46]. That is why 
they are suitable for power generation technologies, as well as heat storage [49].

There are different examples of power plants using molten salts, being most of them 
concentrated solar power plants, which usually implement a storage tank to be able to

Figure 2.3 shows the scheme of installation of a central tower power plant. One 
example of this technology is Gemasolar, located in Almeria, Spain. It is the first

2.2. 
Latent heat storage 
Latent heat storages utilize the energy stored in a material undergoing a phase 
transition, which occurs without a change in chemical composition and at constant 
temperature. Figure 2.6 shows the stored sensible heat and the stored latent heat with 
respect to temperature. It shows that more heat can be stored in the same temperature 
interval if the latent heat is also used. This creates an opportunity to develop new 
systems and research novel materials for thermal storage. 
 
Figure 2.6 Stored sensible (left) and latent heat (right) with respect of temperature [60] 
The phase change energy is calculated using equation 2.2.

L is the specific latent heat of the material and m is the total mass of the material 
measured in J/kg. 
Latent heat storage techniques have been studied for years due to their advantages 
over sensible TES. Latent TES provide high energy storage density and the ability to 
store energy at a constant temperature. Therefore, less space is needed to store the 
same amount of energy. Table 2.4 shows the specific heat capacity and heat of fusion

energy stored in the liquid-solid phase change is 70-80 times bigger than the energy 
needed to increase the temperature by one degree. That means that a much smaller 
volume is needed to store the same amount of energy.

Power plants with direct generation of steam greatly benefit from isothermal heat 
storage. In sensible heat storage, during charging, the steam condensates at a 
temperature tsat, charge while the temperature of the storage material rises from tcold to thot. 
There will always be a minimum temperature difference of Δt from tsat, charge to thot. The 
saturation temperature tsat, discharge must be lower than tcold, also with a difference of Δt. 
Δtsensible indicates the amount of material needed to store a certain amount of energy.

A power plant with a steam generation at 100 bars that charges the storage will have a 
temperature of 311 °C. Assuming a minimum Δt of 5 °C and a Δtsensible for the storage 
of 50 °C will leave a tsat, discharge of 251 °C that corresponds to 40 bars [61]. This steam 
pressure reduction leads to significant loss of efficiency and makes such a system 
unattractive. A power plant of the same characteristics but implementing a latent TES 
will only have a difference of 2Δt between tsat, discharge and tsat, charge instead of thot - tcold + 
2Δt. In this case the new tsat, discharge is 301 °C that corresponds to 87 bars, leading to a 
12 bar pressure drop, which is more manageable [62].

There are three different phase changes from which latent heat is extracted. Solid-
liquid is the most researched and the one this project will be focusing on [64]. Solid-
solid phase change materials (PCMs) have the advantage of having nearly constant 
volume and not leaking from the container, but the heat stored is lower than in the 
solid-liquid phase change. Solid-solid PCMs release heat by a reversible 
transformation between a crystalline or semi-crystalline phase, and another 
amorphous, semi-crystalline, or crystalline phase [65]. Liquid-vapor phase change has 
a lot of disadvantages due to the pressure and volume increase of the gas phase. The 
tank size would need to be large or be able to support very high pressures. 
Consequently liquid-vapor PCMs are not used.

size. 
-

-

- 
Minimum subcooling. 
- 
Chemically stable and able to last many cycles. 
- 
Cheap.

Organic PCMs can be divided into paraffins and non-paraffins. The advantages of 
paraffins are that they are cheaper, available and have low vapor pressure, among 
others. Some of the disadvantages are the low thermal conductivity and the large 
volume change at the phase change [66]. Table 2.5 shows a whole list of advantages 
and disadvantages of organic PCMs. Some examples of non-paraffin organic PCMs 
are fatty acids, alcohols, esters, and glycols. 
Inorganic PCMs include salts, hydrates, hydroxides, and metals. They usually have 
higher latent heat than organic PCMs and better heat conduction [67]. Metals and 
alloys have one of the best thermal conductivities, which makes them a good choice as 
PCMs. The volume change in the phase transition is low compared to other materials 
[68]. Salt hydrates have higher thermal conductivity than other salts and are cheap 
[69]. However, they degrade easily at high temperatures, so their lifetime is reduced 
[70]. Other salts and their eutectic mixtures have a higher melting point and higher 
latent heat but lower thermal conductivity, which poses a big challenge when designing 
systems using molten salts as storage material. Most common salts are chlorides and 
nitrates [70].

2.2.2. Latent TES commercial systems 
Water is one of the most used materials for sensible TES, as shown in section 2.1., 
and has been also used in latent TES for centuries [76]. The liquid-vapor phase change 
is not utilized, so energy stored in the solid-liquid phase change is the one that has 
been used. The melting point of water is 0 °C, so in most cases ice is used as a cooling 
medium. 
One example of ice being used in a system is the Alitalia’s headquarters in Rome [22]. 
The total area of the building is 130000 m2. The capacity of the system used to meet 
the cooling loads of the building is 65000 kWh and the system chosen is ice chiller 
thermal storage coils. The coils are installed in two concrete tanks. The system is 
charged during off-peak hours and discharged during the day to meet the cooling 
requirements. 
The company Thermofin, based in Canada, offers a latent heat storage system for 
cooling called Cristopia. The system is used, for example, in the Kuala Lumpur city 
center to provide cooling to different buildings, such as the Petronas twin towers, or in 
a brewery in Dundalk, Ireland [77]. The system uses a tank filled with balls and HTF in 
a 60-40 ratio approximately. The balls are blow molded from a blend of polyolefins and 
filled with PCM. They use different PCMs for each application with melting 
temperatures ranging from -64 °C to 140 °C. The lifespan of the spheres is between 30 
to 40 years. As a HTF, they use a mix of water and mono ethylene glycol or water and 
propylene glycol, with the ratio of the mix varying with the phase change temperature of 
the PCM. The tanks used in the systems are either rectangular concrete tanks or steel 
cylindrical tanks. They claim that with this system the capacity of the chillers can be 
reduced be 30-70 % and the electricity power by 30-80 % [78], [79]. 
Climate Change Technologies (CCT) and 1414º are two Australian companies that 
offer latent TES using a silicon-based alloy as PCM. The melting temperature of 
silicone is 1414 ºC, and the heat of fusion is 1800 J/g, which is around 6 times bigger 
than water. The PCM is kept in a heavily insulated tank and then is used to produce 
electricity or deliver heat depending on the necessities of the grid. They claim that the

storage is 6 times the energy storage of lithium-ion batteries per volume and 60-80 % 
less money [80], [81]. 
2.2.3. Latent PCMs in building applications 
PCMs are also used in building applications to reduce the heat consumption by 
increasing the thermal inertia of the construction materials. Many reviews have been 
published on this matter [80]–[85]. However, most concepts are still prototypes and 
have not reached commercialization. The use of PCMs in construction materials can be 
separated into two categories, passive and active. 
PCMs are added to the walls, ceiling, or floor because they offer a high surface area to 
transfer the heat to the rooms. In this first case the PCMs operate passively due to the 
temperature changes inside of the building. PCMs are most commonly added to 
gypsum, wallboard, bricks, or concrete. Different studies have tested the performance 
of gypsum materials combined with up to 45 % by weight of PCMs and wallboards with 
up to 60 % by weight. Most studies [86]–[89] compare two identical rooms in the same 
conditions, one with PCM added to the wall and the other one without it. They have 
found a reduction of peak temperature of 3 K in some cases [92]. Concrete only 
accepts 5-6 % by weight of PCM before making it impossible to use it in construction. 
The low thermal conductivity and the small thermal variation that naturally occurs inside 
of a building during a day makes it difficult to utilize the latent heat of the PCM. 
However it has been found that under certain circumstances adding PCMs to concrete 
will smooth out the temperature peaks inside a room [93]. Bricks have not been studied 
until recently and there are not many studies. There are a couple of studies leading to 
conclusions similar to that for concrete applications. Temperature variation are 
smoothed out over time, but, due to the low thermal conductivity deriving from the air 
inclusions that bricks have because of their shape, makes it difficult to charge and 
discharge rapidly [94], [95]. Other materials such as palmitic acid in expanded graphite 
[96], porous graphite filled with PCMs [97] and tiles [98] have been studied with 
promising results. 
Active systems rely on some kind of piping system using water to melt the PCM built in 
the construction material. Several tests have been built with promising results but there 
are many things to improve, for example fire proofing the system [99] and the different 
performance during summer and winter [100]. Ansuini et al. (2011) [100] were able to 
reduce 3.5 K the peak temperature of the floor. Jin et al. (2013) [101], performed 
numerical simulations to find the optimal melting temperature of the PCM for cooling 
and heating in a two layer activated floor. The temperatures found were 38 °C for

18                             Analysis of a novel isothermal latent heat storage based on salt systems 
heating and 18 °C for cooling which released 41 % and 38 % more energy respectively 
than a floor without PCM. 
PCMs integrated in windows, shutters, or slats have also been studied. Most studies 
found that in summer the PCMs were more effective at reducing the heat gain through 
the windows and keeping the temperature in the room 1-2 K lower that in a control 
room without PCMs in the blinds. Some of the limitations that they found were that 
once the PCM completely melts, the performance lowers so a thorough study has to be 
conducted beforehand to find the melting temperature and right amount of PCM. 
Sometimes the PCM did not completely regenerate during the night and there were 
also problems of liquid PCM leakage [102]–[104].  
2.2.4. Latent TES optimization 
Even though there are not many commercially available latent TES, there are many 
publications studying different types of systems. There are two main problems that all 
the systems face when trying to supply and extract heat from a PCM. The first is the 
lower heat conductivity of the PCM in its solid form which hinders the charging of the 
system, making it more difficult to melt all of the material in a reasonable time. The 
second problem comes when the PCM starts solidifying because it stops flowing as a 
liquid and clogs the pipes or the channels where it is contained. These problems have 
led to many different papers studying different optimization methods. 
The optimization techniques fall into one of these categories: 
-

PCM encapsulation can be divided into micro encapsulation and macro encapsulation 
if the container is larger than 5 mm. Encapsulating the PCM increases the surface area 
where heat transfer can occur. There is not a large-scale production of micro capsules 
yet and for the moment it is still difficult. The results, however, are promising, achieving 
micro structures that are chemically stable, leak-free, and thermally stable after 
repeated cycles [105], [106]. In the case of macro encapsulation, the manufacturing

process is easier, but the PCM tends to melt and solidify on the edges, which makes 
the charging and discharging process slower. Several studies have proven that it is 
possible to increase the thermal capacity and thermal conductivity of the system with 
this method [107], [108]. 
The main three materials that are added as additives to PCM to try to improve the 
performance of the system are carbon [109], metals [110] and nanomaterials [111]. Yu 
et al. (2021) [112] used a mix of expanded graphite and SiO2 nanoparticles that was 
added to the PCM. The mix was composed of MgCl2-NaCl-KCl. It was shown that the 
specific heat increased up to 1.36 and 1.63 times in solid and liquid state, respectively, 
and the thermal conductivity increased by 23.2 and 9.2 times in solid and liquid state, 
respectively. Wang et al. (2016) [113] mixed copper foam with paraffin. The 
experiments showed that copper foam improves the internal heat transfer of paraffin 
and reduces the charging time of 40 %. Yin et al. (2019) [114] mixed graphene 
nanosheets with paraffin and studied its thermal properties. The results showed that 
adding 50 ppm could largely improve the charging rate of the PCM. 
Adding fins to the tubes of a heat exchanger has several drawbacks. The fins take up 
space for the PCM, which means that the total capacity of the storage will be lower, or 
the size of the storage will increase. The overall cost will also increase because of the 
manufacturing of the fins. Riahi et al. (2018) [115] compared the heat transfer rate of a 
shell and tube heat exchanger and a fined heat exchanger using NaNO3 as the PCM. 
The result showed that the fins improve the heat transfer. Rathod et al. (2015) [116] 
showed a decrease in melting time and 46.3 % reduction in solidification time by 
adding three longitudinal fins to a pipe. There are several studies optimizing the fin 
shape as shown in figure 2.12 [117], [118].

Direct contact heat exchangers are those in which the PCM and the HTF are in contact 
with each other without any pipe wall or similar in the middle. The heat transfer in this 
type of heat exchangers is very efficient and usually the construction and operation 
cost are lower than in a system with a regular heat exchanger [120]. By eliminating the 
pipes, a higher energy density is achieved. The PCM and the HTF have to be 
immiscible when both are in liquid state and have different density, so they separate 
easily. Different materials have been studied, such as salt hydrates as PCM paired with 
a hydrocarbon fluid for solar energy storage [121], paraffin wax as PCM with water as 
HTF for cold storage [122], erythritol as PCM with silicon oil as HTF for a heat recovery 
system [123], water as PCM with air as HTF [124]. There are a few disadvantages to 
these systems. The PCM and the HTF need to be at the same pressure, and both have 
to be carefully selected because, if they are not immiscible enough, there is a risk of 
mass transfer or chemical interaction and, if the density difference is not high enough, it 
will take longer to separate, and the heat transfer will be lower. 
Ultrasonic vibrations have been proven to enhance heat transfer rate in different 
devices like heat exchangers [125] or in the food freezing industry [126]. Ultrasonic 
==PAGE:33==
Analysis of a novel isothermal latent heat storage based on salt systems                             21 
waves travelling through a fluid causes intense vibrations, acoustic cavitation and 
acoustic streaming which improves the heat transfer rate. These effects can also be 
used in latent heat TES. Choi et al. (1991) [127] used n-octadecane as PCM and a 
frequency of 15-55 kHz and showed a decrease in melting time as the frequency 
increased and an enhancement in the heat transfer due to the ultrasonic vibrations. 
There are several studies with similar results and conclusions regarding ultrasonic 
vibration [128]–[130]. Although it seems promising, using ultrasonic vibrations 
overcomplicates the system and uses additional electricity. 
An electric field can also be used to enhance the heat transfer rate. By applying an 
electric field, an electrohydrodynamically (EHD) induced motion can be achieved. 
Different electric body forces are the responsible for this motion [131]. In this method a 
strong electric field is applied to the PCM using electrodes to induce a potential 
difference. Feizi et al. (2023) [132] applied a voltage to R-11 refrigerant and noticed 
and increased heat transfer rate. Nakhla et al. (2015) [131] achieved a 40% time 
reduction in the melting of a 7 mm thick layer of paraffin wax when applying a – 8 kV 
potential difference. EHD has some advantages over ultrasonic vibrations because it 
uses less electricity and fewer moving parts are required. However not all materials 
would be viable, as they need to be dielectric. Salt hydrates, which are conductive will 
get disturbed by the electric field. The effect of EHD in the melting process has been 
studied several times [131], [133], [134], but the effect on solidification is still unclear. 
The last method consists of moving the PCM instead of having it static and moving just 
the HTF. This should fix some problems like the low heat conduction in the solid phase 
and decrease the charge and discharge time. It also introduces new challenges 
because the liquid will stick to the surfaces and clog pipes when it starts to solidify. 
Screw heat exchanger (SHE) is a type of heat exchanger that has screw with hollow 
flights where usually liquid or gas flows through and the crew is inside a tank where the 
other material is contained. The screw rotates, which forces the material in the tank to 
move forward. It is used in the manufacturing industry for different purposes [135]. Zipf 
et al. [10], [136], [137] built a system to proof this concept. Figure 2.14 shows the 
charging and discharging configurations of the latent TES. 
The eutectic mixture of KNO3 and NaNO3 was used as PCM and oil as HTF. There are 
two shafts separated by a small gap of 8 mm. The movement of the two screws against 
each other prevents the salt to stick to the screws. In the discharging process a crusher 
was added because the pieces of solidified salt had a size that was not convenient to 
store or move around. The energy used by the auxiliary components of the system was

binary mixture: 50% by mole potassium nitrate and sodium nitrate with a melting point 
of 119 ºC [154]. These mixtures were chosen for their price and availability. Preliminary 
melting tests were performed on the salts to study how they solidify. The salts were 
melted on a pan and let cool down until solid. The calcium nitrate salt was discarded.

5. Conclusions 
The aim of this project is to prove the concept of a novel isothermal latent heat storage 
based on molten salt. The idea is to solidify a percentage of the salt inside a plate heat 
exchanger without clogging it. This is beneficial because there is 80 times more heat 
stored in the phase change than in 1 ºC of temperature difference in the salt selected 
for the tests, which is the eutectic mixture of KNO3 and NaNO3. 
A test set up was built to perform different tests and try to solidify a fraction of the mass 
molten salt flowing in the exchanger and access the latent heat of fusion. The set up 
was comprised of two different loops. One located inside an oven comprising two 
sealed tanks, one of them containing 7.5 kg of salt and another one empty. The heat 
exchanger was also located inside the oven to melt salt residues eventually left during 
the previous test. The second loop was the cooling loop, which contained 3.7 L of 
thermal oil. It was located outside the oven and heated with an induction stove before 
running each test. Pressurized air was used to move the molten salt, and nitrogen was 
used for the oil, as there is the risk of oxidative degradation at high temperatures. 
The goal of the tests was to change the inlet temperatures and the mass flows of the 
two fluids to get the salt as close as possible to the solidification temperature and find 
out the maximum amount of salt that can be solidified inside the heat exchanger before 
clogging it. A total of 37 tests were performed. More than half of the tests failed due to 
clogging of the pipes or the heat exchanger. There were 17 successful tests were all of 
the salt was moved from one tank to the other. In most cases the temperature was 
above solidification and there are only two where the data implies that there might be 
solidification. Test number 9 and 13. 
The outlet temperature of the salt in test number 13 is 225.9 ºC, which is 2 ºC above 
solidification. However, it is one of the only two tests with an outlet temperature bellow 
230 ºC that did not clog the mass flow, and the heat flow rate of the oil over the heat 
flow rate of the salt is 112.9 %. 12.9 % excess heat is about twice the average error on 
the experiment. All of that leads to the idea that there is solidification, in this case 
1.2±0.58 % of the flow. 
In test number 9 it is clearer that there is solidification. The outlet temperature is 222.2 
ºC, and the heat flow rate of the oil over the heat flow rate of the salt is 139.5 %. The 
fraction of salt that has to solidify in order to give these results is 10.6±2.1 %. To get 
the same results but without accessing the latent heat, it would be necessary to 
increase the mass flow of the salt by 39.6±7.7 %.

Although it was proven that 10.6±2.1 % of the salt can solidify inside the heat 
exchanger, releasing 39.9±5.6 % more heat than it would be possible only with 
sensible heat, it was challenging to replicate the results and to standardize the tests 
procedure. It was due to the difficulty of adjusting the flow and the starting 
temperatures, which could not be finely tuned. 
5.1. 
Improvements and future research 
This project has shed some light into the topic and has proven the concept that it was 
aiming to. There are some improvements and future lines of research that can be 
explored after this project that would give additional insight into this particular topic. 
As it was shown in the previous section a few seconds of difference while timing the 
flow had a big impact on the results. The set up would greatly benefit from a flow meter 
and two pumps, one for each loop. This would help given that having a flow meter 
would make the flow known at each moment, making the failed tests very useful as 
there would be data of the exact moment when the salt solidifies completely. The flow 
could be modified during the experiment as well because it would not be necessary to 
have a constant flow during all the experiment. Using pumps would help regulate the 
flow better than with pressurized air. Another major improvement would be making the 
storage size at least 3 times bigger. This would triple the test duration. Having more 
time during the experiment would help having a longer period where the temperatures 
are constant. It would also allow for adjusting the flow in the middle of the experiment if 
needed. Another advantage of having more mass is that thermal losses to the 
environment would affect the results to a lesser extent, and there would be more time 
available to reach a pseudo stationary state., leading to a more constant inlet 
temperature. Lastly, adding a sight glass would help see the salt flowing and observe if 
the solidification is visible or not. 
Once the setup is improved and the results in this project replicated, there are some 
future lines of research that could be opened: 
- 
Finding the maximum amount of solidification that can occur. 
- 
Using another salt, such as lithium nitrate and potassium nitrate, which is more 
expensive but has a lower melting point and a higher latent heat of fusion. 
- 
Studying the effect of fluid velocity to determine if a fully develop turbulent flow 
would prevent to some extent crystal adhesion on the plates of the heat 
exchanger.

Applying a nonstick coating to the heat exchanger plates and studying the 
effect. 
- 
Designing the whole system that takes advantage of this concept. 
The initial idea was to compare a regular heat exchanger like the one used in this 
project to a heat exchanger coated with a non-stick solution developed by SiOx. 
The project is showing promising results, and a coated heat exchanger would 
improve the performance of the system. The comparison was not done in this 
project due to the lack of time and would be studied in a later phase.
