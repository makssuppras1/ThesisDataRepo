Abstract 
 
This study presents the design and development of a miniature open-architecture Laser 
Powder Bed Fusion (L-PBF) system optimized for synchrotron X-ray characterization and 
advanced additive manufacturing research. Current L-PBF systems for in situ X-ray imaging are 
often limited in accessibility, modularity, and adaptability for research applications. To address 
these limitations, this system is designed to be compact, transportable, and highly configurable, 
enabling researchers to modify, adapt, and enhance its components while maintaining ease of use 
for training and experimentation. 
 
The system integrates a recoater and vacuum mechanism (RecVac) to ensure precise powder 
deposition and effective post-processing, minimizing residual material interference with X-ray 
penetration. A controlled gas cross-flow system stabilizes the process environment, while a Kapton 
tube enhances X-ray transparency, enabling real-time observation of microstructural evolution. 
Additionally, a novel two-stage piston movement mechanism provides precise layer control and 
facilitates post-build scanning by efficiently managing build height and powder removal. The 
mechanical structure follows a modular assembly approach, incorporating stainless steel and 
aluminium to balance structural integrity with portability. High-precision stepper motors with closed-
loop control enhance motion accuracy and repeatability. 
 
As the first miniature L-PBF prototype developed at the university, this system serves as a platform 
for validating new process monitoring techniques and material characterization methods. Future 
iterations will focus on optimizing build parameters, refining process control, and expanding 
material compatibility. The findings from this study contribute to bridging the gap between 
laboratory-scale research and industrial additive manufacturing applications, advancing the 
capabilities of in situ L-PBF analysis for high-resolution X-ray imaging and complex 3D geometry 
fabrication.

1.   Introduction 
 
1.1. Laser Powder Bed Fusion (L-PBF) and Additive Manufacturing 
Additive Manufacturing (AM) has emerged as a transformative technology, enabling the 
production of highly complex and optimized structures with enhanced material efficiency and 
design flexibility. Among the various AM techniques, Laser Powder Bed Fusion (L-PBF) has 
become one of the most widely adopted methods for metal fabrication. This process relies on a 
high-energy laser beam to selectively melt and fuse metal powders layer by layer, following a 
computer-aided design (CAD) model. The ability to precisely control part geometries and 
microstructures makes L-PBF particularly attractive for applications in aerospace, biomedical 
engineering, and high-performance industrial components (Qi et al., 2024). 
 
To fully understand the functionality of an L-PBF system, it is essential to examinate its core 
components and workflow. While L-PBF machines may vary in design, they generally share 
common structures and sub-systems. A typical system includes a feedstock dosing and 
spreading mechanism to ensure a uniform powder layer, a shielding gas cross-flow to displace 
oxygen and eliminate unwanted byproducts, and a laser that selectively melts the powder to 
form the desired structure (M. B. Kjer et al., 2024). Figure 1 provides a schematic of an L-PBF 
machine provides a visual representation of these components and their interactions 
(Yadroitsev et al., 2021). Additionally, a process flowchart outlines the step by step progression 
from a CAD (Computer-Aided Design) model to the final printed part. Understanding this 
workflow is critical in evaluating how various parameters influence the printing process and final 
material properties. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
While L-PBF offers precise manufacturing capabilities, numerous studies highlight the 
challenges associated with achieving defect-free components. The final properties of a printed 
part are highly sensitive to various factors, including L-PBF process configurations such as laser 
power, scanning speed, and layer thickness, as well as material powder properties like particle 
size distribution, morphology, and composition. Additionally, recoating mechanisms influence 
the consistency of powder deposition, while shielding gas flow plays a crucial role in preventing 
oxidation. Feedstock properties, encompassing overall material quality and consistency (Aversa 
& Fino, 2020). Variations in these parameters can lead to melt pool instability, resulting in 
porosity, residual stresses, and microstructural inhomogeneities that negatively impact 
mechanical integrity. As the demand for higher reliability and precision in L-PBF grows, there is 
an increasing need to better understand and precisely control the process in real-time, ensuring 
consistent quality and structural performance (Chowdhury et al., 2022). 
While L-PBF provides exceptional manufacturing precision, its success depends on the ability 
to control process parameters in real-time. Conventional monitoring methods struggle to capture

critical subsurface defects, leading to process instability and quality inconsistencies. Addressing

1.2. Context and characterization in L-PBF 
Achieving high-quality L-PBF fabrication requires real-time monitoring. However, 
conventional techniques focus primarily on surface-level observations, not allowing to grab 3D 
information as subsurface defects, phase transitions, and melt pool instabilities, which those 
instabilities reduce significantly the mechanical performances of components.  
 
One notable advancement is this field is the development of a miniaturized selective laser 
melting (SLM) device for operando X-ray diffraction studies (Hocine, Van Petegem, et al., 2020). 
This system enables real-time monitoring of micro-alloying effects in metal alloys, providing 
direct insights into crystallographic phase changes and solidification dynamics during the L-PBF 
process (Hocine, Van Swygenhoven, et al., 2020). By synchronizing high-energy X-ray 
diffraction measurements with laser scanning, researches have demonstrated how subtle 
changes in laser power and scanning speed can significantly impact grain structure formation 
and residual stress distribution, highlighting the importance of real-time feedback mechanisms 
in AM research. 
 
To further address these limitations, synchrotron-based X-ray imaging techniques has emerged 
as a powerful tool for high-resolution, real-time analysis of the L-PBF process. These techniques 
provide non-destructive, high speed imaging capabilities, enabling researchers to observe melt 
pool behaviour, powder flow dynamics, and solidification mechanisms with unparalleled detail 
(Zhao et al., 2017). Recent studies have demonstrated that operando X-ray diffraction and 
computed tomography (CT) (Pietsch et al., 2016) are possible to reveal critical insights into the 
formation of defects, such as porosity, keyhole instabilities, and cracking mechanisms, as well 
as the influence of process parameters on microstructural evolution. 
 
Research conducted at the European Synchrotron Radiation Facility (ESRF) introduced a 
miniature L-PBF system for in situ synchrotron X-ray micro-CT experiments (Lhuissier et al., 
2022). This study showcased how X-ray micro-CT scanning enables the reconstruction of full 
3D reconstruction of printed components, offering detailed visualization of internal defects, 
powder bed evolution, and melt pool behaviour. By utilizing rotational scanning, researchers 
have been able to capture fine details of defect formation, including trapped gas pores, lack-of-
fusion defects, and microcrack propagation. This approach was particularly valuable for 
analysing the effect of scanning strategies and powder layer thickness on part quality.  
 
Integrating sensor-based operando monitoring with post-process inspection is crucial for 
ensuring comprehensive quality control in L-PBF. High-speed imaging and thermal sensors play 
a crucial role in in-situ monitoring of L-PBF detect anomalies like melt pool instability and 
spattering, enabling early defect mitigation (Spears & Gold, 2016). Meanwhile, post-process 
methods, such as X-ray imaging and micro-CT scanning, validate final part integrity by 
identifying porosity, cracks, and residual stresses. This combined approach bridges the gap 
between monitoring and validation, reducing material waste, improving defect prevention, and 
accelerating certification. The result is a deeper understanding of the L-PBF process, and the 
various properties involved).   
 
This thesis proposes a solution by designing a compact, modular L-PBF system that integrates 
seamlessly with beamline facilities while maintaining high-precision process monitoring. The 
following section outlines the key constraints and technical considerations that drive the 
system's design (McCann et al., 2021).

1.3. System scope  
To overcome the limitations of existing synchrotron-compatible L-PBF setups, the 
proposed system must address several critical design challenges: 
 
• 
Compatibility with diverse synchrotron facilities: The system shall feature a compact, 
modular division, divided into several sub-assemblies which can be assembled into a single unit 
or divided into separate subcomponents depending on design needs. This ensures easy setup, 
mobility, and transportability, allowing integration into different beamline configurations without 
requiring extensive modifications. 
 
• 
Mechanical simplification: Unlike conventional L-PBF machines with multi-axis motion 
systems, this system will operate using only two linear movements (spreader and z-axis). This 
approach enhances precision while minimizing mechanical complexity and maintenance 
requirements. As a result, it offers a versatile solution that effectively bridges research 
applications and industrial manufacturing methods. 
 
• 
Powder scattering: A suction method shall be integrated in the system to ensure efficient 
removal of unused powder to optimize post-scan imaging. Scanning methods as x-ray radiation 
are diffusely scattered by powders and the scattering increases with decreasing particle size 
(Papini, 1993). This behaviour can be attributed to particle-air interfaces and therefore refractive 
index changes which is not the case in the solid bulk shapes (Schuffenhauer et al., 2020). 
 
• 
Chamber size and recoating operations: The recoater head has to be designed to be as 
compact as possible, minimizing the chamber footprint while still allowing for an adequate 
number of recoating layers to ensure reliable and repeatable operation. The balance between 
compactness and functionality is essential for maximizing system portability while maintaining 
performance. 
 
• 
Open architecture: A flexible, modular system will allow seamless integration into various 
synchrotron setups, prioritizing proof-of-concept validation and future research adaptability 
(Koren et al., 2013).   
 
• 
Sensor-based facility: It has to combine a sensor-based operando monitoring with the 
capability of being incorporated in a post-process inspection as x-ray devices. 
 
Since this is a proof-of-concept prototype, the primary goal is to validate system feasibility rather 
than create a fully optimized industrial product. Defining the key system constraints is crucial for 
ensuring that the proposed L-PBF system meets both scientific and operational requirements. 
To translate these constraints into actionable design goals, the project requirements must be 
clearly outlined. The next section presents the specific technical, manufacturing, and facility-
related requirements that will guide the development of this system.

2.   Methodology Design 
Given the need for a compact, modular, and transportable L-PBF system, a structured 
design methodology was developed to ensure that each subsystem aligns with the research 
objectives. This methodology integrates iterative prototyping, requirement definition, and design 
validation to create a functional system suitable for synchrotron-based experiments. 
 
2.1. Design Method 
In engineering projects, the V-model diagram (Haberfellner Olivier de Weck Ernst Fricke 
Siegfried Vössner Fundamentals, n.d.) is commonly used to guide the different phases required 
for system engineering development. However, this project follows a more iterative approach 
focused on continuous testing and refinement of a prototype or CAD model until a final solution 
is achieved. For this reason, the V-model for prototype development has been adopted, as 
referenced in (Smallbone et al., 2014). As illustrated in Figure 3, the left-hand side of the 
diagram involves conceptualization, CAD modelling, and analysis, where the design is 
continuously tested virtually against project constraints. This iterative process leads to the right-
hand side of the diagram, where the prototype is built, individual components are tested, 
subsystems are integrated, and finally, the overall system is validated. 
This cyclical approach ensures that each design iteration undergoes multiple verification and 
validation steps, guaranteeing that the final system meets all necessary technical, mechanical, 
and operational requirements. 
 
 
Figure 3 V-model for prototyping, based on (Haberfellner Olivier de Weck Ernst Fricke Siegfried Vössner 
Fundamentals, n.d.) and (Smallbone et al., 2014) 
Following this methodology, the initial phase of this project focused on research, analysis, and 
documentation, as outlined in the introduction and this current chapter. With the design 
methodology established, the next step involves defining the technical specifications that will 
guide system development. These specifications ensure that the prototype meets performance 
expectations, remains compatible with beamline environments, and adheres to key practical 
constraints such as portability, mechanical simplicity, and ease of maintenance.  
System approval and verification plans are addressed in 4. Implementation Process, where 
each sub-assembly/component undergoes validation through iterative redesigns until final 
integration into the complete system.

2.2. System Requirements  
At this stage, the focus shifts toward defining the key requirements that will guide the 
design and development of the device. These technical specifications build upon the overall 
project objectives, setting precise constraints and dimensional parameters to ensure the 
intended functionality. Establishing these requirements is crucial to shaping the design process 
and aligning it with the project's goals. 
 
The main system specifications are as follows: 
 
A1. Build stage diameter must remain small to facilitate rapid sample testing. 
 
A2. The layering capacity must be up to 500 hundred layers with an average of a nominal 
thickness of 40-60 µm 
 
A3. The design must comply with supplier datasheets for the polyamide used in X-ray 
imaging, ensuring adherence to dimensional limitations. 
 
A4. Incorporation of a suction system to ensure a post-processing imaging 
 
A5. The width of the build platform has to be maintained as a minimum size 
 
A6. The recoater blades must be replaceable to allow maintenance and material adaptability. 
 
A7. The chamber design should allow for easy opening and cleaning 
 
A8. The recoater should be capable of depose common and specialized metal powders, 
including magnetic materials 
 
A9. The system must operate with two independent movements: a horizontal spreaderº and 
a vertical z-axis piston 
 
A10. The system must prevent powder spillage during recoating process and ensure a clear 
surface for scanning. 
 
A11. The suction system must be implemented to minimize post-processing steps and 
maintain a clean build environment. 
 
By defining these core requirements, the project ensures that the prototype aligns with both 
functional and operational needs while maintaining adaptability for future improvements.

interactions with the device while it is set in the synchrotron.

The design in Figure 5(C), like second design, also utilizes a linear motion but introduces a gap

Each of these approaches presents trade-offs in terms of size, efficiency, and system 
integration. To systematically evaluate these trade-offs and determine the optimal design for the 
main chamber system, a Pugh matrix is utilized (Cervone, 2009). This method provides a 
structured approach for assessing various design alternatives based on predefined criteria. The 
criteria, summarized and scored in Table 1, are further elaborated upon in the following bullet 
points to provide a complete understanding of the system's intended prototype development. 
 
• 
Overall Size & Space Utilization: Optimization of the vacuum and recoater head 
dimensions to ensure compact integration within the main platform. 
 
• 
Modular vs. Integrated Vacuuming & Recoating System: Whether the system is 
designed as a unified component or a modular setup for flexibility. 
 
• 
Recoater Head & Blade Compatibility: Ability to support different blade (soft, hard, flat, 
curved) shapes with an easy replacement, as well as alternative recoater head 
configurations (small, large, rounded). 
 
• 
Platform Space Optimization: Ensuring sufficient space for operational movement 
while minimizing the system’s overall footprint. 
 
• 
Motor Placement & System Complexity: Strategic motor placement to balance 
mechanical complexity and ensure seamless integration with other motion components. 
 
• 
Uniform Powder Deposition & Layer Formation: Ensuring consistent powder 
distribution across the entire build area without inconsistencies. 
 
• 
Vacuuming Efficiency & Powder Removal: System capability to minimize residual 
powder accumulation and ensure effective suction. 
 
• 
Motion & Displacement Configuration: Ensuring smooth, precise, and repeatable 
system motion (rotational vs. linear). 
 
• 
Ease of Maintenance & Additional features: Simplified connection between heads 
and maintaining vacuum efficiency. 
 
• 
Material Considerations for the Chamber: Designed for manufacturability using 
suitable materials, such as stainless steel or aluminum.

2.3.3. Recoater and vacuum heads  
At the end of this section (Subconclusion), the final design selection for each stage is 
outlined based on the sketches. The selected designs are modelled in a later project phase 
(Detailed Design). To ensure that the recoater and vacuum heads are compatible with 
previously discussed designs and meet every specified requirement, their design will prioritize 
the following considerations: 
 
• 
Recoater head priority: The recoater head has precedence over the vacuum head to 
maximize powder storage capacity. 
 
• 
Modular and adaptable design: The system must allow easy blade replacement and 
support modularity for flexibility in operation.  
 
• 
Recoater head functionality: The powder shall be gravity-fed and deposited into the 
build, while excess powder on the platform must be placed using the same recoater 
head (Bidare et al., 2017). 
 
==PAGE:22==

• 
Unobstructed movement: The cable connected to the vacuum head must be 
positioned to avoid interfere with system movement. 
 
• 
Dispenser internal inclination: The internal angle of the recoater head ranges between 
49° and 54°, depending on the alloy type used (Spierings et al., 2016). The maximum 
angle is selected to accommodate multi-material usage and facilitate powder flow 
testing. This range is derived from the avalanche angle, which represents the inclination 
angle between the linear regression of the powder surface heap and its horizontal 
baseline (Soh et al., 2006). Ensuring a smooth and consistent powder flow is essential 
to prevent deposition inconsistencies or clogging. Studies indicate that the internal 
height of the dispenser has minimal impact on these flow characteristics (‘Storage and 
Flow of Powders - Hopper Design’, 2008).   
 
• 
Dispenser mouth: The angle of repose must be considered to optimize powder flow 
from the dispenser onto the build stage. This angle refers to the slope of the powder 
cone formed when the material is deposited onto a flat surface. A minimum dispenser 
opening of 2.54 mm is considered. Previous studies (Spierings et al., 2016) indicate that 
while this size may be small for certain AM processes, it is sufficient for this SLM process, 
which requires only a small amount of powder per layer. This initial design will later 
undergo testing for validation. 
 
 
 
 
Based on these key criteria, three design concepts have been selected, each defined by its mid-
cross section. All designs share the same fundamental recoater head structure, consisting of 
two plates that secure the blades to the main body, along with an internal deposition shape for 
powder flow. Additionally, the vacuum system features an internal suction design with a 
dedicated cable connection gap.

3.1. Enclosure and structure 
This section provides a device structure overview, highlighting the importance of 
simplicity, adaptability, and modifiability to ensure seamless integration with an open-
architecture device. This flexibility allows different users to make modifications as needed. 
 
The following sections will introduce the proposed subassemblies, detailing their specifications 
and dimensional design constraints. However, this section specifically focuses on the materials 
used and the enclosure design, including the definition of the upper and lower chambers and 
their interconnection. 
 
• 
To maintain process integrity, the enclosure must effectively isolate the system from the 
external environment by preventing oxygen from entering while keeping the powder 
contained. To achieve this, stainless steel has been selected over aluminum due to its 
superior stiffness, higher temperature resistance, and ability to ensure laser safety. 
Additionally, non-magnetic stainless steel has been chosen, as it allows for the possibility 
of using magnetic powder for testing. 
 
• 
For cost efficiency and weight reduction, laser-cut sheet metal has been preferred over 
a thicker milled plate as the primary structural material. The selected 1mm thick sheets 
provide sufficient strength while keeping the device lightweight and functional, optimizing 
both performance and cost. 
 
• 
Fasteners have been chosen to facilitate quick setup and disassembly, which is crucial 
for operational efficiency. For a semi-continuous process, this design choice allows for 
easy restoration of the recoater head powder, while A7 emphasizes the importance of 
effortless cleaning of the upper chamber. 
 
Since the device cannot be fully sealed due to its size, using a gas shielding system could pose 
a risk of explosion. To mitigate this, internal sealants are not required; instead, maintaining a 
small separation between the fasteners and sheet metal ensures proper ventilation and 
enhances safety.

To ensure a strong transition between both chambers and prevent the polyamide tube 
from bearing the entire weight of the upper chamber, three aluminum stiffeners, each with a 
diameter of 10 millimeters, are used to provide structural support (Figure 10. Stiffeners

upper chamber’s main platform against the rod and another that connects the bottom chamber 
to the rod from below. Each stiffener is internally threaded to both aluminium plates which 
accommodate the screws, ensuring a stable and secure connection. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Additionally, the use of screws plays a crucial role in facilitating the X-ray scanning process after 
the operation is completed. Since scanning requires either a rotation stage or the ability to rotate 
the entire machine, disassembly is necessary. This design allows the rods to be unscrewed, 
enabling the upper chamber to rotate while the bottom chamber remains fixed with the rods still 
connected. Once the scanning is complete, reassembly is straightforward; simply screwing the 
upper and lower chambers back together, ensuring the device remains fully functional.

3.2.5. Movement RecVac 
To fully understand the movement of the recoater and vacuum system, as well as how 
each step aligns with the external laser operation, the process is explained in key phases. 
 
1. In the initial position or rest position, all components remain stationary, awaiting the 
next cycle. As shown in Figure 17, the recoater head (highlighted in red) contains the 
metal powder that will be spread onto the build stage. The vacuum head (in green) is 
inactive and will only be used at the end of the process to remove any excess powder 
before scanning. Meanwhile, the piston body (in purple) is responsible for controlling the 
vertical movement of the build stage, which is further detailed in a later section (Spring 
pulling mechanism). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
2. To begin the layering process, the piston moves downward, creating a precise gap 
between the build stage and the main platform that matches the required layer 
thickness. Once this gap is established, the recoater head moves forward, evenly 
distributing a uniform layer of powder into the newly created space. The vacuum’s 
suction remains off during this step to ensure that no powder is unintentionally removed. 
If the piston had not moved beforehand, the powder would remain trapped between the 
recoater blades without properly settling onto the build stage. Figure 18 illustrates this 
movement and the correct placement of the first unmelted powder layer. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
3. Once the powder has been spread, the recoater system moves back to its initial position, 
allowing the laser to begin the fusion process. The laser selectively melts the powder 
according to the programmed pattern generated by the software, solidifying the structure 
layer by layer. During this stage, the vacuum system remains off to ensure the powder

3.2.6. Gas shielding 
As specified in requirement R3, the system must support various industrial shielding 
gases with adjustable flow rates. The gas flow within the process chamber is a critical parameter, 
as inadequate removal of excessive powder in the laser path can result in microstructural 
defects. One of the most common defects is spattering, which arises from complex physical and 
chemical interactions between the laser, powder, atmosphere, and solidified structure (Wang et 
al., 2017). Spattering occurs when redeposited particles interact with the laser, locally increasing 
the layer thickness due to the formation of larger spherical particles. This phenomenon 
significantly impacts process stability and, consequently, part quality, where shielding gas flow 
plays a crucial role. Beyond establishing an inert atmosphere, the gas flow is essential for 
removing process by-products such as spatter and welding fumes from the process zone. An 
Figure 19. RecVac system lasering phase 
Figure 20. RecVac system vacuuming phase example

inadequate or uneven gas flow can lead to increased interaction between the laser and these 
by-products, further affecting the process outcome (Sendino et al., 2023). 
 
To mitigate these challenges, the following general recommendations have been established 
based on previous studies and reports: (Kjer, 2023) and (M. B. Kjer et al., 2023) 
 
• 
The gas flow velocity must be sufficiently high to clear soot, smoke, and spatter directly 
over the entire build stage. A shielding cross-flow with a velocity of 1 m/s at the build plate 
is recommended. 
 
• 
The cross-flow should be laminar, maintaining a uniformly constant velocity across the 
entire area. 
 
• 
The flow should be positioned as close as possible to the powder bed to contain the 
powder layer within the gap formed by the piston movement, ensuring that it is not 
disturbed. 
 
• 
According to findings in the (Kjer, 2023) a honeycomb inlet with an aspect ratio of 7:1 
achieves high flow speeds, approximately in the range of 1 m/s. The results demonstrated 
that in the X-direction of the XZ plane, this configuration effectively distributes gas flow 
across the entire build stage. 
 
• 
Initially, the honeycomb cap structure was designed with 1.5 mm hexagonal diameter 
cells and a 9.7 mm length inside, maintaining a realization ratio of 92%. (See Figure 22(B) 
Gas cross-flow perspective). 
 
 
• 
To ensure the above conditions are met, the gas inlet is positioned 1.187 mm above the 
build plate to facilitate the downward flow formation process. This height must be tested 
to confirm that it does not disturb the powder bed while still allowing for uniform gas 
distribution. The chosen height follows the recommendations from (Zhang et al., 2020), 
which indicate that spatter concentration is often observed near the outlet when it is not 
positioned above the main platform. To mitigate this issue, the outlet has been lowered 
to align with the build plane. (See Figure 19(B) Gas cross-flow front perspective)

The system is composed of several key elements: 
 
• 
A M2 screw secures and compresses the build plate against the gasket material and the 
piston body. 
 
• 
The M5x70 screws function as guide rods to define the start and end positions of the 
subassembly. The holes accommodating these screws are slightly larger than the actual 
screw shaft diameter, allowing for smooth movement without generating friction.  
 
• 
The end position is established using a washer and a nylon-locking nut. The washer is 
placed first because it marks the final position where the ball screw driver nut completes 
its movement. It is important to clarify that the piston body is mounted on top of the ball 
screw driver nut (Figure 24). The washer helps distribute impact forces over a wider 
surface, reducing localized stress on individual components and preventing loosening. 
The nylon locking nut follows, securing the washer while effectively resisting vibrations. 
Together, these two components function as a mechanical end-stop, ensuring controlled 
movement while minimizing excess vibrations and impact forces. 
 
• 
The system also incorporates four springs, which serve two primary functions: they 
either secure the case when downward movement is not required or actively contribute 
to the second split vertical motion of the piston. The precise operation of these springs 
and their role in movement control is detailed in a later section. 
 
• 
A gasket ring is positioned to prevent powder from falling into the clearance gap between 
the case and the piston body. Additionally, it helps keep the piston body correctly aligned 
at its top position; ensuring it remains on the same plane as the main platform, and 
maintains proper positioning as it moves downward during the layering process. 
 
• 
The piston body features a hole that is intentionally larger than the lead ball screw 
diameter to eliminate any friction between these two components. Additionally, the end 
surface of this hole serves as a critical movement stop, ensuring precise control over the 
piston’s motion. This function is further clarified and explained in Spring pulling 
mechanism.

strength-to-weight ratio, thermal resistance, and durability. This selection aligns with 
requirement R6.3, which considers future integration of a preheating feature, although this 
capability is not included in the current prototype. 
 
The nuts, screws, and springs are made of stainless steel, providing corrosion resistance and 
mechanical reliability. The gasket is a ceramic material supplied by the workshop, ensuring 
thermal stability and resistance to high temperatures.

for synchrotron applications (Li et al., 2024). Kapton is flame-resistant, beginning to char only at 
temperatures above 800°C, and has exceptional mechanical properties, remaining practically 
unaffected by moisture. Its dimensions were determined based on supplier datasheets to ensure 
an optimal placing.

After the testing and the different procedures done in the project, is essential following the V-model 
an iterative re-design and with more coherence when the product is being prototyping to make sure 
that the final product would be completely efficient and do not have the issues that has been fixed 
before. 
 
 
 
The RecVac system is set to undergo a redesign due to several inefficiencies identified during 
testing. Key findings from the evaluation indicate that the current system does not adequately 
control powder deposition, leading to excessive material spread and inconsistent layering. The soft 
blades in the initial design (A) were prone to bending, causing uneven distribution, while the 
modified design (B) with hard blades improved consistency but still deposited excessive powder 
(RecVac System Analysis). 
 
Additionally, the vacuum system demonstrated varying levels of suction efficiency based on cable 
length, with shorter cables providing stronger suction but still failing to meet the required 
performance for higher layer counts. 
 
To address these issues, the redesign will focus on: 
 
• 
Refining the mouth width to regulate powder spread more effectively. 
 
• 
Optimizing the hopper angle to enhance deposition accuracy. 
 
• 
Using hard blades to maintain structural integrity during movement. 
 
• 
Implementing a closed volume system to contain powder and minimize waste. 
 
• 
Improving vacuum efficiency by reducing the system’s size, integrating the vacuum with the 
recoater head, and repositioning the suction mouth closer to the platform. 
 
• 
External: One re-desing is the possibility to change the motors for new validation phases 
 
These improvements aim to enhance the precision, efficiency, and reliability of the RecVac system, 
making it more suitable for high-layer applications

6.    Discussion 
Following the testing of the upper chamber, several key areas for improvement have 
been identified to enhance the performance, structural integrity, and adaptability of the system. 
One of the primary modifications involves optimizing the piston body for a more robust 
connection to the linear ball bearing, improving both durability and precision. Additionally, motor 
selection requires refinement, as the current prototype revealed an over-dimensioned motor 
choice, exceeding the necessary capacity.  Moving forward, a more accurate motor will be 
selected, ensuring an optimal 200% capacity margin for future iterations while avoiding 
unnecessary oversizing.

Another critical aspect requiring refinement is the vacuum suction system, essential for efficient 
powder removal and improved X-ray imaging quality. While the initial RecVac system tests

efficiency depending on experimental conditions.

component, optimizing functionality, and addressing any unforeseen challenges. By following 
this iterative approach, the design can be progressively improved, ensuring that the final system 
meets the necessary technical requirements while remaining adaptable for future 
advancements and experimental needs.

7.    Conclusion 
7.1. Addressing Key Design Challenges 
To overcome the limitations of existing synchrotron-compatible L-PBF setups, this 
project systematically tackled several critical design challenges through an iterative 
development process, ensuring feasibility, flexibility, and performance optimization. 
 
7.1.1. Compatibility with Diverse Synchrotron Facilities 
The system was designed with a compact and modular structure, divided into multiple 
subassemblies that can either be assembled or redesigned into a single unit or separated as 
needed. This modular approach allows for greater adaptability, enabling integration into various 
beamline configurations without requiring extensive modifications. The final prototype has 
overall dimensions of 342 mm × 111 mm × 229 mm, which meets the initial requirement ensuring 
easy transportability. 
 
7.1.2. Mechanical Simplification 
Unlike conventional L-PBF machines that rely on multi-axis motion systems, this 
prototype was simplified to two linear movements, the recoater head and the piston assembly. 
This approach enhances precision, reduces mechanical complexity, and minimizes 
maintenance requirements while still maintaining the essential powder spreading and layer-by-
layer building process.  
 
7.1.3. Powder Scattering Mitigation 
A vacuum-assisted recoater system was incorporated to reduce powder scattering, 
optimizing post-scan imaging quality. This is critical for synchrotron X-ray scanning, as fine 
metal powders increase X-ray scattering, which can degrade imaging resolution. The vacuum 
system helps to efficiently remove excess powder, ensuring that the X-ray signal primarily 
interacts with the solid material rather than dispersed particles. Initial testing confirmed that the 
vacuum system could effectively minimize residual powder accumulation, improving the clarity 
of the scanned samples. 
Another connotation of having a recoater head is the possibility of reusing the powder sucked 
for next phases. 
 
7.1.4. Optimized Chamber Size and Recoating Operations 
The recoater head was designed to be as compact as possible, balancing chamber 
footprint minimization while still allowing for an adequate number of recoating layers. The first 
prototype demonstrated that the powder volume per recoating cycle is approximately 0.096 dl, 
enabling the system to build up to 146–147 layers per experiment. A future enhancement will 
focus on extending the recoater capacity to support up to 500 layers, ensuring reliable and 
repeatable operation for longer experimental runs. 
 
7.1.5. Open-Architecture Design 
The prototype follows an open-architecture framework, allowing seamless integration 
into various synchrotron setups. This modular approach ensures research adaptability, making 
it easier to implement future process modifications and expand its capabilities based on 
experimental needs. The system can be iteratively reconfigured, whether for new scanning 
techniques, improved powder handling, or alternative process parameters. 
 
7.1.6. Sensor-Based Monitoring and Post-Processing 
The system has been designed to integrate both operando and in-situ sensors, enabling 
real-time process monitoring and post-process inspection using X-ray devices. This feature 
allows researchers to track defect formation, melt pool dynamics, and material behaviour under 
controlled conditions. The first prototype successfully incorporated basic sensor inputs, paving 
==PAGE:54==
44 
Design of a miniature open-architecture laser powder bed fusion system 
 
the way for more advanced high-speed imaging, thermal monitoring, and feedback control 
mechanisms in future iterations. 
 
7.2. Achieving Proof of Concept through Iterative Development 
Since this is a proof-of-concept prototype, the primary objective was to validate system 
feasibility rather than develop a fully optimized industrial product. Each technical constraint and 
design requirement was translated into actionable engineering goals, guiding the iterative CAD 
modelling, component testing, and subassembly integration. The next phase of development 
will focus on refining the bottom chamber, testing gas cross-flow dynamics, and optimizing laser 
integration for enhanced synchrotron imaging compatibility. 
 
This structured development process ensures that the miniature open-architecture L-PBF 
system is not only functional within synchrotron facilities but also capable of continuous 
improvement, enabling future research in laser-material interactions, defect formation 
mechanisms, and additive manufacturing process optimization.
