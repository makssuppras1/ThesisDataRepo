Summary 
Anaerobic digestion is considered one of the valuable substitutes of fossil fuels for energy and 
fertilizer production, as well as waste and pathogens treatment. However, nowadays the majority of 
biogas plants exists because of relevant public subsidies. Therefore, enhancing the anaerobic 
digestion efficiency could render the technology more independent and competitive.  
Nowadays, many full-scale anaerobic digestion plants are operating under an ammonia inhibited 
steady-state, with up to 30% potential loss in methane yield. Among the investigated technologies to 
overcome ammonia inhibition, bioaugmentation with an ammonia-tolerant methanogenic consortium 
is promising. A milestone for bioaugmentation is to create an acclimated colony. Thus, in this work 
the performances of three common reactors (i.e. CSTR, fed-batch and batch) in enriching an ammonia 
tolerant methanogenic consortium were compared. All three reactor types were tested under both 
mesophilic and thermophilic conditions. 
Ammonium chloride and sodium acetate solution in BA medium were used as a source of ammonia 
and substrate respectively. A first baseline evaluation of the inocula was conducted in each reactor, 
by inoculating them under their original ammonia concentrations. Incubation time for 100% product 
efficiency was obtained in 7 and 4 days for mesophilic- and thermophilic batch reactor respectively. 
Full production efficiency was also reported in both fed-batches during all the baseline survey. 
Whereas, in CSTR the production efficiency was 80%, stable in mesophilic but not in thermophilic, 
where it dropped continuously.

Introduction 
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is an attractive technology that gained more and more interests during the 
recent years. In Europe for instance, more than 120 plants have been built between 2001 and 2010 
(Kleerebezem, 2015). The anaerobic digestion is a technology with a low carbon circle that represents 
a way to produce methane, fertilizers, treat pathogens and reduce waste size prior to further treatment. 
(Chen et al., 2008). Many agricultural and industrial wastes can be used for anaerobic digestion, 
because of their content of easily biodegradable materials (Chen et al., 2008). Furthermore, the 
utilization of the digestate as crop fertilizer contributes to the recirculation of nutrients, reducing at 
the same time the use of the synthetic one (Arthurson, 2009). The anaerobic digestion products are 
an energy-rich biogas and a nutrient-rich digestate which is typically used as a fertilizer. The biogas 
composition depends on the used substrate, typically 55-65% of CH4, 35-45% of CO2, whilst 
ammonia, H2S and other organic compounds only constitute less than 1% of the biogas (Angelidaki 
and Batstone, 2011). The vast majority of full scale biogas digesters are manure-based. Co-digesting 
with protein-rich substrate, such as slaughterhouse waste, urea, animal manure, and distillery waste 
is typical in order to rise the methane yield (Chen et al., 2008; Mata-Alvarez et al., 2014). However, 
these substrates have an high ammonia load, which can  severely inhibit methanogenesis or lower the 
potential yield (Nielsen and Angelidaki, 2008; Yenigün and Demirel, 2013). Indeed, many anaerobic 
digestion plants are underperforming up to 30% potential loss of methane yield due to ammonia 
inhibition (Fotidis et al., 2014; Yenigün and Demirel, 2013). Bioaugmentation as a possible solution 
to tackle ammonia inhibition has not been very well explored yet. A key point for bioaugmentation 
is to create an enriched colony and studies about the subject are limited or lacking.  
1.1   The AD process 
Anaerobic digestion is a biochemical process in which complex organic feedstock is broken down 
into simpler organic compounds and converted into biogas and digestate. This conversion occurs in 
the absence of oxygen or any other electron acceptor (e.g. nitrate or sulphate) and is carried out by a 
variety of synergistic microorganisms during complex metabolic reactions (Melville et al., 2014). 
These non-phototrophic microorganisms rely on the catalysis of redox reactions for the generation of 
energy and biomass growth. In particular, in the absence of electron acceptors, they carry out 
fermentation reactions in which the organic substrate is both the electron donor and acceptor of the 
redox reaction (Kleerebezem, 2015).

Figure 1: Flow chart of the anaerobic degradation of organic substances into methane and CO2 and microorganisms 
involved in the process (Tang et al., 2015). 
 
The process can be divided in four main phases, namely hydrolysis, fermentation (or acidogenesis), 
acetogenesis and methanogenesis (Figure 1) (Tang et al., 2015). Each phase uses specific consortia 
of microorganisms, which transform the products of the previous phase into substrates for the 
microorganisms of the subsequent step. If the growth of one specific group of microorganisms is 
inhibited, or if there are changes in the abundance of communities, the overall rate and efficiency of 
the process can be affected. It follows that the rate of the digestion is determined by the speed of the 
slowest phase (Melville et al., 2014). 
1.1.1   Hydrolysis 
During hydrolysis, complex polymeric compounds are firstly disintegrated from the initial organic 
material and afterwards converted into soluble oligomers and monomers by a group of 
microorganisms, called primary fermenting bacteria. In this way, the three main polymeric substrates, 
i.e. carbohydrates, proteins and lipids, are hydrolysed to monosaccharides, amino acids and long 
chain fatty acids (plus glycerol), respectively (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). The solubilisation 
process takes place outside the microbial cells and involves the secretion of substrate-specific 
extracellular enzymes by the bacteria.  
==PAGE:12==
 
12
The speed of the hydrolytic phase depends on the composition of the biomass feedstock to be 
processed and it is found to be lower when the material contains high quantities of lignocellulose 
(Melville et al., 2014). Due to the presence of lignin, the carbohydrates contained in the cellulose 
material are very hard to attack by the microorganisms and this is the reason why hydrolysis may be 
the rate-limiting step of the whole digestion process (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011).  
1.1.2   Fermentation 
Fermentation is a rapid process where many pathways are possible depending on the reactor 
conditions (e.g. concentration of hydrogen or acetic acid), leading to a variety of intermediate 
products (Table 1). Due to their relative abundance, the sugars and amino acids produced from 
hydrolysis are the most common substrates for fermentation and both fermentation reactions are quite 
energy yielding for the bacteria (Angelidaki et al., 2011). Generally, the majority is directly oxidized 
to acetate, with concomitant production of H2, whereas a minor part (typically around 30%) is 
converted to less oxidized volatile fatty acids (VFA), such as propionate, butyrate or lactate. 
Production of alcohols and CO2 also occurs. Table 1 shows some examples of possible pathways for 
glucose fermentation, depending on H2 concentration and pH conditions.

The microorganisms catalysing the reaction are called obligatory hydrogen producing acetogens, 
meaning that they can only utilize H+ as electron acceptor, without the possibility to switch their 
metabolism. Due to this lack of alternative metabolic pathway and the low energy yield of the 
reaction, the partial pressure of hydrogen becomes a crucial parameter for the outcome of the entire 
process. In particular, the oxidation to acetate becomes energetically favourable when the partial 
pressure of hydrogen is low, and the latter is kept low if H2 is utilized by the hydrogen-consuming 
microorganisms (hydrogenotrophic archaea), protagonists of the methanogenic phase (Angelidaki et 
al., 2011). 
There is a strong interdependence between the acetogenic bacteria and the methanogenic consortia, 
exemplified by the syntrophic relationship called interspecies hydrogen transfer (IHT). In order to 
optimize the H2 transfer from the H2 producers and the relative consumers, the microorganisms 
aggregate in compact microbial structures where the low distance facilitates the transfer (optimal 
level < 10 µm) (Boone et al., 1989; Stams and Plugge, 2009). The development of this syntropy is 
considered essential for a complete and high rate methanogenesis, as neither of the microorganisms 
(acetogenic and hydrogenotrophic) is able to degrade the substrate and produce methane without the 
presence of the other (Stams et al., 2012).

A third pathway starting from methylotrophic compounds (e.g. methanol, methylamines) is also 
possible, but it is of minor relevance as, typically, around 60-70% of the methane is produced from 
acetate and the remaining from H2 and CO2. In either cases, methanogenesis is considered the motive

As already mentioned, hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis is favoured by the formation of syntrophic 
associations between acetogens and H2 consuming archaea. In addition to this, the role of H2 and CO2 
as methanogenic substrates becomes more prominent also with the presence of acetate-oxidizing 
bacteria, which can oxidize acetate to form H2 and CO2 (Hattori, 2008). The reaction is energetically 
unfavourable, however once again syntrophic relations with hydrogenotrophic archaea allow the 
reaction to proceed, by consuming H2 and producing methane.

1.2   Factors affecting AD 
1.2.1   pH 
The pH interval at which bacterial growth and survival are not significantly affected varies depending 
on the group of microorganism, but in general is relatively narrow in anaerobic digestion. Values 
below 4.0 and above 9.5 are rarely tolerated and the optimal range goes approximately from 5.5 to 
8.5 (Melville et al., 2014). Methanogenic archaea prefer a neutral to slightly alkaline environment 
(between 7 and 8), whereas acidogenic bacteria have a lower optimum. The main factors affecting 
pH are the concentration of organic acids and carbon dioxide formed during the breakdown of organic 
compounds, which can lead to a drop in pH. On the other hand, the formation of ammonia from the 
degradation of proteins can induce an increase in pH. 
1.2.2   Volatile fatty acids and organic loading rate. 
Volatile fatty acids are fatty acids with an aliphatic tail composed by no more than six bounded 
carbons. In anaerobic digestion they can be produced in deferent steps: from the hydrolysis of lipids, 
through fermentation or, further down in the AD process, through the acetogenesis. The most 
important VFAs for anaerobic digestion are acetate, propionate, iso-butyrate, butyrate, iso-valerate, 
valerate and hexanoic acid. When an anaerobic digester is operating under stable condition, most of 
the substrate is directly converted through the hydrolysis into acetate and hydrogen. On the contrary, 
if methanogenesis activity is inhibited by extremes pH or the reactor is overloaded by an excess of 
acetate/hydrogen, a larger amount of butyrate, propionate and ethanol is produced. Therefore, 
increasing VFAs concentration is an indication of instability, this is particularly true for propionate 
(Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011; Kaspar and Wuhrmann, 1978). For instance Nakakubo et al., (2008) 
have reported VFA accumulation due to ammonia inhibition. Ammonia in fact, as it will be discussed 
==PAGE:16==
 
16
in section 1.3, affects methanogens that use acetate as main substrate. However, the VFAs remain a 
poor indicator of anaerobic digestion condition in fact, no clear boundaries can be defined. In general, 
for a stable anaerobic digestion condition VFA concentrations are between 0.1 and 2.5 g L-1 total 
VFAs (Angelidaki, 2015).  
Organic loading rate (OLR) is the amount of volatile solids with which a microbial community is fed. 
Its value has to be defined in respect to the biological conversion capacity of the system.  
1.2.3   Temperature 
The reactor operating temperature influences both physicochemical parameters, such as phase 
distribution or reaction rates, and also the activity of the microorganisms. Among the three major 
operating ranges, i.e. psychrophilic (0-25°C), mesophilic (25-40°C) and thermophilic (40-60°C), the 
last two are the most utilized for anaerobic digestion experiments (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). 
Figure 2 illustrates the influence of temperature on the biomass growth rate of mesophilic and 
thermophilic microorganisms. It is evident that, in both cases, the growth rate increases with 
increasing temperature until reaching a maximum optimum at around 38°C and 65°C, respectively. 
Furthermore, under thermophilic conditions higher growth rates at optimal temperature can be 
achieved compared to mesophilic. However, between 40 and 50°C, nearly all microorganisms are 
inhibited.

(Kleerebezem, 2015).   
 
==PAGE:17==
 
17
Temperature fluctuations in anaerobic digesters should be kept minimal, as they may cause shifts in 
the dominant microbial populations and affect the overall efficiency of the process. In fact, changes 
in the fermenting bacteria communities may alter the type of substrates supplied to the methanogenic 
archaea and, if these substrates cannot be utilized, then the methanogenesis is inhibited (Gerardi, 
2006).  
Even though thermophilic digesters have shown several advantages compared to mesophilic (e.g. 
increased pathogens destruction, improved availability of substrates, higher degradation rates of 
organic solids), their use has remained limited, mainly because of the larger process instability and 
ammonia inhibition risk (Kim et al., 2002) (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). On the other hand 
mesophilic are generally preferred because of better stability and operational performance, though 
further efforts are needed to remove pathogens in the digestate and the overall conversion efficiency 
is lower (Song et al., 2004) (Kim et al., 2002). 
1.2.4   C/N ratio 
The carbon nitrogen ratio C/N ratio is an important parameter for the anaerobic digestion stability. A 
ratio between 16/1 and 25/1 is optimal in AD. If the ratio is too high, methanogens may consume all 
the nitrogen for protein formation and no more is left to react with carbon material, causing organic 
accumulation and VFA increase. Vice versa, if the ratio is too low, ammonia accumulates and pH 
may rise, further inhibiting the AD (Kleerebezem, 2015; Wang et al., 2016). The accumulation of 
ammonia is a major problem in many full scale anaerobic reactors and its inhibitory effect is discussed 
in section 1.3. 
1.2.5   Nutrients, co-digestion Total and Volatile Solids 
The biosynthesis of cellular components necessary for growth, reproduction and maintenance, 
requires basic nutrients and a source of energy. Nevertheless, an excess of certain compounds could 
inhibit the growth. It is the case of relatively easily degradable compounds like lipids and proteins. 
Their concentration in the feeding stream must be carefully controlled. Lipids can lead to 
accumulation of long chain fatty acids inhibiting hydrolytic and acidogenic bacteria as well as 
methanogenic archaea. Whereas proteins degradation will result in ammonia formation which, in 
turns inhibits methanogens and rises pH, affecting the fermentation as well.  
The organic matter concentration in waste can be described as volatile solids (VS). This indicates the 
amount of dried matter in a sample lost after one hour at 520 °C in air. Most organic materials in fact, 
==PAGE:18==
 
18
ignite and combust at this temperature, whilst most inorganic compounds require higher temperatures 
to be burned. VS can be used to estimate the potential gas production of a certain substrate. Methane 
production in fact, is usually expressed as L CH4 g-1VS at STP conditions. Total solids (TS) indicates 
the sum of volatile solids plus the inorganic parts, and it is obtained by drying the sample 
(Kleerebezem, 2015) .  
Different substrates can have different VS concentrations and nutrients. Nowadays, one of the main 
used substrate in anaerobic digestion is manure. This is a rich substrate in terms of microbes and 
nutrients, furthermore it has high buffer capacity and it can dilute and facilitate the digestion of other 
substrates through co-digestion. AD of single substrates (mono-digestion) such as (1) manure, has 
some drawbacks like low organic loads and high N concentration or, in the case of (2) municipal solid 
wastes, improper materials or high heavy metals concentration. Some other substrates are (3) seasonal 
or only (4) partially degradable like the lignocellulosic one. There are protein rich substrates like (5) 
slaughterhouse wastes with the risk of ammonia and/or inhibition by high long fatty acids 
concentration. Most of the problems associated with the digestion of these substrates can be solved 
with co-digestion (Mata-Alvarez et al., 2014). Therefore, manure is usually co-digested with other 
substrates, frequently high protein and lipid-rich wastes in order to enhance the methane yield 
(Regueiro et al., 2012).

1.4   Solutions for the ammonia toxicity problem 
As discussed in section 1.3, total ammonia nitrogen and more precisely, the free ammonia component, 
is among the major operational problems in the AD of protein-rich substrates (Chen et al., 2008; 
Fotidis et al., 2013; Yenigün and Demirel, 2013). Therefore, a recovery strategy is required and here, 
some of the major investigated solution are discussed. (1) dilution with water, (2) increase the ration 
C/N (to maintain an optimal range of 20/30 – section 1.2.4), (3) reduce the temperature to decrease 
the free ammonia concentration and (4), inject an acclimated colony, capable to overcome the 
inhibition and increase the methane production (Fotidis et al., 2013; Kayhanian, 1999). 
 
Analysing the solutions, it was found that:  
1.   Dilution with fresh water in high total solid media resulted successful both in lab and real 
scale but, the dilution reduces the microbial abundancy as well significantly reducing in the 
short period, the biogas production and solid-treatment capability (Kayhanian, 
1999).Furthermore, it has been identified only as a palliative (I. a. Fotidis et al., 2014).  
2.   Increasing the C/N ratio has been reported as a slow but feasible remediation and only if the 
methanogenic archaea are still active. In fact, if methanogens are seriously inhibited, the 
VFAs accumulate due to the overloading, decreasing consequently the pH and further inhibit 
the digestion. Therefore, the C/N adjustment is successful only if the TAN concentration is 
slightly too high and the adjustment done promptly (Kayhanian, 1999).  
3.   As discussed in 1.3, the NH3 is the main responsible for ammonia inhibition. Its equilibrium 
with the ion form depends on pH and temperature. Decreasing the temperature lowers the free 
ammonia concentration alleviating the ammonia inhibition (Hansen et al., 1998). However, 
the methane yield is also reduced representing then an economical loss. Thus, this approach 
does not represent a definitive solution. 
4.   Acclimating microbial communities to high ammonia levels or, rise the tolerance capacity 
and improve the methane yield in AD, has been proven successful and economical 
==PAGE:21==
 
21
advantageous (Angenent et al., 2002). Fotidis et al., (2014) have reported an increased in 
methane yield when an acclimated methanogenic consortia was inject in a CSTR reactor under 
an inhibited steady state. However, this method is still under investigation. 
1.5   Bioaugmentation 
Bioaugmentation is the addition of a prepared microbial culture in order to improve operational 
conditions of a certain process (Gerardi, 2003). The process could be degradation of a pollutant, boost 
of a certain reaction or overcoming an inhibition problem (I. a. Fotidis et al., 2014; Walker, 2009).  
Up to date, not many studies have been found regarding the use of bioaugmentation to enrich 
ammonia tolerant consortia and overcome its toxicity effect.  
The first step for bioaugmentation is the culture enrichment under the pollutant or the inhibitory factor 
(Walker, 2009). Bioaugmentation of ammonia inhibited steady state reactors seems a promising 
method. However, a clear method on the best way to prepare the colony has not been reported yet.  
The bottlenecks seem that the colony must not only be enriched and acclimated to ammonia, but also 
to be able to adapt to the target environment (e.g. inhibited reactor). The creation of an enriched 
community may introduce such biases in a way to avert microbial settling inside the target 
environment (Walker, 2009). Nielsen et al., (2006) in fact, have found an increased methane 
production after bioaugmentation but the community did not stabilized, being consequently washed 
out. The creation of an acclimated community should consider its ability to adapt to the target 
environment and no being rejected. Furthermore, there are studies confirming a spontaneous 
acclimatization of high ammonia tolerant methanogenic consortia with no need for bioaugmentation 
(Abouelenien et al., 2009). 
 
1.6   Acclimatization processes and types of reactors that could be used to acclimatize 
Acclimatization is a process in which an organism adjusts to a gradual change in its environment. 
The adaptation enables the maintenance of a certain performance across a range of environmental 
conditions. CSTR, fed-batch and batch are the most used reactors in anaerobic digestion (S. Liu, 
2013a). Anaerobic digestion reactors can vary at sophistication levels but they generally share a series 
of common components that allow separation of biogas and digestate from the liquid phase. 
==PAGE:22==
 
22
According to the type of substrate that it intends to treat, if dry or wet and the amount of total solids, 
two groups of processes can be distinguished: continuous (for example continuous stirred tank reactor 
- CSTR) and discontinuous, (batch and fed batch) (Fischer et al., 2014; Melville et al., 2014). The 
main difference between the two groups is the flowrate of treated substrate; the former has a constant 
flow in and out while the second is run with discrete replacement of volume at regular time intervals.  
1.6.1   Batch 
Batch reactor is an idealized closed system, a vessel where the liquid or solid to process is charged 
from the top and only at the beginning of the process. The vessel is consequently sealed for some 
reaction period (for instance till a constant gas production), then the reactor is opened and products 
are removed. Ideally, the batch reactor’s content is fully mixed therefore, the concentration of each 
species is homogeneous within each fluid in the vessel (Nazaroff and Alvarez-Cohen, 2001; Waites 
et al., 2001) 
This kind of reactor can be equipped with stirring, warming/cooling system and connections for 
removal of gas (from the top) and products (for example digestate - from the bottom). After the 
substrate is sufficiently treated, the majority of the substrate volume is removed, keeping 10-15% in 
order to inoculate another batch process (Melville et al., 2014; Tchobanoglous et al., 2003).  
Microorganisms in a batch reactor during fermentation goes through six distinctive growth phases: a 
lag, an acceleration, an exponential, a deceleration, stationary and death. During the lag phase, 
microorganisms adapt to the substrate and the environment so, growth is almost absent and the 
microbial population remain relatively constant (microbes may need to synthetize enzymes to extract 
nutrients or vitamins from the available substrate).  
Afterwards, there is the acceleration phase till the maximum growth rate (µmax) according to batch 
specific conditions, and subsequently the exponential phase starts. In this stage the cells 
number/biomass increase at a constant rate and at any given time of the exponential phase, the 
biomass concentration can be estimated as follow:

1.6.2   Fed-batch 
Fed-batch is a type of reactor technically in between batch and continuous system (CSTR). It was 
developed to keep high growth rate but limited-available substrate, since cell metabolism overflow 
created toxicants for the process itself. Basically, fed-batch starts as a batch by using a seed culture 
and fed when the exponential growth turns to its end. The feeding can be done with various substrates 
and there is no removal of culture fluid or outflow. The feeding strategy means the amount of 
substrate and the interval used time in order to keep the growth of microorganisms at a desired specific 
rate. The feeding can be performed in order to avoid accumulation of inhibitory by-products for 
instance volatile fatty acids in case of anaerobic digestion, making the system always efficient. 
Therefore, there is a metabolic control that can avoid osmotic effects and catabolite repression and 
accumulation of overflow metabolites.(Shuler, 2015). The feeding mode can be modified according 
to the microbial growing phase to optimize the process. The strategy can be continuous, with pulses 
as a shot feeding, with a singular or multiple substrates, increasing linearly, exponentially, or constant 
in time.  
 
The constant feeding is the simplest and widely used strategy, whereas the exponential can be 
adjusted at any rate up to the maximum specific growth rate in the exponential phase of a batch 
growth. The feeding volume increases exponentially (Figure 3). This method however, has the

drawback of possible by-product accumulation a consequent inhibition. The process terminates when 
reaching the maximum reactor volume. 
The fed-batch is a reactor in between batch and CSTR systems, but, with the unique possibility to 
control compound that affect kay reaction rates (Lim and Shin, 2013). It is useful in achieving high 
products concentration as a result of high density of cells and unlike batch, fed-batch is a controlled 
growth system found more productive than batch (Longobardi, 1994). 
The feeding design could be done with a more concentrated substrate compared with the microbial 
biomass in the reactor or follow the production/depletion of compounds/nutrients in order to avoid 
their accumulation. As previously stated, exponential fed batch it is similar to a chemostat reactor i.e. 
CSTR, it is possible in fact to reach a quasi-steady state, with the growth µ = D where D does not 
remain constant like in CSTR but decreases with time (Fischer et al., 2014)

Aim 
The first step to overcome ammonia inhibition through bioaugmentation is to build an ammonia 
tolerant methanogenic consortia. Indeed, it is widely reported the highest sensitivity of methanogens 
in regard to ammonia. Up to date, only few studies have been made on bioaugmentation in anaerobic 
digestion and none reported how to obtain the most adapted microbial community.  
Therefore, the aim of this work is to start building a high tolerant microbial community in order 
overcome one of the biggest anaerobic digesters problems. In particular, the role of diverse reactors 
in enriching ammonia tolerant methanogenic consortia has been investigated. This has been done by 
comparing the enrichment performances of the three most common reactors i.e. continuous stirred 
tank reactor, batch and fed-batch and then identifying under which reactor the highest enrichment is 
obtained. 
CSTR, batch and fed-batch were compared under both mesophilic and thermophilic inocula. The 
inocula were taken from biogas plants in Denmark, Hashøj and Snertinge respectively and the used 
substrate was sodium acetate in basal anaerobic medium (BA).  
Baseline evaluation of the inocula was made in order to evaluate the inocula suitability for the 
experiment. The assay was done by running all reactors for a certain length of time with the inocula, 
under their respective original ammonia concentrations. 
In batch the exposition was done in two ways, directly and stepwise with the scope to compare the 
different incubation times and investigate the acclimation behaviour. Through the direct exposure 
four ammonia concentration were tested immediately, increasing of 1, 2, 3 and 4 grams the original 
concentration. In stepwise experiment the four concentration were increased step by step, re-
inoculating the acclimated colony to one gram of ammonia concentration more than the previous step. 
In fed-batch and CSTR the increment was done instantaneously in all the broth and subsequently, the 
feeding was adjusted with one gram more ammonia than the foregoing period. 
With this study, the first brick to build an ammonia tolerant community for bioaugmentation has been 
settled.

1.12   Specific methanogenic activity (SMA) test 
A specific methanogenic activity (SMA) test on specific substrates has been carried out for 
thermophilic CSTR and fed-batch samples with the scope to investigate which pathway has prevailed 
under the two systems. The samplings from CSTR and fed-batch reactors were done after the end of 
the thermophilic fed-batch experiment (at day 65 period P-VII). The TAN concentration was detected 
at 4.18 ± 0.12 and 6.16 ± 0.02 g NH4
+-N L-1 respectively. The experiments have been conducted in 
triplicates using 118 mL serum bottles, inoculating 20 mL of sample to 19.86 mL BA-medium 
(Appendix), 0.04 mL vitamins, 0.1 mL Na2S×9H2O (25 g L-1) as reducing agent and substrate. The 
tested substrates were formate (0.08M – as sodium formate), acetate (0.02 M – as sodium acetate) 
and hydrogen/carbon dioxide (80/20) under 1 atmosphere. Bottles with only BA medium and inocula 
(no substrate) were used as control. The SMA was calculated as the initial, linear methane 
accumulation rate per biomass VS added for each test. 
1.13   Analyses 
The methane production in batch bottles was measured by gas chromatography in a Shimatzu gas 
chromatograph with a flame ionization detector. Methane content in CSTR reactors headspace was 
measured with a gas-chromatograph (GC-TCD) fitted with a column of 1.1 m _ 3/16 00Molsieve 137 
and 0.7 m _ 1/400 Chromosorb 108 (MGC 82-12, Mikrolab a/s, Denmark). Volatile fatty acids (VFA) 
accumulation in the CSTR reactors was determined with a gas-chromatograph (HP 5890 series II) 
equipped with flame ionization detector and a FFAP fused silica capillary column, (30 m × 0.53 mm 
i.d., film thickness 1.5 μm), with nitrogen as carrier gas. Total solids (TS), volatile solids (VS), total 
Kjeldahl nitrogen (TKN), total ammonia, and pH were determined according to APHA’s Standard 
Methods. The pH fluctuation reactors medium was measured with PHM99 LAB pH meter.

higher temperature i.e. in Tfb, also the free ammonia concentration is higher and the microbial 
community is less reach. Microbial diversity ensures that, another species can take the place of the 
inhibited one to carry out a certain reaction (Carballa et al., 2011). Once again, an appropriate feeding 
strategy could help to overcome intermediate inhibitions and drops in production.

Likewise other studies, fed-batch have been found more productive compared to batch and continuous 
system under certain conditions (Lim and Shin, 2013; Longobardi, 1994; San and Stephanopoulos, 
1984). The reason can be found in the kinetics, the absence of an outflow, the controlled feeding and 
for unmixed fed-batch, the absence of mixing. For microbial enrichment, fed-batch combines 
advantageous aspects from both CSTR and batch systems without their main drawbacks, i.e. (i) no 
loss of microbes like in CSTR, (ii) “dosed” amount of substrate to be processed (and not the full 
amount like in batch); (iii) the absence of mixing, that could affect the IHT and finally, (iii) no need 
==PAGE:58==
 
58
to open the reactor and re-inoculate in order to enrich (like in batch). This process could reduce or 
momentarily stop the microbial activity, since it brings strict anaerobic microbes in contact with air 
and ambient temperature.  
Change in microbial community is also a factor. Tang et al. (2015) detected changes in microbial 
dynamic when fermenting bacteria and methanogens under limiting-available substrate. In this 
condition, certain forms of competition for the substrate arise leading to a selective community. In 
this scenario the microbial community inside the investigated reactors is not affected by ammonia 
only but also by the reactor itself.  
Other considerations: 
1.   CSTR and fed-batch are similar in “running working-load”, but the set-up of the first is much more 
time consuming, nevertheless, it is possible to apply a feeding system to the fed-batch reactor as 
well. The set-up of a batch is easy as that in fed-batch (without pumping system) but, the 
enrichment process requires to re-inoculate the broth, whereas fed batch requires feeding at certain 
time lapsing.  
2.   Energetically talking, CSTR is the only system that requires a higher energy consumption 
compared to the other reactors, for the pumping and the stirring apparatus. However, an automatic 
feeding system can be applied to fed-batch as well increasing the costs but reducing the “running 
working-load”.  
3.   The fed-batch reactor, as described in section 1.6.2, is a controlled system, so the microbial growth 
is controlled by the adopted feeding strategy, whilst in batch it is not. If the feeding strategy is 
appropriate and microbes are fed in order to keep a constant growth (specific growth rate – 
exponential growth, eq. 9), the growth would be optimal otherwise it would be suboptimal. The 
suboptimal feeding issue started from a foregoing experiment, where all the reactors where fed 
with 2 g HAc L-1 (a typical VS concentration in AD) and accumulated in all reactors creating then 
inhibition. Therefore, all the experimental line was re-started for the second time.  
1.19   SMA test 
For all the reactors and both operational temperatures, the formate activity was at least 50% higher 
than the H2+CO2 and Acetate (Figure 30). Furthermore, the H2+CO2 activity was also high compared 
to the one for acetate. This is particularly evident under thermophilic condition, where acetate activity 
is practically absent. Therefore, hydrogenotrophic methanogens (that can use formate and/or 
==PAGE:59==
 
59
H2+CO2) are found to be much more active than the acetoclastic methanogens. This was expected 
and confirms that the former are more robust against ammonia inhibition (Wang et al., 2016; Yenigün 
and Demirel, 2013). The absence of aceticlastic methanogens in thermophilic condition is explained 
by the general reduced microbial diversity observed in thermophilic condition compared to the 
mesophilic one (Carballa et al., 2011) The consumption of formate prevailed over H2+CO2, even 
though methane production from the latest is thermodynamically more favourable (∆G°' = -130.4 and 
- 119.5 kJ mole-1 CH4 for H2+CO2 and formate respectively). This can be explained by their different 
availability: formate is completely dissolved in the medium, thus directly available to 
hydrogenotrophs, while H2+CO2 have to diffuse inside the liquid.

1.20   Conclusions and suggestions 
This study shows a better performance of fed-batch compared to batch and CSTR reactors, in order 
to enrich ammonia tolerant methanogenic consortia under both mesophilic and thermophilic 
conditions. However, the lack of time did not allow to investigate completely the batch reactor under 
stepwise ammonia exposure. Regarding continuous stirred tank reactor, it resulted absolutely not 
appropriate for microbial enrichment. In general, enrichment under mesophilic was more successful 
than that under thermophilic condition.  
In CSTR methane production dropped constantly to 100 NmL CH4 g-1 HAc (about 30% of the 
expected yield), where it became stable. The yield did not recover despite ammonia was not further 
increased. 
In fed-batch, acclimation to +3 g NH4
+-N L-1 (more than the original values) was obtained in 80 and 
60 days for mesophilic and thermophilic respectively, with a final production efficiency of > 75% 
and 74%. In batch direct exposure, 100% production efficiency was reached only under +2 g NH4
+-
N L-1 in 66 and 64 days for meso- and thermophilic respectively (considering the baseline periods). 
However, growth in mesophilic direct exposure was observed under all ammonia concentrations. 
More precisely, under +3 g NH4
+-N L-1 the experiment resulted not reliable because of a very short 
incubation time (probably an operational mistake), whilst under +4 g NH4
+-N L-1 the production 
efficiency reached 73% in about 78 days (at the time of this document and considering the baseline 
period).  
In meso-batch stepwise exposure at +2 g NH4
+-N L-1 the incubation time was shorter than under +1 g 
NH4
+-N L-1. In this scenario, further expositions are promising. 
In thermo-batch direct exposure, growth was observed in all exposures except at +4 g NH4
+-N L-1. In 
stepwise exposure to +2 g NH4
+-N L-1, no growth was seen after about 20 days of incubation. 
The success of batch and fed-batch compared to CSTR is most likely to be related to avoidance of 
the washing-out taking place in the latter. Indeed, no outflow for batch and fed-batch means no loss 
of nutrients and microbes. The success of fed-batch over batch is probably due to better kinetic and 
the absence of other disturbances other than the sole ammonia increase. In fact, batch bottles have to 
be opened and the broth re-inoculated to seed the next experiment. This process could reduce or 
momentarily stop the microbial activity, since it brings strict anaerobic microbes in contact with air 
==PAGE:61==
 
61
and ambient temperature. On the other hand, fed-batch is fed by injections, thus no temperature 
variations or exposition to oxygen occur. 
Finally, substrate concentration and freshness of inocula are key factors in fact, all the experimental 
line failed in the first two attempts. In the first attempt, thermophilic inoculum was not sufficiently 
active and no growth was observed, while in the second a normal VS concentration for AD was used 
but it resulted too high and accumulating in the form of acetate only.  
1.21   Future prospects 
This study represents the first step for developing an acclimated microbial community to high 
ammonia concentration and bioaugmentation purpose. This document investigated the possibility to 
enrich the most ammonia-sensitive microorganisms involved in AD. Nevertheless, anaerobic 
digestion is a complex process that involves various types of microorganisms and reactions. Richness 
in diversity is key factor in AD, as well as microbial cooperation (e.g. the IHT). For this reason, 
acclimating only methanogens does not imply the ability of the enriched inoculum to adapt to actual 
full-scale reactor conditions, although they are accounted to be the most sensitive microorganisms. 
Thus the success of a bioaugmentation process is not implied. In an actual reactor substrate is not 
acetate but a complex mixture and cooperation with other microbes is necessary. In this prerspective, 
further investigations have to be made not only for testing other reactor types but also a more complex 
community and substrates.  
The success of batch and fed-batch compared to CSTR is likely related to the washing-out of 
microbes. Increasing the ammonia stepwise with lower concentrations (especially at the beginning) 
could enable IHT and the related microbes to fully settle inside the reactor. Other solutions to 
overcome the absolute observed inefficiency of CSTR could be the use of a turbidostat system or 
carriers, in order to favour the permanence of biomass inside the reactor.  
Regarding fed-batch, the used feeding strategy hold a pivotal role in microbial growth. In this 
experiment the feeding strategy has not changed during the experiment, however the feeding could 
be harmonized with the microbial status.

Preparation of sterile, anaerobic BA-medium in vials according to Angelidaki et al., (1990).

The stock solutions are specified below. The solution is gassed with mix-gas (20% CO2, 80% N2) for not less 
than 15 min. 
Measure pH – it should be 6.9-7.0, if not the mixture is gassed again. 
Distribute anaerobically into vials, which is done by flushing the vials with the mix-gas and distribute using 
an anaerobic glass syringe, continuously flush the distributed medium in the vials when closing with rubber 
stoppers and cap with aluminium vcrimps. The vials are autoclaved at, 121°C for 20 min. After autoclaving, 
the mixture (pre-BA medium) can be stored at 4°C. 
 
Before use add the following using sterile anaerobic techniques (if you are not familiar with the technique you 
should ask for instructions): 
 
Na2S solution 
1 mL/ 100 ml BA medium 
Vitamin solution 
1 mL/ 100 ml BA medium 
 
The BA medium is now finished and can be used for inoculation.
