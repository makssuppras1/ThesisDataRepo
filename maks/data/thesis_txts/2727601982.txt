ABSTRACT 
This thesis explores the potential of legumes, particularly peas (Pisum sativum), as a valuable source 
of alternative proteins for sustainable food products. Peas are favoured by industries due to their 
higher yield, easy availability, and favourable nutritional profile. The essential amino acid 
composition, a key consideration for protein sources, is addressed, with literature review findings 
facts surrounding lower bioavailability through the calculation of Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid 
Scores (DIAAS) is presented. Misconceptions regarding the presence of anti-nutritional properties in 
peas were dispelled.  
Several methods have been devised to achieve the reduction of anti-nutrients and increase of protein 
content, one of which involves solid state fungal fermentation. This approach harnesses the 
interaction between fungal microorganisms and macromolecules present in the Legume seed 
cotyledons, such as proteins and lipids, in legumes, leading to chemical changes that enhance protein 
accessibility. It is important to necessitate the penetration of fungal hyphae to deeper lengths. To 
facilitate this, cellulases are employed to break down the rigid cell wall, enabling hyphae growth. To 
enhance the efficiency of these enzymes, introduction of expansin is proposed. Expansins are wall 
loosening proteins naturally found in plant cell walls. The similarities between fungal expansins and 
plant expansin help with experimental overview of using fungal expansin, demonstrating the 
synergistic effect of expansin in increasing cellulose hydrolysis of Avicel, a cellulose model substrate, 
under slightly acidic pH and high temperature conditions. 
By exploring the solid-state fungal fermentation approach and introducing expansins, this thesis aims 
to provide statistical, and theoretical information regarding Peas, paving the way for the development 
of novel sustainable food products with improved nutritional value and environmental impacts. 
Key words: Plant based proteins, Bioavailability, Anti-nutritional properties, Solid state fungal 
fermentation, Synergistic effects of expansin and cellulases.

INTRODUCTION 
This thesis report explores the nutritional aspects of proteins derived from plant-based sources and 
its impact on human health and environment. It examines the use of environmentally friendly and 
efficient technologies in the development of sustainable future foods, which are often criticized for 
their reduced digestibility and elevated levels of anti-nutrients. These plant-based foods serve as a 
promising alternative to animal meat, addressing the adverse environmental and health impacts 
associated with its production and consumption respectively.  
We try to approach the challenges in Solid state fungal fermentation, where fungal penetration falls 
short in accessing the nutrients and proteins in the depth of plant material. The main reason for this 
drawback is the thickness and rigidity of the plant cell wall. The enzymes released by the fungal 
organisms alone don’t suffice the amount required to cleave the microfibers and penetrate within 
solid plant material. Hence, use of ‘Cellulase’ an enzyme degrading the cellulose in the plant cell wall, 
is implemented and proven to be efficient with cleaving and accessing plant material. Diffusion model 
for solid state fungal fermentation were addressed in theory. The experimental overview addresses 
the use of ‘Expansin’ that is a wall loosening protein existing in plant cell wall. Their primary function 
is cell wall extension and growth of the plant cell. Consequences of expansin slipping over each other 
and extending plant material is wall loosening, which helps with the enzyme to hydrolyse plant cell 
wall material into reducing sugars efficiently and with increased activity. This synergistic effect is 
studied under acidic pH and high temperatures. Thus, significantly increasing ability to access 
proteins. With increase in protein content the digestibility increases, increasing the amount of amino 
acid bioavailability. 
Addressing this problem reduces the time required for Solid state fermentation significantly and 
increases production. The price becomes cheaper with better product quality, which expand the 
market opportunity for plant-based products. 
As an approach to deal with the challenges faced during production of Plant-based protein foods, this 
report further delves into addressing the Myths and Facts about plant-proteins, by comparing 
different literature studies and data presented in some in-vitro studies of plant-sources. The myth 
about lower protein digestibility and bioavailability, was factually backed by values of DIAAS that plant 
proteins have lower bioavailability as compared to animal proteins. Anti-nutrients present in peas can 
be removed with help of processes like Soaking, Germination, Fermentation, pressure cooking, Heat 
treatment etc which reduced the risk of diseases. Effect of fungal fermentation on plant material does 
increase nutritional aspects which proved right when bioavailability and digestibility were seen 
increased.  
The Experimental work is an attempt to provide with statistical facts to the theory above. Using Avicel 
a microcrystalline cellulose as substrate and Cellulase enzyme, the conversion of cellulose to reducing 
sugars was quantified using DNS reagent at 50 °C over a period of 48 hours. It was compared with 
addition of fungal and bacterial expansin and BSA (bovine serum albumin) at different concentrations 
with same temperature and time. The amount of reducing ends formed (in millimolar) with respect 
to time (hours) was compared for different parameters to understand the Synergistic effect of 
addition of Expansin with Enzyme for hydrolysis.

BACKGROUND  
Proteins have played a crucial role in our diet since infancy. The consumption of protein holds 
immense significance as they serve as the fundamental building blocks of life. They serve as structural 
component in human tissues. Various anabolic processes are dependent on protein intake. Processes 
like formation of enzymes, hormones and haemoglobin are performed by human body with the help 
of proteins. 1   
While the human body has the capability to convert most proteins into amino acids, there exist eight 
essential amino acids that cannot be synthesized by the human body and must be obtained through 
food intake.2 Dietary patterns vary across different regions, leading to diverse nutritional profiles 
among populations. In the Western world, animal proteins are heavily relied upon to fulfil the 
necessary nutritional requirements due to effective cooking methods and the affordability of animal 
meat, poultry, and dairy products. However, this dietary scenario does not apply universally. In 
countries like India, Ethiopia, and Israel, where religious practices restrict the consumption of animal 
proteins in the regular diet, deficiencies in proteins have been observed, particularly among children 
and women3. Similar findings have emerged from surveys conducted in countries like Nigeria and 
others, where poor food standards and un-affordability contribute to inadequate protein 
consumption 4.

As the global population continues to grow, there is an escalating demand for a wider range of food 
options. It is crucial that we remain mindful of the fact that the most significant negative effects on 
the global environment, such as greenhouse gas emissions, stem from irresponsible food production 
practices. Excessive consumption of animal meat not only results in consequences of chronic illness 
but also contributes to a decline in biodiversity. 7 
The superiority of animal protein over plant protein have had significant implications over the past 
years where the demand for consumption increased. This led to a huge wave of increase in animal 
breeding and factory farming.8 From year 1960-2014 there was a huge increase in production of meat 
due to industrial processing and large farm lands.8 The consequences of which are now seen in form 
of increased global warming and decreased food security9. Epidemiological studies have shown that 
higher consumption of red meat and meat products concentrated with sodium have led to higher 
chances of cardiovascular diseases and can cause carcinogenic effects on human health. 10 
The demand for vegan and vegetarian options is increasing in the western world due to wide-spread 
awareness about the harmful effects of Meat which directly, or indirectly have a negative impact on 
human health.  
Impacts of animal-meat production –  
Environmental impact caused due to gruelling efforts that are required for a meagre outcome, has 
made animal-meat production a major cause of greenhouse gas emissions. The total impact of animal 
meat production leaves 12% greenhouse gas emissions in the world. 30% of biodiversity loss has been 
reported as consequences of human activities in livestock handling. 11 The environmental impacts of 
Plant proteins have been calculated based on life cycle assessment (LCA) analysis and compared to 
meat 9. Based on the life cycle assessment, 1 kg of protein from beef generates 45 - 650 kg of CO2 in 
comparison to 10 kg of CO2  generated per 1 kg of Tofu 9.  
We often ignore the toxic health impacts caused by red meat which are chronic and are severe, like 
Diabetes, coronary heart diseases, stroke, cancer, etc. a 100 g/day of unprocessed meat can cause up 
to 11% increase in stroke, consumption of 50 g/day of processed meat resulted in 4% increase in 
prostate and breast cancer, as resulted from cohort studies10 . Allergens are another reason why 
animal proteins are huge concern for human health. 
Factory farming for scaling up the production percentage is causing increasing human pathogens 
which are related to zoonotic diseases. 12 The conditions in which animals are bred and grown have 
caused spill-over of pathogens, adaptations and genetic diversification of these pathogens which are 
harmful to human health as well. Food insecurities are caused by contamination of food through 
microbial diseases due to cross-contamination, growth of pathogens, or physical contact. The 
accumulation of chemical waste in water streams, animal feed, etc, over the years lead to reduced 
life expectancy. Diseases like rabies, influenza, swine-flu, etc are all results of increasing the 
production. 8

Alternative to Animal protein – 
Due to these above-mentioned negative consequences, Alternative Proteins are taking over the 
market with steady growth. Alternative proteins are alternative sources to the existing conventional 
animal proteins. These can be from plant-based sources, or fungi-based sources as well. Legumes like 
peas, fava, soy, and chickpeas13  are growing field of research as they serve as high potential protein 
sources. Plant proteins have shown the same nutritional content and can be sustainable and 
affordable to extract proteins.  
Plant proteins offer significantly greater health benefits compared to animal proteins despite facing 
scrutiny due to lower bioavailability and presence of anti-nutritional factors. Preferring Plant based 
diet have better cardiovascular outcomes compared to an omnivorous diet. Additionally, there is 
reduced risk of morbidity and mortality from ischemic heart diseases, reduced incidences of cancer, 
decreased risk of diabetes, and low metabolic syndrome. 14,15  
Among Plant-based proteins, there are many different types based on which part of the plant has 
been used for the extraction of proteins. The seeds, roots, leaves, etc are all used for the extraction. 
Legumes, cereals, etc are some common plant proteins that are extensively used in the industry for 
producing dairy, or plant-based meat, energy bars, and powders.13,16,17 
The plant-based meat industry is growing faster with increasing consumer demands. With better 
technologies, major research in this field revolves around plant-based meat analogues that mimic the 
animal meat in texture, flavour, smell and taste. Using different technologies of extrusion, thermal 
treatments and fermentation the products are close to meat in nutritional profile as well as providing 
the essential amino acids which are not fulfilled by consumption of a raw vegetable diet. Soy and 
chickpeas have been extensively used to produce tempeh, tofu, and other plant-based meat burgers 
from companies like Beyond burger and Impossible meat. Among the existing market, Peas (Pisum 
Sativum) have also contributed to the processing of energy bars, protein powders, and dairy and meat 
analogues.17After a decade of research, there still exists some white spaces in the field that need 
further study and comprehensive understanding.  
Legumes –  
The species of leguminous family are one of the best alternatives for fulfilling the dietary protein 
consumption requirement. 15 This source of plant-proteins is amongst the very species that can be 
used for efficient production of dietary proteins. The dried seeds of legumes contain high amounts of 
proteins.15 They can serve as protein staple and cash crops for sustainable food instead of animal 
meat. Especially in the developing nations where it majorly contributes to regular diets. 18 The FDA 
advises to consume at least 2 serving per day of any beans from this group.  
Pulses / Beans have been considered to have high protein content, and after years of research they 
have emerged as even better nutrient-rich food where they have soluble dietary fibres. Legume 
proteins are also known to increase the calcium retention when compared to animal proteins. The 
reason is the lower sulphur amino acids (SAA) content of beans which are also responsible for lower 
protein efficiency ratio.15

Peas (Pisum sativum) as a source of plant proteins  
Peas (Pisum sativum) exhibit a wide range of varieties where the chemical and physical attributes of 
Peas are strongly influenced by agricultural conditions. The varieties are formed based on the 
temperature, humidity and water holding capacity of the soil and the surrounding they are cultivated. 
Their remarkable adaptability allows them to thrive even in semi-arid and arid environments. On 
average, different cultivars of peas typically contain about 24-25% protein. The remaining 
constituents of peas encompass polysaccharides (ranging from 24% to 49%), dietary fibres (2-9% 
soluble and 10-15% insoluble), and fat (approximately 1.5-2%) 17.  
Peas encompass two distinct types of proteins: salt-soluble proteins like Globulin and water-soluble 
proteins like Albumin. Both protein types play a significant role in the formation of vital protein 
constituents and amino acid content attached to the (α-β)-structures17. The aforementioned crude 
protein contains a diverse array of specific proteins, where storage proteins constitute approximately 
70-80% of the total protein content 17. Storage proteins primarily serve as sources of amino acids and 
nitrogen which determines the nutritional value of Peas based on dry mass, while the remaining 
proteins encompass enzymes, inhibitors, hormones, and various other 22–24 . A structure of Pea 
protein isolate is shown in Appendix 1 
Among the storage proteins, the predominant components are ‘Globular Proteins’ like Legumin and 
Vicilin proteins with minor amounts of Convicillin24,17. Legumin proteins with higher sulphur content 
prove beneficial in enhancing protein quality. 25 Although each α- β-chain shows some heterogeneity, 
the α-chain is dominated by glutamic acid and has leucine as the N-terminal amino group, whereas 
the β-chain contains more alanine, valine, and leucine and has glycine as the N-terminal amino 
group.24 On the other hand, Vicilin held together by hydrophobic interactions rather than covalent 
disulfide bonds, Vicilin contains low levels of sulphur-containing amino acids(methionine, cysteine) 
and tryptophan, and higher levels of basic (arginine, lysine) and acidic (aspartic acid, glutamic acid) 
amino acids. N-terminal amino groups typically are represented by serine, glutamic acid, and aspartic 
acid.24 Convicillin, the minor content of this third protein is quite different compared to Vicilin and 
Legumin. Convicillin contains higher amount of sulphur-containing amino acids in Peas 26. Finally, 
Albumin the water-soluble proteins comprise enzymes, protease inhibitors, amylase inhibitors, and 
lectins 26.  
The nutritional properties of legumes are determined by the Legumin:vicilin ratio present in them. In 
peas, soybeans, and Faba beans, this ratio ranges from 0.2 to 8.0, 1.3 to 3.4, and 1.7 to 3.7, 
respectively. Various factors such as methods and preparation parameters influence this ratio. When 
preparing isolated protein, it is preferable to have a low Legumin:vicilin ratio because higher levels of 
7S protein result in greater protein yields compared to other types. 17.  
Greatest contribution of amino acid in Peas (dry mass) is from Glutamine, followed by Aspartic acid,

Chemical composition and constituents of Pea seeds  
Complex carbohydrates, like Starch and fibres are major contributors in composition of peas i.e., 
average 46% and 20% in dry matter27. Pea starch contains significant amount of amylose, which 
results in slower digestion. The blood glucose level after digestion is influenced by the digestibility of 
starch in the gastrointestinal tract, while the concentration is determined by the ratio of amylose to 
amylopectin28. The slow digestion of starch can also be caused due to unavailability to access 
amylases present in the rigid Plant cell wall. This slow digestion of starch due to presence of Amylose 
should be noted since, it decreases the digestibility.  
Fibres are mostly accounted for in seed coats and cotyledons. The hull (seed coat) are the water-
insoluble fibres i.e., Cellulose, and the inner fibres (cotyledon) have soluble fibres like hemi-cellulose, 
pectin’s and lignin. These fibres reduce the blood cholesterol and make for low glycaemic index27,29,30.   
Vitamins and Minerals, according to studies conducted 31, the predominant mineral found in Peas is 
Potassium (1.04%), followed by Phosphorus (P) (0.39%), Magnesium (Mg) (0.10%), and Calcium (Ca) 
(0.08%). A study conducted on Canadian Peas 32 mentioned Iron (Fe) content in the Pea samples as 
97 parts per million (ppm), Selenium (Se) was 42 ppm, Zinc (Zn) was 41 ppm, Molybdenum (Mo) was 
12 ppm, Manganese (Mn) was 11 ppm, Copper (Cu) was 9 ppm, and Boron (B) was 4 ppm. However, 
despite the high mineral content in peas, their bioavailability may be reduced due to high 
concentrations of phytate, which acts as an inhibitor of Zn, Fe, and Ca absorption. 
Antioxidants, the seed coat and cotyledons of Peas are rich in phenolic compounds, which are natural 
antioxidants known for their potential to protect against diseases such as cancer and inflammatory-
related conditions33 . Among these compounds, condensed tannins, known for their remarkably high 
antioxidant activity, were specifically found in the coloured seed coats 34.

secondary wall, which contains a higher proportion of lignin 46.

Expansin – Wall loosening proteins.  
Expansins are non-enzymatic proteins found in various plant species, as well as in microorganisms 
such as bacteria, fungi, and algae 48. Expansins consists of 225 amino acids with the N-terminal 
domains, which direct proteins to secondary pathway.  The plant expansins are categorized into four 
subfamilies: α expansin, which are proteins induced by acidic conditions  and contribute to cell wall 
loosening 48; β expansin, which are extensively studied proteins within this group; expansin-like A and 
expansin-like B proteins which show uncertain functions, but are also said to have wall loosening 
abilities when acting on root nodules 45,51. Expansins play multiple roles in plant growth, ranging from 
seed germination to fruit development and the softening process of plants, influencing the physical 
characteristics of specific plant parts. The effects of expansins depend on factors such as temperature, 
moisture, pH acidity, and the duration of exposure.  
Structural identity of expansin is the Canonical shape - torpedo structures, ‘5nm’ long and ‘3nm’ 
broad proteins mostly divided into two domains based on terminal folds. N-terminal domain is a 
double-psi beta-barrel (DPBB) attached to the C-terminal which has a β-sandwich fold. Binding to the 
cellulose polysaccharides within plant largely depends on the β-sandwich fold47. The ability of 
expansins to let the wall polymers slide within the thick cell wall by reducing the forces between two 
cell walls to extend the length of plant cell has made its existence very significant in plant biology. 
They also play a role in cell wall remodelling after cell division. 45  
In an article by 52 where it addresses that Expansin are originally present within the plant cell wall and 
are driving the wall extension. Unlike other proteins, Plant expansin induce stress relaxation and help 
with extension of isolated cell walls in a pH dependent environment. Expansin can also work in normal 
conditions which are found in living cells by causing cell enlargement when they are brought in 
contact. The expansin gene expression depends on factors like pH, temperature and time which helps 
with function of growth or inhibit the growth. Thus, as stated Expansin are catalysts for cell expansion. 
Expansin weaken the non-covalent binding between two isolated cell wall polysaccharide and allows 
the polymer to creep through and access cell material by turgor pressure.52

Diffusion model for expansin and enzyme protein diffusion in solid state media 
Solid state fermentation is the most simple and advanced technology to increase the nutritional 
properties and texture of plant-based meat analogues or Tempeh. It takes place in a moisture/water-
free environment. 
The process of Solid-state fermentation (SSF) starts with depositing starter culture on solid media 
(Legumes/rice/etc.). Fungus develops in its vegetative form and develops hyphae which penetrate 
into the solid media by separating the cell wall. With time colonies are formed across the surface of 
these solid media called mycelium. SSF operates through the dynamic movement of particles or 
molecules, actively interchanging their positions between two distinct mediums. Vacancies are 
created due to stress applied on the cell wall. The particles, that are aligned perpendicular to the 
stress applied, start to migrate from surface and fill the vacancies that are parallel to the stress 
applied. It takes place only on the surface of the solid media. SSF is used in many different applications 
to extract macronutrients from biomass and interact with chemical compositions in the particular 
source.  
The process of SSF, depends on parameters like temperature, molecular mass, pressure applied, 
thickness of the medium, and surface area of interaction for fungal microorganism and solid medium.

THEORY  
Future foods have started becoming a trend within newer generations, but information about the 
nutritional and anti-nutritive factors of such sustainable food are either not understood by 
consumers, or are vaguely understated. Some countries are banning production and research on 
sustainable foods on the grounds of  “mimicking” the actual food product or due to regulatory and 
labelling issues. Consumers are hesitant when it comes to completely switching their dietary needs 
to plant-based alternatives. Since, these foods are being pre-treated with different processes 
speculations are raised before consumption and selling the products on market shelves.  
Such myths and facts have been mentioned in this section based on Review articles, research papers 
and discussions.  
 
Myth or a Fact -  
1. Myth about Bio-availability – “Plant proteins have low bioavailability”. 
Bioavailability is defined as, “the ability of human to consume and convert proteins into available 
amino acids”. The digestion of proteins and absorption of amino acids defines the bioavailability of 
plant-proteins. 57   
The reason why bioavailability holds so important is that it determines the amount of nutrients that 
one would be absorbing based on the bodyweight and consumption patterns. If enough essential 
amino acids are not consumed, human body will not start producing proteins for body requirements. 
The bioavailability of proteins doesn’t seem to be a problem for population that have animal-proteins 
in their dietary intake. This is a potential problem in purely vegan or vegetarian diet.  
Animal protein bioavailability for every individual is almost 100%. Since, they contain all the essential 
amino acids in readily digestible form, making animal derived protein ratio efficient. The protein 
content present in food is defined as the function of nitrogen content (N=6.25) i.e., crude protein, 
which is not the most accurate measure of the dietary protein quality. The dietary protein must match 
the ileal amino acid (IAA) requirements of our body, and the efficiency with which the dietary proteins 
are being converted as defined by dietary protein quality.58 
The fact that plant proteins, in this context ‘Peas (dry/green)’ fall short to have all the essential amino 
acids lowers protein quality. The lower content of sulphur containing amino acids Methionine and 
cysteine create a gap in the nutritional profile. Plant based proteins also contain Phytates and dietary 
fibres which reduce the bioavailability by forming stable complexes with metal ions and proteins. 
Dietary fibres slow down the digestion by reducing accessibility of proteases to proteins by increasing 
the viscosity of gastrointestinal fluids form hydrogels that trap the proteins inside or form complexes 
with proteins or enzymes. Though these seem like antinutrients in plant proteins, they can be easily 
inactivated by Heating and Shearing. All the plant-based proteins are heat treated and go under 
extrusion hence, they inactivate these potential problems of reduced digestion.  
In recent past, the protein contents or nutritional profile was calculated based on Protein digestibility 
corrected amino acid score (PDCAAS) which simply just accounts for the digested crude proteins in 
the digestive tract. Without considering the amino acids that might get absorbed or lost. The single 
value of fecal/crude protein does not account for all the EAA present in food before conversion. 
Considering the shortcoming of previous methods, FAO (WHO) have updated the IAA requirements

Digestibility refers to the percentage of protein that has disappeared from digestive tract after

The rate and extent of protein digestion and bioavailability depends on the efficiency of proteases to 
access peptide bonds in polypeptide chains. The free amino acids released by the proteins after 
passing through gastrointestinal tract are absorbed by the epithelial cells making them essential for 
human health and confirm the bioavailability58.

2. Myth about Anti-nutrients -  “Legumes contain trypsin inhibitors and anti-nutritional factors”. 
Anti-nutritional factors in plant proteins are chemicals present in composition of Legumes, usually for 
plant protection mechanism, have the tendency to interact with macronutrients present in our body 
and disrupt the processes of gastrointestinal tract 40.  
Anti-nutrients are present in all kinds of foods in different compositions. Anti-nutrients like Oxalates, 
Enzyme inhibitors, phytates and toxic substances like phenols, cyanide, nitrate are present in varied 
amounts in plant protein sources depending on the cultivars and species. The antinutritional 
properties of plant protein are the main cause of concern since they are chemical substances that are 
responsible for reduced absorption of proteins, vitamins and minerals65.  
Anti-nutrients in food products can be divided into two groups: heat stable and heat unstable groups. 
Hence depending on the classification, anti-nutrients can either be a cause of concern or can be 
eradicated through processing techniques. Anti-nutrients which are classified as heat stable are stable 
at high temperatures like Phytic acids, Trypsin and Chymotrypsin inhibitors, condensed tannins, 
alkaloids, Saponins, etc. If consumed in higher quantities than recommended these chemicals can 
cause detrimental health issues65.

3. Myth about Saponin – “Saponins in legumes are anti-nutritional factors”. 
Saponins are naturally occurring compounds which are present predominantly in all the legume plants 
at different concentrations. Saponins in Peas are responsible for bitter taste, and astringency of peas.  
Saponins are present in many different types and variants like Soyasaponins, Soyasapogenol, 
Soyasaponin I, II, III etc. Saponins consist of sugar moieties which contain glucose, galactose, 
glucuronic acid, xylose, rhamnose or methylpentose, glycosidically linked to hydrophobic aglycone 
which may be triterpenoid or steroid in nature. 74. The foaming property in peas is attributed to 
existence of Saponin which form soap like foams in aqueous state and constitutes chemically complex 
and diverse compounds. The  complex structure of Saponins is due to variability of aglycone structure, 
the nature of side chains and position of attachment of these moieties on the aglycone.  
The role of Saponin in plants has not been understood completely due to its complex structure since 
many years. The differences in structure and complex chemical compositions makes it difficult to 
extract and isolate various Saponins to study their effects individually. Saponins have varied 
properties for Plants and their role in plant system biology to protect plants against external harm 
and pathogens. Though it does not align its importance when it comes to Human interaction of 
saponin while consumption of legumes, cereals, plant leaves etc. Since all the plant-based foods 
contain saponin it becomes difficult to understand whether they are good or bad for human health. 
For Peas (Pisum sativum) a glycosylated triterpenoid saponin was characterized to be responsible for

5. Myth about sustainability – “Plant protein foods are sustainable and healthy.” 
The food industry, which is both the largest and fastest growing sector globally, holds the title for 
being the primary source of greenhouse gas emissions. These emissions include those generated 
during long-distance transportation of food to various parts of the world. Additionally, the industry's 
consumption of raw materials has been steadily increasing, leading to higher levels of waste. 
Processes like Fermentation involved in food production releases gases such as methane, while 
livestock farming contributes to emissions of nitrous oxide and ammonia. Furthermore, the use of 
fertilizers adds nitrogen and phosphorus emissions, further contributing to pollution. The livestock 
industry alone has a substantial negative impact on biodiversity, accounting for approximately 78% of 
such effects.97  
Livestock farming has intensified their production techniques to meet the growing demands. These 
intensified processes have compromised the quality of animal meat available in markets. It gives rise 
to diseases and amplification of the pathogens involved in processing. Spillover of pathogens, Genetic 
modification and adaptations of these pathogens from animals to humans has been causing world 
health issues like HIV, Ebola and SARS. Not to mention the pollution caused across all three scopes. 
Scope 1; Direct emission from company owned – facilities, processing plants, machineries. Scope 2; 
off-site emissions, from other co-dependent industries, electricity, water processing etc. Scope 3; 
product chain emissions, manure used, fertilizers etc. 
Animal-meat production causes 25% biodiversity loss, almost 41% deforestation takes place all over 
the world, agriculture in itself causes 26% biodiversity loss98. A life cycle assessment (cradle to grave) 
of plant-based protein sources i.e., Texturized vegetable meat alternative (produced from extrusion 
technology) and conventional animal-protein source like Poultry, Dairy and meat were compared on 
the categories like land use, water usage, cumulative energy used etc. 97  
The Vegetable meat alternative accounted for 30 g of protein content per 100 g of product. And the 
carbon footprint for this alternative food was in the range of 130 – 240 g CO2 depending on the 
sources used. Whereas for chicken breast 100 g obtained from processing with composition of 21.4 
g of protein resulted in 342 g CO2 equivalent. This results in three times more release, during animal 
meat processing, of carbon emissions for smaller protein content97.

Materials and Methods –  
Strains, Vector and Cloning –  
E. coli strain ‘DH5-α’ was used for plasmid cloning and amplification of target gene. Plasmid ‘Picza-
AoWLP-31.1’ was used. To transform the E.coli strain with plasmid the strains were left at 37 °C 
overnight after addition of 200 µl of LB media with 25 µg/ml of Zeocin antibiotic to DH5- α, the 
selected transformant cells from agar plate were then mixed with LB media (5ml). After centrifugation 
of this media with precipitate, the pellets containing DNA were re-suspended with Minin prep kit to 
extract the replicated plasmids from cells. Enzyme (MSS1) is used to cleave the circular plasmids and 
convert into Linear plasmids which then can be integrated within P. pastoris. Excess water added is 
removed by evaporating all the water at 65 °C on Thermomixer. The volume of <10 µl was further 
kept for desalting. After removal of salts the mixture was added to thawed pichia cells which was 
transferred to Electroporation cuvette. Immediately after 1ml of YPD was added. This solution was 
incubated at 30 °C for 2 hours. Centrifuged samples were washed with YND and resuspended in 250 
µl YND solution. This solution was then pipetted and plated on YPD + Zeocin plates and incubated 
overnight at 30 °C. 
Colony that was isolated is selected for protein expression in P. pastoris. 
Expression and purification of target protein –  
The protein expression was carried out in Pichia pastoris (yeast) for Aspergillus oryzae (Ao31) 
production. A 50 ml BMGY preculture medium was prepared in sterile flasks. The preculture media 
included 35 ml YEP solution (0.5g yeast extract, 1g peptone dissolved in dil. H2O), 5 ml 13.4% (w/v) 
YNB (yeast nitrogen base powder dissolved in dil. H2O), 5 ml 1M potassium phosphate buffer at pH 6, 
5 ml of 10% glycerol solution, 100 µl of 0.02% biotin solution and 50 µl of 100mg/ml Zeocin (as 
antibiotic). The solution was then inoculated with nicely separated expression host colony from the 
agar plate. The solution was incubated at 28°C at 200-250 rpm for 24 hours.  
The pre-culture solution was then diluted 100 times to calculate OD. The volume of culture to be 
added was calculated based on OD; Vp = 500/ODp, where OD = 1 in the culture media. After calculation 
of OD = 0.03 for 1000x dilution, 16.67 ml of the pre-culture expression inoculated solution was then 
centrifuged, and pellets were recovered after discarding the supernatant. The pellets were then 
transferred to a 500 ml BMMY medium culture consisting of the same solutions as above, except for 
glycerol, 50 ml of 10% methanol was added to the solution. The flask was then Incubated at 20 °C, 
180 rpm for 5-6 days. Every day, 5 ml of pure methanol 10% was added to the culture. After 6th day 
the culture was taken out of the incubator and was transferred into bottles for centrifugation. The 
precipitation could be seen at 6th day and was spun down for 15 minutes at 4 °C at 10,000 rpm.  
Further, to concentrate the media as much as possible a Vivaflow Ultrafiltration was used, where the 
supernatant was purified, and the volume was reduced to less than 50 ml. Purification of proteins 
was carried out in Nickel columns, to finally separate the proteins derived from the expression vector. 
His tag protein bound to the column, once the proteins are immobilized on stationary phase, proteins 
that interact only weakly or non-specifically with the resin are removed by washing the column. The 
purified protein separation based on molecular weight was determined by SDS – PAGE. The 
concentration was measured on Nanodrop. Ao31 was measured at concentration of 15.4 µM.

There can be many methods to characterize Expansins and its effect and binding with cellulose 
material which mimics the plant cell. The potential methods that could have served as an 
understanding for the expansin behaviour were considered during the period of 6 months.  
Biochemical characterization:  
Expansins are quite complex and unpredictable in nature. Since it is not possible to calculate its 
activity by calculating the product formation, it is important to have different ways to understand the 
characterization and stability of these non-catalytic expansin proteins.  
We were particularly interested in bacterial and fungal enzymes since the effect of Solid-state 
Fermentation was to be studied with diffusion of hyphae in the plant material. The amount of 
cellulose being converted into sugars gave us an estimate of the bonds being cleaved or the extent at 
which enzyme is reaching within the plant material.  
The Melting curve (Tm) curve was plotted based on different pH buffers mixed with expansin, at 
temperatures. This provides us with an approximate optimum temperature and the pH at which the 
protein unfolding takes place during thermal treatment.  
Understanding the Thermal unfolding of Expansin 
The assay was prepared with different expansin at different pH. The expansin used were BSEXLX1, 
Ao31, Ao19, derived from bacterial and fungi sources. The reaction volume was 50 µl and the final 
concentration of expansin was 5 µM and was adjusted to the volume with buffer of 200mM with pH 
range of 3 – 9. The final buffer concentration was kept at 50 mM and buffer was hence diluted four 
times in the reaction. The ‘Nano temper’ equipment was used for the calculation of thermal unfolding 
of the expansins at different pH concentrations. Different expansins were expressed from Aspergillus 
and Bacillus subtilis which were characterized using Nano temper Prometheus. The expansins were 
mixed with a varied range of pH buffer HEPES from 3 – 9.  
Biophysical characterization: 
Synergistic effect of bacterial and fungi expansin on cellulosic polysaccharides.  
Cellulose hydrolysis was performed in 96-well microplate. Avicel was used for the experiment as 
cellulose. The reason being Avicel is a crystalline powder of cellulose microfibrils which are 
undissolved components of cellulose in water, and we were interested to see whether enzyme can 
hydrolyse the undissolved components of Peas. Different enzymes were selected and compared for 
synergistic effect and conversion percentage of Avicel to glucose. Synergistic effect was calculated 
based on activity % formula. The samples were prepared with controls for all the experiment. 
Appendix 9. 
‘Cellulases’ were used as enzymes to break down cellulose by hydrolysing the β-1,4-glycosidic 
linkages. In this experiment we use Cellic-Ctec 3 which is a mixture of improved cellulases, 
endoglucanase and ENZECO cellulase, a mixture of endo-cellulase and β-glucosidases.  
These enzymes first bind to the surface of the cellulose fibres. Endoglucanases cleave the β-1,4-
glycosidic linkages at random points along the cellulose chain, creating new free ends called ‘Reducing 
ends’ because they possess a reactive aldehyde group (-CHO). The presence of reducing ends is 
significant because it allows further enzymatic hydrolysis and repeated degradation of cellulose chain 
into small individual glucose molecules.

DISCUSSION. 
Experiment performed for understanding thermal unfolding on expansin.  
Expansins exhibit a strong dependence on temperature and pH during the reaction, and their 
behaviour is highly influenced by these conditions. This was confirmed through additional 
experiments that examined the thermal unfolding of expansin-like proteins at various pH levels and 
increasing temperatures. Even though, expansin do not show enzymatic activity, they are proteins 
which can be denatured and lose their characteristics during the reactions. Especially, when they are 
incubated for longer times like that of the synergistic assay for 48 – 72 hours, sometimes.  
It was observed that the thermal unfolding of different expansins with different pH values occurred 
at temperatures higher than the previously tested, reaction temperature of, 30 °C, which was a safer 
option for assay running for 48 hours and also is ideal fermentation temperature. As a result, the 
temperature in the middle of the thermal denaturation process is labelled as melting temperature 
Tm. Changes to the Tm signify an alteration in protein stability. 
For example, the expansin-like protein obtained from Aspergillus oryzae (Ao31) demonstrated a 
thermal unfolding process that commenced at 70 °C and exhibited relative stability until reaching 82 
°C (refer to Figure 15). This suggests that these expansins function optimally at higher temperatures. 
Furthermore, their pH stability was found to be optimal within the pH range of 3 to 5. In contrast, the 
expansin derived from bacteria (BsEXLX1) exhibited thermal unfolding beginning near 30 °C (refer to 
Figure 16), with the optimum pH range observed between 4 and 6. The unfolding curves imply that 
BsEXLX1 is highly sensitive to temperature, as denaturation initiates at much lower temperatures 
compared to the other expansin-like proteins. Another Aspergillus-derived expansin (Ao19) initiated 
unfolding at 32 °C, while its optimal pH range was determined to be 3 to 5 (refer to Figure 17). 
After careful consideration, the temperature during the assay, a temperature of 50 °C was selected. 
Additionally, the pH was maintained at a constant 5, which is slightly acidic. These conditions 
correspond to optimum activity for cellulases.  
Synergistic effect of expansin on cellulosic polysaccharide  
The synergistic effect studied during thesis presented an exciting challenge due to the dynamic nature 
of expansin and the varying process parameters. Discovering the precise sweet spot, where both the 
expansin and enzyme synergistically interacted without impeding the binding sites in cellulosic and 
hemicellulosic polysaccharides, required careful and dedicated efforts. 
The initial assay employed the use of Cellic Ctec 3 enzyme, Using Expansin BsEXLX1, enzyme Cellic 
Ctec3 (0.002%v/v) and Avicel (1%w/v). Building upon previous studies that examined the binding 
characteristics and synergistic effect of bacterial expansin on cellulose 100. The protocol from the 
referenced study was adapted, and reaction volumes were prepared accordingly. However, instead of 
using Celluclast as the enzyme, Cellic Ctec 3 was utilized for this experiment. Cellic Ctec 3 is a 
commercially available enzyme cocktail that includes cellulases, β-glucosidase, and other enzymes 
beneficial for reducing sugars during fermentation. This particular enzyme preparation demonstrated 
a higher affinity towards the substrate and yielded a greater quantity of reducing ends formed (mM) 
present in the reaction volume. 
 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
58 
 
Surprisingly, no synergistic effect was observed, as indicated in Table 5, where the calculated 
synergistic activity % displayed negative values, contrary to the expected outcome. Consequently, the 
experiment did not yield the anticipated results described in the paper. The concept underlying the 
experiment was based on the ability of expansin to loosen-up the cellulose microfibrils, enabling easy 
access for the enzyme to the plant cell wall material during fungal fermentation. However, it was 
observed that the cellulose material could be accessed with higher reducing ends formed without the 
use of expansin, which contradicts the intended purpose of the experiment. 
There are several factors that could contribute to the validity and reliability of the obtained data (refer 
to Figure 18): 
1. Enzyme Concentration: The concentration of Cellic Ctec 3 enzyme used in the experiment was 
determined based on the lowest signal that still allowed for some activity, as measured by the 
formation of reducing ends. However, the chosen concentration, even at the lowest viable dilution 
(0.002% v/v or 50 times diluted), may have been too low to facilitate significant formation of reducing 
ends. This could explain why such a potent commercial enzyme exhibited a lack of substantial 
reducing end formation. 
2. Avicel Concentration: The concentration of Avicel substrate used in the experiment was 1% (w/v). 
This concentration might have been insufficient to be effectively cleaved by an enzyme with such high 
conversion yield potential. Increasing the substrate concentration could have enhanced the rate of 
product formation, specifically in terms of reducing ends formed (mM) over time. Insufficient 
substrate concentration could account for the observed flat curves with increasing time. 
3. Expansin BsEXLX1: The effectiveness of the bacterial expansin (BsEXLX1) might have been 
compromised due to the lower temperature conditions employed. It appears that hydrolysis 
processes work optimally at higher temperatures, which could explain the limited effect observed 
with this particular expansin. 
4. Expansin Concentration: The concentration of expansin is a critical factor to consider for achieving 
synergistic effects, as demonstrated in a previous study 50. As shown in Figure 18, the concentration 
of 100 µg of expansin per gram of substrate seems to be too low, resulting in similar outcomes when 
compared. This indicates that the expansin concentration used in the experiment may have been 
insufficient. 
5. Temperature: Temperature plays a significant role in the mechanism of expansin proteins. Although 
higher temperatures can risk protein denaturation, a temperature of 30 degrees Celsius might have 
been too low for the expansin to effectively function and exhibit its desired effects. 
Further experiments were performed to optimize the concentrations of enzyme, after the above 
conclusions, it was deemed necessary to check what happens with increase in the enzyme dosages. 
The enzyme concentration was increased to a dilution fold of 20X and 40X and the assay was run for 
smaller time interval, considering that expansin might have been active only for the initial time, while 
the enzyme is still a slow reacting cellulase mix. Also, that bacterial expansin was not compatible, 
expansin A031 was compared, 
Unfortunately, the results were not significant and required a bit more optimization before we could 
see any synergy.

significant changes. This could be attributed to the same factors previously discussed. Additionally, 
the reduced assay time might have contributed to these outcomes.  
It was intriguing to note that BSA consistently generated higher quantities of reducing ends compared 
to the combination of expansin and cellulases even when BSA was utilized solely as a negative control. 
The results were unexpectedly higher, particularly when concentration as low as a 100 µg/g of

It was concluded that maybe, cellulase Cellic Ctec 3 is way too efficient in its activity of hydrolysing

Further experiments were being performed with endoglucanase, which was a single cellulase, specific

CONCLUSION.  
 
In the effect of light, Plant-based proteins and other alternative protein sources would be the way 
forward into a sustainable and healthier future food. Considering the nutritional aspects and the 
comparison with actual animal protein sources, it is highly advantageous to consider a diet with plant-
based protein sources. 
In conclusion, It has been established that plant proteins cannot be uniformly categorized in terms of 
bioavailability, as their compositions and levels of essential amino acids vary. However, it is evident 
that overall, plant proteins exhibit lower bioavailability compared to animal proteins due to the 
presence of antinutrients which reduce the amino acid content in plants. These antinutrients, while 
essential for plant growth and protection, can be harmful to human health when consumed orally, 
particularly when plants/Legumes are eaten in raw form. 
The lower bioavailability of plant proteins can be attributed to the presence of trypsin inhibitors and 
other mineral inhibitors, which hinder the access of digestive enzymes to essential nutrients. 
Nevertheless, research has shown that the bioavailability of plant proteins can be improved only 
through complete amino acid profiles which can be achieved by combining different plant protein 
sources. Other processing methods seem to not increase bioavailability although they increase 
digestibility and protein solubility. 
Regarding peas, it has been found that they contain lower levels of antinutrients compared to other 
plant proteins, suggesting that their antinutritional properties are not toxic. However, it is important 
to note that antinutrients are still present in peas. Various processes can be employed to reduce 
antinutrient levels and mitigate their effects. Methods like solid state fungal fermentation helps in 
increase of nutritive value and the protein digestibility, even before fermentation many antinutrients 
were eliminated during pre-treatments like Soaking itself. Comparing the composition of Fermented 
and unfermented pea protein concentrate gave an estimate of the reduction in Trypsin inhibitors, and 
thus increasing protein digestibility. It can be hence stated as a fact, that antinutritive properties of 
plant proteins are reduced due to solid state fungal fermentation. Moreover, the emphasis on benefits 
of plant proteins from an environmental perspective and as a lifestyle choice is clear after the life 
cycle assessment mentioned in this report. The life cycle analysis revealed that animal-food 
consumption has detrimental impacts on the environment, contributing to rising temperatures. In 
contrast, plant proteins require less land area and have a smaller ecological footprint. 
The experimental investigation of synergism between expansin and cellulases proved successful. The 
study demonstrated that the synergistic effect of fungal and bacterial expansin with cellulase is 
observable, after addition of expansin the activity of cellulases increases and an increased formation 
of reducing ends is observed. Expansins are concluded to be highly sensitive to even minor variations 
in experimental parameters such as temperature, concentration, and pH. Enzyme Cellulase was more 
active and efficient at acidic pH and was inactive at Neutral pH. Furthermore, fungal expansin 
exhibited a higher affinity to cellulose and displayed a greater synergistic effect compared to bacterial 
expansin. 
 
==PAGE:63==
 
 
63 
 
 
As addressed in discussion, the overestimation of reducing ends formed due to addition of BSA, is 
due to increase in thermal stability of enzyme when BSA is added. The binding sites are also left vacant 
for enzyme to be active for longer time as cellulose hydrolysis is a slow process.  
At last, it can be concluded that there are many grey areas for plant-protein sources. Since, a lot of 
their composition is primarily related to plant growth and protection, they cannot be completely seen 
as negative sources. With advancement in technology and research on plant-protein sources, the 
existing problems and theories regarding antinutritional value and lower bioavailability would be 
refuted in near future. As far as expansins are concerned, they are highly advantageous for plant 
material during cell wall expansion and growth as well as prove significant in fungal invasion through 
diffusion in solid state media. Enzymes are limited in their accessibility when plant cell walls are 
thicker than their mechanism, fungal invasion can help with increasing the penetration through wall 
creep.  
Future Work.  
Although, the synergistic effect of expansin proteins compared to BSA proved to be unsatisfactory, 
we can still rely on some further study based on, increasing the concentration of expansins might 
increase the formation of reducing ends when enzyme is added after incubation.  It would also be 
helpful to see the result of assay for longer time, i.e., 72 hours after enzyme was added in the assay. 
Since, cellulose hydrolysis is a slow process and as seen from graphs, the substrate degradation had 
not reached saturation after 48 hours.  
Different concentrations of Expansin and BSA need to be compared before commenting on their 
synergistic effect. Changing the substrate can also prove beneficial to understand the biophysical 
characteristic of expansin. Using PASC or Pea cell fibres should be the next step in analysing 
characteristics. Cellulose microfibril structure of Avicel is crystalline as compared to other which can 
have an effect on synergism. Moreover, Peas contain more than just cellulose in its composition, so 
understanding the effect of expansins (fungal and bacterial) on the structure of dry peas is quite 
important for future.
