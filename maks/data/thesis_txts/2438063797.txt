Abstract 
This thesis uses a part of the Copenhagen urban drainage catchment as a case area to test a model 
predictive control setup. Theory about control of urban drainage systems in general, and model predictive 
control in particular, is presented. A DHI software, currently under development, for setting up and running 
model predictive control is presented and used throughout the thesis. The impact of overflow penalties, 
reference values, sampling times, and prediction horizons are investigated both in their ability to decrease 
total overflow volume and to limit overflow to prioritized areas.  
The model predictive control setup is run using eight historical rain events and is benchmarked against 
passive control and rule-based real-time control, and while vastly outperforming the passive control setup 
it generally also outperforms the real-time control setup. Lastly, the model predictive control is run with 
historical radar nowcast for 4 different events to evaluate the potential when using imperfect forecasts. 
A very simple bias control is used, and while again outperforming the passive control completely, the real-
time control is superior to the model predictive control when using radar-based forecasts. Throughout the 
thesis relevant literature is brought in to explore ideas of further development potential of the model 
predictive control for the case area.  
 
 
Sammenfatning 
Dette speciale anvender en del af det københavnske afløbssystem som caseområde til at teste et model-
prædikativt kontrol setup. Teori om kontrol af afløbssystemer i almindelighed og model-prædikativ kontrol 
i særdeleshed præsenteres. DHI software, der på tidspunktet for udarbejdelsen af nærværende speciale er 
under udvikling, der kan definere og køre model-prædikativ kontrol, præsenteres og anvendes gennem 
hele specialet. Effekten af overløbsstraf, referenceværdier, samplingtider og prædikationshorisonter 
undersøges for at finde både effekt på mindskning af totale overløbsvolumener samt begrænsning af 
overløb til udvalgte områder. 
Det model-prædikative kontrol setup anvendes på otte historiske regnhændelser og sammenlignes med 
både passiv kontrol og regelbaseret realtidskontrol. Den model-prædikative kontrol yder langt bedre ed 
den passive kontrol, og noget bedre end realtidskontrollen. Sidst men ikke mindst anvendes historiske 
radar nowcasts som forudsigelser for 4 forskellige hændelser, for at teste potentialet ved brug af uperfekte 
forudsigelser.  
En meget simpel bias correction anvendes, og den model-prædikative kontrol er igen langt bedre end det 
passive kontrol. Ved brug af radar nowcasts er realtidskontrollen dog bedre end den model-prædikative 
kontrol. 
Gennem hele specialet anvendes relevant litteratur til at udforske idéer til fremtidigt udviklingspotentiale 
for den model-prædikative kontrol i det anvendte caseområde.

1 Introduction 
During the 19th century the development of urban sewer systems, as we know them today, started in 
Europe with the primary purpose of moving waste out of the city in order to develop a higher level of 
hygiene and therefore health among the population (Winther et al., 2013). As the cities grew, so did the 
need for an efficient sewer, and as modern day urbanization created cities with very little permeable 
surface areas, the sewers needed to not only carry waste water from households but also rainwater (García 
et al., 2015). This led to the combined sewer, in which both wastewater and rainwater is transported. The 
rainwater adds a much higher demand on the sewers, and with old sewers being almost exclusively 
underground beneath now very developed cities, increasing the capacity of the sewers is not an easy task. 
During the 1970s in Denmark, it became evident that sewer discharge can be a large source of pollution to 
the receiving waters, which was handled by establishing waste water treatment plants (WWTP) (Henze et 
al., 2014). These treatment plants have since been expanded and further developed, but during rainstorms, 
it can be difficult to transport all water to the WWTP, let alone to have the capacity to treat it. This can 
result in the sewer spilling directly into the environment, either at the WWTP, known as bypass, as 
combined sewer overflow from sewers via overflow structures into water bodies, or as flooding on terrain 
i.e. on streets, in basements and so on. These overflows are quite unwanted since they can have serious 
effects on the local water environment and human health, and they are a big concern for utility companies 
that manage the sewer systems.  
In Denmark “Skrift 27” states that flooding on terrain from combined sewer systems should only happen 
every five years (Spildevandskomiteen, 2005), while “Skrift 22” contains limits for pollutant levels and 
oxygen levels in receiving water bodies (Spildevandskomiteen, 1985). Currently, the EU water framework 
directive is setting demands for these levels to ensure the quality of waterbodies in the future, and it is still 
being figured out how to accommodate this directive in Denmark (Vezzaro et al., 2018). While overflows 
from sewers and bypass from WWTPs are an obvious contributor to the problems, there is no national 
legislation on how often overflows or bypasses can occur - this is decided upon at the municipal level. The 
main goal is clear though: to reduce the emissions of untreated sewage to receiving water bodies. 
While the problem is already quite evident, it will only get bigger as rain storms become more intense due 
to climate change (Spildevandskomiteen, 2005). The municipality of Copenhagen expects that sewers in the 
year 2100 will have to handle 30% more water during maximum situations, if the legal demands are the 
same as there currently are (Københavns Kommune, 2011). This calls for utility companies to take action, 
but with the practical and financial challenges of merely expanding the capacity of the sewer systems, all 
possibilities are being explored.   
One way to optimize the utilization of the sewers is through managing them in real time. This can be done 
with passive control using structures such as gates and weirs. Furthermore, it can be done using real-time 
measurements to control how pumps, gates, and more should act– this is called real-time control (RTC). A 
type of RTC commonly used is the rule-based real-time control, where a set of predefined rules, set as “if-
then-else” statements dictate what happens in the system based on real-time measurements. This type of 
control is considered simple to understand and implement, but can require a large amount of rules and the 
performance will depend on the available knowledge of the system dynamics (García et al., 2015).  A 
further development to this field is adding a predictive element to the control, so-called model predictive 
control (MPC), which will be explored as a control technique for part of the Copenhagen sewer system in 
this thesis.

Model predictive control is an optimization algorithm-based control, that uses forecasts of rainfall runoff to 
predict the future states of the system depending on the actions that are taken by the controllers. These 
actions are chosen based on objectives set offline. The objectives typically include minimizing combined 
sewer overflow and bypass, and the MPC algorithm allows for a prioritization between different areas, i.e. 
choosing where and overflow, if possible to control, would be preferred. For this type of optimization to 
work, an area must have both controllable units and storage space (Gelormino & Ricker, 1994).   
Reports on implementation and results of MPC are available from Quebec (Pleau et al., 2005), Barcelona 
(Puig et al., 2009), and Århus (Frier et al., 2013) among others, while it has been tested extensively by many 
others (for example (Vezzaro and Grum, 2014) and (Vezzaro et al., 2013)). In recent years, the number of 
publications within the field has increased greatly (Lund et al., 2018). 
Model predictive control includes many parameters that are chosen by the modeler, and that may impact 
the performance of the control. Furthermore, most tests are carried out using historical rain series instead 
of real forecasts, meaning the forecasts are perfect, and thereby eliminating what may be one of the 
largest issues of model predictive control: that forecasts are far from perfect (Lowring, 2016),  (Thorndal et 
al., 2013). 
This thesis is written in relation to the Water Smart Cities project, where DHI has already performed an 
initial investigation of the potential for MPC in Copenhagen. During the course of the thesis, the DHI 
software for simulating an MPC strategy within a Mike Urban (MU) model has been under development, 
and the various updates of the prototype have been tested while working on this project. A large amount 
of time in this project went into developing and testing this software prototype, feature requesting and 
reporting bugs.  The software now runs much faster and is more reliable than at the beginning of the 
project period. 
This thesis serves to investigate, how an MPC model is built and how changing parameters such as overflow 
penalties, flow target values and their penalties, sample time, and prediction horizon affect the 
performance of an MPC model with Copenhagen, Denmark as the case area. The MPC is run for a total of 
eight historical rain events and benchmarked against passive control as well as the utility companies’ 
newest setup of rule-based real-time control. Furthermore, a new setup is built to run the final MPC model 
with real radar nowcasts in order to get closer to a setup that could run in reality, and to get an idea of the 
potential when using imperfect forecasts. The MPC is run through the Mike Workbench interface. The radar 
data is extracted through several R scripts and processed through scripts that are written in the Mike 
Workbench interface using Iron Python code. Throughout the thesis, many system dynamics are 
highlighted to give important insights to the weaknesses, strengths, and needs for improvement of the 
MPC.

2 Control in urban drainage systems 
When facing capacity related issues of storm and wastewater management, there are three main 
alternatives to consider: increasing sewer capacity, managing storm water locally or control the sewer 
system to use the capacity available optimally (García et al., 2015). 
Controlling the sewer system can be done at many level: the most simple level is the so-called passive 
control. This can, for example, be a weir set at a certain height so that flow from a dry weather day flows in 
the regular pipes, but at rain events causing flows of a certain size, the water will flow over the weir and 
might be redirected to a detention basin, other larger pipes, or an outlet.  
The most advanced level of control is RTC, which will be described in detail in the following.  
2.1 Real-time Control in general 
RTC is a type of control where measurements from the system in real time are used to decide how the 
system is controlled. Figure 1 shows a schematic view of a general control loop that will be used to describe 
the components and terminology of RTC based on Schütze et al.( 2004). 
 
Figure 1: diagram of a feedback/ feedforward control loop in a hydrological system where the blue lines represent flows and the 
orange dotted lines represent signals. A feedback control loop would include only the sensor to the right while a feedforward control 
loop would include only the sensor to the left. Inspired by Schütze et al.( 2004). 
Figure 1 with the terminology of control can be explained as: 
• 
The disturbance is the water flowing into the system  
• 
The actuator is the physical component that enables the flow. This will often be a pump, but can 
also be a weir, gate, valve, orifice etc. The actuator is often referred to as the controllable device or 
unit and is the main object of interest when investigating if RTC could be implemented in an area, 
since the placement of it determines which catchments can be controlled.  
• 
The sensor measures a certain parameter, usually a flow or a level in a pipe. The sensor 
measurement is sent to a controller.

• 
The controller uses a set point for a requested parameter, for example volume, to create a setting 
for the actuator. A typical tool for this is the proportional-integral-derivative (PID) that uses the 
error between the measurement at the sensor and the set point to generate an input (setting) to 
the actuator. 
Figure 1 shows both a feedback and feedforward control loop. In a feedback control the sensor measures 
the values after the control has happened. A feedforward loop measures the disturbance before it is 
controlled and uses this to create settings.  
The way the set point is decided and the actuators are operated defines the level of RTC. In some cases, the 
sensor measurement is sent to an operating station where someone needs to manually act on the 
information and engage the actuator e.g. open a valve. This is classified as manual RTC. If the actuators are 
engaged automatically but the settings must be approved/decided by personnel, the RTC is classified as 
supervisory and if set points are automatically found (i.e. by an algorithm or pre-defined rules) and 
actuators do not need handling by personnel the entire system can be classified as fully automatic (Schütze 
et al., 2004).  
Furthermore, the control can be classified as local if setpoint is based on local measurements only. The 
control is global if sensor signals from across the sewer system are gathered and the knowledge of all 
signals is used to create settings for actuators across the system. For this a supervisory control and data 
acquisition system (SCADA system) is used. If parameters of the receiving water body or the WWTP are also 
included the control is integrated (Mollerup, 2015). Integrated control will often mean considering not only 
quantities of water but also the quality.  
When the set points are found based on forecasting of the disturbances to the system, the control is called 
model predictive control. 
2.2 Objectives 
The objectives of an urban drainage control scheme can vary greatly and be decided upon by the individual 
implementing it, but is by (Lund et al., 2018) described to generally be for one or more of the following 
reasons: 
• 
Minimize combined sewer overflow (CSO)  
• 
Maximize the use of the WWTP capacity 
• 
Distribute water to obtain equally filled basins 
• 
Minimize flooding 
• 
Minimize changes in the control action 
• 
Minimize water volume in basins 
A control scheme can include more than one objective and is referred to as a multi-objective control 
(García et al., 2015). 
2.3 Model predictive control 
Categorized as a part of RTC is the MPC. This type of control goes beyond the real time measurements and 
uses not only these but also predictions for the future inflows to the system.

Furthermore, MPC is a global control in which an optimizing algorithm as far as possible fulfills priorities 
formulated by the creator of the control to plan actions across the entire area for the common good of the 
system. The framework setup that is used in this thesis is presented in Chapter 3. The following chapter 
aims to present the theory behind MPC.  
 Time horizons  
MPC models use the rolling horizon principle (Rauch & Harremoe, 1999), and the timescales at which 
predictions and decisions for the future are made can vary within a model which gives rise to a 
nomenclature that makes the distinction between the different timescales used. 
 
Figure 2: Schematic overview of prediction horizon (Tp), control horizon (Tc) and forecast horizon (Tf) as well as how they are related. 
Inspired by (Rauch & Harremoe, 1999). 
Figure 2 shows the different horizons involved in MPC.  
• 
The prediction horizon (Tp) is how far into the future the MPC evaluates. The state that is expected 
to be reached at the last point in time and everything until this is what the MPC optimizes towards.  
• 
The control horizon (Tc) is how far into the future the MPC develops set points for the system. This 
can be smaller than the prediction horizon but is in most cases equal to the prediction horizon. 
• 
 The forecast horizon (Tf) is how far into the future there are predicted disturbances. The forecast 
horizon can be both longer and shorter than the prediction horizon, but if the forecast horizon is 
longer than the prediction horizon, it will not all be used. If the prediction horizon goes beyond the 
forecast horizon, an assumption must be made about the forecast in the time gap. This could for 
example be disturbances equaling zero or making use of the naive predictor principle.  
It could also be desirable to not use the full forecast, since the quality of the forecast can only be expected 
to decrease as the forecast horizon increases (Lowring, 2016). 
While the prediction horizons of MPC can be quite long, the settings are continuously updated as new 
forecasts become available and as the dynamics of the system change. The MPC uses current states from 
the system as initial conditions of the optimization, to be informed on the current volumes of water, and 
thereby spare volumes.

Figure 3: Schematic overview of the rolling horizon principle, where the prediction horizon moves by one discrete timestep every 
time a sampling time has been completed and the model receives new initial conditions as well as a new forecast. Inspired by (DHI, 
2016). 
Figure 3 shows the principle of the rolling horizon, that is employed in MPC. The prediction horizon is 
extended by one sampling time (Ts) every time a new forecast and set of initial conditions are given. The 
sampling time is the time interval with which a new optimal set point is calculated and sent to the 
controllers. During the time Ts, the controllers will use only this one set point. The setpoint can either be 
one value, or several. The temporal resolution of the setpoint is referred to as the trajectory time. The MPC 
finds optimal setpoints for the entire control horizon but only uses the first set point of each optimization 
problem. The sampling time is limited by both the computational time for finding a new optimal set point, 
since this must be smaller than the sampling time and the update frequency of the forecast (Lowring, 
2016).

3 MPC framework  
The following describes the MPC framework that is currently under development by DHI which was used 
for this thesis. As described in (Lund et al., 2018) there are many ways to formulate a model predictive 
control problem, why only the implemented method is presented in this paper, along with literature 
references for features that are not included in the applied framework.  
3.1 Mathematical representation of the system 
The following describes the internal MPC model and optimization problem as it is defined in the DHI 
framework that is used in this thesis. The main sources for this chapter are therefore (DHI, 2016) , (DHI, 
2018) and (Halvgaard & Falk, 2017).  
The physical system of reality is described using constraints, and the state of the system is described with a 
mass balance. This representation together with an objective function is used to describe the problem that 
the optimization must solve.  
The components of the state and how they are related can be seen in Figure 4 and include:  
• 
The inputs (U) which are controllable (for example set point for a pump) 
• 
The outputs (Y) which we indirectly aim to control (for example volume or overflow in a basin) 
• 
The uncontrolled boundaries/disturbances (D) (rainfall runoff and dry weather flow) 
 
Figure 4: The mathematical components of the internal MPC model with the example of a virtual tank, where the output is 
volume/overflow, the constraints for output would be volume constraints, the input would be set points for a pump and the constraints 
for input would be pump rates. 
The areas of the sewer are represented through virtual tanks (VT), also known as linear reservoirs, that 
have boundaries that represent the physical properties of the system.

with lengths (k) equaling the number of timesteps in the prediction horizon and the timestep size equaling 
the sampling time. The formulation is thereby linear and the timesteps discrete:

As could be seen in equation (6) the overflow volume is described by the M matrix and the flow vector, why 
an overflow happening in an early timestep will be penalized in all subsequent timesteps within the 
prediction horizon, and not just linearly, due to the quadratic properties of the objective function. 
The disturbances to the system that is obtained through rainfall-runoff modeling is subject to uncertainty 
from both the forecasts and the runoff model (Löwe et al., 2016). In the setup used for this thesis these 
uncertainties are not directly included, but it could be argued that the overflow formulation indirectly 
includes the uncertainty in forecasts, by penalizing the near future overflow more than the far future, 
indirectly giving more value to the sooner and more certain predictions.  
In the literature many examples of including this uncertainty can be seen. One example is DORA (Vezzaro et 
al., 2013) where the uncertainty is accounted for by letting the MPC seeks to minimize the overflow risk, 
not the overflow volume. In the case study given a gamma distribution of the probability is used. In (Löwe 
et al., 2016) DORA is used for the control strategy, but a distribution of rainfall-runoff volume predictions 
are generated using a stochastic grey-box model with an extended Kalman filter that uses observations 
from the real system. (Courdent et al., 2015) showed an approach where the uncertainty was not added to 
one rainfall prediction, but where a numerical weather prediction consisting of an ensemble of 25 rainfall 
scenarios was used.

Figure 5 shows the closed loop configuration that is being used from the DHI framework. A high fidelity 
model (in this instance a MU model) receives a forecast and runs a runoff and network simulation of this. 
The inflows to each VT are then sent to the internal MPC. The internal MPC also receives initial states from 
another hi-fi model, imitating reality at the current time. This hi-fi model is in the current setup also a MU 
model. The optimizer finds the optimal input for the actuator of each VT and sends this as PID setpoints to 
the hi-fi model it is in a closed loop with.  
The optimal set points are found by minimizing the quadratic problem of the objective function and its 
constraints described above, which is done by the MOSEK optimization software that uses the interior point 
method. 
 Initial states 
The initial states, in equation 1 denoted Ox0, are, in the case of the off-line simulations used for this thesis, 
generated from the MU model running in a closed loop with the MPC. The initial states are found through 
measurements of the current volume in all specified nodes, links and basins within the area that makes up 
a VT. When running perfect ex-post forecasts, the disturbances to the MPC and to the MU model are of 
course identical, but if using imperfect forecasts, the MU model would receive the actual rain input, while 
the internal MPC would receive the imperfect forecast. In that case, the initial states truly become 
important.  
If the system was running online, getting the initial states would be more complicated since a measurement 
of all volumes in a physical system is unrealistic to gather. The following are some options for an online 
system: 
• 
In each area of the system corresponding to a VT in the MPC setup, a network of level sensors is 
placed. A correlation between the level at each point and the total volume in the area is used 
(‘Personal communication with Lars Nyrup Drejer’, 2018) 
 
• 
A sensor measurement from one or several places in the system could act as data assimilation for a 
MU (or other Hi-fi) model that runs parallel with the system in real time. The hi-fi model could then 
feed initial states to the MPC and thereby act as a so called soft sensor (‘Personal communication 
with Rasmus Halvgaard’, 2018) 
 
The first suggestion is used in Fiorelli et al.( 2013), where all physical basins have a water level monitor and 
all outlets a flow rate monitor. The latter suggestion would align with the current setup, and a version of 
this is used with an extended Kalman filter in Löwe et al.(2016) among others.

in hydrology. The points most relevant and urgent for a simple use of radar data in this thesis are presented

Data treatment 
DMI states about their radars and the data that “In practice it is almost impossible to keep weather radars 
calibrated in an absolute sense. In part due to wear and tear they will drift.” (Bøvith & Vedel, 2016). This is 
common for radars, why bias correction is implemented. Bias correction uses actual measurements of 
rainfall to find the factor with which the radar data is offset, in order to correct the radar data, thereby 
making it useful. How the bias correction is done, depends largely on the rain gauge measurements 
available and whether the setup is using historical rainfalls or is running online.  
(Thorndahl et al., 2017) describe bias correction as most commonly being done on the hourly scale and 
state that when correction is done at a temporal scale too large, there is the risk of the bias of the radar 
having changed, while at a scale too small random errors in the rain gauge measurements can have too 
large of an influence.  
Similarly, there are important considerations about the spatial scale of bias correction. While using several 
rain gauges for the bias of radar points imposes the risk of not detecting local bias on the radar, using only 
one rain gauge for the bias of a radar point will make the setup more vulnerable to the impact of errors at 
the individual rain gauge.  
Furthermore, a shift in weather type (e.g. from stratiform to convective event) can cause a shift in bias. 
Radar forecasts performance  
(Ochoa-Rodriguez et al., 2015) found that the impact of both spatial and temporal resolution increases with 
catchment size decreasing, while it in (Thorndahl et al., 2017) is described that the spatial resolution can be 
of less importance when the purpose of the simulation for example is total catchment runoff or inflows to a 
WWTP.  
(Thorndahl and Rasmussen, 2013) describe a finding that for lead times of 30 and 60 minutes there is only 
to some extent an increase in error with an incrase in lead time, but with no particular tendency (positive 
or negative). For 120 min lead times though there is a clear underestimation of rainfall.  
The issue of forecasting convective events is repeatedly mentioned, since these often cannot be detected 
until very shortly before occurring, and (Thorndahl & Rasmussen, 2013) mention 1 hour as the cutoff for 
when they can be forecasted. Cloudbursts are usually convective events, why they must be granted high 
importance in predictions for urban hydrology.

5.2 MPC representation 
The above described area is translated into an internal MPC model, by splitting it up based on locations of 
controllable units (in this case pumps). By using tools such as backward flow tracing in MU, and from 
manual investigation, it can be concluded which nodes and pipes contribute to which pumps. The entire 
area that feeds a pump then becomes a virtual tank with the pump as the controllable unit. The internal 
MPC model is shown in Figure 9. It should be noted that internal weirs are not included in the internal MPC, 
meaning that when water moves from one VT to another through an internal weir the MPC cannot predict 
this and will only know from the updated initial state.  
Internal overflows from one VT to another are penalized like external overflows in the MPC, since it cannot 
differentiate based on what happens when the volume exceeds the constraint. Furthermore, the flow that 
can occur from FA to GM is not included in the MPC model, and the same goes for the gravitationally 
draining pipe from LE to OV. This is done well knowing that the predictions of the MPC will be more 
inaccurate because of it. The uncontrollable orifice previously mentioned is closed during high flow, so this 
is not expected to be an issue for the MPC.  
Due to all flows being controlled by pumps and the short distance between pumps and sensors no travel 
time is included in the internal MPC model.

7 Calibrating and testing the MPC 
In the following chapter the volume and outflow/pumping constraints (Ymin, Ymax, Umin and Umax) of the MPC 
are set through an iterative calibration like process in order to reach a realistic representation of the MU 
model. Secondly, the overflow penalties, reference values, sampling times and prediction horizons will be 
varied to look for correlations between them and the performance. Through the calibrating, testing and 
benchmarking process the MU model of the catchment will serve as reality since it is the closest thing 
available.  
7.1 Method 
Simulations of forecasted runoff simulations are performed in MU and use time area curve as the runoff 
model and a simulation timestep of 10 seconds. The network simulations use a dynamic wave model, based 
on the 1D Saint Vernant equations, as model type and a simulation time step of 1 second. No hot-start is 
used for network simulations of the forecast. Network simulations for the closed loop simulation during 
MPC uses the forecast network file as hot-start. Simulations are run from a minimum of 8 hours before the 
first rain until a minimum of 8 hours after the last rain to ensure the prediction horizon is utilized and all 
possible CSO’s are captured. Results presented as being from the MU model are from this closed loop cycle. 
This means that internal MPC results and MU results are different interpretations of the same control 
scheme.  
Appendix 6 shows the script for configuring the MPC. Appendix 7 shows the script for “building” the 
internal MPC model. Appendix 8 shows the script for running the closed loop cycle. All scripts are executed 
in Mike Workbench and are Iron Python scripts. Brief explanations of the scripts are included in the 
appendices.  
In the following section, methods for testing as well as results and discussion of the results will be 
presented. FA, GM, GR, LE, OV and RA refer to the areas described in Figure 9 and are here treated as 
virtual tanks. Letters (A and B) refer to the rain events described.

 Volume 
Maximum volume before CSO occurs for each area (Ymax) is found by adding up all volume of water in pipes, 
nodes and basins for each VT for each point in a simulation. This volume is then compared to a timeseries 
of CSO to see at which volume an area overflows. It is important to note that most areas would experience 
overflow before the physical maximum volume is reached, since the VT is not one basin but a large area 
where water cannot at all times be distributed equally. 
This testing is at first done for the baseline scenarios before MPC has been performed to find a starting 
point of each area and is later manually calibrated to see if the area will experience overflow, if a higher 
volume is attempted to be reached. Since disturbances to the system can be spatially and temporally 
different and dynamics of the system all depend on the individual event, this limit can be different for each 
event, why it is important to find the smallest volume at which overflow is experienced.  
Based on the values found the hard constraint of Ymax is found for the internal MPC. Ymin is not set to zero, 
although this would represent reality, but is set higher. This is done because the MPC is likely to try to reach 
Ymin through pumping, which could result in pumps running dry, since the water, while available in the area 
of the VT might not be available to the pumps. The exact value of Vmin is not significant, since it will mostly 
impact dry weather situations, and must just be low enough to allow sufficient emptying of the system, 
without pumps running dry.

During the use of the MPC, and as will be seen through examples in the following, there are many

7.3 Testing parameters 
In the following overflow penalties, reference flows, sampling times and prediction horizons will be varied 
and tested on the previously mentioned historical rain events, to see if there are any visible correlations 
between the variation in the parameter value and the performance of the MPC. Further investigations to 
display which MPC dynamics are in play when varying the parameters will be performed. Initially a brief 
summary of relevant points from the literature is presented.  
 Lessons from the literature 
(Fiorelli et al., 2013) performed a multi-objective MPC with the objectives to use the storage volume of the 
system homogeneously, keep the sewer outflow to the WWTP close to a reference value and to minimize 
CSO. The team tested combinations of penalties of the 3 different objectives on 2 different rain events to 
find the pareto efficient set of parameters when considering the reference flow and the overflow. The 
results were quite different for a light and heavy rain, but with no clear best choice of parameters for either 
event. A previous article by the same authors-  but in French - from 2010 is cited, stating that with a 
heuristic approach it was found that having the penalty on overflows a factor 5 larger than the other 
penalties was effective as a setting for all conditions, but for heavy rain especially it was preferred to have 
penalties only on overflow. 
According to (Lund et al., 2018) sampling times between 1 to 10 minutes are mostly used with 5 minutes 
being the most common. Furthermore, a horizon of 30-120 minutes is reported as the norm for prediction, 
forecast and control -horizon. The horizons usually depend on the leap time of available forecasts, with the 
forecast often being a radar nowcast. Several articles reported on improvement of performance with 
longer horizons, but when looking for example at (Marinaki & Papageorgiou, 2001), the improvements are 
in the realms of less than 10%, why they also concluded that the true improvement comes from the switch 
from no or passive control to MPC, not from the length of the prediction horizon. 
(Duchesne et al., 2003) found that when allowing surcharged pipes there is no noteworthy difference in 
performance based on prediction horizon. This was tested only on a 5 minute, 10 minute and 45 minute 
prediction horizon though. When surcharged pipes are not allowed, there was an 8% reduction of overflow 
when going from a 5 minute to a 45 minute prediction horizon. 
 Specifications for testing 
For all calibration of volume and testing of different parameters the following settings were used (except 
when that setting was the target of a testing) 
• 
Overflow penalties ρ=1000 for all VT’s except RA that is assigned ρ=10. 
• 
Reference flow 6.3 m3/s for RA with penalty 1e-2. No reference for remaining VT’s 
• 
Sampling time 10 minutes (and therefor trajectory time 10 minutes)  
• 
Prediction horizon 6 hours 
The reference flow in RA is not so much an objective of the MPC as it is a way to always be pulling water 
out of the system.

setpoints more often and thereby pump at the rate that is needed in that moment exactly.

(Pleau et al., 2005) in Quebec, could be used where 2 overall modes exist: one for dry weather and one for

neglected when the more serious issue of overflow arises. One way to implement this could be through a 
setup inspired by the one proposed in (Courdent et al., 2018), where flow type could be predicted 2 days 
ahead by use of numerical weather predictions and hydrological runoff models.

9 Using radar nowcasts 
9.1 Preparing the MPC for simulations with forecast uncertainty  
When working with imperfect forecasts, the hard constraints on volume became an issue during dry 
weather conditions. When the disturbance was smaller than forecasted, the volume of a VT could become 
smaller than the minimum allowed value, leading to an infeasible problem. To accommodate this issue, 
constraints were added to the soft sensor measurements from the MU model that are passed on to the 
internal MPC model as initial states. If a volume lower than the Ymin is measured, the MPC will receive Ymin as 
the initial state. Similarly, if a volume above Ymax is measured, the MPC will receive Ymax as the initial state.  
A similar issue was described by Gelormino & Ricker in 1994 (Lund et al., 2018) who chose to remove the 
constraint of minimum volume.  
Figure 32 shows the performance of the MPC for each event with the new constraint on initial states, and 
without – as used previously. There is a noticeable tendency that overflows become smaller with the 
altered initial states. 
 
 
Figure 32: Performance of the MPC for each event with and without the described constraints on the initial states. All results are for 
the MU model. 
The constraints are truly necessary for Ymin, because if V is not large enough the problem is not feasible 
within the sampling time, since the MPC can do nothing to add extra water. With Y larger than Ymax it is not 
infeasible though since the initial state value by the MPC is allowed to be outside the constraints, while it is 
just the “planned” volume at the end of the sampling time that must stay within the constraints, and the 
slack variable can be activated.  
With constraints, in a situation where the volume exceeds the constraint the initial state for the volume is 
equal to Ymax. This allows for a less aggressive setpoint, only acting on the water that is predicted to enter 
the system during the next sampling time, not on the excess amount of water that is already within the VT.  
It is seen from the events that the MPC with constrained initial states actually perform better because an 
additional volume is utilized in several of the VT’s, which the MPC is unaware of – i.e. a volume beyond

9.2 Setup  
In order to evaluate the MPC setup in as close to reality conditions as possible, historic radar nowcasts will 
be used as the predictive element to the MPC. Radar data for 2017 is available, why events C, E, F and H will 
be used.  
In a realistic online setup, a bias correction would be re-calculated every few hours. For simplicity this is not 
done in this thesis, but instead 2 different “scenarios” are explored. 
In the one scenario the radar data is seen as “reality”, meaning that the nowcasts are used uncorrected for 
predictions, while the radar observations are used as the real rainfall entering the closed loop model. By 
performing this type of simulations the importance of the bias of the radar data becomes negligible, and 
the potential of the MPC, if for example DMI were to correct the data in various ways themselves and 
thereby enhancing the quality, is displayed. 
The second scenario uses SVK data from rain gauges as “reality”, i.e. the rain input to the closed loop 
model. The runoff predictions are bias corrected in as simple a way as possible, i.e. by scaling the nowcast 
input to the runoff model with one factor across time and space. The ratio is found as the net rainfall for 
the entire event run with SVK data divided by the net rainfall for the entire event run with radar 
observations. Although it in real time would not be possible to use this method, it is expected that a more 
sophisticated method that is possible to implement in real time would perform better.

10 Conclusions 
In this thesis an MPC for part of a Copenhagen catchment was altered and tuned in order to investigate the 
performance dependency on overflow penalties, reference flows, sampling times, and prediction horizons, 
and to highlight some dynamics of the MPC and MPC setup. The MPC was benchmarked using eight 
historical rain events. A new setup was created in order to run the MPC with radar nowcasts, thereby 
approaching an online performance as much as possible, and with this setup the MPC was benchmarked 
using four historical rain events.  
The following main conclusions were drawn: 
• 
There is a need for large differences in overflow penalties (i.e. ≥105) if one area shall be prioritized 
completely above another. 
 
• 
It was found that the main need for a small sample time comes not from needing new initial states 
from the MU model or new predictions, but from the MPC not including every point of the forecast 
but rather every future sampling time in its calculations of future states, which was seen to be an 
issue when a large (forecasted) change in disturbance happens within one sampling time.  
 
• 
The effect of reference flows was found to be very different for the two rain events and was 
concluded to only be used in ways that directly align with the objective of the MPC. 
 
• 
When using perfect forecasts and a large reference flow to pull water towards the outlet of the 
model, it was found that the prediction horizon had little effect, even on the coupled events that 
were tested. This could be very case specific and related to the very large maximum outflow of the 
area OV, which means that very intense rain is needed to see a bottleneck effect. If the reference 
flow was lowered though, the prediction horizon made a difference on the high intensity rain event 
that was tested. Furthermore, it is speculated that for smaller events the prediction horizon makes 
a bigger difference than for larger events. 
 
• 
When defining maximum constraints for volume in a VT representing a large area with complex 
dynamics, it is a trade off between ensuring safety by finding a level at which overflow would not 
occur and ensuring complete utilization of the storage volume. If dynamic overflow penalties were 
used to lower penalties in an area after overflow has occurred, a greater use of this volume could 
be seen. 
 
• 
When dealing with the smaller volumes of overflow that occur in the highly prioritized urban areas, 
the system dynamics, distance to a pump within the VT, water not being available at the pump and 
pump start-up times has a large impact on the relative performance of the MU model in 
comparison to what is planned by the much simplified MPC model. 
 
• 
The differences between the MPC and MU model can also often be prescribed to the internal 
overflows within the model and uncontrolled flows between VTs that the internal MPC model does 
not include. This brings in the view of the MPC being only part of the solution, needing to be 
implemented along with physical changes to the system.

• 
When working with imperfect forecasts, it is necessary to alter the initial states given to the MPC 
when they are outside the bounds of the minimum volume constraints. 
 
• 
For eight different historical rain events with the use of perfect forecasts, the MPC outperformed 
the RTC in eight of the events, while they performed equally well in two, and the RTC outperformed 
the MPC in one event. Furthermore, the MPC is superior in limiting the overflow to fewer areas and 
lowering the volume of overflow outside the WWTP. 
 
• 
When using radar nowcast predictions and rain gauge observations as reality, and a bias correction 
with no spatial or temporal variance throughout the event, the RTC performs better than the MPC 
in three out of four events, with both controls eliminating all overflow in the fourth event.  
 
• 
When using radar nowcast predictions for the MPC and radar observations as reality in order to 
eliminate the need for bias correction, the MPC performed much better than the RTC for one ,they 
performed equally well for two events, and the RTC slightly outperformed the MPC for the last 
event. 
 
It seems that with the implementation good bias correction realistic for running online, there could be 
potential for a further reduction of overflow in the Amager part of the Copenhagen catchment through the 
use of radar nowcasts than what is currently achievable. This reduction of overflow would be expected to 
be occurring over the whole area in general, but with particularly good performance in the selected high 
priority areas.

def ConfigRainEvent(tsr, id ): 
    timeStep = TimeSpan(0, 1, 0) 
    predictionHorizon = TimeSpan(1,0, 0) 
     
     
         
    filePaths, timeSeriesPaths = ConfigPaths() 
     
     
    mpcConfig = GetMpcConfig("GM + GR + FA + LE + OV + RA", filePaths, 
timeSeriesPaths) 
     
 
    mpcM1dConfig = GetMpcM1dConfig("GM + GR + FA + LE + OV + RA" , filePaths, 
timeSeriesPaths) 
 
    Config = ClosedLoopConfiguration(mpcConfig, mpcM1dConfig)  
    Config.FilePaths = filePaths 
    Config.TimeSeriesPaths = timeSeriesPaths 
    Config.Quantities = ("WaterLevel", "WaterVolume", "Discharge") 
    Config.Id = id 
    Config.From =(DateTime(2017, 4, 15, 00, 20, 00))  
    Config.To =(DateTime(2017, 4, 15, 20, 00, 00)) 
    Config.TimeStep = timeStep 
    Config.PredictionHorizon = predictionHorizon 
     
    return Config

def RainEventDef(): 
 
    rainEvents = []  
    start = DateTime(2017, 8, 3, 4, 50, 00) #First forecast 
    stop = DateTime(2017, 8, 3, 7, 50, 00) 
     
    end = DateTime(2017, 8, 3, 19, 50, 00) #Last forecast 
    step = TimeSpan(0, 10, 0) 
  
    while start < end: 
        start = start.Add(step) 
        stop = stop.Add(step)   
        minutes = str(start.Minute) 
        if len(minutes) == 1: 
           minutes = "0"+str(start.Minute) 
        Hour = str(start.Hour) 
        if len(Hour) ==1: 
            Hour = "0" + str(start.Hour)

        if len(Month) ==1: 
            Month = "0" + str(start.Month) 
        Day = str(start.Day) 
        if len(Day) ==1: 
            Day = "0" + str(start.Day) 
        name = str(start.Year) + Month + Day + Hour + minutes       
        RE = RainEvent(start,stop) 
        RE.Id = name 
        rainEvents.append(RE)  
    return rainEvents
