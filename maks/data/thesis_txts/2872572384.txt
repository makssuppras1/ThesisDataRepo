Abstract
Over the past decade, reconciliation for hierarchical forecasts has received more attention from
the forecasting community. Because these hierarchies are often large, the dimension of the
reconciliation problem becomes large. As a result, there is growing interest in understanding the
uncertainty in the estimated parameters of the weight matrix. Previous research has formulated
the estimation of these parameters within a general linear model framework, which includes
the observed data. In this formulation, the uncertainty of the parameter estimates depends on
the variance-covariance matrix of the model errors, making accurate estimation of this matrix
essential for describing forecast uncertainty. However, in high-dimensional hierarchies, the variance-
covariance matrix is also large, making it difficult to estimate without imposing some structure. In
this thesis, we impose an AR(1) correlation structure and propose different models for the variance
component of the matrix to reduce the dimensionality of the problem. These models are estimated
and tested using electricity load data from Sweden. The results show that the proposed models
do not perform as well as an unstructured variance-covariance matrix, suggesting that they may
oversimplify the underlying structure.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Understanding forecast reconciliation and its importance
Forecast reconciliation is a concept often used when working with a collection of related time
series data. It is used when the related time series are subject to an aggregation constraint that
can be organized into a single hierarchical structure. To illustrate this, consider a retailer who
publishes sales forecasts for individual stores, regional branches, and the entire country for the
upcoming month. These are all related time series that can be organized hierarchically, with stores
at the bottom level, regions in the middle, and the national level at the top, because national sales
are simply the sum of regional sales, which in turn are the sum of individual store sales within
each region. In other words, the observations across all three levels are coherent, meaning they
consistently add up across the hierarchy. It is therefore desirable to produce forecasts at all three
levels that are also coherent. However, in practice, forecasts are often made independently at the
different levels and therefore do not satisfy these aggregation constraints. To address this issue,
forecast reconciliation is applied. Forecast reconciliation refers to the process of adjusting these
initial incoherent forecasts, typically called base forecasts, so that they satisfy the aggregation
constraints. The reconciled forecasts therefore reflect the structure of the actual data.
Hierarchical structures are not limited to geographic or categorical aggregations. Temporal hier-
archical structures are also common where time series are organized by frequency, such as daily,
weekly, and monthly observations of the same entity. In this case, the levels in the hierarchy are
defined by differences in time resolution. Including multiple frequency levels generally provides
greater insight into the true underlying data-generating process. Low-frequency data (e.g., monthly)
tend to highlight long-term trends and cycles, while higher-frequency data (e.g., daily) capture
short-term seasonal effects. Temporal hierarchies are well suited for achieving simpler models

because each frequency level can be modeled independently using relatively simple models tailored
to that specific frequency, rather than relying on a single complex model to explain all time-based
behaviors at once.
Whether the aggregation is spatial or temporal, the challenge remains the same: independently
generated forecasts at different levels are generally not coherent, and coherent forecasts are desired
due to optimal decision making.
1.2
Overview of established forecast reconciliation methods
Hyndman et al. (2011) were the first to propose a framework for creating reconciled forecasts.
Since then, several methods have been proposed. In this section, we present some of the most
commonly used approaches, including Hyndman’s optimal combination method, which is based on
a linear regression framework.
The traditional and relatively simple approaches for producing reconciled forecasts are the so-called
bottom-up (BU) and top-down (TD) methods. In the BU approach, base forecasts are generated
for each series at the bottom level and then aggregated upward according to the hierarchy. In the
TD approach, base forecasts are generated at the top level and then disaggregated down to the
bottom level. Each of these approaches have advantages and disadvantages, but their limitations
are not complementary, meaning that combining them does not eliminate their weaknesses. The
TD approach requires forecasts from only one time series, the one at the highest level, but results in
a significant loss of information because it ignores new or local insights available at the bottom
level. Additionally, disaggregating forecasts down the hierarchy is a challenging task. The BU
approach, on the other hand, preserves all local information, but it requires forecasting many time
series, which may be highly noisy. For example, consider small stores selling rare products. As
a result, the signal to noise ratio is often low in the BU method. Moreover, it ignores potential
relationships between series at the bottom level by treating each one independently, and therefore
fails to exploit correlations or shared patterns across different series (Nystrup et al. (2020)).
Before introducing Hyndman’s optimal combination method, some initial notation is necessary.
The notation throughout this thesis follows that used by Møller et al. (2025). We will frequently
distinguish between base forecasts, reconciled forecasts, and observations, as well as between
bottom-level, top-level, and full observations and forecasts. Therefore, an overview is provided
in Table 1.1. As will be explained later, "top-level" refers to all levels in the hierarchy except the
bottom level.

1.3
Estimating reconciliation weights with a GLM
The model in equation (1.2) describes the coherency error. But from a forecasting point of view,
we are mostly interested in the variance and correlation of the forecast errors, not the errors
related to coherence. Even if the coherency errors are very small, that doesn’t mean the forecasts
are very accurate, they could still be far from the actual observations. This is one of the issues
with Hyndman’s regression formulation of the reconciliation problem, it does not include the
observations directly.
Møller et al. (2025) addresses this by suggesting that the weight matrix P should be estimated as
part of a general linear model (GLM). He shows that the reconciliation problem can be written as a
linear model using the forecast errors at the bottom level:
y −ˆybot = Xβ + ϵ;
ϵ ∼N(0, Σ)
(1.5)

1.4
Contribution to the discussion
The dimension of the reconciliation problem is in general much larger than in the simple example
of the quarterly time series. In this thesis, we consider the case of electricity load forecasting, where
forecasts are generated once every 24 hours, each covering the next 24-hour period. These forecasts
are produced at the following levels: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, and 24 hours. This setup implies that
n = 60 and m = 24, which means there are 864 free parameters to estimate in the weight matrix
P . Because this is such a large number, a shrinkage estimator is typically used in the forecast
reconciliation process to reduce variance, which would otherwise become very large.
The main contribution of this project is to reduce the dimensionality of the forecast reconciliation
problem. In particular, we aim to reduce the number of parameters in the variance–covariance
matrix Σr by imposing a parametric structure. When estimating the reconciliation weights with
GLM, we also need to estimate the variance–covariance matrix of the bottom-level forecast errors.
For a specific time point t, this matrix has dimensions m × m. In our case, with m = 24, the
matrix Σr is a symmetric 24 × 24 matrix. If this matrix is treated as unstructured, it contains
m(m + 1)
2
= 300 free parameters.
Before proposing different variance–covariance models, we will in Chapter 2 take a closer look at
the forecast reconciliation problem in a GLM setting and its connection to Hyndman’s formulation
of the problem. This chapter mainly serves as a summary of already proven results from Møller
et al. (2025).

2.2
Residual maximum likelihood (REML)
The MLE of Σ is slightly downward biased. This bias arises because the ML approach treats
β as if it were known, when in fact it is estimated from the same data. Residual maximum
likelihood (REML) addresses this issue by modifying the likelihood function to reflect the additional
uncertainty introduced by estimating β.
The intuition behind REML is that, instead of optimizing the likelihood of the raw observations
directly, it optimizes the likelihood of the full residuals (observations minus the part explained
by the model). These residuals contain all the relevant information about the variance-covariance
parameters.

Matrix notation
For convenience, we will switch between matrix and vector notation when representing observa-
tions, base forecasts, reconciled forecasts, and mean-value parameters. The matrix form of the
observations, Y ∈RT×m, is intuitive, where each row contains a full bottom-level observation
vector at time t and each column corresponds to a single bottom-level series across all T time
points. In our case, this means that column 1 contains observations for the first hour of the day,
column 2 for the second hour, and so on. The same structure applies to the base forecasts and
reconciled forecasts in matrix notation.
However, staying only in matrix notation complicates the representation of covariance structures.
In this case, the covariance becomes a tensor, which is challanging to express analytically without
drowning in subscribtes, and also difficult interpret and manipulate. By keeping the observation
matrix it a column vector, we keep the standard Tm × Tm covariance matrix, which can often
be expressed compactly using Kronecker products. For example, when the observations are
uncorrelated over time but share a common cross-sectional covariance Σr, the covariance of the
vectorized observations can be written as Cov(vec(Y )) = IT ⊗Σr as seen in (2.9).
Nonetheless, vector operations can be computationally expensive, even when the underlying algebra
is straightforward. This is one reason why matrix notation is often preferred in practice, particularly
when dealing with sparse structures, which is the case for the full design matrix and the full
covariance matrix.

Parametric covariance matrix
As mentioned in the previous chapter, a key challenge is the increased uncertainty that arises when
a large number of free parameters must be estimated. In hierarchical time series, the bottom level
dimension can be high, which we have seen in the electricity load example used in this project. The
unstructured variance–covariance matrix of the regression errors therefor contains too many free
parameters to estimate reliably. In this chapter, we develop models that aim to reduce the number
of free parameters in Σr.
A practical approach is to impose a parametric covariance structure. This idea is common in
regression modeling, where well-known choices such as compound symmetry or an AR(1) structure
replace the free matrix with one described by only a handful of hyper-parameters. We will in this
section introduce 4 candidate for a parametric models for Σr
Since the bottom-level observation vector in the hierarchy are time series, we start by assuming
a simple first-order auto-regression (AR(1)) process as the underlying data-generating process.
Based on this assumption, we will construct covariance models that reflect the AR(1) structure. For
both algebraic clarity and numerical stability we use the variance–correlation decomposition
Σr = DRD
where D is a diagonal matrix containing the standard deviations for each time point and R is a
symmetric correlation matrix that describes how the time points are related to each other.

link functions are smooth bijections between R and the desired domains. In our case we will have

Each cubic polynomial has the form Pi(x) = α0i + α1ix + α2ix2 + α3ix3. Since there are K + 1
sub-intervals and each has four coefficients, we start with 4(K + 1) parameters in total. To ensure
the spline is smooth, we require that the function is C2 continuous at each interior knot ξ1, . . . , ξK.
This means that at each knot, the function itself, its first derivative, and its second derivative must
all be continuous. These conditions give us 3 constraints at each of the K interior knots, reducing
the number of free parameters to 4(K + 1) −3K = K + 4. A natural cubic spline adds two more
constraints at the endpoints, which is that the second derivative at both endpoints, a and b, must be
zero, i.e., S′′(a) = S′′(b) = 0. This ensures that the spline behaves linearly outside the boundary
knots, causing the curvature to "die out" at the ends. These two additional constraints reduce the
number of free parameters further, leaving K + 2.
In practice, we use the function splines::ns(), which is available through one of R’s core
base packages (R Core Team (2024)), to generate these basis functions. Figure 3.2 shows four basis
functions generated using splines::ns() for x ranging from 1 to 24, with interior knots at 8
and 16. The figure includes four basis functions because the implementation includes an intercept

4.4
Parameter estimation and uncertainty
To estimate the optimal parameters θ, we use the nlminb() function. This optimizer allows
the user to supply both the gradient and Hessian of the objective function, which is particularly
convenient in our case, as we have implemented an analytical expression for the gradient. To
ensure the optimization process has a suitable starting point, we first fit a simple AR(1) model
corresponding to Σr,M1(θ). The initial guesses are chosen to correspond approximately to
σ = ϕ = 0.5, which in the unconstrained domain translates to θ1,1 = −0.5 and θ2,1 = 1. The
resulting estimates are then used as starting values for more complex models in which Σr is
parameterized by more than two parameters.
Computational efficiency is important when numerically maximizing the likelihood, as the function
must be evaluated many times. By moving the computation of ˆβ
m
top outside the likelihood function
and constructing the reconciled forecasts using a lower-dimensional dense design matrix, we were
able to reduce the runtime by more than 30 minutes when estimating parameters for the model
Σr,M2(θ), which includes 25 parameters.
Adding the gradient to the optimizer noticeably reduced both the number of iterations and the
overall running time. However, the optimizer then returned a false convergence warning, which
did not occur when only the likelihood function was provided. This suggests that there may be
an error in the gradient implementation. When comparing the analytical implemented gradient
with a numerical approximation obtained using grad(), a small difference was observed in
the derivatives related to the variance parameters, while no difference appeared in the entry
corresponding to the correlation parameter. This points to a possible bug in the implementation
of equation (4.5). Due to time constraints, the source of the error was not identified during the
course of this project. As a result, we did not supply an analytical gradient when estimating the
parameters.
When estimating a large number of parameters, the likelihood surface tends to become more flat,
and the optimization problem more ill-conditioned, meaning that small changes in input can lead

Since all the models are nested, we will now use the likelihood ratio test (LRT) to evaluate whether
simpler models fit the data as well as more complex ones. In the LRT, we compare two models
where one is a reduced (simpler) version of the other, and we test the hypothesis that the smaller
model fits the data just as well as the larger one. In Table 5.6, we test the following two hypotheses:
• H1 : Σr,M4 fits the data just as well as Σr,sREML
• H2 : Σr,M3 fits the data just as well as Σr,M4
The test statistics for these hypotheses are calculated as:
GΣr,sREML→Σr,M4 = −2(ℓΣr,M4 −ℓΣr,sREML)
and
GΣr,M4→Σr,M3 = −2(ℓΣr,M3 −ℓΣr,M4).
These test statistics are compared to a χ2 distribution with 275 (300 −25) and 15 (25 −10) degrees
of freedom, respectively.
The results show that for region 2, hypothesis H1 is not rejected, meaning that the simpler model
Σr,M4 fits the data as well as the full model. In this case, we proceed to test hypothesis H2, which
is rejected, suggesting that further model reduction is not appropriate for this region.
In contrast, for the other regions, hypothesis H1 is rejected, indicating that the reduced model
Σr,M4 does not fit the data as well as the full model. Since H1 is rejected in these cases, testing
H2 is not meaningful, as it would involve comparing models that are already too simplified.
Overall, the LRT results suggest that the proposed reduced models are too simplified.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion and conclusion
The goal of this project has been to reduce the dimensionality of the forecast reconciliation problem.
As shown, the number of parameters in the weight matrix is typically large, and estimating the
uncertainty of these parameters requires even more estimated quantities. Ideally, we would like to
test whether some of the parameters in the weight matrix are equal to zero. However, this is not
straightforward. When using shrinkage, the projection of the observations onto a lower-dimensional
parameter space is no longer orthogonal. This means we do not have a suitable test statistic for
such hypothesis testing.
By framing the reconciliation problem within a GLM setting, we can apply common techniques for
reducing the dimensionality of the covariance matrix estimation. These techniques have been used
and explored in this project.
What we generally observe when applying the proposed dimension-reduced models to electricity
load data in Sweden is that the quality of the forecasts does not improve compared to the full model.
Furthermore, the reduced models do not describe the variance-covariance more accurately. When
evaluating how well the models fit the data they were trained on, the LRT showed that the largest
model includes parameters that are statistically necessary. However, the BIC indicated that the
model with 25 parameters captures the essential structure more efficiently compared to the largest
model.
These findings suggest that the proposed models may oversimplify the variance-covariance structure,
and that the optimal model likely lies somewhere between the full model with 300 parameters and
the reduced model with 25. A natural next step would be to introduce a correlation structure with a
higher autoregressive order. It may also be useful to allow the variance to depend on more than
just time. Factors such as wind speed, weather conditions (e.g., rain or snow), or whether it is a
weekday or weekend could be relevant. Similarly, the correlation parameter may vary throughout

constraints to the spline-based model could also improve its performance.
As shown in Table 4.1, estimating the model parameters was somewhat computationally demanding.
The next step beyond this project would first be to implement the gradient correctly, which would
likely speed up the estimation process. Providing the analytical form of the Hessian could further
improve performance. In the current implementation, the optimizer estimates the parameters for
the variance-covariance matrix directly. However, we could re-express the model in terms of the
precision matrix (i.e., the inverse of the covariance matrix) to improve numerical efficiency. For
example, in a stationary AR(1) process, the precision matrix is tridiagonal, meaning that all entries
more than one step from the diagonal are zero. In our case, however, we use the forecast version of
the AR(1) correlation, which approaches stationarity. Therefore, we would first need to derive the
inverse of this covariance matrix before being able to re-express the model.
