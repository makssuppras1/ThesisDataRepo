Abstract
The shares of renewable energy sources are growing, both globally and in Denmark.
Many renewable energy sources are characterised by intermittent and ﬂuctuating pro-
duction, which present challenges when the energy production and consumption in the
grid is balanced. The heating and cooling systems of buildings can be used to make parts
of the electricity demand ﬂexible. By implementing smart control, which is control that
reacts on information from the grid, the loads can be moved from peak hours to oﬀ-peak
hours.
In this thesis an algorithm for smart heating and cooling control in buildings is developed,
and implemented into the simulation software IDA ICE. It controls the heating- and
cooling systems in the building based on weather and price forecasts. The algorithm is
used to assess the potential savings for energy, cost and emissions from implementation
of smart control in new and old oﬃce and residential buildings. Two kinds of heating
and cooling systems are used, fast-response with a radiator and cooling panel, and slow-
response with a heating/cooling ﬂoor.
The results show that the algorithm successfully shifts load from hours with high prices
to hours with low prices. The total cost for electricity is reduced in all the simulated
models. Smart control improves the thermal environment by pre-conditioning the rooms
before peak load occurs, for example by pre-cooling before a large solar load on a sunny
day. Also, by heating and cooling pre-actively the smart control enables down-sizing
of the heating and cooling systems, because the peaks in demand are reduced. Cooling
capacity reductions in oﬃce buildings show the highest potential, because of high internal
loads combined with solar radiance. Cost reductions were seen in all buildings, and the
highest potential was seen for new buildings, for both oﬃce and residential, and for
cooling more than heating. The algorithm is able to reduce the total emissions from
electricity for heating and cooling.
It can be concluded from this study that the developed algorithm shifted loads from
high-price hours to low-price hours in the simulation models. The implementation of the
algorithm to the simulation software IDA ICE was successful, and there is great potential
for cost and emission reductions, as well as peak load reductions, by implementing smart
control in buildings.
Furthermore the algorithm is useful for improving the thermal
environment. Future works includes further optimisation, and tests with real forecast
data for weather and prices.

Sammendrag (Norwegian
abstract)
Andelen av fornybare energikilder er i vekst, både globalt og i Danmark. I energinet-
tet skal energiproduksjonen alltid være lik forbruket. Energiproduksjon fra fornybare
energikilder, for eksempel sol- og vindenergi, er ﬂuktuerende og ukontrollerbar og gir der-
for utfordringer når produksjon og forbruk skal balanseres. Varme- og kjølesystemene
i bygninger kan brukes til å gjøre deler av forbruket ﬂeksibelt. Ved å implementere
smart kontroll, som gjør nytte av informasjon fra el-nettverket, kan man ﬂytte deler av
elektrisitetsbelastningen fra perioder med høy belastning til timer med lav belastning.
I forbindelse med dette prosjektet er det utviklet en algoritme for smart kontroll av varme
og kjøl, og hvordan denne er implementert i bygningssimuleringsprogrammet IDA ICE.
Algoritmen styrer varme- og kjølesystemet basert på prognoser for vær og elektrisitet-
spriser. Den brukes heretter til å ﬁnne potensialet for reduksjoner i elektrisitetskostnader,
energiforbruk og CO2-utslipp. Modeller av nye og gamle kontor- og beboelsesbygninger
med forskjellige varme- og kjølesystemer er inkludert i rapporten.
Arbeidets resultater viser at man, ved bruk av den utviklede algoritmen, kan ﬂytte
belastning fra timer med høye elektrisitetspriser til timer med lave elektrisitetspriser.
Totalkostnaden for elektrisitet er redusert i alle de simulerte modellene. Ved bruk av
smart kontroll forbedres det termiske inneklimaet, fordi rommene blir kjølt pre-aktivt,
og dermed unngås mange timer med overoppheting i forbindelse med for eksempel høye
sollaster.
Ved å kjøle og oppvarme pre-aktivt er det mulig å redusere de installerte
kapasitetene for systemene, fordi spissbelastningene blir redusert. Det største potensialet
for kapasitetsreduksjoner er for kjølesystemer i nye kontorbygninger på grunn av de høye
interne lastene kombinert med sollaster. Kostnader til elektrisitet blir redusert i alle
modellene, men de største reduksjonene ﬁnnes for nye bygninger, og i høyere grad for
kjøling enn for oppvarming. Algoritmen er i stand til å redusere de samlede utslippene
av CO2.
Det konkluderes at algoritmen fører til endret forbruk av energi, ved at forbruket ﬂyttes
fra timer med høy elektrisitetspris til timer med lav pris. Implementeringen av algorit-
men i programmet IDA ICE var suksessfullt, og det er stort potensiale for reduksjoner
af kostnader, CO2-utslipp og spissbelastninger ved bruk av smart kontroll for varme-
og kjølesystemer. Det termiske inneklimaet forbedres ved bruk av smart kontroll. Al-
goritmen kan med fordel videreoptimeres, hvor usikkerheter i vær- og pris-prognoser
implementeres.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Across the world higher shares of renewable energy sources are introduced to the elec-
tricity systems. Renewable energy is an attractive alternative to fossil energy for many
reasons; lower green house gas emissions, reduced dependency on limited fossil fuels
and less local pollution causing expensive health problems are some examples. Most
renewable technologies are not controllable due to the nature of their energy sources.
Wind turbines can only produce energy within the limits of the current wind levels, and
PVs can only produce energy when the sun is shining. As more intermittent and uncon-
trollable energy is supplied to the energy systems, new challenges related to ensuring
security of supply arise. One way of approaching these challenges is through smart grid
technologies, which aim to increase the ﬂexibility of the energy systems.
Buildings consume around 40 % of all energy in the European Union [“Directive 2010/31/EU”
2010] and thereby constitutes a large part of the total demand. For many years energy
savings has been the primary goal of the building sector, and savings in energy consump-
tion have been obtained through better building design, renovations of the building en-
velope, more eﬃcient building systems etc. By the use of smart control it is possible to
shift the energy consumption from peak hours during periods of high demand to those
that are beneﬁcial for the system, for instance when renewable energy is abundant. The
aim of a smart building is not to save energy, but to manage it’s use when it is cheaper
and/or less polluting to generate.
In order to estimate how a building functions under given conditions, building simu-
lation tools are widely used by contractors and consultants. Building models provide
data for annual energy consumption, indoor climate and other building speciﬁc per-
formance parameters. Simple building calculations are necessary through the building
regulations, but it is increasingly common to supply advanced building simulation mod-
els for more detailed indoor climate calculations and dimensioning of building systems
for heating and cooling. As the use of BIM (Building Information Modelling) is get-
ting more widespread among architects, consultants and contractors, the use of building
simulations for estimations on energy performance will become faster and easier.
As smart buildings become more commonplace, building simulation tools will be used
to estimate the potential ﬂexibility of buildings. However, the software available today
is not readily able to simulate buildings with smart control.
There is a need for a
method for simulating buildings with smart control that can be implemented relatively

1.2
Problem formulation
This project aims to ﬁnd a method for implementing smart control of the heating and
cooling systems of a building, and to implement it in the software IDA ICE. The control
should react to variables such as forecasts for weather, price and CO2-emissions, and
adjust the heating and cooling control accordingly.
The goals for the report are:
• to develop an algorithm for heating and cooling of buildings that takes into account
weather forecasts and price data forecasts
• to implement the algorithm into the building simulation software IDA ICE
• to investigate how the smart control inﬂuences the total annual energy consump-
tion for heating and cooling
• to investigate how the smart control inﬂuences the total cost of electricity for
heating and cooling
• to investigate how the smart control inﬂuences the thermal indoor climate
• to determine whether the smart control can be used to reduce peaks in heating
and cooling, and reduce the installed capacity of the heating and cooling systems.
1.3
Outline of report structure
The report is divided into six chapters, including this chapter. The ﬁrst chapter describes
the motivation, and the goals that the report aims to fulﬁl. The second chapter includes
a description of smart grids, and the development and potential for implementation of
smart grids and smart buildings, as well as a description for diﬀerent models of buildings
controlled with weather forecasts and/or price data. The third chapter describes the
work that has been done in relation to this project, including the development of the
control algorithm, implementation of said algorithm into IDA ICE and description of
the simulations. In the fourth chapter the results from the simulations are described. In
the ﬁfth chapter the potentials for smart control and the usefulness of the simulation
model are discussed. The sixth chapter outlines the conclusions from the work.

CHAPTER 2
Background
In this chapter some basic concepts and terms necessary to the project are explained, in
order to set the context in which the smart buildings are expected to operate.
In the ﬁrst part of this chapter the smart grid is described. The reasons why it is likely
that the future of most electric power systems involves smart grid technology are many,
and they are described in this chapter. The current development and outlook for smart
grids in Denmark are discussed, and the potentials and challenges for implementing
smart control of electrical appliances and systems in buildings are elaborated on.
In the second part of the chapter common building control types are described, and
the third part of the chapter discusses the previous attempts to model buildings that
respond to weather forecasts or price forecasts.
2.1
Smart Grids
The future of the energy grids is moving towards the implementation of smart technology.
Buildings will at some point be forced to relate to the smart grid, and can choose to
take advantage of the possibilities the smart grids present. The goal of a smart building
is to make use of the information that the smart grid provides to spend electricity in
a way that beneﬁts both the building and the system. This section describes how the
smart grid works, the current development that is being undertaken to implement it in
Denmark, and how it is beneﬁcial from the point of view of society, system-operators,
building professionals and building owners.
According to IEEE [Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers 2015] a smart grid
is a next-generation electrical power system that is typiﬁed by the increased use of
communications and information technology in the generation, delivery and consumption
of electrical energy.
In this thesis the focus is on the consumption of energy, and increasing the ﬂexibility of
the energy consumption in buildings. The goal of implementing smart grid technology in
buildings is to use the energy when it is convenient and sensible to use it. This situation
occurs for instance if there is high availability of renewable energy, and/or when the price
is low. Shifting loads will normally cause a total increase of energy consumption, since
there is an energy loss associated with energy storages. For instance a battery may only
be able to deliver 90 % of the energy that was loaded onto it, depending on the battery
and the time when the energy is stored. The same applies for load shifting in buildings;

2.1.1
Energy and climate change
Reducing the emissions of green house gasses is an ever-present challenge, and this has
also become a commercial argument for building owners, who gain from pushing a green
image. The smart building can be a tool for obtaining lower emissions, because it in-
troduces ﬂexibility for the energy consumption and is able to respond to ﬂuctuating
production. Renewable energy sources are often uncontrollable and with a highly vari-
able production, for example power production from solar and wind energy, both in
growth at the moment.
2.1.2
Utility management and energy storage
A smart grid makes management of the power system easier, and the utility managers,
such as energy plant managers and TSO’s (Transmission System Operator), will beneﬁt
from a system that responds to the price of electricity.
The reason for this is that
the price follows the scarcity of electricity; if the electricity is scarce the price goes up
and vice versa. Making the buildings respond to this price signal makes it easier to
constantly match the production with the consumption. By reducing the consumption
when the price is high, the smart grid can enable smoothing of consumption peaks, which
in turn can reduce the need for capacity expansions on the physical grid and production
capacity.
An example of a system with a high share of wind power is the Danish power system. In
systems with large share of wind power, the ﬂuctuating and uncontrollable power produc-
tion may cause the production to surpass the consumption. This happens regularly in
Denmark at present time, as shown in ﬁgure 2.1. The ﬁgure is based on real data from a
[Energinet.dk], and shows the electricity consumption and wind production in Denmark
West from 3rd to 11th of February 2015. According to Energinet’s annual report for 2014
[Energinet.dk 2014] 38 % of the power consumption in Denmark was covered by wind
power. As the share increases in the future, the ﬂuctuations in energy production will
increase, and there will be more periods with surplus electricity production.

The investigations in this report relies on the possibility of storing energy in the thermal
mass of buildings. The energy cannot be released in the electricity system again, but
can be used to reduce or eliminate future demand for electricity for heating or cooling
in the building. The use of energy storages as a way to increase the ﬂexibility of a
system has been investigated with promising results. A study made by [Barton and
Inﬁeld 2004] investigated how much intermittent wind power that could be included in
an energy system by using diﬀerent types of energy storages. The study showed that
storage technologies allowing for storage of energy over long periods of time (several
hours to days) allowed for larger shares of intermittent power production compared to
short-term storage technologies (minutes to hours). A technology able to store energy for
10 minutes allowed 10 % more intermittent energy, while storage over 24 hours combined
with energy curtailment allowed for up to three times as much intermittent electricity
absorbed in the system.
Energy storages are furthermore an economically sound solution. A study by [Kiviluoma
and Meibom 2010] showed that there is economical potential for heat storages in systems
with large shares of wind power from a system investment point of view. In the simulation
software Balmorel, several diﬀerent areas with varying heat generation and consumption
were investigated. The results showed that when optimising the system for cost, heat
storages are one of the main tools for increasing the ﬂexibility of the power system. While
not using buildings as energy storages in their study, they can be seen as an alternative

2.1.3
Potential for smart grids in Denmark
The Danish government has the goal to reduce the carbon emissions by 40 % within year
2020 compared to the 1990-level, as part of the climate goals of the European Union.
Other European countries have similar goals, such as Germany, who have the same goal
for emission reductions as Denmark, and the UK is aiming for 34 % [Regeringens klima-
plan 2013]. The Danish climate plan includes the goal of an energy system, including
energy for transportation, which relies on 100 % renewable energy sources, and oil and
coal phased out from the electricity and heat production by 2035.
The renewable energy production goals of the Danish government require investments in
the grid technology and home technologies. Hourly metering is one of the investments
that are necessary for each building to beneﬁt from smart consumption. The Danish
Ministry of Climate, Energy and Building published [Smart Grid Strategy 2013], wherein
they acknowledge that hourly settlement for consumers is a prerequisite for implemen-
tation of a smart grid system. The ministry expects that the hourly metering will be
established before intelligent building control is widespread, for the reasons of increased
competition in the electricity market as well as general energy savings from increased
awareness in consumers.
The establishment of remotely read hour meters in buildings is being pushed forward
in the growth plan passed in 2013 [Vækstplan DK 2013].
In 2013 half of all homes
in Denmark had remotely read meters. In the plan the government requires that the
remaining 50 % of homes are ﬁtted with remotely read meters. Hourly remote metering
coupled with a varying hourly price, gives both possibility and incentive for demand
side management of individual buildings. The end-user has the beneﬁt of saving money
(not necessarily energy) and the system operators get the beneﬁt of a greater ﬂexibility
in the system. There are in other words prospects of smart buildings in a foreseeable
future in Denmark.
The Danish government invests in further development of smart grid technology, and is
participating instance through the project IEA EBS Annex 67. The IEA (International
Energy Agency) is an organisation which works to enable its member countries to obtain
clean, reliable and aﬀordable energy. EBS (Energy in Buildings and Community), is a
sub-organisation which focuses on the built environment. One of the ongoing projects is
Annex 67, Energy Flexible Buildings, which aims to demonstrate how and to which ex-
tent buildings can be used to increase ﬂexibility in the energy systems, and how to utilise
the buildings as part of the Smart cities [EBC, Energy in Buildings and Community no
date].

Relative cost savings increased electricity price and volatility

Flexibility sold on the day-ahead market
At present time there are no electricity products which have time-based prices for private

not only on cost savings, but the ﬂexibility of a building can possibly become an asset
that can be sold on a day-ahead market. This beneﬁts both the building owner and the

Simulations including smart control can be used to ﬁgure out what the potential is for
energy storage/peak shaving for the entire building mass. For instance the Ministry of
Energy has a clear beneﬁt from knowing the potential of smart control in the building
mass, if they are to decide whether or not to invest in smart building technology on a
large scale. Simulations of diﬀerent building classes and types can be used to examine
where the potential beneﬁts are highest. The results from such studies make grounds
for political decisions regarding smart buildings in general.

2.1.5
Challenges and potentials related to smart control in
residential and oﬃce buildings
Smart control may be applied to many systems and appliances in a building, and some
are easier implement than others. When introducing a control system that not only
responds to input from the occupants but also to information from the grid, the feeling
of losing control of their immediate environment may occur in some occupants. The
importance of user control is discussed below, as well as a discussion on why heating and
cooling is a good place to start when looking for potential smart control implementation
in buildings.
Perceived user control
Maintaining perceived user control, i.e. the feeling the occupants have of being in con-
trol of the immediate environment, is crucial for the successful implementation of smart
technology. The occupants may have a larger need for controlling some electrical appli-
ances than others, and this inﬂuences how much smart control that can be introduced
in the buildings.
The inclusion of household appliances controlled by the price of electricity was tested
in the new development project Norra Djurgårdsstaden north of Stockholm in Sweden.
The occupants experienced several technical problems with the equipment and control
settings. The inhabitants said that most of the problems were easily ﬁxable (for example
the oven switched oﬀwhen the inhabitants left the kitchen), but it required an electrical
technician to perform the changes, which was of great annoyance [Ahlbom 2015]. These
problems are typical for emerging technologies and will be eliminated in time, but the
experiment shows that when interfering with private spheres, it must be done seamlessly
if people are to accept it.
The same experiences are seen related to the thermal indoor climate. [Frontczak and
Wargocki 2011] shows that when providing occupants with the possibility for controlling
the thermal indoor climate, the satisfaction with the indoor environment goes up. A
study on Finnish homes and oﬃces showed that people are less happy with the thermal
environment in oﬃces compared to in their homes. This is linked to the fact that the
occupants had better control over the thermal environment in their homes [Karjalainen
2009].

Smart control of the thermal environment may for this reason not be accepted by the
occupants. However, controlling the temperature will possibly feel like less of an intru-
sion than controlling household appliances, and if the occupants are able to set a range
of acceptable temperatures, the perceived control may be suﬃcient.
The potential for shifting consumption in buildings depends on which utility that can
be shifted the most while causing the least possible inconvenience for the users of the
building. Energy consumption in buildings is commonly separated into the following
categories: heating, cooling, ventilation, lighting, and appliances (TVs, computer, re-
frigerator etc.). Some of these energy consuming categories are possible to shift, while
others are more challenging.
Appliances
It is possible to shift the electricity consumption of some electrical appliances, and
products that allow for time-delayed starts are already available on the market. Some of
these products can be time-delayed without interfering with the users daily life, such as
freezers and refrigerators. Other products, such as washing machines and other electrical
appliances (TV, coﬀee machine etc.) cannot be utilised without an interference in the
user’s daily life.
Light
Light demand cannot realistically be shifted, since it requires an energy consumption at
the exact time as the lighting is activated. Presently the only possible way to shift light
demand is to use a battery.
Ventilation
Electricity consumption for ventilation is diﬃcult to shift, although it is be possible
to ﬂush a room to reduce the pollutants and CO2-levels and stop the supply air ﬂow
for a short period of time. It is diﬃcult to predict the ventilation demand, because it
depends entirely on the often varying usage patterns of the occupants. The only likely
scenario where occupant behaviour can be predicted with high success probability is
meeting rooms where the schedule of the room is known. Even in this particular case
the possibility for postponing the ventilation is limited, as the amount of pollutants will
rise fast and the ventilation will have to be commenced to maintain comfort standards.
Many buildings are not equipped with ventilation, which limits the potential for the total
building mass. The potential for shifting electricity demand for ventilation, if heating
and cooling is achieved through other systems, is limited. Night cooling by ventilation
is discussed in the following section ”Heating and cooling”.
Heating and cooling
Heating and cooling has potential for load shifting, due to the fact that the optimal
thermal comfort is achieved within a range of temperatures, and because the thermal
energy can be stored in thermal mass already present in the building mass.
Load shifting and reduction of peak loads for cooling has been utilised for many years
through night cooling by ventilation. Ventilation air (mechanical or natural ventilation)
is used to cool the thermal mass during night when the external air is colder than the

indoor environment. During the day the thermal mass reduces the operative tempera-
ture in the room by absorbing thermal energy from the room air. The building is cooled
using free and available night air in order to reduce the energy cost at the time where
cooling is more expensive, i.e. during the day. As such the method is the same for the
smart building, where loads are shifted to times where the energy is more abundant and
cheaper. A literature review by [Braun 2003] reviewing papers on pre-cooling in com-
mercial buildings concludes that there is great potential for cooling peak load reductions
by applying night cooling combined with thermal mass.
The use of night cooling through ventilation is a energy eﬃcient way to cool thermal
mass in a building when the night air is cooler than the operative temperature inside,
which is the case for almost all days in Denmark. The introduction of cool night air can
be achieved through natural ventilation, in which case the only delivered electricity is
for opening the windows. When opening the windows is not an option, the mechanical
ventilation system can be used. The mechanical ventilation system uses electricity for
fans, which can be controlled through a smart control system that takes into account
the price or carbon emissions of electricity.
Experiments on load shifting with electrical heat pumps have been conducted on the
Danish island of Bornholm.
Full scale tests on ﬂexible heating were carried out on
several houses that were equipped with heat pumps, which were controlled to maintain
a temperature within a range that was considered comfortable. The load was shifted
from high price-hours to low price-hours by a control system considering the electricity
price. The users of the tested houses had a feedback system that delivered results on
the electricity price developments and daily and monthly consumption reports, as the
project presentation [EcoGrid 2015] shows. The results for the project are presented in
[Gantenbein et al. 2012], and they show a potential for using the heating systems for
stabilising the electricity system. At the same time, both small and large consumers
wereable to save money on electricity.

Deterministic variables are variables that are pre-deﬁned and not random.

Most control systems for heating and cooling control in buildings use deterministic vari-
ables, in form of measured data or deﬁned forecast weather data. The use of stochastic
variables can improve the control systems to also take into account the inaccuracies and
mistakes that weather forecasts are subject to. This can possibly improve the robustness
of the control systems.
There are several types of control systems that are used for heating and cooling systems
in buildings. The rule-based system is the simplest type of control.
Rule-based control
In control of heating and cooling in buildings it is common to use rule-based control
systems.
In systems that use rule-base control a set of rules and responses are pre-
deﬁned. Radiator thermostats is an example of a very simple control of heating that
reacts to a measured temperature. The rule is: if the temperature is too low: heat. If
the temperature is adequate: do not heat. Proportional, PI- and PID-controllers are
examples of rule-based controls that are commonly used for heating and cooling systems
in larger buildings.
Model predictive control (MPC)
MPC-models are dynamic optimisation models. This means that the model describes
the development of a system, for example a building, over time, and can be subject
to variable inputs and constraints. The model optimises for an objective function by
ﬁnding the input variables that either maximises or minimises the value for the given
time period. The set of input values that provide the maximum or minimum objective
function is called a solution vector. MPC uses feedback processes, where the time frame
is expanded constantly and updated when new input data is available. In building models
the objective function describes the cost for heating and cooling, and the solution vector
deﬁnes the supply of energy for heating and cooling. The input is the price- and weather
forecasts that are constantly updated as time passes.
The MPC-model can be expanded by including stochastic input variables for weather
and price forecasts. It is then called Stochastic Model Predictive Control. The use of
stochastic model predictive control has been shown to perform well compared to both
rule-based control and MPC with deterministic variables, as demonstrated in a study
by [Oldewurtel et al. 2010].

3.1.2
Algorithm for heating and cooling control
Based on the considerations discussed in the previous section an algorithm is designed
to move loads from high price hours to low-price hours. The algorithm consists of four
main steps.
In the ﬁrst step the prediction of future loads takes place, which is done with the use of
a simpliﬁed version of the building model. The loads per hour in a day are summed, so
that the total heating and cooling loads for each day are found.
The second step involves redistribution of the loads. The hours in the days are ranked
after price, so that the hour with the lowest price is ranked as number 1 and the hour
with the highest price is ranked as number 24. The load for each day is distributed on
the hours with the lowest rank, determined by the installed capacity of the heating and
cooling systems.
In the third step of the algorithm the loads per hour are translated into signals for the
heating and cooling system. A signal of 1 means that the system is running at full
capacity, and 0 means that the system is shut oﬀ. The signal is named future load
signal.
In the fourth step the future load signal is coupled with a proportional controller that
reacts to the indoor temperatures. This temperature signal is implemented to ensure
that the indoor temperatures are within the comfort range even if the future load signal
fails to deliver the necessary load. The ﬁnal signal thereby takes into account both
future loads and the current temperatures in the building.

The software/methods that were considered as prediction models for the algorithm are
discussed in the following section:
Be10
Be10 is a validation program developed by SBi. It is typically used to estimate annual
energy consumption and for regulatory approval calculations. Be10 provides an annual
heat balance for the building on a per-month format, and results on a hour to hour basis
are not possible to obtain from this program.
Excel based calculations
Excel based hour to hour-calculations are more detailed than the Be10-output, for in-
stance the Bo Adamson calculation method and hourly calculations based on EN ISO
13790. These models are hour based and include a simple heat balance including ven-
tilation, usage schedules, ambient conditions etc. They do not take into account all
the physical relations that are included in more elaborate simulation models, and are
therefore not as accurate. The excel ﬁles need manual input on areas, window proper-
ties, loads and heating systems. When the input is deﬁned the calculation is practically
instantaneous. This kind of prediction can be very useful for implementation in real
buildings, as it does not require expensive software. In relation to this report the adding
of data and running the excel ﬁle takes longer time than creating a simpliﬁed model in
IDA ICE, and it has therefore not been used.
BSim
Simple simulation programs such as BSim could be utilised for a prediction model. BSim
is a building simulation tool that is developed by SBi (Statens Byggeforskningsinstitut,
[www.sbi.dk]. BSim provides an opportunity for building a model of a building, and
adding more details than the excel-based calculations do.A prediction model made in a
simpler simulation program requires the modelling of the building zone in both programs.
This increases the time spent on creating the model. Furthermore the programs have
diﬀerent engines bases that reach slightly diﬀerent results for the same input.
IDA ICE with feedback loop
In the study by [Petersen and Svendsen 2011] a prediction model which is embedded
into the simulation model is used. If possible this would provide a prediction with the
same accuracy as the real model. According to Patrik Skogqvist in EQUA, a feedback
mechanism could theoretically be implemented in IDA ICE but it requires that an energy
plant is created for each of the models (real and prediction), and module programming
would be necessary to allow for communication between the two models. For this reason
the method has not been pursued further.

model twice in IDA ICE, which would be the case for the simple Excel base models, is
weighed out by the fact that creating the prediction model is fast when there is already

is considered the best prediction model for this project.

3.1.5
Creating signals for the heating and cooling systems
The loads need to be translated into a language that the heating and cooling systems can
understand. Heating and cooling systems are normally controlled by signals between 0
and 1, where 1 represents a fully switched on system and a signal of 0 means that the
system is switched oﬀ. Most heating and cooling systems can also adjust the supply to
a fraction of the maximum supply, to deliver for example half of the maximum capacity
to a signal of 0.5. The translation of the necessary loads into signals for the heating and
cooling system is explained in the following section.
Future load signal
The maximum supply possible to introduce to the room, corresponding to a signal of
1, is the installed capacity of the systems. When redistributing the loads from high
price hours to low price hours it is desirable that largest possible amount of supply is
delivered in the hours with the lowest price. The load is therefore translated into signals
by dividing the assigned supply with the installed capacity. For all hours the signal will
be 1, except the last hour to be assigned loads, which has a value between 0 and 1. In
ﬁgure 3.5 this part is labelled with the numbers 3-4.
Temperature signal
To ensure that the indoor temperatures are maintained within the comfort range a tem-
perature sensor is included. A proportional controller measures the indoor temperature
in the room creates a signal for the heating and cooling system. In ﬁgure 3.5 this part
is labelled with the numbers 1-2.
Final signal for the heating and cooling systems
The ﬁnal signal for the heating and cooling systems includes both the temperature
signal and the future load signal. To maintain a signal format that the heating and
cooling systems understand the following method is implemented. The signals from the
temperature measurement and from the load prediction are summed, creating a signal
ranging between 0 and 2. A proportional rule is used to scale the signal to the wanted
[0 .. 1] format: if the summed signal is 0, the ﬁnal signal is 0. If the summed signal is 1
or above, the ﬁnal signal is 1. A summed signal between 0 and 1 will remain the same
due to the proportional control. In ﬁgure 3.5 this part is labelled with the numbers 5-7.

3.4
Implementation of algorithm into IDA ICE
In this section the implementation of the algorithm into IDA ICE is explained. The
establishment of a prediction model, with the use of a simpliﬁed heating and cooling
system, is described, as well as the implementation of the control system in the advanced

3.4.1
Load prediction in IDA ICE and load redistribution
The model of the building is simulated in IDA ICE, equipped with oversized ideal heaters
and coolers and the Denmark.dry weather ﬁle (perfect forecast).
Ideal heaters and
coolers are theoretical room units that are not connected to the main energy system
of the building, so they have no delay in heating power, nor any eﬃciency losses. For
further use in the algorithm the hourly energy consumption is exported, which represents
the estimated amount of heating and cooling that is required to maintain the comfort
temperatures.
The heating demand per hour is exported into Excel in a per-hour format. Here the
values are summed for each 24 hour period, and distributed on the hours with the lowest
prices. Then the reassigned loads are translated into heating and cooling signals, which
are exported for each hour to a source ﬁle for import into IDA ICE control.

3.6
Building models
In order to get an image of how well the smart control works, and the potential for the
implementation of such control in the existing building mass, several heating system
types and building types and qualities are tested.
Oﬃce buildings and residential buildings have diﬀerent occupancy proﬁles, which has an
impact on the energy balance of the buildings. Especially the internal loads are diﬀerent,
which impacts the need for both cooling and heating. The potential for implementing
smart control may therefore diﬀer for the two types of buildings.
Because old buildings constitute a very large share of the total building mass compared to
the new buildings, the potentials in both new and old buildings are tested. The diﬀerence
between the new and old buildings are limited to the U-value of the façade, the U-value
and g-value of the windows and the eﬃciency of the ventilation heat exchanger. The
new building model thereby also covers renovated buildings with an improved façade
and heat exchanger.
The level of activation of the thermal mass potentially has a large inﬂuence on the ability
to store energy in a room. Therefore two heating and cooling-systems were tested. One
system is a so-called fast-response system with limited exchange with the thermal mass,
and another is a slow-response system with a higher exchange with thermal mass. The
systems are supplied by a heat pump with a COP of 3 for both heating and cooling.
3.6.1
Heating and cooling systems
Fast-response systems
Fast-response systems are applied to all combinations of old and new oﬃce and residen-
tial buildings. The fast-response system consists of a water radiator for heating and a
cooling device with water medium for cooling. These systems react fast to changes in
the heating and cooling signal, but do not activate the thermal mass as much as other
systems. To view the dimensioning of the systems, see Appendix A and B.
Slow-response systems
Slow-response systems are applied to all combinations of old and new oﬃce and residen-
tial buildings. The slow-response system consists of a heating/cooling ﬂoor system which
is embedded in concrete. The supply from these systems change slowly to changes in the
heating and cooling signal, and activate the thermal mass to a large degree compared
to other systems. To view the dimensioning of the systems, see Appendix A and B.

3.7
Studies and simulations
In this section the performed studies and simulations are described.
First a couple
of preliminary studies that were performed during the development of the algorithm
are explained. This includes a study that investigates whether the load redistribution
should be done over 24 hours, or if shorter time periods are more beneﬁcial. The other
preliminary study investigates which hour the periods should start in, for example at
midnight or if it is a better option to start the period some hours before.
Hereafter the studies for testing the functionality of the algorithm and estimate the
potential for smart control in various types of buildings are explained.
3.7.1
Preliminary studies
Length of load distribution period
According to its design the algorithm distributes supply across a series of hours, or a time
period, and the optimal length of this time period was determined though a preliminary
investigation. The algorithm was tested for periods of 24, 12 and 6 hours.
The ranking procedure explained in chapter 3.1.4 describes how the future demand for
heating and cooling is redistributed on the hours of the day based on a ranking of the
price (or emissions) data. When applying shorter load distribution periods the predicted
load for heating and cooling is summed over a shorter period (24, 12 or 6 hours). The
ranking of the hours based on price and emission is likewise done over 24, 12 and 6
hours. This means that the loads are distributed twice per day for the 12 hour period,
and thrice per day for the 6 hour period.
The diﬀerent ranking periods were tested in the residential building with a fast-response
system. The reason for this is that it is expected that the slow-response model performs
better with a longer simulation period compared to the fast-response model.
If the
fast-reponse model beneﬁts from a long period, the slow-response model will too.
Start/end time of load distribution period
The timing of the start of a new load distribution period can have a large impact how
much load it is possible to shift. In chapter 3.1.1 it is explained how days with a high
load may require several hours for delivering the energy in a system with limited capacity.
As seen in ﬁgure 3.8, showing the average price ranking of each hours of the day, most
of the low ranking hours occur between around 22 and 04 in the morning. When using a
starting point at midnight, the hours that receive the distributed loads are most likely in
either the beginning, or in the end of the day, which means that some of the distributed
load will be introduced when the day is almost over. This causes a supply deﬁciency
during the day, and as a result the temperatures will not be maintained within the
required range. The temperature sensor control will kick in and heat/cool the building,
disregarding the price data, reducing the beneﬁt of the smart control.
This preliminary study compares the accumulated energy demand with the accumulated
==PAGE:49==

supplied energy. The accumulated energy demand is the sum of the energy demand that
is necessary for maintaining the indoor temperatures up to any point in time.
The
accumulated supplied energy is the sum of all the energy that has been supplied to
the room throughout the time period. In order to ﬁnd the balance between supply and
demand the diﬀerence between the accumulated energy demand and supply is calculated
for each hour. When the supplied energy is higher than the demand it is assumed that
the thermal conditions are met. When the demand is higher than the supply, there is
an energy deﬁciency.
The diﬀerent starting points were tested in the new residential building with a fast-
response system. The number of hours where the accumulated demand is larger than
the accumulated supply is counted for the periods starting at 21, 22 and 24 hours. The
result with the lowest amount of hours represents the best starting point.

The main part of the results are created on basis of the annual simulations. The annual

heating and cooling season as well as the intermediate seasons with both heating and
cooling demand.
All the building models are run for one year. The delivered energy for heating and
cooling to the rooms is extracted for each hour of the year. For the cooling season the
results are shown for the period of 31 st of May to the 8th of June as a representative
week. For the heating season the results are shown for the period of 1 st to the 9th of
December as a representative week.
The key results for total heating and cooling consumption is the total delivered energy
delivered to the room for heating and cooling for the full year. The cost for heating and
cooling is calculated by multiplying the electricity price data for the speciﬁc hours with
the loads, and the sum is divided by 3 to take into account the COP of the heat pump.
3.7.3
Capacity reductions
For each building model it is investigated whether the smart control can be used to
reduce the peak demand for heating and cooling, and as a parallel result, how much it is
possible to downscale the installed capacity without reducing the quality of the indoor
environment.
All models are simulated for one year with (D)-control (dumb control), and the capacity
of the heating and cooling systems is adjusted so that a small amount of hours per
year is outside the comfort zone. Thereafter the models are run with (S)-control (smart
control), and the capacity is adjusted again so that only few hours per year are outside
of the temperature comfort range. In other words two capacities are found for each
model, and both capacities are tested with (D) and (S)-control.

The price data used for this investigation is historical electricity

The results from the simulations investigating diﬀerent ranking lengths are discussed in
this section. The energy consumption and total cost for energy is displayed in ﬁgures
4.1 and 4.2.
The results show that the energy consumption for heating is slightly higher for the 24-
hour period than for the 12- and 6-hour periods. The energy consumption for cooling
is lower in the 24-hour period than 12- and 6-hour periods. This is due to the fact
that the 24-hour period allows for pre-cooling during the night. The cost for heating
is similar for all three periods. However, for cooling there is a large advantage in the
24-hour period compared to the shorter periods. This is both due to the lower energy
consumption but also because the energy is consumed during the price-favourable night
hours. Redistribution of the demand from day to night is not possible when the periods
are shorter than 24 hours. The simulations in the remainder of this report will be based
on ranking periods of 24 hours length.

Heating load, fast-response systems
The heating load is shown in ﬁgure 4.6 and 4.7 with the electricity price and indoor
temperatures respectively.
The heating demand is characterised by being more stable throughout the day compared
to the cooling demand during summer. The typical cooling demand occurs during few
hours where the direct solar radiation is high and angled towards a façade with a window.
During winter the heat losses through the façade and ventilation are the most prominent,
and these are dependent mainly on the external temperature. This causes the heating
demand to be stable over the course of 24 hours.
The results for the fast-response systems simulation shows that the (S)-control shifts
parts of the heating loads. While the heating in the (D)-model is constantly activated,
the (S)-control has occasional bursts of extra load on the hours where the price is lowest,
and requires a lower load when the prices are higher.
The temperatures are highly inﬂuenced by the supplied energy to the room. For the (D)-
model the heat supply changes gradually to accommodate the variation in heat losses
from the room, and the temperatures are maintained at a stable level above the heating
set-point of 20.5 ◦C. The temperatures for the (S)-model is on the contrary tied to the
shifting heat supply.

Capacity reductions in slow-response systems
The results from the capacity reduction investigation for the slow-response systems are
shown in this section. Figure 4.16 shows the installed capacity in each of the models,
where is can be seen that the heating system capacity is 13 % (D) and 24 % (S) lower
in the (S)-model compared to the (D)-model, and the cooling system is 43 % (D) and
46 % (S) lower.
The results show that there is a potential for reducing the size of the heating system, but
the potential is limited because the heat demand is relatively stable. This means that
there are no demand peaks that are reduced by the redistribution. There is however
huge potential for reducing the capacity of the cooling system when using (S)-control.
This is because the cooling load is characterised by high peak loads, which are eﬀectively
distributed over many hours when using (S)-control. The pre-cooled thermal mass takes
up much of the peak solar load, and reduces the need for cooling during the hours with
higher solar loads to a minimum.

4.17. The (S)-control is able to maintain the temperatures for both the system sized for
(D)- and (S)-control, while the (D)-control is not able to maintain the temperature in
the lower capacity model. This suggests a better utilisation of the capacity when using
(S)-control.

4.7
Weather forecast robustness
The results of the investigation on forecast robustness for wrong weather predictions are
displayed in this section.
The results for the winter scenarios are shown in ﬁgures 4.55 and 4.56 and the summer
scenarios in ﬁgures 4.57 and 4.58, where the diﬀerence between the (D)-model and the
(S)-model is shown for each weather forecast scenario.
The temperature deviations do not cause large reductions in savings compared to the
prefect forecast. The forecast deviation in solar radiation does reduce the savings. The
cost for heating is in all cases lower when using (S)-control than (D)-control.
The the scenario with the biggest reduction in savings is the summer scenario that is
sunnier than forecasted. This is because the extra sun increases the cooling load during
the day, compared to what was expected. This means that the extra load has to be
removed at the hours where the price is higher. As a result the cooling patterns look
more like the (D)-control, and the cost savings are reduced.
The (S)-models have savings in all scenarios despite the deviations between the forecasted
weather and the actual weather. This is partly because only part of the load is shifted,
and there is still loads required in the dayhours in both the (D) and (S)-models. This is
because the (S)-control is not perfectly optimised. The left-over loads that occur during
the day in the (S)-model functions as a buﬀer for deviations in the weather. This results
in a robust (S)-control, but the potential savings could be higher with a more optimised
algorithm.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
In this chapter the general observations from the thesis works are discussed. First the
results obtained through the building simulations are discussed. Hereafter light is shed
upon the limitations to the algorithm and thesis in itself. Following the experiences from
the thesis work is a discussion on the prerequisites for smart control to be implemented
in real life, as well as an elaboration on the potential uses that the algorithm can have
for diﬀerent stakeholders. In the end of the chapter possible future work is outlined.
5.1
Potential in simulations of smart buildings
The general results for the functionality of the algorithm is assessed in this section. The
potential for smart control of heating and cooling in buildings is not uniform for all types
of buildings and systems, and the areas where the highest potential for cost reductions
with smart control is seen are highlighted.
5.1.1
Shifting peak loads from high-price hours to low-price
hours
The results have shown a potential for moving the peak loads to other times of the
day. For the building owner this results in a lower total cost for energy, since the load
is covered during hours with a lower price. The potential for saving money/emissions
by moving loads is bigger if the installed capacity of the systems is large compared to
the demand, because a larger share of the load can be covered during the lowest priced
hours.
Even if the peaks in the building itself are not reduced by this eﬀect, the peaks for
the system can still be reduced. In a situation where most buildings do not respond
to changes in price of electricity the buildings who do have smart control contribute to
peak reduction while still have the same peak consumption. This is because the total
consumption the system is reduced at the peak-hour and increased at the hours with
low consumption.
5.1.2
Reducing peak demand for heating and cooling
The other way of utilising the smart control is by reducing the peak loads within the
building. The results show that a heating system controlled with the (S)-control can
be downscaled compared to a system with (D)-control. In other words, the capacity
==PAGE:94==
82
5 Discussion
needed for heating and cooling is lower with (S)-control than with (D)-control. This
can be used to decrease the cost of installing heating and cooling systems, especially in
new buildings. The savings for cost for energy is not as high for a down-scaled system
compared to an ”over-sized” system with peak load shifting.
This feature is also valuable if working with buildings with systems incapable of meeting
the actual load. For instance, if a new building has a cooling system, but still overheats.
The results show that applying smart control to a system can improve the indoor envi-
ronment, because the loads are supplied pro-actively before the loads occur. This means
that the utilisation of the existing system is more eﬀective.
5.1.3
Potential for heating and cooling
The results show that the (S)-control is especially favourable in buildings with a high
cooling load. This is because the night hours can be used to pre-cool the thermal mass
and the cooling demand is reduced or eliminated during the day-hours where the price
is high. As opposed to heat losses or gains caused by the ambient temperature, the solar
load is characterised by peaks over few hours in the day. The load distribution enables
capacity reductions that are much higher for cooling than for heating.
The peak load shifting by (S)-control has beneﬁts for the thermal environment, espe-
cially for reducing the risk of overheating. By pre-cooling the thermal mass the amount
of solar heat absorbed is increased, and the indoor temperature increment is not as no-
torious. The pre-cooling also means that in addition to the heat absorption from the
thermal mass, additional heat can be removed from the remaining cooling system capac-
ity. The conventional control starts cooling when the temperature exceeds the set-point
temperature, and if the capacity is lower than the load, or if the lag time in the cooling
system too large compared to the increment in temperature, the temperatures will rise
beyond the thermal comfort range.
5.1.4
Fast- or slow-response heating and cooling
The results indicate that the slow systems work better in new buildings than in old
buildings. The storage of energy in the thermal mass that is obtained by using ﬂoor
heating and cooling is better utilised in buildings where the energy losses and gains
through the façade and ventilation are low. On the other hand the slow response systems
were unable to function in old buildings, because the amount of energy required for
storage was either too high or the losses from the building envelope caused the stored
energy to vanish before it could be put into use. In these buildings the fast-response
systems worked better, but were not able to store much energy for later use.
5.1.5
Potential for oﬃce- and residential buildings
The potential for savings in oﬃce buildings are large, following the observation that the
potential is bigger in cooling loads than in heating loads. Oﬃce buildings in general
==PAGE:95==
5.1 Potential in simulations of smart buildings
83
have a higher cooling load than residential buildings, because of the large internal loads.
Also oﬃce buildings have high peak cooling loads because the occupancy hours coincide
with solar loads.
Residential buildings have a lower internal heat load from equipment and occupants than
oﬃce buildings, because there are more square meters per occupant. Older apartment
buildings as well as old and new houses are rarely ﬁtted with cooling, but heating load
is substantial in these buildings. The experience from the building industry is that new
apartment buildings are often unable to meet the requirements for overheating in the
building requirements, and cooling systems will be installed in these buildings in the
future.
5.1.6
Potential for new and old buildings
New buildings have a large cooling load and a limited heating load due to the highly
insulative façade and large windows. The smart control of buildings are especially suited
for new buildings, because the cooling loads occur over a limited amount of hours, and
require a large cooling load over a short amount of time. The heating season is very
short in new buildings, because the heat losses are small compared to the internal loads.
For the new oﬃce building the results showed close to non-existent heating demand over
a year. However, the need for cooling was prevalent, also for the winter period. As
cooling has higher potential for load shifting, the potential in new buildings is naturally
large.
New buildings can easily be ﬁtted with systems that are compatible with (S)-control,
such as systems that activate the thermal mass and the required electrical control sys-
tems. There is a movement in the building industry that new buildings may be heated
and cooled with the use of electricity through heat pumps. This is the most direct way
that the building systems can interact with the electricity grid, as opposed to to district
heating which is the most common heat source for old buildings in Denmark.
In renovated buildings there is a clear potential for smart control, because the heating
system (normally no cooling is available in renovated buildings) is oversized compared
to the new required heating load. This enables peak shifting, as a large share of the
required heating can be delivered over few hours, which are chosen to be the hours with
the lowest price/emissions.
Old buildings have a high energy loss from the building envelope, and storing energy in
the building mass is less eﬃcient. Therefore the possibility for moving loads is limited,
and the potential for smart control is therefore also limited.

5.2
Simpliﬁcations and limitations to the building
model
The largest uncertainty for the simulation models is that varying internal loads is not
taken into account. Constant loads have been used for occupancy and equipment loads,
which is not the case in the operation of a real building. The internal loads constitute
a large fraction of the loads in a building, especially new buildings, and therefore have
high inﬂuence on the internal temperatures and thereby heating and cooling demand.
The simulations are performed with CAV-ventilation, because the losses are easier to
predict. VAV-ventilation is used in many new buildings, and as the occupancy varies,
so does the heat losses through ventilation. As the heat exchangers are increasingly
eﬃcient the eﬀect of non-predictable ventilation rates is reduced.
When running the models with (S)-control the loads are not completely shifted from
high price hours to low price hours. This is because the algorithm and method are not
optimised. The fact that not all load is shifted means that the models are robust when
faced with uncertain weather forecasts, but the savings could theoretically be higher. It
is possible that when the load is distributed, the installed capacity should not be used
to deﬁne the maximum load per hour. The output from the systems do not necessarily
correspond to the installed capacity, as was seen especially for slow-response systems.
Also not taken into account is the extra energy losses that are connected to early supply
of energy. Ventilation and losses through the façade due to stored energy in the thermal
mass may increase the total demand for energy. This issue would be taken care of by
the implementation of feedback loops, but that has been outside the scope of this thesis.
Another simpliﬁcation is the use of prefect forecasts for weather and price, and this could
be tested with the use of historical forecast data. For the model to take into account
uncertain forecasts, stochastic variables could be used.
In real life there is a correlation between weather and the price of electricity. For instance
there is more wind power when there are high wind velocities, and the ambient temper-
ature inﬂuences the demand for heating and cooling in the general building mass, which
in turn inﬂuences the price. This correlation is not taken into account in this project,
as reference weather data has been used with historical price data for electricity.
5.3
Implementations in real buildings and possible
input sources for weather-, price- and emissions
data
As discussed earlier, the internal loads as well as weather- and price/CO2 forecasts can
be unpredictable.
This has to be taken into account in the further development of
algorithm for heating and cooling control that consider price and/or CO2 data as input.
==PAGE:97==
5.3 Implementations in real buildings and possible input sources for weather-, price-
and emissions data
85
There is a possibility of including feedback mechanisms in real buildings.
This will
reduce the vulnerability to unpredictable internal loads, as well as bad weather forecasts.
The best way to implement feedback mechanisms is probably through Model predictive
control, using stochastic variables for weather and price.
The use of cooling is in many buildings superﬂuous, because the necessary cooling can
easily and much lower electricity consumption be achieved with night cooling through
natural ventilation. However, there are several reasons why natural ventilation may be
a suboptimal solution. In buildings that are close to roads noise and pollution might be
a problem during the night for residential buildings, and during the days in oﬃces. The
obtainable cooling during the day is also limited because of high ambient temperatures,
and this could be a problem especially for oﬃce buildings due to their high cooling
demand in the day.
Safety is another issue, either due to break-ins, but also if the
building is tall and there is a risk of falling out.
A large fraction of the building mass use district heating (and cooling) as energy sources,
and are thereby not directly linked to the electricity grid.
However there are times
that the district energy is cheaper to produce. For instance when there is excess wind
power, electricity can be stored in the district heating and cooling grid as thermal energy.
Depending on the operation of CHP-plants (Combined Heat and Power) there can be
an abundance or lack of energy in the district energy systems that could be reﬂected in
the price. This requires hourly metering and ﬂexible prices, which can be challenging
to implement given the price scheme for district energy. As of now the price for district
heating is determined in the end of each year by distributing the total cost for production
over the consumed energy of each costumer.
The potential implementation in real buildings requires certain input for the algorithm
to function.
One is a 24 hour weather forecast and the other is a 24 hour price or
CO2-forecast. There are several sources for weather forecasts that are available online.
Energinet.dk creates CO2 prognosis for the same day, and the prognosis for the next day
is made available at around 15.00 hours the day before [CO2 predictions 2015]. These
predictions are available for anyone at request, and can therefore be used as input for
the smart control.
It present time there are no electricity distribution companies that oﬀer hourly prices,
and hourly prices are a prerequisite for smart buildings. Products for electricity with
hourly prices are in the future going to be introduced to the market, and the prices for
the end-consumers depends on the design of the price model that will be chosen. It is
possible that the hourly prices for consumers will be connected to the day-ahead market,
the balancing (real-time) market or something entirely diﬀerent. When such electricity
products are introduced on the private market, there is a possibility that the companies
have interest in delivering price forecasts for the following day. In that case the smart
control could use this forecast as input.
As an alternative to price forecasts developed by electricity companies, the day-ahead
prices can be used. The day-ahead market is the market place where the electricity
==PAGE:98==
86
5 Discussion
producers sell their expected electricity production for the next day. The end consumers
get their prices from the balancing market, which is where the production is balanced
to match the demand in real-time. The price in the balancing market is more or less
similar to the day-ahead market price, depending on inaccuracies in weather forecasts
or unexpected events such as the failing of a power plant. For consumers with a smart
building the electricity day-ahead market price, which is hourly based, can give a pointer
to which hours that can be expected to have the lowest and highest prices. The algorithm
developed in this project does not need to know the exact prices (nor CO2-emissions),
but rather which hours that are cheaper than the other hours. Therefore the day-ahead
market prices can possibly deliver adequate information about the future electricity
price.
5.4
Uses for simulations of models with smart
control
Simulations of building in IDA ICE can be used to estimate the potential in smart
control in buildings in general, which is useful for governmental bodies, TSOs and energy
companies. The results from such investigations is valuable input to decisions regarding
investments in grid capacity, optimised operation of the grid through lower peaks and
ﬂexible demand, and potential savings in emissions and energy.
The simulations are also useful for estimating the potential savings in single buildings
before investments are made for a new control system. On the other end of the table
building designers compete on delivering the best building designs for their clients, and
being able to simulate and design smart grid ready buildings give a huge advantage in
the highly competitive building sector.
For real building control the use of IDA ICE as a simulation tool is not as practical as the
use of simpler equation based estimations. These are faster to run, and provide adequate
results for existing buildings. By using parameter estimation the models can deliver very
accurate results for load prediction, and adding the weather and price forecasts using
stochastic variables result in a robust MPC-system that is able to save on heating and
cooling cost in real buildings.
5.5
Future work
There is potential for further optimisation and development of better algorithms for load
shifting. There is a higher potential for cost savings if a larger share of the high-price
consumption was shifted into the low-price hours.
The use of real forecasts for weather and prices should be looked into, either as noise in
the deterministic variables or by using stochastic variables as input.
The implementation of feedback loops in simulation programs, such as IDA ICE, could
==PAGE:99==
5.5 Future work
87
open for new possibilities in simulations of smart buildings. For instance implementa-
tion of weather forecasts with noise and mistakes could better be investigated if the
simulation program was able to reset after each simulation period and take into account
the consequences from the deviations between the real and the forecasted input data.
The use of MPC-models in a simulation environment like IDA ICE could provide better
results for the cost reductions in buildings. For future investigations it is suggested that
possibility of creating a method like this is investigated: intermediate results from the
building model in IDA ICE is extracted for each 24 hours, and this is used as input to an
optimisation model, possibly in another environment, for example MATLAB. Thereafter
the results from the optimisation is imported back into IDA ICE as input for the control
system. This would enable MPC-control in a complex simulation environment, with a
feedback loop for higher accuracy.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
For this thesis an algorithm designed to move heating and cooling loads to more beneﬁcial
times was developed. It was implemented in the simulation software IDA ICE and used
to investigate the potentials for smart control in several building models, including new
and old oﬃce and residential buildings, equipped with fast- and slow responding heating
and cooling systems.
The results show that there are deﬁnite cost savings related to implementation of smart
control, and that savings can be found in all the building types that were tested. Specif-
ically, the results showed that new buildings have larger potential than old buildings,
due to the quality of the building envelope and heat exchanger that allow for storage of
energy for a longer time. As the algorithm typically moves loads from day hours to the
night hours, the cost for cooling were in general greatly reduced. For the same reason
it was possible to reduce peak loads in the buildings for both heating and cooling, and
cooling showed the highest potential. Furthermore the implementation of smart control
reduced the number of overheating hours by pre-cooling the night before large loads
occurred. It was seen that the fast-response systems functioned better in old buildings,
and that slow-response systems improved the performance of new buildings.
When hourly electricity prices are introduced for private and commercial consumers, an
algorithm like the one developed in this thesis could be useful for many parties, including
building designers, developers and owners. This tool may also be useful for governmental
bodies or TSOs for investigating the potential for ﬂexible demand in the building mass.
In the future the algorithm can be developed and improved further. The robustness of
the model, when faced with wrong forecasts for weather, was maintained by the fact
that the algorithm was not optimised, and therefore moved only parts of the loads from
the hours of demand to lower priced hours. In the future work the algorithm should be
optimised further, so that the highest possible savings can be found. However, the ro-
bustness for wrong forecasts must be taken into account. This could be done for example
by implementing stochastic variables for weather and price forecasts. Another way to
improve the model would be to implement MPC-control into a simulation environment
like IDA ICE. This could possibly be done by running an optimisation model alongside
IDA ICE, and use the results from the optimisation model as input for the control.

A.3.1
Internal loads
All internal loads are set to 0. There are several reasons why this approach was chosen.
The occupancy proﬁle is diﬃcult to estimate for residential buildings, and an internal
load of zero would be a good estimation for an empty apartment. The results of how
well the building reacts to information from the grid would be less clear, if a varying
occupancy proﬁle was added. Finally the algorithm for controlling the heating system
would be more complicated, also taking into account the internal loads, which in reality
are diﬃcult to predict.
Since the building should be able to maintain a comfortable indoor temperature also
when the occupants are not present, the internal loads were set to 0, and no additional
ventilation through window opening is possible in summer. This means that the heating
system is not aided by internal loads, and the cooling system is not helped by occupant
control of natural ventilation. The old residential building is however cooled by window
opening, as an old residential building seldom has cooling. The summer comfort is not
considered for the old residential building.

To simulate an oﬃce environment occupancy is included in the simulation. An oﬃce

hours the internal load is 0. Internal heat load from lighting is not included.
