Abstract
This thesis explores the generation of identityôÙpreserving synthetic hand images using
stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart deep learning techniques for biometric recognition. Traditional biometôÙ
ric systems rely on vast datasets of realôÙworld images, raising significant privacy conôÙ
cerns and creating a demand for synthetic alternatives. This work applies Latent Diffusion
Models (LDM) and StyleGAN3 to generate realistic hand images while preserving unique
biometric characteristics essential for identity recognition. We propose a novel identityôÙ
preserving mechanism that integrates Angular Margin loss with diffusion models to mainôÙ
tain identity across generated samples. The synthetic images are evaluated through a
hand recognition system against realôÙworld data. The results demonstrate competitive
accuracy in both verification, as well as openôÙset and closedôÙset identification tasks, valôÙ
idating the utility of synthetic data for biometric applications. This approach addresses
privacy concerns while providing an alternative source of training data for biometric modôÙ
els, advancing the field of hand recognition with identityôÙpreserving synthetic data.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Modern biometric systems using deep learning techniques are models that learn to exôÙ
tract and recognize desired features on images provided by a capturing device. These
devices vary from cameras adjusted to capture face images or retina images to fingerprint
scanners. The systems, being mostly based on deep neural networks, require substantial
amounts of training data to achieve a satisfying accuracy reaching or going beyond that
of humans. In the traditional scenario, researchers have been using publicly available
images of people found on the Internet [1]. However, as time went by and such models
became more popular, some regulations were introduced regarding privacy of the subôÙ
jects captured on the images from the datasets. Depending on the region, law makers
concluded that some of these databases can violate peopleãs right to remain anonymous
and to own and control their identity [2]. As a result, a significant number of datasets (e.g.
MSôÙcelebôÙ1M [3]) has been withdrawn forcefully and some others ôÙ to avoid potential accuôÙ
sations. Data availability has become an issue preventing research from progressing and
threatening the development of better methods and models not only regarding biometrics,
but image generation for many other purposes as well.
To tackle the newly created problem, researchers have turned their attention to synthetic
data. The discipline itself has been growing separately from biometrics, but since then, it
has become a significant part of the field. The idea is to use artificially generated images
for model training to achieve high accuracy on real life data in testing. Naturally, that asôÙ
sumes the existence of an initial real life dataset as the starting point for many synthetic
datasets. Such dataset must be of extremely high quality and variety to suffice: so includôÙ
ing images both in higher and lower quality of people of different age, ethnic background
and gender. That itself has turned out to be a challenging task [4]. In case of biometric
systems, a crucial requirement in the generation of synthetic images is the identity preserôÙ
vation of the subjects. Identity preservation in this case means maintaining the biometric
characteristic of a given subject across multiple synthetic samples. That again raises the
question of which of the elements of human physiology that carry unique information are
eligible for a biometric system.
1.1.1
Crime Scene Use Case
At the crime scene, any information that can be helpful in identifying the criminals is exôÙ
tremely valuable. A lot of research has been done in the area of face recognition systems
[5] as well as fingerprint extraction and evaluation [6]. Despite these methods being exôÙ
tremely efficient and accurate, these personal characteristics are easy to hide by using
masks and gloves. Upon this realization, some researchers focused on identifying patôÙ
terns of walking and that turned out to be useful. However, frequently omitted topic is hand
recognition. Hand biometrics is an important biometric feature that is rarely explored alôÙ
though in the crime scene use case, they are not always covered and even then, some
biometric information can still be extracted as the shape of the hand as well as certain
ratios remain unchanged.
1.1.2
Hand Recognition Systems
Usually, hand recognition systems are designed to authenticate a user or recognize a
gesture. The most common examples of the latter are sign language interpreters [7]
or models enabling a contactless interaction with a computer [8]. However, both of the

applications assume strict restrictions in how the hand images are captured (ranging from
distance to the camera to the background and image quality). In case of sign language,
the set of gestures is also not exactly the same as in other scenarios and at the same
time the identity of a person whose hand was captured is irrelevant, making it even more
distant topic from identity recognition based on hand images.
Similarly to facial expressions, hand gesture can impact the identification which is already

the privacy concerns raised above. The proposed solution will be utilizing existing stateôÙ

improved depending on results.
1.2
Research Questions
Given the motivation behind the project, the following research questions were estabôÙ

1. RQ1: Can existing identityôÙpreserving face generation approaches be used for hand

3. RQ3: Can diffusion models combined with promptôÙguided techniques be used for
hand image synthesis?

1.3
Paper Contributions
The main contribution of this paper is the novel identityôÙpreserving Latent Diffusion ModôÙ
els (LDM) optimization utilizing the Angular Margin as a loss function in order to include
identity embeddings in the training process of a latent diffusion model generating synthetic
images of hands. The resulting data is used to train hand recognition models. We verify
the modelãs claimed feature first against the same dataset as used for image generation
and then on a dataset of images unseen during training. The proposed model is compared
in verification and identification tests with a recognition model trained using real images.

2.1
Biometrics
Biometrics refers to the measurement and statistical analysis of peopleãs unique physical
and behavioral characteristics [9]. These characteristics, also known as biometric idenôÙ
tifiers, include fingerprints, facial features, iris patterns, hand geometry, voiceprints, and
even behavioral traits such as gait or typing rhythm. Biometric recognition are unique to
each individual, making them an effective means of verifying identity. Biometric recogniôÙ
tion is used in various domains to enhance security and streamline processes. Some of
the key applications include:
ãÂ Law Enforcement: Biometric identification is widely used in law enforcement for
criminal identification. Fingerprints collected from crime scenes are compared against
databases to identify suspects. Facial recognition technology is also used to scan
surveillance footage and match it against criminal databases.
ãÂ Border Control and Immigration: At airports and border checkpoints, biometric sysôÙ
tems are used to identify travelers. For example, facial recognition systems can
match a travelerãs face against a database of known identities to flag individuals on
watchlists.
ãÂ Access Control: In secure facilities, biometric identification ensures that only auôÙ
thorized individuals gain access. Employees or visitors might be identified through
fingerprint or iris scans to enter restricted areas.
ãÂ Banking and Financial Services: Banks use biometric identification to enhance seôÙ
curity for transactions and account access. Biometric identifiers like fingerprints or
facial recognition can be used to verify the identity of customers at ATMs or during
online banking transactions.
ãÂ Healthcare: Biometric identification ensures accurate patient identification, reducing
errors and improving the quality of care. Hospitals and clinics use biometric systems
to match patients with their medical records, ensuring the right treatment is provided
to the right individual.
As technology continues to advance, the applications of biometrics are expected to exôÙ
pand, offering even greater potential for enhancing security and convenience in various
aspects of daily life.
2.1.1
Identification and Verification
Depending on the application context, biometric systems can operate in verification or
identification mode [10]. Biometric verification is the process where the input probe is
compared to one reference template (i.e., 1:1 comparison) to verify a claimed identity.
In contrast, biometric identification compares the input probe to all references (i.e., 1:N
comparison) in order to find the biometric identifier associated to the probe [10].
Identification is divided into two subôÙcategories:
ãÂ closedôÙset identification ôÙ a task where an unidentified individualãs biometric features
are known to be contained in the dataset and the system attempts to determine their
identity.

2.1.2
Biometric System
A biometric system is a computer program utilizing the existence of human biometric feaôÙ
tures. In case of identification, the process generally involves the following steps (see
Figure 2.1):
1. Enrollment and Capture: A biometric sample is collected from the individual. This
can be a fingerprint, facial image, hand image, iris scan, voice recording, or another
biometric trait.
2. Extraction and Storage: Specific features are extracted from the captured biometric
sample. For instance, in fingerprint recognition, the system identifies unique patôÙ
terns such as ridges and minutiae points. In case of enrollment, the features are
stored in a database.
3. Comparison: The extracted features are compared against a database of stored
biometric templates. In identification mode, the system performs a oneôÙtoôÙmany
comparison to find a match from the database.
4. Matching: If a match is found, the system retrieves the corresponding identity assoôÙ
ciated with the matched biometric template. If no match is found, the system may
indicate that the individual is not recognized.

machine learning solutions for pattern recognition. Such systems or models require
substantial amounts of training data, which facing a growing number of regulations,
increases the costs of acquiring that data and raises the question of the ethicality of
such solutions.
ãÂ Data Security: Biometric data must be securely stored and protected. Breaches can
lead to serious consequences, including identity theft.
ãÂ Accuracy and Bias: Biometric systems must be designed to minimize errors and

2.2
Artificial Intelligence for Image Synthesis
The field of artificial intelligence (AI) has witnessed significant advancements in recent
years, particularly in the domain of image generation. AI algorithms have evolved to create
images that are increasingly realistic and complex, with applications spanning various
industries, including entertainment, art, healthcare, and security. These developments are
largely driven by deep learning techniques, which have revolutionized the way computers
interpret and generate visual content.
One of the foundational methods in AI image generation is the use of neural networks,
specifically convolutional neural networks (CNNs), which excel at processing gridôÙlike data
such as images. CNNs have been integral in advancing image classification, segmenôÙ
tation, and enhancement tasks [12]. However, the generation of entirely new images
required more sophisticated architectures and algorithms.
Generative models have become the cornerstone of AI image generation. These models
learn to understand the distribution of data and generate new instances that resemble the
training data. Among the most prominent generative models are Generative AdversarôÙ
ial Networks (GANs) [13] and a class of algorithms based on diffusion processes called
Diffusion Models (DMs).
Both GANs and diffusion models are to be explored in this thesis, utilizing their strengths,
but also evaluating their drawbacks at the same time.
2.2.1
Generative Adversarial Networks
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), introduced by Goodfellow et al. [13], have
been particularly influential, employing a twoôÙpart system consisting of a generator and
a discriminator that compete in a zeroôÙsum game. The goal of a generative model is to
study a collection of training examples and learn the probability distribution that generated
them [14]. In practice, GANs fall into the category or implicit generative models [15], which
means that it avoids generating the entire probability density function and instead learns
just the sample generation process.
In the process of training GANs, the generator attempts to capture the data distribution in
an image and the discriminator tries to assess the probability that a given sample came
from the training data, rather than the generator. Then, the more mistakes the discrimiôÙ
nator makes in its task (converging to 50% accuracy, which is the equivalent of randomly
guessing), the better the generated distribution and the whole model itself (see Figure
2.2). This adversarial process results in the generation of highly realistic images.
2.2.2

Diffusion Models (DMs) are another implementation of the idea of training inference and
generative probabilistic models against each other [16]. The goal of the training of DMs is
to find a diffusion process that will convert a given image to a known and simple distribution
(simple to express mathematically, e.g. a Gaussian distribution) and then learn to reverse
this diffusion to achieve the distribution of the data and generate an image based on it
(see Figure 2.3). In essence, Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DPM) or Diffusion Models are
parameterised Markov chainôÙbased approaches that are trained by variational inference
to produce samples corresponding to the data after a finite amount of time [17]. The

To achieve that, various loss functions have been developed. Here, we name some of
the most important ones in terms of image synthesis.
Perceptual Loss
Perceptual loss is designed to measure the difference between highôÙlevel feature repreôÙ
sentations of the generated and target images rather than focusing solely on pixelôÙwise
differences [20]. Unlike traditional loss functions such as mean squared error (MSE) or
mean absolute error (MAE), which operate directly on pixel values, perceptual loss leverôÙ
ages preôÙtrained convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to extract feature maps at various
layers. These feature maps capture semantic content and texture information, which are
more aligned with human visual perception. As a result, minimizing perceptual loss often
leads to generated images that are sharper and more visually appealing, at the same time
suppressing semantically meaningless information, which make up for the majority of bits
on a digital image [21].
Adversarial Loss
Adversarial loss is crucial to Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [13], where it is
used to train the generator in a minimax game against a discriminator. The adversarial
loss for the generator is typically defined as the negative log probability of the discrimiôÙ
nator classifying the generated data as real [22]. This loss encourages the generator to
produce samples that are realistic enough to fool the discriminator, leading to highôÙquality,
diverse outputs. The adversarial loss, however, is known for its instability during training
and susceptibility to mode collapse, where the generator produces limited variations of

samples. To address these issues, various techniques such as Wasserstein loss [23] and
gradient penalty [24] have been proposed.
Reconstruction Loss
Reconstruction loss, often used in autoencoders and variational autoencoders (VAEs),
measures the difference between the input data and its reconstruction from the latent
space representation [25]. This loss is typically implemented as MSE or MAE, aiming
to ensure that the encoderôÙdecoder network can effectively capture and reconstruct the
essential features of the input data.
In practice, generative models often benefit from a combination of several loss functions
to achieve the desired balance between different aspects of image quality. For instance,
a combination of adversarial loss, perceptual loss, and reconstruction loss might be emôÙ
ployed in image generation tasks to ensure that the outputs are not only realistic and perôÙ
ceptually convincing but also faithful to the input data. The combination of different loss
functions allows the model to leverage the strengths of each, thereby producing more
robust and highôÙquality generative outputs.

A number of metrics was used to assess the achieved models, as well as to represent

2.3.1
Frûˋchet Inception Distance (FID)
Frûˋchet inception distance (FID) was introduced in [26] and it is a popular metric used
for assessing GAN models. It uses the notion of a Gaussian, which is the maximum
entropy distribution for given mean and covariance. The metric is then expressed by the
difference between two Gaussians: one of real images and one of generated images.
That difference is calculated using the Frûˋchet distance. FID being a distance metric
expressed good quality results as lower values and worse quality results as higher values.
As mentioned in [27], FID metric has some limitations, especially in reflecting some more
complex distortions on images and it fails to capture the gradual improvement of iterative
models. Thus, the authors suggest that human assessment is still the golden standard
for image generation. They performed an empirical verification of their hypothesis, where
the FID scores were calculated for 2 different models and images generated by these two
models were shown to a group of people. It is then shown that the FID score is lower
(better) for the model, that was chosen by only 6.9% of respondents. 92.5% of them
chose the model with higher FID score.
Despite this finding, since it is the recommended metric for StyleGAN3 model and at the
same time the one used as the main source of conclusion in the original paper, it is used

3.1
Hand Recognition
The problem of identifying subjects based on images of hands is a topic investigated
far less than facial recognition. It can be related to the fact that humans naturally are
used to recognizing faces, so it is more intuitive to figure out the features that are crucial
for a successful identification. In case of fingerprint recognition, on the other hand, the
features are relatively easy to define and then extract. Hand recognition, however, opens
up a new way of approaching the task of identification and some of the most commonly
used practices are presented in this section.
Afifi in [33] proposes a classifier based on four SVM classifiers for gender recognition and
identification of subjects. The identification process was performed on two categories of
images: images of the dorsal side (the back side) of hand and the palmar hand (also
called volar surface ôÙ the palm side). The authors report that their model provided results
close to the ones achieved in [34]. Both models achieve good results on what they call
unrestricted hand pose environment, however what they mean is that the rotation and the
spread of fingers can vary, but the hand in the image has to be spread flat.
A similar constraint was assumed in [35] where the main purpose was biometric identificaôÙ
tion using mobile devices. By default, the authors realized how different the input images
can be, causing a number of distortions. They utilize this property by extracting hands
from images on different settings, to later overlap the samples creating a result close to
the original hand. Using the shape of the hand they were able to extract 57 different
features analyzed later for identification. The authors also explore the features looking
for robustness. In the results an important issue has been raised, claiming that because
of the lack of publicly available data it is not trivial to measure the effectiveness of the
solution.
Baisa et al. [36] address the issue of identification of criminals based on hand images.
They propose a model of MBAôÙNet involving two attention modules: for spatial information
about the pixels and for image channels, that process the input in separate branches,
and a third branch without attention. These three branches follow the common backbone
network, for which a ResNet50 was used. The data used for training and experiments,
the authors stress out the fact that they used two independent datasets: [33] and [37].
The datasets include images of right dorsal, left dorsal, right palmar and left palmar, and
cover a wide range of skin tones. The results show a superior performance in comparison
to other reported stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart models: RGAôÙNet [38] and ABDôÙNet [39].

In the context of image synthesis, diffusion models are commonly used to generate highôÙ
quality images by starting with a simple noise signal and gradually refining it to generate
realistic images. By controlling the diffusion process, the model can produce images with
desired characteristics, such as specific content or style. As they base on likelihood,
diffusion models do not suffer from training instabilities as GANs. However, they require
significant resources for training and evaluation which makes them more difficult to apply
and develop in more realistic environments.
This issue is addressed in [21], where the authors propose a Latent Diffusion Model (LDM)
that provides high quality reconstructions without requiring too much regularization of the
latent space and at the same time, maintaining the desired quality of the resulting image.
Latent diffusion models are inspired by the thermodynamic concept of nonôÙequilibrium [60]
where given a volume of atoms at a given time T0 with nonôÙzero energy content will change
to a state at T1 with a certain probability P. The model uses only pixelôÙspace loss functions
to ensure local realism and avoid blurred areas. This novel technique, which sequentially
integrates denoising autoencoders with probabilistic denoising diffusion models (DDPM),
greatly reduces the computational complexity of diffusion models, while offering higher
performance in image synthesis than the previous GANôÙbased methods [21]. The results
show a very wide range of tasks where the model is superior or close to stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart
solutions, without having a taskôÙspecific architecture.

the authors used Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) and Contrastive

ofôÙtheôÙart models, showing VCTãs superiority in both categories (the lowest LPIPS and
the highest CLIP score). In the context of the goal of this project, VCT could potentially

Resources Used
The purpose of this section is to narrow down to the methods and datasets that were
introduced so far and describe some properties of them, that are going to be helpful for
better understanding of the experiments.
4.2.1
Generative Models
Two models were initially employed for image generation: StyleGAN3 (StyleGAN) and LaôÙ
tent Diffusion Model (LDM). The purpose of that was to enable comparisons and broaden
the number of possible adjustments to achieve satisfying results. These two models utiôÙ
lize different architectures and are reported to have varying strengths. Since it is not the
realism that is the leading purpose of this project, but the identity preservation, it was not
trivial to conclude which model will perform better, although achieving full realism would
of course ensure the identity preservation. It was possible, however, that a certain model,
despite scoring lower on any other measurement, would be able to represent a subjectãs
identity better than the other model.
On top of that, one novel method is proposed: LDM with Angular Margin loss. This model
is described in detail in section 5. Additionally, one model was trained using real images,
to serve as a baseline. All models were trained using images of size 256x256 pixels
with batch size 256. However, for each setup, 3 configurations regarding the number of
features in the network were explored: 128, 256 and 512.
4.2.2
Data
The dataset selected for the project is HaGRID dataset. Its extensive labeling containing
both the identity of subjects and the hand gesture enables a thorough analysis of identity
preserving features of the generative models.
This dataset contains images submitted online by people, who were asked to make a
specific gesture. Since each individual had the freedom of choice in terms of the environôÙ
ment and camera specifications while taking the pictures, the dataset is relatively diverse
considering image quality, lighting, distance from camera, background and location of the
hand on an image. The last element was then impacted by the creators of the dataset,
who provided bounding boxes of hands to make research easier without introducing too
many simplifications. The individuals could also choose a subset of the gestures that they
wished to submit, which is the reason for an uneven distribution of samples per gesture.
Originally, the dataset contained 554,800 images collected from 37,583 subjects. These
images were then analyzed and filtered by quality score, as described in Section 4.1.1,
using only right hand images, leaving 82,452 images and 11,890 subjects. These images
were then used as high quality training data for StyleGAN3. In experiments involving LDM
it was possible to control the image quality using a conditional mechanism incorporated
into the model.

Discussion
7.1
Image Generation
The generated images provide an interesting insight into what corresponds to oneãs idenôÙ
tity on a hand image. Since none of the models used in the experiments had a define
area of focus in preserving the identity, nor did they know which feature the recognition
system is going to prioritize in its assessments, they could naturally develop their own
way to express the identity. Intuitively, it is some fineôÙgrained detail on a hand, or even
a fingerprint, that would allow for a successful identification, so the pursue for relatively
high quality of images in terms of resolution seems like the obvious solution. However
there are features that are far easier to express on a generated images, that require lower
quality and are easier to notice even for humans. Since the images used for training of
the models were taken in a fully unconstrained environment with little to none restrictions
regarding the exact placement of fingers for each of the gestures, a high variety is seen
in that regards even in the generated images. The gestures that utilize this element the
most are the ones that let the fingersã flexibility to be seen. A good example of that are the
images generated by SynHandAM shown in Table 6.3 for ãpalmã and ãthree2ã gestures.
The way people position their hands in imitating one of these two gestures turns out to
be a very distinct characteristic: how much people stretch their hand or how spread apart
certain fingers are. Some would argue, that in a few cases not only the identity can be
verified, but also gender of the given subject (of course based on just the hand, not any
of the facial features contained in the image).
It was noted also, that some of the generated hands include rings on fingers and nails with
polish. This seems to be a natural way to distinct one person from another, especially if
the given person provided just one picture or took all of them ãat the same timeã, meaning
without changing the setup of the images (which can be assumed to be the common
case). This observation inspired the inspection of images generated for a subject that
involve such features. Figure 7.1 shows an example of a set of images generated for one
randomly selected subject. Indeed, almost all of these images include at least one ring (or
something that resembles a ring). It is clear that the model attempted to keep the number
of rings and their placement concise when possible. Almost all of the images include also
nails with polish on them. The nails tend to be a little longer and have a pointy shape.
Interestingly, color of the polish changes across the samples.
It is worth noting also, that the background on all images looks like all these images could
have been taken in the same room, which represents the modelãs attempt to make the
generated samples have as much in common with the original image, as possible.

Conclusions
The optimization of a pure LDM model using Angular Margin loss function to express the
subjectãs identity during training of the model has been proven to successfully improve
the identity preservation in generated images. The results show this modelãs superiority
over SynHand for every gesture by a relatively large margin.
As expected, the new model is outperformed by the generative model trained using imôÙ
ages from HaGRID dataset in all three categories of experiments (verification, closedôÙset
identification and openôÙset identification) using data from the same dataset. That, howôÙ
ever, changes when both models are used in a more realistic scenario, in which the imôÙ
ages that they have to work with were not seen during training. What is important here, is
that not only the images were unseen before, but also they introduce new subjects. This
has been shown to wipe out the advantage that the Real model had over SynHandAM,
arguably proving that it would be even better to use SynHandAM, especially for closedôÙ
set identification task. To the best of our knowledge, that has never been achieved before
and it serves as a proof that synthetic images can be used for identityôÙpreserving deep
learning models successfully.
With that, it is now possible to give answers to this projectãs research questions:
1. RQ1: Can existing identityôÙpreserving face generation approaches be used for hand
image synthesis?
Yes ôÙ LDM and StyleGAN3 were used in this project. LDM has been successfully
utilized to generate hand images that were later used for hand recognition tests
against a model trained using real images. StyleGAN3, however, although being
eventually discarded in this project for practical reasons, has not been proven to
not be eligible for hand image generation and remains as potential topic of future
research.
2. RQ2: Can hand recognition systems be used as guideline for the generation of
identityôÙpreserving hand images?
Yes ôÙ as shown in the experimental part of the paper. In this project, synthetic images
were used to train the hand recognition model, which was then tested against real
data, as it would be used in a realôÙlife scenario, showcasing an example pipeline of
using a hand recognition system as a guideline for synthetic image generation.
3. RQ3: Can diffusion models combined with promptôÙguided techniques be used for
hand image synthesis?
Yes ôÙ the underlying architecture of SynHandAM was a diffusion model and it has
successfully generated identity preserving hand images.
4. RQ4: What are the minimum requirements or preôÙprocessing steps to generate hand
images?
As mentioned in the Section 4.2.2, the original images included more background
around the subjectsã hands and were cropped using the bounding boxes provided
by the authors of the dataset. This simple step ensures more precise training and
makes the model focused on hand images.
Furthermore, the images were filtered by their quality score using the PCRôÙHIQA
metrics at an arbitrarily chosen threshold. It introduced a compromise between the
quality of the images used in training and the number of training samples. It was also

Limitations and Future Work
SynHandAM, although achieving promising results, is restricted in terms of the number
of subjects possible to generate. At most, it can be the same number of subjects as
used in the training process. It is important to underline the fact that the model generates
images of hands in a given gesture, but in current form is not able to generate new identiôÙ
ties. To become a fully robust and to find largeôÙscale real life applications, either an even
bigger dataset in terms of the number of subjects is needed, or a way for the model to
generate artificial identities. Another limitation is mentioned before lack of diversity in ethôÙ
nic background of the subjects included in the dataset, which potentially could influence
the reported performance. The ethnic bias is a more serious concern in face recognition
models, but should not be overlooked in case of hand recognition.
Another aspect requiring more attention would be evaluating StyleGAN3 as the underlyôÙ
ing model of hand image generation. Here rejected, it is possible that it achieves acceptôÙ
able results when given more time for training. For that matter, despite LDMs being the
stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart synthetic data models, it is worth comparing SynHandAM against other
architectures reaching beyond mentioned in this project StyleGAN.
Further research is also viable for different hand recognition approaches. Despite the
attempts to identify the stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart algorithm for this project, it remains possible that
different result would be achieved given a recognition system that considers a different
set of features of hands.
