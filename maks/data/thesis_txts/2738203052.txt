Abstract
Following significant fluctuations in global economic conditions and the need for a sustainable
approach in maritime logistics, the tramp shipping sector faces the challenge of optimising vessel
routing and scheduling. This sector, characterised by its flexible operations through ad-hoc con-
tracts and spot market transactions, necessitates a comprehensive strategy to maximise revenue
while minimising operational costs.
To address this, an in-depth analysis of the current tramp shipping operations has been con-
ducted, focusing on a homogeneous fleet of Panamax vessels operating in a combined contract cargo
and spot market environment. The study proposes the implementation of an advanced Greedy Ran-
domized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP) to plan vessel routes and schedules several months
ahead. This methodology aims to minimise deadheading, optimise fuel consumption, and balance
earnings, thereby contributing to both economic and environmental goals.
The proposed GRASP-based model is designed to integrate and optimise multiple aspects such
as vessel speed, chartering costs, bunkering, and operational logistics. By employing this approach,
the study aims to enhance the efficiency of maritime operations, reduce emissions, and improve
overall financial performance. The model’s versatility allows for application across various shipping
scenarios, offering a unified framework for future maritime research and practical applications in
tramp shipping.

Introduction
In the context of tramp shipping, the Tramp Ship Routing and Scheduling Problem (TSRSP)
arises as a critical focus for research and practical application. At its core, the TSRS problem
involves the intricate task of strategically planning vessel routes and schedules to maximise revenue
while concurrently minimising operational costs. The multifaceted nature of this problem neces-
sitates a comprehensive understanding of various factors spanning from vessel characteristics to
market dynamics.
Unlike liner shipping, which adheres to fixed schedules and routes, tramp shipping operates
with greater flexibility, where vessels engage in ad-hoc contracts and spot market transactions.
This inherent flexibility grants operators a degree of autonomy but introduces challenges in long-
term planning and optimisation.
Tramp shipping further divides into dry bulk or tanker shipping and full shiploads or parcel
cargoes. Full shipload shipping fills the vessel’s capacity with a single cargo, while parcel cargoes
transport smaller quantities for multiple customers simultaneously.
This study focuses on tramp shipping for full shiploads, assuming a homogeneous fleet of
Panamax vessels, which operate within a combined contract cargo and spot market, fulfilling fixed
transportation with precise time windows and ad-hoc requests, respectively. Upon reaching the
destination, vessels are emptied and sometimes cleaned before proceeding to the next assignment.
Ideally, subsequent requests originate from the same port, but vessels need to sail empty to
other destinations for more lucrative opportunities, a process known as deadheading. Repositioning
of vessels may occur due to trade imbalances between ports and regions.
To overcome the challenge of unpredictable demand and revenue in individual ports, planning
is conducted on an aggregated level, dividing the world into up to 34 regions, each treated as a
single destination.
The primary objective of this problem is to maximise revenue while minimising operational
costs, including fuel consumption, vessel chartering costs, crew management, port logistics, and
market fluctuations. This thesis aims to plan several months ahead to minimise deadheading and
optimise earnings, thereby reducing fuel consumption and emissions. Additionally, speed optimisa-
tion is used to find the right balance between earnings and fuel consumption. While there is a fixed
fleet, additional vessels can be chartered if beneficial, with specific agreements outlining availability
and return locations.
Each vessel requires a bunkering plan to ensure sufficient fuel for the journey. The model is
solved using the Greedy Randomised Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP), which iteratively con-
structs solutions by combining greedy heuristics with randomization, followed by a local search to
refine these solutions and enhance overall optimisation.
Operated within a rolling horizon setting, the goal is to establish a plan (longer time horizon
that Alberto’s model) for the next XXXX (12) months, focusing on decisions concerning the initial
leg for each vessel. The model’s efficiency allows for daily updates with updated spot prices and

1.1
Motivation
Reason 1.- The importance of maritime transportation in global trade and the green transition.
On the one hand, maritime transportation serves as the cornerstone of international trade and
the worldwide economy. Sea transport handles over 80% of the global trade volume of goods, a
figure that is even greater for the majority of developing nations [1].
Nonetheless, the maritime industry stands at a critical juncture due to the intricate uncer-
tainties and risks influencing global economic conditions. These factors include weakened global
economic growth, heightened inflation influencing consumer expenditure, disruptions arising from
geopolitical tensions such as the conflict in Ukraine, and the persistent impact of stringent COVID-
19 containment measures on China’s economic and trade dynamics.
Additionally, since early 2022, maritime trade, especially involving dry bulk and tanker ship-
ments, has been affected by the conflict in Ukraine. This conflict has altered shipping routes and
increased the distances travelled for commodities, particularly oil and grain. As a result, there has
been a higher increase in ton-miles (the total distance traveled by cargo) compared to the growth
in the volume of cargo itself in 2022, 2023, and as projected for 2024.
Despite the prevailing uncertainties, the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development
(UNCTAD) anticipates a modest growth of 2.4% in total seaborne trade, marking a favourable
turnaround from the decrease experienced in the preceding year. However, the trajectory beyond
2023 reveals a tempered pace of expansion, with UNCTAD forecasting an average annual growth
rate of 2.1% for maritime trade during the period spanning from 2024 to 2028. In light of this, it
is crucial to closely examine the intricacies of maritime logistics, especially concerning the Tramp
Shipping Routing and Scheduling Problem (TSRSP).
On the other hand, the maritime industry is compelled to navigate towards a sustainable and
greener future. This imperative shift requires that the industry undergoes a profound transfor-
mation, marked by decarbonisation and the adoption of digitalisation. Undoubtedly, the foremost
challenge facing the sector is striking a delicate balance between decarbonisation and sustaining
economic growth.
In this endeavour, ensuring environmental sustainability, meeting regulatory
standards, and fulfilling economic requirements are indispensable for fostering a prosperous, equi-
table, and resilient maritime transport future [1].
To steer the industry towards this sustainable journey, several crucial measures must be em-
braced. Firstly, the avoidance of deadheading stands out as a pivotal strategy. By optimising vessel
routes and cargo loads, the shipping industry can significantly reduce unnecessary fuel consumption
and emissions, thus mitigating its environmental footprint.
Furthermore, the adoption of reduced speed initiatives emerges as another fundamental tactic.

1.1
Motivation
Slowing down vessel speeds not only diminishes emissions but also enhances operational efficiency
and safety. While reducing speed may extend the duration of a voyage, the decrease in fuel con-
sumption results in cost savings. Also, operating vessels at lower speeds can lead to reduced wear
and tear on equipment, potentially lowering maintenance costs and increasing the longevity of the
vessels. Regarding safety, reduced speeds provide more time for navigation and response to hazards,
consequently lowering the risk of accidents and associated costs. This strategic adjustment reflects
a commitment to both environmental responsibility and economic optimisation.
Additionally, efficient utilisation of vessels is a key part in the transition towards a greener
maritime industry. Maximising the capacity and effectiveness of vessels ensures optimal resource
allocation while minimising waste and emissions. By leveraging advanced technologies and data-
driven insights, operators can streamline operations and enhance overall sustainability performance.
Despite uncertainties surrounding future decarbonisation measures, including their impact on
logistics costs and trade, the sector should remain committed to fleet modernisation, renewal of
ageing vessel capacity and adopting low-carbon pathways. Amidst regulatory, commercial and sus-
tainability pressures, meeting carbon emission targets is a formidable yet positive challenge.
By thoroughly analysing the aforementioned challenges and their impact on vessel routing and
scheduling, this thesis aims to uncover valuable insights and methodologies essential for managing
the complexities of modern maritime operations. Through careful study and innovative approaches,
the thesis seeks to not only address immediate challenges but also pave the way for resilient, effi-
cient, and environmentally sustainable tramp shipping operations. In doing so, it can help reduce
transportation costs and promote environmentally friendly practices by transporting goods with
fewer emissions.
Reason 2.- Importance of a more complete and cohesive model.
Most maritime research has been driven by concrete applications, resulting in a high number
of different models tailored to address specific constraints within the studied context [2].
Both exact (Column generation and Branch-and-price) and heuristic solution methods (Local
search, Tabu search, Large neighbourhood search, sampling heuristics, Adaptive Large Neighbour-
hood Search, and Heuristic branch-and-price) have been used so far for solving the basic version of
the TSRS problem. Moreover, these methods have also been applied to extensions of the problem,
leading to an increase in the level of detail and depth of the content.
However, in 2022, the first cohesive model able to handle speed optimisation, chartering costs,
bunker planning, and hull cleaning was formulated in [2]. The model employs dynamic column
generation, where each column denotes a potential vessel route, while the master problem manages
the mandatory constraints. The pricing problem is effectively tackled through a time-space graph,
enabling rapid resolution of substantial TSRSP instances.
Therefore, the objective of this project is to provide an efficient heuristic solution method to
solve this model that contains all common constraints.
Given the effectiveness of metaheuristic methods in successfully addressing diverse transporta-
tion and scheduling challenges, coupled with the discernible advantages inherent to the GRASP
metaheuristic, this study is directed towards investigating its potential to yield high-quality solu-

1.2
Main contributions
This thesis introduces several novel modelling aspects and solution techniques:
• The presented tramp ship routing and scheduling model integrates both contract and spot
cargo considerations, alongside optimisation for vessel speed, chartering costs, bunkering and
bunker-contracts. This renders it one of the most comprehensive model in the current litera-
ture.
• Unlike conventional approaches, routes for chartered vessels are directly constructed in our
model, eliminating the need for penalties associated with vessel chartering.
• An efficient solution methodology is presented. To find initial feasible routes for a vessel, a
greedy heuristic algorithm is formulated. This heuristic inserts ports as long as it is prof-
itable and reduces the possible "states"/ports to visit at a specific time (time-space graph).
Afterwards, the GRASP is applied to the previously constructed greedy heuristic with the
objective of comparing the results of both methods and assessing the benefits of probabilistic
decisions.
• Bunkering operations are discretised. As a result, the pricing problem is solvable in pseudo-
polynomial time. Regarding the bunkering strategy adopted, the bunker level is not considered
as a resource in the PP but is considered after the SPPRC is done and the route of the vessel
is constructed. This strategy is a more naive bunkering strategy, which heuristically modifies
the dynamic column generation approach.
• Results from real-life instances demonstrate the model’s capability to identify highly promising
routes, thereby maximising earnings. Additionally, the solution method consistently produces
high-quality results within reasonable timeframes, even for large-scale instances.
• Adding to the previous point, the model is capable of generating more diverse routes than the
dynamic programming method used in column generation.
By contributing these advancements, the model not only consolidates previous research but
also lays the groundwork for future extensions, particularly those aimed at addressing forthcoming
emission constraints. Moreover, this study is to be evaluated against the benchmark provided by
the project [2], which represents a highly significant contribution to the field.

2.1.1
Basic version of the TSRSP
In the simple version of the tramp ship routing and scheduling problem, a ship operator oversees
a heterogeneous fleet designated for cargo transportation within a defined planning horizon, which
typically spans from a few weeks in short-sea shipping to a couple of months in deep-sea shipping
scenarios, during which the fleet remains fixed. Each vessel possesses distinct characteristics, in-
cluding the capacity for carrying cargo, sailing speed, and fuel consumption. Given the continuous
nature of the planning operations, each ship originates from a designated point—be it a port or a lo-
cation at sea—and becomes available for new transport tasks at the onset of the planning horizon [3].
A predetermined set of cargoes is arranged for transport by the fleet over the planning hori-
zon, comprising mandatory contract cargoes, for which transport agreements are in place, and
optional spot cargoes, which are subject to transport if deemed profitable and within fleet capac-
ity. Regardless, each cargo entails a specified quantity of products to be picked up at a loading
port, transported, and unloaded at a delivery port within specified time windows for loading and
unloading. Moreover, each spot cargo yields a defined revenue upon transportation.
The crux of the TSRSP lies in determining which spot cargoes to transport and planning op-
timal routes for fleet vessels to maximise profit (i.e., the total revenue from all transported cargoes
minus the variable sailing costs (fuel, port, and canal expenses). The solution must ensure the
servicing of all mandatory contract cargoes, adherence to time windows, and compliance with ship

2.1.2
Historical evolution
In the early years, research in tramp ship routing was limited. However, a notable exception is
the pioneering work of Appelgren (1969), who investigated a tramp ship routing problem involving
full shiploads, focusing on the transportation of refrigerated products. This study is recognised as
one of the earliest to utilise a Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition approach, with ship routes generated
through dynamic programming in subproblems. Appelgren’s initial solution method faced challenges
in guaranteeing integer solutions, which were addressed in a subsequent paper (Appelgren, 1971) [3].
In the following two decades, research on cargo routing problems in tramp shipping remained
sparse, primarily focusing on specialised shipping segments. However, a subsequent survey by Chris-

tiansen et al. (2004b) revealed a significant increase in the number of papers concerning industrial
shipping involving full shiploads. Many of these studies, such as those by Kim and Lee (1997),
Bausch et al. (1998), and Sherali et al. (1999), relied on the Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition method
with predefined routes as input. Furthermore, these studies often served as case studies for specific

problems and companies [3].
Over time, the introduction of a collection of datasets accessible to the public inspired the
gradual incorporation of increasingly sophisticated solution techniques, both heuristic and exact.
Examples of such advancements can be found in the works of Brønmo et al. (2007a), Korsvik et al.
(2010), and Yamashita et al. (2019), each proposing distinct heuristics to solve the fundamental
tramp ship routing problem. More recently, Homsi et al. (2020) introduced both an exact branch-
and-price approach and a hybrid genetic search algorithm tailored for the basic version of the tramp
ship routing problem, with full shiploads and partial cargoes. Notably, the former method demon-

al. (2014) within approximately one hour, while the latter produces solutions with an average gap
of only 0.01% in just a few minutes. Consequently, these methodologies now stand as cutting-edge
solutions for addressing this complex problem [3].
2.1.3
Extensions
Brønmo et al. (2007b, 2010) and Korsvik and Fagerholt (2010) studied a common scenario
where cargo quantities are flexible within a ±10% limit, allowing for adjustments to optimise cargo
selection and potentially increase profits by accepting more spot cargoes. The first authors tackled
this problem with a Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition approach with a priori route generation, while
Korsvik and Fagerholt (2010) employed a tabu search algorithm for their solution.
Pioneering the study of load splitting in tramp ship routing problems, Andersson et al. (2011a)
proposed a solution method based on pre-generated ship routes and two alternative path-flow mod-
els. However, limitations in handling large-scale instances led to further advancements. Korsvik et
al. (2011) and Stålhane et al. (2012a) respectively developed a large neighborhood search heuristic
and a branch-and-price method, addressing the scalability issue. These studies consistently demon-
strated the potential of load splitting, highlighting its ability to significantly increase profits by
facilitating the transport of more optional spot cargoes.
Within tramp ship routing, Norstad et al.
(2011a) incorporated speed optimisation with a
local search heuristic named Recursive Smoothing Algorithm (RSA). However, this heuristic’s re-
quirement for consistent fuel consumption across all sailing legs limited its practical application.
Building on the work of Norstad et al. (2011), Wen et al. (2016) integrated load-dependent fuel
consumption into their model, making it more realistic for full-shipload tramp ship routing. With
a branch-and-price algorithm with heuristic column generation, the limitation of a consistent fuel
consumption assumption was addressed.
In previous studies, the assumption prevailed that bunkering, or refuelling, could occur at
any port without explicit planning. However, this assumption may not always hold true. Bunker
prices typically vary significantly across ports, and ship operators often negotiate favourable rates
with select ports. Consequently, integrating bunker optimisation into ship routing can be highly

2.2
Solution Methods for the TSRSP
The pioneering effort by Appelgren to achieve an optimal solution for the tramp ship routing
and scheduling problem (TSRSP) dates back to 1969, as mentioned in Section 2.1.2.
Initially,
solutions relied on exact algorithms, such as Dantzig-Wolf decomposition in Appelgren (1969) and
branch and price in Appelgren (1969) [3].
Most work on TSRSP and its extensions involves problem-specific arc flow formulations, which
are often linearised and theoretically solvable using standard commercial optimisation software for
mixed integer linear programming. An example of this approach can be found in Norstad et al.
(2015). However, this approach proved too time-consuming for real-life-sized instances [4].
Recently, exact methodologies were deployed to tackle the most elaborate and unified model,
formulated as a non-linear mixed integer program with a path formulation in [2]. To address the
challenge of quickly closing the MIP-Gap and managing the large number of variables in the optimal
solution, a dynamic column generation technique based on Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition has been
adopted as the preferred solution approach.
Naturally, the choice of method depends on the complexity of the problem and the require-
ments for solution quality and computation time. Among the broad range of methods present in
the literature, column generation has garnered significant attention for its effectiveness in solving
the TSRSP [4].
Despite these advancements, exact resolution for large-scale instances remains predominantly
unfeasible, given the NP-hard classification of the tramp shipping problem. To overcome the con-
straints and limitations of exact algorithms in practical applications, researchers have developed
numerous heuristic methodologies since the late 1990s. These methods, including tabu and local

2.2.1
Heuristic Solution Approaches
The performance of exact approaches typically deteriorates as problem size increases, making
large problem instances intractable. Consequently, exact methods are usually applied only to small
or simplified problems. To address the limitations of mixed integer linear programming (MILP) for-
mulations and excessive running times of exact procedures, heuristic approaches are often employed.
Heuristic methods aim to construct good solutions quickly and flexibly, although they may
not guarantee optimality or provide a quality measure of the solution’s proximity to the optimal
[4]. These approaches are preferred for their speed and flexibility, especially when incorporating
real-world complexities.
This section reviews heuristic approaches within the field of TSRSPs, divided into heuristic
modifications of column generation approaches and general metaheuristic techniques [4].
2.2.1.1
Heuristic Column Generation Procedures
Heuristic modifications of column generation procedures for TSRSPs are natural considera-
tions, as column generation is one of the best-performing exact methods. These modifications aim
to accelerate the algorithm without sacrificing significant optimality. Examples include discretising
service start times (Fagerholt, 2001) and reducing the number of schedules based on heuristic rules
regarding ship capacity utilisation (Fagerholt and Christiansen, 2000). Stålhane et al. (2014) also
use these modifications by setting constraints on the inventory deliveries and limiting the number
of spot cargoes a ship can transport.
Dynamic column generation itself can be heuristically modified. Brønmo et al. (2010) allow
flexible cargo sizes using a two-stage approach: discretising continuous cargo quantities for dynamic
programming and then optimising continuous load quantities for the best solutions. Vilhelmsen et
al. (2014) integrate bunker planning by discretising continuous bunker purchase variables. Stål-
hane et al. (2012) limit the arcs considered and the length of generated paths in their subproblem
networks for more efficient dynamic column generation.
Heuristics are also crucial for solving the Shortest Path Problem with Resource Constraints
(SPPRC) within the column generation framework. Solving SPPRCs to proven optimality is nec-
essary only in the final pricing step to show that no negative reduced cost paths exist. In early
iterations, it suffices to approximately solve the SPPRC, i.e., to compute any negative reduced-
cost feasible paths. Classical pre-processing techniques eliminate arcs and reduce resource intervals
(Desrochers et al., 1992). Heuristically, this can be done by solving SPPRC on a hierarchy of re-
stricted networks, each containing a limited number of arcs defined by the nearest neighbours of
each node. If no solution is found, the network size is incrementally increased. This approach has
been used in many implementations (Dumas et al., 1991; Savelsbergh and Sol, 1998; Larsen, 1999;
Irnich and Villeneuve, 2003). Another technique involves using less accurate resources to simplify

2.2.1.2
Metaheuristics
Some of the most promising heuristic search techniques are metaheuristics [6].
Metaheuristics encompass a broad spectrum of algorithms designed to efficiently navigate the
solution space without the limitations of large mathematical programming formulations. They are
highly effective for providing high-quality solutions while solving complex combinatorial optimisa-
tion problems encountered in real-world scenarios, including the TSRSP [4] [7].
Prominent examples of metaheuristics include local search algorithms, tabu search, genetic
algorithms, Variable Neighborhood Search (VNS), Large Neighborhood Search (LNS), Adaptive
Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS), among others. These methods iteratively guide the search
towards promising regions and employ strategies to escape local optima [4] [7].
Local search algorithms iteratively explore neighboring solutions, accepting those that improve
the objective function. A notable application within TSRSP is by Brønmo et al. (2007a), who
proposed a multi-start local search heuristic for multiple cargo TSRSP. This approach generates
several initial solutions through a mix of random and deterministic methods, each refined using
quick and extended local search procedures across various neighborhoods.
Tabu search, introduced by Glover (1986), improves local search by prohibiting moves to re-
cently visited solutions and allowing non-improving moves to escape local optima.
In TSRSP,
Korsvik et al. (2010) applied tabu search to the multiple cargo problem, achieving better perfor-
mance than multi-start local search heuristics, particularly for large, constrained problems. Their
method was later adapted by Fagerholt et al. (2011) for project shipping with specific constraints,
and by Korsvik and Fagerholt (2010) for flexible cargo sizes.
Variable Neighborhood Search (VNS), proposed by Mladenović and Hansen (1997), employs
perturbation to explore distant neighbours of the current solution. Malliappi et al. (2011) used
VNS for multiple cargo TSRSP, achieving superior performance compared to other metaheuristics
through a structured shaking and local search process.
Large Neighborhood Search (LNS), introduced by Shaw (1997), explores significantly different
neighboring solutions. Korsvik et al. (2011) applied LNS to ship routing with split loads, using a
destroy-and-repair algorithm combined with local search to generate effective solutions.
Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS), extending LNS, dynamically adjusts neighbor-
hood weights based on performance. Hemmati et al. (2014) and Hemmati et al. (2015) applied
ALNS to TSRSP, demonstrating high-quality results and competitiveness with exact methods.
Finally, genetic algorithms (GAs) mimic biological evolution through selection, crossover, and
mutation of a population of solutions. Lin and Liu (2011) and Kang et al. (2012) applied GAs to
TSRSP, showing their capability to outperform traditional mathematical formulations in specific
scenarios.
Another noteworthy example of this approach is the Greedy Randomised Adaptive Search
Procedure (GRASP), developed by Feo and Resende (1989, 1995). GRASP is a multi-start meta-
heuristic, meaning it iteratively constructs solutions and, then, refines them. Each iteration involves
two key phases: construction and local search. During construction, a greedy randomised adaptive
algorithm is employed to build a candidate solution. If this solution is infeasible, a repair procedure
is applied or a new attempt at achieving feasibility is made. Upon obtaining a feasible solution, its

2.3
Greedy Randomised Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP)
The approach taken by the greedy heuristics involves selecting the optimal insertion available
at each algorithm iteration. Nonetheless, extensive application of metaheuristics has indicated that
consistently opting for the best choices may not always be advantageous. Surprisingly, sub optimal
decisions within the algorithms can sometimes pave the way for accessing better solutions later in
the process [7].
As outlined in Section 2.2.1.2, the GRASP method is an iterative metaheuristic that comprises
two main phases: construction and local search.
In the construction phase, a solution is generated using a greedy randomised adaptive algo-
rithm [7], which consists of 3 parts: the greedy algorithm, the adaptive function and the proba-
bilistic selection [11]. At each iteration of the algorithm, the solution builder creates a candidate
list through a greedy procedure. This list is composed of elements that can be added to the on-
going solution without hindering its feasibility. Each element selected for inclusion is determined
based on a greedy evaluation function. This evaluation forms a restricted candidate list (RCL) of
top-performing elements, from which one element is probabilistically chosen for inclusion. After
an element is incorporated, the adaptive function dynamically updates the candidate list and re-
evaluates the incremental profits, ensuring that the construction phase remains responsive to the
evolving solution context. This adaptive process allows the algorithm to balance greediness and
12
==PAGE:23==
2.3
Greedy Randomised Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP)
randomness, enhancing its ability to explore diverse solution spaces. The previous steps proceed
until no candidate elements remain. If the solution produced is not feasible, a repair procedure is
applied to either correct it or initiate a new feasible solution construction.
Once a feasible solution is constructed, the local search phase then explores its neighbourhood
to identify a local minimum. The algorithm iterates through potential improvements until no fur-
ther enhancements can be found. The best solution found is retained as the final result.
The GRASP method stands out for its straightforward implementation process. It requires
minimal parameter tuning, allowing to concentrate on optimising data structures for faster itera-
tions. Typically, only two parameters need to be set: the number of iterations or time limit, and
the parameter used to limit the size of the RCL during the greedy randomised construction phase.
Despite its simplicity, GRASP proves highly effective, often yielding optimal solutions for diverse
problems [7].

• Sequential Insert Method: This methods employs three steps to devise an initial solution

availability times. Conversely, for full shiploads, the routing and scheduling problem simplifies.

and unloading—reducing the problem to a version of the Vehicle Routing Problem with Time
Windows [3].

2.4
Conclusion
The literature review in this thesis highlights the significant challenges and developments in
the tramp ship routing and scheduling problem (TSRSP). This problem is essential within mar-
itime logistics, aiming to optimise vessel routes and schedules to maximise revenue and minimise
operational costs.
Initially, exact methods like column generation and branch-and-price were used but proved com-
putationally intense for large-scale instances. This led to the adoption of heuristic and metaheuristic
approaches, with methods such as GRASP and ALNS showing promise in delivering high-quality
solutions efficiently. The review also notes the trend towards incorporating additional complexi-
ties into TSRSP models, such as speed optimisation, bunkering, and chartering, to better reflect
real-world operations. These advancements underscore the importance of robust algorithms in ad-
dressing TSRSP effectively.
In summary, the literature review establishes a foundation for the research in this thesis, high-
lighting the evolution of solution methods and the need for continued development of efficient
algorithms for the dynamic field of tramp ship routing and scheduling.

3.1
Tramp Ship Routing and Scheduling
This section provides a comprehensive overview of the TSRS problem. Initially, it is outlined
in its basic form to then proceed with extending the model.
In its simplest form, the Tramp Ship Routing and Scheduling Problem involves both a fixed fleet
of vessels and a set of cargoes for transportation. The tramp operator owns all vessels, which will
remain available throughout the planning period. Cargoes are categorised into long-term contracts,
known as Contract of Affreightment (CoA), and spot cargoes, which are optional and subject to
market availability. The primary objective is to optimise profit by determining routes for each vessel
that ensure the transportation of fixed CoA and a selected subset of spot cargoes.
In this scenario, vessels are homogeneous, possessing identical speed, efficiency, and capacity.
Therefore, fixed costs are discarded, and any vessel type can transport all cargoes, with only one
cargo per vessel at a time. Moreover, each vessel is associated with a specific region and availability
time. Cargoes have designated revenue, pickup, and delivery time windows, along with loading and
unloading operation times and costs.
The mathematical model for the basic problem, including the option to charter spot vessels for
individual cargoes to ensure feasibility, is detailed in Christiansen et al. (2007), where chartered
vessels are treated as dummy variables rather than specific routes.
Adding Speed Optimisation
Tramp Ship Routing and Scheduling Problem with Speed Optimisation (TSRSPSO) is considered in
Norstad et al. (2011b). To solve this problem a multi-start local search heuristic is applied and a
continuous speed range between 14.1 and 22 knots is considered. Furthermore, the authors suggest
using a Recursive Smoothing Algorithm (RSA) to address the problem for the entire route, although
this method can only be applied in cases when the fuel consumption function is the same at every
leg. In the context of this thesis, this is not the case as laden and ballast sailing are differentiated.
As a result, speed is a decision variable that has to be selected at leg level.
Adding Bunkering
Vilhelmsen et al. (2014) researches the Tramp Ship Routing and Scheduling Problem with Bunker
Optimization (TSRSPBO). In this study of the problem, vessels speed remains constant but a dis-
tinction between laden and ballast sailing consumption is made. Additionally, the authors take into
account several bunker choices according to the port and time period. To minimise the problem
magnitude, just one bunker operation is permitted during the planning horizon. Because of the
enormous turnaround in shipping, the authors report a considerable rise in the marginal contri-
bution, ranging from 0.1% to 0.7%, which they claim is significant in absolute terms. It should
be noted that because the vessels in their context are continually moving slowly, consumption is
likewise quite low.
By including the previously described speed optimisation, the aim is to further improve the
TSRSPBO. Thereby, if fuel prices are extremely high, slower sailing may be the better option;
alternatively, faster sailing may be chosen to carry more cargo.
Adding Chartering of Vessels

Mathematical formulation
Vessel chartering is usually incorporated into TSRSP models by adding dummy variables. In this
manner, there is no significant increase in the problem magnitude. On the other hand, since no
routes are made for chartered vessels, it is not possible to find solutions that have the same spot
ship transporting multiple cargoes during the course of the planning horizon.
Additionally, neither speed optimisation nor changes in chartering rates are not feasible for
vessels that are chartered. In fact, it can be beneficial to charter a vessel and sail it at high speed to
enhance earnings, depending on the costs associated with bunkering and chartering; however, the
opposite might also occur. Lastly, there is no reason to neglect the transportation of spot cargoes
when planning routes for spot vessels.
Adding Bunker Purchasing with Contracts
According to Plum et al. (2014), contracts are frequently used to bunker a predetermined quantity
of fuel in order to obtain a discount and improve supply certainty. Within their context, which
takes liner shipping into account, the routes and schedule are established in advance. Because of
this, only the bunker plan is updated every day to reflect changes in bunker pricing. In contrast, in
bulk shipping, as routes are not established beforehand, decisions regarding which cargoes to move
and bunker operations must be made at the same time.
3.2
Mathematical formulation
Mathematical formulations for the TSRSP in its basic form are available in Christiansen et al.
(2007), focusing solely on speed optimisation in Norstad et al. (2011b), finding bunker optimisation
in Vilhelmsen et al. (2014), and integrating bunker contracts in Plum et al. (2014). In this section,
an arc flow non-linear programme formulation is introduced in order to comprehensively incorporate
all aforementioned extensions, including spot charter vessel routes.
The time-space multigraph G represents a day’s sampling resolution, where multiple arcs can
connect the same pair of nodes. This occurs when moving between regions with and without cargo,
with the former having higher consumption and, in theory, higher profit as well.
Each arc is linked with resources such as profit and bunker consumption. Speed optimisation
involves adding multiple arcs from the same time-space node to the same region but at different
arrival times. The most efficient speed within the range arriving on the same day is prioritised. In
addition, this speed optimisation also allows for numerous arcs to represent the same cargo.
Refueling quantities are determined during the optimisation.
Similar to Vilhelmsen et al.
(2014), the bunker levels are discretised during the solution process, enabling refuelling at discrete
levels L higher than the current value. However, formulation (1) assumes continuous bunker levels.
The discretisation process and its benefits are discussed in Section 4.2.
For the chartered vessels, a new multigraph takes into account the daily chartering cost for a
charter contract starting at place i and at time j.
As noted by Vilhelmsen et al. (2014), setting proper boundary conditions for bunker levels is
crucial to avoid empty tanks at the end of the planning period. The authors’ approach consists in
buying (or selling) bunkers that are deficient (or excessive) at the average price by the end of the
planning period. This transaction occurs on virtual arcs extending from the planning horizon’s end
to a virtual end node. For chartered vessels, a virtual arc from the cargo transportation’s end node
to the virtual node is included, symbolising the charter out operation post-cargo transportation.
Additionally, vessels concluding in potentially profitable regions after the planning horizon receive
a premium, while those ending in unprofitable regions incur a penalty. These premium and penalty

4
Analysis
As previously mentioned in Section 1.1, the first unified model, as detailed in [2], effectively
handles all common constraints in tramp shipping, including speed optimisation, bunkering, charter
costs, and hull cleaning using exact methods.
Dynamic Column Generation based on Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition has been shown to provide
optimal solutions for the linear relaxation of the Restricted Master Problem (RMP) and near-
optimal solutions for the RMP itself, as demonstrated in [2]. The experiments indicated that nearly
optimal solutions with bunker contracts (MIP-Gap below 1%) were achievable across all instances
considered. Therefore, this unified model consolidates previous research and serves as a benchmark.
Fast solution times were achieved with columns corresponding to possible vessel paths and the
pricing problem (PP) was solved in polynomial time using a time-space graph. The results show
the importance of rich planning models for future bulk shipping industry efficiency and market
information exploitation.
However, solving large-scale instances with exact methods remains unfeasible due to the NP-
hard nature of the tramp shipping problem. Heuristics and metaheuristics have shown their capa-
bility to produce high-quality solutions within reasonable times for large instances. Consequently,
this thesis focuses on these methods to solve the problem. Initially, greedy heuristics are employed
to construct a feasible solution, followed by the application of metaheuristics to further improve
this solution.
The primary objective of this study is to find the best possible solution to the extended TSRSP
within a reasonable timeframe and with a higher degree of diversity in the vessel routes. In addition,
identifying sources of complexity and analysing the feasibility of solving the problem with heuristics
techniques are two goals to achieve. The results are compared to those provided by the model in
[2].
4.1
Network Description
The generic network for long-term vessels and the network for chartered vessels are described
in this section. The model is based on directed acyclic multigraphs, where all vessels owned by the
tramp ship operator are assumed to lie on the same multigraph. In this scenario, an average vessel
is considered, allowing for the utilisation of the same graph.
The multigraph represents a time-space graph, with each node representing a region at a specific
time. This choice ensures the multigraph is acyclic, albeit at the expense of a significantly larger
number of nodes and arcs. This increase in complexity is justified by the avoidance of cycles, which
would render the RCSPP NP-hard in the strong sense.
Edges in the graph correspond to various operations, including the following:
• Cargo movement. The consumption accounts for the increase brought on by the cargo itself
and the loading and unloading times.
• Waiting at port, which is always a one-day operation, and only port costs are considered.
• Moving, which is the movement of a vessel between regions. Feasible movement is calculated
beforehand when the multigraph is developed using the maximum and minimum speeds.
Consumption is calculated in relation to speed.

• Bunkering, which is also a fixed one-day operation. The vessel fuel tank is full-filled up to
the threshold level (greater than the current level and refuelling at least a significant amount),
given the cost of refuelling on that specific day.
• Ending operation, where a region-dependent premium or penalty is assigned and the bonus
bunker is aligned with the initial level at an average bunker cost.

regions without a cargo and - arcs illustrate ending operations.
Different speeds and bunker consumption are provided implicitly by the starting and arriving
nodes of an arc in the time-space graph. The possibility of bunkering between a pickup and delivery
operation is disregarded due to the variability in the traversed regions during different legs of the
route. For a known leg and set of regions, the time and bunker consumption for the deviation are
computed during the multigraph construction, facilitating the addition of cargo transport arcs with
bunker options.
For chartered vessels, all arcs include an additional cost corresponding to the duration of the arc
and the chartering cost of the vessel. Additionally, chartering costs are computed in advance, and
the graph is augmented with further ending arcs after all cargo transportation, allowing chartered
vessels the possibility of ending their charter period before the end of the planning horizon.
4.2
First stage: building a basis for the solution
In this section, an SPPRC/labelling algorithm was implemented to compute a feasible initial
solution, a route, for each long-term vessel owned by the tramp operator.
This algorithm has the aim of maximising the overall profit while keeping the problem feasible
w.r.t. the amount of fuel. Thus, the resources are the profit and the bunker amount. In this case,
only the profit was maximised while bunker level is just forbidding some operations (except for
bunker reselling at the end of the planning horizon).
Notice that bunker level can always change values and be increased by decreasing the profit
by a certain amount and spending some time. This means that at every node, where bunker is
available, a certain amount of non-dominated labels can be generated.

All 2L labels (if present) are extended with all feasible arcs a from node n. If the resulting

node. As |N| is the number of nodes and E[A] the average number of arcs from a node, then the
overall computational cost is O(2L|N|E[A]). The required memory space to store both matrices is

An empty Initial Label is created and initialised as well as the Initial Profit, set to −∞. They
are inserted into the dominance and label matrices respectively. The algorithm first iterates through
every node in the multigraph for the owned vessels. Then, for every node of the graph, it iterates
through every bunker level, and ultimately, through each arc.
As it was mentioned before, the algorithm generates new labels based on the type of arc,
meaning the type of operation the vessel performs:
• If the arc corresponds to operations of cargo movement, waiting at port or moving be-
tween regions, then a new label is created with several updated attributes based on the current
arc.
The parent field is the previous label from the node where this arc originates. The updated
profit is calculated as the sum of the parent’s profit and the current arc’s profit, which has
the bunker cost already integrated into the profit. The bunker update involves setting the
remaining bunker fuel with the necessary bunker to go through this arc. The destination node
is the node to which the arc leads. Then, the label includes the arc index, which refers to
the index of the current arc. The discrete bunker level is calculated using the formula from
the function D2, which determines if there is enough fuel for the next bunkering. Ultimately,
to update the last cargo, the arc must be a cargo movement operation; otherwise, it remains
unchanged.
It should also be noted that, if a cargo movement procedure is performed, the algorithm
ensures that a cargo is not transported more than one time by the same vessel and checks

4.4
Greedy Randomised Adaptive Search Procedures (GRASP)
In the previously described greedy heuristic, the followed strategy entails selecting the most
profitable insertion at each iteration and, when the routes are formed, checking if there is need
of refuelling if the bunker level at the end of such routes is below 20% of the bunker level at the
beginning of the route (maximum amount available).
However, extensive use of metaheuristics has revealed that optimal choices at every step may
not always yield the best overall solutions. In fact, suboptimal decisions within algorithms can
potentially lead to improved outcomes later on. The Greedy Randomised Adaptive Search Procedure
(GRASP) offers a structured approach for building feasible solutions by incorporating both greedy
and probabilistic elements.
The GRASP framework is implemented in the customised heuristic detailed in Section 4.3, while
Section 4.4.1 outlines the general GRASP framework. Subsequent sections detail its application to
the greedy heuristic, algorithm specifics, parameter tuning, and a brief conclusion.
4.4.1
The GRASP Framework
Algorithm 8 provides the pseudo-code for the GRASP framework. As detailed in Section 2.3,
this method operates in two stages during each iteration: generating a solution and conducting a
local search to improve this solution. The algorithm runs for a predetermined time duration or a
specific number of iterations, and the best solution obtained throughout these iterations is returned.
A key feature of GRASP is the combination of both greedy and probabilistic decisions within
the solution builder function (i.e., GRASP_vessels_paths in Algorithm 8). During each step of the
solution builder, a candidate list is initially constructed with a greedy procedure, followed by the
random selection of a specific number of candidates to continue with the labelling algorithm. This
process iterates until the generated solution is finished.

4.4.2.1
Greedy Randomised Constructor
The pseudo-code for the greedy randomised constructor is available in the following algorithms:
GRASP_vessels_paths, Algorithm 9, Algorithm 10 and the function process_arc. It shares identi-
cal characteristics with the greedy procedure outlined in Section 4.3. In this context, the restricted
candidate list is defined as the collection of the top K potential insertions at each stage of the
solution construction. In simpler terms, rather than focusing solely on the single best insertion, the
RCL stores the K most profitable insertions (i.e., for each node, the K arcs whose insertions are the
most profitable over all remaining arcs). Subsequently, a predetermined number of arcs, denoted
as num_arcs, are randomly selected from this set of K candidates for the following iteration of the
algorithm.
There are several strategies that can be used to create the RCL in GRASP [7]. The decision
to choose the K-element candidate list option, also known as cardinality-based strategy, for this
application seems reasonable based on the following considerations:
• The degree of complexity of the implementation is an important factor. Choosing a simpler
approach, like selecting a fixed number of K best candidates, can make the implementation
more straightforward compared to more complex strategies [7] [11].
• When the range of possible objective values is not known in advance, a fixed K-item candidate
list provides a consistent way to control the size of the restricted candidate list at each iteration.
More advanced strategies like selecting elements within a certain percentage of the best may
require more parameter tuning [7] [11].
• The specific value of K can be tuned experimentally to balance the trade-off between greediness
and randomness. A larger K will make the selection more random, while a smaller K will be
more greedy [7] [11].
Randomly selecting from a restricted candidate list of the K best elements is a common and
effective technique used in GRASP. It allows for some greedy selection of high-quality elements
while still introducing randomness to explore different solutions [7] [11].

• It then sets up an optimisation model with the computed routes from the previous algorithm.
• The model is then optimised.
A maximum number of iterations, N_max is set, and the function enters a while loop that

• It calls GRASP_vessels_paths algorithm to generate new routes using the GRASP method,

• Then, these new routes are added to the optimisation model.
• For the last step, the Algorithm 11 re-optimises the model with each new set of routes to find
the best possible solution within the time limit.
Lastly, the function returns the optimised model instance.

5.1
Data analysis
In this section, the performance of the algorithm was studied. The code has been developed
in the Julia programming language v1.10.3 using JuMP v1.22.0 (Lubin et al.(2023)) and HiGHS
solver v1.9.0 (Huangfu and Hall (2018)). The experiments were conducted for the most part on
Windows 11 using a processor Intel(R) Core(TM) i5-1035G1 CPU @ 1.00GHz with a maximum
turbo frequency of 3.60GHz, featuring 4 cores and 8 threads. The bigger instances were run on
DTU servers due to the need for more RAM capacity.
5.2
Instance description
The algorithm is evaluated using historical data from [2]. Instances are derived from historical
voyages spanning from January 2021 to December 2023, each covering a four-month period, with a
half-month overlap. This results in two instances per month and 24 instances for each year. Each
instance includes a varying number of owned vessels, averaging 19 per four-month period, including
long-term charter vessels. Each vessel has its own starting point and time. Vessel speeds range from
12-14.5 knots unloaded and 11-13.5 knots loaded, in 0.5-knot increments. These sailing speeds have
their corresponding fuel consumption, which is provided by the operator.
The world market is split into non-overlapping economically significant regions, varying from 7
to 34. Daily chartering costs use the Baltic Panamax Index (BPI) relative to the region where the
vessel is chartered, with penalties for ending in non-profitable regions. The penalty is computed as
the average product of the difference between the BPI in profitable regions and the ending region,
and the travel time required at maximum speed. Consequently, routes terminating in non-profitable
regions factor in the additional cost needed to transition to a profitable region.
Cargoes, drawn from historical data, range from 27 to 77 per four-month period, with an
average of around 60. These cargoes are predominantly spot cargoes. Some cargoes involve up to
three pick-up and three delivery locations. These locations are specified in advance by contracts,
which explicitly detail the operation sites. This scenario is particularly common in the Panamax
segment.
Bunker costs have been sourced from major ports within each region, with each region rep-
resented by its most significant bunker port. Data availability was limited to specific dates, thus
linear interpolation was needed to estimate bunker values between these dates. Additionally, as
the number of regions increases, some regions lack historical price data due to infrequent refueling.
Consequently, the resulting multigraph may have regions without available refueling options.
Bunker contracts remain constant throughout the planning horizon but may vary in terms of
lower and upper bounds and discounts across regions. Penalties for breaching these bounds are
calculated as twice the average bunker price in the respective region. If a region lacks a spot bunker
option, the penalty is zero, allowing lower bound violations without incurring a loss.
Each vessel has a fixed tank capacity of 2500MT, with a minimum feasible fuel level of 125MT
(5% of the tank) and a minimum bunkering amount set at 100MT.

Given that the algorithm is intended to function as a greedy heuristic, it is essential to limit the
consideration of arcs to ensure computational efficiency. In the labelling algorithm from the study
by [2], each node typically has an average of 65 arcs. For this reason, higher values of K (e.g., 55
to 65) were also tested, but the objective value was worse and computational efficiency decreased.
To maintain a balance between computational feasibility and solution quality, the parameter
tuning was performed with K values ranging from 20 to 50, in increments of 5. This range ensures
a comprehensive evaluation while aligning with the principles of the greedy heuristic approach.

7.4
Discussion of results
This section synthesises the key findings and insights derived from the analyses conducted in
the previous sections, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the main results obtained in this
thesis. Furthermore, it discusses the extensive use of the methods developed, identifies possible
limitations, and offers suggestions for overcoming them.
The results presented highlight the effectiveness of the GRASP algorithm in comparison to
the Greedy and exact methods. The comparative analysis showed that GRASP consistently out-
performed the Greedy method, though it did not fully match the exact method in terms of profit,
indicating a balance between solution quality and computational efficiency. The experiments run
revealed that for certain instances, both Greedy and GRASP produced consistent profits across
iterations, suggesting that the initial GRASP solutions were robust, leaving minimal scope for
improvement during the local search phase. However, other instances exhibited more variability,
allowing for different outcomes across iterations.
The algorithm’s performance with larger instances demonstrated its scalability and robustness.
GRASP maintained its effectiveness even as the problem size and complexity increased, handling
extended planning horizons and a larger number of regions efficiently. However, the higher utilisa-
tion of chartered vessels in the GRASP method, which typically cover shorter routes, could explain
its lower profit margins compared to the exact method. While this may limit profit maximisation,
it provides greater flexibility in fleet management, enabling the system to adapt more readily to
fluctuating demands.
Additionally, the bunkering strategy employed in GRASP, which refuels vessels when fuel levels
drop below 20% at the end of a route, is relatively naive. This simpler approach contrasts with
the exact method, which treats bunkering as a resource within the labelling algorithm used in the
Column Generation’s Pricing problem. This disparity in bunkering strategies likely contributes to
the profit differences observed.
Increasing the number of iterations generally improved solution quality, although some in-
stances reached a local optimum with constant profits. CPU time increased as iterations grew,
reflecting the higher computational effort required. The number of regions visited remained fairly
constant despite this addition of iterations.
Overall, GRASP demonstrated a balanced performance, with notable improvements over Greedy
but remaining less effective than the exact method in profit maximisation. GRASP’s computational
efficiency surpassed the exact method, though it remained more intensive compared to Greedy.
Furthermore, GRASP’s robustness and flexibility in real-world applications were highlighted by its
capacity to investigate a wider range of solutions with higher route diversity.
Extensive use of the method and limitations
55
==PAGE:66==
7.4
Discussion of results
The extensive use of the GRASP method in this thesis underscores its applicability in solving
complex routing and scheduling problems in tramp shipping. However, certain limitations of the
method were identified during the analysis.
One notable limitation is the GRASP method’s solution quality, particularly when compared to
the exact method. This can be addressed by improving the GRASP algorithm, such as by limiting
the Restricted Candidate List (RCL) by quality (value-based) instead of by the number of elements
(cardinality-based). Using a threshold parameter to form the RCL can enhance the algorithm’s
effectiveness. In addition, there are strategies like Reactive GRASP, which vary the RCL threshold,
and may also lead to better and more diverse solutions by providing greater diversification and
reducing reliance on parameter tuning.
Moreover, hybridisations of GRASP with other metaheuristics, such as ALNS, could further
improve performance. Incorporating principles from these metaheuristics can lead to efficient hy-
bridisations and result in superior algorithms for specific problems.
In conclusion, while the GRASP method shows significant promise and applicability, ongoing
improvements and hybridisation with other techniques are essential to fully leverage its potential
and overcome its limitations.

Conclusion
The primary motivation for this thesis was to address the complex and dynamic Tramp Ship
Routing and Scheduling Problem (TSRSP), which presents significant operational challenges for
maritime logistics. The goal was to develop and evaluate advanced heuristic methods that could
provide practical, efficient, and high-quality solutions for the scheduling and routing of a homoge-
neous fleet of Panamax vessels, balancing contract cargo and spot market engagements.
The problem has been tackled over the years using exact optimisation methods, which re-
vealed significant computational challenges, especially with large-scale instances. Consequently, the
study pivoted towards heuristic and metaheuristic methods, with the Greedy Randomised Adaptive
Search Procedure (GRASP) emerging as a particularly effective strategy. GRASP demonstrated a
notable balance between solution quality and computational efficiency, consistently outperforming
the Greedy method and demonstrating its capability to generate more diverse and robust solutions.
Despite this, it did not fully match the profit maximisation capabilities of the exact method, indi-
cating potential areas for enhancement.
The investigation into the impact of increased iterations on algorithm performance showed
general improvements in solution quality. However, some instances displayed a plateau in benefits,
suggesting convergence to local optima. GRASP’s strategic use of owned and chartered vessels was
also examined, revealing that while the higher reliance on chartered vessels provided operational
flexibility, it also contributed to lower profit margins compared to the exact method. Additionally,
the simpler bunkering strategy employed in GRASP, compared to the exact method’s resource-based
approach, highlighted further areas for refinement.
Key insights were gained from additional tests and analyses, particularly regarding the high
frequency of repeated candidate lists and the algorithm’s convergence to local optima. These find-
ings confirmed the robustness of GRASP’s initial solutions but also indicated the need for strategies
to diversify solution paths further.
In conclusion, the methods developed and tested in this thesis represent significant advance-
ments in solving the TSRSP, offering an effective and adaptable framework for various shipping
scenarios. However, there remain several areas for improvement and future research:
• Adopt an Improved Bunkering Strategy: Enhancing the bunkering strategy to move beyond
the simpler refueling rule currently used could lead to better overall efficiency and cost savings.
• Implement Penalties for Route Repetition: Introducing penalties for visiting the same routes
repeatedly could ensure higher route diversity. This diversity would provide planners with
different routing options, aiding in managing congestion at ports.
• Enhance the GRASP Method: Incorporating new strategies into the GRASP method, such as
a value-based approach to form the Restricted Candidate List (RCL), employing a Reactive
GRASP strategy, and experimenting with other metaheuristics like ALNS, tabu search or
local search, could further improve the solutions.
These enhancements and future research directions promise to build on the solid foundation
laid by this thesis, driving further advancements in maritime logistics and operational efficiency in
tramp shipping.
