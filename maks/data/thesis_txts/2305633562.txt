Abstract
Planning a transportation system such as railway transportation requires a
number of steps, line planning, time tabling, rolling stock scheduling and crew
scheduling. This thesis is a study of line planning, the generation of line con-
cepts that can satisfy the demand ﬂowing through a network.
This paper approaches the topic with dual objectives, minimizing both opera-
tional cost and the total passenger time. While this might sound contradictory,
the results are achieved by solving for the optimal operational cost and then
evaluating the solution by solving a multi-commodity ﬂow problem based on
the solution.
Based on network data provided by DSB, we generate several line pools using
an algorithm implemented in C++. These line pools are used as input in two
models which are solved using a CPLEX solver.
The results obtained in this thesis, are a number of solutions that satisfy the
demand for intercity trains in the Danish railway network. They are not as
expected going into the project, as they diﬀer from the theoretical values we
had anticipated.

In the introduction chapter we break down our thesis by ﬁrst explaining our mo-
tivation, then by describing our problem formulation. We then give an overview
of the thesis content by describing the thesis framework. Finally, a literature
review section is given, in which a short description of the existing research

1.1
Motivation
Annually, there are more than 170 million passengers that use railway trans-
portation in Denmark ([BaneDanmark, 2016]).
Every day, more than 3, 000
passenger trains and freight trains drive on the state’s railway, this is about
40, 000 arrivals/departures at the stations ([BaneDanmark, 2016]). In order to
relate costs, revenue, passenger quality, administration and operation, it is im-
portant to have a suitable plan for the public rail transportation system (more
on the planning process in section (1.4)).
One of the fundamental steps in transportation system planning, is the develop-
ment of a line plan. The primary obstacle in line planning is ﬁnding a balance

1.2
Problem formulation
The goal of this thesis is to:
Analyze the literature within line planning, and apply the theory to a real life

To achieve this, we have cooperated with DSB (the main Danish railway trans-

1.4
Planning public transportation systems
The process of planning a public rail transportation system, is usually divided
into ﬁve steps ([Schöbel, 2012]). Figure (1.2) illustrates the planning steps in the
order of occurrence. The steps are often solved separately and then adjusted,
so that the best feasible solution can be found in relation to all the steps.
Crew scheduling
Rolling stock scheduling
Time tabling
Line Planning
Infrastructure
Figure 1.2: The planning steps
First, an infrastructure is designed. The infrastructure represents a public trans-
portation network (PTN), G = (V, E), with stations (V ) and tracks (E).
In the next step, the line plan is designed. It is assumed that a PTN is given
and ﬁxed. In line planning, the goal is to generate railway lines that are possible
to utilize in the PTN. A railway line is a path in the PTN which describes a
return trip between two stations, an origin station and a destination station.
The frequency, which describes how often a line will be operated in the PTN
in a speciﬁc time period, should also be included in the line plan. A line plan
and its frequency are called a line concept ([Schöbel, 2012]). Overall the line
concept should:
• Be feasible to operate in the PTN.
• Be sensible in respect to the passengers traveling in the PTN.
• Have low costs.
A lot of diﬀerent optimization models have been developed to ﬁnd line concepts
that meet the above criteria as well as possible. In the following section (1.5),
literature regarding some of the optimization models will be described.
==PAGE:13==
1.4 Planning public transportation systems
5
The next step in the planning process is to integrate a timetable. For a given line
concept, solving the timetabling problem should determine an arrival time and
a departure time at each track and station. In [Raviv, 2013] and [Schöbel, 2011]
the line plan and the timetable have been considered as one problem.
The
objective of the problem is to establish a set of lines and a time table for the
operation of these lines.
When a feasible line plan and timetable have been found, a rolling stock schedule
and a crew schedule can be determined. [Knut Haase, 2001] gives an approach
to solving the rolling stock and crew scheduling problems.

1.5.1
Cost-oriented models
In [Claessens et al., 1998], a model which minimizes the operating costs, sub-
ject to service constraints and capacity requirements, is developed for the Dutch
railway system. The model optimizes lines, line types, routes and train lengths.
At ﬁrst the model is developed as an Integer Nonlinear Programming Model
and then transformed into an Integer Linear Programming Model. In the paper
an algorithm has been presented which solves the model to optimality. The al-
gorithm is based on constraint satisfaction and a Branch and Bound procedure
(B&B procedure). The algorithm has three major components, a model refor-
mulation to reduce the problem size, lower bounding to reduce the size of the
branch and bound tree, and sub-problem selection to obtain a good or optimal
solution quickly. Other than ﬁnding a cost optimal solution, the model can also
be used to determine costs for existing lines, thus helping to ﬁnd the best line
system.
In [Goossens et al., 2004] the authors introduce a model which allows for lines
to have diﬀerent halting patterns. This is done by assigning a type number to
every node such that the larger the stations which are represented as nodes, the
larger the type number. Given these types a line of type 1 will stop at every
station, a line of type 2 will stop at stations with type ≥2 and lines with type
3 will stop at stations with type ≥3, etc. This method creates lines that can
bypass smaller stations, only servicing sizable stations with a deﬁned number of
passengers.
Three models are presented, The multi-commodity ﬂow formulation (MCF),
the integer programming formulation (IPXY ), and another integer programming
formulation (IPX), the diﬀerence between the two models being the rerouting
variable ye which is omitted in the latter in favor of additional constraints in
order to route the commodities.
Building on what [Goossens et al., 2004] determined to be the most promising
of the three models, IPX, [Rezanova, 2015] describes the models utilized in the
development of the line plan created for use in 2016 by the Danish railway
company DSB. The paper uses a cost-oriented integer programming approach
to minimize the cost of the line plan and evaluates the result using a passenger-
oriented network ﬂow model that minimizes the traveling time.
Whilst the original IPX model allows for train capacities, the extended model
also incorporates diﬀerent carriage compositions, meaning it is possible to utilize
several combinations of ﬂeet type. This is necessary in order to achieve optimal
placement of a restricted number of each train type at disposal.

1.5.2
Passenger-oriented models
In [Schöbel, 2012], the three most common objectives in modeling passenger
quality are mentioned; maximizing direct travelers, minimizing the sum of rid-
ing times for all passengers and minimizing the sum of traveling times for all
passengers. Direct travelers are travelers which can travel from their origin to
their destination without changing lines. The riding time is the time a passenger
spends sitting in a train, thus transfer times between lines is not included. The
traveling time is the sum of both riding times and transfer times, meaning all
aspects of time spent are included for each passenger.
In [Bussieck MR, 1996], a mixed-integer linear programming (MIP) formulation
of the line optimization is presented.
The objective function maximizes the
number of direct travelers.
Data from the Dutch railway company and the
German railway company is used to test the model. Due to the huge number
of variables in the MIP model, a branch and bound algorithm fails to ﬁnd an
optimal solution. Therefore a reduced MIP model has been developed in which
LP-relaxation allows for a solution, after which a cutting plane approach is
utilized in order to ﬁnd the integer solution.
In [Schöbel and Scholl, 2006] a passenger-oriented model is presented that al-
lows free routing of all passengers across a given pool of lines. The objective is
to minimize either the number of transfers for each passenger, or the traveling
time in general for each passenger where a constant value is used to symbolize a
waiting penalty during transfer. In order to restrict the model so that a direct
route is not chosen for every OD pair, a budget constraint is in place along with
a cost for every line.
In order to allow free routing, a change & go graph is used to allow travel between
lines. As this graph allows for a large combinatorial problem, [Schöbel and Scholl, 2006]
implements column generation using Danzig-Wolfe decomposition.
1.5.3
Line pool generation
As one of the only articles in the ﬁeld of line pool generation, [Gattermann et al., 2015]
proposes two algorithms that, in conjunction, generate a pool of lines that can
be used by other models to further create a line concept for a line planning
problem. The idea behind the method is to look at the passenger density of
each section of the public transportation network and from this create a num-
ber of minimum spanning trees (MST). From each MST a number of lines can
be generated, after which parameters governing the priority of each edge in the
network are altered before once again creating a MST. After a suﬃcient number

Chapter 2
Modeling
In this section a cost-oriented optimization model will be formulated, the model
will be based on the model from [Goossens et al., 2004] and from [Rezanova, 2015].
First a single-type problem will be formulated, followed by a multi-type problem.
The objective of both optimization models is to minimize the operational costs of
the public transportation network (PTN), so that every combination of line, fre-
quency and train composition is chosen only if it is part of the optimal solution.
After developing the cost-oriented optimization models a multi-commodity ﬂow
model is developed, which measures the passenger quality of the solution from
the multi-type model, the model is based on the model from [Rezanova, 2015].
The passenger quality model will be expanded, in order to include transfer times
in the model. After every model section a small data-set is utilized to test the
models. The data and models are implemented in Optimization Programming
language (OPL).

SLPM (2.1-2.4) only gives an optimal solution for lines of the same type. In
real life many passengers switch types, when they are traveling e.g. from a fast
line to a slow line depending on their destination. Therefore the SLPM will be
extended to a multi-type line planning problem (MLPM).

2.4
Change-and-go model
The previous models do not take into account that, in real life, passengers of-
ten have to change platforms and often wait between departures when changing
lines. Therefore, PQM (2.11-2.14) can be changed so that the time spent chang-
ing lines is taken into account.
In [Schöbel, 2012] and [Rezanova, 2015] a change-and-go graph, GCG = (V CG, ACG),
is introduced. The idea is to expand the PTN so that a copy is made of each
station for each line that passes through. We call these copies platforms and,
in order to use a line, a passenger must ﬁrst switch to the appropriate plat-
form from the station. The nodes V CG are divided into two subsets, one set
representing the stations and one set representing the platforms. The arcs in
the change-and-go graph are likewise divided into three subsets, transfer arcs,
station to platform arcs and traveling arcs for every line.
Figure (2.11) is the change-and-go graph for the optimal line plan from MLPM.
The blue dotted edges are edges from stations to platforms, the red dotted edges
are the transfer edges where the passengers have an opportunity to change lines,

Line pool generation
In this section we will formulate an algorithm used to generate a line pool, which
we will utilize in our line planning models for larger sets of data. We will go
through each section of the algorithm used, going into detail regarding viability
of lines and balancing computational time against optimality. Subsequently, we
will brieﬂy describe the network algorithms used within our line pool generating
algorithm. Finally we will list the parameters governing the precision of the
algorithm.
3.1
Line pools
In order to utilize the models described up to this point, it is necessary to have
generated a viable line pool in advance, or to generate them within the line
planning model, using methods such as column generation, where columns are
added dynamically if they improve the solution. We have chosen to approach
the line planning problem with a predetermined line pool.
A viable line pool is a set of routes through a given graph G = (V, A) that has
been created so that all nodes have been covered. While it is viable to have a
cyclic line, the algorithm we propose in this section creates lines by connecting
end points and or large stations and will therefore, out of practicality, not cre-
==PAGE:52==
44
Line pool generation
ate cyclic lines. All lines in the pool must be over a certain length so that no
unrealistic lines exist, such as a direct line between two stations 2 kilometers
apart which would be a waste.
Generating lines is a balancing act. It is important to have suﬃcient lines for
your line planning model to choose an optimal path, seeing as every line you
omit gives the model less chance of ﬁnding the true optimal line plan. On the
other hand it is important not to create too many insigniﬁcant lines, as it will
needlessly increase the computational time required to solve the model. The
formulation of the following line pool generation is loosely based on the work
set forth in [Gattermann et al., 2015].
Initially we have a graph, G = (V, A), made up of vertices and weighted arcs,
and a matrix containing origin-destination data. The OD data deﬁnes the num-
ber of passengers traveling from a given origin to a given destination. More on
our data can be found in the Data section (4).
The ﬁrst phase of the algorithm is ﬁnding the shortest path for each OD pair
given the time it takes to traverse each edge. This is done by implementing
Dijkstra’s algorithm.

Shortest path using Dijkstra’s algorithm
Dijkstra’s algorithm is a shortest path algorithm developed by Edsger W. Dijk-
stra in 1956. The main concept of the algorithm is to ﬁnd the minimum distance
to every node from a given node of origin. The algorithm functions by having
two sets of nodes, visited, S, and not visited, Q. In every iteration of the algo-
rithm the closest node to the pool of visited nodes is marked as visited and its
distance from the node of origin is noted. This is done until all nodes have been
reached and the minimum distance to each node is known. The pseudocode for
the algorithm can be seen in Algorithm (2), it returns the minimum distances
for each node to the source node and information regarding the previous node
of each node. The shortest path to each node from the source can then be found
using reverse iteration.

3.4
Line pool generation algorithm
In order to generate lines it is necessary to deﬁne potential end points in the
MST. The obvious end points are the leaves of the MST, as trains cannot go
further and must exit in the direction from which they arrived.
Practically
however, it might be a good idea to utilize heavily used stations as additional end
points, we will call these "terminals". In [Gattermann et al., 2015] a terminal
is deﬁned as a node for which the line degree is within a certain range of the
maximum line degree in the network. For any given node in a network, its line
degree is the number of lines that enter it. In contrast, the node degree is the
number of edges entering the node.
deg(v, L) ≥terminal_degree · deg(L)max
(3.1)
Whilst our use of terminal_degree is a decent assumption for our thesis, it
might not be that realistic in reality, as not all stations have the capability, i.e.
track real estate, for a train to go back from whence it came. In real life a
railway provider will have information regarding legal terminal locations.
In this formulation the parameter terminal_degree ∈(0, 1).
For the ﬁrst iteration of the algorithm however, we do not have any lines, so
instead of the line degree we use the node degree of the original PTN to de-
ﬁne the terminals as suggested by [Gattermann et al., 2015]. When deciding on
whether to use this approach, we reasoned that a station’s node degree repre-
sents its status as a travel hub, meaning that the higher the node degree, the
more passengers must use the station.
Armed with all possible end points, we use a breadth-ﬁrst search to ﬁnd a path
between each combination of end points, leaving out symmetrical paths from
the opposite origin. Once a path has been found we check its feasibility.
For a path to be feasible it must take over a given amount of time to traverse,
as we want to be able to control the length of our lines. It is also required that
the line does not reverse its direction before reaching its end destination. We
implemented this rule after implementing additional type edges, as we experi-
enced that lines would mistakenly use these lines to double back as seen in ﬁgure
(3.2), where the red edge in (3.2a) is illegal, causing the line to fold.
We refer to this phenomena as the "line folding".
We avoid line folding by keeping track of which edges have been visited, by
storing the reverse edges, and which edges are covered by our edges with type >

5.1.1
Reading the data
Given data for DSB’s entire network it is crucial that we sort and ﬁlter it such
that we only have data covering the stations and tracks utilized by intercity

In the edge data we were given by DSB, each edge was paired with
the time taken to traverse it, halting time at the following station, and a type,
i.e. intercity, regional, high speed etc.. In order to only have relevant data we
ﬁltered out all non-intercity or high speed rail lines, thinking that we would thus
avoid all regional and international tracks. Though this worked, it turned out
that we had a multitude of duplicate edges with varying travel times. As our
model will always choose the fastest version of an edge we decided to omit the
high speed rail and delete any surplus copies of the same edge, giving us the
aforementioned 194 edges and 103 nodes. For each edge we combine both travel
time and halting time.
In importing the OD data, it is important to ﬁlter it such that we only use
data for the 103 nodes we isolated in our edge data. In our ﬁltering process we
removed all OD pairs that contained nodes which were not included in our node
list. We also down-scaled all OD demand to an hourly basis and subsequently
removed any OD pairs containing a demand of less than one person per hour.
This resulted in a set of OD-pairs containing 556 elements.

5.1 Line pool generation
61
We create edges of a chosen type by selecting a source node of the type and
locating the nearest node of the same type in each direction, updating the travel
time (without halting times) accordingly. We do this by using a breadth ﬁrst
search, checking the type of adjacent nodes, storing them if they are of the same
type and placing them in a queue if they are not. Once the queue is empty, we
have exhausted all available paths, either ﬁnding a node of the same type or
reaching a leaf node.
We added a halting time of 4 minutes to each newly created edge. We chose this
value as all of the type stations are over a certain size, meaning there are many
passengers, so it might take longer for all passengers to embark and disembark
the train.
5.1.3
Generating lines
The process of generating lines and the algorithm we used has been described in
section (3.4). When implementing the line generation algorithm we used both
Dijkstra’s algorithm and Prim’s algorithm. The C++ source code for these two
algorithms, on which we based our code, was found at [RosettaCode, 2015] and
[Barnwal, 2015] respectively.
Having generated a line pool we realized that certain edges were not included in
any lines, meaning certain sections of our network were not represented in our
line pool. We found that this was because our algorithm would generate lines
which would get prohibited by our anti folding constraint, after which it would
generate the exact same lines in subsequent iterations, once again prohibiting
them as they were illegal. To counteract this we intentionally banned speciﬁc
edges in one iteration, after which we would reopen them, and ban all other
edges using the same track segment in the following iterations. This resulted in
legal lines containing the previously missing edges.
5.1.4
Output from C++
Following the line pool generation we use the lines to create a number of outputs
which we rely on in our OPL model.
For each edge we store the lines in which they exist. Each of these lines we
combine with a frequency, f ∈{1, 2, ..., F} where F is the highest frequency we
allow a line to have, and a number of train carriages, c ∈{1, 2, ..., C} where C
==PAGE:70==
62
Implementation
is the highest number of carriages usable by a line.
Each line has a corresponding cost, we deﬁne these costs as the total time
taken from start to ﬁnish. These line costs are stored in a vector with index
corresponding to their line number.
Furthermore we need both the demand for each edge, and the track covers
for each edge of type 1. The track covers are created while generating type
edges. This is done by back-tracing from the nearest type edge to the source,
recollecting the nodes visited and their order. Any edges used during this process
must be covered by the type edge.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis has been to explore the concept of line planning within the
ﬁeld of railway transportation, analyzing the literature and applying relevant
theoretical models to the real life case of intercity trains in Denmark.
Studying the literature we were able to gain an insight into some of the various
methods and objectives already explored and tried in other research papers.
The most common approaches are to solve for either operational cost or to solve
in favor of the passengers, be it either through maximizing direct travelers or
reducing the total travel time. In this thesis we have followed in the footsteps of
the work done in [Rezanova, 2015], attempting to utilize the proposed models
to ﬁnd the optimal line plan for the Danish intercity railway network.
We utilized two models in our attempt at solving the line planning problem. Our
ﬁrst model, MLPM, gave us the optimal operational cost for the lines available in
the line pool, while still satisfying the demand. In order to check the passenger
quality of the solution found in the MLPM, we used our CGPQM to minimize
the passenger time for the solution found in the MLPM.
Prior to analyzing our results, we expected the solution quality would have a
positive correlation to the size of the line pool used as input. After analyzing
our results however, we found that the best solution we could produce came
from one of the smallest line pools. Whether this is due to a ﬂawed line pool
==PAGE:88==
80
Conclusion
generation algorithm, or simply just a result of an under-scaled demand, we do
not know, as our attempts at increasing the demand proved futile.
It was our expectation that lowering the value of the three constants in the cost
component wpass, would improve the solution in favor of the passengers. While
this expectation proved correct for a limited number of adjustments, we could
not deﬁne a general tendency in the behavior of the results. This might well
be because we performed manual iterations of the greedy heuristic and given a
second opportunity, we would deﬁnitely attempt an automated implementation
in C++ or a similar programming language.
Even though our results were not as expected, we managed to produce results
from which we could establish Pareto-optimal curves, displaying a connection
between operational cost and passenger quality.
7.1
Research limitations and future research
Although we are pleased with the solutions we have found, we believe that we
could achieve a more realistic solution if we were to implement a line-splitting
functionality. By this we mean a mathematical method of, for example, splitting
the carriages of a train crossing from Fyn to Jutland, so that half of the carriages
go north toward Aalborg and the other half go west toward Esbjerg. We know
that this is a possibility in real life, and given further time, we would like to
attempt an implementation in our model.
Another aspect that we decided to omit is the inclusion of additional rolling
stock types. At the moment we only concern ourselves with IC3 trains, but it
would be interesting to include other rolling stock types, each having diﬀerent
capacities and top speeds as seen in Appendix (A.1.1). Accompanying this we
would like to expand the network with, for example, regional tracks which would
also make the network more interesting from an optimization perspective.
