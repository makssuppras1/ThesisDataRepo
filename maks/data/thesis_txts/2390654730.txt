ii
Abstract
In 2011 the Danish Government declared the goal of becoming coal, oil and gas inde-
pendent by 2050 while at the same time offering a high degree of security of supply.
An increased share of electricity from intermittent renewable energy sources, sup-
ports the transition away from fossil fuels, but challenges the power system stability,
thus the security of supply. In order to maintain a stable power system frequency
either the production or the demand side need to offer increased ﬂexibility. On the
demand side, electriﬁcation of e.g. heating installations is mentioned as an example
of increasing the ﬂexibility of the energy system. The Energy Agreement from 2012
bans oil boilers in new buildings from 2016, which further encourages the transition
towards electric heating installations. The question is whether demand-response is
feasible under the current market regulation.
This thesis examined the private-economic feasibility of implementing gas-electric
hybrid heat pumps into the Danish power market through the case of a provider of
heat, Best Green. The option of producing heat from either electricity or gas makes
the hybrid-heat pumps an interesting topic within demand-response. Through an
optimization model various scenarios of load shifting based on the Danish day-
ahead market and participation in the regulating power markets were assessed. The
study includes scenarios with and without the additional ﬂexibility that the gas-
boiler holds. Using the results of the optimization a cash ﬂow analysis was per-
formed on the concept of Best Green. The results of the cash ﬂow analysis were
compared to a base case, where no demand-response was considered.
All scenarios yielded positive NPVs, but not all proved better than the basecase.
The NPV was sensitive towards changes in heat load and average ﬂow temperature,
but within reasonable changes, the NPV remained positive. The study found that
under the current heat load (290 MW/year), heating capacity (94 MW) and storage
capacity (1600 L), the ﬂexibility was limited without applying the gas boiler as well.
Buffer tank sizes of 3000 L and 2500 L proved optimal for scenarios without and with
gas boilers respectively, if new investments were to be made. One of two applied set-
tlement models showed Net Present Value (NPV) improvements of 18%-24%. This
was found allowing demand-response through load shifting and regulating power.
The latter only yielded small improvements, while having high entry requirements.
Another ﬁnding was, that given the applied heat load curve, shifting to hourly set-
tlement can improve the NPV by 1%-2%. As gas boilers were being replaced or
supported by heat pumps, reductions of CO2- emissions of 55%-68% were found.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The purpose of this chapter is to introduce the motivation and scope for the project
through a statement of the problem and a look into other studies in the ﬁeld of de-
mand response (DR). Furthermore the aim is to depict the technical and regulatory
framework in order to identify possible interesting scenarios to conduct a feasibility
study on. This includes an overview of the existing business of the Danish supplier
of heating solutions, Best Green (bestgreen.dk), the applied technology and market
perspectives in western Denmark (DK1).
1.1
Motivation
1.1.1
Problem Statement
An increased share of electricity from renewable energy sources, e.g. wind energy
(Energinet.dk, 2016g), results in an electricity production that is more ﬂuctuating
and harder to predict. In 2025 electricity from wind power is expected to make
up 60 % of the electricity consumption, whereas this number was 2% in 1990 (En-
erginet.dk, 2016a). This tendency is observed in the entire European Union, and
is expected to continue in order to fulﬁll the EU goals as agreed upon in the Paris
Agreement on climate change (European Commission, 2017). In order to keep the
power system frequency constant, the power balance between production and con-
sumption of electricity must constantly be upheld. The production side can con-
tribute with ﬂexibility by including storage and export in times of high wind power
production. At times with less wind energy and high consumption peak plants can
be activated. Likewise activation of the demand side, i.e. DR, can contribute signif-
icantly to a ﬂexible energy system (Hedegaard and Münster, 2013). The ﬂexibility
can be provided through load shifting due to price signals or by voluntary discon-
nection due to frequency drops (Xu, Togeby, and Østergaard, 2008). By ﬂattening
out the consumption, less peak capacity would have to be installed. A study (Hede-
gaard and Münster, 2013) analyzes the implementation of individual heat pumps
(HP) in individually heated areas in Denmark (716,000 houses, 4.3 TWh −e) from
a socio-economic perspective. This implementation allows increased investment in
wind power and the study proves to be socio-economically feasible. The effects of
ﬂexible operation of heat pumps with storage are moderate, i.e. amounting to aver-
age savings of 60-200 EUR/house/year from a socio-economic valuation (Hedegaard
and Münster, 2013).
In the Energy Strategy 2050 of the Danish government the electriﬁcation of e.g.
heating installations is mentioned as an example of increasing the ﬂexibility of the
energy system (The Danish Ministry of Climate and Energy, 2011).
The Energy
Agreement from 2012 bans oil boilers in new buildings from 2016 (Energistyrelsen,
2012), which further encourages the transition towards electric heating installations.

1.1.2
Studies of private-economic Perspectives
The private-economics of ﬂexible operation of heat pumps (HPs) have been investi-
gated in a number of studies both through simulations and pilot projects. In the
ForskEL-funded pilot project "Styr din Varmepumpe" (Energinet.dk, 2012) by En-
erginet.dk 300 HPs were installed in residential houses to investigate how HPs can
deliver ﬂexible power consumption and balancing services. Based on four years of
data, analysis showed that even on the coldest day, while maintaining a comfort
level (+/- 1.5 ◦C) the electricity consumption could be shifted two hours. If 200.000
HPs were to be installed 300-600 MW of power would be available for load shift-
ing (Intelligent Energistyring AmbA et al., 2015). Due to technical challenges the
focus was on technical adjustment and optimization rather than bidding optimiza-
tion, since energy effective operation is found more important for economical feasi-
bility (Energinet.dk, 2012). As part of the pilot project an open source software and
hardware has been developed and demonstrated. It is able to monitor and control
a large number of heat pumps through an open-standard communication protocol
XMPP (Intelligent Energistyring AmbA et al., 2015), proving the technical feasibility
of controlling the heat pumps remotely.
Biegel, Andersen, et al. (2013) used data from the "Styr din Varmepumpe"-project
to conduct a one-year market optimization simulation on a scaled heat pump portfo-
lio on the Danish power market. Assuming aggregation of 10,000 HPs with nominal
power consumption of 4 kW in the portfolio two scenarios were simulated; one op-
timized for the spot prices and one optimized for the spot prices and providing reg-
ulating power. Temperature interval bounds were used to limit the duration of load
shifting. The study showed that under ideal circumstances, i.e. without any delays
or ramping constraints included, the average spot price, at which the electricity was
bought could be decreased by 20 %. This would mean annual savings of approxi-
mately 500 DKK for a household, where close to 90 % of the savings were caused by
the spot price optimization. These results point out the need for low installation and
equipment cost in order to ensure economic feasibility.
Other studies investigate the effects of using the ﬂexibility of heat pumps with
thermal storage through load shifting (Good et al., 2013; Schachter, Good, and Man-
carella, 2015). Both studies are modeled assuming the regulatory framework for the
UK-market, where the imbalance settlement differs from that of the Danish system.
The business cases for both studies is that the aggregator of the HPs is also the Bal-
ancing Responsible Party (BRP) and is hence exposed to imbalance costs.
Schachter, Good, and Mancarella (2015) studies the optimal purchase strategy
under decreasing mean prices and increasing volatility, considered likely future sce-
narios in the study. This study also considers a two-stage stochastic optimization

2015) shows that controlling a larger number of HPs according to day-ahead prices
is technically feasible and Biegel, Andersen, et al. (2013) and Schachter, Good, and
Mancarella (2015) proves that economical gains can be achieved by optimizing the
use of HPs by load shifting. The economical gains, however, do not necessarily
justify the investments for the private consumer. To achive the highest savings on
imbalance costs, a stochastic optimization model should be applied. Further savings
could be met by implementing a TES and gains could be achieved by providing
regulating power. If prices were to become more volatile, the business case might
improve.
The described studies all depict private economic perspectives of ﬂexible HP
operation for private HP owners. This study will contribute to the research ﬁeld
with an investigation into a third party owning and operating the HPs, hence selling
Heat-as-a-Service (HaaS). The HPs are hybrid heat pumps (HHP), hereby contain-
ing further ﬂexibility potential. Providing the ﬂexibility in DK1 potentially by load
shifting and offering DR services should clarify whether the market conditions are
incentivizing for private consumers interested in investing in HPs. The thesis uses
the case of Hatting School, one of the 13 sites in Horsens operated by Best Green.

1.3
Technical Framework
The focus of this project is not to thermodynamically model the HPs in detail, but in
order to understand the possible services (space heating, DHW, ﬂexibility) and the
power consumption, the heating system will be presented in this section.
The HP installed by Insero are as mentioned HHPs, meaning the HP is placed in
series with a gas boiler (see an example from Hatting Skole in ﬁg. 1.2. The heat is
supplied to both space heating and domestic hot water (DHW). The heat for DHW is
generated solely by the HP, whereas the HP either in combination with the gas boiler
or on its own produces hot water for space heating. The HPs have been coupled with
the existing gas boilers, so that during the coldest days the gas boiler can support
the HPs.
The HPs in question are air-to-water one-stage HPs, as the schematic of ﬁg. 1.1
shows for Hatting Skole. This means that air as a heat source with an ambient tem-
perature evaporates the refrigerant using forced convection. The refrigerant is com-
pressed to condensation pressure. In the condenser the refrigerant exchanges heat
with the return water from the central heating system. This heat is transferred to the
heat sink, being the indoor rooms, walls and interior.

1.3.3
Gas Boiler Characterization
The gas boilers in this HHP-setup are condensing gas boilers. Condensing gas boil-
ers are characterized by the condensation of water vapor within the combustion
gases as it is exposed to temperatures below the dew point (Pilaar, 2007). The phase
shift releases latent energy as heat, which is transferred, in this case, to the water
returning from and ﬂowing towards the central heating system. Heat that would
otherwise have been lost in the ﬂuegas is now exploited, which leads to higher efﬁ-
ciencies. According to Durkin (2006) the thermal efﬁciency of conventional boilers
range between 80%-88% and for condencing boilers efﬁciencies are between 88-95%.
For the high efﬁciencies to occur, the return water temperature must be below 54◦C
(Consortium for Energy Efﬁciency, 2001). The fact that the water is led in series from

1.3.4
Control, Communication Flow and Modes of Operation
The HHP is controlled through a Siemens RVS21 HP controller which uses as input
the evaporation, ﬂow (Tflow), return (Treturn), buffer tank (TBT ) and outdoor temper-
ature (Toutdoor) to give the output signal of HP: on/off (see ﬁg. 1.5). The controller
thereby registers relevant temperatures and activates the HPs and gas boiler when
needed.
If only one HP is installed, the compressor is activated as soon as the temper-
ature sensor placed in the buffer tank measures a temperature lower than the set
point temperature. The set point of the buffer tank is determined by a selected
heating curve, which evaluates on the outdoor temperature. When multiple HPs
are installed on a site, they are placed in cascade, so they can be activated one at
a time when necessary. The next HP in line is activated when the count of degree
minutes reaches -250. This implies that the buffer tank temperature has been a cer-
tain amount below the set point temperature (∆T) for a certain duration (∆t), i.e.
∆T · ∆t = −250. The temperature out of the HP is controlled by the return temper-
ature to the HP, due to a ﬁxed temperature difference between the two. When the
buffer tank temperature exceeds the set point temperature by 2◦C the HP is turned
off for 20 minutes.
An additional control option, the Power Limiter, has been implemented to avoid
overcurrent due to excessive load. The amount of amps on some of the sites are just
enough, which can and has caused outages. Based on the current, the Power Limiter
can break an entrance in the Siemens Control module and the HP is shut off for
a period of 20 minutes, to avoid that the compressor constantly switching on/off.
This feature allows for controlling the HPs based on other signals than the count of
degree minutes.

1.3.5
Flexibility Possibilities & Limitations
Aspects as historical HP activation, storage options, control and communication has
been presented in the previous.
It was noted that both of the HPs between Nov. 16th 2016 and Feb. 16th 2017
were activated for 80% of the time with average off-periods of 20 minutes. During
the coldests months of the year, the data hence points to limited options for load
shifting. This should look more promising for warmer months. As the HPs are
HHPs the gas boilers can offer additional ﬂexibility. As of today the gas boiler is only
activated in case the HPs cannot provide enough heat. To fully exploit the added
ﬂexibility of the gas boiler the activation order of the heating elements (HP1, HP2 or
gas boiler) must be variable. As of now the gas boiler always has last priority, while
in an optimized system it should be possible to turn the gas boiler on independently
of the HPs, if that is the most optimal option.
The available energy storage option with a TES of 1000 L plus 600 L DHW tank
is only able to shift the load for 0.14 hours at times with maximum load, which may
prove to be a limiting factor.

1.4.1
The Danish Power Market & its Participation Requirements
The Danish power market facilitates trading and balancing of power as different
products depending on the time-horizon relative to the delivery hour as depicted
in ﬁg. 1.7. The market is regulated by the Danish Transmission System Operator
(TSO) Energinet.dk. The Danish electricity market covers two synchrounous areas;
DK1 (West Denmark) and DK2 (East Denmark) divided by the Great Belt. DK1 is
synchronized with the continental European system, whereas DK2 is synchonized
with the Nordic system. An interconnection with a transmission capacity of 600 MW
exists between the two areas (Energinet.dk, 2014a).
In order to participate in the market you must be a Balancing Responsible Party
(BRP). This requires liquidity to receive a credit line, due to the fact that the BRPs
are ﬁnancially responsible for the imbalances that they might cause (Energinet.dk,

1.4.2
Futures & Forwards
Futures and forwards are ﬁnancial products, price of a volume to be delivered in the
future is ﬁxed at a given point. The settlement between the trading parties is then the
difference between the future and the spot price. Futures are traded on NASDAQ
OMX and forwards are traded bilaterally (Energinet.dk, 2016d).
1.4.3
Day-Ahead
Day-ahead (DA) trades are handled by the power market Nord Pool. The day-ahead
market is the largest market of Nord Pool and this is where 70% of the total electric-
ity consumption of Scandinavia is traded (Energinet.dk, 2016d). BRPs of producers
and consumers can enter bids (MW at a certain price during a given hour or block
bids for a minimum of three hours) up until the deadline at noon before the day of
delivery. These bids are placed in merit order and a market price is found for the
given hour. The pricing is hence uniform, i.e. as long as accepted everyone pays
and receives the same price regardless of their bids. At 1 pm the BRPs are notiﬁed
of their production/consumption schedule and the market prices for the 24 hours of
the next day.
The DA price varies heavily over the hour of the day depending on e.g. the
amount of electricity produced from wind energy. Market data is available from
Nordpool for the past four years (2013-2016). In table 1.2 a summary of the data is
shown. As seen the prices vary from -463 DKK/MWh to 14911 DKK/MWh with
the highest standard deviation in 2013. This spread in prices makes load shifting
interesting. The difference in average prices from year to year ranges between -23
% and 31% with a trend towards lower prices. During the same years an increase in
the share of electricity produced by wind turbines is also observed.
1.4.4
Intra-Day
In case a producer or consumer cannot fulﬁll the plan they agreed upon in the DA
market they have the option of trading intra-day to reduce expected imbalances.
Between 2 pm and up until one hour before delivery intraday-trades can be done.
These also take place at Nord Pool. The trades are done continuously matching
highest buy price with lowest sell price. The pricing is pay-as-bid (Energinet.dk,
2016d). The traded volumes on the intra-day market are assumed to increase over
the coming years due to a continuous increase in renewable energy technologies
prone to more imbalances (Energinet.dk, 2016d).

1.4.5
Regulating Power Market
When entering the hour of operation, Energinet.dk stabilizes the frequency of the
grid by balancing the production and consumption, if the previously traded vol-
umes do not match the actual consumption/production of a particant. In order to
perform the balancing, Energinet.dk has entered ’agreement of balance responsibil-
ity’ with BRPs. The participant providing regulating power can enter one of two
agreements; 1) offering availability of manual reserves, committing to place bids of
certain volume over a certain period of time or 2) entering regulation bids whenever
wanting to. Option 1) receives availability payment and energy payment, whereas
option 2) only receives energy payment. The BRPs submits bids (up- or downregula-
tion) to Energinet.dk, which are then placed on the Nordic Operational Information
System (NOIS). Bids for manual reserves must be between 10-50 MW and are en-
tered as DKK/MWh over the 24 hours of operation (Energinet.dk, 2008). The bids
are used to form a merit order from which the availability payment is found. After
the hour of delivery, a regulating power price is settled for the given hour, being
either up- or downward regulation depending on the direction of the regulation.
The pricing is uniform, where the most expensive accepted bid sets the price (En-
erginet.dk, 2016f). If both up- and downward regulation occurs within one hour,
the direction of the aggregated result is settled according to the regulating price,
whereas the other direction receives pay-as-bid (Energinet.dk, 2008). Regulation
power price is settled for the entire Nordic area, and is hence the same in DK1 and
DK2 unless bottlenecks on the transmission lines occur.

The average standard deviation is higher for the regulating power prices, making

1.4.6
Balancing Power Market
Every participant in the DA-market that does not live up to its offtake- or produc-
tion schedule is forced into the balancing market, where the imbalances are settled.
Two price systems exist (Energinet.dk, 2008); a one-price system for consumers and
a two-price system for producers. The one price system means that regardless of
whether the consumer takes off too much or too little, the price to pay or to be paid
back is the regulating power price. Regarding the two-price system a producer will
either be paid the regulating power price (when in direction of the total system im-
balance) or the DA-price (when in opposite direction of the total system imbalance)
for the given hour, and in no situation will it be beneﬁcial to be off balance. The
imbalance payments are handled by the BRPs/suppliers who typically charge their
customers a ﬁxed imbalance fee per kWh bought.

1.4.8
Strategic Reserves
Today, if no settlement is reached on the DA market, i.e. when production does not
meet the consumption, Nordpool shortens all consumption bids by a certain share to
match the production bids. The consumers cannot necessarily comply to the short-
ening, which causes imbalances during the hour of delivery and the manual reserves
are activated as mentioned in the regulating market. If additionally something un-
expected occurs the reserves are already in the market, and cannot act twice. In
DK2 Energinet.dk has entered long term agreements for 2016-2020 with ﬁve differ-
ent manual reserves amounting to 630 MW at the cost of 1 billion DKK for the ﬁve
year period. In DK1 the reserves are found on daily auctions.
In the search of achieving security of supply in a cost-effective way a tender was
announced for so-called strategic reserves in DK2 for the same period (Energinet.dk,
2014b). The strategic reserves were to be brought into play, when a match between
production and consumption failed to occur. A reserve should expect an average of
5 hours of activation in a year and maximum 8 consecutive hours. Both production
and consumption units were eligible for placing bids. Consumption units should
place price ﬂexible bids of 2999.9 EUR/MWh for at least 5 hours a day into the DA
market. In the case of a gap between consumption and production, the consumption
unit would be curtailed just before the maximum spot price (3000 EUR/MWh) was
reached (Energinet.dk, 2015b).
The strategic reserves scheme was recommended in (Energinet.dk and Quartz+co,
n.d.) as a means of increasing the ﬂexibility of the power market and ensuring sufﬁ-
cient capacity. Similar models have been introduced in Sweden due to the closing of
Barsebäck ((Hammarstedt and Nilsson, 2014),(Svenska Kraftnät, 2016)) and in Fin-
land (Energiavirasto, 2017), which were also considered in the preparations of the
Danish strategic reserves. Eventually the tender was cancelled and the strategic re-
serves scheme not carried into effect.

gas boiler, one DHW tank and one buffer tank. Figure 2.1 depicts the system as mod-

First this chapter aims to ﬁnd a correlation between heat generation and the tem-

Based on the output of the optimization of the mathematical models a cash ﬂow

inﬂation and time value of money must be taken into consideration. The calculations
making up the model are shown in table 2.2. The economic parameters applied in
the model are provided in conﬁdential Appendix E and the entire model is shown
in Appendix F .
2.3.1
Nominal Value
Following the convention of many ﬁnancial analyses (Prasanna Chandra, 2015) this
cash ﬂow analysis uses nominal values. The nominal value is the price of an item
at the given point in time. The following year the inﬂation will have increased the
price and hereby also the nominal value. Using inﬂation rates, in, of each year, n,
applied by The Danish Energy Agency (Energistyrelsen, 2017c) (see Appendix C)
the real value of a given base year, λreal,baseyear, is converted into nominal value,

as in table 2.2. The inﬂation index of the baseyear, iin=baseyear, is set to one. The
power prices for the four years 2013-2016 are all converted into real 2016-values
before applied in the optimization model. Hence the optimization objective, z, is
given in real2016 value and is converted into nominal values in the cash ﬂow model.
2.3.2
Inﬂows & Expenditures
For each kWh of heat consumed, the customer is charged a ﬁxed heat price which is
constant in nominal terms. Best Green applies two different pricing models for their
various projects. For Hatting School Settlement Model B is applied. Pricing model
A charges the ﬁxed heat price, λh,modelA and a yearly customer subscription. Model
B charges a lower ﬁxed heat price, λh,modelB = 60% · λh,modelA, the yearly customer
subscription and an initial connection contribution. Both the subscription fee and
the heat price are constant in nominal terms over the contracting period.
It is a regulatory requirement to perform annual maintenance on the HPs. Best
Green has compentencies internally to perform maintenance and an estimated OPEX
to cover the hours spent. The OPEX is considered to follow the inﬂation, hence
introduced as real2016 prices and converted into nominal values. In SCENB, the
OPEX covers both the HPs and the gas boiler. OPEXSCENB = 150% · OPEXSCENA
is assumed to make up for this.
The cash ﬂow analysis is performed for both settlement models applying annual
consumption of 263 and 293 MWh for SCENA and SCENB respectively.
2.3.3
Time Value of Money
The time value of money acknowledges that a sum of money received today is worth
more than the same sum of money received in the future. This is not due to inﬂation,
but due to the possible earnings that the money could yield if invested. The time
value of money is taken into account through discounting the future values by a
private-economic nominal pre-tax discount rate provided by Best Green.
With the expenditures and inﬂows converted into nominal values, the EBITDA
(Earnings before Interest, Taxes, and Discounting) is calculated as seen in table 2.2.
Dividing the EBITDA by the present value factor, the present value is found. The
present value indicates what the value of a future sum on money is worth presently
for a given discount rate.
As a CVR-registered company, Best Green must also pay corporate tax of their
revenue, which for 2017 makes up 22% (Skatteministeriet, 2016a). The company is
charged VAT for all products and services they buy, but this is cancelled out, as they
charge their customers VAT. Best Green are taxed on their earnings deducted by de-
preciation, applying straight line depreciation over the contracting period. Instead
of implementing the taxation, this study, as mentioned, applies a pre-tax discount
rate. The pre-tax discount takes into account the taxation, and is hence higher than
a post-tax rate.
2.3.4
Assessment Measure
Finally the Net Present Value (NPV) is determined. NPV is a measure often applied
to evaluate a project over its entire lifetime, as it takes into account the inﬂows, ex-
penditures, time value of money and the initital investment. If the NPV is positive,
the project is considered economically feasible. In this analysis the NPV is compared
to that of the basecase.

Power from HP The uniform-temperature tank model assumes a two-step circula-
tion pump from the heat pump, i.e. either it delivers the nominal heat load,
or it does not. In reality the pump can vary between 70-100% of the nominal
ﬂow. Additionally the pump can actually turn on after being off for 20 minutes,
whereas the hour-granulated model only allows either to be on or off during a
given hour. A sensitivity analysis is performed on the nominal heat load vary-
ing it between +/-30%. This does not entirely reﬂect the lack of ﬂexibility, but
gives an indication of heat provided in smaller or larger amounts.
Heat load The contract with Horsens Municipality states that Best Green should
supply sufﬁcient heat to meet the demand. This is highly dependent on the
weather through i.e. the ambient temperature. The total heat load can change
as well as the heat proﬁle over the year, e.g. for more or fewer heating degree
days. As previously stated these have varied by 13% in 2016/2017 from the
mean of previous years. Assuming the heat load to be able to vary +/-30%
from the historical heat load, the mean of the heat load is varied in increments
of 10%. The heat proﬁle is kept the same, with only small changes in standard
deviation, a result of avoiding negative heat loads.
COP The COP applied is taken from the charts provided by WAMAK. As they are
provided by the producer itself these are most likely measured under ideal cir-
cumstances, i.e. not during initiation phase. The other uncertainties regarding
COP come from the source and sink temperatures. The ambient temperature
derives from the reference year. DMI has recently published a new reference
temperature, and this could also be tested against the data. The COP is given
for a ﬁxed ﬂow temperature (45 ◦C). The COPs are also given for ﬂow temper-
atures of 35◦C, 55◦C and 65◦C. The optimization model is hence run applying
each of the four sets of data of the four ﬂow temperatures.

3.5
Risk Analysis
3.5.1
Commodity Price Development
The prices on the spot market have been declining between 2008-2016 (European
Commission, 2016). According to Energistyrelsen (2017b) this trend will change.
Despite more renewable energy sources in the power market, spot prices are ex-
pected to increase 3 øre/kWh from 2016-2020, 12 øre/kWh between until 2025 and
19 øre/kWh until 2030. This is based on a so-called frozen-policy approach, meaning
that the projection is calculated based on existing regulation and approved regu-
lation. An expected increase in fuel costs is the primary reason for the spot price
increase. Extended transmission connections to Germany and the Netherlands are
==PAGE:72==
Chapter 3. Results
59
assumed to add to the increase due to converging prices towards the higher prices
of neighbouring countries. An increase in the spot price poses a risk to the project
regardless of which scenario or base case is applied. As seen settlement Model B is
more vulnerable to higher expenses.
At the same time, the volatility is expected to increase due to increasing amounts
of ﬂuctating energy sources (Energistyrelsen, 2017b). This makes a difference in
selection of load shifting vs basecase. As noted and shown in table 3.14 a decrease
may change the recommendations regarding SCENA. Currently Energinet.dk is also
looking at increasing the price ceiling of the spot market from 3000 DKK/MWh
to 15000 DKK/MWh as an incentive for ﬂexible consumption (Capion and Larsen,
2017). It would further enhance load shifting of SCENA and SCENB.
For SCENB the development in gas prices should be considered. As mentioned
price declines of more than 20% yield exponential growth in the NPV. From 2013-
2016 the wholesale gas prices in the EU have decreased by 50% ((European Com-
mission, 2016)). The causes to explain this are low global demand, falling oil-price
indexed gas contracts and increasing amounts of LNG. To a further extend than the
wholesale electricity is the gas price affected by global development according to
(European Commission, 2016). In (Capion and Larsen, 2017) it is argued that the
high transportation costs limit the price convergence. The future gas prices though
are believed to be formed by the production costs of American LNG plus transporta-
tion coverage. Again, the pure energy price makes up less than half of the total gas
price due to taxes, which lowers the risks (both positive and negative) associated to
gas price changes. An increase in gas prices affects the NPV to lesser extend, as the
HPs take over the heat production.
3.5.2
Regulatory Intervention & Market Changes
The energy sector plays an increased role in national and international politics. This
therefore increases the risk of political intervention with changes in regulation (En-
erginet.dk and Quartz+co, n.d.).
The volumes traded on the intra-day market (see section 1.4.4) are expected to
increase the coming years concurrently with the increase in energy from renewable
sources. As these are harder to predict, the imbalances between scheduled produc-
tion and actual production increase (Energinet.dk, 2016d). Also Energinet.dk sug-
gests to look into the possibility of trading closer to the hour of delivery than the
one hour deadline given today (Energinet.dk, 2015a). This could reduce the imbal-
ances during the delivery hour and hence reduce the gain from regulating power
market participation. This thesis has not looked into the intra-day market option,
but it could be interesting to consider for the entire HHP portfolio of Best Green.
As of today bids into the regulating power market (either up or downwards)
must be larger than 10 MW. For Insero to act as aggregator in the regulating power
market it would require 330 sites with a similar HP capacity (30 kW) and load pattern
as Hatting Skole. In 2015 Intelligent Energi suggested to reduce the bid size require-
ment from 10 MW to 1 MW (Intelligent Energi, 2015). For now it has been decided to
reduce it to 5 MW, which is expected to be in place ultimo 2017 (Nielsen and Bruhn,
2017). The 1 MW initiative has also been discussed, but is not yet implemented.
An analysis (energiområdet, 2017) of the most socio-economic optimal design
of levies and subsidies in the energy sector for reaching the energy targets presents
recommendation that may affect relevant regulation changes:

3.5.3
Demand Projections
Two risks are identiﬁed regarding demand projections: Competition from district
heating and on a smaller scale the heat load and its curve at the sites.
A potential risk for Best Green’s HaaS concept is competition from district heat-
ing due to expansion of the district heating grid. The district heating utility Fjern-
varme Horsens are already expanding in Horsens. The grid at the site of Hatting Skole
is expected to be completed in 2021 (Fjernvarme Horsens, 2017a) and that of Gedved
Skole in 2022. Contracts with customers are entered for longer periods e.g. 12 years
(min. 8 years) at Hatting Skole. The risk is therefore not present in already realized
projects. Looking at demand projection for the pipeline of future projects around
Horsens it may be a challenge.
Secondly, as seen in the sensitivity analysis the NPV is sensitive to the heat load.
In particular this concerns projects with settlement model A. Reductions in heat load
may derive from both weather conditions as well as energy savings initiatives at the
sites. The latter is already planned for Gedved Skole. This may affect both lower the
heat demand as well as the ﬂow temperatures. This may mean a decrease in heat
payment earnings but also result in a more energy efﬁcient operation. As seen from
the sensitivity analysis a decrease in ﬂow temperature (COP) of 20% (10 ◦C) means
an increase in NPV of 60%-80%.

4.1
Study Results
The thesis investigated the HaaS concept of Best Green through the scenarios; base-
case*, SCENA, SCENB, SCENB*, SCENC and SCENC* compared to a base case ap-
plying two different settlement models; A and B and four sets of spot prices from

Flexibility Potential
Running the optimization model it was seen that the ﬂexibility potential was higher
using SCENB and SCENC with the gas boiler compared to the basecase and SCENA.
Comparing the scheduled power consumption of the four sets of spot prices with
different mean and volatility indicated how well the model responded to the spot
prices. Through the activation spread and the average temperatures of the four years
it was evident that the scheduling was very similar amongst the four years applying
SCENA with identical activation in 81% of the time. As the mean and volatility of
the spot prices deviates, the curve of the prices may show similar pattern due to
the typical load pattern over the day. This could explain the small differences in
the power schedule from year to year resulting in low activation spread for SCENA.
On the other hand, this should also show in SCENB. This is not the case as SCENB
yields identical activation in 38% of the time.
The volume-weighted prices also conﬁrm, that the ﬂexibility in SCENA is limited
compared to SCENB/SCENC where the gas boiler is in play. Calculations of the
Basecase* reveals that between 32%-72% of the change in volume-weighted price

can be attributed to the shift from monthly average to hourly settlement. These
numbers are 29%-53% in SCENB.
The study has also examined the effects of larger buffer tanks on the ﬂexibil-
ity potential. For SCENA introducing larger buffer tanks decreases the activation
spread exponentially, i.e. the model is increasingly able to perform load shifting.
This also shows on the objective, that decreases for increasing mass of the buffer
tank. In SCENB there is smaller positive effects from larger buffer tanks.
This model only uses the storage potential of the buffer tanks, but the thermal
capacity of the buildings should be examined as well in order to further account
for the ﬂexibility. Arteconi, Hewitt, and Polonara (2013) considers a house with
radiators and a house with underﬂoor heating to investigate the effect on the room
comfort of the thermal mass of the system. In a system with underﬂoor heating,
the thermal comfort can be upheld, whereas in a system with radiators a TES is
necessary to provide the same thermal comfort.
To sum up, the ﬂexibility potential is characterized by the amount of storage, the
ability to switch between gas and power, but also from the amount of heat load. The
power schedule during two 48-hour intervals of January and June, i.e. high load
and low load periods, were examined. This clearly showed the increased ﬂexibility
potential during June compared to January.
DR Participation
With the HHPs operating in DK1, the possibilities of conducting DR in DK1 were
considered. When discussing market participation, Best Green must operate through
a BRP who has access to the wholesale markets.
In the DA market, load shifting can be performed, as the prices vary over the
day. Four years of price data (2013-2016) was examined and showed high variation
in minimum and maximum prices.
Offering ancillary services was not found suitable for the HHPs in this case. Sec-
ondary reserves must bid in with symmetrical bids, which would require one HP to
be in operation in order to either activate the second HP or deactivate the active HP.
Regarding primary reserves, the bids must be in blocks of four hours, and with the
limited storage options and heat load requirements this seems challenging. Addi-
tionally, to be able to provide ancillary services, Best Green must be able to register
frequency deviations.
Though the bid size requirement of 10 MW is a hindrance to regulating power
market participation it was considered in this study. The bid size requirements are,
as mentioned decreasing and the HHPs could be pooled into a BRPs portfolio.
The strategic reserves and the intraday market were not investigated in this the-
sis, but may be of interest for Best Green. A strategic reserve scheme is not im-
plemented, but as it was up for tender in 2015, it may become reality at some point
again. With the increased amount of energy from renewable energy sources, the con-
ventional power plants are seeing decreasing proﬁt. (THEMA Consulting Group,
2015). Many power plants are also at the end of their technical lifetime and during
2008-2013 35% of the Danish power plant capacity has been taken off the market
(Capion and Larsen, 2017). This challenges the capacity adequacy of the power sys-
tem. As mentioned the intraday market is expected to expand. The intraday market,
as mentioned is currently open until 1 hour before the hour of delivery. Best Green
could place sell bids on the intraday market, assuming scheduled for purchase dur-
ing the hour in question and let the gas boiler cover the heat load.

Gains from DR
The thesis examined the gains from DR in SCENA, SCENB, SCENB*, SCENC, and
SCENC* by comparing the results (Objective, NPV, volume-weighted power price)
to those of the Basecase and Basecase*. The highest gains from DR were found using
settlement model A.
As noted, the optimal DR case was SCENC* applying settlement model A. Only
using the 2013 spot prices did it show a 1% higher NPV than SCENB*. The remaining
years no difference was found between SCENC* and SCENB*. Comparing the DR
case SCENA to the non-DR case Basecase* 3 out of 4 years of spot prices showed no
difference in NPV.
In no case was the gas boiler ownership a beneﬁt. Taking over the ownership of
the gas boiler was assumed to be free of charge, but an additional OPEX cost and
the cost of the gas consumption was imposed on Best Green. The options SCENB*
and SCENC*, where the ﬂexibility of the gas boiler is made use of, but the own-
ership lies at the shool yielded better results for both settlement models. Applying
settlement model A, improvements of the NPV between 18%-24% were found, when
using the HHPs for DR. Using settlement model B for SCENB* and SCENC* the NPV
improves by 4%-11%.
The ﬁndings of this thesis regarding the savings from spot price optimization
compared to basecase are showing the same tendencies as Biegel, Andersen, et al.
(2013). The proﬁts from price optimization found in the study of Biegel described in
section 1.1.2 are higher than in this thesis. Bidding into 1) the DA market and 2) the
DA and the regulating power market reduced the average spot price by 21 % and
24% respectively. This thesis only yielded between 1%- 7% reduction. Similarly the
total costs were reduced by 18%-20% in (Biegel, Andersen, et al., 2013), whereas only
a 1% reduction was found for SCENA. As seen in the sensitivity analysis, the mass
of the buffer tank applied in SCENA is not optimal. This may explain the identiﬁed
lower gains.
Good et al. (2013) has likewise looked at the possible savings of implementing a
TES for terraced and semi-detached building. The electricity costs can be reduced by
1.8%-1.96% for 145 L and 2.05%-2.62% introducing a TES of 300 L. The HP maximum
rated power is varied. This corresponds to the sensitivity analysis performed in
this study of the nominal heat capacity. Increasing the power of the HPs by 25%
in the study further increases the cost reductions to 2.07%-2.64%, i.e. only marginal
improvements are seen compared to the improvements of adding a TES. Good et al.
(2013) only looks at the cost reductions of the power consumption and does not take
into account the investments of the TES. This thesis performed a mass optimization,
where the investments of larger buffer tanks were taken into account. If no buffer
tank is available at Best Green’s project sites, investments in 3000 L and 2500 L for
SCENA and SCENB respectively are the most viable.
A British study Schachter, Good, and Mancarella (2015) studies the gains from
optimization of HP operation based upon DA-prices in the UK compared to non-
optimized operation, termed base case in this thesis.
The model is a two-stage
stochastic model, where 1) the DA optimization according to price forecasts is per-
formed and 2) the settlement is conducted with the actual prices of the hour. Un-
der the actual prices the optimized model yielded annual electricity cost savings of
66£/house. The study has varied the mean and volatility of the DA-prices. The high-
est beneﬁts are found for higher volatility with linear dependency between annual
changes in cost and volatility changes. This study shows ⇠3% decrease in annual

costs for a 50% increase in volatility, where this thesis showed 0.6-1% decrease in
annual cost for the same change in volatility.
Baeten, Rogiers, and Helsen (2017) studies the effect of DR participation from
500.000 HPs used for residential space heating in Belgium. The model is performing
a trade-off between local- and system-costs. It is found that the required genera-
tion capacity can be signiﬁcantly reduced. When implementing a TES the amount

Business Model
Depending on whether it is decided to continue with the Basecase or shift to either
Basecase*, SCENA, SCENB, SCENB*, SCENC or SCENC* new business models may
be necessary.
Basecase* can yield 1%-2% increase in NPV by changing supplier contract to

SCENA resulted in 1%-3% increase in NPV. This scenario requires Insero to form

SCENB* showed NPV improvements of 18%-22% and 4%-5% using settlement

ing aggregator as SCENA*, but with more incentive to overcome these.
Lastly applying SCENC* yielded the highest NPV with improvements of 18%-

Choice of model, Assumptions & Improvements
4.2.1
HP Model and Set-point Temperature
First of all, it was chosen to model the entire year using the uniform-temperature
model instead of the multinode model. The high ﬂow through the tank did not
fulﬁll the underlying assumption of stratiﬁcation of the multinode model and hence
would not depict the actual situation of the tank.
In Schütz et al. (2015) a comparison between the multinode (’stratiﬁed TES’) and
the uniform-temperature (’simpliﬁed TES’) as well as a simpliﬁed and a detailed
(’online’) HP model is performed. The simpliﬁed HP model uses assumptions of
the ambient temperature as source temperature and the nominal ﬂow temperature
of the heating curve as sink temperature, whereas the detailed HP model uses the
==PAGE:78==
Chapter 4. Discussion
65
source temperature resulting from the optimization model during each time step.
For an optimization of an entire year the choice of HP model has a larger impact on
the model than the TES model. Regardless of the TES model, applying the simpliﬁed
HP model leads to an underestimation of operational costs of 28% compared to the
detailed HP with the multinode model TES due to an overestimation of heat output.
This number is only 4% for the detailed HP model combined with the uniform-
temperature TES model.
This thesis has used a version of the simpliﬁed HP model, but only varying the
source temperature and using the stated COP for an assumed constant ﬂow tem-
perature of 45◦C. This is the average ﬂow temperature of the available data from
Best Green for Nov. 2016 - Apr. 2017, i.e. the coldest months of the year. As the
set point temperature today is based upon a heating curve, the ﬂow temperature
during the remaining months should be lower. This points to the model possibly
underestimating the COP by using the 45◦C all year round.
In Glembin et al. (2015) applying a variable set point temperature according to
a weather compensation curve rather than a constant set point temperature leads
to lower power consumption due to lower condenser temperature when lower am-
bient temperatures occur. This could indicate that the power consumption in this
thesis is overestimated. On the other hand, the heat load is a function of the source
temperature, hence most heat is consumed during the coldest hours, which could
result in an underestimation. In conclusion, to improve the reliability of the results,
the HP model should be reviewed to take into account the possible under- or overes-
timation of power consumption. As the sensitivity analysis proved the NPV highly
sensitive to the ﬂow temperature, this improvement is considered very relevant.
A set-point temperature depending on the weather compensation curve was
sought implemented, but the GAMS optimization model proved infeasible. If larger
masses for the storage were applied, the model could run. This suggests that the
capacity constraints of the thermal storage were too tight to reach the required tem-
perature. In the optimization of Schütz et al. (2015) it was possible to apply the
varying set point temperature as lower bound for TES temperature. This could be
explained by simulation inputs of storage of 1000 kg with a peak thermal heating
demand of 5.2 kW using a HP with nominal heating capacity of 9.60 kW1. In the
case of Best Green (and this optimization model) the thermal storage amounted to
1600 kg for a peak thermal heating demand of around 90 kW and a nominal HP
heating capacity of two times 47 kW2. Instead this model applied a minimum and
a maximum allowable TES temperature. This means that during certain hours the
TES temperature does not fulﬁll the requirements of the demand.

Heat load and heat production
The simulation of the heat load was conducted as mentioned in section 2.4.1. The
heat load applied in the optimization model is 4368 hours of historical data based
on the condenser heat and the remaining is estimated. For the extension of data the
remaining heat load is distributed with inverse proportional respect to the temper-
ature of each hour. As seen in ﬁg. 2.8 these two approches create different patterns.
Instead the heat load of the entire year could also have been simulated using an
assumed UA-value of Hatting Skoles premises.
As mentioned the applied method, does not take into account the heat loss over
the pipes and TES or the possible time lag due to the storage potential of the TES.

As the latter is very limited due to the storage vs demand ratio, this may be a fair

In the model, it is assumed that the gas boilers can turn on for any amount of
heat. To better reﬂect the actual on/off options of the boiler, its turndown ratio
should be applied. Turndown ratio, being the maximum ﬁring rate divided by the
minimum ﬁring rate is typically 10:1-12:1 and some even up to 35:1 (Ofﬁce of Energy

of re-starts, hereby reducing the losses from heating up the heat exchanger (Pilaar,
2007). Additionally, the thermal efﬁciency is higher for low-ﬁre than for high-ﬁre
(Durkin, 2006). This thesis does not vary the gas boiler efﬁciency depending on its
heat output or frequency of on/off cycles. On the other hand this thesis runs the
model with time steps of one hour, which limits the on/off-cycles.
The heat generated by the HPs can only attain its nominal value, 47 kW per
HP, i.e. the variations in mass ﬂow rate and temperature difference between return
temperature and ﬂow temperature are not accounted for.
4.2.3
Optimization model & Data
This thesis assesses the proﬁt from DR of the HHPs compared to a base case without
DR, i.e. where the heat production is controlled by the TES temperature. The base
case in the optimization model is using the COP to estimate the power consumption
during the given hour. This means, that it can optimize for COP, which is not the
case at the moment. It may thereby underestimate the current base case cost and
hence the earnings from optimizing the run. To avoid the COP-optimization, the
model could be run for a ﬂat COP and schedule the hours of power consumption
solely based on the load. Hereby the expenses could be recalculated using the COP
of the WAMAK varying each hour. This though would lead to differences in the heat
supplied.
In the optimization strategies, the aggregator would be able to use forecasts for
COP depending on e.g. the weather forecasts, as applied in the model. In reality
though the COPs are not deterministic, but probabilistic. One can rely on weather
forecasts for planning the schedule, but uncertainty exists regarding the forecasts.
Hereby as the optimization model runs the HPs during the hours with better COPs,
they may in fact not be the most cost-efﬁcient hours due to the uncertainty.
The model is simulated with hourly time steps, following the granularity of the
day-market prices. The HP though is able to turn on/off for shorter time intervals, as
seen in ﬁg. 1.3 and hence the ﬂexibility of the HHPs is underestimated in the model.
Running the model for shorter time steps may hence result in increased potential of
the optimized scenarios. This could also help to explain, why the model is infeasible
when applying a weather compensation curve for the set point temperature.
The current model and actual system at Hatting School holds two HPs and one
gas boiler. It could be interesting to add additional heat pumps in the model and
perform the cash ﬂow analysis with the increased investment costs, and presumably
lower objective. The sensitivity analysis showed that varying the nominal heat from
the HPs did not improve the NPV, but additional HPs possibly with lower nominal
heat may.

Future practice
According to several studies (Hedegaard and Münster, 2013; Baeten, Rogiers, and
Helsen, 2017) DR is socio-economically feasible. It is beneﬁcial to the power system
as a whole as it can allow integration of more wind in the energy system, reduce the
generation capacity and support the electriﬁcation of the system. So to support the
increased DR participation the grounds for private-investors must be incentivizing.
This study has found that, given the business case of Best Green at Hatting
School, providing DR is private-economically feasible, particularly under settlement
model A. Implementing load shifting and regulating power market participation
(SCENC*) yields the highest NPV. The earnings from the regulating power market
are relatively small, considering the costs of aquiring the required real-time meter.
In the case of Hatting School where HaaS is already implemented the suggestions
for future practice are thus:
Hourly settlement With settlement model B in place with Hatting school, the earn-
ings from providing DR are limited and require establishing an aggregator.
Hourly settlement would yield small improvements, but can easily be imple-
mented by changing the electricity supplier contract. The latter is hence sug-

Chapter 5
Conclusion
This study investigated the feasibility of implementing gas-electric hybrid heat pumps
(HHP) into the Danish power market using a speciﬁc HaaS concept with Best Green
and Hatting School as a case. Three scenarios were optimized according to the day-
ahead prices (SCENA, SCENB) and the regulating power prices (SCENC) and com-
pared to a basecase without price optimization. In SCENB and SCENC the gas boiler
could provide heat to support the heat pump.
Running the model for four different years of prices, the ﬂexibility potential was
investigated.
With a TES of 1600L the ﬂexibility was limited without using the
gas boiler. 1%-7% reduction in volume-weighted spot price was found in SCENA,
whereas this range was 3%-7% in SCENB. Up to half of the savings derived from
simply shifting to hourly settlement without load shifting.
A cash-ﬂow analysis was performed using two different settlement models; A
and B. Given the business case of Best Green at Hatting School, providing DR was
found private-economically feasible, particularly under settlement model A. The
highest NPV was achieved by implementing load shifting and regulating power
market participation. Under settlement model A the NPV could be increased 18%-
24% compared to the basecase. This result was found assuming that Best Green were
not owners of the gas boilers, but still being able to use and control them. In no case
was it more viable for Best Green to own the gas boilers.
Through a mass optimization it was found most optimal to invest in a 3000 L TES
in SCENA and a 2500 L TES in SCENB, if buffer tank investments had not already
been made. In the case of Hatting School, where a buffer tank is already in place,
new investments do not improve the NPV.
This thesis concludes, that there are economical gains from implementing the
HHPs into the Danish power market; DA and regulating power market. The study
recommends though, for the particular case of Hatting School, to seek an hourly set-
tled spot contract. The fact that settlement model B is already in place decreases the
incentive for DR participation. Regarding possible future projects, contracts with
settlement model A should be pursued and investments should be made in the op-
timal TES size. The earnings from the regulating power market are relatively small,
considering the costs of aquiring the required real-time meter. It is therefore recom-
mended to participate with load shifting, given that a reasonable aggregator solution
is found.
The NPV proved sensitive towards changes in heat load and average ﬂow tem-
perature, but within reasonable changes, the NPV remained positive. Yet, in order to
verify the gains from DR or hourly settlement, further examination of the expected
heat load proﬁle should be made.
