Abstract
This project is centred around developing and validating a three-dimensional numerical model
for inverse electromagnetic scattering aimed at reconstructing biological nanostructured surfaces
from reflectance measurements. To do so, we first consider the forward scattering problem, which
we solve using the Method of Auxiliary Sources, leveraging its eﬀiciency and accuracy.
The inverse problem is addressed within a Bayesian framework using a Gaussian process prior
and the preconditioned Crank–Nicolson algorithm for eﬀicient posterior sampling.
The model architecture is implemented using C++ combined with a limited use of embedded
Python. The forward model is validated by checking whether the transmission conditions are
upheld. Additional validation attempts using power and comparisons with finite difference sim-
ulations are explored using Meep, offering valuable qualitative insight but limited quantitative
agreement.
Results demonstrate that the model achieves acceptable errors for transmission conditions on
certain surfaces, supporting its potential as a computational tool for surface characterisation.
The process and final results were significantly impacted by the time-consuming implementation
and debugging of the forward model, which delayed some tasks and rendered others impossible to
take on within the available timeframe. We therefore also outline a plan for further development
of the solver.
All implementations done in this project can be found on our GitHub repository, https://
github.com/persilleChristie/bioscat3d.

Biological nanocoatings occur in many places in na-
ture and can exhibit properties such as hydrophobic-
ity or anti-reflectivity.
Understanding the surfaces
can therefore offer inspiration for synthetic materi-
als mimicking these properties. It is possible to char-
acterise such surfaces using methods such as atomic
force microscopy (AFM), electron microscopy, reflec-
tometry, and polarimetry [1, 2, 3].
The term nanocoatings means naturally occurring
surfaces, where the structures on the surfaces are on
the scale of 10−9 m, also referred to as nanostruc-
tures. Figure 1.1 offers two examples of what such a
surface could look like.
The findings of such nanocoatings inspire efforts to
assess the microscopic structures characterising the
surface more eﬀiciently. Rather than relying solely
on physical measurements, one can hope to acceler-
ate the investigation, understanding, and utilisation
of functional nanocoatings using mathematical mod-
elling. In this thesis, the aim is to use the interaction
between light and biological nanostructures to char-
acterise the nanostructures on the surface.
From a physical and mathematical perspective, the
problem of interest is an inverse electromagnetic
scattering problem.
The interaction of light with
the surface and underlying substrate is governed
by Maxwell’s equations. Although electromagnetic
scattering is well understood in classical physics, the
nanoscale nature of the problem introduces non-trivial behaviour. In particular, the similarity
between the characteristic scale of surface features and the wavelength of light leads to complex
effects that complicate analytical solutions.
We develop a three-dimensional model for the problem and provide the underlying mathematical
framework and justification. We also offer a model for the inverse problem. Together, these two
parts give the tools to identify surface geometry without relying on physical height measure-

We approach the forward problem using the Method of Auxiliary Sources (MAS), primarily
inspired by [4] and [5]. MAS is a surface-based discretisation technique that is both fast and
accurate for electromagnetic scattering problems. Rather than solving the complete Maxwell
system directly, we approximate the scattered field as a linear combination of fields generated
by a finite number of auxiliary sources located near the surface.
Given a suﬀiciently fast and accurate forward solver, a Bayesian framework can be employed to
solve the inverse problem. Because the forward model must be evaluated thousands of times in
such a setup, computational eﬀiciency is crucial. In our work, we employ a Gaussian process
prior in conjunction with the preconditioned Crank–Nicolson (pCN) scheme for eﬀicient sampling
acceptance/rejection.
The Gaussian process prior enforces smoothness on the reconstructed
surface while also allowing the incorporation of prior knowledge and having excellent flexibility.
We present theory related to the forward problem in Chapter 2. Notably, we account for relevant
electromagnetic concepts, the MAS model and the theory of array equations. In Chapter 3, the
inverse solver is presented; here, the theory of Gaussian processes, the Bayesian framework,
Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling and pCN is covered. In Chapter 4, we present our work
and considerations on implementation. We outline the work done on the forward and inverse
solvers, as well as the structures, languages, utilities, and packages used. In Chapter 5, we
present the validation of the model. We have used two different approaches to the validation
of the model. The first is calculating the transmission errors. The second is based on power
comparison. In the last chapters, we discuss the results, suggest further work, and conclude
with the findings and achievements of the work.

Chapter 2
The Forward Scattering Problem
The forward scattering problem is at the core of the problem we are addressing in this thesis.
Before we can hope to solve the inverse scattering problem, we need a fast, accurate and validated
forward solver.
Solving the forward problem involves determining the electromagnetic field
scattered by a scatterer, specifically a surface with nanostructures, illuminated by incident
light. Solving such a system analytically is unlikely to be feasible, and solving it with classical
numerical methods is likely very computationally expensive. Therefore, an approximate method
is needed to obtain a workable inverse scattering solver.
In this project, we will use the Method of Auxiliary Sources.
We choose MAS because it
has shown improvements in both computational eﬀiciency, accuracy and numerical stability
compared to alternative methods [6]. MAS is a discretisation method that approximates the
field in a system by fields emitted by a set of auxiliary sources. The sources are placed on both
sides of the surface considered, and then transmission conditions are enforced on the boundaries.
These conditions yield a linear system whose solution is the amplitudes of the auxiliary sources,
which can then be used to approximate the scattered field. In general, the types and placements
of sources can be done in various ways, and different kinds of sources can be used. For this
project, we will use Hertzian dipoles.
In the following, we first present the assumptions made in the project. We then outline the
necessary theory of electromagnetism, including Maxwell’s equations, tangential components,
and transmission conditions, as well as calculations of the fields from a uniform plane wave and
from Hertzian dipoles. We then describe two approaches to modelling a surface and build the
final linear system according to the latter. This allows us to construct a solution and calculate
results, which can be compared to measurements.
As we will address later, there is a significant risk of computational challenges with solving the
linear system. Aiming to circumvent these issues, we will lastly present the theory of array
equations and briefly account for and exemplify their use in this context.

For the modelling, we assume that the materials are dielectric and non-magnetic, meaning that

they provide a useful starting point for constructing a computational model, and it is reasonable
to expect that they can yield accurate approximations of the surface geometries, as well as guide
future developments.

Method of Auxiliary Sources
We now have the theory in place to address the Method of Auxiliary Sources. The method
requires a discretisation of the surface that we consider. We will therefore introduce test points
denoting points on the surface. Moreover, we will refer to auxiliary points, which are points with
two Hertzian dipoles placed in orthogonal directions. The total field values can be approximated
as a linear combination of the fields of such dipoles and the incident field.
Inspired by literature addressing similar tasks as ours, particularly [5] and [10], we have con-
sidered two different setups. Our initial approach to modelling the surface was that of several
nanowires on a flat substrate, as visualised in Figure 2.3a. In the later approach, we addressed
the surface more directly as a single surface separating air and substrate, see Figure 2.3b

2.3.3
Placing Points in MAS
Having settled the general setup for the single surface approach, we must now decide how to
place the test points and auxiliary points. The goal is to satisfy the transmission conditions
(2.15) over the entire surface. Since we are discretising the surface in the form of test points, we
cannot expect to satisfy the transmission conditions everywhere on the surface. If we set up the
system such that (2.16) has a unique solution, we get a solution that satisfies the transmission
conditions at all test points. However, we cannot guarantee that the resulting approximation
will have reasonable errors for transmission conditions at surface points that are not test points.
To get around this issue, we instead build an overdetermined system by choosing more test
points, such that 2M > (N′ + N′′). This will typically make the system inconsistent, so instead
of finding an exact solution, we approximate a solution by minimising the least squares error,
y = arg min
˜y
∥A˜y −b∥L2.
With this approach, we can hope for a solution that has minor errors everywhere, as opposed to
zero errors in the test points. As a starting point, we make the system doubly-overdetermined,
i.e. M = (N′ + N′′). To obtain a doubly overdetermined system, we place approximately two
test points per dipole pair.
Next, we must consider where to place the auxiliary points and how many to use. We place
both the test points and reference points equidistantly along the x- and y-axes. After defining a
grid for the auxiliary points, we choose the two dipole directions as the tangents of the surface
at the corresponding grid point. We then place the auxiliary points at a certain distance from
the surface along the normal at the corresponding reference point.
We must be cautious when deciding both the number of auxiliary points and the distance to
the surface. Particularly, the system matrix can become ill-conditioned if the distances between
the auxiliary sources and test points are too large [5]. However, we will later see that too little
distance between sources can also cause issues, as discussed in Chapter 5. We outline a proposed
method of placing points in algorithm 1.

The Inverse Scattering Solver
In the inverse problem, we aim to determine the surface structure based on reflectance measure-
ments. Solving this analytically is typically impractical, even in the best case, and very costly,
if possible at all. We therefore intend to solve the problem using statistical inference, where the
goal is to draw conclusions about unobserved values based on numerical data. Specifically, we
will use Bayesian inference, where the statistical findings are conditioned on the observed value
[14, pp. 4-6].
The basis of the Bayesian framework is Bayes’ theorem, also known as Bayes’ rule. Based on
observed measurements R, we can determine the probability of a certain shape of the surface,
U, being the cause of the measurements. That is, the probability that the surface has a certain
shape, given a set of measurements. This is called the posterior density p(U|R), and Bayes’ rule
states,
p(U|R) = p(U)p(R|U)
p(R)
,
where p(U) is the prior, p(R|U) is the likelihood and p(R) denotes the marginal probability of
observing that measurement.
The prior represents our beliefs, assumptions, or knowledge about the surface. Sampling from
this distribution allows us to generate plausible realisations of what the surface might look like.
The likelihood represents the probability density of the observed measurements, conditioned on
a specific shape of the surface.
We will use the forward model to calculate the likelihood by comparing the model prediction
based on some surface with the observed data. This surface is generated by sampling from the
prior. We will use a Gaussian process prior. For a Gaussian process, we will need to determine
the kernel, mean function and parameters. These can be chosen to fit existing knowledge about
the actual data. The estimate is then refined using Markov Chain Monte Carlo, where new
samples are proposed and either accepted or rejected according to a specified criterion. Based
on the accepted guesses, we can then estimate the posterior distribution.
3.1

For sampling surfaces, we use a Gaussian process prior. Realisations of this process correspond
to randomly chosen functions, with smoothness properties based on the selected parameters. It
is highly flexible and therefore well-suited as a prior in cases where there is limited knowledge
of the function or where generality is desirable.
In this project, we will use the following definition of a Gaussian process.

3.3
Markov Chain Monte Carlo
Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is a method that draws values of U from approximate
distributions. It then iteratively corrects the draws to get a better approximation of the target
posterior, p(U|R). MCMC is used when it is impossible or infeasible to draw samples of U from
p(U|R). The samples are instead drawn iteratively, and the idea of the method is then that in
each step, we expect to draw from a distribution closer to the desired posterior [14, p. 275].
Since the distribution sampled at each step only depends on the previous sample, the samples
form a Markov Chain. Let us define precisely what that means.
Definition 3.2 (Markov Chain). Let Un be a stochastic process with values in a set E. If for
any states u1, u2, ..., uk, un in E and any integers n1 < n2 < · · · < nk < n, we have
p(Un = xn|Un1 = u1, Un2 = u2, ..., Unk = uk) = p(Un = xn|Unk = uk).
Then Un is a Markov Chain [18, p. 4].
We will use the MCMC method in the form of the preconditioned Crank-Nicholson (pCN)
scheme [15] to update our samples for the chain, as it is eﬀicient for high-dimensional problems
[19]. Following our analysis in (3.1), and omitting constant terms, because they cancel in the
likelihood ratio, the log-likelihood is

The details of pCN are described in algorithm 2. We generally expect a need for many iterations
to achieve convergence. The convergence time depends on several factors, one of which is the
chosen values of δ and γ, so handling these values effectively will be important. We could also
have too highly correlated samples, resulting in fewer effective samples from the distribution
[15].
Choosing the parameters δ and γ involves balancing the exploration and convergence
rates.
If we choose a value that is too small for δ, our proposal will be very close to the
previously accepted value, and the algorithm will converge to the true posterior slowly. On the
other hand, if we choose a too large δ, we will not use the knowledge gathered over iterations
to get closer to the posterior. Selecting a small γ will mean that we accept more proposals,
which is beneficial initially, as we want the algorithm to explore the state space. However, as
the algorithm progresses, we want to fine-tune the search to ensure that we stay close to the
suggested posterior.

Estimating the Nanostructure
When the algorithm has terminated, the samples should, in theory, represent the posterior
distribution. However, since the MCMC method is an iterative process, the first samples are
likely to be quite far from the actual distribution. We therefore introduce a burn-in period. The
idea is that the method requires some iterations before it is tuned, and we will consequently
discard the results from the burn-in, as they cannot be assumed to be representative of the
posterior distribution.
Based on the choice made in [14, p. 282], we suggest discarding the first half of the result. After
some initial trials, a less conservative approach might be considered to reduce the number of
needed iterations.

Implementation
With the theory covered in Chapters 2 and 3, we can now move on to implementations. In this
chapter, we will present a combination of implementation architecture and choices, as well as
programs and packages, and address some computational issues.
We have implemented several components and subcomponents of the model, and for related
tasks.
The most critical piece is the forward solver, as it is the core machinery for solving
the inverse scattering problem. However, several other components are also essential for the
implementation to work and for calculating the relevant results.
We have implemented the
forward model, the inverse model and the calculation modules. In addition, we have implemented
test and validation setups, simulation tools and tools for plotting and processing data.
A perfect implementation of a solver for the problem we address would include two main parts.
Firstly, an extremely fast and accurate forward solver that can calculate the scattered field from
a given surface, from which reflectance could be calculated. Secondly, an inverse solver that can
utilise the forward solver to determine the probability densities of the individual surface heights.
Besides the core parts, a significant number of utilities would be required, and the setup would
need to be fully optimised to utilise high-performance computing (HPC) techniques. This task
is a big undertaking, and we will outline a way forward in Chapter 7.
In our implementation, we have focused on making the code fit for further development. Particu-
larly, we have implemented the model in C++ instead of using a high-level language like Python,
and we have considered at all stages how to prepare for optimisation and HPC calculations.
4.1
Software Choices and Architecture
Given the high computational cost and memory demands of the problem, we chose to implement
the model in C++. We selected C++ as the implementation language due to its execution speed,
extensive ecosystem, and strong support for scientific computing. While a C++ implementation
requires more manual effort compared to higher-level languages, we expected it to offer a good
balance of performance, control, and flexibility. Moreover, we expected it to be more relevant
for future expansions to the work done here.
C++ offers easily usable packages for many relevant tasks. Particularly, Eigen [20] has been a
highly valuable library, offering implementations of matrix algebra, numerical solvers, and more.
We have chosen to use Eigen, since it is an eﬀicient way to work with dense matrices and large
vectors. It should also provide a smooth transition to HPC.
For the specific task of surface interpolation, we use Python. Given time constraints, we sought
a ready-made and reliable implementation of bicubic splines, which we found in the SciPy

We intended to use Meep through a Python interface as a validation tool for our forward model.
Meep is a finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) method for Python, used for simulating elec-
tromagnetic fields [23]. It is intended to produce simulated data that we could use for validation
purposes.
To become acquainted with Meep, we first set up a simulation of a two-dimensional UPW
incident on a scatterer on a surface. From there, we moved to three dimensions and began
implementing a simulation with an arbitrary angle of incidence and variable polarisation. The

early scripts, as well as the latest and most relevant version we have produced. We refer to
Meep’s oﬀicial webpage for details about the components of a Meep simulation.

4.3
Computational Issues
During the project, we encountered some issues that can cause the solver to be either very slow
or even crash. Firstly, the memory usage of the system matrix from the equation system might
exceed the memory available to the CPU; when this happens, the system crashes. Secondly,
large runtimes can be large due to the size of the test cases; when this happens, you need a lot
of computing power or time to be able to achieve a result. Time like this is not available since
the inverse solver needs to run thousands of times.
4.3.1
Memory Usage and Scaling
Memory usage is a critical concern.
The main computational task in the forward model is
solving the linear system, and the size of the system matrix increases rapidly with the size of the
computational domain. As a result, storing and accessing these matrices becomes a significant
factor and a likely bottleneck in the overall computation.
Assume that we work with a quadratic sample of size b × b and that the incident wave has
wavelength λ.
Working with a auxiliary points per wavelength, we will have N′ = N′′ =
N = (ab/λ)2 dipole pairs in each material, and the system matrix will have 2(N′ + N′′) = 4N
columns. Since we want the system to be doubly overdetermined, we therefore need 4M = 2·4N,
or M = 2(ab/λ)2.
All elements of the matrix are complex doubles, meaning that the memory usage per element is
128 bytes. The total memory usage, T, will therefore be
T(a, b, λ) = 4M · 4N · 128 B = 4096

point, and exploring the feasibility of solving models of different sizes is highly relevant. For this
reason, we suggest using array equations, introduced in Section 2.4, as a promising remedy, as
it enables field calculations for larger surfaces while only running MAS on smaller patches.
4.3.2
Parallelisation and Computational Optimisation
The computation time required to run the forward model is substantial. Even a relatively small
setup can take several minutes to solve on a CPU. Depending on wavelength and polarisation,
we have experienced runtimes of up to 30 minutes for surfaces as small as 3µm × 3µm. In the
inverse problem setting, where the forward solver must be executed thousands of times, this
becomes a critical bottleneck. As such, careful attention to runtime optimisation is essential for
a practical implementation.
The good news is that the model lends itself well to parallelisation. In both the forward and
the inverse solver, similar calculations need to be repeated for a large number of points. Given
suﬀicient memory and cores, the overall wall-clock runtime can surely be significantly improved.
Achieving realistic runtimes for samples of a full-size problem will require significant optimisa-
tion, both in the code setup and particularly in the use of HPC resources. Solving a relatively
small sub-problem instead of the full-scale problem might solve part of the problem while still
enabling the solution of a system relevant to scientists working on the physical problem. Still,
optimisation should be done in any case.

Chapter 5
Validating the Forward Solver
In this chapter, we will present the results and details of the validation process we have per-
formed. An important feature and advantage of MAS is that it is a radiation boundary integral
method that fulfils Maxwell’s system by construction. Since it consists only of a finite set of
sources, each of which is a solution to Maxwell’s system, and because this system is linear, we are
guaranteed to obtain a physical solution when applying MAS. Hence, we do not need to validate
that the resulting solution satisfies Maxwell’s system; we only need to validate the correctness
of the solution.
To validate the correctness of the model and the implementation, we will analyse transmission
conditions on the boundaries. Minor errors in transmission conditions on the interface suggest
that the system is well approximated in a neighbourhood of the interface. If this is the case, then
it follows that the approximation is accurate throughout the domain. For further validation, we
investigate how polarisation and wavelength affect the transmission errors. Another means of
validation, we have explored, is to compare our results with those from simulation tools. The
idea in this setup is to compare the power over a given surface between the model and the
simulation.
Firstly, we will test the transmission errors. In Chapter 2, we derived the transmission conditions
(2.9), which we used in the forward model to find the amplitudes needed to calculate the fields.
We also motivated why we solve an overdetermined system, hoping that this would make the
errors in transmission conditions over the entire surface small, while allowing for some non-zero
errors in the test points.
Secondly, we have used Meep to obtain simulated results for comparison with the model re-
sults. As introduced in Chapter 4, Meep is an FDTD solver, but it includes some frequency-
domain functionalities, and frequency-domain results can be extracted from time-domain sim-
ulations [23]. Showing the same tendencies in the Poynting flux over a given surface would
be a strong indication that the model provides a good approximation, since the results would
stem from radically different approaches to the problem. Since the field values can be scaled
arbitrarily by changing the amplitude of the incident field, the numerical values should not be
given weight in these comparisons.

Chapter 6
Discussion
The goal of this project was to develop a mathematical model to solve the inverse scattering
problem. To this end, we needed to develop and implement an eﬀicient solver of the forward
problem. Based on this implementation, we then had to implement an inverse solver. Lastly, to
run the model on real data, we needed a highly optimised HPC implementation.
The main goal was to create a working forward model, a challenge that required us to familiarise
ourselves with a significant amount of theory, including electromagnetism. Simultaneously, we
embarked on an ambitious course that required us to learn a wide range of programming-specific
topics, in addition to the mathematical and physical topics. Particularly, building and validating
a basic implementation of the forward model required a significant amount of time.
Writing a mathematically complex code setup with many components and interactions proved
challenging beyond our expectations. We have had to moderate ambitions, particularly in the
tuning of the model and the processing and production of results. While we have implemented
a functioning and mathematically correct forward solver, it took a lot longer than we had hoped
to get it working and validated. As a result, many other tasks that we might have expected to
address earlier were not feasible to undertake at the time. Ideally, we would have done a lot more
work on analysing results, optimising the code and running HPC calculations. Time pressure
also meant that we settled for a ready-made Python implementation of spline interpolation;
something we would have built in C++ to ensure seamless integration, given more time.
Looking back, there are many things we would have done differently. Notably, we would strive
to adopt a significantly more professional approach to coding. We would aim to map the code
structure precisely, use systematic organisation, testing and especially debugging. Only when
we used advanced debugging tools did we manage to eliminate the last error in the forward
model.
Because we were learning both coding and structuring along the way, several tasks
ended up being harder to solve than they needed to be. However, if we had to go back and
redefine the problem we took on, we would not do so. We chose the problem at hand because it
encompassed several aspects that we wanted to learn about. We entered this project with limited
experience in all relevant subjects, except, of course, mathematics, so it was not surprising that
the work was challenging. It was unexpected that it took so long to get a working forward solver,
which we considered a necessary prerequisite for starting other processes, specifically testing the
inverse model and migrating to HPC. So, the only regrets we have are that we did not develop
a professionalised approach to coding earlier, and that the debugging held us back for so long,
holding us back from learning HPC implementation.
In the following sections, we will discuss the results, findings, and the process we have undergone.

6.1
Validation
To validate the model, we have compared errors in transmission conditions. We have found that
we can choose parameters to get error below the 5% mark for a representative surface.
As part of the model, we have implemented Algorithm 1 to determine the distance between the
surface and sources, as well as the subsequent placement of sources. The main idea behind the
algorithm was to develop a fully automated solver that could work on any surface, even with
no knowledge of the structure. Through our validation, we quickly realised that the relatively
simple algorithm we had developed was insuﬀicient to achieve good results. However, we believe
the algorithm may become relevant in conjunction with other adjustments made to improve the
model’s performance for a broader range of surfaces and surface patches.
To validate other parts of the solver, we therefore did tests using fixed radii. In practice, a
distance of approximately one wavelength between the surface and sources, corresponding to a
radius of 5, proved to perform best for the wavelengths that we were able to run on CPUs. We
consider it validated that the model is working for the right choices of parameters. The errors
we observe are likely connected to geometric and numerical complications or arise due to errors
in derived calculations of field values or flux.
For varying polarisation, we have observed that the model performs well for different polari-
sations given the right parameters, and we have seen no signs that it should not work for all
polarisations. The implementation should, in theory, work for oblique incidence. This is not
something that we have tested or validated. Testing oblique incidence, polarisation should be
revisited to investigate whether it still works when the angle of incidence changes. For now,
however, we consider the model to be working and validated for any polarisation.
Wavelengths play a considerable part in the setup. We ideally want to be able to calculate for
all wavelengths in the visible spectrum. Running the model for all relevant wavelengths has not
been feasible on CPUs. Therefore, we only have validation for wavelengths between 600 and 800
nm. Apart from computational issues, small wavelengths have also resulted in diﬀiculties with
numerical stability. We have decided the number of sources linearly based on wavelength. We
suspect that an alternative approach might be better suited for the task, where the number of
sources is handled differently in relation to curvature, wavelength and surface size. Particularly,
we believe that MAS has issues with sources placed too close together, because the auxiliary
points are singularities. To place more sources effectively, we need to consider different methods
for positioning them. One approach might be to place them in layers at different distances
from the surface. This way, you can have more sources without having them too close to each
other. Another approach could be to place sources in the direction of the normals, so that each
auxiliary point has three dipoles instead of two. In any case, we believe that ideas along these
lines should be explored and tested, provided that a HPC-optimised implementation is available.
We have observed that the model performs poorly on small patches. This is unfortunate, as cal-
culating small patches is fast, and we had hoped that array equations would provide a framework
for solving the full system quickly. We still have hopes that this might succeed, but optimi-
sations for small patches are needed. We have observed that we cannot achieve appropriate
accuracy before encountering numerical instability on small surfaces. To use array equations,
we therefore either need a new way of placing sources or an optimisation of the code so that we
can feasibly consider larger patches.
All in all, we have achieved partial validation based on errors in transmission conditions. Wave-
lengths, source placements and surface dimensions all seem to influence the results, and we have
only been able to validate the model for subsets of the parameters and dimensions.

6.2.
PERSPECTIVES FOR MAS
We have also explored the idea of validating the power calculation. This can be done by compar-
ing it to simulation data, other models, or measurements. For this setup, we have implemented
flux calculations, obtained real data, and performed simulations using Meep. Sadly, the lateness
of the forward model imposed constraints on what we could achieve in this area. Since we have
a model with minor errors in transmission conditions, we expect the near- and far-field calcula-
tions, as well as the resulting flux, to be accurate. During the implementation process, we have
verified field calculations for dipoles and UPW in comparison with alternative implementations
developed by Andreas Hannah. However, the preliminary results for the flux comparison are
not satisfactory. We have not been able to produce similar results in Meep and MAS. At the
time of writing, we are uncertain about the trustworthiness of any of the outputs and do not
have the resources or time to investigate the matter further.
Given that we have so far not been able to validate the flux or field calculation from the model,
we believe that it would be beneficial to compare results from the model to analytical results
based on simple geometries, for instance, using Mie scattering.
6.2
Perspectives for MAS
We find that MAS modelling shows promising behaviour. The model can be run very quickly for
systems that are small relative to the wavelength, and we have been able to produce solutions
with satisfactory tangential errors.
A significant advantage of the model is that it provides
a Maxwellian solution by definition, and any solution produced by the method is inherently
physical. Moreover, the method is flexible and can be adjusted to different setups. We expect
it to work well on the types of problems we address, provided further development is made.
Nonetheless, we have observed numerical issues, including extremely long computation times.
Even for simple surfaces, we found that we could not achieve 5% errors due to numerical instabil-
ity, possibly arising from the excessive number of auxiliary points per wavelength. Placement of
sources is an integral part of a good MAS model, and it is not a trivial task. For this reason, we
propose exploring alternate methods of placing auxiliary points, possibly changing the method
used based on wavelengths and sample sizes.
To make MAS truly useful, the implementation should be migrated to HPC calculations to
enable broader testing and validation of the model behaviour. Along the way, we had planned
to run tests and validations on HPC, but it did not make much sense to attempt setting this up
without a working forward model. When we finally validated some results, we had run out of
time and had to focus on obtaining the results we could produce.
Finally, we note that the approach to testing surfaces we have used could have been better
thought through. We developed test surfaces of a random nature, which we consider relatively
representative of a variety of possibly relevant structures. However, in hindsight, we should have
developed a systematic testing approach based on different surfaces, with structures of varying
size, shape, and smoothness. This again highlights the implementation and debugging as the
primary barrier we encountered.
6.3
Bayesian Inference
We have implemented and illustrated sampling from Gaussian processes. We have implemented
an inverse model using the Bayesian framework. Since we did not have a forward model for the
longest time, and computation times are large, we have only conducted minimal testing of the
inverse setup. We expect it to be functional, but due to a lack of testing, we cannot claim the
correctness of the implementation.
All in all, we find that we have a forward model that we trust is constructed and implemented
mathematically correctly, and a model that we trust can deliver excellent results with further

Chapter 7
Further Work
In this chapter, we outline the path forward for developing an inverse solver setup using MAS
and a Bayesian framework. The goal is to solve inverse electromagnetic scattering problems with
a level of quality that can provide meaningful support to our collaboration partners in Geneva.
Before outlining the practical tasks we believe are important, we want to emphasise that someone
taking on this task should become familiar with the data and the needs we aim to meet from
the outset. A general-purpose model would be very nice, but we believe it is very likely that a
model tailored for the specific problem will be more successful.
Now we outline the most critical tasks that we find relevant to address in a partially prioritised
order. Several of the suggested tasks can be initiated or completed in parallel. Still, we aim to
present them in order of importance, corresponding to the sequence in which we would address
them if we were to continue working on the subject.
The implementation must be migrated to HPC. The calculations are too heavy to be performed
on the CPU, even for testing the forward model, and running the inverse model on the CPU is
completely infeasible. Doing this was always the plan, and realising this plan is of the utmost
importance for the project’s success. Besides just running the code on the GPU using HPC
techniques, the code should also be optimised for this purpose. This can be done gradually
during the implementation, following profiling to identify bottlenecks. Generally, the potential
for parallelisation should be significant. When a well-structured HPC setup is in place, the
forward solver should be rigorously tested throughout the entire parameter space. It should be
examined whether the model works for all relevant wavelengths and polarisations, as well as for
different numbers of auxiliary points per wavelength. Also, runtimes should be registered and
taken into consideration.
Once the HPC setup is in place, we suggest testing the inverse solver. Using the specific surface
and parameters, where we know the model works well, it should be feasible to test whether the
inverse model converges and gives the correct solution on some test cases. Given a functioning
inverse model that converges to the correct surface or a suitable posterior distribution, it would
be interesting to optimise the parameters in pCN to decrease the number of iterations. Similarly,
it would be relevant to optimise the kernel parameters and explore different kernels and kernel
combinations that fit different types of data.
Returning to the forward solver, we have observed that it does not perform well for all parameters
and surface sizes.
This leads to the next task of adjusting and optimising the model.
We
particularly suggest that the placement of sources be examined. Considering different schemes
and tuning parameters should enable better performance of more surfaces for more parameters.

FURTHER WORK
We have suggested some ideas for alternative and supplementary schemes in Chapter 6.
Another aspect of the forward solver that is lacking is the calculation of fields and fluxes. We
have implemented said functionalities and described the theory, but we have not tested them
suﬀiciently, and the preliminary results are not convincing. Therefore, they need testing and
possibly corrections. We suggest making comparisons with analytical data, simulation data, or
measurement data to validate this part of the model.
When field and flux calculations are validated, we suggest revisiting the topic of array equations.
Investigating whether this method can provide accurate estimates is a very intriguing question
in this context, specifically, and in general for MAS, because it might offer a way to significantly
reduce the runtime of the forward model beyond what optimisation and parallelisation can
provide.
Another exciting question is what patch sizes can be feasibly solved with a given access to
computer resources. This question might even be something that one would want to address
much earlier than after the above suggestions. Determining the expected runtimes and resource
usage for the forward model in general, as well as for patches of varying size and structure,
would be helpful.
In the implementation, we have allowed for oblique incidence, making it optional to expand the
model to accommodate oblique incidence. This should be relevant and could be a nice extension
element to test once the basic functionality is in place.
Biqubic spline interpolation is used for surface representation, and is implemented using Python
and pybind11. Removing this dependency by implementing it in C++ or using alternatives, if
that proves to be a better strategy for source placement, would result in a cleaner implementa-
tion. However, based on the experience so far, the implementation is working correctly.
We wholeheartedly recommend taking on this task as it is a fascinating and educational subject.
Moreover, we recommend that careful plans be laid for the development and implementation.
The tasks can easily become overwhelming, but we believe that the results can be equally
rewarding.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
In this thesis, we have presented electromagnetic theory, MAS theory, and array equations,
which we have used to build and implement a forward solver for an electromagnetic scattering
problem for a nanostructured surface.
We have presented and implemented an inverse solver. We have used pCN within the Bayesian
framework in the implementation, and we have reported relevant draws from Gaussian processes.
We expect the inverse solver to work, but its runtimes make it unsuitable for CPU calculations.
We have presented the implementation process that required a lot of learning on our part.
Building a complex coding structure in C++ proved challenging, and we were unable to complete
all the tasks we had hoped to address. Specifically, we would have ideally attempted to run the
inverse model and conduct tests using HPC. We would have expected to start testing the forward
model and setting it up much earlier.
We have validated the forward model using transmission conditions. We have presented the
validation results and found that the model works in some instances; however, we have yet to do
a broader testing of the model. During our validation, we have identified some potential issues
that need to be addressed in further work. We have also implemented calculations of near and
far fields, as well as power, but the results from the test runs have not been convincing so far.
Moreover, we have attempted to validate the model using flux calculations in comparison with
finite difference simulations, but the results in this regard have not been satisfactory.
We believe that the initial testing shows promising tendencies, and have therefore given a detailed
account of future perspectives and tasks that can make the MAS approach to the problem
successful. We remain optimistic about the prospects of using MAS to model nanostructures, and
we believe that it can provide improvements in surface estimation compared to measurements,
provided that the remaining issues with the model are addressed.
