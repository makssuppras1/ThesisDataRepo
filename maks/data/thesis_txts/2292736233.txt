1 Preface 
This thesis is the result of a study carried out at the International Centre for Indoor Environment and 
Energy, Department of Civil Engineering (DTU BYG), Technical University of Denmark in the period 
of 26th January 2015 to 26th July 2015. The principal supervisors of the study have been Professor 
Arsen Krikor Melikov and Assoc. Prof. Zhecho Dimitrov Bolashikov. The thesis is worth 30 ECTS 
credits. 
 
The thesis deals with Textile duct ventilation and it’s possibility to improve inhaled air quality of 
rooms with occupants seated for relatively long periods of time, such as classrooms, meeting rooms, 
etc.

2 Summary 
In the present study the performance of textile duct ventilation with regard to its ability to provide clean 
air to the breathing zone was studied and optimised through full-scale measurements. Its performance 
was compared with the performance of the most common total-volume air distribution methods used 
today, namely mixing ventilation and displacement ventilation. The different ventilation principles 
were evaluated based on the provided air quality and thermal comfort. 
 
A class room with a row of tables with three (five) occupants was simulated in the climate chamber. 
One breathing thermal manikin and two (four) heated dummies were seated behind the tables facing the 
same direction. Numerous textile ducts with different permeability and size and pattern of air supply 
nozzles were employed. The textile ducts were mounted on the suspended ceiling above the occupants, 
one textile duct at the time. The studied textile ducts generated different airflow distribution patterns 
inside the climate chamber. The interaction of the flow generated by the textile ducts with the 
buoyancy flow generated by the simulated occupants and its impact on the airflow pattern in the 
chamber was initially investigated through smoke visualizations. A parametric study was conducted for 
one (initial) textile duct design. Supply airflow rate, supply air temperature, location of the occupants 
relative to the textile duct (seated below or 0.2 and 0.4 m apart of the textile duct), position of the 
exhaust diffusers, installation height of the textile duct above the floor were the parameters. Based on 
this parametric study ten improved textile duct designs were developed and tested. 
 
Two tracer gases (N2O and R134a) were used for simulating common contaminants indoors. Feet and 
groins of the simulated occupants, table and exhaled air were selected as contaminant sources. The 
exposure of the “occupants” was quantified by measuring the tracer gas concentration in the breathing 
zone of the manikin and the dummies. The tracer gas concentration was also measured at selected 
locations in the chamber. The obtained results were used to validate the observed airflow pattern inside 
the room identified during the smoke visualizations. The thermal comfort of the occupants was 
evaluated based on heat loss from the manikin. Air velocity measurements were performed above and 
in front of the manikin to help understanding the interaction better the interaction of the supplied 
airflow and the buoyancy flow generated by the simulated occupants. The temperature was kept at 26 
°C at 1.1 m at the center of the climate chamber during all the measurements.  
The textile duct with permeability of 150 m3/m2/h and row of holes at an angle of 155° (from vertical) 
on both sides, provided good protection against cross contamination between the occupants sitting 
below the duct. The supply airflow rate influenced the performance of the textile duct ventilation: 
above certain flow rate the performance of the textile duct ventilation remained unchanged. The 
location of the occupants relative to the textile duct was of limited importance. The position of the 
exhaust diffuser was of limit importance. The supply air temperature was also of limited influence for 
the personalized exposure. The installation height of the textile duct had impact on the personalized 
exposure – lower installation height improved the personalized exposure. The number of occupants 
below the textile duct didn’t affect the improved protection of the textile duct from the simulated 
==PAGE:7==
 
 
 
7
pollution; expect the protection against table-generated pollution, which was reduced. 
 
Textile duct ventilation was compared to mixing ventilation and displacement ventilation. The textile 
duct ventilation provided increased performance in terms of personalized exposure meaning that a 
higher air quality was achieved in the breathing zone of the occupants. The textile duct ventilation 
provided the same level of contaminant concentration outside the breathing zone of the occupants, i.e. 
in the occupied zone. 
 
Air velocity measurements showed that initial textile duct design created higher air velocities, above 
and in front of the occupants, compared a mixing and displacement ventilation system. However, the 
air velocities remained within air velocity limits defined in DE/EN 1752 class A for a classroom. 
The buoyancy flows generated by the occupants seated below the textile duct had a strong effect on the 
distribution of the flow supplied from the textile duct and the field of the air velocity. 
 
The textile duct ventilation generally provided cooling of the upper body and heating of the lower body 
compared to mixing and displacement ventilation. However, the difference between the ventilation 
methods was somehow limited. 
 
The present study identified that textile duct ventilation can be applied to provide better air quality in 
the breathing zone compared to mixing and displacement ventilation in spaces with occupants seated 
for relatively long periods of time, such as classrooms and meeting rooms. 
 
It is recommended to conduct human test subject experiments to further investigate the perceived air 
quality and thermal comfort of humans, provided by textile duct ventilation.

3 Resume 
I dette studie er tekstil rør ventilation blevet testet ved fuld skala målinger. Tekstil rør ventilation blev 
testet and sammenlignet med de mest almindelig brugte total volumen ventilation system – 
opblandings- and fortræningsventilation. De forskellige systemer blev evalueret på basis af luft kvalitet 
og termisk komfort. 
 
Et klasserum med en række border med tre (fem) personer var placeret i et klimakæmmer. En termisk 
manikin og to (fire) opvarmet dummies blev placeret bag bordene med ansigtet orienteret den samme 
vej. Tekstil rør med forskellige tæthed og hul mønster var anvendt. Tekstil rørene var monteret på det 
nedhængte loft over personerne, et tekstil rør af gangen. Dette resulteret i forskellige observeret 
luftbevægelsesmønster inde I klimakæmmeret. Rør simuleringer blev udført for at kunne anskueliggøre 
hvordan de forskellige tekstil rør påvirkede luftbevægelse mønstrene inde i rummet. Et parameter 
studie blev gennemført for et (oprindelige) tekstil rør. Parametre som blev undersøgt var, 
volumenstrømning, position af personerne ift. Tekstil røret, temperatur af indblæsningsluft osv. På 
baggrund af dette parameter studie blev ti nye tekstil rør designs lavet of testet. 
 
To forskellige slags sporegasser (N2O and R134a) blev anvendt til simulering af nogle af de hyppigst 
fremkommende forureningskilder indenfor. Fødder og skød af personer, bord og udåndingsluft blev 
valgt som forureningskilder. Eksponeringen af personerne blev afgjort ved at måle sporegas 
koncentrationen i åndingszonen af manikinen og dummies. Sporegas koncentrationen blev også målt 
ved udvalgt steder inde i klimakæmmeret. Fordelingen af det forurenende luft fra forureningskilderne 
blev målt, ved fire positioner hver ved otte højder, således at kunne validere det observeret 
luftbevægelsemønster fra røg visualiseringerne. Den termiske komfort af personerne blev evalueret ud 
fra varmetabet fra manikinen. Derudover blev farten af luften også målt foran og foroven af manikinen. 
Dette blev gjort for at bedre kunne forstå interaktionen mellem indblæsningsluften og opdriften af luft 
fra de simuleret perosner. Temperaturen var holdt ved 26 °C i 1.1 m højde i centeret af klimakæmmeret 
under alle målingerne. 
Tekstil røret med en tæthed på 150 m3/m2/h og rækker af huler placeret på begge sider med en vinkel 
på 155° (fra lodret), gav god beskyttelse mod kryds forurening mellem de siddende personer under 
røret. Volumenstrømningen havde en betydning for ventilation systemets ydeevne. Dog, efter en hvis 
volumenstrømningsmængde var nået forblev ventilation systemets ydeevne det samme. Positionen af 
personerne ift. Tekstil røret var af mindre betydning. Placeringen af udsugningsarmaturet var også fra 
begrænset betydning. Ligeledes var undertemperaturen på indblæsningens luften også af begrænset 
betydning for ventilation systemets ydeevne ift. Den personlige eksponering af forureningerne. 
Betydningen af højdeplaceringen af tekstil røret var også undersøgt. Det blev målt at jo kortere afstand 
imellem tekstil rør og åndingszonen jo bedre ydeevne ift. Den personlige eksponering. Antallet af 
personer under tekstil røret påvirkede ikke den forbedret ydeevnen af ventilation systemet, pånær en 
mindre reduktion i beskyttelsen imod forurening fra bordet.

Tekstil rør ventilation blev sammenlignet med opblandingsventilation og fortræningsventilation. 
Tekstil rør ventilation tilvejebragt en forbedring af den personlige eksponering af forurening, hvilket 
betyder at luft kvaliteten var højere indeni åndingszonen af de siddende personer. Det var målt at tekstil

siddende personer dvs. opholdszonen. 
 
Luft farts målinger viste at de oprindeligt tekstil rør design gav anledning til højere luft farter, over og 
foran personerne, ift. Opblandings- og fortrængingsventilation. Dog forblev air farten indenfor 
grænserne givet i DE/EN 1752 klasse A for klasserum. Opdriftsluften genereret fra personerne af de 
siddende personer viste sig at have en yderest mærkbar effekt på fordelingen af flowet fra tekstil røret. 
 
Tekstil rør ventilation gav generelt anledning til køling af overkroppen og opvarmning af underkroppen 
ift. De to andre ventilation systemer. Dog var størrelsen imellem ventilation systemerne relative lille. 
 
Det foreliggende studie viste at tekstil rør ventilation kan blive anvendt til at tilvejebringe bedre luft 
kvalitet i åndingszonen, ift. Opblandings- og fortræningsventilation, i rum hvor siddeende personer 
opholder sig relative længe af gange, f.eks. klasserum og mødelokaler. 
 
Det anbefales at udføre forsøg med mennesker for at nærmere kunne undersøge den af mennesker 
oplevede luft kvalitet og termiske komfort ved brug af tekstil rør ventilation.

5 Induction 
Ventilation of buildings has the purpose of providing clean air for breathing. Often the ventilation air is 
conditioned to maintain indoor thermal environment comfortable for the occupants. The ventilation is 
intended to insure a healthy and comfortable environment for the building occupants. The indoor 
environment is considered comfortable when good air quality and thermal comfort are provided within 
the occupied zone of the rooms. In the literature it has been reported that ventilation is strongly 
associated with comfort (perceived air quality and thermal environment) and health (sick building 
syndrome symptoms, transmission of airborne infectious diseases, asthma, allergy, inflammation, short 
term sick leave) (Müller et al. 2013; EPA, 1991; Wargocki el al. 1999; Li et al. 2007). Association 
between ventilation and productivity is also documented (Wargocki et al., 2002; Melikov, 2004; EPA, 
1991). In Denmark buildings are made more and more airtight resulting in increase in discomfort (e.g. 
reduced perceived air quality, etc.) and health problems such as sick building syndrome (SBS) 
symptoms. This causes loss in productivity and increased short-term sick leave (Cao et al., 2014). 
Therefore, the need for higher ventilation rates and more effective air distribution patterns increases in 
tight buildings. 
 
Li et al. (2007) show that there is a strong and sufficient evidence to support the claim that ventilation 
and air movements in rooms affects the spread/transmission of airborne infectious diseases. Therefore 
efficient air distribution, in occupied spaces is important in terms of health and transmission/spread of 
airborne infectious diseases. 
 
This project focuses on improving ventilation in public buildings, especially educational facilities, such 
as schoolrooms, lecture rooms, libraries. The aim is to investigate and improve the performance of 
textile ducts for ventilation in schoolrooms. Use of textile ducts for ventilation has several advantages 
compare to other air distribution methods used today (e.g. low price, easy installation and cleaning, 
decreased energy use due to low pressure drop, etc.). These issues are discussed later in this report. In 
the following chapter specific topics related to indoor environment, and in particular to ventilation and 
air distribution, in educational premises are reviewed in brief.

6 Indoor environment and ventilation in schools - Literature review 
Before investigating the potential of textile ducts for ventilation of rooms, it is important to understand 
how the fundamentals work. In this chapter the impact of inhaled air quality and indoor temperature on 
the performance of pupils during schoolwork and adults during office work is discussed. The 
importance of location of heat and pollution sources, the ventilation (air distribution) principles used 
today, major flows in rooms (including the free convention flow around the human body and the flow 
of ventilation) and their interactions for the air quality is discussed. 
 
6.1 Indoor environment and performance of pupils 
The impact of inhaled air quality and the indoor temperature on the performance of children has been 
investigated in many studies. The results indicate that the ventilation airflow rate and the indoor 
temperature are of great importance when considering the work and learning performance of children 
and adults. 
 
The indoor environment in Danish school has been discussed a lot in the past few years. Several studies 
referred in the following have been performed. The results reveal that there is a great problem with the 
indoor environment in Danish schools (Danmarks radio, 2014). The Danish schools are housing more 
than 500.000 children and 50.000 teachers, i.e. quite many people are exposed to a bad indoor 
environment (Sundhedsstyrelsen, 2012). A bad indoor environment can lead to several discomfort 
problems, such as: irritated eyes, nose and throat, skin irritation and reddening, feeling heavy headed, 
fatigue, difficulty to concentrate, malaise, etc. Sundhedsstyrelsen (2012) reports that 36 % of the 
students in schools often or very often experience uncomfortable indoor air quality. However, a bad 
indoor environment may also cause sickness conditions: allergies, respiratory disorders and respiratory 
illness (Toftum et al., 2011; Sundhedsstyrelsen, 2012). If a densely populated room is insufficiently 
ventilated the risk of airborne transmission of contagious diseases can be high.  
The Danish building regulations (BR10, 2014) require that the indoor concentration of CO2 is kept 
below 1000 ppm for longer periods of time. However, Toftum et al. (2011) reported that this was only 
the case in 44 % of the Danish schools leaving 56 % of the schools with concentrations above 1000 
ppm of CO2 for longer periods of time. These numbers indicate that there is a big problem to be dealt 
with and if it is not solved serious health problems as described above may happen. Too high CO2 
concentrations are most often found in natural ventilated classroom; 69 % of the classrooms with 
natural ventilation have concentrations above 1000 ppm. Too high CO2 concentrations are found in 
38 % of the classrooms with mechanical ventilation and in 50 % of the classrooms with only exhaust 
ventilation. The same study report that increasing the ventilation rate from 6 l/s/person to 8.5 or 
10 l/s/person will give a schoolwork performance increase of 6 % and 9 % respectively. Poor air 
quality can reduce the performance of office work, adults, by 6 % to 10 % (Wyon D. and Wargocki P., 
2006).  Wargocki and Wyon (2006) and Toftum et al. (2011) present similar results showing that a 
doubling of the amount of the supplied air would give an 8 % and 1.5 % performance increase of 
schoolwork (children) office work (adults) respectively. According to Wyon and Wargocki (2006) the 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
13
performance increase by 1.1 % for each 10 % reduction of the proportion of dissatisfied with the indoor 
air quality. 
 
The room temperature has proven to be also an important factor. The performance of the pupils and 
adults is improved, for schoolwork and office work, by 2-4 % and 1 % respectively, for each degree 
below 25 °C until the temperature levels of 20-21°C has been reached (Toftum et al., 2011; Wargocki 
P. and Wyon D., 2006). If the temperature is kept around 20 °C to 21 °C the performance may be 
improved by up to 20 %. According to Sundhedsstyrelsen (2012) 42 % of the children in school can 
often or very often experience too low or too high indoor temperatures.  
 
The above discussion exhibited that the indoor environment has a significant effect on the occupants’ 
performance and why a bad indoor environment is worth improving. From an economic point of view a 
lot of money can be saved due to less need of teaching hours, more effective education, etc. e.g. 
Toftum et al. (2011) present that doubling the ventilation rate can give a 8 % performance increase, 
which may reduce the schooling by almost one year over a ten year span. The Danish public school 
budget is 41,433 Mio kr. in 2014 (KL, 2014); this means that the possibilities for large savings are 
present. 
 
6.2 Heat & pollution sources in buildings 
There are several heat and pollution sources present in buildings. These are presented and described in 
the following subchapters.  
6.2.1 Heat sources 
In buildings there are several internal and external heat sources. Internal heat sources are: occupants, 
lightning, printers, monitors, etc. External heat sources, with respect to the building, are e.g. sun 
radiation. Internal and external heat sources are of interest as they increase the room temperature and 
thereby affect the occupants’ thermal environment. 
 
The heat sources in spaces generate heat that is exchanged with the human body by convection and 
radiation (Skistad et al., 2002; Müller et al., 2013). Convective heat exchange is driven by the velocity 
of the fluid and the media it moves over/in. The convective part interacts directly with the indoor air 
causing a relatively fast temperature increase of the indoor air temperature Thermal radiation is energy 
emitted by matter as electromagnetic waves, due to the pool of thermal energy in all matter with a 
temperature above absolute zero. Thermal radiation propagates without the presence of matter through 
the vacuum of space. Due to the thermal radiation the indoor surfaces get warmer and heat the indoor 
air by convection when the surface temperature is above the ambient air temperature. 
 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
14
6.2.2 Pollution sources 
Pollution sources emit gases or particles into the indoor air (Skistad et al., 2002). Pollution sources 
encountered in rooms are many, but normally the following are most likely considered: building 
materials, occupants, paints, PCs, printers, floor covering. Pollution is important for occupants’ comfort 
(perceived air quality – PAQ) and health (inhaled air quality and toxicity) (Lagercrantz et al., 2000; 
Wargocki et al. 1999). They have been linked to cause Sick Building Syndrome (SBS) symptoms 
(EPA, 1991). The pollution sources can be subdivided into active and passive pollution sources. Active 
pollution sources are pollution sources that emit heat and pollution at the same time. The pollution, 
from active sources, is transported by the convection flow generated by the same source. This means 
that the pollution is usually transported towards the ceiling (in case of heated source). How it is 
distributed in the space depends on the interaction of the convection flow (the plume) with the airflow 
supplied by the ventilation system, proximity to other heat sources, etc. Examples of active pollution 
sources were: humans, printers, PCs, etc. The passive pollution sources emit pollution but do not 
produce heat. In practice this means that the pollution from the passive sources would stay close to the 
pollution source until room airflows, e.g. ventilation airflow carries it away. Examples of passive 
sources are: carpet, paint, etc. (Zukowska et al., 2012; Skistad et al., 2002). 
 
6.3 Ventilation of schools at present 
6.3.1 Mixing ventilation 
Mixing ventilation (MV) has been used for more than a century (Boyle, 1899). MV is one of the most 
used mechanical ventilation principles for buildings today. The main aspect of MV is to dilute the 
contaminated indoor air by mixing it with clean outdoor air. This results in a lower contaminant 
concentration of pollutants in the indoor air inhaled by the occupants (Müller et al., 2013). Well-
designed MV provides a uniform temperature and contaminant concentration distribution throughout 
the whole room. MV is quite energy consuming, compared to other solutions, since the whole room air 
volume is ventilated. Thereby a large airflow rate is needed in order to provide enough clean air to the 
room. It is not possible to adjust the ventilation according to the needs of each individual. Draught can 
be avoided by carefully design of the air distribution pattern in the room. However, the air distribution 
pattern may not always occur in practice since the air distribution pattern is sensitive to the interaction 
of the ventilation flow with thermal plumes (Cao et al., 2014; Müller et al., 2013). 
The performance of MV in terms of health is not high. Because of the uniform contaminant distribution 
in the room, MV has the lowest contaminant removal effectiveness of all ventilation systems (Skistad 
et al., 2002). When considering person-to-person contaminant transport MV provides no protection. 
6.3.2 Displacement ventilation 
Displacement ventilation (DV) is based on the physical principle of thermal stratification (Skistad et 
al., 2002). Clean outdoor air is supplied, cooler than the room air, over the floor area. The clean supply 
air remains in the lower region of the room; afterwards it moves up into the occupied zone due to the 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
15
thermal buoyancy effect pushing the warm air upwards to the exhaust (Skistad et al., 2002). Heat 
sources may create an upward air movement, which transport the contamination upwards resulting in a 
lower contaminant concentration at the breathing level, provide the pollutant source is not in the lower 
(cleaner) region of the ventilated space. These interactions will result in vertical gradients of the room 
air temperature and the contaminant concentration (Skistad et al., 2002; Müller et al., 2013). DV can’t 
be used for heating due to short-circuiting of the supply air. 
DV requires considerations in term of thermal comfort due to the vertical air temperature gradient 
present and the risk of draught at feet. Draught at the feet is normally only a problem if people are 
seated too close to the air terminal diffuser. 
 
The thermal stratification can be relatively sensitive to disturbances such as walking persons, which 
can disturb the thermal and contaminant stratification (creating mixing) and brings the performance of 
DV in terms of air quality and thermal comfort closer that the performance of mixing ventilation. The 
disturbances may also result in a lowered performance of the DV system in terms of health compared 
to MV (Halvonova and Melikov 2010). A study by He et al. (2011) reports that DV performs better 
than MV in terms of lowering exposure to cross infection. However, studies by Bjørn and Nielsen 
(2002) and Nielsen (2009) shows that DV is not advantageous in terms of protection against cross 
infection.  
 
DV can be more energy efficient than MV. This is because DV can have a higher extract and supply air 
temperature than MV due to the thermal stratification. Thus less cooling may be required (Skistad et 
al., 2002). Karimipanah et al. (2008) reports that DV uses 20-34 % less energy than MV, which may be 
explained by lower pressure loses in the ventilation system (diffusers). 
6.3.3 Diffuse ceiling ventilation 
Diffuse ceiling (DC) utilises the suspended ceiling for supply of clean air. The space above the 
suspended ceiling (plenum) is used as a pressure chamber and clean outdoor air is supplied to the 
occupied zone through perforations in the suspended ceiling (Hviid et al., 2010). DC supplies air with a 
low impulse, due to the perforations in the suspended ceiling, thus it is not likely to cause draught. 
Hviid and Terkildsen  (2009) have been studying the performance of DC in a classroom. The DC 
system is able to perform satisfactorily in terms of air movements and air temperatures. Jacobs et al. 
(2008) and Bertheussen et al. (2013) report that DC can provide acceptable PAQ and IAQ in a 
classroom. Hviid et al. (2009) reports that a DC can create perfect mixing in a room and thus it is 
possible to have a uniform contaminant and temperature distribution. Thus DC performs equally as 
good as MV in terms of health,  (Bertheussen et al. 2013). Dust may be collected and transported to the 
room from the suspended ceiling. This may have a negative health effect on the occupants. 
DC is suitable for rooms with high concurrent ventilation and cooling demand such as in classrooms 
due to the low impulse of the supply air (Hviid et al., 2010; Jacobs et al., 2008). However, draught may 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
16
still occur due to airflow interactions in the room and when having high heat loads especially uneven 
heat load distribution (Bertheussen et al., 2013).  
DC can be used for heating but it is inefficient (Bertheussen et al. 2013). 
6.3.4 Textile duct ventilation 
Ventilation of buildings is traditionally carried out by use of metal ducts and with air terminal devices 
attached. Textile duct ventilation (TV) is a different way to approach ventilation of buildings. It is 
based on supply of air through ducts placed in the room and made of textile with the air terminal 
devices “integrated” into the textile duct. Textile duct ventilation is originally only used for supply of 
cold air in slaughterhouses with the intent of supplying large amounts of air at low velocities and thus 
to reduce draught in the occupied zone. However, textile duct ventilation has developed since and it can 
now be used for air distribution in offices, classrooms, etc.  
Textile duct ventilation has been installed in classrooms in the Netherlands and United Kingdom. The 
systems are able prevent draught while maintaining a low CO2 concentration level (Euro Air, 2011; 
KE-Fibertec, 2011).

permeability of textile, geometry, colour of textile, number and placement of nozzle and/or laser-cut

configuration. 
 
One of the typical designs of textile duct ventilation is the low impulse design: the air is supplied 
through the whole area of the duct, causing the air to leave at a very low velocity. The air flows 
vertically down into the occupied zone. The supply air has limited mixing with the room air. Hence this

system are also delivered prefabricated with the right measures. The time for installing the textile ducts 
is short and thereby less money is spent on mounting the system. (KE-Fibertec). 
6.3.5 Personalized ventilation  
The personalized ventilation (PV) is a personalized system, which can provide clean air to every 
occupant (Melikov, 2004). PV consists of having the air terminal device close to the breathing zone of 
the occupant. This will avoid extensive mixing with the room air and thereby is able to provide clean 
air. PV has two advantages compared to the total volume ventilation systems: 1) It is possible to 
improve inhaled air quality and protect occupants from airborne infection when using it (Melikov et al., 
2006, Cermak et al., 2007), and 2) Occupants are giving the option of adjusting the airflow rate, 
temperature, direction of air supplied according to personal preference (Melikov, 2004).  
 
Ductless personalized ventilation (DPV) is a DV system, which is supplying the air along the floor so 
the ductless PV system can suck it up at each workstation and supply it to the breathing zone 
(Halvonova et al.2008; 2009; 2010). A DPV system performs better than a DV system, in terms of 
PAQ and thermal comfort, due to elevated facial velocity of the supply air and the transport of clean air 
from the lower room levels to the breathing zone. The elevate velocity diminishes the negative impact 
of relative humidity, high temperature and pollution concentration. However, an increase in eye 
dryness sensation may be present. (Halvonova and Melikov 2010; Dalewski et al., 2012; Dalewski et 
al. 2014). Ceiling Mounted Personalized Ventilation (CMPV) is another version of PV, Bin et al., 
2010. A diffuser is placed in the ceiling above the occupant supplies the clean air. CMPV is able to 
provide a good PAQ due to elevated facial velocities. Furthermore it is possible to improve inhale air 
quality compared to MV alone. However, when designing such a system the entrainment of polluted 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
19
room air into the supply air jet has to be considered. Depending on the design of the diffuser it is 
possible to protect the occupant from cross contamination (Makhoul et al., 2013 A-C; Bin et al., 2010). 
PV has been tested when it was combined with MV and DV. The performance, in term of inhaled air 
quality, of a MV and a DV system is increased when used in conjunction with a PV system. This is due 
to the direct supply of fresh conditioned air at the breathing zone (Cermak et al. 2005, Li et al. 2013). 
PV has also been tested when it is used in conjunction with a chilled ceiling, Lipczynska et al. (2014A). 
PV in combination with a chilled ceiling offers improves thermal sensation compared to a MV system 
combined with chilled ceiling. Furthermore, PV reduces the time needed for improving the thermal 
comfort after being exposed to other thermal conditions. The perceived air quality is also improved. 
The PV system may provide high air quality at the workstation; however, the air quality is not 
improved in the background. However, Lipczynska et al. (2014B) shows that the PV system as the only 
ventilation system combined with chilled ceiling may be an optimal solution in many buildings. 
 
PV shows great potential in terms of inhaled air quality and thermal comfort, PV has the possibility of 
performing better than MV and DV (Melikov, 2004). However, PV has some problems. Generally no 
guidelines are given for design and implantation of PV. The implementation and installation of PV is 
difficult due to air ducts affecting indoor aesthetics and the flexibility of furniture layout (each diffuser 
needs a duct). The ductwork is costly in terms of material and man-hours. However, a PV system will 
not be used as the only ventilation system since another ventilation system will be needed for 
background ventilation for temperature control (Melikov, 2004). The need for two systems will be 
more costly than a single stand-alone ventilation system. 
Studies show that PV has a great potential to save energy (Sekhar et al. 2003; Schiavon and Melikov 
2009 and 2010; Yang et al. 2010; Lelong et al. 2013). The energy savings are due to a lower total 
supply airflow rate need and the possibility to have higher supply air temperatures in the building.

6.5 Airflows in rooms  
There are several different airflows present in rooms. The different airflows of interest are presented in 
this section. 
6.5.1 Ventilation flows 
Ventilation systems may use different kind of flows such as free circular jets and free plane jets. In 
order to define them it is necessary to start defining what a free jet is.  
 
A free jet is a jet issuing from an opening or a nozzle into an open air space that will in principle be 
infinite. This means that the jet and its flow pattern are not influenced by solid boundaries. More details 
on free jet theory are given in appendix 15.1.  
6.5.2 Buoyancy flows 
Buoyancy flows rise above warm objects, like people and PCs, or descends from cold objects, like 
outer walls, windows, due to buoyancy forces exist in rooms (Skistad et al., 2002). The buoyancy 
forces are present due to the density difference between cold and hot air. The hot air rises since the 
density of air decreases the hotter it gets, making the buoyancy forces push the warm air up above the 
colder air. Similarly an upward free convection flow exists around the human when having a calm 
environment (Lewis et al. 1967; Melikov et al., 1996; Homma and Yakiyamma 1988; Lincina et al. 
2014; Melikov 2015). At the lower part of the human body the free convection flow is slow and 
laminar with a thin boundary layer and becomes faster and turbulent with a thick boundary layer at the 
height of the head (Lewis et al. 1969). The free convection flow around a person may reach a mean 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
22
upward speed of around 0.25 m/s and a boundary layer thickness of 0.2 meter or more at the height of 
the head. However, this depends on many factors such as the body shape, posture, room air 
temperature, etc. The bigger the difference between air temperature and skin temperature the stronger is 
the free convection flow. The free convection flow around the human body induces and transport air 
(including pollution if present) from lower heights in the room to the breathing zone. A portion of the 
inhaled air by a sedentary person is from the free convection flow. It is essential to understand the 
physics of the free convection flow in terms of inhaled air quality and thermal comfort, because it acts 
as transport media of surrounding room air and influences the heat loss from the body. 
 
Several studies have been performed in order to identify and characterise the free convection flow 
around the human body (Lewis et al. 1969; Homma and Yakiyamma 1988). Lewis et al. (1969) made 
one of the first studies on the nature of the free convection flow around a standing naked man and its 
role as an active transport of pollutants and particles. The free convection flow starts very close to the 
floor. The air that attaches to the feet (around the ankles) goes upwards the along the legs. This air is 
accelerating and the air layer becomes thicker. At the knees the layer is 1-2 cm thick while continues to 
accelerate until it reaches the chest. At 1 meter above the floor the free convection flow changes from 
laminar to turbulent. It becomes full turbulent at 1.5 meter (at chest). When, it reaches the shoulders 
most of it breaks away upwards. The free convection flow is strongly modified when it reaches the 
head. Its upward movement is affected by the contours of the face: jaw, neck, nose and the pinna of the 
ears. Some of the air goes along the convex structure of the jaws upwards, the remaining goes under the 
surface of the chin. The part of air that overcomes the chin passes over the lips and become part of the 
air inhaled. The other flows over the eyes, along the cheeks, the forehead and joins the air that comes 
from the back and side of the head and the shoulders (Lewis et al., 1969). 
The chin affects the free convection flows peak velocity at the forehead region. The peak velocity is 
highest at a sitting position compared to a standing position (Licina et al., 2014). 
However, the above is true for a standing naked man, which is seldom the case. The free convection 
flow is altered by factors such as: clothing, body posture etc. 
 
The free convection flow changes characteristics when the occupant changes from a standing to a 
sitting position. When the occupant is sitting the free convection flow starts to form at the lower 
abdomen and interacts with the air that breaks away from the knees and thighs. This results in a slightly 
different free convection flow over the face compared to a standing position (Zukowska et al., 2012). 
When sitting, the free convection flow layer becomes wider compared to a standing position. This 
gives a bigger area able for entrainment of surrounding air. This increase of thickness may enhance 
cross flow and cross contamination between occupants (Licina et al., 2014). The concern is especially 
of interest in densely populated rooms.   
The posture is of interest when considering the breathing zone. A study by Licina et al. (2014) shows 
that a backward leaning manikin creates the strongest flow in the breathing zone while the upright 
posture creates the second strongest flow leaving the forward leaning posture the weakest. When the 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
23
body is leaning forward the thickness of the free convection flow is reduced with no air movement 
away from the face, this cause a high turbulence intensity level in facial region. Licina et al. (2014) 
registered a peak velocity, in the facial region, when leaning forward 45 % and 50 % lower than the 
upright and backward leaning posture respectively. The thermal plume is pulled from the head towards 
the back of the body when leaning forward, which may explain why the free convection flow layer is 
thinner at the face. The chin may also have a part. A study by Dowell et al. (2001) shows that people 
spent 75 % of the time in a forward leaning or upright-seated position when doing office work or 
working with a computer. 
The clothing of the occupant is another important factor when considering the free convective flow. If 
the thermal insulation coming from the clothing is increased the surface temperature of the clothing is 
reduced. It will result in lower velocities in the free convective flow and makes the free convective 
flow layer thicker (Homma and Yakiyama, 1988). Loose clothing has trapped air in the wrinkled 
clothing layers giving additional isolation and hence affects the strength of the free convection flow. 
Furthermore, loose clothing causes a more complex flow with high vorticity compared to tight clothing. 
The peak velocity in the breathing zone is also affected by the amount of clothing and if it is tight or 
loose. Loose clothing makes the biggest reduction on the peak velocity in the breathing zone (Licina et 
al., 2014). 
 
Zukowska et al. (2012) measurements show that hair on the head is also affecting the free convection 
flow. Hair on the head intensifies the mixing of the free convective flow at the head level. A baldhead 
would give 15 % less volumetric airflow. However, a baldhead will contribute to higher velocities at 
the facial region due to the higher heat loss from the head compared to a head with a wig (Licina et al., 
2014). 
 
There exits many different kinds of chair and table designs. The chair and table has affect on the free 
convection flow. Licina et al. (2014) shows that the chair design has an effect on the velocities (up to 
24 % difference) in the facial region. The table creates a barrier for the free convective flow when 
placed close to the occupant. In this case it causes a development of a new boundary layer over the 
table surface that is thinner. This makes it easier to penetrate the breathing zone with clean air 
(Bolashikov et al. 2009). The table most likely redirects the flow from the legs to the sides and later on 
the shoulder. Bolashikov et al. (2009) have showed that the air velocities in the facial region are lowest 
when having the table touching the body compared to having the table 10 cm away. The highest 
velocities are obtained when no table is present (Licina et al. 2014). 
 
The free convection flow turns into a thermal plume when the air goes above the human. The thermal 
plume resulting from the free convection flow is also depending on factors such as: clothing, body 
posture, body shape, furniture arrangement, exhalation through mouth or nose, etc. (Zukowska et al. 
2012). 
 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
 
24
The body posture (sitting or standing) is affecting the thermal plume. The thermal plume is 
asymmetrical for a sitting occupant due to the flow rising from the legs and thighs. When sitting, the 
thermal plume becomes wider compared to a standing position. This gives a bigger cross section, 
which makes a bigger area for entrainment of surrounding air making the thermal plume bigger. This 
may enhance mixing of the room air and its’ pollutions (Licina et al., 2014). A study by Licina et al. 
(2014) shows that when the occupant is leaning forward the thermal plume is pulled toward the back of 
the body. Thereby the thermal plume is not leaving from the top of the head but rather from the back 
and back of the head. 
 
The thermal plume is not significantly affected by breathing through the nose. It is because the exhaled 
air coming from the nose most of the time breaks free of the convective flow around the body. 
Zukowska et al. (2012) showed that the air exhaled from the nose acts as a warm cloud outside the 
thermal plume, in non-ventilated situations, and therefore hardly has any effect on the thermal plume. 
Rim and Novoselac (2009) showed that inhalation and exhalation through the nose has a minor 
influence on the airflow in the breathing zone and also that it only has a small effect on the thermal 
plume 0.25 m above the head. At low metabolic activity, it is most likely that people exhale through the 
nose. Exhalation through the mouth will increase the cross section area of the thermal plume (Zhu et 
al., 2005). 
 
The clothing of the occupant is also affecting the thermal plume. Additional insolation will, as for the 
free convection flow, result in lower velocities in the thermal plume and it makes it thicker (Homma 
and Yakiyama, 1988). Loose clothing causes a more complex flow with high vorticity compared to 
tight clothing. The high vorticity causes a faster entrainment and mixing of the air coming from the 
room resulting in a wider thermal plume with greater volume flow above the occupant. It is therefore 
loose clothing requires more attention when simulating. The velocities are lower in the thermal plume 
and the width of the thermal plume is increased compared to tight clothing. 
 
A study by Zukowska et al. (2012) shows that the hair on the head is not just affecting the free 
convection flow but also the resulting the thermal plume. Since, hair on the head intensifies the mixing 
of the free convective flow at the head level, the flow rate and size is increasing of the thermal plume. 
 
The chair design has no effect on the thermal plume. However, the presence of a table is important for 
the flow characteristics of the thermal plume due to the changes of the free convection flow describe 
above (Zukowska et al. 2012). The thermal plume is wider, having a greater airflow and a more 
symmetrical structure when having the table touching the occupant compared to the situation with no 
table or table placed 10 cm away (Zukowska et al., 2012). 
 
The thermal plume can be considered in terms of velocity and excess temperature distribution. 
Zukowska et al. (2010) measured the thermal plume 0.70 m above a sitting occupant. It turns out that 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
 
25
the average width of the velocity profiles are steeper than the temperature profiles being more spread. 
The reason for this is because the temperature excess in the plume disappears faster than the velocity 
due to the entrainment of the surrounding air. 
6.5.3 Interaction of flows  
The interaction between the free convection flow around the human body and the ventilation airflow 
pattern is of great importance when the aim is to deliver clear air to the breathing zone of the occupant, 
as well as for occupants’ thermal comfort. The interaction is largely influenced by the characteristics of 
the ventilation airflow such as: direction, velocity and turbulent intensity (Licina et al., 2014). Licina et 
al. (2014) shows that it is easier for a transverse flow compared to a downward flow to peel of the free 
convective flow around the occupant. The reason is the mass of the flow. The downward flow needs 
higher velocity to push down the plume which can be as high as 90 L/s, while transverse flow can do so 
at much lower flows, i.e. PV can do it at 10 l/s (Bolashikov et al 2003). Therefore a higher momentum 
is necessary for the downward flow in order to reach the breathing zone. The transverse flow can 
strongly disturb the thermal plume at 0.175 m/s while downward flow has hardly any effect at that 
speed. A downward flow reaches the breathing zone at 0.30 m/s causing intensive air mixing resulting 
in a high degree of turbulent intensity. It does not completely offset the thermal plume but it lowers the 
velocity of the flow. The downward flow destroys the thermal plume at 0.425 m/s (Licina et al., 2014). 
It is not only the velocity of the air that affects the thermal plume it is also the temperature of the 
ventilation air. The above highlights the importance of the velocity magnitude of the ventilation flow 
and airflow interaction in general. The interaction between the free convection flow and the ventilation 
airflow is also dependent on the distance between the human and the air terminal device. The longer the 
distance the more space does the thermal plume have to grow and entrain air and the further the 
ventilation airflow has to travel longer before reaching the head (Melikov, 2013). Therefore it is 
beneficial to have a short distance between the air terminal device and the breathing zone if one wants 
to deliver less clean air to the breathing zone. 
 
The interaction between the free convection flow, the ventilation airflow and the cooling effect on 
occupants is also dependent on the size of the ventilation jet. The size of the air supply diffuser, e.g. the 
size of a nozzle from a terminal air device and the initial airflow conditions at the exit define the size of 
the target area in contact with the body. The target area is defined as the cross section of the jet where it 
first meets the occupant. If the airflow rate remains constant increasing the size of the nozzle will 
reduce the air velocity and will increase the size of the target area of the jet. The opposite notion, i.e. 
decreasing the size of the nozzle, would result in higher air velocity and a smaller target area which 
may not cover the occupants’ body (Yang et al., 2009). This changes impact the interaction between 
the jet flow and the buoyancy flow. 
 
The free convection flow is able to entrain and transport infectious pollutants from the lower level of 
the room to the breathing zone. A downward flow that is able to peel of the free convection flow 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
 
26
around the breathing zone can protect the occupant from pollution located below the breathing zone or 
in those cases that the occupant is the pollution source itself (Skistad et al., 2002, Licina et al., 2014;). 
However, this is true in an ideal piston flow ventilated room or underfloor ventilated room. ASHRAE – 
Design Manuel for hospitals and Clinics (2013) recommends a vertically downward air distribution as 
an efficient pollutant removal strategy that reduces the risk of airborne cross contamination. However, 
consideration regarding the thermal comfort is needed when applying a downward ventilation flow due 
to the ventilation air velocities needed to peel of the free convection flow. The interaction of the 
ventilation airflow with the thermal plume generated by the human body is of great importance for the 
cooling of the body (Melikov, 2009, Melikov 2013). However, the airflow rate is more important than 
the supply air temperature when considering heat loss form the human body (Melikov, 2013). People 
may not sit symmetrically belong the air terminal device causing an asymmetrically cooling of the 
body. This effect is important to note since this may lead to thermal discomfort. The air velocity 
required of the downward flow may exceed the recommended comfortable velocity range (ISO 7730,

From Table 1, it can be seen that with regard to the contaminant removal efficiency the textile duct 
ventilation has been reported to perform equally well like mixing ventilation system (Nielsen and Topp 
2006, Nielsen et al. 2005). The studied textile duct is without any holes and thereby only the diffusing 
of air through the textile is studied. These studies are performed in a small office room with two sitting 
occupants at their own table at the centreline of the room. The textile duct is placed either at the centre 
of the room or near the wall. Based on this setup the performance of the textile duct ventilation system 
is tested at different airflow rates. The studies do not look into the possibility of altering the textile duct 
design and thereby alter the airflow interactions inside the room. They only change the position of the 
textile duct. Thereby they do not study the possibility to supply air directly to the breathing zone of the 
occupants through holes in the textile duct thus to increase inhaled air quality. They do not study 
whether it is possible to remove the free convection flow around the occupant or how the airflow 
interactions are in the room in order to increase the inhaled air quality. They report contaminant 
removal efficiency to be in the range of 0.8-1.4. However, the contaminant removal efficiency is 1.0 
for a centre placed textile duct, i.e. it is not high. The personalized exposure effectiveness is reported to 
be between 1.0-1.2 and it is 1.0 for a centre placed textile duct. Therefore the ventilation effectiveness 
of a centre placed textile duct will be performing like mixing ventilation. The performance of textile 
duct ventilation may be improved. The following can be suggested to achieve improvements: a) 
improving the air distribution from the textile duct by changing its design and b) by studying the 
interaction of the airflows. The changing in the design of the textile ducts should aim at supply of clean 
air more directly into the breathing zone of the occupants and to avoid/reduce it’s mixing with ambient 
room air. By studying the interaction of airflows (the supplied ventilation air and the thermal plume 
generated by occupants) it may be possible to find appropriate way for supplying clean air to the 
breathing zone without extensive mixing and thus to increase the inhaled air quality. It may be possible 
to peel off the free convection flow around the occupants and avoid the transport of pollutions to the 
breathing zone associated with it.

An important advantage of textile ducts is the possibility to change its permeability and to change the 
holes and their size and pattern. In this way the interaction of the ventilation flow from the textile duct 
with the buoyancy generated by the occupant may be controlled. However, how changes of the textile 
duct design affect these interactions has not been studied before. It needs to be study in order to better 
utilize the many design possibility offered by textile ducts. The aim will be to focus on improvement of 
the inhaled air quality at the breathing zone not the whole volume of the occupied zone by exploring 
the advantages of textile ducts design, i.e. to develop breathing zone ventilation. Such improvement, 
together with other advantages including low cost, low energy consumption, easy installation and 
cleaning, etc. will make textile ventilation attractive for use in spaces with sedentary occupants, such as 
schools, etc. 
 
The Teknological Institute in Denmark had investigated textile duct ventilation. The tested textile ducts 
were provided by the company KE-Fibertec. The focus was to study the performance of the textile 
ducts with regard to inhaled air quality. It was concluded that the studied textile duct design couldn’t 
provide clean air to the breathing zone of the occupants without mixing with the polluted room air 
(Madsen et al. 2014). The tests were made for a classroom setup with rows of tables each with four 
occupants. The pollution was simulated by dosing tracer gas (N2O) from the mouth of the occupants 
through a dust mask. It was found that the simulated pollution from the occupants was recirculated 
back into the breathing zone by the supply air, which decreased the performance of the ventilation 
system. The problem was that the interaction between the thermal plume generated by the heat sources 
used to simulate occupants and the supply air was not considered during the study. Furthermore the 
occupants were simulated by cylindrical shaped box, which gave an unrealistic representation of the 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
31
thermal plume (Zukowska et al. (2012A). This gave an unrealistic airflow interaction, which affected 
the tracer gas and air speed measurement results. Therefore more detailed studies have to be made. 
 
Textile duct ventilation has been installed in classrooms in the Netherlands and United Kingdom (Euro 
Air, 2011; KE-Fibertec, 2011). These cases indicate that textile duct ventilation have potential for 
providing a satisfactory indoor environment. However, the textile duct ventilation system used made 
use of the mixing principle, aiming to create a uniform environment, which is less efficient compared 
to a localized one in terms of health and comfort. Data on the performance of the textile duct 
ventilation with regard to inhaled air quality as well as non-uniformity of the environment generated in 
the room occupied zone were not provided. 
 
The textile ducts can be installed directly above the occupied zone to provide local effect. Potentially, 
less energy may be required when only ventilating a smaller part of the room compared to ventilating 
the whole room, as in the case with MV and DV, since a smaller airflow rate may be required.  Nielsen 
et al. (2005) report that textile duct ventilation is able to generate comfortable conditions up to a 
thermal load that has the same level as the maximum thermal load for mixing ventilation and 
displacement ventilation, i.e. textile ducts were thus able to obtain the level of thermal satisfaction as 
the traditional ones. However the textile ducts may supply cold air to the head of the occupant, which 
may be an advantage in terms of thermal comfort (people like to have a cool head and warm feet) but it 
may also cause draught discomfort at the head region (if neck region is cooled), especially when 
occupants are seated in the vicinity of the downward buoyancy flow from the textile duct.

7 Objectives 
The main objectives of this project were: 
1)  to investigate the potential to improve inhaled air quality with regards to body generated 
pollutants (bio-effluents) and pollution emitted in the vicinity of the human body by textile 
ducts used for ventilating rooms with occupants seated for relatively long periods of time, such 
as classrooms, meeting rooms, etc. 
2) to define the advantages and disadvantages of the airflow distribution through textile ducts 
compared to other air well known distribution air systems, i.e. mixing and displacement 
ventilation principles. 
3)  to study and to improve the interaction of the supplied ventilation air by textile duct ventilation 
with the thermal plume generated by occupants with focus on providing clean air to the 
breathing zone of occupants and minimising thermal discomfort;

The measurements were carried out in a climate chamber. The chamber’s size could be varied: the

Textile duct design 1: HDC 150 - 160° 
The textile duct design 1, HDC 150 - 160°, was just like the initial textile duct design. The only

8.1.4.2 
Textile duct design 2: HDC 50 - 180° XL holes 
The textile duct design 2, HDC 50 - 180° XL holes, used incorporated plafond through which the 
ventilation air was supplied. The textile duct was made of HDC 50. There were laser-cut 29 mm holes 
with a spacing of 333 mm. The textile duct design is illustrated in Table 3 design number 2. 
The plafond was attached, by a ducting of diameter 29 mm at the connection expanding to a diameter 
of 60 mm at the end, to one of the laser-cut holes in the textile duct. The plafond was placed 30 cm 
above the head of occupants. The shape of the plafond could be adjusted. The plafond could therefore 
be either completely open or closed, Figure 12. Both configurations were tested. The idea behind the 
design was that the plafond would shorten the distance between the supply of clean air and the 
breathing zone. By having a shorter distance the supply air would be less polluted before reaching the 
breathing zone of the occupant seated below it.

8.1.4.5 
Textile duct design 5: HDC 50 - 55° - 155° 
The textile duct design 5, HDC 50 - 55° - 155°, was made in an effort to address the re-circulation 
problem discussed later in section 11.1. The textile duct design was based on the initial textile duct 
design (HDC 150 - 155°). The holes, in the bottom, were kept but additional holes at angle of 55° were 
added on the two sides on top of the duct. The idea with these holes was that they would counteract the 
entrainment of the re-circulated polluted air entrained by the supply air from the holes placed at the 
bottom. The new holes were made with the idea that they would create an under pressure, which would 
made the re-circulated polluted air go up and would be pushed away along the ceiling instead of going 
down with the clean supply air from the bottom of the duct. The textile duct was made of HDC 50. The 
design of the textile duct can be seen in Table 3 design number 5. 
8.1.4.6 
Textile duct design 6: HDC 50 - 155° 
Textile duct design 6, HDC 50 - 155°, was geometrically the same as the initial textile duct design. The 
only change was that the material of the textile was changed from HDC 150 to HDC 50. The intention 
was to make the textile material more thick and thereby having more of the supply air going through 
the laser-cut holes. Thus the supply air from the laser-cut holes would have a stronger jet, which may 
be able to supply cleaner air to the breathing zone of the occupants. The design of the textile duct can 
be seen in Table 3 design number 6.

8.1.6 Simulation of heat and pollution

8.1.6.1 
Heated dummy 
The heated dummy was made to represent the human body shape in a simplified manner, Figure 18. 
Zukowska et al. (2012A) had investigated the impact of geometry of a sedentary occupant on the 
generated thermal plume. The study recommended using a heated dummy (instead of a cylindrical or 
rectangular shape box) if a thermal manikin was not available. The heated dummy consisted of three 
parts: legs, torso and head and was made of galvanized ducts. The legs and head were made out of 
circular pipes with a diameter of 12.5 cm and 19 cm respectively. The torso was rectangular shaped 
with the dimensions 20 cm X 60 cm X 35 cm (length x height x width). The heated dummy was 135 
cm high when seated. The mouth was located at a height of 110 cm.

The dummy was heated by four light bulbs placed in the head, in the torso and in each leg. There was a 
fan placed inside the torso of the dummy in order to make a more uniform distribution of the heat 
generated by the light bulbs.  
 
The thermal plume was an important factor when considering the textile duct ventilation. It was 
essential that the thermal plume above the human head could be simulated properly when conducting 
experiments concerning flow interaction at the breathing zone with a supplied air coming from above.  
8.1.6.2 
Breathing thermal manikin 
A breathing thermal manikin (BTM) shaped like a real person (an average Scandinavian woman 1.7 m 
tall) was used to simulate the heat generated by an average occupant and to measure the heat exchange 
between the simulated occupant and the environment. The control of the manikin maintained its surface 
temperature equal to the skin temperature of a person in thermal comfort at given activity level.  
The BTM consisted of 23 body parts. Each body part was individually controlled by computer 
software. The BTM shell was made of 3 mm glass armed polyester. Nickel wires were wound around 
the shell of each body part. The nickel wires were 0.3 mm in diameter. The nickel wires in each body 
part were independently heated. The nickel wires were placed with a distance of less than 2 mm 
between in order to achieve a uniform temperature distribution along the manikin surface. This secured 
a uniform a heat loss over the entire section. Therefore it was possible to control each of the 23 body 
parts as desired. In appendix 15.4 is given a list of the 23 body parts of the BTM together with a photo 
of the BTM. The BTM can be adjusted in sitting, standing and laying posture.

8.3.2 Tracer gas measurements 
During the tracer gas measurements the constant-emission method was used. This method can be used 
for long-term continuous air change rate measurements in a single zone. However, the method can also 
be used for evaluating the ventilation effectiveness (Contaminant removal effectiveness and 
personalized exposure effectiveness, section 6.4). 
 
The Innova 1303 multipoint sampler and doser were used during the tracer gas measurements. During 
measurements the data was stored from the Innova 1303 to a computer. The measurement values were 
wrote to a text file, which later was analysed in Excel for calculating the ventilation effectiveness.  
As two tracer gasses were used for four pollution sources, after measuring the concentrations for two of 
them, i.e. feet and groins, the dosing locations were switched to the other two pollution sources, i.e. 
table and exhaled air from the manikin. 
 
The time required for one measurement in a point was approx. 2.5 min for all six channels when using 
one tracer gas and it was approx. 4 min when using two tracer gases. The average tracer gas 
concentration in a point was based on at least 8-10 samples in order to get statistically correct value. 
This meant that the time needed for measuring with six channels was approx. 30 min when using one 
tracer gas and approx. 45 min when using two tracer gases. The measurements were made when steady 
state was reached. Steady state had been reached when at least the last 8-10 samples were at the same 
concentration level. 
 
After a tracer gas measurement the measurement setup can be altered. It takes around 3 changes of the 
air volume of the whole space in order to reach a 90% steady state level. Therefore it varied how long 
time it would take before the new steady state level had been reached based on the background 
ventilation airflow rate.

11.5 Smoke visualization: Discussion 
The purpose, of the smoke visualization, was to investigate how the interaction between the supply 
airflow and the free convection flow around the occupant was for each duct design. The initial textile 
duct design was thoroughly studied. It was seen that airflow rates of 60 l/s and 80 l/s were promising in 
terms of peeling off the free convection flow around the occupant and bringing more clean air to the 
breathing zone compared to the lower flow rate of 40 l/s. When having 80 l/s the free convection flow 
was peeled off at the mouth level in every studied distance of the manikin in relation to the textile duct. 
However, that was not the case for 60 l/s, which performed best when the manikin was positioned 
0.2 m behind the textile duct. These results seem reasonable when considering the design of the duct 
and its supply air jets. An airflow rate of 40 l/s was too low to have a sufficient impact on the free 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
 
70
convection flow. This was because the supply air jet was too weak in order to oppose the rising plume 
above the head and peel off the free convection flow. It was observed, for all of the four airflow rates, 
that the polluted air from the back of the simulated occupant went up to the ceiling and was then 
”pulled” down (by the flow from the textile duct) towards the breathing zone of the occupant. This was 
due to the supply air jet, which created an under pressure causing the polluted air to be sucked into the 
jet. It was also observed that the position of the occupants relative to the duct had an influence. This 
was of course reasonable to expected, however, it could not be assessed in advanced how much it 
would affect the system performance in terms of air quality. Therefore tracer gas measurements were 
performed as a follow-up (discussed in the next section). 
The airflow pattern in the whole room was also studied. It was found that there was a vortex present in 
front of the occupant. The vortex was recirculating the polluted air in the zone above the table (upper 
half of the room) and back into the breathing zone, Figure 31. The vortex contributed in polluting the 
supply air and thereby lowering the system performance with respect to air quality. The vortex may be 
dealt by changing the duct design. It was likely that the vortex mixed the room air to such a degree that 
the ventilation system would perform on the same level as a mixing ventilation system when in front of 
the table. The tracer gas measurements discussed in the following clarify this. 
Textile duct design 1(Table 3 design number 1), HDC 150 - 160°, was made in order to see if a sharper 
angle of the holes would affect the airflow pattern. The smoke visualization showed promising results 
too. Earlier studies, conducted by KE-Fibertec, showed that this design would create a higher turbulent 
intensity compared to the initial textile duct design (private communication). An increase in turbulent 
intensity may increase the mixing of the supply air with the room air resulting in a lower system 
performance with respect to air quality. 
Textile duct design 2 (Table 3 design number 2), HDC50 - 180° XL holes, was studied because it was 
interesting to see if a shortened distance between the breathing zone and the supply air by a plafond 
opening would increase the performance of the ventilation system. The plafond, for the lighting, can be 
combined with the supply opening for the fresh air taken from the textile ducts. When the plafond of 
the lamp was open the performance was higher compared to the closed plafond case. This may be due 
to less pressure loss when traveling through the plafond. 
Textile duct design 3 (Table 3 design number 3), HDC150 - 174°, had a good ability to peel off the free 
convection flow in front of the occupant. This was due to the holes placed at the bottom of the duct. 
The idea was that when having holes at the bottom the supply air jet would merge together creating an 
air curtain making it more likely to reach the breathing zone with clean air. The results were promising; 
however, the thermal comfort had also to be checked to see if the duct won’t cause thermal discomfort. 
Textile duct design 4 (Table 3 design number 4), HDC150-168°+180°, was an iteration of the Textile 
duct design 3 (Table 3 design number 3), HDC150 - 174°. The intention was to see if an additionally 
row of holes would increase the system performance by providing cleaner air to the breathing zone. 
This design showed to be capable of peeling off the free convection flow. However, the existing 
concerns regarding the thermal comfort still remained. The concern was that increased cooling of the 
occupant would appear due to the increased proportion of supply air coming from the holes compared 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
 
71
to the initial textile duct design. By having more supply air through the holes the air velocities may 
increase causing increased risk of draught, especially in the neck area. 
Textile duct design 5 (Table 3 design number 5), HDC50-55°+155°, was made in an effort to 
counteract the vortex generated in the room, Figure 31, and thereby reduce the recirculating of polluted 
air. The smoke visualization showed improved performance in terms of the ability to peeling off the 
free convection flow around the occupant compared to the initial textile duct (HDC 150 – 155°) design. 
Textile duct design 5 was also able to reach the crown of the occupant to a higher extend compared to 
the initial textile duct design. This may be beneficial when considering thermal comfort since people 
generally like to have a cool head and warm feet. The airflow pattern was changed behind the 
occupant. This may have influence on the system performance with respect to air quality. This was 
clarified during the tracer gas measurements (following section). 
Textile duct design 6 (Table 3 design number 6), HDC50-155°, was an iteration of the initial textile 
duct design. The intention was to see if a less permeable textile duct would increase the performance. 
By having a less permeable textile the proportion of supply air going through the holes would increase. 
The textile duct was able to peel off the free convection. However, it was seen that because the fraction 
of supply air going through the holes was increased the entrainment of recirculated air was also 
increased. This may be because the air velocity at holes had increased making a higher under pressure, 
which increased the entrainment of the polluted air.  
Generally the system performance, in terms of inhaled air quality, can’t be assessed only based on 
smoke visualizations. Therefore the ducts were tested thoroughly with tracer gas measurements in order 
to assess the system performance. 
The mixing and displacement ventilation system established the expected airflow patterns as intended 
for the systems. The MV system made a lot of mixing of the room air as intended. Some of the supply 
from the floor went into the free convection flow around the occupants. The MV system was observed 
to make a lot of mixing as intended, which may indicate that the system would provide good mixing. 
The DV system made a thermal stratification of the air, which was the intention with the system. The 
supply air that reached the occupant joined the free convection flow around the occupants and went 
upwards. The DV system may provide low protection against pollution sources located on the floor, on 
the body of the occupant and close to the occupant. This was because the free convection flow would 
transport the polluted air upward toward the breathing zone of the occupants. However, both the MV 
and DV system did not peel off the free convection flow around the occupants. This may lead to a 
worse system performance compared to the TV system. 
The advantage of TV compare to MV and DV was the ability of the TV to peel off the free convection 
flow around the occupant. These air jets may also improve the thermal comfort due to cooling of the 
upper body. The disadvantage of TV was the recirculation of polluted air. However, this recirculation 
may be small that the one provided by mixing ventilation. 
 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
 
72
11.6 Smoke visualization – Conclusion 
The different textile duct designs were able; in varying extend, to bring clean air to the breathing zone 
of the occupants. It was observed that the airflow rate and position of the occupants relative to the duct 
had an impact on the airflow interaction. All designs managed to peel off the boundary layer in front of 
the occupant at supply flows exceeding 40 l/s. When the occupant was positioned directly below the 
textile duct the plume did not allow the supplied air to reach the breathing zones. At flows below 60 l/s 
this happened when occupant was 0.2 m behind the textile duct. When the person was 0.4 m behind the 
textile duct, the clean air glided in front of the boundary layer and over the table. Generally all of the 
duct designs showed that recirculation of polluted room air was present in the breathing zone, however 
to a varying extend. The recirculation of polluted air seemed to increase with increase of the supply air 
velocity due to the increased under pressure created by the supply air jet. None of the studied duct 
designs were capable of altering the airflow pattern in the room in such a way that recirculation was 
completely avoided though with some designs it was lowered. The duct designs iteration showed to be 
more promising with respect to improving the air quality performance. 
The MV and DV system performed as intended based on their designs. Both MV and DV did not 
remove the free convection flow at the breathing zone. TV may be able to provide an increased system 
performance compared to MV and DV due to this airflow interaction.

11.7 Tracer gas measurements – Initial textile duct design, HDC 150 - 155° 
The tracer gas measurements were conducted in order to investigate the influence of parameters such 
as: supply airflow rate, position of occupant with respect to textile diffuser, supply air temperature, 
textile duct height, etc. on the air quality generated by the flow from the textile ducts. Furthermore, 
textile duct ventilation was compared to mixing ventilation and displacement ventilation. During the 
measurements the following condition was selected/used (if nothing else is stated) as reference: 1) the 
thermal manikin and dummies were placed 0.2 m behind the textile duct (distance from center of duct 
to center of head) and the duct was lifted 2.32 m from the floor level, 2) the supply airflow rate was 60 
l/s (ΔT=3.9 K) from the textile duct, 3) the air temperature of the laboratory hall and the chamber were 
set at 26 °C (at 1.1 m height from floor), 4) the pollution sources were placed in the groins and feet of 
the manikin and in the table in front of position 1, 5) The manikin was placed in position 1 (Figure 7). 
The results obtained with the reference case (Initial textile duct design, HDC 150 - 155°) will be 
plotted as a horizontal line for comparison in the following figures below. The breathing function of the

11.9 Tracer gas – Discussion 
Performance of the initial textile duct design, HDC 150 - 155° 
The airflow rate had a significant effect on the system performance. However, once a certain airflow 
rate, for 50 l/s for feet and table pollution and 60 l/s for groins pollution, had been reached the system 
performance seemed to be stable and not increasing with increasing of the airflow rate. It was then 
possible for the supply air to reach the breathing zone. The reason for the same system performance 
might be the recirculation flow remained unaltered both behind and in front of the occupants. 
 
The position of the occupants relative to the duct had been also investigated. It was found that the 
performance of the ventilation system was decreased a little in the case of the table pollution source, 
when moving backward relative to the textile duct as expected. The reason may be that the supply air 
dropped down on the table instead of near the breathing zone when the occupant sat 0.4 m behind. This 
effect was observed during the smoke visualizations. 
Generally, the performance of the ventilation system was the almost same, regardless of position when 
considering the groins and table pollution. For the feet it was not the case. The reason for this could be 
that the supply air jet can’t reach directly the feet pollution source, and thereby push it away to the 
same degree, as in the case with the pollution from the table and groins. 
 
When the exhaust was moved to the floor the improvement, when considering groins as pollution, may 
be explained due to the fact that the table blocked part of the convection flow preventing it to rise up 
and bring pollutants to the face. Furthermore it could be that the exhaust had a positive effect on the 
vortex pulling it away from the occupants. 
The polluted air from the feet pollution source may be more likely to follow the free convection flow 
around the occupants, resulting in the same system performance, than being sucked out directly by the 
exhaust at the floor. 
The ventilation effectiveness was generally high when looking at the table pollution source. This may 
be due to the fact that it was pushed away from the table surface by the incoming downward supply air 
and then being captured by the recirculation zone. The performance was generally the same as when 
having the ceiling based exhaust for the other pollution sources. The reason could be that a lot of 
mixing of the room air had happen for each of the pollution sources, which made the pollution level in 
the room air at the floor and ceiling almost the same. However, the performance may be very different 
when having active pollution sources in the room. The ventilation effectiveness was the worse when 
having an airflow rate of 45 l/s as expected. The performance was; however, almost 2 times better than 
in the case of having an airflow rate of 40 l/s with ceiling based exhaust. 
 
The effect of changing the supply air temperature was also tested, Figure 44. Changing the supply air 
temperature had no big effect on the performance of the system in terms of personalized exposure. The 
reason may be that the air was mixed too much with the surrounding air before it reached the breathing 
zone to have any effect on the personalized exposure effectiveness. The heating panels radiate heat 
==PAGE:97==
 
 
 
97
onto the dummies, which increased the surface temperature of them. This increase of surface 
temperature may also increases the thickness of the free convection flow around the dummies, which 
may balance out the effect of supplying cooler air. The theory was that the colder air being heavier 
under the cases with the heated windows had a stronger momentum and managed to better peel off the 
boundary layer at the face of the occupant. 
The contaminant removal effectiveness was increased for every pollution source. The highest 
contaminant removal effectiveness was obtained during this case compared to the others. The reason 
could be that the thermal stratification was lifted up inside the room. Hence the cleaner zone was larger 
compared to the other studied cases. This indicated that the system might perform better under high 
heat loads. However, the exhaust temperature remained the same as the room air temperature of 26 °C. 
It was chosen not to calculate Archimedes and Reynolds numbers for the textile duct, at the different 
supply air temperatures. This was because it would be quite difficult to assess the Archimedes number 
due to the fact that the supply air was coming through both the laser-cut holes and the permeable 
textile. Each was a different velocity and supply area. Similar calculations were made by Nielsens et al. 
(2007). However, the textile duct used, in that study, was only supplying air through the permeable 
textile, which made the problem simpler. 
 
The performance of the ventilation system with respect to air quality changed when moving the 
polluting manikin from position 1 to position 2, Figure 45 and Figure 46. This variation was expected 
since when the manikin was placed in position 1 only the left side of the manikin was close to an 
adjacent person resulting in a weaker free convection flow compared to the situation when the manikin 
was placed at position 2, i.e. between the two dummies. 
It was observed, as expected that the person seating at position 3 had consistently more clean air than 
the one at position 2, when the polluting occupant sat in position 1. This was because the distance from 
the pollution source was longer for the exposed person at 3. However, the difference in the 
personalized exposure effectiveness between position 2 and 3 was relatively small. When the manikin 
was placed in position 2 the performance dropped due to the stronger free convection flow enhanced by 
the boundary layers of the neighbouring occupants. The personalized exposure effectiveness was, as 
expected, almost the same regardless of which side left or right the occupant sat. However, the 
personalized exposure effectiveness was better for three of the pollution sources at position 1. This 
could be due to the power distribution inside each of the dummies. The dummies had the same total 
power output. However, the power output for each of the legs of the dummies was not the same causing 
a small difference in the free convection flow around the dummies. The power output differed 10 W. 
15 W and 25 W bulbs were installed in the legs. 
 
It seemed that the height from floor (installation height) of the textile duct was of some importance 
until the airflow rate reached a certain level of 60 l/s (50 l/s was not tested) above which the supply air 
was able to reach the breathing zone. The installation height of the textile duct was of importance when 
one wanted to be able to lower the necessary airflow rate to reach the breathing zone while maintaining 
==PAGE:98==

the same ventilation effectiveness at a lowered supply flow rate, i.e. 45 l/s. In terms of energy 
consumption this strategy can prove beneficial. It should be noted that generally the system 
performance was overall increased. It seemed reasonable that lowering the height of the duct would 
lead to a decrease in need of air for the same performance and increased performance at the same 
airflow rate since the distance between the textile duct and breathing zone became shorter. 
Furthermore, it was less likely that the polluted air in the vortex would be entrained by the supply air 
jet. The reason was that the polluted air was instead located close to the ceiling, which in this case 
reduced its recirculation. 
 
The influence of the number of occupants below the duct was also investigated. It was found that the 
system air quality performance for pollution from groins and feet remained the same, as expected. It 
was expected to be the same since the airflow pattern was likely to remain the same around each 
occupant. However, the system performance had decreased for the table source pollution. A possible 
explanation could be that the strength of the vortex (Figure 31) in front of the table had increased due 
to increased heat load in the room making it broader. Thereby the vortex may have been recirculating 
the table pollution directly into the breathing zone compared to the case with 3 occupants. 
 
The influence of the breathing cycle was investigated when measuring the vertical concentration 
distributions. It was found that the breathing (continuous exhalation or breathing in a cycle) had no 
effect on the measurement results. The reason was the measurement locations were far enough from the 
breathing manikin. Therefore the air was very much mixed by the time it reached measurement 
locations and thus the calculated contaminant removal effectiveness was the same regardless if the 
manikin was continuously exhaling or breathing in a cycle. Furthermore, it was also likely that the 
room air had mixed a lot, due to the vortex, making the choice of breathing cycle mode redundant. 
 
It was measured that the room air was the cleanest behind the occupants. This was likely due to the 
position of the pollution sources. Most of the pollution sources were placed in the front of the occupant, 
which made the polluted air more likely to spread towards the front side of the occupants. However, 
also the chairs limited the development of the free convection flow at the back of the body.  
It was measured that the cleanest air, in front of the occupants, were found in position 1 when 
considering the pollution from the manikin (exhaled air). The reason was that the exhaled air by the 
manikin rose up (the exhaled air was heated), which also explains why the air was cleaner near the 
floor. The most polluted air could be found in position 3, which was due to the vortex present in the 
front half of the room (Figure 31 and Figure 51) that mixed the room air a lot.  
When considering the table pollution it was found that the most polluted air was located near the floor 
at positions 2 and 3. This may be due to the fact that the cold supply air pushed the pollution away from 
the table surface, which was also a passive pollution source, making it drop down towards the floor. 
Above 0.50 m the system performance with respect to air quality was close to that of a mixing 
ventilation system, which might be due to the vortex present in the room. The air behind the occupant

was the cleanest with respect to table generated pollution, which can be explained by the position of the 
table relative to measurement position 4. The air in position 3 was cleaner compared to position 1 and 
2. This may indicate that the polluted air might have spread in one direction from the table, i.e. in a 
plane perpendicularly to the table. This could be explained by the vortex present in the room. 
The vertical concentration distribution for the pollution from the groins may be explained in the same 
way as for the table source. The cold supply air pushed away the polluted air down to the floor at

The air, in position 3, was cleaner compared to that in position 1 and 2 because it was located further

The vertical concentration distribution for the pollution from the feet were also investigated. The 
explanation for the variation between position 1 and 2 was due the presence of the vortex. Most of the 
polluted air moved under the table with the convection coming from the lower body. It was then pulled 
in by the recirculation zone (vortex) in front of the table, Figure 31 and Figure 54. The polluted air was 
then carried up again at a higher height at position 2, explaining the mirror image of the measurement 
results for position 1 and 2. 
 
The three different ventilation systems, MV, DV and TV, were tested. The TV system had a 
contaminant removal effectiveness of around one for the three different pollution sources. This 
indicated that the TV might not provide a high air quality performance for the occupant outside of the 
breathing zone. However, the performance was as good as or even better than MV and DV. It was 
observed that all the three systems were best at protecting from the table pollution source, when 
considering the personalized exposure effectiveness. This could be explained by the fact that the table 
source was located outside the free convection flow of the manikin making it harder for the table 
pollution to reach the mouth compared to the other pollution sources simulated. In case of TV the 
downward air swept it off the table. With MV apparently the background flow pattern helped to dilute 
the pollutant. With DV the clean supply air along the floor was entrained by the convection flow 
enriching it with clean air from the lower room levels. The DV system performed the worst of the three 
systems, in terms of personalized exposure effectiveness. It was expected from the literature that the 
DV would had performed better than the MV system. However, this can be explained based on the 
nature of the pollution sources used. The pollution from the feet and groins travelled along with the free 
convection flow up to the breathing zone of the occupant.  
In terms of personalized exposure effectiveness the TV system did the best, the reason may be that the 
TV system provided the supply air more directly into the breathing zone, avoiding a lot of mixing, 
compared to the MV and DV systems. Furthermore the TV system could peel off the free convection 
flow, which contained the pollutions making the breathing air cleaner. The MV system performed as 
expected in terms of level of performance: the personalized exposure effectiveness, for the MV system,

was around one because the distance between the feet and the mouth was long, which made it possible 
for the MV system to mix the polluted air. However, the performance of MV system was low for the 
groins pollution due to the short distance to the month. The measurement results for the personalized 
exposure effectiveness supported the idea of the TV system as a valid ventilation system for providing 
clean air to the occupants. However, this may not be the case if the textile duct was instead of

The airflow pattern may be disrupted by a walking person and alike, but due to the supply air jets and 
the setup, it was assessed that the airflow pattern around the occupant would most likely remain. The 
following was of greater concern: person’s posture (leaned back, forth etc.), movement on desk while 
working etc. since those can affect the airflow interactions to a greater extent. 
 
The cross contamination between a standing person and the sitting occupants was tested for each of the 
three different ventilation systems. 
The performance of the TV system was almost the same (1.03 – 1.14) regardless the position at the 
tables and pollution source. The air quality performance was at the level of ideal mixing indicating that 
the TV system generated a lot of mixing of the supplied air before it reached the sitting people. These 
results made sense when considering the smoke visualisations, which showed that a vortex in front of 
the table was present causing mixing of the air between the standing manikin and the sitting dummies. 
Based on these measurements and the previous measurements (section 11.7.10) it could be seen that the 
TV system had the highest air quality performance at the breathing zone of the occupant when the 
pollution sources were placed inside the zone of the sitting occupants. When the pollution source was 
placed outside the area of the tables the TV performed at the level or better than a perfect mixing 
ventilation system. 
The performance of the MV system was between 0.90 - 1.41. The MV system performed best against 
the pollution coming from the groins. The MV performed similar to the TV system when considering 
the pollution coming from exhaled air. The MV system performed better than the TV system for 
pollution coming from feet, this could be because the supply air from the MV system was going along 
the ceiling and walls until it hit the floor from where it spread pushing the pollution from the feet away 
from the dummies. This airflow pattern was observed during smoke visualisations. 
The performance of the DV system varied from 0.56 - 9.98. The high performance of the DV system 
when protecting the dummies from the exhaled air of the breathing manikin seemed reasonable. This 
was because the DV system was designed to vertically push up the pollution and since the mouth of the 
manikin was already located above the mouth of the dummies it was unlikely for the pollution to reach 
the dummies. Furthermore the exhaled air was heated to 34 °C, so it went up. Also with DV there was 
no high background air velocities, which made it move up undistributed by the background flow. 
However, in the case of feet and groins pollution varied air quality performance was measured. The 
low air quality performance for the feet pollution can be explained by the fact that the pollution likely 
had spread along the floor until it reached the free convection flow of the dummies from where it had

been transported up to the mouth of the dummies. The same seemed to be the case for the groins 
pollution. With DV the background velocities are low so the free convection flow existed undisturbed 
and served as a transport media for pollutants generated from body, i.e. feet and groins. 
The large variation in the performance for the feet and groins may be explained with differences in the 
strength of the free convection flows around the dummies. This was because not exactly the same

The difference in the measurement of the breath pollution could also be caused by the fact that the

The cross contaminant from the sitting occupants to the standing person was tested for each of the three 
different ventilation systems. The TV system performed on the same level as a perfect mixing 
ventilation system. This could be due to the fact that the vortex in front of the table caused a lot of 
mixing. The TV performed the worse compared to MV and DV when considering the table pollution. 
The reason was that the polluted air from the table was pushed away and towards the standing occupant 
merging with the free convection flow around the standing occupant.  
Based on this and the previous measurements it can be observed that the TV system may not provide 
any enhanced performance compared to the MV and DV when considering cross contamination. 
 
Performance of the improved textile duct designs 
The improved textile duct designs were also tested, look section 11.8. It was found that the textile duct 
design 1, namely HDC 150 - 160°, performed worse compared to the initial textile duct design HDC 
150 - 155°. The reason for this could be due to increased mixing of the supplied air as a result of the 
higher turbulent intensity of the supply air jets. KE-Fibertec reported in an earlier study that textile duct 
design 1, HDC 150 - 160°, had a higher turbulent intensity than the initial textile duct design used in 
this project (private communication with KE-Fibertec). 
The textile duct design 2, HDC50 - 180° XL holes, was measured to perform at a lower level than the 
initial textile duct design, HDC 150 - 155°. The design only managed to perform close (worse) to the 
initial textile duct design for all of the pollution sources. This could be because the cold supply air 
dropped down on the table sweeping the polluted air away and bypassing the breathing zone of the 
seated occupant. This could also explain the low personalized exposure effectiveness when considering 
the pollution from the groins and feet. The smoke visualizations supported this theory. 
The textile duct design 3, HDC 150 - 174°, performed worse than the initial textile duct design, HDC 
150 - 155°, with respect to air quality. The idea was that the supply air jets would exhibit a stronger 
ability to push away the polluted air. However, the results showed that it was not the case. The reason 
could be that the supply air jets created a higher level of entrainment of surrounding air causing the air 
at the breathing zone to be more polluted. Another reason could be that the free convection flow wasn’t 
==PAGE:102==
 
 
 
102
completely peeled off at the breathing zone of the occupant as observed during the smoke 
visualizations for the studied duct design. 
The textile duct design 4, HDC 150 - 168° - 180° had a lower performance than the initial textile duct 
design, HDC 150 - 155°, in spite of the promising airflow pattern, which removed the free convection 
flow in front of the occupant during the smoke visualizations. The reason for the lower performance 
could be due to enhanced entrainment and mixing of the room air. This would lead to a higher 
concentration of pollutions in the breathing zone. 
The textile duct design 5, HDC50 - 55° - 155°, had improved performance when considering the 
pollution from the feet compared to the initial textile duct design. A possible explanation for this could 
be that the recirculation zone behind the person was moved further behind the occupant. So the 
pollutant coming from feet was pulled away from the free convection flow in front of the occupant 
(Figure 31 and Figure 38). The textile duct design had low performance when considering the pollution 
from the groins. This may be connected to the change in the airflow pattern behind the occupant, which 
somehow directed the pollution towards the breathing zone. 
The textile duct design 6, HDC 50 - 155°, was made in an effort to investigate if a less permeable 
textile would be beneficial in improving occupants’ air quality. The results showed that the textile duct 
design 6, HDC 50 - 155°, did not perform better than the initial textile design. Therefore a less 
permeable textile was not beneficial for this kind of textile duct design. As the textile was less 
permeable, more air came from the laser cut holes. As a result, the downward flow, through the textile, 
was decreased and was not that strong to push down the plume above the head of the occupant! (Figure 
39 and Figure 63).

Tracer gas – Conclusion 
Initial textile duct design, HDC 150 - 155° 
• The initial textile duct design was tested through tracer gas measurements. It was found that the 
performance of the system was independent of the airflow rate once an airflow rate of 60 L/s 
was reached. 
• The position of the textile duct with respect to the occupant, i.e. x=-0.4 m – 0.0 m, was found to 
be of less importance on air quality performance for pollution generated from the body (feet, 
groins and exhaled air) or from passive pollution from furniture (table). However, the air quality 
performance was affected for the feet pollution at x=0.0 m. Thereby the system was able to 
allow for some flexibility in terms of placement of the workstation relative to the textile duct.  
• The position of the exhaust diffuser was found to be beneficial in terms of pollution from 
groins. Generally the same performance was obtained for the other pollution sources, feet, 
exhaled air and table. This was due to the high amount of mixing of the room air outside the 
occupied zone. The ceiling based exhaust may be more beneficial when having stronger or 
more active pollution sources.  
• The influence of the supply air temperature was found to be very limited in terms of 
personalized exposure effectiveness. 
==PAGE:103==
 
 
 
103
• The performance of the system, when considering cross contamination, was best when having 
the pollution at position 1 compared to having the pollution coming from position 2. 
• The textile duct installation height from floor was found to be affecting the system 
performance. The air quality performance was better the closer the textile duct was to the 
occupants. 
• The number of occupants below the textile duct was of less importance. It was found that the 
system performance would remain the same if the number of persons per meter textile duct 
remained the same below the textile duct. However, the air quality performance was affected 
for the table pollution. 
• It was found that the TV system performed in the same way as MV and DV systems when 
considering the contaminant removal effectiveness for the reference setup. The TV system 
performed better than the MV and DV system for all of the three different pollution sources 
(groins, feet and table) in terms of personalized exposure effectiveness.  
• TV showed to deliver a constant level of performance when consider the cross contamination 
between a sitting and standing persons. However, TV showed varying performance compared to 
MV and DV. 
Improved textile duct designs 
• None of the improved textile duct designs reached a system performance higher than the initial 
textile duct design (HDC 150 - 155°).

The air velocities were measured above the head of the manikin along a radial line starting from the

supply air jet propagating towards the head of the occupant peeling off the free convection flow around 
the occupant. This situation can be acceptable as long as the occupant doesn’t experience draught. 
However, this may be determined through human subject experiments. It is known that people likes to 
have a cold head, which may be provided by such localized jet, thereby it may increase the local and 
overall thermal comfort. 
It was observed that the manikin created a blocking effect, Melikov (2009). The presence of the 
manikin had a big impact on the air speed at the height of 10 cm. The air velocities went from 0.28 m/s 
to 0.12 m/s, which can be explained by the interaction between the free convection flow and the 
thermal plume from the occupant. However, TV performed reasonably well compared to the limits 
given in DS/EN 1752 for air velocity. The maximum mean air velocity in a classroom, class A, during 
the winter is 0.15 m/s, which is higher than the measured 0.12 m/s. Therefore the TV system still 
performed quite well despite having higher air velocities than MV and DV, Figure 65. 
 
The air speed measurements horizontally above the head of the manikin showed again that TV reached 
higher air velocities than MV and DV. TV was measured to have the highest air velocities 25 cm above 
the head, especially in the range 0 - 15 cm. The air velocities dropped afterwards, which could be 
because of the joining of the free convection flows coming from the shoulders with the plume above 
the head. So this flow became much stronger than the downward supply flow from the TV and 
dampenes the resultant speed (counter flows). The DV system reached quite low air velocities, since 
cooler air supplied close to the floor may reduce the lower body surface temperature and thus weaken 
the plume. The MV system was observed to have constant air speed profile and the same speed at 
25 cm and 50 cm, which was expected since a uniform environment was desired as a result of good 
mixing. The maximum air speed for the TV system moved to the right as the height was increased. The 
explanation may be due to the joining of the free convection flows coming from the shoulders with the 
plume above the head had weakened.

Air velocity – Conclusion 
• The air speed measurements had showed that TV generally resulted in air velocities higher than 
the air velocities obtained with a MV or a DV system. This was due to the supply air jets made 
by the textile duct. These high air velocities may result in increased thermal comfort since 
people like to have a cold head. However, human subject experiments have to be made to 
justify this.  
• It was observed that the presence of occupants and their thermal plume had affected the

speed of 0.07 m/s, which was nearly half of that with TV. Generally, DV resulted in the lowest 
measured air speed. 
• TV performed (0.12 m/s at 10 cm above the height) within the limits for a classroom according 
to DS/EN 1752 class A for winter conditions.

Studies by Lee et al. (1998) showed that humans JND (just noticeable difference) values of cutaneous 
thermal sensation corresponding to the skin, room temperature and temperature change rate were

The initial textile duct design, HDC 150 – 155°, may improve the thermal comfort for people feeling 
warm or hot (ASHRAE 55, 2010; ISO 7730, 2005) due to its increased air velocities compared to MV 
and DV (Figure 65). However, it may also decrease the thermal comfort for people feeling neutral or 
cold due to the convective cooling effect of the TV supply air jet. 
The six improved textile duct designs were tested and compared to the initial textile duct design. It was 
observed that generally all of the new textile duct designs led to increased cooling for the whole body 
compared to the initial textile duct design. An explanation for these results could be that most of the 
new textile duct designs had a higher proportion of the supply air coming through the holes compared 
to the initial textile duct design. Thereby the momentum of the supply air jets coming from the holes 
and directed towards the body, would be higher making it easier for the supply air jet to peel off the 
free convection flow leading to more cooling of the body. All of the new textile duct designs were 
either having more laser-cut holes per meter of textile material or a less permeable textile leading to an 
increased supply air going through the laser-cut holes. It was observed that all of the new textile duct 
design didn’t lead to increased cooling of back of the neck, which could be beneficial in terms of 
thermal comfort since people can be more sensitive at the back of neck (Müller et al., 2013).

• Generally, all the new textile duct designs were creating a cooler overall thermal sensation

12 General Discussion 
A series of measurements were carried out in order to study the performance of textile duct ventilation 
(TV). An initial textile duct design, HDC 150 – 155°, and ten improved textile duct designs were 
studied. At present, the initial textile duct design proved to have the highest potential in terms of 
ensuring improved air quality and thermal comfort to the occupants compared to mixing ventilation 
(MV) and displacement (DV). The major differences between the textile duct designs were the 
permeability of the textile and the additional hole-pattern on the textile. Textile duct ventilation was 
compared to MV and DV. Each of the three different kinds of ventilation systems was studied under 
well-defined conditions. The experimental setup and the conditions used were the same. This was 
crucial, because it allowed for a comparison of the performance of the different ventilation systems. 
The criteria for evaluation were air quality, assessed on the basis of the distribution of the different 
simulated contaminant sources (table, feet and groins of occupant and exhaled air), air velocities in 
front and above the occupants and thermal comfort of the occupants. The results are discussed in detail 
in the corresponding chapters. The most importance findings are discussed in the following.  
 
Smoke visualization 
The airflow pattern, and the corresponding airflow interactions, established by the different textile duct 
designs and ventilation systems were studied through smoke visualizations. The smoke visualizations 
gave a good idea of how the particular textile duct design would work in practice. The major interest of 
the smoke visualizations was to see how the different designs interacted with the free convection flow 
around of occupants and the thermal plume above their heads. If the free convection was removed the 
chance of having a good inhaled air quality was more likely. However, this could be difficult to assess 
if it was removed or not, in some cases, due to fluctuation of the air pattern. It was found that 
recirculation of polluted air was happening, at the back of the occupants and in front of the tables, for 
the textile ducts, which may not have been found as easily through the tracer gas measurements. 
However, it was very difficult to quantitatively assess if the recirculation of polluted air was decreased 
or increased between the different textile duct designs. Generally it was found that the position of the 
occupants and the airflow rate used was of importance for the airflow interactions. 
 
Tracer gas measurements 
The smoke visualizations showed those of the textile duct designs, which could affect/remove the free 
convection flow around the occupant. Thereby it was possible to provide cleaner air to the breathing 
zone of the occupants. MV and DV represent some of the most common ventilation systems in 
practice. In the present study, MV and DV were used for comparison with textile duct ventilation. The 
use of textile duct ventilation provided improvement of the air quality in the breathing zone of the 
occupants compared to MV and DV. The amount of clean air provided to the breathing zone was 
influence by the textile duct design. The initial textile duct design proved to be the best in terms of air 
quality. The reason for the improved air quality can be explained by the fact that the air jets from the 
textile duct removed the free convection flow, which prevented transport of pollutants to the breathing 
==PAGE:116==
 
 
 
116
zone by the free convection flow. 
It was found through a parametric study that the performance of the initial textile duct design was quite 
stabile to changes in the system, meaning that the performance of the system didn’t change much from 
the reference conditions. Changes such as position of the occupants relative to the duct, supply air 
temperature, amount of people beneath the textile duct and etc. were studied (section 11.7). The 
explanation may be that as long as the supply air jets removed the free convection flow around the 
occupant the less importance had the different parameters on the performance. 
 
The textile duct ventilation system may provide a stable performance beneath the textile duct. 
However, the system generally doesn’t allow a flexible room design. The reason is because the textile 
duct is most effective when the occupants are sitting near to/under the textile duct. It is more unlikely 
to be effective when sitting far from the textile duct. The applied mounting system of the textile duct 
didn’t allow for adjustment of the position of the textile duct in case of change in the table layout. 
Therefore the tables had to be close to the textile ducts. The initial textile duct design may only service 
one row of tables at a time making it necessary to have a textile duct at each row of tables which may 
be unpractical in some cases and very expensive solution. It is therefore suggested to look into the 
possibilities of a flexible mounting system for the textile duct allowing adjusting of the textile duct 
position. Another solution may be that a damper is installed at each textile duct. Thereby it is possible 
to open the dampers for the textile ducts if needed for the particular table layout. 
 
It was observed during the smoke visualizations that recirculation of polluted air occurred for almost all 
of the textile duct designs. An effort was put to decrease the recirculation. However, it didn’t work as 
intended. In order to achieve an even higher performance of the ventilation system it would be 
recommended to further study this effect and how to decrease/prevent it. 
The cross contamination between the occupants beneath the textile duct was studied. It was found that 
the initial textile duct design provided protection against pollutions from contaminant sources beneath 
the textile duct. However, the design provided on average the protection against contaminant sources 
located outside the zone beneath the textile duct compared to MV and DV. 
 
The discussion presented so far is concerned mostly with the distribution of active (heated) 
contaminants generated from localized sources, when not considering the plane table source. However, 
there exist many more contaminant sources in the indoor environment. In the present study, the 
contaminant from a plane source located on the table was examined as well. This represented pollution 
(VOCs, SVOC etc.) from the table surface. However, in further studies it would be recommended also 
to simulate the pollution from a plane passive source located on the floor such as a carpet made from 
PVC or linoleum, since these had been linked to a prevalence of sick building syndrome symptoms. It 
would be expected that the initial textile duct design, HDC 150 – 155°, would perform near the same 
level as for the table source since they are both passive pollution sources not located on the heated body 
==PAGE:117==
 
 
 
117
of the occupant. 
 
Air speed measurements 
The air measurements showed that the initial textile duct design, HDC 150 – 155°, created higher air 
velocities, above and in front of the occupants, compared to MV and DV. This was expected since the 
intention of the textile duct ventilation system was to remove the free convection around the occupants. 
The free convection flow cannot be peeled off from the occupants without having somewhat elevated 
air velocities close to the occupants. This interaction had to be considered carefully in terms thermal 
comfort, since elevated air velocities may have an unwanted cooling effect on the occupant especially 
when felling thermally neutral or cold. However, it could be beneficial for occupants felling warm or 
hot and can lead to an improved thermal sensation. 
 
Thermal comfort 
The thermal comfort provided by the ventilation system was of importance. Especially in the case with 
the textile duct ventilation system that had air jets directed towards the occupants. The room air 
temperature was kept at 26 °C at 1.1m at the centre of the climate chamber for all cases. The studies 
showed that the difference between all of the three different ventilation systems (initial textile duct 
design, MV and DV) were minor. However, the initial textile duct design, HDC 150 – 155°, provided 
in generally more cooling of the upper body and higher thermal sensation for the lower body compared 
to MV and DV. This could be beneficial in terms of thermal comfort since humans normally prefer a 
cold head and warm feet. For some of the body parts the difference was quite little which made it 
unlikely that the occupants would notice a change at these parts of the body. 
 
Classroom arrangement 
The furnishing and layout of a classroom may have a great effect on the resulting airflow pattern and 
thus the distribution of contaminants. In order to narrow down the amount of possible arrangements 
different classrooms were visited. It was found that in generally two types of layouts were used. Tables 
in rows and two/three table put together as a group. It was decided to simulate tables in rows in order to 
have a simple arrangement, which fitted the geometry of the textile duct designs applied. Furthermore, 
the intention of the study was to investigate the possibility to improve inhaled air quality of rooms with 
occupants seated for a relatively long periods of time. This was assessed to be most likely in the case 
when having tables in rows since it would be most applicable for lectures and alike. Evaluation of the 
impact of classroom arrangement could be problematic to assess and generalize. In the present study 
three occupants were placed next to each other facing the same way. The contaminants were generated 
close to the occupant seated to the left in most of the cases. It was expected that due to the closeness of 
the contaminant sources the pollutions were more likely to reach the breathing zone of the occupants 
compared to a pollution sources placed far away. This set-up provided the most unfavourable 
conditions. It was expected that the air jets from the textile duct would be able to provide improved air 
quality for the occupants due to the air jets, which affected the free convection flow around the 
==PAGE:118==
 
 
 
118
occupants. A cross contamination experiment showed that the exposure of the two occupants sitting to 
the right and left of the polluting occupant had almost the same exposure. This suggested that the 
distance from the pollution source, located beneath the textile duct, might not have a large impact on 
the transmission of pollution to the occupants placed beneath the textile duct. The exposure would be 
the same within vicinity of the polluting source.

13 Conclusion 
Textile duct ventilation was studied with regard to the airflow interaction between the supplied 
ventilation air occupant’s free convection flow and thermal plume generated by occupants, air quality, 
air speed and thermal comfort. 
 
The airflow interaction between the supplied ventilation air, the free convection flow around the human 
body and the thermal plume generated by occupants was studied through smoke visualizations. It was 
found that:  
• The airflow rate and position of the occupants relative to the textile duct had importance for the 
airflow interaction. 
• It was possible to alter the airflow pattern by changing the design of the textile duct.  
• It was possible to peel off the free convection flow around the occupant by having holes in the 
textile duct angled toward the occupant. This was a great advantage compared to mixing and 
displacement ventilation (section 11.1 and 11.2). This made it possible to provide clean air to 
the breathing zone of the occupants. However, having supply air jets may cause recirculation of 
polluted air into the breathing zone of the occupants due to entrainment by the supply jets. This 
was a disadvantage of the airflow pattern. 
• A vortex was present in front of the occupants causing recirculation of the polluted air. This 
was also a disadvantage of the airflow pattern. 
• The textile ducts can be made into any design, which give the possibility to customize the 
textile duct to many different situations. This gave a great advantage in terms of changing the 
airflow interactions. 
• The airflow interaction between the supplied ventilation air, the free convection flow around the 
human body and the thermal plume generated by occupants was studied and improvements 
were suggested. Ten new textile duct designs were made and tested. 
 
Textile duct ventilation, with the initial textile duct design HDC150 – 155°, was generally capable of 
providing a good air quality into the breathing zone of the occupants (section 11.7). It was found that: 
• The airflow rate had an influence on the performance of the ventilation system. However, after 
reaching an airflow rate of 60 l/s the performance of the ventilation system remained the same. 
• The position of the occupants relative to the textile duct had limited importance for the 
performance of the ventilation system within the range of 0 - 40 cm from the textile duct. 
However, the air quality performance was affected for the feet pollution at x=0.0 m. Thereby 
the system was able to allow for some flexibility in terms of placement of the workstation 
relative to the textile duct. 
• The position of the exhaust was of relatively limited importance due to mixing of the room air 
outside the breathing zone of the occupants. However, a floor based exhaust diffuser reduced 
==PAGE:120==
 
 
 
120
the pollution from the groins at the breathing zone of the occupants. A ceiling based exhaust 
system would be recommended in terms of system flexibility. 
• The supply air temperature (ΔT=3.9-9.3 K) had a limited influence on system performance in 
terms of personalized exposure effectiveness. 
• The distance between the breathing zone of the occupant and the textile duct was influencing 
the performance of the ventilation system. The shorter the distance the better the air quality 
provided to the breathing zone of the occupants. 
• The textile ventilation system provided good protection against cross contamination between 
the sitting occupants below the textile duct. 
• The number of occupants below the textile duct was not affecting the performance of the 
ventilation system, except the protection against table pollution, which was reduced. 
 
Ten new improved textile duct designs were tested. None of these designs proved to be better than the 
initial textile duct design in terms of air quality. 
 
Textile duct ventilation was compared to mixing ventilation and displacement ventilation (section 
11.7.10). 
• Textile duct ventilation provided the same level of performance in terms of contaminant 
removal effectiveness. However, textile duct ventilation provided increased performance in 
terms of personalized exposure effectiveness. The air supplied to the breathing zone was cleaner 
with textile duct ventilation compared to mixing ventilation and displacement ventilation.  
• The initial textile duct design, HDC 150 – 155°, was found to perform similarly to mixing and 
displacement when considering cross contamination between a standing person and sitting 
persons. 
• Air speed measurements were conducted. It was found that the initial textile duct design, HDC 
150 – 155°, created higher air velocities, above and in front of the occupants, compared to 
mixing and displacement ventilation systems. However, the air velocities remained within air 
speed limits defined in DE/EN 1752 class A for a classroom. 
• The presence of occupants below the textile duct had a profound effect on the air velocities. 
This was due to the airflow interaction between the thermal plume and the supply air jet.  
• The thermal comfort provided by textile duct ventilation, mixing ventilation and displacement 
ventilation was compared. It was found that textile duct ventilation generally provided cooling 
of the upper body and heating of the lower body compared to the other two ventilation systems.  
• The improved textile duct designs were all found to provide increased cooling of the whole 
body compared to the initial textile duct design.

15.1 Ventilation flows 
The static pressure inside the free jet is the same as the static pressure of the enclosing air space. The 
free jet can be divided into four zones each with its own characteristics in term of turbulent intensity, 
centreline velocity and velocity profile. The four zones can be observed illustrated in Figure A. 1 
(Awbi H., 2003). 
 
 
Figure A. 1: The four zones of a free jet. X, length. U0, velocity at supply. Um, centreline velocity. 
The potential core is the zone immediately downstream of the supply opening. The free jet is not 
completely mixed with surrounding air at that point. The length of the potential core is a function of the 
geometry of the supply opening, the turbulent intensity and the Reynolds number (<3x104) of the air 
supplied. However, the length of the potential core will usually be 5-10 times the diameter of the 
opening. Inside the potential core the centreline velocity is constant and equal to the velocity at the 
supply opening (Awbi H., 2003; Hansen et al. 2013).

15.1.1.2 Free plane jet 
The free plane jet is one of the basic flow configurations. A free plane jet is a jet produced by a long 
rectangular opening. A plane jet can be assumed when the jet is issuing from a slot opening with an 
aspect ratio greater than 40 (length of the wide compared to the length of the height). The variations are 
negligible in the direction of the slot length. The plane jet can be divided into regions that reminiscent 
that of the three dimensional jet. The plane jet consists of a core region, a characteristic decay region 
and an axisymmetric region. The length of the potential core and the characteristic decay region is a 
function of the aspect ratio of the supply opening (Awbi H., 2003). The transition distance from the 
characteristic decay region to the axisymmetric region is depending on the aspect region. The higher 
the ratio the longer is the distance. The potential core region of the plane jet can be seen in Figure A. 4 
(Jensen H. T., 2005). 
 
 
Figure A. 4: Potential core of the plane jet. 
The plane jet will like the circular jet has a turbulent mixing zone between the potential core and the 
surrounding air. The zone will increase in thickness along the axial distance until the potential core is 
gone. Afterwards the jet is turbulent and will entrain the surrounding air.

15.1.1.3 Temperatures effect on free jets 
The temperature of the supply air, relative to the room air temperature, is a factor that affects the flow 
of the free jet. The density of air is a function of temperature. The colder the air is the heavier it is. 
Therefore is a cold free jet supplied horizontally more likely to dropdown before an isothermal free jet 
due to buoyancy. When supplying the air downward the cold supply air will reach higher downward 
velocities than an isothermal one due to buoyancy. Due to this effect the undercooled supply air (going 
downwards) is able to reach further into the room compared to an isothermal.

15.5 Calibration of Artificial Lung 
The exhalation and inhalation flow rates were set prior to the measurement including exhaled air form 
the manikin. The airflows, from the manikin, had to resemble the pulmonary ventilation of an average 
person at a light activity level i.e. 0.24 l/s and breathing frequency 10 min-1. The airflow through the 
lungs should equal 6 l/min (Hyldegaard et al. 1998). During one cycle (6s) the inhalation/exhalation 
was 0.6 l, but the inhalation/exhalation lasts only 2.5 s each. Because of that the airflow should be 
adjusted to: 0.6 l / 2.5 s = 0.24 l/s. The artificial lung did not have a flow meter mounted in the air 
distribution system to allow for direct measurements of the pulmonary ventilation. Therefore the lungs 
were calibrated before the measurement. The calibration was carried out by connecting a gas flow 
meter (Figure A. 14) to the tube used for inhalation and exhalation respectively. The lungs operated on 
continuous mode. The airflow rate was measured by the gas meter. The calibration was done for 10 s 
which should result in 2.4 l. Further adjustments were done by regulating the two air pumps: one for 
inhalation and one for exhalation.  
 
 
Figure A. 14: Gas meter. 
Next objective in the calibration of the lungs was to set the proper gas content in the exhaled air. This 
was done in order to keep the density of the air as that of the air exhaled by a real person 1.144 kg/m3. 
Thereby it was possible to take into account the buoyancy of the exhaled jet. The tracer gas 
concentration was regulated with a rotameter mounted on the tube, which supplied the tracer gas into 
the exhaled air (Figure A. 15). 
==PAGE:140==
 
 
 
140
 
Figure A. 15: Float type rotameter. 
The next step, the lungs were set to continuous exhalation and a sample of 20 ml, exhaled air, was 
taken with a glass syringe from the mouth of the manikin (Figure A. 16). The sample was released into 
a closed jar with a volume of 3.15 l (Figure A. 16). The jar was flushed with “clean” air (without tracer 
gas) before releasing the sample into the jar. The tracer gas was diluted and the concentration inside the 
jar was measured by gas monitor Innova 1303 connected to the jar. 
 
Figure A. 16: Tracer Gas calibration set.

For calibration of the thermal manikin the thermal manikin was placed in a climate chamber. The

Expanded uncertainty 
The expanded uncertainty U can be obtained by multiplying the standard uncertainty Ust or the 
combined standard uncertainty Ust,c by a coverage factor k. Based on the value k chosen the confidence 
interval cover a interval with a certain level of confidence. If the probability distribution was given by y 
and Ust and was normal distributed and the effective degree of freedom of Ust is of significant size 
(≥10), the coverage k =1 and k = 2 will produce an interval ! ± !!!" corresponding to level of 
confidence of 68 % and 95 % respectively. For this project k = 1 had been used and thereby 68 % 
confidence intervals had been used.
