Abstract
The demand for a sustainable protein source is increasing with the growing population. Sea-
weed is considered a sustainable protein source and may contribute to sustainable development
and potentially contributing to the Sustainable development goals (SDG). Thus, this report
aims to evaluate quality, processing and digestibility of protein in seaweed of Nordic interest as
a future source of protein for animal feed and human consumption. This is tested by screen-
ing the following relevant seaweed species Alaria esculenta, Chondrus crispus, Fucus serratus,
Fucus vesicolusus, Furcellaria lumbricalis, Gracilaria spp., Palmaria palmata, Saccharina latis-
sima and Ulva spp.. The most promising seaweed species were found to be P. palmata and F.
lumbricalis based on total amino acids results (8.3 ± 0.9% and 9.1 ± 1.1% respectively). The
aﬀect of fermentation on P. palmata showed overall decrease of free amino acids (FAA) but
increase of speciﬁc FAAs (Phenylalanine; 5-6 mg/ 100 g , isoleucine; 0.75-1.1 mg/ 100 g and
methionine; 0.1 mg/ 100 g). Blanching of P. palmata decreased more than the fermented sam-
ples. Diﬀerences between TAA method and dumas method was observed including variations
between seaweed species. Total amino acid (TAA) score and essential amino acid (EAA) score
did not quite reach the same levels as the soybean products. However, F. lumbricalis and P.
palmata had a TAA score of 108% and 99% respectively compared to egg and EAA/TAA ratios
was similar to the reference proteins (soybean products and egg). Thus, seaweed may be more
suitable for human consumption than animal feed but it depends on the species. However, sea-
sonal variation was not included but plays a crucial role for the composition of macro-nutrients
in seaweed and harvest adapted to seasonal variation is expected to increase protein quality.
Bio-accessibility results showed unreliable results as bio-accessibility exceeded 100% due to un-
known error. In addition P. palmata was dried samples, thus rehydration was needed before
processing could take place. Fresh seaweed should be used for further investigation. In addition
seaweed may be able to substitute some of the protein in animal feed and add health properties
to it. Overall more results are needed and further investigation are needed to conﬁrm ﬁndings
in these study and to complete unsuccessful experiments.

Background
The global population is expected to reach 9.8 billion by 2050 and the growing population
increases the demand for energy and food and with it protein (Alkemade et al., 2021). The
protein quality is higher for animal protein than for plant based protein, but so is the envi-
ronmental impact (Afseth et al., 2019). Including both indirect and direct green house gasses
(GHG) agriculture is estimated to account for approximately 33% of global emissions, of which
65% is from livestock, and may increase with up to 30% by 2050 if no improvements are made
(Alkemade et al., 2021). GHG sources from agriculture includes fertilizers, pesticides, land
conversion, soil erosion and biomass burning (Alkemade et al., 2021). Agriculture has not only
led to increase of GHG emissions but also loss of biodiversity and deforestation due to land
conversion. In addition the global pasture accounts for over twice the cropland and animals
consumes 33% of the cropland (Afseth et al., 2019; Alkemade et al., 2021). The depletion of
resources leads to high demand of sustainable protein.
The main problem is not producing enough food as if the resources were distributed equally
the global protein demand could be met. The main challenge is the development and need for
a sustainable production. A possible solution is a change in diet from animal based to plant
based allowing mixing of diﬀerent plant protein if needed. However, a part of the challenge
is cultural. Furthermore, a low conversion rate for plant protein into animal protein remain
a challenge along with the fact that livestocks account for the main contribution to climate
change (Afseth et al., 2019).
Seaweed is a potential source of sustainable protein as it is considered an eco-friendly source
of protein as it can be grown with no use of fresh water, fertilizers, pesticides, no land use as
would be needed for terrestrial cultivation (Alboofetileh, Hamzeh, and Abdollahi, 2021). Sea-
weed has the potential to contribute to a sustainable development in several sectors including
animal feed, improvement aquatic ecosystems, uptake of nutrients (reuse from wastewater for
cultivation of sea lettuce) and removal of excess nutrients from marine environments), habitat
and coastal defense from waves and potential carbon sequestration (Ara´ujo et al., 2021; Den-
mark, 2010). Removal of 40 kg N and 5kg P for each metric ton of seaweed produced (DW).
Thus, seaweed cultivation cleans the water and re-use valuable nutrients (Denmark, 2010). Sea-
weed can be an important player when it comes to achieve the Sustainable Development Goals
(SDG), see ﬁgure 1 (Ara´ujo et al., 2021). The SDGs is an agenda adopted by world leaders
in 2015, and implemented in 2016, consisting of 17 sustainable development goals aiming for a
more sustainable world by 2030. The yearly SDGs report’s purpose is to guide countries based
on collected and available data towards more sustainability (Sachs et al., n.d.). Denmark also

2.1
Seaweed
Macro algae, also known as seaweed, is an aquatic photosynthetic multicellular organism often
wrongly referred to as a plant but deviate as it has exposed zygotes and no roots, leaves nor
stem (Bak, 2018; Hills et al., 2011).Seaweed requires nutrients and light, and absorbs nutri-
ents directly via the cell membrane (Bak, 2018; Hills et al., 2011). Thus, great variations are
observed as seaweed composition depends on the surrounding habitat (Cherry et al., 2019).
Growth of seaweeds are correlated with available light, water motion and nutrient, with nitro-
gen as the limiting nutrient in marine environments, thus protein content depends on external
factors (Bak et al., 2019a). Some seaweeds can store excess nitrogen for later utilisation when
nutrition is scarce. Former studies indicates that the total nitrogen (TN) did not change sig-
niﬁcantly due to depth but indicated seasonal variations did aﬀect TN. This is most likely due
to variations in available nitrogen in the water(Bak et al., 2019a). Fatty acid and protein com-
position changes due to seasonal variation with highest lipid content during winter. Seaweed
has low lipid content but high lipid digestibility, up to 98% for adults. Polyunsaturated fatty
acids (PUFA) accounted for 6.7%-69.1% of total total lipid content including the essential n-3
and n-6 fatty acids (Cherry et al., 2019). The greatest protein content in most seaweed species
occur during winter (Reboleira et al., 2021). However, A. esculenta and S. latissima have their
protein peak in spring and summer and P. palmata peaks is in October to January (Grandorf
Bak, 2019b; Teagasc, 2012). Growth is aﬀected by temperature and seasonal variations and
great variations of protein content in seaweed has been observed in several seaweed species
(Khairy and El-Shafay, 2013). Furthermore, the bio-accessibility also aﬀects the quality of the
protein and has been estimated by in vitro to be 14.7% for F. Vesiculosus, 45% for C. Crispus,
56% for P. Palmata and around for 70% terrestial plants (Cherry et al., 2019).
The composition of seaweed is highly dependant on the surrounding environment. Thus, local
and seasonal variations has been very prominent. The theory behind the season variation has
not been fully disclosed. However, the seasonal protein cycle is theorised to be that seaweed
is in competition with microalgae during spring for light and nutrients (Chiswell, Calil, and
Boyd, 2015; Kreus et al., 2014). During spring and summer seaweed will build sugars due to
photosynthesis and in the fall the microalgae will die and release nutrients (Kreus et al., 2014;
Norsuzila Ya’acob et al., 1989a) . Remineralisation occur during fall and winter as nutrients
are being released due to winds and storms (Kreus et al., 2014). Thus, during winter/spring
all the building blocks are available for growth and the seaweed can begin to embody nutrients
into protein. The relative amount of protein in the seaweed depends on the amount of sugar

Diversity in biomass composition, chemical composition, bioactivity and amino acid (AA) pro-
ﬁle may be explained by a variety of factors such as seasonal variations, depth, location, tem-
perature, habitat, environmental surroundings (including pollution) and salinity (Bak et al.,
2019a; Khairy and El-Shafay, 2013; Marinho et al., 2015a; Marinho et al., 2015b).
Seaweed feature bioactive peptides and can after hydrolysis extraction be produced artiﬁcially.
However, structural protein extraction remain a challenge due to the complex structure of the
cell wall. The cell wall includes polysaccharides and polyphenols which reduces extractability
of protein. Thus additional adapted steps are required for protein extraction (Pliego-Cort´es
et al., 2020). In dry weight protein can reach up to 47% in red algae, 9-26% green algae and
3-15% brown algae, and reach a maximum of 4.5% (red algae), 2.98% (green seaweed) and 1.9%
(brown algae) protein of the Reference Nutrient Intake for a 5g meal dry weight(Alboofetileh,
Hamzeh, and Abdollahi, 2021; Cherry et al., 2019). Thus, seaweeds are of economic interest
especially due to high dry weight value and distinct umami taste (for some species) and high
average kg price of 800 DKK but can reach up to 1800 DKK/ kg (Ara´ujo et al., 2021; FAO,
2021; Mette Møller Nielsen and Schmedes, 2019). Macroalgae is understood as a sustainable
protein source, but may also supply a sustainable source of extracted PUFAs (Cherry et al.,
2019).
Protein polysaccharides in the seaweed can prevent break down of protein into smaller digestible
compounds. However, enzymatic treatment can increase bio-accessibility (1.7-3-fold for P. pal-
mata, two diﬀerent treatments) (Cherry et al., 2019).

seaweed ﬁbres needs more investigation before approval for potential marketing as nutritional
ingredient with associated health properties (Deluyker, 2017).

Soybeans are commonly used for oil extraction and the leftovers are commonly used as animal
feed. It can either be soybean cake or soybean meal which only diﬀer in the oil extraction
method used. Soybean cake has a higher protein content than soybean meal but not more than
1.5% (Rayaprolu et al., 2015). Digestibility of soybean cake is observed to be 87.9±3.2% for

and Peres, 2008; Schwenke, 1988). In addition some compounds in soybeans are observed to
prevent vitamin uptakes (P. B. Brown, Kaushik, and Peres, 2008). However, trypsin inhibitor
is absent in the stem and leaves and is a suitable source of dietary ﬁbres and feed (Koivisto,
2006). Raw soybean are unﬁt for human consumption and as feed for poultry and pigs. How-

2.3
Protein
Protein is a macro nutrient and is essential for all living organisms. Protein has various and
diverse functions e.g. enzymes, hormones, growth and storage and almost all cellular functions
rely on protein (Hills et al., 2011; Welle, 1999).
In simple terms protein metabolism con-
sists break down of protein into AA (catabolism) and formation of protein by AA (anabolism).
Protein metabolism includes AA synthesis, transcription, translation and post translation mod-
iﬁcation. Denaturing of protein takes place in the stomach (HCl) and are transported to the
liver via the bloodstream. In the liver the AA are either synthesised into new proteins, most
often into plasma, or distributed to other organs as free AA (FAA). Excess protein can not
be stored, but can be degraded and used for energy or converted into fatty acids and glucose.
Protein contains nitrogen, and thus ammonia (NH3) are produced during deamination, which
can accumulate to toxic levels, and further processed by liver and kidneys into urea. Thus,
excess protein intake may lead to renal damage (kidney failure), due to too much nitrogenous
waste (Welle, 1999).
20 amino acids are commonly found in nature and form peptides and protein, where peptides
consists of shorter amino acid chains than protein(Bowring et al., 2015). Thus, amino acids are
essential for normal growth and function of new tissue. Amino acids consists of an amino group,

Amino Acids (AAs)
Amino Acids (AAs) can be categorised into diﬀerent groups based on their function in the
organism; essential amino acids , non-essential amino acids (NEAA), branched amino acids
(BCAA), bound and free amino acids (FAA). AAs are a relevant measure of protein as AAs are
absorbed faster than protein and an AA proﬁle tells something about the quality of the protein.
Free amino acids refers to single amino acids which are not bound in a chain. Free amino acids
are ready for digestion and the body does not need to break down the peptide bonds before
utilization. Free amino acids means faster utilization and better bio-accessibility. Other impor-
tant compounds containing nitrogen is creatine, peptide hormones and some neutransmitters
(Mart´ınez Sanz et al., 2018).

2.5
Processing
Processing of food is very common nowadays and food processing may aﬀect diet quality, food
and human health. The recent main focus has been to enhance processes, productivity, safety
and stability and increase health. Nonalcoholic fermentation, freezing and drying are consid-
ered beneﬁcial by the food industry. In addition food contributing directly to health is in high
demand. Thus, demand is not only to satisfy hunger anymore. Hence, functional food research
and food industry is a key branch in global economy (Norsuzila Ya’acob et al., 1989b).
The freeze drying (FD) process is the sublimation of ice to water vapor, thus no liquids are
involved. FD is often chosen in the industry as preservation method of food as it extend shelf
life.
Additional advantages of FD is reduced weight and volume which aids transport and
storage. However, FD food does not reach the same quality after rehydrating compared to
the original defrosted food. Seaweed are not normally FD commercially due to large expenses.
Additional problems can be loss of texture and aromas (Clark, 2009). Seaweed has to be dried
before industrial processing as seaweed contains 75-85% of water and drying reduces storage
volume, prevents microbial growth and preserve desirable qualities (Rosario and Mateo, 2019).
2.5.1
Blanching
It has been reported that hot water pre-treatment of seaweed (Kappaphycus alvarezii) at 88◦C
for 5 seconds can increase production and income. Thus, blanching of seaweed before drying
tends to be proﬁtable due to decrease of drying time (Rosario and Mateo, 2019). However, if
the seaweed are blanched for too long the reduction of moisture will be lesser and the tissue

blanched samples. Ash content and crude protein did not diﬀer signiﬁcantly after blanching
but crude ﬁbre content was aﬀected by blanching. Blanching improved retention and levels of

Color change and reduction of iodine in S. latissima after blanching has been reported in C. W.
Nielsen et al. (2020). Protein can not tolerate long heat exposure or increased temperature as
the heat breaks the protein bonds and destroys AAs. Furthermore, protein loses ﬂexibility and
mass due to moisture loss when exposed to increased temperatures (Rosario and Mateo, 2019).
Thus, freeze drying are chosen as drying method for this project to maintain the protein as
much as possible.
Sensory and nutritional attributes of seaweed, texture, color and odor had no signiﬁcant change
after correct blanching. However, unwanted change will happen during storage if the seaweed
is not pre-treated before drying. No signiﬁcant diﬀerences between blanching temperatures
between 80-100◦C (Rosario and Mateo, 2019). However, blanching may reduce microbial counts
in some vegetables and can turn brown seaweed into bright green but may decrease ﬁrmness.
The change in color of S. latissima may be more attractive for consumers and texture may be
more chewable (Akomea-Frempong et al., 2021)
2.5.2
Fermentation
Fermentation is originally deﬁned as the process that happened in fruit driven by yeast. Nowa-
days, fermentation includes several microbial processes spanning over diﬀerent industries and
ﬁelds of study. In the food industry it has been used to preserve food and to enhance bio-
accessibility (Reboleira et al., 2021). The original deﬁnition of fermentation was ”life without
oxygen” by Louis Pasteur. Today more than one deﬁnition applies and diﬀers among indus-
tries but an overall deﬁnition is the degradation of organic compounds aiming to obtain a new
product (B¨ock, 2009; Reboleira et al., 2021). Fermentation process consists of break down
of sugars by enzymes from microogranisms under anoxic conditions. Lactic acid fermentation
includes break down of starches and sugars into lactic acid (which protects the food from mi-
crobial spoilage) and other waste products(Fan et al., 2017). Thus, several methods can be
used to achieve a fermented product (B¨ock, 2009; Steinkraus, 2009). In this process two diﬀer-
ent fermentation methods will be investigated one including a microbial cultivation, which is a
common industrial method, and a natural fermentation where only saltwater are added to the
anoxic environment.

Fermentation enables alteration of products which are nearly impossible to achieve by other
means. Thus, fermentation is highly used in the industry as it is an important tool to achieve
added-value compounds and studies show it can enhance antioxidants, antimicrobial, anti-
inﬂammatory and anti-diabetic activities. Seaweed may be suitable for fermentation as it has
water content of > 80% making it suitable for wet biomass processing.
Seaweed has been
tested as an alternative source for fermentation by the biofuel industry and been used to pro-
duce methane and ethanol. However, fermentation of seaweed for pharmceuticals and food
have not been investigated very much and pre-treatment prior to fermentation of seaweed may
occur (Reboleira et al., 2021).
Furthermore, fermentation is a desirable processing method as it avoid the disadvantages of the
chemical industry, it is very energy eﬃcient and are expected to increase its relevance in the
feed and food industries and may be a source of natural bioactive compounds as it can enhance
the bioactive potential of complex edible substrates. Fermentation of plant material is mostly
used to optimize single compound processes based on former research but new methods are
being investigated and new factors are being looked into (Reboleira et al., 2021). Fermentation
are already a common mean to achieve desired products like dairy product, fermented foods
and is an important player when it comes to food preservation (B¨ock, 2009).
Poor bio-accessibility of protein has been recorded for P. palmata but fermentation may increase
bio-accessibility. Thus, fermentation may be able to increase bio-accessibility of red seaweeds
(Monteiro et al., 2021). However, seaweed fermentation remains an undeveloped research area
thus making it interesting and relevant to investigate but is a suitable medium for fermentation
due the hight water content.
Pre-treatment may be necessary and fermentation of brown
seaweeds showed no signiﬁcant change of proteins but improvement of mineral composition
(Monteiro et al., 2021). In addition knowledge of the composition due the seasonal variation is
important in order to maximise yield of the wanted component . Hence, high amounts of e.g.
sodium can interfere with the process(Schiener et al., 2015).

5
Discussion
5.1
Protein diﬀerences between methods
Signiﬁcant diﬀerences in protein (AA) and protein (N) were observed between the seaweed
species but not all species. However, several species also showed insigniﬁcant diﬀerences in
protein (AA) and protein (N). This indicates that there is variation among some species but
other species may be suitable to substitute one another. In addition both methods can be used
for some species but for others it will change the result signiﬁcantly. Furthermore, protein (AA)
is considered a more accurate protein estimation than that of protein (N), and it is shown on
ﬁgure 7 that all species except A. esculenta has a lower N to protein factor than the mandatory
of 6.25 and the standard of 5 for seaweed. Thus, food declaration of crude protein of food con-
taining seaweed are likely to be incorrect according to this study. Angell et al. (2016) suggested
47
==PAGE:62==
Master Thesis
Naja Ritzau Ishøj Nielsen
The National Food Institute
Technical University of Denmark
an universal N to protein factor of 5 based on a mean N to protein factor of seaweed of 4.76.
In addition an overestimation of 43% was observed in the study by Angell et al. (2016) when
using the dumas method. Thus, this study supports the ﬁndings of 6.26 N to protein factor
being an overestimation of seaweed protein content. However, this study also indicates a N to
protein factor of 5 appears to be too high for several species. An explanation may be that some
seaweed species can store nitrogen in their leaves for later utilisation and seasonal variation.
Furthermore, signiﬁcant diﬀerences are observed among species indicating a species speciﬁc N
to protein factor is needed. In addition protein (AA) is believed to be the most accurate since
N in DNA and inorganic N is not included in this method.
The results also indicates a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in protein content between F. serratus from
late summer and spring. This may be due to variation of N storage due to seasons. Sea-
sonal variation was not tested thoroughly in this study but former studies concluded seasonal
variation of protein in seaweed e.g. Marinho et al. (2015a) and Khairy and El-Shafay (2013).
Thus, even though protein (AA) of P. palmata from the screening experiment did not reach
47% as stated in Khairy and El-Shafay (2013) it may be due to seasonal variation. Seasonal
variation and location of harvest may have to be included in the speciﬁc N to protein factor to
achieve accurate protein estimations based on N content. In general overestimation of protein
(N) occur for most species according to results in this project assuming protein (AA) is the
most correct measure of protein. Locational variation in protein content was not tested in
this study. However, diﬀerences between cultured and wild Gracilaria spp. was tested and no
signiﬁcant diﬀerence was observed. Thus, knowledge of wild seaweed protein content can be
used to predict whether a location is suitable for cultivation or not. Known protein content of
wild seaweed stock can thus help determine if a location is suitable for seaweed cultivation of
the same species as the wild stock. However, this is just an indication and further studies are
needed to conﬁrm the former statement.
Results of A. esculenta are not reliable as the correct mass of the sample was not obtained
throughout the experiment. The standard method is to use the same mass as that of water
when running analysis of liquid samples. Results showed great variations between triplicates
and 3x0.5 mL of liquid A. esculenta samples was weigh. The weighting showed great variations
between the weights thus the results are not reliable as the correct mass was not possible to
obtain. Analysis of A. esculenta should be run again to get applicable results.
The most promising seaweed species was based only on protein (AA) excluding A. esculenta
due to missing biomass information. Seasonal variations and location was not taking into ac-

Industry
The average kg price of dried seaweed is around 800 DKK but can reach up to 1800 DKK
for P. palmata (Mette Møller Nielsen and Schmedes, 2019). Soybean meal has a kg price of

Conclusion
Variation between protein content between species was observed, however some species showed
insigniﬁcant diﬀerences among species for both protein (N) and protein (AA). It is notable
that the relative variation between species was not the same within each method. Furcellaria
lumbricalis had the highest protein (N) and protein (AA) with the respective values of 16.5
± 1.9 and 9.1 ± 1.1 with no signiﬁcant diﬀerence to Gracilaria spp. species for the dumas
method. Furcellaria lumbricalis and P. palmata had the highest protein (AA) content of 9.1 ±
1.1 and 8.3 ± 0.9 respectively. Thus, Furcellaria lumbricalis and P. palmata was selected for
further experiments but F. lumbricalis was deselected after unsuccessful fermentation. Only
P. palmata was analysed after processing and digestion experiment. N to protein factor should
be further investigating as protein (AA) is believed to be a more correct measure of protein.
Hence, N from DNA and inorganic (e.g. nitrate) is not measured in the AA method. The
hypothesis of expected variation in protein content between species can be partly conﬁrmed.
No increase of FAA was observed for either fermentation or blanching. However, an increase of
TAA was observed for both processing methods with the highest increase in the blanched sam-
ple. This is probably due to higher loss of minerals and ash during the blanching process than
fermentation. Thus, there is no indications of increased FAA after either processing methods.
However, fermented samples showed increased levels of isoleucine (0.75-1.1 mg/ 100 g), methio-
nine (0.1 mg/ 100 g) and phenylalanine but only phenylalanine (5-6 mg/ 100 g) increased for
the blanched samples (2.6 mg/100 g). Thus, conﬁrming fermentation hydrolyzed the samples
more than the blanched but did not release more FAA compared to the non-processed control.
Thus, the hypothesis of change in proteinous compounds after processing can be conﬁrmed.
However, only in regards to change in FAA composition.
Some seaweed species seems to be more suitable for human consumption whereas others may be
more suitable for animal feed. Protein extraction from seaweed may increase it’s relevance as
animal feed protein. Seaweed may be more suitable as a supplement for human consumption,
as the TAA score and EAA score is closer to egg than that of soy proteins. According to these
results the seaweed did not reach the same levels of EAA and TAA compared to soy protein.
However, similar EAA/TAA ratio was observed to that of soy and egg. In addition relatively
low protein content was observed compared to the values in table 3 and 4. It should be noted
that the soybean protein used as comparison was processed soybean products. Seasonal vari-
ation (harvest time) is crucial when identifying whether or not seaweed is suitable as soybean
protein substitute. Studies including seasonal variation and location should be made before
ﬁnal conclusions can be made when determine protein quality of seaweed. In addition seaweed
