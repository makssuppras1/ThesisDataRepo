Abstract
With the increasing upstream titer in biopharmaceutical production, downstream opera┬¡
tions have not been able to keep up.
Both crystallization and precipitation are alternatives, which need extensive development
time, but are cheaper and more flexible in production. To increase the use of precipitation
as a standard unit operation in downstream processing, also for molecules with higher
molecular weight such as monoclonal antibodies, the experimental design, and upscaling
of processes need to be made easier.
For an industrial course at BTEC about crystallization and precipitation, the precipitation
of the model protein lysozyme and green fluorescent protein was characterized using
Hofmeister salts as precipitants. In addition to the basic screening of required protein and
salt concentrations, agitation as a factor was considered. To remove impurities as a factor
and instead focus on the abilities of EasyViewer and ParticleTrack technology, lysozyme
was used in a pure solution with salts from the Hofmeister series. The exemplary impact
of different salt types, stirring, agitation types, salt concentration, protein concentrations,
and seeding was examined in this model system.
Especially, how the particle size distribution changes over time in a batch process was of
interest, as this showed the value of online process analytical technology usage. The use
of seeding crystals aimed at combining two parts of the lab course, by using the lysozyme
crystals from the stationary crystallization lab to change the kinetic process monitored in
the EasyMax 102 workstation. Using lysozyme and common salts at 20 ┬░C, at pH 4 in
sodium acetate buffer, provides a good model system to exemplify the impact of different
factors on induction time, particle count, and chord length distribution (CLD), as the nec┬¡
essary material is easy to source. While a PVM probe was mostly beneficial for processes
including crystals, the FBRM probe was a great in┬¡situ tool for observing changes in CLD.
In the future, this equipment could be employed in the pharmaceutical industry to gener┬¡
ate the data for machine learning in process development, as well as monitor industrial
processes in real┬¡time.

Introduction
Even 100 years after Nordisk Insulinlaboratorium commercialized the production of in┬¡
sulin in Europe, the investments in biopharmaceutical manufacturing are larger than ever.
Biopharmaceutical manufacturing relates to the production of biologics: pharmaceutical
drugs produced in bacterial, fungal, or mammalian cells such as Escherichia coli, Pichia
pastoris, or Chinese Hamster Ovary (CHO) cells. Currently, the global bioprocessing ca┬¡
pacity is around 17.5 million liters spread over more than 1600 active facilities. The global
biologics market is expected to more than double by 2030, reaching a value of about a
trillion USD (Statista 2022). Depending on the process, purification steps can constitute
up to 80 % of the total manufacturing costs (Hunt et al. 2001, Costioli et al. 2010).
The rising demand for biopharmaceuticals, as well as the increasing titers in upstream
processing (USP), cause bottlenecks in downstream processing (DSP), especially purifi┬¡
cation (Santos, Carvalho, and Roque 2017). After harvesting the fermentation broth, the
protein of interest should be isolated from critical contaminants such as proteases and
glycosidases. In this first purification step, the capture step, the focus is on capacity and
speed instead of resolution.
Currently, chromatography is the most┬¡widespread unit operation for purification and pol┬¡
ishing. That said, this prolific operation has substantial drawbacks in terms of its associ┬¡
ated resin purchase cost, the missing compatibility with single┬¡use technology, and prob┬¡
lematic upscaling due to an increase in pressure drop with an increase in column size
(Martinez et al. 2019, Shukla and Gottschalk 2013). Despite these disadvantages, chro┬¡
matography enables an easy and reliable process design and is therefore still the method
of choice and gold standard in the biopharmaceutical industry (A. S. Rathore, Kumar, and
Kateja 2018).
Meeting the increasing demands for protein┬¡based medicines poses a significant industrial
challenge in terms of cost┬¡effectiveness and reliability during biopharmaceutical manufac┬¡
turing (Yang et al. 2019). Crystallization of proteins as a one┬¡step process offers higher
efficiency compared to conventional purification techniques like chromatography, result┬¡
ing in proteins with higher purity.
To improve productivity and decrease cost, protein crystallization and precipitation can
be used for both the initial and intermediate purification, as well as formulation steps in
biopharmaceutical production processes. Additionally or alternatively, is a simple unit op┬¡
eration, which can be used both for volume and impurity reduction (Lohmann and Strube
2020).
Protein crystals exhibit greater stability and provide advantages in storage, formulation,
and drug delivery.
Placement of the precipitation or crystallization step further down┬¡
stream provides a more pure and controlled feed solution and a more predictable out┬¡
come. Nevertheless, placement earlier in the downstream process provides the most ad┬¡
vantages, because crystallization and precipitation both concentrate and purify the feed
solution (Shukla, Etzel, and Gadam 2006). Precipitation is already used to remove impuri┬¡
ties such as nucleic acids, lipids, and salts from the protein solution to isolate, concentrate
and stabilize the protein of interest (Buddha et al. 2016). However, it is important to note
that protein precipitation can also cause protein denaturation and aggregation, which can
affect the stability and activity of the protein product. Therefore, it is crucial to optimize
Real┬¡Time Characterization of Protein Precipitation and Crystallization
3
==PAGE:14==
the conditions of the precipitation process to minimize protein degradation and ensure the
production of high┬¡quality biologics with full biological activity.
Currently, precipitation is mostly used for proteins with a comparatively low molecular
weight. Examples include the Cohn process, where subsequent precipitation steps are
used to purify albumin in blood plasma (Cohn et al. 1946). Other examples of precipitated
biologics include the hepatitis B vaccine (Hilleman et al. 1983) and the 5.6 kDa hormone
insulin (Siew and W. Zhang 2021).
To increase the use of precipitation as a standard unit operation in downstream process┬¡
ing, also for molecules with higher molecular weight, such as monoclonal antibodies, the
experimental design, and upscaling of processes need to be made easier. Currently, the
downscaling of the process conditions is problematic. As of now, stationary dynamic light
scattering (DLS) is used to scan for successful conditions (Pantuso et al. 2020). However,
this does not accurately depict the effect of stirring and its influence on the precipitation
process. For better process understanding, turbidimeters or focused beam reflectance
measurements (FBRM) that obtain online kinetic data can be used as process analytical
technology (PAT) for small┬¡scale runs.

Literature Review
3.1
Biopharmaceutical Production
Biopharmaceutical production can be differentiated into four different stages. One stage
makes up upstream production, and three stages are associated with downstream pro┬¡
duction: Recovery, Purification, and Formulation and Fill.
Purification of a biopharmaceutical molecule usually includes a capture chromatography
step, and two chromatographic polishing steps, followed by ultrafiltration/diafiltration. In
the case of a mammalian host cell, a viral inactivation and filtration step are included
as well. Often, the capture chromatography step is performed on a Protein A column,
whereas the polishing steps use anionic and cationic columns.
To overcome the limitations associated with chromatography, such as high costs of resins,
diffusion issues, and capacity constraints, there is a growing interest in exploring alter┬¡
native approaches known as Anything but Chromatography methodologies. Currently,
non┬¡chromatographic techniques for DSP are experiencing renewed interest, including
precipitation and crystallization methods (Santos, Carvalho, and Roque 2017).
Figure 3.1: The four stages of biopharmaceutical production, adapted from Gilleskie, Rut┬¡
ter, and McCuen 2021.
3.2
Proteins in Solution
When proteins are dissolved in water, water forms a solvation layer around the hydrophilic
surface residues, forming a rigid matrix of counterions on the proteinÔÇÖs surface. The pres┬¡
ence of these solvation layers reduces the amount of ionic interactions with other pro┬¡
teins and decreases the likelihood of aggregation. These repulsive electrostatic forces
decrease protein┬¡protein attractive forces. As fermentation broths are aqueous and con┬¡
tain ions, purification through precipitation or crystallization requires a reduction of the
hydration layer.
3.3
Precipitation
Protein precipitation is a technique that separates and purifies proteins from a solution
by exploiting differences in solubility between proteins and the other components of the
solution (Burgess 2009, Oelmeier, Ladd┬¡Effio, and Hubbuch 2013).
There are two separation strategies available for purifying biologics. The first strategy
involves precipitating side components, such as deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and host cell
proteins (HCP), while keeping the target protein in solution to avoid subsequent protein
dissolution and degradation issues. However, it can be difficult to precipitate numerous
Real┬¡Time Characterization of Protein Precipitation and Crystallization
5
==PAGE:16==
impurities with unknown and diverse properties using this approach. The second strategy
involves using precipitation to isolate the target protein itself, which is more convenient
because optimal parameters can be selected for one specific target component.
Both strategies involve adding a precipitating agent to the protein┬¡containing solution. The
first strategy causes the impurities to aggregate and become insoluble, whereas the sec┬¡
ond strategy causes the protein of interest to precipitate, while other components remain
in the soluble phase. The resulting protein aggregates can be easily separated from the
solution by centrifugation or filtration. Removing proteases early in the procedure is crucial
to prevent protein degradation, which requires a quick, gentle, scalable, and reasonably
priced unit method such as precipitation. In addition to the fractionation, this method can
significantly enhance the concentration of the enriched protein (Burgess 2009).
Precipitation does not require a complex equipment setup, a continuous stirred tank re┬¡
actor (STR) or a tubular reactor with a filter for solid┬¡liquid separation works well. It can
deal with high process volumes and rising titers and can have shorter process times than
chromatography. Furthermore, once it is on a large scale, the volume of the STR is eas┬¡
ily scalable, and continuous operation is feasible (Hammerschmidt, Tscheliessnig, et al.
2014═¥ Hammerschmidt, Hobiger, and Jungbauer 2016, Li et al. 2019, Burgstaller, Jung┬¡
bauer, and Satzer 2019). Currently, precipitation is mostly used for the production of
low┬¡value products, but due to its advantages, research has been conducted on high┬¡
value products, such as therapeutic proteins in the biopharmaceutical industry (Santos,
Carvalho, and Roque 2017).
3.4
Crystallization
Protein crystallization is a form of precipitation in which the precipitate is formed in an or┬¡
dered manner (Santos, Carvalho, and Roque 2017). Typically, proteins are amorphous as
solids and prone to denaturation, but the crystalline lattices possess greater stability and
can resist denaturation (Santos, Carvalho, and Roque 2017, Peters, Minuth, and Schr├Âder
2005). In polishing steps, crystallization is an effective means to ensure product quality
by influencing particle size, purity, and product yield. In the pharmaceutical industry, the
crystallization of an active pharmaceutical (API) connected to the crystal size distribution
has one of the greatest impacts on the quality and effectiveness of the API, as well as
its mode of delivery (Liang et al. 2013). Changes in crystal size and shape will also influ┬¡
ence the processability, exemplified by filtration and drying performance, and change the
bioavailability and efficacy (Kempkes, Eggers, and Mazzotti 2008). A crystallized product
can be stored for years without significant product degeneration, and therefore the time
delay (for example due to transport) between the manufacture of the protein therapeutic
and fill and finish activities can be tolerated (Peters, Minuth, and Schr├Âder 2005).
For many years, the central focus has been on crystallization for the production of diffraction┬¡
quality single crystals for protein structure determination by crystallography. While the
demands on crystals are quite different for structure determination or purification, much
can be learned from crystallography and applied to bulk protein crystallization (Shukla,
Etzel, and Gadam 2006). However, many of the precipitants used in crystallography are
not practical for process┬¡scale separation processes, as they would cause wastewater
treatment issues (Peters, Minuth, and Schr├Âder 2005).
3.4.1
Crystallization Mechanism
To understand the underlying principles of crystallization, the concept of supersaturation
needs to be understood as the driving force for crystal nucleation and growth. Nucleation
is the creation of a new crystal nucleus, either undirected from solution (primary nucle┬¡
6

==PAGE:17==
ation) or in the presence of existing crystals (secondary nucleation). During nucleation,
an adequate amount of molecules associates in three dimensions to form a thermody┬¡
namically stable aggregate, which provides surfaces suitable for crystal growth. Crystal
growth is the increase in the characteristic length of existing crystals, it immediately fol┬¡
lows nucleation and is governed by the diffusion of particles to the surface of the critical
nuclei and their ordered assembling onto the growing crystal (Krauss et al. 2013).
The relationship between supersaturation, nucleation, and growth can be simplified to
equations for the growth (Equation 3.1) and nucleation (Equation 3.2) rate.

(3.1)
where G stands for the growth rate, kg for the growth constant, g the growth order, and
ÔêåC for supersaturation.
B = kbÔêåCb
(3.2)
where B stands for the nucleation rate, kb for the nucleation constant, b the nucleation
order, and ÔêåC for supersaturation.
A visual relationship of the nucleation rate, growth rate, and crystal size can be seen in
Figure 3.3.
Changing the crystal┬¡size distribution requires changing the relative nucleation and growth
rates. This may be accomplished by adjusting the supersaturation over time. Generally,
nucleation rates increase more rapidly than growth rates with increasing supersaturation
(b > g). The growth order for primary nucleation is typically much higher than for sec┬¡
ondary nucleation or crystal growth. In addition, the degree of supersaturation required
for primary nucleation is typically much higher than for secondary nucleation, which in
turn is much higher than the threshold for the metastable region. Therefore, operation
at low supersaturation favors the formation of large crystals, whereas high degrees of
supersaturation, and operation close to the upper limit of the metastable region, favors
the formation of many nuclei, producing many small crystals (Shukla, Etzel, and Gadam
2006).
A two┬¡dimensional phase diagram, illustrated in Figure 3.2, can be used to conveniently vi┬¡
sualize the entire process of crystal growth. The diagram displays the stable states, which
include liquid, crystalline, and precipitate, as a function of two variables related to crys┬¡
tallization. When the concentration of a protein solution surpasses its solubility limit, the
solution becomes supersaturated. This zone on the diagram can be divided into three re┬¡
gions based on the degree of supersaturation: precipitation for very high supersaturation,
where molecules come together to form amorphous aggregates, labile for intermediate
supersaturation, where both nucleation and growth occur, and metastable for lower su┬¡
persaturation, where only growth takes place. The boundaries between these regions
are not well┬¡defined, as kinetic parameters influence them. Agitation and temperature
influence the width of the metastable zone, for example.
It is difficult to characterize the phase behavior of protein solutions completely due to
the complex kinetics that determine the equilibrium, the multicomponent character of the
solution, the number of solution parameters that can be adjusted, and the variety of dense
phases that can form (Callewaert and Michiels 2010).
The established and recommended approach to protein crystallization is the initiation of
nucleation at the lowest level of supersaturation within the labile region, followed by a

gradual decrease in protein concentration to enter the metastable zone, where growth
proceeds slowly. However, determining these optimal conditions can be challenging, and
it may be necessary to separate nucleation and growth stages in order to obtain high┬¡
quality crystals.
This can be achieved, e.g., through the addition of so┬¡called crystal
seeds. The chemical space of crystallization experiments is multidimensional, with dif┬¡
ferent zones corresponding to nucleation and growth of various crystal forms. Predicting
the specific conditions needed to crystallize a protein from its chemical┬¡physical properties
is currently not feasible. A modification of a single experimental parameter can affect mul┬¡
tiple aspects of a crystallization experiment simultaneously, and therefore the process of
protein crystallization is not linear (Gieg├® 1999, Pusey and Ayg├╝n 2017, Rupp 2011, Liang
et al. 2013).
3.4.2
Seeding
Often, the high levels of saturation needed for nucleation are not reached (Figure 3.3). To
overcome this limitation, a so┬¡called nucleant or seed can be added to the process. This
leads to a higher local concentration of macromolecules, lowers the energy barrier for
nucleation, and bypasses the high kinetic barrier of spontaneous nucleation (Alexander
McPherson 1999).
3.5
Parameters Impacting Precipitation and Crystallization
Many factors can affect protein crystallization and precipitation. In general, they can be
grouped into biochemical, chemical, and physical factors. Physical factors include tem┬¡
perature, agitation, pressure, gravity magnetic fields, electric fields, dielectric properties,
viscosity, vibrations and sound, time, equilibration rate, nucleants, methodology, the sur┬¡
face, and shape of the crystallization device, and sample handling. Chemical factors
involve the precipitant type and concentration, the pH value, the buffer type, the ionic
strength, as well as the sample concentration, presence, and use of metal ions, polyions,

are the sample purity and homogeneity, the sequence, posttranslational and chemical
modifications, aggregation, proteolysis, sample pI and ligands, co┬¡factors, and inhibitors
(Alexander McPherson 1999, Gieg├® 1999, Alexander McPherson 2004). Of all those
mentioned factors, generally, only the pH, salt, buffer, and temperature are changed in
optimization experiments.
3.5.1
Purity
As the conformational and chemical purity of the sample affects the ability to grow crys┬¡
tals, it is important to use similar feedstock quality for similar results. Impurities can also
serve as additional nucleation points and thus change the kinetics of the process. Sodium
dodecyl sulfate┬¡polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS Page) and mass spectrometry
can be used to assess purity, amongst others.
3.5.2
Temperature
The variation of the acid/base reaction constant of the protein side chains as a function of
temperature explains the dependence of protein solubility on temperature. Thus, some
proteins, exhibit a decreased solubility with decreasing temperature (Rosenberger et al.
1993, Chernov and Komatsu 1995). Crystallization can be achieved both by cooling and
evaporation, however, as proteins are heat┬¡sensitive, evaporation is not commonly used
in industrial scale┬¡purification. As the product of interest is usually in an aqueous solution,
and water has a high specific heat capacity, evaporation is not economically feasible,
and extraction of the protein precipitate through filtration for example can save energy
in the process and help reduce cost. However, vapor diffusion is used as a screening
tool with hanging drop crystallization (Alexander McPherson and Gavira 2014), with the
drawback that the data generated from sitting drop and microbatch experiments only gives
a qualitative insight into crystal growth kinetics and yield. Only the experiments in stirred
tanks scale allow quantitative analyses (Hebel et al. 2013).
The pKa values of ionizable groups in proteins are affected by the ionic strength of the
medium, with an increase in temperature leading to an increase in pKa values at low ionic
strength and a decrease at high ionic strength, though solubility variation is minimal at
high ionic strength. The temperature┬¡solubility relationship is not a property of the protein
alone but depends on the protein┬¡solution system. Temperature also affects nucleation
and growth rates and the equilibrium position of the trial during crystallization. While most
crystallization experiments are performed at 4 ┬░C and 20 ┬░C, new devices offer better
temperature control to optimize crystal growth and form (Krauss et al. 2013).
3.5.3
pH
Due to the presence of multiple ionizable groups with different pKa values, even a small
change of 0.5 pH units can significantly impact the solubility of proteins, and in some
cases, even a slight pH variation of 0.1 units can cause noticeable changes. This is
because pH affects the precise nature of protein┬¡protein interactions, altering the ability
to form critical salt bridges and hydrogen bonds that are necessary for the formation of
specific crystal contacts (Dumetz et al. 2008). The protonation state of amino acid side
chains, which determines electrostatic interactions, plays a vital role in binding specificity,
protein hydration, and interactions with small molecules and ions that may facilitate crystal┬¡
packing contacts. At the isoelectric point (pI) of each protein, the positive, and negative
charges balance each other, making it seem like the ideal condition for crystal growth
since there would be no electrostatic repulsion between protein molecules. However, an
analysis of nearly ten thousand unique protein crystal forms showed that this was not the

3.5.4
Precipitating agents
The choice of precipitating agent depends on the specific protein being purified and the
desired properties of the protein product. Common precipitating agents used in protein
precipitation include (NH4)2SO4, ethanol, and polyethylene glycol (PEG). These agents
work by reducing the solubility of the protein in the solution, leading to the formation of
protein aggregates.
They strengthen the attractions among biomacromolecules through various mechanisms,
such as altering the activity coefficient of water (in the case of salts) (Hofmeister 1888),
changing the dielectric constant of the solvating medium (in the case of organic solvents),
or increasing molecular crowding (by using high molecular weight polymers such as PEG)
(A. McPherson 1976). Precipitants that work via different mechanisms are not typically in┬¡
terchangeable, and crystals that form with one type of precipitant are unlikely to form if the
precipitant is switched to a functionally different one without substantial process develop┬¡
ment. However, it has been extensively demonstrated that combinations of precipitating
agents with distinct mechanisms can be synergistic and enhance the likelihood of crystal
growth (Majeed et al. 2003).
Metal ions can play a vital role in maintaining certain structural features of the protein
and are sometimes necessary for the proteinÔÇÖs biological activity. In some cases, metal
ions stabilize intermolecular contacts in the crystal. Studies have shown that the use of
biocompatible, water┬¡soluble ionic liquids, organic salts, and salts with melting points at
or below room temperature as crystallization additives can yield promising results. Small
organic molecules are another useful additive class that enhances long┬¡range electro┬¡
static interactions by reducing the dielectric constant, impacts the solvent structure, and
may modify the hydrophobic effect. Crystallization of certain proteins often requires the
presence of natural ligands in the crystallizing solution, which ensures the conformational

Salts
The solubility of macromolecules in concentrated salt solutions is a complex phenomenon,
but it can be simplified by considering it as a competition between salt ions, particularly
anions, and macromolecules for water molecules, which are crucial for maintaining solubil┬¡
ity (Hofmeister. 1890). When the salt concentration is high enough, the macromolecules
lose an adequate amount of solvent, leading them to associate with each other to fulfill
their electrostatic requirements. This environment can result in the formation of ordered
crystals or disordered amorphous precipitates. Additionally, certain salt ions, primarily
cations, are necessary to ensure the solubility of macromolecules. At extremely low ionic
strengths, there is insufficient availability of cations to maintain macromolecule solubility,
and crystals may also form under these conditions (Alexander McPherson and Gavira
2014).

3.5.5
Polyethylene glycol
Polymers are frequently used in protein precipitation, the most commonly used being
PEG, usually 20 % (w/v) of PEG with molecular weights > 4000 Da due to the higher
efficacy compared to smaller PEG molecules. In solution, PEG not only attracts water
molecules from the protein surface but also has an effect in sterically excluding proteins
from its hydrodynamic volume, enhancing protein┬¡protein interactions leading to protein
precipitation (A. McPherson 1976).
PEG has shown promising results for the isolation of large proteins like antibodies. While
the mechanism for PEG precipitation is not well enough understood yet, the phenomenon
is well described and models were built accordingly (Oelmeier, Ladd┬¡Effio, and Hubbuch
2013, Hammerschmidt, Hobiger, and Jungbauer 2016).
3.5.6
Ethanol
The presence of organic solvents, such as ethanol, decreases the dielectric constant of
the medium. As the concentration of organic solvent increases, the attraction between
macromolecules becomes stronger, rendering the solvent less effective (reducing the kD)
and promoting the formation of a solid state. When using organic solvents, it is recom┬¡
mended to work at a low temperature, preferably at or below 0 ┬░C, and to add them slowly
while ensuring thorough mixing (Alexander McPherson 1999). In general, it is advisable
to keep the ionic strength low and employ any available means to prevent denaturation.

3.5.7
Agitation
Crystal growth and morphology can be influenced by stirring the crystallization solution.
This technique helps to control crystal growth by minimizing the concentration gradient
across the crystal surface and preventing excess spontaneous nucleation. Stirring can
also accelerate the growth of protein crystals, which results in larger and higher┬¡quality
crystals. Adachi, Takano, Niino, et al. 2005 proposed that stirring increases the interac┬¡
tions between protein molecules, leading to the formation of large clusters that evolve
into stable nuclei. However, gentle stirring is recommended to avoid issues such as spon┬¡
taneous nucleation, protein denaturation, and damaged crystals (Adachi, Takano, Mat┬¡
sumura, et al. 2004). It is important to note that both the stirring technique and speed
affect crystal growth and morphology. Different agitation can be achieved through orbital
shakers, stir bars, and overhead stirrers.
Yaoi et al. 2004 observed increased lag times for crystal nucleation in stirred samples
compared to unstirred samples. While the time for crystal formation was longer, the quality
of the observed crystals was higher, as they had a complete shape and a size of up to 1
mm.

The selection of experimental agitation methods with targeted types and intensities of
stresses can facilitate a better understanding of protein degradation mechanisms and
predictability for industrial applications. When comparing stresses induced by different
types of agitation, the vortex mixer causes the most intense stresses, while a stir bar
system presents locally intense shear close to the hydrophobic surface of the stir bar.
Orbital shakers provide intermediate┬¡level stresses, with a stable platform for consistent
homogeneity (Bai et al. 2012).

3.6.2
Ion Exchange Chromatography
Cation and anion exchange chromatography are often used as polishing steps in biomolecule
purification.
IEX utilizes the differences in the surface charge of proteins to achieve a highly precise
separation with a large capacity for sample loading. This separation process relies on
the reversible interaction between a charged protein and a chromatographic medium of
opposite charge. When loaded onto a column, proteins bind to it. Then, the conditions
are modified to selectively elute the bound substances. Elution is commonly achieved
by increasing the concentration of salt or adjusting the pH, either gradually or through
stepwise changes. In most cases, a gradient elution with salt (NaCl) is employed. During

concentrated form. The net surface charge of proteins varies depending on the pH of their
surroundings. Typically, if the pH is above the proteinÔÇÖs isoelectric point, it will bind to an
anion exchanger, while if the pH is below the pI, it will bind to a cation exchanger. However,
itÔÇÖs important to note that binding is influenced by the charge, and surface charges alone
may be sufficient for binding even on the opposite side of the pI. IEX is typically used to
bind the desired molecule, but it can also bind impurities if necessary. By repeating IEX
at different pH values, multiple proteins with distinct charge properties can be separated.

Process models
Up to this point, there is no process model available in literature that fully encompasses

Biologics
Biologics are a class of medicinal products made from living organisms or their compo┬¡

illnesses, such as cancer, autoimmune disorders, and infectious diseases.
Unlike traditional small molecule drugs, biologics are large and complex, often consisting

Proteins that are sensitive to variations in pH, temperature, precipitants, and agitation
pose a challenge for bulk crystallization. In addition, the process of bulk protein crystal┬¡

amino acids, and organic drugs. While small molecules can crystallize within minutes to

Problem Statement
Purification is currently a bottleneck in the production of biologics. Well┬¡designed protein
precipitation and crystallization can replace one to all chromatography steps, either only
for initial purification or for polishing steps as well.
While precipitation and crystallization are well┬¡established unit operations in synthetic
chemistry, the process development structure cannot be fully transferred to the crystalliza┬¡
tion of biomolecules such as proteins. This is because of the complex biological structure,
and heterogeneity of proteins, as well as the biological variation of the raw material.
As agitation and shear have a considerable impact on the hydrodynamic layer around
proteins, and thus impact the kinetics of precipitation and crystallization, they need to be
considered when establishing a new process for protein precipitation. Subsequently, the
experimental design and upscaling of processes need to be improved.
Currently, the scalability of the process is problematic, as it can be difficult to scale up
the process to industrial┬¡scale production, e.g., the effect of shear is not included in initial
dynamic light scattering (DLS) experiments. This can be alleviated with the use of pro┬¡
cess analytical technology (PAT), for example, turbidimeters, or focused beam reflectance
measurements that obtain online kinetic data, as well as lab┬¡scale stirred precipitation pro┬¡
cesses in defined environments.

Materials and Methods
6.1
Considerations for Design of Experiments
Initially, SAS JMP software was used to design experiments with a fractional factorial,
using precipitant concentration, salt concentration, buffer type, pH value as variables. This
choice of variables did not result in feasible conditions to test. Certain buffers only work in
certain pH zones, and there is a minimum protein concentration as well as an interest in
using as little salt as possible. There was an interest to keep sodium acetate as a buffer, to
keep the buffer identical to existing DLS and crystallization lab sessions of the Precipitation
and Crystallization short course at BTEC. Sodium acetate has buffering capacities in the
pH range, where lysozyme keeps its activity, and has been used extensively in literature
as well. According to Howard et al. 1988 the solubility of hen egg┬¡white lysozyme at
pH 4 at 20 ┬░C is at 4 mg/mL with 6 % NaCl (corresponding to 1 M), 2 mg/mL at 7.5
% NaCl (corresponding to 1.3 M) and 1 mg/mL at 10 % NaCl (corresponding to 1.7 M).
Thus, it was initially aimed to stay above these protein concentrations. Industrially relevant
concentrations were decided to be up to 10 g/L lysozyme. Sodium acetate works in the
range of pH 3.6 to 5.6, so pH 4 was chosen as a condition. Literature says that the pH
does not have a relevant effect on the precipitation of lysozyme, or an unpredictable one
(Forsythe, Russell A. Judge, and Pusey 1999), thus, it was not considered as a factor
in this thesis. To keep sample handling easy, and reduce power consumption, 20 ┬░C
was chosen as the fixed temperature. The ideal stirring speed was determined as part of
the characterization experiments. For screening, experiments were performed once, and
interesting conditions were repeated up to three times.

7.2.6
Impact of Stirring
As stirring requires a high energy input on a larger scale, and too high agitation will cause
shear and dissolution of protein aggregates, finding the optimal stirring speed and type is
important.
When increasing the stirring speed of the stir bar every two hours, and also in the following
experiments, where the stirring speed was kept for four to eight hours, it can be seen that
the stirring speed has an impact on precipitation kinetics. When the stirring was below 50
rpm, no precipitation was observed during the observed timeframe, when the lysozyme
concentration was at 10 g/L. With a lysozyme concentration of 5 g/L, the stirring needed
to be increased to 200 rpm to observe protein precipitation.
Figure 7.10: Change of mean particle size of a solution with 10 g/L lysozyme in 1.5 M
NaCl and 0.1 M NaOAc at pH = 4 and variable stir bar speed (purple line = stir bar speed,
green line = mean chord length size, blue line = particle count).
In Figure 7.10, a part of the stirring speed determination is shown. While the agitation
was between 100 and 300 rpm, the mean particle size decreased, and the particle count
increased. With a protein concentration of 10 g/L, above 400 rpm, the particles were
broken up, and above 500 rpm, the formation of bubbles distorted the measurements of
the mean particle size.
In experiments with fixed stirring speed, the lag phase for a process with 10 g/L lysozyme,
1.5 M NaCl and 0.1 M NaOAc differed between 10 min and almost 16 h, with only the
stirring speed differing between 50 (for 16 h) and 200 (for 10 min) rpm.
Impact of Agitation
When comparing the impact of different agitation modes by running an identical process
both in the EasyMax 102 with a stir bar, and in a shake flask, the stir bar caused 90.3 %
of the lysozyme to precipitate within 24 h, compared to 94.4 %. While pellets could be
observed with all stirred conditions, no pellet formed in the static condition.

Discussion
The precipitation and crystallization of proteins are dependent on many factors, and real┬¡
time characterization of these phenomena can aid process understanding and design.
The ParticleTrack G400 is good at providing online data about the CLD and giving a con┬¡
tinuous overview. The EasyMax 102 workstation enables continuous monitoring of the
temperature of the reactor as well as the jacket temperature, and the pH. However, the
pH drift of the SevenExcellence probe makes it difficult to monitor and set the pH value
accordingly. For precipitation processes, the ability of the EasyViewer to take pictures is
less relevant, as the particles are too small to be more than small white dots. Additionally,
the CLD and mean particle size data obtained with the ParticleTrack G400 is more mean┬¡
ingful, and relevant for following filtration steps, than the turbidity data obtained with the
EasyViewer.
The reflective nature of the NIST microspheres interferes with the measurements of the
ParticleTrack and EasyViewer probe (Figure 7.2), and thus it is not a suitable option to
test the accuracy of their measurements. Walla et al. 2023 mentions that the aggregation
and/or precipitation in protein crystallization processes is a major challenge, as these pro┬¡
cesses may interfere with the focused laser beam and affect the duration of the backscat┬¡
tered light as well as the pulse intensity. These problems have not been observed with
biological systems, but only with non┬¡aggregating NIST particles.
Depending on the purpose of the crystallization or precipitation process, it can be benefi┬¡
cial to have a longer or shorter induction phase. Using PAT to monitor this process can
enable decision┬¡making in regard to the addition of additional protein or salt to speed up
the process.
Vortexing a solution in a reaction tube, followed by centrifugation at 14000xg for 10 min can
serve as a confirmation that a condition will lead to precipitation in a stirred system (shown
by changed absorption at 280 nm or cloud point formation). However, an unsuccessful
screening does not necessarily show that a condition will not lead to precipitation in a
stirred system. Therefore, the screening setup needs to be improved, to be able to find
the condition that is the most resource┬¡efficient, reducing costs both for the production
company and the environment. While the exact reasons for this are unclear, it shows that
the vortex created by the vortexer has a different impact on the protein┬¡salt solution than
the stir bars. According to Bai et al. 2012, vortexing exhibits a stronger force on the fluid
than stirring. This means, that the vortexing timeframe in the screening is either too harsh
or too short to increase protein┬¡protein interactions.
The shear increased with velocity and accelerated the nucleation rate of lysozyme and
thus shortened the induction time, which is consistent with the crystallization kinetics of
other proteins (Yang et al. 2019, Liu and Rasmuson 2013).
Xiaopeng et al. 2006 observed a shorter timeframe for their purification of eGFP using
(NH4)2SO4 than shown in Table 7.2 and Figure A.4. Furthermore, they were able to re┬¡
move impurities at a low saturation of (NH4)2SO4, which does not match with the results
of this thesis, where the contaminants of the Q┬¡Eluate got precipitated as well. This can
be either due to the use of Tris┬¡HCl and the pH of 8, or the difference in protein solution
composition.

While the addition of NaCl to lysozyme in NaOAc buffer at 20 ┬░C did not yield any precipi┬¡
tation or crystallization without stirring or decrease of process temperature (Section 7.2.6),
Yaoi et al. 2004 reports that stirring, even only at 100 rpm resulted in an increased time for
crystal nucleation to occur, and thus an increased lag phase.They observed that stirring
at 25 rpm or 50 rpm reduced the induction time, indicating that 100 rpm causes shear and
breakage of particles. Thus, stirring can stimulate nucleation of lysozyme proteins, and is
a relevant parameter to study and determine in a precipitation and crystallization process.
Additionally, through stirring, fewer, but larger, lysozyme crystals were created compared
to unstirred experiments. Opposite to this, other authors observed that higher shaking
rates or higher concentrations resulted in smaller crystals, which indicated the number of
crystals would be higher, and the edge of crystals became irregular. The largest crystal
obtained by Yaoi et al. 2004 was about 1 mm in length, and obtained at a stirring speed of
100 rpm and NaCl concentrations from 6 % up to 12.5 %. Their findings, that the flow of
the protein solution caused by stirring causes the production of large, high┬¡quality crystals,
cannot be confirmed by the data obtained for this project, as no big crystals were obtained
through stirring of the protein and salt solution. This may be explained by different protein
concentrations, or different modes of agitation, at different temperatures. Furthermore,
Hebel et al. 2013 found no correlation between stirrer speed and crystal growth kinetics,
at least in their tested range of 100 to 300 rpm. As different stirrers have different power
inputs and cause different fluid dynamics, the comparison of stir rates alone is likely not
helpful.
The obtained data does show that the nucleation rate is increased with an increase in
lysozyme solution or stirring rate, as observed by Yang et al. 2019. However, a higher
initial concentration does not cause a different (smaller) size of lysozyme crystals, the
PSD of 2 g/L lysozyme and 5 g/L are almost overlapping.
There are different lysozyme crystal shapes, and Hebel et al. 2013 observed mostly com┬¡
pact irregular tetragonal┬¡like crystals, which is in line with microscopy data from the crys┬¡
tallization experiments (compare to Figure 7.14 and Figure 7.15). In the SEM, crystal
clusters in the approximate size of 70 ┬Ám to 120 ┬Ám were observed, which is the size
range that Hebel observed as well (Figure 7.14). While Hebel et al. 2013 did not observe
any needlelike crystals, those occurred occasionally in the 15 mL centrifugation tubes
used for the creation of seeding crystals.
Since in situ microscopy probes are commonly available today from a diversity of compa┬¡
nies, the image┬¡based monitoring tool is proving to be a promising concept for industrial
protein crystallization in combination with deep learning image analysis for challenging
protein crystallization process conditions such as those involving aggregates, precipitated
proteins, heterogeneous particles, and low┬¡resolution imaging devices (Bischoff, Walla,
and Weuster┬¡Botz 2022). Combining different monitoring modalities shows great poten┬¡
tial. Other authors have combined image analysis with Raman spectroscopy, to obtain
information both from the solid and the liquid phase. This joint information helped to es┬¡
timate the nucleation kinetics as well as crystal growth kinetics. Based on the results
obtained in an aqueous taurine solution saturated at 30 ┬░C and 40 ┬░C, their approach
proved to be more sensitive and provided more reproducible nucleation detection com┬¡
pared to FBRM (Bischoff, Walla, and Weuster┬¡Botz 2022). Indeed, the need for offline
analysis for concentration measurements is a drawback in the current BTEC lab setup,
and the purchase or use of the probes in combination with others, e.g., the Raman probes
used in bioreactors, could aid further process understanding. In addition to improved hu┬¡

probes in protein crystallization monitoring, enabling real┬¡time analysis of protein crystal

Conclusions and Outlook
To overcome the limitations associated with chromatography, such as high costs of resins,
diffusion issues, and capacity constraints, there is a growing interest in exploring alterna┬¡
tive approaches to meet the rising titers and rising demands in biopharmaceutical produc┬¡
tion. There are many parameters that need to be optimized to ensure that crystallization
and or precipitation are feasible replacements, requiring many process development and
optimization steps. The use of PAT and machine learning can speed up these processes
and enable their successful establishment in FDA┬¡controlled environments, such as the
biopharmaceutical industry. As there is a plethora of literature available on the precipi┬¡
tation and crystallization of lysozyme as a model protein, there is a solid basic process
understanding. The use of PAT, specifically an FBRM and PVM probe in a controlled,
stirred environment, is ideal to characterize the kinetics of the precipitation of lysozyme
and GFP. Different to SEM or light microscopy, the PVM probe can show the size and
shape of protein crystals in solution, thus making process monitoring easier. By produc┬¡
ing rich data sets for future use, future experiments can be predicted and planned using
machine learning, on the basis of the experiments conducted for this thesis.
Lysozyme with salts from the Hofmeister series is a good teaching example for the in┬¡
fluence of different parameters and possible effects, as all required materials are com┬¡
paratively cheap and easy to obtain. By adding a laboratory module on the precipitation
of lysozyme in the EasyMax 102 and tracking the development of CLD in a precipitation
process with the ParticleTrack G400 and the development of turbidity and crystal seeds
with the EasyViewer 100, the short course ÔÇ£Protein Precipitation and CrystallizationÔÇØ got
improved.
The crystallization of lysozyme in a stirred system requires more process development.
While the GFP purification from Q┬¡Eluate was unsuccessful using only Hofmeister salts,
it was possible to precipitate all proteins and thereby increase the concentration of the
solution.
The EasyMax system, while not sterile, is great for scaling up, as the geometry of the 100
mL vessel is similar to larger reactors, and there is a wide variety of stirrers to choose
from, to mimic large┬¡scale conditions.
In the future, crystallization and precipitation will play a larger role in biopharmaceutical
production, and thus it is important to teach the industrial workforce the underlying princi┬¡
ples and show how PAT, in interplay with machine learning and robot┬¡assisted screening
for process conditions, will enable the widespread implementation, finally decreasing de┬¡
pendence on expensive resins while maintaining work in an authority┬¡approved design
space.

OVERVIEW/ 
SUMMARY: 
We utilized dynamic light scattering (DLS) to screen for conditions conducive to 
precipitation and/or crystallization. However, static plate-based methods do not account 
for the effect of stirring, the influence of shear, and the shortened length scale on a typical 
stirred tank batch precipitation process. We will deploy an inline process video monitoring 
probe, a turbidimeter, and a focused beam reflectance measurements (FBRM) probe to 
gather real-time kinetic data for improved process characterization and discuss 
applications in process development, modeling, and control. 
 
 
OBJECTIVE(S) 
FOR THIS 
ACTIVITY: 
ÔùÅ Explain the value-add and capabilities of inline monitoring for our batch 
precipitation process.  
ÔùÅ Explain the effect of shear in a stirred tank. 
ÔùÅ Identify conditions for monitoring and examine the real-time kinetics of protein 
precipitation.  
ÔùÅ Discuss potential use cases of Process Analytical Technology (PAT) in precipitation 
and crystallization processes. 
 
 
BACKGROUND 
INFORMATION: 
Agitation increases the frequency of protein-protein interactions through improved mixing 
and shortening of the length scale. Mechanically induced protein conformational changes 
are also possible in high-shear fluid flow. While full denaturation requires more extreme 
flow velocities, minor protein stretching can alter molecular surface charges and thus the 
propensity to aggregate. Stirred tank batch conditions are not easily replicated in a static 
environment. Inline process monitoring takes place continuously within the process itself, 
offering a window into the real process state while removing sources of sample 
manipulation involved in periodic at-line sampling. Turbidity data is acquired inline by the 
EasyViewer 100 probe. The ParticleTrack G400 probe utilizes focused beam reflectance 
measurement (FBRM) technology. FBRM is an in-situ method used to monitor the 
evolution of particle populations and is able to provide a highly precise chord length 
distribution. As an optical, probe-based instrument, it is inserted directly into the vessel to 
track changing particle size and count. The range for this real-time particle size analyzer is 
between 1 ┬Ám to 1000 ┬Ám, and it can be used to characterize conditions that affect 
aggregation, nucleation, polymorphism, and other system attributes.
