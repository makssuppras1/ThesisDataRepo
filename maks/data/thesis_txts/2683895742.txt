Abstract
It is estimated that the construction industry is accountable for 40 % of global CO2 emis-
sions, 11 % of which results from the manufacturing of new building materials. Furthermore,
construction materials and the building sector is responsible for 1/3 of global resource consump-
tion. Parallelly, up towards 40 % produced waste originates from construction and demolitions.
Recovery of building materials have become a priority area and ultimately increased focus on
proper management of construction and demolition waste.
This study uses an intelligent prediction model to estimate reuse and recycling potential of
buildings based on typologies. The prediction model uses expected construction and demolition
waste streams collected by the municipality of Copenhagen and building specific information
to approximate material composition of a building. Information on building material stock is
used to assess a total and an annual urban mining potential.
Numerous restrictions have been identified in current practices and management of construc-
tion and demolition waste. Available data is of lacking quality since systems does not provide
any follow-up. As a result, quality intelligent learnings from current data are limited. An ap-
proximation of Copenhagen’s recoverable material stock is estimated to be around 37 million
tons. Furthermore, an annual material outflow is approximated. Denmark’s residential build-
ing stock is estimated to contain 146 million tons recoverable materials. Results are considered
no more than plausible due to low prediction accuracy.
Overall, it is concluded that current conditions of Denmark allow low quality data driven
recovery which ultimately could support recycling and reuse processes. New regulations are
likely to increase performance of a similar model in the future.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Project Motivation
It is estimated that the construction industry is accountable for 40 % of global CO2 emis-
sions, 11 % of which results from the manufacturing of new building materials [IEA, 2019].
Furthermore, construction materials and the building sector is responsible for 1/3 of global
resource consumption [EMF, 2019]. On the contrary, 40 % of produced waste in Denmark
came from construction and demolitions [MST, 2020]. Recovery of building materials have
become a priority area and ultimately increased focus on proper management of construction
and demolition waste (CDW).
The Waste Framework Directive 2008/98/EC presented demands on 70 % CDW recovery by
2020 [European Commision, 2008]. This initiative was one of the first steps towards EU’s
circular economy action plan. While the initiative was an important step in the right direc-
tion, it did not promote the right processes. Waste management practices of CDW recovery is
mainly based on back filling operations and low-grade recovery. Denmark reached 90 % recov-
ery before 2020. However, it is estimated that only about 36 % of Danish CDW is subject of
higher-grade recycling [MST, 2020].
Initiatives such as Nordic Swan Ecolabel and voluntary sustainability classes are likely to in-
crease demand on carbon neutral buildings in the future. Eventually, CO2 emissions could
even become a legislative requirement to consider for builders. A necessity of increased demand
would be innovation of CDW management with focus on reuse and recycling.
Prices, legislation, planning, and lack of knowledge have been identified as barriers for recy-
cling and reuse of building materials. Current CDW management practices do not support
knowledge of when, where, and how much material is available. Denmark is one of few Euro-
pean countries with requirements for sorting CDW in fractions. However, management and
lack of traceability significantly reduce possibilities of eﬀicient reuse and recycling of building
materials.
1.1.1
Traceability systems
Building information modeling (BIM) and materials passports can support pre-demolition au-
dits for identifying recoverable building materials. These are identified as the future within
traceability systems. Detailed knowledge of future buildings is certain, but knowledge of ex-
isting buildings is in short supply. Even though experience have thought us a lot, history
has a way of repeating itself. History have seen unknowingly use of hazardous materials that
was later identified as being of health risk. Furthermore, raw materials are consumed and

1.3
Thesis Objectives
The experimental part of this thesis covers an investigation of a method for determining a
buildings material stock based on CDW streams. The objective of the thesis is to investigate
the potentials of data driven recovery. To find out how available CDW stream data can be
used to estimate an urban mining potential. Analysis will identify possibilities to determinate
this by applying knowledge of buildings typologies based on previous studies and demolition
cases. Knowledge of materiel stock should theoretically be useable in evaluating an urban
mining potential of a building, city or even a nation. Prior to the beginning of this thesis
two hypotheses were conducted to serve as the objectives of the work. The hypotheses are
presented below:
– H1 The material type, quantities and conditions of Danish building materials can be
approximated to a precision that allows an estimation of a buildings reuse and recycling
potential.
– H2 The approximation of H1 can solely be based on existing Danish building data
through statistical models.

CHAPTER 2
Theory and Literature Study
The following chapter provides an overview of relevant theory and literature for data driven
recovery. Sections will cover fundamentals of data mining and machine learning. Furthermore,
concepts of circular economy and urban mining processes are presented. The chapter provides
an outline of methodology used for determining material composition prior to demolition and

2.1
Circular economy
Economic growth has given millions of people access to goods and materials like never before.
A result of rising prosperity is an increase in natural resources consumption [Miljø- og føde-
vareministeriet, 2018]. The fundamentals of a circular economy are making growth sustainable
and keeping natural resources inside the system. In other words, raw materials are not taken
out of their cycles, but their value is preserved by optimizing reuse and recycling.
The more sustainable way of leading an economy have gained political interest. In 2008 the
European commission defined a waste hierarchy in their Waste Framework Directive (WFD)
[European Commision, 2008]. The directive defines the general concepts and definitions of
waste management. The waste hierarchy presents the priority order of waste management for
EU countries. Waste prevention is the highest priority and disposal is the lowest.
Figure 2.1. The waste hierarchy according to and as presented in the Waste Framework Directive
The WFD sets clear targets for the reduction of waste and requirements for recycling. This
includes quantitative recovery targets to be archived by 2020. 70 % of CDW should be reused,
recycled, or used for other recovery by 2020. Denmark’s total share of waste for recycling and
recovery increased from 66 % to 71 % from 2013-2019. Approximately 90 % of construction

2.2
Urban mining
In comparison to primary mining, urban mining focuses on the materials that are already a
part of a system. Urban mining originally focused on electrical and equipment waste (WEEE).
However, increased demand for materials and concerns of the effects of waste have accelerated
interest in urban mining from numerous perspectives including economic and environmental
[Koutamanis, 2018]. Urban mining concepts are interesting, especially for the construction
industry, as they globally are a major user of materials and a primary producer of waste.
Arora et al. developed a methodological framework for estimating city-wide urban mining,
recovery and reuse potential of building materials and components [Arora, 2020]. A cities
total urban mining potential is defined as the entire material stock of the given city. Ignoring
practical complexities, every material could theoretically be taken out of a city. From this total
urban mining potential, a portion is released each year. This portion is defined as the annual
urban mining potential. Arora et al. additionally presents the terms of recovery and reuse
potential. Urban mining processes often requires efforts that can either damage or destroy
a component or material.
Recovery potential account for the losses at this stage.
Of all
recovered materials, not everything can be directly reused due to damage and losses in post-
recovery processing. The reuse potential account for damage and losses at this stage. The idea
of the following model was conceptualized by Arora et al. [Arora, 2020].

Material flow and stock analysis is an established methodology. Material stock analysis gener-
ally involves the quantification of material stocks of urban mines. According to Zhu there are
two approaches to material stock analysis [Zhu, 2014]. They are respectively “top-down” and
“bottom-up”. The bottom-up approach quantifies material stocks by measuring them directly.
Zhu defines the bottom-up approach as following: first it identifies major uses of a given type
of material, second it determines the typical amount of the materials in each unit. Then it
measures the size of each use, next calculates the material stock for each type of use and lastly
computes the total material stock [Zhu, 2014]. This approach supports material stock analysis
of urban areas.
2.3
Recovering and reusing building products
The EU waste framework directive of 2008 describes the interpretation of recovery operations.
Respectively reuse, preparing for reuse, recycling, and other recovery.
Preparing for reuse
is defined as being: “checking, cleaning or repairing recovery operations, by which products
or components of products that have become waste are prepared so that they can be re-used
without any other pre-processing” [European Union, 2020]. The main difference between reuse
and preparing for reuse is whether the material or component have become waste per definition.
Recycling is defined as: “any recovery operation by which waste materials are reprocessed
into products, materials or substances whether for the original or other purposes. It includes
the reprocessing of organic material but does not include energy recovery and the reprocessing
into materials that are to be used as fuels or for backfilling operations” [European Union, 2020].
Lastly, other recovery is defined as any recovery operation which fail to comply with the spe-
cific requirements for preparation for re-use or for recycling [European Union, 2020]. In other
words, “other recovery” means materials are used for the last time and reuse and recycling
means that materials once again will become part of the value chain.
Despite a rather high recovery rate of almost 90 % of CDW in Denmark there is still a signifi-
cant potential in recycling and reuse of materials. Practices have shown that the high recovery
rates are a product of other recovery processes of crushed concrete and asphalt. Reuse of CDW
is limited to few fractions e.g., bricks and tiles.

Numerous studies have been conducted where main barriers and limitations of reusing and
recycling building materials have been identified. SBi conducted a case study in 2015 that
identified six major challenges [Mortensen, 2015].
Some of the major ones was legislation,
economy, and planning. In relation to reuse and recycling legislation have been a main barrier.
Previously CE-marking restricted reuse of materials e.g., old bricks [European Commision,
2021]. However, in 2018 Gamle Mursten, assisted by Miljøstyrelsen, obtained the CE-marking
for their old bricks [Gamle Mursten, 2018]. Economical matters are both seen as a driver and a
barrier. Saving money on disposal charges provides incitement for selling and reusing building
products [Mortensen, 2015]. However, the quality of the product must be known to reduce
pollution from potential hazardous materials.
Hazardous waste and problematic materials are also a central barrier of reuse and recycling.
These materials must be identified and sorted separately to reduce risk of contamination of
clean materials. “Materialeatlas” defines common materials reuse and recycle potential by map-
ping out use of hazardous materials [Oberender, 2016]. In 1990 Denmark developed technical
specifications for recycled crushed concrete as aggregate in new concrete. Even so, consequence
of risk of pollution reduced the interest of recycling of concrete [Deloitte, 2015].
Both Deloitte [Deloitte, 2015] and SBi [Mortensen, 2015] found that a main issue for recycling
was linked to past and current building tradition and practices. Especially, lacking practices
for coordination of demolition activities between stakeholders.
Uncoordinated planning of
demolition can be a barrier of reusing and recycling of building materials. SBi identified that
building schedules often are under a lot of pressure, why mapping of existing buildings for
demolition and pre-demolition audits often are neglected [Mortensen, 2015].
2.4
Danish ministerial decree on building waste
The definition of waste and how it is handled is a European matter. Danish legislation comple-
ments the overall European framework. Some rules are common for all EU countries, others
are an expression of an individual national choice reflected in national rules. It is the Danish
legislation that must be followed in Denmark. Denmark is one of few European countries with
specified waste sorting requirements. This means that waste must be separated in fractions at
demolition site. The rules and requirements are explained in the ministerial decree on building
waste [Miljøministeriet, 2020].
According to the ministerial decree on building waste (§ 64) all waste-producing companies
must separate hazardous waste, PCB-containing waste, and double glazing from their CDW.
The separation of CDW must be conducted on-site or at authorized sorting facilities [Miljømin-
isteriet, 2020]. Furthermore, sorting is required for a minimum of 10 named fractions:
1. Natural stone e.g. granite or flint
2. Unglazed brick and tiles
3. Concrete

2.5
Building typologies
Building typologies are a classification concept for buildings according to a set of parameters.
These parameters could be anything from age, size, and purpose. Classifications are a form of
generalization. A generalization is where low-level data are replaced by higher-level concepts
[Han, 2006]. For example, year of construction can be generalized to a construction year class,
a categorical attribute, that represents a period with a custom way of building.
Numerous studies have developed building typologies based on unique parameters and classi-
fications. In 2013 the states building research institute (SBi) created a design guide for en-
ergy, daylight, and indoor environment renovation. Collaborators of the design guide included
several stakeholders of the construction industry.
The design guide presents nine building
typologies with unique architectural, technical, and functional properties. The nine typolo-
gies are based on three building types and three construction periods. Single-family houses,
apartments, and oﬀice buildings of three construction periods ranging from 1850-1980 are rep-
resented. According to the design guide these represent a majority of building mass based on
floor area and energy consumption [Marsh, 2013].

2.6 Building material composition
9
The “Typology Approach for Building Stock Energy Assessment” (TABULA) project is an-
other typology study. As a part of a larger European project, building typologies have been
developed for 13 European countries. Each national typology consists of a classification scheme
which group buildings according to parameters [Institut Wohnen und Umwelt GmbH, 2016].
Furthermore, the project contained a set of exemplary buildings representing building types.
A Danish typology for residential buildings was established in the TABULA project. Three
main buildings types were used, and each main building type was again split up in nine periods
of construction. The buildings were single-family houses, terraced houses, and apartments and
the building periods represented typical building tradition and insulation levels. The overall
purpose of the project was to establish a comparative methodology framework for European
countries.
According to TABULA there are two fundamental ways of defining building typologies. They
are respectively average buildings and example buildings. The average building is presented as
being “composed of small areas of all kinds of construction types, [...]. Each of the sub-areas
represents the share of that particular construction type found when auditing the entire building
stock of a specific construction period. Thus the artificially average building does not exist in
real life, but is a statistical valid representation of a selected share of the entire building stock”
[Wittchen, 2012]. The example building is defined as “a representative example of a specific
building type and age [Wittchen, 2012]. Each of the building typologies can be used for different
purposes.
2.6
Building material composition
Detailed information on composition of the building stock is a necessity for eﬀicient recycling
of demolition waste. To project the future availability of recyclable material from demolition
activities, it is essential to gather information about the material composition of the building
stock and the buildings life expectancy. Numerous approaches on gaining knowledge about
characteristics of building stock exist. However, detailed information on material composition
is often limited. A major challenge is generating data on individual buildings and utilizing this
information for selected area of interest. There are three methods for generating specific values
about the material composition [Kleemann, 2016]. The three methods presented by Kleemann
et al. are:
1. Determination by using information about materials used to construct the buildings
2. Determination by investigating the buildings prior to their demolition
3. Determination by analyzing the generated waste streams after demolition
Studies prior to this one has used these methods and identified some of the major individual
challenges. A main challenge of the third method is, that estimating composition of build-
ing stock based on waste streams face the challenge of under or overestimating the content
[Kleemann, 2016]. A main challenge has been identified as the diﬀiculties in collecting large
quantities of waste streams of heterogeneous composition. Furthermore, the quality of the
data can often vary significantly as it is reported by demolition contractors and collected by

Data mining refers to the extraction or “mining” of knowledge from large amounts of data [Han,

Machine learning have become more common in society then most people might think. Any
thing from face and image recognition to personalized advertisement built on fundamental
machine learning techniques to power the process. Machine learning can be categorized into
two learning methods. They are respectively supervised and unsupervised machine learning.
Supervised learning is defined by its use of labeled datasets to train algorithms to classify
data or predict outcomes accurately [IBM Cloud Education, 2020]. Training sets are used to
produce desired outputs. These training sets are based on inputs and correct outputs, that
allow the model to train and learn. Supervised machine learning can be separated into classi-
fication and regression when data mining. Classification is used to accurately assign data to
categories. On the contrary, regression is used to understand relationships between variables
and are commonly used for projections [IBM Cloud Education, 2020]. Linear regression is a
popular regression algorithm. An example of supervised learning is predictive analytics. Pre-
dictive analytics systems allow the user to anticipate results based on a given output variable.
An example of this could be material quantities of buildings.
Common challenges of supervised machine learning have been identified as: models can require
high level of expertise to structure accurately, training models can be time intensive, and
datasets can contain human errors, resulting in algorithms learning incorrectly [IBM Cloud
Education, 2020].

3.3.1
Classification for buildings
Creating typologies is a form of generalization of data. In this generalization low-level data
are replaced by higher-level concepts through the use of concept hierarchies. For example, cat-
egorical attributes, like address, can be generalized to higher-level concepts, like postal codes
or cites. Similarly, values for numerical attributes, like year of construction, may be mapped
to higher-level concepts, like a construction year classes that defines common traits. Concept
hierarchy generation is a powerful tool for data mining, as it allow the mining of data with
different levels of abstraction. Although detail is lost by such data generalization, the general-
ized data may be more meaningful and easier to interpret [Han, 2006].
Concept hierarchies was used to generalize data for analysis and interpretation. Another main
purpose of the concept hierarchies was to establish typologies for estimating the urban mining
potential of the entire building stock. Classification concepts of common buildings was created
according to building purpose and construction period. This methodology was inspired by
Danish building typologies from the TABULA project [Wittchen, 2012]. Following sections
will present the concept classification used for this study on building data.

3.3.2.2
Regression analysis
Regression analysis is a statistical methodology that is most often used for numeric prediction.
Regression can be is used to approximate given data [Han, 2006]. A random variable, y (called
a response variable), can be modeled as a linear function of another random variable, x (called
a predictor variable), with the equation:
y = wx + b
(3.2)
Multiple linear regression is an extension of linear regression, which allows a response variable,
y, to be modeled as a linear function of several predictor variables. The regression model of
this study was based on three predictors, respectively floor area, building period, and building
type. Building period, and building type are both categorical data and floor area is numeric.
The model was designed as a single response model meaning waste amounts did not account
for relations between materials.
3.3.2.3
Model training and testing
5-fold cross validation was used for model training and testing. In 5-fold cross validation initial
data are randomly partitioned into 5 exclusive folds of approximately equal size. Training and
testing of the model are then performed 5 times [Han, 2006]. In the first iteration, the first
subset is reserved for testing and the four others are then used for training. In the second
iteration, the second subset is reserved for testing and the four others are used for training,
and so on. The following figure illustrates the process of cross validation.
==PAGE:35==

Figure 3.9. The process of k-fold cross validation of a prediction model presented by Analytics Vidhya
For prediction modeling, the error estimate is calculated as the total loss from the 5 iterations,
divided by the total number of initial tuples [Han, 2006]. In other words, it calculates the
average test error over each fold. This method provides an estimation of the predictive accuracy
of the final model and is often considered well suited for smaller data sets.

3.5
Estimating urban mining potential
Final objective of this thesis is to estimate an urban mining potential of an area of interest.
Per definition, urban mining potential represent the entire material stock. However, theory
have shown that urban mining processes typically require efforts for mining the resources and
materials. It would be more interesting to identify the recovery potential of Copenhagen which
theoretically should represent the entire recoverable material stock. In the results of this thesis
the calculated urban mining potential is going to represent the actual recovery potential.
3.5.1
Bottom-up analysis
General ideas of the bottom-up analysis for urban mining, support the thought of seeing
cities as material banks. The approach identifies major uses of a given type of material e.g.,
recoverable building materials. Typical amounts of materials are then determined in each unit,
like a building type of a specific building period. Total material stock is then computed through
calculations of the material stock of each type. This methodology was used to estimate urban
mining potentials.
Figure 3.10. Concept of cities seen as material banks
Through detailed knowledge of building materials, high grade reuse and recycling is achievable.
Combining this information for a given area interest ultimately results opportunities for eﬀicient
planning of sustainable construction.
3.5.2
Total urban mining potential
Copenhagen’s total urban mining potential is estimated by use of the bottom-up approach
introduced in section 2.2 [Zhu, 2014]. Common material composition of each type within each
building period was identified. Knowledge was then converted to an urban level. The total
urban mining potential are presented as the summarized weight of all materials also known as
material stock. These summarized values can be further sub-categorized by defined fractions.
Recovery potential is defined as [Arora, 2020]:
==PAGE:40==
32
3 Methodology
Recovery PotentialTotal = Urban mining potentialTotal × Recovery Eﬀiciency
(3.7)
Recovery potential present an interesting quantity since it defines recoverable materials. Minis-
terial decree on waste require separation of waste in defined categories. Theoretically materials
marked for recovery should represent the recovery potential. This information provides insight
regarding reuse and recycling potentials of material stock.
Similar approach was tested on a national level. However, data was limited in comparison to
those of Copenhagen resulting in additional uncertainties. These issues will be addressed in
the analysis.
3.5.3
Annual urban mining potential
Copenhagen’s annual urban mining potential is estimated by use of the bottom-up approach.
Annual urban mining potential indicates the material outflows of each year. In other words,
when will materials be released from the total urban mining potential. This information is
vital to planning of construction. Annual urban mining potential is defined as [Arora, 2020]:
Urban Mining PotentialAnnual = Recovery PotentialAnnual = OutflowAnnual
(3.8)
Annual outflows are a product of building lifetime and release rates. These are mainly influ-
enced by socioeconomical factors. In the analysis an assumption is made that there will always
be a constant material outflow as a result of demolitions. Calculations are based on required
CDW data and is presented in the analysis.

4.2.3
Part conclusion
Analysis have shown that it is possible to construct a model for predicting recoverable fac-
tions of a building’s material stock based on waste streams. However, numerous limitation
and barriers have been identified. With a data set of limited size and lacking quality it can
be diﬀicult to choose the appropriate data for analysis. With current conditions compromises
must be made between quality and quantity data. High quantity leads to easier identification
of unique traits and the same goes for quality. Through a strict selection process significant
data was excluded and left out for analysis. This ultimately means less data for training and
testing. Unfortunately, data structure and lacking traceability of CDW in Copenhagen limits
the possibilities.
A prediction model is only as good as the data used. This study constructed a model based
on expected generated waste rather than actual generated amounts. This decision is going
to significantly decrease result authenticity and quality. During the production of this thesis,
real quality data of actual generated amounts are a rarity. The only people with access to
actual generated amounts specific to buildings are private demolition contractors. Significant
prediction errors are a result of lacking quality and quantity of CDW data. Low amounts
of data limit the model’s predictions on uncommon building types. More data and increased
quality should result in improved predictions and limited prediction errors. This part of the
study has presented and identified limitations of the constructed predictive model.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
This thesis aims to support circular business models and increase knowledge of material stock
through data driven recovery. By identifying patterns of the buildings stock, it should theoret-
ically be possible to identify and estimate recovery potential of a city’s buildings stock. These
patterns are identified through investigations of waste streams and use of machine learning.
Methodology and results will be discussed in this chapter regarding limitations and opportuni-
ties for future investigations. Furthermore, the big picture and the role of this thesis’ findings
will be addressed.
5.1
Methodology
This thesis investigated the potentials of data driven recovery based on available building data
in Denmark. It examines the possibilities of determining recoverable quantities of fractions,
based on waste streams from demolitions. Concepts hierarchies and generalizations of building
information provide foundation of prediction modeling based on building typologies. These
typologies are then used to identify urban mining potentials of an area of interest.
An urban mining potential of a city is defined as the total material stock of the city. A cities
total material stock is composed of the building material stock of every building in the city. A
buildings material stock is also defined as the material composition. Analysis have shown that
the material composition of a building can be determined based on waste stream after demo-
lition. By investigating waste streams of CDW it should theoretically be possible to estimate
an urban mining potential of a city. By utilizing generalizations for typologies this process
was carried out in this thesis.
Process of the thesis is identified by four major parts. The first part covers data collection
and preparation. Current conditions of Denmark do not contain quality information on CDW
streams on a municipal level. CDW quantities are estimated on a national and municipal level
based on inventories of waste plants. No traceability result in diﬀiculties in utilizing information
of waste production. However, Copenhagen, amongst other municipalities, gather information
on expected generated CDW amounts. The system is inconsistent, and it is estimated that
approximately 80 % of cases are not reported. Furthermore, the quality of the reported cases
vary significantly since no punishment is involved in wrong registrations. Information of the
reported cases provide little detail of traceability. Common cases contain an address, the floor
area, and the expected amounts of waste to be generated. All of which are parameters that
are likely to be misinterpreted. A fraction of this data was provided for the making of this
thesis. This data was combined with publicly available building data acquired through BBR
to increase individual traceability and data level. Classifications was made based on building
type and building construction period. Tendencies of the acquired data was analyzed, and
==PAGE:76==
68
5 Discussion
characteristics was found to be somewhat realistic.
Second part of the thesis covered the making of a prediction model. Theory have shown that
material composition of a building can be estimated by investigating waste streams of demo-
litions. The prediction model was based on a selection of cases chosen from provided CDW
data. Selection of cases was based on a concept what sought to filter out unusable data cases
that would work as outliers. In this process a significant number of cases was discarded. An
assumption was made that cases marked for demolition would logically contain the complete
material stock of the given building. Theoretically this should be true, but the quality of the
data indicated that the assumption could be off. Demolition cases contained information of
each expected generated CDW amount divided in 60 unique fractions. It was assumed that
the entire material composition could be identified based on these. The prediction model was
made to predict the recoverable materials of a building based on type, building period and size.
In theory this approach should support identification of hazardous materials as the number of
recoverable materials should be directly affected. Common building periods with unique traits
was used to generalize year of construction. The traits of the used building periods have been
recognized by other studies. An assumption was made that the material composition would
solely be based on building type, building period and size.
The third part tried to assess and validate the constructed prediction model. Performance of
the model was found to be like the quality of the data used for training and testing. A case
study was performed on two different buildings with varying results. Predictions was found
to be somewhat in line with ones made on an oﬀice building but well off on a day care center.
Traits of building periods interpreted by the prediction model was found to be dissimilar to
unique traits previously identified in other studies. Misjudgment was assumed to be caused by
lacking quality of data.
Fourth part of the thesis sought to investigate an urban mining potential of Copenhagen. An
assumption was made that the prediction model was appropriate for this analysis. Concepts
of building typologies suggest that a building stock can be divided into generalized classifica-
tions. In theory this meant that every building of a specific type within a specific building
period could be assumed to contain the same material composition. Quantities of the material
composition would then only be influenced by the size of a building. This assumption meant
that all buildings of a specific building type of a specific building period could be considered
as one building. The size of this building would be represented by the accumulated floor area
of every building falling under this classification. This concept was used to calculate a total
recovery potential of a few selected building types in Copenhagen and Denmark. Theoretically,
this approach should be appropriate on an even larger scale. Lastly, an annual urban mining
potential was estimated. The annual urban mining potential was defined as the outflow of
materials. Numerous factors outside the construction industry influence CDW streams. How-
ever, an assumption was made that a constant amount of waste would be released annually as
a minimum. This amount was based on the average lifetime of a buildings and an inevitable
demolition percentage.

5.2
Building information and data
The analysis of this thesis was first and foremost an investigation and evaluation of available
building information and CDW data. Building data comes in different levels of quality and
availability. Denmark has a rather limited size of available building data and the available
data is of a low level of detail. A main provider of building information in Denmark is the
Building and Housing Register (BBR). Other providers of building information are the energy
labeling system and the SAVE initiative. It is estimated that about 1/3 of Danish buildings
are covered under the last two mentioned. BBR covers a larger portion of the building stock
and provides knowledge of building purposes, year of construction, floor areas, outer wall, and
roofing materials. All of which fall under a classification system. Classification systems that
provide little to no information of material compositions. Even though BBR contain informa-
tion of the outer wall and roofing materials of a building, it does not say much of the rest of
a building’s material composition quality and quantity.
Classifications used by BBR of building purpose or usage is valuable information. In this thesis
classifications of BBR are used to define building typologies based on building purpose, sizes,
and year of construction. Combining this knowledge with traits of different construction peri-
ods enables possibilities of generalizations. One could assume that there is a high probability
of buildings of the same type, built within the same periods, within proximity of each other, to
contain a somewhat similar material composition. Complexities go way beyond this; however,
it is still a fair assumption to make and the likelihood is there.
Working with a limited set of data justify the utilization of generalizations and concept hier-
archies. Throughout this thesis multiple generalizations and assumptions was made in order
justify actions and initiatives. Although detail is lost when generalizing data, the outcome is
often more meaningful and easier to interpret.
Generalizing building types made sense in more then one way.
It is fair to assume that
buildings of similar purpose are going to have somewhat similar traits. The likelihood of two
single-family houses having similar traits, in comparison to a single-family house and a retail
store, is significantly higher. On the contrary, assuming a uniform material composition of a
building type based on the construction period is less likely to always be true. An assumption
was made that material composition within classifications was uniform and quantities was lin-
early related to floor area. Floor area is suboptimal figure of identifying material intensities
and using gross volume [m3] would have made more theoretical sense. However, information
on gross volume is not available and can only be estimated with current available building data.
This study used classifications of building periods identified by other studies. The Danish
building stock have been divided in three periods with individual traits of construction prac-
tices. Real world scenarios are likely to be much more complicated than this. Why relevant
investigation of different traits in building periods, of unique building types, could be an in-
teresting subject of further analysis. Current conditions do support the construction of better
building typologies. Instead of assuming that material composition would be solely based on
type and building period a further division could be made. BBR contain information on outer
wall and roofing materials. This information could be utilized to subcategories building typolo-
gies. It would be fairer to assume that a single-family house, with brick walls and roof tiles,

brick walls and roof tiles, built in 1961. This seems more likely then if one of the buildings

5.3
Construction and demolition waste streams
An autopsy is a medical procedure where a subject is thoroughly investigated at the end of life.
Knowledge and experience have identified tendencies and made discoveries more predictable.
Findings of an autopsy can tell a lot about the contents of the subject such as healthy, dam-
aged, and contaminated parts. This analogy directly translates to the process of investigating
waste streams of buildings. Waste streams present a picture of what a building contained and
what is has been subject of. It may have been contaminated by hazardous insulation material
or maybe it was still healthy and ready to live on. This way of thinking was the foundation of
the construction of this thesis.
General interest in analyzing CDW streams is not an uncommon practice. However, detailed
information is in short supply. Studies prior to this one has identified lacking quality and avail-
ability as barriers of proper investigation. However, Denmark is one of few European countries
with legal requirements for fraction sorting of construction and demolition waste. These re-
quirements are practiced and communicated through the ministerial decree on waste. CDW
is identified as a focus area of the ministerial decree and it contains requirements of reporting
expected generated amounts. However, the management and traceability of the data is absent
which results in missed opportunities. Requirements state that demolitions and renovations
generating more then 1 ton of waste or covering more the 10 m2 must be reported to the KK.
However, KK estimates that up towards 80 % of cases are not reported. Reasoning behind
this could be that there is no punishment involved in not reporting expected waste to KK.
Usually when CDW is produced it is transported to a nearby waste plant. However, these
waste plants can retrieve produced waste whether the reporting was made or not. Thereby,
a large portion of CDW ends up in piles with little to no traceability of where it originated from.
Amended ministerial decree on waste management 2021 present new initiatives in CDW man-
agement. It contains new requirements to the builder, carrier, and receiver of the waste. These
initiatives should significantly improve traceability of CDW and provide an even better foun-
dation for defining material stocks based of CDW streams. Methodology used in this thesis
should be applicable and better results would be obtainable through further investigations of
new improved data.
5.4
Predicting availability of materials
This thesis presented a simplified method for determining a constant annual outflow of mate-
rials based on expected lifetime of buildings. Predicting availability is a complicated task as
outflows are directly influenced by numerous socioeconomical aspects. A correlation has been
identified between prosperity and produced CDW. Economical growth equals shorter lifetimes
of buildings and more comprehensive renovations. Another major influencer are political ac-

tions which can increase CDW production indirectly through bills and grants.
National inventories of CDW have not yet been published for the year of 2020. One could
assume that a global pandemic would leave identifiable traits in the quantities of produced
CDW. However, numerous discoveries suggest that the construction industry is thriving de-
spite a national lockdown. Employment rates of the construction industry increased by 2.3 %
from third to fourth quarter, which meant the fluctuation caused by lockdown was nullified
[DS, 2020]. The building pipeline showed minor fluctuations at the beginning of the lockdown
in May 2020. However, a report from the builders’ association from October showed that the
pipeline had regulated to levels like the ones seen in January 2020 prior to lockdown [Bygher-

The report indicated that the pipeline contained projects of collected
value up towards 90.1 billion DKK. Production of CDW of 2020 could be assumed to be like
the quantities seen in 2019.
Predicting availability of building materials comes with various benefits and keeping materials
inside the loop, support circular business models.
The idea of predicting availability is to
enable planning of recycling and reuse of materials. Once building materials are released, they
ought to be given new purpose in another construction within a short span of time. Storage
of recoverable materials is a diﬀicult matter as it comes with a chance of contamination and
there may be a lack of space for storage of larger quantities. In a perfect world a complete
data set of all materials available at any given time would be known and change accordingly
to socioeconomical traits of the time.
This thesis investigated intelligent leaning and used supervised machine learning for prediction
modeling. The prediction model used in this analysis was a single response linear regression
model. Another model may be more suitable when the right data is available. Challenges
of supervised machine learning was identified as; high requirements of expertise to structure
models accurately, and chances of algorithms learning incorrectly. It is likely that a multi
response linear regression model would have an easier time recognizing patterns of relations
and traits. An artificial neural network model could also be a better fit as the output can be
graduated and more suﬀicient use of renovation cases would be enabled. A lot of data was left
out for analysis as it did not fit the criteria of the chosen model. Identifying the most eﬀicient
intelligent learning model could be an interesting subject for further analysis.
5.5
Building material recovery
This thesis identified potentials of significant quantities of recoverable materials in Copenhagen
and Denmark. Utilizing this information would not only reduce CDW production but could
also have significant environmental benefits. It is estimated that the construction industry
is accountable for 40 % of global CO2 emissions, 11 % of which results from manufacturing
building materials [IEA, 2019]. Despite a rather high recovery rate of almost 90 % of CDW in
Denmark there is still a significant potential in recycling and reuse of materials. It is estimated
that approximately 52 % of recovered CDW is used for “other recovery”. These quantities are
led by crushed concrete and asphalt used as aggregate in road work and pavement. While
this is considered better than using natural resources and virgin materials, the process is not
compatible with the ideas of a circular economy.
The principles of circularity are to keep
==PAGE:80==
72
5 Discussion
materials inside the value chain where they can be either reused or recycled. Other recovery
means that materials are used for the last time.
Goals of the WFD of 2008 stated that 70 % of CDW should be recovered by 2020. Denmark
amongst other countries reached this goal long before. While the initiative was well intended,
it could also be seen as rather unambiguous. 52 % of other recovery implies that only about
38 % CDW is reused or recycled. Hence, kept inside the value chain. Furthermore, the goal is
contradicted by the No net land take by 2050 initiative from EU [European Commission, 2016].
A natural consequence of the initiative could be reduced demand on road building materials
like crushed concrete and asphalt. This would lessen use of recovered concrete and asphalt and
significantly decrease use of recoverable CDW. One could assume that EU would eventually
apply requirements for recycling of CDW and move a step up the ladder in the waste hierarchy.
Recovery of concrete is identified as an important area of CDW management. Concrete is
the most widely used construction material in the world. The main ingredient of concrete is
cement. As a result of chemical and thermal combustion processes involved in the production
of cement; it is estimated that cement production is accountable for around 8 % of global
CO2 emissions [Chatham House, 2018]. This emphasizes the importance of recycling concrete.
In 1990 Denmark developed technical specifications for the use of crushed recycled concrete
as aggregate in new concrete [Deloitte, 2015]. However, the risk of pollution and numerous
problems have reduced the interest of recycling of concrete in Denmark. Generally, trust in
material quality for recycling and reuse is an inhibition of circular practices. In 2020 a scandal
was uncovered in Copenhagen where a constructor had cheated with properties of concrete
used in a high-rise. The contractor had used recovered concrete and tiles as aggregate in parts
of the construction. This meant that the strength of the concrete was found to be insuﬀicient
for the purpose [Teknologisk Intitut, 2020]. Scandals like these could significantly reduce the
legitimacy of high-grade recycling of concrete and reduce trust in recycling and reuse.
In the future initiatives like the Nordic Swan Ecolabel of buildings could help tackling circular
issues by addressing requirements of healthier buildings with minimal CO2 emissions [Ecolabel,
2020]. Another program initiated by the government was the voluntary sustainability classes
[Trafik-bygge og boligstyrelsen, 2020]. The program contained CO2 goals for buildings that
should eventually become requirements of the Danish building regulations.
Initiatives like
these could increase demand of recycling and reuse building materials. E.g., a builder may be
motivated to use recycled bricks rather than newly produced and save 0.5 kg CO2 emission
per brick. Decisions of recycling may provide more room for other things in a construction
with requirements for CO2 emissions.

Conclusion
This study has identified that material types, quantities, and conditions of building materials
in use can be approximated to a precision that allows an estimation of buildings reuse and
recycling potential. This approximation is achievable based on existing building data. Current
conditions allow estimations of 60 unique material fractions defined by type and condition. Ap-
proximations are attainable through intelligent learning of CDW streams. Existing available
data in Denmark do not allow high quality approximations.
Theory have shown that it is possible to assess a buildings material composition based on waste
streams of demolitions. The municipality of Copenhagen utilizes a system where builders re-
port their expected quantities of different waste types prior to renovation and demolition.
However, the system is flawed as it does not provide follow-up and it is estimated that 80
% cases are not reported. New regulations per 2021 should improve this system and ensure
ease of traceability. These actions should support future optimization and innovation of CDW
management.
Utilizing quality CDW data with building specific information allow estimations of a city’s
materials stock. Current Danish CDW stream data is only based on expected quantities and
does not provide any traceability other then an address. This study used this knowledge to
combine detailed building specific information to each case. Updated cases served as input for
a prediction model that allowed an approximation of CDW based on a defined building typol-
ogy. Predictions was assumed per theory to represent material stock of a building. Knowledge
of material composition based on building typology was used to estimate an urban mining
potential of Copenhagen and Denmark.
Quality of a prediction model is only ever going to be as good as the data used for model
training and testing. The only available waste stream data with any sort of traceability, are
reported cases of expected quantities of CDW. A thorough investigation identified numerous
uncertainties regarding the quality of the data. Characteristics was recognized as lack of knowl-
edge and misinterpretations of essential information. Therefore, results of total and annual
recovery potentials presented in this study should be considered of equal validity. Data quality
was identified as a product of insuﬀicient management.
Proper management of CDW and recycled materials have been linked to major benefits in
terms of sustainability and quality of life. Additionally, it can provide opportunities for the
construction industry as it can increase demand of recycled materials.
Production of new
construction materials are accountable for a noticeable share of global CO2 emission and raw
material consumption. Significant savings should be obtainable through eﬀicient reuse and re-
cycling. Increased demand of recycled materials is inevitable as tendencies lean towards focus
on sustainable construction. New sustainable labeling initiatives are emerging, and they are

This study has recognized possibilities of eﬀicient use of data driven recovery and identified
barriers and limitations that serves as an interesting subject for further investigations within
the field.
