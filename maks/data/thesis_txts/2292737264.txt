ABSTRACT 
Intumescent coatings currently represent the preferable fire insulation for steel struc-
tures and the main viable solution where exposed steel elements are used as an architec-
tonical design feature. Nevertheless, the behavior of intumescent coating during fire is 
not fully understood and current design procedures are not accurate, nor very reliable 
(Bartholomai et al., 2003; EN 13381-4, 2002; Li et al., 2012). This study aims at high-
lighting the effect of different heating conditions on the thermal resistance of intumes-
cent coatings. In serving this purpose, two sets of experiments were conducted. The first 
set of experiments was aimed at highlighting the effect of different heating rates on the 
intumescent coating and was carried out on RHS profiles heated in an electric oven. The 
second set of experiments was aimed at highlighting the effect of increasing radiant heat 
and was carried out on small steel sheets heated in an electric mass loss cone. All steel 
samples were protected with 1 mm dry film thickness of intumescent coating. In both 
sets of experiments, the behavior of the coating was assessed by means of a qualitative 
analysis based on visual inspection of the sample (color, expansion, possible cracks or 
loss of paint) as well a quantitative assessment of the activation temperature, loss of 
insulation, and calculation of equivalent thermal resistance based on the Eurocode for-
mula of protected steel (EN1993-1-2, 2005). While no clear correlation between the 
equivalent thermal resistance and the heating rate was found, the experiments showed 
that the use of an equivalent constant value of thermal resistance allows predicting the 
steel temperatures only in the range of 300-500 °C. Temperatures below 300 °C will be 
underestimated. The first set of experiments also showed an underestimation of temper-
atures above 550 °C, independently of the applied heating rate. This loss of functionali-
ty could be explained by cracks formed in the intumescent char after long-term heating. 
Results from a similar test conducted on an IPE-profile suggested that cracks formed in 
the intumescent char due to the convex shape of the tested RHS profiles. Ultimately, the 
findings in this study support the assumption that the applied thermal resistance needs to 
comprehend the fire condition for which the steel element is designed for.

2 INTRODUCTION 
The discipline of fire protection engineering is known to date back to ancient Rome, 
where its first application was seen, as the Emperor Nero, in response to a devastating 
fire, ordered the city to be rebuilt under the implementation of passive fire protection 
techniques such as spaces between buildings and non-combustible building materials 
[1]. However, due to the decline of the Roman Empire and the onset of the Dark Ages, a 
technical approach to fire protection of buildings first reappeared in the 17th century 
after the Great London Fire in 1666 in which over 80 percent of the city was destroyed 
[1] [2]. During the Industrial Revolution, innovations in fire protection technologies 
intensified in order to meet new and greater fire hazards posed by industrial properties 
such as factories and warehouses. Also, in this period, the amount conflagrations 
dropped as conventional timber structures were generally replaced by masonry. In the 
20th century, construction of increasingly taller steel structures amplified the demand for 
quantification of fire resistance of structures. In the second half of the 20th century, the 
development of a general body of knowledge in the field of fire protection engineering 
took place along with the formation of a professional society, and fire engineering con-
sultancy was initiated as an independent profession. Subsequently, a standardized tem-
perature-time curve was developed followed by standardized test methods for building 
elements [3].   
 
Today, standardized fire exposures are still widely applied both in testing of building 
materials and in the fire protection design of structures. In the Eurocode [4], the stand-
ard fire curve is comprised by the ISO834 standard fire curve presented in Figure 1. The 
standardized temperature-time relationship was implemented to provide compatibility 
between tests conducted in different laboratories around the world. However, the stand-
ard fire curve is fixed and forever increasing in temperature, thereby presenting an unre-
alistic heat exposure for most design applications. Hence, there has been a demand in 
the structural fire engineering community for a more flexible temperature-time relation-
ship based on parameters like compartment geometry, available ventilation, amount of 
combustibles, and characteristics of compartment boundaries [7].   
 
This has led to the introduction of parametric fires as an alternative to the standard 
fire curve. Implementation of parameters like opening factors, fuel load, and thermal 
inertia of compartment boundaries, enables parametric fires to more accurately predict 
the progression of realistic fires.

2.1 Parametric fire curves 
A handbook dealing with the design of steel structures using such parametric fires 
was first presented by Pettersson et al. [8] in 1976, introducing temperature-time rela-
tionships based on the level of opening factor in the compartment as well as the thermal 
inertia of the its boundaries. This assessment method was later verified by Hadvig [9] 
during his experiments on the influence of opening factor fires on structures of wood. 
As the application of these parametric fires was limited to a compartment area of 200 
m2, Hertz [10] presented a guideline in 2012, which justifies application of opening fac-
tor parametric fires to compartments larger than 200 m2. 
Along with improvements in available computational methods, implementation of 
parametric fires has supported the development of performance-based designs. As alter-
natives to conventional prescriptive designs, where adequate fire protection is achieved 
through compliance with a set of acknowledged solutions, performance-based designs 
utilize computational modeling tools, such as CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics), to 
analyze the impacts of fire in more complex compartment geometries. Additionally, by 
implementing parametric fires in performance-based designs, real fire effects can be 
addressed based on worst case design scenarios. This provides engineers with a much 
wider set of design tools and allows for a more accurate assessment of the fire hazards 
in any particular compartment, and architects are left with more freedom in the design 
of building geometries and interior arrangements [5].Evolutions in the building technol-
ogy involves building envelopes containing a much higher level of insulation in order to 
reduce energy consumption and meet the increasing demands set by the building author-
ities. Consequently, building envelopes are provided with thick layers of insulation as 
well as low-energy windows. However, in the case of fire, such improvements restrict 
the heat from being transferred through the compartment boundaries, and the highly 
insulating windows are often resistant enough to keep integrity during the high heat

fires in such compartments are potentially confined, leading to longer fire durations

For decreasing opening factors it is observed that fire durations are increased whilst 
the peak temperatures decrease. This means that inserting highly durable windows will 
potentially lead to longer fire durations. 
Furthermore, Figure 2 shows the impact in the parametric temperature-time curves 
made by variations in the thermal inertia of the compartment boundaries. It is seen that 
by reducing the thermal inertia, as is the case for highly insulated modern buildings, an 
increase in the peak temperature of a compartment fire can be expected.  
The graphs seen in Figure 2 indicate that the current evolution in the building design 
could lead to longer fire durations with higher peak temperatures.  
While rapid fires with high temperatures usually constitute the most conservative de-
sign case for evacuation purposes, the aforementioned slow fires with longer duration 
present the most dangerous design case in the reduction of the load-bearing capacity of 
structures. This is due to the fact, that it takes time for the fire impact to penetrate a 
cross-section, thus, longer duration of the fire exposure increases the possibility of the 
cross-section temperature to invoke structural failure [6]. On this motivation, focus in 
this thesis is brought to the slow fire impacts on structural fire protection. 
2.2 Fire protection of steel structures 
Fire protection engineering is usually divided into active and passive systems. Active 
systems comprise installations, which are activated during the case of fire. Examples of 
such active systems are automatic fire alarm systems, automatic sprinkler systems and 
==PAGE:16==
 
4 
fire ventilations systems. On the other hand, passive systems are inherently part of the 
building design and include division of the building into fire sections, non-combustible 
interior and exterior surfaces and planning of escape routes.  
The fire protection of structures presents an example of passive fire protection sys-
tems and is often required in order to ensure that the bearing capacity of the structure is 
retained either throughout the entire course of a fire or until all occupants have evacuat-
ed. 
Indeed, fire protection of structures has become an increasingly important issue in 
the construction industry over the past decades. The ongoing construction of taller and 
lighter steel structures demands adequate protection against fire-induced damages. 
Moreover, the taller structures are required to withstand fire for a longer period of time 
in order to sustain safe evacuation of all occupants along with providing safe interven-
tion from the fire brigade [7]. 
Steel is often chosen as the main structural material in such buildings. This is due its 
high strength, which enables the possibility of designing slim structures with low self-
weight – features which often are architecturally desirably when designing structures 
that should convey an impression of lightness and airiness. In addition to high rise 
buildings, this architectural feature is often pursued when designing stadiums, airport 
terminals, and exhibition halls [8]. 
However, steel has a high level of thermal conductivity, and as most materials, the 
structural properties of steel degrade with increasing temperatures. At a temperature of 
600 °C the strength of steel is reduced to approximately 50% relative to that at ambient 
temperature [9]. Consequently, steel structures must be provided with sufficient fire 
protection in order to maintain stability during the case of fire in order to prevent fire-
induced damages and fatalities. 
2.3 Conventional fire protection materials 
Appropriate structural fire safety of steel members is often achieved by covering the 
elements with conventional fire protection products such as plaster boards. Plaster 
boards are usually based on exfoliated vermiculite, which is a hydrous silicate mineral. 
Vermiculite boards are incombustible and their low conductivity provide durable fire 
protection for steel structures.  
Another method of achieving appropriate fire protection for steel structures is by 
wrapping the steel elements in low conductivity mineral wool. The application process 
is simple and tables are often provided by the manufacturer, showing the exact thick-
nesses needed to ensure certain levels of fire protection. Also, spray protection systems 
are available as cementitious formulations and as sprayed mineral fiber products. They 
are cheap and detailed connections are easily covered by applying a layer of the low 
conductive foam. However, they must be applied on-site, and since it is a wet trade, 
drying time needs to be accounted for in the construction program.

The conventional methods of fire protection mentioned above are inexpensive and 
easily applied, however, they are often deemed aesthetically unpleasant and therefore 
not a desirable choice for visible steel structures. For this reason, intumescent coating 
systems now present the preferable alternative for protection of visible steel structures, 
which are integrated as an architectural feature in the building design. Airport terminals 
and stadiums are examples of such buildings in which architects often wish for the steel 
structures to be part of the design (see Figure 3).

2.4 Intumescent coatings 
While this project focuses on the use of intumescent coatings as fire protection for steel 
structures, they can also be applied to other substrates such as wood. Additionally, in-
tumescent coatings are used in a wide array of different applications. One of these is in 
the protection of fuel tanks against heat exposure, which can cause the tank to rupture 
and potentially explode [10]. Another application of intumescent coatings is in the fire 
protection of interior walls and doors in ships [11] [12]. Also in the oil, gas and chemi-
cal industries, intumescent coatings are applied to protect offshore and onshore facilities 
against fire hazards [13]. In military applications, intumescent coatings are used to pro-
vide protection to intense elevated temperatures of a wide range of devices such as ve-
hicles and munitions [10] [14]. Amongst other applications lists airplanes and space 
crafts. 
Intumescent coatings are paint like substances which are inert at low temperatures 
but swell up at temperatures of 200-300 °C, providing the substrate with an insulating 
low conductive charred layer [7]. The first product utilizing the intumescent process for 
fire protection purposes was patented by H. Tramm in U.S. in 1938. However, the im-
plementation of the product in the building industry remained very limited until the 
mid-1980s. In Figure 4, the increase of revenues in the intumescent coating market is

2.4.1 Functionality of intumescent coatings 
The composition of intumescent coatings is usually a combination of an acid source 
(ammonium polyphosphate), a carbon source (pentaerythriol), and a blowing agent 
(melamine). Upon heating, the acid source decomposes and releases an acid, which re-
acts with the carbon source and forms a phosphorous ester. At a critical temperature, 
usually around 250 °C, the blowing agent is activated, triggering a gas-producing, endo-
thermic reaction, which absorbs part of the heat from the substrate. The gas bubbles 
produced during this reaction are trapped inside the molten carbon matrix, forming an 
expanding multi cellular char, which, given by its high porosity, provide a low conduc-
tivity, foam-like thermal barrier, lowering the speed by which the temperature of the 
substrate increases. During this process, it is of key importance that the molten carbon 
matrix is of an appropriate viscosity so that penetration by the gas bubbles is possible. 
The carbon matrix typically incorporates a thermo-setting polymer, so that further heat-
ing promotes cross-linking reactions, which hardens the structure.  The expansion of the 
char continues until the blowing agent is exhausted or the carbon matrix is insufficiently 
viscous. The intumescent process increases the thickness of the intumescent coating by 
a factor 5-100 times the original thickness [16]. The following four steps describe the 
intumescent process chronologically. 
 
[1] During the early stages of heating, the intumescent coating absorbs a large 
amount of thermal energy, thereby increasing its temperature. 
[2] At a critical temperature, the polymer matrix melts and degrades to form a 
viscous fluid, and the inorganic acid compound normally undergoes thermal 
decomposition at temperatures of 100 °C to 250 °C. 
==PAGE:19==
7 
 
[3] At temperatures between 280 °C to 350 °C, the blowing agent decomposes, 
releasing a large amount of gas, a fraction of which is trapped within the

2.4.2 Current design practice 
In the current design practice, manufacturers of intumescent coatings usually provide 
tables, presenting the required dry film thickness (DFT) needed to ensure that the tem-
perature of a structural steel member is kept under a specified design temperature in a 
specified amount of time during which the structural capacity needs to be ensured. 
However, this method of presenting design data is problematic for several reasons. 
Firstly, the data is based on tests performed by means of the standard curve (Figure 1). 
Nevertheless, due to the reactive nature of intumescent coatings, it is not appropriate to 
extend the results from a standard fire test to other fire scenarios without investigating 
the behavior of the intumescent coating under these conditions first. Hence, this stand-
ardized evaluation method implies the faulty assumption that the thermal properties of 
intumescent coatings are independent of the fire condition. On the contrary, intumescent 
coatings are reactive, meaning that their thermal properties depend on the fire condi-
tions. Unlike traditional methods of fire protection, such as vermiculite boards, concrete 
or spray plasters, whose thermal properties only depend on temperature, the thermal 
properties of intumescent coatings rely on the level of fire exposure [17].  
==PAGE:20==
 
8 
Secondly, most tabular data from the manufacturers only clearly states the time to 
which the critical temperature is reached, leaving out any information regarding the 
evolution of the temperature during the test. In the process of a structural fire design 
according to [9] this only enables the use of simplified design procedures such as the 
method of critical temperature (MCT). In this method, the fire protection of singular 
structural members is dealt with rather than concerning entire structural systems [18]. 
Thus, the design of structural fire protection does often not include the overall stability 
of a structure nor the additional forces introduced at connections due to heating-induced 
dilation effects. 
2.4.3 Methods of assessment 
Both numerical and mathematical models of various complexities have been developed 
by different researchers to study the heating of substrates protected by intumescent coat-
ings. These models focus on establishing a reliable method of predicting the tempera-
ture-time relationship of steel structures protected by intumescent coatings. Most of the 
models are based on one-dimensional heat transfer through the intumescent layer, in-
cluding chemical and kinetic reactions occurring during the intumescence process [19] 
[20] [17] [21]. This method was first proposed by Cagliostro et al. [22] and has later 
been enhanced by including the expansion ratio of the intumescent layer – a parameter 
which has become an increasingly acknowledged factor in the evaluation of the thermal 
resistance of intumescent coatings [17]. The expansion ratio is described by the thick-
ness of the fully developed intumescent char relative to the original thickness of the 
intumescent coating.  
However, a common problem with these models is that the expansion ratio is chosen 
as a constant input parameter. Due to the fact that the expansion of the intumescent char 
depends on the heat exposure, the expansion ratio cannot be known beforehand and 
should therefore constitute an output parameter rather than an input parameter. 
The intumescent process is complex and still not completely understood, and there-
fore more research is needed before these models can fully describe the thermal re-
sistance intumescent coatings.  
The method of effective thermal conductivity or equivalent thermal resistance poses 
a simpler, more intuitive design method which is more easily adopted in the everyday 
design practice than the complex models described above. This method utilizes the in-
verted formula for the protected steel temperature reported in the Eurocode [9], yielding 
an expression of the thermal resistance. Using this formula along with steel tempera-
tures achieved through testing, a constant equivalent thermal resistance can be calculat-
ed and used further in the evaluation of the performance of the intumescent coating. 
This method was investigated in a recent study [23], where small-scale and full-scale 
steel members were tested in a furnace under a standard fire exposure, producing good 
agreement between theoretical and experimental results. 
==PAGE:21==
9 
 
However, as the equivalent thermal resistance does not only depend on the tempera-
ture of the steel but also on the fire condition, these results are not compatible with real-
istic fires.  
2.5 Project objective 
Based on the literature study presented in the previous pages, the purpose of this study 
is to investigate the behavior of intumescent coatings when exposed to different fire 
scenarios. The method of equivalent thermal resistance will be applied in evaluating the 
response of the intumescent coating. In doing so, attention is brought to the possible 
risks connected to the design of intumescent coating protection systems based on a con-
stant thermal resistance, which is achieved solely through standard fire testing.  
Serving this purpose, two sets of experiments were conducted, representing two 
types of heating exposure. The first set included a total of 10 small-scale experiments 
conducted in an electric oven. In these experiments, 300 mm long sections of 150 mm x 
100 mm x 5 mm RHS profiles were heated under implementation of four different heat-
ing rates. As the response of intumescent coatings to low heating rate fires is empha-
sized in this study, the heating rates chosen for implementation in the oven experiments 
are all lower than the ISO834 standard fire curve.  
In the second set of experiments, steel sheet samples with dimensions 100 mm x 90 
mm x 5 mm were heated in a mass loss cone setup. In this setup, three different levels of 
incident heat flux were applied, and for each level, one experiment was conducted on a 
blank steel sheet and on a steel sheet protected by intumescent coating. This set of ex-
periments serves to investigate the behavior of intumescent coatings when exposed to 
different levels of shock heating.  
The application of intumescent coating was left to a professional coating workshop.  
All samples were provided with three layers of intumescent coating, comprising a dry 
film thickness of approximately 1 mm, and both samples have a section factor of 200 m-
1.

3.1 Preliminary experiments 
During the initial phase of this project, a range of preliminary experiments have been 
conducted prior to the main experiments. These served to identify the problems related 
to the experimental work in terms of practical issues such as placement and fixation of 
thermocouples, but also to make sure that experimental results can be verified with ana-
lytical calculations. 
Initially, a range of preliminary experiments were carried out on 90 mm x 100 mm x 
5 mm steel sheets similar to the samples tested in the second set of experiments. The 
steel sheets were coated manually with an ordinary paint brush and an ordinary paint 
roller and heated in the mass loss cone setup. This range of experiments served to find a 
suitable method of attaching thermocouples to the steel sample but also to give a first 
impression on the behavior of the intumescent coating.  
Blank steel samples were heated in both experimental setups, and the steel tempera-
ture measurements were verified by means of analytical heat transfer equations. This 
was done in order to make sure that the heat exposure is fully understood for both setups 
prior to the heating of the coated samples.  
For the experimental setup in the electric oven, this initial phase also included a 
study of three different methods of insulating the edges of the samples so that heating 
from the inside of the RHS samples could be prevented. In finding the optimal solution, 
the measured steel temperature-time curves were compared with the analytical solution 
in which the ideal section factor (corresponding to a continuous beam) was used. A 
method comprising two pieces of Gyproc gypsum board gave the smallest error in rela-
tion to the calculated temperature-time curve, and therefore, this solution was adopted in 
the final experimental setup. The full study on edge insulation for the RHS profile sam-
ples is found in Appendix A.

In the following, the development of the equivalent thermal resistance is used to out-
line the general behavior of the intumescent coating, dividing it into the four general 
phases shown in Figure 22. 
These four phases comprise the inert phase, which is the period of time before the in-
tumescent coating reaches its reaction temperature, the transient phase, being the phase 
during which the intumescent char is formed, the steady phase, which is the phase dur-
ing which the equivalent thermal resistance keeps a constant level, and the decay phase, 
which is where the insulating effect of the intumescent coating is considered to de-
crease. 
The term "end of reaction" refers to the end of the heat absorbing chemical reaction 
in the paint. The exhaustion of this endothermic reaction is considered the only thing 
that could result in such a distinct change in the equivalent thermal resistance. There-
fore, the peak indicates the point, where the chemical reaction stops, ending the intu-
mescence of the char. After this point, the gradual decline of the thermal resistance can 
be explained by the gradual consumption of the carbon binder in the char structure. This 
would also explain that, for experiments stopped during this decline, the char is still 
black/gray. However, for the experiments stretching beyond this phase and into the 
steady phase, the color is completely white, indicating that no carbon material is left in 
the paint. 
In the following sections, the phases of the development of the thermal resistance are 
related to the physical and chemical behavior of the intumescent coating.

5.1.1.1 Inert phase 
Before the temperature of the intumescent coating reaches its reaction temperature at 
approximately 250 °C, the inherent thickness is kept and no additional fire protection is 
provided to the steel substrate. During this phase, the intumescent coating is slowly 
melting, increasing its viscosity considerably. 
During this phase, the measured temperature of the steel is shown to be similar to 
that of unprotected steel.  
5.1.1.2 Transient phase 
As the intumescent coating approaches its reaction temperature, the blowing agent in 
the coating is activated, starting and an endothermic, gas-producing reaction. This reac-
tion emits gas bubbles into the top layer of the intumescent coating, which at this time 
has assumed a consistency viscous enough to be penetrated by the bubbles. During this 
process, the intumescent coating is transformed into a multicellular structure of char. At 
a certain point, the endothermic reaction comes to an end due to the exhaustion of the 
blowing agent. This point is observed as peak of the equivalent thermal resistance with-
in the transient phase. At this time, the intumescent char is considered to be fully devel-
oped. This means that all coating material has reacted, as there is no unreacted virgin 
material left, the multicellular char structure now constitutes the full thickness as it is 
shown in Figure 5. The subsequent decline in the equivalent thermal resistance is ex-
plained by a gradual combustion of the carbon binder, which is the component forming 
==PAGE:46==
 
34 
the char structure. This is substantiated by the observations of char color in Figure 23, 
which shows the char color for experiments stopped during and after the transient phase. 
 
 
Figure 23. Selection of char formation pictures for experiments stopped during and after the transient 
phase. 
Figure 23 shows that the char color changes from black/gray to towards white. In ad-
dition to the change of color, the texture of the char changes from being rather cohesive, 
heavy, and flexible for the experiments stopped during the transient phase to being 
lightweight and very brittle for those stopped after the transient phase. All this indicates 
that the carbon binder, which is responsible for both the dark color and the cohesion of 
the char, is combusted during the transient phase.  
5.1.1.3 Steady phase 
Entering the steady phase, all combustibles in the char has been burned. Thus, given 
that the endothermic reaction is exhausted and the carbon material is combusted, the 
only contribution to the thermal resistance at stage is provided by the white, multicellu-
lar char structure, which, for a short period of time, is able to hinder the transfer of heat 
to the underlying substrate, and thereby keep the increment of the steel temperature 
equal to the increment of the oven temperature. 
5.1.1.4 Decay phase 
At high temperatures, cracks begin to occur in the char structure. The convex shape of 
the RHS profiles are considered to promote the formation of cracks, as this shape de-
mands an extra amount of coating at the corners in order to sustain development of the 
full char thickness as the perimeter of the char structure increases. Ultimately, as the 
extra material is not provided, cracks occur at the surface of the char due to the lack of 
material. The effect of the cracks is seen in the decay phase in, where an increased in-
crement of the steel temperature is observed. The cracks were observed for all experi-

ments driven beyond the initiation of the decay phase. Figure 24 shows the RHS-profile 
sample from experiment O.F-B.b.P1.13 after opening the door to the heating chamber. 
Here, as the sample is 750 °C, the cracks formed in the char structure are clearly indi-
cated by the radiating steel sample. 
 
 
Figure 24. Indication of cracks in the char structure for the sample O.F-B.b.P1.13 
The decay phase is initiated at a temperature around 700 °C and continues until the 
steel temperature equals the oven temperature. This effect occurs at temperatures around 
950 °C and indicates the total loss thermal resistance provided by the intumescent coat-
ing. At this temperature, the color of the char has turned completely white. In this state, 
it is extremely brittle and a single touch or blow can remove parts of the char structure. 
This indicates that all of the carbon binder, which provides the cohesion of the char 
structure, has been combusted.  
The degradation of the performance of the intumescent coating is also described in a 
study from 2011 by Y. Zhang et al. [19] performed using a cone calorimeter. In this 
study, the degradation is found to occur at temperatures above approximately 700 °C.  
5.1.1.5 Austenitization 
Generally, a sudden drop is observed in the measured steel temperature as it reaches 
about 730 °C. This is due to the molecular transformation of steel, which occurs at this 
temperature, as its crystal structure changes from ferrite to the face-centered cubic con-
figuration of iron, also known as austenite. This transformation occurs at the critical 
eutectoid temperature of 730 °C [30]. Since this transformation absorbs heat, the sudden 
drop of temperature located at exactly this steel temperature can be explained by this 
phenomenon. The drop in temperature has an impact in the derivation of the equivalent 
thermal resistance, which is suddenly increased at this point. In Table 4, the steel tem-

By examining the calculated design curves using the different values of the constant 
thermal resistance, it is evident that the temperature range for which this value is calcu-
lated, plays a significant role. This is due to the obvious variations in the equivalent 
thermal resistance that directly relates to the temperature of the intumescent coating as 
described in the aforementioned phases of the functionality of the intumescent coating. 
In the Eurocode [31], the calculation of the constant design value of the thermal re-
sistance is based on the equivalent thermal resistances found for the steel temperature 
interval 350-750 °C. Based on the relation between the steel temperature and the derived 
equivalent thermal resistances presented in the graphs above, this interval should com-
prehend both the transient phase and the steady phase of the intumescent coating. How-
ever, as the inert phase is left out in this interval, using this value could result in an un-
derestimation of the actual steel temperature during this phase. In terms of practical sig-
nificance, this underestimation could induce unexpected thermal dilations in steel ele-
ments due to insufficient thermal insulation. In turn, this effect could lead to collapse of 
the element. This effect is highlighted in a recent study by J. Kolšek et. al [18], where in 
particular the shear connection plate is emphasized as a weak point for this kind of ac-
tions. 
In addition, the indication of elevated reaction temperatures found for decreasing 
heating rates in Figure 25 adds the risk of underestimating the steel temperature during 
the inherent phase of the intumescent coating.   
As seen from Figure 43, both methods of calculating a design value based on the 
equivalent thermal resistances seem to provide good results for the one experiment con-
ducted on an IPE-profile (O.F-I.c.P1.17) instead of the RHS-profiles. This is considered 
to be due to the concave shape of the IPE-profile, which, contrary to the convex shape 
of the RHS-profile, does not force the intumescent char to stretch beyond its limits to 
keep up with the increasing perimeter of the char surface, as the intumescent coating 
expands.  
As described in section 5.1.2, this is suggested to be the reason for the cracks occur-
ring in the char structure at the RHS-profiles and the related rise in the steel temperature 
during the decay phase.  
As no cracks occurred at the IPE-profile, and therefore no extraordinary rise in its 
steel temperature, the applied methods seem to work better for this type of profile.

5.3 Errors 
This section contains a discussion of the susceptibility of the results to random and sys-
tematic errors. Whereas random errors, as the name suggests, are random in nature and 
therefore very difficult to predict and prevent, systematic errors are related to repeating 
mistakes or irregularities in for example the experimental routine. Random errors often 
have a Gaussian distribution, thus, their impact on the results can be assessed through 
statistical analysis. Increasing the amount of data will reduce the influence of random 
errors, as the correct results would then stand out.  
==PAGE:70==
 
58 
5.3.1 Experiments in electric oven setup 
This section provides a discussion of the random and systematic errors associated with 
the experiments conducted in the electric oven setup. 
5.3.1.1 Random errors 
In this study, the first set of experiments, only ten experiments were conducted on coat-
ed profiles. This was due to the large amount of work used on preparing the experi-
mental setup along with the significant amount of time needed to prepare and conduct 
each individual experiment. The low number of repetitions makes the results very sus-
ceptible to random errors. Errors such as delamination of the intumescent coating during 
experiments or areas where the coating did not react are considered the most significant 
random errors in this study. The impact of these random errors is seen by the large 
standard deviation of the calculated thermal resistances within the experiments conduct-
ed for each heating rate, which only highlights the need of more repetitions for this ex-
perimental setup. 
5.3.1.2 Systematic errors 
As seen in Figure 13, the heating rate provided by the electric oven showed no to be 
completely consistent throughout experiments. Only the experiment conducted with a 
“very slow” heating rate showed a regular heating rate during the entire length of the 
experiment. Moreover, even though the same controller setting was applied, the oven 
did not provide the exact same temperature-time curve each time. This error is most 
significant for the “fast” heating rate experiments, and consequently, this affects the 
comparison of the results. 
Furthermore, during the preliminary experiments for this experimental setup, differ-
ences in the oven temperature across width, height, and depth were measured in the or-
der 10-15°C, where the highest temperatures were measured close to the inner surfaces 
and the lowest at the center. An attempt to solve this was done by measuring the oven 
temperature on each side of each tested samples, so that a correct mean temperature 
could be detected. However, the difference in the exposure of intumescent coating on 
the individual sides of the tested samples was not accounted for.  
In this set of experiments, great care was taken in investigating different methods of 
applying the thermocouples so that the correct steel temperature was measured.  
5.3.2 Experiments in mass loss cone setup 
In this section, the errors associated with the mass loss cone experiments are discussed 
in terms of the above described random and systematic errors. 
5.3.2.1 Random errors 
Due to the simplicity of this experimental setup, the results are not as susceptible to ran-
dom errors as for the oven setup. This is partly because of the use of a smaller sample 
==PAGE:71==
59 
 
size, which has no corners. Therefore, the risk of cracking and delamination of the coat-
ing was reduced significantly. The fact that a horizontal orientation is used also reduces 
the risk of the intumescent char collapsing under its own weight.   
5.3.2.2 Systematic errors 
In order to maximize the incident heat flux directed to the coated sample, the standard 
distance between the surface of the sample and the cone heater was kept at 25 mm. 
However, as the intumescent char expands, the surface moves closer to the cone heater, 
and the incident heat flux towards the surface of the intumescent char is increased corre-
spondingly. No solution was found to this problem, however, as proposed another study 
[19] in which intumescent coatings are heated under a mass loss cone, this deviation in 
the incident heat flux can be addressed by measuring the heat flux at different distances 
beforehand. The mentioned study found an approximately linear relationship between 
the distance to the cone heater and the measured heat flux. Therefore, if the height of the 
intumescent char is continuously monitored, this relationship can be used to estimate the 
correct incident heat flux as the intumescent coating expands.  
In addition to the distance between the sample surface and the cone heater, the inci-
dent heat flux is known to decrease slightly towards the perimeter of the sample. There-
fore, it is assumed that the intumescent coating along the edges of the samples was ex-
posed to a slightly smaller heat flux than the center of the samples. As the effect of the 
intumescent coating was assessed by means of the ratio between the measured steel 
temperatures of the unprotected and the protected samples, this effect is considered to 
be negligible

6 CONCLUSION 
Two sets of experiments were conducted in this project. The first set featured 300 mm 
long sections of coated 150 mm x 100 mm x 5 mm RHS-profiles, which were heated in 
an electric oven in order to investigate the influence of the heating rate on the intumes-
cent coating in terms of general behavior and thermal resistance. In the second set, 90 
mm x 100 mm x 5 mm coated steel sheets were heated in a mass loss cone apparatus in 
order to investigate the effect of the intumescent coating, as it is exposed to different 
constant levels of incident heat flux. For the two sample types, which both have section 
factors of 200 m-1, a dry film thickness of approximately 1 mm was applied by a profes-
sional contractor.  
Based on the steel temperature profiles, the derived values of the equivalent thermal 
resistance, and visual observations of the intumescent char, the behavior of the intumes-
cent coating was categorized into four phases. These were qualitatively described and 
related to the chemical and physical behavior of the coating. The four phases can be 
described as follows: I) The inherent phase, in which the coating melts and thereby in-
creases in viscosity without providing any significant restriction for the heat transfer to 
the steel substrate. II) The transient phase, which is initiated by the reaction of the blow-
ing agent (occurring at a steel temperature of approximately 250 °C), which caused the 
coating to expand and form a char structure. This was observed in the equivalent ther-
mal resistance, which increased significantly during this phase. Moreover, the equiva-
lent thermal resistances showed a distinct peak during the transient phase. This was con-
sidered to be due to the exhaustion of the endothermic chemical reaction of the intumes-
cent coating, which is responsible for the intumescence of the char. Consequently, this 
was also identified as the point at which the intumescence process comes to an end. 
Subsequent to this peak, combustion of the carbon binder was considered to be the rea-
son for the change of color from gray/black to white, which was noticed as the experi-
ments were continued beyond the transient phase. III) The steady phase, in which the 
thermal resistance is approximately constant as the reaction of the blowing agent has 
ended, leaving a fully developed char structure. The initiation of the steady phase is 
considered to occur, when the complete combustion of the carbon binder has taken 
place, leaving only a char structure consisting of a white, brittle coating residue. IV) 
The decay phase, in which cracks in the char structure cause the steel temperature to rise 
until the oven temperature is reached.  
Amongst the main findings in this thesis was an indication of correlation between the 
heating rate and the temperature at which the reaction of the intumescent coating was 
==PAGE:73==
61 
 
observed to initiate. More precisely, it was indicated that a decrease in the heating rate 
was found to increase the reaction temperature of the coating. 
Furthermore, the convex shape of the tested RHS-profiles was considered to have an 
effect on the decay phase by causing the char structure to crack. The effect of the profile 
shape was further investigated by substituting the RHS-profile with an IPE-profile 
(which has a concave shape) in an otherwise similar experiment, showing no sign of 
cracks or depletion in the effect of the intumescent coating for the same steel tempera-
ture. This underlined the suggestion that the decay in the effect of the intumescent coat-
ing could be due to cracks caused by the convex shape of the RHS-profile. 
In order to assess the influence of the heating on the thermal resistance of the intu-
mescent coating, two methods of calculating a constant value of the equivalent thermal 
resistance was applied. However, no correlation between the heating rate and the ther-
mal resistance was found. In fact, the thermal resistances calculated according to one of 
the applied methods showed to be almost equal to the value   
                  
 , which was achieved by means of an ISO834 test provided by the manufacturer of 
the coating. This value agrees well with the thermal resistance of three-layer intumes-
cent coatings of 0.086, which was proposed by Pettersson et al. [26].  
Two methods for selecting a constant design value of the thermal resistance were 
used. These were based on the averages of the equivalent thermal resistances throughout 
the entire course of the experiments and within the defined steady phase, respectively. 
Using these two values, the design steel temperatures were calculated using the Euro-
code formula [9] for insulated steel elements. The temperatures calculated with the first 
value showed a general underestimation outside the steel temperature interval 300-
550°C. This agrees well with the findings made in a recent study [23], in which the 
method of equivalent constant thermal resistance was found to agree well with test re-
sults within the range of 400-600°C. Conversely, the temperatures calculated with the 
second value provided a reasonable estimate of the steel temperature over the entire 
length of the experiments except within this interval and after the initiation of the decay 
phase, where the steel temperatures were overestimated, outside this interval, however, 
it overestimated the temperature for the vast majority of the experiments.  
 
Ultimately, the findings in the study emphasizes that care should be taken, when 
implementing intumescent coating systems for structural protection of steel elements. 
This applies to the use of a constant thermal resistance in combination with the Euro-
code formula (15) in general, as the simplicity of this method will does not allow it to 
comprehend the different phases in the functionality of the intumescent coating.  
 In particular, the indication of elevated reaction temperatures for slow heating rates 
along with the underestimation of the steel temperatures for steel temperatures below 
the reaction temperature, should be taken into account when designing intumescent 
coating systems for steel elements that must be efficient at low temperatures.

6.1 Further studies 
In this study, the derivation of the equivalent thermal resistance was based on a total of 
10 experiments. However, as highlighted in the discussion of errors, more experiments 
are needed in order to move past the random errors related to this kind of experiments. 
Based on the experimental methods and routines established in this project, a future 
project should focus on conducting a larger number of experiments and implementing 
only three variations in the heating rate.  
In addition, the findings of this study indicated that the concave shape of an RHS-
profile could induce cracks in the intumescent coating after extensive heating. By con-
ducting an array of experiments in which only the profile type is varied, this effect could 
be highlighted.  
The effects of the fire scenario on the performance of intumescent coatings was high-
lighted in this study. In particular, this study aimed at highlighting the influence of the 
heating rate on the thermal resistance of the intumescent coating, however, different 
types of heating are also considered to cause different behavior of the exposed intumes-
cent coating. Further studies in this field should include a more detailed investigation of 
the influence of the type of heating on the behavior of the intumescent coating. In addi-
tion, as real fires can be very far from these simulated conditions, a third set of experi-
ments should be conducted, in which coated steel samples are exposed to real fire con-
ditions. Such conditions can be simulated in a flashover room (Figure 47) in which 
samples are placed and tested during a simulation of a natural post-flashover fire, using 
different fuel loads in order to achieve different steel temperatures. Moreover, this setup 
could lead to an assessment of the effect of different ventilation conditions and of the 
cooling phase. 
 
 
Figure 47. Flashover room shown from: a) outside, b) inside with controlled gas burner, c) front side, 
showing the controllable ventilation opening. 
In the end of this project, a limited amount of time was found to investigate the re-
sponse of the intumescent coating exposed to different levels of irradiation under the 
mass loss cone. However, as time was not found to fully establish a viable heat transfer 
==PAGE:75==
63 
 
model by which the equivalent thermal resistance could be derived, this remains one of 
the main aspects of research in a possible future study. 
In order to facilitate a more correct and accurate comparison with values achieved in 
an ISO834 test, the collection and presentation of data needs to be performed in the 
same way. This procedure is described in EN13881-8. While the results of the ISO834 
test provided for this study was analyzed using a simplified method, using the method 
reported in EN13881-8 would yield the actual thermal resistance that would be applied 
in the design of intumescent coating systems. Furthermore, this would give a better un-
derstanding of the error introduced when a value of the thermal resistance, which 
achieved through this code, is applied for design applications introducing various heat-
ing rates.

A PRELIMINARY STUDY ON EDGE INSULA-
TION 
Heating of the samples through the edges and from the inside results in a much larger 
section factor than would be the case for a continuous element with a similar cross sec-
tion. This problem is most severe for heating of the RHS-profile, since the IPE profile 
does not have any inner surfaces. Therefore, in order to apply equation (15) for calculat-
ing the protected steel temperature, the RHS-profile samples must be thermally closed 
at the ends. Otherwise, the results would be misleading, as the heating from the inside 
occurs through an unprotected steel surface, which cannot be reflected in a calculation 
of the temperature of protected steel. 
This chapter serves to investigate different methods of insulating the inside and the 
edges. In order to assess the effectivity of the different types of edge insulation, the 
measured temperature curve for unprotected steel is compared with the corresponding 
calculated temperature-time curve using equation (7). Representing a continuous beam, 
this curve is calculated with a section factor of 200 m-1. In comparison, the reference 
sample with fully exposed edges has a section factor of 384 m-1. 
Three different methods were investigated for their ability to prevent heating through 
the ends of the sample. These three methods comprised mineral wool, Gyproc gypsum 
board, and Insulfrax board. In the following the methods are described and the results 
from the conducted tests are presented along with the difference between the calculated 
ideal temperature-time curves. Table 7 shows the tests conducted during this range of 
experiments along with the type of edge insulation applied and whether or not a refer-
ence sample was tested alongside with the edge insulated sample.

Discussion of results 
The ability of each of the three proposed methods of thermally closing the ends of the 
RHS-profile samples are assessed by means of the difference between the calculated 
ideal temperature-time curve, which uses the ideal section factor of 200 m-1, and the 
measured steel temperature of the tested sample. 
The graphs show the absolute difference between the ideal and measured tempera-
ture-time curves, and the large differences in the beginning of the experiments are ne-
glected, as they are caused by dividing with a low temperature in the calculation of the 
difference. By examining the graphs, it is observed that a small difference of approxi-
mately 2 % is achieved by implementing method b as the edge insulation.  On the other 
hand, method a and c both resulted in differences of approximately 5 %. 
On this background, method b is chosen for implementation in the experimental set-
up in the electric oven.

ON THE ISO834 STANDARD FIRE CURVE 
In order to investigate, whether or not results achieved through tests with the standard 
curve are applicable to heat exposures with temperature-time relations with far lower 
heating rate, reference to tests made with the standard fire curve (ISO834) must be 
made. However, the ISO834 heating curve has a very fast heating rate, in particular in 
its initial 30 minutes after which the temperature reaches 842°C. The average heating 
rate for these first 30 minutes approaches                      . Since the oven 
available for this study does not sustain a heating rate higher than 8.47      , replicat-
ing the ISO834 fire curve was impossible. 
However, the manufacturer of the tested coating product has provided this project 
with test data from an ISO834 test, allowing the comparison of the equivalent thermal 
resistance to be made.

times to which the average steel temperatures reach certain values. Additionally, a num-
ber of combinations of various steel sections and DFTs has been tested. To be able to 
use the results as reference to the low heating rate experiments conducted in this study, 
the test with the closest possible section factor and DFT was chosen as reference. The 
section factor of tested sample is 212 m-1 and the DFT is 1.243 mm. The ISO834 test
