Abstract
Electric vehicle (EV) market is accelerating its expansion due to emerging road decarbonization
demands. Decreasing renewable energy generation (REG) prices boost such trend. Intended
for converter number reduction, DC network is advancing in EV charging field. Considering
factors mentioned above, it is well established that a large­scale DC­based REG­integrated EV
charging infrastructure system (EV­CIS) will be in urgent need. This thesis project aims to figure
out stabilization strategies in DC­based EV­CIS to ensure its future smooth operation. Specially,
it investigates identification mechanism in attacking scenarios and regulation equipment under
normal power variation.
In identification part, DC­based EV­CIS is simplified as a distributive DC network, where each
DC/DC converter is taken as an independent node for energy exchanging. To validate proposed
identification methods, including DCM formulation, impulse current injection, ripple correlation
and oscillation correlation, both a 800V simulated network using MATLAB composed of four
Buck­Boost converters and a 120V practical network composed of three Boost converters us­
ing dSPACE MicroLabBox have been built and tested, no matter under self sensor attack or
neighbour attack. The results imply the effectiveness of these strategies.
Without detected attacks, oscillation manipulation is targeted to meet regulation requirements.
A double­port DC/DC converter was proposed and investigated, which owns a wide operation
region and quick duty ratio calculation for EV charging. Both local batteries and EV batteries are
integrated to EV­CIS through this converter, playing the role of power regulator and EV charger
simultaneously. A 800V simulated case using PLECS and a 30V practical converter were tested
to confirm the validity of such topology.
These results suggest that EV­CIS stability is able to be achieved through cycling control loop
from identification to manipulation. On this basis, the proposed attack identification mechanism
should be adopted in future DC­based EV­CIS, and novel multiport converters are expected to
be deployed for better systematic performance.

Introduction
Considering boosted growth of global electrical vehicle (EV) market, which has accounted for
2.6% of car sales and about 1% of car stock in 2019 [1], large­scale EV charging infrastructure
system should be established and prepared to support huge charging demands in future. Simul­
taneously, in order for carbon neutralization targets [2], [3], increasing penetration of renewable
energy generation (REG) in power system is clearly foreseen and inevitable, and decreasing
REG installation prices accelerate this process [4]–[8].
Therefore, with potential abundant renewable energy sources integrated into power grids, EV
is regarded as a disruptive alternative for conventional vehicles to consume electrical power
instead and get rid of high reliance on fossil fuel, eventually decarbonizing road transportation.
Charging infrastructure plays a pivotal role prospectively to consolidate EVs and REGs. Com­
bining current situation and future tendency discussed above, more investigations on emerging
EV charging infrastructure systems(EV­CIS) with REG integration should be carried out to en­
sure its future smooth and reliable operation.
In this thesis, voltage oscillation problem is mainly discussed in EV­CIS scenarios, since voltage
distribution is commonly considered a critical index to evaluate systematic stableness in power
grids. Corresponding stabilization strategies are exploited as well to meet specific grid code in
EV­CIS.
1.1
Thesis scope
1.1.1
Voltage oscillation and energy storage
Due to intermittent nature of REG and charging demands, periodic power unbalancing would be
an universal challenge for EV­CIS with REG integration. Regarding solar energy, even its long­
term output profile tends to be statistically stable and quantitatively superior related to future
climate variability, it still suffers uncertainties and prediction errors [9], [10]. Short­term fluctua­
tion problem caused by daily irradiation intermittency worsens the situation, since a higher daily
average irradiation and a nearly constant distribution indicates a wider daily fluctuation range
with same installed photovoltaics(PV) capacity [11]. Similar issues regarding output volatility
and forecast difficulties can be also found in wind power generation [12]–[15]. From the per­
spective of EV charging demand, it highly depends on user behaviour and imposes a seriously
negative impact as well on EV­CIS stability through peak charging power during working hours
[16], [17]. With potentially up to 350kW for single EV charging in future, it would propose a
stronger peak power demand to EV­CIS [18]. Such power generation and consumption gyra­
tions are ineluctable, implying that short­term energy regulation is mandatory except frequent
active load shedding or generation curtailment is acceptable.
Compared to spontaneous power imbalance, which can be anticipated during system planning
stage, forced power imbalance attributed to intentional attacks would exhaust regulation capa­
bility rapidly and then exacerbate the instability of whole system constantly, eventually leading
to excessively low or high grid voltage and even triggering a black­out . Relative real cases have
happened or been implemented recently [19]–[21]. It is essential to discuss on such phenom­
ena in EV­CIS as well, not only limited in conventional power grids. Unlike power plants and
substations, EV has a stronger connection to civilians but weaker relay protectors restricted
by its own size and manufacture cost, although that its charging voltage can be even higher
than grid voltage[18], [22]–[24]. Even compensated by converter modulation, there would ex­
ist a problem of charging voltage oscillation under unstable grid voltage. Lower voltage than

No matter the source of power imbalance or the form of energy distribution (either AC or DC), a

power converters are mandatory for their connection to power grid.
To summarize, in view of voltage oscillations caused by power imbalance, one effective solu­
tion is extra local BESS together with switching converters to enhance systematic regulation
capability in most scenarios. In later parts of this thesis, it is assumed that local BESS is an
essential part of EV­CIS, which regulate power fluctuation and releases peak power pressure.

1.1.3
Oscillation identification and manipulation
Since DC­based EV­CIS with REG and local BESS integration has been selected as discussed
above, the oscillation problem only happens in the form of unstable voltage magnitude. More­
over, dynamic oscillation origins propose a requirement of various corresponding suppression
strategies. Generally, spontaneous power imbalance should be regulated by BESS while forced
power imbalance has to be removed as soon as it has been detected.
There are opportunities to identify observed oscillations first through relative measurement data,
eventually providing a reference for next­step action [37], [38]. While under simple signal shield­
ing attacks, those measurement data become inaccessible and only conventional passive relay
protection can be relied on. Active authentication mechanism is expected to improve the relia­
bility and efficiency of attack identification process, especially in some extreme scenarios where
most sensors are lost or untrustworthy. An universal authentication protocol should be followed
by all instruments before system initialization. Once that measured oscillations are identified as
intentional attacks and relative attackers are distinguished, external inference on attackers and
active disconnection from neighbor nodes are recommended.
Regarding those oscillations identified innocent, regular local BESS and EV chargers should
work. Moreover, under attacking scenarios, residual oscillations are mandatory to be mitigated
as well among remaining innocent equipment[39], [40]. Considering switching nature and phys­
ical limits of converters, these oscillations can be manipulated according to specific regulation
rules, instead of being eliminated entirely. In order for operation cost minimization, related
parameter of manipulation strategy can be investigated and optimized. Meanwhile, the refor­
mulated DC­link voltage still has to follow a certain grid code in EV­CIS.
Through the ”identification­manipulation” loop, the DC­based EV­CIS is expected immune to
grid attack and keep in stable and optimal operation, with lower BESS capacity and less depen­
dency on external grid support. The target of research conducted in this thesis is to investigate
concrete regulation procedures under such framework.
1.2
Thesis objective
1.2.1
Oscillation identification
The oscillation identification focuses on attack detection and attacker searching. In this thesis,
several prerequisites on investigated EV­CIS are given as listed for simplification:
• The DC­based EV­CIS can be equivalent to a converter­based DC distributive network,
where most received messages should be authenticated before adoption. Each converter
works independently as a agent, no matter it inject or absorb energy during operation;
• The system information is shared and accessible for all converters while all information
is untrustworthy until authentication. All converter controllers should reach and follow a
consensus through distributive communication;
• The converter controller is open­loop, which means the duty ratio of driving signals is
constant until its input reference variable changes;
• The measurement sampling frequency is similar to switching frequency, indicating there
is no possibility of duty ratio measurement in several switching periods.
In such a simplified distributive DC network, if attacks happen, a complete attack detector and
identifier would be initiated quickly through an attacking­event trigger. After specific identification
procedures, not only can the existence of attacks be confirmed, but also would the source of
attacks be found. The objective of oscillation identification is to avoid long­term existence of
forced power imbalance, in order to ensure long­term systematic stability.

1.2.2
Oscillation manipulation
Faced with natural intermittent power imbalance, both local BESS and EV batteries should
utilize their regulation capability by adjusting their charging or discharging power. From the
perspective of upper­level systematic controller, commonly an optimization problem would be
formulated and solved. In a certain period, whose length is determined by optimization step
size, each converter’s objective output power is assumed constant.
Intended for switcher number reduction, multiport converter is adopted, especially considering
natural demands on simultaneous charging or discharging of several batteries at EV­CIS. In
this thesis, a dual­portal DC/DC converter is proposed and investigated. Compared with con­
ventional single­input multi­output(SIMO) converters, relative duty ratio calculation needs to be
rapid while each portal can keep a wide operation range. Through determined charging or
discharging power, the working status of such converter should be constant as well.
The objective of ripple manipulation is to shave short­term oscillations economically. The rea­
son of this nomination rather than ”Oscillation shaving” or ”Oscillation elimination” is that pure
oscillation suppression is expensive and meaningless due to widespread existence of ground
capacitance and slow converter status transition. There exist a more economical scheme to ma­
nipulate oscillations, contributing to lower operation cost, less dependency on BESS capacity
and potentially lower EMI distribution.
1.3
Thesis outline
This thesis discusses voltage stability strategies in various time scales in DC­based EV­CIS.
Chapter. 2 discusses on attack identification mechanism detailedly, which is also verified by
both simulation and hardware experiments. Afterwards, a novel dual­port DC/DC converter is
proposed and analyzed in Chapter. 3, intended to regulate spontaneous power imbalance. A
comprehensive controlling strategy for system stabilization is given in Chapter. 4, where relative
weaknesses are explained as well. Chapter 5 summarizes this work and draws the conclusion.
Previous manuscripts written by the author himself during master education are the main ref­
erence source of this thesis [41], [42]. For further explanation, oscillation identification part has
been fully developed during the author’s master thesis project, and a relative paper manuscript
has been preprinted in Jan, 2021 [41]. The preprinted version will be polished and modified

ing their permission as well. Regarding oscillation manipulation part, [42] were finished in July,

2
Oscillation identification
2.1
State of the art
Considering distributive communication in EV­CIS to guide its physical behaviour, its security
and stabilization problem can be firstly taken as similar issues in a specific cyber­physical sys­
tem (CPS). A brief survey on attack detection for CPS has been conducted in [43], including
central controller and distributive controller scenarios in general CPS. Focusing on vulnerability
analysis, [44] summarizes weak points in various layers at CPS and introduces several attacking
examples, providing a reference for future CPS designer.
All works above discusses general methodology in cyber­physical system, while specific prop­
erties and requirements on power system should be taken into consideration as well. A model­
based physical attack detection strategy for generation control has been proposed in [45], and
intrusion detection in distributed frequency control for microgrid was investigated in [46]. Be­
sides, several model­free methods were discussed to detect false data injection attack in power
grid as well, for instance sparse optimization, wavelet transform and deep neural networks[47],
[48]. All detection methods above are intended for AC grid. Specific physical properties of DC
microgrid should be taken into consideration additionally.
For an emerging converter­dominated DC microgrid, signal temporal logic formalism can be
employed to detect attacks and evaluate relative impacts on system stability[49], while it cannot
help identify the attack source. In [50], a stealth cyber­attack detection strategy using cooper­
ative vulnerability factors was proposed, while based on a hypothesis that neighbours’ output
current signals are accessible and trustworthy. A distributed cyber­attack detection scheme
using a Luenberger observer has been investigated in [51], while it still relies on two trustworthy
nodes. All strategies above cannot realize attack source identification merely depending on self
data utilization. The motivation of the present work in this paper is to address this challenge.
2.2
Preliminaries on attacks in DC-based EV-CIS
2.2.1
Attack classification
The DC­based EV­CIS is dealt with as a DC microgrid for modeling simplification. The main
objective of grid attack is to lead to a substantial gap between measurement signals and reality,
which means some attacked nodes are equivalently blind to malicious power flow. It would
result in massive forced power imbalance, dragging grid to another status or triggering relay
protectors. The attackers would grab illegal profits from energy theft system failure in the end.
Particularly in EV­CIS, some rule breakers can fully charge its EV gratuitously with deceptive
message distribution, if there is no robust detection mechanism to figure out those attackers.
Such circumstance is unacceptable for both network particaipants and system operators. For
future designed detection strategy diversity, grid attack can be categorized into three patterns,
as shown in Fig. 2.1, including signal shielding, signal substitution and agent instigation.
For each independent converter,the shielded or intentionally modified signals should be ne­
glected actively by receivers, while those substituted data can be difficult to distinguish. Faced
with an instigated agent, who should be treated as an evil node directly, all innocent neighbour
nodes should drop all information received from this bad converter, and isolate it until external
intervention. However, when Sybil attack is confirmed, the attacked node needs to disconnect
with all external DC links initiatively instead of further identification procedures, in order to save
computation sources and minimize attacking impacts[41].

Signal shielding
Sensor failure, cyber­link disconnection and cyber­link jamming are typical
attacking modes to block important information for relative nodes. Equivalently blind to network
status information, it is highly possible for a single node to lose physical synchronization with
neighbours if some oscillations happens without any notification. For further illustration, accord­
ing to communication protocol vulnerability analysis, the jamming attack can be implemented
through denial of service (DoS) and selective packet delay[52]. Since cyber­link jamming may
occur sometimes in normal daily operation, especially when system communication source is
limited, the jamming attack would should avoid being designed to directly call for external in­
tervene even it has been distinguished. Nonetheless, direct equipment damage is taken as an
opposite case. Considering physical destruction on sensors or communication links is expen­
sive and risky for attackers, cyber­link jamming is a suitable choice for signal shielding attack.
Mere jamming attack is difficult to influence local stability, hence it is commonly combined with
other attack options and comes efficient and powerful.
Signal substitution
Sensor deception and message substitution are designed for signal sub­
stitution attack, while the latter one can be categorized into package manipulation and repeti­
tion, both of which are difficult to realize considering mature message authorization mechanism.
However, combined with signal shielding attack, only a few cyber­links and sensors need to be
focused since some specific nodes have no choice but to utilize these messages from attacked
components, which are still assumed innocent at first. The attacking efficacy of such combina­
tion can be high even the total attacking cost are still limited.
Agent instigation
Byzantine attack and Sybil attack are classified in accordance with the
magnitude of instigated agents[53], [54]. Unlike instigating a limited number of nodes in Byzan­
tine attack, Sybil attack indicates a high penetration ratio of traitors. These evil nodes will try
to distribute deceptive information and adopt aggressive physical behaviour to realize a certain
attacking target. For further explanation, an instigated node usually claims a wrong voltage
level, and its neighbour nodes would drop or raise their output voltage accordingly to meet the
previous power­sharing consensus. Afterwards, under biased voltage from evil nodes, exces­
sive power will flow between the attacker and those neighbour nodes, finally destabilizing other
nodes and triggering a systematic breakdown. There also exist possibilities of combined attack
using all three attacking modes above, imposing severer impacts on network.

2.4.2
Identification procedures
As discussed above, a coordinated attack with signal shielding and false message injection
seems more efficient for attackers, since it can generate stealth unmatched power flow for a
long period. The proposed detection flow chart is illustrated in Fig. 2.8. The attack detection
is implemented personally by the node whose detector is triggered, and its own sensors and
neighbors should coordinate with him to avoid wrong judgment and further grievance proce­
dures. Before commands sent out, the detector should broadcast its future action to avoid
triggering other detectors, and start polling its neighbours to check whether examination period
is available. Such polling mechanism should be designed and followed by all nodes, based
on an universal communication protocol for instance IEC 61850 and scalable MAC Protocol
(SC­MP) [55], [56].
The only problem of such consensus is that an evil node would send coordination requests
maliciously to its neighbor nodes, finally jamming action space of other nodes. Actually, trust
mechanism for distributive network is still an open question[57], and in this paper, trust level
on neighbors’ cooperation request relies on action space redundancy, and limited request fre­
quency is considered an effective solution, even attack responding speed would be influenced.
In accordance with detection flow chart in Fig, 2.8, as self sensor examination is always in
advance of agent examination, rapid­response­required part is put afterward, violating original
intentions of strategy choice. For industrial application, the order of examination should be
exchanged, or let self sensor check be a routine before detection triggered. The procedure order
in Fig. 2.8 is only designed for REG­integrated microgrid, where internal regulating capacity is
enough and a quantity of nodes are naturally stochastic and unmanageable generation units,
indicating that sensor attack in this network is more cheap and frequent.
If no attacker is identified in previous two procedures, node fault should be checked first. How­
ever, relay protection is commonly integrated and operates immediately when node fault hap­
pens. The step of node fault is illustrated by dashed line in Fig. 2.8, meaning that it would be
dropped in most industrial application considering existence of relay protection. While it may
be kept if some minor faults won’t trigger relay protector and still needs to be paid attention, for
instance surging current generated by EV charging while neglected by low­sampling­freqeuncy
sensors. If no attack or fault happen, the network parameter data Y and C should be calibrated
based on impedance modeling methods [58].

3
Oscillation manipulation
In this chapter, a double­portal converter is proposed and investigated, trying to manipulate
voltage oscillations on DC­link for economical and stable EV­CIS operation [42].
3.1
State of the art
DC­based EV­CIS suffers DC­link voltage oscillations caused by power imbalance. A few rel­
ative works have been reported. Regarding an unidirectional converter, in [59], an isolated
snubber­integrated SEPIC converter was employed and exploited, and it concluded that there
exists possibilities of input current ripple suppression utilizing a specific auxiliary power unit.
Meanwhile, extraordinary switchers and capacitors increase deployment cost and weaken sys­
tematic reliability. a DC/DC converter owns a high step­up coefficient was proposed and investi­
gated to achieve minimized output voltage oscillation, which is equivalent to employing an extra
inductor for voltage signal stabilization [60]. Such strategy is still constrained by indutor current
tolerance. Another resonant dual flyback converter was introduced, not only to realize peri­
odic zero voltage point for soft switching, bust also to minimize current oscillations, eventually
shaving DC­link voltage ripples [61].
Bidirectional converters naturally suitable for BESS integration have also been exploited, search­
ing for possible schemes fort to realize voltage oscillation suppression. A virtual infinite capacitor
was formulated and imported by utilizing an extra buck­boost converter to eliminate DC­link rip­
ple. However, it have not considered the capacitor voltage limit integrated in auxiliary converter
similarly, which means its operation range and employment performance highly depends on this
capacitor, and capacitor parameter variation would impose a severe impact on the whole circuit
performance [62]. Taking an active buck­boost regulator as external regulation equipment, the
theoretical minimum capacitance was analyzed and calculated or low­frequency ripple elimina­
tion [63]. However, such buck­boost topology may suffer from capacitor uncertainties as well.
In [64], equivalent resonant current paths have been introduced in a two­stage bidirectional con­
verter, alleviating current variations in a switching period, and it does not suit BESS integration
due to complicated charging or discharging profile.
Even voltage oscillations are clearly undesirable in EV­CIS due to risks in grid code violation,
while the attitude on voltage ripples over batteries should be discussed further. Actually, the
influence of voltage ripples on battery service life is still an open question. Several classic charg­
ing modes have been mentioned in [65], including pulse charging mode and multistage constant
current­constant voltage(MCC­CV) mode, both of which is illustrated in Fig. 3.1. According to a
practical test on lithium­ion battery, these two charging mode is proven to help release battery
aging pressure. Additionally, lower impedance rising rate can be achieved by pulse charging
mode, especially compared to MCC­CV mode. [66], [67] emphasized that pulse charging in high
frequency imposes a positive impact on battery service life extension. Therefore, the converter
should be designed by taking these factors into consideration as well.

4
Comprehensive analysis
In a DC­based EV­CIS, a merged control flow composed of circulating ”oscillation­manipulation”
procedures is recommended. After recognizing the category of voltage oscillations cased by
power imbalance, corresponding stabilization strategies should be employed. If it belongs to
abnormal oscillations under intentional attacks, the attacking source could be found through
proposed identification procedures, then recognized attackers would be removed from EV­CIS
through neighbours’ active disconnection. Without attacks detected, those oscillations are as­
sumed spontaneous and should be regulated following specific rules. Regarding a determined
regulation command, a novel double­port converter is introduced to achieve better regulation
performance. DC­link voltage would be able to keep stable through such merge control flow.
However, such controlling method still suffer several inherent drawbacks. Regarding the part of
oscillation identification, the weakness are listed as
• The proposed work flow is only suitable for open­loop application, which means each
switchers own a constant duty ratio for a fixed period. Meanwhile, internal PID controller
as a lower­level controller is widespread utilized in most industrial scenarios.
• Under an attacking scenario, the polling mechanism actually imports oscillations actively,
which may worsen the situation and accelerate system splitting up.
• DCM status estimation is only suitable for unidirectional converters, and Impulse current
injection response can be difficult to observe in low­power scenarios.
• The ripple estimation and measurement can be difficult to deploy due to incomplete sys­
tem information, limited sampling frequency and accuracy. Phase­shift technology can
be employed to compensate for sampling frequency while improved sampling accuracy
usually indicates much higher deployment cost.
• The oscillation estimation is out of service if local load resistance is not small enough.
After identification stage, the oscillation manipulation would be implemented, while it still suffers
some shortage as discussed below.
• In order to ensure only one switcher is conducting during each stage, at least four MOS­
FET should be employed, leading to same MOST numbers if two parallel Buck­Boost
converters are employed.
• The ripple manipulator works in an open loop, potentially leading to transient peak voltage
and violating grid code.

5
Conclusion
In this thesis, both spontaneous and forced power imbalance have been investigated and rela­
tive regulation strategies are proposed. Through corresponding simulation and hardware tests,
oscillation identification and manipulation in DC­based EV­CIS are proved feasible. A stable
voltage distribution in network is expected under such control flow.
More polishing works should be conducted in future especially on a practical double­portal con­
verter following proposed basic operation principles. At the same time, lower­level PID con­
troller should be integrated into each converter, and oscillation identification strategies should
be updated correspondingly. Moreover, detailed optimization problem for oscillation manipula­
tion should be formulated based on proposed topology, including EMI reduction and operation
loss minimization.
