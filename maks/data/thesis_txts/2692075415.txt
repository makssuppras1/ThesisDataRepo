Abstract
Brain maturation is a protracted and complex process, and understanding it provide an im-
portant context from which illness or injury can be studied. Part of maturation takes place
in late childhood and adolescence, which is thought to be a key period in brain rewiring.
Brain maturation in children and adolescents has been studied for several decades on a group
level. However, it has yet to be discovered if and how individuals display unique matura-
tional behavior.
The goal of this thesis was to characterize individual diﬀerences in the maturational trajec-
tories of white matter tracts and subcortical brain structures. Generalized Additive Models
(GAM), Generalized Additive Mixed Models (GAMM), and Gaussian Processes (GP) were
applied to longitudinal acquired diﬀusion weighted magnetic resonance imaging data, car-
rying information about the underlying brain microstructure. Seventy-six subjects, 48 girls
and 28 boys in the age range from 7.5 to 18.9 years were included, with four to eleven as-
sessments per individual creating a data set of 678 scans. The 17 distinct brain regions used
in the investigation included 11 major white matter tracts and 6 subcortical gray matter
structures. To quantify maturation, the common diﬀusion measures fractional anisotropy
(FA) and mean diﬀusivity (MD) were used.
Density plots were created displaying data distribution of FA and MD in diﬀerent brain
regions at a range of ages. They were based on Gaussian mixtures of individual trajectories
modelled using GP and conﬁrmed expected FA increases and MD decreases with age. These
increases and decreases were also found in the average trajectories modelled using GAMM.
Results veriﬁed maturational trends reported in the literature.
A correlation analysis conﬁrmed an interrelationship between some data obtained from the
same structures in the left and right hemisphere of the brain.
Furthermore, some other
high correlations between structures could be explained by the sharing of the same voxels
as separation of anatomically proximal structures were not always possible. Other high cor-
relations might represent biological variability, which is an interesting future research avenue.
The ﬁtting of GAM suggested that individuals mostly display linear deviations from sex-
speciﬁc average trajectories. Furthermore, the ﬁtting of GAMMs indicated that the indi-
vidual diﬀerences primarily were due to diﬀerences in intercept and that individual linear
slopes deviated only very slightly from the average trajectory. Thus, the results suggest that
early life factors may be more important to observed FA and MD, than factors at play in
the investigated age interval, as development largely occur in parallel.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Motivation
Late childhood and adolescence are linked to overall brain maturational progress as well
signiﬁcant behavioral, emotional, and cognitive changes. It is considered a key period of
brain rewiring, and this developmental process is complex and protracted. The structural
changes are subtle but eﬃciently captured with modern neuroimaging methods such as dif-
fusion weighted imaging (DWI) that uses water molecule diﬀusion to quantify aspects of the
local neural microstructure [14] [34].
One goal of understanding healthy brain maturation through neuroimaging is to provide
important context to study injury or disease [34]. Knowledge about typical brain develop-
ment constitutes a baseline from which divergent maturational patterns can be compared
and investigated [36]. Adolescence is a period of particular interest, as most psychiatric
disorders emerge during this period, but a lot still needs to be understood about the neu-
robiology in the brain and its possible relationship to the emergence of psychopathology
[13] [17]. However, this project seeks to move beyond the typical characterization of brain
maturation by determining individual diﬀerences in brain development. The long term goal
of understanding individual diﬀerences is determine why some individuals are diﬀerent in
their personalities, behvior, cognition, ect.
Most studies investigating brain maturation are cross-sectional, meaning they only capture
relationships between diﬀusion measures and age across a population of individuals. The
few longitudinal studies that exist only obtained a few assessments per individual, which
makes them ill-posed to capture the within-subject changes given the high inter-subject
variability and to characterize potential non-linear individual maturational trajectories [5]
[25]. In comparison, the data used in this thesis had an unprecedented time resolution with
up to 11 assessments per subject acquired over a period of 6 years. The average number
of scans per individual was 8.9 which uniquely allows for more advanced and data-driven
modelling, from which it is possible to gain better insight into the individual maturation.
2
==PAGE:10==
Aim
Two major aims of this project was to:
• Characterizing individual diﬀerences in the maturational trajectories of major white
matter (WM) ﬁbre tracts and subcortical grey matter (GM) nuclei.
• Explore and apply a method that can characterize temporal patterns of brain matura-
tion.
The project will consider temporal changes in FA and MD by generation of group trajectories
estimated with GAMMs and annualized rate of change curves. Furthermore, GPs will be
utilized to quantify the density of data found within the investigated age range, and charac-
terization of individual diﬀerences in FA and MD will be carried out by ﬁtting of GAMs and
GAMMs. Lastly, coherence in temporal development between diﬀerent brain regions will be
investigated using a correlation analysis.
Outline
The thesis starts with a background chapter on brain neuroanatomy and the current knowl-
edge about brain maturation. Furthermore, it introduces the technique used for data acqui-
sition, namely, diﬀusion weighted imaging (DWI) as well as diﬀusion tensor imaging (DTI).
Diﬀusion and measures used in the current project to quantify it are explained along with
microstructural inﬂuences on brain diﬀusion behavior.
In Chapter 3 the data set is considered containing the demographic information as well as
preprocessing steps carried out to format the data into measures used in the modelling. Fur-
thermore, the steps carried out during the data cleaning process are explained.
The following chapter on methods summarizes the theory behind the modelling approaches
used: generalized additive modelling (GAM), generalized additive mixed modelling (GAMM),
and Gaussian processes (GP). It then goes on to describe the implementations of all analyses
carried out.
Chapter 5 presents and describes the results, which are discussed in chapter 6, along with
limitations of the data, the methods used, and potential future analysis work.
Finally, a conclusion summarizes the main ﬁndings and conclusions of the project.

Chapter 2
Background
This section describes the basic aspects of the brain microanatomy and the changes that
happen during part of its maturation process in childhood and adolescence. Furthermore,
techniques used to quantify water diﬀusion in brain tissues will be explained, as well as how
diﬀusion patterns are related to the brain’s microarchitecture.
2.1
The Brain and its Development
2.1.1
Neuroanatomy
On a microscopic level, the brain consists of two main types of cells, neurons, being the
information carrying and processing cells and neuroglia, functioning as supportive cells [24].
Neurons are arranged in a network of connections facilitating complex processes such as
thoughts, sensations, feelings, actions, response to stimuli, integration of information and
motor and neuroendocrine responses [16]. Neurons consist of three main components; The
cell body (soma), a long extension called an axon, and shorter extensions denoted dendrites.
The latter two being functional processes from the soma as visualized in Figure 2.1. The cell
body contains essential life-sustaining organelles, such as the nucleus, endoplasmic reticulum,
ribosomes, Golgi apparatus, mitochondria, and other lifesustaining structures. The axons
function as the main conductors in which electrical signals, also called action potentials,
travel to new locations connecting with diﬀerent neurons. The axon of one neuron and a
dendrite of another neuron synapse with one another to establish a chemical communication
between the two cells using neurotransmitters.
Neuroglia constitute the majority of cells in the central nervous system (CNS) and comprise
several supportive functions, including protection and metabolic regulation for normal neural
function. There are several types, including astrocytes, microglia, and oligodendrocytes, of
which the latter is of main importance in this thesis. Oligodendrocytes have several processes
that reach out and wrap around axons at multiple locations resulting in myelinated segments
as portrait in Figure 2.1 [24]. Myelin acts as an insulator that prevents ion movement across
the membrane of the axon in the regions covered.
As a consequence, this increases the
conduction velocity of the action potentials, as this electric signal can make leaps along the
traveling direction [26].
4
==PAGE:12==
Figure 2.1: Illustration of a neuron and oligodendorcytes myelinating its axon. [40]
Macroscopically, the tissue in the nervous system can be divided into two types, namely
WM and GM corresponding to their histological colors. Continues changes in the volumes
and diﬀusion behavior in either is indicative of the maturational process taking place and is
therefore of interest. GM, that mainly consists of cell bodies and their dendrites, have very
little myelin. GM is primarily found in the outermost layer of the brain called the cortex as
well as in the deeper lying grey matter structures, i.e., the subcortical structures, such as
the thalamus, basal ganglia, hippocampus and amygdala [26] [36].
WM, on the other hand, mainly consists of axons and oligodendrocytes, and the lipid-rich
myelin is the cause of its white appearance. These myelinated axons form ﬁber tracts in
which axons extend between and connect diﬀerent brain regions. The majority of axons
in the ﬁber tracts lie parallel to one another but can also fan out or do multiple crossings
[42]. The WM tracts are categorized into three types according to their connectivity; projec-
tion ﬁbers, association ﬁbers, and commissural ﬁbers. Projection ﬁbers connect the cerebral
cortex with deeper and more central structures such as the deep gray nuclei, brainstem,
cerebellum, and spinal cord. Association tracts connect diﬀerent areas of the cortex within
the same hemisphere. Finally, commissural tracts connect similar cortical areas of the two
hemisphere, with each other [20].

2.1.2
Brain maturation in children and adolescents
Human brain development is a complex and protracted process. Although the main struc-
ture of the brain is in place at birth and 90% of its volume is achieved at age ﬁve, signiﬁcant
rewiring and restructuring occur well into adulthood. Childhood and adolescence are periods
of profound behavioral, emotional, hormonal, and cognitive changes, which are inherently
linked to the developing brain structures. Technology at the time of early brain maturation
research have historically been limited to histological investigation on postmortem brains.
This provided some key insight into typical brain structure and maturation, but as children
and adolescents are generally healthy, pediatric post mortem brains are hard to come by, and
longitudinal data collection was not possible. However, neuroimaging technology have made
in-vivo investigations of the human brain possible, and is capable of revealing longitudinal
development, and remodeling throughout childhood, adolescence, and adulthood, which are
results of functional and structural changes [36].
Conventional MRI only provides access to the macrostructural architecture of the brain by
means of T1, T2, and FLAIR sequences. However, with diﬀusion weighted imaging (DWI),
which is an MRI technique using a specialized sequence, it is possible to obtain information
about the microstructure in the brain. Furthermore, DWI, and diﬀusion tensor imaging
(DTI) in combination with trajectography, allows mapping of neuroanatomical architecture
such as white ﬁber tracts [20]. The details of these techniques will be covered in Section 2.3.
Structural Changes
Brain maturation is a dynamic protracted process characterized by both progressive and
regressive alterations inﬂuenced by complex genetic factors and experience-dependent plas-
ticity due to environmental factors [36]. Structural changes result from of a complex balance
between several important processes taking place in the neural tissue, including the few
described in the following sentences: Firstly, neurons undergo dendritic branching forming
new synapses between neurons, thus making way of new signal paths within the brain, a
progressive process called synaptogenesis. Secondly, synaptic pruning is the regressive pro-
cess of eliminating existing synapses to reﬁne the brain network [36]. Lastly, myelination
contributes to the maintenance of axonal integrity, neural survival and inﬂuences neural size
and axonal diameter, thus reinforcing existing pathways and increasing signaling velocity
within. [16] The overall structure and the major white matter tracts emerge during the
fetal period and is thus in place at birth. During infancy and early childhood, peak brain
growth occurs, with the majority of synaptogenesis taking place during the ﬁrst few years of
life. Continued myelination as well as structure reﬁnement and remodeling through synaptic
pruning continues throughout both childhood and adolescence, which becomes evident as
gray and white matter changes [29].
The overall volume of gray matter in the brain during a lifetime follows an inverted U-
shape, with an increase in early childhood and early peak followed by a reduction in late
childhood and adolescence [4] [11].
White matter volume, on the other hand, has been
observed to steadily increase roughly linearly throughout the same period. Furthermore, the
white matter increase is consistent with the gray matter decrease with age. It has thus been
hypothesized that continuous myelination of existing gray matter transforms it into white
matter as maturation precedes. Part of the gray matter volume decrease becomes evident
as a visible spread of myelin outwards into the cortex, a process denoted apparent cortical

Brain Investigation using diﬀusion MRI
The structural changes taking place during the ﬁrst few years of life are prominent enough
to be visible on structural MRI scans. However, the changes occurring after this period are
more subtle, but DWI is sensitive to the changes occruing during childhood and adolescence
[16] [17]. FA and MD are commonly used parameters in this line of research quantifying
the degree of directionality of diﬀusion and the size of overall diﬀusion. Both measures are
indicative of the underlying microstructure of the tissue, and a more detailed account is
given in Section 2.3.3 [34]. As local FA and MD are not directly associated with the volume
of the brain region, these measures likely reﬂect microstructural alteration and maturation,
rather than just gross anatomy changes [17]. Generally, DWI is to a large extent associated
with the characterization of WM, and the studies using DWI on WM outnumber the ones on
GM ﬁve-fold [31]. The neurobiological mechanisms causing variations in diﬀusion measures
in GM in healthy individuals are poorly understood. It is known that cellular structures and
membranes, in particular, hinder water diﬀusion. Thus variation in diﬀusion measures could
be due to variation in membrane density, tortuosity and/or permeability [18]. However, DWI
has successfully been used to characterize maturation in GM subcortical structures in two
studies by Lebel et al. from 2008 and 2011, respectively, that closely relate to the approaches
taken in this project [14] [17].
Generally, the overwhelming majority of cross-sectional DTI studies investigating maturation
show that FA increase and MD decrease with age during childhood and adolescence [29].
The cross-sectional study by Lebel et al. (2008) showed this for most of the 20 structures,
they investigated by ﬁtting exponential models to FA and MD measures.
Furthermore,
they explored maturational patterns for the 20 brain regions, and some are visualized in

During the brain maturation of children and adolescents, it is the microstructural changes
that are of interest.
Unfortunately, the microstructures themselves, are as the name in-

Generally, the strength and orientation of the hindrance inﬂuencing the measurable diﬀusion

2.3.2
Diﬀusion Weighted Imaging (DWI)
To obtain information about the microstructural architecture of the brain, diﬀusion behavior
of diﬀerent brain structures can be observed using a speciﬁc imaging technique of MRI. MRI
is most often used to create structural images of soft tissue structures such as the brain,
using one of the multiple sequences, gradient ﬁeld changes, and radio wave excitation pulses.
Thus, MRI can also be sensitive to water diﬀusion when appropriate magnetic ﬁeld gradients
in specialized sequences are applied [20].
To obtain DWI initial radio wave excitation pulse causes all hydrogen nuclei precess with
the same frequency and the subsequent process to obtain the signal from this excitation is
visualized in Figure 2.5. A dephasing gradient is applied and causes the magnetic ﬁeld to
decrease slightly along a single 3D axis, so the nuclei will start precessing at diﬀerent speeds
relative to one another resulting in diﬀerences in their phases across. This ultimately leads
to a momentary loss of signal as this is the of of all nuclei in the voxel of interest. The
molecules are then allowed to diﬀuse for a brief time interval, typically about 50-100 ms,
which is short relative to the water exchange between intra- and extracellular compartments
but long enough to not allow water to diﬀuse and deﬂect of microstructural surfaces. Subse-
quently, a new opposite rephasing gradient is applied, which causes the same sized magnetic
ﬁeld to increase along the same axis as before. If no diﬀusion had occurred, full signal re-
covery would be observed, as perfect realignment in phase of the nuclei preceedings would
result. However, if diﬀusion did occur, imperfect realignment of the phase would result in
only partial signal recovery.

Diﬀusion in Microstructures
The ordered structure of the neural tissue is thought to be the main contributor of the
anisotropic behavior of water diﬀusion observed in the brain and changes in this behavior
is a generally accepted quantiﬁcation of maturation. FA is commonly used in studies in-
vestigating brain development as it is very sensitive to the changes occurring in the brain
microstructure during brain maturation. Although some degree of anisotropy is present at
birth, FA increases during maturation along with structural changes aﬀecting diﬀusion, in-
cluding axonal density, axon size, extracellular space volume, myelin thickness, ect. [42].
Neural tissue, especially WM, displays a larger degree of anisotropy than most other bodily
tissues. Thomsen et al. conducted early measurements of water diﬀusion in human WM
tissue and proposed that regional diﬀerences in diﬀusion could be due to the orientation of
myelin structures [1] [41]. The lipid bilayers of myelin that encloses the axons have lim-
ited permeability of water and would thus be expected to inhibit diﬀusion perpendicular
to the axonal direction resulting in anisotropy. According to a study by Gulani et al. [6]
myelination does, in fact, contribute to anisotropic behaviour as shown in animal models,
although the extent of it was not clear. However, a study conducted by Beaulieu and Allan
[2] showed that signiﬁcant anisotropic diﬀusion was present in intact non-myelinated nerves
as well. Although, it is tempting to consider myelination as the main structural contributor
to increased FA during maturation, other ordered structures might give rise to the observed
anisotropy as well.
In a study by Schwartz et al. [9], correlations between diﬀusion coeﬃcients and axon mor-
phometry were investigated. Two main ﬁndings of the study showed particular importance
of axon diameter and extracellular volume fraction of the diﬀusion behavior.
Firstly, the study showed a strong correlation between longitudinal diﬀusion and axon di-
ameter [9]. A possible explanation for the positive correlation could be due to the inverse
relationship between axon diameter and cytoskeleton protein density as these may act as
diﬀusion barriers. However, the study by Beaulieu and Allen did not ﬁnd microtubules or
fast axonal transport to have a signiﬁcant role in diﬀusion [2]. As axon diameter increase
during healthy maturation, this might partly explain the observed increase in FA.
Secondly, a signiﬁcant positive correlation of perpendicular diﬀusion with extracellular vol-
ume and axon spacing was shown [9]. Furthermore, a negative correlation was found with
axon density and myelin fraction. If the number or size of axons increase, so will myelin
fraction, which in turn occupies extracellular space resulting in reduced perpendicular dif-
fusion. As extracellular volume decreases and axon count and myelin fraction increase with
maturation, a total decrease in perpendicular diﬀusion can be observed.
In conclusion, these ﬁndings support that besides increasing myelination, an increase in ax-
onal diameter and decrease in extracellular might be important reasons that FA increase is
observed during brain maturation.
However, there is a vast complexity of microstructure and their inﬂuence on water diﬀusion
in the neural tissue and discrepancies found between some studies underline this. Studies are
often conducted in very controlled environments and on models, which does not necessarily
translate well to the environment of the human brain. No single microstructure is responsible
14
==PAGE:22==
for diﬀusion changes, and as many alteration processes of brain structure naturally occur in
parallel it can be diﬃcult to trace back which microstructural changes aﬀected the diﬀusion
and to which extend. Although a more complete picture of how exactly microstructures
inﬂuence water diﬀusion is still needed, changes in diﬀusion are still widely accepted as
quantiﬁcation of maturation.

Data and Preprocessing
This chapter brieﬂy describes the the demographics of the participants included in the study,
the image acquisition method and image preprocessing used. The resulting data will be
described and the process of cleaning with regards to removal of outliers will be explained.
3.1
Participants
The data included in this project was collected as a part of the longitudinal HUBU (Hjernens
Udvikling hos Børn og Unge) project initiated in 2007 in which 95 children and adolescents
were included. They were recruited from three diﬀerent elementary schools in the Copen-
hagen suburban area at the ages of 7 to 13 years, corresponding to ﬁrst to sixth grade.
The participants were followed over nine years and cover the age span of 7.5 - 18.9 years in
the current project. Oral and written explanation of the aim and procedures of the study
were conveyed to both participants and parents, and informed assent was obtained prior to
participation from all children as well as written consent from their parents. Furthermore,
written consent was obtained from the participants themselves as they turned 18. The study
was conducted in agreement with the Declaration of Helsinki and was approved by the local
Danish Committee for Biomedical Research Ethics (protocols: H-KF-01-131/03 and H-3-
2013-037).
All participants were scanned up to 12 times using structural MRI and diﬀusion-weighted
MRI, with the ﬁrst 10 scans being obtained with 6 months intervals and the 11th being
obtained a year after the 10th scan. From the last 12th round of scanning no DWI was
obtained. All participants were typically developing with no parent-reported neurological or
psychiatric illnesses or brain injury at the time of inclusion. [38]

Image Preprocessing
3.3.1
Extraction of Subcortical Structures
Image preprocessing was carried out in MATLAB using scripts based on SPM 8 routines
(Statistical Parametric Mapping). The images were corrected for spatial distortions based
on registration to the mean b0 and structural images and the scanner-speciﬁc gradient non-
linearity proﬁle. Diﬀusion tensors images (DTI) were ﬁtted using the least-squares-ﬁt by non-
linear optimization, employing a Levenburg-Marquardt algorithm and constrained Cholesky
decomposition. Subject movement during the DWI scanning was estimated by calculating
the root mean square (RMS) deviation of six rigid transformation parameters found in the
coregistration of DW images to the mean b0 image, where the brain was approximated using
a spherical volume radius of 60 mm. In order to spatially normalize the ﬁtted DT images,
all individual DWI volumes were wrapped into a common brain atlas. This was created
speciﬁcally for this study in a two-step process using the DTI-TK software tool [15]. First,
a within-subject template image was created by registering together DT images obtained
for that subject across all time points. Next, all within-subject templates were registered
together resulting in a between-subject template image and ﬁnal atlas. Then, DWI volumes
could be wrapped into the atlas using a concatenation of the within- and between-subject
registration deformation ﬁelds. Finally, images of diﬀusion measures for the regions listed
in Table 3.1 could be created holding FA, MD, RD, and AD, respectively, and a brain mask
was employed on the FA and diﬀusivity images to remove non-brain voxels. [38]
3.3.2
Extraction of White Matter Fibers
Like most other biological structures, the brain’s topological anatomy varies considerably be-
tween individuals and over time. Thus, identifying similar structures across scans obtained
from multiple individuals at diﬀerent points in time is a prerequisite for comparing the dif-
ference in diﬀusion between them. Several approaches of such identiﬁcation and alignment
exist, however, no method is perfect.
Firstly, the DW images were preprocessed by correcting for head motion and eddy current
included geometric distortions utilizing a global aﬃne registration procedure. Subsequently,
the automated longitudinal intra-subject analysis (ALISA) was developed in 2014 by Aarnink
et al. to reliability obtain ﬁber tract segmentation across diﬀerent subjects and time points
and the WM ﬁber extraction conducted on the data in the present project, utilized this
method [21]. Firstly, whole-brain deterministic ﬁber tracking was executed using ExploreDTI
for each DTI data set. The following parameters were speciﬁed for the tracking: an isotropic
2 mm seed point resolution was utilized throughout the whole brain, the FA threshold were
0.2, and the angle threshold were 30◦for seed point selection and termination of tracking,
and the step size was set to 10 mm. Furthermore, ’AND’-gates as well as ’NOT’-gates were

applied to ensure extraction of the speciﬁc WM tracts of interest listed in Table 3.1. From
each of the reconstructed WM ﬁber tracts, average FA, MD, RD, and AD were calculated.

and properties of GAMMs are given in methods chapter Section 4.3. Large residuals could
be indicative of oddly behaving data and thus outliers. The residuals of observations with
regards to trajectories predicted with these models were investigated group-wise with a single
subject and a single tract or ROI as one group.
3.5.2
Tract Outliers
Tract variables represent the number of streamlines found for the individual WM ﬁber struc-
tures. If they display highly deviating residuals, it could be indicative of the tractography
being erroneous, and thus the tracts and corresponding diﬀusion measures should be consid-
ered for exclusion. Two criteria were considered for outlier detection. Firstly, the residuals
in each group (data points for one subject in one tract) were averaged, and if this average
did not fall within the interval of -3 and +3, the raw data of that observation was manually
inspected further. This was carried out in order to determine if the tracts and corresponding
measures of interest should be excluded entirely. Secondly, each observation of each group
was examined to determine if it lay within 2.5 standard deviations of the mean value of that
group. If it did not, the observation was manually investigated further as well. The manual
inspection resulted in exclusion of the FA, MD, RD, and AD, which the tract corresponded

to if reason was found to do so, which was typically due to a poor tractography of the speciﬁc
ﬁber tract.
3.5.3
Outlying Diﬀusion Measures
The residuals of variables containing the FA, MD, AD, and RD measures of WM ﬁbers and
subcortical structures were individually treated by similar means as the residuals of tract
variables. If the average of each group did not fall within the interval of -3 and +3, the raw
data was investigated further and excluded if a reason to do so was found. Furthermore, each
observation of each group was examined to determine if it lay within 2.5 standard deviations
of the mean value of that group. If it did not, the observation alone was excluded from the
data.
3.5.4
Final Data Exclusion
Observations that did not display an RMS value were also excluded, as RMS was a quan-
tiﬁcation of patient movement during the scan. The result was the exclusion of data from
subcortical structures obtained from two scans and data obtained from WM tracts from
seven scans. Furthermore, subjects having obtained less than four successful scans were ex-
cluded from the dataset as well, as the analysis focus on the characterization of trajectories
from individuals over time, which is best obtained from multiple time points. Lastly, it was
ensured that if removal of single observations resulted in ROIs only containing four or fewer
observations for a subject, these would be removed to ensure at least four measurements per
group (data points for one subject in one tract).

4.2
Gaussian Process
Given a set of data points, there are potentially an inﬁnite amount of functions that ﬁt
the data. Gaussian processes utilize these potential functions by assigning a probability to
each of them and let the mean of their probability distribution represent the most probable
function. A unique quality of the Gaussian Process modelling is the ability to incorporate
conﬁdence of predictions, thus quantifying the uncertainty of the predictions made. The
majority of the information about the GP in the following section is extracted from ”A
Visual Exploration of Gaussian Processes” by Jochen G¨ortler from 2019 [33].
4.2.1
Multivariate Gaussian Distribution
Multivariate Gaussian distributions provide the very building block of Gaussian processes.
The input x is said to be multivariate Gaussian distributed if each of the variables it consists
of x1, x2, ...xD are distributed normally and if a linear combination of them follow a normal
distribution as well with some mean µ and variance σ. The distribution of x can be written
as x ∼N(µ, Σ), where µ is a D-dimensional vector containing expected values of each
distribution and the mean of each variable contained in x and Σ is a DxD-dimensional
covariance matrix. This symmetric and semi-deﬁnite matrix describes the variance σ2
i along
each dimension i in its diagonal and the correlation σij between variables i and j when
i ̸= j in its oﬀ-diagonal elements. As a result, Σ determines the shape of the multivariate
distribution, whereas µ determines the centre of it.
An advantage of Gaussian Processes as an analytical tool is that the central limit theorem
justiﬁes their modelling of noise in the data. Generally, the behavior and thus the distribution
of the noise is not assumed to be known, but the central limit theorem states that the
distribution of means of multiple subsets of noise will approximately be normally distributed,
which is a valid assumption in many cases.

4.4.1
Model Implementation Software
The data was loaded into R version 1.4.1103, in which all analysis and processing took place
except for GP modelling. The R package mgcv version 1.8.33 was used extensively for the
modeling of GAM and GAMM itself. The GP models however were ﬁtted in Python version
3.8.5 by use of the GPy framework version 1.9.9.
4.4.2
RMS Considerations
The large amount of noise in the data arises from a multitude of diﬀerent sources, for most
of which the underlying cause cannot be identiﬁed. A small amount of the noise however,
was expected to be approximately linearly dependant on the RMS value of the scans. This
eﬀect was thus modelled linearly, to compensate properly, but the RMS measure itself is not
of interest and as the eﬀect of RMS varied between diﬀerent ROI’s it was ﬁtted to them
individually. To take the eﬀect of RMS into account in both GAM and GP, two diﬀerent
implementations were used.
For the GAM’s and GAMM’s the RMS was directly incoorporated into the models as merely
a linear ﬁxed eﬀect.
In GP slightly more preparational work was done in order to remove the linear eﬀect of RMS
before modelling. A simple linear regression line was ﬁtted to the data of individual ROIs as
a function of RMS. This linear ﬁt was subtracted from all points, and the intercept of the ﬁt
was added back onto the data. The ﬁnal GP models were subsequently ﬁtted to this data.

4.4.5
Annualized Rate of Change
To further investigate the dynamics of the changes in the brain regions, running annualized
rate of change were calculated from the trajectories ﬁtted using the generic GAMM model
from Section 4.4.3. These were calculated utilizing the Equation 4.25, but with C% being the
rate of change for an age centered between the ages one year apart from each other and with
f(min) and f(max) representing predicted ROI values at these ages. The age span for which
C% was calculated was 8.083 and 18.45 years, being half year after the age of the youngest
obtained scan and half a year before the oldest obtained scan. The same process was carried
out for the upper and lower 95 % conﬁdence interval limits of the average trajectories and
included as shades in the visualizations.
4.4.6
Maturational Trajectories of Individuals using GAM
Following the initial analysis steps, two approaches were taken to model maturational trajec-
tories of individuals aiming to determine diﬀerences in maturational patterns among them

using GAMM and GAM respectively. The main diﬀerences between the two, is the inclusion
of id by age as a random eﬀect in the mixed eﬀect model GAMM, and as a ﬁxed eﬀect in
the GAM.
Individual Trajectories using GAMM
This ﬁrst model approach was based on the generic ﬁtted GAMM described in Section
4.4.3, Equation 4.26. From the equation it becomes clear that to ﬁt individual trajectories,
coeﬃcients corresponding to speciﬁc individuals needs to be inserted into the equation. This
was carried out by deﬁning a vector aij spanning the age-interval in which the participant
was investigated closely sampled at 0.02 years. si was en equally long vector containing 0
or 1 corresponding to boy or girl depending on the individual, and rij was a vector with
elements ﬁxed to the mean RMS value for that individual. b0 is a vector containing random
intercepts for the individuals, and therefore contributes to the individual trajectory with a
single oﬀ-set. Similarly, b1 contributes with individual linear slope deviations.
However, this modelling only allow individuals to deviate from the general trajectories lin-
early, which restricts the shapes they can take as well as ways they can deviate from each
other. This might be problematic as it is unknown how they might in fact deviate from the
average trajectory as well as from each other. Recall Figure 2.3. The linear diﬀerences al-
lowed between participant in this approach, only make a combination of variable asymptotes
and slope variability possible, whereas they cannot vary in phase or shape.

non-linear smooth functions instead of linear eﬀects. This allowed phase variation and some
degree of shape variability between individual ﬁtted trajectories.

Evaluating Non-linear Individual Eﬀects
As will be apparent from the results in section 5.1, the two diﬀerent modelling methods
results in sets of individual trajectories with very minor diﬀerences. It is thus speculated
if non-linear modelling of individual eﬀects is necessary and next the amount of non-linear
deviation in individual trajectories relative to the average trajectory is investigated.
The GAM model explained in Section 4.4.6 was constructed again although while using
gamm4() instead of gamm() and a default optimization setting ”newton”. This essentially
creates the same models using slightly diﬀerent engines and the default optimization. How-
ever, it allows for the computation of standard deviation point estimates of the individuals
nonlinear deviation from the sample mean, as well as conﬁdence intervals for this standard
deviation using the functions VarCorr() and conﬁnt(), respectively.
These computations
could not be obtained with the prior way of constructing the model using gamm(). A point
estimate of exactly zero infers that the individual’s deviation from the average trajectory
is exactly linear. As mentioned earlier, the variance of the smoothing λ = 1
σ [39], meaning
σ = 0 ⇒λ = ∞resulting in inﬁnite penalization of curvature, and thus linearity. A small
point estimate of the variance of the individual’s nonlinear deviation indicates close to linear
behavior and thus linearity can arguable assumed.
However, what qualiﬁes as ”small” or ”small enough” to make this assumption of linear
deviation is debatable. In this thesis, this problem will be approached by an investigation
of the models in which largest values of point estimates of standard deviation are found.
If individual deviation in these ROIs can be accepted as linear, the same assumption is
applicable for the remaining of ROIs as deviations are even more linear in those cases.
The extent of linearity is evaluated visually, by creating plots displaying the deviation be-
tween individual trajectories and the average trajectory. Individual trajectories were ﬁtted

4.4.7
Fitting GP Individual Trajectories
The GP models ﬁtted trajectories for data of each individual in each ROI after the eﬀect of
RMS was subtracted as described in Section 4.4.2. Eﬀectively that meant ﬁtting to a low
number of points between 4 and 11. A RBF kernel was deﬁned in order to carry out the mod-
elling. Priors were set for all parameters of kernel, with the length scale parameter belonging
to a prior inverse gamma distribution of α = 8 and β = 2 and with the variance parameter
belonging to a prior inverse gamma distribution of α = 1 and β = 0.5. Furthermore, a
Gaussian noise prior was set for the random error of the model with α = 1 and β = 0.5 and
all parameters were optimized using the optimize() function. Finally, the function m.predict
was used to predict individual trajectories. A range 1000 ages were deﬁned evenly covering
the entire investigated age span, and individual and individual trajectories were ﬁtted on at
age point within the age range in which scans were obtained for them.

Correlation between Brain Measures
A ﬁnal analysis step sought to investigate spatial coherence in maturation between the inves-
tigated brain regions. This was carried out by computation of correlations between structures
in two separate analyses for FA and MD respectively.
Correlation itself ρ(X, Y ) is a measure of how data from one variable X and data from
another variable Y vary from the mean with respect to each other and it is thus used to
quantify the strength of the relationship between the two. The correlation matrix is a square
matrix containing all pairwise correlations between the structures. The Pearson’s correlation
was used to calculate the individual elements in the correlation matrix, and was deﬁned as
follows:
ρX,Y = Cov(X, Y )
σXσY
(4.33)

Cov(X) = (X −µX)(Y −µY )T

where X contains a vector of raw data for one variable and Y contains a vector of raw data
for another variable, where σX and σY are the standard deviations of the data contained in
X and Y , and where µX and µY are the means calculated for each person, and subtracted
from data for that same individual in X and Y to avoid level diﬀerences between individuals
contributing to the correlation. All columns vectors were arranged in the same order in
regards to individuals and their scans and individual data points missing from the data were
replaced with 0.
Positive correlation values indicate that the structure measures increase or decrease together,
while negative correlations indicate that while one measure increase, the other decrease. The
absolute value of the correlation indicate the strength of their relationship which also means
that a correlation of zero indicate that the two measures are independent of each other.

pane in Figure 5.1.

relatively large negative percentage value at the beginning of the investigated age interval.
This translates to a negative slope in the trajectories. However, most of the annualized rate
of change curves for MD measures will have reached close to zero well before the end of the
investigated age interval, as illustrated well in the lower left pane of Figure 5.1 for the Body
of Corpus Callosum (BCC) MD measure. This translates to a level-oﬀin the trajectories,
which interestingly could indicate that the particular region has reached maturation. The
pattern, also observed in BBC MD of girls reaching 0 % change at around 13-14 years of age
and much earlier than boys, generally displaying more linear curves, constitute the majority
of annualized rate of change plots for MD.

data is collected. Lastly, some subcortical structures, including the caudate, lies very close
to cavities containing cerebrospinal ﬂuid, which have a 10-fold MD and an FA of zero.
Although extensive measures were taken to erode ROIs to purely structure relevant voxels,
partial volume eﬀects might still contribute to the noise.

sually be observed well, namely that the shape of the curves is very similar across individuals
but highly deviating in their intercepts.

5.2.1
Density plots
The plots displayed in Figure 5.6 are of visualizations of probability distributions of data
for four diﬀerent measures in a range of ages, and the remaining density plots are found in
Appendix 8.11.
Each pixel in the plots denotes the probability of ﬁnding respective FA and MD measures
at certain values given an age. From visual inspection it becomes clear that more certainty
is obtained in the middle region of the age range. At ages below 10 years and ages above 16
years the distribution is ﬂatter. These diﬀerences in certainty arises as more data generally
had been collected in the middle region of the age interval and as individuals contribute with
ﬂat distributions in age ranges, where no data had been collected from them. Although the
probability is spread over a range of FA and MD values, the general tendency of slightly
increasing FA and slightly decreasing MD is still observed in a majority of measures. This
conﬁrms that although more ﬂexible modeling is used, collectively the trajectories obtained,
still display commonly expected tendencies. Inspecting plots in the Appendix 8.11, it is
worth noting that fornix MD displays a relatively large spread of MD values relative to
other structures. This might be caused by the partial volume eﬀect of cerebrospinal ﬂuid
voxel surrounding the fornix tract, as this ﬂuid contributes with MD around 5-6 fold that of
brain voxels. Thus, the general extent of the spread of data might not be based on biological
diﬀerences in diﬀusion alone but could also be aﬀected by the noise in the obtained data.

5.3
Correlation between Brain Regions
2D correlation matrices visualizing the size of the correlations between pairs of structures
are displayed in Figure 5.7 and 5.8 for MD and FA, respectively. As the correlation matrix
is symmetric, and the diagonal contains the variance of the individual measures, these are
removed in the visualization.
When examining Figure 5.7, it appears that, the majority of correlations are positive on an
overall level which is to be expected, as almost all structures display the common behavior
of decreasing MD with age. High correlation is seen between left and right hemispheric brain
regions, including thalamus, putamen, CST, amygdala, and to some extent hippocampus,
indicating that these structures in particular display symmetric behavior.
The fornix MD seems to generally display correlations close to zero, which might relate to
the noise level found in the data for this tract. In the brain, the fornix is surrounded by
cerebrospinal ﬂuid, which has about a 5-6-fold MD relative to brain regions. Thus only a few
of these voxels contributing as a result of partial volume eﬀect can cause large inaccuracies
in the obtained MD. Furthermore, the forceps major MD display similar low correlations.
However, this might relate to the average maturational trajectory for this tract being mostly
ﬂat, which does not give rise to any correlation.
The right putamen MD appears to display an overall low correlation between pairs, which
is even negative paired with some measures. When examining the the average trajectories
in Appendix 8.9, it can be observed that the female average trajectory for right putamen
MD does display a somewhat unique non-monotonic behavior as it changes from decreasing
to increasing at around 13 years of age. This might partly explain the inverse nature of the
relationships seen for this measure.

Chapter 6
Discussion
In this chapter discussion and interpretation of the results will be presented. The challenges
arising from the data are mentioned, the models used and some of the choices made during
analysis will be discussed. Lastly, future work with regards to potential analyses steps, that
could be taken will be shortly described.
In the present thesis longitudinal data on typically-developing children and adolescents aged
seven to 19 years, were investigated to characterize temporal patterns and individual dif-
ferences in maturational trajectories. On average each participant was assessed 8.9 times,
creating a unprecedented time resolution. The high number of time points should in prin-
ciple yield more better modeling of the maturational trajectories compared to other studies
on brain maturation in children and adolescence that typically only include up to 3 time
points per individual [34].
Average maturation and Sex Diﬀerences
First, the results of the calculated percent change values, generic GAMMs (Section 4.4.3)
and annualized rate of change graphs for FA and MD in diﬀerent brain regions conﬁrmed
what has previously been found in other studies, namely, that there is a general increase
of FA and decrease of MD with age [17] [30] [34] . The only brain regions that generally
did not follow this pattern in the results was the Fornix MD. However, as this structure
was surrounded by cerebrospinal ﬂuid having and approximate 5-6-fold MD relative to brain
tissue, the partial volume eﬀect could have inﬂuenced this.
Furthermore, investigation of average trajectories of FA and MD modeled with GAMMs
and corresponding annualized rate of change graphs showed dynamics of the trajectories as
a function of age. These showed a pattern of more rapid increases and decreases at early
ages and drop in rate of change by the end of the age-range, which is again similar to what
has been reported in existing literature [17] [34] [30]. However, this trend was much more
distinct in girls, were boys showed a much more constant rate of change and linear trajectory
in the investigated age-range, and girls displayed more ﬂuctuating patterns. Although it has
been hypothesized that this maturational pattern may relate to the earlier puberty found in
females relative to males, the evidence is inconclusive [34].
Still, the most striking changes in rate of change found in girls were right around the age of
58
==PAGE:66==
menarche, the onset of ﬁrst menstruation. For most of the FA and MD measures displaying
ﬂuctuating annualized rate of changes in the graphs have the most prominent changes roughly
between the ages of 12-14. The average age of menarche in Danish females was found to
be around 13 years of age in a study from 2019 [32]. Puberty is associated with changes
in release of a range of diﬀerent hormones.
Some of the hormonal changes even start a
year before actual physical changes associated with puberty begin to develop [44]. Although
its observed that the age range of rapid change and age of menarche co-occur on average
measures, it might merely be a coincidence.
However, it is also possible that the many
hormonal changes that accompany puberty, is also what is aﬀecting the dynamics in FA and
MD measures and brain development. It has been shown through research, that hormones
associated with puberty inﬂuences structural changes as well as brain plasticity [37] [27]. It
could thus be interesting to investigate further if the alterations in hormones associated with
menarche inﬂuence the observed FA and MD patterns in brain development, as the results
in this project could indicate.
Diﬀerences in Individual Trajectories
The GAMM modeling framework successfully modelled individual maturational trajecto-
ries, that made it possible to characterize individual diﬀerences. Although constraints were
imposed, it made it possible to capture variation between subjects that was biologically
meaningful.
Modeling individual trajectories with GAM indicated, that their deviations from average
sex-speciﬁc trajectories were very small and could be approximated linearly, although due
to the nature of the inference procedure this is not direct evidence of a linear relationship.
In continuation of approximating individual deviations linearly, GAMMs were used for mod-
eling, as these only allowed for individuals to deviate linearly with a random slope and
intercept. The main diﬀerences found between individual trajectories were the individual
variation in intercepts. As generally high ICC values were found, intercept diﬀerences be-
tween individuals explained an important part of the variation found in the data.
The individual variations in slope were small in comparison. Not ﬁnding evidence for in-
dividual diﬀerences in slope does not rule that these diﬀerences might exist, however, they
were too small to be eﬃciently detected. The small size in diﬀerences in slope also indicates,
that even if there was systematic individual diﬀerences in slope, they are likely too small to
be relevant in a biological context.
Overall, these results suggests that early life factors may be more important to the observed
FA and MD value in individuals than maturational factors at play in the investigated age
interval, where the development follow largely parallel patterns. In other words the small
diﬀerences seen in slope suggest a more predetermined developmental pattern. The greater
diﬀerences seen in intercept indicate that the variations seen among individuals present early
in life, which might be due to underlying neural architecture rather than the maturational
alterations of the tissue that occur later. As already mentioned in the Background Section
2.3.3 anisotropy does present before myelination and other maturational changes initiates
and thus other neuroarchitectural diﬀerences caused by something else than maturational
processes, such as myelination and increase of axonal diameter, might be more relevant for
the of levels of FA and MD. Another example of parallel developmental patterns was shown
in an study by Holland et al. (2014) [22]. Although they investigated individual diﬀerences
59
==PAGE:67==
in structural growth trajectories during the ﬁrst three months of postnatal life, their results
illustrated strikingly similar tendencies.
They too found, approximately parallel growth
trajectories with quite diﬀerent individual intercepts. In conclusion, it is possible that the
general characteristics of the brain are largely determined early, and that any kind of follow-
ing change follow a very similar pattern across individuals.
Distribution of Data and Maturation Patterns
The individual trajectories modeled using GP were unlikely to describe the slowly and
steadily microstructural changes in the brain, as they were much too ﬂuctuating and dis-
playing highly non-monotonic behavior. This is expected as the current GP based model
only take into account data from each individual. However, collectively they helped gain
insight into distribution of the data through 2D density plots made from the GP models
and their uncertainties.
These conﬁrmed and visualized the large spread in MD an FA
measures seen relative to respective increases and decreases that was observed. From this
it became apparent why the individual information is of such importance when using the
models for prediction. As individuals were shown to mature roughly in parallel, the indi-
vidual intercepts helps immensely to narrow down the probability distribution that can be
expected for single individuals. It also emphasised that less can be concluded with respect
to the high and low end of the age-range as it summarizes over a smaller subset of individuals.
The correlation analysis conducted investigated if similarities could be found between the
data of diﬀerent brain regions. As expected some of the highest correlations found were
between structures that due to their anatomical proximity shared voxels. Other high corre-
lations were found between the same type of structures found in either of the hemispheres
indicating that some structures seem to mature in symmetry, while others less so. Finally,
a few seemingly independent structures also displayed high correlation in both MD and FA
measures, namely left and right CST and left and right thalamus. The high level of inter-
connection between the two might play a role in why the data in them display such similar
patterns.
6.1
Strengths and Limitations
Working with empirical data often pose challenges as it contains noise, and the given data
was no exception as DWI and derived diﬀusion measures are noise prone. A series of pre-
processing steps were carried out to obtain single values of FA and MD for each structure
and these included the averaging over a group of voxels for individual structures. This is
commonly done across studies as tracts hold axons that lie in parallel, are organized, and
connects speciﬁc regions and frankly makes the results easier to compare across analyses and
data sets. This averaging gives a more stable measure as some noise is averaged out, and a
more simple data set to work with. However, it adds the assumption of equal maturation
over the entire volume of the region or tract of interest which might not be accurate. It
has been speculated whether some tracts attain myelination gradually starting at one end
spreading to the other. Furthermore, in some subcortical structures and WM tracts, diﬀerent
parts interconnect with diﬀerent regions of the brain, and thus it has also been speculated
whether maturational patterns might also be found to vary across the structure [12].
60
==PAGE:68==
The high noise level in the data that inﬂuenced the accuracy at which especially individual
trajectories could be estimated and the calculation of covariances between brain regions.
This limitation could have been compensated for in three ways; an increased number of
scans per participant, an increased number of participants and more accurate FA and MD
estimations for the investigated brain regions.
In a previous study Lebel et al. (2011) quantiﬁed maturation based on only two data points
per participant in the age-range of 5.6 to 29.3 years [17]. They both found increases and
decreases in FA, and both increases and decreases for MD as well. From the data in this
project it was clear that with the amount of noise present in this type of data, increases and
decreases in FA and MD between two subsequent scans are not due to biological changes in
brain microstructure alone. In this project 4-11 scans per participant was used, and although
this is considerably more data to compensate for the noise relative to the prior mentioned
study [17], it still constitute a challenge when attempting to characterize individual brain
maturation.
Furthermore, including more participants could also have given rise to more accurately esti-
mated average trajectories, although this would probably not have helped in the characteri-
sation of individual diﬀerences.
However, acquiring data is expensive and resource consuming for both researches as well as
the participants and their families. Although more data from a theoretical point of view
always results always provides a better basis for modelling, it should be considered carefully
if it is enough to justify the extra expense. The current data set is quite unique due to its
high time resolution, which gives the best bases for modeling individual trajectories.
As it is unclear how most of the noise in the data arises, it is currently diﬃcult to take any
actions to lower its impact. The preprocessing data as well as the tractography used, were
state of the arts methods, and most of the noise is expected to come from the rawdata itself.
Female average trajectories tended to level-oﬀat a point between 12-14 years of age indi-
cating a point at which maturation could be considered to be achieved. Male trajectories,
however, generally did not level-oﬀin the investigated period covering up to age 18.9 years.
If the investigated time period had been expanded to cover the third decade of life, it might
have been possible to detect a similar point at which male participants trajectories level oﬀ,
and thus analyse diﬀerences between maturation ages in boys and girls
6.2
Data Cleaning Process
The purpose of the data cleansing process was to only exclude data points and measures
from single individuals which displayed behavior very diﬀerently from the rest of the data.
Thus, the exclusion aimed to be very conservative such that only data points which was
known almost certainly to be outliers were removed. Furthermore, as longitudinal behavior
was of interest, participants from which only 3 or fewer scans had been obtained were dis-
carded. It could be argued that for average trajectory estimation, keeping as many points
as possible would have beneﬁcial. However, to keep the data set consistent across diﬀerent
parts of the analysis, the same exclusion decisions were made for all data used. On a ﬁnal
61
==PAGE:69==
note, discarding more data would ensure higher consistency and quality in the remaining
data as what can be considered borderline outliers would be removed as well, but having
more data in the end, was valued higher.
6.3
Models
Using the given data, the GAM and GP both ﬁtted individual maturational trajectories and
provided unique information and contributed with very diﬀerent perspectives on the data.
Maturational trajectories are expected to ﬂuctuate rather slowly, and allowing more wiggling
to get higher ﬂuctuating trajectories could make them less plausible from a biological per-
spective. Because of the noise-level, a some ﬂuctuation seen in the raw data is not expected
to be due to maturational changes in the brain microstructure, and thus the restriction in
terms of smoothing is needed. Smoothing is applied in both types of models, but in two
diﬀerent ways.
6.3.1
GAM(M)
GAM provided a framework in which it was possible to predict average as well as individual
maturation trajectories. However, these models were restricted in two main ways such that
mostly linear shapes were obtained unless strong evidence existed for the non-linear behav-
ior. Firstly, the small selected k limited the number of basis functions allowed to describe
the function, which ultimately results in a smoother ﬁt. However, as most trajectories are
expected to be roughly linear, it would most likely not have made any diﬀerence. Secondly, a
penalization of the magnitude of the functions second-order derivative was enforced directly,
when ﬁtting model parameters.
In section 5.1.1 it was concluded that individually ﬁtted trajectories using GAM allowing
individual non-linear deviation from the average trajectory, roughly ended up displaying lin-
ear individual deviations regardless. As a result, the non-linearity of individual deviations
were discarded and age-by-id was included as a random eﬀect rather than a ﬁxed eﬀect in
the remaining analysis. While it was true only slight non-linear individual deviations were
observed in most cases, the ﬁtting of the deviations itself penalized non-linearity. Non-linear
individual deviation patterns would only have been prominent in the estimated trajectories,
if strong evidence was found for it, but this was not the case. Although it is certainly possible
that individual trajectory deviations were more non-linear than what was modelled, the high
noise-level made it diﬃcult to detect these accurately. Consequently, the argumentation of
assuming linearity of individual deviations from the average trajectory is in its nature some-
what circular, but the assumption was accepted reasonable enough to continue the analysis
based on it.
6.3.2
GP
The GP models had their wiggliness restricted by the predeﬁned priors. The smoothness
assumption in the model dictating that the proximity between two input points inﬂuences
the proximity between their respective output as well. The GP models did not estimate
individual trajectories in a way that made them obtain a plausible shape, as they deviated
62
==PAGE:70==
from the monotonic increases in FA and decreases in MD respectively. Deﬁning diﬀerent
sets of priors might have inﬂuenced the resulting models, but again balancing between re-
striction to obtain more monotonic trajectories, would most likely result in ﬂattening the
trajectory shape. However, the measure of individual models uncertainty makes it possible
to quantify the probability of ﬁnding diﬀerent values of FA and MD at speciﬁc participant
ages in diﬀerent brain regions.
Furthermore, a notable part of the Gaussian bell shape making up the probability contribu-
tion from each individual in many cases extended beyond the expected interval of the MD
and FA measure predeﬁned as described in Section 4.4.7, which made it diﬃcult to calculate
Gaussian mixture that accurately represents the ﬁnal probabilities. This might be because
the assumption stating that the output is Gaussian is ill suiting. It would be interesting
to consider changing the output distribution or transforming the output variable to make
it adhere more to the current setting. However, these changes would make the inference
analytically intractable and hence require a sampling framework to be implemented, which
was outside the scope of the current thesis.
6.3.3
Comparing GP and GAMM
The trajectories ﬁtted with GP were generally subject to less restriction with respect to
wiggling and non-linear ﬂuctuations relative to models ﬁtted with GAM, which explains the
observed diﬀerences in shapes for the individually ﬁtted trajectories. Although the GAM
model approach is much more restrictive, it gives rise to much more biologically plausible
models compared to the GP. GAMs used information the entire data set to model individual
trajectories, and as long as it accepted that individual trajectories to some extent follow the
common patterns, this results in more statistical power, and thus better approximation of
the underlying true function. On the contrary, GP models for a speciﬁc measure are only
based on data from single participants. This results in data points inﬂuencing the speciﬁc
shape of individual trajectories to a much larger extent, which allows them to take much
more diverse shapes, but comes at a cost of being more inﬂuenced by noise.
This means that although plausible individual trajectories did not seem to be obtained with
GP models, an evaluation of certainty about data placement could be quantiﬁed based on
them instead, while this is something that could not be obtained with the GAMs.
6.4
Analysis decisions
The method used for compensating the systematic noise of RMS when ﬁtting GP could ide-
ally have been carried out in a way more similar to how it was done in the GAM modelling.
Fitting GP can take multidimensional input utilizing multiple kernels for ﬁtting. If RMS
had been included as an input, it would have been taken directly into account when ﬁtting
the GP and resulted in more correct optimal parameters. However, as this would require
deﬁning speciﬁc priors on the modelling of RMS dependence in the individual, likely requir-
ing an hierarchical approach, this was not considered in the current implementation.
Furthermore, more advanced GP predictions using even more input variables and thus di-
mensions and kernels for sex and id could potentially have been ﬁtted. However, this would
again require deﬁning speciﬁc priors on the modelling and likely a hierarchical approach. As
a result trajectories more similar to those of the GAM would be obtained, and possibly more
63
==PAGE:71==
true to the true underlying function. However, it might not be as useful in the density plots
for describing the probability of ﬁnding data at speciﬁc ages.
When carrying out GAM modelling, the complexity size of k = 5 was utilized repeatedly
in smooths. However, this choice was based previous successful GAM implementations of
similar models on roughly the same data [38]. It could have been investigated in more depth
if diﬀerent values of k would have been more suitable in the individual models and variables.
As there is no objective way of discovering the ideal k setting a rather small value in all
cases, was the more simple choice although rather restrictive.
Finally, evaluating the point estimate standard deviation of the linear individual slopes
was done relative to an overall linearly estimate of the average trajectory as described in
Section 4.4.6.
It is known that the average slope changes non-linearly, and that for the
young age span of the trajectories slope is underestimated and for the older age span it is
overestimated by the linear approximation. Comparing the linear individual point estimate
standard deviations to this will thus aﬀect diﬀerently depending on which par of the age
range in which the individual was investigated. However, the ﬁnal results consisted of very
small percentage values, and taking this into account would likely not have changed the
analysis outcome.
6.5
Future work
Several additional analysis steps could have been carried out as an extension of the work
done in this thesis had the time and scope allowed for it.
Monotonicity constraint
In future analysis work of the current data, a constrain could be added to all trajectories to
enforced monotonicity. It is expected that the biological changes in the brain microstructure
cause FA and MD to change slowly but consistently. Especially for the GP models where
the individual trajectories took very ﬂuctuating shapes, this could have helped make them
more biologically plausible. The GAMM models themselves were already quite monotone,
thus not in need of this additional constraint.
Fixing model parameters
When modeling individual trajectories using GP, instead of setting priors of the length scale,
variance and noise variance one or more of them could have been set to ﬁxed values. After
all, it is expected that at least the length scale is similar across diﬀerent individuals and brain
regions. More work could have been put into investigating if variance and noise variance
displayed consistent patterns justifying setting them to ﬁxed values.
Correlation analysis
The correlation analysis could have been extended further. Firstly, the signiﬁcance level of
correlation could have been determined, evaluating which structures were in fact displaying
signiﬁcantly similar diﬀusion behavior.
Secondly, a K-means clustering could have been
applied grouping similar structures. This was attempted brieﬂy and for FA measures, where
the clustering with K = 2 did result in the dividing of subcortical structures into one
group and WM tracts into another. The same clustering of MD measures singled out single
64
==PAGE:72==
structures into one cluster, while the rest remained in the other cluster. Although the results
on the FA measures show great potential for further investigation, due to time constraints
this was considered outside the scope of the current work.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Average maturational trajectories of FA and MD measures in various brain structures were
obtained using GAMM models, and annualized rate of change curves were calculated to
describe their temporal dynamics. Generally, FA was found to increase and MD to decrease
with age for the investigated age-range from 7.5 to 18.9 years. Typically in females the most
rapid changes were found at the beginning of the investigated age range and a slowing down
by the end. Male trajectories more often displayed a constant annualized rate of change
because of their mostly linear maturational trajectory. However, some individual structures
showed exceptions to these trends. Including subjects in the early adulthood might have
revealed a slowing in the rate of change of brain maturation at a later point, as males have
been observed to mature later than females.
Models of individual maturational trajectories were determined using GAM, GAMM, and
GP. When modeling with GAM at the given noise levels in the current sample, evidence was
not found for non-linear behavior, making it reasonable to approximate individual diﬀerences
linearly. However, as non-linearity was explicitly penalised in the model, and hence traded
oﬀagainst noise it could not be concluded that these relationships were linear.
Next, the corresponding modelling only allowing individuals to deviate linearly with a ran-
dom slope and intercept was with GAMM. The resulting models did display individual
variation in intercepts; however, the individual variations in slope were tiny in comparison.
This does not rule that diﬀerences might exist in the individual slopes, however, they were
too small to be eﬃciently detected given the noise level. These results suggest that early
life factors may be more important to the observed FA and MD value in individuals than
factors at play in the sampled age interval.
GP models of individual trajectories were much more ﬂuctuating, displaying highly non-
monotonic behavior, and although this was unlikely to describe actual microstructural changes
in the brain, GP models provide an uncertainty measure of the ﬁtted model. The density
plot constructed based on these models revealed the probability of obtaining diﬀerent values
of FA and MD at diﬀerent ages and conﬁrmed the prior observed general FA increase and
MD decreases.
The most important diﬀerences between the GAM and GP approaches were the means by
which ﬂuctuations and non-linear behavior were penalized and the amount of data used in
66
==PAGE:74==
the model predictions. The GP only utilized data points belonging to the speciﬁc measure
and individual, whereas GAM used information from speciﬁc measure but individuals from
the entire data set.
Finally, spatial coherence between maturation in diﬀerent brain regions was investigated by
correlation analysis. High correlation was primarily found between the same structures in
the two hemispheres and between structures that shared voxels due to their spatial prox-
imity. However, high correlation was also found between thalamus and CST and putamen,
respectively, which could possibly be attributed to biological relationships causing similar
maturational patterns.
