Abstract
Asset tracking is becoming increasingly popular day by day. People are tracking
their packages, vehicles, wallets, animals, etc. One of such asset which is commonly
being tracked is a bicycle. There are hundreds of bike tracking solutions available
and are developed to prevent the bikes from getting stolen.
Most of these bike
trackers are based on GSM and GNSS technologies, both of which are power hungry
and not optimized for battery powered application. Hence, these trackers have very
short battery life and are usually costly.
This thesis proposes the use of LPWAN technology SigFox which promises low power
consumption as well as long coverage range for the development of an end-to-end
bike tracking system. This includes designing the tracking hardware based on SigFox
which can be installed in the bike frame. Once installed in the bike, the device is
capable of detecting bike movement and distinguishing between the user of the bike
and the thief by using Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE). The device can notify the user
with a GNSS based location if the bike gets stolen and is also capable of providing
an approximate position without the availability of GNSS service. This is achieved
by using SigFox’s location detection system named ATLAS. The development of the
device is done keeping in mind the cost and power eﬃciency of the components used.
Keywords: Internet of things, IoT, LoRaWAN, SigFox, NB-IoT, LPWAN, Asset
Tracking, Bike tracking

Introduction
1.1
General Overview
Asset tracking is a method of tracking courier boxes, valuable assets, animals and
humans either by using barcode printed on labels, using Bluetooth Low Energy
(BLE), Radio Frequency Identiﬁcation (RFID) or by using Global Positioning Sys-
tem (GPS). In the logistics industry, all the items are usually identiﬁed by attaching
a unique barcode to each item and then scanning it manually by barcode readers
or by smartphone using a dedicated app. Attaching a simple piece of paper with a
barcode printed on it has revolutionized the logistics industry and also has enabled
a feature like real-time tracking of courier[1]. RFID tags are handy when it comes
down to tracking animals, for example, if the animals in the farm have the RFID tag,
it can help the farmer to know the approximate location of the animals. Another
advantage of having an RFID tag is that it can also help farmers with pedigree,
medical state and traveling habits of each animal. RFID tags can also be useful for
tracking domestic animals, and it could be very useful in locating lost pets [2] [3].
GPS tracking is used when an asset has to be tracked, usually referring to a non-
stationary asset. Most of the vehicles used in the ﬂeet management industry have
either a portable or a stationary tracker to ﬁnd out the real-time location of the
vehicle. Tracking can also be useful for ﬂeet business to track location, speed, the
direction of travel and condition of their vehicle.
Tracking in ﬂeet business can
help to keep an eye on the vehicle as well as the employee, it can also be used for
improving the safety of the employees, and it can also help to utilize the resources
in a better way. Asset tracking also has some useful medical applications such as
tracking of patients with dementia disorder. Such a tracking device is made with
ultra-reliable components and has features like real-time tracking, alarm notiﬁcation
and in some cases, with geofencing capabilities. Geofencing is a term referred for a
virtual geographical boundary typically speciﬁed by the user of the device[4].
So far we have only looked at the outdoor asset tracking, but due to the advance-
ment in the technology, it is possible now to detect the location of an asset indoors
with high accuracy using BLE beacons. A beacon is an ultra-eﬃcient Bluetooth
transmitter. This transmitter emits low energy signals which can be received by
smartphones and other BLE receivers. Therefore an asset with a small BLE beacon
module attached can be found easily by detecting these emitted signals. Also, if
there are multiple BLE beacon receivers installed indoors it is possible to locate a
moving asset in real time by combining the Received Signal Strength (RSS) values
from each receiver [5].

Internet of Things
The Internet of Things (IoT) is a network of physical devices which have the ability

According to Ericsson, by the end of 2022

thing, that the Internet of Things is going to get a substantial momentum over the
next few years. A survey done by International Data Corporation (IDC) shows out
2300 industry executives over 48% have already implemented some IoT solution(s)
in their company.
Figure 3.1.1: Basic IoT Architecture
The basic architecture of IoT shown in ﬁgure 3.1.1 can be divided into four categories
devices or end nodes, gateways, internet, data centers, and application servers.
Devices or end nodes are usually built with sensors and actuators which can sense
or interact with the surrounding environment. These devices also contain a micro-
controller to process the data locally and some wireless module for the device to

transfer the information to the gateway. Gateways in IoT architectures are similar
to the base stations in mobile communication technology. They accept the incoming
wireless packet in some cases authenticate them and forward it over the IP backbone.
The information received by the gateways can be forwarded to the cloud using a

Based on diﬀerent characteristics, use cases, and IoT applications, IoT implementa-

Massive IoT refers to services or application which usually require a huge number

• Logistic, tracking and ﬂeet management
• Smart agriculture
• Smart cities
• Utility management e.g. Smart meters
On the other hand, critical IoT demands ultra reliable, highly available and a very

• Remote healthcare
• Vehicle-to-Vehicle communication
• Smart grid automation
• Industrial application and control
• Military applications e.g. unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) control
Irrespective of the application type, IoT networks should be able to provide connec-

of the diﬀerence between traditional cellular network and IoT network is the ability
of the IoT network to provide deep indoor coverage [13, 14, 15].

3.3
LoRaWAN
LoRaWAN is a Media Access Control protocol (MAC) protocol that allows low-
powered devices or end nodes to communicate with their application server over the
wireless communication link. LoRaWAN uses LoRa as a wireless layer technology
and is a proprietary physical layer protocol developed by Semtech. LoRa uses Chirp
spread spectrum as a modulation technique. This modulation technique is used by
military and space organizations around the world because of its range of commu-
nication. Signals modulated with Chirp Spread Spectrum are capable of traveling
a long distance with much lower interference. A single gateway with LoRa modu-
lation is enough to provide coverage to a city or several hundred square kilometers.
LoRaWAN is a non-cellular LPWAN technology and therefore is operated in ISM
band. In Europe, LoRaWAN operates in 863-870 MHz with diﬀerent uplink and
downlink subbands. In the US LoRaWAN operates in 902-928 MHz frequency band
whereas in Australia it uses 915-928MHz while in China LoRaWAN operates on
779-787MHz and 470-510 MHz [19].
3.3.1
LoRaWAN Network Architecture
Figure 3.3.1: LoRa Architecture
Most of the sensor wireless technologies use a mesh architecture in order to increase
the range of the network, but one of the drawbacks of this implementation is the end
nodes need to have routing and packet forwarding capabilities. This could aﬀect the
battery life of the device. Figure 3.3.1 shows LoRaWAN network architecture. To
avoid that LoRaWAN uses a star topology, where the end nodes are connected to a
device called as LoRaWAN gateways and these LoRaWAN gateways are then con-
nected to a network server. The Network server and the gateway can be connected
using a wired or a wireless backbone link. Network server then can be connected to
the Application server. Let’s take a look at each of these components one by one.
17
==PAGE:29==
3. State of the Art: LPWAN Technology
• End Nodes:These are the devices having sensors and actuators which can
interact with surroundings. A typical example of such a device could be a
smoke alarm, smart water meter, etc.
They are typically battery powered
devices. Depending upon the communication needs of the device, LoRaWAN
classiﬁes end devices into three subcategories Class A, Class B, and Class C.
We will take a look at them later in this chapter.
• Gateway: LoRaWAN Gateways are the devices with a transceiver installed
into them. The basic function of these devices is to listen on available LoRa
wireless channels and accept the incoming transmission and forward the re-
ceived information to a Network server.
In LoRaWAN end nodes are not
associated with a particular gateway, usually, all the gateways in the range of
the end node can receive the transmitted packet and then all of them forward
the same information to the Network server.
• Network Server: Network server is the one which handles all the complex
function. As an example network server ﬁlters the received multiple duplicate
messages from the gateway, authenticates the message, schedules acknowledg-
ment to the most suitable gateway, performs any downlink transmission and
also routes the data to respective application server.
• Application Server: Application server is the one usually deployed by the
developer, It has software running to understand the incoming data and to
perform an action over it, e.g. pushing notiﬁcation to the user in case of an
alert triggered by the device
3.3.2
LoRaWAN Device Classes
Diﬀerent IoT applications have diﬀerent data exchange requirement.
This has
mainly to do with the applications they are used for and the battery requirements
for the same. Therefore most of these devices are pre-conﬁgured with diﬀerent LoRa
classes. Depending upon how and when these devices receive the downlink packets
determines whether they are Class A, Class B or Class C devices [20].
1. Class A:
Every LoRa end-device is has a basic class implementation of Class A. Gen-
erally the devices with very low downlink data frequency are implemented
with class A. This ensures very low energy consumption, and this ﬁts per-
fectly with low power requirements of the devices. In class A devices, once the
uplink transmission is done, the device waits for two short downlink receiver
windows, RX1 and RX2. Figure 3.3.2 shows the transmission between the
end-device and the network server. The device listens for a downlink trans-
mission RX1 after a delay of RXDelay1 on the same channel as that of uplink
frequency. The window RX2 is opened after an interval of RXDelay2 after the
uplink transmission. In the case of EU frequency 863-870 MHz, the value of
RXDelay1 and RXDelay2 are 1s and 2s respectively.

NB-IoT
Narrow-Band Internet of Things(NB-IoT) is a technology introduced by 3GPP in
the LTE release 13 which is capable of providing LTE based wide area coverage
for LPWAN IoT devices. NB-IoT is designed in a way such that it can provide
24
==PAGE:36==
3. State of the Art: LPWAN Technology
ﬂexibility, longer battery life, massive IoT devices support and a signiﬁcant increase
over the existing LTE coverage. NB-IoT is designed in a way so that it can co-exist
with existing LTE infrastructure or even with the GSM architecture. NB-IoT, when
deployed on LTE network, uses a 180 KHz carrier for uplink as well as downlink
transmission. NB-IoT uses licensed frequency for communication, and this allows
the use of the devices for real-time applications, e.g. video surveillance camera. Use
of licensed frequency also makes communication more secure by avoiding frequency
jamming. It also allows operators to guarantee Quality of Service(QoS), Quality of
Experience (QoE), bit rate and end-to-end delay in the network. Unlike LoRaWAN
and SigFox use of licensed frequency helps in having no restrictions on the trans-
mission power and the frequency of transmission. To have a complete worldwide
coverage of NB-IoT device should support a minimum of 6 bands 2,3,5,8,20 and
28 [33] [34]. Also to support roaming services between operators of same or diﬀer-
ent countries NB-IoT operators must deploy speciﬁc feature set, e.g. Power Saving
Mode (PSM) conﬁguration, Extended Discontinuous Reception (eDRX) etc.
3.5.1
Network Architecture
Figure 3.5.1: NB-IoT Network Architecture
Nb-IoT architecture is very similar to LTE and developed on top of the architecture
of the Evolved packet system(EPS). NB-IoT architecture deﬁnes two new optimiza-
tions for Cellular Internet of Things (C-IoT), optimizations in control plane and
optimization in the user plane. Depending upon how the operator has decided to
deploy NB-IoT, there are two ways deﬁned by 3GPP in which User Equipment (UE)
can attach to the network.
Attach With Packet Data Network (PDN) connection: This is the tradi-
tional way of attaching UE to any of the 3GPP EPS networks, In this type of setup,
UE is supposed to request for a PDN connection(IP address). This is not really
optimized for the battery-powered UE’s since it requires UE to ask for an IP ad-
dress every time it wakes up from the long deep sleep (above Power Saving Mode
Timer(PSM)) and wants to transmit the data.
Attach Without PDN connection:It is a new feature deﬁned in NB-IoT stan-
dards allowing UE’s to send information and remain attached to the network with-
25
==PAGE:37==
3. State of the Art: LPWAN Technology
out a PDN connection. In this type of connection, only SMS service is available.
Although, the UE can use SMS capabilities to establish a PDN connection.
Optimizations in C-IoT also supports in diﬀerent ways in which the UE can send
the data. NB-IoT supports data transfer using User-Datagram-Protocol (UDP) as
well as Transmission Control Protocol (TCP).
IP over Control plane: This allows UEs, which occasionally transmit a short
amount of data to use the control plane resources to send data to the Mobility
Management Entity (MME). This helps reduce the total amount of control signals
and air time required for the transmission of data.
IP over User Plane: This allows UE’s to spend a signiﬁcant amount of data over
the user plane. This could be more power eﬃcient than sending chunks of data
multiple times over the control plane.
In NB-IoT, Evolved UMTS terrestrial radio access network (E-UTRAN) is used in
order to manage the connections between UE and MME. UE is connected to eN-
odeB’s using C-IoT Uu air interface. In case of IP over control plane, MME transfers
the data to Serving Gateway (SGW) which forwards data to Packet Data Network
Gateway (PGW) and then it is transferred to the application server on the inter-
net. In the case of non-PDN connection, a new service capability exposure function
(SCEF) is deﬁned in NB-IoT. SCEF allows application servers to use network API
to access data sent by the UE. In case of IP over User Plane connection, SGW and
PGW can handle both IP and Non-IP connections to the application server [38].

4.2
System Algorithm
Figure 4.2.1: System Algorithm for Bike Tracking
The prototype was designed considering the application of it as to be a bike anti-theft
tracking device. After considering diﬀerent implementation ideas algorithm in ﬁgure
4.2.1, was developed. The idea was to make the device aware of the surrounding
environment and trigger actions based on that. The development board used in
building this prototype is Pycom SiPy and Pytack, Pytack comes with an onboard
3-axis accelerometer and a Qualtel L76-L GNSS module. This accelerometer is used
in order to monitor the motion of the bike. When the development board is powered
on it keeps on checking its orientation in space and stores the accelerometer reading
in the memory. After a regular interval of time, the microcontroller in SiPy checks
for the new reading from the accelerometer and compares them with the previously
stored value. If both values are the same with some minute variations, it goes back
to idle mode and keeps on repeating the same procedure again and again until the
accelerometer reading changes. The microcontroller allows a minute variations in the
32
==PAGE:44==
4. Development board based prototype design & implementation
reading. This is because the accelerometer reading is very precise and is subjected
to change due to a small vibration, therefore it is done to avoid false triggers. If
the microcontroller detects signiﬁcant changes in the accelerometer reading, it starts
the BLE advertisement. The BLE advertisements are broadcasted for 30 seconds.
Meanwhile, the microcontroller is waiting for the bike owner’s smartphone to connect
with the Bluetooth. After the advertisement, the period is over, and if the BLE
connection is successful with the user’s smartphone, the microcontroller goes into
a loop where it checks for the BLE connection status periodically. This is done in
order to detect the duration of the bike ride. After the bike ride is over the user
locks the bike and walks away from the bike forcing BLE connection to break, this
is when the microcontroller decides to go back idle mode and waits for the vibration
detection.
If after the vibration detection BLE veriﬁcation fails, the microcontroller starts
GNSS module. The GNSS module in Pytrack extension shield has support for GPS,
GLONASS, Galileo, and QZSS based location services. Therefore, after the BLE
veriﬁcation has failed microcontroller waits for GNSS module to get a location ﬁx.
After getting the Location value, the microcontroller sends this information using
SigFox module. If GNSS fails to get a location ﬁx in 60 seconds, the microcontroller
sends an empty SigFox message. In this case, the location is calculated using the
ATLAS positioning service provided by SigFox. After the SigFox message is sent
the microcontroller goes into sleep for 10 minutes, wakes up again, tries to fetches
the location and sends the position information to SigFox.
4.3
System State Diagram
System state diagram in ﬁgure 4.3.1 is a representation of all the states the device
enters into. As soon as the device is powered on it is in an "idle" state. As long as
there are no vibrations detected by the accelerometer the devices will stay in "idle"
state. As soon as the accelerometer detects vibrations the device changes the states
and enters into "waiting for BLE connection" state. The device is expected to be in
this state for 30 seconds after which based on the BLE connection status device can
jump into two diﬀerent states. In case the BLE connection with the user’s smart-
phone is established the device enters into "waiting for BLE to disconnect"state.
The device stays in this state as long as the user is riding the bike and the BLE is
connected, as soon as the bike ride is over the devices goes back to "idle" state. If
after waiting for 30 seconds the BLE veriﬁcation with the user’s smartphone fails,
the device jumps into "waiting for GPS lock " state. The device spends a maximum
of 60 seconds in this state and then enter into a "send data using SigFox" state. The
device sends location information in this state and enters into "Waiting for Sigox
duty cycle" state. The device stays in this state for 10 minutes. This state duration
is based on the assumption that the device is registered with SigFox’s Platinum
subscription which allows about 140 messages per day, making it possible to send a
new message every 10 minutes. Once the waiting time is over the device goes back
to the "waiting for GPS lock" state and cycles through it over and over again unless
it’s been manually restarted.

5.2
System Algorithm
Figure 5.2.1: Revised System Algorithm for Bike Tracking
According to the validation tests done in section 4.5, System algorithm developed
for the ﬁrst prototype was already performing as required by the design criteria (dis-
cussed in section 2.2). The only functionality missing was the ability of the device
to enter into the deep sleep mode. Deep sleep mode is an ability of the electronic
module to enter into lowest possible power consumption state. The algorithm de-
veloped for the second prototype has this functionality built into it. In order to
achieve long battery life, it is important to put the device in a deep sleep for as long
46
==PAGE:58==
5. Custom hardware based prototype design & implementation
as possible. The algorithm in ﬁgure 5.2.1 tries to do just that. When the device is
powered on for the ﬁrst time, it goes straight into deep sleep mode. The only way to
wake device backup is to either to reset the microcontroller or by using a hardware
interrupt to the microcontroller. The ATmega 328P supports two separate hardware
interrupts one of which is attached to the vibration sensor. The vibration sensor
detects the motion around the device and triggers the interrupt. As soon as the
interrupt is triggered the microcontroller wakes up from deep sleep and checks for a
reason of the interrupt. This is done by checking the status of ﬂag values stored in
the ROM of the microcontroller before going entering into deep sleep mode. Once
the microcontroller is awake and conﬁrms the bike was not stolen before waking up
from the deep sleep, it turns on the BLE module and waits for 30 seconds. The
BLE module starts advertising the device name and waits for the user’s smartphone
to be paired. If the user manages to pair to the BLE, the BLE module makes its
status pin HIGH. This status pin is attached to the microcontroller. After the 30
seconds, the waiting period is over the microcontroller reads the value on this pin. If
the value is HIGH, it authenticates the user otherwise if the microcontroller enters
into the tracking mode.
If the authentication is successful the microcontroller enters into deep sleep mode
again. However, in this case, the microcontroller uses internal Watch Dog Timer
(WDT) to wake up from the sleep. WDT can be set up for a maximum of 8 seconds.
Therefore microcontroller wakes up from a deep sleep every 8 seconds. After waking
up, microcontroller needs to know if the user has stopped riding the bike or not. This
check is performed in order to decide whether to go in deep sleep mode permanently
or to go in deep sleep using WDT. In order to decide that there was a need to
use a secondary vibration sensor. Therefore the microcontroller uses this secondary
vibration sensor to determine the duration of the bike ride. When there are no more
vibration readings from the secondary vibration sensor, the microcontroller goes into
deep sleep and can only be woken up by using external hardware interrupt. The
primary vibration sensor cannot be used for ride duration because the pin where it
is connected is disabled. This is done to avoid multiple registrations of the same
external interrupt.
If the BLE authentication fails the microcontroller turns on the GNSS module.
Then it waits until the module gets a location ﬁx, if after one minute the module
still does not have a location ﬁx then the microcontroller sends an empty message
using SigFox backend and relay on SigFox’s geolocation service ATLAS for location
data. If the GNSS Module gets a location ﬁx, then the microcontroller will send the
latitude and longitude data in the SigFox message. The device will be in the same
loop until it’s been restarted externally.

System state diagram in ﬁgure 5.3.1 represents all possible states the device could
be in.
Initially when the device is ﬁrst powered on it straightaway enters into
"Deepsleep" state. The device will stay in this state as long as the microcontroller
does not receive any hardware interrupt. As soon as the device receives a hardware
interrupt it enters into "Waiting for BLE connection" state, the microcontroller waits

N-Channel MOSFET as a Switch
Metal Oxide Semiconductor Field Eﬀect Transistor (MOSFET) is widely used in
electronic devices for switching and ampliﬁcation of electronic signals. MOSFET
is a three terminal device Gate, Source and Drain as can be seen in ﬁgure 5.4.6.
MOSFET when operated in an enhanced mode, the voltage applied at the gate of
MOSFET can be used for turning ON or OFF the conductivity of the MOSFET. In
other words, if there is no voltage at the gate of the MOSFET, the device does not
conduct thus act as an open switch. Similarly, when there is voltage at the gate of the
MOSFET which is higher than a threshold voltage, it starts conducting, similar to a
closed switch. This property of MOSFET is used in this prototype to switch ON and
OFF diﬀerent modules like BLE, GNSS and SigFox using a microcontroller output.
Although all the selected components were chosen for the prototyping support sleep
mode, by adding a MOSFET before the component practically makes them consume
zero current when not needed. Although, each MOSFET has some leakage current

the voltage at the pin should be HIGH, to achieve this the pin is connected to VCC

VCC and GND when the vibration switch detects the vibration it connects the
interrupt pin to ground. Since interrupt pin is connected to VCC, this creates a
short circuit and can damage the microcontroller. Therefore there is a register to
limit the ﬂow of current. As soon as there are vibrations are detected by vibration

Conclusion
This thesis tries to provide a solution which has the potential to solve the every-
day challenges and fears of a bike owner in terms of security of their bike. The
solution provided is the development of an end to end bike tracking system based
on relatively newly introduced LPWAN IoT technology SigFox. The asset tracking
solution has been designed after performing an extensive study of already available
market oﬀerings and recommendations from the insurance companies.
Most of the anti-theft bike tracking solutions available in the market more or less
are based on a design principle. Also, the choice of the technologies like the use
of GNSS and GSM by these manufacturer reduces the battery life of the tracker
signiﬁcantly. Therefore to improve the power eﬃciency of the tracker LPWAN net-
work technologies, LoRaWAN, SigFox and NB-IoT were analyzed. After analyzing
the diﬀerent technologies and implementation techniques, the design criteria for the
prototype was described. The idea was to develop a SigFox based anti-theft bike
tracking solution which is capable of providing at least three years of battery life,
does not require user interaction to turn ON and OFF the device and is extremely
cost-eﬃcient. The primary reasons to choose SigFox were, availability of SigFox
coverage in 70 countries and SigFox provide GNSS less location detection system
ATLAS.
A total of two diﬀerent prototypes were developed in the thesis. The ﬁrst prototype
was based on IoT development boards manufactured by Pycom, SiPy, and Pytrack.
The tracker was implemented based on the system algorithm developed in section
4.2.
After performing through end to end test, it was found out the device is
capable of working as per the functionality but has too high power consumption
and lack of ability to control individual hardware components.
This resulted in
the development of the second prototype, where each hardware component was
chosen very carefully and primarily based on the power consumption speciﬁcation
and cost of implementation. These selection criteria led towards the development of
a complete tracking system which was both cost-eﬀective and provides long battery
life.
After the implementation of the prototype, the devices were used to perform SigFox
coverage tests and ATLAS based location accuracy tests in the university campus
as well as in Copenhagen. Based on the results it was found out that the SigFox
ATLAS location system can provide a high level of accuracy when the devices are in
close vicinity of the SigFox base station, but otherwise the location accuracy can be
up to several kilometers. Also, calculation accuracy solely based on RSSI values in
the received messages can lead to providing a less accurate location. Based on the
coverage test results around campus out of 129 sent SigFox messages, 54 messages

8.1
Future Work
An obvious future step would be to design a PCB to accommodate all the diﬀerent
modules used in the second prototype and do thorough testing to discover hidden
ﬂaws in the implementation and design of the prototype. Also to implement SigFox
downlink message to reset the device remotely if the device enters into tracking
mode accidentally. Another area for improvement is to design the smartphone app
which is capable of receiving notiﬁcations and the location on a map from the
web server developed in this project.
The app can also be used for automatic
pairing with the BLE module used in this project.
It would also be interesting
to investigate alternative antenna designs to ﬁnd out if the signal quality of the
transmitted messages can be improved.
Additionally, it would be interesting to build the same device using NB-IoT and
LoRaWAN, and then compare the network performance and coverage of all three
technologies. Also, NB-IoT in 3GPP release 14 will add support for GNSS-less loca-
tion detection feature called as Observed Time Diﬀerence Of Arrival (OTDOA) with
accuracy up to 50 meters [62]. LoraWAN is also adding RSSI and Time Diﬀerence
of Arrival(TODA) based location detection technique and is accurate up to 20-200
meters [63]. Therefore it would be interesting to compare the GNSS-less location
accuracy of all three LPWAN technologies. Apart from LPWAN based tracking, it
would be interesting to investigate the use of surrounding Wi-Fi access points’ data
for location detection. This method can provide additional information in order to
either provide selfstanding geolocation or complement existing GNSS or LPWAN
location technologies.
