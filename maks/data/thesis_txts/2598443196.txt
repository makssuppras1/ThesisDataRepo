Abstract 
 
CD4+ T cells play critical roles in the orchestration and effectuation of anti-tumor immunity, 
including assisting cytotoxic CD8+ T cell responses. This thesis evaluates the capacity of a 
liposomal-based nanoparticle vaccine to stimulate cognate CD4+ T cells in vitro and in vivo 
in the syngeneic, murine B16-OVA melanoma model. The liposomal nanoparticles carry a 
toll-like receptor 7 agonist and a CD4+ epitope (OVA323-339) that is recognized by CD4+ T 
cells from TCR-transgenic OT-II mice. 
 
Both in vitro and in vivo, the liposomal nanoparticles induced maturation of antigen-
presenting cells. Nanoparticle-treated dendritic cells stimulated proliferation of cognate OT-
II CD4+ T cells in vitro, demonstrating that the CD4+ epitope remained functional after 
formulation into liposomes. When the efficacy of the nanoparticles was evaluated in vivo, 
intratumoral vaccination mediated a transient delay in tumor growth as opposed to systemic 
vaccination. This in situ delivery approach activated both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells in the 
tumor-draining lymph node and tumor, where an increased frequency of tumor antigen 
specific CD8+ T cells was also detected. Post-transfer vaccination has been demonstrated 
to enhance the persistence of adoptively transferred T cells, which correlates with clinical 
response to adoptive T cell therapy. Here, systemic vaccination was combined with adoptive 
transfer of OT-II CD4+ T cells hypothesizing that the liposomal nanoparticles would provide 
in vivo re-stimulation of the cells and lead to enhanced anti-tumor efficacy. Prior to transfer, 
the OT-II CD4+ T cells were polarized toward a Th9 phenotype using a 4-day in vitro 
differentiation protocol that was established as part of this project. Successful Th9 
differentiation was evaluated based on IL-9 and IL-21 production and lack of IFN𝛾 
expression. However, in this preliminary study, the combination of systemic vaccination and 
adoptive transfer of OT-II Th9 cells only had a minor negative effect on tumor growth. 
 
The liposomal nanoparticle vaccine remains to demonstrate its full in vivo potential in this 
context. Future studies will investigate increasing the dose of OT-II Th9 cells and combine 
cell transfer with intratumoral rather than systemic vaccination, and hopefully contribute to 
the growing body of literature that support priming of tumor reactive CD4+ T cell responses 
for more effective cancer immunotherapy.

1. Introduction 
Immune involvement in cancer development was first suggested by Paul Ehrlich in 1909 [1]. 
Almost fifty years later, Thomas and Burnet proposed the immunosurveillance hypothesis 
stating that the immune system can surveil and protect host tissues from development of 
malignant disease [2-4]. Due to insufficient experimental evidence, the hypothesis was 
largely discredited until the 1990s, where the concept was revisited [5]. Since then, 
extensive work has established that T cells can recognize and eliminate malignant cells and 
have the potential to generate immunological memory that provide long-term protection. 
This knowledge has pioneered the breakthrough of cancer immunotherapy and reignited the 
prospects of curing late-stage cancer patients.  
In the 21st century, cancer ranks as the 1st or 2nd leading cause of premature death in 134 
countries [6]. Surgery, radiation therapy and chemotherapy are widely applied and may be 
curative for treating primary tumors. Yet, these therapies rarely cure or protect from 
metastatic spread of disease, which is responsible for most cancer related deaths [7, 8]. The 
discovery of immune checkpoint blockade, for which the Nobel prize in medicine was 
awarded in 2018 [9], has been an extraordinary turning point. Today, immune checkpoint 
inhibitors (ICIs) have been approved as first- or second-line treatments for multiple solid 
cancers [10]. Advanced melanoma patients receiving combined treatment with the ICIs, anti-
programmed cell death protein 1(PD-1) and anti-cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated 
protein 4 (CTLA-4), have a five-year overall survival rate of 52% [11] compared to 
approximately 10% historically [12, 13]. 
Still, only a subset of patients benefits from ICIs. Clinical response correlates with a high 
tumor mutational burden [14], which may associate with greater immunogenicity and higher 
levels of tumor-reactive T cells that can be re-invigorated by ICIs [15-17]. Numerous novel 
immunotherapies 
are 
underway 
including 
oncolytic

T 
cell 
targeted 
immunomodulators, therapeutic cancer vaccines and cell-based therapies, the latter 
accounting for the largest number of active immune-oncology drugs in development in 2019 
[18]. However, as many new drugs fail as monotherapies, it is increasingly evident that 
synergistic combination treatments may hold the key to future curative cancer therapy [19].

1.1. The Cancer Immunity Cycle 
Generation of an adaptive immune response through mobilization of T cells is fundamental 
in anti-tumor immunity. Here, T cells recognize peptide antigens presented on major 
histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules on tumor cells, and this enables antigen-
specific elimination of malignant cells and establishment of immunological memory [20]. The 
immune recognition and killing of tumor cells are described in the cancer immunity cycle 
[21], represented in Fig. 1.

3) subsequently migrate to the secondary lymphoid organs. Here, dendritic cells present tumor antigens to 
cognate naïve T cells. Activated T cells 4) traffic to and 5) infiltrate the tumor. In the tumor, T cells 6) recognize 
their cognate antigen on major histocompatibility molecules and 7) induce killing of tumor cells. The release of 
new tumor antigens propagates the cycle. Abbreviations:  APCs, Antigen-presenting cells; CTLs, Cytotoxic T 
lymphocytes. Figure is adapted from Chen et al. [21].

Dendritic cells (DCs) bridge the gap between innate and adaptive immunity and are present 
in tissues, blood and the secondary lymphoid organs [22]. Whereas all nucleated cells 
express MHC class I molecules, only professional antigen-presenting cells (APCs) including 
DCs, macrophages and B-cells express MHC class II. In the tumor, DCs can sample and 
process antigens that may be released from dying tumor cells. Depending on the mode of 
death, dying tumor cells may also release danger-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) 
that trigger pattern-recognition receptors (PRRs) on tissue-resident DCs [23]. The 
downstream signaling induces DC maturation and upregulation of MHC class I/II and co-
stimulatory molecules. Activated DCs also secrete inflammatory cytokines and chemokines 
that may activate tissue-resident cells and attract peripheral immune cells to the tumor [24].  
 
Upon upregulation of C-C chemokine receptor (CCR)7, DCs can migrate to the tumor-
draining lymph node (tdLN) [25-27]. Different subclasses of DCs have specialized functions. 
Overall, DCs can be divided into plasmacytoid (pDCs) and conventional (cDCs) DCs, which 
are further subdivided in cDC1s and cDC2s [28, 29]. Whereas cDC1s excel at cross-
presenting exogenously derived antigens on MHC class I molecules to CD8+ T cells [30-32], 
cDC2s are important for antigen-presentation on MHC class II molecules to CD4+ T cells 
and promoting CD4+ T cell-driven anti-tumor immunity [33]. After an expansion phase, 
activated T cells can traffic to and infiltrate the tumor. Here, cytotoxic T-lymphocytes (CTLs) 
kill cancer cells in an antigen-specific manner via the perforin/granzyme and Fas/Fas ligand 
pathways [34].  
 
The release of new antigens from dying tumor cells propagates the cycle [21] and may prime 
a novel T cell repertoire against previously untargeted tumor antigens. This process is 
termed epitope spreading and may prevent tumor escape by broadening the range of 
targeted tumor antigens [35-37]. Ideally, the cancer immunity cycle should drive a potent 
anti-tumor response and generate tumor-specific memory T cells that provide long-lasting 
immunity against the malignancy. Conversely, the cycle is frequently disrupted in cancer 
patients. Immunotherapeutic interventions aim to restore the cancer immunity cycle and 
drive anti-tumor responses potent enough to induce disease regression.

1.2. CD4+ T cells in the anti-tumor response 
The conventional cancer immunity cycle outlined above is centered around CD8+ T cells, 
which have been a key topic in the field of immune oncology for the past decades. CD8+ 
CTLs have been considered as the key mediators of cancer immunotherapy due to their 
cytotoxic activity against tumor cells. In contrast, the primary role of CD4+ T cells has been 
believed to lie in their ability to assist the CD8+ T cell response. Yet, accumulating evidence 
also points towards a critical role of CD4+ T cells in the orchestration and effectuation of 
anti-tumor immunity. Multiple studies have demonstrated that CD4+ T cells not only augment 
anti-tumor CD8+ T cell responses [38-42], but also confer protective anti-tumor immunity on 
their own [43-46]. 
 
1.2.1. Help for CD8+ T cells 
Priming of CD8+ T cells requires 1) T cell receptor (TCR) stimulation via recognition of 
peptide:MHC class I complexes, 2) activation of co-stimulatory receptors and 3) cytokine 
receptor signaling [47]. CD4+ T cells support the activation of CD8+ T cells, as well as their 
differentiation into effector and memory cells [48-52]. DCs activated by innate signals may

induce primary CD8+ T cell responses, but in the absence of CD4+ T cell help fail to generate 
proper memory formation.  
 
In recent years, a two-step model of T cell priming has emerged, where CD4+ T cell help is 
delivered in the second step [51]. First, naïve CD4+ and CD8+ T cells are activated by distinct 
populations of migratory cDCs in the secondary lymphoid organs. In the second step (Fig.2), 
cognate CD4+ and CD8+ T cells interact with the same lymph-node resident XCR1+ cDC1 
[51, 53, 54], which have received antigen-transfer from a migratory cDC1. A key pathway of 
CD4+ T cell help is delivered via engagement of CD40L with CD40 on the DC [51, 55, 56]. 
CD40L expression is upregulated on CD4+ T cells upon TCR/MHC class II restricted 
interaction. CD40 stimulation enhances antigen-presentation and expression of CD80 and 
CD86 that both activate the co-stimulatory receptor CD28 on T cells, which key for effective 
T cell priming [57]. Production of cytokines, including interleukin (IL)-12 and IL-15, is also 
enhanced and promote effector differentiation [51].  
 
In addition, CD4+ T cell help is mediated by CD70 [58, 59], which is upregulated on DCs 
upon CD40 stimulation. CD70 is the ligand for the co-stimulatory receptor CD27 present on 
both naïve CD4+ and CD8+ T cells. CD27 stimulation supports the clonal expansion of 
activated CD8+ T cells, effector and memory CD8+ T cell differentiation and improves the 
quality of the resulting anti-tumor response [42, 58-60]. Agonistic antibodies targeting CD40 
and CD27 are being explored as strategies to replicate CD4+ T cell help in vivo and have 
both demonstrated potential benefit in the clinic [61, 62]. 
 
1.2.2. Anti-tumor effects of CD4+ T cells in the tumor microenvironment 
CD4+ T cells are plastic and can acquire distinct effector T helper (Th) or regulatory 
phenotypes by responding to local cytokine cues. Th1 cells have an IFN-𝛾 effector cell 
signature and are well-studied, as their presence correlates with a good prognosis in human 
cancers [63]. In contrast, CD4+ regulatory T cells are immunosuppressive and have the 
opposite effect in many solid cancers [64].  
Tumor-infiltrating CD4+ T cells can function as local cytokine reservoirs. Th1 cells secrete 
IFN𝛾 upon recognizing their cognate antigen and promote a broad range of anti-tumor 
responses in the tumor microenvironment (Fig. 3) [65-67]. Overall, IFN𝛾 creates

inflammation that favors anti-tumor immunity and IFN𝛾-related gene expression in the tumor 
associates with better response to anti-PD-1 [68]. Moreover, mutations in IFN𝛾-pathway 
genes are correlate with lack of primary response and acquired resistance to ICIs [69-71].  
Specifically, IFN𝛾 can upregulate antigen-presentation on cancer cells and thus, enhance 
recognition by tumor-reactive T cells [72-74]. In the tumor microenvironment, IFN𝛾 can also 
inhibit angiogenesis [72, 75, 76] and tumor cell growth [75, 77].

Several studies have shown that CD4+ T cells can promote infiltration of CD8+ T cells [39, 
78, 79] and via IFN𝛾-secretion induce production of C-X-C chemokine ligand (CXCL)-9, 
CXCL10, CXCL11 by tissue-resident cells, which attract T cells and natural killer (NK) cells 
to the tumor [32, 78, 80].  
Murine studies have demonstrated that CD4+ T cells can both directly and indirectly induce 
killing of malignant cells. Most human tumors do not express MHC class II molecules [81]. 
Yet, IFN𝛾-induced signaling may increase MHC class II expression, which is associated with 
a favorable prognosis in several solid human cancers [82] and better response to ICIs [83-
86]. In the setting of MHC class II positive tumors in mice, CD4+ T cells can be directly 
cytotoxic and kill cancer cells in an antigen-specific manner via the perforin/granzyme B 
pathway [43, 44, 87, 88]. Although MHC class II molecules classically present exogenously 
derived peptides, autophagy can promote the presentation of intracellular peptides [89]. In 
humans, CD4+ CTLs have been studied mostly in the context of chronic viral infections [90-
93] and autoimmune diseases [94]. Although a study has identified CD4+ CTLs in the 
circulation of melanoma patients after anti-CTLA-4 treatment [95], their role in malignant 
diseases still remains largely unknown. Loss of HLA class I expression is a common cancer 
escape mechanism, by which tumors avoid recognition by CD8+ T cells [69, 96-98]. Thus, 
immunotherapies that harness the potential of CD4+ T cells could be explored in the context 
of MHC class I negative tumors. 
CD4+ T cells can also indirectly mediate killing of cancer cells by mechanisms dependent 
on NK cells [45, 99] or tumor-infiltrating macrophages (TAMs) [100, 101]. In the setting of 
MHC class II negative tumors, TAMs can sample and present tumor antigens on MHC class 
II molecules to cognate CD4+ T cells. This interaction combined with IFN𝛾-induced signaling 
activates and polarizes TAMs to a so-called M1-like phenotype, which can exhibit 
tumoricidal activity via secretion of nitric oxide [102]. 
 
1.3. Therapeutic cancer vaccines harnessing CD4+ T cell responses 
Therapeutic cancer vaccines are based on immunization with tumor antigen(s) to generate 
anti-tumor immune responses, notably by (re-)stimulation and expansion of tumor-reactive 
==PAGE:15==
 
8 
 
T cells. This can be both via activation of a naïve T cell repertoire and re-stimulation of 
memory T cells.  
 
Vaccines contain antigen(s) and an adjuvant, which is incorporated to induce activation and 
maturation of DCs. Adjuvants can simulate DAMPs or pattern-associated molecular patterns 
(PAMPs) and activate PRRs. Antigens can be formulated as full-length proteins, peptides, 
RNA, DNA or be part of a whole tumor preparation [103]. Peptides of 8-10 amino acids (aa) 
and 13-18 aa can bind directly to MHC class I and II molecules, respectively. Alternatively, 
vaccines can also deliver longer peptides of 20-30aa that include both CD4+ and CD8+ 
epitopes. Tumor antigens can be categorized as tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) or 
tumor-specific antigens (TSAs) [104]. TAAs are abnormally expressed self-antigens, which 
can be subdivided into overexpressed antigens, differentiation antigens and cancer-testis 
antigens. TSAs include viral antigens and neoantigens, both of which are non-self and not 
expressed by healthy tissue. Neoantigens are mutated self-antigens that arise from 
missense mutations that accumulate during oncogenesis. 
 
Trials with therapeutic cancer vaccines have often been successful in increasing the 
frequencies of circulating tumor-reactive T cells in patients. However, historically the 
majority of therapeutic cancer vaccines have failed to mediate disease regression [105, 
106]. Several studies have demonstrated that vaccination with antigens containing CD4+ 
epitopes is favorable both in pre-clinical and clinical settings. Furthermore, novel adjuvants 
and vaccine platforms are designed to induce stronger APC activation and repolarization of 
the tumor microenvironment. Collectively, these factors may contribute to increasing the 
clinical benefit from therapeutic cancer vaccination. 
 
1.3.1. Vaccines targeting CD4+ T cells  
In pre-clinical studies, inclusion of CD4+ T cell epitopes promotes the effector function, 
cytotoxicity and secondary expansion capacity of CD8+ T cells [38, 40, 42, 60, 107, 108]. In 
a model of therapeutic cancer vaccination, Ahrends and colleagues showed that CD4+ T cell 
help increased CD8+ T cell trafficking and tumor infiltration mediated by upregulated 
expression of chemokine receptors (CX3CR1 and CXCR4) and metalloproteases, 
respectively [42]. ‘Helped’ CD8+ T cells also had lower expression of inhibitory receptors, 
==PAGE:16==
 
9 
 
were more cytotoxic and exerted better tumor control. These effects were largely dependent 
on CD70, suggesting that the CD4+ T cell help was delivered during priming. 
 
In another study, the influence of CD4+ T cell help was directly evaluated in a clinical trial 
with stage III/IV melanoma patients. Patients were vaccinated with DCs pulsed with either 
MHC class I- or both MHC class I and II-restricted epitopes from the TAAs gp100 and 
tyrosinase [108]. Only vaccination with both MHC class I and II epitopes correlated with 
improved clinical outcome compared to the control group receiving chemotherapy.  
 
Vaccines focused on priming tumor reactive CD4+ T cells have also appeared promising in 
the clinic. In two phase I/II trials, late stage melanoma patients were vaccinated with six 
MCH class II-restricted peptides from different TAAs with or without granulocyte-
macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) [109-111]. Overall, the vaccine generated 
long-term responses and increased the 5-year overall survival rate to 57% versus 16% of 
matched paired controls [111], which was quite considerable in the years before the use of 
ICIs. Interestingly, the vaccine induced epitope spreading to TAA-specific CD8+ T cells with 
reactivity towards peptides that did not overlap with the vaccine peptides [112]. 
 
1.3.2. Predicted neoantigens raise CD4+ T cell responses 
High-throughput technologies and algorithms for predicting peptide:MHC binding affinities 
have pioneered the generation of neoantigen-based therapies and reinvigorated the 
promise of personalized cancer vaccination [103, 113]. Neoantigens are mutated self-
antigens and thus not subject to central tolerance mechanisms. Whereas most neoantigens 
are patient specific, some can be shared among a group of patients. 
 
A comprehensive study from 2015 tested neoantigen vaccination in several common murine 
cancer models [114]. The neoepitopes were selected based on predicted MHC class I 
binding affinity and synthesized as 27mer peptides with the mutation in position 14 or 
alternatively encoded as RNA. In all models, the vast majority of the immunogenic mutations 
raised CD4+ T cells responses. Vaccination with a single immunodominant neoepitope in 
the B16-F10 melanoma model exerted tumor control, which was dependent on CD4+ and 
not CD8+ T cells. In 2017, two clinical trials reported induction of durable and protective T 
==PAGE:17==
 
10 
 
cell responses in late-stage melanoma patients upon vaccination with multiple personal 
neoantigens delivered as long peptides [115] or RNA encoding 27mers [116]. Whereas Ott 
et al. [115] selected neoepitopes based on predicted MHC class I binding, Sahin et al. [116] 
applied both MHC class I and II prediction algorithms. Similar strategies were tested in two 
recent trials with glioblastoma [117, 118]. In all four trials, the neoantigens predominantly 
primed CD4+ rather than CD8+ T cell responses, which parallel the pre-clinical evidence. A 
potential explanation is that the MHC class II binding groove has open ends and allows more 
promiscuous binding of peptides [119].  
 
A confounding study from 2018 observed that frequent driver mutations in human cancers 
are universally predicted to bind poorly to MHC class II molecules [120], implying that tumors 
are under selection pressure against driver mutations that give rise to strongly binding CD4+ 
neoepitopes. Notably, there was a higher selective pressure against mutations binding 
strongly to MHC class II molecules than to MHC class I. These findings suggest that tumors 
evolve to avoid recognition by CD4+ T cells and emphasize the pivotal role of CD4+ T cells 
in anti-tumor immunity.  
 
1.4. Adoptive T cell therapy  
Adoptive T cell therapy (ACT) is based on infusion of ex vivo expanded tumor-reactive T 
cells. More than two decades ago, ACT with autologous tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) 
was pioneered by Steve Rosenberg and colleagues at the National Cancer Institute [121-
123]. Since then, TIL therapy has been successful in treating metastatic melanoma patients 
with objective response rates of 40-50% and 10-25% of patients experiencing complete 
tumor regression [124]. TILs are grown from surgically removed tumors and stimulated and 
expanded ex vivo prior to being reinfused to the patient (Fig.4A). As the T cells have been 
primed in vivo against the tumor, the expanded cell product is expected to harbor intrinsic 
tumor-reactivity. Before reinfusion, preconditioning in the form of cytotoxic chemotherapy is 
administered to the patient to induce transient lymphodepletion. It promotes engraftment of 
the transferred lymphocytes [123] and can diminish the competition for cytokines between 
the endogenous and reinfused cells [125, 126]. The adoptive transfer is followed by a dose 
of IL-2 to stimulate the proliferation and survival of the reinfused T cells. While TIL therapy

has been explored for multiple other solid cancers [127], it continues to be most efficacious 
for treating melanoma patients. 
 
Novel ACT therapies rely on genetic modification of the T cells ex vivo (Fig.4B). T cells can 
be readily harvested from the blood and genetically modified to express a cloned tumor 
specific T cell receptor (TCR) or synthetic chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) recognizing 
tumor surface antigens [124]. While CAR T cell therapy based on recognition of CD19 has 
been very efficient in treating B-cell malignancies [128], it remains challenging to realize the 
potential of CAR T cells to treat solid tumors [129]. To minimize the risk of on-target off-
tumor toxicity, efforts are undertaken to identify surface antigens that are tumor specific and 
not found on healthy tissues. Whereas CAR T cells can only recognize surface antigens, 
TCR-engineered T cells can target both surface and intracellular antigens. Off-target toxicity 
may also be a considerable concern, which became evident after a trial with T cells 
engineered to express an affinity-enhanced TCR targeting the cancer-testis antigen MAGE-

A3 [130]. Shortly after re-infusion, two patients succumbed to cardiogenic shock due to 
cross-reactivity of the T cells with an epitope expressed on contracting cardiac tissue.  
 
1.4.1. CD4+ T cells for adoptive T cell therapy 
The therapeutic potential of adoptive transfer of CD4+ T cells has been demonstrated in 
multiple murine models of ACT [43-45, 88, 131-133]. Additionally, the synergistic effects of 
concurrent transfer of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells have been explored. Co-transfer of CD4+ T 
cells increases the persistence, tumor infiltration and effector capacity of adoptively 
transferred CD8+ T cells [39, 41, 78, 79, 134]. In the clinic, CD4+ T cells have predominantly 
been transferred as part of a bulk TIL product. Nevertheless, several individual case studies 
have reported a significant clinical benefit from transfer of autologous tumor reactive CD4+ 
T cells. 
 
Some reports on TIL therapy associate a high number [135] or proportion [136] of CD8+ T 
cells in the reinfused product with a better objective response. These observations motivated 
a study, where two groups of metastatic melanoma patients received either CD8+ enriched 
TILs or bulk TILs, containing both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells [137]. Although the difference was 
insignificant, there was a tendency towards a higher objective response rate in the group 
receiving bulk TILs (12/34) versus the group receiving CD8+ enriched TILs (7/35). A potential 
contributing factor could be the lack of CD4+ T cell help in the CD8+ enriched TILs. A case 
study from 2012 reported substantial tumor regression in an advanced melanoma patient 
after reinfusion of TILs containing mostly CD4+ T cells with HLA-DR restricted tumor-
reactivity in vitro [138]. After a few months, the cancer regressed, and a recurrent lesion was 
surgically removed. The recurrent lesion was HLA-DR negative, implying that direct tumor-
recognition by the transferred CD4+ T cells had played a direct role in the tumor regression.  
 
TILs with reactivity towards TAAs, including MART-1 and gp100 [139, 140], were already 
identified more than two decades ago. Since then, next generation sequencing and high-
throughput screening methods have allowed researchers to determine the broader 
specificities of TIL repertoires [141-143]. Neoantigen-reactive CD8+ and CD4+ T cells are 
commonly found in TILs from patients with melanoma [142, 143] and epithelial cancers [144-
146] and are suggested to be central mediators of TIL therapy [143]. Melanomas have a 
==PAGE:20==
 
13 
 
high number of somatic mutations [147] and a high predicted neoantigen load associates 
with clinical benefit from TIL therapy [148]. Moreover, neoantigen-specific T cells have been 
found to persist in circulation of responding patients [142, 149, 150]. A patient with acral 
melanoma was reported to have a complete remission following TIL therapy despite of a 
very low tumor mutational burden [150]. Post re-infusion, there was a durable increase in 
peripheral CD4+ T cells recognizing the shared neoantigen BRAF600E. 
 
To increase tumor-reactivity, TILs can initially be divided into fragments and tested for 
reactivity towards TAAs or TSAs, including predicted neoantigens. Fragments containing 
tumor-reactive T cells can then be selected for expansion. Transfer of TILs enriched for 
neoantigen-recognition have mediated durable clinical responses in individual patients with 
metastatic colon [151] and breast cancer [152]. Similarly, a patient with metastatic bile-duct 
cancer was treated with autologous neoantigen-reactive CD4+ TILs [153]. The patient 
received two transfers containing 25% and >95%, polyclonal CD4+ T cells reactive towards 
a mutation in erbb2 interacting protein. More than a year after the first infusion, the second 
treatment was administered due to disease progression. Subsequent regression lasted 35 
months [143], clearly demonstrating the therapeutic potential of targeting neoantigen-
specific CD4+ T cells. 
 
Despite modest experience with adoptive transfer of CD4+ T cells in humans, the studies 
mentioned above support the pre-clinical evidence suggesting that CD4+ T cells can be 
critical for driving tumor regression in cancer immunotherapy. Nevertheless, future 
investigations are required to determine the therapeutic relevance of ACT with CD4+ T cells.  
 
1.4.2. Th9 cells in murine models of adoptive T cell therapy 
Current ACT therapies face challenges that limit the clinical benefit. During the prolonged 
period of ex vivo expansion, T cells may become exhausted and terminally differentiated 
and failure to persist after reinfusion is associated with worse patient outcome [154, 155]. 
Furthermore, the T cells may fail to infiltrate the tumor and undergo suppression in the tumor 
microenvironment. Pre-clinical studies with adoptive transfer of CD4+ T cells have illustrated 
that T helper cell differentiation during the ex vivo expansion can influence the efficacy of 
the cells upon reinfusion. With its IFN𝛾-secreting signature, Th1 cells have long been sought 
==PAGE:21==
 
14 
 
after as the optimal T helper subset for driving anti-tumor immunity [65-67]. However, recent 
murine studies have highlighted the superiority of Th17 [131, 156, 157] and subsequently, 
Th9 cells in the setting of ACT [88, 132, 133].  
 
ACT of Th9 cells have been investigated in models of adoptive transfer with antigen-specific 
T cells from TCR-transgenic animals. In a recent paper, Lu and colleagues compared the 
transfer of antigen-specific Th1, Th17 or Th9 cells in combination with a DC-based 
vaccination in the B16 and B16-OVA melanoma models [88]. While the transferred Th1 or 
Th17 cells only abrogated tumor growth or reduced tumor burden temporarily, the transfer 
of Th9 cells induced durable tumor regression and long-term survival, which was largely 
independent on CD8+ T cells. In contrast to the short lived Th1 cells, the transferred Th17 
and Th9 cells both had a high persistence in the tdLN and spleen. Subsequent analyses 
revealed that the transferred Th9 cells had a less exhausted, hyperproliferative and fully 
cytolytic effector cell signature. The Th17 cells had an ‘early-memory and/or stem cell like’ 
phenotype and didn’t exhibit similar cytotoxicity. 
 
Th9 cells are pro-inflammatory and characterized by secretion of IL-9 and IL-21. IL-4 and 
transforming growth factor-𝛽 (TGF-𝛽) are the key drivers of Th9 differentiation in vitro [158-
160]. Multiple other cytokines and ligand/receptor interactions can also further promote Th9 
differentiation [161] including IL-1𝛽 [162], tumor necrosis factor-𝛼 [163] and type I IFNs 
[164]. Th9 cells harbor a broad range of anti-tumor properties that can be predominantly IL-
9 [132, 133, 162] or IL-21 [162] dependent. Th9 cells have also been proposed to play a role 
in several autoimmune and allergic diseases and in anti-parasitic responses [165]. IL-9 can 
activate mast cells, which have been implicated as effector cells in Th9 immunity [132, 166]. 
Th9 cells also secrete IL-3 that can stimulate the survival of DCs [167]. In the B16-OVA 
pulmonary melanoma model, ACT of IL-9-secreting Th9 cells led to increased tumor 
recruitment of DCs in a CCR6-dependent manner [133, 168]. Th9 transfer also promoted 
expansion and infiltration of tumor antigen specific CD8+ T cells. These were suggested to 
be primed by migratory cDC1s carrying tumor antigens that were found in increasing 
numbers in the tdLN.  
 
==PAGE:22==
 
15 
 
CD4+ T cell derived IL-21 is known to be crucial for formation and persistence of cytotoxic 
CD8+ T cell responses that exert control of chronic viral infections [169-173] and tumors 
[173]. Furthermore, It has been shown that IL-21 secreted from Th9 cells promotes IFN𝛾 
secretion from both CD8+ T cells and NK cells [162].  
 
In addition, Th9 cells can be directly tumoricidal via the granzyme B/perforin pathway [88, 
132]. IL-9/IL-9R signaling has also been shown to induce apoptosis in certain tumor cells 
[174], however, it is still debated if this occurs in vivo in human cancers [175]. 
 
1.4.3. Th9 cells in human cancers 
There is limited research on the role of Th9 cells in human malignancies and the findings 
have been confounding. Originally, IL-9 was described as a growth factor for T cells [176] 
and mast cells [177]. IL-9 promotes tumorigenesis in several hematological cancers [178-
180] and has also been negatively implicated in mycosis fungoides, which is the most 
common form of cutaneous T cell lymphoma [181].  
 
Supported by the pre-clinical evidence above, Th9 cells are believed to be anti-tumorigenic 
in melanoma. Th9 cells have been identified in human metastatic melanoma lesions, yet in 
lower abundances than in healthy human skin [132]. Single nucleotide polymorphisms in the 
IL-9 gene associate with higher risk of cutaneous malignant melanoma, signifying a 
protective effect of IL-9 [182]. Moreover, melanoma patients responding to anti-PD-1 
therapy have increased frequencies of peripheral IL-9+ CD4+ T cells [183] and baseline 
serum level of IL-9 can be a predictive biomarker for response to TIL therapy [184]. 
 
Other investigations cautiously propose a potentially protective role of Th9 cells in colorectal 
[185], cervical [186] and breast cancer [187]. The latter study detected elevated levels of IL-
9 and IL-9 secreting CD4+ T cells in the circulation of breast cancer patients. The cytotoxicity 
of the isolated T cells towards autologous cancer cells in vitro was significantly lowered upon 
addition of IL-9 or IL-21 blocking antibodies. In contrast, Th9 cells have been associated 
with a pro-tumorigenic role in hepatocellular carcinoma [188] and malignant pleural effusion 
[189]. A study from 2012 observed that higher frequencies of pleural Th9 cells correlated 
==PAGE:23==
 
16 
 
with reduced overall survival in patients with malignant pleural effusion and that IL-9 
promoted the proliferation of lung cancer cells [189]. 
 
Currently, there is limited knowledge of Th9 cells and their anti- or pro-tumorigenic roles in 
human solid cancers. While targeting Th9 cells could have clinical benefit in certain cancers, 
such as melanoma, it remains to be explored in the clinic.  
 
1.5. Combining vaccination & adoptive T cell therapy 
In cancer patients receiving ACT, persistence of the re-infused T cells correlates with a 
better outcome [154, 190, 191]. Providing in vivo re-stimulation of the transferred cells is a 
strategy to promote T cell persistence and hence, also response to ACT. Pre-clinical studies 
have demonstrated that antigen-specific post-transfer vaccination can enhance the 
persistence, expansion, tumor infiltration and functionality of adoptively transferred T cells, 
leading to enhanced anti-tumor responses and tumor regression [192-194]. Combining 
vaccination and ACT has also been explored in early clinical trials that have demonstrated 
both safety and feasibility, although the effects have been modest [195-198]. Yet, in two 
recent trials, researchers reported an increase in the persistence [199] or polyfunctionality 
[198] of the transferred T cells following vaccination. Furthermore, in a phase II trial, patients 
with hepatocellular carcinoma were treated post-operatively with adoptive transfer of 
autologous activated T cells and DCs pulsed with tumor lysate [200]. The treatment resulted 
in a significant improvement of 5-year recurrence-free survival and overall survival 
compared to patients only receiving the curative operation.

3.5. In vivo studies 
3.5.1. Cancer cell line and tumor challenge

Tumor volume (mm3) = Length (mm) x (Width (mm))2 x 0.5

4. Results 
This thesis evaluates the anti-tumor properties of a liposomal nanoparticle vaccine and its 
capacity to prime cognate CD4+ T cells in vitro and in vivo. The liposomal nanoparticles 
carry an adjuvant in the form of a lipidated TLR7 agonist, TMX, and a CD4+ epitope (OVA323-
339) that is anchored to the surface via cholesterol moieties (Fig. 5A). TLR7 is an intracellular 
PRR located in the endosomal compartment and recognizes viral single stranded RNA [201, 
202]. TLR ligands are being extensively explored as adjuvants in immunotherapies [203] to 
stimulate APCs and other immune cells. The antigen is linked to the cholesterol via a 
reducible linker that is cleaved in the lysosomal compartment upon cellular uptake, 
facilitating antigen release. The nanoparticles are PEGylated and thus, classified as ‘Stealth’ 
liposomes [204]. While i.v. injected liposomes accumulate rapidly in the spleen and liver, 
PEGylation can increase circulation time as well as lymph node and tumor delivery [204-
206].  
 
The liposomal nanoparticles are designated ‘Stealth:TMX’ and ‘Stealth:TMX:AC003’, the 
latter carrying the CD4+ epitope (OVA323-339). Fig. 5B lists the physiochemical properties of 
the liposomes including size, polydispersity and zeta-potential as well as the concentration 
of lipid, antigen and adjuvant. This highly versatile vaccine platform is designed for systemic 
administration and thus, overcomes vaccine depot formation that can lead to local T cell 
aggregation and apoptosis in response to chronic antigen-exposure at the vaccination site 
[207]. Furthermore, a systemic RNA-lipoplex vaccine inducing TLR7 adjuvant effects has 
recently been demonstrated to prime tumor-reactive T cell responses preclinically [208, 209] 
and is currently investigated in a phase I/II clinical trial (ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: 
NCT02410733).

evaluated in vitro and in vivo in the context of CD8+ T cell anti-tumor immunity in previous 
studies from the group.

investigations point towards a role of the formation of anti-PEG antibodies, which induce

injection.

therapeutic effect in vivo and allows for repeated dosing by limiting the systemic exposure 
of the liposomes.

4.5. Establishing a Th9 differentiation and expansion protocol 
In previous publications, IL-4 and TGF-𝛽 have been used to induce Th9 differentiation of 
CD4+ T cells in vitro [158-160]. Activation of their respective cytokine receptors triggers a 
complex signaling network and transcription factors including PU.1, IRF1/4/8 and BATF, 
which drive Th9 differentiation and secretion of IL-9 and IL-21 [175, 210, 211]. Adding IL-1𝛽 
to this cocktail increases IL-21 production and enhances the anti-tumor efficacy of adoptively 
transferred Th9 cells in an IL-21-dependent manner [162]. Blockade of IFN𝛾 is also a 
common feature of in vitro Th9 differentiation protocols, as IFN𝛾 can inhibit IL-9 production 
[158, 212]. An interesting paper from 2019 compared Th9 cells differentiated with IL-4 and 
anti-IFN𝛾 in combination with either TGF-𝛽 or IL-1𝛽 [213]. Th9 cells differentiated with IL-
1𝛽 were less exhausted and performed better in vivo. Collectively, this literature and a series 
of pilot studies laid the foundation for testing two Th9 differentiation cytokine cocktails: 
 
• Th9IL-4+IL-b 
IL-2 (20ng/mL), IL-4 (25ng/mL) and IL-1𝛽 (10ng/mL) 
 
• Th9IL-4+IL-b+TGF-b 
IL-2 (20ng/mL), IL-4 (25ng/mL), IL-1𝛽 (10ng/mL) and TGF-𝛽 (10ng/mL) 
 
Notably, none of the Th9 differentiation cocktails listed above include anti-IFN𝛾. To my 
knowledge, no study has reported that inclusion of anti-IFN𝛾 generates Th9 cells with 
enhanced efficacy in the setting of ACT. IL-2 was added to both cocktails to stimulate 
expansion of the cells. Th0 cells, only cultured with IL-2, were used as controls. 
 
The two cytokine cocktails were tested in a 4-day protocol for in vitro expansion of primary 
T cells. The protocol is illustrated in Fig. 10A and described in detail in method section 3.4.2. 
Briefly, splenic CD4+ T cells were isolated from WT C57bl/6JRj mice and cultured in plates 
==PAGE:44==
 
37 
 
precoated with 𝛼CD3 and 𝛼CD28. After 24 hours, the Th9 differentiation cytokine cocktails 
were added to the final concentrations stated above. On day 2, the cells were removed from 
the stimulation. On both day 2 and 3, the cells were split. Finally, on day 4, the supernatants 
and cells were harvested for ELISA and flow cytometry, respectively.  
 
On each of the days, the cells were counted to determine if the Th9 differentiation cocktails 
influenced the in vitro expansion potential of the cells. The Th0, Th9IL-4+IL-b and Th9IL-4+IL-
b+TGF-b cells expanded similarly during the 4 days of in vitro culture (Fig. 10B). There was a 
tendency towards a higher expansion of the Th9IL-4+IL-b cells (4.5±0.5 fold) compared to the 
Th9IL-4+IL-b+TGF-b cells (3.1±1.2 fold) after 4 days, yet the difference was not significant. 
 
In much of the current literature, Th9 differentiation is evaluated using flow cytometry with 
the percentage of IL-9+CD4+ T cells being the most common readout [88, 133, 163, 213]. 
These data are frequently supported by IL-9 ELISA on culture supernatants and/or Il9 gene 
expression analysis. Moreover, several studies use IL-9 expression combined with low 
expression of IL-4, IFN𝛾 and IL-17 as a positive indication of Th9 differentiation [88, 133, 
162]. Prior to flow cytometry, different ways of re-stimulating cells allow for optimal detection 
of different cytokines in distinct cell types. To determine the best protocol for evaluation of 
Th9 differentiation using flow cytometry, cells were left unstimulated or re-stimulated with:  
 
• Plate bound 𝛼CD3 (5𝜇g/mL) and soluble 𝛼CD28 (5𝜇g/mL) 
or 
• A Cell Activation Cocktail containing PMA and ionomycin. After 1 hour, Golgi plug 
was added to block cytokine secretion. 
 
After 5 hours, the cells were surface stained for markers of general T cell differentiation, 
CD44 and CD62L, followed by intracellular staining for IL-9, IL-21 and IFN𝛾 (see 
Supplementary Fig. S5 for gating strategy). On day 4, Th0, Th9IL-4+IL-b and Th9IL-4+IL-b+TGF-b 
cells were viable (>70%) and pure CD4+ populations (>98%) (Supplementary Fig. S6). 
CD4 was downregulated upon re-stimulation, especially after treatment with the Cell 
Activation Cocktail and Golgi Plug (Supplementary Fig. S6).

the unstimulated samples, re-stimulation might be unnecessary to evaluate Th9 
differentiation by flow cytometry in any forthcoming studies. However, if IFN𝛾 is included as 
a negative marker of Th9 differentiation, re-stimulation is required.

5. Discussion 
This project has explored the concept of immunotherapy with a liposomal nanoparticle 
vaccine providing in vivo stimulation of adoptively transferred CD4+ T cells. The functionality 
of the liposomes was evaluated both in vitro and in vivo, before testing the efficacy of 
vaccination as a monotherapy by different routes of administration. Finally, the liposomal 
nanoparticles were combined with adoptive transfer of cognate OT-II CD4+ T cells. Prior to 
transfer, the cells were polarized towards a Th9 phenotype with the aim of enhancing their 
anti-tumor potential in vivo. In this section, the preliminary findings from the study are 
discussed and suggestions are proposed for future work. 
 
5.1. Vaccination activates endogenous DCs specialized in CD4+ T cell 
priming 
DCs are drivers of effective immunotherapy [29] and play an essential role in pre-clinical 
models of ACT [24]. Most research has focused on the role of cDC1s known for cross-
presenting antigens to CD8+ T cells [28, 32]. In this study, liposomal nanoparticles target 
endogenous APCs to prime a CD4+ T cell response directed against the OVA antigen. The 
liposomes must be taken up and induce activation of DCs for effective antigen-presentation 
and co-stimulation of OVA-reactive CD4+ T cells. cDC2s are speculated to be critical, as 
Binnewies et al. [33] have recently shown that adoptively transferred CD4+ T cells fail to 
expand in mice with reduced numbers of cDC2s, but not in mice lacking cDC1s. 
 
DC activation following treatment with the liposomal nanoparticles was demonstrated both 
in vitro and in vivo (Fig. 6). Specifically, activation of splenic cDC1 and cDC2 subsets was 
investigated in response to i.v. vaccination with Stealth:TMX or Stealth:TMX:AC003 
liposomes. TMX is an agonist for TLR7, which is highly expressed by pDCs and triggers 
secretion of large amounts of type I IFNs [201, 202] that enhance the functionality and cross-
priming capacity of cDC1s [28, 219, 220]. TLR7 is also expressed by both human and 
murine cDC2s, while cDC1 TLR7 expression remains debated [29, 220, 221]. In this work, 
both cDC1 and cDC2 populations were activated in the spleen of tumor-bearing mice, 
==PAGE:51==
 
44 
 
measured 24 hours post i.v. vaccination. This is in line with previous literature showing that 
vaccines with TLR7 adjuvant activity can induce cDC activation [219, 220]. 
 
It may have been unnecessary to use mice with tumors for the in vivo DC activation study. 
However, solid tumors can exert systemic immunosuppressive effects [222] and pre-existing 
tumor burden has recently been shown by Allen et al. [223] to repress splenic DC activation 
following infectious challenge. As the liposomal nanoparticles have been designed for 
therapeutic use, it is most relevant to investigate the effects of vaccination in disease-
bearing animals. Additionally, the study was initially set up with the tumor-bearing mice with 
the aim of examining DC activation in both the spleen and tumor. The diameter of the 
liposomes is approximately 120nm and thus, within the range of 10nm-500nm that favors 
passive tumor accumulation via the enhanced permeation and retention (EPR) effect [224]. 
The EPR effect is mediated by leaky tumor vasculature that permits entry of macromolecules 
and subsequent retention due to ineffective lymphatic drainage. Unfortunately, on the day 
of analysis, the tumors were too small to be able to harvest sufficient material for flow 
cytometric analysis. Thus, to analyze DC activation in the tumor, the study would have to be 
repeated with mice bearing larger tumors. Alternatively, immune activation in the tumor 
could be addressed by gene expression analysis of type I IFN stimulated genes by qPCR.  
 
5.2. Activation of endogenous T cells may be delaying tumor growth  
Despite of the EPR effect, only few percentages of systemically administered PEGylated 
nanoparticles end up in tumors, whereas the majority ends up in the spleen and liver [204, 
225]. In human patients, the EPR effect may be even less pronounced [226, 227]. Tumor 
accumulation may be improved by local delivery approaches. In situ delivery of 
immunostimulatory agents was explored by W. Coley already in 1891 [228, 229]. Coley 
injected living and later inactivated bacteria, known as ‘Coley’s toxin’, directly into the tumors 
of cancer patients in the belief that infection could cause tumor shrinkage. In recent decades, 
i.t. injection of various TLR or other PRR agonists have been widely tested as 
immunotherapies inducing local inflammation and directly targeting immune cells in the 
tumor microenvironment [230, 231]. 
 
==PAGE:52==
 
45 
 
When testing the efficacy of the Stealth:TMX:AC003 nanoparticles as a monotherapy, only 
i.t. vaccination induced a transient delay in tumor growth (Fig. 8). Up until this point, the 
liposomal vaccine platform had never been tested before using in situ delivery. Yet, this 
study has several limitations and the results must be interpreted with caution. As mentioned 
above, the sample size (n=4-8 mice/group) was relatively small, since several mice were 
euthanized before the primary endpoint. Secondly, the study failed to address that i.t. 
injections may trigger sterile inflammation in the tumor microenvironment leading to brief 
tumor control. Therefore, a control CPX group receiving i.t. injection of PBS should have 
been included. Finally, it would have been relevant to test the therapeutic effect of i.t. 
vaccination with Stealth:TMX compared to Stealth:TMX:AC003, in order to separate the 
therapeutic effects of the TMX adjuvant and the antigen. 
 
These findings were followed up by a mechanistic study to assess endogenous T cell 
activation in the tumor and tdLN following i.t. vaccination with Stealth:TMX or 
Stealth:TMX:AC003 liposomes (Fig. 9). 24 hours after the first and second vaccination, 
tumors and tdLNs were harvested for analysis. We hypothesized that in situ vaccination 
would induce local inflammation and activation of tumor-resident immune cells, including T 
cells. In accordance with this notion, there was a marketed activation of CD4+ and CD8+ T 
cells in the tumor following i.t. vaccination. The T cell activation extended to the tdLN 
indicating that the liposomes also act in the periphery despite being injected locally. 
According to Spitzner et al. [232], systemic immunity is critical in the tumor rejection phase 
following effective intratumoral immunotherapy.  
 
Interestingly, after the second vaccine dose, there was a higher frequency of activated T 
cells in the tdLN of mice vaccinated with Stealth:TMX:AC003 as opposed to Stealth:TMX. 
Concurrently, stimulation of tumor antigen specific CD8+ T cells was detected in the tumor 
of mice vaccinated with Stealth:TMX:AC003. To uncover if these findings translate into 
superior therapeutic effect of i.t. vaccination with Stealth:TMX:AC003 compared to 
Stealth:TMX liposomes, a new efficacy study would have to be set up directly comparting 
the two therapies.

5.3. Th9IL-4+IL-b cells might be distinct from classical Th9 cells 
With the purpose of establishing a protocol for inducing Th9 differentiation, primary CD4+ T 
cells were cultured in vitro with two different cytokine cocktails: Th9IL-4+IL-b (IL-2, IL-4 and IL-
1𝛽) and Th9IL-4+IL-b+TGF-b (IL-2, IL-4, IL-1𝛽 and TGF-𝛽) (Fig. 10). Successful Th9 
differentiation was evaluated based on the production of IL-9 and IL-21, combined with low 
IFN𝛾 expression. Intracellular IL-9 and IL-21 was detected in both Th9IL-4+IL-b  and Th9IL-4+IL-
b+TGF-b cells using flow cytometry. Whereas IL-9 production was confirmed by ELISA, IL-21 
could not be detected in any of the samples. Upon re-stimulation, a high percentage of the 
Th9IL-4+IL-b+TGF-b cells produced IFN𝛾, suggesting that the Th9IL-4+IL-b+TGF-b cells might in fact 
be Th1 skewed. Furthermore, the Th9IL-4+IL-b cells had a higher proportion of central memory-
like cells compared to the Th9IL-4+IL-b+TGF-b cells. Based on these results, the Th9IL-4+IL-b 
cytokine cocktail was selected for the final Th9 differentiation and expansion protocol.  
 
The Th9IL-4+IL-b cocktail was inspired by Xue et al. [213], demonstrating that TGF-𝛽 is not 
required to induce Th9 differentiation. Gene expression analysis revealed that Th9 cells 
generated in the presence of IL-4, IL-1𝛽 and anti-IFN𝛾 were phenotypically distinct from 
classical Th9 cells. To my knowledge, Th9 cells differentiated with the Th9IL-4+IL-b cocktail 
used in this study have not been described previously in the literature. While the findings of 
Xue et al. [213] could suggest that the Th9IL-4+IL-b cells might also be phenotypically distinct, 
only a more extensive analysis would determine to what extent the Th9IL-4+IL-b cells resemble 
classical Th9 cells.  
 
5.4. Evaluating the phenotype of OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells before and after 
transfer 
In the final efficacy study (Fig.11), the Th9IL-4+IL-b cells were generated from splenic CD4+ T 
cells isolated from OT-II mice. Due to the low supply of OT-II mice, all preceding in vitro 
experiments were performed using splenic CD4+ T cells isolated from WT C57bl/6JRj mice 
(Fig.10). At this time, it remains unknown, if OT-II CD4+ T cells respond differently than WT 
CD4+ T cells to the Th9IL-4+IL-b cytokine cocktail. Due to time restrictions, the OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-
==PAGE:54==
 
47 
 
b cells were not re-stimulated in vitro for flow cytometric evaluation of IL-9, IL-21 and IFN𝛾 
expression. Hence, future studies are required to verify that OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells have a 
similar phenotype as described in Fig. 10. Ideally, the antigen-presentation assay in Fig. 7 
should also be repeated using 4-day differentiated OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells instead of naïve 
OT-II CD4+ T cells. 
 
CD4+ T cells are plastic and may not be terminally differentiated upon acquisition of certain 
Th phenotype [233, 234]. Therefore, it is possible that OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b  cells adopt a different 
phenotype upon transfer. Treatment with TLR7 agonists is known to stimulate production of 
type I IFNs and Th1-polarized immunity [235, 236]. In vitro, type I IFNs can also promote 
Th9 differentiation [164]. Earlier studies have shown that adoptively transferred Th9 cells 
maintain their Th9 polarization and do not revert to a Th1 phenotype in vivo [133, 162]. Yet, 
in this setting, it would be interesting to test if the Stealth:TMX:AC003 liposomes promote or 
skew the phenotype of adoptively transferred OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells in vivo. 
 
5.5. Future studies and determining the mechanisms of action 
Combining systemic vaccination with Stealth:TMX:ACO03 liposomes and adoptive transfer 
of OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells only resulted in a minor delay in tumor growth (Fig.11). As i.t. 
vaccination with Stealth:TMX:AC003 liposomes alone was demonstrated to mediate a 
therapeutic effect (Fig. 8), it would be interesting to test if adoptive transfer of OT-II Th9IL-
4+IL-b cells would synergize better with i.t. rather than i.v. vaccine administration. Additionally, 
increasing the dose of OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells might also enhance the therapeutic effect. 
 
It would be relevant to validate that the OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells are in fact re-stimulated in vivo 
when delivered in conjunction with the vaccine. Before transfer, OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells could 
be stained with CellTrace Violet and at later timepoints, different organs would be harvested. 
CellTrace Violet positive OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells could be identified by flow cytometry and cell 
proliferation determined by dilution of the CellTrace Violet signal. Moreover, staining the 
cells for a proliferation marker, such as Ki67, would support the data. Performing analysis 
==PAGE:55==
 
48 
 
of multiple tissues such as spleen, lungs, tdLN and tumor would also determine the 
persistence, location and tumor infiltration of the cells post transfer.  
 
Most solid cancers do not express MHC class II molecules, yet IFN𝛾 may induce MHC class 
II expression as observed for the B16 melanoma cell line [43, 44]. Studies have 
demonstrated that Th9 cells can be directly cytotoxic [88, 132] and thus, it would be relevant 
to measure granzyme B expression in the OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells both before and after 
transfer. Alternatively, it is possible that the OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b  cells exert their anti-tumor effect 
via other immune cells, such as cytotoxic CD8+ T cells. This hypothesis could be addressed 
by conducting depletion studies at a later time, if a more significant therapeutic effect is 
observed upon ACT and vaccine treatment. Either way, it would be interesting to examine 
the effect of the transferred OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b  cells on the tumor infiltration of endogenous 
CD8+ T cells and the expression of CX3CR1, which is upregulated on CD8+ T cells in the 
presence of CD4+ T cell help [42, 173]. Cancer and chronic viral infections are both diseases 
characterized by chronic inflammation and T cell exhaustion [237]. Studies have reported 
that CD4+ T cells can rescue exhausted CD8+ T cells during both chronic infection [238] and 
cancer [239]. As mentioned previously, this project emanates from a series of studies 
combining vaccination with Stealth:TMX liposomes carrying the SIINFEKL (OVA257-264) 
epitope with adoptive transfer of SIINFEKL-specific OT-I CD8+ T cells. While this treatment 
effectively mediated tumor regression in the B16-OVA model, the tumors always relapsed 
eventually, which was correlated with loss of OVA expression [240]. A natural next step 
would be to combine the CD8+ directed therapy with adoptive transfer of OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b 
cells and Stealth:TMX:AC003 vaccination. However, if loss of OVA expression is inevitable, 
tumor relapse may be difficult to prevent if not by induction of intermolecular epitope 
spreading and priming of endogenous tumor-reactive T cell responses. 
 
5.6. Translational value 
Historically, therapeutic cancer vaccines have had limited success in the clinic [105, 106] 
and novel vaccine technology is warranted to potentiate anti-tumor T cell responses in 
patients. Furthermore, combining vaccination and ACT has shown potential in pre-clinical 
models of cancer [192-194]. The liposomal vaccine platform developed at the CBIO group 
==PAGE:56==
 
49 
 
at DTU Health Tech has potent adjuvant activity, is highly versatile and can be repurposed 
towards any tumor-antigen of choice. Selection of tumor-antigen(s) for the vaccine requires 
knowledge of the reactivity of the transferred T cells. In this study, OVA-specific CD4+ T cells 
are transferred in the murine B16-OVA melanoma model with transgenic OVA expression. 
Yet, in the clinic, the specificities of bulk T cell products for ACT are often unknown. 
Technologies are advancing to enrich TILs with reactivity towards specific neoantigens - a 
therapeutic strategy, which has been successful in several case studies [151-153]. In 
addition, improving TCR-engineered T cell therapies is an area of much research [241, 242]. 
Currently, treatment with personalized neoantigen-specific TCR-T cells is being tested in a 
clinical trial at the National Institutes of Health (ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT03412877). 
Finally, novel strategies for re-stimulating CAR T cells using post-transfer vaccination are 
also under development [199, 243-245]. In these settings, combining liposomal-based 
nanoparticle vaccination and ACT might be beneficial.  
 
Whereas most research has focused on adoptive transfer of CD8+ T cells, the essential roles 
of CD4+ T cells in anti-tumor immunity are increasingly appreciated. While ACT with pure 
CD4+ T cells may be less clinically relevant, this project aims to contribute to the growing 
body of literature highlighting the potential of targeting and using CD4+ T cells for adoptive 
immunotherapy. Moreover, polarizing T cells towards certain effector phenotypes prior to 
transfer might also hold potential in the clinic. In a study from 2014, Lu et al. [246] show that 
CD8+ T cells cultured with Th9 differentiating cytokines also harbor enhanced anti-tumor 
efficacy upon transfer in murine melanoma models. Future investigations are required to 
determine, which human cancers might respond favorably to adoptive therapy with Tc9/Th9 
skewed T cells.

6. Concluding remarks 
This thesis evaluated the therapeutic potential of a liposomal-based nanoparticle vaccine 
‘Stealth:TMX:AC003’ carrying a lipidated TLR7 agonist and CD4+ epitope (OVA323-339) in the 
syngeneic, murine B16-OVA melanoma model. The functionality of the liposomal 
nanoparticles is based on effective uptake, activation and antigen-processing of APCs. Both 
in vitro and in vivo, Stealth:TMX:AC003 liposomes triggered DC activation. In an in vitro 
antigen-presentation assay, nanoparticle-treated BMDCs induced proliferation of cognate 
OT-II CD4+ T cells. Upon in vivo testing, i.t. vaccination induced a transient delay of tumor 
growth as well as T cell activation in the tumor and tdLN. Stimulation of tumor antigen 
specific CD8+ T cells was also detected in the tumor after administration of the second i.t. 
vaccine dose.  
 
Vaccination with Stealth:TMX:AC003 liposomes was finally combined with adoptive transfer 
of OT-II CD4+ T cells. Before transfer, the cells were polarized towards a Th9 phenotype, 
based on literature reporting that adoptively transferred Th9 cells elicit superior anti-tumor 
responses compared to Th1 and Th17 cells [88]. The OT-II CD4+ T cells were polarized with 
a Th9IL-4+IL-b cytokine cocktail using a 4-day in vitro differentiation and expansion protocol, 
which was established as part of this master’s project. WT Th9IL-4+IL-b cells were shown to 
consist of a significant proportion of central memory-like cells and produced both IL-9 and 
IL-21 after 4 days of in vitro culture. Combining i.v. vaccination and adoptive transfer of OT-
II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells only mediated in a minor therapeutic effect in vivo. Future studies will 
investigate if the OT-II Th9IL-4+IL-b cells are re-stimulated by the nanoparticles in vivo and if 
combination with i.t. instead of i.v. vaccination can improve the anti-tumor efficacy. 
 
Although the field of immunotherapy has been revolutionized by ICIs, only 12.46% of cancer 
patients are estimated to be responsive [247]. In addition, ACT has been successful in 
mediating long-term complete responses, yet predominantly in melanoma and B-cell 
malignancies. Ideally, enhancing the persistence of transferred T cells by post-transfer 
vaccination will broaden the range of cancer patients benefiting from ACT in the future. 
Furthermore, as the critical roles of CD4+ T cells in anti-tumor immunity are explored further, 
ACT approaches based on CD4+ T cells and Th9 polarization may also reach the clinic.
