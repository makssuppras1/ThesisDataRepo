1. Scope 
The scope of this project is to document the work and findings during synthesis and interstitial alloying of 
high-entropy alloys. The alloys are evaluated through use of standard metallurgy laboratory equipment and 
analysis tools such as scanning electron microscopy, light optic microscopy, microhardness testing and x-ray 
diffraction. 
The results are expected to support a deeper understanding of the mechanisms associated with interstitial 
nitrogen alloying of High-Entropy alloys, which is used on other materials to build up compressive stresses 
in the surface, thus increasing the fatigue resistance of nitrided components.

3. Introduction to high entropy alloys 
This chapter is the result of a literature study and has been constructed to take the 
reader into the world of high entropy alloys, which are multiple-element stable solid-
solutions. The chapter can to a large extend be seen as a brief recap of the theory and 
findings presented in “High-Entropy Alloys” by Murty, Yeh and Ranganathan [1], which is 
the foundation of this introduction. 
The common understanding of the term ”alloy” often requires the companionship of a major element, like 
“titanium alloy” or “nickel based alloy”. From this perspective, the alloying elements’ effect on the major 
element has been thoroughly investigated for centuries. The approach is easy to relate to; you take 
something, which has proven to be good, and then try to enhance it by adding something extra to it. For 
example titanium alloy ASTM grade 9 that has added aluminum and vanadium (Ti-3Al-2.5V), which greatly 
improves the strength-to-weight ratio. The nature of these alloys usually places them near the edges of our 
binary or ternary phase diagrams. 
An entirely different approach for understanding and designing alloys, is looking toward the center of phase 
diagrams (PD). Some elements, such as nickel and copper, eagerly form single phase solid solutions (see 
Appendix A: Phase diagrams), whereas many other binary combinations of metals, like iron and chromium, 
can form intermetallic phases, which is often undesired as bulk material due to their brittleness. From a 
physical/chemical perspective, it is the nature of all matter to strive toward lower levels of free energy. The 
intermetallic phases are favored due to a negative enthalpy of formation, ∆H, which according to the 
equation of Gibb’s free energy, ∆G, leads to a decrease in free energy of the given system: 
 
∆𝐺= ∆𝐻−𝑇∆𝑆 
Equation 3.1 
In order to prevent the formation of the intermetallic phases and favor a random mixing (solid solution), 
the product of temperature, T, and entropy of mixing, ∆Smix, needs to supersede the enthalpy of mixing 
(∆Hmix = -∆Hintermetallic). This suggests the dissolution of intermetallics at elevated temperatures, which as an 
example can be seen on the Cr-Fe phase diagram. But for a certain branch of alloys, high entropy alloys 
(HEAs), it is possible to maintain a solid solution and suppress the intermetallic formation while the alloy 
cools down to room temperature. 
To understand the concept of HEAs, it is necessary to first get familiar with the entropy of mixing, which 
consists of 4 contributions: configurational, vibrational, magnetic dipole and electric randomness entropies 
[1]. In HEAs the greatest of these contributors is, by far, the configurational entropy, ∆Sconf, which can be 
calculated as;

From Equation 3.3 it is easy to see that a higher number of elements leads to a larger configurational 
entropy. There is no unambiguous definition of HEA except obviously that the entropy must be high. The 
generally accepted criteria is that a HEA must contain at least 4 or 5 different elements. 
3.1 
Crystal structure 
Using X-ray diffraction (XRD) it has been proven that the elements in HEAs can form an average lattice – 
that is a common crystal structure with an average lattice parameter (Figure 3.1). For many HEAs a body-
centered cubic (BCC) lattice is favored as BCC has more of an open structure compared to the closer packed 
face-centered cubic (FCC). The resulting crystal structure has been found to comply with that of eager 
binary formers among the elements. 
To a certain extent, the same general principles applies to HEAs as to binary alloys: FCC, opposed to BCC, 
has more slip systems, making it easier to deform. Also FCC has fewer, but larger interstitial spacings. 
 
Figure 3.1: 2-D representation of a 10-element solid solution arranged in an average lattice. [1] 
In addition to the effect of high entropy, three other effects are of high importance to HEAs. These effects 
are; severe lattice distortion, sluggish diffusion and cocktail effect. 
3.2 
Severe lattice distortion 
The difference in atomic size causes a severe lattice distortion. This effect hinders the movement of 
dislocations, thus increasing the yield strength of the material and reduce ductility.

3.3 
Sluggish diffusion 
It is well known that diffusion is dependent on the number and size of vacancies or interstitial lattice 
spacing. The number of vacancies relies on temperature (relative to Tmelt) and also the degree of 
deformation. The interstitial lattice spacing is directly related to the crystal structure of the phase, where 
FCC structure, compared to BCC, has larger interstitial spacing even though the structure is closer packed. 
Figure 3.3 illustrates the activation energy of diffusion, normalized by melting temperature, of various 
elements into selected pure metals, steels and the HEA CoCrFeMnNi. For all diffusing elements, the 
activation energy is highest in the HEA. 
The randomness of atoms neighboring to vacancies and the distorted lattice are believed to be the main 
reasons for the sluggish diffusion, as this leads to smaller vacancy sites.

During cooling, the sluggish diffusion results in restrictions in diffusion controlled phase transformations 
and nucleation growth, allowing some solid solutions to be metastable at room temperature. 
 
3.4 
Cocktail effect 
Cocktail effect covers the results of different phases that might form in HEAs interacting with each other, 
sometimes counterintuitively enhancing the properties of the material. The different phases interlocks and 
act as a sort of composite. 
3.5 
Formation of phases 
During the past two decades, an increasing number of scientific teams have contributed with their 
experimental data from investigations of and attempts to create new HEAs, but not all combinations of 
metals leads to the desired results. It has been found that certain parameters must be met in order to 
create a stable solid solution. The primary rules of thumb is commonly referred to as Hume-Rothery rules 
(H-R), which were developed in the 1920s to describe the solubility of one element into another [1]: 
1) The radii of the solute and solvent atoms must not differ by more than about 15 %: For complete 
solubility, the atomic size difference should be less than 8 %. 
2) The crystal structures of the two elements must be the same for extended solid solubility. 
3) Extended solubility occurs when the solvent and solute have the same valence. 
4) The two elements should have similar electronegativity so that intermetallic compounds will not 
form.

H-R have been refined by Pettifor in the 1980s, where he added the effect of bond orbitals and assigned 
numbers that he called Mendeleev numbers to each element (Figure 3.4). 
 
Figure 3.4: Pettifor’s table of elements with assigned Mendeleev numbers. [2] 
The Mendeleev numbers can be used to predict the (crystal) structure of alloys and applies to HEAs, bulk 
metallic glasses (BMGs), quasi crystals and intermetallics. 
3.6 
Non-equiatomic 
HEAs can also exist as non-equiatomic alloys. In fact, changes to the composition of one element only 
results in minor changes to the entropy level. Just as with the approach first mentioned in this report, 
adding a bit more of one component, might enhance desirable properties of the alloy, like favoring a special 
crystal structure or allow precipitation to make age hardening a possibility. 
 
3.7 
Processing routes 
When producing HEAs, several different routes can be taken, each presenting its own benefits and 
limitations. 
3.7.1 
Melting and casting 
The route most commonly used has been to mix all the metals and heat up by vacuum arc melting or 
vacuum induction melting until they are all liquefied. The temperature of vacuum arc melting is 
approximately 3000 °C, which is high enough to melt all but a few metals. For vacuum induction melting, 
==PAGE:10==

the temperature cannot go as high, but it can be controlled to avoid vaporization of metals with lower 
boiling temperatures. 
Due to restrictions in cooling rates, the melting and casting route often results in dendritic microstructure, 
which consists of two phases, one being richer in one or more elements than the other phase. 
Laser cladding is also a possible route to synthesis of HEAs, but the temperature and cooling rates are very 
difficult to control. 
3.7.2 
Powder metallurgy 
Another route to HEA synthesis is powder metallurgy, using mechanical alloying, where solid chunks of all 
the components are milled to fine powder particles, while the mixture is cold welded by high energy ball 
milling (similar to kneading a dough). Afterwards, the mixture is vacuum sintered while under a heavy load, 
allowing the particles to diffusion bond. 
3.7.3 
Deposition 
The third known route to HEA synthesis is deposition processes. Deposition processes are used to coat 
HEAs onto other substrates, e.g. for corrosion protection, wear resistance or diffusion barriers. Especially 
plasma nitriding and magnetron sputtering have been used. 
3.8 
Microstructure 
The microstructure of HEAs can be single phase solid solution, multi-phase compositions (often dendritic) 
with segregation of some elements being more represented in one phase or two different crystal 
structures, FCC and BCC, coexisting. 
Due to the sluggish diffusion effect, precipitation mechanisms are restricted, allowing room for the 
possibility of creating HEAs capable of age hardening.

5.2 
Arc furnace melting and drop casting 
The metal granules were arc melted using Edmund Bühler AM, arc melting furnace (see Figure 5.2). The 
furnace consist of a copper base and an air tight chamber with sight glasses, furnace pliers and a movable 
electrode. The copper base is connected to an external cooling unit and has 3 pockets for mixing and a 
cavity for insertion of various drop or suction casting molds. The mold used produces bars of ~3 x 16 x 100 
mm3. The chamber is connected to a vacuum pump and a gas inlet system (uses argon). The pliers are 
operated from the right side of the machine and can be used to turn or move small lumps of material inside 
the chamber without having to open, thus saving time on evacuating the chamber. The tungsten electrode 
are operated from the top of the machine and can be adjusted in power from the handle and is connected 
to a high performance power supply.

Procedure: First, the inside of the furnace chamber was thoroughly cleaned in ethanol. Then, the metal 
granules were mixed in two of the pockets and a lump of titanium was placed in a third pocket. The 
chamber was closed and then the air inside was evacuated (pevac = 2.5 x 10-5 mbar) to get rid of oxygen and 
other contaminating elements. After evacuating, the chamber was flushed with argon (pflush = 200 mbar) 
and this procedure was repeated 3 times to end with high quality vacuum (pevac). 
Molten titanium has a strong affinity for most contaminating elements, so before starting to melt the 
granules, the lump of titanium was arc melted. This, together with the strict evacuation procedure, ensures 
that when the granules are being melted, they will only be subject to their own impurities (like oxides in the 
surface) and not any other reactive agents that might affect the casted product. Then the metal granules 
were arc melted and briefly allowed to cool down. With the furnace pliers, the two lumps of material were 
flipped and melted again. This procedure was repeated 2-3 times for homogenizing their elemental 
contents, and then they were both placed in one pocket, arc melted and flipped another 2-3 times. Finally, 
the single lump of material was placed in the pocket (funnel) above the copper casting dye and arc melted. 
After successful casting the aperture was disassembled and the cast specimen removed from copper mold 
(Figure 5.3). The inside of Cu mold was very gently ground and then cleaned with ethanol before next 
casting. 
For some of the bars, the casting did not succeed 100 % and the entire process of cleaning, evacuating, 
flushing and casting had to be repeated.

Grinding and polishing 
When nothing else is stated, all samples used for HV and XRD have been prepared to a finish corresponding

For grinding and polishing the applied procedure is similar to, although not exactly as, standard’s 
descriptions [3]. 
During polishing of samples from some nitriding batches porous surface particles complicated the polishing 
procedure as these would constantly break loose and easily scratch the surface due to their high hardness. 
6.4 
HV 
Hardness being a measure of the materials resistance to deformation has been carried out with Vickers 
indentation. For bulk hardness 500 grams force (HV500) has been used and standards have not been 
violated with regards to distance between indentations and 10 measurements have always been used for 
statistics [4].

nitriding. This is investigated and further discussed in Chapter 7 Nitriding of HEAs.

surface hardness has not been made with this awareness in mind. Some samples of HEAs X09N and x10N

6.5 
Etching agent 
Etching is necessary to highlight features - without etching LOMs would often just be images of highly 
reflective surfaces. But finding the right etching/contrast agent is also critical, so the features of interest 
become visible. Sometimes in experimental metallurgy something special might have happened, but 
without the proper means to detect it, no one would ever know. For base materials in this work, the bulk 
with its GBs and microstructure is of interest. Regarding the nitrided samples, the surface effects of the 
nitrogen, the compounds and phases formed as well as depth of diffusion are of highest interest. 
3 different “etching” agents have been tried: 
==PAGE:19==

1) Struers Active oxide polishing suspension (OPS): 
Alkaline silica oxide (0.04 µm) suspension used in conjunction with a rubber disc for polishing. 
OPS has proved acceptable for giving contours to GBs as well as the microstructure in HEAs D and F. 
The procedure is to add an extra polishing step, where a rubber disc is used instead of canvas or 
cloth. For coolant and abrasive suspension a 50/50 mix or tap water and OPS is used, and the 
samples are treated for 2 minutes superseded by thorough rinse in tap water. 
2) Kalling’s No. 2:

6.7 
SEM/EDS 
By bombarding the sample with electrons SEM can analyze the elemental composition by measuring the 
energy emitted by excited electrons, energy-dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS). To some extent the 
distribution of elements can also be mapped by detecting backscattered electrons (BSE), where heavy 
elements appear brighter on the image. With BSE, SEM is expected to reveal if some elements seem to 
have affinity toward or repulsion from other elements. With magnifications up to around x10k SEM is also 
capable of producing high resolution images, called secondary electron images (SEI). 
Before loading the samples into the SEM, it must be assured that the embedding is conducting and then 
grounded to the sample holder with copper tape in order to discharge. The embedding is made conducting 
with carbon coating, which requires very clean and ultra-dry samples. The coating is performed in vacuum, 
where high current is sent through a small carbon fiber thread that partially sublimates and some of the 
gaseous carbon quickly attach to the sample. 
The sample loading procedure is very strict, as a false move can cause severe damage to the SEM.

Bragg’s law (Equation 6.1) is the fundamental equation regarding XRD: 
 
2𝑑∙sin 𝜃= 𝑛∙𝜆 
Equation 6.1 
Where d is the lattice spacing, θ is the angle of incidence and angle of scattering and n is an integer of wave 
length λ. When Bragg’s Law is fulfilled, the intensity of diffraction will be at its maximum (showing a peak). 
Each crystal structure has a specific relation for its peaks, which is explained in more details in Section 6.8.3 
Confirming the crystal structure. 
When an austenitic sample is nitrided, the interstitial nitrogen atoms causes an expansion to the austenitic 
crystal (hence the name expanded austenite). According to Bragg’s Law (Equation 6.1), when the lattice 
spacing is increased, while n and λ are kept constant, the peak intensity would shift to a lower 2θ value. 
It is known that internal residual stresses can cause an anisotropic distortion. For FCC, the (111) peak is 
weaker than the (200) and (220), which means that it would be more affected.

7.1 
Effect of heat treatment 
At elevated temperatures metals and alloys can experience recovery, recrystallization and grain growth. 
These effects relieve internal stresses (from the casting) and reduces the dislocation density, thus softening 
the material. It is however in the nature of HEAs to slow down these effects due to the severe lattice 
distortion and sluggish diffusion. 
Another mechanism that might take place at elevated temperatures is precipitation. Precipitation is a 
diffusion controlled process that also requires a driving force to take place: 
First of all, the precipitate needs to be of a more thermodynamically stable phase (here Equation 3.1 of 
Gibb’s free energy comes into play). In this context it is necessary to understand that at room temperature 
HEAs are only metastable solid solutions. But when the temperature is high, the high entropy of mixing 
makes the solid solution the more stable phase.  
Secondly, diffusion of substitutional atoms must be possible. The reason for this is that one lone atom in 
itself is no precipitate – it is merely a substitutional atom in solid solution. Therefore the atoms must 
migrate and cluster, which can only happen when the interfacial energy of the precipitate’s nucleus is 
overcome by the reduction in energy through segregation. Therefore the coherency of the nucleating phase 
is also an important factor.  
With a fast cooling, the atoms in HEAs are frozen in place and diffusion is prohibited. As with all diffusion 
controlled processes, precipitation takes time. For this reason the formation of otherwise stable 
intermetallic compounds are suppressed. 
The mechanisms behind precipitation hardening (age hardening) also consists of two contributions. The 
precipitates in itself increase the hardness of the material. On the other hand, the atoms used to form the 
precipitates also plays a strengthening role when in solid solution. Because of this, many small and evenly 
distributed precipitating particles greatly increase the material’s hardness, but when the precipitates are 
given time to grow and merge, the hardness will drop.

7.6 
Discussion and sub conclusions on the effects of nitriding HEAs A, C, D, E and F 
Evaluating hardness changes is a fast and simple method for testing if a material has changed. 
The hardness can be a contribution from many sources: 
1) Severe lattice distortion of HEAs 
2) Interstitial alloying: Expansion of austenite, where solute metal atoms with high nitrogen affinity 
(“getters”) increase the nitrogen solubility in the matrix 
3) Precipitation (age) hardening:  
a. Precipitation of many fine metal nitrides 
b. Perhaps precipitation of intermetallic phases 
Since the bulk hardness is more or less unaffected by the temperature, the increased hardness can be 
attributed 100 % to the presence of nitrogen, thus ruling out intermetallic precipitates. 
Approaching the matter with awareness of diffusion mechanics, nitrogen is constantly diffusing into the 
surface, building up concentration of solute nitrogen until the maximum solubility is reached. Meanwhile, 
the diffusion flux towards the bulk increases (in accordance with Fick’s first Law, as the material can be 
considered homogeneous). When solubility reaches its maximum, if the driving force is high enough to 
nucleate metal nitrides, and if the temperature is high enough to allow diffusion of substitutional atoms 
(since precipitation is a diffusion controlled process), nitrogen and “getters” will start migrating to nearby 
nucleation spots and leave the solid solution. Nitrogen is being kept added, so the concentration in the 
solid solution is kept at the maximum, but because the “getters” are used to form metal nitrides, the 
nitrogen solubility start to decrease. After a time, the solid solution will no longer contain the “getters” and 
the austenitic phase is no longer truly expanded. As time passes by, the metal nitrides will cluster into 
larger and larger particles (over ageing), and they are no longer inducing the local stress field that hardens 
the material.

7.7 
Alternative method for XRD: SuperNova 
Samples of x07N have all been analyzed in SuperNova, an aperture designed for transmission x-ray 
diffraction on crystallized proteins and small samples. The aperture has built-in copper and molybdenum 
radiation and measures using a high resolution area detector. It can be used in reflection mode, which 
reduces the signal by half. However the scan time is greatly reduced due to the area detector and scan 
times are in the interval of seconds to minutes, whereas using chromium radiation with the previously 
mentioned conventional XRD method takes many hours. 
 
Molybdenum has the advantage of a greater depth of penetration, which increases the number of counts 
and yields more information of the bulk material.

some expanded austenite.
