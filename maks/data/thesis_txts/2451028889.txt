Abstract
The technical complexity of buildings is increasing, and at the same time, there are stricter
requirements for energy consumption, sustainability considerations and indoor climate in re-
gards to new buildings. However, problems have appeared, as many new buildings do not
work as intended at handover, although the technical systems are designed according to the
latest standards. The commissioning process is a quality assurance process that runs parallel to
the building project. This process can accommodate these challenges by prioritizing resources,
money and time on the technical functionality and cross-disciplinary of technical systems. This
thesis will focus on the operation phase, as the commissioning process usually ends after the
handover process of the building project. The study was carried out in cooperation with DTU
Campus Service, and the laboratory building 202 at DTU Lyngby Campus has been applied as
a case study. This building was chosen based on its technical complexity and starting troubles
in the operation phase. The study aimed to ﬁnd commissioning strategies for DTU Campus
Service in the operation phase, which would generate value for the organization and the contin-
uous operation. The data collection consisted of 11 interviews, including an even distribution
for case study interviews and expert interviews. Also, energy building data from the main me-
ters for heating, cooling, water, and electricity in building 202 were applied as quantitative data.
The case study gave insight into DTU’s own commissioning model and future operation strate-
gies for DTU Campus Service. DTU’s building 202 was examined based on energy building
data, performance gaps, and the prior commissioning process. The analysis and results consid-
ered commissioning in the operation phase more generally concerning beneﬁts and challenges
as well as possible commissioning activities for the operation phase. Based on these prelim-
inary studies, two strategies for DTU Campus Service were recommended. The ﬁrst strategy
is aimed at DTU’s future building projects, where it is possible to inﬂuence the commission-
ing process from the initial phases in the building project. Five focus areas for commission-
ing activities are included: 1) Financial incentives and milestones for building performance, 2)
Testing procedure, 3) O&M (operations and maintenance) material, 4) Operating requirements,
and 5) Planning of data application. The second strategy is aimed at DTU’s existing buildings
and recommended the following ﬁve focus areas: 1) Energy management/monitoring-based
commissioning, 2) Checklists, 3) Collection of valuable O&M (operations and maintenance)
information, 4) DTU Standards and 5) Training of operating staff at DTU Campus Service. The
study indicated that the commissioning process in the operation phase remains at an early stage
in the Danish building industry. DTU Campus Service is on the right track in terms of devel-
oping DTU’s own commissioning model and integrating ongoing operating knowledge in the
DTU standards. It is assessed that there is potential for further development of the commission-
ing process for DTU’s buildings and future building projects to handle the increased technical
complexity of the buildings and utilize these new opportunities as part of the commissioning
activities.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Today, buildings account for 35% of the total CO2-emissions and Europeans spend 90% of their
time indoors in the buildings (Plesser and Teisen, 2018). Therefore, it is of great importance
that buildings are energy optimized without compromising on the indoor climate and the quality
of the technical facilities. The commissioning process is a tool that runs parallel to the building
process. This process can help tackle the challenges associated with the increasing complexity
of buildings and the associated problems that arise during the operation phase due to previous
decisions made in the earlier stages of the building project (Teisen et al., 2013). Besides, EU
has chosen to promote building automation further, as larger non-residential buildings are re-
quired to be equipped with building automation and control systems from 2025 (EU, 2018).
This is a step towards an increase in the awareness of energy optimizing in the operation phase
based on digitalization and data collection.
Based on results from a 3-week individual course: ”An investigation of applying Commission-
ing in the operation phase of a building project”, it is evident that there is a great potential
for implementing commissioning in the operation phase of a building project. However, the
challenge is that current practice in the Danish building industry is not considering the beneﬁts
of applying commissioning in the operation phase (Kjerulf, 2019). Figure 1 indicates the op-
eration costs after the handover of a building and it is evident that a commissioning process is
generating a signiﬁcant value to the overall costs in relation to operating a building (Teisen et
al., 2013). Additionally, research from the United States indicates energy savings of approxi-
mately 13% and an average payback period of 4.2 years when implementing a commissioning
process (Mills, 2011).

Thus, there is clear evidence that a commissioning process can pay off in the long run when
new buildings are constructed, so how come building commissioning is not a higher priority in
building projects today? Introducing a successful commissioning process can be a difﬁcult task
to perform in practice. The combination of a large amount of information, regulatory require-
ments, conﬂicting interests and all the other tasks that must be carried out by various stake-
holders in the building project makes it a complicated and to some extent an unmanageable
task to address. This thesis aims to discuss and examine the value of applying commission-
ing throughout the lifetime of a building, and which strategies that can be used to achieve a
successful commissioning process in the operation phase of a building project.
1.2
Problem Statement
When considering the angle of the research, it was especially important to delimit the subject to
uncover more in-depth areas and at the same time, consider the possibilities within the project’s
time frame of ﬁve months. Thus, it has been chosen for this study to focus on the operation
phase and what this entails. Furthermore, reservations are also made for the earlier stages of
a commissioning process that are relevant when considering the operation phase. DTU Cam-
pus Service is applied as an example of how the framework for a commissioning process in
the operation phase can be addressed, as this organization has a great interest in optimizing
their building operation. This leads to the main research question and sub-questions, which are
stated below.
The main research question is: ”Which strategies for commissioning should be applied in the
operation phase of a building project for an organization such as DTU Campus Service?”
This provides the basis for the following research sub-questions:
• What are the preconditions for applying an ”ongoing” commissioning process?
• What deﬁnes a commissioning process in the operation phase? Which stakeholder(s)
is/are responsible for conducting the activities?
• What are the advantages of implementing a commissioning process in the operation phase
and what are the related main challenges?
• Which commissioning activities would generate gains for the building owner to maintain
and optimize the performance of a building?
1.3
Report Structure
The report is divided into seven main chapters: Introduction, Methodology, Theory and Liter-
ature Study, Case study: Commissioning at DTU CAS, Analysis and Results, Discussion, and
Conclusion (Figure 2). The introduction includes a background section, which explains the rel-
evance of the research. Also, the main research question is presented together with the research
sub-questions for the thesis. The methodology will encompass the presented theory as well
as the data collection and address how the data are processed. The theory and literature study

need for room heating depending on the outside temperatures. Hence, the weather-normalized
energy consumption was calculated by applying degree days for the months and the degree
days for an average year based on historical degree days. Degree days were retrieved from
the Danish Technological Institute, which is based on measurements from Landbohøjskolen

Theory and Literature Study
The chapter will ﬁrst clarify the deﬁnition of ”performance gap”, which forms the basis for
building commissioning being applied as a quality management service for closing the perfor-
mance gap related to buildings. Besides, the chapter will provide an introduction of building
commissioning and the related Danish and international practice. However, the international
practice is chosen as the basis for addressing commissioning in the operation phase. This will
lead to an assessment of the concept of ”ongoing commissioning”. Finally, two earlier case
studies from Denmark will be evaluated.
3.1
Performance Gap
In line with the trend towards greener and more sustainable buildings, increased requirements
are reﬂected in the new building codes, which results in demands for higher standards and
lower energy consumption. This has led to a reduction in energy consumption in the operation
phase of new buildings. However, the complex technical installations in the new buildings are
an evident challenge to manage for engineers, designers, construction teams, facility organi-
zations, owners, and users. This issue has led to the appearance of the performance gap, as
buildings are not functioning as expected. The initial performance targets are not considered
in the operation phase of the building project, which generates additional costs in the design
and construction phase. In addition, it is often seen that buildings cause higher operating costs
than intended (Plesser and Teisen, 2018). Thus, this leads to the following question: how come
that buildings often perform worse than expected in the operation phase? There can be several
reasons for this, and giving a clear answer to the question is a complicated task. Besides higher
energy consumption, a poor indoor climate can also have a signiﬁcant impact on the produc-
tivity of employees, which can contribute to even more consequences in terms of additional
costs (Plesser and Teisen, 2018). PhD student Helle Lohmann Rasmussen from DTU Manage-
ment Engineering has mapped 12 different types of performance gaps that can occur during the
operation phase (Figure 6).
Figure 6: Facilities Manager’s typology of performance gaps (Rasmussen, 2018)
==PAGE:23==
3. Theory and Literature Study
11
Another research ”Mind the energy performance gap: Evidence from green commercial build-
ings” (Liang et al., 2019) has identiﬁed the top three reasons for the energy performance gap
in commercial buildings based on a survey completed in 2017 by 117 facility managers in the
United States. These three are 1) building occupants use more energy, 2) more occupants are
added, and 3) there are failures with energy-efﬁcient technologies (Liang et al., 2019). Further-
more, based on literature research, the study has identiﬁed a framework for the driving factors
of the energy performance gap (Figure 7). The ﬁrst one is ”Engineering factors”, which include
technology degradation, ineffective usage of energy-efﬁcient technology, inaccurate design and
modelling. The issue is often that only model-based energy consumption is considered under
standardized operation conditions, as the energy consumption based on actual performance is
not a requirement for green building certiﬁcations. The second one is ”Behavioural factors”,
which include the behaviour of the occupants and individual preferences, leading to an im-
pact on the actual energy performance of the building. User behaviour such as window and
door opening related to noise level and air quality can be difﬁcult to predict in the simulation
models, thus generating an overall increase in the energy consumption. The third one is ”Orga-
nizational factors”, which include the mismatch of interests between the tenants and landlords.
Landlords are not considering the interests of the tenants in the decisions regarding energy ef-
ﬁciency investments. Consequently, this can lead to unplanned changes, mismatch of loads,
additional operating hours and a miscalculation of the actual number of occupants. Also, the
working experience of the facility management organization is important and can inﬂuence the
energy performance gap due to the management structure. The study concludes that ”Engi-
neering”, ”Behavioral” and ”Organizational” factors are equally essential when assessing the
energy performance gap (Liang et al., 2019).

Life-cycle and stakeholder perspectives” (Zou et al., 2018) of 227 relevant publications, current
researchers have established two kinds of building energy performance gap (BEPG) (Figure
8). The ﬁrst one is the gap between the simulated (predicted) and measured building energy
performance. This energy gap is mostly applied by researchers. The second gap is deﬁned as
the gap between the target set by a standard (or guideline) and the measured building energy

performance. The second energy gap is especially applied by governments. However, it is
possible to conclude if ”Gap 1” is accomplished, then ”Gap 2” is no longer relevant, as it will
not exist (Zou et al., 2018).
Figure 8: Two types of BEPG (Zou et al., 2018) - Adapted by Lin Kjerulf
Furthermore, the study indicates the leading causes of BEPG, which can be identiﬁed within
three main groups: 1) causes that arise from the design stage, 2) causes related to the construc-
tion and 3) causes related to the operation phase. The ﬁrst cause is related to designers not
being able to predict the operation situation of the building due to lack of data, knowledge, and
experience. Thus, it is primarily the occupant behaviour which indicates several uncertainties.
Also, simulations from energy modelling are challenging to compare with actual conditions, as
a model is only a simpliﬁed version of the reality. The design concept is furthermore playing
a role, as complex energy saving technology is not equal to a high energy performance. This
is due to overestimations by the designer, systems are having troubles with accommodating
changes in the environment and ﬁnally, the users are not able to operate the energy system
correctly. The second cause related to the construction is regarding a misalliance between the
construction quality and the design speciﬁcations. The quality of the construction could be
inﬂuenced by limited experience and knowledge, changes in orders or cut-down of materials.
The third cause is related to the operation, which concerns occupant behaviour. The occupants
have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the energy performance, as they control all the systems, thus,
occupant behaviour is the leading cause of BEPG. However, proper control strategies by an
energy manager can lead to an essential reduction in the building’s energy consumption (Zou
et al., 2018).
The study suggests strategies for closing BEPG, which is based on researchers and practitioners
(Zou et al., 2018):
• Passive and active design: Passive buildings can potentially reduce the unnecessary en-
ergy consumption, for instance, by controlling the artiﬁcial light based on the amount of
daylight. However, a successful passive design is highly dependent on a comprehensive
understanding of both climate and the energy systems by the designer and users. An ac-
==PAGE:25==
3. Theory and Literature Study
13
tive design may be implemented successfully if the designers are provided with sufﬁcient
information to predict the user behaviour in the operation stage.
• Human-in-loop: Information is an essential factor in building energy, as the more infor-
mation a designer possesses, the more accurate the design. The data can be collected by
pre-occupancy data or/and post-occupancy data.
• Technology and method (T&M): T&M includes estimating the energy consumption by
accurate calculations, T&M for energy-related method for data collection and analysis,
T&M for simulations and modelling for the accurate occupant behaviour, and ﬁnally T&M
for controlling energy systems.
• Policy: There are several policies to optimize the building performance, for instance,
Display Energy Certiﬁcate (DEC), Energy Performance Certiﬁcations (EPCs) and Soft
Landings. The challenge is that stakeholders may be unwilling to cooperate, as there are
no direct interests.
• Rating systems, benchmarking and standards: Initiatives such as UK’s ”Building Re-
search Establishment Environment Assessment Method” (BREEAM), US’s ”Leadership
in Energy and Environmental Design” (LEED), and the German certiﬁcation standard
”Deutsche Gesellschaft f¨ur Nachhaltiges Bauen” (DGNB) are helpful to use for closing
BEPG, as these initiatives can be applied within different stages of a building’s life cycle.
However, the challenge is the reliance on predicted energy performance, and the fact that
each building is unique, which makes the knowledge transfer from building to building
complicated.
• Collaboration and communication: Several researchers found that collaboration and
communication among the energy-related stakeholders are highly essential. However, this
may lead to issues related to different objectives, knowledge and prior experience among
the involved stakeholders in the building project.

Building Commissioning
Commissioning (Cx) can be deﬁned as a pro-active dialogue and quality based management
process with the aim of considering the overall costs and the interactions of the technical in-
stallations. The goal-oriented result is to establish an energy-optimized building with satisﬁed
users and a dedicated operating organization. The commissioning process runs parallel with the
building project and can continue until the end of the building’s lifetime (Teisen et al., 2013).
The process of the commissioning activities before the handover of a new building is shown
in Figure 10. The graph (Figure 10) illustrates an increase in the commissioning activities for
the project before operation of the building, and the number of activities is peaking towards the

phase are not shown on the graph, however, the graph indicates a decrease in activities for
continuous commissioning.

end. Due to an increase in the complexity of technical installations for new buildings today, it
is necessary to implement a commissioning process (Plesser and Teisen, 2018). The process
creates a link between the different stakeholders by involving the operation, users, designers,

3.4.1
AMEV: Technical Monitoring
AMEV Recommendation number 135: ”Technical monitoring as an instrument for quality
assurance of building technology” is published by ”Mechanical and Electrical Engineering
Working Party of National, Regional and Local Authorities of Germany (AMEV)” in August
2017. The publication is an ofﬁcial recommendation within ”Quality Management practices”
in the building sector with a technical and functional purpose. The process is deﬁned from the
planning of design to commissioning and operation. The role of the third-party quality manage-
ment is set in the document, which concerns the building’s function, economic commissioning
and the continuous operation of the building (AMEV, 2017). Figure 16 shows technical moni-
toring (Tmon) as an instrument for guaranteeing building performance by quality control loops

• Prepare a ﬁnal Cx process report.

the process.

3.5
Comparison of Danish and international practice
When comparing the Danish Standard DS3090 to LEED ”Enhanced commissioning”, several
similar activities are deﬁned for the operation phase. One similarity is to provide a 10-month re-
view of the building in operation after completion. This requirement is included in the DS3090,
as this standard must be able to be followed while at the same time striving for a sustainabil-
ity certiﬁcation such as DGNB and LEED (Kjerulf, 2019). However, one crucial difference is
”Verify operator and occupant training delivery and effectiveness”, which is stated in LEED
v4 (Table 7). DS3090 is addressing an activity for ”planning of education operating staff” in
the construction phase, but the activity is not further addressed for the operation phase. This
generates a risk for planned educational activities, which are not being followed up. The Dan-
ish Standard states that ”performance of tests” must be done in the operation phase, whereas
LEED v4 deﬁnes that the tests must be veriﬁed according to seasonal testing (Table 7). In
relation to DS3090, there is a risk for technical installations not working as intended for the
different seasons, as the formulation can be interpreted in many ways.
As mentioned earlier, the Danish Standard enables the possibility for ”Planning of consecu-
tively commissioning activities”, however, it is difﬁcult to ﬁgure out which activities are meant
to take place at this stage. LEED v4 therefore continues where the DS3090 releases and allows
the further activities to be interpreted. In relation to ”Enhanced and Monitoring-Based Com-
missioning”, LEED suggests how to monitor the building’s further performance in operation.
When comparing LEED ”Path 2” (Table 7) with the methods in AMV Technical Monitoring
(TMon) Recommendation 135, several similarities are present. TMon is mostly focused on
technical installations without further considerations towards usage and management (AMEV,
2017). Thus, the central aspect is to test and evaluate whether the building meets the perfor-
mance targets. LEED includes similar activities in regards to ”trend monitoring” and evaluation
of performance. Nevertheless, one difference is that ”Path 2” in LEED (Table 7) furthermore
indicates ”training to prevent errors”, which requires planning of operator and occupant train-
ing to detect the technical systems before possible failure/error. Thus, the method for TMon
indicates a ”TMon Service Provider” to provide expertise for the monitoring project (Figure
17). Also, TMon indicates that in-house personnel is necessary, however, the recommendation
is not addressing how to manage a training program in case of an inexperienced operating staff
(AMEV, 2017).
DS3090 and LEED v4 do not indicate which stakeholders are responsible for getting the ac-
tivities completed. This can create confusion in the process if the building owner cannot make
the individual stakeholders accountable for carrying out the activities. ASHRAE Stakeholder’s
guide is a suggestion for which stakeholders will typically be responsible or co-responsible
for the implementation of the various activities (Kettler, 2018). Hopefully, this guide can help
address the complex problem of the necessary collaboration between stakeholders across dis-
ciplines. The stakeholder’s guide is from the United States, which means that the roles and
==PAGE:39==
3. Theory and Literature Study
27
responsibilities could vary from Danish practice. For example, the architect’s role in Den-
mark is mainly related to aesthetic solutions, whereas, for other nations, the architect’s role
varies signiﬁcantly in relation to technical competences. Other important contributing factors
are ensuring the correct use of valuable communication in terms of meeting building owner’s
expectations. A clear division of roles and involvement of the operating team will create an
essential value for the building project, as greater transparency in the process ensures fewer
errors and communication losses (Kjerulf, 2019).
3.6
The concept of ongoing commissioning
An ongoing commissioning (OCx) program aims to address a framework, which considers
the maintenance of the building performance throughout the building’s lifetime and addresses
methods for continuous optimisation of the building performance. The building performance
will degrade over time without a plan for ongoing commissioning. Based on a Texas A&M
study, the ﬁnding was that ”most buildings will lose up to 30% of their energy efﬁciency in the
ﬁrst three years of operation” (Mcfarlane, 2014). Degradation occurs due to various reasons,
for instance, decrease in performance at equipment, component, subsystem and system level.
Ongoing commissioning (OCx) is a process, which considers the last phase of a completed
commissioned building. The initial project requirements are setting the needed level in terms
of how the OCx program will monitor the building performance. The ﬁrst step is to identify
degradation as early as possible. This can be done by different methods, for instance, manual
site visits or by real-time automated fault detection and diagnoses (AFDD) (Mcfarlane, 2014).
An OCx program varies for each building project. However, the basic level includes an on-
site visit to manually review the systems in the building to assess the performance. Typically,
this includes a review of the building automation system (BAS) trend data, setpoints and data
for utility. Furthermore, interviews with the facility operation team can be useful to identify
potential problems in terms of how the systems are operated. At the more advanced level of
OCx, the activity will typically include a type of remote connection of the BAS. This enables
the commissioning engineer to evaluate the data trends to identify fault areas (Mcfarlane, 2014).
The value of implementing an ongoing commissioning process has been studied by the Building
Commissioning Association and Lawrence Berkeley National Lab. The study included 120 re-
sponses from the building industry, 83% commissioning providers and 17% owners, managers,
engineers and contractors from all regions in North America. Ongoing commissioning or long-
time building performance measurements were part of the market survey. Based on the survey,
77 of the respondents answered the following question: ”In how many buildings did you install
an Ongoing Commissioning system or apply an OCx process the past 12 months?”. More than
90% answered that they had completed an OCx in approximately 1-2 buildings (BCA, 2018).
It is assumed that the remaining 43 respondents, approximately 36%, have not performed an
OCx, as they skipped the question. Based on the survey results, an average simple payback for
OCx according to respondents was estimated (Table 9). The results show that by implementing
OCx, 65% of the respondents indicated an average payback period within the ﬁrst 2 years for
the buildings (BCA, 2018).

management was ﬁrst introduced in SBi report 117: ”A system for energy-efﬁcient operation in

for comparison with existing known key ﬁgures. Thus, it is possible to implement corrective
actions, leading to energy efﬁciency. Finally, a status and a budget of the energy consumption
is evaluated for the following year (Aggerholm, 2015).

buildings by monitoring and manage the building operation (Jradi al., 2017).
The COORDICY approach is shown in Figure 19. Commissioning has been part of the building
project from construction towards a continuous commissioning process to optimize the building
operation. Besides, re-commissioning is implemented based on diagnostics, which detects
corrective actions (SDU Energy Informatics, 2018). This method enables the possibility of

heat recovery, underﬂoor heating, LED lights, PV modules, sub-meters for heating, lighting

The reason for the large gap is due to simpliﬁcations and assumptions made in the Be10 tool,

the energy performance. The tool includes a holistic energy model, which runs on a daily ba-

the accumulated weather data, occupant schedules and inputs from the building management
system (BMS). These simulation results are compared to the actual performance data, which is

an ”Online Energy Performance Monitoring Tool”, the building performance is continuously

3.7.3
Value of a Commissioning process
The case study from the master thesis project indicates that application of a commissioning
process throughout the building project has signiﬁcant effects on the building’s continuous op-
eration in regards to energy savings. Furthermore, the case study indicates the great value
of developing a plan for O&M and including the O&M staff in the building process. The
case study concerning the university building UO44 validates the fact that the predicted energy
consumption from Be10 is far from the actual energy performance of a building. Thus, it is
necessary to monitor the building performance in order to optimize the energy consumption,
meet the initial targets, and close the performance gaps. However, it can be challenging to mea-
sure the direct value of a commissioning process, since all buildings are unique, and therefore
there are often large deviations in for example the project team, the building’s use and the way
commissioning is implemented in the project. In addition, it is essential to observe the value
of commissioning besides the immediate savings in energy consumption. The commissioning
process also ensures a satisfactory indoor climate, which can generate enormous savings in
relation to work productivity and reduction in sick days. Other value-creating initiatives as a
result of commissioning can, for example, also be an assurance of ﬁre safety measures, which
potentially can help save lives. Furthermore, the priority of selecting high-quality materials
and technical solutions can have a signiﬁcant impact on the continuous operation and the need
for building maintenance. Finally, the operation of the building highly depends on the user be-
haviour in the building and whether the building’s continuous energy use is monitored to detect
corrective actions for optimization of the building’s performance.

building project. The advantages were that some redundant checkups were avoided and the
communication channels were shorter in terms of getting things done. Furthermore, he states
that if your own company is not performing as expected, it is a difﬁcult situation for the com-

which measurable requirements the company should be evaluated against (Albretchsen, 2019).

4.2.4
Comparison of case studies
The ﬁrst presented case study in the theory concerning two Danish shopping malls demonstrates
the value of applying a commissioning process by comparing two similar building types. The
contractor and the owner for both building projects were the same, which enabled the oppor-
tunity to learn from previous experiences and transfer valuable knowledge through the same
involved stakeholders for the new building project. DTU Campus Service is in a similar sit-
uation, as the organization is involved in several building projects, and thus can learn from
previous project experiences internally in the organization. Nevertheless, the building projects
vary in terms of size and complexity. The building project 202 belongs to a more extreme case,
as the building includes 250 laboratories and a signiﬁcant size of 27.500 m2. Unfortunately,
DTU CAS only had a limited amount of experience at the time when building 202 was to be
built. As previously mentioned, until 2012, DTU CAS had not built any new buildings for a
long period of time (Bechmann, 2019). As new buildings today become more technically com-
plex combined with the special technical installations for a building as B202, this building is a
striking contrast to DTU’s existing yellow brick buildings from the 1960s. Hence, the building
project 202 represents one of the ﬁrst types within new laboratory buildings at DTU Campus,
which requires comprehensive planning and prioritization of the technical installations in the
building project. Additionally, the case study concerning the two shopping malls, represents
a building type with less technical complications compared to a laboratory building as B202.
This means that there are several more aspects to consider in a laboratory building, which com-
plicates the process of valuable knowledge transfer. Nevertheless, DTU CAS strives to include
prior experiences by developing DTU standards for implementation in future building projects,
and there is an increased focus of prioritizing the commissioning process with DTU’s new com-
missioning model (Bechmann, 2019).
The second case study regarding the teaching building (OU44) at the University of Southern
Denmark represents a learning lab for applying commissioning in the operation phase. The con-
tinuous monitoring of the building’s usage includes a comprehensive planning process, as the
building is equipped with multiple cameras for counting occupants, energy meters and sensors.
The case indicates that the application of ”monitoring-based” commissioning requires plan-
ning of how to evaluate the collected data. Also, the optimization process requires resources
and competencies to analyze the data. When comparing with case study B202, a data-driven
energy optimization plan is not thought into the building. Consequently, it is not possible to ac-
cess all the necessary data in detail and there were issues with obtaining valid data from energy
meters (Table 18). In addition, building 202 still struggles with performance gaps 2.5 years
after completion (Table 19). As a result, ongoing errors and deﬁciencies that need to be ﬁxed
in the building are primarily the focus of work tasks for the operating staff (Simonsen, 2019).
According to Dorthe Bechmann, building 202 accounts for a huge energy cost and it would,
therefore, make sense to establish a plan regarding how to lower the energy consumption in the
operation phase considering the size of the building (Bechmann, 2019).

4.3
Evaluation of case study
The presented case study concerning commissioning at DTU CAS, including the building
project 202 indicated several interesting results. The results demonstrate a development to-
wards a greater focus on quality assurance for DTU’s building projects. Also, it has been an
aim to comply with DGNB Gold for new building projects (Table 14), however, the future aim
is to obtain DGNB Gold certiﬁcation for the building projects (Bechmann, 2019). This indi-
cates an increased focus on integrating sustainability for the projects, as compliance with the
criteria will be evaluated. The Danish standard DS3090 was not applied as the foundation for
the building project 202’s Cx process, as the standard was completely new at this time. Nev-
ertheless, building project 202 has helped to form the basis for optimizing processes within
a more internal controlled commissioning process and development of own DTU standards.
Thus, ongoing work is underway to ensure an improved transition in the handover process
from the construction management team to the operating staff. This also includes an increased
focus on adequate O&M material and earlier involvement of operating staff in terms of choos-
ing operational friendly solutions for the future DTU building projects. This must help to
form the framework for a more painless move-in process and a minimization of performance
gaps that continue into the operation phase. Furthermore, the results indicate that the com-
missioning process ends approximately one year after the building goes into operation. Thus,
DTU CAS is not planning any building projects with the purpose of implementing ”ongoing”
or ”monitoring-based” commissioning process, where operating- and energy optimization is
intended as ongoing activities. Therefore, there is room for further development of the com-
missioning process for DTU’s building projects and the result analysis will examine the value
of the commissioning process in the operation phase further, including the handover process.
In this way, the case study sets the framework for further investigation in terms of establishing
recommendations for commissioning strategies in the operation phase for DTU CAS.

5.1
Preconditions for application of ongoing commissioning
In order to apply an ongoing commissioning process, several of the expert interviews pointed
out the importance of ensuring an ”error-free” building project. However, it is important to
mention that a building project can never be completely ”error-free” due to ﬁnancial constraints
and the fact that each building project is unique. As mentioned earlier in the theory, ”ongoing
commissioning” considers the last phase of a completed commissioned building (Mcfarlane,
2014). Thus, the ongoing commissioning process carries on the previous Cx activities for the
building project and is dependent on the efﬁciency of the prior commissioning process. The
case study building 202 indicated a common issue when new complex buildings are handed
over from the construction management team to the operating staff according to the expert in-
terviews. New buildings often go into operation with numerous deﬁciencies, which ends up be-
ing a hurdle to the building owner. This problem indicates that there remain several challenges
to overcome in regards to the functionality of new building projects. According to Thomas
Rysgaard Jacobsen, the largest problem persists in achieving an error-free building project and
correcting all the mistakes. Therefore, it is a matter of handling the mistakes and issues in the
building, when it is not completed. This often results in additional expenses for the building
owner in terms of correcting the mistakes from the construction process. Furthermore, there is
a tendency that the Danish building industry has grown accustomed to the operating staff will
complete the building project in terms of ﬁnding and correcting faults. Nevertheless, the work
tasks for the operating staff should be related to issues based on operational conditions and not
unﬁnished solutions derived from the building project (Jacobsen, 2019). Ole Teisen points out
another common issue in regards to the transition from the building project to the operation
phase. He states that there remain performance gaps, as building projects often separate the
construction budget from the operating budget (Figure 28).

5.2
Commissioning in the operation phase
The interviews indicated a wide confusion in terms of the commissioning process in the opera-
tion phase, as there is no clear deﬁnition of what this entails. As a result, there remain attitudes
towards that the commissioning process is only applied in terms of getting the building into
operation. Thus, several of the interviews indicated that they do not associate a commission-
ing process with operating optimization or can see the connection to already known principles
such as energy management. Mogens Bang Nielsen states that when a building is handed over
according to Danish building regulations, the Cx process should be completed and any deﬁ-
ciencies will be registered for corrections. Also, he believes that operation optimization is not
part of a Cx process (Nielsen, 2019b). According to Bo Lind-Høj, the Cx process should con-
tribute to the building working faultlessly after 1-1.5 year, where all the deﬁciencies are found
and corrected at this time. From this point, the building is transiting into another phase, which
includes the planning of continuous maintenance and operation activities (Olsen et al., 2019).
Nevertheless, the theory and literature study points out that Cx in the operation phase also in-
cludes activities within the optimization and maintenance of building performance. In addition,
it may be difﬁcult to separate the boundary between ”getting the building into operation” and
”operation phase” (Figure 15). The Danish standard 3090 lacks the division, as the two phases
are merged. Thus, all the activities included for the operation phase in DS3090 except ”plan-
ning of consecutively commissioning activities” represent measures for getting the building
into operation. This means that the Danish practice (DS3090) does not address the ”ongoing”
part of the commissioning process, however, the standard suggests the possibility for planning
consecutively Cx activities (Danish Standards Association, 2014). Ole Teisen is one of the ex-
perts for this study, and he is furthermore one of the authors behind the Danish Standard 3090.
According to Ole Teisen, there are reasons to reconsider the Danish Standard, as it is now 5
years old. He states that monitoring-based commissioning should be deﬁned further. Besides,
the phases should be adapted according to the phases in the new AB18 agreement to ﬁt with the
current Danish practice for building projects (Teisen, 2019). In order to address what deﬁnes
a Cx process in the operation phase, it is important to separate between Cx activities related
to the completion of the building project and Cx activities related to the building’s continuous
operation. This also means that the established commissioning group for the building project
will change, when the building is ready for ongoing Cx activities.

6.1
Results and limitations of the research
The section will include a general evaluation and discussion of the collected data for the thesis.
Furthermore, a comparison of the case study building 202 and Niels Bohr building project will
be discussed. Finally, the barriers of the Cx process in the operation phase and the value of
implementing an ongoing Cx process will be discussed.
6.1.1
Data collection and processing
The data collection consisted primarily of personal interviews, which gives an uncertainty in
the results, as personal/individual attitudes and prior experiences will inﬂuence the answers. In
addition, doubts can be raised as to whether these interviewees represent reality. However, it
has been attempted to conduct interviews with people who differ from each other in regards to
job function. Nevertheless, particular emphasis has been placed on the fact that all the intervie-
wees have many years of working experience within the subject. In regards to the case study
interviews, there was an overrepresentation of operating staff from DTU CAS. The reason for
this was that the selected interviewees represented the greatest knowledge of building 202 con-
cerning the technical systems in the operation phase. This was also the reason why interviews
of the users in building 202 were not included in the research, as it was assumed that this group
would not generate sufﬁcient knowledge regarding the building’s technical function. Never-
theless, the perspective from the users would be interesting in case of more in-depth studies
within the subject. Another uncertainty is the data processing, as there is a large difference
between spoken language and written language. The transcriptions are very much accurate to
the interviewees’ own words to ensure clariﬁcation and avoid misunderstandings. However, it
was necessary to do a translation from Danish to English as well as a conversion of the spo-
ken language to a more formal written language as the basis for the results. This generates
a risk of distortion the statements, as elements such as tone of voice, body language, the ex-
act timing of wording cannot be included as part of the results. Furthermore, Danish sayings,
wordplay, ﬁgurative language, and choice of words may be lost in the translation from Danish
to English. Besides, it is hard to determine whether the right questions have been asked. The
risk of conducting interviews is the limited time to ask prepared questions. Would the study
show improved results if other questions were asked? This is difﬁcult to answer as it would
require additional repeated interviews with the same people. Nevertheless, it was attempted to
ask in-depth questions in case the interviewee exhibited more knowledge within a particular
question. The experience of conducting interviews as part of the data collection has been a
learning process. Thus, the ﬁrst interviews have helped to set the framework and improve the
prepared questions for the further interviews in the thesis.
The quantitative data collection proved to be associated with more challenges than expected.

6.1.2
Comparison of building project 202 and Niels Bohr building project
DTU’s building 202 and the Niels Bohr building project are characterized as large technically
complex laboratory buildings. Furthermore, both buildings are intended for research purposes
at the universities, Copenhagen University (KU) and the Technical University of Denmark
(DTU), located in the capital area of Copenhagen. Therefore, it makes sense to compare the
building projects, as they include several similarities. The Niels Bohr building includes 52.000
m2 and the construction period was planned from 2015-2017 (Juulsager, 2019). Nevertheless,
the building project is more than two years delayed and the budget has increased from the
initial budget of 1.6 billion DKK to 2.9 billion DKK (Richter, 2018). Both building projects
have the similarity that reconstructions had to be made for the projects, which caused delays in
the time tables. For the building project 202, reconstructions were made at the same time, as the
users were moving in. Hence, the building project 202 complied with the construction budget
and the handover date. Nevertheless, the operation phase for building 202 has been associated
with signiﬁcantly higher operation costs and parts of the building project were deferred to the
operation stage (Table 19). Thus, there is great prestige in getting building projects completed
on time and comply with the construction budget, whereas buildings’ operational functionality

is more or less neglected in this context. As a result, the Niels Bohr project is often referred to
in the media as the ”scandal building” because the focus is on the substantial delay and the large
exceeding of the construction budget (Juulsager, 2019). On the other hand, DTU’s building 202
is valued for its impressive architecture as well as the compliance of the construction budget and
the scheduled handover date (Kokkeg˚ard, 2017). But what are the success criteria for a building
project and does it make sense only to evaluate success based on these two parameters? This
does not seem to make sense in practice, and the experts ﬁnd it difﬁcult to understand why the
interaction between stakeholders in complex building projects remain an issue in the Danish
building industry. In addition, the building owner is expecting that technical installations and
systems are thoroughly tested and functional by the time the users move into the building. The
ﬁnancial driving force for cheaper solutions and optimistic price estimates in buildings projects
remains an issue. As a consequence, expensive reconstructions and operating costs usually end
up being more costly than making adequate sustainable investments at an early stage in the
building project. Therefore, success criteria of building projects should be assessed more from
a holistic point-of-view, where ﬁnancial investments are to a greater extent governed by quality
measures such as operational reliability and long-term durable solutions.
6.1.3
The barriers of the Cx process in the operation phase
The results showed that there are several barriers to implement a commissioning process in
the operating phase. Besides, multiple preconditions in the building project are an obstacle for
continuing an efﬁcient Cx process, which must form the basis for ensuring the building’s con-
tinuous operation. Larger structures such as contractual basis and economic incentives must
not be underestimated in this context, and these may contribute to the Cx process having a
problematic starting point. Thus, it is essential to keep in mind that the Cx process is running
parallel to the building project, which makes the Cx process highly dependent on the project
basis. Unfortunately, the Cx process is often neglected in case of budget overruns or time con-
straints in the building project. Nevertheless, the connection between the building project’s Cx
process and the building’s continuous operation unite the potential for considering operational
friendly solutions for the beneﬁt of the building owner and the users. However, is it possible
to shift the focus in building projects so that operational conditions gain higher priority? As
mentioned earlier, the division between the construction budget and operating budget is one
of the main barriers (Figure 28). This also affects the stakeholders’ sense of responsibility,
as there is a lack of ﬁnancial incentives to built operational friendly buildings. However, the
expert interviews mentioned possible solutions to this problem that may help the process of
investing in the building operation. One of the initiatives is OPP, which is a private-public part-
nership. OPP includes a 30-year construction and operating budget, which is handled by the
same partner (Teisen, 2019). Another initiative is ESCO (Energy Service Companies), which
is a form of collaboration for achieving energy savings in existing buildings. The reduction of
energy consumption will pay for the optimization of the building’s technical systems (Bygn-
ingsstyrelsen, 2016). However, it has been chosen not to go into depth with these initiatives, as
this study aims to ﬁnd strategies for DTU Campus Service based on methods within commis-
sioning. Thus, ESCO is not relevant for DTU CAS to implement, as an external partner would
interfere quite a lot with the existing activities. According to an informal conversation with
==PAGE:85==

DTU CAS project leader Lene Kuszon, the process would require setting up of new compo-
nents, even though several of them work as they should. In addition, DTU CAS would have a
lack of control over the process as another operating system had to be integrated. Furthermore,
DTU CAS would have to notify the private partner every time there were changes in the en-
ergy consumption patterns in the buildings, which would require too many resources. Dorthe
Bechmann emphasizes that OPP would not make sense for DTU CAS to implement on future

6.2
Recommendations for DTU CAS strategies
At DTU CAS, ongoing work has been done in order to achieve a more smooth transition from
the building project towards getting the building into operation. This is furthermore the rea-
son why the commissioning process has been highly prioritized for the new building projects,
as it was simply a necessity based on prior experiences. The case study building 202 was a
learning experience for DTU CAS, which generated the need for controlling and manage the
commissioning process more internally in the organization, as to ensure the interests of DTU.
The organization has the advantage of handling all facility management functions as the build-
ing owner and the responsible stakeholder for the buildings’ operation and maintenance. This
is reﬂected in closer collaboration and improved communication channels among the operat-
ing staff and project leaders for the DTU projects. Also, an open space ofﬁce building 409 at
DTU Lyngby Campus facilitates easy access to each other and the possibilities of knowledge
sharing. Nevertheless, the implementation of a commissioning process remains relatively new
for the DTU projects, which enables the potential for further development. The commissioning
process in the operation phase is an overlooked topic in practice, which was furthermore the
case for DTU CAS.
Recommendations of strategies for commissioning in the operation phase for DTU Campus
Service are developed on the basis of own assessments by application of results from the col-
lected data and knowledge sharing throughout the thesis period. Furthermore, two strategies are
developed, as to consider one strategy in regards to future DTU building projects and another
strategy for considering the existing buildings at DTU Campus. In this way, the commissioning
process is observed from different stages in the building process. This is due to the fact that both
the presented theory and results point out the importance of inﬂuencing building projects at an
early stage in the process. An efﬁcient commissioning plan from the beginning of the project as
part of initial decision making can contribute to avoiding costly faults, which typically reveal
themselves in the operation phase. However, proactive actions made in the building project, as
well as the operation phase, may remedy these issues throughout the building’s lifetime.

Conclusion and further research
7.1
Main ﬁndings
This thesis examined aspects of the commissioning process in the operation phase with the
purpose of developing commissioning strategies for DTU Campus Service. Hence, DTU CAS
has been applied as a case study and sets the framework for the study. The data collection in-
cluded eleven interviews and energy data from the main meters in building 202, which formed
the basis for processing the results. Through the presented results in the sections ”Analysis and
Results” and ”Discussion”, several conclusions can be drawn to answer the research questions.
Firstly, the preconditions for applying an ”ongoing” commissioning process demonstrate that
the Cx process in the operation phase is highly dependent on the success level of the building
project and the efﬁciency of the prior Cx process. Therefore, previous mistakes made in the
building project will generate a domino effect in the operation phase, which can potentially
prevent the implementation of an ”ongoing” Cx process. Based on statements from the ex-
pert interviews, a list of barriers for achieving an error-free building project was developed.
The main barriers are: lower priority of technical systems for the building projects, the sepa-
ration between the construction budget and the operating budget, and a tendency to prioritize
cheap solutions in the building projects rather than choosing long-term operational solutions.
In addition, a description of the characterizations of an effective commissioning process was
established. An efﬁcient Cx process includes a united effort among the involved stakeholders
and planning of the Cx process according to the timing of asking the right questions to the right
people at the correct times in the building process. Also, the building owner must take owner-
ship of the Cx process and give the surrendered authority and trust to those who are responsible
for implementing the Cx process to achieve the necessary priority among the stakeholders.
The deﬁnition of the commissioning process in the operation phase can be divided into two
phases: 1) getting the building into operation and 2) the continuous operation of the building.
The ﬁrst phase is related to the completion of the building project and includes the following
stakeholders: the Cx leader, contractors, manufacturers, suppliers and the design engineers.
These stakeholders are part of the building project until approximately one year after the han-
dover process. The second phase is related to the continuous operation until the end of the
building’s lifetime. The results indicate doubts as to whether operating staff today possesses
the adequate competencies to handle Cx activities within BMS application and analysis of en-
ergy data. Nevertheless, ”ongoing” Cx activities can be conducted by the operating staff and/or
an external partner.
Although there was confusion about what ”ongoing” commissioning entails, there was a broad
consensus among the expert interviews that a continuation of the Cx process would create
value in the operation phase. This resulted in several advantages in regards to implementing a
Cx process in the operation phase. These include: increase in user satisfaction, cheaper opera-
tion expenses, decrease in energy consumption and CO2-emissions, improved indoor climate,
greater control of the technical systems, and ensuring of compliance and follow up with the ini-

lenges in terms of getting organizations to comprehend the gains of prioritizing a Cx process
in the operation phase. These challenges include: lack of resources for operating optimization
among organizations, the building owner/tenant is usually not willing to spend money on on-
going Cx activities, and there remain continuously faults from building projects. Furthermore,
the operating staff is typically occupied with daily ad hoc tasks, and there is a lack of focus
on technical systems like BMS in the building projects. Finally, it is difﬁcult to establish a
business plan for optimizing building operation, thus, there is generally a lower prioritization
of investing in building operation among organizations.
When addressing which Cx activities that would generate gains for the building owner in re-
gards to the building’s continuous performance, several suggestions for Cx activities were de-
veloped based on the expert interviews. According to the expert interviews, the operation phase
is often neglected in terms of planned Cx activities. The building owner is in a vulnerable po-
sition due to the difﬁculty of placing legal responsibility in case of operational faults from
the building project. As a result, the valuable Cx activities include increased considerations
towards legal obligations to the involved stakeholders in the building project. Moreover, the
suggestions for Cx activities are primarily focused on the documentation, management, and
testing of the technical systems as well as the possibilities within new building technologies.
Finally, two commissioning strategies in the operation phase for DTU Campus Service were
developed, as to consider one strategy in regards to future DTU building projects and another
strategy for considering the existing buildings at DTU Campus. Both strategies encompassed
ﬁve focus areas, where each focus area entails Cx actions and suggestions for the responsible
stakeholder(s). The ﬁrst strategy is aimed at the future building projects and includes the fol-
lowing focus areas: 1) Financial incentives and milestones for building performance, 2) Testing
procedure, 3) O&M material, 4) Operating requirements and 5) Planning of data application.
The Cx actions suggest an increased legal obligation to the stakeholders, an extension of the test
period before occupancy and proper descriptions of the standard operating procedure (SOP) of
the technical systems, which includes test procedures and revised descriptions of the technical
systems. Besides, incorporation of increased operating requirements until the 5-year inspection
is recommended to ensure technical functionality. Additionally, the following actions are also
recommended: developing a plan for the level of detailed energy building data and validation of
accuracy from the meters/sensors by technical reviews. Thus, this will form the basis for addi-
tional actions within energy optimization in the operation phase. The second strategy is aimed
for the existing buildings and includes the focus areas: 1) Energy management/monitoring-
based Cx, 2) Checklists, 3) Collection of valuable O&M information, 4) DTU Standards and
5) Training of DTU CAS staff. The recommended Cx actions concern: analyzing building data
for corrective actions, checklists for the setting of operating working time for passive systems,
and continuous update of the O&M material in the program DaluxFM. Furthermore, additional
requirements in the DTU Standards are suggested for energy meters/sensors concerning the
level of detail and follow-up of data accuracy. Lastly, the Cx actions include establishing of

educational courses within BMS and data application for the operating staff at DTU CAS.
This research pointed out that the Cx process in the operation phase is at an early stage in
the Danish building industry. Thus, the beneﬁts of considering operation optimization are not
integrated as an essential part of building projects today. DTU CAS is on the right path towards
prioritizing the Cx process for the building projects and implementing valuable knowledge in
the DTU Standards. However, there is potential for further development of the Cx process in
the operation phase for DTU CAS to address the buildings’ increased technical complexity and
utilizing these new possibilities as part of the Cx actions.
7.2
Further research
Based on this research of the comprehensive topic of building commissioning, there are many
opportunities for additional investigations. Thus, the subject of the Cx process in the operation
phase remains as relatively new in a Danish context. Further research within this ﬁeld would
be helpful in terms of generating an increased awareness of the Cx process in practice. This
section will address potential recommendations for a continuation of the conducted thesis.
The ﬁrst recommendation is considering the beneﬁts of the established DTU Commissioning
model. At the time of this thesis, it was not possible to evaluate DTU building projects, where
the commissioning process was set by DTU’s own Cx model. Thus, it could have been inter-
esting to compare newer building projects, which included the DTU Cx model, with the case
study building 202. From here, an evaluation of the implemented Cx process for the building
projects could identify the gains of controlling the Cx process more internally by DTU CAS.
Besides, a comparison of the performance gaps could provide an interesting insight towards the
performance level of the building projects at the time of handover.
The second recommendation is evaluating possible gains of energy management/monitoring-
based commissioning by comparing the energy building data from building 202 with an older
building from the 1960s at DTU Lyngby Campus. The expert interviews indicated that meth-
ods within energy optimization in practice are mostly concerned with buildings that are at least
10 years old (Nielsen, 2019a). Thus, the study could identify actions for energy optimization
for respectively, a new building, which includes greener technology and passive systems with
an older building, which primarily comprises active systems. This raises questions like: How
can ”green” technology help address energy efﬁcient actions? And what are the differences in
energy gains for an old building compared to a newer building?
The third recommendation deals with the validation of the two recommendations for DTU
CAS strategies. Hence, the suggested Cx actions are investigated more in-depth in terms of
implementation and potential value in practice. This could be done by qualitative methods such
as interviews and/or surveys/questionnaires aimed at the DTU CAS organization. In addition,
the study could investigate the possibilities of implementing the strategies in practice for similar
organizations. In this way, it is possible to develop more general Cx strategies, which can be
adapted by other organizations based on individual conditions and preferences.
