Abstract
This project develops and evaluates bidding models for a grid-connected battery in the DK2
region participating in Day-Ahead and Frequency Containment Reserve-Disturbance mar-
kets. Two widely researched models for decision-making under uncertainty, namely stochastic
and feature-driven models are investigated. The key differences and performance outcomes
of these models are presented, along with the benefits of employing simpler models such as
rule-based and deterministic models. Findings highlight the importance of accurate price
forecasts and the corresponding bid acceptance models. In cases of substantial forecast errors
and non-acceptance of the bids, the more complex and risk-averse stochastic and feature-
driven models demonstrated enhanced reliability. However, incorporating conservativeness
into the bid prices drastically increased the acceptance of the bids, elevating the performance
of the simple deterministic model over complex models. Furthermore, given a realistic fore-
cast error, the project reveals that the participation in D-2 auction proved to increase the
reliability over relatively more volatile D-1 auctions. On the other hand, if accurate fore-
casts are available, huge peaks in D-1 prices could be exploited. These insights guide market
participants in selecting and implementing bidding models for profit optimization based on
market conditions and risk preferences.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The growing penetration of variable weather-driven renewable energy sources, such as wind
and solar power, introduces a significant amount of uncertainty for the power system [1]. The
inherent uncertainty associated with these energy sources significantly impacts grid stability
and necessitates strategic planning to address the balance between electricity production
and consumption. Moreover, it is estimated that the demand for flexible assets and reserves
will increase 25%-75% by the year 2032 [2].
Several avenues are available for enhancing flexibility in power systems, such as district
heating [3], demand-side flexibility [4], interconnections [5], storage systems, and many more.
Among these options, grid-scale battery storage has emerged and proven competitive for grid
stability and balancing services [6]. In Denmark, the first grid-integrated battery storage was
installed in 2017, as a part of EnergyLab Nordhavn project [7]. The battery was installed
for optimally utilizing power from renewables and supporting the grid by peak-shaving. The
business case for grid-scale batteries has revealed a vast repertoire of lucrative opportunities
for battery operators in terms of services and economic value. Hence, a potential battery
owner can pose the questions:
• Which electricity markets or services present the most advantageous opportunities for
the battery to participate in as a price taker?
• Once the market has been selected, what is the optimal operational and bidding strat-
egy to maximize profit for the battery?
Both research questions are highly compelling; however, this project focuses specifically on
addressing the second question to determine the optimal operational strategy for chosen
electricity markets. The objective of the project is to explore and compare widely used and
researched bidding models to optimize profits. Through rigorous analysis and evaluation,
the project aims to shed light on the distinctive features, advantages, and limitations of each
bidding model, providing valuable insights into their applicability and effectiveness for profit
optimization.
1.2
Literature review
Various revenue streams and applications for grid-scale batteries are explored in [8] with
respect to some of the relevant factors for batteries such as discharge duration and annual
cycles.
Among the different services investigated, frequency services exhibit shorter dis-
charge durations and reasonable annual cycles compared to other services like congestion
management, energy arbitrage, and renewable integration. On a similar note, [9] examines
multi-market bidding in Nordic frequency markets. This paper highlights the significance
of considering battery usage in decision-making, emphasizing that Frequency Containment

Reserve - Disturbance (FCR-D) market proves to be a better choice when accounting for bat-
tery degradation, despite potentially generating higher revenue from Frequency Containment
Reserve - Normal operation (FCR-N) due to its higher frequency of activation. Hence, this

Furthermore, an energy-intensive Day-Ahead (DA) market is also considered which can be

regulation in FCR-D. The tender conditions for FCR-D, as well as the technical prequalifi-
cation of these markets used for model development, are provided by Energinet [10], [11].
With regard to the bidding models, two widely researched models: stochastic and feature-
driven models will be explored in this project. Firstly, stochastic programming has emerged
as a valuable tool by leveraging probabilistic techniques and statistical methods to account
for uncertainties. The books [12] and [13] lay foundational work for building multi-stage
stochastic models and provide insights on uncertainty modeling and scenario generation
methods. An application of a two-stage stochastic program in DA market is presented in
[14] for joint wind and hydro storage assets. A sequential trading decision in reserve, DA, and
intraday markets is formulated as multi-stage stochastic programming in [15]. The compu-
tational intractability associated with the "curse of dimensionality" in multi-stage stochastic
programming is addressed in [16] through a rolling-horizon procedure. These papers serve
as guiding references for developing the multi-stage stochastic model in this project, estab-
lishing temporal relations for storage variables and constructing scenario trees to represent
decision stages.
Secondly, feature-driven frameworks are gaining popularity for their flexibility and adapt-
ability by incorporating relevant attributes into decision-making. [17] implemented valuable
predictors (called features) such as the historical wind speed and the forecast of future wind
speed to improve the forecast of wind power to make trading decisions in the electricity mar-
ket. [18] also implements a feature-driven framework in the context of a joint electrolyzer
and wind operation, and shows improved performance in revenue from combined wind trad-
ing and hydrogen production. The two references will serve as the starting point to build
the feature-driven model which will be applied for bidding in FCR-D and DA markets.
1.3
Contribution and research question
In light of the advancements in stochastic and feature-driven models for various applica-
tions, this project aims to compare both models for battery operation in FCR-D and DA
markets. In addition, the project also compares the two models with simpler models, such
as rule-based and deterministic models, which are more commonly used in the industry[13].
The construction of these models is presented, followed by a detailed examination of their re-
spective performance and thorough analysis to gain insights into the optimal model selection.
The project is guided by two research questions, the first objective involves a comprehensive
comparison between the two stochastic and feature-driven models:

What are the key differences and performance outcomes of stochastic and feature-driven
optimization models used for bidding in DA and FCR-D markets?
The second research question examines the benefits of employing simpler rule-based and

1.4
Performance evaluation and metrics
In order to adequately evaluate and objectively compare the performance of different models,
it is imperative to conduct diverse analyses and follow a common quantifiable metric. Across
all the models and analyses, the generated profit will be used as the main performance metric.
Profit = pricemarket · bidmarket(x) −cost(x)
(1)
Profit is described in (1), where the revenue is given by price times the bid quantity, sub-
tracted by the operational cost. x denotes decision variables in vector representation. The
formulations for each of the terms are described in detail in later chapters. To evaluate the
effectiveness and robustness of the proposed models, a two-fold approach is adopted. Firstly,
the results will be presented under standardized conditions, ensuring a fair and direct com-
parison of the models’ inherent capabilities and limitations. This analysis provides a clear
understanding of each model’s performance and facilitates meaningful comparisons. Sec-
ondly, a sensitivity analysis will be conducted to explore the models’ response to variations
in input parameters. By systematically altering these parameters, the analysis unveils the
comparative advantages and unique characteristics of each model, enabling a comprehensive
assessment of their potential and robustness.

2.1
Market overview
In Denmark, the wholesale market consists of Day-Ahead (DA), intraday, and ancillary ser-
vices. In DA and intraday markets, energy is traded, whereas in ancillary service markets,
reserves are traded to maintain stable frequencies. The trading, clearing, settlement, and
associated services for day-ahead and intraday markets are operated by Nordpool, whereas
the ancillary services are operated by Energinet. Denmark is separated into two bidding
zones: DK1 and DK2 for western and eastern Denmark, connecting the continental Eu-
rope and nordic system respectively. In DK2 region, there are various ancillary services to
be delivered, including: Fast Frequency Reserve(FFR), Frequency-Controlled Disturbance
Reserve up/down (FCR-D↑/↓), Frequency-Controlled Normal operation Reserve(FCR-N),
Secondary Reserve(aFRR), and Manual Reserve(mFRR). These ancillary services differ in
terms of their activation mechanisms, response times, and intended purposes. As mentioned,
the battery owner chooses to participate in three markets: DA, FCR-D↑, and FCR-D↓. The
FCR-D markets have two bidding closures as of the tender conditions released on 20 April
2023 [10], two days before the day of operation (FD2) and the remaining part is procured
the day before (FD1). Figure 1 shows the timeline of the three markets of interest, with gate
closure times.
Figure 1: Market timeline for FCR-D and DA markets with correspond gate closure times
The FCR-D market follows a pay-as-bid1 scheme where hourly bid price and quantity should
be submitted, and if the bid is accepted, the participant gets paid the bid price.
The
participant is informed about the acceptance by D-2 4pm for FD2 bids and D-1 8pm for
FD1 bids, shortly after their corresponding gate closures. The DA market follows a uniform
price scheme where the bids are paid the clearing price if accepted.
2.2
FCR-D market
FCR-D is a fast and automatic reserve designed to regulate the frequency of major system
disturbances. There are two services for FCR-D depending on the direction of regulation
required: up- and down-regulation.

Although the rule-based model exhibits very advantageous aspects of simplicity and effi-
ciency, there are several caveats to the current setup. One notable drawback is the exclusion
of D-1 bids, preventing the incorporation of updated forecasts. Consequently, the model
cannot take advantage of high FD1 prices. Furthermore, the performance of the model is
highly contingent upon the predefined thresholds. With the existing threshold values of 90%
and 110% of the DA price forecast, the battery is compelled to discharge and charge at
least once daily, potentially leading to a sub-optimal operation. The model’s potential can
be extended by incorporating data-driven methods for determining the thresholds instead
of relying solely on expert decisions. Additionally, the current implementation lacks rules
applied to FCR-D prices, under the assumption of high DA prices. The model also assumes
FD2 bids are never activated which may cause infeasibility in real-time operation. Nonethe-
less, the rule-based approach can be easily expanded by introducing nested if-statements to
accommodate more complex scenarios and pricing conditions.

3.4
Stochastic model
The stochastic model represents as an extension of the deterministic model, enhancing its
capabilities by incorporating uncertainties to the forecasted parameters in the determinis-
tic model. Instead of using a point forecast, a probabilistic forecast offers a discrete set
of scenarios that encompass potential outcomes for the operational day. The inclusion of
uncertainties consequently decreases the expected profit when compared to the perfect fore-
cast of the oracle model. However, it also offers a more risk-averse approach that can prove
advantageous in real-time operations.

3.5
Feature-driven model
Compared to the deterministic and stochastic models, the feature-driven optimization model
incorporates a more flexible and adaptable framework for decision-making. By explicitly
reconstructing the bidding quantity decision as a linear function of coefficients and features,
the model can capture the complexities and nuances that can be explained from features
other than the market prices themselves (as in the case of deterministic and stochastic
models).

3.6
Real-time operation model
The real-time operational model plays a crucial role in evaluating the feasibility and the
effectiveness of bids generated by the different models: oracle, rule-based, deterministic,
stochastic, and feature-driven models.
The real-time model checks that the bids conform to the operational constraints on the ac-
tual day of operation. It assesses whether the charge and discharge limits are violated, and
penalizes with respect to the deviation of the established limits. The information passed
from the bidding model contains the bid quantities for FCR-D and DA, and the bid prices
for FCR-D markets. The input from the realized market data includes the cleared price for
DA, the volume-weighted average of FCR-D prices, and the real-time activation. By incor-
porating these inputs, the model calculates the revenue obtained from each market, the true
operational cost and penalties. The total profit as a final output is labelled henceforth the
realized profit whereas the objective function from the bidding models is labeled henceforth
the anticipated profit. The real-time operation model applies the following algorithm 2.

a broad spectrum of cutting-edge techniques. However, given the specific emphasis of this

significantly reduces the size of the scenario tree which will improve computational time.
Since the key benefit of the multi-stage stochastic model is the construction of a series of
recourse decisions after the unknown parameters are realized, the input parameters should
also reflect the scenario tree to make more informed recourse decisions instead of simply
cross-pairing scenarios.

6
Discussion
In the preceding Chapter 5, comprehensive results were presented regarding the performance
of different bidding models and their sensitivity to various relevant factors. Building upon
these findings, this chapter aims to derive meaningful insights from the results and address
the research questions.
6.1
FCR-D market bidding insights
The price forecast and its acceptance were clearly influential factors in the models’ perfor-
mance. The benchmark provided by the oracle model showed that the up-regulation markets
have greater potential for generating revenue compared to the down-regulation markets. This
outcome was expected, considering the larger volume and higher price per volume observed
in section 2.2.3 for up-regulation. A carefully designed forecast and acceptance model rep-
resenting this general trend should be employed to maximize revenue, while considering risk
measures to avoid over-dependence on one market type.
The accuracy of the forecast has a direct impact on how revenue is distributed across various
auctions. When working with a forecast that has a realistic margin of error, it is reasonable
to anticipate some level of bids not being accepted. In this context, the D-2 auctions provide
a safer option for relying on the majority of bid quantities compared to the D-1 auctions.
While taking advantage of high D-1 auction prices carries the risk of non-acceptance, the
decision to tap into D-1 auctions depends on risk preferences and the accuracy of frequency
deviation forecasts.
6.2
Stochastic vs feature-driven models
The two key models developed in this project are stochastic and feature-driven models. In
order to address the first research question, the notable disparities and performance outcomes
between these two models are presented below.
Approach to uncertainty
The stochastic model explicitly incorporates uncertainties by generating probabilistic sce-
narios to represent uncertain inputs or parameters. It quantifies uncertainty and produces
probabilistic outcomes.
The feature-driven model may not explicitly capture uncertainty as the stochastic model.
Instead, it focuses on extracting relevant features or patterns from the available data to
linearly fit coefficients.
51
==PAGE:59==
6
Discussion
Input considerations
The stochastic model often requires explicit specification of probability distributions for un-
certain parameters or inputs. In this project, the generation of probabilistic outcomes is
highly simplified by selecting historical days as scenarios, aiming to preserve the intertem-
poral relations and interdependencies among different parameters. However, with the one-
to-many structure implemented, the interdependencies between the prices are lost.
The feature-driven model primarily relies on historical or observed data available at the
time of bidding.
Both intertemporal and interdependent relations of the input data are
preserved since the feature-driven model uses historical data with one-to-one structure. In
addition, the feature-driven model allows the incorporation of any feature that can improve
its performance.
Modelling approach
One notable benefit of the multi-stage scenario tree structure is the ability to make informed
recourse decisions in the later stage based on different expected outcomes in the previous
stage. Moreover, the decision variables are always feasible for subsequent stages, ensuring
the overall robustness of the decisions. The stochastic model, by design, enables the formu-
lation of bid curves within the same hour, given that the price scenarios are constructed in
accordance with a calibrated acceptance model and their corresponding weights.
The feature-driven model provides a flexible framework, which can employ a range of features
and techniques to capture dependencies. However, the applied approach for the feature-
driven model in this project composed a lower degree of freedom, due to the daily coefficients
architecture and the simple linear dependence with the feature matrix X. The feature-driven
model also enables the formulation of bid curves by reconstructing the prices and bids into
multiple discrete segments, that can also incorporate acceptance models and weights.
Challenges in modelling
The stochastic model requires careful calibration of the scenario probabilities to achieve an
optimal balance of the expected profit and the weightage of risk aversion. In addition, to
properly represent the bidding day it is crucial to ensure meticulous sampling of the prob-
abilistic scenarios while preserving the intertemporal relationships and the interdependent
correlations among the parameters.
The training data and the choice of features are naturally the critical aspects of the model,
which easily cause over-fitting and capture patterns which are non-repeatable beyond the
dataset. Notably, the robustness of this approach stems from the inclusion of historical data,
however, as with the stochastic model, it can prove both advantageous and disadvantageous.
52
==PAGE:60==
6
Discussion
Performance differences
The stochastic model shows a better performance with a perfect forecast compared to the
feature model. However, in cases of low acceptance, the stochastic has a slightly lower av-
erage realized profit compared to the feature-driven model (7.7% lower in base case). The
multi-stage stochastic model is computationally intensive due to the need to consider mul-
tiple scenarios and decision points. As the number of stages and scenarios increases, the
computational complexity grows exponentially.
The feature-driven model demonstrates consistent performance with the smallest standard
deviation of the realized profit in the base-case. It exhibits greater risk aversion than the
stochastic model but only when employing the daily coefficient architecture. Consequently,
the feature-driven model’s performance is highly contingent on the number of adjustable
parameters and the availability of training data. Furthermore, in terms of computational
complexity, the feature-driven model proves advantageous due to its focus on analyzing
predefined features.
6.3
Simple vs complex models
The presented results primarily compared bidding models with standardized inputs gener-
ated from the same dataset, which somewhat restricted the exploration of distinct qualities of
the stochastic and feature-driven models. Although this approach provided valuable insights
into model characteristics and potential areas for improvement, it’s important to consider
that the comparisons below may not fully reveal the untapped potential of the complex
models.
While the findings suggest that a simple deterministic model performs the best overall, the
performance varies across the test days, and each model demonstrates its strength in different
aspects and circumstances. The question remains: when is it beneficial to deploy a simpler
model rather than the more complex stochastic and feature-driven models? To address the
second question, the following factors are considered below:
Dependency on point forecast
As discussed in the forecast accuracy section 4.2.2, the forecast error has an asymmetric
consequence. A positive forecast error (bλD
t −λD
t > 0) penalizes the deterministic model more
than stochastic or feature-driven model due to higher dependency on the acceptance of the
point forecast. However, if the positive forecast error (bλD
t −λD
t < 0) is low, the deterministic
model optimizes the bid quantities close to a perfect forecast. Since the point forecast is
considered to be externally generated, depending on its accuracy, the corresponding risk-
averse model should be chosen.
53
==PAGE:61==
6
Discussion
Conservativeness of the bid price
Conservativeness can manifest in different ways, such as using risk-averse model structures in
complex models or adjusting bid price forecasts with γ for acceptance assurance. Increasing
conservativeness in bid prices improved bid acceptance and enhanced the performance of
all models.
However, introducing γ was less advantageous for the inherently risk-averse
stochastic and feature-driven models, potentially leading to lost profit due to excessive risk
aversion. Therefore, employing conservativeness to bid price forecasts within a deterministic
model framework offers a promising approach.
Acceptance
Different acceptance modelling approaches have been explored, including volume-weighted,
fully-accepted, and point forecast dependent. These acceptance models have a disproportion-
ate impact on the performance of the bidding models. While complex models benefit from
considering scenarios of non-acceptance based on historical data or probabilistic forecasts
to inform their decision-making, deterministic models rely entirely on the acceptance model
applied to the price forecast. This highlights again the crucial role of price forecast accuracy.
Nevertheless, it is recommended to construct an acceptance model using bid curves and past
experience.
Flexibility and potential model improvement
The deterministic model’s scope for improvement is primarily limited to enhancing the ac-
curacy of its price point forecast and the acceptance model. For the complex models, there
exist several avenues for developing further which can be advantageous for a more uncertain
market behaviour.

7
Conclusion and Future Works
This project developed bidding models based on the DA and FCR-D market data in DK2
region. The models were then evaluated for different test cases within the period January
to March 2023. The results were presented with sensitivities of different parameters to gain
insights into model characteristics and performance.
The first research question addressed notable disparities and key performance outcomes be-
tween the stochastic and feature-driven models. The main difference lay in their modelling
approach. The multi-stage stochastic model involves sequential decision-making, where de-
cisions are made over multiple time periods, taking into account the evolving information
and uncertainty. The feature-driven model, on the other hand, is primarily concerned with
identifying and leveraging relevant features or variables to make decisions based on their
intrinsic relationships. In terms of performance, the feature-driven model showed slightly
higher realized average profit and more reliability with a smaller standard deviation in prof-
its.
Nevertheless, it is worth emphasizing that the stochastic and feature-driven models
utilized in this project are implemented in their most basic or simplified forms.
Conse-
quently, these models possess significant potential for further refinement and improvement
to highlight their distinctive qualities.
The second research question addressed the comparative advantage of employing simpler
models such as rule-based and deterministic models over more complex models. As listed in
the discussions, several factors are to be considered. In summation, when the point forecast
is adjusted for conservativeness, it exhibits minimal error and a high acceptance level for
which the inclusion of historical data proves ineffective as it merely ‘pollutes’ an already
accurate forecast. Therefore, in such cases, a simpler deterministic model is sufficient. The
only instance where more complex models can provide an advantage is when the forecast
overestimates the realized price, where a more risk-averse model demonstrated larger relia-
bility.
There are several limitations in the construction of the models in Chapter 3 as well as
assumptions in the implementation of the model input setup in Chapter 4. The sections
below outline key avenues for developing the models further both general and model-specific
improvements.
7.1
General model improvements
1. Price point forecast methods:
Throughout the report, a simple approach employing an hourly mean point forecast was
utilized. However, it became apparent that the accuracy of the forecast significantly
influenced the bidding decisions of the models, consequently impacting their overall
performance. Enhancing the quality of the point forecast holds the potential to reshape
the necessity for the inclusion of conservativeness γ, thereby potentially altering the
model that achieves the best performance. Furthermore, a sensitivity analysis of the
55
==PAGE:63==
7
Conclusion and Future Works
forecast error (mean error) for each input parameter should be conducted to prioritize
and calibrate forecasting models.
2. Acceptance model:
As previously discussed, the construction of an acceptance model based on bidding
curves offers a more precise understanding of bid acceptance, ultimately leading to in-
creased revenue. Consequently, the acceptance model should not solely rely on volume-
weighted average prices but instead, consider the distribution of accepted bids along
with their corresponding bid prices. This approach enables the possibility of bidding
above the average price while still securing acceptance, thereby maximizing the poten-
tial for revenue optimization.
3. Including D-1 updates:
All bidding decisions were fixed at the D-2 stage. However, if an equivalent model
were developed to optimize the update of D-1 bids based on the information obtained
from the D-2 clearance, it could potentially lead to increased revenue for D-1. Addi-
tionally, it would be intriguing to explore which model would benefit the most from
the implementation of D-1 update models.
4. End-of-horizon constraints:
Extending the time horizon of the rolling horizon algorithm from 1 day to 1.5 or
2 days enables the optimal placement of the state of charge (SOC) at hour 24 of
day 1, considering the projected behavior of the battery for the following hours/day.
This extension provides a more comprehensive and forward-looking SOC optimization
strategy. However, this comes at the cost of extending the time horizon, where the
second-day solutions are not utilized for bidding purposes.
5. Optimizing for both prices and bids:
To avoid non-linearity, prices were fixed as parameters in the model, which proved
significant importance with respect to acceptance. Instead, the model can be solved
iteratively using coupled optimization to obtain both optimal bid prices and quantities.
This increases the computational time as it iterates over every fixed variable, but the
possible ranges of variables can be adjusted for faster convergence.
7.2
Model-specific implementation improvements
7.2.1
Stochastic model
1. The setup presented in section 4.4 removes the interdependencies between the prices
due to the one-to-many structure with historical days as samples. To preserve the
interdependencies, a bias sampling method can be implemented where the FD1 price
is sampled from a distribution set that is related to the price range of FD2. Likewise,
other parameters can be sampled this way so that the scenario tree is more represen-
tative of realistic outcomes.
56

2. Another way to influence the temporal and inter-parameter dependencies is to modify
the probability of the scenarios, to assign greater weight to the most likely outcomes.
However, this approach can be highly intricate, especially when dealing with a multi-
stage structure, as it necessitates the fine-tuning of each parameter.
7.2.2
Feature-driven model
1. The accuracy of the point forecast holds considerable significance in the feature-driven
model, as they directly impact the construction of bid quantities using feature forecasts
and bid prices using price forecasts. The model can leverage the inherent forecast error
to train the coefficients by incorporating historical forecast error data into the training
dataset. To account for forecast error, the formulation includes an additional penalty
term in the objective function, as shown in 8.6 in the Appendix.
2. One of the most notable benefits of the feature-driven model is the ability to incorporate
additional features, enabling more informed decision-making. As discussed in Chapter
6, adding more features should be carefully considered given the possibility of capturing
false dependencies in the short term. Nevertheless, the feature set can incorporate
attributes that explain frequency deviations such as weather conditions, production
schedules of nearby plants, and planned outages to enhance their predictive capability.

Scenario weights
The stochastic model uses three different weights in its objective functions, πωS1 , πωS2 and
πωS3. They are all assumed equiprobable, but other weights can be used. To accomodate for
the relationship between different stage and time aspect the weights are changed such that
πωS1 is a array of size D, πωS2 is a 2D matrix of size D x D and πωS3 is a 3D matrix of size
D x D x D.
πωS1 has been given the weights [0.8,0.4, 0.3, 0.2,0.1, 0.8] such that the first day is given a
lot of emphasize ie. 0.8 and the forecast as well.
πωS2 introduces the relationship between FD2 and DA, so the correlation between the FD2
and DA prices are considered as well as the time weights in πωS1. The correlation is calculated
as (0.29 + 0.24) / 2 = 0.265.
Creating a 2D matrix of a combination of the temporal
relationship: [0.8,0.4, 0.3, 0.2,0.1, 0.8] and the value 0.265.
πωS3 introduces the relationship between FD2/DA, DA/FD1 and FD2/FD1.
Where the
correlation are as follows:
FD2/DA = (0.29 + 0.24)/2 = 0.265
