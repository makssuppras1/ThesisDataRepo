ABSTRACT 
 
Incineration is the major way of management of municipal solid wastes (MSW) in 
Denmark, and its amount per capita is the highest in Europe. The method itself is 
straightforward, but the disposal of the products created during the process can be a 
problem, and a threat for the environment. Mentioned products, like the fly ash, are 
complex and hazardous materials, which under right conditions can leach heavy metals 
to its surroundings. However, if heavy metal concentration limits are met, municipal 
solid waste incineration (MSWI) fly ash could become a secondary product. The research 
on upscaling electrodialytic remediation (EDR) process of the fly ash is still ongoing.  
This work shows the results of MSWI fly ash treated by EDR method, and focus on 
the removal and behaviour of Cd, Cu, Cr, Pb, Sb, Se and Zn. The results were pre-wash 
of the ash was applied in order to remove present salts, and enhance the possibility of 
reuse are presented. The washing removed 96% of Cl and 84% of SO4 from the studied 
material. Two types of cells were used, the relatively new laboratory scale set up, of two 
compartment cell and bench scale cell, called Mega-lab. The results confirm that the 
acidification of suspension in 2-compartment cell takes less time, due to the direct 
placement of the anode in the compartment. In general the removal rates in the 2-
compartment cell were poor, with some exceptions for Cd (58%) and Cr (91%). The 
highest removal rates in Mega-lab were obtained for Cd (89%), Sb (94%), (Se 49%) and 
Zn (42%). Moreover, during the laboratory scale experiments, EDR process was 
conducted under higher temperatures (40℃, 60℃) in order to investigate how this 
parameter affects the removal of heavy metals from the fly ash. In case of Cd, Cu, Cr, Pb 
and Zn higher temperatures had positive effect and improved the removal of those 
heavy metals. The results, showed that EDR can be a method for upgrading MSWI fly 
ash, however different heavy metals favour different remediation conditions.

INTRODUCTION  
 
It is estimated that the amount of Municipal Solid Waste Incineration (MSWI) fly 
ash produced is in the order of 1-3% of the waste input mass (Sabbas, 2003). According 
to The Danish Ministry of Environment (2015), 25 tons of fly ash from co-incineration 
were produced in 2013 by Danish energy plants. MSWI fly ash is a highly complex 
material and contains high amounts of leachable heavy metals and salts. Therefore if it 
is landfilled, there is a risk of ground water contamination. By law, it is not allowed to 
be landfilled in Denmark. Therefore it is both exported and deposited in special landfills. 
Langøya located in Holmestrand fjord, Norway, or backfilled into old salt mines in 
Germany (Margo, 2014). By creating efficient way of removing hazardous contaminants 
from the MSWI fly ash, it could become secondary waste and be reuse in the production 
of concrete and cement, as it is happening today with coal fly ash. Electrodialytic 
remediation is one of the possible techniques which if developed into larger scale could 
be used for remediation of the MSWI fly ash. The method is mainly used to remove 
heavy metals from soil, harbour sediments and sewage sludge ash (Hansen et al., 1999, 
Pedersen et al., 2015, Pazos et al., 2010). In this process an electric current force the 
heavy metal ions in the ash to migrate through the ion exchange membrane towards 
the electrodes with the opposite charge. In this way heavy metals are separated from 
the aqueous solution suspension to a separate solution. The electrodialytic remediation 
of fly ash is still under development and improvement. For years different conditions 
and cells were tested. Many publications mention temperature as one of the important 
parameter affecting the removal rates of the heavy metals, but limited number of 
research investigate its influence on the treatment efficiency (Penn et al., 1997; 
Kristensen, 1999). 
Present M.Sc. thesis investigate the effect of washing of MSWI fly ash with 
distilled water on the removal of heavy metals during the EDR treatment in small scale 
2-compartment cell. The performance of Mega-lab batch scale cell on heavy metals 
removal was also taken under investigation. Furthermore, the influence of higher 
temperatures on EDR experiments was investigated in small scale ED cell. In total 12 EDR 
experiments, using two different cells, were conducted in the laboratory scale to answer 
following questions:

 Previous work on EDR of metals from MSWI fly ash  
 
The electrodialytic remediation, was originally developed in the 90s for remediation of 
the heavy metals from polluted soil, and was patented in 1995 (PCT/DK95/00209). With 
time the method was used to separate and extract heavy metals also from other solids, 
like harbour sediments (Kirkelund et al., 2009), sewage sludge (Pazos et al., 2010), and 
biomass combustion fly ash (Kirkelund et al., 2013).  
The first major publication describing extraction of different heavy metals from 
the MSWI fly ashes was published in 2002 in the form of Ph.D. thesis (Pedersen, 2002). 
The work focused on chemical and physical characterisation of the fly ashes and 
developing electrodialytic remediation method for removing heavy metals from this 
material. It was found that traditional EDR cell used for soil remediation was not suitable 
for fly ash, therefore a cell with stirred suspension was introduced, what significantly 
improved the removal of heavy metals. Another Ph.D. thesis published in 2005, the main 
finding was that applying a preliminary treatment to the fly ash to remove major 
constituents can reduce the time of the EDR treatment (Ferreira, 2005). Since then 
several studies were conducted, to improve the removal of heavy metals from MSWI fly 
ash. 
Most of the studies used assisting agents to further increase solubilisation of the 
heavy metals and form stable charged complexes which can be transported by the 
electric field. At the same time the pH of the fly ash is kept at the alkaline level to prevent 
dissolution of a high quantity of the ash occurring in the acidified environment 
(Kirkelund et al., 2015). By using sodium gluconate, Ferreira (2005) reached higher metal 
removal from MSWI fly ash, than when the distilled water was used. In turn, Pedersen 
(2002) found that mixture of ammonium citrate and NH3 is the best compromise for 
removal of Cd, Cr, Cu, Pb and Zn. However NH3 was most effective for the removal of 
Cd, and for the removal of the Pb Na-citrate turned to be slightly more effective than 
ammonium citrate.  
Improvements occurred also in the cell design, and resulted in decreased number 
of cell compartments compare to the beginnings of research on ED remediation. 
Originally laboratory scale EDR cell used for remediation of fly ash, was adopted from 
==PAGE:13==
  
 
 
 
4 
 
EDR soil remediation and consisted of 5-compartments (Pedersen, 2002; Ferreira, 2005). 
Despite its higher efficiency to a certain extent, set up with 3 compartments cell was 
developed, gained research attention and was gladly tested, becoming the most popular 
set up for conducting laboratory experiments. 
In 2013 a patent was applied for a new, 2-compartment cell design (EU 13183278). 
One of the two compartments contains the stirred sediment suspension with the anode, 
and is separated from the circulating electrolyte in the second one, by cation exchange 
membrane. The advantage of the 2-compartments cell lies in faster acidification of the 
anode compartment resulting in higher conductivity, what led to an increase in the 
removal of heavy metals in EDR process (Chen et al., 2015). 
The reuse of the MSWI fly ash, i.e. as a basis for construction materials is limited 
due to the high heavy metal and chloride content. If used, it could led to the leaching 
problems or in case of chloride it would cause corrosion in reinforced concrete (Ottosen 
et al., 2006). Therefore removal of chlorides is also important before the reuse. Washing 
of fly ash in order to remove chloride and soluble salts, like SO4, not only eliminated high 
level of salts but also turned out to drastically increase the removal of metals and reduce 
fouling of the ion-exchange membranes (Ferreira, 2008). 
Very little attention in the literature was given to the influence of the temperature 
on removal of heavy metals.  It is mentioned as one of the important parameter 
influencing the efficiency of the EDR process (Hansen et al., 1999) and some research on 
its influence on electrodialytic and the electrokinetic process were carried (Kristensen, 
1999; Krause, 1993; Penn et al., 1997; Baraud et al., 1999; Cherifi et al., 2009). Variation 
in the temperature are influencing all the parameters controlling the electroosmotic 
permeability. Rising temperature decreases pore fluid viscosity and give an increased in 
the electroosmotic flow (Penn et al., 1997). Ash extraction was found to vary with 
temperature, and most metals shows an increase in extraction efficiency at 85℃ 
(Forrester, 2008). The positive influence of the higher temperature on the removal rates, 
could be very useful in case of upscaling the process. The still hot fly ash batch could be 
loaded to the ED cell at the location of the incineration plant. However, some studies 
mention electromigration as the only electrokinetic transport phenomenon which have 
any importance in the electrodialytic remediation of the fly ash. As the reason for this 
indicating non-stable and very reactive nature of fly ash minerals. Also, ED remediation 
process with continuous stirring is closer to the electrodialytic process, than 
electrokinetic remediation therefore electroosmosis and electrophoresis phenomena 
should be completely omitted in the ash suspension (Pedersen, 2002).  In general the 
influence of higher temperature on heavy metals extraction should be visible in EDR 
process, since increasing the temperature lowers the viscosity of the water (Kristensen, 
1999).

 Electrodialytic remediation process  
 
Electrodialytic remediation is a separation process combining the electrokinetic 
remediation and electrodialysis process. The general principle of electrodialytic 
remediation is based on the use of low-level direct current (dc), generating electric 
current density of the order of milliamps per square centimeter (mA/cm2), passing 
between a pair of electrodes and set in motion the ions present on the suspension. 
The general principle of electrodialytic laboratory cell is depicted in Figure 1. Cell can 
consist of number of compartments (from 2 to 5), separated between each other by ion 
exchange membranes. However, 3-compartment cell is the most common tested. In Fig. 
1, cation exchange membrane is placed between the cathode compartment and 
suspension compartment (II-III), to prevent the passage of anions into the suspension 
and permits the cations present in the suspension to pass into the cathode 
compartment. In turn an anion exchange membrane, placed between anode 
compartment and the suspension compartment (I-II), prevents cations from passing to 
the suspension, except H+. 
 
 
Figure 1 Schematic principle of the electrodialytic process and transport mechanisms in 3-compartment 
cell. CAT – Cation exchange membrane; AN – Anion exchange membrane. Electrolyte compartment I and 
III; Suspension compartment II (Gonçalves, 2015). 
 
When a direct current passes between a pair of electrodes placed in the system, ions 
desorbed from polluted aqueous suspension are forced to migrate toward one of the 
==PAGE:15==
  
 
 
 
6 
 
electrodes, through ion exchange membrane. Depending on the speciation of the ions 
they migrate either to the anode or the cathode. Electrodialysis brings improvement 
over electrokinetic process in that way that cathode does not allow anions to pass into 
suspension compartment, what led to more efficient transport of heavy metals to 
cathode, however it is possible in case of the anode (Ribeiro, 1998).  
Three main mechanisms are responsible for ions movement inside the cell. The 
main one, electromigration, transports contaminants in electrodialytic remediation 
process by driving positive ions in the electric field towards the cathode and negative 
ions towards the anode. It is due to the electromigration that the ions like heavy metals, 
cross the membrane and can be deposited on the electrode or stay in the electrolyte 
solution.  
Electroosmosis, provides the movement of water in the pours medium in an applied 
electric field, and results in a net transport of water molecules towards the cathode. The 
electrophoresis, is a transport phenomenon allowing dispersed particles to migrate 
through the electric field. Charged particles are attracted electrostatically to one of the 
electrodes and can be pushed away from the other (Michell, 1993) therefore it is the 
opposite process to electroosmosis. It is considered to be a minor mechanism in the EDR 
(Jensen, 2005). 
 Chemical processes occurring in the cell during EDR treatment 
 
During the electrodialytic process, changes in pH and heavy metals concentration 
in different parts of the cell occurs. Before applying the current, both stay constant, but 
soon after the current starts to be driven towards the suspension compartment, H+ and 
OH- ions are formed at the electrodes. The OH- ions are not able to pass from the 
cathode to the suspension compartment do to the membrane. The anode membrane 
will delay the passage of the H+ ions from the anode to the suspension compartment, 
but will not prevent it. This results in the propagation of acid front into the suspension 
compartment. It is also partly due to the water splitting at the anion exchange 
membrane (2). With time, soluble anions and cations in the suspension compartment 
continue to transfer towards the electrodes of opposite charge. As a consequence of 
using electrodes in the process, the electrolysis of water at the electrodes occurs. The 
following electrolytic reactions are happening in the system: 
 
 
 
 
 
At the cathode: 
2 H2O + 2e- → H2 ↑ + 2 OH- 
(1) 
At the anode:  
2 H2O → O2 ↑ + 4 H+ + 4 e- 
(2) 
==PAGE:16==
  
 
 
 
7 
 
Those reactions are the primary reasons of the pH changes in the system. As a result, 
the catholyte becomes more alkaline and the anolyte becomes more acidic. The 
solubility of heavy metals is boosted at low pH, therefore acidification is a favoured 
phenomenon, due to the fact that only metals in the liquid solution can be affected by 
the electric field. The dissolution of metals placed in the suspension occurs in the whole 
compartment if enough time is given. With time, the level of metal ions decrease in the 
suspension until the satisfactory level (Ribeiro, 1998). Unless the pH is adjusted, acidic 
and the alkaline front can significantly affect the magnitude of electroosmosis reaction, 
solubility, state and charge of ions as well as the level of the contaminants during the 
process (Probstein et al., 1993). In addition, redox reactions of ions presents in the 
electrolytes take place. When the concentration of metals in the electrolytes increase, 
secondary reactions, like precipitation of metals (Me) occurs at the surface of the 
electrode (Acar et al., 1993): 
 
At the cathode: 
H+ + e- → ½ H2 ↑ 
(3) 
 
Men+ + ne- → Me 
(4) 
 
Me(OH)n + ne- → Me + nOH- 
(5) 
 
If chloride content in the remediated material is high, oxidation of chloride (Cl-) at the 
anode take place and gaseous chloride is formed: 
 
At the anode: 
2 Cl- → Cl2 ↑ + 2e- 
(6) 
 
Because increasing amount of metal precipitation on the electrodes can interrupt the 
electric field and chloride gas is poisonous, both reactions are unwanted (Christiansen, 
2004).

 Advantages of using 2-compartment cell 
 
As said before, the majority of research on electrodialytic removal of heavy metals was 
conducted using 3-compartment laboratory scale cell, because of its advantages over 
the original 5-compartment cell. The advantages of using two ion exchange membranes 
(3-compartemnts cell) in EDR process was widely described by Ribeiro (1998). However, 
recently another improved cell, consisting of 2-compartments was developed and 
patented in 2013 at the Technical University of Denmark (EU 13183278). The schema 
with differences in the construction are shown in Fig 2. The new design is using only one 
ion exchange membrane. Compartment with the suspended material is separated from 
the electrolyte compartment by cation exchange membrane. The novelty of this set up 
is based on direct placement of the anode in the polluted suspension. The consequence 
of this is a direct acidification of the compartment, by all the H+ ions produced during 
the electrolysis reaction at the anode. Therefore acidification time is significantly 
reduced. Desorbed elements from the contaminated suspension migrate to the 
electrolyte while hydroxyl (OH-) ions generated during electrolysis reactions at the 
cathode are restrained by the membrane from entering the suspension. It was proven 
that the final pH of the suspension and the voltage appear to be lower (Pedersen et al., 
2015a). When compared with 3-compartment cell the clean-up levels during 
remediation, longer than 48h, favour new 2-compartment design. The previous studies 
revealed high removal efficiency of the 2-compartment cell, on the level of the 94% (Cu), 
73% (Pb), and 96% (Zn). In addition new set-up is confirmed to be more energy 
consumption efficient. (Pedersen et al., 2015b).

 Previous bench-scale and pilot-scale studies  
 
The electrodialytic removal of heavy metals from MSWI fly ash is still developing 
process with focus on laboratory experiments. Although several studies on bench and 
pilot scale were conducted and some examples of work on MSWI fly ash until today are 
presented. All of the used EDR set ups differs from each other, so they cannot be directly 
compared, i.e. in form of table. However, conducted studies and ED set-ups are 
interesting therefor are worth describing.  
Summary of main applications of EDR process in bench- or pilot scale conducted in last 
years by the researchers from The Technical University of Denmark can be found in Table 
1. Electrodialytic process was applied to different polluted materials and four different 
set ups were tested.  
Pedersen (2004) and Christiansen (2005) treated chromated-copper-arsenate 
(CCA) waste wood with electrodialytic process in 2m3 pilot plant. It consisted of PVC box 
(3m×1m×1m) with electrode units placed every 30 cm. Both investigated several 
parameters, and concluded that the use of collecting units was beneficial for the 
remediation. It was also observed that the remediation of the wood chips <2 cm was 
significantly better than the remediation of the bigger wood chips. The distance 
between the electrodes differed during 7 conducted experiments (60-270cm). It 
affected the removal of Cu and Cr. Removal decreased if the distance between 
electrodes was increased, in turn that did not affect the remediation of As. The removal 
rates were high for both Cu (87%), Cr (81%) and As (95%).

Kirkelund (2009) used electrodialytic pilot plant for removal of heavy metals and salts 
from raw carbonated MSWI APC residue, and tested different tank designs. In addition 
raw APC residue was carbonated by reaction with CO2 in order to reduce leaching of 
some heavy metals. In this study, pilot plant consisted of  a rack with 100 L suspension 
tanks, pumps and a compartment with 50 cell pairs (530 cm2 of active surface area per 
cell) separated by ion exchange membranes. The results were promising and showed 
that with some optimization, combination of carbonation and EDR can give significant 
reduction in heavy metals (As, Ba, Cd, Cr, Cu, Mn, Ni, Pb, Zn) and salts (Cl, Na, SO4) 
leaching.  
Jensen (2010a), focused on raw APC residue, water-washed residue, acid washed 
residue and acid treated residue. Samples were treated with continuous-flow bench-
scale reactor adapted from electrodialytic cell previously developed for treatment of 
fermentation products. The membranes surface area was 530 cm2. It was found that 
acid treatment during ED treatment provides the best reduction in Cd leaching. 
Stack ED cell was used by Pedersen (2014) for the removal of heavy metals from 
harbor sediments. The experiments were conducted parallel with 2- and 3-compartment 
cell, indicating that longer time and more electric charge and energy would be necessary 
to achieve clean-up levels similar to the small scale cell. The removal levels were; Cu 
(21%), Pb (42%) and Zn (73%). 
Jensen (2015) used the same pilot plant as described in Kirkelund (2009) with focus 
on APC residue from semi-dry, wet flue-gas cleaning system and MSWI fly ash without 
flue-gas.

MATERIALS AND METHODS 
 
The following chapter describes how laboratory work was performed. Number of 
experiments to characterize samples and in total 12 EDR experiments to recover heavy 
metals from the MSWI fly ash were performed. For the better overview of the conducted 
experimental work Figure 2 presents the summarized workflow.  
 
 Experimental material 
 
Two samples of MSWI fly ash used in this study were collected from I/S Vestforbrænding 
solid waste incinerator plant. It is the biggest incineration located west from 
Copenhagen, in Glostrup (Denmark).  Approximately 600,000 tonnes of waste per year 
are incinerated there, that gives annually 15,000 tons of APC residues per year, what 
includes fly ash (Sabbas et al., 2003). Samples were collected in different years, from 
wet flue gas cleaning system before the neutralisation of the acidic components by the 
wet scrubber process and were sieved through a 1 mm sieve before being collected to 
the buckets with a close-fitting lid, named Batch 1 and Batch 2. Batch 1 collected in 2014, 
was used for small scale experiments. Batch 2, collected at the beginning of 2016, and 
was used for Megalab experiments. Part of both samples was washed with distilled 
water, in three stages with total liquid to solid ratio (L/S) of 2.5. The washed fly ash 
samples were named PWA.B1 and PWA.B2. After washing samples were dried at 105°C 
for 24h, and stored for later use.

2.4.1 Small scale cell set-up 
 
All small scale experiments were performed in two compartment cell that consist 
of two plexiglas cylindrical compartments with internal diameter of 8 cm, the length of 
anode and cathode chamber was 10 cm and 3.5 cm respectively. Both compartments 
were separated from each other with cation exchange membrane (GE Ionics Inc®), with 
surface area of approximately 50 cm2. The electrodes used were made of platinum 
coated with titanium wire (Ø = 3 mm). Power supply (HP® E3612A) was used in order to 
maintain a constant current in the cell. Suspension was stirred with a flexible plastic flab, 
attached to a glass stick and set in motion by overhead RW11 IKA® stirrer.

 Characterization of the ash 
 
Two batches of fly ash used for the ED experiments were statistically analysed by 
GrafPad Prism 7 software in order to know how different they were. The t-test was 
carried in order to compare all individual parameters (Table 2). The analysis confirmed 
that samples are significantly different.  
3.1.1 Chemical properties

higher. The second batch showed higher organic content, but lower water solubility. The 
concentration of all heavy metals was higher in Batch 2. 
Due to the high leaching potential of heavy metals and salts present in the MSWI fly ash, 
leaching behaviour of raw and prewashed samples was measured and the results are 
present in Table 3. In general, heavy metals leaching of the initial samples in both 
batches is on the same level, but the leaching of salts in batch 2 is significantly higher. 
With some exceptions prewashing reduces the leaching potential of the fly ash, 
however, as it will be discuss later, not to the level where limiting values are met.

3.2.1 The pH, conductivity and voltage 
 
The pH and conductivity of the suspension were followed every day during the EDR in 
2-compartment cell experiments. The values present on Figure 16 were measured 
before everyday acidification. It can be seen that the pH in the suspension chamber 
decreases, almost linearly with time, from the initial value of 12.41 (RFA.B1) and 11.06

is slightly lower than pH level of the suspension after 7 days, found in previous studies 
(Margo, 2015; Ferreira, 2005). This acidification is expected due to the water splitting 
and the supply of H+ ions to the suspension compartment, and also it is wanted since 
metal removal can effectively occur in the acidic environment.

Conductivity of the raw fly ash (Figure 16) decreases faster during first days of

desorbed species begins to control a conductivity and cause its constant increase.

was on very low level from the beginning of the experiment, since the easily soluble 
substances were already removed by the wash. The acidification from the anode 
reactions was steadily increasing the conductivity over the experiment time. 
Voltage drop, presented on figure 5, shows the values recorded before and after 
acidification, therefore values are connected with the line and represent voltage 
evolution over the 7 days experiment. The right graph represents the values for the 
experiments where washed ash was used. It can be seen that the voltage increases 
rapidly to the maximum output of the power supply in the experiments when higher 
temperatures were applied. Voltage pattern was following the pH pattern, and was 
growing with the increasing pH, and decreasing when pH of the electrolyte was acidified. 
The voltage peaks can be explain by too low conductivity of the samples, not being able 
to maintain the current without already washed out soluble parts. When lower current 
(25mA) was applied, the voltage growth was not increasing so drastically. Relatively low 
and stable voltage (2-6 V) could be observed for the experiments were raw fly ash was 
tested. The reason of this stability lies in the high content of easily soluble salts, 
providing high conductivity of the suspension samples.

experiments were prewashed ash was used, however still under 73%. Recovery rates of 
Sb for experiments where raw ash was used vary between 39 and 73%, and can be 
considered very low. The experiments where prewashed ash was used so extremely high 
recovery between 534 and 663%. The reason for this is very low initial content of the 
metal in the sample, which is not the case for Batch 2. The most probable explanation is 
extremely high inhomogeneity of Sb in the sample. Finally, recovery rates of V can be 
consider satisfying for experiments 3, 6, 7 and 8, the rest is too high.  
There can be few reasons behind the mass balance variations. As mentioned before 
fly ash is not homogenised, however the initial concentration of given heavy metal is 
calculated based on digestion of 1 g triplicates. It is assumed to be a concentration 
representative for the whole batch, and the sample used for the EDR experiment. When 
inhomogeneity is the reason, it causes the mass balance ratios above 120%. The low ash 
recovery in the experiments is a consequence of dissolution of high amounts of easily 
soluble salts, as e.g. NaCl, present in the raw fly ash (Chen et al., 2015; Kirkelund et al., 
2015). This phenomena is responsible for the recoveries below the proper rates. Table 
12 confirms this trend, and shows that after experiments were raw fly ash was used, 
weight losses are up to 18%. In the experiment 5, loss in the suspension volume 
occurred, therefore the weight loss increased to 22%. It is another reason for the low 
recovery rates. During the experiments, transfer of the electrolyte to the suspension 
compartment can occur, due to the faster velocity of the electrolyte in the input of the 
cathode compartment, than on the output. This exert pressure on the membrane and 
the electrolyte can pass the membrane. As the effect the increased volume of the 
suspension can lead to the leakage through the stirrer hole.  
Removal  
 
The consistent definition of removal does not exist in the literature, and various 
authors differently define when the removal of heavy metal occurs. Therefore in this 
work two definitions and two different values are presented. Removal1) presents the % 
rates of heavy metals, which at the end of the experiment are present in the electrolyte, 
cathode and the membrane. However, one can argue that the most important is a final 
product, which is remediated dry fly ash, therefore removal2), which take into the 
==PAGE:37==

account concentration of the metal after ED from all parts of the cell, without the 
concentration in the dry ash is the more appropriate. Author of this work leans toward 
second definition, therefor discussion will focus those results.  
 Removal rates obtained after 10 ED experiments in 2-compartment cell are present

3.2.4 Leaching behavior of ash before and after small cell EDR 
 
As seen from the pH dependent desorption experiment (Figure 30), the mobility 
of heavy metals is directly related to the pH, and increases in the acidic environment. In 
table 6 the threshold leaching limits, and the leachability of the metals after 10 
experiment conducted in 2-compartment cell are given. The results are compared to the 
Danish ordinance BEK 1414/2015, regulating the use of the other type of materials in 
construction sites in Denmark (Miljøministeriet, 2015).  The regulation distinguish three 
categories of contaminated materials by setting up the leachability limits of the 
elements present in the material. The three categories divide the materials by the 
allowable usage. Material falling under Category 1 have the lowest level of the 
contamination, and therefore can be applied without any restrictions. Category 2 
contains materials which usage is restricted, and finally if the material falls into 
contamination limits of category 3 or exceeds them, then its use is severely restricted. 
Green values in the table 6 represent concentrations of elements, which do not exceed 
limits recommended for category 1, orange color values does not fall into category 1, 
but still can be used under severely restricted use. Subsequently red values, exceed the 
limits of category 3.  
The limits of category 1 were met for Cu in washed ash and samples after all ED 
experiments. Furthermore, concentration of the elements in the samples after ED were 
reduced compared to the raw fly ash and prewashed ash. Values of Pb exceeded the 
limits in both raw and washed fly ash, but with help of EDR the leaching values were 
reduced and in case of experiment 7 and 8, fall into category 1. Major results for the Zn 
show satisfying values, with 4 results meeting values of category 1, however there is no 
pattern pointing out the favorable EDR conditions. Material from experiment 5 and 7, 
were raw fly ash was ED treated under elevated temperature, showed increased 
leaching and exceed the initial value. However that was not the case for experiment 9, 
but it can be carefully assumed that EDR of heavy metals in raw ash, under higher 
temperature is not beneficial for the leaching behavior of Zn. Leaching values were 
elevated after EDR for the Cd, Cr and Se. Initial leaching of Cd qualified material for 
category 3, however after ED experiment 5 and 7, were temperature was increased, 
concentrations significantly exceeded even the values for the deposition of mineral 
waste for any constituents. Results for Cr, are not promising and show that EDR increase 
the leaching of this element, especially under increased temperature. Initial values for 
raw and prewashed ash exceeded values of C3, and EDR moved those values further 
away from meeting the limits. However, experiment 9 and 10 show entirely different 
results, despite the same conditions. Results of Se obtained by ICP in most cases, are

There are no threshold values for Sb and V, but it can be seen that the leaching after ED 
increased in all experiments, beside experiment 5, and exceeds the mineral waste limits

reduced by EDR treatment (Figure 19).  The washing of the fly ash itself, reduced the

Recovery and Removal 
Table 15 contains the  final results of heavy metal concentrations after ED 
treatment of fly ash in the Mega-lab cell. Removed metals (mg) are the sum of metal 
concentration found in electrolyte, membrane, on the electrodes and in the aqueous 
phase of the ash slurry after dewatering. Metals in aqueous phase (mg) represents the 
amount found in the aqueous phase of the ash slurry after filtrating the suspension. The 
recovery %, is defined as the percentage of heavy metal concentration (mg/kg) in the 
dry ash sample after EDR, divided by the heavy metal concentration (mg/kg) in the 
sample before the EDR treatment (7). The concentration in the dry ash after EDR, refers 
to the mass (mg/kg) in the samples determined by a total digestion procedure. 
The experiment 11, where raw fly ash was used, show very good recovery rates 
for all the heavy metals (88 - 114 %), beside Sb (48%). However, rates in the experiment 
12, were washed ash was used are acceptable only for Cr and V (87 and 105% 
respectively). Rates for other metals vary between 53-73%.  The removal1) and removal2) 
of Cd, Sb, Pb and Zn was higher after ED of prewashed ash, for Cr, Cu and Se removal 
was higher in the experiment with raw fly ash. The removal of V stayed on the same 
level for both experiments. Better performance on removal rates of prewashed ash, can 
be due to the operational issues during experiment 11. Particles of raw fly ash were 
heavier and therefor almost all of them were deposited at the bottom of the Mega-lab 
cell within the first days. Therefore, the fly ash particles were not circulating between 
the tank and the ED cell, but stayed on the bottom of the cell (Appendix 1, Fig. 37-38).

CONCLUSIONS 
 
This study aimed to understand if pre-washing of the MSWI fly ash is an effective 
supplement for EDR process, and if applied together can lead to obtaining fly ash 
material which could be reused as an additive to the construction materials, without 
being a potential risk of contamination for the environment.  
The experiments were conducted on two batches of MSWI fly ash, collected from 
Vestforbrænding solid waste incineration plant. The characterisation of the samples 
confirmed high content of salts and heavy metals. The statistical analysis (t-test) was 
used to compare the initial characteristics of those two samples. The results showed 
that those two batches were statistically significantly different. Samples were collected 
in different years, and fly ash is known for its inhomogeneity.    
It was found that the water washing as a pre-treatment process stabilizes the fly 
ash properties by removing significant amounts of soluble salts like chloride and 
sulphate. This can led to the increase of ED efficiency, because heavy metals like Cd, Pb, 
and Ni is not be able to form complexes with chloride, which decreases speciation during 
the EDR process. Water-washing did not cause the difference in the pH dependent 
desorption of heavy metals. 
Although EDR treatment removes heavy metals from the fly ash, it also makes some 
of the residual metals more available for leaching than in the untreated ash. As seen 
from XRD diffractograms, EDR influence the morphology of the ash and lean to the 
formation of secondary minerals. 
Application of 2-compartments in ED led to the faster acidification of the suspension 
if compared to 3-compartment cell studies of other authors. Higher temperature slightly 
improved the acidification of suspension compartment. The removal rates for all 10 
experiments show high fluctuations and are very poor, with the exceptions for Cd (58%) 
and Cr (91%). However, it is worth to point out that in case of Cd, Cu, Cr, Pb and Zn 
elevated temperatures had positive effect and improved the removal of those heavy 
metals, and helped in moving heavy metals from the fly ash to the electrolyte.   
Although higher temperature improved the treatment on both prewashed and raw 
fly ash, it can be observed that prewashing itself was not beneficial for the removal of 
heavy metals, and experiments where raw fly ash was used have higher removal rates.  
The removal rates of the experiments conducted in Mega-lab cell are much better, 
for all elements, with the highest removal of Cd (89%), Sb (94%), (Se 49%) and Zn (42%) 
==PAGE:67==
  
 
 
 
58 
 
from prewashed ash. This opposite to 2-compartment cell trend could be due to the 
deposition of the raw fly ash on the bottom of Mega-lab cell.  
Leaching test reveal that washing as a pre-treatment decrease leaching of Cl, SO4, 
and also of Cu, Se and Zn. However, it enhances the leaching of all other investigated 
elements; Cd, Cr, Pb, Sb, and V. For Pb and Sb this influence can be neutralize with ED 
treatment under elevated temperature. In case of Zn washing combined with ED is 
beneficial, but only under relatively low temperature. Leaching concentrations of the 
samples treated under 20℃ reached the limits of the C1 category what cannot be said 
about samples treated with 40℃ or 60℃.  
 The factor that affects removal and leaching of the heavy metals from fly ash is the 
final pH of the MSWI residue. The optimum was achieved after within 2 days of the 
remediation or just with pre-washing itself. 
It is possible to obtain leaching concentrations of Cl, Cd, Cu, Pb, Se and Zn below the 
Danish leaching limiting values when applying electrodialytic remediation of the MSWI 
fly ash using 2-compartment cell. Mega-lab cell successfully lowered the leaching 
concentrations of Cd, Cu, Zn and salts, Cl and SO4. However bad leaching properties 
occurring after the treatment could be solved with longer remediation time, or with the 
stabilisation of the ashes after the treatment.  
 
 Future developments 
 
The experienced gained during this research can be used for future improvements of 
the EDR set up. 
 
I. 2-compartment cell 
 
 An improvement in the electrolyte circulation, by applying different pumping 
system, would solve the problem of higher velocity on the input and lower on 
the output, which often leads to the transfer of the electrolyte to the suspension 
compartment.  
 Better thermal isolation of the cell and all tubes, by using materials with lower 
thermal transmittance, during the experiments where elevated temperature is 
provided, would reduce the temperature fluctuations.  
 To avoid deformation of the stirrer shovels during the ED conducted under 
elevated temperatures, different material could be used. 
 
==PAGE:68==
  
 
 
 
59 
 
II. Mega-lab cell 
 
 Although, results for prewashed ash were promising, the shape of the Mega-lab 
cell does not suit the raw fly ash were the particles are heavier and tend settle 
on the bottom, without the possibility of circulation between the tank and cell. 
Therefor a cell which does not allow particles to settle is required.  
 Using pH adjustment system in order to keep the pH on constantly low level 
would improve the maintenance of the both cells, and could improve the ED 
removal effectiveness.  
 
III. Electrodialytic remediation process 
 
 As a supplement for the better understanding of the heavy metals behavior 
during the ED process, chemical speciation could be conducted. 
 Use of an acidifying agent before the EDR process, could increase the removal 
efficiencies for heavy metals and decrease the remediation time.
