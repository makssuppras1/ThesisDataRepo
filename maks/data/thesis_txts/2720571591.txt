Abstract
In recent years, road congestion as an effect of travel demand growth has been increasing in the Czech
Republic and around the world, and a rapid increase is also expected in the coming years. Additional time
spent by traveling due to congestion effects results in economical loss and significant environmental impact.
A model is to be developed to investigate travel patterns of citizens of the city of Pilsen in Czech Republic
based on predetermined assumptions during specific periods of the workday when the travel demand reaches
its peak level as in that time the congestion on the road network is the most likely to occur. As the road
congestions are very likely caused by individual vehicle transportation when the road capacity is exceeded, in
order to investigate possible mitigation of congestion, multi-modal transportation model is to be developed
as a tool to monitor the travel pattern shift from individual to public transportation mode for the specific
case of the Pilsen city.

Introduction
With the rapid expansion of urban areas over recent decades, the travel demand has significantly increased
leading to a rapid growth of vehicular traffic. To fulfill travel needs of cities inhabitants, individual au-
tomobile travel mode is used in a vast majority of trips. As a consequence of meeting the travel demand
by cars, overall traffic has reached capacity constraints of the transportation network infrastructure. The
current state of the traffic, with overusing of individual car mode is leading to road congestion, air and
noise pollution, decreased safety, increased travel time and other negative factors affecting the quality of
life of local inhabitants and commuters. The empirical results of development in US cities has shown, that
even with expanding road infrastructure it is not possible to meet all travel demand by automobiles, due
to their inefficiency of utilizing space [naj´ıt nˇejakou studii]. Due to above mentioned reasons, the other
modes of transport has become attractive and urban travel behaviour has evolved from a single mode to a
diverse usage of complex multi-modal means of travel. To better utilize transport network infrastructure,
many tools have been developed to aid decision makers with assessment of the travel policies, including
mathematical models of traffic flow.
Road congestion is a considerable nuisance to travelers and, importantly, constitutes a large annual societal
loss. Furthermore, the additional environmental impact is considerable, and in general more than 15% of
the greenhouse gases emissions in the Czech Republic is caused by the transportation sector, and this share
is expected to increase [1]. In order to be able to limit the consequences of congestion and reduce the
environmental and social impacts, it is important that the current road network is utilised effectively and
that long term planning of the transportation infrastructure projects are supported by realistic prognosis of
their impacts. To aid decision makers, selected solutions will be designed, implemented and their assessment
will be provided for achieving a more efficient mobility system from a theoretical and solution point of view,
based on the transport network analysis methodology.

Literature review
When traveling from origin to destination by using transit modes, usual approach nowadays is to find the
most efficient path in the route planning application installed on the traveller’s smartphone. Depending
on the situation, proposed trip can include pure transit mode, e.g. bus or metro, or any form of combined
modes, such as commuter rail and tram, bus and metro etc. The transfer node can be viewed in different
forms, depending how the travel mode choice set is modeled. For car-transit mode it can viewed as public
transport terminal with a large parking lot or when further distinguishing between transit modes it it can be
seen as any node,representing multiple terminals, where the transfer occurs in same place. With including
park and ride mode as well, the additional issues issues need to be considered while designing the demand
model. Compared with pure car mode traffic assignment model, that in the route choice stage requires single
origin destination matrix, the multimodal case must be enhanced to include used mode for each route in
the transport network. The most used approach in the reviewed literature is to have constructed separated
network for each mode with transfer nodes included between them. This methodology is usually refered as
supernetwork [9]. Supernetworks for Combined Travel Choice Model. It is usually defined as an augmented
network containing a ”basic network” for route choice and a ”virtual network” for other travel choices.
The supernetwork approach was first introduced by Gartner in [10] in order to solve the variable demand
traffic assignment problem. Initially, two types of supernetworks - zero cost overflow formulation and excess-
demand network representation were described. Sheffi [5] reformulated the supernetwork representation in
order to solve combined trip distribution traffic assignment problem, originally solved by Evans in [11]. Sheffi
also presented two supernetworks applications for the combined modal split traffic assignment problem with
the automobile and public transit mode considered. In the first example of Sheffi, each origin and destination
was decomposed by modes and connected by virtual links. All virtual links had zero cost and represented
entry and leave links for each network. In this example both traffic and transit assignment were driven by
User Equilibrium principle [12]. However, in case of passengers assignment, this behavioral assumption was
concluded as unreasonable. In the second example, travel time over equivalent public transportation network
links was given by composite link performance function, which was dependent on the public transport trip
demand, automobile flow and transit flow, in order to characterize modal choice with logit formula. The
basic network was represented by real link performance cost functions, such as volume delay function, also
known as BPR function [13], due to its formulation origin in the Bureau of Public Road.
Following on excess demand formulation, in the work of Ryu, Chen and Choi, the method was used to
formulate elastic demand traffic assignment problem [14]. Ryu, Chen and Choi implemented path-based
gradient projection algorithm with several modifications in order to solve excess demand reformulation
of elastic demand traffic assignment problem. Originally, the gradient projection algorithm as a solution
procedure for fixed demand, user equilibrium traffic assignment was implented by Jayakrishnan et al. in
[15]. The main difference lied in the flow updating equations used during the equilibriation phase, based
on the modified network representation, following the supernetwork methodology as proposed in [5]. The
origin destination trip demand equilibriation procedure determined amount of trips for each O-D pair based
on the travel times over the links in the network, by equilibriating supply and demand levels, while the path
flow equilibriation procedure determined, how to allocate path flows in order to achieve a user equilibrium
state.
By expanding the path based gradient projection algorithm, modified version of the technique was imple-
mented by Ryu, Chen and Choi in [4], to solve excess demand reformulation of combined modal split traffic
assignment problem. This time, the excess demand formulation was adapted to consider mode choice, in-
10
==PAGE:12==
stead of travel choice as in [14]. The excess demand function was therefore formulated as a modal split
function, in which the excess demand corresponded to trip demand of public transit mode. The mode choice
step adopted the binary logit function to determine the modal split and the route choice step allocated the
automobile flow to the road network according to the user equilibrium principle. The modal split equilibria-
tion phase determined an appropriate mode share ratio for each origin destination pair based on the network
congestion level by equilibriating between excess demand (modal split) function and the origin-destination
cost supply function, while the path flow equilibriation step allocated flows to the used path in order to
achieve user equilibrium. The equilibriation was based on the condition, which compared travel cost on the
shortest path with the excess demand cost. If the travel cost on the shortest path was smaller than the
excess demand cost, automobile trip demand was increased. If otherwise, public transit trip demand was
increased. If the costs were equal, within the certain accuracy range, the equilibrium solution was achieved.
The procedure proposed by Ryu, Chen and Choi in [4], was implemented with modified public transport
cost function in this thesis.
Continuation on the previous works, Ryu, Chen and Kitthamkesorn expanded the combined modal split
traffic assignment excess demand reformulation from bi-modal version to a multimodal case in [16]. In this
case, the mode choice was modelled as a nested logit model in which the excess demand was distributed
across alternatives, which corresponded to a different modes of transport. In the upper nest, there were
two alternatives, public transit and private transport mode, following the structure from the previous works
of Chen and Ryu. The lower nest accommodated specific alternatives, such as private automobile in the
private mode nest and bus, metro and bus-metro combination in the public transit nest. Modified gra-
dient projection algorithm extended the equilibriation of private vehicles (e. e., traffic assignment) while
considering equilibriation of multiple transport modes (modal split step) by using two-hase procedure in
which the equilibriation in the upper nest was conducted in the first phase followed by the equilibriation of
modal splits among the public transport modes in the second phase of the solution procedure. Although
the implementation of more than bi-modal assignment is out of the scope of this thesis, the implementation
of the Ryu’s, Chen’s and Kitthamkesorn’s procedure is considered in the case study of Pilsen as the possible
extension of the current thesis.
In the 1977 study [17], Florian et al. introduced a combined trip distribution modal split and trip assignment
model for binary set of modes - car and bus. The route choice element was based on Wardrop First principle
[12], deterministic user equilibrium, whether the mode choice was implicitly given by a logit model. Being
one of the first solution of bimodal traffic assignment, several simplifications were assumed. As the logit
model does not allow for correlation between alternatives, the combination of modes was not allowed. The
bus flow was modeled as equivalent to a certain number of private vehicles on a link and by considering all
the buses that use the link per time period. To unify utilization unit, vehicle occupancy rate was introduced,
where the car occupancy rate was assumed to be 1. With these assumptions, the model was established as
an convex programming problem with linear constraints, where the Frank-Wolfe method [18] was used to
solve this problem.
Fernandez et al. (1994) [19], formulated a stochastic user equilibrium traffic assignment model, that was
taking into consideration combination of modes as a reaction of implementing new transport policies in
California, that encourages commuters to use transit by the park and ride combined mode (e.g. ride a
car to an outskirt of a urban area and continue by using public transport when traveling to city center).
When municipalities had started to build parking lots on the outskirts of the cities and provided transit
infrastructure to the areas of economic activities, combined modes became attractive for travelers and their
11
==PAGE:13==
share is still increasing.
The authors formulated several questions that are necessary to consider when
modelling multi-modal network equilibrium travel demand model with combination of transport modes.
• Which choices made by the traveller are modelled by the demand model and which choices by the
network model?
• How should the generalized cost (disutility) be specified in the demand model in order to ensure
compatibility with corresponding measures obtained from the network model?
• How should network equilibirum models be stated in order to correctly model ”pure mode” and
”combined mode” trips?
• What is the structure of the resulting network equilibrium models and are there efficient solution
algorithms?
Taking these issues into consideration is requiring to distinguish which choices are made made on the mode
choice level in the demand models and which choices are made on the route choice level in the network
models, resulting into different mathematical formulation, when considering combined mode as distinct in
the set of choices for travel modes or when having combined mode trips dependent solely on the route choice.
Fernandez et al. proposed 3 models with different formulation.
In the first model, they considered the choice of combined mode and the transfer node point to be element
in the route choice model only. The Wardrops User Optimum method, where the travelers are choosing
their route to minimize their travel times (or generalized travel costs, depends on the perspective) was
implemented. The number of trips using pure modes or combination was result of the used paths in the
estimated flows. The flow of the combination mode was given as the one passing through transfer node.
According to the authors, this approach is naive as it does not capture the preferences of travellers and
cannot reproduced empirical travel behaviour.
The second, Partial Performance Demand model considered combined mode as ”pure mode”, assuming
that the travellers were choosing between modes accordingly by User Optimum principle in a mode choice
function represented as a logit type function dependent on the traveler’s perception of the generalized travel
cost of travelling from origin to destination by selected mode. After the mode choice was performed, they
chose the routes on a distinct subnetwork.
It was assumed that the combined mode alternative is not
relevant when the metro mode is available for travel on an O-D pair, hence the formulation assumed that
the car-metro combined mode was considered as an alternative for O-D pairs where destination can be
reached by metro, however origin requires a car access to the metro. The considered situation is depicted
on the figure below. The formulation of these conditions always resulted in the bi-modal traffic assignment
model where the route choice and assignment was made for each type of network resulting in the equal travel
costs perception for all combined mode paths. As the transfer node was always result of the User Optimum
principle, without considering any other factors, this model was concluded as too simplistic to reflect real
travel behaviour.
The last model in the arcticle used Model 2 formulation in its core but it was enhanced by including explicitly
the choice of transfer node for the trips that used the combination of modes. As a result the user’s perception
of the generalized travel cost for combined mode via the element of the transfer node set was included as
a term in the additional logit function of generalized travel cost perception for the combined mode via all
transfer nodes, that modeled the proportion of trips taken between O-D pair for each transfer node. Thus,
the number of trips using car to drive to the terminal and change the mode at the transfer node was given as
12
==PAGE:14==
a product of mean value of trips, mode choice function and transfer node function. From this formulation,
the transfer OD matrices using origin i or destination j as transfer mode, could be infered, added to the
”pure mode” OD matrices the values allocated to the corresponding multi-modal links.
Although the model of Fernandez et al. consists of only one public transport mode, the metro, it can be
used for multimodal systems as well by merging the networks of each mass transport modes and use it as a
transit sub-network in the formulation of bi-modal stochastic network equilibrium model. As a consequence,
change of shares between mass transport modes cannot be infered, however due to smaller computational
power requirements, this approach can be suitable in the case of very large networks
In the study [20], Heinz Spiess and Michale Florian developed a framework to determine route choice for
public transport passengers in order to simulate ridership when traveling from origin to destination. They
have developed a concept of strategy as an element of travelers’ choice set. The strategy was defined as a
set of rules for travelers to reach their destination when it is applied. This allowed to formulate problem
of not only finding a path from origin to destination that minimizes the expected total travel time, which
corresponds to Wardropian conditions but also include walking from origin to public transport stop, waiting
for the arrival of vehicle, quality of ride in terms of comfort, alighting a vehicle, walking between transfer
nodes and egress from transit stop to destination. The more information have the travellers at their disposal,
the more complex strategies can be formulated. The advantage of Spiess and Florian approach is that it can
take into consideration complex behaviour of passengers, however since it is defined as solely linear where
the link travel times are constant it is inappropriate for implementation of public transport modes operating
on shared segments of the road network as a congestion effect needs to be considered.
Abrahamsson and Lundqist [21] formulated and estimated 3 types of Network Equilibrium models by al-
ternating the sequence of Trip distribution, Modal split and Route assignment steps in the 4 stage travel
demand modelling framework. The models’ schemes are depicted on the figure below. It was assumed that
the trip generation is exogenously given and subjected to the land usage in the investigated urban area of
Stockholm. In the study, they assumed the road network and the transit network are independent on each
other, providing a simplification to the multi-modal traffic assignment problem. This assumption could hold
due to the fact that the core of the public transportation system in the Stockholm consists of undeground
railway network, which does not interfere with the road segments. In Traditional nested, Reverse nested
and Simultaneous models presented in the study, the trip distribution components in all 3 variants were
estimated as a the doubly constrained gravity type model, the logit model was used for mode choice and
Wardrop’s User Optimal principle was used for modelling route choice of car traffic. Assuming the route
choices of transit travelers were independent of route choice and and congestions in the road network, the
matrix of travel times could be computed exogenously. The generalized travel costs of travel was substi-
tuted by travel times. By reviewing the article it was concluded, that due to the listed assumptions and
simplification the solution is inappropriate in case of the city of Pilsen.
How passengers behave, when choosing the most appropriate transit route for their trip was analyzed in [22].
Chriqui and Robillard formulated an optimization problem within a probabilistic context, the assignment
of passengers on the distinct route was based on the ’percieved’ travel time. The problem was formulated
in terms of determination of the expected travel time as an optimization problem. The arriving vehicles to
the stop were treated as random variable. Two cases of arrival were considered. In the first, the time for
the first vehicle to arrive was uniformly distributed. This assumption could hold, in the situations, where
the headways on each route were constant. Such assumption was reasonable, for dedicated public transport

==PAGE:15==
system such as commuter rail or metro. In the second case, the time for the first vehicle to arrive followed
an exponential distribution, modelling the bus arrivals as Poisson process. However due to memorylessnes
of exponential distribution, the times to next arrivals were independent of past history, so the congestion
effect could not be considered.
Although the private vehicle assignment over road network had experienced rapid development, with the
increasing computational power, the public transport passenger assignment, and its specific challenges re-
mained uncontested for a significantly longer period of time. One of the aspects that has not been considered
is public transport systems limited capacity, generating congestion effects at the terminals, during boarding
and alighting of the passengers. DeCea and Fernandez proposed a new formulation for the assignment
problem over congested transit networks. In their work [23], public transport assignment problem for con-
gested systems was based on the concept of ”transit routes”. Deriving from a common lines methodology
proposed in [22], a ”transit route” was defined as any path that a transit user could follow, in order to travel
between any two nodes. In terms of network representation, it was identified as a sequence of nodes, the
first one being the origin of the trip, the final one represented the destination and all intermediate nodes
represented transfer points. The formulated graphs edges represented either waiting links, to represent the
waiting process of the passengers experienced at transit stops or in vehicle travel links. Such a formulation
could allow to involve queening process, where passengers arrived randomly to the stop at given average
rate and were served by vehicles of different lines. The service rate for each queue was dependent on the
available capacity. Although the queuing approach considered realistic assumption, in practice, it turned
out to be difficult to solve, due to a complex formulation and computational requirements for the large
urban network.
When investigating algorithms to solve the combined mode split-assignment problem, various approaches
were considered.
Combined Modal-split-assignment problems are typicially using modal split variables,
which give the shares for each considered mode, that passengers take in order to conduct their trips between
origins and destinations. One of the pioneered solution technique was initially proposed by Evans [11],
for combined trip distribution and assignment problem and reformulated by Leblanc and Farhangian [24],
in order to provide solution for combined modal split assignment. In their work, mathematical program,
consisting of three components, was formulated. The objective function of the optimization problem was
composed by auto and bus link impedance functions and modal split component. For each origin destination
pair, in the first component, the objective was to minimize each link’s travel time which was dependent on
the automobile flow, the second represented minimization of passengers flow dependent waiting time on
corresponding link and the third component determined the modal split between each origin-destination
pair. The objective function was subjected to multi-modal conservation of flow constraints, which required
that the amount of trips leave origin by either mode and travel by the chosen mode to the estimation.
When the computational performance of the Evans’ and Frank-Wolfe algorithms was compared, the Evans’
technique reached convergence faster on behalf of slightly larger memory requirements.

Theoretical background of the 4-stage travek demand forecast-
ing model
The classical 4-stage model used for macroscopic transport modelling in the planning process should be
viewed as a conceptual framework rather than a specific mathematical model with a clear structure. The
components of the transport modelling processes are decomposed into a 4 main consequent steps given by
specific purpose for each part. The various forms of collected data are used as an input to the model and
the model outputs provide background to analyze travel demands, predict future travel patterns, investigate
utilization of the route network, estimate modal-split and asses different pricing scenarios given by empirical
conditions. As the framework is being subjected to improvements and further research, the above mentioned
applications provide only the most frequent areas of usage. The overview of the 4-stage model framework,
obtained from [6] is depicted on the figure 4 below.
Figure 4: Overview of the 4-stage model framework.
6.1
Trip generation
To begin with the first step in the four stage planning framework, In the trip generation, the level of transport
in the system is defined by determining the number of trips generated in each zone, and the number of trips
attracted to each zone. Trip generation is the first step of the four-stage travel demand forecasting process.
It aims to predict the number of trips that originate from or are directed towards a particular transport
15
==PAGE:17==
system analysis zone by looking at households and non-households land uses. Households ”produce” trips,
while non-households ”attract” them, and it is important to consider both production and attractions for
origins and destinations of the trips.
Trip generation is often modeled as a function of the social and
economic attributes, demographics and gender structure of households, as well as the number of jobs,
schools, commercial zones and and retail activity in the area.
The general approach for trip generation modeling usually involves three steps: first, conducting a travel
survey (typically, a collection of trip diaries); second, creating a trip generation model that is based on the
results of the survey; and, lastly, assessing whether the survey data is representative of the population at
large and, if necessary, making adjustments to the model to create a more accurate estimate of population-
wide trends. By using a set of equations, it is possible to estimate the number of trips originating from or
headed towards each traffic analysis zone, based on its residential and employment characteristics. For each
spatial unit, the extent to which it acts as an origin or destination for trips can be estimated. The results
are typically an amount of trips produced or attracted by a particular spatial unit.
6.2
Trip distribution
Assuming the amount of trip is exogenous given (or estimated in the trip generation step) and fixed a
question of how the trips are distributed among the various destination nodes have to be raised. In the
trip distribution step, the goal is to determine the most likely flow patterns between all possible origins and
destinations. A spatial interaction model is often used to estimate movements, accounting for factors such
as distance. The trip distribution process constitutes an allocation of trips generated in one zone to other
zones in the study area, be it within or outside of the study area. In general, the longer the distance of the
destination, the more burdensome the trip.
The trip pattern in a study area can be represented by an origin-destination (O-D) matrix, which is essen-
tially a two-dimensional array of cells where the rows and columns signify each zone in the region. The
entries in each row i indicate the number of trips originating in zone i with all the zones in the corresponding
columns as their destinations. The total number of trips emanating from zone i is marked as Oi, while the
total number of trips attracted to zone j is marked as Dj. To ensure consistency, the sum of all the trips in
each row must be equal to O, and the sum of the trips in each column should equal to D
6.3
Mode choice
In the four-step model of transport modelling, mode choice is regarded as the third step in the sequence.
Following trip distribution and prior to network assignment, person trip tables (matrices with rows repre-
senting production and columns representing attraction) are generated. Mode choice involves establishing
the proportion of trips that will be taken using each available transportation mode for every cell in this
matrix. This percentage is then multiplied by the original trip table to create a new matrix containing only
trips taken with specified modes.
A popular choice of modal split function is the logit formula, which considers how travellers make decisions
based on their available options. The logit formula allows for situations where automobile and public transit
travel times are not equal at user equilibrium. It then approximates the appropriate flow of travellers for
each origin-destination pair to another, thus providing an estimate of modal shares of how people will choose
to travel.

6.4
Route choice
Given by travel demand (the number of vehicles willing to drive along a road segment) and capacity available,
the principle of assigning the traffic to the network can be viewed as a demand/supply problem, where the
users of the system try to minimize costs associated with travel and the costs associated with travel depends
on the usage of the system. The optimal solution can be seen as the one where the demand and supply are in
the equilibrium sate. The system approach of assigning the traffic flow on routes to achieve the equilibrium
can be summarized in following points
• Changes in one route may change all the decisions of all the users
• changes propagate to the system until a condition of equilibrium is reached
• The equilibrium flow pattern that would prevail under each scenario can be found only by analyzing
simultaneously all elements of the transportation network
This approach is based on behavioral assumptions:
• Every traveler will try minimizing the travel time when traveling from the origin to the destination of
the trip
• This does not mean that all travelers between each origin and destination pair should be assigned to
a single path
• The travel time on each link changes with the flow and therefore the travel time on the networkpaths
changes
6.5
Deterministic User Equilibrium
Deterministic User Equilibrium, also known as a Wardrop’s First principle (1952) [12], is a mathematical
model describing the principle of assigning the traffic flow on congested infrastructure network. The original
statement is as following:
Wardrop’s first principle: ‘Under equilibrium conditions traffic arranges itself in congested networks in such
a way that no individual traveller can improve his/her path cost by switching routes’
Under Wardrop’s First principle, the travelers behave according to assumptions:
• Travelers have full information and know the travel time on every possible route
• Travelers consistentlymake the correctdecisions regarding route choice
• All travelers are identical in their behavior
When reaching the User Equilibirum condition on a network, the travel time on all used paths is equal, and
(also) less than or equal to the travel time that would be experienced by a single vehicle on any unused path
for each origin-destination pair.

6.6
Graph model of network representation
In empirical meaning, the term ”network” is being used for describing some structure, that can be either
physical (streets, pipes, electricity lines) or conceptual (information flow, task management etc.). Starting
with the mathematical definition of the ”network” term (also known as a graph), defining network as a set
of nodes (in some literature also vertices or points) and a set of links (or edges or arcs) connecting these
nodes. The links can be either directed or not. The links in the network have their impedance, representing
how the usage of link affects the flow in it. The nodes are not associated with impedance. In case of traffic
network, it is more common to use level of service term instead of impedance. The level of service is usually
represented by travel time on each link as it is the primary deterrent for traffic flow. In some cases, it may
be appropriate to enhance impedance, by including cost measures, such as fuel costs or fare costs, the travel
time can be decomposed into a waiting and in-vehicle times, the travel comfort can be represented by vehicle
occupancy rate. When including multiple factors into level of service measure, the term generalized travel
cost is used as a form of impedance.
The graph model of the infrastructure network used in this thesis leads to a connected network, where is
it possible to get from one node to any other node by following a route through the network. A route (or
a path) is a sequence of directed links from one node, leading to another node. The route impedance is
the sum of particular impedance on each link. In the traffic infrastructure network representation of urban
areas, the number of paths connecting each pair of nodes can lead to enormous amount of combinations,
leading to a high computational requirements.
6.7
Traffic zones
In order to decompose large urban areas in the transport planning process into smaller regions, partitioning
into traffic zones is the usual approach to achieve reasonable computation ability. A various size of zones,
from a town block to a whole neighbourhood are modelled to represent investigated area. The amount of
traffic zones can vary from few to several thousands conditioned by required scale of the model. In terms
of network representation, each zone is represented by a node, also known as centroid of the zone. Unlike
other nodes in the network, such as the ones representing intersections or public transport terminals, the
centroids are unique, in terms of traffic movement as it either originates or destinates in these nodes. When
the set of centroids is determined, the traffic movement in the network can be represented in the origin-
destination matrix form. The OD matrix is a numerical representation of the flow between every origin and
every destination centroid in the network. The centroids are connected with the other nodes by dummy
links, which are directed outwards or inwards, if the traffic originates or terminates respectively, in these
zones. The traffic in the zones is not in the scope of analysis, thus the zone size determines the scale of the
investigated area. For further explanation, Network representation of a traffic zone, including a centroid

6.9.1
How vehicles of different modes affect travel times?
Before moving to mathematical formulation, it is necessary to analyse specific situation from the empirical
point of view. Consider that the origin and destination are connected by road segment and there are cars
and buses types of vehicles riding this road. The vehicular flows for each of the modes affect how long it is
needed to travel to get from origin to destination. When comparing bus with car, it is obvious that due to
its dimensions and mass the bus has different riding properties such as moving speed, acceleration, braking
time. It needs to be driven carefully to ensure safety and comfort of its passengers and it is taking more
space on the road. As the buses and cars are riding on the same road segment, they interact between each
other. From the above mentioned properties it can be concluded that the impact of bus flow on the total
travel time on the road segment is different than the impact of cars on the travel time, when riding the same
road. For this specific example, in the language of math, the specific property of different vehicles producing
non-identical impacts on travel times is treated in the way that the derivative of the bus travel cost function
with respect to the bus flow is not equal to the derivative of the automobile travel cost function with respect
to the automobile flow.
As a consequence of the property of cost functions derivatives, the multi-modal assignment problem cannot
be formulated as the equivalent optimization problem via Beckmann formulation of User Equilibrium cond-
tion, due to the fact that the Jacobian of link cost functions is asymmetric. The case of the multimodal
route assignment problem, when the matrix of partial derivatives of cost functions with respect to link flows
is not symmetric is known as traffic equilibrium problem with assymetric link flow interactions [5]. The
usual practice then is to formulate the problem as Variational Inequality in the feasible set of link flows and
modal demands. However, in the case of this thesis, this has been resolved by having unified flow variable
to model both private and public transport cost function, and since the public transport cost function uses
same BPR form, just expanded with specific constants, the cost functions derivatives are approximately
equal to each other, and the problem can be formulated as an equivalent optimization problem.

I have been also deciding on which type of assignment should I choose. Whether the deterministic one,

The stochastic type of multi-modal assignment was considered as well, where the travelers choose their
routes based on ”percieved time” with the included random error component. The foundational basics of
this type were taught in the course Transport System Analysis: Performance and Operations. But even it
is less complex than dynamic type, I have not feel confident enough to implement it for multi-modal case.
Therefore the final decision was to implement classic, deterministic type of assignment. Then it was neccesary
to research the types of the algorithmic solutions for the problem. There are 3 types of algorithms : link-
based, path-based and bush-based.
Link-based algorithms only keep track of the aggregated flows of links, but not the path flows which are
results of the summation of the link flows. As the amount of links in a larger networks is significantly
smaller than the amount of paths, link-based algorithms are way more less demanding for the computer
memory. Because of this property, link-based algorithms were the first to be used in practical application
in the past decades, when optimization for the usage of computer memory was much more important than
nowadays. Reviewed link-based algorithms are Frank Wolfe and Evans. Provided by the knowledge of Frank
Wolfe algorithm, that has been taught in the course, the first attempt was to implement this type. However
link-based algorithms are not intuitively appropriate for the public transport passenger assignments as the
public transport routes and lines are better to approximate by path based solutions.
Path-based algorithms differ from link-based algorithms in that they keep track of the path flow vector
instead of just the link flows. Obviously, this approach uses more memory but preserves more information
that is lost when aggregating path flows from link flows. This additional information can lead to faster
convergence, especially when a highly accurate solution is desired. However, the large number of paths in
a network makes the coding of path-based algorithms more complex, as it requires the use of efficient data
structures and algorithms to minimize the number of paths that need to be stored. Intuitively, this type of
algorithms seemed to me appropriate for modelling of passengers behaviour, as the passenger usually care
only for the place where and when he/she has to start the travel and how far is the terminal from hers/his
destination.
The last type to consider was Bush-based algorithms. The Dial B bush based type of algorithm is imple-
mented into the uni-modal solver of the traffic assignment in the TraMod environment, for more information,
recall [30]. Bush-based algorithms are a newer development that aim to provide speed similar to path-based
algorithms while using less memory, although still more than link-based algorithms. They achieve this by

selectively aggregating path flows. At equilibrium, the set of paths used by travelers from the same origin or
destination forms an acyclic subgraph (a bush), and bush-based algorithms aim to identify these bushes for

algorithms tend to provide solutions with higher entropy, which is useful when interpreting the path flow

Link interactions can be categorized as symmetric or asymmetric. When interactions are symmetric, the

there is no known equivalent minimization program that can be used to find the equilibrium flow pattern.
Then it is neccesary to formulate is as Variation Inequality problem. The case of Pilsen was obviously

hold, and the travel cost function of the alternative mode, is somehow flow-dependent as the bus traffic

8.2.1
Introduction to Gradient projection algorithm
Gradient projection is an example of a path-based algorithm, which tracks the path flows h in addition
to the link flows x. Since there can be multiple billions of combinations of paths in the network, it is not
feasible to keep track of literally every path’s flow, due to memory requirements. One of the drawback of
the path-based algorithms is therefore larger memory usage, compared to the link-based algorithms such as
Frank-Wolf or Evans. However, since they are keeping more information about potential solution, they are
faster to converge, compared to link-based techniques.
We define a set of working paths Πrs = {π ∈πrs : hπ > 0}, where hπ is the flow on path π for each OD pair
rs, and only track the flows on these paths. We will let this set grow and shrink over successive iterations.
A general framework of path-based algorithms is following:

3. Within each OD pair, shift flows among paths to move more near the equilibrium point.

9
Implementation of the algorithm into the TraMod Environment
Traffic Modeller (”TraMod”) is a macroscopic transport modelling enviroment that has been developed
in cooperatin with transport engineers, mathematical modelers, software developers and geographic infor-
mation systems specialist. It offers a possibility of implementation for smartphone and web application,
providing an opportunity for public sector representatives at municipal levels, such are urban architects,
transport planners and municipality officers to test several traffic scenarios without the neccesity to install
proprietary software. As the user interface is very intuitive, there is no need to have expertise in other
transport modelling softwares in order to be able to orient in the environment.
9.1
Architecture description
TraMod works as a server based solution for urban networks traffic modeling that provides the option to
the users to change various network parameters in order to analyse the impacts of the created transport
modelling scenarios. This is made possible by TraMod application interface [27]. Key functional features
are listed below [3].
• Calculation of traffic volumes in a transport network in near real-time (depending on the size of the
road network and server capacity calculation the time may vary from seconds to minutes).
• Implementation of three algorithms for the traffic assignment providing optimal calculation schema
for every use case.
• Calculation a what-if analysis with user-defined scenarios which may include:
– road free-flow speed editing
– road capacity editing: e.g., road/lane closure
– road segment geometry editing: e.g., change of intersection layout
– addition of a new road segments: e.g., construction of a new city ring road
– editation of traffic generators and attractors: e.g. consideration of the new census data
– addition of new traffic generators and attractors: e.g. construction of new neighbourhood, special
cultural event or newly built city mall
• Using TraMod Web App Calculated traffic scenarios including proposed edits in traffic network/gen-
erators can be saved and shared.
In order to be able to model scenario in the TraMod Environment, following input data needs to be provided:
• Urban road network that is topologically correct and contains traffic attributes necessary for traffic
volume calculation. Mandatory attributes include: road capacity, free flow travel time and allowed in-
tersection directions (”turns”). TraMod is ready to be implemented for bidirectional and unidirectional
road networks.
• Traffic generators/ attractors linked to the traffic network: Traffic generators and attractors are
zones in which traffic originates or terminates in the urban network. Examples of traffic generators
are: housing city blocks, schools, office buildings, malls etc.
• Origin-destination matrix containg amout of trips from each origin to each destination
• Traffic count survey on the urban network neuralgic points used for calibration and validation of
the model.
The workflow of transport scenario modelling can be summarized as follows: An initial base scenario is
calculated in the traditional way by traffic engineers provided by in depth knowledge about the area of
31
==PAGE:33==
interest (demographical structure of inhabitants, urban network structure, Traffic count survey) or it can
be calculated by TraMod (then calibration needs to be run as well). A client/server solution functionality
works in the way that:
• Interactively gather users input (road closures, new road planning, etc.) via HSlayers based GUI.
• Calculate the traffic flows of the modelled scenario in near real-time and illustrate the output in a
web-application based client.

9.2
Data structure of the model in the TraMod Environment
Data structure of the transport model in the TraMod Environment consist of five key elements:
• Node - table of nodes
• Edge - table of edges
• Turn restriction - table of turn restrictions at specific nodes
• Zone - table of zones (traffic generators/attractors)
• ODM - Origin Destination Matrix
Table of nodes represents set of vertices of the graph representation of urban network. Each node represents
either cross road or initial and terminal nodes which are connected to the zones through unique key, where
the traffic is generated or attracted.
Table Edge contains set of edges of the graph representation of urban network.
Each edge is one way
directed, meaning that in order to model both directional road segment, two edges between nodes, each for
one directions, are required to include into the table.
The next element represents a Turn restriction table where the rules for the traffic flow movements over the
edges running through the nodes are specified. Besides regular turn restrictions, it is used when modelling
traffic flow on roundabouts, and since the restriction can be specified within the certain time frame, it is
used to model traffic at the signalised junctions as well. The graphic schemes on the figures 14, 15, 16
and 17 obtained from [28] better illustrate TraMod input data structure in tabular form alongside with the
network representation.

of zones can be for example neighbourhoods, apartment block complexes where the traffic originates or
schools, industrial zones, commercial areas, representing destination of the trips.
Origin Destination Matrix and Turn restriction tables differs from the previous elements, as the geometrical

The variables were encapsulated and getters and setters methods was defined.
9.4.3
Creating Gradient projection class
TmGradientProjectionAssignment class was created in order to perform the excess demand assignment. It
consists of instantiating method, calculation method and methods for encapsulation. In the instantiating
method, initial costs of the edges are obtained and stored into the array for each origin-destination pair.
Calculation method consist of several loops, and conditions. The most outer is the while loop which checks
whether the current iteration is within the defined limit of 100 iterations, otherwise it performs next iteration.
Underneath there is another while loop which check whether the convergence criterion (relative gap) has
reached the limit, otherwise the program proceeds with the calculation. Followed by while loops, the next
one is an if else condition. If the current iteration is equal to 0, the program first finds the shortest path of
the graph and add it to the list of used paths, for each origin destination pair and then it performs initial, All
or Nothing assignment, which assigns whole demand on the found path, then it calculates automobile costs,
excess demand costs and store them into arrays, so in the next step a new shortest path can be calculated
based on the stored values, otherwise, it proceeds with the shifting flows and demands. As the part of the
excess demand cost is the cost of the bus mode, it has to be calculated as well.
For the bus mode, a restricted set of edges has been determined, which contains edges on which this mode
operates and edges, that ensure connection with each origin and destination zones. This edges were manually
selected in the QGIS to ensure that each zone have access to the bus network. The shortest path is found
on this restricted set and for the edges on such a path, travel costs of alternative mode is calculated. This is
a simplification as such an approach does not ensure that buses are going to follow specific lines that would
mirror reality. Improvement of the simplification is considered in the future development.
The shifting step functions as following: At first for loop is defined, to be able to loop through the list of
used paths, within the loop the maximal travel costs path is found, in order to shift the flows from this
path. If this is the first iteration after performing All or Nothing Assignment, this path corresponds to
the one found in the previous step. Then, the new shortest path is found, based on the updated travel
costs, if the used path list does not contain this new path, it is added to the list. In order to be able to
perform Newton’s method for shifting flows, derivatives of the cost functions must be calculated. A path
cost derivatives list is defined. Into the defined For loop for the edges on the maximum path, another for loop
for the edges on the shortest path is nested. Then the path costs derivative are calculated, by comparing
the edges identification numbers. If the edges IDs are not equal, for these edges, the derivatives are added
38
==PAGE:40==
to the list. This procedure ensure that we have calculated derivatives only for the edges that are either on
the first one path or another but not both, this way the consistency with the theoretical introduction for
the gradient projection algorithm is ensured.
After adding paths costs derivatives to the list, derivatives of the alternatives mode are calculated as the sum
of shortest path cost derivative excess demand costs derivative following the formula given in the equation
21. As the excess demand cost function is composite of modal split logit function and the bus travel cost
function, the excess demand cost function derivative is calculated as the derivative of modal split function,
with respect to the bus demand (excess demand) plus derivative of the bus cost function with respect to
the flow.
The auxiliary calculation of sum of the other path flows is performed. This is understood as a sum of path
flows for the paths that are not used in the shifting step. This corresponds with the middle sum term in
the equation 17. Afterwards, auto shortest path cost and excess demand cost are compared to perform the
shifting procedure for the path flows and demands, accordingly to corresponding case.
The last step is to update flows and costs in the array. Then the relative gap is calculated. If the convergence
criterion has not been reached yet, current iteration counter is incremented and the whole procedure is
repeated.
