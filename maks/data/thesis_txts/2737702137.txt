Abstract
Industry 4.0 and Lean have become a necessity in the manufacturing industry. Industry
4.0 is an integrity of technologies that are beneficial for industries. Lean is a way of think-
ing about creating needed value with fewer resources and less waste.
The aim of this thesis is to examine the benefits and limitations of implementing Industry
4.0 technologies on existing Lean tools for inbound logistics within industrial manufac-
turing. These two subjects are highly involved in the manufacturing industry making
them relevant topics for research. Case studies, a structured interview, government and
non-government sources, and existing literature were used to obtain the relevant informa-
tion for the analysis. The analysis shows the connection between Industry 4.0 and Lean
and how that connection can affect inbound logistics in the manufacturing industry by
enhancing certain aspects within it. The information collection and sharing that Industry
4.0 provides, is an important asset for upper management wanting to achieve the best opti-
mal decision-making possible when it comes to inbound logistics. The information helps
with enforcing continuous improvements, using key performance indicators as a way of
measuring the current state of the focus area. Sustainability and demand predictions are
examples of what Industry 4.0 in collaboration with Lean can improve on. Understanding
which technologies and tools are available to enhance activities within a company enables
a more competitive advantage if used effectively. Collectively, the thesis has demonstrated
the importance of Industry 4.0 and Lean in inbound logistics. Acquiring detailed knowl-
edge of Industry 4.0 and Lean can give companies a competitive edge over their peers and
better prepare them for the implementation of Industry 4.0 into their work environment in
the future.
Keywords: Industry 4.0, Lean, Inbound logistics, Decision-making, Sustainability

Introduction
Industrial manufacturing has become a necessity in today’s culture. It’s involved in peo-
ple’s daily lives, be it a car, phone, or public transport among many others. All of these
things need to be manufactured as well as transported in order for society to prosper.
Inbound logistics focuses on all activities that are involved in obtaining supplies for man-
ufacturing, assembly, or sale (Minner, 2019). These activities can be classified as the
foundation that keeps the supply chain running smoothly. Lean philosophy is popular
among companies as well as being thoroughly researched. Additionally, Industry 4.0 of-
fers technologies that are beneficial to companies. This prompted the idea for the thesis,
to research and analyse inbound logistics and how Lean and Industry 4.0 affect it.
Based on the discussion above, the research question that captures the topic of the thesis
is as follows:
What are the benefits and limitations of implementing Industry 4.0 technolo-
gies on existing Lean tools for inbound logistics within industrial manufac-
turing.
Lean philosophy puts its focus on efficiency, it has become a fundamental philosophy that
manufacturing companies have adopted to improve efficiency in their operations. Lean’s
emphasis on continuous improvements becomes an important way of thinking for compa-
nies. The business landscapes are changing regularly due to technological advancement
in the world. A new industrial revolution has started due to new emerging technologies,
this revolution is known as Industry 4.0. The tools within Lean and technologies within
Industry 4.0 can be integrated together to enhance each other’s benefits. Understanding
how the tools and technologies within Lean and Industry 4.0 work together can be a great
asset for companies pursuing to enhance their inbound logistics operations.
This thesis aims to analyse and provide an understanding of how Industry 4.0 technologies
and Lean tools can work together to better inbound logistics. The paper is divided into
three distinct core chapters, each serving a different objective to convey information. The
objective of these chapters is the following:

2.1
Industry 4.0
Industrial production is a pillar in today’s business landscape and has been for some time.
It is often characterised by the usage of machinery for the production of parts or fin-
ished goods, though it has not always been like that. Since the 18th century, drastic
changes have influenced how industrial production is done. It all started with the first
industrial revolution that would jump-start humanity’s business landscape into something
completely new (Britannica, T. Editors of Encyclopaedia, 2024). This would further de-
velop and transition into the digital age, which we now describe as Industry 4.0.
2.1.1
History of the Industrial Revolutions
The first Industrial Revolution refers to a period in the 18th century when the imple-
mentation of machine usage in production first occurred and would change how pro-
duction would continue in the future. Before that, most production was done by hand,
3
==PAGE:15==
2.1. Industry 4.0
worked by skilled individuals such as blacksmiths and jewelers. The revolution mainly
centered around coal, iron, and textiles. The revolution has its origin in Britain which
would later expand to other countries, changing how production would be performed for-
ever (Britannica, T. Editors of Encyclopaedia, 2024).
The second Industrial Revolution saw a breakthrough in technology which enabled
factories and other sectors to become more efficient due to their growth resulting in
economies of scale. This would make it possible to have mass production and assem-
bly lines. Technological advancements also enabled production to use electricity as a
resource (Mokyr & Strotz, 2000). With the advancement of technology, the exploita-
tion of natural resources started to become more apparent as well as the usage of energy
sources that hadn’t been readily available before due to the lacking of necessary technol-
ogy (Britannica, T. Editors of Encyclopaedia, 2024).
The third Industrial Revolution saw the implementation and usage of automation, elec-
tronics, and computers. Computers became increasingly more essential following the
creation of the Internet back in 1983. The third revolution introduced Cyber-physical
systems, although it was inefficient due to the lack of computer capabilities at the time.
These digital implementations would pave the way for the fourth industrial revolution,
commonly known as Industry 4.0 (Park, 2017).
The fourth Industrial Revolution, which will now be referred to as Industry 4.0, builds
on the third revolution but adds on it, similarly to what happened throughout the previous
revolutions. Industry 4.0 involves the usage of Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS), Internet
of Things (IoT), and networks among other things that will be discussed in more detail in
chapter 2.1.2.
Each individual industrial revolution improves on the previous one due to the technolog-
ical advances that occur due to mankind’s curiosity and desire for improvements to the
existing technology. Figure 2.1 shows the evolution of the revolutions and the important
elements that characterise each one.

2.1.2
Industry 4.0: Definition and basic concepts
There are many different definitions of what Industry 4.0 is, and to simplify things the
definition from operation management (OM) will be used. Ivanov, Tang, Dolgui, Battini,
and Das (2020) define Industry 4.0 in relation to OM as the following:
Industry 4.0 is an integrity of technologies, organisational concepts, and
management principles underlying a cost-efficient, responsive, resilient, and
sustainable network, data-driven and dynamically and structurally adaptable
to changes in the demand and supply environment through rapid rearrange-
ment and reallocation of its components and capabilities.
The ideology of Industry 4.0 consists of multiple technologies that are beneficial for in-
dustries. Similar to the definition of Industry 4.0, there are multiple ways to define what
elements Industry 4.0 consists of. Rüßmann et al. (2015) provides a good explanation of
what these elements are, where he identifies 9 pillars of technological advancement that
are essential for Industry 4.0. Figure 2.2 shows an overview of these pillars.

2.1. Industry 4.0
Cyber-Physical Systems
Cyber-Physical System (CPS) is a combination of two systems, cyber and physical. It usu-
ally consists of sensors, software, and computers/hardware that communicate and work
together to show the current status of the system (Pan et al., 2019). By having a bet-
ter understanding of the system’s current status, it becomes easier to make sure safety
and quality standards are upheld. Acatech (2011) gives a clear explanation of what the
supposed capabilities of a CPS are supposed to be. These capabilities should:
• Directly record physical data using sensors
• Evaluate and keep data and interact both with the physical and digital world
• Connect systems through digital communication facilities
• Use globally available data
• Have a series of interfaces for human and machine interaction
In short, CPS provides industries with the communication abilities between the physical
and digital world as well as opening up doors for more automation in the production due
to easier optimization of processes thanks to CPS (Acatech, 2011).
Even though CPS is not mentioned in Rüßmann et al. (2015) 9 pillars, It is still an essential
part of Industry 4.0 since CPS is the connection that these systems have with each other.
This is done by using the internet and internal data analysis to make predictions.
Internet Of Things
IoT is a term that refers to information sharing between devices with the use of the Inter-
net. IoT is widely used in the industrial sectors as well as in everyday life. Gubbi, Buyya,
Marusic, and Palaniswami (2013) define IoT in a "smart" environment as the following:
Interconnection of sensing and actuating devices providing the ability to share
information across platforms through a unified framework, developing a com-
mon operating picture for enabling innovative applications. This is achieved
by seamless ubiquitous sensing, data analytics, and information representa-
tion with Cloud computing as the unifying framework.
IoT enables a more seamless interaction in machine-to-machine (M2M) communication
(Tripathi, Lata, Kumar, & Raja, 2021). Good communication between machinery results
in a higher efficiency due to real-time data being transmitted effortlessly between M2M,
decreasing the need for human interaction regarding decision-making.

2.1. Industry 4.0
IoT provides customers with increased accessibility in relation to the status of the product.
An example of this is the tracking of a product that is in transport which most online re-

get a better understanding of the market’s needs, enabling improvements to their products

ufacturer also becomes easier due to increased monitoring, tracking, and identification of
products with the help of IoT.
Cloud-based systems
The cloud is a globally recognized technology, people use it every day through their
phones when uploading digital content to it for example, but what does "the cloud" mean
in a manufacturing context? The cloud can be split into two parts, a virtual factory, and a
resource library, see table 2.1 for a description of each part. These parts work together by
passing information between them. For instance, this can be used when making predic-
tions. These predictions can be made using historical data from the resource library and
comparing it to the results presented by the virtual factory. Being able to make accurate
predictions helps companies minimize risks and uncertainties, for example when plan-
ning on changing the current product that is being produced to a different one (Zhang, Li,
Wang, & Cheng, 2017).

Description

user requirements, performance indicators etc.
Like with everything that uses digital resources, the cloud includes security risks. The
cloud has a controller in its server that connects itself to a physical sensor in order to
transmit information. This information can be obtained and used illegally when compa-
nies become the victims of cyber-attacks. Confidential information related to business
practices, as well as employee information, are sometimes used for blackmail or some
other sort of personal gain (Akbarian, Tärneberg, Fitzgerald, & Kihl, 2023). This shows
the importance of having an up-to-date security system and a competent IT department in
order to be diligent in prevention practices so that these security attacks are less likely to
occur.

2.1. Industry 4.0
Big Data
Modern industrial companies often require large amounts of data to work properly and
to make future decisions. The type of data could be for example sales numbers, on-hand
inventory numbers, and production numbers just to name a few. To keep track of this data,
datasets are used to hold it together. Larger datasets are often referred to as "Big Data".
Kaisler, Armour, Espinosa, and Money (2013) refers to Big Data as "The amount of data
just beyond technology’s capability to store, manage and process efficiently". Depending
on the size of a company’s operations, Big Data can be an important part of its day-to-day
activities. Predictions can be made for factors that affect a company’s profitability. Big
Data handles a large amount of volume of information. This enables it to find correlations
between elements that the database stores and therefore make predictions. Predictions
have to be made in the right environment so they can be usable. If not, the prediction
could become irrelevant and potentially harmful for future decision-making (Márquez &
Lev, 2016).
3D Printing (additive manufacturing)

2.2. Lean
Lean manufacturing increases efficiency and productivity by streamlining operations. The
two most common versions of efficiency used in manufacturing are flow efficiency and
resource efficiency with the latter being much more common (Modig & Åhlström, 2012).
However, Lean tends to favour flow efficiency because of its emphasis on minimizing
waste and delays, resulting in a more agile production system.
2.2.1
Resource efficiency
Resource efficiency focuses on getting the most out of the available resources that add
value within an organization (people, machines, and housing for example). One principle
of resource efficiency is to divide jobs into smaller tasks that are to be carried out by
different individuals and organizational functions (Modig & Åhlström, 2012). This is
done with the idea of increasing the utilization of the resources needed to try and get
more out of each resource.
2.2.2
Flow efficiency
Flow efficiency, unlike resource efficiency, focuses on what is being processed within the
organization. Within manufacturing, the unit is usually the product that is often made
up of components. Within the service industry, the unit is often considered to be the cus-
tomer, and his needs are met with different activities. Flow efficiency can be considered as
a measurement of how many units flow through the process during a specific time frame
(Modig & Åhlström, 2012).

the visualisation of Lean management and how it operates. It shows a few of the Lean
methods as components to a house that together achieves the desired structure and hold
up the goal of low cost and short lead time, represented as the roof of the structure. Figure

Kaizen
The Japanese word Kaizen means "continuous improvement". The Kaizen philosophy
involves everyone in every aspect of life, be it our professional life, social life, or private
life, everyone should try to continuously improve on all fronts. The improvements under
the Kaizen philosophy are often small and incremental but can result in dramatic changes
over a longer period of time. There are many aspects within a company that can be
improved, Kaizen philosophy aims to improve on all things but puts a slight emphasis
on quality over other aspects such as cost or delivery time. Quality in this example does
not only refer to the final quality of the product that is being produced or the quality of
the service being provided but also the quality of the processes that are involved in the
production or the service(Simmons, 2018).

5S
The Kaizen principle of 5S, or the 5S of good housekeeping, stands for five words that can
be used to organize the workplace. The 5S, with time, has become increasingly important
for companies within the manufacturing industry (Simmons, 2018).
According to Simmons (2018), these five S’s of housekeeping are the following:
• Seiri: or Sort in English, revolves around the separation between things that are
necessary and things that are unnecessary and rid the process of the unnecessary.
• Seiton: or Straighten in English, means to put all the essential items in order so that
they can be accessed easily whenever they are needed.
• Seiso: or Scrub in English, represents the importance of a clean environment. Clean
the tools that are used, clean the machines regularly, clean the workstations and
other places.
• Seiketsu: or Systematize in English, means to make cleanliness a routine and to
extend and practice the prior three steps.
• Shitsuke: or Standardize in English, represents the building of the self-discipline
needed to make the prior four steps a standard routine that is never ending and can
be continuously improved upon.
There are some sources that add another S to this list, making it the "6S". The 6S contains
all of the 5S’s mentioned above along with the addition of "Safety".
Jidoka
The Japanese word Jidoka, embodies a manufacturing philosophy that combines the ma-
chine automation with human intervention. Jidoka was developed by Toyota’s founder Sa-
kichi Toyoda back in 1896. Jidoka represents a shift in industrial automation, attempting
to integrate human intelligence with the standard mechanical automatic machines within
the production process. This technique started in Toyota’s textile industry as a mechanism
that halted the entire production if it detected a broken thread. Jidoka therefore separated
itself from the more traditional manufacturing practices at the time by prioritizing the
quality and efficiency through standardized error detection (Modig & Åhlström, 2012).
By this method of detecting errors and halting the production whenever necessary, Jidoka
not only improves the product’s quality, but it also strives towards the reduction of waste
and continuous improvements.

Kanban is a physical supply and scheduling system that ensures JIT and Lean production.

Six Sigma
Six Sigma is an approach that helps companies put an emphasis on developing and de-

statistical term for standard deviation and it measures how far away the process is from
perfection (Carayannis, 2020).
According to Carayannis (2020), the three main elements of quality within the Six Sigma
philosophy are:
• Customer: The customers define what "quality" means. The customers have expec-
tations regarding delivery times, prices, product reliability, and so forth.
• Processes: In today’s market, many organizations are able to deliver similar prod-
ucts or services for similar prices and therefore it is not always enough to simply de-
liver what the customer expects, organizations need to understand their customer’s
perspectives. It can be a major leap for an organization to use the perspective and
look at the processes of an organization. Doing that enables us to understand the
customer’s point of view further than the quality of the product or service.
• People: Employees must have the know-how, the skills, and the motivation to cre-
ate, and improve processes in a successful manner.
Muda
Toyota identified 7 different versions of waste within their production process. Their def-
inition of waste was everything that did not add any value to the customer or to the pro-
duction itself (Issar & Navon, 2016). They called this the seven muda ("muda" meaning
"wastefulness" in Japanese). Issar and Navon (2016) categorize the waste as the follow-
ing:
• Overproduction: Each individual section in the production process that produces
more than the customer specifically requests can be considered a waste in the form
of an overproduction.
Page 16 of 71
==PAGE:28==
2.2. Lean
• Waiting: A product that is waiting needlessly for a machine or a worker to become
available is considered waste.
• Transportation: Transportation of a product should be minimized by designing
the factory layout so the product requires minimum transportation. Unnecessarily
long transportation of products is a waste of time and resources.
• Processing: Conducting more work on a product or a part of a product than the cus-
tomer expects is waste. Doing work that is more expensive than is necessary also
falls under this term. Doing this adds to the expense without the customer request-
ing it, resulting in work being conducted that the customer does not necessarily
want to pay for.
• Inventory: Inventory are goods that have monetary value to them but are tied down
to the process. Inventory also needs extra housing that brings its own cost, along
with the extra resources needed to operate the warehouse.
• Movement: The workplace should be set up so that workers need minimal move-
ment to do their work effectively. This can mean keeping the tools and materials
each worker needs close to that worker, saving time and money in the production
process while increasing the worker’s output.
• Defective products: Each product that is produced but cannot be sold for a full
price represents waste.

Push and Pull Manufacturing
Push manufacturing is a predictive or forecast-driven manufacturing philosophy. For a
Push-based manufacturing system to succeed, a relatively accurate prediction of future
customer demand is needed. Manufacturers need this to plan the allocation of resources
to meet that demand strategically. This Push method tends to benefit markets with a pre-

==PAGE:31==

dictable and stable demand more than those with a higher level of uncertainty (Nelson,
2016).
To contrast the Push-based manufacturing system, there is the Pull-based system where
every individual station (that partakes in the Pull system) waits for instructions from the
station that is positioned upstream compared to itself, within the assembly line. In a Pull-
based system, each station only produces exactly what is demanded from the upstream
stations. Stations in the Pull based system only work when the required output is "pulled"
from them by the upstream demand, each station needs an outside demand and cannot
work independently. This method of pulling output using the upstream demand can allow
manufacturers more variation within the product line. Another benefit of a Pull-based
system is that it tends to minimize both the finished goods inventory and resources tied in
unfinished processes. Some large manufacturers, however, allow the formation of a few
incomplete products to accumulate and act as buffers in case the manufacturing line shuts
down, which would allow manufacturing to continue for a short amount of time while the
problem is being taken care of. That being said, this buffer approach often fails when
shutdowns or other problems take longer to fix and the stations run out of their safety
buffer of unfinished products (Nelson, 2016). Figure 2.9 show the comparison between

manufacturer, manufacturer-distributor, and other combinations of players involved in
the creation of a product or service. Logistics are a crucial part of the supply chain as
it involves the planning, shipping, and storage of products (Minner, 2019). For a supply
chain to be successful, logistics within it have to be up to standards. Logistics can be split

up into two, outbound and inbound logistics. Outbound logistics focus on the later stages
of the supply chain while inbound focuses on the earlier stages. This chapter will focus
on the latter, inbound logistics.
2.4.1
Characteristics of inbound logistics
As mentioned earlier, Inbound logistics focuses on the early stages of the supply chain.
This means that it is all activities that are involved in getting supplies to manufacturing,
assembly, or sales (Minner, 2019). Transportation, planning, storage handling, and com-
munication between actors in the supply chain are all activities that fall under inbound
logistics. To ensure as smooth and as uninterrupted production as possible, material plan-
ning has to be on point. This sort of planning needs to involve a set deadline between
respected parties and a set amount of raw material needed for operations on the produc-
tion front to remain as uninterrupted as possible. To do that, companies need to have a
strong relationship with their suppliers. That relationship needs to have frequent and in-
formative communication, negotiation needs to be healthy and the two organizations need
good collaboration in order to sustainably function in the long run.
When receiving or sending out material and products, scheduling and sequencing are an
important aspects of inbound logistics that aim at limiting wait times for carriers. This
revolves around the utilization of shipment capacity (Minner, 2019).
Another aspect of inbound logistics is the inventory management. The input and output
of a company’s raw material stock have a significant impact on how a company’s inbound
logistics are set up and behave. Likewise, the storing of internally manufactured mod-
ules or components also has an effect on the inbound logistics. Some companies apply
the Lean technique, JIT, which requires tight planning in order to minimize inventory.
However, a small inventory can lead to delays resulting from its lack of safety inventory.
These delays can happen in case of a sudden unexpected increase in demand or negative
changes on the suppliers end (Minner, 2019). For inventory management within inbound
logistics, storing material before or in between processing or production might have to be
taken into account. Placing these storage locations strategically could effectively mini-
mize costs and delays.
The manufacturing industry is highly reliant on logistics, therefore any production irreg-
ularities that occur can have a negative impact. This makes it so that companies have to
optimize their logistics as possible, often with the use of Lean to eliminate non-value-
adding activities (Jinhe, Nan, & Qinggang, 2009). It is therefore important in inbound

Key performance indicators
For stakeholders to have a good idea of the current state of a company’s viability and

Not all KPIs are as straightforward as the annual sales and profit margins, sometimes it

• Average transportation time
• Average transportation utilization
• Average transportation emission
• Supplier lead time
• Utilization of storage in warehouses
• On-time delivery

Bullwhip effect
One of the main drivers in a supply chain is the demand, it affects the number of sales
which dictates the production quantity. The flow of information related to demand is
therefore crucial for the supply chain in order to make proper forecasts that will affect
the procurement of raw materials, production volume, and shipment frequency. Since de-
mand is essential in the supply chain, it is also essential for inbound logistics. Demand
is not a steady phenomenon, it fluctuates due to multiple reasons. Some of those reasons
are outside a company’s control, an example is the COVID-19 outbreak that dramatically
changed demand in certain sectors, while some are due to a company’s sub-optimal busi-
ness practices.
The bullwhip effect explains how the fluctuation in demand affects processes in the supply
chain. The further upstream in the supply chain a process is, the more fluctuation is
between orders. That is, supplier orders are less reliable than orders between sales and
buyers (Campuzano & Mula, 2011). This fluctuation often leads to an overestimation
of what the demand is, leading to increased inventory levels and increased unnecessary
production. Figure 2.10 shows how the bullwhip affects the supply chain. There are
different reasons for these fluctuations, Campuzano and Mula (2011) states that the four
main causes are:
• Processing demand and lead times that are not null

2. Related to adjusting the demand forecast with sub-optimal decisions.

2. Aims to achieve economy of scale in lot-changing activities.

1. Customer demand is more likely to increase if scarcity is present.
2. Can result in unfulfilled orders which harms companies
• Price variations
1. Lowering prices to increase demand

Lewin’s Model for Change
By some, Kurt Lewin is considered to be the father of social psychology and Lewin’s
Change Theory is the centerpiece of his legacy within the world of psychology. The
model was developed in the 20th century and is widely recognized for implementing
changes within organizations. This model emphasizes the importance of the preparation
before the change and executing it effectively before solidifying the new process to ensure
its lasting effect (Psychology, 2023).
At the base of Lewin’s Model for Change is the idea of driving and resisting forces that
act as building blocks for the theory. The idea is that these two forces act in two different
directions creating a dynamic balance. Driving forces are what drives change in people
while the resisting forces prevent changes, working against the driving force. If the driv-
ing force is stronger than the resisting force, then there is an opportunity for change to

occur. However, if the resisting force is equal or grater than the driving force, change
is unlikely to take place. By that logic, if the change is desired, you can address both
the driving and resisting forces in order to make the chance of a successful change more
favorable (Psychology, 2023).
Kurt Lewin’s Model for Change proposes a way of successfully implementing long-term
changes. In order to fully understand this model, a block of ice is often used as an exam-
ple. If you have ice in the shape of a cube and you want to change the shape of it into a
cone without losing any volume from the ice by cutting it and discarding the remains for
example, how could one go about that? Lewin’s Model revolves around the idea that you
could unfreeze, change, and then refreeze the ice. If the ice cube is melted then it can be
reshaped into a cone in its water form before freezing it again making the new cone form
more permanent (Psychology, 2023).
• Unfreeze: To approach the unfreezing phase, Lewin believed that you could ap-
proach it from three different angles.
– Increase the driving forces.
– Decrease the resisting forces.
– Combine the two.
Increasing the driving forces will drive the behaviour towards the desired change,
while decreasing the resisting forces loosens peoples grip on the status quo making
the change easier as a result. The last combines the two in order to move the bal-
anced equilibrium towards an easier change.

The eight steps to a successful change were identified by Dr. Kotter who had observed

plained in further detail below (Bedrad, 2023).

creation of a sense of urgency for the change. Using passion and purpose, inspiring
people to achieve and to act when opportunities present themselves. This step is
crucial for gaining some momentum and convince the organizations employees that
the changes are necessary. With this sense of urgency, the employees can more
easily go out of their own comfort zone and have an easier time engaging with the
changes.
• 2. Build A Guiding Coalition: Initiatives for a successful change require a strong

• 4. Enlist A Volunteer Army: Widespread collective action can often result in

3.5
Reliability and validity
Reliability and validity are important topics that must be taken into account when writing
a thesis. This chapter aims to explain the two topics as well as emphasize the importance
of them. The chapter builds on how Fitzner (2007) defined both reliability and validity.
Reliability in research refers to the consistency of measurements. Reliable measurements
should give similar results every time. This can be split into 4 key categories, each fo-
cusing on different variations of reliability(Fitzner, 2007). According to Fitzner (2007),
these categories are as follows:
• Test-retest reliability: Consistent results from tests over time
• Interterm consistency: Consistent results from a variety of written or verbal sources
• Internal consistency reliability: Consistent results across multiple items within a
test or tool
• Interrater and intrarater reliability: Multiple test givers or interviewers come to
the same results
Validity in research refers to performing accurate measurements on the thing that is being
observed/tested. There are many ways to categorize validity, though there are 7 categories
that are of particular importance when conducting research of any kind. According to
Fitzner (2007), these are the following categories that validity can be split into:
• Face Validity: Is the question/tool a good measure?
• Content validity: Expert review of the question/tools in order to ensure they are
acceptable.
• Criterion validity: How well do measurements align with other measurements.
• Construct validity: Overall agreement of theoretical concepts.
• Concurrent validity: Do different types of tools give the same result.
• External validity: Can the result be generalized?
• Internal validity: Was the analysis made without bias?
Reliability and validity help thesis students and researchers ensure practical research to
further educational inquiries. Ensuring that research is reliable and has validity will help
in building trust with the reader, empowering them to potentially put more thought into
the research and potentially embark on their own scientific journey.

4.3
Demand and its effect on the supply chain
The manufacturing industry is large and splits into different sectors, each having their re-

dependency on logistics in their operations as well as the dependency on demand. This
chapter will take a look at how Industry 4.0 and Lean can affect decision-making regard-

Manufacturing sectors are highly reliant on customer demand, having unreliable informa-
tion on said demand can lead to a loss in profit. Decision-making related to demand is
therefore a big responsibility that affects almost all aspects of manufacturing, from ac-
quiring raw materials to product manufacturing. Having a reliable information network
assists in decision-making so that they become more effective. There are ways to use

Before thinking about how to improve a current process, the problems have to be identi-
fied. The problem regarding demand prediction is usually demand fluctuations that ripple
through the supply chain. The bullwhip effect helps in explaining the reason behind these
fluctuations, see chapter 2.4.3 for a more detailed explanation. Having a good understand-
ing of how the bullwhip affects the supply chain enables companies to take preventive ac-
tions. These actions could potentially prevent damages created by the demand fluctuation.
To properly evaluate the seriousness of the bullwhip effect, KPIs have to be established.
Calculating KPIs helps with showing the performance status in specific areas. Chapter
2.4.3 introduced three KPIs that can be measured to evaluate performances in relation to
bullwhip. The most common KPI used is the Order Rate Variance Ratio, which is the
ratio between the variance of actors in the supply chain and the variance of the customer.
It measures how demand amplifies through the supply chain and shows either a geometric
or exponential trend upstream. If the order rate variance ratio has a value higher than 1,
then it signifies that a bullwhip is active in the supply chain, if it’s lower than 1 then the
orders have less variability in comparison to the demand (S Cannella & Relvas, 2013).
The inventory variance ratio is similar to the order rate variance ratio, it however focuses
on how inventory amplifies instead of the demand. Average Inventory shows the average
inventory levels over a specific period of time.

4.3.2
Handling demand and bullwhip
Chapter 4.3.1 shows how the fluctuation in demand can affect the supply chain. It can be
even more drastic in real life where each actor in the supply chain has different lead times,
holding costs, transportation costs, and different inventory capacities. The examples aim
to show how by creating a simple supply chain, small changes in the downstream demand
can affect upstream suppliers. When the supply chain becomes more complex, such as
using multiple sources to obtain raw materials, the importance of handling demand be-
comes increasingly more important.
Chapter 2.4.3 explains the four main reasons for the bullwhip effect.
• Processing demand and lead times that are not null
• Order lot sizing
• Scarcity of rationing and scarcity of finished goods
• Price variations
Processing demand and lead times that are not null are often the main factors in the fluc-
tuation in demand (Campuzano & Mula, 2011). The inventory levels and the unnecessary
production that is associated with this factor can have large financial costs for a company.
Companies have to take precautions to minimize the effect of bullwhip as much as possi-
ble, to make more precise decisions regarding production and material acquisition.
Communication between actors in the supply chain is important, it helps companies real-
ize the current state of the demand, inventory levels from suppliers, routes for products,
etc. Technologies within Industry 4.0 can be great additions in the supply chains, sup-
porting a better decision-making process and facilitating better communications. These
technologies can enhance current Lean tools as well to handle demand fluctuations.

The benefit of CPS and IoT for decision-making
CPS uses sensors, software, and computers to be able to see the current state of the system
(Pan et al., 2019). CPS can therefore immensely help with the estimation of both produc-
tion and demand. For instance, using sensors to track inventory levels helps employees
keep a realistic overview of the output the company is capable of sending out at any given
time. This helps with the making of sales predictions as well as informing on how much
raw material or finished goods the company needs to request from suppliers. These in-
ventory levels can also be presented to suppliers since CPS uses digital communication to
connect systems. Data regarding inventory levels makes Lean tools such as "muda" and
5S easier to keep in check since knowing the current status helps with preventing waste
and makes 5S easier to follow through.
IoT can also be a supportive technology that builds on what CPS is already doing. It
provides a more seamless communication between machines which could help with both
data collection and estimating the current state of a system, due to the decreased human
interaction that IoT provides. This communication increases accessibility through dif-
ferent entities in the supply chain, an example would be between supplier and customer.
This accessibility has the potential to increase the reliability of data being communicated.
Reliable data makes it so that the people who are involved in the decision-making process
regarding logistics can make a more realistic scenario that will give more accurate results.
Limitation of CPS and IoT for decision-making
Knowing the limitations of technologies is important, it can help companies manage their
reliance on each of them. One obvious downside to CPS and IoT is the threat of cyber-
attacks which are always present whenever working with something that is connected to
the internet. In order to minimize that threat, companies need to invest in strong IT de-
partments that have both a good understanding of cyber-attack prevention as well as the
proper procedures in case such an attack occurs. Sensitive information could end up get-
ting leaked following a cyber-attack and it could harm the company and its stakeholders.
To highlight the vulnerabilities that CPS and IoT face, Yaacoub et al. (2020) mentions
three different vulnerabilities:
• Management vulnerabilities
• Platform vulnerabilities
• Network vulnerabilities
Cyber-attacks are not the only threat that the systems face. Since CPS consists of both a
cyber and physical system, it will also be prone to physical damage depending on which

physical system is being used. Chapter 4.3.2 mentioned the use of sensors, they might get
damaged somehow and could provide incorrect data, which might result in sub-optimal
decision-making regarding logistics.
Push vs pull strategy for decision-making
Knowing what kind of strategy a company uses regarding logistics will most likely bring
positive results. Deciding which strategy a company should use is the main hurdle that
they have to overcome. Different manufacturing companies benefit more from having a
push strategy rather than a pull strategy and vice versa, it all depends on what is being
produced and how the market works. For instance, if a company has a yearly fixed out-
put, a push strategy is likely more appropriate. Elkem Iceland, the company that the thesis
students interviewed, utilizes a push strategy that greatly reduces the effect of fluctuation
from the market, see chapter 4.6.2. Having a clear strategy helps information flow work
more efficiently. This flow can be further expanded on by utilizing Industry 4.0 technolo-
gies, like CPS, to increase the accuracy of the information and make it easier to maintain.
Information is one of the key elements that can keep the bullwhip in place, and knowing
which strategy is best suited helps with making the market and production information
clear, which in turn helps with decision-making in the future.
Maintaining an effective pull manufacturing and inbound logistical system can get com-
plex but there are a few tools and technologies in Lean’s and Industry 4.0’s catalog that
could prove useful. JIT and pull manufacturing can complement each other as they share
some similarities. While pull manufacturing relies on upstream demand to dictate the rate
of production, JIT focuses on the response to the demand. The Kanban method can be
added to a JIT system in a pull manufacturing and logistical strategy. Kanban can be used
as a method to visualize the upstream demand and request material or an activation of a
process in a JIT system, keeping it on track and making sure that everything happens just
in time as requested by the upstream demand. These tools can use data from CPS and IoT
to make a better evaluation of the current status of the system.

4.4. Industry 4.0 on automation in manufacturing
Many Industry 4.0 technologies can be used to enhance automation within manufactur-
ing. Industry 4.0 has many digital solutions that can be used to aid with automation such
as the Internet of Things, Artificial intelligence, and big data. CPS can also influence au-
tomation as it can monitor operations, control them, and even optimize them in real time,
allowing for greater responsiveness and flexibility within production.
Automated guided vehicles (AVGs) are an example of how influential Industry 4.0 can be
on inbound logistics and material handling in general within manufacturing. The AVGs
need to be equipped with all sorts of sensors, cameras, and navigational systems that en-
able them to maneuver dynamic environments that are subject to changes and still avoid
obstacles, all while interacting with other machines around them and communicating with
nearby workers. Industry 4.0 could allow AVGs to communicate with other systems to
optimize different routes they need to take, coordinate the route with the production sched-
ule, and alter their behaviour in response to changes in demand and inventory levels of
any given material.
Similarly to the AVGs, automated storage systems (AS) and retrieval systems (RS) can
also be transformed by Industry 4.0 technologies. They can be made more intelligent,
efficient, and adaptive to changes. IoT and different types of sensors within CPS could
be utilized to optimize the warehouse’s inventory management. Industry 4.0 could also
help AS and RS systems provide real-time visibility into inventory levels and perhaps au-
tomate the task of reordering more raw materials for production using demand patterns,
the production schedule, and current inventory levels.

necks, and possibly increase the quality of the final product.

manufacturing processes more Lean. There are Industry 4.0 technologies that can enable
real-time monitoring and analysis of current production processes which helps managers
identify different sources of waste (or muda 2.2.3) so that they can be eliminated, con-

Drawbacks
There is potential for much gain when it comes to integrating new Industry 4.0 tech-
nologies into existing automation and robotics solutions, such as the chance of increased
efficiency and utilization. There are however also some possible drawbacks as with many
things. For instance, integrating new technologies can come with a high installation fee
and it might even be expensive to continually maintain. This investment might be too
high of a barrier of entry for many smaller or even medium-sized companies.
In some cases, existing infrastructure might pose some limitations on a project of this
magnitude. Existing infrastructure, which in this case includes physical infrastructure
and all of the organizations existing systems and processes may not be compatible with
the new and desired technology. For companies in rural areas for example, some of them
might need to invest in a costly, new, and improved network infrastructure or even an
increased electrical bandwidth to successfully operate some of the energy-intensive tech-
nologies brought by Industry 4.0. Addressing these drawbacks might require some exten-
sive planning and good collaboration between different departments to ensure a successful
adoption.
On top of the high investment cost and possible infrastructure issues, the implementation
of these solutions might be technologically complicated. Existing staff members might
need extra education or added training for them to successfully operate and maintain these
advanced systems. Setting up these systems also requires some specialized knowledge,
and finding the skilled workforce to do so might pose problems for some companies.

Management of change
For a company to successfully implement Lean tools and Industry 4.0 technologies, it
needs to prepare for substantial changes that can affect how the entire company oper-
ates. As manufacturing companies embrace these changes to become more efficient with
their resources, increase innovation, and optimize existing and future processes, manag-
ing these changes is vitally important and needs to be handled correctly.
The company’s management and leadership team need to have a strategic vision of how
to implement the changes. A clear vision helps with the articulation of the objectives
and the benefits using the expected outcome of the change. Along with the vision, good
stakeholder engagement is critical to the support that the project will have. Clear commu-
nication and potential involvement throughout the changes can lead to higher stakeholder
satisfaction, potentially even increasing the project’s chance of success.
As mentioned in the chapter 2.4.2), establishing relevant KPIs and other metrics is im-
portant. Relevant KPIs can be used to measure the impact that changes are having on
the company and its processes. The KPIs can then be used to identify the relevant ar-
eas that are under-performing and are in need of improvement. Seeing the inefficiencies
clearly with well-defined KPIs can make the entire company more adaptable to changes.
This adaptability can be used with the Lean methodology of continuous improvements
by regularly reviewing the performance of all KPIs and pivoting each solution as needed,
especially during the early stages of changes. Helping the company’s management and
leadership team make informed decisions regarding ongoing changes and projects is all
part of the management of change.

Discussion
In this chapter, some key findings from chapter 4 will be highlighted and interpreted.
The goal is to connect these findings to the research question of the thesis, as well as
examine them. Limitations in the thesis will be discussed followed by a proposal for
future researchers wanting to contribute to the subjects of Industry 4.0, Lean, and inbound
logistics. The research question of the thesis is as follows:
What are the benefits and limitations of implementing Industry 4.0 technolo-
gies on existing Lean tools for inbound logistics within industrial manufac-
turing.
5.1
Key Findings and reflection on the thesis
Industry 4.0 technologies along with Lean tools can benefit each other, they collaborate
to provide clearer, more reliable, and improved information for companies to work with.
This collaboration minimizes sub-optimal decisions when it comes to inbound logistics
planning which in turn affects numerous areas within a company’s operations. According
to the analysis, the areas that stand to gain somewhat the most from this collaboration
are demand handling, sustainability and automation. Technologies within Industry 4.0 al-
low upper management to monitor a system’s current status, be it production, warehouse,
shipping, etc. Knowing the current status enables the identification of inefficient practices
which can be dealt with in collaboration with Lean tools.
The results from the analysis put an emphasis on decision-making and how it affects
inbound logistics in manufacturing. There are multiple ways to reach a conclusion re-
garding decision-making, and how to do it depends on the specific company’s priorities.
This might indicate that before the decision process can even begin, a clear focus on the
subject has to take place. This might indicate that further research into "focus strategy" is
needed and comparing it to inbound logistics, potentially combining it with Industry 4.0
and Lean.

5.2. Limitations of the thesis and suggestions for future research
Industry 4.0 and Lean are highly relevant subjects to this day. There is plenty of literature

5.2
Limitations of the thesis and suggestions for future
research
The structure and how data collection was conducted, and the opinions of the thesis stu-
dents are highly influenced by previous literature. Though the literature has been reviewed
and its reliability has been vetted, the literature might not fully reflect how things are in
the real world. Though the thesis students conducted an interview to get a better idea of
how things are in real life, the sample size was not sufficient to make a standardised anal-
ysis. The interview only contained one specific manufacturing company in a specialised
industry, though it did help with generating ideas of what could be in the thesis. Another
factor which affected the thesis was the scope of it. It could be considered too wide which
could make for less precise research.
Anyone wanting to further research the topics covered in this thesis can benefit from ob-
taining as much real-life data from companies as possible. That could be conducting
interviews, planning documents, decision processes, etc. Having a wide range of data
from companies gives an advantage when analysing the topics that are being researched.
From this, identifying what technologies and tools a company is using helps with narrow-
ing down what the focus should be.
It could be beneficial to narrow down the scope of the subject. In the present thesis,
the subject scope of "manufacturing" is wide which might limit in-depth analysis. For
example, by focusing on "textile manufacturing" or "silicone manufacturing", a more
in-depth analysis can be performed. This increases the odds of finding new flaws and
opportunities within the given scope.

6
Conclusion
The thesis aimed at analysing the benefits and limitations of implementing Industry 4.0 on
existing Lean tools for inbound logistics within industrial manufacturing. The connectiv-
ity of Industry 4.0 and Lean was analysed through quantitative and qualitative methods.
These methods consisted of a structured interview, case studies, government and non-
government sources and existing literature. The results show that the connection between
the two subjects can enhance the benefits that they bring to inbound logistics within the
manufacturing industry.
The main result after analysing Industry 4.0 technologies was its ability to provide infor-
mation. Information is of utmost importance when it comes to logistics planning since it
could enable optimal decision-making. These technologies can be combined with Lean
tools to increase the benefits that are possible. The three main areas that were analysed
(Demand, Sustainability and Automation) all indicate that the combination of Industry
4.0 and Lean can be beneficial to manufacturing companies.
Demand within the supply chain will affect all that comes after it and therefore it is impor-
tant to focus on a said area when inbound logistics is being analysed, the best example of
this is the Bullwhip effect. The example created in chapter 4.3.1 showed how companies
in the supply chain are affected by fluctuation in the supply chain, further upstream that a
company is situated the more it is affected. CPS and IoT are useful technologies within
Industry 4.0 that enable better communication throughout the supply chain. Having reli-
able information helps with mitigating the effect of the main perpetrator responsible for
the bullwhip effect, processing demand and lead times are not null. This reliable infor-
mation network enables better decision-making that is associated with inbound logistics.
CPS and IoT can work together with a company’s logistics strategy, be it a push or a pull
strategy. Pull strategies are highly reliant on demand so having the right technologies in
place helps with the creation of an effective system.

Inbound logistics have a great effect on sustainability due to its activities related to the
transportation of finished goods, raw materials, and movements of inventory just to name
a few. Ways to increase sustainability with Industry 4.0 and Lean were revealed through-
out the analysis. For companies to increase sustainability, the analysis suggests taking a
closer look at the types of transport, waste, material used, and all non-value-adding ac-
tivities within the company. Using combinations of Lean and Industry 4.0 technologies
that enhance each other can be used to lower the negative effects these things have on the
company’s sustainability goals. For instance, cloud-based systems can enhance the value
stream mapping which makes finding non-value-adding activities easier. By implement-
ing these technologies and tools together, SDG targets can be pushed forward to increase
sustainability.
Automation within manufacturing has much to gain from the integration of Industry 4.0
technologies. This addition could enhance the efficiency and quality of manufacturing
while eliminating muda (waste) further. For logistics systems, Industry 4.0 could for ex-
ample utilise its AI, IoT, and various sensors to optimize various AVGs or storage and
retrieval solutions as well as inventory management. For Lean, the implementation of
Industry 4.0 on automation for manufacturers could decrease the amount of delays and
eliminate bottlenecks all while improving the overall quality of the final product. As with
many things, there are some potential downsides to implementing Industry 4.0 into au-
tonomous systems. Installation can be very expensive for some of the solutions and it
might even be costly to maintain after the setup stages. Existing infrastructure might also
limit what is possible and a company’s current processes may not be compatible with the
requirements of the desired technology.
Anyone thinking about implementing Industry 4.0 technologies needs to inform them-
selves about how that implementation will enhance the gathering of information. It is
important to know what information is needed and which technologies best suit the com-
pany. There is no need to try and reinvent the wheel, it would be better to focus on
enhancing current activities and seeing how Industry 4.0 can enhance them, which could
be improving Lean within the company as an example. Integrating Industry 4.0 and Lean
needs to have a decisive implementation plan. A decision has to be made on which area
the integration should focus on and if it will be a vertical or horizontal integration. KPIs
are also necessary for future assessments of how the systems are responding to the inte-
gration.

Interview with Elkem Iceland supply chain manager
Question 1
Interviewer: I am here with Gunnar Harðarson for an interview. so let’s dive into it.
Has Covid-19 changed some things in relation to how you conduct certain
business processes?
Interviewee: First of all, Covid and the conflict in Russia and Ukraine can be put together
in a pot. It has made people rethink safety stock and what volume you have
in the warehouse. the world had gotten used to everything running smoothly,
Interviewer: So the change is related mostly to safety stocks?
Interviewee: Yes, though Elkem Ísland was little affected by it since since we don’t
obtain a lot of materials from China. Though i know that in Norway they
are getting materials from China. There they have to consider not having all
their eggs in the same basket.
Question 2
Interviewer: Do you feel like decision-making has changed in recent years due to emerg-
ing technologies, such as big data and increased usage of internet accessibil-
ity
Interviewee: Yeah most likely. You are quicker to obtain information related to the other
side of the supply chain, suppliers and alike. I think we can make a more
thoughtful decision a little faster. It of course depends on the company and
how far that company has come. I’m not really sure about big data, I mean
its all (the information) in a closed database where we can get it straight
away. Just by having inventory systems, matters more for us on the produc-
tion front. We see sooner if we are going off track, keep better records of
production, and therefore, maximize value which come from the ovens (used
in producing silicone). it is OK to also mention that personally there are
some downsides. It’s not a traditional information chaos, but it is a massive
amount of information that come quickly.
Interviewer: So some information might get between the cracks?
Interviewee: Yes, as well as it is a challenge to "stay the course" where you’re constantly
getting new information and you need discipline and a good structure to be

able to take these information in keep going instead of changing the course
regularly.
Question 3
Interviewer: How involved is your company emerged in Lean culture? if so what tools
have been implemented?
Interviewee: Elkem uses "Elkem business system" (EBS) which is a business model
which takes a lot of parts from Toyota, it basically is the Toyota school of
thought.
5s is the most visible Lean tool I can think of that we use, since it feels easy
to try and implement. We are using continuous improvement, we are doing
a "cost road-map" project to see the potential savings and what should be
changed. These projects are then followed through with at the end of the
year to see what has been accomplished.
When I thinking about Lean tools I imagine Toyota type assembly lines, Kan-
ban stock and all those ideas. But the nature of our production is that we have
a fixed output where we are more in a push system rather then a pull system.
Interviewer: Have you seen any improvements since using Lean concepts?
Interviewee: Well the type of waste (muda) has helped out, we focus on that so that
for instance to prevent overproduction, too much inventory, keep quality in
check, and those types of things.
Question 4
Interviewer: Has the topic Industry 4.0 been talked about in the office?
Interviewee: Is that some AI stuff? we have heard about the AI but never talked or heard
about Industry 4.0, at least not in my case.
Question 5
Interviewer: How has the use of sensor technology helped with manufacturing and how
has it affected inbound logistics?

Interviewee: We have all potential sensors on our ovens for instance cooling systems,
and pressure sensors too keep operations going. We also have new sensors
to measure the height of "deiglur" so we arent putting too much liquid metal
inside it. We are driving a project now is sensors/cameras on our assembly
lines where we should be able to measure inline moisture in our coals and
sense fluctuations. These cameras use some sort of AI which should detects
which metrials are good to use for production. Regarding maintaining the
ovens, this is a huge step up where it helps for instance that the ovens don’t
get too much fine material so that we can make proper countermeasures if
needed
Interviewer: Would you say this is good regarding the movement of material within your

Interviewee: Yes well, you can at least see these fine materials if we are moving our
materials correctly. This AI also detects if we are accidentally moving the
wrong type of raw materials on our assembly lines. Basically some sort of
a quality check. We also have sometimes made use of drones in order to
make a 3-D model of our plant and estimate our stock from that, so there are
many options of usage regarding sensors and similar tech. But when it comes
directly to inbound logistics there is nothing really that comes to my mind.
Question 6
Interviewer: Does the company focus on minimizing the bullwhip effect, if so how?
Interviewee: I Wouldn’t say so really. I would say that on the production front, the
"whip" wont be powerful in that regard. We have contracts in place, but
sometimes we overestimate so we have some excess inventory but nothing
drastic in the whole picture.
Interviewer: Would you say these contracts are somewhat of a safety precautions re-
garding bullwhip?
Interviewee: You could say that. Though the risk related to that is when these contracts
are expiring but our lead time might be longer than that. Maybe we have a
customer that is ordering more and more. We then might project that he keeps
on buying more in the same trajectory and then there is an available contract
and maybe we cant get them, or the demand lowers. But in regards to raw
materials, it all depends on our production plan. The main thing is we have

business. They might have the "sky is the limit" demand or something alike.
In our case we know exactly what our limit is.
Interviewer: You said earlier that you had a push system, does that make it so that the

Interviewee: Yes, since we have 100% known capacity on the production.

internal logistics, the movement of containers or pallets. However our in-
frastructure doesn’t necessarily leave room for automation. Though there are
clearly some but we don’t really have a lot of repetitive tasks. That’s why I

more complicated process, for instance Elkem might send out an expedition
