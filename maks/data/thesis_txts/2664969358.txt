Abstract
There are currently more than 3 billion smartphone owners worldwide and the number is steadily grow-
ing [ODe20]. Studies have shown that there is a difference in how males and females use smartphones
and an even larger difference between different age groups [And+16; Van+15]. However, there are few
studies on smartphone usage in rural areas.
This is a global large-scale study on the difference between smartphone usage in rural and urban
areas. This study shows that urban users spend more time on their phone than rural users. Furthermore,
there are differences between which apps are more used in urban and rural areas. Urban users tend to
use apps categorised as communication apps, navigation apps and news apps while rural users tend to
use social and entertainment apps.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
There are currently more than 3 billion smartphone users worldwide and the number is steadily growing
[ODe20]. More than a third of smartphone owners report that the phone is the first thing they reach
for when they wake up in the morning [Ma15; Del17b], almost 50% report that they use their phones
during the night [Del17b] and 81% of U.S. smartphone owners say that they keep their phone nearby
during nearly all waking hours [New15]. People are becoming more and more dependant on their phones
and the idea of being without it is actually frightening to many [Ma15; Elm14]. There is even a word
for this phenomenon: nomophobia, which is defined as ”fear or worry at the idea of being without your
mobile phone or unable to use it” [nom21]. The word was chosen as the people’s word of the year in
2018 by the Cambridge Dictionary [Wor18] which shows that the phenomenon is not uncommon.
People have always worried about the effects of new technologies. One of the oldest examples of this
is Socrates worrying about the effects of writing compared to face-to-face communication and arguing
that it will make people forgetful [CH+97]. Ever since the telephone first emerged scientists have studied
the consequences of its usage. In 1957, long before the smartphone was invented, telephone usage was
linked to addiction and anxiety [Har57]. Since the arrival of the smartphone there have been mixed
opinions about its effect on peoples lives. Some argue that smartphones can reduce social isolation
[Cho15] while others argue that the smartphone reduces the quality of face-to-face interaction [RST17].
Some argue that it can improve students’ learning experiences [Váz14; All09], while others see it as
a distraction in a teaching environment [HS16]. Some claim that smartphones can be used to reduce
symptoms of anxiety [Fir+17] while others argue that smartphones are causing anxiety [DAA15].
Studies have shown that people, especially younger people, are becoming addicted to their smart-
phone and develop problematic phone usage patterns [Lop+14; Par05]. Excessive mobile phone usage
has been linked to low sleep quality, depression and anxiety [CG10; DAA15]. The long term conse-
quences of smartphone addiction and excessive use are not clear, since the smartphone is rather new
and research is limited, but there are indications of smartphone usage having a negative effect on peoples
attention span and memory [WSC17].
Research on the effects of smartphone usage is limited and to some degree conflicting.
As the
smartphone is becoming more omnipresent all around the world [TS19], it is crucial to understand this
new technology and how it is used. Studies have shown that there is a large diversity in how, and how
much, the smartphone is used [Fal+10; Zha+16]. This diversity has been explored and studies have
shown that age and gender are factors that do impact phone usage [And+16].
The smartphone in rural areas
Rural life is often associated with an idyllic life in harmony with nature and with close family ties
[Clo03] in contrast to the anonymity and the impersonal, superficial relations of urban life [Wir38]. On
the other hand, rural life is also often seen as old-fashioned and in need of modernisation [Wir38].
When the telephone emerged in the 20th century it reduced the social distance and isolation in rural
areas [Fis94] and it was probably more valuable in rural areas than in urban cities. However, telephone
companies did not recognise the market in rural areas to begin with [Fis94]. The same pattern repeated
itself when cars first emerged. Manufacturers marketed them as a luxury to the urban people even
though they were arguably more useful to the rural people [Fis94]. One could draw a parallel to the
recent emergence of the smartphone which has the potential of further reducing the social distance in

rural areas but is nevertheless more used in urban areas. Studies have shown that the smartphone
adoption rate is higher in urban areas than in rural areas [Cen17].
Multiple studies have been conducted on smartphone usage with different angles and rural and
urban life has been studied and described in numerous papers [Clo03; Wir38; Fis94], but there are very
few studies on smartphone usage in rural areas. In this thesis I will investigate whether the difference
in rural and urban life is reflected in the app usage of residents. Previous research has shown that there
is a difference in how people in rural areas and people in urban areas use technology and social media
[GKS08; Per15] and which apps rural Americans and urban Americans use [GPD16], however, to the
best of my knowledge, this is the first study to examine the difference in app usage in rural versus urban
areas on a global scale.

Scope
The goal of this study is to analyse app usage using a global large-scale dataset.
The focus is on
investigating whether there is a difference in the app usage in rural areas compared to urban areas.
In this study I will aim to answer the following questions:
• Is there a difference in the overall phone usage in rural areas compared to urban areas?
• Is there a difference between the types of apps used in rural areas compared to urban areas?
• Is the app usage affected when users move from rural to urban areas and vice versa?
• What features are important when trying to classify users as urban or rural?
The data used in this study was collected by a global smartphone and electronics company over a
period of 4 years, from 2015 to 2019. The data contains information on app usage from around 3 million
anonymised users and was collected using a tracking app on the user’s phones. Additionally, the data
contains information about the users such as gender, age and country of residence.

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
This chapter provides an overview of the literature related to this study. Even though smartphones
are a big part of the everyday life of a large portion of the world population [TS19], the technology is
relatively new and the literature on this subject is limited. This chapter starts with some facts about
smartphone usage and some insights into the usage and the motivation of smartphone usage. Then the
subject of social media is briefly touched upon. We then move unto the subject of rural versus urban
people, first generally and then more specifically when it comes to smartphones and social media usage.
Additionally, there is a section about a small case study which gives an insight into the impact the
internet can have on a rural community. The chapter is summarised with a short section on the main
points.
2.1
Smartphone usage
The number of internet users worldwide has been steadily increasing in the last decades and there are
now at least 3.5 billion internet users [Ort19]. This means that at least 45% of the world population is
now online. The use of digital media has shifted from being primarily from computers before the year
2013 to now being more used from smartphones [Ort19]. The number of people who own smartphones
is also increasing.
In 2015 a global median of 43% reported owning a smartphone [Pou+16].
The
percentage of people who owned smartphones was highest in the U.S. and Europe and lowest in Asia
and Africa [Pou+16]. In 2018 76% of adults in advanced economic countries and 45% of adults in
emerging economic countries owned a smartphone [TS19].
Different users have different usage patterns when it comes to smartphone usage. A small scale
study based on data from a total of 255 users from 2009 [Fal+10] shows that the number of sessions
per day and the mean session length varies a lot, both within single users and across users. The study
showed that users have from 10 to 200 sessions on average a day and the average session length per day
varies from 10 to 250 seconds. Another large-scale study based on data collected from ∼100,000 users
in 2015 [Zha+16] supports the claim that there is a difference in how people use their smartphones.
In this study, the authors used clustering to identify 382 distinct types of users. A third more recent
study from 2019 [De +19] separates users into two groups according to their app usage: keepers and
explorers. Keepers are users that more or less keep the same apps while explorers are users that tend to
add and remove apps more frequently. This term is related to more general terms in behaviour analysis
where subjects are categorised as those with constant behaviour and those with changing behaviours
[De +19].
Multiple studies suggest that demographics have an impact on smartphone usage. Young, more
educated and wealthier people are more likely to own smartphones than older, less educated and less
wealthy people [Pou+16]. It has been shown that females have higher usage than males and that usage
is decreasing with age [And+16; Van+15]. A study from 2016 based on data from over 30,000 users
showed that females daily spend on average 10 minutes more than males on the phone and that teenagers
spend on average over three hours a day on the phone while the oldest group (51+) spend just under
two hours on average a day on the phone [And+16]. There also seems to be a difference in what kind
of apps people use. Females tend to use more social and communication apps while males spend more
time on games, entertainment and news [And+16; Wei08; Van+15]. Younger people spend more time
on entertainment, communication and social apps and they are generally more likely to use everything
the phone has to offer and have a higher number of different apps [De +19; Wei08], while older people

use the smartphone more in a traditional manner, e.g. for calls and for informing themselves [And+16].
This is also supported by older studies on internet usage activities where it has been found that females
and young people are more likely to use the internet for social interaction [Teo01]. The income level
might also influence what kind of apps users favour. One study showed that high-income users spend
more time on travel and health- and fitness apps than lower-income users [Zha+16].
2.2
The motivation behind using smartphones
Several studies have been carried out which attempt to shed light on the motivation behind using
smartphones and apps. A study from 2011 [Böh+11] concluded that most sessions last less than a
minute and that communication apps are most frequently the start of a session. This suggests some
kind of checking behaviour where users often take out their phone to check their communication apps.
This claim is supported by another study from 2011 [Oul+12] that concluded that a large part of
smartphone usage was the result of checking habits i.e. very short sessions including only one application.
The reported reasons behind these short sessions were time killing, diversion and entertainment. The
study also points out that checking habits can be a gateway to other apps, meaning that users start a
session with a checking habit and then continue their session with other apps [Oul+12].
Social capital might also impact how the internet and smartphones are used [HH08]. The term
social capital was introduced by Coleman in 1988 [Col88] and is a broad term that is defined by its
function in a social structure. Social capital is related to the terms: trustworthiness, norms, intellect,
healthy relations and engagement [Por98; FK00]. Studies have shown that users with higher social
capital are more likely to seek content that is capital enhancing [HH08; ESL07]. Young people with
higher education and of a more resource-rich background are more likely to seek activities that are
capital enhancing such as exploring career opportunities or seeking information about politics, finance
and health opposed to seeking pure entertainment [HH08].
2.2.1
Uses and gratification theory
Uses and gratification theory (UGT) studies the reasons why people are drawn to certain kinds of
media and types of content based on the gratification it gives the user [Rug00]. The theory has been
around for decades, but is still highly relevant, maybe more than ever. UGT has previously been used
to understand and predict usage of new mass communications media such as newspapers, radio and
television. Today it can be used to understand the use of modern technology such as the internet and
smartphones [Rug00].
Several researchers have been using the uses and gratification theory when trying to understand
the usage of the internet, smartphones and social media [PR00; WW13; Wei08].
Based on UGT,
motivation for using social media can be divided into two types: instrumental and passive [Wei08;
JS13]. Instrumental usage is goal-oriented usage, i.e. usage with a specific purpose such as organising
or information gathering while passive usage is less goal-oriented and more for diversion or filling free
time [JS13; PR00]. One study concluded that the more users viewed the phone as instrumental in
fulfilling their needs the more likely they were to use it for communication and staying informed and
the more users viewed the phone as a mean to pass time (i.e. for passive use) the more likely they were
to use it for playing games [Wei08]. Another study found ten uses and gratifications for using social
media, the top 3 are social interaction, information seeking and pass time [WW13]. A third study
concluded that Korean’s smartphone usage is more affected by instrumental motivation than passive
motivation [JS13].
2.2.2
Effects of smartphone usage
When the telephone first emerged, its usage was linked to addiction and anxiety [Har57]. The smart-
phone, with its endless possibilities, has become an important part of peoples lives [New15; Ort19],

but not without consequences. Studies have shown that smartphones induce addictive behaviour, es-
pecially among young adults and children [Lop+14; Par05]. Smartphone usage has also been linked to
depression, anxiety, low sleep quality and loneliness [DAA15; RST17; CG10]. On the other end of the
spectrum, scientists are also praising the benefits of the smartphone as a tool that can bee used to e.g.

2.3
Social media
The use of social media has been growing rapidly since it first emerged in the early 2000s [Ort19; Per15]
and a large part of this usage is from smartphones [Ort19]. Research from Pew Research Center shows
that in 2005 7% of all Americans used at least one social media site and in 2015 this number had grown
to 76% [Per15]. In 2018 5 social media platforms had reached over 1 billion users (Facebook, YouTube,
WhatsApp, Instagram and WeChat) [Ort19]. In 2018, Facebook was the social media platform with
most users [Ort19] but recent studies suggest that the popularity of Facebook might be starting to
decrease [AJ+18]. A study from 2018 on American teenagers aged 13 to 17 showed that YouTube,
Instagram and Snapchat were more used than Facebook in this age group [AJ+18] and another study
from Pew Research Center in 2019 showed that Facebook was more popular among adults aged 25-49
than among the young aged 18 to 24 [PA19].
Several studies have been carried out investigating the effect social media has on teenagers’ well-
being [VPS06; Sel+09; Ahn11]. Asking American teenagers themselves almost 50% believe that social
media has neither positive nor negative effect, almost a third believe that it has mostly a positive effect
and a quarter believe that it has a negative effect [AJ+18]. Studies have linked social media usage
to depression and social anxiety [Sel+09] and to both a negative and a positive effect on self-esteem
[VPS06]. Studies have also highlighted the benefits of social media for its ability to help users build
social capital [Ahn11].
2.4
Rural and urban areas
The terms rural and urban areas are broadly defined as the country and the city. More specific definitions
are based on a combination of population density, land use (e.g. industrial areas are often categories
as urban) and distance to nearest urban centre [Rat+16]. In the past, a larger fraction of people lived
in rural areas than in urban areas, but the population in urban areas has been growing faster than in
rural areas and around the year 2008 the fraction of people living in urban areas surpassed that of the
rural areas [Rit18]. However, in many low-income countries, the majority of the population still lives
in rural areas [Rit18].
People living in rural areas are generally older than people in urban areas [Par+18; Hal+10]. Rural
people are generally less educated and earn less than people living in urban areas [Par+18; IM+16;
Hal+10]. In the U.S., almost 80% of residents in rural areas are white while in urban areas only 44%
of residents are white making rural areas much less racially diverse compared to urban areas [Par+18].
2.4.1
Internet, smartphones and social media in rural areas
Generally, rural areas lag behind urban areas regarding internet access [FK07; Str01; Hal+10; HQC14;
Per19] and rural residents use the internet less than urban residents [Hin00]. In 2019 roughly 63% of
rural Americans had home broadband internet compared to 79% in urban areas [Per19]. Other factors
such as age, race, educational level and income also play a central role regarding internet access [Hal+10;
Str01; WWR03].
Data from Pew Research Center shows that smartphones are also more common in urban areas than
in rural areas [Cen17] in the U.S. In 2019 71% of rural Americans and 83% of urban Americans owned
a smartphone [Cen17]. The gap between rural and urban has been decreasing over the last decade. In

2011 21% of rural residents and 38% of urban residents owned a smartphone [Per19]. The difference
has decreased from 17 percentage points in 2011 to 12 percentage points in 2019.
Research from Pew Research Center shows that in 2015 58% of rural residents in America used social

in the popularity of certain social media platforms in rural versus urban areas in the U.S. Among the

A study conducted in 2007 using 4000 American MySpace users, investigated the difference in how

Rural users set their profiles to private more often than urban users and also the study showed that
females accounted for a larger portion of rural users than urban users.
2.4.2
Small case study
The internet has the capability of connecting otherwise remote locations to the rest of the world. A
small study conducted in the years 2005-2007 [CW10] attempted to portray the difference the arrival of
high-speed internet had in an isolated town with around 2300 inhabitants. Residents compared the town
to a small island as the nearest town is 200 Km away. The study was conducted in two rounds: the first
administered before high-speed internet was widely available in the town and the second consisting of
surveys and focus groups conducted in the first years after the arrival of high-speed internet. The study
showed that, even after the arrival of the internet, the landline phones and in-person exchanges were still
the most used form of communication within the town. Some participants reported that the internet
had a negative impact on their family time, which is something other studies have shown too [Mes06].
However, the use of online social interaction showed to be positively correlated with participation and
interaction within the local community, challenging the myth that people who are socialising on the
internet are less social in real life. Other research has also contradicted this myth [WW10]. The biggest
advantage users reported was the ability to keep in touch with friends and family that lived outside
the community as well as communicating and interacting with distant institutions such as banks and
medical institutions. This made life in the isolated town much easier and made the residents feel less
isolated. Only a few years after its arrival more than 80% of residents reported that they would miss
the internet if it became unavailable [CW10].
2.5
Summary
The smartphone is making its way into increasingly many aspects of our lives and the number of
smartphone owners is steadily increasing all around the world [ODe20]. In recent years, researchers
have been trying to understand how people use smartphones and what the effects are. Studies have
shown that there is a large diversity in how much people use their phones and for what purpose [Fal+10;
Zha+16; And+16; Van+15]. Young people have significantly higher and more diverse usage than older
people who use smartphones in a more traditional manner [De +19; Wei08; And+16]. Females tend to
spend more time on their phones and especially use it for social media and communication, while males
use smartphones more for games and entertainment [And+16; Wei08; Van+15].
Researchers are trying to understand the effects of smartphone usage and the use of social media
which is a big part of smartphone usage [Ort19]. Studies have linked smartphone and social media
usage to depression, anxiety, low quality of sleep and low self-esteem [DAA15; RST17; CG10], while

CHAPTER 3
Data
This study is based on data collected using a tracking app installed on users’ phones. The data spans over
a period of almost 4 years, from 1’st of October 2015 to 1’st of June 2019 and contains a total number
of 2,8 million users. The data used in this study consists of the following four datasets: User data,
application data, application metadata and home location data. This chapter contains a description of
the datasets and of the data preprocessing. The first part gives an overview of the unfiltered data. This
is followed by a description of the data cleaning and preprocessing. Finally there is a description of the
cleaned data which is used throughout this study.
3.1
User data
The user data contains information on gender, age, weight, height, home country of the users and
urbanisation index of home location. The gender, age, weight and height are specified by the user when
they start using the tracking app. The home country is determined based on the location data of the
users. The location data is a separate dataset that contains the location history of the user. If the
location data contains multiple countries for a user, the home country is determined as the country in
which the user has spent the most time in the span of the dataset. The urbanisation level of the user is
determined from the home location combined with urbanisation data from the European Commission
Joint Research Centre [Pes+19].

tion level for each cell based on population data and built-up area data obtained from Landsat images.

3.4
Privacy restrictions
There are privacy restrictions regarding the access to the data and therefore the access to the sensitive
part of the raw data is restricted to only a few people.
Sensitive data is data that can be used to identify users in real life. An example of sensitive data is
the birthday of users. Another example is the location data since it contains information on the location
of users at specific points in time. The raw application data which contains information on when users
use specific apps and for how long is also considered sensitive. The application data is sensitive because
with some effort it might be possible to match this data with public data from apps such as Facebook
or Twitter and possibly identify users.
All the data, acquired for this study, has been anonymised. The application data has been aggregated
by day in order to get rid of information on the specific time apps were used. This ought to make it
impossible to identify users based on the application data. The aggregation and preprocessing will be
explained in section 3.5.
3.5
Data preprocessing
The data has gone through two levels of preprocessing. The raw data has been cleaned and anonymised
before I was granted access to it, and thereafter I did some data cleaning myself. This section describes
the previous preprocessing and then my own preprocessing.
3.5.1
Previous preprocessing of app data
The preprocessing that was done before I acquired the data was done by previous students and re-
searchers. The data was cleaned by identifying and removing background apps, removing users with
too little data coverage and removing sensitive information. All sensitive apps were removed. Sensitive
apps are for example dating apps, religious apps, political apps etc.
First, users that had neither birth year nor gender information were removed. Also any user that
had less than seven days of data recorded was removed.
Then background apps were removed. Background apps are apps that run in the background and are
incorrectly registered as app usage. It is not trivial to identify these apps and previous students decided
to remove all single sessions with duration over 4 hours and all apps that had an average session length
of over 6 hours. On top of this, a white-list was created for apps categorised as ”other” by manually
going through the top 250 apps in this category. All apps not on the white-list were removed.
Sensitive apps were identified by manually going through the top 50 apps in each category and each
genre (see section 3.2.1 for the difference between genre and category). This means that only the top
50 apps in each category and each genre are actually included in the app data. This was done in order
to not have to go through all apps as there were over a 100,000 different apps. Taking only the top 50
in each category and genre accounted for around 80% of all app usage.
After having removed the sensitive apps, the data was aggregated by day and all the app sessions
put into a list along with the total duration and the number of sessions for each app that day.

The home location data contains information on the home location of users for each day and thus
information on when users move. In order to find valid moves, the data has gone through some filtering.
By exploring the data, it became clear that not all changes of the home location were likely to be real
moves. The dataset contains 3 million users in total and in the data, home locations are changed 4.3
million times i.e. there are 4.3 moves in total. Figure 3.3 shows the distribution of the average number
of days between moves for users. It can be seen that the median is around 25 which means that half
the users have 25 or fewer days on average between changing home location. This strongly indicates
that not all moves are real.

Part of the reason for the large number of moves detected might be that the home location is
calculated as the top location in a window of 30 days. This means that if for example, a user goes
on a long holiday it will be detected as a move. In an attempt to filter out invalid move, only moves
where the home location was stable for three months before and three months after the move were
considered. The three months threshold was chosen as it seemed reasonable to assume that if a person
stayed somewhere for three months it is considered a move. However, it must be noted that when doing
this filtering, many of the real moves will be filtered out too because of a ”false” move that is detected
shortly before or shortly after the real one. Another possibly better option would be to compute the
home location over a larger window of maybe three months instead of just one or in some other way be
less sensitive to small changes that are not real moves. However, there is not much that can be done
about this at this stage as the underlying location data is not accessible.
Additionally, to filtering out ”false” moves, users also need to have a sufficient coverage of app data
around the move. The period of three months before and after a move was chosen as the focus for this
part of the analysis as this should be long enough to start seeing some patterns if any are present. Ideally,
we would use a longer period but the amount of available data drops significantly when considering a
longer period. In order to get stable results, users should have a certain amount of coverage for the six
months considered. The threshold of 80% was selected and this gives in total around 3000 movers from
urban to rural and 2800 moves from rural to urban. Figure 3.4 shows the number of users when using
different thresholds for coverage.
As the home location is computed using a window of 30 days the change of home location is actually
detected 15 days after the move. For this reason, the moving day is shifted to 15 days prior to the one
detected in the data.

CHAPTER 4
Methods
This chapter gives an overview and a discussion on the tools and methods used in this study. The first
section focuses on the tools used, the second describes some of the statistical measures used and the
last section describes and discusses the procedures used in random forest classification.
4.1
Tools
The main tools used for the data processing were Python, IPython, Jupyter Notebooks and PySpark.
The following sections provide an overview of the tools used and motivation for using them.
4.1.1
Data storage
The data is stored on a server located at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). For privacy
and security reasons data is not allowed to be transferred from the server and for this reason, all data
processing must be carried out on the server. The server can only be accessed via the DTU Compute
network which in turn can be accessed remotely using a VPN connection. To bring files in and out of
the system, an FTP-client must be used. To access the DTU Compute network I have used the Cisco
AnyConnect client [Cis14] and to transfer files in and out of the system I have used WinSCP [Win20].
4.1.2
Python and IPython
Python is a high-level, general-purpose programming language that was first released in 1991. It is a
powerful programming language whilst still being easy to use and is thus a popular choice among data

4.1.3
PySpark
Spark was developed by a group of computer scientists at the University of California with the goal
of creating a unified engine for processing distributed data. It was first released in 2010 and has since
==PAGE:37==

been updated regularly. The Python implementation of Spark is accessed through the library called
PySpark [Zah+16].
The most fundamental data structure in Spark is called Resilient Distributed Datasets (RDDs)
which are fault-tolerant collections of objects that may be distributed across multiple clusters and can
be manipulated in parallel. RDDs are evaluated lazily which means that computations are not carried
out until it is needed. This means that transformations just return an object representing the result,
but computations are not carried out until an operation is requested that returns a non-RDD object.
When such an operation is requested Spark creates and then carries out an optimised execution plan.
RDDs can contain complex objects and this makes optimisation hard. In 2015 Spark was extended
with a new data structure called DataFrames which unlike RDDs are organised into named and typed
columns and are thus more similar to tables in relational databases. This new data structure allows
optimisations that were not possible using RDDs and thus gives a performance boost [Zah+16].
The DataFrame data structure is the primary data structure used in this study. Most of the data is
stored in Avro format which is a data serialisation system developed by Apache. Avro provides compact
and efficient data structures which rely on JSON schemas [Apa20]. Avro files work well together with
Spark DataFrames and DataFrames are easy and efficient to work with therefore this is the main data
structure used throughout the data processing.

4.2.1
Central tendency indicator
The central tendency indicator of a distribution is a way of indicating the centre or the central position
of the data. Probably the most used central tendency indicator is the mean or average. However, the
mean is very sensitive to outliers and is not the best option when the data is skewed because outliers
and skewed data drag the mean away from the typical values. The median is the middle value of the
distribution and is therefore not affected in the same way by outliers and skewed data.
Looking at our data it is obvious that it is not normally distributed and therefore the median is
used as the central tendency indicator. Plots illustrating the distribution of the daily screen time for
four random example users and the distribution of median screen time for a small subset of users can
be seen in appendix C.
4.2.2
Estimating standard error of the median
The standard error of the median is used in order to evaluate and give an insight into the accuracy
of the median. The standard error of the median is estimated using the bootstrap method which was
introduced in the late 1970s by Brad Efron [ET94a].
The idea is to mimic taking multiple samples from the population in order to calculate the standard
deviation of the median. Since we do not have access to the actual population, only to our own sample
we re-sample from this using random sampling with replacement to mimic creating multiple random
samples from the population itself. Assuming that the distribution of our data resembles that of the
overall distribution, the distribution of the re-sampled data will have the same statistical attributes as
if it was sampled from the population. Hence calculating the error of the median from the bootstrap
samples will give an estimate of the true error of the median [ET94a]. From the distribution of size n
the procedure is as follows:
1. Randomly sample with replacement n datapoints from the distribution. This is called a bootstrap
sample.
2. Calculate the median of the bootstrap sample
==PAGE:38==
4.2 Statistics
27
3. Repeat steps 1 and 2 B times and then calculate the standard deviation of the B medians. This
is the bootstrap estimate of the standard error the median.
When selecting the number of bootstrap samples, B, it is a question of computational power versus
accuracy. Efron himself mentions that 100 is a good choice and numbers higher than 200 are seldom
required [ET94b]. Based on this B = 100 was selected for estimating the standard error of the median
throughout the analysis.

4.2.4
Mann–Whitney U test
The Mann-Whitney U test is a statistical test used to determine the statistical difference between two
distribution. The Mann-Whitney U test is a non-parametric statistical test which means that it assumes
nothing about the shape of the underlying distribution. However, the two samples must be independent.
The null-hypothesis states that the two distributions are drawn from the same population and thus low
p-values reject the null-hypothesis and state that there is a significant statistical difference between the
two distributions [08b].
4.2.5
Kolmogorov–Smirnov test
As the Mann-Whitney U test, two-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov test (KS-test) is a test used to determine
the statistical difference between two distributions. It is a non-parametric statistical test which means
that it assumes nothing about the shape of the underlying distribution only that it is continuous. The
statistical value used by the KS-test is the maximum distance between the two distributions. The null-
hypothesis is that the samples are drawn from the same distribution and thus low p-values reject the
null-hypothesis and state that there is a significant statistical difference between the two distributions
[Mas51]. The KS-test give a signed output to indicate which distribution contains higher values.
Kolmogorov-Smirnov test considers the whole distribution while the Mann-Whitney test is based on
ranking the observations and therefore focuses on the difference between the means [08a]. The KS-test
is used for all continues distributions in this study while the Mann-Whitney U test is only used in the
few cases when the distributions are discrete.
4.3
Random Forest Classification
Random forest classifier is a classifier constructed using an ensemble of multiple decision trees and using
the majority vote as the final output. Each decision tree is constructed using a bootstrap sample (i.e.
random sample with replacement) from the original training data and each tree is grown using random
feature selection. This means that not all features are considered at every split. Using different training
data and random feature selection at each split prevents the trees from becoming too correlated and
thus increases the overall accuracy and reduces overfitting [Bre01].
==PAGE:40==
4.3 Random Forest Classification
29
Even though random forest generally works well without any parameter tuning there are different
parameters that can be investigated. The most relevant parameters to tune are normally the number
of trees used in the forest, the number of random features to consider at each split and the tree depth
[GPT10; BS16].
There are multiple procedures one can use when trying to find the best parameters. One common
strategy is grid search where each combination of the different parameters is evaluated and the best
combination is chosen. When there are multiple parameters to tune and the number of possible values
for each parameter is large, the search space soon becomes very large and the grid search therefore
quite time-consuming. Another approach is random search where instead of trying every combination
of the different values, a number of random combinations are tried out and evaluated. This approach
often yields just as good or even better results than grid search [BB12].
When tuning the parameters one can either use a constant train-test split, or one can use k-fold
cross-validation. When using a constant train-test split the model is repeatedly trained on the same data
and then tested with a constant test data, also called validation set. When building machine learning
models it is important to keep the training data and the final test data separate. When evaluating the
final model it should be tested on an unseen dataset, which it has no information about, in order to
fairly evaluate the model. When doing parameter tuning it is therefore crucial to only use the training
data, which can be split into training and validation.
When cross-validation is used in parameter tuning the whole training data can be used both for
training and for validating. In k-fold cross-validation the data is randomly split into k subsamples and
the model is repeatedly trained a total of k times each time with one of the subsamples left out for
testing. The results are then combined to give a final score of the model’s performance. This ensures
that the whole data is used both for training and testing, but there is never any overlap between the
training and testing data [BG04]. Random search can be used in combination with cross-validation
by cross-validating each randomly chosen combination. This can be computationally demanding, but,
utilising the parallelisation provided by Pyspark, this is feasible even with a large number of random
combinations.
4.3.1
Class imbalance
Class imbalance is something that can have a significant effect on the results from a classifier [JS02;
LWZ08].
When there are relatively few examples of one class the model has a hard time learning
about this class.
Various re-sampling techniques can be applied to improve the class balance such
as oversampling, undersampling and synthetic generation of new samples. Oversampling consists of
randomly re-sampling the smaller class until it has as many samples as the other class, undersampling
consists of randomly removing samples from the larger class and synthetic generation of new samples
consists of generating new samples of the minority class using techniques such as SMOTE [Cha+02].
One drawback of oversampling is that it can lead to overfitting and one drawback of undersampling
is that it discards potentially important samples. Synthetic generation of samples is an attempt to do
oversampling without leading to overfitting [Cha09].

4.3.2.2
Area under the curve
Another evaluation measure is the area under the ROC (receiver operating characteristic) curve. The
ROC curve is created by plotting the true positive rate against the false-positive rate at various different
decision thresholds. The area under the ROC curve (AUC) is a good way of scoring the model as it
rewards true positive while penalising false positives and thus gives low scores to both random guessing
and to one-class-only classification. Also, it is insensitive to class distribution [Faw06; Bra97; JS02].
Another advantage of using AUC compared to the confusion matrix is that it gives a single score which
makes it easy to compare multiple models when doing e.g. parameter tuning. The confusion matrix
can be used as a way to illustrate the model’s performance on the specific classes.

There are multiple ways to measure the popularity of an app. One could look at the median daily
usage per user including all users, however, this would result in a lot of zeros as there are many users
with no usage at all for a specific app. To avoid too many zeros, one could instead look at median usage
when only including users that have some usage for the app, or one could look at median usage among
the users that have a median above zero. One could also simply look at the fraction of users that have
ever used the app or the fraction of users that have a median usage above some threshold.
After some data exploration, the decision fell on plotting the median usage for users excluding those
with a median of zero. To give an insight into how many users use the different apps, the fraction of
users who have a median usage above zero is also plotted. The latter will often be referred to as the
user fraction, as it is the fraction of users who have a significant usage.
In order to take into account days with zero usage for an app, missing days have been replaced by
zeros if there have been any recordings from other apps for that particular user and day. This means
that it is possible to distinguish between missing data and zero usage, at least to some degree. This is
different from screen time where there is no way to know if missing data means zero usage for that day
or a missing day in the dataset.

5.7.6
Comparisons using KS-statistics
A KS-test has been used to compare the distributions for rural and urban users for a number of different
apps as well as categories and genres. We are expecting to see some of the same trends as in the previous
section, but the KS-test compares the distributions directly and not just the medians so there might be
some differences that are not spotted when only working with the median. The null-hypothesis is that
the two samples are drawn from the same distributions i.e. that there is no difference between the usage
in rural and in urban areas. The KS-test outputs two values: the KS-statistic, which is the maximum
vertical distance between the two distributions, and the p-value. The null-hypothesis can be rejected if
the p-value is below some threshold. In my case, I chose to only show apps where the p-value is below
0.001. A low p-value will also confirm that the differences we have seen in this section are significant.
The KS-test has been used to compare the top 150 apps when looking at the number of users. These
have at least 7000 users each.
Figure 5.25 illustrates the result of the KS-test for the top 30 apps sorted by the absolute value of
the KS-statistic. The bars indicate the signed KS-statistic and only apps where the p-value is below
0.001 are shown. Positive values indicate that the values for the urban users are higher and negative
values indicate that the values for rural users are higher. Many of the same apps that were analysed in
the previous section can be seen. The KS-results for all apps with a p-value below 0.001 can be seen in
appendix E.5.
In the previous section LINE, Facebook and WhatsApp were identified as the apps that seemed to
be most different in urban compared to rural areas. When looking at the KS-statistics it can be seen
that LINE is also the app where the KS-statistic has the largest positive magnitude, indicating that the
distribution for urban users does indeed have higher values which corresponds to what was seen when
looking at the medians. Looking at Facebook, it can be seen that the KS-test confirms what we saw in
the previous section.
However, when looking at WhatsApp, the KS-test showed higher values for rural users while the
median usage was higher for urban users. The median usage was calculated after excluding users with
zero median usage while the KS-test is conducted on all users. This might explain the difference. To
investigate the difference between results from KS-test and the median analysis, the median is calculated
for all users and the results show that when including all users the median is higher for rural users, but
when excluding users with a median usage of zero, the median is higher for urban users. The medians for
WhatsApp can be seen in appendix E.6. This shows that there is a difference between rural and urban
users considering WhatsApp, but whether it is rural or urban users that have higher usage depends on
how ”high usage” is defined. When only looking at users with a median above zero, the urban users
show a larger usage, but when including the users with a median of zero, the rural users who have a
higher usage.
The top five apps identified with higher usage for rural users by the KS-test are all social media or
messaging apps while the top apps for urban are a mix of messaging, communication, navigation and
weather forecast apps.

CHAPTER 6
Analysis of users who move
between urbanisation
levels
In order to further investigate the difference between rural and urban users, the study will now continue
with people who move from rural to urban areas and from urban to rural areas. Investigating users who
move from one urbanisation level to the other is a good way to test the legitimacy of the differences
we have seen between rural and urban users. If people change their behaviour when they move, the
differences are not spurious and there is a strong indication that the app usage is actually affected by
the urbanisation level of users.
In order to have something to compare to, a control group has been created for each type of move
i.e. one for rural-urban movers and one for urban-rural movers. The control group has been created by
using one ”fake” mover for each real mover. This has been done the following way:
• For each mover, randomly select another person with the same age, same gender, same home
country and same urbanisation level as the mover had before the move
• Set a fake moving date for the sampled person that is equal to that of the mover
This means that for each person who moved, another person has been sampled who is similar to
this person but who did not move. By creating this control group we have a group of users who are
similar to the movers which we then can compare to. If we see the same changes in the control group
we cannot say that the changes occur because of the move, but if we do not see the same changes in
the control group then there is a strong indication of the changes we see having something to do with
the move.
This part of the study is conducted only on users who move from urban to rural and from rural
to urban areas and this is a small subset of 2709 and 2954 users respectively (and their corresponding
control groups with the same amount of users).
13 weeks before and 13 weeks after the move are
considered.
For each part, first the median usage will be considered and then the relative change.
When calculating the relative change, all users with a median of zero before the move are excluded as
relative change is not defined for them. When investigating specific apps, only users that have used the
app at some point before the move are considered. There are two reasons for excluding users with no
usage. Firstly, it is because too many users with zero usage will skew the data and make it harder to
see any trends for the users that actually use the app. Secondly, it is because it requires an extra effort
for users to first install the app and then start to use it, compared to just increasing or decreasing their
usage. The number of users considered for each app and the number of users considered after excluding
users with a median of zero before the move can be seen in table F.1 in appendix F.
First the overall median screen time for users around the move will be investigated and then I will
look into a selection of specific apps.

experienced by the urban-rural group is more significant.
The usage of Facebook for users who move is more puzzling. In section 5.7 Facebook was identified
as an app that was more used in rural areas, but in this section, we have seen that all groups decrease
their usage and the users moving from urban to rural areas are the ones decreasing their usage the

over the last year. However, it is surprising that users who move from urban to rural areas are the ones

and users moving from rural to urban areas increase their usage. However, the KS-test shows that the
changes are not that different to the changes experienced by the control group.

CHAPTER 7
Random Forest
Classification
In order to better understand what characterises rural and urban users, it was decided to try to fit a
random forest model to the data. The main purpose of this is to explore what features are important
when predicting whether users are urban or rural. Random Forest generally provides good results for a
variety of different problems without much tuning [BS16], which is why this classifier was chosen above
others. The data is split into two sets: a training set and a test set with the ratio 75:25, meaning that
75% of the data is used for training and 25% of the data is used for testing. As described in section 3.6
the users are distributed randomly between 300 buckets of data and the distribution of urban and rural
users is approximately the same in each bucket. Therefore the random split between the two datasets
can be done directly by taking 75% of the buckets for testing and 25% of the buckets for training. This
gives 900,000 users in the training data and 300,000 users in the test data. The features used in the
model have been selected based on the analysis in section 5.7.
In order to have something to compare the results to, a baseline model was also created. The baseline
model is trained on data that does not contain any app data but only the demographic information
about users i.e. birth year, home location (continent) and gender. The purpose of the baseline model
is to see if does improve the score when the app data is added. The app data consists of median usage
for a number of different apps, categories and genres.
This section describes the approach and the results obtained. First, the parameter tuning is described
and then three different models are trained and tested: the baseline model, the full model and a Europe-
only model.
7.1
Parameter tuning
Even though random forest generally performs well without much tuning [BS16] it was decided to try
some parameter tuning in order to get the best performance. One important aspect of our dataset is
the imbalance between the amount of rural and urban users. Only 12% of the users are rural users
while 70% of users are urban users. The rest are suburban users. As discussed in section 4.3 there are
multiple ways to handle class-imbalance. As the amount of data is quite large and to keep the data
as pure as possible it was decided to use undersampling in order to create more balance between the
classes.
Parameter tuning has been implemented using random search and k-fold cross-validation.
The
parameter tuning is carried out on a random sample of 1/15 of the training data in order to save
computation time. The features investigated are:
• Downsample ratio.

downsampled, not the validation data.
• Number of trees. The number of trees in the forest.
• Maximum depth. Maximum depth of the trees.

Summary
Three different models are trained and tested on the data. The first was a baseline model that did not
use any app data, the second was a full model using the full data and the last one was a model using
only European users. The full model performed slightly better than the baseline model, which shows
that the app data does bring some useful information for the classifier. The feature importance showed,
however, that the most important feature by far was whether the user lived in Europe or not. This

than in the full model. This makes sense since we have seen that Facebook is more popular and there
is a bigger difference between rural and urban usage of Facebook in Europe than in Asia, where most
of the users are from in the full model.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
This chapter contains a summary of and discussion on the main findings in this study. The first three
sections discuss the initial analysis of screen time and different apps, the analysis of users who move
and the random forest classification. Following this, is a reflective section discussing possible reasons for
the difference in screen time for urban and rural users. The chapter is concluded with a short section
listing the main limitations of this study.
8.1
Discussion of characterisation of urban-rural differences
This study has shown that there is a difference between the smartphone usage of rural and urban users.
Urban users generally have higher screen time than rural users. Although there is not much literature
on smartphone usage in rural compared to urban areas, this is not unexpected since previous research
has stated that rural people use the internet less than urban people [Hin00] and that the smartphone
adoption is higher in urban areas than in rural areas [Cen17].
Looking at some of the other factors investigated, the results comply with what has been found
in the literature.
We have seen that females have higher screen time than males and that screen
time decreases as age increases, which corresponds to what has been found by other studies [And+16;
Van+15]. Furthermore, we have seen that the continent with the highest median screen time is South
America and the continent with the lowest median screen time is Europe.
When stratifying by gender and by age group and looking at the difference between rural and urban
users, we have seen that the urban users consistently have higher screen time across genders and across
age groups. When looking at the continents separately, urban users also have higher screen time on
every continent, but the difference is much larger for North America than any other continent. The
fact that urban users have higher median screen time across different grouping factors shows that the
difference does not simply come from the different population demographics in urban compared to rural
areas.
8.1.1
Selected apps
The analysis on specific apps was focused on seven popular apps: Snapchat, Instagram, LINE, YouTube,
WhatsApp, Chrome and Facebook. The analysis showed that there is a difference in what apps are
used in rural and in urban areas and this was confirmed with KS-tests. Facebook and Snapchat are
identified as apps that are more popular in rural areas and LINE and WhatsApp are identified as apps
that are more popular in urban areas. Chrome and YouTube have a slightly higher usage in urban than
in rural areas, although the difference is not large for these apps.
When looking into the usage of the selected apps by gender, age and continent there are some
differences between the groups. Females have higher usage for all the social apps and the only app
where males have higher usage was YouTube. Previous studies have shown that females tend to use
more time on social and communication apps and males spend more time on entertainment and games
[And+16; Van+15] which corresponds very well with these findings. Age also has an impact on the apps
used. The youngest users have the highest usage in WhatsApp while the young adults and middle-aged
adults use both Facebook and WhatsApp. The fact that Facebook is not the most popular social media
app among the youngest users corresponds to previous studies done on Americans [PA19]. Among the
==PAGE:83==
8.2 Discussion of analysis of users who move between urbanisation levels
72
older adults, LINE is the most popular app and this probably comes from the fact that LINE is very
popular in Asia and Asia has a larger fraction of older users than the other continents. Looking at the
different continents there are some interesting findings. In South America, almost 100% of users have a
median usage above zero for WhatsApp and the median daily usage for these users is over 50 minutes.
The reason for the great popularity of WhatsApp in South America probably comes from the fact that
the cost of mobile texting in South America has been very high compared to other parts of the world
and WhatsApp offers a way to text for free using the internet [Koc19]. There is generally a difference
in what continents the apps are most popular in. Snapchat is most used in Australia, Instagram is
most used in South America, LINE is almost only used in Asia, Chrome is most used in Australia and
Facebook is most used in Africa.
When looking at the rural-urban differences regarding these selected apps, there is also a difference
between the continents. Facebook is more popular in rural areas in Asia, Australia and Europe, but
more popular in urban areas in North America and Africa. The fact that Facebook is more popular in
urban areas than in rural areas in North America corresponds to research on social media usage in the
U.S. where Facebook is said to be more used in rural areas [PA19]. Since the majority of users in this
dataset are from Asia and Europe, Facebook will come out as an app more used in rural areas in this
study. However, this does indicate that it would be relevant to study the continents separately. When
looking at the rural-urban differences within age groups, the trends are quite consistent across all age
groups. The only real difference is in WhatsApp where the user fraction is higher in urban areas for
the two youngest age groups while the user fraction is higher in rural areas for the two older age groups.
The median usage is higher in urban areas for all age groups, however. Chrome and LINE are more
used in urban areas across all age groups, while Facebook is more used in rural areas across all age
groups.
The KS-test further confirmed that there is a difference between app usage in rural and in urban areas.
The KS-test has also been applied to categories and genres, and the results indicate that communication
apps, news apps and navigation apps are more used in urban areas, and social and entertainment apps
are more used in rural areas. Using terms from uses and gratification theory [JS13; PR00], this suggests
that urban users use the smartphone more for instrumental usage i.e. goal-oriented usage and rural
users use it more passively i.e. for diversion and filling free time.
8.2
Discussion of analysis of users who move between
urbanisation levels
After analysing the screen time and app usage for different apps, there is a strong indication of a
difference between urban and rural users. However, the reasons for these differences are unclear. The
demographic factors: gender, age and continent have been investigated in order to determine whether
these were cofounding factors for the urban-rural differences, but it showed that the differences persisted
across different demographic groups.
Investigating the smartphone usage of people who move between urbanisation levels is a method to
test the effects of the urbanisation level on smartphone usage. By comparing the app usage before and
after the move all other factors are kept constant while it is possible to investigate what happens to
the app usage when the urbanisation level changes. One additional factor that is changing and could
influence the smartphone usage, is time. In order to compensate for this, the results are compared to a
control group where the users are similar to the movers and experiences the same time change but do
not move.
The median screen time for users moving from urban to rural areas does decrease after the move. The
difference between the median before and after the move is not large, but when looking at the relative
change on a user level, there is a significant difference compared to the control group. The relative
change is 2.6 ± 0.7% for users who move from urban to rural areas. The control group experiences
a small increase of 0.4 ± 0.5% and a KS-test shows that there is a significant difference between the
movers and the control group, which does indicate that something happens when people move from
==PAGE:84==
8.3 Discussion of random forest
73
urban areas to rural areas. This does support the previous results that showed that the screen time
was generally higher for urban users than for rural users. However, the screen time does not change as
much when users move from rural to urban areas.
The usage of Chrome, Facebook and WhatsApp is analysed for the users who move. Chrome has
been identified as an app that is slightly more used by urban users than rural users. The analysis of
the users moving does correspond to this. Users moving from urban to rural areas decrease their usage
and the KS-test shows that the difference is significant compared to that of the control group. For the
users moving from rural to urban areas, the change is not significant compared to the control group.
WhatsApp also gives results that correspond to what we expected to some degree, but the KS-test
shows that the changes experienced by the movers are not that different from the changes of the control
group.
For Facebook, the results are somewhat conflicting with the results from my previous analysis. The
movers, as well as the control groups, decrease their usage of Facebook. This might simply be explained
by the fact that the popularity of Facebook is decreasing which other studies have pointed out [AJ+18].
However, the fact that the urban-rural movers decrease their usage the most and there is a significant
difference compared to the control group is surprising since I have previously identified Facebook as
an app that is more used in rural areas than in urban areas. There are numerous possible reasons for
this. One possible explanation is the overall decrease in screen time for users who move from urban to
rural areas. The urban-rural movers decrease their screen time more than the control group and thus
the decrease in Facebook usage could simply be a part of this. This requires further analysis. Another
possible hypothesis is based on the fact that research has shown that a common reason for people
to move to rural areas is that they are originally from there and want to move back [HC95; BH12].
Research has also shown that one of the main motivations for using Facebook is to keep in touch with
friends and family [Joi08]. This could be a possible explanation for the decrease in usage of Facebook as
people move closer to friends and family and therefore lose some of the motivation for using Facebook.
The number of users used in the move analysis is quite limited (∼2800 for each type) and there
are therefore not many apps with sufficient amount of data to give a meaningful analysis. It would be
relevant to look into categories and genres as there would be more data for specific genres and categories
than for specific apps.
8.3
Discussion of random forest
The purpose of the random forest classifier is to determine whether it is possible to classify users as
urban or rural based on their phone usage. Additionally, and maybe more importantly, the motivation
behind this is also to get an insight into which features are important when doing this classification.
First, two models were created: one baseline model using no app data and one full model. This was
done in order to see if the app data had any significance when classifying users. The results showed that
the full model got a slightly higher AUC score than the baseline model. When looking into the feature
importance it became clear, that the question of whether the user lived in Europe or not, was the most
important deciding factor. Because of this, another model was trained using only European users. This
model did not perform as well as the full model, but it did manage to classify around 60% of the urban
users correctly and 60% of the rural users correctly. The feature importance of the European model
showed that the most important factor was the category ”Travel and Local” followed by the usage of
WhatsApp and Facebook. This does correspond with my previous findings where the genre ”Travel &
Local” and the app WhatsApp were more used by urban users and Facebook was identified as an app
more used by rural users.
8.4
Reflection
This study has demonstrated that there is a difference between the smartphone usage of rural and urban
users. Especially that rural people seem to spend less time on their phones than urban people. There are
==PAGE:85==
8.5 Limitations of this study
74
several possible reasons for this. The simplest one is that most of the functionalities of smartphones rely
on high-speed internet which is not as common in rural areas as in urban areas [FK07; Str01; HQC14;
Per19]. Another explanation could be found in the different lifestyles in rural and urban areas. In urban
areas, a large fraction of jobs are administrative and office-based, while in urban areas a larger share of
employment is in areas such as agriculture, construction and manufacturing [Owe+13]. Smartphones
can be an integrated part of administrative jobs as they are used for emails and communication, while
smartphones are not typically associated with jobs in agriculture, construction or manufacturing. This
could be a reason that urban people spend more time on their phones than rural people do.
The
smartphone might simply be an integrated part of their daily life to a larger extent than for rural
people. This hypothesis also corresponds to some of the findings when looking at specific apps and
categories where it seemed like urban users used the smartphone more for goal-oriented usage and rural
users used for diversion and filling free time.
A third hypothesis is that rural people are more relaxed and less stressed and therefore are not as
addicted to their smartphones as urban people. Studies have found that people in rural areas experience
a greater life-satisfaction than people in urban areas [KG10; Sør14] and that being in nature reduces
stress [Har+03]. Research on smartphone usage has revealed that, to some extent, smartphone usage
is characterised by addictive behaviour [Oul+12; Lop+14; Par05] and stress has been known to induce
addictive behaviour [Sin08]. Based on this there is reason to think that urban people may be more
addicted to their smartphones than rural people.
8.5
Limitations of this study
The age and gender of users are added by the users themselves at the time of sign up and there is no
guarantee that this information is correct. The fact that the default gender is male and there are so
many males in this dataset compared to females indicates that a lot of users do not change the default
values. Furthermore, previous preprocessing has removed the birthday of a lot of users with default
birthdays which indicates that a lot of users did not change the birthday either. We are assuming
though that most users do provide accurate information.
The home location and thereby the urbanisation level of users is not provided by the users themselves
but is computed from the location data of the user. In the user data (not to be confused with the home
location data) each user is given a single home location and if a user has multiple homes during the
span of the dataset, the home location is simply determined as the location where the user has spent
the most time. This means that the home location and thereby the urbanisation level of users might
not always be accurate.
The home location data has similar issues where the home location might not always be accurate.
The home location is computed as the top location in a window of 30 days and after inspecting the data
it became clear that all moves detected could not be true moves. There was no trivial way of knowing
which moves were real and which were not, and the moves were simply filtered by the criteria that the
home location must be stable for 3 months before and 3 months after the move. It is very likely that
some of the real moves are being filtered out because they happen close to a false move. The number
of users in the cleaned moves data is therefore quite limited.
The biggest strength of this study is the huge amount of data available and the fact that app usage
is registered directly from users’ phones, compared to many other studies that rely on surveys and self
reported usage.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The number of smartphone users is growing rapidly all around the world [ODe20] and people are
becoming increasingly dependant on their phones [Ma15; Elm14; New15]. Studies have revealed a large
variety in smartphone usage for different demographic groups. Females tend to use smartphones for
social apps while males tend to use them for entertainment and games [And+16; Wei08; Van+15].
Young people have much higher and more varied usage than older people who used it more like a
traditional phone [And+16; De +19; Wei08].
A few small American studies have revealed that there are more smartphone owners in urban areas
than in rural areas in the U.S [Per15], that there is a difference in the popularity of social media
platforms in rural and in urban areas [PA19] and that there is a difference in how social media is used
by urban and by rural users [GKS08]. The research on the difference between smartphone usage for
rural compared to urban residents is, however, limited. This study is an attempt to fill this gap and
shed light on what characterises these two types of users.
The dataset used in this study contains over a million users and spans over two and a half years.
The analysis has revealed that there is a difference between the smartphone usage of rural compared to
urban users. Urban users have a higher usage across genders, age groups and continents. The study has
also revealed a difference between the apps used in rural and in urban areas. Urban users tend to use
communication, navigation and news app more while rural users tend to use social and entertainment
apps more. Analysing users who move between the urbanisation levels has revealed that users that
move from urban to rural areas do decrease their screen time after the move. This supports the claim
that rural people have a lower screen time than urban people.
There are numerous possible reasons for rural people having a lower usage of smartphones. The
simplest one is that rural areas generally lack good internet connections [FK07; Str01; HQC14; Per19]
which is necessary for many of the functionalities of smartphones. Other possible explanations can be
found in the different lifestyles in rural and urban areas. Urban people have different types of jobs than
rural people [Owe+13] which could influence the tendency to use smartphones for work-related matters.
Research has also shown that people in rural areas experience greater life satisfaction and less stress
than urban people [KG10; Sør14; Har+03], which could reduce smartphone addiction in rural areas.
9.1
Future work
There are multiple possible directions for future work. The analysis on users who move gave some
promising results, but there were also conflicting results. This area is an obvious choice to continue
work on. The biggest weakness of that analysis is the limited number of users with valid moves. This
could possibly be improved using a different method to detect moves. One possibility is to use a larger
window than 30 days to determine the home location of a user. It would also be interesting go further
with the investigation of different app categories opposed to focusing on specific apps, as the analysis
has revealed that there seems to be a difference in what types of apps are used in rural and in urban
areas. It would also be relevant to study the correlation between population density and app usage.
This study has revealed differences between urban and rural users but the reasons behind these dif-
ferences are unclear. A number of hypotheses have been presented and there are numerous possibilities
for further research that could shed a light on the reasons behind the differences.
