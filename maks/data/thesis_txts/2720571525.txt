II 
 
Executive summary 
Many industrial bioprocesses are carried out as multi-phase gas-liquid systems in stirred tank 
reactors. Commonly a sparger is applied at the bottom of the reactor, providing gas to the system, 
which is further distributed into the liquid by agitation, ensuring proper dispersion and mixing. This 
accounts for many growing chemical industries, especially insulin, polymers, and antibiotics. The 
bioindustry often works with mammalian cells, microorganisms, or oxygen-dependent biocatalysts, 
which all need oxygen, making this a critical growth factor.  
An organism's oxygen demand between the two phases is determined as the oxygen mass 
transfer, which many process parameters can impact, for example, mixing, pressure, airflow, and 
viscosity. In many fermentation processes, viscosity increases as a microbial growth factor, making 
it more viscous. This viscosity change can impact oxygen transfer between the two phases, affecting 
the produced biomass and product quality.   
This project investigates the changing viscosity effect on volumetric mass transfer coefficient 
(KLa) and gas bubble size distribution by simulating the viscosity of a non-Newtonian liquid using 
carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC). The study will focus on the gas bubble size distribution in a 200L 
bioreactor with a shadowgraphic endoscope. To investigate the interfacial area, dividing KLa into 
liquid film mass transfer coefficient (KL) and specific gas-liquid interfacial area per liquid volume 
(a), determining which parameter is most affected by the viscosity. Adding salt will create a non-
coalescing liquid, preventing the gas bubbles from colliding and impacting the gas bubble size 
distribution. The experiments will be conducted with two agitation speeds, 250 RPM and 400 RPM, 
to gather information on the agitation's impact on gas bubble size and KLa. Additionally, four CMC 
solutions at 0.1%, 0.25%, 0.5%, and 0.75% will be used to simulate the changing viscosity during 
fermentation. 
The overall conclusion from the project is that the enzyme glucose oxidase (GOx), under these 
process conditions, has a two-stage deactivation rate during the lifetime of 7 hours. Within the first 
approximately 8.5 minutes, the enzyme deactivates with a rate constant of 0.84 min-1, whereafter, the 
enzyme deactivation rate changes to 0.042 min-1. Additionally, it can be concluded that between the 
gassing out method and the hydrogen peroxide, the most suitable under these conditions is the gassing 
out method. Which also provides less spill of raw materials and less harm to the people working.  
Determined in the project with increasing viscosities has shown that viscosity significantly 
affects the volumetric mass transfer coefficient. The most significant impact is seen on the liquid film 
mass transfer coefficient compared to the interfacial area. A reasonable assumption of this is because 
of the change in liquid film during increasing viscosities. Furthermore, the gas bubble size distribution 
is decreased under viscous conditions, which may be related to applying salt and non-Newtonian 
liquid. The experiments were conducted under two agitations speed, where it can be concluded that 
the change in agitation speed under increasing viscosity showed an insignificant impact on liquid 
mass transfer but mainly affected the gas bubble size.

Chapter 1 
1. Introduction  
Production with mammalian cells and microorganisms is a growing industry, among others, 
for producing antibiotics, polymers, and insulin. Most productions are carried out in multi-phase gas-
liquid stirred tank reactors. When producing products aerobically with mammalian cells and 
microorganisms, it is essential to focus on the needed nutrients and substrate to optimize cell- and 
energy production. One of the main components, but also the most critical parameter within microbial 
growth, is oxygen supply (P. M. Doran, 1995a). Oxygen is most commonly introduced into the reactor 
from the bottom by a sparger and distributed in the media by agitation, forming a uniform distribution 
and mixing.  
As the fermentation process progresses, it has been found that the viscosity increases due to 
microbial growth, creating problems for the mixing and distributing oxygen to the cells by impacting 
the gas bubbles' size (Schuegerl, 1981). Therefore, to obtain optimum productivity and product 
formation, it is crucial to understand the parameters affecting the oxygen transfer rate, such as mixing, 
vessel design, and fluid properties.  
The process impact on the volumetric mass transfer coefficient, KLa, is a widespread problem 
in daily production and during upscale from laboratory to production scale. Much research has been 
done on the overall KLa, but breaking it into the mass transfer liquid film coefficient, KL, and the 
interfacial area were the mass transfer occurs, a. It is found that parameter a is most often an unknown 
factor due to the impact of the unknown size of the dispersed gas bubbles. Determining the gas 
bubbles' size is challenging because the process and liquid parameters impact the size during the 
process. 
The interest in this project comes from the importance of oxygen supply for aerobic 
fermentation processes. Due to increased cell concentration, fermentation broth becomes more 
viscous over time. This change in viscosity has an effect on both parameters and process conditions, 
which can change microbial behavior and product formation. 
This challenge becomes a problem for production because a suitable quantity of cells is 
required to obtain the desired biomass growth for the product. Therefore, understanding the changing 
viscosity impact on gas bubbles' size in the overall mass transfer is essential to research to improve 
microbial growth and product quality. 
The experimental work to understand this viscosity change impact on oxygen mass transfer 
and gas bubble size distribution will be investigated in a 200L bioreactor using glucose oxidase with 
carboxymethyl cellulose to simulate a change in viscosity. Determine the gas bubble size with 
innovative technology. Capturing pictures of the gas bubbles and assessing the size and shape inside 
the reactor (Dynamics, n.d.-b).

1.1 Scope  
One of the main issues for oxygen transfer is that aerobic microbial growth has been shown 
to increase viscosity, hindering mass transfer. The volumetric mass transfer coefficient, KLa, is an 
essential factor for process optimization due to the critical element of aerobic microbial growth. The 
gas bubble size distribution and gas holdup affect the interfacial area, which can lead to a lack of 
oxygen. The project aims to examine the apparent viscosity in enzymatic processes in a bioreactor.  
The proposed project will analyze the impact of gas bubble size distribution on the oxygen 
mass transfer due to changes in apparent viscosity simulated with carboxymethyl cellulose. 
Furthermore, the apparent viscosity impact on the specific KL and a will be examined to determine 
which specific parameter is mainly affected by apparent viscosity changes.  
To investigate the apparent viscosity changes' impact on the gas bubble size distribution and 
overall oxygen transfer, experiments will be executed in a 200L bioreactor, with an endoscopy 
technique, to obtain images of the gas bubbles and analyze their size distribution. Furthermore, an 
investigation of oxygen concentration at different heights in the bioreactor will be obtained to 
investigate the apparent viscosity effect on oxygen distribution.

2. Bioprocess theory  
2.1 Fermentation 
The industrial perspective on fermentation is mainly divided into five commercial production 
groups: production of biomass, enzymes, microbial metabolites, recombinant product, and 
modification of a compound (transformation process) (Stanbury, Whitaker, & Hall, 2017b). Most 
fermentations are operated with oxygen-dependent microorganisms and mammalian cells, making 
oxygen a limiting factor for the process.  
The oxygen demand for an industrial fermentation process is mainly satisfied by aerating and 
agitating the broth. An overall stoichiometric equation of glucose oxidation may be represented 
(Stanbury, Whitaker, & Hall, 2017a): 
𝐶6𝐻12𝑂6 + 6𝑂2 ↔6𝐻2𝑂+ 6𝐶𝑂2 
(1) 
 
Some of the carbon is converted into biomass and product, meaning the stoichiometric 
equation does not apply to the microorganism's oxygen demand. Hence the oxygen demand very 
much depends on the source of carbon. It is inadequate to base the provision of oxygen for 
fermentation simply on an estimation of overall demand due to the culture's metabolism being 
affected by the concentration of dissolved oxygen (DO) in the broth. The effect of DO concentration 
on the specific oxygen uptake rate is of the Michaelis–Menten type, meaning the reaction rate depends 
on the substrate concentration. The specific oxygen uptake rate (OUR) increases with an increase in 
DO concentration up to a certain point referred to as the critical DO concentration, above which no 
further increase in OUR occurs. If the DO concentration were to fall below the critical level, then the 
metabolism of the cells may be metabolically disturbed. (Stanbury et al., 2017a) 
2.2 Gas-liquid mass transfer  
Many bioprocesses are multi-phase, commonly for aerobic fermentation is gas-liquid, 
meaning a mass transfer occurs between different phases. In the case of aerobic bioprocess, the 
microorganisms require a continuous transfer of oxygen to obtain the wanted biomass production. 
The air supply is mostly sparged from the bottom of the reactor, where the gas bubbles are distributed 
into the liquid, often by mixing. For the oxygen to transfer from the gas bubbles to the 
microorganisms, it must overcome different transport resistances. The oxygen transport is determined 
==PAGE:14==
 
Page 4 of 102 
 
by eight steps from the interior of the gas bubble to the site of intracellular reaction, visually displayed 
in Figure 1 (P. M. Doran, 2013b; Shuler, Kargi, & Delisa, 2017) 
 
 
Sparging the gas bubbles into the reactor, the oxygen diffuses from the interior of the gas 
bubbles to the gas-liquid interface (1), where it is transported across the interface (2) and diffused 
through the liquid film surrounding the gas bubble before ending up in the bulk liquid (3). Ensuring 
a well-mixed liquid, the oxygen is transported to the cells' surroundings (4), making it possible to 
diffuse through the cell's liquid film (5). Here it can move across the liquid-solid interface (6) and 
transport through the cytoplasm to the intracellular reaction site (8). If the cell is a cell clump and not 
an individual cell, the oxygen must diffuse through the solid to reach a particular cell (7) before 
reaching the reaction site (8). (P. M. Doran, 2013b; Shuler et al., 2017) 
The DO is crucial for aerobic processes and, most often, the limiting factor in cell growth. 
Therefore, oxygen can be the limiting factor throughout the fermentation if the cell concentration rate 
exceeds the rate of oxygen supply. Oxygen is continuously supplied into the bioreactor, determining 
the specific growth rate but not the maximum formed cells. Therefore, it is essential to keep the DO 
level above a critical oxygen concentration so the growth becomes independent of DO. The amount 
of DO depends on the mass transfer of oxygen from a gas bubble to the bulk liquid. Since oxygen has 
low solubility in aqueous solutions, the liquid-film resistance is assumed to be the limiting factor. (P. 
M. Doran, 2013b; Shuler et al., 2017)

2.2.1 Two-film theory 
The rate-limiting step of gas-liquid mass transfer is transporting oxygen from gas to liquid 
phase for many aerobic processes. The two-film theory often models this oxygen transport from one 
phase to another. The two-film theory describes the flux of a component, A, through the gas- and 
liquid film in mass transfer (M. Doran, 2013). The two-film theory is based on molecular diffusion, 
meaning the flux of component A occurs under concentration difference, described by the potential 
or driving force (Lewis & Whitman, 1924). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The two phases are the exterior gas film surrounding the gas bubble and a liquid film on the 
liquid surface. Illustrated in Figure 2. The flux, JA, through the two films is determined based on the 
concentration gradient and the mass transfer coefficient for gas phase KG and liquid phase KL. 
(Whitman, 1962)   
For the determination of flux across the liquid film, the difference in liquid concentration of 
compound A is determined based on the interface concentration, cA,i, and the concentration on the 
other side of the liquid film, cA. (Lewis & Whitman, 1924),(Whitman, 1962).

(2)

gas film. Divided by an interface. (Luis, 2018)

An important factor related to diffusion per unit area is gas solubility. When working with a 
low-soluble gas, the concentration difference across the liquid film will be minimal, meaning the 
absorption rate will be low. In the case of Henry's law, the gas is too soluble to permit the gas film 
but not soluble enough to neglect the liquid film. (Lewis & Whitman, 1924)  
Another essential factor for the diffusion rate is the film's thickness. Both films are very thin, 
but the gas film is expected to be thicker than the liquid film due to the viscosity/density ratio being 
greater for gas than liquid. Hence the overall flux is determined based on the liquid film. (Whitman, 
1962) 
Many process parameters can affect the diffusion rate, including agitation speed and gas 
velocity. The agitation speed enhances the diffusion of liquid film, whereas higher gas velocities 
enhance the diffusion of the gas film (Lewis & Whitman, 1924).  
Another essential process parameter is temperature. An increase in temperature will impact 
the gas solubility, resulting in a decrease in the absorption rate. The impact depends on film thickness 
and diffusivity. In the case of the liquid film, an increase in temperature will decrease film thickness 
and enhance the liquid film coefficient due to lower viscosity and greater diffusivity. The opposite is 
observed for the gas film coefficient. An increase in temperature will decrease the coefficient because 
the density/viscosity ratio is affected, meaning the film layer will be thicker. (Lewis & Whitman, 
1924)

2.2.2 Oxygen mass transfer 
The volumetric mass transfer coefficient, KLa, is a composition of the liquid film mass transfer 
coefficient, KL, and the interfacial area, a. Therefore also influenced by different parameters during 
operation. KL is mainly affected by operating conditions and configuration, which a primarily by

2.3 Hydrodynamics  
To study oxygen mass transfer, it is essential to understand liquid hydrodynamics. The most 
important parameters to understand are the gas-liquid properties, such as superficial gas velocity, gas 
holdup, gas bubble size, viscosity, and liquid flow. These are parameters that, on a certain level, affect 
the oxygen mass transfer and, thereby, the product formation.  
2.3.1 Liquid flow   
The flow pattern can change when operating a stirred tank reactor (STR) in multi-phase. 
Depending on the operating conditions, namely aeration and agitation, two flow regimes occur: 
homogeneous and heterogeneous. For STR, the flow is aimed to be homogeneous, meaning the flow 
is defined by mixing, and the gas and liquid phase are well-mixed. When the flow pattern results from 
the effect given by gas flow and not on mixing, it is defined as heterogeneous. In that case, it will be 
impossible to obtain a well-mixed process resulting in loss of mass transfer, affecting the product's 
quality. Parameters such as impeller speed and superficial gas velocity impact flow regime transition 
from homogeneous to heterogeneous. (Nauha, Visuri, Vermasvuori, & Alopaeus, 2015) 
Impeller flooding is often defined as when the gas is not distributed horizontally but with an 
axial flow through the impeller plane. Flooding is unwanted due to gas no longer being distributed 
homogeneously in the reactor. This can result in dead areas that are no longer mixed with gas, thereby 
affecting the oxygen mass transfer. (Nauha et al., 2015)  
Superficial gas velocity defines the average rate of gas dispersed into the liquid. Expressed as 
volume feed of gas phase in the reactor, Qg m3s-1 over the cross-sectional reactor area, Ac m2.

2.4 Reactor design for optimal process  
It is crucial to consider the fermenter design because it affects most of the hydrodynamics 
properties. Multiple reactor types are used in the industry, among other bubble columns, gas-lift 
reactors, and stirred tank reactors. In this project, the reactor type worked with is STR, operated as a 
two-phase system. This chapter will outline some of the essential specifications in STR which 
influence mass transfer and general process performance. An overview of a general STR is displayed 
in Figure 5. (Eibl, Eibl, Pörtner, Catapano, & Czermak, 2020) 
2.4.1 Mixing in bioreactors  
In STR, a crucial process parameter is a mechanical agitation. The agitator is supposed to mix 
the soluble components, keep a homogenous solution, and disperse the gas bubbles through the 
reactor (P. M. Doran, 1995b). 
Agitating can create vortex and swirling, so most STR have baffles installed. Baffles are 
vertical strips placed on the reactor wall and can be either directly mounted on the wall or at a distance 
of 1/50 to the reactor diameter or an angle. In viscous solutions, the baffles are often placed away 
from the wall to avoid stagnant zones on the inner edge between the wall and the baffle. They are 
often positioned as four equally spaced with the size of 1/10 to 1/12 of the reactor diameter. The size 
of the baffles depends on the impeller design and fluid viscosity. (Spier, Vandenberghe, Medeiros, & 
Soccol, 2011)  
Baffles are often placed in STR to enhance the mixing performance. Still, in some cases, STR 
is operated without baffles, resulting in swirling and vortex, reducing the mixing efficiency (Foukrach 
& Ameur, 2019). When a vortex is created, it results in circular radial mixing of the liquid. Changing 
the agitation speed results in a deeper vortex, with a shape depending on impeller speed, diameter, 
and acceleration of gravity, proposed by Froude number (Villadsen et al., 2011).

2.4.2 Gas dispersion 
Mentioned that the gas dispersion significantly impacts the mass transfer and how the gas 
dispersion is affected by gassed power number and agitation. The gas dispersion pattern is illustrated 
in Figure 9 to show the relationship between gas flow, agitation speed, and dispersion pattern (P. M. 
Doran, 1995b). 
From the left, the high gas flow rate can be observed in combination with low agitation speed. 
This pattern shows that the high gas flow rate is not distributed into the liquid but ends up flooding 
the impeller. The stirrer cannot handle the amount of gas capacity; therefore, the agitation does not 
reach the liquid, only the gas flooding around the impeller. Moving to the right in the dispersion 
pattern, the gas flow rates decrease, and the agitation speed is enhanced. This creates a better 
dispersion pattern due to gas being captured behind the impellers and dispersed into the liquid. Nib 
indicates the right agitation speed and flow rate to reach complete gas dispersion, creating a 
homogeneous flow regime. When further increasing the agitation speed after reaching the optimum 
for gas dispersion, the system will begin to generate recirculation of the gas flow, meaning the gas is 
dispersed back to the agitator. This high agitation speed creates small gas bubbles thrown away from 
the cavities into the bulk liquid (P. M. Doran, 1995b).  
The bubbles are often formed at the sparger and dragged into the impeller zone, from where 
they are distributed. This means the dispersion pattern is independent of the sparger type as long as it 
is placed below the impellers (P. M. Doran, 1995b).  
Many industrial processes operate under pressurized conditions, especially in oxidative 
processes, because the increase in pressure affects oxygen solubility (Kojima, Sawai, & Suzuki, 
1997).  The advantage of adding pressure into the system is the effect on the gas bubble size, which 
decreases when the applied pressure increases (Srivastava, Mishra, & Suresh, 2011). The gas bubbles 
decrease in size due to pressure because of the effect pressure has on the coalescence rate, bubble

work on the gas stream depends on the hole diameter in the sparger. The pressure has been shown to 
have a better effect when applied to a system with a sparger with small diameter holes (Kojima et al., 
1997).

3.2 Gas bubble size distribution 
The determination of specific gas bubble size distribution is an essential factor for optimizing 
industrial fermentations due to the influence on oxygen mass transfer. The investigation of fluid 
hydrodynamics is significant in viscous liquids due to the impact on gas-liquid interaction (Kováts,

A previous study by (Buchholz & Schügerl, 1979) investigates different gas bubble size 
determination methods. The two researched methods are the photographic method and the electrical 
conductivity method. Determination of gas bubble diameter by the photographic method is achieved 
by taking a picture of the column. The other investigated method is electrical conductivity, where the 
bubble diameter is determined based on the distance between two parallel needles. The study 
concluded that there are some size limitations to both methods. The photographic technique is shown 
not to detect larger bubbles, whereas the electrical conductivity does not see bubbles smaller than 0.6 
mm. Using porous plate aerators with a 50 µm pore diameter, the two methods have shown 
comparable results in bubble diameter. Hence at a smaller pore diameter of 5 µm, a significant 
difference in bubble diameter is detected.

3.3 Determination of volumetric mass transfer coefficient in viscous 
solutions 
Studies have investigated how the change in viscosity influences the KLa measurements. A 
widely applied KLa determination method is the dynamic gassing out, which was proposed back in 
1966 by Taguchi and Humphrey. Later, it was disclosed that parameters such as electrode response, 
viscous film resistance, and gas dynamics influenced the results (Jorge M.T. Vasconcelos & Alves, 
1991). However, the literature has described multiple variations of dynamic methods depending on 
the evaluation system (Linek, Beneš, Vacek, & Hovorka, 1982). 
Generally, a viscous media is characterized by a bimodal bubble size distribution, where 
bubbles appear either too large or too small. The larger bubbles have an average oxygen 
concentration, with a specific interfacial area and liquid film. Smaller bubbles do not have an average 
concentration, the interfacial area is independent of aeration conditions, and the liquid film coefficient 
is significantly smaller than bigger bubbles (Jorge M.T. Vasconcelos & Alves, 1991). The distribution 
of gas bubble size originates in viscous non-Newtonian liquids from the coalescence of smaller gas 
bubbles and rapid bubble breakup (Kawase & Moo‐Young, 1987). 
(Tuffile & Pinho, 1970) investigated, the viscosity influence by three KLa methods to disclose 
the most suitable determination in viscous media. The tested methods were the gassing out static 
method, gassing out dynamic method, and steady-state oxygen balance. The steady-state oxygen 
balance is a well-used method in the industry due to its simplicity and no impact on the process. The 
experiments were conducted during a streptomyces fermentation in a baffled 300L pilot fermenter, 
which acts as a highly viscous non-Newtonian liquid. The study's results showed similar KLa values 
for both gassing-out methods. However, the mass balance is shown to obtain significantly higher 
values, and the same similarity is observed in the oxygen uptake rate. For the mass balance method, 
no assumption about the cells, surface-active agents, or viscosity needs to be made. Where gassing 
out assumption about a rapid disengagement of air bubbles when terminating the aeration is assumed, 
which does not account for highly viscous non-Newtonian liquids. 
Additionally, a study by (Heijnen, Riet, & Wolthuis, 1980) discloses the influence of tiny gas 
bubbles (<1 mm) on the gassing out dynamic method in viscous media. The experiments were 
conducted in two baffled reactors, with a diameter of 45 and 36 cm and liquid height of 1.8 times the 
diameter, agitated with Rushton turbines. The media tested were viscous non-Newtonian. Gas holdup 
where determined when the aeration was stopped, shown to take a longer time in viscous media, 
assumed to be because of the smaller gas bubbles having a small rise velocity, impacting the overall 
residence time, compared to the actual gas phase. This study concluded that the concentration in tiny 
bubbles below 0.2 mm would equilibrate with the liquid within seconds, which makes the dynamic 
method problematic in viscous systems. A similar study by (Kawase & Moo‐Young, 1987) examined 
CMC solutions with the same method and under similar conditions, indicating similar results. 
However, they disclose that the influence of tiny bubbles was insignificant in mass balance compared 
to gassing out, with an underestimation of approximately 30%. Comparing the deviation in KLa to the 
results provided in (Tuffile & Pinho, 1970). The deviation in this study showed a significantly lower 
==PAGE:35==
 
Page 25 of 102 
 
comparison between the two methods than the other study. The conclusion of tiny bubbles' impact on 
mass transfer by gassing out dynamic method may be prone to error.  
Comparing the two approaches, it can be seen that they use two different equations to 
determine KLa. In the study by (Tuffile & Pinho, 1970), the equation takes the specific oxygen uptake 
rate per mass of respiring microorganisms and concentration of respiring organisms into account, 
which (Heijnen et al., 1980) and (Kawase & Moo‐Young, 1987) does not consider. Instead, they 
determine KLa considering gas holdup, thereby letting the tiny gas bubbles in the system impact the 
determination. Furthermore, the gassing out dynamic method can be used both in the case of 
biological consumption or without biomass. In case of no biological consumption or biomass present, 
the equation can be simplified. The simplification of the gassing out dynamic method is established 
as one of the applied methods in this project, Equation 46 (Cerri, Esperança, Badino, & Ribeiro, 
2016). The simplification has shown accurate estimations when the used oxygen probes' response 
time is sufficient (Garcia-Ochoa & Gomez, 2009). The simplification of the gassing out dynamic 
method was used by (Ali & Solsvik, 2021) in the investigation of KLa in similar conditions of CMC 
solutions with 0.15%, 0.3%, and 0.5%  showing realistic results.

4.4 Method for KLa estimation 
Numerous methods have been proposed to determine the volumetric oxygen mass transfer 
coefficient in aerated systems. Two approaches were selected for this project, the hydrogen peroxide 
steady-state method and the gassing out dynamic method. The two methods were tested to determine 
the most suitable for the project's purpose. General for both is the requirement to measure the oxygen 
concentration inside the reactor. The oxygen concentration was measured with an optical oxygen 
robust probe: OXROBSC (Pyro Science, Aachen, Germany), which has a fast response time of < 6 
sec. This was tested to ensure precise changes in dissolved oxygen concentration. 
The measurement principles of the oxygen probes are based on the RED FLASH technology. 
Inside the oxygen probe is a red light source at wavelength 610-630 nm and a RED FLASH indicator 
showing an oxygen-dependent luminescence near-infrared, NIR 760-790nm. Oxygen molecules 
collide with the oxygen probe, creating a phase-shifted sinusoidally modulated emission in the NIR 
spectrum. Based on the Stern-Vollmer theory, this phase shift is converted into oxygen units. The 
principles are illustrated in Figure 22. When a low amount of oxygen is present, a higher NIR 
emission is emitted compared to a high amount of oxygen molecules. (PyroScience GmbH, 2022)

4.4.2 Gassing out dynamic method 
The simplest way of determining KLa is by the dynamic gassing out method. This method 
keeps agitation and aeration at fixed rates to ensure a stable DO concentration. At time t0, airflow is 
turned off, and by sparging in nitrogen, the reactor is deoxygenated. When air saturation reaches zero 
percent, the measurement period is started, the nitrogen flow is turned off, and the airflow is started 
at the same flow rate. An overview of the method is displayed in Figure 26. (P. M. Doran, 2013b)

4.6 Measuring gas bubble size 
The gas bubble size distribution was conducted with a shadowgraphic endoscope (Dantec 
Dynamics, Skovlunde, Denmark). The endoscope is divided into two regions: the external region 
consists of a focusing ring for the telescopic probe tip and C-mount camera/ high-speed camera, and 
the internal region, where the particle flow is determined, consists of a light source/ShadowStrobe 
LED, and the probe to change the wide of measurement plane. Depending on the measured liquid 
density, the measurement plane between the ShadowStrobe LED and the telescopic lens can extend 
from 40mm to 120mm. The experiments in this project are conducted at 40mm. The setup is 
illustrated in Figure 28.    
The shadowgraphic endoscope can determine the particles' size, shape, and velocity using 
LED backlighting. The light source backlit the gas bubbles, and the camera acquired based on the 
shadow of the bubbles. Between the backlight and camera, there is an accurate synchronization device 
making it able to freeze the particle in motion and get high-resolution images.  
 
 
From DynamicStudio software, it is possible to image process to find the size and shape of 
the gas bubbles. Sauter's mean diameter, Equation 13, is incorporated in the software to determine 
the gas bubble size distribution, which can be further used to determine the interfacial. Based on the 
correlation between the displacement of two images, it is possible to get the velocity with the basic 
particle tracking algorithm. In this project, the focus will be on size and shape. Therefore, the velocity 
function will not be considered. (Dynamics, n.d.-b, n.d.-a)  
The acquired pictures are taken as a sequence with a chosen number of images to conduct 
information for the analysis. In this case, 400 images per sequence were decided, with a time between 
Figure 28. Dantec Dynamic setup of  Shadowgraphic endoscope (Dynamics, n.d.-a)  
==PAGE:55==
 
Page 45 of 102 
 
each image of 1250 µs. This amount of pictures should make it possible to detect many bubbles with 
various sizes, to get the best understanding of gas bubble size distribution.  
Each sequence of images was analyzed with adaptive shadow tracking. The analysis is divided 
into three boxes, Processing, Advanced, and Filters and Tracking. Pictures of the software and the 
analysis are displayed in Appendix 3.  
The first box, Processing. The red frame indicates the area to which the analysis should be 
applied. Whereafter it is decided to either work with Denoised or no preprocessing. This is more 
relevant when a camera with lousy quality is used, meaning it can improve the images, which is not 
needed in this case. The following two focus on the pixels of the bubbles. With higher pixels, it is 
more sensitive to smaller bubbles.  
In Advanced, the first option is the bounding box scale, which is the blue square created 
around each detected bubble. When moving the scale, the size of the box is changed. This is useful if 
two bubbles lay close to each other and the scale is high, meaning the two boxes will lay on top of 
each other and be unable to detect both. By increasing the value, it will be able to detect smaller 
objects, but the bigger ones are more at risk. Depending on the darkness of the images in the picture, 
the threshold can be changed. Lower values of approximately 30% mean the analysis is more sensitive 
for the darker objects than the greyer areas in the pictures.    
In Tracking and filtering, the focus is on the roundness of the particles. In the first particle 
eccentricity, if this parameter is close to 1, then it means that only spherical bubbles are detected. In 
this case, all shapes of bubbles were wanted. The following setting is the roundness, which makes a 
Y and X-axis in the bubbles, taking the difference between these axes. Whereafter the particle size is 
chosen if there is a wish only to detect some of them. In this case, all bubbles were wanted, so the 
minimum was set to the lowest and the maximum at the highest. The tracking options are used if the 
bubbles' velocity and direction are wanted. 
To ensure a more precise analysis of the gas bubble size distribution, calibration of the 
endoscope is needed. Taking an object with a known size, placing the measuring plane of A and B, 
and inserting the value (in this case, 5mm), the shadowgraphic endoscope knows the precise detection 
in the focus of the measurement plane of 5 mm. In Appendix 3, the picture used to calibrate is 
disclosed.  
The data from the analysis is exported to comma-separated values files (CSV) to further work 
with the data in a python script. When taking an image, each bubble gets a frame ID to distinguish 
the bubbles from each other. If the bubble disappears behind another bubble and then appears on a 
picture again, it will have the same ID but appear as a new line in the file, changing the mean value. 
Therefore, a python script displayed in Appendix 3 was created to take all bubbles with the same 
bubble ID and average them. Furthermore, the script detects the minimum, maximum, and mean 
bubble diameters.

6. Discussion  
Throughout the project, questions about the results and the summary of previous work have 
been raised, which will be outlined in this chapter. The primary purpose of this project was to use 
new technology to determine the gas bubble size distribution under changing viscosity at industrial 
representative conditions. 
6.1 Enzyme 
 Based on previous work, the amount of GOx was chosen to be 0.05 g/l, and the substrate 
glucose for the enzyme to catalyze the reaction established in Equation 32 was selected to be 0.1M. 
The enzyme activity results under operational conditions are outlined in section 5.1 Enzyme activity. 
The process stability experiments showed (Figure 29) that the enzyme with 0.1M glucose had activity 
over approximately 7 hours. Looking further into the deactivation of the enzyme, two deactivation 
constants were established (Figure 31), meaning that within the first approximately 7 minutes, the 
enzyme deactivates with a rate of 0.84 min-1, which afterward deceased to 0.042 min-1. This change 
in reaction rate would impact the estimated KLa, because of the change in oxygen uptake. Therefore, 
it was chosen that future experiments should be conducted without glucose. 
Furthermore, glucose impacts the lifetime of the enzyme, meaning that with glucose and the 
addition of oxygen, the enzyme would deactivate faster due to the reaction the enzyme would 
catalyze. Without glucose, the consumption of GOx is assumed to be slower, making it possible to 
conduct the experiments without a considerable impact from the deactivation of the enzyme (Kleppe, 
1966). The concern about the effect of the deactivated enzyme (Figure 29) was that the oxygen 
concentration changes significantly, thereby affecting the change in oxygen concentration, resulting 
in noncomparable KLa values. Furthermore, the zoom of the stable time (Figure 30) showed with 
glucose to be approximately 10 minutes, which is too short a stable time for KLa determination. This 
short stability time will give a considerable error in the estimations, wanting to compare experiments 
conducted over multiple hours.   
6.2 Viscosity  
CMC was used to simulate the change in viscosity to make it comparable with the viscosity 
change observed in industrial fermentation. One reason for choosing CMC was shear thinning 
properties, similar to yeast fermentation, meaning that the results with CMC will be comparable to 
the industrial processes. The precise viscosity for yeast fermentation is often a secret in the industry. 
==PAGE:80==
 
Page 70 of 102 
 
Therefore, it is unknown whether the viscosity used in this project is accurate with industrial 
fermentation. Four CMC concentrations were chosen to get a broad perspective of viscosity's effects 
on mass transfer and gas bubble size. Given that the CMC is non-Newtonian, the higher agitation 
speeds give lower viscosity than CMC concentration at low agitation speed. This is useful when 
comparing industrial processes to determine agitation speed improvement on the gas bubble size 
distribution and the overall mass transfer.    
6.3 KLa method 
Previous work has looked into which KLa determination method is suitable for viscous liquids, 
giving contradictory conclusions. Therefore, two methods were chosen to be compared in the viscous 
system. Figure 32-47 outlines the oxygen concentration profiles for the two agitation speeds and four 
liquid solutions, determined with the gassing out dynamic method and the hydrogen peroxide steady-
state method.   
For the gassing out method, it is essential that the liquid is well mixed and the agitation speeds 
are operated at high values, eliminating the liquid boundary layers on the oxygen sensor (P. M. Doran, 
2013b). Looking into the gassing out method, it can be seen that the sensors obtain steady values, 
which is similar for all four sensors, concluding that the agitation speed is assumed to be enough for 
being well mixed and eliminating the layer. Furthermore, the sensor's response time needs to be less 
than 1/KLa (P. M. Doran, 2013b). Section 4.4 method for KLa estimation has shown a response time 
of 2-4 seconds, meaning it works for values as high as 900 h-1. Looking into the determined KLa 
values, this is more than enough for the conducted experiments.   
In the hydrogen peroxide method, the addition of hydrogen peroxide was added from the top 
of the reactor, creating a concentration gradient. The time to achieve the new plateau (Figure 25) was 
faster at the top compared to the bottom, given a deviation in the obtained KLa values when comparing 
the four sensors. Looking into the concentration profiles, it was clear that the concentration gradient 
influenced the sensors, giving instability for especially sensors P1 and P2.  
Considering the economic aspects, especially on an industrial scale, hydrogen peroxide is also 
a non-preferable method because of the enormous amount needed, which is expensive and exposes 
people to danger. Furthermore, when catalase is added to the reactor, it begins to deactivate when 
reacting with oxygen, which can cause a problem, especially for the more viscous liquids, which takes 
longer for the oxygen concentration to reach the new stable plateau.  
Comparing the two methods, the gassing out method is much faster than the hydrogen 
peroxide. Looking into the determined KLa values, the ones obtained with hydrogen peroxide are 
almost double as high as for the gassing out method, creating nonrealistic numbers for the system. 
Also, more considerable deviations in the three replicates for the hydrogen peroxide were given 
compared to the gassing out method.  
==PAGE:81==
 
Page 71 of 102 
 
Therefore, it was chosen to further work with the gassing out method because of the more 
stable oxygen concentration profiles, less spill of resources, and lower deviations for the replicates. 
Furthermore, the method was chosen based on previous work by (Ali & Solsvik, 2021), which showed 
reliability in gassing out for non-Newtonian CMC solutions.   
In the case of section 5.3 Viscosity influence on liquids hydrodynamics KLa was again defined 
to ensure similarity to the previous experiments and show that the process is replicable. Comparing 
the KLa values, it was clear that they were comparable with a similar deviation in replicates. For the 
second time with KLa, it was clear that there was some problem with p2 for water, which had unstable 
values, creating much lower estimates. What was wrong is unknown since it worked without 
problems when conducting the following experiments.

6.5 Viscosity influence 
The conducted experiments in this project have shown that increasing viscosity results in 
decreasing gas bubble size distribution (Figure 66). Literature shows contradictory results on 
viscosity impact on mean bubble diameter. However, a study by (Ali & Solsvik, 2021) with the same 
methods used to determine gas bubbles in similar liquids has shown a similar correlation between gas 
bubble size and viscosity.  
Where other studies by (Besagni et al., 2017) have the opposite conclusion, determining the 
viscosity to increase the gas bubbles. This contradiction could be argued with the addition of salt 
impacting the surface tension or the properties of a non-Newtonian liquid. The studies with 
correlating results by (Ali & Solsvik, 2021) were conducted in the same non-Newtonian liquids, 
whereas the study by (Besagni et al., 2017) was conducted in Newtonian.   
The added salt creates a non-coalescing liquid and changes surface tension. Therefore, the 
bubbles do not collide and become bigger under viscous properties. The knowledge of tiny bubbles 
states that bubbles smaller than 1 mm in diameter become irrelevant for the mass transfer because the 
oxygen concentration equilibrates with the liquid (P. M. Doran, 2013b). This could be one of the main 
reasons that KLa decrease under increasing viscosity and not due to the formation of bigger bubbles. 
As stated by (Khare & Niranjan, 1995), tiny bubbles are more rapidly generated in non-Newtonian 
==PAGE:85==
 
Page 75 of 102 
 
liquids. Based on the raw images, a few larger gas bubbles can be seen entering the measurement 
plane. These bubbles are too big to be detected, but looking through the sequences, the amount of 
larger bubbles is insignificant. This means that the overall distribution of gas bubbles becomes smaller 
with viscosity, where only a few larger gas bubbles are formed, not interfering with the overall 
distribution.  
The study by (Khare & Niranjan, 1995) also states that the tiny bubbles below 1 mm still 
impact KLa under agitated systems. This observation is also seen in the displayed results for the 
project because all of the detected diameters are below 1 mm, and oxygen mass transfer is still 
observed, thereby still contributing. 
This impact on gas bubble size can be directly correlated to the change observed in KL because 
smaller gas bubbles tend to react as rigid spheres with no gas circulation, thereby decreasing the liquid 
film mass transfer coefficient (P. M. Doran, 2013b). This corresponds with the fall in KLa observed 
when the gas bubbles become smaller under increasing viscosity. Additionally, the KL is similar when 
comparing the two agitation speeds, so when increasing the agitation speed, KL is not enhanced. 
Comparing the effect on KL (Figure 68) and a (Figure 67), the agitation only seems to influence the 
interfacial area. Except in the case of water (Figure 68), here, the difference in mean gas bubble size 
is more significant between the two agitation speeds (Figure 66), where it can be seen that KL is higher 
at lower agitation speeds.  
Compared to KL, the interfacial area shows more considerable deviations between the two 
agitation speeds. The interfacial area depends both on gas holdup and gas bubble size distribution. 
Both gas holdup and mean bubble size diameter decrease with increasing viscosity, but in the case of 
interfacial area, the water, 0.1%, and 0.25% CMC are the only experiments conducted with rising 
values. This increase corresponds with smaller gas bubbles assumed to have a larger overall 
interfacial area. However, for 0.5% and 0.75%, the interfacial area shows a drop (Figure 67), possibly 
due to the problems of detecting larger bubbles. The 0.5% and 0.75% seem to have similar values as 
the water experiments for the lower agitation speed. The error bars show great significance, so 
improved and more precise results for the interfacial area could be done with more experiments or, 
as mentioned earlier, have sequences with more images. 
The gas holdup found based on Equation 12 states the change in liquid height with and without 
gas. A high gas holdup is preferable because more air is distributed in the liquid, impacting the mass 
transfer (Hofmeester, 1988). Investigated by (Ali & Solsvik, 2021) shown that smaller bubbles should 
improve the gas holdup because of the lower velocity, meaning they can get stuck in the liquid flow 
pattern and stay in the liquid longer. Besides the gas bubble size, the addition of salt and the bioreactor 
configuration also influence the gas holdup, which in this case (Figure 48) gas holdup decrease with 
viscosity, even though the gas bubbles are decreasing in size.  
What could be contradictory to the results found is that the higher viscosity yield a higher 
amount of larger gas bubbles, creating a destabilization of the homogeneous flow regime  (Besagni 
et al., 2017). This change in the homogenous flow regime can be seen from the estimations of the 
Reynolds number, which indicates that the two highest CMC concentration results in the transition 
==PAGE:86==
 
Page 76 of 102 
 
state. This can be because a correlation may be needed in the case of non-Newtonian liquids, the 
additions of salt, or local gas holdups.  
When determining the viscosity, the shear rate is taken into consideration. A determination of 
the shear rate is displayed in Equation 24. Well-known that the shear rate in a bioreactor most often 
depends on location in the reactor, this equation takes an average shear rate into account, creating a 
small uncertainty. Since the shear rate is only used for viscosity calculations, it has no further 
influence on the rest of the results other than the precision of viscosity and, thereby, the correlation 
between viscosity, mass transfer, and gas bubble size.

7. Conclusion  
The project aimed to investigate the viscosity effect on oxygen mass transfer, an industrial-
recognized issue, especially during fermentation. The experiments are conducted in a 200L pilot plant 
with a concentration of 0.05 g/l GOx, 1972 g MgSO4ꞏ7H2O, and different CMC concentrations. The 
addition of salt is used to ensure a non-coalescence liquid. The gassing out dynamic method was 
chosen due to stable oxygen concentration profiles and the small deviation on the replicates. The most 
significant conclusion is listed below: 
1. Through the KLa determination, the similarity of all four oxygen sensors placed at different 
heights concludes that the viscosity change does not impact the oxygen distribution along the 
reactor wall. 
 
2. Determining the gas bubble size distribution by the shadowgraphic endoscope, the results 
show an impact on Sauter mean diameter both by agitation speed and viscosity changes. The 
mean gas bubble diameter is lower at higher agitation speeds due to more frequent gas bubble 
breakup (Figure 66). Furthermore, it can be found that the gas bubble size decreases by 
increasing the viscosity.  
 
3. Based on the image processing, the interfacial area where specified by Equation 16, revealing 
(Figure 67) that higher agitation speed improved the interfacial area. The more viscous 
experiments show a decrease in the interfacial area, most likely due to the reduction of gas 
holdup and the lower Sauter mean diameter.  
 
4. Additionally, the mass transfer liquid film coefficient (Figure 68) observed a significant 
correlation between KL and viscosity. It can be concluded that the viscous liquids obtain lower 
KL than water, most likely due to the increased liquid film thickness due to higher viscosity. 
Moreover, the distinction between the two agitation speeds showed an insignificant role in the 
case of KL.  
Considering the uncertainties, it can be concluded that by changing the viscosity, the 
volumetric mass transfer coefficient is mainly affected by the change in liquid film mass transfer 
coefficient compared to the interfacial area. Furthermore, the change in viscosity results in smaller 
gas bubbles when increasing the viscosity. Additionally, it can be concluded that the agitation speeds 
significantly impact the gas bubble size compared to the liquid film mass transfer working in viscous 
media.

8. Future Perspective 
Concerning the project, it would be essential for the next step in optimizing the fermentation 
process to do experiments conducted in more industrially relevant production by execution of yeast 
fermentation. By implementing the same experimental setup with the shadowgraphic endoscope and 
multiple sensors for collecting oxygen concentration into a yeast fermentation, it should be possible 
to see how much the viscosity changes during the fermentation and how this affects the gas bubble 
size distribution and overall mass transfer coefficient. From the project results, the shadowgraphic 
endoscope made it possible to divide the mass transfer coefficient into KL and a. This will provide 
more precise results for establishing a model specific to yeast fermentations since yeast is expected 
to provide lower KLa values than the biocatalytic process. It would be interesting to see if the same 
trend would be observed in yeast fermentation or if other parameters like cell growth play a significant 
role.      
Furthermore, the experiments could be done in yeast or CMC-simulated fed-batch cultivation 
to investigate the changing volume and viscosity effects on the gas bubble size distribution, gas 
holdup, and mass transfer. Knowing that fed-batch cultivation provides high cell density and higher 
product concentration at a lower growth rate than a batch reactor (Shuler et al., 2017). It could be 
expected that the investigation into gas bubble size could help provide better mass transfer by 
changing the affecting parameters during the changing volume and maybe thereby enhance the time 
for the cultivation phase.  
Some questions were stated in Chapter 3 Previous work, which was planned to be investigated 
in this project. Unfortunately, there was no time to execute them all during the project. Based on the 
experimental results and the questions stated in Chapter 3, some experiments to conduct in the future 
could be: 
1. Previous work has been shown to improve mass transfer by up to approximately 60% using 
impellers other than Rushton turbines (Chemineer, 2013; Chemineer Inc., 2013). Changing 
the impeller type would be essential to see the effect on gas holdup, overall mass transfer, and 
the gas bubble size distribution. Furthermore, it could be interesting in the pilot scale to 
conduct the experiments with mixed impeller type. Previous work has shown that combining 
the impeller types makes it possible to improve bubble breakup and distribution and apply the 
right amount of shear rate. 
 
2. Experimental results show that the mean gas bubble size decreased at higher viscosity. This 
high amount of smaller gas bubbles under viscous liquid detected in the bottom of the reactor 
may change when the gas bubbles rise through the reactor due to the impact of the liquid’s 
==PAGE:89==
 
Page 79 of 102 
 
properties. Therefore, an experiment to get more data on the gas bubble size distribution 
would be to use the shadowgraphic endoscope at different heights in the reactor, as illustrated 
in Figure 71. The relationship between viscosity changes and KL showed (Figure 68) that 
increasing viscosity significantly affected KL. Therefore, it could be interesting when finding 
out how the gas bubble size distribution changes when rising through the reactor to see how 
this will affect and thereby establish if KL is constant in the entire reactor or if it changes.   
 
3. From the determination of the liquid flow properties, it could be seen that the viscosity and 
agitation speed impacted the Reynolds number, resulting in transition flow for higher 
viscosity. A previous study by (Besagni et al., 2017) also determined this change in the liquid 
flow regime, indicating that the change in flow regime occurs due to viscosity and the change 
in gas bubble size. By using a see-through reactor, it would be possible to see the flow pattern 
at different conditions, hence determining if there is a risk of dead zones when the viscosity 
increases, for example, due to poor mixing.

4. From the data treatment of the gas bubble images, it was found that it was problematic to 
conduct images with a good view during the slope of the gassing out method. Knowing from 
the previous work investigated by (Khare & Niranjan, 1995), a non-Newtonian solution has 
been shown to take 2-6 minutes to reach a steady state of the gas holdup, which can affect the

bubble size in non-Newtonian solutions. This could also test whether the gassing out method

conditions.  
 
5. The determination of gas holdup is often defined as a total gas holdup, meaning it is assumed 
that it is a comprehensive picture of the entire volume. A discussion with other companies has 
discovered that, in some cases, local gas holdup is observed in the reactor, creating different 
“layers” of gas holdup. This could be interesting to look into with the see-through reactor and 
see if these “layers” create different sizes of gas bubbles and what impact it could have on the 
overall system.    
 
6. The endoscope can determine the u (east flow) and v (north flow) velocities to understand the 
gas bubble flow pattern within a bioreactor. Within gas bubble size distribution, the smaller 
bubbles are expected to follow the flow pattern from the agitation, where the larger and 
heavier bubbles tend to flow directly upward. To discover whether this flow assumption is 
accurate, an analysis could be made to conduct the velocity and compare it against the gas 
bubble size distribution.   
 
7. Based on the previous discussion, salt could significantly impact the gas bubble size 
distribution under viscous properties. Salt is often used as nutrition in the industrial process, 
affecting coalescence tendency. It could be interesting to conduct the same experiments 
without salt to confirm or disprove this consumption.

3. Hydrogen peroxide-catalase method.  
The procedure in this exercise to determine KLa is based upon the decomposition of hydrogen 
peroxide to free oxygen in the presence of the enzyme catalase (Methology based on ref. 2 and 4).  
𝑐𝑎𝑡𝑎𝑙𝑎𝑠𝑒 
 
                                 H2O2    →         H2O  + ½ O2       
  
  
(14)  
  
When adding H2O2 to the water, the liberated oxygen will dissolve in the liquid phase, which 
consequently will be supersaturated. The oxygen is transferred from the liquid phase to the gas phase 
(i.e. the opposite process as the one normally happening in fermenters) with a rate which depends on 
the mass transfer coefficient. The KLa value for the system is the same irrespective of the direction of 
the oxygen flow.  
  
The oxygen transfer rate is considered to be a first order reaction with respect to hydrogen 
peroxide as well as with catalase. The practical procedure is to keep the catalase concentration high 
enough for hydrogen peroxide to be the limiting reagent. The oxygen concentration is measured 
before H2O2 addition is initiated, having a fixed well defined airflow to the vessel and a supposed 
saturated oxygen concentration. Hydrogen peroxide is now added at constant rate and a new 
(supersaturated) steady state oxygen concentration is established. This is visualized in figure 6, which 
also indicates the return of oxygen concentration to the normal level when the hydrogen peroxide 
addition is stopped.

equals the flow rate of H2O2, and similarly the transfer rate of O2 must be equal to the O2 formation

in the experimental procedure.  
The oxygen sensor output (oxygen tension) is in % of saturation which has to be converted to 
concentration. When calculating the saturation concentration c*, it is important to consider the 
position of the sensor in question.
