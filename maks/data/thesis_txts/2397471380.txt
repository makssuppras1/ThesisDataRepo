Introduction 
 
 
Abstract 
 
Background: The numbers of endovascular (EV) surgeries conducted each year is on the rise. We 
see old surgeries change from open to endovascular. Claudication was difficult to treat 50 years ago 
but today endovascular angioplasty is a routine. This requires advances in training and education, 
and there are many unmet needs. Current research shows pre-surgical patient specific simulation 
practice, can shorten EV surgery time, decrease the use of ionizing radiation and contrast, and lower 
the amounts of minor and major errors.  
 
Purpose: Evaluate available training and practice simulators for endovascular procedures and 
develop and produce a simulator to cater for unmet needs and enable patient specific training. 
 
Method and Materials 15 available EV simulators for medical training have been evaluated with 
current scientific methods and classification schemes. Each simulator has also been evaluated based 
on specific technical features, price and value. This information in combination with unmet needs 
of simulation centers and the attractive proposition of patient specific training, has been used as 
development requirements for new simulation. 
 
Results: The design for a new simulator was developed and the production and materials were 
chosen to meet as many needs as possible. During the process the simulator was communicated as 
a business opportunity, to raise funding. This communication dubbed the project Lumendo. The 
Lumendo simulator is a physical simulator enabling low cost training of endovascular skills. The 
material cost for the simulator is a factor 75 lower than computer-based simulators with similar 
features. 
 
Conclussion: It is possible to create a simulator that can be used to train the skills that are not 
covered in simulation training today. The design and production process developed shows a feasible 
case for enabling pre-surgical patient specific practice. A full-scale prototype is in the making and 
will be finished within first quarter of 2018.

Introduction and background 
This report is a development study and a master thesis in biomedical engineering. The product has 
been developing and building a new surgical simulator to train endovascular procedures. This 
report documents the considerations in the making of the simulator, and the experiments made to 
properly design and create it.

The report can be read from different perspectives; The teaching professor, the technical 
engineer, The business developer, the doctor or student in training, or the hospital director. A

Chapter 1: The patient 
 
To understand the field of Endovascular surgery one must understand the both the anatomy and 
physiology involved in the cardiovascular diseases, that are currently treated in this manner. This 
section and subsections will map the vascular system so that a surgical simulator, can be developed 
with relevant features. 
 
Cardiovascular anatomy and physiology 
 
In this subsection, I will describe the basic cardiovascular anatomy 
and physiology. The cardiovascular system covers the heart and all 
the vessels transporting blood to and from. It is a system of pumping 
and transporting. The heart is at the center receiving deoxygenated 
blood from the veins pumping it though the pulmonary arteries and 
veins, to oxygenate, and then out into every corner of the body. The 
heart pumps approximately 70 times per minute with each beat 
pumping roughly 70 ml of blood. As blood leaves the heart it does so 
upwards into the aortic arch. The first branches are behind the aortic 
valve, to supply the heart muscle through three arteries. Thereafter 
the aortic arch has branches into the subclavian arteries supplying 
the shoulders and arms, and the carotid arteries supplying the head 
and brain. About 60% of the blood1 continues to supply the bulk of 
the body; the organs in abdomen, and all the skeletal muscles below 
the heart. That make roughly 3 liters blood flow per minute. After 
passing through the diaphragm the vessel branches of multiple times 
before splitting into the two common iliac arteries. Of the many 
branches, the most notable are the two renal arteries, The superior 
and inferior mesenteric arteries, the celiac trunk. The common iliac 
arteries follow the pelvis and split into internal and external iliac 
arteries. The external iliac artery is very superficial and is therefore 
often used as access for endovascular surgery. 
 
 
Cardiovascular diseases 
 
Cardiovascular disease is a leading cause of death worldwide. In USA it is the causes 633.000 annuals 
deaths2 which is 40.000 more than cancer. The group of diseases in this area spans wide, from 
diseases in the heart tissue, to restriction of blood flow and many more. In this report we focus on 
diseases in the vascular system.  
 
Epidemiology

11 
Old age is a dispositional factor for cardiovascular disease, and with the globally modernizing 
healthcare we grow ever older. The decreasing population living under the poverty line and the 
increased availability of unhealthy diets also poses a dispositional factor for heart disease. For the 
first time ever, we se the poorest part of the population grow obese. Among the risk factors are age, 
obesity, hypertension, smoking, stress, inactivity. 
 
 
Peripheral Vascular Disease (PVD) 
 
Peripheral Vascular Disease covers all the blood vessels besides those that 
supply the heart and those that supply the brain. Claudication is a 
common outcome, when blood supply to the legs is compromised. The 
cause of disease is commonly arthrosclerosis, which builds up scar tissue 
and plaque in the vascular wall. Over time this can become so severe that 
the circulation is completely blocked off. Supplying less blood to different 
areas of the body has varying effects. If an organ is the suffers from low 
blood flow, it can be fatal, but if it hits larger arteries supplying skeletal 
muscles the symptoms are commonly fatigue and inability to be active for 
longer durations of time. If Left untreated in can evolve so the limb 
impacted stops being able to heal normally or in worst-case gangrene. This would require 
amputation. 
 
 
Abdominal Aortic Aneurism(AAA) 
 
The typical aneurisms patient has a history of smoking, is obese and has untreated hypertension. 
Aneurism is a blood vessel expanded in balloon like fashion without being able to reobtain normal 
shape and size. The blood vessels are able to dilate and constrict, but in an aneurism the vessel 
has been pushed beyond the normal boundaries. This has 
weakened the smooth muscles and the vessel wall and created 
eddies in the blood stream. This in turn causes accumulation of 
debris and clots. AAA can therefore be a risk factor for 
downstream emboli or at worst be a of rupture. Approximately 
85% occur below the kidneys before or including the iliac 
bifurcation3, where the artery still has an average size between 
20-30 mm4

Medical imaging in cardiovascular diagnostics 
 
When suspecting vascular diseases patients are investigated with medical imaging to locate and 
diagnose the circulatory problems. The methods used usually include ultrasound and a combination 
of x-ray and contrast agents. The latter can both be CT scans or planar x-ray. This section will briefly 
explain these technologies and how they can contribute knowledge for diagnosis and treatment 
planning. The discipline of imaging arteries is called angiography and the images obtained 
angiograms.  
 
Ultrasound 
 
Ultrasound utilizes the propagation of sound waves through the body’s 
tissue.5 A transducer induces sound waves from a number of elements. 
When the wave propagates and transfers through one tissue type to 
another a wave bounces back. The signal is however only able to “see” 
a certain distance into the body since it attenuates correlated linearly 
with depth and frequency of waves. The reflected signals are measured 
with the transducer and used to recreate an image from the echo. 
Ultrasound is also used for arterial diagnostics. It is a useful tool since it 
can make flow images with no risk for the patient at a low cost compared 
to CT and MRI. The technology is best for investigating vessels close to 
the surface of the body. 
 
Catheter Angiography 
 
Catheter angiograph is conducted with a catheter in the vascular system 
during intermittent x-ray. The catheter can be used as injector of 
contrast agent making the vessels “light up” on the image. The figure on 
the right is an example of just this. X-rays images recorded before 
injection of contrast can be subtracted to get the optimal image quaility. 
 
CT Angiography 
 
Computed Tomography (CT) Angiography has in the later years 
been implemented as a fixed part of cardiovascular diagnostic 
procedure. The basis of the mechanism is an x-ray tube rotating 
around the patient stretch, radiating from multiple angles. On 
the opposite site of the x-ray tube the attenuation of radiation 
measured with an array of detectors. After a full half a 
revolution of the data can be reconstructed into an image using 
a technique called filtered back projection6. This computer aid 
to reconstruction is where the technology gets its name from. 
Since x-ray imaging is density dependent, the density of the 
==PAGE:13==
13 
tissue is represented as intensity in the 3D image in cubic elements known as a voxels.  
 
The next advent in this technology is multiple energy CT. Here, the image is taken twice with 
different levels of energy sent to the X-ray tube. This makes it possible to achieve much higher 
contrast between different tissue types7 and better segmentation of vascular diseases and plaques. 
 
Procedural steps: 
To produce an image the patient is placed on a stretch, which is mechanically moved through the 
gantry, while the x-ray tube and sensory system is rotating. For cardiovascular imaging a contrast 
agent is utilized to better separate blood from surrounding tissue. This contrast is most commonly 
based on iodine, a soft halogen that naturally occurs in the body8. 
 
 
The CT-scanner to the right is Toshiba’s Aquillon One, 
which has markedly improved imaging in cardiovascular 
diagnostics. This CT-scanner has a detector array that is 
16.5 cm wide, which makes it possible to record up to 330 
image slices per revolution, where conventional scanners 
only acquire one. This makes it possible to image the heart 
and a big portion of the vascular system at one point in the 
heart beat. This is important for cardiac imaging but is also 
an advantage for vascular imaging. 
 
 
MR Angiography 
 
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) can also be used for angiograms (MRA). The technology utilizes 
a strong magnetic field to align molecules in the body, like small compass needles9. These molecules 
are then pushed out of this alignment with another magnetic field. Measuring the signal they send 
out as they realign with the strong field, it is possible to reconstruct 3D images of the inside of the 
body. The recording of transmitted signals can take up to 30 seconds in MRA and therefore requires 
the patient to stay still and hold breath for longer durations of time10. This can also be problematic 
aligning signal recording with the distortion of a moving heart, since this beats approximately once 
a second. The image quality with MRA is very good and the procedure is not exposing the patient 
with ionizing radiation as CT and X-ray imaging.  
Surgical treatment of vascular patients 
 
This section describes the surgical treatment of patients with vascular diseases. I have chosen two 
operations to describe the use of surgery today. Repair of Abdominal Aortic Aneurisms (AAA) and 
Percutaneous Transluminal Angioplasty (PTA). Vascular surgery can be differentiated in two groups; 
the open surgeries and the endovascular surgeries.  
Open surgery is the traditional mode of treatment with fully anesthetized patients, who are 
opened(laparotomized) to access point of intervention. Endovascular means “within the vessels” 
and covers treatment, through the circulatory system.

bigger part of medical treatments and more of these procedures are performed.

Basics of Endovascular Surgery 
 
As already mentioned, the name endovascular surgery implies surgery from within the vessels. 
These procedures are, in contrast to open surgery, conducted through small access-points where 
small wire equipment is feed into the bloodstream. The two basic types of tools in these surgeries 
are guidewires and sheaths. Guidewires are up to two meters long, and can vary in stiffness, 
hydrophobic/hydrophilic, angle of ending and more. These are used in combination with sheaths 
and catheters. Sheaths are the gates from outside the body to inside the vessels. They are hollow 
and allow for guidewires and catheters to be slid into the circulatory system. Catheters can slide 
over the guidewires and like the guidewires, they also come in multiple variations of size, tip shabe 
and more. The combination of the two tools can be used to navigate through the vasculatures in 
the human body in a variety of ways. Adding to this are specialized catheters, which can contain 
balloons, stents, or even simple ultrasound elements3. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
AAA Repair procedures 
 
Surgical intervention for AAA repair can be treated either with open surgery or endovascular 
surgery. The specific treatment is chosen based on the patient’s anatomy, pathology, availability 
of competences4. 
 
Open surgery Repair (OR) 
 
As implied these surgeries include laparotomizing the abdomen, which requires the patient to be 
fully anaesthetized. When access to the abdomen is established, the aorta is localized and 
clamped to block flow to the aneurysm. Reparation is then conducted by opening the aorta, 
removing clots and installing an implant that bypassed the diseased vessel. The implant is 
anastomosed in both ends of the aorta to existing healthy tissue, before closing the aorta around 
the implant and reintroducing flow. This procedure is mainly taught with apprenticeship training in 
Denmark4 
 
==PAGE:16==
16 
EndoVascular Aortic Repair (EVAR) 
 
EVAR has become a great part of AAA repair, currently covering 33% in Denmark as seen in the 
figure in the beginning of this section. The procedure is minimally invasive compared to OR, since 
everything is performed through access points in groin on either side. As mentioned earlier the 
most common placement is below the kidneys close to the bifurcation into the iliac arteries. The 
prosthesis’s used are made of specialized materials that allows it to be folded, inserted through 
the access sheaths and unfolded and adhered to the vessel wall. The procedure of implanting is 
done by building the prosthesis by modules; starting at the neck of the aneurysm with a large 
module which has two “legs”. These legs are then extended with further modules and eventually 
adhered to the common iliac artery. The end result resembles a pair of pants within the vessel 
occluding flow to the aneurysm5. 
 
Percutaneous Transluminal Angioplasty (PTA) 
 
Also known as angioplasty or balloon angioplasty covers a procedure to alleviate vascular 
claudication, i.e. the revascularization of obstructed flow. This is done by maneuvering a balloon 
to the stenosis section of the artery and inflating it to push the lumen to its natural size over the 
course of several inflations lasting 30-60 seconds. The procedure may also include stenting to keep 
the lumen to size and shape6. 
 
Endovascular intervention as assisting tool 
 
In resent years endovascular interventions have started to become part of surgeries outside the 
circulatory system. An example is the blocking of flow to critical areas while undergoing surgery. 
An embolization can be introduced endovascularly to alleviate severe hemorrhage in patients with 
pelvic fracture7. In surgical treatment of spinal metastasis, a preoperative embolization has been 
shown to shorten surgery time and reduce intraoperative blood loss8.

Chapter 2: Medical Simulation and training 
Medical Simulation Classification 
 
Currently, a simulation is used in medical and surgical education. Just as in pilot training, education 
of surgical skill includes increasing amounts of simulation.1,2,3,3. Some simulators imitate the 
human body very precisely, i.e. SimMan (high fidelity) and other teach specific skillsets without 
similarities to the human body (low fidelity), i.e. basic vascular anastomosis training on flow 
phantoms4. Some simulate many parts of a complex procedure and others selected parts of a 
more complex procedure. This section covers how simulators can be classified.

with a four to six point scale (Appendix 1).  The simulations as seen

objectives and give an overview that can be helpful in reaching them.

At the Surgicon Project conference in Göteborg 2013, Maxim D. Gorshkov presented a 
classification system built on top of Alinier’s and Gaba’s theories with a system of layers7.7. These 
were also correlated to cost and cover what Gorshkov referred to as the triplication rule as seen 
below; providing an exponential relation between fidelity and cost. He proposed that each level 
increased fidelity of the simulation. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Both Alinier’s and Gaba’s classification systems for simulations are based on the situational 
experience of the participant. Gorshkov’s classification is similar to Alinier’s, but is based on the 
features of a given simulation device. 
 
Features and Layers 
 
All of the previously mentioned classification schemes touch upon the term “fidelity”. Both to the 
fidelity describing the resemblance to a clinical patient treatment and to the fidelity of the 
hardware involved. Since fidelity can be used to describe a simulation’s part elements, setting and 
more without enabling the comparison, the term is arguably flawed8. To explore classification 
further this section will propose to elaborate Gorshkov’s layers with feature descriptions in a 
simulation device.  
 
Analogue and digital

mannequins, computer-based, or computer controlled mannequins. The devices can be divided in 
two categories; physical or virtual simulator modalities - I will refer to them as Analogue or Digital. 
A mannequin that can be inspected would classify as analogue; where screen-based or computer 
simulation would classify as digital. Computer controlled mannequins classify depending on the 
interface of the task involved. If the mannequin simulates breathing, eye movement or corporal 
movements, and the task is to physically interact. i.e. chest compressions, the simulation is

Realism/Fidelity 
 
In literature describing simulation, the degree of realism is usually ranged on a scale of “similarity 
to real scenario”. This is commonly referred to as High or Low Fidelity. Studies comparing the 
training efficiency of the two has shown that the level of fidelity does not correlate to clinical 
performance output9, one study found that the learning was significantly better with simulators 
focusing on specific tasks8.  
In literature describing simulation the degree of realism is 
usually ranged on a scale of “how close to real scenario”. This 
is commonly referred to as High or Low Fidelity. Studies 
comparing training efficiency of the two have shown that the 
level of fidelity does not correlate to clinical performance 
output9.

As Alinier describes simulation classification, a level 0 is reading a 
book and level 5 is a comprehensive simulation set in an

device feature, it can be set on a scale of simple to comprehensive.

Price 
 
The price of a simulator can be made up in various ways. Acquiring a simulator is the most obvious 
cost. Following Gorhskovs Triplication rule7 degree of fidelity is the leading factor in this. 
Maintenance, simulation space, and instruction are among other factors than cost. With the need 
for experienced surgeons to make simulation training for interns effective,10 poses potential costs 
in the clinical absence of the same surgeon. Balancing price with educational value is a complex 
task both for a possible customer and for a developer.

Description

The arterial map on the cardboard background gives a clear indication of the training scenario in

As argued in previous chapter, endovascular treatment is a relevant area to investigate for training 
and educational needs. This section therefore assesses unmet needs in simulation-based training, 
interesting potentials and future needs. 
 
Needs study 
 
Nayahanagan LJ et al. has in 2017 conducted a Delphi study to 
identify relevant target areas for medical simulation in vascular 
surgery training and education. This included a  
“list of 19 technical procedures in vascular surgery that should be 
integrated in simulation based curriculum” see appendix 4. Of these 
19 points, four regards endovascular procedures. All the needs of 
these four categories can be seen in the table on the right. Some 
points here are that the availability of analogue simulators is 
currently scarce. The market for endovascular training is dominated 
by high fidelity digital simulators, level 4 and 5 on Alinier’s 
classification scheme. Each need was assessed with a score, 
depending in clinical frequency, potential impact on patients, doctors 
needed in a department to fill the needs, and the feasibility of 
learning the skills in simulation. The latter was assessed by a panel of 
experiences vascular intervention doctors. 
 
The need to train Percutaneous angioplasty (PTA) of iliac arteries and 
basic skills with surgery tools scored the highest score of the endovascular procedures. This 
procedure is the most performed endovascular procedure in Denmark, accounting for 57% of all 
endovascular procedures1. 
The second highest regarded endovascular training need is “basic skills”. These range from 
maneuvering the tools in a vessel to operating the fluoroscopy and training radiation safety. Thereto 
comes EVAR, which accounts for 9.7% of the endovascular procedures11 and training of more 
advanced skills.  
 
 
Educating vascular surgeons 
 
According to the Danish plan for positions as specializing doctors within the field of vascular 
surgery, the amount unfilled positions are 29%2. Introducing medical students to vascular 
simulation early on has been proved to increase interest in the field3. If the amount of 
endovascular procedures keep rising and the amount of specialized doctors does not follow the 
planed dimension, simulation has the potential to increase interest in the field.

At Technical University of Denmark, a group of researchers investigate ultrasound flow imaging 
(Center for Fast Ultrasound, DTU Electro). Here computer simulations are compared with scans of 
physical ultrasound phantoms. The models are built from CT-scans to emulate the human anatomy

3D modelling 
 
This section describes the basics of 3D modelling and how it has been used to fulfil its place in the 
production line successfully. 
 
Basics modelling 
 
Representing data in three dimensions can be in a variety of ways. In 3D medical imaging, the data 
is stored DICOM files, as a series of two-dimensional matrices. Together these make up a three-
dimensional point cloud where the coordinate of a data point represents a place in space. In 
computer graphics and 3D printing, the handling of data is done with surfaces. Surfaces are made 
up of connected points. Modeling surface data can be by manipulating these points and 
connections. 
 
Requirements 
 
For our production line, there are two requirements. 
1. Converting segment to surface data. 
Enabling 3D printing by changing the data format to the relevant structure. 
 
2. Post-processing of data 
Ensuring quality of new data format 
 
Methods 
 
1. Data converting: 
As the DICOM data is represented as a point cloud or voxel, and 3d modelling and printing are 
done with surface data files known as STereoLithography (STL) files, it is necessary to convert the 
data. The standard method for this is called “marching cubes”1 translates the connected cubes in a 
binary data matrix to a surface.  
 
2. Post processing: 
The output of conversion needs to be post-processed to become of printable quality2. In the figure 
below the 3D surface to the left is the converted medical segment to surface data and the figure 
on the right is the post processed 3D model. The surface was smoothed  and the number of 
surface elements was updated in a remesh, to achieve a homogenous structure. The post 
processing can be done with 3D modelling software, in this case, it has been done with the 
software MeshMixer. Specifics of smoothing are argued by different sources, but rescanning 3d 
prints from smoothed segments have shown promising results2,3.

3D printing 
 
The 3D printing technology is also known as additive manufacturing (AM) and covers multiple 
technologies of producing three-dimensional structures layer by layer. This subsection describes 
the basics; the requirements for the specific place in the production and how they have been 
tested and met during the project. 
 
3D printing Technologies 
 
Hereunder, the four most common 3D print technologies and materials are briefly introduced1: 
 
Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) also known as Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF) 
 
FDM or FFF utilizes filaments of thermoplastics. A heated nozzle softens the materials and prints a 
layer on a heated built plate. This technology offers a variety of materials and the four most 
common are mentioned below: 
 
PolyLactic Acid (PLA) 
PLA is a biodegradable polymer. It offers great print quality for the price. Being printed at low 
temperatures (180-220 degrees Celsius) ensures a low degree of deformation.  
 
Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS) 
ABS, is a synthetic polymer. It is famous form injection molding fabrication and is one of the most 
used plastics today. LEGO is produced of ABS plastic. The large industry adabtation to this material 
has ensured a very low price. The material requires relatively high temperatures when 3D printing. 
Therefore, it is also prone to warping, when producing large objects. 
 
High Impact PolyStyrene(HIPS) 
HIPS is often used as a support material for ABS prints. HIPS is dissolvable in the natural terpen D-
Limonen, which ABS is not. 3D manufacturing with multiple materials where one is dissolvable 
enables the construction of complicated objects. HIPS prints at 230-260 degrees. 
 
PolyVinyl Alcohol (PVA) 
PVA, not to be confused with PVA cryogel is commonly used as support material printing with PLA. 
It is a very attractive printing material since it is water-soluble. PVA could potentially be the easiest 
material to remove from a cast. The material is, however, challenging to print, since it works at 
very specific temperatures. At low temperature the material is very brittle and at temperatures 
exceeding 215 degrees it burns to tare. Only certain FDM/FFF printers are capable of printing this 
material and even so printing PVA objects is challenging. Thereto PVA also has a higher price than 
other materials. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
32 
Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) 
 
Selective laser sintering is a production grade 3D printing method. It covers a building platform 
with layers of nylon powder, solidifying specific areas by laser sintering, before covering with next 
layer.  
 
Stereolithograhy (SLA) 
 
Stereolithography uses photopolymers and laser light. Much like SLS, SLA technology uses laser 
light to solidify a print material. Here the manufacturing is done upside down, where the build 
plate is lifted up of a container with photopolymer, and a laser underneath focusing and solidifying 
the object layer by layer. 
 
Material Jetting, commonly known as Polyjet1. 
This is the current frontier of what AM is able to do today. The material is UV cured polymers and 
it is possible to produce objects in a variety of colors and hardness. The techniques involved are 
similar to old inkjet printers, where droplets are sprayed. Polyjet does however also cure the 
droplets with UV light.

2. Manufacturing large structures. 
To make the large structures printable on 3D printers with smaller build volumes, the segment, 
which is between 50 and 60 cm is cut into 10 cm pieces. These pieces are all fitted with square plugs 
and sockets to make them assessable. 3D printers are not 100% accurate, and each is different and 
depends on current condition and material. A test print of four sizes of sockets and plugs were made 
in ABS, PLA and PVA on the Zortrax M200 3d Printer to test general inaccuracies.  
The object printed in PLA and ABS was accurate within less than 0.06 mm or 0,15%. The attached 
plugs and indented sockets had inaccuracies of 0.16mm at each surface with a standard deviation 
of 0.04, independent of size. PVA had a larger inaccuracy of 0.23 mm with a standard deviation of 
0.05.  
Since the printing quality depends on the present maintenance condition of a given printer it does 
not make sense to make more tests or to generalize, as this could be a study in itself. The findings 
were implemented in the design of plugs and sockets as a tolerance of 0.3 mm between each 
touching surface for PLA and ABS and 0.5mm for PVA. 
 
3. Mold making 3D printing 
 
To obtain the best casting results with 3D prints as molds, there are a few challenges. To ensure a 
3D print is not filled with casting material, making it difficult or impossible to remove post curing, 
the prints are manufactured with 100% infill. Thereto, they are surface treated to reduce the risk 
of damaging the cast upon removal. The surface treatment also increases the quality of the final 
product leaving fewer ripples inside the canals in the cast. Appendix 5: Experiment 4 has 
documented the experiments conducted to enable surface treatment. ABS and HIPS are 
dissolvable in organic compounds such as acetone. Experiments with acetone fume chambers 
have been conducted to test the feasibility of surface smoothing large complicated structures and 
found that actively producing fumes was the most successful method. Producing acetone fumes 
does come with risks; It is toxic to breath and can be ignited. 
PLA is not dissolvable in acetone. It requires a stronger dissolving agent to smooth. Other methods 
for smoothing PLA include painting or using compounds such as Chloroform. 
PVA did as PLA not smooth with acetone. The prints of PVA fell apart where the layers were 
initially fused. Therefore, smoothing by application of PVA glue was tested. Depending on the 
smoothing casting material this deemed good results. Casting with water-based materials was, 
however, impossible with PVA prints altogether  
 
4. Removal of 3D prints 
 
Removal of prints after casting was experimented with by dissolving the prints. In Appendix 5: 
Experiment 6, it showed to be challenging to remove objects printed in ABS and cast in urethane 
rubber. PVA showed to be the most viable candidate since it could be removed with water. This 
can be seen in Appendix 5: Experiment 8.

Casting Tissue mimicking material 
 
The chosen design solution includes a tissue mimicking casting material. For the production to be 
successful, a list of requirements has to be met. This section describes the specifics of these 
requirements, the candidate materials, and testing of how these materials fit the requirements. 
 
Requirements 
 
To cater for the given requirements the following solutions  
1. Combinable with 3D printing 
The specific production chosen utilises casting around a 3D printed vessel and removing said 
vessel post-curing. The material has to be able to withstand the process of removing the larger 3d 
printed structure within. This can be done chemically, as done with ultrasound phantoms; these 
are cast in PVA cryogel, and the print is mechanically removed, sometimes aided by chloroform 
melting the print. 
 
2. Transparency 
The material has to be transparent to track the movement of tools inside the finished canals. 
Transparency need not be within the visible light spectrum, for example, visualization could be 
done with infrared light 
 
3. Elastic / low hardness 
By inspection of material samples in consultation with relevant medical staff a material hardness 
of durometer shore A 15 to A30 is chosen to be the goal. The shore scale is defined by pressure 
testing with a durometer with specific configuration depending on scale. To qualify as tissue 
mimicking the focus is on the haptic feedback and the experience of handling tools in the final 
product. 
 
4. Reasonably priced 
To ensure an affordable end product the cost of material is an essential factor. 
 
5. Durable 
The material has to be able to withstand the use of endovascular tools in a cast of a vessel. The 
final product also has to be reusable to ensure that multiple training sessions can be conducted on 
the same phantom. In the value proposition mentioned in the following chapter, this requirement 
will also be elaborated upon. 
 
6. Reasonable production method 
The production method should not require many high-tech and expensive equipment. A nontoxic 
material would be of preference.

Candidate Materials 
 
A series of materials have been chosen to test for compliance with the requirements. 
 
Natural gels 
 
Gelatin is used in a variety of fields to simulate human tissue. A typical simulation scenario is that 
of ballistics testing, where the substance has manifested itself as ballistics gel. It is commonly 
produced from bone and skin tissue from animals, but in the last years, synthetic versions have 
become commercially available.  
 
Agar-agar is another natural gel, which comes from algae. It is commonly used in the dessert 
kitchen, plates in microbiology, etc., and has similar qualities to Gelatin. Both materials are mixed 
with hot water to produce a gel. The amount of agar and gelatin can be regulated to obtain 
desired consistency. 
 
The qualities of gelatin and agar that makes it a candidate are: 
1. Cheap and available 
2. Transparent 
3. Reusable 
4. Modifiable consistency 
5. Non-toxic 
 
The primary objectives are to find if the material can endure the use of endovascular tools in a 
cast canal and designing a production method that allows for casting of large, complicated 
structures 
 
PVA Cryogel 
 
PVA Cryogel is a material with physical properties that make it very suitable for testing imaging 
methods such as ultrasound and MRI1,2. Current methods production methods vary in the curing 
phase and in whether or not it is cast in multiple stages to achieve multiple structures of different 
elasticity of the final phantom. The production also includes mixing graphite to increase the 
quality of acoustic properties even further, for enhanced ultrasound simulation.  
The production is done by mixing flakes of PVA in water at a temperature just below boiling. The 
mixing ratio is 10% PVA with 1% Silica gel and 89% water1. The term cryogel covers that the 
material cures through cycles of freezing and thawing.  
 
The qualities of PVA cryogel that makes it a candidate are:

Urethane rubber 
 
Urethane rubber is a very durable rubber material. It exists in a large variety each tailored for 
specific use cases. In our case, we need casting urethane rubber, which is two component. 
Available casting rubbers are Urethane or Silicone based and for our purpose Urethane is 65% of 
the price of matching silicone rubbers. The production is done by mixing two liquid resin 
components. After this, all the bubbles that have become trapped during mixing can be removed 
by placing the mix in a vacuum chamber. Lastly, the material is poured into the mold and left to 
cure for 24-72 hours, depending on the material requirements. 
 
Qualities 
1. Durable 
2. Transparent 
3. Lower price than silicone 
 
 
Silicone Rubber 
 
Silicone rubber is an elastomer and is commonly used in mannequins within the field of medical 
simulation. The material is very durable and can be modified in many ways to produce the right 
hardness or color of a phantom. Transparency is however innate to specific solar encapsulating 
casting silicones. Casting silicone rubber is as Urethane usually a two-component solution. Some 
silicones do however cure, via evaporation of a solvent, when in contact with air. After mixing the 
rubber is degassed, poured and left to cure for 24-72 hours. 
 
Qualities 
1. Durable 
2. Transparent 
3. Non toxic

Natural gels 
 
Gelatin and Agar was not able to tolerate the movement of endovascular tools. The cast samples 
fissured when pressure was applied with endovascular guidewires. 
The transparency was not as good as was first anticipated. It was possible to see the lumen with a

use of infrared camera the visual access was difficult to achieve.

PVA Cryogel 
 
The PVA cryogel was more cumbersome to produce than anticipated. It did, however, yield a very 
attractive result as to the feel of touch. It was nowhere near transparent, and all light was 
scattered when attempting to cast shadows from the tools in the canal. 
 
Urethane Rubber 
 
Transparent urethane rubber is available as a two-component solution, where the components 
are toxic to skin and lungs. Therefore it required chemical fume hood to produce. 
The degassing process was difficult to complete. The trapped bubbles did inflate, when the mix 
was put in a vacuum chamber, however many bubbles did not evacuate. I assume this is because 
of the surface tension of the material, combined with the relatively short pot life. 
 
Silicone Rubber 
 
The silicone had the desired ease of casting, transparency, durability. The price is high compared 
to all other candidates, but it covers all other requirements the best. The high degree of 
transparency allowed for further testing into fluoroscopy optimization. As proposed, the 
fluoroscopy can be achieved with a solution supplying visual access through a camera. Two factors 
posed a problem; The surface finish inside the vessel representation left a rough surface distorting 
the visual access and the concave shape of the surface skewed the visual access. 
Mitigating the surface roughness could be done with surface treatment of the 3D print before 
casting. The skewering can be mitigated by producing a liquid with the same refraction index as the 
silicone cast. This liquid was produced by mixing sugar with water and had very successful results. 
The structure of the 3D print visually disappeared when the liquid was introduced in the cast.

Chapter 6: Value Proposition 
The LumEndo Simulator 
 
During development and testing of production methods and materials, a business case was built, to 
seek funding for the project. This dubbed the project and the simulator “Lumendo” a wordplay on 
Lumen and Endo. The business case was built around producing low cost simulators with relevant 
features at relevant levels of fidelity. The specific use case was to eventually supply clinical surgeons 
with patient specific training. 
 
The Value Proposition and business case 
 
It is possible to develop a good patient specific simulator and cutting the cost by a factor of ten or 
more, compared to available Virtual Reality Simulators. Additionally, it is possible to make these 
simulators mobile and thereby available to more training settings and trainees. This could double 
or triple the amount of training hours on a given simulator. These two features alone can lower 
the price per effective training hour by a factor 30. This in turn enlarges the market, since many 
more simulation centers and medical educational institutions can afford such a simulator without 
the need for external funding. 
Simulation centers and educational institutions are great places to test and validate simulators. By 
conducting usability testing and comparing with available simulators it is possible to get validate 
the positive influence of a new simulator. These places are also commonly connected to a 
university hospital, with clinics performing endovascular surgery. This means that stepping from 
training in education into pre-surgical practice is a goal within reach.

Chapter 7: Evaluation and Conclusion 
Evaluation and testing 
 
In the evaluation of the simulator, there are three main things to test: 1) usability testing, 2) 
comparison to available simulators and literature, and 3) testing potential impact with pre-surgical 
practice. 
 
Usability testing 
 
Testing the usability of the simulator can be performed for each feature. One test is blind testing 
whether doctors experience difference haptic sensation between the new simulator and cadaver 
vessel, or between blood mimicking fluid and real blood. General usability testing would also 
include Design Failure Mode and Effect Analysis (DFMEA). 
 
Comparing with available simulators 
 
Comparing the new simulator with available ones, could help evaluate the strengths and 
weaknesses of the new simulator. This could be used to evaluate the value of analogue simulation 
compared with digital and the educational value of high price low access compared to low price 
easy access simulation. Specific comparisons could be comparing the Mentice VIST, at CAMES with 
the Lumendo simulator. This test could be done with the same patient anatomy in both 
simulators. Comparing with the products currently on the market, could be utilized to build a basis 
for advertisement and sales of the newly developed simulator. 
 
Potentials in patient specific surgical practice. 
 
As mentioned in Chapter 2 recent literature suggests a decrease in surgery time, when using 
patient specific pre-surgical training. In order to achieve this in reality requires extensive testing. 
Testing that can be compared with current research results as well as testing the possible uses for 
a variety of endovascular interventions. Evaluating the Lumendo simulator in comparison with 
commercially available simulators would also make it possible to compare results in patient 
specific surgical practice and cross validate results hereof. 
 
Other testing 
 
The Lumendo simulator has been developed around the simulation experience. Other 
endovascular analogue simulators, mentioned in the section on available simulation, focus 
primarily on the realistic conditions of pressure, flow, pumping and other physiology.

Usability testing of medical devices 
 
With the 2017/745 regulation of European Council new requirements for usability testing of medical 
devices enter into force the 26th of May 2020. These apply for devices already certified and devices 
on the way to market2. Because of this there is a growing need for simulation that can be used to 
test devices. Devices are operated by people and cannot be simulated with digital simulation. For 
endovascular surgery the market dominators with digital simulations are unable to provide usability 
testing, since their products show a virtual version of a tool and are not able to test the real world 
use. Much of the usability testing currently performed is done (so) on animals which is a 
controversial matter. Many EU countries have regulations limiting the possibility for animal testing. 
Therefore device testing is often done in one of the few countries where it is possible. With an 
increase in need for physical simulation and limited availability of current methods there is a need 
for new ways to perform usability testing. Interest is already growing for physical simulators that 
mimic real patient scenarios. Companies wish to future proof their products. In the near future 
analogue simulations are going to be required for certifying all medical devices in EU. This poses 
another big potential market for simulators as the Lumendo simulator.

Classification in simulation 
 
The scientific classification schemes used for medical simulation in this report was not made for 
evaluating simulators as devices3,4. The articles describing these classification schemes are well-
cited papers within the field of medical simulation education. Other articles have also mentioned 
this problem of simulator devices being ranked by fidelity alone5. Fidelity is a one dimensional 
describtion used for all simulators to describe what Gaba did not capture with his 11 dimensions. 
The term is used scientifically incoherently about many different features of a simulatos.  
There is a potential for future work in evaluating medical simulators as devices. This could include 
the realism of the interactions with a device haptically, visually and interface, i.e. with realistic tools. 
Such a classification would potentially also require analyzing and mapping the elements of the 
clinical task simulated. As the section on medical simulation classification described, some 
classifications are multidimensional, some are linear, and some are based on the layers included in 
a simulator. The surgical interaction with a patient is very different in endovascular surgery and in 
open surgery. Therefore a classification scheme for simulation devices would need to evaluate only 
necessary features. Endovascular surgery is done primarily looking at fluoroscopy, therefore 
simulators like the Laerdal SimMan, contributes little to the specifics of training of endovascular 
intervention. It does however rank as the highest level on the Alinier’s scale because one can treat 
the doll as a patient. The Mentice VIST, fills a more concrete place in training of endovascular skills, 
by enabling training of procedural skills and clinical teamwork. By analyzing the Lumendo simulator 
by features in the classification schemes, it could potentially resemble the Mentice VIST. However, 
the different focuses on haptics and procdures do make the two simulations distinctively different.  
Using the classification schemes by Alinier and Gaba to compare any EV simulators with simulators 
for other procedures, could potentially create even more confusion. Future work in simulation 
classification could potentially enable better comparison and ranking of simulations. I would 
propose this could be done by analyzing features in simulators across medical fields and 
decomposing the clinical tasks into categories that could be better assessed. This could potentially 
enable a better understanding of how knowledge transfer as well. 
 
Automatic skill assessment 
 
With learning and training follows skill asessment. In traditional schooling, this means books and 
exams, but there are no broad-spectrum standards for medical simulation. Current literature on 
endovascular training and simulation training evaluate by fluoroscopy, amount of contrast used, 
and the duration of surgery. Strøm et al. developed a scale for evaluating competence in EVAR 
procedure6 called EVARATE. EVARATE is a rater based method, where a training session is 
assessed on seven different points by an expert. It would be attractive to produce a technical 
solution to measure competence, without requiring expert evaluation. This could be potentially be 
achieved with solutions similar to what Svendsen et al. described for colonoscopy7, or what Colella 
et al. described for Bronchoscopy8.
