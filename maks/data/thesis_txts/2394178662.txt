Abstract
The objective of this thesis is to present a set of depth graded W/Si multilayer mirror
coatings for high energy grazing angle telescopes, that are optimized to extend the
capabilities of the telescopes to the hard x-ray and soft γ-ray spectrum. The mirror
coatings are optimized through numerical analysis, using the modelling software IMD
to simulate the reﬂectivity of the multilayers, with a Metropolis-Hastings algorithm
to determine the optimal values of the coating parameters based on various ﬁgures of
merit. Two theoretical telescope optics with radii of 0.05 m - 0.75 m and focal lengths
of 20 m and 50 m, are designed and applied with the mirror coatings.
The mirror
coatings are evaluated by comparing the eﬀective area of the telescopes with that of the
probe-class HEX-P telescope. The eﬀective area of the telescope is simulated with the
ray-tracing software McXtrace for both on-axis and oﬀ-axis incidence for a more realistic
performance evaluation of the mirror coating designs.
A mirror coating recipe is found for each of the telescope designs that, on the theoretical
calculations of the eﬀective area, performed on par or better than HEX-P. Simulations
of the 20 m focal length telescope indicate that the conical approximation of Wolter
Type I telescopes become unreliable at high energies, causing the eﬀective area of a true
Wolter Type I telescope to be less than that of a conical approximation. It is found for
the 20 m focal length telescope that at 200 keV, the diﬀerence in eﬀective area between
a simulated true Wolter Type I and theoretically calculated conical approximation is
as much as 20%. The oﬀ-axis simulations at 5 arcminutes and 10 arcminutes angles
unexpectedly display similar eﬀective areas at low energies, although higher oﬀ-axis
angles lead to more losses at high energies. At 200 keV, an 81% loss in eﬀective is seen
at 5 arcminutes angle, and 92% loss is seen at 10 arcminutes angle.
The on-axis eﬀective areas of the telescopes show by comparison to HEX-P that it is
possible to optimize W/Si multilayer coatings to achieve a desirable eﬀective area for
long focal length grazing angle telescopes in the hard x-ray and soft γ-ray regimes. The
deviation of the conical approximation from true Wolter Type I could have potentially
large eﬀect on similar projects in the future.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The last few decades have seen a stark increase in the number of space missions employ-
ing grazing incidence telescopes[1] to study the x-ray universe. The mirror technology in
the grazing angle telescopes utilize the total external reﬂection (TER)[2], which occurs
when x-rays strike a surface at suﬃciently low angles, to focus the x-rays towards the
detectors. Wolter Type I grazing incidence telescopes such as Chandra X-ray Observa-
tory (CXO)[3], XMM-Newton[4], and Suzaku[5] have made great contributions to the
study of the E<10 keV spectrum, with the newly proposed ATHENA[6] telescope by
ESA set to signiﬁcantly further this area of research. The high energy photon spectrum
above 10 keV on the other hand, has historically been much more diﬃcult to study due
to the considerable decrease in x-ray reﬂectivity at high energies.
In recent years however, the development in reﬂective mirror coatings has provided
a solution to measure the high energy x-ray spectrum, and the 2015 space mission
NuSTAR[7] has already taken advantage of this technology to extend to an impressive 80
keV. The solution is depth graded multilayer coatings[8], which are stacks of alternating
layers of heavy and light material, where the thickness of the layers decreases with
depth. Whereas a regular periodic multilayer would provide a very high reﬂectivity at
a speciﬁed energy, deﬁned by the D-spacing of the layer through Bragg’s law, a depth
grading provides a high reﬂectivity over a broad spectrum by having the individual layers
tuned to diﬀerent energies. Tungsten and silicon have emerged as key materials in the
1
==PAGE:15==
Introduction
2
development of depth graded multilayers due in part to small interface imperfections
between the materials [8], and in part because the material combination is found to
be robust in withstanding the space environment; an important quality for spacefaring
technology. Furthermore, W/Si has already been found to present high reﬂectivity up
to 70 keV where the tungsten K-absorption edge lies, and has been utilized as coating
material for the outer rings of NuSTAR[9].
1.2
Motivation
Hard x-ray (5-100 keV) telescopes that extend well into the soft γ-ray spectrum are
particularly of interest for studying the active galactic nuclei (AGN) through dust and
gas clouds where only high-energy radiation can penetrate[10]. The study of AGN is
vital to our understanding of black hole evolution and the role of black holes in shap-
ing the large-scale universe. As it stands now, models suggest that the most obscured
black holes represent a key stage in black hole growth, calling for the necessity to extend
our telescopes further into the hard x-ray and soft γ-ray spectrum. The mass accre-
tion surrounding AGN is likewise important for our understanding of how a black hole
shapes the local universe, but as of now there are only eight supermassive black holes
with a thoroughly studied spin, making the catalogue severely lacking. Hard x-ray mea-
surements from up-scattered microwave background radiation in conjunction with low
energy radio waves are interesting as they allow for the mapping of intracluster mag-
netic ﬁelds, which have information about galaxy mergers and accretion. Lastly, the
hard x-ray and soft γ-ray spectrum allow for study of Ni decay in supernovae through
the 158 keV γ-ray line, as well as the 511 keV line that appears due to positron-electron
annihilation, which is still not well understood[11].
1.3
Aim of Thesis
This thesis will present a set of W/Si depth graded multilayer mirror coatings, which will
be optimized for the explicit purpose of bringing grazing angle Wolter type I telescopes
further into the hard x-ray and into the soft γ-ray spectrum. The coatings presented in
the thesis are optimized using numerical solutions, which provide a signiﬁcantly faster
==PAGE:16==

3
approach to optimization than analytical solutions. Mirror coatings are simulated us-
ing the reﬂective ﬁlm modelling software IMD, and optimized in combination with a
Metropolis-Hastings algorithm for time eﬃciency. The overall aim of optimizing W/Si
depth graded coatings for high energy telescopes, will be separated into six minor goals
as follows:
1. Design a set of telescope optics with large radius (0.05 m - 0.75 m) and focal
lengths (20 m and 50 m respectively) which the coatings can be applied to.
2. Design a McXtrace ray-tracing simulation of the telescopes, and utilize it to assess
their focusing properties.
3. Design mirror coatings recipes for each telescope, which optimizes the eﬀective
area up to 200 keV and 600 keV energies respectively.
4. Calculate the theoretical on-axis eﬀective area of the telescopes with their respec-
tive coating recipes, and evaluate the performance of the coatings by comparing
the eﬀective area to that of NASA’s HEX-P[10] telescope.
5. Simulate the telescopes with their respective mirror coatings to determine a more
realistic on-axis eﬀective area.
6. Simulate the eﬀective area for various oﬀ-axis incidences to estimate the perfor-
mance change under these conditions.
1.4
Content of Thesis
The thesis contains six chapters including the introduction, theory chapter, and conclu-
sion. The remaining three chapters each describe a phase in the project where one or
more of the above goals are accomplished. The chapters following the introduction are
as follows:
Chapter 1 is the introduction, brieﬂy discussing the motivation behind the work done
in the thesis, the aim of the thesis, as well as outlining the individual chapters.
Chapter 2 brieﬂy discusses the theory necessary to understand the work in the project.
The ﬁrst section in the chapter brieﬂy discusses x-ray reﬂection from surfaces, and the
==PAGE:17==

4
second section describes the function of a Wolter Type I telescope, and the geometrical
equations necessary to design one. The third section touches upon the theory behind
Metropolis-Hastings algorithms, which is a key aspect of the coating optimization in this
project. The last two sections in the chapters brieﬂy describe the modelling software
utilized in the thesis.
Chapter 3 is phase 1 of the project. In this phase, the geometry of the Wolter Type
I telescope optics is designed using the equations laid out in the theory chapter. Two
telescopes optics are designed, one with 20 m focal length and one with 50 m focal length.
The focusing properties of the telescopes are evaluated using a ray-tracing simulation.
Chapter 4 is phase 2 and comprises the main work of the project. This phase is about
designing and optimizing the mirror coatings for the telescopes designed in phase 1.
Three recipes are designed in total for the two telescopes, each optimized with respect
to diﬀerent energy spectra. The theoretically calculated on-axis eﬀective area of each
recipe is presented, and compared to the eﬀective area of the HEX-P telescope which
likewise feature a focal length of 20 m.
Chapter 5 is phase 3 of the project. Here, the telescope simulation implemented in
phase 1 and the mirror coating recipes designed in phase 2 are combined for a more
realistic evaluation of the performance of the coatings. The simulated eﬀective area of
one of the recipes for the 20 m focal length telescope is presented, for both on-axis and
oﬀ-axis ray incidence.
Chapter 6 is the conclusion, where the results from the three phases are summarized
and further prospects of the work in the described in the thesis are discussed.

Chapter 2
Theory & Method
The aim of this chapter is to describe the theory necessary to understand the work
done in the thesis. The ﬁrst section will describe the relevant theory and equations of
x-ray reﬂection, leading up to how to calculate x-ray reﬂections from multilayers. The
equations and principles will not be manually applied at any point, but are included in
order to better understand how the simulation software performs its calculations. The
second section outlines the key equations that deﬁnes the geometry of a Wolter Type I
telescope, which is the basis for the designing of the telescopes in chapter 3. The third
section brieﬂy discusses method for designing an exponential depth graded multilayer.
The fourth section discusses the theory behind the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm, which
is a tool used extensively in chapter 4 to optimize the mirror coatings. Understanding
the theory behind the algorithm is important to justify its usage. Sections ﬁve and six
brieﬂy discuss the simulation software utilized in this thesis.
2.1
X-rays
X-rays are highly energetic electromagnetic waves, classiﬁed by an energy between 100
eV and 100 keV, with energies below 5 keV being referred to as soft x-rays and energies
above 5 keV being referred to as hard x-rays. X-rays can be described by the com-
plex form of their electric ﬁeld E(r, t) and their magnetic ﬁeld H(r, t), given in three
dimensions by the equations below[2].

electric ﬁeld that is parallel to the plane of incidence, while the electric ﬁeld of s-polarized
waves is perpendicular to this plane.
2.1.1
Refractive Index and Total External Reﬂection
From classical optics, it is understood that when a ray of light propagates from one
medium to another, it changes direction based on the diﬀerence in refractive index n
between the two media, as described by Snell’s law[2].
cos(α) = n cos(α′)
(2.2)
While refractive indices for visible light are typically between 1 and 2, the refractive
index of a medium for x-rays is notably diﬀerent.
As x-ray frequencies are usually
higher than all transition frequencies in atoms and molecules [2], the refractive index in
the x-ray region is less than unity. Assuming absorption in the medium, the index of
refraction at X-ray energies can be written as a complex number [12]
n = 1 −δ + iβ
(2.3)
with the deviation from unity δ being in the order of 10−5 in most materials, and the
imaginary part being much smaller than that. From Snell’s law, it can then be inferred

2.4
Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm
The Metropolis-Hastings algorithm[19] is named after Nicholas Metropolis, co-author
of the paper ﬁrst proposing the algorithm, and W. K. Hastings, who made signiﬁcant
improvements towards making the algorithm work in more general cases. At heart, it is
a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) technique for generating random samples from
a distribution for which direct sampling can be diﬃcult. The algorithm borrows from
Monte Carlo theory by sampling from the posterior, meaning that the choice for each
new proposed state is aﬀected solely by the current state.
The general function of the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm is to start at a point θc in the
sample space, containing the initial values of each variable. The algorithm then proposes
a new point θ∗generated at random from the proposal distribution Q(θ∗|θc) which is
determined by θc, and depending on the value of θ∗, either accepts this new point or
rejects it. If the new point is accepted, θ∗becomes the new starting point (θc = θ∗)
for the next proposal, and the algorithm starts over.
If the proposal is rejected, θc
remains the starting point (θc = θc) and the algorithm must generate a new proposal
and determine whether it is accepted or rejected.
The proposal distribution Q (also commonly referred to as the jump distribution), can
be either symmetric or asymmetric, but is most commonly symmetric so that Q(θ∗|θc)
= Q(θc|θ∗). This can be achieved by making the proposal distribution Gaussian, so that
Q(θ∗|θc) = N(0, σ) where the variance σ is ﬁtted to the algorithm.
The acceptance probability α of a proposed state needs to be ﬁt in order to satisfy two
criteria: (1) It needs to be high enough for the algorithm to reliably move around in
the entire sample space. (2) It needs to be low enough for the algorithm to congregate
towards the high density regions of the sample space. Chris Sherlock and Gareth Roberts
proved in 2009[20] that under suitable conditions, the optimal acceptance rate for a
random walk Metropolis with multiple variables is 0.234, but as long as the acceptance
rate is not too close to either 0 or 1, the algorithm will perform adequately. There are

likelihood function that determines the value of the proposed steps, typically a chosen
ﬁgure of merit; (4) An acceptance function that determines the acceptance probability
of the proposal, and decides whether to accept or reject.
2.5
Modelling Software
2.5.1
McXtrace
McXtrace[21] is a Monte Carlo ray tracing software package for optimizing x-ray beam
instrumentation and performing virtual x-ray experiments [21]. Originally developed for
x-ray tomography, McXtrace provides a ﬂexible and open structure that allows the user
to easily change the structure of the simulated optics. For the purposes of simulating
the telescope designs in this project, ﬂexibility in a simulation program is valuable, as
changes in design can be introduced as further development takes place.
The optical setup of an instrument in McXtrace is described in a Domain Speciﬁc Lan-
guage (DSL), and the description is then automatically translated into C-language to
be compiled and executed. McXtrace physical structures are denoted components, with
each component has its own structured ﬁle with code, which describes the interaction
between the component and an x-ray. A component should at the very least be de-
scribed by its optical parameters and spatial location, and McXtrace will then handle
coordinate transform and transport of x-rays between components.
==PAGE:28==
Theory & Method
15
The telescope implemented in this thesis is represented by two components, one for the
paraboloid structure and one for the hyperboloid structure. Each of the two structures
have their own component structure ﬁle. The full template exists in the instr data type,
which is a special ﬁle type within McXtrace, where the components for a simulation
are set up and their input variables declared. In the remainder of this thesis, any time
a components position is described by a set of coordinates (x, y, z), the unit of the
coordinates are assumed to be in meters.
2.5.2
IMD
The software utilized to simulate the reﬂectivity of the multilayer coatings is IMD[22],
built in the Interactive Data Language (IDL) programming language. IMD is a mod-
elling software built speciﬁcally for estimating optical properties of multilayers, and
provides the user with extensive options in terms of deﬁning multilayers (periodicity,
size, roughness, angle of incidence, etc.), x-ray sources (wavelength, polarization, etc.),
as well as options in the desired output (reﬂectivity, transmittance, etc.). IMD also has
features for data analysis, with options to plot or ﬁtting functions to experimental data.
The key feature of IMD in this thesis is the FRESNEL function of IMD, which is based
on the application of the Fresnel equations to determine reﬂectivity and transmittance,
modiﬁed to account for surface imperfections.

Chapter Summary

this thesis. A point-by-point summary of the chapter is as follows:
• The refractive index of x-rays is less than unity, an aspect that separates x-rays
from lower energy light.
• Total external reﬂection occurs for x-rays at suﬃciently low angles which are de-
scribed by the refractive indexes deviation from unity.
• Reﬂection and transmission from a single layer can be described by the Fresnel

angles.

3.1
Optical Geometry Design
This project will focus on two particular telescope designs, both Wolter Type I. Both
feature a radius of 0.05-0.75 m, and focal lengths of 20 m and 50 m respectively. These
sizes and focal lengths are quite large compared to current space missions, but needs
to be large in order to maximize the eﬀective area.

large projected area of the telescope, and large focal lengths ensure low grazing angles,
both resulting in increased eﬀective area. The ﬁrst design, with a focal length of 20
m (henceforth referred to as D20), will feature three telescope modules for increased
eﬀective area. This choice is made for better comparison with the Wolter Type I HEX-
P telescope which likewise feature three modules. The second design, with a focal length
of 50 m (henceforth referred to as D50) will feature only a single module.

3.2
Test of the Focusing Properties of the Telescopes
With the three core radii Rp, Rm, and Rh determined, in section 3.1 the telescope ge-
ometries can be implemented in simulation software to determine that the telescopes are
functioning before moving on to designing mirror coatings for them. Furthermore, the
simulation setup built in this section will be utilized later on to simulate the combination
of telescope and mirror coating. The telescopes will be implemented in the ray-tracing
simulation software McXtrace, and the test will be to determine that the focal points of
the telescopes are in the expected places, 20 m and 50 m behind the telescopes respec-
tively. If the focal points are at the expected lengths and focus is suﬃciently small to
encompass only a single pixel in any realistic detector, the telescopes will be deemed as
working properly. The McXtrace code for the telescope geometry is found in appendix
B.
3.2.1
Simulation Setup
The simulation set up consists of four parts: An x-ray source, the telescope itself, and
two imaging detectors. The parameters of the individual parts are as follows.

to be high enough to allow for hits in all shells of the telescopes.
2. Telescope: Placed with its center at global coordinates (0, 0, 10). Global coordi-
nates need to be suﬃciently far from the source that the rays do not spawn inside
the telescope.
3. Detector 1: Height and width of 4 × 10−11 m, 501 × 501 resolution, placed at the
focal point (0, 0, 10 + Z0). Detector needs to be small enough to capture when the
focus starts to break up.

3.2.2
Results and Discussion
From the images in ﬁgures 3.2 and 3.3, it is seen that both telescopes display good
focusing properties, as the focus does not break up until we reach a scale of 10−11 m,
which is much smaller than what any realistic detector can separate. What stands out
on the images is the halo of hits around the center. These hits are likely the result of
stray light [26], light that is lost from the focal point due to reﬂecting either too few
times or too many times in the telescope. The halo from D50 shown in the bottom of
ﬁgure 3.3 indicates that the halos are made up of several much thinner halos, which
stack to make up the full halo. Each of these smaller rings is likely the stray light of a
speciﬁc shell in the telescope.
As the stray light is eﬀectively a loss in regard to telescope performance, the presence

Solutions by Numerical Analysis
With the telescope optics designed and tested in phase 1, we can begin phase 2; designing
and optimizing the W/Si mirror coatings for the phase 1 telescopes. The goal in this
phase is to achieve as high eﬀective area at high energies as possible for the telescopes
by using numerical analysis to optimize the mirror coatings. To this end, optimization
of the coatings will be based on various ﬁgures of merit (FOM) that in turn are based
on the reﬂectivity of the mirror coatings, as eﬀective area is directly proportional to the
square of the reﬂection amplitude.
Before the true optimization process can begin however, the number of optimization
parameters must be reduced to increase time eﬃciency. In section 4.1 is done a brief
analysis of the diﬀerent methods for layering in mirror coatings in order to determine if
there is an optimal method which can be locked in for future use. Section 4.2 demon-
strates the impracticality of using a bruteforce algorithm for optimization due to time-
ineﬃciency, and section 4.3.1 utilizes a Metropolis-Hastings algorithm to determine if
any further parameters can be locked in. Once the number of parameters is suﬃciently
low, the true optimization of the mirror coating recipes using the Metropolis-Hastings
algorithm will begin in section 4.3.2. The ﬁnal section of the chapter, section 4.4 will
brieﬂy summarize the chapter.
Determining the recipe for the mirror coating will be done analytically, using the IMD
Modeling and Analysis of Multilayer Films software to simulate the reﬂectivity of various

4.1
Material Layering Methods
In this section, ﬁve well-known methods for layering materials in x-ray mirror coatings
will be tested in order to determine which method performs best at high energies. The
section is not meant to give a thorough investigation of the various methods, but rather
to give a brief assessment so that a preferred method can be chosen for further use in
the project.
4.1.1
Simulation Setup
The ﬁve methods of layering that will be tested in this section are:
– Single layer: A single layer of heavy material.
– Bilayer: A single bilayer consisting of a heavy material at the bottom and a light
material on top.
– Periodic Multilayer: A multilayer consisting of multiple, stacked, bilayers.
– Linear Depth Grading: A linearly depth-graded multilayer, where the bilayer
thickness decreases linearly from the top.
– Exponential Depth Grading: An exponentially depth graded multilayer, where
the bilayer thickness decreases exponentially from the top.

4.2
Numerical Method: Bruteforce Algorithm
Optimizing an exponentially depth graded coating necessitates that many diﬀerent pa-
rameters must be considered due to the complexity of this layering method.
These
are Dmin, Dmax, c, Γ and N. Given this many independent parameters, testing each
combination of parameters manually is nigh impossible, and so a computational algo-
rithm that performs the optimizing can be utilized instead. The purpose of this section
is to demonstrate the possibility of utilizing a speciﬁc computational algorithm, the
bruteforce algorithm, to optimize the coating recipes. The bruteforce algorithm is the
simplest optimization algorithm imaginable, making it a reasonable starting point. The
==PAGE:43==
Phase 2: Mirror Coating Solutions by Numerical Analysis
30
bruteforce method works by going through the parameter combinations one-by-one with
a given step-size, and calculates the FOM for each combination.
The particular bruteforce algorithm developed for this project only considers four of the
ﬁve parameters, as N is, for the sake of simplicity, ﬁxed at a constant value of N = 300
where the highest FOM was observed in section 4.1. The algorithm is implemented in
the software language IDL, and calculate the RA using the FRESNEL function in IMD.
It then calculates the FOM as the sum of all RAs from 10-600 keV for D50, and 10-200
keV for D20.

4.3
Numerical Method: Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm
Although a bruteforce algorithm is an improvement from manual search, the high number
of parameters to consider in optimizing the recipes warrants a faster method as shown
in section 4.2. One particularly faster method is the Metropolis-Hastings maximization
algorithm described in section 2.4.
This algorithm features an intelligent statistical
method to localize the maximum of the likelihood (the FOM in this case) and as such
wastes little time with low FOM recipes. A custom Metropolis-Hastings algorithm has
been implemented in the IDL programming language, and uses the FRESNEL equation
==PAGE:46==
Phase 2: Mirror Coating Solutions by Numerical Analysis
33
of IMD to calculate the reﬂectivity of a given recipe. The likelihood function of the
algorithm for each telescope design is its corresponding FOM. The acceptance rate has
been multiplied by a factor of 1/3, in order to force the acceptance rate towards 0.3 to
increase eﬀectiveness of the algorithm, as explained in section 2.4. The jump distribution
is a normal distribution centered at the current step of the algorithm, and the standard
deviation of each parameter is set to reﬂect the interval of the parameter. A run with
the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm will last until a speciﬁed number of ”steps” has been
taken, where a step means that the algorithm jumps to new values for the parameters.
Before moving on to using the algorithm, there is one important point to note.
As
discussed in chapter 3, the telescopes are designed from a theoretical ideal, in that all
the mirror shells are unique which is not feasible from a practical point of view. In the
ideal scenario, each individual mirror shell would be equipped with a unique coating
optimized for that particular shell. In the project we wish to consider the practical
limitations to ensure a more realistic and feasible set of results which can be employed
in real world. For this reason, the 350 mirror shells of the telescopes will likewise be
separated into 10 groups containing 35 shells each, wherein all mirror shells in a given
group will use the same coating recipe. The number of groups chosen is based on other
telescopes such as NuSTAR (10 groups[29]) and ATHENA (20 groups[30]). The groups
are arranged such that group 1 contains shells 1-35, group 2 contain shells 36-70, and so
on. For further simplicity, the coating recipe for a given group will be optimized only
with respect to the middle shell of a group, but be applied to all shells in the group.
4.3.1
Reducing the Number of Parameters
Exponential depth graded multilayers are fairly complex in structure compared to other
types of layers, and necessitates that a large number of parameters must be considered
when optimizing a recipe using this layering method. The purpose of this section is to
explore the possibility that any of the ﬁve parameters Dmin, Dmax, c, Γ and N, can be
locked to a ﬁxed value thereby simplifying the optimization process. The Metropolis-
Hastings algorithm will be used to determine a large number of high FOM coating recipes
for group 1 of D20, in order to identify if any of the parameters have a distinctive value
where maximum FOM occurs.
If it is found that a parameter has a speciﬁc value
where the FOM is generally higher, the parameter can then be locked to this value for
==PAGE:47==
Phase 2: Mirror Coating Solutions by Numerical Analysis
34
consequent algorithm runs, thereby removing the need to further optimize with respect
to this parameter. Although the test will only be done for one group of the D20 telescope,
the ﬁndings will be generalized to all remaining telescope groups in order to spare the
time required to perform similar tests for each individual group. It would be reasonable
to assume a small loss in performance of the coating due to such a generalization, but the
groups are suﬃciently similar that a high FOM area for group 1 should also give relatively
high FOM for group 10. Any small loss incurred by this generalization will therefore be
negligible in comparison to the advantage of a simpliﬁed optimization process.

minor goals.

energies higher than 70 keV, and the advantage of D50 was shown to increase
throughout the spectrum.

5.2
The Conical Approximation Deviation
5.2.1
Theory
In ﬁgure 5.3 is illustrated an arbitrary shell with the conical approximation and the true
parabolic and hyperbolic shapes (although greatly exaggerated), along with an on-axis
ray for visual eﬀect. The point of the illustration is to show how the incidence angle of
a ray coming in on-axis will diﬀer depending on where on the parabolic and hyperbolic
structure it hits due to their curvature.
Figure 5.3: Illustration of the parabolic and hyperbolic mirror shells (exaggerated)
and their respective conical approximations. A ray is added for visual eﬀect.
Rays hitting closer towards the center of telescope where the two mirrors meet will
reﬂect with angles closer to the α angle that deﬁnes the conical structure. rays hitting
the top part of the parabolic further from the center, will due to curvature reﬂect at a
smaller angle, but will then hit the hyperbolic structure further down where the angle
is larger. Due to the smaller reﬂected angle from the parabolic, such a ray will then also
have a higher angle of incidence upon the hyperbolic, resulting in further net increase
in angle of incidence. Mathematically, this can be explained as such: An on-axis ray
is incident on parabolic structure with angle θp in = αp −δp where αp is the conical
approximation angle, and δp is a perturbation due to curvature. For the parabolic, the
perturbation decreases the angle and is therefore added as a negative. The ray will
reﬂect from the parabolic with the angle, θp out = 2(αp −δp) with respect to the original
axis of propagation, and thus be incident on the hyperbolic with the angle
θh in = αh + δh −2(αp −δp) = αh + δh −2αp + 2δp
(5.2)
==PAGE:63==
Phase 3: Simulation of Telescope with Mirror Coating
50
Here αh is the angle that deﬁnes the conical approximation of the hyperbolic, and δh is a
perturbation due to curvature. Although the ray will see a small increase in reﬂectivity
at the parabolic structure due to lower angle of incidence from the perturbation, from
equation 5.2 it can be seen that the ray will experience a large increase in angle of
incidence in the hyperbolic due to both of these perturbations. A change in angle of
incidence has more signiﬁcant impact on the reﬂectivity at higher energies, which can
explain why the deviation of the on-axis simulation in ﬁgure 5.1 diﬀers more from the
theoretical calculation at high energies.

Chapter 6
Conclusion & Further Work
6.1
Conclusion
The aim of the thesis was to present a set of exponential depth graded W/Si multilayer
mirror coatings, optimized speciﬁcally to extend the capabilities of Wolter Type I tele-
scopes to the hard x-ray and soft γ-ray regimes. The overall aim was separated into six
minor goals such that by accomplishing each of the minor goals, the overall aim would
be achieved as well. Each of the minor goals were then accomplished in one of the three
phases of the project.
In phase 1 of the project, two telescopes were designed with radii of 0.05 m - 0.75 m, and
focal lengths of 20 m and 50 m respectively, and the focusing properties of the telescope
designs were tested using ray-tracing simulations.
The results from the simulations
showed that the focus does not start to break up until we reach scales of 10−11 m, thereby
demonstrating good focusing properties. The simulations also displayed presence of stray
light, likely due to simpliﬁcations made in the design process.
Three mirror coating recipes were designed in phase 2 of the project, with two of the
recipes being made for the 20 m focal length telescope, and one recipe being made
for the 50 m focal length telescope. Recipe A for the 20 m focal length telescope was
optimized in the 10-200 keV spectrum, and was shown to have a theoretically calculated
on-axis eﬀective area comparable to that of the probe-class telescope HEX-P, and even
having a slight advantage throughout the spectrum. That the coating recipe is able to
compete with latest coating technology developed by NASA, is a strong indication of
53
==PAGE:67==

54
good performance by the coating recipes developed in this thesis. Recipe B for the 20
m focal length telescope was optimized for the 100-200 keV spectrum, and while the
on-axis eﬀective area shows an overall poorer performance than recipe A, recipe B has a
roughly 40%-50% eﬀective area advantage from 140 keV onward. The coating recipe for
the 50 m focal length telescope was shown from the theoretical on-axis eﬀective area to
outperform both recipes for the other telescope at all energies. The eﬀective area at 600
keV for the 50 m focal length telescope is even larger than the eﬀective area at 200 keV
for the recipes for the 20 m focal length telescope, thereby demonstrating the possibility
of using long focal length telescopes to extend to 600 keV.
The 20 m focal length telescope with recipe A was simulated both on-axis, and oﬀ-
axis with 5 arcminutes and 10 arcminutes angle in phase 3, and the eﬀective area was
calculated from the results of the simulations.
The on-axis simulated eﬀective area
shows a small deviation at low energies, that becomes larger with increasing energy.
This was explained in the thesis to be partly because of stray light, and partly because
of the conical approximation of Wolter Type I telescopes becoming unreliable at high
energies. Further investigation into the exact eﬀect of each of these phenomena would
be interesting, but was conducted in this project due to time constraints.
The oﬀ-
axis simulations show ﬁrst and foremost an initial drop in eﬀective area, which can be
explained by an increasing rate of stray light, and then a continued performance decrease
that scales with oﬀ-axis angle. The initial drop in eﬀective area was shown to be similar
for both oﬀ-axis angles, meaning that increasing oﬀ-axis angle has little inﬂuence on the
eﬀective at low angles.
To conclude the project: Although the oﬀ-axis simulations display unexpected behavior
particularly for low energy x-rays, the theoretical calculations of the eﬀective areas of
the telescopes, as well as the on-axis simulation, demonstrate that is possible to optimize
W/Si mirror coatings to allow grazing angle telescopes high eﬀective area in the hard
x-ray and soft γ-ray regimes. Additionally, it was found in this project that the conical
approximation of Wolter Type I becomes unreliable at high energies, causing the eﬀective
area of a true Wolter Type I to be lower than that of a conical approximation.

6.2
Further Work
The work done for this thesis is preliminary in nature. Although the techniques and
methods used to design the telescopes and mirror coatings discussed throughout have
been previously studied, the ﬁnal designs are unique to this thesis, and the application
of the designs is not a thoroughly explored subject. The preliminary nature of the work
means that there are several relevant and interesting ways to continue the work, which
were outside the scope of this thesis. Below are shown the ones that I would consider
the most interesting.
Determining the course of the low-energy phenomenon in oﬀ-axis simulations
The oﬀ-axis simulations in chapter 5 displayed unexpected behavior at low energies,
where the further increase in oﬀ-axis angle has little-to-no eﬀect on the eﬀective area.
Determining the course of this behavior was not performed in this thesis, but would be
a relevant subject to investigate.
Determining the exact eﬀect of the conical approximation deviation
The thesis has shown that past the tungsten K-absorption edge at 70 keV, the conical
approximation starts to quickly become unreliable, causing the eﬀective area of a true
Wolter Type I to become increasingly worse in comparison to the conical approximation
eﬀective area. The eﬀect of the deviation is substantial enough that it should be con-
sidered for all future Wolter Type I telescope designs aimed at the same high energies
as D20 and D50, and determining the precise eﬀect of the deviation would be highly
relevant in anticipating this error.
Determining the on-axis eﬀective area of D50 from simulations
Due to time constraints, it was not possible to perform simulations of D50 in order to
determine the more realistic eﬀective area. This would be particularly interesting for
D50, where it can be assumed that the eﬀect of the conical approximation deviation
would be even larger as D50 is designed to extend to 600 keV.
Evaluating more recipes with diﬀerent FOM
Only three optimal recipes were designed in this thesis, but with more time it could be
investigated whether recipes optimized with a diﬀerent set of FOM, could reach higher
eﬀective areas for the telescopes. Tangentially, it would be interesting to see if recipe B
for D20 could achieve higher FOM with more optimization time.

Making the telescope designs more realistic
It was mentioned in the thesis that the telescope designs are unrealistically ideal as all
350 mirror shells are unique which is unfeasible to fabricate. It would be interesting
to redesign the shells to take fabrication limitations into account, and determine the
performance of the redesigned telescopes for more realistic results.
