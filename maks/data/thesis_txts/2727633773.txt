Abstract
This thesis concentrates on exploring the buckling load factor (BLF) and stiffness of hierarchical
structures, particularly those incorporating 5 and 8-element cells and increasing levels of hierarchy.
Additionally, it aims to measure the potential performance improvements gained through the use
of size, shape, and combined optimization methods.
Initially, the physical system is modeled using truss or beam elements. The accuracy of these
models in predicting BLF is assessed against Euler’s buckling equation. A thorough parametric
study then identifies the optimal geometries for the 5 and 8-element cells, considering the aspect
ratio and hierarchical order. When examining these cells, the optimal hierarchical order is de-
termined by the aspect ratio of the structure’s longest bar. It was discovered that the combined
optimization approach yields the most significant improvement in BLF. Moreover, the introduction
of an adaptive checking scheme assures accurate sensitivity information during optimization iter-
ations. This study shows that hierarchical cells can significantly boost a structure’s buckling load,
especially when sections undergoing local buckling maintain an aspect ratio less than 0.1.
The insights derived from the design of hierarchical structures can contribute to creating safer
truss-like structures, such as towers or bridges. These structures can endure higher buckling loads
while maintaining equivalent weight. The findings also have potential applications in modeling and
optimizing hierarchical metamaterials across various length scales.
Keywords: Hierarchical structure, Size and shape optimization, Buckling optimization, Truss
optimization, Beam optimization

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Nature is a perilous place, teeming with fierce competition as organisms continuously struggle for
survival throughout history. To secure an edge, both the natural world and humans persistently
adapt and evolve. Consider bamboo, an extraordinarily resilient plant, and the fastest naturally
occurring bio-composite material, renowned for its high strength-to-weight ratios (Youssefian and
Rahbar 2015). It ascends vertically towards the sky in a relentless pursuit of sunlight, outperform-
ing any competitors. Similarly, engineering designs vie for superior performance, for example
competing to build the highest building. The Eiffel Tower, effectively utilizing hierarchical truss
structures, managed to hold the title of the tallest building until 1930, see the cover photo of this
thesis.
Hierarchical structures are favored by both the natural world and humans, serving as a tool to
secure advantages in competition by improving mechanical properties like strength, toughness,
resistance to buckling, robustness, and damage tolerance (Meza et al. 2015). The basic under-
standing of a hierarchical structure: implies that structural features manifest on multiple length
scales. As a result, hierarchy exists across multiple scales. This is evident in natural materials
such as wood, tendons, and bamboo. Take human bones, for example, they possess outstanding
damage resilience and lightweight properties (Meyers et al. 2008). These attributes seemingly
emanate from complex mechanical networks that encompass multiple hierarchical levels. This
principle of hierarchical structuring paves the way for the development of novel microstructures,
potentially exhibiting unusual mechanical properties such as a negative Poisson’s ratio. On the
macro scale, hierarchical structures support extensive constructs like the Eiffel Tower, space an-
tennas, and bridges, while preserving a lightweight build. The Eiffel Tower, being a third-order
hierarchical structure, has a relative density ρ
ρ0 that is 800 times less than iron (Lakes 1993).
The inherent properties of hierarchical structures, characterized by numerous slender elements in-
terconnected within a sparse framework, lend themselves to a beneficial approach. It is proposed
that the use of line elements such as truss or beam elements for the construction of systems
that represent hierarchical structures is an efficient strategy. When compared to solid elements
used in finite element analysis, truss or beam elements demand significantly fewer parameters for
description and computation. The optimization of truss-like structures, subject to local or global
stability constraints, has been a topic of extensive research over the years (Kočvara 2002; Guo,
Cheng, and Olhoff 2005; Torii, Lopez, and Miguel 2015; Weldeyesus et al. 2020) among others.
This thesis presents an innovative approach to merge hierarchical structures with size and shape
optimization, offering fresh inspiration for designing new meta-materials or multi-scaled structures
optimized for stiffness and buckling load.
The ground structure approach is the most commonly used methodology for the design of truss-like
structures (Dorn, Gomory, and Greenberg 1964). This approach encompasses a design domain
partitioned into a network of nodal points, interconnected by provisional bars. The complete or
partial assembly of these potential bars is referred to as a ground structure. The goal typically
involves minimizing material usage while maximizing performance. However, this technique often
encounters the challenge of ground-structure dependency (Kočvara 2002), a problem that is al-
most inevitable in truss design. One way to alleviate this effect is to utilize highly detailed ground
structures or growth-based methods (Martínez, Martí, and Querin 2006), among other strategies.
However, it’s important to note that the use of growth methods can disrupt the hierarchical struc-
ture. Hence, in this thesis, when optimizing hierarchical structures, the number of elements is kept

constant, and the design variables are confined to the cross-sectional area and nodal positions of
the joints.
Optimizing for a lightweight structure often results in more slender formations, which are sensi-
tive to buckling. Buckling analysis is a critical component of structural mechanics, as it explores
the stability of structures under compressive loads. Consequently, enhancing buckling loads is
prioritized in conjunction with size and shape optimization for truss-like hierarchical structures.
The objective of this thesis is to explore and optimize the stiffness and buckling strength of hi-
erarchical structures through size and shape optimization. The design domain is predominantly
macro-scale, thus making Finite Element Analysis (FEA) a fitting tool. The study concentrates
on improving the performance of space truss and frame systems, particularly in terms of their
resistance to buckling.
1.2
Research Objective and Approaches
The primary objective of this thesis is to analyze how the performance of hierarchical structures,
in terms of compliance and Buckling Load Factor (BLF), changes when combined with shape and
topology optimization. This analysis takes into account variables such as the cross-sectional area
of elements and the positions of nodes. The study explores the relationship between buckling
strength and hierarchical order. The ultimate goal is to enhance the structure’s resistance to
buckling.
This work aims to answer the following questions:
• How does the stiffness and BLF performance trend behave as we increase the structural
hierarchy while maintaining a constant volume?
• What degree of enhancement in stiffness and BLF performance can be realized through the
implementation of size optimization, shape optimization, and their combined use within a
hierarchical structure?
• Is there an optimal hierarchy level for achieving the best stiffness or BLF?
In this thesis, Finite Element Analysis (FEA) is deployed to simulate the physical system, with the
use of truss and beam elements. Size optimization is employed to alter the cross-sectional area,
whereas shape optimization is leveraged to change the structural form.
1.3
Outline
This thesis is organized into seven chapters. The first chapter serves as an introduction, and it
outlines the basic hierarchical cell element definition and the strategies for optimizing the buckling
load factor. In chapter 2, we introduced the finite element formulation for both truss and beam ele-
ments, considering buckling, accompanied by a few examples discussing the accuracy of buckling
when using these element types. In chapter 3, we conduct a detailed parametric study on perfor-
mance variation with different designs of the basic hierarchical cell and hierarchical order before
applying optimization. Chapters chapter 4 and chapter 5 contain detailed formulations for both
size and shape optimization using truss or beam elements, aimed at optimizing for compliance
or buckling load factor. In chapter 6, we showcase three examples that have been enhanced by
hierarchical cells and optimization. The final chapter concludes the thesis and suggests potential
future research directions.

The general mathematical way to describe a structure optimization problem is shown in eq. (1.1).
It contains three major parts, the objective function, design variable, and constraints.
• Objective function (f): A function for categorizing design variations. For each conceivable
design, the function yields a value signifying the design’s overall quality and effectiveness.
In the context of this thesis, it may represent either the compliance or the buckling load
factor (BLF), which are used to evaluate the performance and stability of the design.
• Design variable (x): A function or vector characterizing the design and modified throughout
the optimization process to refine and enhance structural overall performance and efficiency.
For truss members, the design variables can be the relative element densities ρ, cross-
sectional areas A, and the positions of the nodes.
• Constraints
• Equilibrium constraint: This indicates that the system is examined under specific phys-
ical conditions. The equilibrium constraint is the governing equation for the finite ele-
ment system. For the elastic response of truss structure, we have Ku = p.
• Design constraint: Each design variables are usually constraint with: lower bound (lb) ≤
x ≤upper bound (ub).
• Behavioral constraint: The general form can be written as g(y) ≤0, where g denotes
a function that represents, overall truss volume, compliance, or BLF. y represents the
state variable that describes the response of the mechanical system, such as displace-
ment, stress, and strain, providing insight into the system’s behavior under various
conditions Christensen and Klarbring (2009).
1.5
Three Types of Structural Optimization Problems
Structural optimization focuses on arranging a collection of materials to support loads in the most
efficient and effective manner, enhancing the objective value. Now considering the truss structure
with three types of structural optimization.
• Sizing optimization: The design variables are chosen to be the cross-sectional area A for
each truss element, which helps optimally distribute the material throughout the structure.
• Shape optimization: The design variables are chosen to be the nodal position for each
truss element, which adjusts the location of all truss elements to achieve optimal positioning.
• Topology optimization: The design variables are chosen to be the relative density for each
truss element. It selects truss elements that take the most loads and removes the redundant
truss elements.
The primary distinction between topology optimization and size optimization lies in the penalization
factor and the design variables’ range. In topology optimization, the penalization factor is typically
greater than 1, and the design variables are allowed to approach zero, effectively removing some

elements from the structure. In contrast, size optimization generally does not employ a penaliza-
tion factor greater than 1, and the design variables are not allowed to go to zero, maintaining the
elements’ presence within the structure.
1.6
Optimization Algorithms
To address the structural optimization problem delineated in eq. (1.1), which involves many de-
sign variables, this thesis employs three distinct optimization algorithms: Optimality Criteria (OC)
Methods, fmincon with Interior-point methods, and the Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA), ba-
sic information can be found in appendix A. It’s crucial to note that the primary focus of this thesis
isn’t to probe the disparities among these optimization algorithms. As such, only fundamental for-
mulations are elaborated, while in-depth insights can be obtained from the referenced literature.
1.7
Hierarchical Structure

Hierarchical structures are made up of struc-
tural elements that themselves possess struc-
ture, frequently referred to as self-similar struc-
tures. In a 0th-order hierarchical structure, the
material is seen as a continuum. A first-order
structure may represent a lattice framework
composed of continuous ribs or the atomic lat-
tice of a crystal. For higher orders, hierarchical
structures begin to exhibit fractal-like charac-
teristics. Such fractal-like designs are found
in natural materials, such as shells and bones,
and have attracted significant attention due to
their mechanical robustness and damage tol-
erance (Lakes 1993).
At the macro scale,
the Eiffel Tower serves as an example of a
third-order hierarchical structure, as depicted
in fig. 1.1.
In the paper ”Some Performance Trends in Hi-
erarchical Truss Structures” by Murphey and
Hinkle, the authors discovered that the four
longeron truss-columns shown in fig. 1.2a,
which were subjected to both local and global
buckling constraints, were typically ten times
lighter than the corresponding 1st order truss-
columns and had a diameter that was two to
four times larger, as illustrated in fig. 1.2b. By
allowing the structure to expand its diameter
with increasing hierarchical order, a substan-
tial benefit is realized in terms of bending and buckling performance (Murphey and Hinkle 2003).

In a recent study, Lucas et al. conducted both experimental and numerical tests on nanolat-
tices designed using hierarchical principles. They observed a near-theoretical scaling of struc-
tural strength and stiffness with relative density fig. 1.3, surpassing the performance of existing
non-hierarchical nanolattices. Interestingly, they found that additional levels of hierarchy beyond
the second order did not contribute to an increase in strength or stiffness. This finding suggests
an optimal degree of hierarchy that maximizes resilience (Meza et al. 2015).
In the context of this thesis, the diameter of the structure does not exhibit substantial changes
for hierarchical orders beyond one. This is primarily due to the specific hierarchical element cells
selected, which will be demonstrated in the upcoming section. In practical scenarios, the size
restrictions imposed by boundary conditions may limit the degree to which a hierarchical structure
can expand its diameter.

Figure 1.6: Flowchart of building hierarchical structure
The process of constructing a hierarchical structure is demonstrated via a flowchart in fig. 1.6. The
cell types under consideration are the 5 and 8-element cells, with a desired hierarchical order (HO)
and an α angle. The 5-element cell comprises 4 nodes and 5 elements, whereas the 8-element
cell contains 5 nodes and 8 elements. Both types of cells have the same length L as the HO 0. As
the HO increases, each element in the original HO evolves into the corresponding 5 or 8-element

1.8
Optimization for Buckling Load Factors
Buckling analysis is a crucial aspect of structural mechanics, as it helps engineers understand
the stability of structures under compressive loads. When a structure experiences a compressive
force, it may undergo sudden deformation or collapse, known as buckling, even when the material
has not reached its yield strength. This phenomenon can cause unexpected structural failure and
can be dangerous in engineering applications.
Within the scope of structural mechanics, buckling is a type of buckling associated with the sudden
loss of stability of a given equilibrium configuration, and the switching to another one, triggered
by infinitesimal imperfections. An elementary example of bifurcation is provided by a straight
compressed column. When an axial compressive load reaches a critical magnitude (referred to
as the critical load Pcr), the straight pre-buckling configuration loses its stability. Consequently,
an alternative buckled configuration becomes possible at the critical load Pcr.
In this thesis, linear buckling analysis is specifically carried out on bar (truss element) and frame
(beam elements) structures for reasons of simpler implementation and computational efficiency.
Buckling is often categorized into local and global buckling or stability. Global buckling typically
occurs when the entire structure (or a large part of it) undergoes buckling. On the other hand, lo-
cal buckling is characterized by a small part of the structure buckling. The Euler column buckling
formula is the most common way to address local instability constraints on every single element.
Global instability often involves using the geometric stiffness matrix. However, global buckling
constraints are associated with many challenges in the context of topology optimization. In both
size and shape optimization, problems such as eigenvalue multiplicity (Thomsen, Wang, and O.
Sigmund 2018) leading to non-differentiability (Seyranian, Lund, and Olhoff 1994) and the exis-
tence of many local minima are encountered, posing serious convergence issues. During the
optimization, a large number of buckling modes are likely to become active and therefore must be
considered with an aggregation function (Ferrari, Sigmund, and Guest 2021).
Previous studies have been carried out using topology optimization with truss elements, consid-
ering both local and global instability constraints, such as those by (Guo, Cheng, and Olhoff 2005;
Weldeyesus et al. 2020), and others. Other research has used beam/frame elements, such as
(Torii, Lopez, and Miguel 2015; Mitjana et al. 2019) and others. In Torii’s work, he noted that
in certain cases, the Euler formula is not able to accurately model the structural behavior as in
reality, the endpoint of a bar can’t be described purely as either fixed or pinned. Furthermore,
topology optimization may remove some bars from the ground structure, thus redefining the effec-
tive buckling length of the bars (Zhou 1996). This effect can result in the algorithm miscalculating
the actual Euler buckling load, leading to a sub-optimal solution. Moreover, using the geometric
stiffness matrix and beam elements to perform global stability analysis considers local buckling
loads simultaneously, saving the need to use the Euler column buckling formula. In the follow-
ing thesis, the author starts with truss optimization considering global instability and moves on to
beam elements considering both local and global buckling constraints.

Beam Element
The beam model used in this thesis is based on the Euler-Bernoulli beam theory. A beam-column
element is typically a long, slender structural member subjected to transverse loading, which en-
tirely induces bending rather than twisting or axial effects. This model proposes the linear-elastic
behavior of the beam, assuming that plane cross sections are perpendicular to the longitudinal
centroidal axis of the beam before bending occurs remaining plane and perpendicular to the lon-

assumption when the beam’s length surpasses its largest cross-sectional dimension by a factor
of at least ten, suggesting that the aspect ratio show be smaller than 0.1. In this thesis, the aspect
ratio of an element is defined as the ratio of its width to its height.

In this chapter, hierarchical structures’ performance gets evaluated based on compliance and

4.1.2
Shape Optimization
In the context of shape optimization, the design variable is characterized by the nodal positions of
element degrees of freedom in the x and y axes, specifically xi, xj, yi, and yj. The total volume

total height and width of the structure. For instance, if the structure measures 1 by 1 meter, and
we allow a ten percent range for shape optimization, this implies that each node can move up,
down, left, or right by 0.05 meters.
4.1.3
Combined Optimization
In this work, a combined size and shape optimization is realized via simultaneous methods as pro-
posed by (Achtziger 2007). This method combines the design variables of cross-section area and
nodal positions into one vector and updates them simultaneously. The corresponding sensitivity
is obtained from the same process as size optimization and shape optimization.

The adjoint method is an efficient procedure used to calculate the gradient of an objective function
and constraints with respect to a large number of design variables. It is applied by solving two sets
of equations: the original (forward) problem and a corresponding adjoint problem. The solution
to the adjoint problem can then be used to compute the gradient of the objective function (f)
with respect to the design variables. The adjoint method is especially useful when the number of
outputs (objective functions) is significantly smaller than the number of inputs (design variables).
The computational cost of the adjoint method is independent of the number of inputs, yielding it
more efficient than alternative methods, such as finite difference methods, for problems involving
a large number of inputs like TO Cao et al. (2003).

Fundamental Buckling Load Factor
(BLF) Optimization
This chapter introduces the Buckling Load Factor (BLF) Optimization problem formulation, fol-
lowed by sensitivity analysis and two aggregation functions. It also presents examples employing
truss and beam elements. Most literature, including references (Guo, Cheng, and Olhoff 2005;
Torii, Lopez, and Miguel 2015; Weldeyesus et al. 2020), primarily considers the first BLF λ1 as a
global stability constraint. This thesis, however, takes into account multiple eigenvalues (BLFs)
using a KS-function as an aggregation technique, similar to the problem formulation presented in
(Ferrari, Sigmund, and Guest 2021), and optimizes for maximum BLF. This proposed formulation
accommodates the non-differentiability resulting from eigenvalue multiplicity, as evidenced by the
simultaneous modes observed in previous studies (Seyranian, Lund, and Olhoff 1994; Thomsen,
Wang, and O. Sigmund 2018).

A widely used method for handling numerous constraints is constraint aggregation. This process
merges constraints into one overall constraint using an aggregation function. By concentrating
on several merged constraints, the computing effort needed for sensitivity analysis is greatly de-
creased (Verbart, Langelaar, and Keulen 2017).

5.5
Summery of BLF Optimization using Truss Element
This section presents several insights from experiences with size and shape optimization using
truss elements, even though specific examples are not provided. As emphasized in section 2.3.5,
truss elements may not accurately predict the local buckling of geometries of interest. However, it
provides a well-defined optimization problem implemented as described in the previous chapter.
A few notable observations follow.
For truss elements, which do not carry moments, the requirement for a specific aspect ratio is not
applicable. Thus, any cross-section area may be chosen. Throughout the optimization process
for an 8-element cell, the initial count of required eigenvalues (neig) might become unsuitable as
the structure evolves. To ensure the MMA optimizer receives accurate sensitivity information, it
is essential to adjust the neig to align with the appropriate scenario for the 8-element cell. This
adjustment can avoid objective function getting exploded, and decrease oscillations in the con-
vergent history.
With shape optimization alone, there won’t be substantial differences in the optimal structure. This
is because any attempt to relocate a node will alter the volume, which is fixed in the constraints and
will therefore only cause minor changes. When considering a minimum length constraint (Lmin)
on the truss system, it has been noticed that at certain iterations, the optimizer tends to rotate one
of the truss members to align it with the rest, resulting in a singular stiffness matrix. One alternative
solution to this problem is to enforce box constraints on the nodes to prevent overlapping. How-
ever, as the HO increases and the truss elements shorten, the freedom for movement becomes
considerably constrained. Greater flexibility can be achieved by first implementing shape opti-
mization, then constructing the subsequent HO, reapplying shape optimization, and so on. When
combining size and shape optimization, the results demonstrate a substantial enhancement: the
Buckling Load Factor (BLF) can rise by over 300% in comparison to size optimization alone when
optimizing on HO 1.
Observations from higher HO truss structures indicate a significant number of repeated BLFs,
leading to a substantial number of repeated sensitivity values. This repetition could pose a chal-
lenge to the optimizer in finding the global optimum. Furthermore, the issue of aligned elements
becomes inconsequential (non-singular stiffness) for beam elements. The geometric stiffness
matrix can now account for both local and global bucklingTorii, Lopez, and Miguel 2015. These
reasons led to the decision to focus on beam elements, which are better suited for the 5 and
8-element cell setup targeted for optimization.

5.6.1
Eigenvalue Multiplicity
Given the high symmetry of the structure and the examination of the first 50 eigenvalues, it has
been observed that the occurrence of repeated BLFs (eigenvalue multiplicity) can lead to non-
differentiability. In the problem definition, the KS aggregation is used, making the objective function
differentiable. A critical point to note is that the number of repeated BLFs may change as the
optimization progresses. If fewer eigenvalues are aggregated than the number of repeated BLFs,
this can lead to incorrect sensitivities. To circumvent this issue, an adaptive checking scheme, as
shown in fig. 5.1, has been introduced. Here, neig represents the number of eigenvalues that are
solved, which is equivalent to the number of BLFs used in the aggregation KS function. In this
scheme, the difference between two eigenvalues less than 0.01 will be included. All optimization
cases in this thesis employ this scheme. In the subsequent examples, the initial value for neig
is set to 1, although different starting values were also tested. The optimization results appear
invariant by this value, given the proposed adaptive checking scheme. However, it is worth noting
that using too many eigenvalues should be avoided as it significantly increases computational
time at each iteration.

7
Conclusion
In this dissertation, a methodology for constructing a hierarchical structure at any hierarchical
order (HO) on any truss or beam mesh is presented. With minor modifications, this method can
incorporate any desired base unit cell to build the hierarchy. Moreover, size, shape, and combined
optimization approaches have been implemented for both compliance and Buckling Load Factor
(BLF) optimization, using truss or beam elements in a 2D setting. Moreover, a detailed parametric
study was conducted on the BLF performance concerning the geometric setup and the HO of the
predefined 5 and 8-element cells. Now, let’s address the questions proposed at the beginning of
this thesis.
• How does the stiffness and BLF performance trend behave as we increase the structural
hierarchy while maintaining a constant volume?
Answer: The performance trend greatly depends on the geometric setup and type of load-
ing. For a bar under axial compression, increasing HO will only decrease the stiffness.
However, if it is subject to transverse loading or moment, the structure can benefit from an
increased HO. However, this aspect is not fully investigated in this thesis. Regarding BLF,
it will keep increasing with HO, until the longest bar in the structure fails to meet the aspect
ratio requirement of 0.1.
• What degree of enhancement in stiffness and BLF performance can be realized through the
implementation of size optimization, shape optimization, and their combined use within a
hierarchical structure?
Answer: In this thesis, optimization is carried out only at the hierarchical cell level for HO
1 and 2 for 5 and 8-element cells. In these cases, the BLF gains most from combined
optimization, ranging from 20 ∼40% with constant compliance. However, the BLF can
decrease if the compliance constraint is tightened.
• Is there an optimal hierarchical order for achieving the best stiffness or BLF?
Answer: In terms of stiffness, the answer is similar to the first question, stiffness largely
depends on the geometry and loading type. As for BLF, there does exist an optimal hier-
archical order. From the parametric study presented in chapter 3, as long as the aspect
ratio of the bar is small enough (the length being at least ten times longer than the width),
there will be more gain in terms of BLF for 5 and 8-element cells, until the longest bar in the
structure fails to meet the aspect ratio requirement. In general, if the structure’s first buck-
ling mode is a local one, and the aspect ratio is satisfied, a 5 or 8-element cell can replace
it, guaranteeing BLF improvements. If the increased HO structure continues to exhibit local
buckling, then the above method could be applied iteratively.
In addition to the aforementioned findings, it was discovered that using beam elements for BLF
optimization is significantly more precise than employing truss elements. The application of the
geometric stiffness matrix enables calculation of both local and global buckling. It was also ob-
served that for each bar that might undergo local buckling, at least two beam elements are required
to achieve results with less than a 2% error. Considering the geometry of the 5 and 8-element cell,
the design angle α is the most crucial design parameter, with an optimal value existing for each
HO. The slimmer the HO 0 bar is, the smaller the optimal angle, and the greater the gain in BLF.
The existence of this angle is confirmed as the first BLF shifts. Regarding the BLF optimization,
an adaptive check on eigenvalue multiplicity is introduced to ensure that during all iterations of the
optimization, the optimizer receives accurate sensitivity information. The last example proposed
Optimization of hierarchical structures
47
==PAGE:58==
suggests that optimizing the initial design and applying hierarchical cells is more efficient than
attempting to optimize the hierarchical structure itself.
Reflecting on the initial project plan, a few aspects did not proceed as expected. For example,
the inclusion of geometry non-linearity, material non-linearity, and extension to 3D was not re-
alized. Indeed, the original plan was ambitious. As the project progressed, the first phase of
optimizing using truss elements took several weeks of exploration before it was concluded that
it was insufficient for BLF optimization. Moreover, the high symmetry of our chosen geometry
posed a challenge for BLF optimization, and fine-tuning parameters for the MMA optimizer took
longer than expected. Nonetheless, observing the fractal structure truly revealed the beauty of
nature. As Richard Feynman stated in his book ”The Character of Physical Law”: ”Nature uses
only the longest threads to weave her patterns, so that each small piece of her fabric reveals the
organization of the entire tapestry”.
7.1
Future work
Some possible future directions for research could include exploring other loading conditions for
the basic hierarchy cell, such as transverse loading or moments. Additionally, investigating more
types of basic cells could be interesting - for instance, introducing square cells that can increase
their aspect ratio as they grow in hierarchy order. Including material and geometric non-linearity
would offer a more accurate estimation of the physics involved in buckling events. Extending the
research to a 3D setting could open up avenues to explore torsion modes, which are non-existent
in 2D.
With regards to the optimization of the BLF, two potential improvements could be considered. First,
introducing a strategy for merging nodes, aligning colinear elements, and removing thin elements
could simplify the buckling optimization process. Secondly, design variables could be placed only
at the joints of the structure, rather than at each node. This approach would allow the hierarchical
cell to transform entirely into another cell through mesh refined nodes.

The Optimality Criteria (OC) method represents an effective and universally applicable strategy for
solving minimum compliance problems. The approach intuitively allocates material to structural
members based on each member’s strain energy, aiming to achieve a state of uniform strain
energy across all active bars. In this thesis, the OC method is exclusively applied for truss topology
optimization targeting minimum compliance.

A.2
fmincon with Interior-point Methods
The fmincon, an in-built optimizer in MATLAB, is designed to find the minimum of a constrained
nonlinear multivariable function. Although it has five distinct algorithms, this thesis utilizes the
default interior-point methods. This choice is in line with MATLAB’s recommendation for its ap-
plication in handling large, sparse problems as well as smaller, dense ones. Importantly, the
algorithm satisfies bounds in all iterations and possesses the capability to recover from NaN or Inf
results. Being a large-scale algorithm, it finds application in all optimization problems addressed
in this thesis (MathWorks 2023).
The interior-point method represents an effective strategy employed in mathematical optimization.
This approach is part of a broader set of algorithms designed to solve linear and nonlinear convex
optimization problems. The underlying concept of the interior-point method is to navigate through
the feasible region of an optimization problem in a manner that avoids the region’s ”boundaries”.
This method achieves this by transforming the original constrained problem formulation into an
unconstrained one through the use of barrier and penalty functions (Boyd and Vandenberghe
2004; Arora 2011).
