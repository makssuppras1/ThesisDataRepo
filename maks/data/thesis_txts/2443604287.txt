Abstract
Penicillium expansum is one of the most investigated fungal species due to its ability to produce
patulin in great amounts, especially during infection of apples. Annually, fruit spoilage caused
by P. expansum infection, exceeds 4.5 million dollars in USA alone. When ingested, patulin is
very harmful, and therefore governmental regulations have been outlined in multiple countries.
Several studies have tried to elucidate the role and importance of patulin during P. expansum
infection of apples, however, with contradicting results. Therefore, investigating a non-patulin
producing strain of P. expansum during its colonization of apples, is of high importance. In
this study, diﬀerent variations of Agrobacterium tumefaciens mediated transformation (ATMT)
protocols were tested on P. expansum to develop an eﬃcient protocol for targeted engineering
in Penicillium spp.. Furthermore, it was attempted to delete patK (essential gene in the patulin
gene cluster) in order to obtain a non-patulin producing mutant. However, the study concluded
that the transformation protocols tested did not result in a non-patulin producing strain. This
was mainly due to problems with the used batch of the fungicide hygromycin B used for selection
of potential transformants. The used hygromycin B batch was ruined possible due to wrong
storage conditions. ATMT with liquid induction medium seemed to be the best candidate for
obtaining transformants, and this will simplify previously described protocols of ATMT.
As no non-patulin producing mutant of P. expansum was obtained in this study, it was im-
possible to perform the model-investigations on the role of patulin during infection of apples.
Instead diﬀerent strains of P. expansum were investigated for their secondary metabolite pro-
duction on various media. The secondary metabolites patulin, chaetoglobosin A, roquefortine
C, communesin A, linoleic acid, aurantioclavine, andrastin A, and expansolide were detected in
all strains, but not detected in all media tested. Other secondary metabolites such as citrinin,
communesin B, ergosterol and chaetoglobosin C were detected in only a few of the investigated
P. expansum strains. For the IBT 34017 strain, YES seemed to be the best medium to get
the full metabolic picture, while for the IBT 5887 strain, PDA seemed to be best medium for
secondary metabolite production. The strains IBT 6096 and IBT 15305, produced most com-
pounds on CYA. These results show, that even though all the strains are P. expansums, they
still posses diﬀerences in their metabolic proﬁle, when they grow on the same type of medium.
Keywords: Agrobacterium tumefaciens mediated transformation, HPLC, UV-spectra, MS-spectra
Penicillium expansum, USER-cloning, PCR

Preface
In this study, diﬀerent variations of Agrobacterium tumefaciens mediated transformation (ATMT)
protocols were tested in order to develop an ATMT protocol suitable for targeted engineering
of Penicillium spp.. This was tested in Penicillium expansum ATCC 24692 IBT 34017, where
the goal was to delete patK, an essential gene in the patulin gene cluster of P. expansum, and
investigate the secondary metabolites produced using HPLC-DAD and UHPLC-MS. Obtaining
a non-patulin strain of P. expansum is of great importance to elucidate the role of patulin during
the infection process of P. expansum in apples. As revealed in the report, this study did not
succeed in obtaining a non-patulin producing strain. Instead, secondary metabolite proﬁles of
four P. expansum strains were compared and discussed.
This Master’s thsis was conducted at the Department of Biotechnology and Biomedicine at
Technical University of Denmark. The thesis accounts for 30 ECTS points, equal to 5 months
work, with supervision of Professor Jens Christian Frisvad, Lector Rasmus John Normand
Frandsen and Ph.D. student Andreas Møllerhøj Vestergaard.

1.1
Penicillium expansum in apple rot
Penicillium expansum is one of the most common food-borne, post-harvest fungal pathogens of
fruit. P. expansum is very often found in fruits such as apples and pears, but also on cherries,
peaches, apricots, plumes, vegetables, cereals, and several nuts (Tannous et al., 2018; Wright,
2015). The fungus is a huge concern in the apple and pear industry due to its association with
mycotoxin production and food spoilage. Losses due to growth of P. expansum in the fruit
industry have been estimated to exceed 4.5 million dollars annually in USA (Jurick et al., 2017;
Tannous et al., 2018). When P. expansum infects apples, it grows as blue mold. Blue mold is
a soft, brown and watery rot, with blue-green colored conidia and conidiophores, that develop
on the fruit surface (Jurick et al., 2017; Wright, 2015). P. expansum enters the fruit by wounds
on the fruit surface caused by punctures, bruises, or ﬁngernail scratches, often occurring during
harvest of the fruit. The fungus can also enter the fruit by naturally occurring openings such
as cracked lenticels, stem ends and open calyx (Tannous et al., 2018; Jurick et al., 2017).
P. expansum produces mycotoxins such as patulin, citrinin and chaetoglobosins (Andersen et al.,
2004). If P. expansum is not detected during storage of the fruit, the fungal contaminate will
be present and produce mycotoxins such as patulin during food storage and food processing.
These mycotoxins will then be present in the food products for human consumption (Tannous
et al., 2018).
Patulin is destroyed during fermentation processes, hence it is not found in
alcoholic beverages like ciders. However, patulin does survive pasteurization processes, and will
be present in fruit juices etc. for human consumption (White et al., 2006; Jurick et al., 2017).
Initially, patulin was screened as a treatment for common colds. Inhalation of the drug was
said to kill germs in the nasal area, but trials showed no eﬀect, and many of trial reports were
very biased (Clarke, 2006). Patulin is of particular interest in fruit industry, and has been
studied intensely (Tannous et al., 2018), due to its toxic eﬀects (Moake et al., 2005). When
consumed, patulin induces immunological, neurological, and gastrointestinal diseases leading to
serious health problems in humans (Li et al., 2015). Because P. expansum so often occur in the
fruit industry, governmental restrictions have been outlined. In Europe, the restricted limit of
patulin in fruit juice and processed fruit products is set to a maximum level of 50 µg/L (Jurick
et al., 2017).
In Penicillium spp. patulin functions as a quorum sensing inhibitor and an antibiotic (Rasmussen,
2005). Researchers have given various explanations of the role of patulin during P. expansum
infection of apples (Li et al., 2015; Ballester et al., 2015; Barad et al., 2014; Sanzani et al.,
2012), some with the conclusion that patulin has no role in P. expansum infection of apples
(Li et al., 2015; Ballester et al., 2015), while others state that patulin has a signiﬁcant role,
and that non-patulin producing strains of P. expansum are not able to infect apples (Barad

the role of patulin during P. expansum infection of apples.
1.2
The Genera Penicillium and identiﬁcation of species
The genus Penicillium is numerous and compromises around 450 recognized species (Visagie
et al., 2014). Penicillium spp. are generally soil fungi. They are anamorphic fungi and part of
the ascomycetes fungal group (Pitt and Hocking, 2009). Penicillium spp. are highly important
for the food industry, as they are used in the production of cheese (P. roqueforti in roquefort

food decay (Pitt and Hocking, 2009). Early studies by Garber and Beraha (1965) investigated
genetics and parasexual cycle of Penicillium expansun. Diﬀerent phenotypic mutant strains
were obtained by UV radiation or by the Mat method, originally developed for Aspergillus
fumigatus (Berg and Garber, 1962). In the study by Garber and Beraha (1965), the conidia
from segregant color sectors, or auxotrophic sectors, were then streaked on media to detect
crossing-over and examine the parasexual cycle. In another study Beraha and Garber (1965)
investigated growth of diﬀerent mutants of Penicillium spp. on apples (spp. Jonathan), to

1.3
The patulin gene cluster in Penicillium expansum
Patulin is a tetraketide mycotoxin. The biosynthesis of patulin consists of at least ten enzymatic
steps (Li et al., 2015). Li et al. (2015) and Tannous et al. (2014) identiﬁed a cluster of 15 genes
which are coupled to the patulin biosynthesis pathway of P. expansum. Both studies compared
the patulin gene cluster of P. expansum with that from Aspergillus clavatus. P. expansum is
one of the Penicillium spp. that is able to produce a great variety of secondary metabolites.
When comparing genomes and secondary metabolite clusters, Ballester et al. (2015) found,
that P. expansum had the highest number of secondary metabolite gene clusters. The gene
cluster for patulin in P. expansum was similar in many ways to the patulin gene cluster of
A. clavatus, but it was also found, that several other Penicillium spp., that are unable to
produce patulin, contained several genes of the patulin gene cluster.
From this, it can be
hypothesized that production of patulin is an ancestral trait of the Penicillia and other closely
related genera (Ballester et al., 2015). Figure 1 shows the patulin gene clusters for P. expansum
and A. clavatus, together with the non-functional patulin gene clusters of P. chrysogenum and
P. digitatum (Tannous et al., 2014). The patulin gene clusters confers the same genes, but the
gene organization diﬀers between the two strains. Dashed arrows in the ﬁgure indicate genes
not present in the patulin cluster.

2.1
USER-Brick vector construction system and USER fusion cloning
Uracil Speciﬁc Excision Reagent fusion cloning technique (USER fusion cloning) allows multiple
fragments (bricks) to be cloned together to design speciﬁc vectors intended for genome modi-
ﬁcations. The USER fusion system consists of a set of standardized building blocks for vector
construction, such as vector backbones, selection markers and promotors, that when combined
create a vector (Frandsen et al., 2012). The technique relies on the Uracil DNA glycosylase
and DNA glycosylase-lyase Ando VII to identify and excite 2-deoxyuridines from the ends of
the PCR-ampliﬁed fragments. Combined, the two enzymes remove the speciﬁc 2-deoxyuridines,
which creates a hole in the end of the double stranded DNA string. The short end of the DNA
string dissociates. This results in the introduction of 3’-sticky ends, creating unique overhangs
for each building block (DNA fragment/brick). Because the overhangs are unique, they allow
directional assembly of the fragments/bricks during the USER enzyme step, see Figure 4.
Figure 4: Assembly of the DNA fragments containing USER-tails (colored, sticky ends) to
plasmid.
The deoxyuridines are introduced into the DNA fragments by adding additional nucleotides
(will be the unique USER-overhang) inclusive a deoxyuridine to the 5’-end of the used PCR
primers. PCR is then used to amplify fragments from vector backbones, thereby creating the
DNA fragments, that, when treated with the USER enzyme mix, generates the unique single
stranded overhangs (Frandsen, 2015).
By amplifying DNA fragments and applying USER fusion cloning, three diﬀerent plasmids will
be constructed in this study. DNA fragments will be ampliﬁed from the pRF-HUE vector. The
pRF-HUE vector contains a constitutive Aspergillus nidulans gpdA promotor. It also contains an
antifungal selection marker cassette: The hygromycin B resistance gene hph originated from E.
coli, and controlled by the A. nidulans PtrpC promotoer and TrpC terminator (Sørensen et al.,

2.2

Transformation of P. expansum will be performed with Agrobacterium tumefaciens mediated
transformation (ATMT). Agrobacterium tumefaciens is a soil bacterium that functions as a
phytopathogen. It generates tumors in plants by its natural ability to transfer segments of
its Ti-plasmid (Tumor inducing), called T-DNA. The T-DNA integrates randomly into the
nuclear chromosome of the plant (Zhang et al., 2013).

The T-DNA is bordered by two directional imperfect 25 bp long direct repeats, known as left and

contains genes encoding enzymes responsible for formation of plant hormones (cause tumor
growth) and formation of metabolites that only the bacterium can degrade. These genes are
only expressed in the plant, and not in the bacteria (Frandsen, 2015).

As seen in Figure 7, the T-DNA-VirD2 complex enters the plant cell. Afterwards it receives
a protective coat, mediated by the VirE2 protein molecules, producing the T-complex. The
T-complex has a viral-like particle structure. It is compact, but still ﬂexible. Via the actin
cytoskeleton using myosins as molecular motors, cytoplasmic traﬃcking of the T-complexes
may occur.
The T-complexes are imported into the plant nucleus through pores generated
by interactions of nuclear localization signals encoded by VirD2 and several host factors (see
Table 2. Both host factors and Vir proteins may play a role in targeting the T-complex to the
plant chromosome, and release its bacterial and host proteins, possibly during or prior to the
integration process. It is thought that it is the host factors, and not the Vir proteins, that
mediate integration of the T-DNA into the plant chromosome. Several studies have tried to
identify the various host factors mediating the T-DNA integration, sometimes with very diﬀerent
and contradicting results. It is still yet to be solved, whether T-DNA molecules integrate in the
host via a single mechanism or by several mechanisms (Tzﬁra et al., 2017).
The A. tumefaciens mediated transformation was ﬁrst utilized in plants research, but in 1995,
the ﬁrst use of ATMT in fungi was described by Bundock et al. (1995). They worked with S.
cerevisiae. They showed that the ATMT was also suitable for genetic transformation in several
ascomycetes and a single basidiomycete (de Groot et al., 1998). In recent years, ATMT has
become increasingly popular as a transformation method for fungi and many species have been
transformed using ATMT techniques. The advantages of using ATMT compared to other genetic
transformation techniques, is that it does not require creation of protoplasts, and that ATMT
usually results only in a single T-DNA insert pr. transformant (de Groot et al., 1998). Generally
the transformation eﬃciency is also increased compared to other transformation methods (Zhang
16
==PAGE:31==
2
THEORY
et al., 2013). Another advantage is the ability to transform and integrate large DNA fragments,
up to 75 kb DNA fragments. This has been showed in Fusarium oxysporum and Aspergillus
awamori by Takken et al. (2004) (Frandsen, 2015).
The in vitro A. tumefaciens virulence system for genetic transformation has been adapted to
consist of a binary vector system. A. tumefaciens contains the 100 kb, natural Ti plasmid
with virulence genes and T-DNA transfer machinery, while a small shuttle plasmid contains the
DNA to be integrated (T-DNA) in the host cell, surrounded by LB and RB restriction sites,
previously described. The Ti plasmid recognizes the LB and RB, cuts out the T-DNA and by
its natural ability, introduces the T-DNA into the host genomic DNA (Frandsen et al., 2012).
The beneﬁts of using a small shuttle plasmid without the Ti plasmid machinery, is that it is
easy to modify the T-DNA region via standard molecular techniques using E. coli. This can for
example be done by USER fusion cloning described in previous section 2.1.
When performing ATMT in vitro, the induction medium is an important factor. In nature, the
environmental sensing is particular important to the A. tumefaciens. The bacterium exists in the
rhizosphere in the ground, and interacts with wounded plants. This has led to a highly sensitive
chemotaxis system. Ti-plasmid has the ability to respond to nanomolar amounts of phenolic
compounds excluded from wounded plants, such as acetosyringone. This ability is based on the
virulence genes of the Ti-plasmid, virA and virG. At higher acetosyringone concentrations, the
proteins VirA and VirG mediate a second response, inducing the other vir-operons, in order to
start the transfer of T-DNA to the plant host (C. V. Palmer and Shaw, 1992).
2.3
High Performance Liquid Chromatography
High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is a tool to separate compounds in a sample.
In Liquid Chromatography (LC) a column is closely packed with beads, also known as the sta-
tionary phase. Dependent on the type of chromatography, the beads can have diﬀerent chemical
properties. The mobile phase runs continuously through the column. The sample is injected into
the mobile phase and is thereby carried through the column (Waters.com). Interactions between
the mobile and stationary phase, such as polarity and size, will allow the diﬀerent compounds in
the sample to elute at diﬀerent times, called retention time (RT). Molecules with high aﬃnity to
the stationary phase elute slower while compounds with low aﬃnity elute faster (Snyder, 2010).
HPLC uses a thin stainless steel column packed with small particles. This setup demands high
pressure pumps to pump the mobile phase through the column. Ultra-High Performance Liquid
Chromatography (UHPLC) is an even more rapid separation method. The general technique
is the same as for HPLC, but even thinner columns and smaller particles (1-3 µm) are used,
which therefore demands higher pressure to be applied. The advantage of UHPLC is that it
gives a faster and more precise separation of compounds compared to HPLC (Nov´akov´a et al.,
2017). UHPLC is therefore well-suited for small molecules, as secondary metabolites.
17
==PAGE:32==

THEORY
The eluent (mobile phase) in the reversed-phase chromatography must be suﬃciently polar and
provide reasonable competition for the analyte molecules at the absorption sites in the column.
The eluent should be a compound that contains no double bonds, otherwise, it will be detected
in the Diode Array Detector. The most common eluents in reversed-phase chromatography
are acetonitrile, dioxane, ethanol, methanol, isopropanol, tetrahydrofuran and water (Seton
Hall University, 2018). The eluents can be used in two ways; Isocratic HPLC analysis and
Gradient HPLC analysis. Isocratic HPLC analysis is when the mobile phase does not change
its composition throughout the analysis. Several problems are associated with Isocratic HPLC
analysis. If the range of analyte polarities is broad, some analytes are poorly retained, while
other analytes are more hydrophobic with long retention times. It may also be possible that
some analytes could be irreversibly adsorbed and contaminate the column. These problems can
be overcomed with the use of Gradient HPLC. In Gradient HPLC the mobile phase composition
changes throughout the analysis. The initial composition of the mobile phase is chosen so that
the strength of the eluent is appropriate to retain and resolve early eluting analytes.
The
elution strength is increased over time to elute compounds with optimum resolution. The ﬁnal
composition of the mobile phase is chosen to ensure that all compounds are eluted within a
reasonable retention time (Taylor, 2012; LCGC, 2013).
After UHPLC analysis, the data is
detected, and the results are showed in a chromatogram, with retention time on the x-axis, and
the intensity of the most abundant peaks for each eluted compound on the y-axis.
Reversed-phase chromatography was used in this study. Reversed-phase chromatography can
separate polar, non-polar, ionizable and ionic compounds and is suitable for separation of small
molecules, as secondary metabolites. The compounds were separated based on their polarity,
with a non-polar stationary phase and a polar mobile phase, hence the most polar compounds
elute ﬁrst, and the least polar compounds elute last (Snyder, 2010).
2.4
UV spectroscopy with Diode Array (UV/vis)) Detector
To analyze and identify compounds separated by HPLC, UV spectra can be helpful.
The
Diode Array Detector (DAD) stores all data and from the DAD chromatogram it is possible
to extract UV-spectra for all compounds that absorb ultraviolet (UV) or visible light. The
UV/vis-detectors usually work at a range from 200-800 nm (200-400 nm UV light, 400-800 nm
visible light). The UV/visible spectroscopy detects chromophores. Chromophores are conjug-
ated double bonds or chemical groups such as C=C, C≡C, C=O, N=O, C-Br or C-I. Natural
compounds such as secondary metabolites often have chromophores that lead to a speciﬁc
UV-spectrum, and can therefore be used to identify compounds in a sample (Michigan State
University, 2018). After liquid chromatography separation, the compounds run through the
DAD via the Detector Flow Cell. Figure 8 shows the schematic diagram of a Diode Array

If a compound contains chromophores, these will be detected in the Detector Flow Cell. The
grating is build up by many mirrors and functions so that all wavelengths can be tested on a
compound at the same time. Each wavelength of light has a particular energy associated with
it. A speciﬁc amount of light is used to excite an electron into a higher, empty orbital, and
when the amount of light used is exact, the wavelength is absorbed and the energy is used on
the electron. The DAD detects absorbed light for a speciﬁc wavelength at short time intervals.
The DAD consists of many diodes and the data from these diodes are stored in the computer,
which gives an output (Crawford Scientiﬁc, 2018). The output is an absorption spectrum/UV-
spectrum. The UV-spectrum has wavelength on the x-axis and the amount of absorption on
the y-axis. Since not all compounds contains chromophores, no UV-spectra can be conducted
for these compounds.
2.5
Mass spectrometry
Mass spectrometry (MS) is a technique to identify the chemical composition of compounds
by separating their gaseous ions according to mass and charge. After liquid chromatography
separation, the molecules are ionized by knocking oﬀone or more electrons to create positive
and negative ions. The ions are then accelerated so that they have the same kinetic energy.
After acceleration the ions are deﬂected by a magnetic ﬁeld according to their mass and the
number of positive charges. The more an ion is charged, the more it gets deﬂected. The ions
are then separated by their mass to charge (m/z) ratio. The ions run through the MS-device
and gets detected. The ions are shown as peaks in a mass spectrum. The MS-device (qTOF)
consists of an ion source (ESI), a mass ﬁlter and a detector. In order to keep the pressure down
i the MS-device, a vacuum system is applied. This ensures that the ions can run through the
MS-device. (Chem Guide, 2018).

2.5.1
Ionization using electrospray
Ionization of compounds can be carried out with electrospray ionization (ESI). ESI is a soft
ionization method, which makes it possible to detect and analyze intact molecular ions. It is
well-suited for ionic compounds with high polarity. ESI is carried out as follows: The sample
solution is drawn to the tip of a capillary tube where high voltage (3-5 kV) is applied. Outside
the capillary tube, nebulizer gas ﬂows to spray the sample. This creates a ﬁne mist of charged
droplets. These droplets have the same polarity as the applied voltage. As the charged droplets
move, the solvent evaporates. The droplets reduce in size, which makes the electric ﬁeld increase
on the surface of the droplets. The droplets become smaller and smaller in size, before ﬁnally
being liberated into the gas phase, see Figure 9.
Figure 9: Overview of Electrospray Ionization (ESI) (Shimadzu, 2018).
The adducts and losses formed during ESI are shown in mass spectra as ions. These ions are
used to ﬁnd the monoisotopic mass (Shimadzu, 2018).
2.5.2
qTOF
This study used Quadrupole Time-of-Flight mass spectrometry (qTOF). The qTOF consists of
one quadrupole followed by a collision cell (hexapole) and TOF analyzer (Agilent, 2014). The
quadrupole provides collisional damping and mass ﬁltration or serves as a transmission element.
The quadrupole selects precursor ions that are fragmented in the collision cell into product ions.
The product ions are impelled to the detector at a perpendicular angle to the original path.
The product ions are then impelled to the detector (TOF analyzer) (Agilent, 2014), see Figure
10.

Figure 10: Overview of qTOF used for UHPLC-MS (Agilent, 2014).
In the collision cell, the ions undergo collision-induced dissociation (CID). The parent ions and
the resulting fragmented ions are then cooled and focused. The ions are re-accelerated to the
required energy and focused by ion optics into a parallel beam that continuously enters the ion
modulator of the TOF analyzer (Agilent, 2014; Chernushevich et al., 2001). When the ions
enter the TOF analyzer, they move in their original direction, due to a ﬁeld free modulator
region. A pulsed electric ﬁeld is applied with a frequency of several kilohertz (kHz) across the
modulator gap, in order to push the ions in an orthogonal direction to the original path into
the accelerating tube. The ions arrive in a ﬁeld free drift space in the accelerating tube. It is
in the accelerating tube where TOF mass separation happens. The ratio of velocities in the
orthogonal direction is selected, based on the fact that the ions reach the ion mirror and then
reach the TOF detector without requiring additional deﬂection in the drift region. The detector
consists of two microchannel plates (Agilent, 2014; Chernushevich et al., 2001). The data are
stored in mass spectra. In a mass spectrum, ions are shown as peak. The mass spectrum has
x-axis m/z values for the ions and on the y-axis the intensity of the ions for each compound
in a sample. In order to ﬁnd the monoisotopic mass of a compound, the diﬀerences between
the peaks are calculated and compared. The places where the isotopomers have the diﬀerence
1 on the x-axis are found, which means that there is a charge of 1. The diﬀerences of the ions
to nearby ions (adducts and losses) are analyzed by calculating the common mass diﬀerences

4.1.1
Puriﬁcation of genomic DNA from Penicillium expansum
Three agar plugs containing P. expansum were inoculated in 50 mL liquid deﬁned penicillium
medium (DPM) in a 300 mL baﬄed Erlenmeyer ﬂask and grown for 7 days at 25°C and 150

washed three times with sterile MQ water.
The mycelium was then squeezed by wrapping
the miracloth containing the mycelium with paper tissues, in order to remove as much water
as possible. The mycelium was divided in approximately 1 g aliqouts and each transferred to
separate sterile 1.5 mL tubes (Microtubes, Sarstedt, AG & Co., Germany) and freezed at -20°C
overnight. Next day the frozen mycelium was transferred to an ice cold motar and covered with
liquid nitrogen. With an ice cold motar, pestle and spatula, the frozen mycelium was grinded
into a ﬁne powder and transferred to a 50 mL centrifuge tube (VWR, Labcon, USA) with 15
mL EB buﬀer containing 10 mM DTT, 1 mL 20% SDS and 10 µL RNase A (100 µg/mL) before
vortexing. The mixture was then incubated for 10-20 minutes at 60°C to lyse the cells. Then 5
mL 5M K-acetate was added before vortexing for 30 seconds. The mixture was incubated on ice
for 20 minutes, followed by centrifugation at 10.000 G at 4°C for 25 min. The supernatant was
then ﬁltered through Miracloth in a funnel into a new 50 mL centrifuge tube (VWR, Labcon,
USA). 15 mL isopropanol was added, the mixture was vortexed and incubated for 5 min on
ice. The mixture was centrifuged again at 10.000 G at 4°C for 25 minutes. The supernatant
was discarded and the pellet was dry aired for 20 minutes. The pellet was then dissolved in
0.7 mL TE-buﬀer (50:10 pH 8) and transferred to a 1.5 mL tube (Microtubes, Sarstedt, AG &
Co., Germany). Then 75 µL 3M sodium acetate (pH 5-2) and 500 µL isopropanol were added
to the tubes. The mixture was vortexed and incubated for 10 minutes on ice. The mixture was
then centrifuged at 13.000 rpm for 10 minutes. The supernatant was removed, and the pellet
was washed with 70 % ethanol by gently adding the ethanol and remove it again. The pellet
was then dry aired for 20 minutes. The pellet was dissolved in 300 µL TE-buﬀer (10:01 pH 8).

4.1.2
Construction of USER fragments by PCR
In order to construct the USER bricks for USER-cloning, E. coli containing plasmid pRF-HUE
and E. coli contaning plasmid pAC1179 were inoculated on LB+kanamycin (50 µg/mL) plates
and LB+ampicillin (100 µg/mL) respectively, at 37°C overnight. One colony from each con-

medium in a 50 mL centrifuge tube (VWR, Labcon, USA) and incubated for 16h at 37°C, 150
rpm. Plasmids from E. colis were puriﬁed by plasmid puriﬁcation kit (Sigma Aldrich, Germany)
and veriﬁed and restriction enzyme digest with NcoI followed by gel electrophoresis.
In order to check the optimal annealing temperature, PCR mixes (50 µL each) were prepared for
each USER fragment by adding 28 µL water, 10 µL CxL buﬀer, 5 µL dNTPs, 2.5 µL forward
primer and 2.5 µL reverse primer, 1 µL X7 DNA polymerase and 1 µL template.
USER
fragments HygRI and Hyg-mRFP had PCR mix (50 µL each) 25 µL PhusionU Mastermix, 2.5
µL forward primer, 2.5 µL reverse primer, 1 µL template and 19 µL water. The PCR mixes
were ampliﬁed by the gradient PCR program ID 98°C for 5 min, denaturation 98°C for 30 sec,
annealing at 50-60°C for 30 sec, elongation at 72°C for 90 sec for 35 cycles. This was followed by
an extension phase at 72°C for 5 min and then hold at 12°C. The PCR mixes were then checked

Results for ATMT of P. expansum
5.1
Analysis of P. expansum hygromycin B sensitivity
This part of the study aimed to develop a transformation protocol of Penicillium spp. by using
ATMT. Diﬀerent variations of ATMT were tested with the purpose to delete the patK gene of
the patulin gene cluster of P. expansum. The ﬁrst step in the development of a transformation
protocol was to ﬁnd the optimal concentration of hygromycin B that would inhibit growth of
wild type P. expansum. In literature Buron-Moles et al. (2012) reported 200 µg/ml hygromycin
B to be an inhibitory concentration for P. expansum growth, so this concentration was used in
the ﬁrst test.
In the ﬁrst test P. expansum 1*106 spores/ml were inoculated on IMAS plates with ﬁlters. After
5 days of incubation on the non-selective plates, the ﬁlters with fungal growth were moved to
DPM plates supplemented with 0, 50, 100, 150 and 200 µg/mL of hygromycin B. After the ﬁlters
were moved to the selective DPM+hygromycin B plates, the fungus did not alter nor decrease
its growth. It was hypothesized that this behavior may be caused by a combination of a too high
spore concentration compared to the amount of hygromycin B or caused by a too long incubation
time on IMAS plates before moving the ﬁlters with fungus to selective DPM+hygromycin B
plates. Increasing concentration of spores can alter the amount of antimicrobial to be added
to a selective medium. It is also possible that the ﬁlter may work as a protective layer against
hygromycin B. Therefore, the optimal concentration of hygromycin B was determined again via
a new spore concentration at 1*105 spores/ml inoculated directly on DPM plate with 0, 50,
100, 150, 200 µg/mL of hygromycin B with no ﬁlter. As colonies still emerged it was decided to
repeat the test with DPM plates supplemented with 0, 100, 150, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 700,
800, 900 and 1000 µg/mL of hygromycin B with no ﬁlter. The growth of P. expansum was then
inhibited at 200 µg/mL hygromycin B. This concentration of fungicide was used in solid DPM
during ATMT. Note: The used hygromycin B batch was later found by lab colleagues to have
reduced antibiotic activity with other well tested fungi.

5.3
Protocol for Agrobacterium tumefacies mediated transformation in P.
expansum
Two diﬀerent approaches were used for ATMT. The ﬁrst was inspired by Frandsen (2015), and
is based on ATMT of Fusarium spp.. After 5 days of incubation time on selective media with
ﬁlters, no transformants were visible, so the ﬁlters were removed, and the plates were incubated
for 7 days to check if any fungal growth would emerge. After ended incubation time, no obvious
transformants were visible. Spots of fungal growth was present on the edge of the petri dish,
which was assumed to be left-over fungi grown on the edge of the removed ﬁlters. These spots
of fungi were transferred to new selection media, but were not able to grow on the selective
media without ﬁlter. Therefore the transformation protocol was altered.
A second attempt of ATMT was carried out, inspired by published protocols where Penicillium
spp. have been transformed via ATMT (Buron-Moles et al., 2012), (Zhang et al., 2013) and
(Wang and Li, 2008). Diﬀerent variations of the protocols were carried out.
After 5 days incubation on selection media, colonies emerged on selection plates with the 2
days-liquid-incubated mix of P. expansum and ∆A. tumefaciens electroporated with pRF-
HUE:mRFP, pRF-HU2F:patK or pRF-HU2F:patK/mRFP. The colonies emerged on selective
media containing both 200 and 400 µg/mL hygromycin B + 300 µg/mL cefoxitin (now referred
to as LT (liquid transformation), see Figures 19, 20 and 21. No Wild Type P. expansum growth
was visible in the control plate 1*105 fungal spores/mL plated on DPM + 200 µg/mL hygromy-
cin B. It was later hypothesized that a proper control would have been 1*105 fungal spores/mL
in liquid IMAS, incubated for 48 h in 25°C as well, since Wild type P. expansum might begin to
germinate in the liquid induction medium, within the 48h incubation time, and then potentially
show less sensitivity to the added hygromycin B in the selective plates.

DISCUSSION FOR ATMT OF P. EXPANSUM
6
Discussion for ATMT of P. expansum
The aim of the ﬁrst part of this study was to develop a transformation protocol for P. expansum
by using ATMT. This was carried out by attempting to delete the patK gene in the patulin
gene cluster of P. expansum.
6.1
Hygromycine B resistance of P. expansum
The study showed that the used batch of hygromycin B did not exhibit the eﬀect as intended.
Several other studies uses hygromycin B as selective agent in ATMT with Penicillium spp.,
such as Zhang et al. (2013) (50 µg/mL) and Wang and Li (2008) (75 µg/mL).
It was ﬁrst hypothesized that inoculating spores of P. expansum on DPM plates with 200 µg/mL
hygromycin B would prevent growth of fungi, elucidated by own studies and by Buron-Moles
et al. (2012). Therefore, 200 µg/mL hygromycin B should be enough to prevent growth of
untransformed P. expansum. Decreased sensitivity of hygromycin B was discovery, when the
fungal colonies were inoculated by scraping oﬀmycelium of WT P. expansum and inoculate it
on DPM +200 µg/mL hygromycin B plates. The fungus grew as if no hygromycin B had been
added to the plates.
Similar problems were also discovered by other lab colleagues that had used the same hygromycin
B batch in their work with other well-studied fungi. The use of the hygromycin B batch turned
out to be a problem when obtaining potential transformants, both from the liquid transformation
and from the ﬁlter transformation.
Even though it was believed that isolation of potential
transformants had been successful on the selective plates, the PCR screening showed that none
of the potential transformants had T-DNA correctly inserted into their genome.
The used
hygromycin B batch was stored in a freezer that everyone in the lab had access to. A possible
explanation for the lack of activity, could be, that before storage in freezer, the hygromycin B
batch had been stored at room temperature for too long. Another possible explanation is that
the freezer has been turned oﬀby mistake, or that the freezer door has been open for too long
at some point. Further, a defrost of the freezer may also result in the ruining of the hygromycin
B batch. According to Invivogen, Hygromycin B should be stored at temperature 4°C to -20°C
and that freeze thaw cycles should be avoided (InvivoGen).
6.2

6.2.1
Identiﬁcation of potential transformants with PCR
An important ﬁnding during the identiﬁcation, was that the wild type P. expansum showed a
DNA fragment at expected band size for both hyg-588 (588 bp) and mRFP-seq (475 bp) when
ampliﬁed with Phire polymerase. This was a surprise as no matches were found, when checking
the Hyg-588 and mRFP primerpairs on the theoretical WT P. expansum genome in CLC Main
Workbench. This could be explained, if the supplier of the fungal spores had sent an already
worked-on sample of P. expansum ATCC 24692 with the insertion of the hygromycin B resistance
gene. However, this was investigated and was not the case. Therefore, DNA fragmenst were
sequenced and the data showed high consensus with the theoretical hygromycin B resistance gene
and the red ﬂuorescence gene. It was not expected, that the wild type P. expansum would confer
both hygromycin B resistance gene hph AND mRFP. An explanation for this result could be that
the ampliﬁcation of the DNA fragment had been performed with Phire polymerase and buﬀer.
The phire polymerase does not proof-read the ampliﬁed DNA in the same extent as regular X7
polymerase, hence the sequenced DNA might contain errors, that by chance are similar to the
hph and mRFP genes. The same test with hyg-588 primers had been performed on wild type P.
expansum genomic DNA with X7 polymerase, where no band emerged on the gel for hyg-588.
The most obvious explanation for the unexpected bands is pollution of the one of the reagents
for the PCR mix before ampliﬁcation with Phire polymerase. Another explanation is that the
hygromycin B resistance examined with PCR should have been performed for another part of
the hygromycin B resistance gene. The sequence for the investigated part of the Hygromycin B
resistance gene could be a part of the wild type P. expansum genome, that has a homologous
region compared to the hyg-588 region tested for, which would give a false-positive result when
performing PCR and gel electrophoresis. A third explanation of the sequencing results could
be, that the quality of the genome sequencing might be of poor quality. Genome sequencing has

during PCR with Phire polymerase.

To investigate the secondary metabolite production of Penicillium expansum strains, the fungi
were inoculated and cultivated on various media for 6-7 days at 25°C, except for when cultivated

All extractions were performed as duplicates.
The samples were run on HPLC-DAD, and
samples from IBT 34017 were run on UHPLC-MS, both positive and negative ionization.
Because the duplicates had similar growth growth conditions and their Base peak chromato-

Compounds in a sample were identiﬁed by a dereplication process.
This method combines
analysis of base peak chromatograms (BP) of multiple samples, with UV-spectra, mass spectra
and search in databases and in literature. When studying and comparing BP chromatograms
of multiple samples, the deconvolution process is a powerful tool. The deconvolution process
is carried out by Extracted Ion Chromatograms (EIC), where ions in a mass spectrum are

checked if they are adducts belonging to the same peak of multiple chromatograms. A potential
adduct of a speciﬁc peak in a BP chromatogram, can be checked by EIC, to elucidate if that
adduct and peak also belongs together in other BP chromatograms. Deconvolution process is
also advantageous when a peak representing a compound is very small in a chromatogram. If
the mono isotopic mass and retention time is known for a compound, the EIC can be used to
evaluate whether the speciﬁc compound is present in a sample or not.

DISCUSSION FOR SECONDARY METABOLITE STUDIES OF P. EXPANSUM
8
Discussion for secondary metabolite studies of P. expansum
8.1
Comparison of metabolites produced by P. expansum ATCC 24692 IBT
34017 in apple and AMA (w/wo peel)
Inoculation on a new apple medium (AMA, AMA+peel) and a fresh apple (spp. Discovery)
were investigated and compared.
Secondary metabolites were investigated with UV-spectra
using HPLC-DAD. Metabolites patulin and linoleic acid were found. An important ﬁnding was,
that both patulin and linoleic acid were present in larger amounts in samples extracted from
P. expansum ATCC 24692 IBT 34017 grown on apple-peel (fresh apple (rot/spores+rot) and
AMA+peel), than compared to on AMA without peel. This might suggest, that the apple-peel
is an important factor during growth of P. expansum. The study showed that when P. expansum
grow on fresh apple, it produce larger amounts of both patulin (mean area of peaks for patulin
1381.9 (Apple rot), 1637.3 (Apple spores+rot)) and linoleic acid (mean area of peaks for linoleic
acid 1505.1 (Apple rot), 117.9 (Apple spores + rot)) compared to AMA (patulin 363.2, linoleic
acid 46.8) and AMA+peel (patulin 630.7, linoleic acid 1130.5). The production of patulin in
AMA was decreased 4 times when compared to in apple. This suggests, that even though AMA
(w/wo peel) was made with moshed apples mixed with agar and water and apple-peel on the
top, it might be possible that the heat during the autoclavation or the moshing of the apples
have destroyed important factors for patulin production of P. expansum in the apple-mimicking
medium.
8.2
Dereplication of secondary metabolites produced by P. expansum ATCC
24692 IBT 34017 on CYA, YES, PDA, AMA and AMA+peel
Besides the investigation of the secondary metabolism with UV, P. expansum ATCC 24692
IBT 34017 was also investigated with mass spectrometry using UHPLC-MS. Patulin was con-
stitutively produced in all media tested. Patulin is a well-known secondary metabolite of P.
expansum and it is diﬃcult to ﬁnd a medium, where patulin is not produced. This is in contrast
to the ﬁndings of Dombrink-Kurtzman and Blackburn (2005) where four strains of P. expansum
have been investigated for their patulin production on media supplemented with manganese.
In the study, it was found that P. expansum strains produced large amounts of patulin, but
also that three out of four strains only produced patulin on PDB (potato medium), while the
fourth P. expansum strain produced patulin in a larger range of media (Dombrink-Kurtzman
and Blackburn, 2005). In another study by Andersen et al. (2004) where several P. expansum
strains were investigated for their secondary metabolism, they found that patulin was persist-
ently produced in both CYA, YES and PDA (Andersen et al., 2004). This complements the
ﬁndings in the present study, where patulin was found to be produced in all ﬁve media invest-
igated; AMA, AMA+peel, CYA, YES and PDA. In the same study by Andersen et al. (2004),
83

citrinin was found to be produced in higher amounts in YES, compared to other media (Ander-
sen et al., 2004). In the present study, citrinin was only found to be produced in YES, and was
only found in the UV-data. It is a possibility, that citrinin also could have been present in the
other media, if more plugs had been extracted. In the study by Andersen et al. (2004), chaeto-
globosins such as chaetoglobosin A and C, communesin A and B, expansolide and roquefortine
C were also persistently produced (Andersen et al., 2004), which supports the results obtained
in this study.
One important ﬁnding from the comparison of UV-data to MS-data, is that some compounds,
that were only found in UV-data from some media, where also found on other media when
MS-data were investigated: Chaetoglobosin C was only found in CYA and YES in UV-data,
but when the MS-data were investigated, chaetoglobosin C was found in CYA, YES and PDA.
Roquefortine C was only found to be produced in CYA, YES and PDA when the UV-data
were investigated, but according to MS-data, roquefortine C was produced in AMA+peel as
well. This shows, that using multiple tools and analysis methods is important to get the full
picture of the secondary metabolite production of an organism, since it is possible, that some
methods, such as HPLC-DAD, is less sensitive compared to UHPLC-MS. When investigating
the secondary metabolite production of several P. expansum strains, Andersen et al. (2004)
used tools and analysis methods TLC, HPLC-DAD and UHPLC-MS.
8.3
Comparison of secondary metabolites from P. expansum IBT 34017,
5887, 6096 and 15305 on selected media
Three strains of P. expansum, IBT 5887, IBT 6096 and IBT 15305, were investigated for their
secondary metabolite production on various media using HPLC-DAD and compared to the
control P. expansum IBT 34017. No wild type non-patulin producing strain of P. expansum
was found in this study. Both chaetoglobosin A and patulin are produced in vast amounts. P.
expansum IBT 34017 produced a greater variety of secondary metabolites, than the three other
P. expansum strains, such as citrinin, communesin B and ergosterol. This was unexpected since
Andersen et al. (2004) had previously shown that IBT 5887 and IBT 6096 are able to produce
citrinin and communesin B, and that IBT 15305 is able to produce communesin B (Andersen
et al., 2004). It might be a possibility, that similar results could have been obtained, if the
extractions from IBT 5887, IBT 6096 and IBT 15305 had been subjected to mass spectrometry
as well.

CONCLUSION AND FURTHER STUDIES
9
Conclusion and further studies
Penicillium expansum is one of the most investigated fungal species. This is due to its ability
to produce patulin in great amounts, especially seen during infection of apples. Losses due
to fruits spoilage caused by P. expansum infections, exceed 4.5 million dollars annually in the
USA alone. When ingested, patulin induces immunological, neurological, and gastrointestinal
diseases leading to serious health problems in humans, and therefore governmental regulations
have been outlined in several countries. The original aim of this study was to develop a genetic
toolbox for deleting genes in Penicillium spp. and to investigate the role of patulin during P.
expansum infection processes by deleting the essential patK gene in the genome sequenced P.
expansum ATCC 24692. During the study, unexpected challenges emerged during the develop-
ing process of the ATMT protocol for P. expansum. It turned out not to be possible to obtain a
transformant where the targeted patK gene was deleted. The used hygromycin B batch in the
developing process of the ATMT protocol did not exhibit the expected fungicidal eﬀect, pos-
sible due to incorrect storage. This turned out to be a problem during the selecting of potential
transformant candidates for the screening process as untransformed fungi also grew.
In spite of the challenges, a total of 45 candidates were selected for identiﬁcation. Screening
PCR with X7 polymerase gave weak bands for three potential transformants #11-LT, #30-LT
and #7-FT. When repeating the screening PCR with Phire polymerase, thick bands emerged
at expected bands sizes for several transformants, but when screening for the RFP signal, no
signals were detected.
It might be possible, that the screening PCR with X7 polymerase,
revealed a random insertion of the hph gene into P. expansum genome. It could be possible
that the UV-lamps for both the used UV-detector and microscope could be broken, or a wrong
combination of ﬂuorescent ﬁlters, which might explain the lack of a ﬂuorescence signal.
Even though the study did not result in a successful ATMT protocol with the identiﬁcation of a
non-patulin producing strain, important conclusions can still be drawn from the results obtained
in the study, which can be used in a future development of an ATMT protocol for Penicillium
spp..
First, the incubation time should not exceed 2 days before transfer of the ﬁlters to
selective plates, as the investigations in the study showed that growth of non-transformed fungi
then will be too concentrated and will show less sensitivity towards selection with hygromycin
B fungicide.
Second, the spore concentration of Penicillium spp.
should not exceed 1*105
spores/ml, as the study has shown, that a too high spore concentration demands a higher
amount of fungicide to be added as a selection agent. As the hygromycin B batch did not
show the expected fungicidal eﬀect, it was diﬃcult to conclude whether ATMT with liquid
transformation was an advantage compared to ATMT with ﬁlter transformation.
However,
since ATMT with liquid transformation gave more bands at expected band size compared to
ATMT with ﬁlter transformation at screening PCR with X7 polymerase, it would be interesting
85

to repeat a study on ATMT with liquid transformation. ATMT with liquid induction medium
is a more simple process and thus will be a great advantage compared to previous described
protocols. However, it was diﬃcult to deduce whether the transformation eﬃciency was higher
with liquid transformation compared to ﬁlter transformation, due to the ruined used hygromycin
B batch. It is important that a new batch of hygromycin B should be used in future studies. The
new hygromycin B batch should be stored in preferably a laboratory freezer with a temperature
monitor. A laboratory freezer is better a keeping a constant low temperature, compared to a
common freezer.
The protocol for ATMT with liquid transformation can be outlined as follows:
1. Construct plasmids by USER fusion cloning.
2. Insert plasmid into A. tumefaciens by electroporation.
3. Grow ∆A. tumefaciens for 2-3 days at 28°C on a LB+kan plate
4. Inoculate on colony of ∆A. tumefaciens for each plasmid in 10 mL liquid LB+kan at 100
rpm 28°C.
5. Transfer 300 µL liquid ∆A. tumefaciens to fresh 10 mL liquid IMAS+kan medium
6. Grow for approximately 24 h at 28°C, 200 rpm, until it reach OD600 0.7-1.0.
7. Dilute spore suspension from Penicillium spp. to 1*105 spores/ml in liquid IMAS medium
8. Mix equal volumes of ∆A. tumefaciens and fungal spore suspension. Incubate at 25°C for
48 hours with no shaking.
9. Plate 200 µL of the mix on selection plate DPM + 200 µg/mL hygromycin + 300 µg/mL
cefoxitin. Incubate plate for 5 days at 25°C.
10. Isolate transformants on new DPM + 200 µg/mL hygromycin plates. Incubate at 25°C
for 7 days.
Since a veriﬁed patK-deletion mutant of P. expansum could not be obtained successfully in
the present study, the study was changed.
Diﬀerent strains of P. expansum were investig-
ated for their metabolite production on CYA, YES, PDA and AMA (w/wo peel), to check
for diﬀerences and similarities in the secondary metabolite production using HPLC-DAD and
UHPLC-MS. Patulin, chaetoglobosin A, roquefortine C, communesin A, linoleic acid, auranti-
oclavine, andrastin A, expansolide were found to be produced in all strains, although not found
in all media. For IBT 24692, YES seemed to be the best medium to get the full metabolic
picture, while for IBT 5887, PDA seemed to be best medium for secondary metabolite produc-
tion. IBT 6092 and IBT 15305 produced most compounds on CYA. The secondary metabolite

production of IBT 25692 was further investigated with mass spectrometry, and in this case,
some compounds found to be produced on other media as well, where the same compounds had
not been detected using only UV-data. First, this shows that using multiple tools and analysis
methods is important to get the full picture of the secondary metabolite production of P. ex-
pansum, since some methods, such as HPLC-DAD, are less sensitive compared to UHPLC-MS.
Second, ﬁnding a non-patulin producing strain, and a medium where P. expansum does not
produce patulin, are diﬃcult, because all strains investigated constitutively produced patulin
in all media.
When a successful transformant with patK deletion is obtained, the original second aim of the
study can be investigated.
The non-patulin producing mutant can then be used in studies
investigating the infection process of P. expansum on various media when it is not able to
produce patulin. One model of the investigation could be to inoculate the mutant P. expansum
strain on apple, with and without natural occurring competitors of yeast, fungi and bacteria,
such as Sporobolomyces roseus (Mercier and Wilson, 1994) and Monilinia fructicola (Beckerman
et al., 2016). This might elucidate the function of patulin and P. expansums ability to infect
and/or compete with the other species.
When an eﬀective transformation protocol has been developed, other secondary metabolites
would be interesting for targeted deletion, in order to investigate their role in the infection
process of P. expansum. An interesting secondary metabolite to examine is chaetoglobisin A,
as it is produced in large amounts by P. expansum on various media.
The developed transformation protocol suitable for P. expansum, should also be tested on other
Penicillium spp.. As described in the introduction, Penicillium spp. produces a great variety of
secondary metabolites, that could have great potential in the food- and pharmaceutical industry
(Demain, 2014). Antibiotic resistance is a huge problem, and in recent years, the problem has
only increased (MacGowan and Macnaughton, 2013). An eﬃcient transformation protocol will
help to discover new secondary metabolites with antimicrobial eﬀect, and can also be used to
create eﬀective cell factories for production of useful compounds in the food- and pharmaceutical
industry.
This study forms the basis for several future research in this ﬁeld, and outlines a potential useful
future protocol for ATMT with liquid transformation, which is a more simple method. Even
though P. expansum is one the most studied fungal species, much more can be investigated on
this fungus, and here, a proper non-patulin producing strain should be of ﬁrst priority.
