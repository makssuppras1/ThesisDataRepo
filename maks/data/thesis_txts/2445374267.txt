Abstract
The current genetic admixture analysis is usually obtained by means of principal component analysis
followed by a maximum likelihood estimate of individual ancestries with the ADMIXTURE software
tool. We examine the possibility of obtaining similar results using deep generative models, speciﬁcally
by means of variational auto-encoders (VAE) for dimensionality reduction and its extension the Gaus-
sian mixture variational auto-encoder (GMVAE) for genetic clustering. The GMVAE model proposes
a multi-modal distribution as the prior for the latent variables instead of the uni-modal distribution
in VAE. The deep generative models also give the ability to obtain a noisy reconstruction, exact re-
construction or new samples of the input genotypes which might be useful in various situations such
as up-sampling a dataset, generating genotype simulation or simply having the ability to make noisy
reconstructions publicly available data.
We evaluate the latent space representation learned by the VAE on simulated and real genotype
data and ﬁnd a richer representation compared to the usual PCA with clearer distinctions within each
population group. The relative low dimensions of the latent representation also allow for more costly
operations such as t-SNE which resulted in clear and well-separated clusters. Reconstruction accuracy
varies due to the high feature low samples nature of genotype data and we can at best obtain a 75 %
reconstruction accuracy for the Human Origins data. GMVAE model performs poorly at clustering
genotype data and needs further considerations.

Introduction
Population genetics is the study of genetic diﬀerences within and between populations and plays a
major role in evolutionary biology. Several applications exist in the ﬁeld such as ancestry testing, the
evolution of genetic systems, analysis of genetic variation etc. In recent years the interest for pop-
ulation genetics analysis has increased due to the new advancements in computing powers, machine
learning and public interest in general. Computing power is needed in this ﬁeld in particular due to
the usually large size of the DNA datasets at hand. The general growing mathematical interest in
machine learning is also important as researchers always seek new areas of engineering to apply novel
as well as old models and put them to practice.
DNA sequences are represented as a sequence of letters (AGTC) and each individual inherits, a
sequence of ∼3 billion A, G, C, Ts from each parent. If we compare two random human individuals,
we observe that on average 99.9 % of the sequences will be identical since both belong to the same
biological species of Homo Sapiens. In population genetics, we are interested in DNA locations where
the individuals diﬀer, where usually each can carry one of two types. We ﬁnd this pattern for every
∼300-1000 position in the DNA. Because we carry the 3 billion pairs even when neglecting the 99.9
% we still end up having around 1 million positions where individuals might diﬀer. These diﬀerences
are called single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNP) and are subject to a variety of studies other than
population genetics. Each parent donates a mixture of your grandparent’s chromosomes due to a pro-
cess called recombination, which ensures continuous diversity in populations. As illustrated in ﬁgure
1 for each generation through this process, pieces of DNA are passed along in block-like fashion, and
thereby determining which individual you are most related to changes along the genome [9].
Figure 1: The DNA inheritance process through genetic recombination from a) grandpar-
ents to b) parents to c) child. Grandparents contribute each around 25 % to the child’s
genome. [20]
In population genetics we compare several individuals to each other and determine their in-between
relatedness based on who is their common ancestor, thus saying that a particular person is more re-
2
==PAGE:6==
lated to you than another person, is saying that you share a more recent common ancestor with that
person. The relatedness and proximity of diﬀerent ethnic groups are determined by looking at the
genotype data and performing dimensionality reduction and unsupervised clustering analysis [10].
In this analysis, we focus on two main aspects of population genetics work, namely dimensional-
ity reduction of DNA genotype data and the genetic admixture analysis. In dimensionality reduction,
we reduce the number of features by obtaining a set of principal axes. This way we can compare data
points and detect outliers and other between samples structures. We explore the possibility of intro-
ducing variational autoencoders as a novel method in the ﬁeld of population genetics and compare
the results to principal component analysis (PCA) and t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding
(t-SNE). Likewise, individual clusters or ancestries are currently obtained by maximum likelihood
estimate with the most recent software tool ADMIXTURE [10], and here we wish to test Gaussian
mixture variational autoencoders as a possible alternative. The motivation for seeking alternatives in
the ﬁeld of deep learning is largely due to the advanced mathematical architectures that are able to
model complex features of data, that traditional methods may lack the ability to do.

Methods
In this section, we introduce the theoretical background for variational auto-encoders (Hereafter VAE)
and Gaussian mixture variational auto-encoders (Hereafter GMVAE). The VAE model will be com-
pared to the usual PCA and how it learns to represent the data on the lower dimensional latent space.
The GMVAE is a natural extension to the VAE, which aims at, besides input reconstruction and
latent space, also learn the underlying clusters which gave rise to the observed data. Thus GMVAE
is a deep learning method for unsupervised clustering [5], [11].
5.1
Variational Autoencoder (VAE)
An autoencoder is a type of artiﬁcial neural network used mainly for unsupervised learning tasks
in the sense that it learns a speciﬁc data encoding. These compressed encodings are then used for
dimensionality reduction. An autoencoder consists of 3 main parts, namely an encoder network, a
decoder network and a low dimensional encoding of the inputs. Input data are fed into the encoder
network, which compresses it into the lower dimensional encoding. This encoding is then passed on
to the decoder which reconstructs the original inputs. The training task is then simply to minimize
the reconstruction error or the diﬀerence between the original input and its reconstructions. The
learning is usually done by means of gradient descent algorithm where the parameters of the network
are iteratively updated until convergence.
This architecture is very data speciﬁc and has several
applications and advantages especially in deep learning image analysis. An autoencoder can be used
for denoising tasks where a noisy input image can be denoised and we obtain a more clean image
reconstruction [14].
Figure 5: An autoencoder architecture consists of an encoder network and a decoder
network. An input image is fed to the encoder network which represents it on a lower
dimensional encoding. Then it is passed on to the decoder network which reconstructs the
original input.
One issue with the autoencoders is that the learned compression space is an arbitrary function that
can only be used for reconstruction of the input. Enters a VAE which uses the same autoencoder
architecture, but makes an assumption about the distribution of the latent variables. Thus instead
of learning this arbitrary function, we learn a latent variable that follows a speciﬁc probability distri-
bution. The network then computes the parameters for this distribution. This allows us to sample a
random variable from this distribution and generate new data and therefore a VAE is called a genera-
tive model. This method allows for a latent space that describes the relationship between the various

6.1
PCA and previous work
An important part of population genetic analysis is reducing the high dimensional genotype data to
two or three components. This is useful to assess the evident clusters of the sample and how they
relate to each other. Current DNA ethnicity testing companies use especially PCA to construct a
database of so-called founder individuals for each ethnic group, where principal components should
reveal that the database has a clear number of separate clusters [3] [4] [13]. We similarly use PCA
and other dimensionality reduction methods like t-SNE, to represent our data on a two-dimensional
space. For further supervised ADMIXTURE analysis one usually ﬁxes a set of founder samples that
has 100 % cluster membership in the principal axes, and by doing this we can determine a suitable
number of sub-populations/clusters K to compare to [19].

IP (independent sub-population) and MX (Mixed sub-populations) and they shall be used for model

6.2
VAE
In this part, we present the results from our VAE experiments on the Fashion-MNIST, Simulated
genotypes and the Human Origins data. The initial VAE model was implemented using the Keras
high-level neural network API for the MNIST experiments and the rest is implemented using Ten-
sorFlow [1]. Input data for VAE must be binarized and ﬂattened before passed to the encoder, thus
both Fashion-MNIST and genotype must be preprocessed. The tricky part is to ﬁnd a meaningful way
of representing the genotypes in binary. For this, we one-hot encode the genotypes, followed by re-
shaping to a ﬂat structure and thereby obtaining a binary representation that can be fed to the encoder.
From Sønderby et al [15] it is shown that batch normalization and initial warm-up epochs improve the
results of the network, thus for genotype data we use a 100 epochs warm-up where the VAE is trained
as a usual autoencoder. In order to prevent the network from over-ﬁtting, we apply a 20 % dropout

7
Discussion
There are several pros and cons in using variational autoencoders in population genetics that we must
address in this section.
Data dimensionality and structure
One immediate issue that arises in this ﬁeld, in particular, is the unusual dimensions of the datasets at
hand. Population genetics research usually deals with sample sizes from 200 to 2000 and feature sizes
from 100.000 to 1 million which easily tend to make neural network models over-ﬁt. This problem
is especially well-known in phenotype prediction with deep neural networks, and some solutions have
been proposed like the deep neural pursuit (DNP) model by Bo et al [8]. We also ﬁnd problems related
to the dimensionality of our data, where in case of 300 samples and 10.000 SNP loci we get a model
that over-ﬁts the data highly and with 300 samples and 100.000 SNP loci we get bad reconstructions.
The inability to obtain accurate reconstructions is also present when analyzing Human Origins data
which consists of 1941 samples and 600.840 SNP loci where we obtain a mean accuracy of 74 %. That
being said, we clearly are able to obtain an informative latent space that is enforced to be a smooth
representation of the data. Another possible issue that can aﬀect the performance of the model, is
the increased sparsity of the input when one-hot encoding is introduced and especially if modeling
missing values is included.
Over-ﬁtting
In this analysis we do not overcome the problem of data dimensionality mentioned in the previous
point, thus we heavily over-ﬁt our smaller simulated genotype datasets after a few number of epochs,
but it appears that the model quickly learns all that it can before beginning to over-ﬁt. Dropout
seems to help delay this over-ﬁtting by some epochs but does not solve the problem in general. We
could also have deployed a kind of early stopping where the training is terminated after some criteria
to avoid over-ﬁtting. In practice we can say that, there’s a trade-oﬀbetween how accurate our model
can be and how close its latent variables can be to the unit Gaussian distribution.
VAE/GMVAE architecture
Compared to Fashion-MNIST which is pixel features in binary, the genotype data is very diﬀerent
and lead to clearly non-Gaussian latent space representation. This could be the reason behind the
GMVAE inability to cluster the data. Other distributions may be experimented with in this case to
describe genotype data better. The GMVAE model described by Dilokthanakul et al [12] that uses
an extra latent variable in inferring the clusters actually yielded in some good clustering but ﬁnally
proved to be very unstable for our type of data and thus discarded from further consideration. As our
hierarchical clustering model in ﬁgures 32, 33 and 34 show, we are able to obtain a certain proﬁle for
the ethnic groups, and based on this, we could consider applying clustering analysis on a latent space
with larger dimensions.
The number of nodes in the hidden and latent layers appear to have an eﬀect on the model’s learning
process, where on smaller simulated genotype it is correlated with faster learning ability and with
larger datasets the size of the hidden layers and latent space aﬀects learning the posterior distribution
of the data as ﬁgure 31 shows and its continuations in the appendix. The amount of missing data
in the ancient samples and our model’s lack of taking them into consideration, results in poor and
insigniﬁcant results in projecting ancient samples onto the learned latent space.
40
==PAGE:44==
Advantages of VAE
As our VAE results from the Human Origins and genotype simulations show, we are able to obtain
rich latent space structures that show the relationship between populations and provides an oppor-
tunity to perform t-SNE or hierarchical clustering.
We notice a diﬀerence in the analysis quality
when comparing the t-SNE plots in ﬁgures 9 and 31, where it appears that t-SNE on the latent space
is more informative and show clearer population clusters than t-SNE on PCA with 50 components.
Other dimensionality reduction methods may also be applied to the learned latent space like Uniform
Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) which is competitive with t-SNE for visualization
quality and can preserve more of the global structure with better run time performance [16]. When
not over-ﬁtting, we also get a smooth latent space that can be used as a genotype simulation tool
that can sample genotypes that resemble ones in existing ethnic groups. These simulations may be
used for further benchmarking of new models or simply up-sample a dataset with unbalanced ethnic-
ity classes. We also show that if enough sample size exists, a VAE model can create very accurate
genotype reconstructions and in some scenarios concerning data privacy, these may be used to create
noisy sequences that can be made public data.
8
Conclusion
We have shown in this analysis that VAE is a powerful tool that is applicable in population genetics
analysis. We have also shown that our models are able to obtain rich latent structures that learn
the relationships between individual samples that come from diﬀerent ethnic groups. The generative
aspect of the VAE provides us with a novel tool for simulating human genotypes for various applications
ranging from privacy in data publication, up-sampling parts of a dataset and ﬁnally generating near
real data for future model testings and comparisons. We have also shown that GMVAE models perform
poorly in genetic clustering by merging all clusters into one, but obtains latent space representation
that is highly similar to standard VAE. Thus GMVAE in this setting needs further work to overcome
the main issues with genotype data.
