Chapter 1
Abstract
This thesis gives an overview of social engineering, especially, the focus is on spear-
phishing attacks from the technical as well as the psychological perspective.
The technical part examines the available data resources and tools for social engi-
neering as well as some data analysis models that assist by gleaning for the information
gathered to proﬁle a target.
The psychological part examines the relationships between the principles of decision-
making, the psychological triggers and cultural dimensions, and how they can be brought
together to achieve an advanced spear-phishing attack.

Hacking is the attempt to ﬁnd and exploit computer security vulnerabilities of a com-
puter target system. It is a process that goes through several phases: Reconnaissance,
scanning, exploitation and the ﬁnal phase referred to as ”post-exploitation & maintain-
ing access” [1].

The reconnaissance phase (also referred to as footprinting) is the ﬁrst and arguably
the most signiﬁcant and time-consuming step. The principal goal of this phase is to col-
lect as much information as possible about a target. For example, the target IP address
range, mail server, DNS records, etc...Spending a signiﬁcant amount of time to tap into
all available sources for valuable information will increase the success of the following
phases.
Once attackers have completed the reconnaissance phase and acquired a profound un-
derstanding of the target, they will move to the second phase—the scanning phase. In
3
==PAGE:9==
Chapter 2 Introduction
this phase, attackers start actively exploring the target machine or network for weak-
nesses. For instance, port scanning to ﬁnd out which ports are open and what services
are available or by starting vulnerability scanning to search for known vulnerabilities.
After identifying what ports are open, which services are running on this ports and
locating vulnerabilities on the target system, ethical hackers move to the exploitation
phase by exploiting these weaknesses to gain control over the system.
This can be
performed by using popular tools like Medusa (that attempts to gain access to remote
services), Metaspoilt (which is a powerful hacking tool) or John the Ripper (for cracking
passwords).
The exploitation phase gives access to the system. However, this access is only tem-
porary. Therefore hackers perform the ﬁnal ”post-exploitation & maintaining access”
step. This means that they create some persistent backdoors which can be used in the
future whenever they desire accessing the system.
As described above, the ﬁrst step of hacking is the reconnaissance phase. Reconnais-
sance can be both active and passive. Both use tools and public resources to gather
information about a destination. The diﬀerence is that passive reconnaissance does not
involve interaction with the victim, whereas active reconnaissance additionally involves
direct interaction with the victim to get unauthorized access to the target system. For
example, this can be achieved through the use of social engineering.
Social engineering has become popular as software vendors have become better at de-
veloping software that is robust and harder to break. As a consequence, hackers need to
ﬁnd new ways to circumvent these countermeasures and, to some extent, have realized
that human is the security’s weakest link. [2].
2.1 Motivation
Mitnick [2] has described social engineering as follows:
”Social Engineering uses inﬂuence and persuasion to deceive people by con-
vincing them that the social engineer is someone he is not, or by manipula-
tion. As a result, the social engineer is able to take advantage of people to
obtain information with or without the use of technology”.
Social engineering is nothing new in our society. It has always been used from gen-
eration to generation. For example, children are considered the best social engineers.
They know how to manipulate their parents to make them act in the direction they
want. Salespeople need to master many aspects of social engineering skills in order to
be able to determine people’s needs in order to oﬀer the right products. They should
be able to use some psychological tricks to gather as much information as possible and

try to use that information to inﬂuence their target to buy their products. Doctors,
psychologists, and lawyers use social engineering tactics to manipulate people to talk
about themselves or act in the direction they want them to. Thus, it may be concluded
that social engineering can be used maliciously and for good intention [3].
So the success of social engineering depends on the psychological manipulation and
deception of the human element. It is the art of using social disguise and psychological
tricks to manipulate computer users to take actions from which the attacker beneﬁts.
For example, to make a victim click on a link that leads to the installation of a virus on
the victim’s computer or to make the victim reveal personal information. An example
of social engineering is spear phishing attacks.
2.2 The Aim of this Thesis
The purpose of this thesis is to get an overview of social engineering attacks. In partic-
ular, the focus will be on how to perform spear phishing attacks on a technical as well
as on a psychological scale.
During the technical part of the thesis, we design and implement a social-engineering
tool fulﬁlling the following speciﬁcation
• The tool should be able to search diﬀerent types of data sources.
• The tool should be extensible (i.e. new data sources can be easily integrated).
• The tool should support a database for persisting the extracted data.
• The tool should interrelate data from diﬀerent sources according to deﬁned rules.
• The users of the tool should not need to perform complex queries to ﬁnd links
between these diﬀerent objects.
• All the pieces of data and the established relationships between them should be
graphically displayed.
• Graph analysis algorithms such as path analyses or clique models should be im-
plementable.
• The system should derive a job hierarchy (potentially only partial) from the ex-
tracted data.
Since the psychological part plays an important role in the success of spear phishing
emails, we should also examine this aspect closely.
The psychological part will deal with some typical questions within social engineering
like:

• Which factors contribute to the success of social engineering attacks?
• What makes some people more vulnerable than others?
• What psychological principals play a role?
• How can the cultural dimension assist in the success of social engineering attacks

Chapter 3
Background
3.1 Social Engineering
Social engineering is an attack vector that relies on human interaction and is often em-
ployed to manipulate people to violate security procedures to gain access to systems,
networks or physical locations.
Today, there are further factors that favor social engineering:
• The decrease in communication: Nowadays, people talk less face-to-face with each
other but use communication channels such as Skype or instant messengers. The
communication partner is also usually identiﬁed via an e-mail or a virtual proﬁle.
Thus, it is easier for an attacker to take over identity and/or to convince a victim
that the attacker is the someone who pretends to be. [4]
• Social networking sites: Twitter, Facebook, and other social media networks are
used on a daily base in private life and business.
People disclose explicit de-
tails about leisure activities, business activities, hobbies, and much more personal
information. All these pieces of information can be seen as bricks to build a so-
phisticated social engineering attack.[4]
• Decentralized data access: Many companies give their employees the opportunity
to work remotely from their home. Sometimes they also hire people that reside in
a diﬀerent country. This implies that companies need to make their shared data
available ( typically through online channels or cloud services). This results in
certain risks, particularly because many people publish information without con-
sideration of security and privacy.[4]
• Flexibility: Many companies provide their employees with a laptop rather than
a desktop computer to give them the ﬂexibility to work from diﬀerent places. If
attackers succeed in compromising the laptop of an employee, this will potentially
enable them to obtain access to the network of the organization. [4]
There are four approaches that a social engineer can use to attack [4]:
7
==PAGE:13==

• Physical approach: As the name implies, the physical approach requires some
physical eﬀort to obtain information about the victim. One technique, known as
dumpster diving, is to look for valuable information such as social security numbers
or valid system credentials in a company’s trash.
• Technical approach: The technical approach is mainly carried out via the Internet.
Users tend to use weak passwords that can be quickly guessed using free tools (e.g.,
Hachkat, John the Ripper), or users use the same password for diﬀerent accounts.
People also share a lot of personal information for free on social networks. Web
search-engineer can access all this valuable information and use it for own purpose.
• Social approach: This approach uses socio-psychological techniques such as Cial-
dini’s principles section 3.3 to persuade a victim to disclose sensitive information.
This type of attack is usually done by phone.
• Socio-technical approach: This strategy is a combination of social and technical
approaches. Baiting attack section 3.5.2 is one example of such methods in which
both technical aspects (e.g., infected devices) and social aspects (curiosity, greed)
are mixed to carry out an attack.
Attacks can be carried out in various ways. For example, websites, e-mail, phones,
instant messengers, social networks, the cloud, and physical networks. E-mail is the
most used medium for phishing and reverse social engineering attacks. Websites are the
most appropriate channel for waterholing attacks (attackers compromise websites that
are regularly visited by a speciﬁc group of interests and inject these websites with mali-
cious code). Websites can also be used in combination with e-mails to perform phishing
attacks section 3.5.2 [4].
3.2 The Psychological Principles of Decision-Making
Apparently, the success of social engineering depends on to what extent people can be
manipulated to make bad decisions that are not proﬁtable to them. That raises the
question of why people make the wrong decisions in certain situations, even though they
know that it deﬁnitely has a bad outcome for them.
Therefore, we would like to examine some psychological principles of decision-making
to understand some of the factors a social engineer can use to inﬂuence people to make
bad decisions.
Every day we have to make many decisions to do the right things. We need to weigh
the pros and cons of the options available to make the most appropriate decisions. That
sounds like decision-making is a process that is based on the logical reﬂection of the
matter. However, experiences have shown that there are many psychological principles
8
==PAGE:14==
3.2 The Psychological Principles of Decision-Making
people can face when making decisions. Here are some:
3.2.1 Cognitive Bias
A cognitive bias is a systematic way of thinking that causes people to make a particular
decision and judgment, often based on past experiences. This pattern can be helpful
in assisting a person to more quickly evaluate and process the information available to
make faster decisions. However, this pattern can lead to poor decisions and bad judg-
ments due to limited information processing capacity [5].
There are diﬀerent types of cognitive biases that distort the person’s thinking. This
contains [5]:
The Framing Eﬀect
The framing eﬀect is a cognitive bias that inﬂuences people’s response to a particular
choice, depending on how the situation is presented. For example, a study has shown
that 93 percent of Ph.D. students enrolled early, as a penalty fee for late registration was
emphasized, and only 67 percent when it was reported as a pre-registration discount [6].
The Availability Heuristic
The availability heuristic is a mental shortcut to make quick decisions based on the way
information or cases come into the person’s mind. If something can be remembered or
recalled, it must be relevant or at least more relevant than other alternatives that are
not immediately revived. For example, most people would probably think that more
people would die from a shark attack than from a vending machine, because they can
instantly remember news and movies about shark attacks, but not often hear news or
stories that someone is crushed from a vending machine although statistics support the
opposite .
The Conﬁrmation Bias
Conﬁrmation bias is the tendency to selectively seek or remember information in ways
that reinforce an opinion or belief. This information recall will inﬂuence people’s con-
clusions by focusing on one possibility and not considering any other alternatives.
3.2.2 Physiological States
The physiological states can inﬂuence the quality of the decisions. For example, research
has shown that people who are sleepy and tired tend to make optimistic decisions by
overestimating their chances of success. Many people are mad and cranky when they are
hungry, so they tend to make risky decisions. Our genetic ﬁngerprints state that hunger
is a threat and that food is essential to our survival.

==PAGE:15==

3.2.3 External Factors
Environmental conditions such as temperature and ambient light do not directly aﬀect
our decisions to choose one thing over the other. An attacker can not use the thermostat
to make us feel warm or cold. However, it does aﬀect our feeling about a situation. For
example, physical warmth makes people more conﬁdent and generous, the high light
level increases what we feel, and vice versa. So if you are in a good mood, a bright room
will enhance that feeling. But, if you are angry and upset, you will feel even worse at
the same level of light. Another interesting eﬀect is peer pressure or compliance. Our
social environment inﬂuences our decisions and judgments. If everyone else is running
from the pretty cat with the exceptional mane, one should probably run, too.
After understanding some factors that inﬂuence the decision-making process, we should
next investigate how these aspects can be used remotely.
So, a good phisher who is familiar with the decision-making process knows that he/she
has a good chance of shortening our logic if he succeeds in formulating a compelling mes-
sage that elicits strong emotions in us.
So, the next question that arises is what emotions can make people do unreﬂective
actions.
3.3 The Psychological Triggers
To understand the reasons for the success of social engineering attacks, it is essential to
identify the psychological principles of inﬂuences that aﬀect people.
According to Cialdini[7], there are six major elements to inﬂuence human behavior
and persuade them to comply with requests: reciprocity, consistency and commitment,
social proof, liking, authority, and scarcity.
3.3.1 Reciprocity
Reciprocity is a principle that has been used for a long time and developed in human
history. Many relationships are based on the exchange of services and mutual favors.
It is based on the rule of repaying what others have provided to someone. People who
want to take but do not want to give back are not really liked by others. For example,
if someone gave us a gift, we feel obliged to return a gift as well.
3.3.2 Consistency & Commitment
Once people have committed to something, they are willing to comply with requests
from that part, as they are under pressure to be consistent with what they have already
10
==PAGE:16==
3.3 The Psychological Triggers
done. The force to be consistent with our commitments inﬂuence people to make deci-
sions that are not to always in their best interests.
3.3.3 Social Proof
People tend to copy the behavior of others under unknown circumstances because they
assume that these people know how to behave properly in such situations. It also means
that we trust others who are like us, such friends and their actions. Ryan [8] conducted
an experiment in which he created a false identity of a woman who has a position
within cybersecurity.
This position suggested that she had passed the government’s
trusted background checks, implying some degree of trust that was granted to her. At
the end of the experiment, the false identity collected hundreds of links across various
social media sites. Even cybersecurity professionals trusted her through social evidence
without any veriﬁcation of her trustworthiness and accepted her friendship request only
because their peer did so.
3.3.4 Liking
People tend to be convinced and inﬂuenced by people they like or have a certain sim-
ilarity with. The linking eﬀect drives us to meet envisaged decisions about all sorts of
things. The reason for this is the motive of people to cultivate and establish a relation-
ship.
Ryan [8] deliberately chose a young attractive woman for his false identity. Being a
woman in the man’s controlled security ﬁeld has increased people’s interest in her. The
liking eﬀect inﬂuenced by sex and appearance caused some people to connect to her and
even have sent her personal messages.
3.3.5 Authority
Authority is probably one of the most common principles. Many people have likely al-
ready experienced this in their lives. Milgram [9] conducted an interesting experiment
about obedience to authority. It has shown that the use of authority may let people
comply with some requests even if those requests were against their will or beliefs.
There are two types of authority. One based on the relative position in the hierarchy
and one based on one’s expertise. Researches have classiﬁed the expert power as soft
tactics where inﬂuence factors originate within the inﬂuence agent (e.g., Credibility)
and hierarchy-based power as a harch tactics where harsh inﬂuences are determined
externally of an existing social structure [10].

3.3.6 Scarcity
People can get a weakness if it seems that a shortcut could arise. The feeling that this
may be the last chance can make them take action that they would not otherwise do.
3.3.7 Other factors
Other authors have proposed some psychological principles that can be applied in social
engineering. For example, David Gragg [11] presented some triggers claiming to be ef-
fective such as:
Strong Aﬀect
A strong aﬀect is a trigger that seeks to create a strong state of emotions (e.g., fear,
excitement, or panic) that will weaken the victim’s ability to think rationally, evaluate
objective facts, and develop counter-arguments.
Overloading
Overloading occurs when too much information is presented, but there is not enough
time for processing. In such situations, the logical functioning of people is weakened

We have learned some of the psychological triggers that trigger certain emotions in peo-

It is clear that the inﬂuence of these triggers diﬀers from culture to culture, as each one
of these cultures has diﬀerent characteristics and norms that distinguish them. There-

Geert Hofstede, Gert Jan Hofstede & Michael Minkov have deﬁned six cultural dimen-
sions: Power distance (PDI), Individualism versus collectivism, Uncertainty avoidance,
Masculinity versus femininity, Long-term versus short-term orientation, and Indulgence
versus restraint [14]:
3.4.1 Power Distance (PDI)
PDI is deﬁned as follows:
”the extent to which the less powerful members of organisations and institu-
tions accept and expect that power is distributed unequally.”
In small-power-distance countries, The dependence between boss and subordinates is
limited. They are emotionally distant. Subordinates can tell their opinion and, if neces-
sary, contradict the boss. In the family, the children are treated more or less the same.
They learn to say no and can also confront their parents. The goal of parental education
is to give them control over their lives as long as the children can decide for themselves.
In large-power-distance countries, there is a signiﬁcant dependency between superiors
and subordinates.
It is unlikely that the subordinates would directly confront their
superiors. In the family, children are expected to obey their parents and sometimes it is
even an authority order between them.
3.4.2 Individualism versus Collectivism
Hofstede has deﬁned as:
”Individualism pertains to societies in which the ties between individuals
are loose: Everyone is expected to look after him- or herself and his or her
immediate family ”.
”Collectivism as its opposite pertains to societies in which people from birth
onward are integrated into strong, cohesive in-groups, which throughout peo-
ples lifetime continue to protect them in exchange for unquestioning loyalty
”.

In individualistic cultures, speaking the truth and expressing one’s own opinion is an

Neo4j is easy to learn, easy to use and ensure high performance when searching after

4.2.2 Exploit The Relationship
One way to exploit the relationship is the use of principles such as elicitation to make
people feel comfortable and want to open up. The attacker can then form questions in
such a way that will stimulate people to take a path of behavior the attacker wants.
Elicitation works well for many reasons. These include:
• Most people would like to be polite, especially to strangers.
• Professionals want to seem well informed and smart.
• If somebody admires you, you will tend to talk more and disclose more.
• Most people react politely to people who appear concerned about them.
• Most people will not lie just for the purpose of lying.
Being able to use elicitation eﬀectively will enable attackers to make the victim comply
with their requests and reveal more information [3].

6.4.2 Mitigations
There are some principles that can be learned to cover yourself from social engineering
attacks [5]:
• Critical thinking: It is about to take time reading emails and ask yourself some
questions before taking any actions. Thinking about these question can help to
detect email-phishing. These can include:
– Did I expect this email?
– Has the email been sent by someone I know?
– Does the email imply some psychological triggers such as authority, fear or
curiosity to inﬂuence me to take particular actions?
• Learn to hover: Before clicking on a link, it is sometimes a good idea to move the
mouse over it and see if you can get an URL destination that may seem unusual
or impractical.
• Analyzing e-mail headers: Taking a profound look at the e-mail headers can reveal
the source of the e-mail and other diﬀerent types of information that may give a
hint whether the email is a phish or not.
There are some policies and procedures that organizations can consider to secure them
against social engineerings attacks [15]:
• Well-deﬁned policies: Organizations should provide a well-documented and acces-
sible security policy. Employees must have no doubt regarding how to proceed and
what to do.

Chapter 7
Conclusion and Future Work
Hacking is about ﬁnding vulnerabilities in a system and using these weaknesses to get
access to it. Instead of hacking a computer, social engineers try to exploit the human
psyche to get conﬁdential information. This can be achieved through the use of various
psychological triggers that can release strong emotions, that will paralyze our logic for
a moment so that we make decisions that are less beneﬁcial for us.
People who trust others tend to become victims rather than those who are suspicious.
Greedy people have a weakness for free things and are therefore more likely to open
email attachments or follow links hoping to get something for free. Curiosity, empathy,
excitement, fear, etc. are emotions that an attacker can exploit to make a computer user
perform the desired action.
Some triggers are more likely to be eﬀective by some people than others due to various
factors. An example of these factors is the cultural dimension, in which certain struc-
tures and rules are determined, which in turn favor certain triggers. For example, people
from hierarchical cultures are more susceptible to the authority trigger than people who
are from a country where a hierarchical structure is not enforced.
A social engineer who is familiar with all of these psychological aspects will be able
to proﬁle the victim and create an advanced spear-phishing email that increases the
likelihood that the victim will perform the intended action.
7.0.1 Future Work
We chose DTU Compute as our target. We have written a generic program that takes
multiple URLs from the DTU as input and extracts data from those URLs. Some uni-
versities may have the same website structure as DTU, others may be slightly diﬀerent
or even very diﬀerent.
An extension of the program could be to look at what changes and conﬁgurations are
needed to make the program as general as possible so that it can be used at as many
universities as possible.
