A B S T R A C T
Tests of physical models for the design an optimization of marine
ducted propellers are both time consuming and costly. This has cre-
ated a demand for computer aided simulations to provide compara-
ble results at lower costs and with less time spent.
The hydrodynamic performance of a ducted propeller was ana-
lyzed using the BEM based panel code ESPPRO. In this study, a pro-
gram was developed to generate the geometry and mesh of a hub
and a duct for any given propeller. The mesh consisted of skewed
quadrilateral panels. ESPPRO was modi’¼üed to be able to account for
the new geometries and their respective boundary conditions. Vari-
ous meshing options have been implemented and tested to optimize
the mesh and its stability.
The performance of the model is tested against experimental data
of propeller Ka4-70 inside duct 19Am and 19A. Ten test cases examine
the in’¼éuence of different meshing options on the results in terms
of local circulation, pressure distributions, velocity ’¼üelds and total
forces.
Finally, one test case was prepared to be used as part of a RANS-
BEM coupling with the commercial software SHIPFLOW. It was shown,
that this will ultimately allow a simulation of a nozzle propeller in the
behind ship condition with varying wake’¼üeld.

I N T R O D U C T I O N
Marine propellers require, in some applications, high thrust at low
velocities. In these cases an accelerating duct, also known as a Kort
nozzle, can increase the overall thrust and propulsion ef’¼üciency by
decreasing the tangential and axial loses in the propeller. Tugs and
trawlers are typical examples of such applications, but also azimuth
trusters and tankers use ducted propellers.
If the performance of a ducted propeller can be accurately pre-
dicted, the testing and design process of such systems can be made
more streamlined and ef’¼ücient. Boundary Element Method (BEM) is a
technique that is based on inviscid potential ’¼éow theory and has been
used and improved for simulation of propellers and ducts during the
last decades. The method has proven to give reasonable results, while
keeping the computational effort at a minimum.
The objective of this work has been to modify the existing propeller
simulation code ESPPRO to include a hub and a duct in its simula-
tions as well as to build a tool that generates the geometry and grid
of these appendices. The BEM model can be coupled with a Reynolds-
Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) solver to include viscosity in the sim-
ulation. By doing so, interactions of, for example, a ship hull and a
working propeller, may be accounted for. The initial goal of simulat-
ing a nozzle propeller in the behind ship condition was however not
reached and instead, the main focus has been directed towards the
BEM-part of the project.
The present work is structured in three main parts: First a brief
description of the panel method is presented in Chapter 2 together
with some of the hydrodynamic aspects of a propeller and a duct.
Secondly, the chapters 3 and 4 discuss the grid generation along with
the necessary modi’¼ücations made to ESPPRO. The third part consists
of various test cases presented in Chapter 5-11, in which a conver-
gence and stability analysis of the grid is carried out and where the
results of the simulations are compared against experimental data.
Finally, a conclusion is drawn in Chapter 12 with considerations for
future work.

2.1.2
Wake model
The wake model used for the simulations is a rigid wake based on a
model proposed by Streckwall[11] which does not include contraction
of the wake. The variation in pitch follows a formula proposed by
Greeley & Kerwin[6].
2.1.3
Gap model
Kerwin et al. [7] proposed a model to capture the viscous ’¼éow through
the gap. Baltazar et al. [1] also used a gap model and included a
model to incorporate the in’¼éuence of the duct boundary layer on the
pitch of blade tip vorticity.
This model does however not include a gap: The nodes of tip of
the propeller coincide with the closest nodes on the inner side of the
duct. The mesh program does allow a gap, which then is calculated as
a percentage of the propeller radius. Nevertheless adequate boundary
conditions are not yet implemented in ESPPRO.

2.2.2
MESH
MESH creates the geometry of a hub and a duct using the ’¼üle for-
mat of ESPREP. The geometry is based on the propeller ’¼üle from
ESPREP, which is a point ’¼üle with the cross-section of the duct and

mesh with the blade-wake, see Chapter 3.
2.2.3
ESPPRO
ESPPRO is a potential ’¼éow solver based on BEM. The code was de-
veloped together with ESPREP by Poul Andersen and Svend Vogt
Andersen as part of the development of the Kappel propeller. It has
options for different rigid wake models and can perform unsteady,
time-dependent simulations e.g. with a given wake-’¼üeld.
The unmodi’¼üed version uses only a propeller geometry as input,
while the modi’¼üed version can handle any combination of a propeller,
a duct and a hub.
Initial conditions are given in terms of the ship speed V, option-
ally a wake-’¼üeld, the number of revolutions n or the advance ratio J.
Furthermore, the time-step dt of the unsteady analysis must be given
along with various plot-options.
The programs ESPREP, MESH and ESPPRO are all written in For-
tran. The BLAS routine1 and OpenMP2 is used to reduce computa-
tional time for critical parts of ESPPRO.

3.1

The mesh de’¼üning the geometries consists of quadrilateral or skew

R A N S - B E M C O U P L I N G
The duct from case 8 was tested in a RANS-BEM coupling developed
by Pelle Bo Regener at DTU. The coupling uses the inviscid result
from ESPPRO in combination with a wake ’¼üeld from the RANS based
’¼éow solver SHIPFLOW.
Initially, a uniform in’¼éow is given to ESPPRO. An updated wake
’¼üeld is calculated by SHIPFLOW based on the geometry of the duct
and based on the forces and induced velocities delivered by ESPPRO.
ESPPRO now performs a new calculation, which depends on the up-
dated wake ’¼üeld. This process continues until a certain convergence
is obtained or a maxim number of iterations is reached.
The duct was imported into SHIPFLOW using the parametric com-
mands wsection and rotbody to mesh the duct pro’¼üle and rotate it
into a duct. The effective wake was calculated based on a linear inter-
polations of a probe-plane placed 0.55 radii upstream of the propeller
center in axial direction. The ’¼éow domain was set to a cube with side
length 30R. An overlapping grid was added around the duct, see Fig.
55.
The presented case reached a semi-stable state after 50 iterations.
Figure 56 shows a contour of the axial propeller forces, while Fig. 57
shows the axial and tangential velocity ’¼üeld after 90 iterations. The
acceleration of the ’¼éuid due to the propeller is clearly seen inside the
duct, while the rotation of the ’¼éuid is seen 2R aft of the propeller. A
fairly large vortex due to viscous separation is noticed at the outer
side of the duct.
Although the current model has not been ’¼ün-tuned, it still presents
a working example of the coupling concept including the interaction
between propeller and duct. This gets truly interesting once the hull
of a ship is added and hence a realistic wake ’¼üeld is included in the
simulations. The setting ’¼üles for this run are presented in Appendix
B.3.

C O N C L U S I O N
The BEM based potential ’¼éow solver ESPPRO was designed at DTU
to perform ’¼éow calculations on propellers. During the work of this
thesis a meshing tool based on quadrilateral panels was developed
to generate the geometry and grid of a hub and a duct aligned to
any given propeller. Furthermore, ESPPRO was modi’¼üed to be able
to account for these new geometries and their respective boundary
conditions. This enables ESPPRO to simulate a propeller with a hub
in combination with any given duct geometry.
In an axial direction, the geometries were divided into three parts;
a TE-part aft of the propellerŌĆÖs TE, a Foil-part along the foil of the
propeller and a LE-part forward of the propellerŌĆÖs LE. All parts can be
individually controlled by number of node points and choice of node
distribution.
The sectional pro’¼üle of the hub and duct can be build from given
parameters or can preferentially be loaded as a point ’¼üle. The grid
can optionally be aligned to the nodes of the propeller blades and
its trailing wake. This node-alignment algorithm works for any wake
model and can be included in an unsteady calculation with varying
wake.
Ten test cases were designed to test the original and the modi’¼üed
performance of ESPPRO. The model was tested against experimental
data for the propeller Ka4-70 inside duct 19Am and 19A. The hub
was modelled without a wake and showed generally to improved the
precision of the model.
The duct was ’¼ürst modelled as a simple cylinder without wake.
This model was stable and the result showed an increased tip loading
on the blades of the propeller. Although the ’¼éow was not realistic,
the model might still be used as part of a RANS-BEM coupling, where
the RANS part solves the general ’¼éow around the duct.
Several variations of ducts with wake were tested. The ducts using
pitch aligned panels were generally unstably and produced results
with large deviations compared to experimental data. The solution
of the simulations were very sensitive to small alterations in mesh
resolution. Unfortunately, it was not possible to elucidate the basis
of this behavior. However, it is believed, that the key to the problem
is to be found in the skewness of the panels, which results in highly
non-planar panels. A suggested solution would be either to use trian-
gular panels or to use a combination of semi-rectangular panels and
a viscous vortex core model.
87
==PAGE:102==
88
conclusion
Case 3, 4, 6 and 7 used the coordinates of the tip section of the pro-
peller as part of the duct mesh. Furthermore, a no-neighbor boundary
condition was implemented for the duct cells adjacent to the propeller
blade. This clearly improved the local potentials around the blade and
duct.
Case 8 used semi-rectangular panels and produced fair results com-
pared to experimental data. The deviation in terms thrust coef’¼ücients
over a range of advance ratios was in the order of 0-15% with the
largest deviations arising for highly loaded propellers.
A RANS-BEM coupled simulation using SHIPFLOW was performed
for case 8, and showed a converging tendency of the solution. If this
path is followed, it is believed that a fair prediction for a ducted pro-
peller in the behind ship condition is possible.
In conclusion, this project has allowed ESPPRO to model ducted
propellers and to use the results as part of a RANS-BEM coupling. How-
ever, further validation and development of the code is suggested in
order to get precise and reliable results.
