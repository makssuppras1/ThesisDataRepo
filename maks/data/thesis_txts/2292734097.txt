Abstract
The substantial and economically important ﬁshery after Norway Lobster
(Nephrops norvegicus) in Skagerrak is mostly done by trawling the seabed,
which often causes large amounts of by-catch. A large part of the by-catch is
unsellable and thus discarded. One of the commonly discarded species is the
European plaice (Pleuronectes platessa), but large plaice are rarely discarded
as they have a commercial value and high marked demand. However, indi-
viduals below the minimum landing size are illegal to sell and consequently
discarded. The focus of the study was therefor to examine the short-term
mortality of the discarded undersized plaice. Additionally, to evaluate if be-
havior response and injuries could be used as a predictor for mortality. The
study required the development of a method to monitor the individuals and
to assess the behavior and injuries while in the ﬁeld.
The report reviews the landing obligation of the European Union, the ﬁsh-
ery and management of the plaice in the North Sea and Skagerrak. Further,
the experimental catch composition is discussed, while the considerations of
the method and diﬀerent perspectives of discarding is described together with
a review of previous studies. The article describes the method and results
of the experiment, which showed that plaice had an average short-term mor-
tality of of 85%, ranging from 61-100%, after 240 hours of monitoring. The
experiment indicated that environmental factors inﬂuenced the survival, with
the largest mortality occurring when the trawl was contaminated by excess
mud and large pieces of peat. The mortality prediction showed that injuries,
and not reﬂex behavior, was the best predictor and primary driver on the
mortality of the plaice in this experiment.

1.
Introduction
1.1
Discards
An integral part of the ﬁshing industry is the often signiﬁcant diﬀerence be-
tween what ﬁshermen catch and what is brought back to harbor. The former
is deﬁned as the ‘landings’, and the diﬀerence between this and the catch
is discards [STECF, 2013, ICES, 2014a], e.g. ”discards are the portion of a
catch which is not retained on board during commercial ﬁshing operations
and is returned to the sea” [Catchpole et al., 2005a].
Discards exist due
to several reasons and has potentially many fates [Broadhurst et al., 2006,
Benoˆıt et al., 2013]. The main reason is due to speciﬁc catch being unsellable
or having low economic value, i.e. target ﬁsh that are under the legal min-
imum landing size (MLS), non-marketable invertebrates (small crabs and
crustaceans, starﬁsh, etc.) or if the ﬁsherman is not able to land the catch
due to not having quota on a caught species [Feekings et al., 2012, Graham
et al., 2007]. The quota issue occurs when the ﬁshing operation has met the
quota set or if a ﬁshery does not have a quota for a species that is caught to-
gether with the target species (bycatch) [STECF, 2013]. The unwanted ﬁsh
or invertebrates are returned to the sea by the ﬁshermen and depending on
the ﬁshery the discards can be a considerable part of the catch. For the North
Sea the total discard quantity was once estimated to almost 1 million tons,
comprising almost a third of total yearly catches [Catchpole et al., 2005a].
It is mainly demersal trawl ﬁsheries that are considered responsible for the
majority of discards [MegaPesca, 1999] from vessels using bottom beam- and
otter-trawls targeting ﬂatﬁsh and a mixed ﬁshery, respectively. The beam-
trawlers ﬁsh predominantly in the southern North Sea targeting common sole,
Solea solea and European plaice, Pleuronectes platessa with discard rates
of 70-95% on primarily plaice below MLS, common dab, Limanda limanda
and invertebrates [Catchpole et al., 2005a]. The otter-trawlers operate in
the northern North Sea and target Norway lobsters, Nephrops norvegicus
(commonly referred to as Nephrops) or a mixed demersal ﬁshery targeting
Atlantic cod, Gadus morhua, haddock, Melanogrammus aegleﬁnus and whit-

ing, Melangius merlangus. The discard rate for otter-trawls varies between
20-65% depending on the species and ﬁshery [Catchpole et al., 2005a], but as
with the beam trawls the discards mainly consists of dab and target species
below MLS.
1.2
Landing obligation
Due to the waste of a potential natural resource the European Commission
began working on an initiative to document and identify problems and possi-
ble solutions to discarding [STECF, 2013]. By 2007 the European Parliament
and European Council recognized the need for “a progressive introduction of
a discard ban (COM(2007), 136 ﬁnal). It took public pressure [STECF, 2013]
and additionally two years to implement it as a legislative proposal in 2009
(COM(2009), 163 ﬁnal), but as of January 1st 2014 the new Common Fish-
ery Policy (CFP) of the European Union changed focus from regulations on
landings to catches. This meant an introduction of the landing obligation
under article 15, which prohibits discarding of species subject to catch lim-
its. However, in the article under section 2(b) is a possible exemption from
the landing obligation in “species for which scientiﬁc evidence demonstrates
high survival rates”.
This rather non-speciﬁc statement enables a ﬁshery
with “high” discard survival to evade the landing obligation and to continue
to discard the unwanted catch. There is no deﬁned speciﬁc high survival
rate, and it remains a decision based on economically, biologically and en-
vironmentally tradeoﬀs to be made on a management level. Survival rates,
however, is connected to the previously mentioned fate of the discarded or-
ganisms, where literature categorize the inﬂuencing factors into three major
categories: biological, environmental and operational [Davis, 2002]. The bi-
ological covers the diﬀerence in species, sizes, ages and potential spawning
exhaustion, which is linked to physical condition and predation. The envi-
ronmental category includes seasonal changes, e.g. temporal diﬀerences in
temperature, weather and light, or the diﬀerence in pressure or salinity due
to rapid depth changes. Operational factors is due to diﬀerent ﬁshing meth-
ods and -gear, tow speed and duration, the catch composition, handling and
air exposure if the ﬁsh is sorted on deck [STECF, 2013].

1.4
Danish Nephrops ﬁshery
The Danish Nephrops ﬁshery is an economically high value ﬁshery [Frand-
sen et al., 2009] where the majority of the catch is processed and exported.
Among the highest paying catches in Denmark (avg. 77,5 Dkk/kg in 2015,
NaturErhvervstyrelsen), it is an important ﬁshery centered in the northern
Jutland where most of the catch is landed. Around 3,000 tons of Nephrops
is landed yearly from Skagerrrak and Kattegat [NaturErhvervsstyrelsen] by
mainly Danish and Swedish vessels [ICES, 2015b] and includes a large by-
catch of other economical important species as cod, plaice and haddock etc.
[Krag et al., 2008]. The ﬁshery is done throughout the year on muddy sea
bed deeper than 20 meter preferred by the burrowing Nephrops [Rice and
Chapman, 1971]. In Kattegat and Skagerrak there is a minimum landing size
of 40 mm carapace length and the ﬁshery is mainly done in a mixed otter-
trawl ﬁshery with substantial by-catches [Madsen et al., 2013, Frandsen et al.,
2010].

1.5
The study and experiment
The importance and magnitude of the Danish Nephrops ﬁshery makes it
an obvious contender for examination of its bycatch discard survival as this
has both biological and economical potentials [Krag et al., 2008, STECF,
2013]. In this study the focus was on the discard mortality of plaice in the
Nephrops ﬁshery, where the catch was examined and monitored to deter-
mine short-term mortality. In this scenario short-term is deﬁned as several
days to a maximum of a few weeks, while long-term would refer to months
or years. Since a large part of the discards in European Nephrops ﬁshery
consists of plaice below the minimum landing size [Catchpole et al., 2005b,
Feekings et al., 2012, Frandsen et al., 2009], the attention will be mainly
on smaller individuals below MLS (since larger plaice was assumed to be
landed and sold).
The study involved an experiment in which the plaice
was caught during trawling for Nephrops, individually examined to a vitality
assessment and transported to an inshore facility for 10 days of monitoring
to account for possible delayed mortality. The vitality assessment and mor-
tality relationship was analyzed in an attempt to examine its potential use
as a predictor of mortality. The experiment was carried out in according to
and with inspiration from the ICES guidelines on discard mortality [ICES,
2014a]. Hence the main objective of this master thesis was to develop and
demonstrate a method to examine the short-term discard survival of plaice in
Nephrops trawl ﬁshery and discuss the results in regards to the EU landing
obligation and potential mortality predictors.

2.
Mangement of North Sea plaice
2.1
Biology
The plaice, Pleuronectes platessa is a bento-demersal ﬂatﬁsh of the Pleu-
ronectidae family and the order Pleuronectiformes that encompasses the ﬂat-
ﬁsh. Age of maturity diﬀers between males and females, where the former
approaches maturity after two or three years and the females typically a few
years later [ICES, 2005]. Spawning occurs on deep water where the plaice
rely on tides or currents to transport the larvae into shallow coastal areas
[ICES, 2015e]. The juveniles stay near the coast and as they grow larger and
near maturity they move into deeper waters. The maximum size and age for
plaice is 100 cm and 50 years, respectively, but in the targeted ﬁshery the
majority of plaice are caught with a total length (TL) of 25-45 cm and are up
to 10 years old [ICES, 2005, 2015e]. The plaice prefer sand sediments and can
tolerate low-saline environments. The ability to withstand brackish waters
enables a large spatial distribution along the coastal zone of the North-east
Atlantic, ranging all the way from North Africa to the north of Norway, even
occasionally found oﬀGreenland [ICES, 2015e]. The diet of an adult plaice
consists mainly of polychaetes, bivalves and small crustaceans [ICES, 2005].
2.2
Spatial deﬁnition of the experiment
When assessing the impact of the ﬁshery on the plaice population, it is crucial
to realize that most ﬁshery management is deﬁned into diﬀerent speciﬁed
areas.
These separate areas of the sea are in reality potentially strongly
connected. This is especially the case with the population of plaice in this
experiment. As can be seen in Figure 3.2 the experiment was done on the
border between Skagerrak and Kattegat, which are historically considered
part of the same management unit called Division IIIa, as seen in Figure 2.1.
At the beginning of the century Skagerrak were proposed as an indepen-
dent area [ICES, 2015e] when doing stock assessments and was surveyed and

The ﬁshery
The majority of plaice ﬁshery in the North Sea is done by beam trawlers in

ﬁshery after Nephrops and cod, respectively, together with a small ﬁshery
using large meshed (>100 mm) gear targeting greater sized plaice [ICES,
2015e]. An innovative new ﬁshery pattern is emerging with the increase in
Pulse trawl operations, a ﬁshery using electric pulses instead of heavy tickler
chains to get the burrowed ﬂatﬁsh into the beam-trawl [Decostere et al.,

2.5
Fishing mortality
The ﬁshing mortality serves as a representation of the pressure the ﬁsheries
assert on the stocks [Jennings et al., 2001]. In short, the mortality of a ﬁsh can
come either from natural sources or by being ﬁshed by humans. With a large
ﬁshing pressure, a high value of F, a big share of the stock is taken by ﬁshery
each year as the natural mortality is often considered constant [Jennings
et al., 2001, ICES, 2015e]. Depending on the size of the stock the reactions
to an increase in the ﬁshing mortality can diﬀer substantially. In Figure 2.2
the stock, ﬁshing mortality and catch of the North Sea and Skagerrak enables
a basic cause and eﬀect analysis. Around 1987 catches increased greatly with
only a slight increase in ﬁshing mortality (due to a big spike in recruits a
few years prior [ICES, 2015b]). However, despite being the third highest
ﬁshing mortality of the entire period, the SSB was the highest measured at
that point. In the following year the ﬁshing mortality was kept high, but
with the large and strong cohort gone, the SSB and catch steadily decreased.
Attempts of increasing catches with increases in the ﬁshing pressure only
caused further decrease in the SSB. Since the beginning of the century the
ﬁshing mortality has been considerably decreased to an all time low, which,
most likely, is resulting in the current very large spawning stock biomass.
For Kattegat the same tendencies can be seen (Figure 2.2), although it is
most likely another driver causing the current increase in SSB. In 2004 a cod
recovery plan was established that intentionally caused an overall decrease
in ﬁshing pressure (EC regulation 423/2004). This appears especially visible
in Kattegat and is causing a present ﬁshing mortality below the maximum
sustainable yield (MSY, see section 2.6) enabling growth of the stock biomass.
2.6
TAC and catch history
TAC is the total allowed catch that the member states of the EU and, in the
case of the plaice, Norway share mainly through a predeﬁned agreement and
partly through negotiations.
While a maximum of ca. 350.000 tons of plaice was caught in the middle
of 1980, the current catch level is below 150.000 tons per year in the North
Sea and Skagerrak. The TAC in 2015 is 128.376 tons in the North Sea and
10.056 for Skagerrak (since the advice in 2014 still considered Skagerrak an
independent population). The vast majority of the plaice is caught by the
Netherlands, Denmark and the United Kingdom that together share 80% of
the total allowed catch [ICES, 2015c]. The catch in Kattegat and Skagerrak
is mainly caught by the Danish ﬂeet.
8
==PAGE:14==
The TAC is, however, only the total landings, hence there is an additional
part of the catch that is discarded. For the plaice ﬁshery this is a signiﬁcant
quantity of about 40% by weight [ICES, 2015d]. This is evident in the advice
for 2016 where, enabled by the current magnitude of the spawning stock
biomass, the recommendation is a 30% increase in TAC for North Sea and
Skagerrak [ICES, 2015b]. This leads to an increase in TAC to 159.197 tons,
which in turn implies a total catch of 216.345 tons including discards.
2.7
Management plan
There is no current management plan for plaice in Skagerrak or Kattegat.
However, there is a minimum landing size of 270 mm TL on plaice caught in
the North Sea, Skagerrak and Kattegat. Additionally, the plaice in the North
Sea is together with the sole part of a multiannual management plan started
in 2007 (EC regulation 676/2007). It is a two staged plan, where the ﬁrst
objective is to rebuild the stock to within safe biological limits, where after
the stock can be exploited at a level equal MSY. Maximum sustainable yield
is a theoretical concept where the catch roughly equals the yearly growth
potential of the stock. With the resent high level of SSB and low ﬁshing
mortality the primary objective of the plan is considered completed [ICES,
2015d]. There is, however, a conﬂict since the original plan states that the
MSY ﬁshing mortality (Fmsy) of stage 2 is targeted to 0,3, whereas the most
resent calculations limits Fmsy to 0,19 [ICES, 2015d].
In the North Sea there is a large marine protected area called The Plaice
Box located close to shore with restricted ﬁshing, i.e.
engine power and
eﬀort, to protect undersized plaice. The eﬀect of the Plaice Box, that was
established in 1989, was until recently thought to have be negligible [Beare
et al., 2013], but in the light of the current very high SSB, this notion could
change.
When ﬁshing Nephrops, the target species of the experiment, there is
minimum mesh size regulation of 90 mm in Kattegat and Skagerrak, however
a smaller mesh size of 70-89 mm is allowed when increasing the selectivity of
the ﬁshery with the use of a grid and a square-mesh codend [Graham et al.,
2007]. North of 56°N and east of 5°E in the North Sea, the targeted plaice
trawl ﬁshery has a minimum mesh size of 120 mm [ICES, 2005]. South of this
area, in the beam trawl ﬁshery, the minimum mesh size allowed is 80mm.

3.1
The vessel and gear
The experiment was carried out on the ﬁshing vessel RI 286 Tana (pictured in
3.1) with the skipper Jørgen Michelsen. The glass ﬁber vessel was constructed
in 1999 on The Faeroese Islands by P/F AWI Boats and is registered in Hvide
Sande. Its weight of 10,5 tons fully loaded is powered by a 125 KW diesel
engine, and has the dimensions of 11,65 meters in length, 3,57 meters in width
and a draft of 1,7 meters. It has a cargo capacity of 3,1 tons, which is used
for several types of ﬁshing depending on time of year and weather conditions.
10
==PAGE:16==
The ﬁshing vessel is equipped with gears allowing for both longlining with
hooks and trawling operations. The longlining is used for mackerel ﬁshing in
the Norwegian Sea, whereas trawl is used locally on day trips. During the
experiment the vessel was geared for Nephrops trawling with a single trawl.
The trawl used during the experiments was a speciﬁc otter-trawl made for

3.2
Fishing grounds
The experiment was carried out in the southeastern Skagerrak, just north of
Kattegat. Figure 3.2 illustrates the transects registered during the experi-
ment and shows that all tows were done in a small area of ca. 10 x 10 km.
On the 2nd of July only one tow was completed, which was due to strong
weather and big waves causing the loss of an entire catch during hauling.
The bottom part of Figure 3.2 shows a GPS tracker continuously recording
the ﬁshing and steaming on the 3rd of July. It illustrates a discrepancy be-
tween the noted and recorded tracks. The exact reason for this is unknown,
but apart from the fact that ﬁshing rarely occurs along a straight transect,
there are a few possible explanations for the discrepancy that day. First and
foremost, the expedition on that day was nearly canceled due to bad weather
with strong winds and high waves (7-10 m/s SE and 1-1,5 meter), and al-
though it never became hazardous, it could be the reason why the trawls do
not follow the planned straight transect as the vessel was hard to keep on
course. It also shows a potential mistiming of the noting. The ﬁrst tow most
likely ends in what appears as a little dot in the furthest eastern point, but
due to a delay in the notation, the coordinate is not written down before
the vessel has drifted a bit to the north. Additionally, the area is heavily
populated by wrecks (i.e. see Figure 3.3) and the possibility of the skipper
having to navigate around the wrecks could explain the peculiar navigation

Length distribution
With length and weight recorded of all individual plaice, it enables a com-
parison between the experiment and the extracted subsamples of the catch.
However, as the experimental ﬁsh was weighted when dead or by the end
of the trial, and assuming after having lost some weight, the most obvious
comparison is the total length. Ideally, the distributions of the plaice from
the experiment and the subsamples should be identical, as it would indicate
that the experiment is done on the same population and that it represents
the catch.

a strong similarity and validates the experiment as being representative of
the catch. A short discussion on the causality of the size-bias is included in

4.
Method process and considerations
When leaving for a research expedition on sea preparation is key. When iso-
lated on the sea, one has to secure and plan for unexpected changes, so that
through adaptation and improvisation the experiment can continue. This
demands for the researcher to master an overview and strong logistics. That
is why executing an experiment at sea requires an almost tedious amount of
detailed planning. The method of the study is split into an experiment on
sea with the objective of collecting the ﬁsh and a second part that covers
monitoring the short-term mortality of the ﬁsh in holding tanks. Obviously,
there is a chronological order of operations, but as the ﬁrst batch of indi-
viduals are caught and brought back for monitoring, the two parts of the
experiment runs parallel. This chapter covers the underlying considerations
and details that were not possible to include in PaperI.
4.1
Preparations
Before leaving Hirtshals there was a need to check and bring a lot of diﬀerent
equipment, of which much was absolutely critical for the accomplishment of
the experiment and proper collection of data. We brought and used various
instruments that included a length-measure board, an Avery Dennison Mark
II tagging gun including numerical t-bar tags, a Handy Polaris hand-held
Oxygen meter, a Star:Oddi DST CTD, and pre-printed schematics on water
tolerant paper (Appendix B). The schematics was used as guidance and en-
abled the noting of data while collecting and physical inspecting each ﬁsh.
By bringing video- and regular cameras we could document and ﬁlm the ex-
periment, which also served as the purpose of supplying illustrations to this
and other reports. Obviously, as the experiment was done at night on sea,
appropriate work clothing was needed and required to keep one dry, warm
and safe while working. Additionally, duct tape, zip ties, pencils, other equip-
ment and spare parts was brought in case something needed to be replaced
or repaired.
We used a truck with a large 1000 L thermo isloated water tank to trans-

port the live experimental ﬁsh back and forth from the harbor. Before de-
parture, the tank was ﬁlled with saltwater and a few 20L plastic containers
of solid ice, to keep the water cold without diluting the saltwater. The ﬁsh
was transported in plastic boxes with covers that was modiﬁed to ﬁt into

4.2
Experiment at sea
By the end of the typically 3 hour long tow, the deck was prepared for
working, which consisted of setting up the pound and arranging workspace,
giving us room to handle and perform the experiment on the incoming ﬁsh.
There was two time notations on the survey sheet; the “Time, haul end”
was the time where the codend left the water and the “Time” was when
the catch was released into the pound. Depending on possible complications
during the hauls, there could be a noteworthy diﬀerence. The time noted
when the ﬁsh was released into the pound was used to calculate the “time
on deck”, which was the duration of each individual’s potential time out of
water. In a non-experimental situation a ﬁsh in a pound has two futures as it
will either be landed or discarded and the time to either of that depends on
the physical position in the pound. Previous studies have shown that there
is a negative relationship between time on deck and discard survival [Davis,
2002, Benoˆıt et al., 2013], why we found it important to include this variable.
The environment of a pound can diﬀer substantially between vessels and Tana
had a wet pound with a conveyor belt. After the catch was released from the
codend into the pound, it functioned by being ﬁlled roughly a third with sea
water and then a conveyor belt was used to transport the ﬁsh on to a sorting
table. The approximately 0,5 m2 large sorting table had an opening in the
side leading to a chute that exited on the portside of the vessel allowing the
unwanted ﬁsh to be discarded while sorting. Inside the pound the majority
of the ﬁsh was emerged in water that was refreshed periodically.
As the
ﬁsh became fewer the eﬃciency of the conveyor belt was reduced, which was
oﬀset by letting out water and thus lowering the water state in the pound to
allow the catch to reach the conveyor belt again. Often there was subsequent
need to reﬁll and thereby renewing the water.
As mentioned, a similar experiment was done simultaneously with Nephrops,

4.4
Vitality assessment
In addition to the capture and further holding of the plaice to examine the
short-term mortality, each individual was examined for injuries and tested
for reﬂex impairment. The inclusion of reﬂex impairment takes inspiration
from previous literature that examines the prediction of delayed mortality
using immediate response deﬁciency on discarded individuals [Davis, 2007,
Davis and Ottmar, 2006]. The concept is based on the assumption that ﬁsh
stress and weakening can work as a proxy for short-term mortality, so that
by measuring the performance, or lack thereof, of diﬀerent actions, it enables
a prediction of possible short-term mortality [Davis, 2010]. Easily accom-
plished in the ﬁeld, e.g. aboard ﬁshing vessels or tank-side in aquaculture,
signs of stress is assessed for each individual by using diﬀerent tests, which
aggregates to a score purposed to illustrate a possible vitality impairment.
The reﬂex action mortality predictors (RAMP) covers a wide variety of ex-
aminations [Humborstad et al., 2009, ICES, 2014b] and the choice of tests
depends on the species, size and logistics of the experiment. As the impair-
ment of an individual increase, so does the risk of mortality [Davis, 2002,
2007].
In Figure 4.3 is an example of a reﬂex impairment and mortality rela-
tionship on cod showing a logistic sigmoid curve (from Humborstad et al.
[2016]). The graphs shows how an increase in reﬂex impairment follows an
increase in probability of mortality. However, it appears that ﬁsh are able
to tolerate a certain degree of stress before reaching a critical point where
24
==PAGE:30==
mortality increase drastically, as seen similarly in other studies [Davis and
Ottmar, 2006, Davis, 2010, Humborstad et al., 2009]. Depending on the sce-
narios, in the case of Figure 4.3 the diﬀerence in environment between lab,
longline and pot, the critical point seems to be at a lower impairment for the
ﬁeld experiment, most obvious in lab versus pots. This explanation could
be that ﬁsh caught in the ﬁeld are more stressed than if they were tested in
a laboratory. However, the result show that longline appear to have a less
harmful eﬀect than pot ﬁshery [Humborstad et al., 2016], which, as passive
gear, is generally considered less damaging [Broadhurst et al., 2006, STECF,
2013]. In this case the explanation is that when entering the pots, the cods
appear active leading to frequent collisions with the netting and the same
happening during hauling, causing injuries and increased stress [Humborstad
et al., 2016].

5.1
Historical discard studies on plaice
Assessing discard mortality is an old discipline and the ﬁrst documented
study on ﬁshery and catch related mortality was done in Scotland in 1890.
The study included experiments on plaice, although only on the immediate
mortality [Broadhurst et al., 2006, Fulton, 1890]. Another early study focused
on the relevant, in the context of this report, vitality of plaice during trawling.
That study showed that plaice discard mortality was connected with tow
duration and deck conditions and estimated the discard mortality of otter-
trawled plaice to be more than 90% [Borley, 1909]. Evidently, even 100 years
ago there was an awareness that discarded individuals during trawl ﬁshery
was in high risk of mortality. However, to enable comparison the primary
focus will be on more recent literature that represent the current and relevant
ﬁshery.
As the ﬁrst of relevant examples, a study in 1990 on the discard survival
of sole and plaice examined the mortality of escapees during ﬁshing (exclu-
sively on sole) and subsequent discard morality [van Beek et al., 1990]. The
experiment was done on commercial otter- and beam-trawlers in the North
Sea, where the focus on the otter-trawler was mainly on the eﬀects of tow du-
ration, whereas the experiment on the beam-trawlers included several other
variables. The results can be seen in Table 5.1. The study found a signif-
icant diﬀerence in discard survival between the two gear types in favor of
otter-trawl. An explanation of this, apart from the obviously diﬀerent gear
conﬁguration, could be the large diﬀerence in tow speed, as beam-trawling is
done considerably faster (3 knots and 5,5 knots, respectively). Additionally,
it appears that there is a diﬀerence in survival with respect to tow dura-
tion, as the survival is lower for the 60 minutes compared to 120 minutes
for the otter-trawl. This apparent paradox is explained by the low number
of individuals (15, 17 and 46) in the experiments with a tow duration of 60

5.2
Economical trade-oﬀby discarding
As a consequence of discards only being caught to be returned to the sea,
public understanding and support has historical been sparse [Catchpole et al.,
2005a]. In fact, part of the reason for the implementation of the EU landing
obligation can most likely be attributed to the British chef Hugh Fearnley-
Whittingstall, who in 2010 began a crusade against discards with the tele-
vision campaign Fish Fight on Channel 4. This resulted in a partition with
close to a million people signing for an end to ﬁsh discarding, and although
the European Commission was already working towards a solution to dis-
cards, the massive public and media attention probably inﬂuenced the vot-
ing for the new CFP in 2012 [STECF, 2013]. The public debate was focused
on the natural resource waste of discarding, but herein was also implied an
economical conﬂict as the ﬁshermen obviously cannot sell discarded ﬁsh.
Without going into a discussion on quota sizes and system, something
outside the topic of this report, the public perception and the underlying as-
sumption in much ﬁshery management is a discard mortality of 100%, which
Table 5.1 actually disproves for plaice. Evidently, there are discarded in-
dividuals that have survival and potentially may return to the population.
And these individuals are possible to catch again where circumstances en-
ables landing, i.e. after growth to above the MLS or from a vessel or time
where quota is available. Additionally, the surviving discards are given the
possibility to spawn and further contribute to the population. With a land-
ing obligation, however, any unwanted by-catch, with or without marked
demands, has to be brought to harbor.
Potentially, one can imagine the
possibility of selling ”the discards” to industrial ﬁsh processing plants with,
all others equal, a lower expected price per kilo. With this in mind, the
following section examines the question of the number of years to pass before
there is an economical beneﬁt of discarding.

5.3
Discard avoidance
In the previous section the main assumption is that discards are unavoidable
and while that might be the case in some ﬁsheries [Broadhurst et al., 2006],
36
==PAGE:42==
one of the main reasons for implementing the discard ban was to increase
the incentives of the ﬁshermen to change behavior and ﬁsh more selective
[STECF, 2013]. With towed gear there are two general sources of mortality;
typically unseen, but nonetheless important, is the mortality from individu-
als avoiding or escaping the gear while towing or hauling and another is the
discard mortality post-hauling [Broadhurst et al., 2006, Madsen et al., 2008].
Damages on escapees can be attributed to fatigue from the escaping behav-
ior in combination with scale and/or mucus loss due to the potential contact
with the netting, which can further cause infections or fungus [Broadhurst
et al., 2006]. Kaiser and Spencer [1995] examined a small sample of ﬂatﬁsh
with a codend-cover and found a very high mortality of >84% for species as
dab and sole. However, Broadhurst et al. [2006] lists several studies showing
superior escape mortality and suggest that since discards is the factor con-
tributing most to mortality in the ﬁshery, the focus should be on improving
the selection by having the unwanted catch escape the gear during towing.
There are two general types of selection, e.g. discard avoidance, and it is
typically divided between technical and manageable [Catchpole et al., 2005a,
Broadhurst et al., 2006].
The manageable part is what an authority can govern through rules and
regulations. Examples of this would be restricting areas from ﬁshing (i.e.
Plaice box), mesh size regulations, minimum landing size, restrictions on
days at sea or even implementations of certain mandatory deck conditions
that could reduce air exposure time, which several studies has shown to
impact discard survival [van Beek et al., 1990, Ross and Hokenson, 1997,
Humborstad et al., 2009, Benoˆıt et al., 2013]. Even a restriction on the tow
duration could potentially lead to reduced discard mortality, as described in
section 5.1.
The technical discard avoidance is the improvement of the selection of the
ﬁshing gear by facilitating escape behavior, which for plaice below MLS with
a similar Nephrops-gear conﬁguration is 72%, e.g. only 28% of the catch is
retained after towing and hauling [Madsen et al., 2008]. The technical avoid-
ance overlap with the manageable avoidance, since mesh size is directly, and
thus minimum landing size indirectly, translated into the physical conﬁgu-
ration of the gear with deﬁned L50 for most target species. L50 is the ﬁsh
length where 50% of the individuals are retained in the codend.
In the North Sea, Skagerrak and Kattegat Nephrops ﬁshery, several gear
conﬁgurations of speciﬁc mesh size and shape has been tested in an eﬀort
to reduce the by-catch and subsequently the discards. A rigid sorting grid
placed just in front of the codend is an example of a highly selective gear
modiﬁcation. From a study by Frandsen et al. [2009] the grid shows a drastic
decrease in catch of plaice with no change in the L50, indicating that the
37
==PAGE:43==
grid reduced the quantity without changing the size selectivity signiﬁcantly.
The reduction in catch is, however, also the case for all other species than
Nephrops, causing a potential decrease in economical value of the landings.
Most trawl conﬁgurations use diamond meshes in codends, but there is
an alternative in square meshes. The square mesh is designed to avoid clos-
ing when stretched, something that is an essential characteristic of diamond
meshes. The diﬀerence between square and diamond meshes is that square
mesh should allow larger openings for the ﬁsh to escape. This obviously de-
pends on shape of the ﬁsh and studies have shown that ﬂatﬁsh, and plaice in
particular, have larger L50 with diamond meshes contrary to roundﬁsh that
can ﬁt through a squared mesh opening [Frandsen et al., 2010].
Another gear selection device is the inclusion of a square mesh panel
(SMP) in a codend of diamond meshes. Adding a SMP to the top of the
codend should allow ﬁsh an escape window and thus reduce the by-catch
in Nephrops ﬁshery [Krag et al., 2008, Frandsen et al., 2009, 2010]. In the
previously described study by Frandsen et al. [2009] the addition of 120mm
SMP did not seem to reduce the total catch of plaice nor the share below
MLS. Additionally, it signiﬁcantly reduced the L50 from 21,9 and 21,5 of the
standard and grid conﬁguration, respectively, to 18,8 cm with the SMP. In
another study diﬀerent positions and mesh sizes of a SMP was examined
with similar results; for plaice the eﬀect of a SMP was marginal at best,
however in comparison between a 90mm and 120 mm SMP, the latter would
be preferable economically [Krag et al., 2008].
T90 is a type of diamond mesh that improves selectivity by turning the
meshes 90° and thus is supposed to work similar to a square mesh. However,
as with the SMP, the T90 showed no improvement in the selectivity of plaice,
with addition to increasing the proportion of individuals below MLS retained
in the codend [Madsen et al., 2012].
Eventually, as the escape behavior of plaice appears to be staying low in
the trawl, a square mesh panel in the top of the codend seems futile [Frandsen
et al., 2010]. That leaves the the grid as the best selection device with the
trade-oﬀof reducing the economical value of the catch substantially.
5.4
Discard fate
With the introduction of the landing obligation the outcome of the discards
might seem irrelevant. However, one could argue that the study into the fate
of discards never has been more important since a future without discards
can have potential ecosystem eﬀects. Discard mortality rates below 100%
makes it possible that survivors remain in the population, where they can
38
==PAGE:44==
contribute to the stock and ecosystem or be available to catch another time
with an economical beneﬁt.
It is a common saying among ﬁshermen that demersal trawling is like
a plow that disturbs the sea bed, making burrowed organisms available as
food to the demersal fauna and crustaceans. While this might be wishful
thinking by the ﬁshermen, theoretical studies has shown that under the right
circumstances, e.g. bottom-up ecosystem with a resilient benthos, this could
be plausible [van Denderen et al., 2013]. Similar is the idea that dead discards
reaching the bottom could feed the demersal and benthic community, which
does ﬁnd support in the literature, however it is only a very limited number
of commercial species that scavenges on the dead discards [Catchpole et al.,
2006b]. Furthermore, there is a big population of seabirds, primarily gulls,
where a big part of their daily consumption comes from the discards of the
many ﬁshing vessels on the sea.
There is examples of up to 57% of the
discards taken by the seabirds [Catchpole et al., 2006b] and this study shows
that the majority of the plaice below MLS can be taken by large seagulls (see
PaperI).
It is outside the scope of this report to decide if the fate of the discarded
ﬁsh are to be processed into ﬁshoil or to end up in the stomachs of seagulls
and demersal scavengers. However, returning them to the sea at least allows
for the possibility that the discards survive and contribute to the ecosystem.

Final remarks
Previous studies have shown reﬂex impairment as a tool to predict stress and
delayed mortality in diﬀerent species of ﬁsh [Davis, 2007, 2010, Humborstad
et al., 2009, 2016]. In this experiment the RAMP impairment was not suf-
ﬁcient to illustrate a signiﬁcant relationship with discard mortality, which
was likely due to the very high short-term mortality. This lead to the inclu-
sion of injuries in the vitality impairment score, which eventually illustrated
how the initial increase in vitality impairment was without high associated
mortality. With increasing levels of vitality impairment, often as a result of
several injuries, the mortality was subsequently high.
The uncoupling of reﬂex impairments from the delayed mortality leaves
the injuries as the best mortality indicator, which could be useful as predictor
since injury assessment can be done in the ﬁeld without accessories. Conse-
quently, using vitality impairment as a mortality predictor for plaice could
be plausible, as the results show a high mortality after medium accumulated
impairment, e.g. several recognizable injuries.
Ultimately, all studies in Table 5.1 ubiquitously show that the demersal
trawl short-term discard mortality of plaice, independent of gear, is high and
only a few studies have a mortality below 50%. In the landing obligation the
exemption called for an undeﬁned ”high survival rate”, something STECF
[2013] suggested should be at least half the discards. This was only achieved
in a single of the van Beek et al. [1990] experiments and the Millner et al.
[1993] study, which was done with short tows in very shallow waters. The
remaining of van Beek et al. [1990] experiments had discard mortalities in
the range 77–85%, which is in line of the results from this study that showed
61-100% discard mortality.
This illustrates and strengthens the need for
additional studies to show if and how demersal trawling of Nephrops can de-
crease the discard mortality of the bycatch without using a grid. An obvious
variable to include in a future study would be the inclusion of diﬀerent tow
durations. In comparison with the other trawl experiments in Table 5.1, the
tow duration of 180 minutes is 50-300% longer than previously done studies.
And since this has been shown to have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the discard mor-

decreasing the tow duration could be a good place to start.

Estimation of discard mortality of under-sized European plaice
(Pleuronectes platessa) from small-scaled Nephrops (Nephrops
norvegicus) trawl ﬁshery in Skagerrak
Marc Eskelund
National Institute of Aquatic Resources, Technical University of Denmark (DTU Aqua)
January 24, 2016
Abstract
The Nephrops (Nephrops norvegicus) ﬁshery is a high valued ﬁshery with a substantial amount of
by-catch that is subsequently discarded. Among the regularly caught species during Nephrops trawling
is the European plaice (Pleuronectes platessa).
Large individuals have commercial value, but the
individuals below the minimum landing size are discarded. The objective of this study was to estimate
the short-term mortality of the discarded undersized plaice. Furthermore, to evaluate if reﬂex action
response and injuries could be used as a predictor for mortality.
Plaice were tagged and followed
individually to assess the reﬂex behavior and injuries while in the ﬁeld. The experiment was carried
out on a small otter-trawler targeting Nephrops in Skagerrak.
The results showed that plaice had an average short-term mortality of of 85%, ranging from 61-
100%, after 240 hours of monitoring. The experiment indicated that environmental factors inﬂuenced
the survival, with the largest mortality occurring when the trawl was contaminated by excess mud and
large pieces of peat. The mortality prediction analysis showed that injuries was the primary driver on
the mortality in this experiment. A pilot study on avian predation indicated that a large part of the
discarded plaice were taken by seagulls.

Several studies have examined the discard mor-

The

et al., 2013], and 31-52% [Depestele et al., 2014],
with tow durations varying between 60-120 min-
utes and observation periods of 3-5 days.
Fewer
studies have examined the otter-trawls with van
Beek et al. [1990] and Millner et al. [1993] showing
a short-term discard mortality of plaice of 46-100%

==PAGE:49==
and 7-37%, respectively, with tow duration between

typically are otter-trawls, none of the cited studies
have examined the discard mortality of plaice when
Nephrops are the target species.
The objective of this study is therefore to exam-
ine the short-term mortality of the plaice caught
in a small scale Nephrops trawl ﬁshery. The plaice
selected in the study were mainly individuals below
the minimum landing size (MLS) of 270 mm. This
was based on the assumption that plaice above the
MLS are landed and sold, since plaice is a species
with high marked demands. Included in the short-
term mortality study will be a quantitative vitality

4
Discussion
4.1
Method
The vessel used in the experiment is small in com-
parison to the majority of the Danish Nephrops
ﬁshing ﬂeet, which consists of >12 m twin-trawlers.
The choice of experimental vessel was based on the
assumption that a higher discard survival could be
found in a small scaled ﬁshery, which could allow
for better possibilities to test methods and predic-
tors with a less intrusive ﬁshery.
In the pursuit of a random length distribution
the method was to select the ﬁrst observable in-
dividuals, however the result shows a length bias,
e.g. larger individuals in the experiment than in
the subsample of the catch. A reason for this could
be that visual selection favors larger individuals as
they stand out on a crowded sorting table. Addi-
tionally, there was the possibility that larger indi-
viduals were more active and attracted attention,
although this theory rely on an assumption that
the RAMP-score is positively correlated with size,
which ﬁnds no support in the results (Spearman’s
test non-signiﬁcant). Another possibility is due to
the workstation was to the left of the sorting ta-
ble, leading to the majority of individuals being
selected from that side.
If, for some reason, the
conveyor belt favored a certain size of ﬁsh to go
towards the left side of the sorting table, selecting
most individuals on the side would result in a size
bias, which, in any case, is hard to test retrospec-
tively. However, although the results indicated that
a slight diﬀerence in size distribution, the averages
are close and the diﬀerences most likely negligible.
The comparisons of the 4 control tows showed
no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the method and
untreated controls. This indicates that the holding
conditions and the method, although potentially in-
trusive with skin piecing tags, was not the primary
source of mortality. The diﬀerence in temperature
from the seabed and holding tanks was signiﬁcantly
diﬀerent, but within the tolerance level of the plaice
and should not aﬀect its general condition [Clarke
and Johnston, 1999]. However, several survivors in
the post-experiment examinations showed signs of
irritation and beginning inﬂammation around the
tag position, which signals a possible maximum du-
ration of this method of tagging.
8
==PAGE:56==
4.2
Short-term mortality
In Wassenberg and Hill [1993] it is suggested that
the majority of mortality occurs within 3 days. The
mortality of this experiment is indeed largest in the
ﬁrst 3-4 days, but the mortality curve does not ﬂat-
ten until the 5th or 6th day.
Additionally, it is
worth noting that a single individual from the ex-
periment died outside the observation period of 10
days, an individual with a low impairment score
and no visible tag inﬂammation. After 10 days it is
diﬃcult to identify the mortality cause as it could
be both lagged mortality from the ﬁshery and/or
captivity induced, although a fasting plaice in the
holding conditions should not die of starvation (es-
timated from Fonds et al. [1992]).
A horizontal
asymptote of mortality is reached around 7-8 days,
which seems a ﬁtting duration in consideration of
the potential tag implications.
The overall short-term discard mortality of the
experiment was 85%, ranging between 61-100%
for individual tows. This is within previously ex-
amined discard mortality proportions with demer-
sal towed gear [van Beek et al., 1990, Kaiser and
Spencer, 1995, Revill et al., 2013, Depestele et al.,
2014], although the majority of studies are done on
substantially shorter tow durations of maximum 2
hours. As the tow duration is thought to have an
impact on the discard mortality [Broadhurst et al.,
2006], the minimum 50% increase in towing time
compared to previously studies does not show a
substantial increase in mortality.
However, this
could also be due to a less intrusive handling of
the catch in a small scale ﬁshery with hauls of only
200-300 kg.
A single study done by Millner et al. [1993] shows
a very low discard mortality of only 7-37% during
very similar conditions to this study. However upon
a closer examination some crucial diﬀerences sur-
faces as the depth of the trawling is very shallow
of only 5-20 meters, the tow duration is only 120
minutes and the experiment was done on fewer indi-
viduals. The most signiﬁcant diﬀerence though, is
that the mortality curves do not seem to be ﬂatten-
ing by the end of the observation period, indicat-
ing that the short-term mortality in the experiment
could be substantially lagged and an extension of
the observation period could potentially result in
increased mortality.
In Figure 2:B only two tows were considered
clean and the remaining had either excessive mud,
peat or both. The individual tows are possible to
identify in Figure 2:A as the two clean tows are the
experimental tows with lowest mortality, and the
tows with a combination of mud and peat reach
100% mortality half-way through the observation
duration. The peat caught in the trawls were of
substantial size, some pieces even requiring the hy-
draulic winch to dispose of as they were too heavy
to lift out of the pound.
The origin of the peat
can be traced back more than 8000 years to the
Littorina transgression, a pronounced event in the
creation of the Baltic Sea [Personal correspondence
with Ass. Proﬀ. Michael Houmark-Nielsen of Nat-
ural History Museum of Denmark].
The diﬀer-
ence between the clean and dirty halls is signiﬁ-
cant, which most likely can be contributed to dirty
hauls often contained several lumps of peat, each
up to 50-70 kg, that damaged the catch in the co-
dend during the tow. This was evident by a large
amount of abrasion and bleeding injuries during the
tows with peat.
Similarly, in the case of excess
amounts of mud into the codend, the potential for
a hypoxic environment increases drastically, and al-
though plaice has a high tolerance of hypoxia [Stef-
fensen et al., 1981], the combination of stressors
appears to be high, leading to possible cumulative
lethal eﬀects [Davis, 2002, Broadhurst et al., 2006].
4.3
Deck time
Being out of the water appears to have a potential
impact on the plaice mortality, conﬁrmed by several
studies [van Beek et al., 1990, Ross and Hokenson,
1997, Humborstad et al., 2009, Benoˆıt et al., 2013].
This study shows similar results with a negative
correlation between time on deck and the number
of days to die and a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in time on
deck for survivors and dead individuals. However,
it is worth noting the large variance in deck time
for both dead and surviving individuals.
Where
some survivors was on deck for almost 50 minutes,
other individuals died despite only being on deck
for 4 minutes. This indicates that although deck
time has an inﬂuence on the discard mortality, it
appears to no be the primary explanatory factor in
this experiment.
The nature of a wet pound is intended to function
as a water tank to increase the quality of the catch.
To imitate the condition of vessels without a wet
9
==PAGE:57==
pound, the pound was used without water during
the ﬁrst haul, which then greatly reduced the eﬃ-
ciency of the conveyor belt. By prioritizing the con-
veyor belt to function, the pound contained water
in the remaining hauls. With water in the pound,
the environmental condition was quite diﬀerent to
air exposure, as potentially hundreds of kilos of ﬁsh
and invertebrates shared a few hundred liters of sur-
face temperature water (ca. 17-18 `C, CTD data)
with possible rapidly declining oxygen levels. Fur-
thermore, since the area trawled was mud-bottom
the hauls often contained mud, which could be an-
other factor potentially reducing the oxygen con-
ditions in the pound. Although ﬂatﬁsh are known
for superior resilience to low oxygen and air ex-
posure in comparison with roundﬁsh [Broadhurst
et al., 2006], and plaice in particular have possi-
ble cutaneous O2 uptake [Steﬀensen et al., 1981],
the potentially hypoxic environment of the pound
could have an eﬀect on the survival [Davis, 2002].
This appeared evident in the study by the diﬀer-
ence in deck time between surviving and dead in-
dividuals. The O2 saturation of the pound was not
measured, however, the signiﬁcant negative corre-
lation between time in the pound, e.g. deck time,
and the duration of short-term survival suggests an
eﬀect in line with previous studies.
4.4
Mortality prediction with vital-
ity impairment
Previous studies have shown reﬂex impairment as
a tool to predict stress and even delayed mortal-
ity in diﬀerent species of ﬁsh [Davis, 2007, 2010,
Humborstad et al., 2009, 2016]. In this experiment
the RAMP test was not suﬃcient to illustrate any
meaningful relationship with mortality. A few in-
dividuals with a RAMP score of 0 survived, while
many with a score of 3 died. This could be due
to the general high mortality, resulting in multiple
deaths despite individuals showing low impairment
immediately after being caught (Figure 3:A). More-
over, a low number of tests were selected due to lo-
gistic limitations on board the vessel. This lead to
the necessity of including the injuries to a vitality
impairment score (equation 3), similar to Davis and
Ottmar [2006] and as described in the ICES discard
mortality report [ICES, 2014a]. The illustration in
Figure 3 show how an initial increase in vitality
impairment is without high associated mortality,
indicating a sub-lethal state of the ﬁsh, where po-
tentially further injuries and stress could lead to
vulnerability to delayed mortality. At higher levels
of vitality impairment, often as a result of several
injuries, the mortality is high.
The necessity to include injuries illustrates that
reﬂex impairment was not a strong indicator of de-
layed mortality and that instead the injuries could
be the dominating mortality eﬀect in this study.
The uncoupling of reﬂex impairments from the de-
layed mortality leaves the injuries as the best in-
dicator, which could be useful as mortality predic-
tor since injury assessment can be done in the ﬁeld
without accessories.
Consequently, using vitality
impairment as a mortality predictor for plaice could
be plausible, as the results show a high mortality af-
ter medium accumulated impairment, e.g. several
recognizable injuries. It is, however, not without
challenges to assess injuries on plaice as its natu-
ral camouﬂage can show resemblance to abrasions
while some internal injuries might not be visible
immediately.
4.5
Avian predation
The main reason for the birds to avoid the discards
was due to inﬁghting with other birds or because
the discard submerged suﬃciently fast and deep for
the birds not to notice. Additionally, towards the
end of the experiment the birds showed an onset of
lacking appetite.
Although the experiment was a small pilot study,
two critical factors in available light and speed of
the vessel were apparent. The experiment was car-
ried out in the morning with full daylight, but much
Nephrops ﬁshery is done at night. With less light
the depth of which the ﬁsh has to reach to become
unnoticeable would decrease. The speed of the ves-
sel could inﬂuence the bird predation rate, since
speed is related to the distance between the ship
and discarded ﬁsh. High vessel speed requires the
birds to be more selective as they will be far be-
hind the vessel if they decide to pursue a ﬁsh. The
vessel was cruising at a slow speed while doing the
experiment, allowing many birds to be present. A
vessel cruising in high speed during the night could
potentially have a lot of discards escaping the birds.
The preliminary experiment showed that the ma-
jority of plaice below MLS was eaten or taken by
the seagulls. Another study estimated that ca. 60%
10
==PAGE:58==
of the discarded material [Catchpole et al., 2006]
was taken by seabirds, which suggest a potential
inﬂuence on the bird population with the landing
obligation.
4.6
Landing obligation
By the end of the study, the guidelines for the land-
ing obligation in the North Sea, including Skager-
rak, was oﬃcially announced. The current imple-
mentation allows discarding of plaice in otter-trawl
ﬁsheries with a mesh size <100 mm. A possibil-
ity in the legislation for exception to the landing
obligation called for “species for which scientiﬁc
evidence demonstrates high survival rates” (CFP,
article 15.2b). This study shows, in line with sev-
eral others, that the discard mortality of plaice in
demersal trawling is not 100%, but in fact varies
signiﬁcantly depending on both operational and en-
vironmental factors, i.e. tow duration and seabed
conditions. Furthermore, a simple calculation on
the economical consequences, not included in this
article (see STECF [2013] for similar analysis), re-
veals that even a low discard mortality can have
potential economical beneﬁts. In addition, any sur-
viving plaice discards will only have a positive eﬀect
on the population if the alternative is to be landed.
