Abstract
This thesis investigates the contribution of airborne nonlinearities to harmonic distortion
in a high¬≠frequency loudspeaker. While electromechanical sources of distortion in loud¬≠
speakers are well understood, the role of nonlinear acoustic propagation in air remains
less clearly quantified. A series of experiments, including amplitude¬≠dependent measure¬≠
ments, vacuum chamber tests at varying ambient pressures, and laser Doppler vibrome¬≠
try, were conducted to isolate and characterize potential airborne effects.
To further separate the contributions of different mechanisms, finite element simulations
were employed using measured diaphragm velocity as a prescribed boundary condition.
A nonlinear acoustic simulation of far¬≠field pressure showed strong agreement with mea¬≠
sured second harmonic distortion above 3 kHz, while a linear model did not. This suggests
that airborne nonlinearities, though secondary at low frequencies, become significant at
high frequencies. The findings offer valuable insight into the complex interplay between
electromechanical behavior and acoustic propagation in tweeters, with implications for
transducer design and high¬≠fidelity audio applications.

1.2
Linear vs. nonlinear systems
Having explained the linear acoustic wave equation, it is also worthwhile to explain why a
linear description of an acoustic system is desirable, despite its reliance on assumptions.
Compared to nonlinear acoustic systems, systems described by the linear acoustic wave
equation have advantageous mathematical properties that greatly simplify analysis and
simulation. These properties are:
‚ÄĘ Additivity: In linear systems, the response to a sum of inputs is equivalent to the
sum of the individual responses to each input:
f(x1 + x2) = f(x1) + f(x2)
‚ÄĘ Homogeneity: In linear systems, if an input is scaled by some factor, then the
response to this input will also be scaled by the same factor:
f(őĪx) = őĪf(x)
These two properties are often referred together as the superposition principle.
‚ÄĘ Time¬≠invariance: The linear acoustic wave equation has no explicit dependence
on time‚ÄĒonly on derivatives of time. Therefore, the relationship between input and
output is constant with respect to time:
If y(t) = f (x(t)) , then y(t + t0) = f (x(t + t0))
‚ÄĘ For linear time¬≠invariant (LTI) systems, complex exponentials of the form ejŌČt serve

same frequency sinusoid scaled in amplitude and shifted in phase:

Although analytically powerful, the linear acoustic wave equation is fundamentally lim¬≠

interaction between frequency components, nor can it describe waveform changes that
arise due to amplitude¬≠dependent effects. There exist several important phenomena in
acoustics that are direct results of these effects, and accurately modeling these phenom¬≠

work is that of distortion.
In the context of signal processing, distortion refers to an alteration in the shape of a
signal as it passes through a system. Viewing this definition through the lens of Fourier
analysis‚ÄĒwhere any periodic waveform can be represented as a sum of sine waves with
specific frequencies, amplitudes, and phases‚ÄĒan alteration in the shape of a signal im¬≠
plies that the frequency content of the signal has been modified. In other words, the output
signal contains frequency components that were not present in the input, or the relative
amplitudes and phases of the original components have been altered in a way that can¬≠
not be explained by independent, linear scaling and shifting of the original components.
Neither of these effects can exist in a system that obeys the superposition principle.

1.5
Loudspeakers
Loudspeakers are transducers that convert electrical energy into acoustic energy by me¬≠
chanically exciting a diaphragm, which then radiates sound into the surrounding medium.
The most common type is the electrodynamic loudspeaker, which operates on the Lorentz
force principle: a current¬≠carrying voice coil placed in a static magnetic field experiences
a force, driving the diaphragm forward and backward in accordance with the input signal.
[4] The loudspeaker used in this study is of this type.
The human ear is capable of perceiving a remarkably wide range of frequencies. This
creates a unique challenge in the design of loudspeaker transducers. A transducer ca¬≠
pable of emitting low frequencies requires physical properties that are almost opposite to
those of a transducer capable of emitting high frequencies. As such, it is nearly impossi¬≠
ble to create a single transducer that covers the entire range of human hearing. Instead,
a loudspeaker system will almost always consist of multiple transducers, each designed
for different passbands.
A transducer that is designed for low frequencies is generally referred to as a woofer and
is characterized by a large diaphragm and a compliant suspension, allowing for the large
air displacement necessary to emit low frequencies. A transducer that is designed for
high frequencies is generally referred to as a tweeter and is characterized by a small, stiff
diaphragm and a stiff suspension, which ensures that the diaphragm moves uniformly (pis¬≠
tonically) with the voice coil even at very high frequencies and maximizes the frequency
range in which the radiation pattern is mostly omni¬≠directional.

not drawn to scale.
1.6
Loudspeaker nonlinearities
The reason that this study analyzes air nonlinearity in tweeters specifically is due to the
fact that their construction and operating characteristics make them most susceptible to
this nonlinearity relative to other loudspeakers. A small diaphragm means that the lo¬≠
cal pressure is highly concentrated, and this is often exacerbated by the fact that many
tweeters employ a waveguide, which further increases compression. Additionally, the
high frequencies (i.e. short wavelengths) result in much more aggressive wave steep¬≠

13
==PAGE:22==
ening. Recall from section 1.3 that wave steepening compounds for each successive
wavefront.
While this study focuses on nonlinearities arising from acoustic propagation in air, it is
important to acknowledge that most harmonic distortion in loudspeakers originates from
electromechanical sources. These include, but are not limited to, nonlinear behavior in
the suspension system (spider and surround), which exhibits asymmetric stiffness K(x)
with displacement, and in the motor structure, where the force factor Bl(x) and inductance
L(i) vary with coil position and current, respectively.
These nonlinearities are typically most prominent at low frequencies and large excursions,
and they give rise to both even¬≠ and odd¬≠order harmonic distortion depending on the
symmetry of the mechanism. In contrast to air nonlinearity, which scales primarily with
pressure amplitude, frequency, and distance, electromechanical nonlinearities dominate
in the displacement and current domains. A key aim of this study is to distinguish these
sources of distortion inherent within the transducer from those introduced by the medium
of sound propagation itself. [4]

The primary objectives of this study are twofold: (1) to determine whether airborne non¬≠

electromechanical nonlinearities. These objectives are addressed through a series of in¬≠

to the complexity of isolating specific nonlinear mechanisms, not all investigations yielded
conclusive results. Nevertheless, each step provides incremental insight into the interplay
between acoustic and electromechanical sources of nonlinearity in the system.

This investigation aims to isolate the contribution of air nonlinearity from other distortion
mechanisms in the tweeter. Two complementary finite element simulations are performed.
In the first, the measured diaphragm velocity response of the fundamental, as well as the
harmonics, is used as a prescribed boundary condition in a linear acoustic FEM model.
This isolates distortion arising purely from the transducer‚Äôs internal nonlinearities.
In the second simulation, the opposite is done: the measured diaphragm velocity re¬≠
sponse of only the fundamental is used as a prescribed boundary condition in an non¬≠
linear acoustic FEM model. This approach removes the electromechanical nonlinearities
from the simulation, isolating the effects of nonlinear sound propagation in air. Together,
these simulations allow a direct comparison of the contributions of the air nonlinearities
and electromechanical nonlinearities to harmonic distortion.

3
Conclusion
This study investigated the contribution of airborne nonlinearities to harmonic distortion
in a high frequency loudspeaker, combining experimental measurements with numerical
modeling. A series of controlled tests, including amplitude sweeps, pressure¬≠dependent
measurements in a vacuum chamber, and laser Doppler vibrometry, were used to identify
the origins of second and third harmonic distortion. These experiments were supported by
finite element simulations designed to isolate the effects of nonlinear acoustic propagation
from electromechanical sources.
Results from the amplitude sweep experiment showed clear quadratic scaling of the sec¬≠
ond harmonic with input level, consistent with theoretical predictions from the Westervelt
equation and suggesting that nonlinear acoustic propagation may contribute to distortion
under certain conditions.
The measurements made in reduced ambient pressures suffered from limitations in mea¬≠
surement precision making definitive conclusions difficult. However, they provided some
notable insights regarding the effect of asymmetric loading of the diaphragm on the re¬≠
sponse of the loudspeaker.
The most direct evidence of airborne nonlinearity and its contribution to harmonic distor¬≠
tion came from finite element simulations: when the measured diaphragm velocity was
applied as a boundary condition in an FEM model, the simulated far¬≠field pressure from
the nonlinear acoustic model exhibited a strong correlation with the measured second
harmonic response above 3 kHz.
Overall, the results demonstrate that while electromechanical nonlinearities dominate at
lower frequencies and higher¬≠order harmonics, airborne nonlinear propagation plays a
non¬≠negligible role in high¬≠frequency harmonic distortion. This insight has implications
for both transducer design and nonlinear acoustic modeling, particularly in applications
requiring ultra¬≠low distortion or high output levels.
