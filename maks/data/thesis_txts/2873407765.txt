Abstract
In this thesis, two complementary strategies for advancing high-band-gap selenium (Se) photovoltaics
and their integration into tandem architectures with silicon (Si) are explored. The ﬁrst series of exper-
iments reproduces and extends inverted Se solar cells incorporating PEDOT:PSS and PCBM as hole
and electron transport layers. Through systematic optimization of annealing parameters including
temperature, annealing time, annealing method, and oxygen pre-treatment, the quality of the thin Se
ﬁlms deposited on ITO and ITO/PEDOT:PSS substrates was optimized. The optimal conditions (O2
pre-treatment followed by annealing in air at 190°C for 4 minutes on the pizza oven) for enhanced crys-
tallographic orientation and larger grain size were conﬁrmed by SEM, AFM, and XRD measurements.
Although the obtained photovoltage and currents are still low when compared to devices reported in
the literature, the crystallographic and morphological improvements on device prototypes with the or-
ganic layers were clear. Thus, this work can provide crucial insides on the rational design of Se/organic
solar cell.
The second approach investigates a novel gluing method using hot-press bonding of thin amorphous Se
ﬁlms under 1200 kPa at various temperatures. Selenium itself serves as the adhesive between di↵erent
substrate types. By systematically varying the pressing temperature, the crystallinity and morphology
of Se ﬁlms hot-pressed between two ITO substrates and between PEDOT:PSS and PCBM layers was
optimized. Under optimal conditions, the ﬁlms are relatively ﬂat, exhibit strong preferential grain
orientation, and feature grains nearly ten times larger than those formed at atmospheric pressure
(approximately 2–4 µm in size) for the samples on ITO. Concerning the hot pressed samples on
ITO/PEDOT:PSS and ITO/PCBM, the uniformity of the ﬁlms can still be improved. This method
also demonstrated that Si wafers can be hot-pressed against amorphous Selenium (Se) to produce
strongly bonded samples without fracturing the brittle silicon. XRD analysis of the Se/Si interfaces
shows a more random grain orientation (R ⇡0.72, this ratio being the one of the (101) peak over the
(100) peak of the XRD di↵raction pattern of Se) but a very homogeneous Se ﬁlm without an excessive
amount of pin holes.
Key words : Selenium, invert cell structure, tandem solar cells, organic layers, gluing

1
Introduction
This section aims to explain why solar panels represent a solution to the economic challenges posed by
new energy demand, and to introduce the major developments in the solar cell literature, speciﬁcally
selenium solar cells, organic solar cells, and tandem solar cells.
1.1
The Energy Economy
During the past decade, the global increase in energy demand, driven largely by industrial growth,
urbanization, and rising standards of living, has continued to be met predominantly through the
combustion of fossil fuels. This dependency has contributed to a sustained increase in carbon dioxide
(CO2) emissions, with global energy-related emissions reaching a historic peak of 37.8 gigatonnes
in 2024, marking the fourth consecutive annual increase [14]. Although fossil fuels have historically
supported economic development and technological progress, their environmental cost has become
increasingly unsustainable. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has established
with high conﬁdence that human activities, particularly fossil fuel combustion, are the principal drivers
of observed global warming since the mid-20th century.
Furthermore, the current fossil fuel infrastructure alone is projected to emit enough CO2 to exceed
the 1.5°C global warming threshold (before 2050), even in the absence of new investments [15]. The

1.2
The Development of Photovoltaics
As well as being produced more intensively and more cheaply, photovoltaic solar panels are also
increasingly eﬃcient.
The development of solar photovoltaics began in 1873, when Willoughby Smith discovered the photo-
conductivity of selenium, observing a drop in electrical resistance when exposed to light [16]. Building
on this, Adams and Day demonstrated in 1876 that light could generate an electric current in sele-
nium, establishing the ﬁrst photovoltaic e↵ect in a solid-state material [17]. In 1883, Charles Fritts
constructed the ﬁrst photovoltaic cell by coating a selenium semiconductor with a thin gold layer,
achieving an eﬃciency of about 1% [18]. Theoretically, a major advance came with Einstein’s 1905
paper explaining the photoelectric e↵ect, which laid the quantum foundations for photovoltaic oper-
ation [19]. In 1941, Russell Ohl invented the p–n junction silicon cell, demonstrating that a built-in
electric ﬁeld could separate photogenerated carriers [20]. This principle enabled the creation of the
ﬁrst practical silicon solar cell by Chapin, Fuller, and Pearson at Bell Labs in 1954, with an eﬃciency
of around 6% [21]. Technological progress continued with the development of thin-ﬁlm materials like
cadmium telluride and amorphous silicon, as well as improvements in crystalline silicon fabrication.
Figure 2: Power conversion eﬃciencies of multiple solar cell architectures [3]
Today, commercial photovoltaic cells, based primarily on monocrystalline and polycrystalline silicon,
operate at eﬃciencies exceeding 20%, marking the culmination of more than a century of advances in
semiconductor physics and materials engineering 2. Some solar cells now even exceed 40%, but most
of them rely on solar concentrators or on technologies that have not yet been deployed at large scale.
As the Figure 2 above shows, the Power Conversion Eﬃciency (PCE) has gradually improved over
the past years, using new technologies or reﬁning existing ones. The new type of technologies such as
tandem solar cells or multijunction solar cells even breached the Shockley-Queisser (SQ) limit that is

1.3
Tandem solar cells
The purpose of this section is to show the current state of the art of tandem solar cells, starting with
the ﬁrst existing one and following the major steps over the last years to arrive at the best technology of
tandem solar cells. This section will focus only on the tandem solar cells with two (or sometimes three)
absorbers in sequence. However, some can use more absorbers to try to achieve better eﬃciencies.
1.3.1
The tandem solar cell technology
The main advantage of a tandem solar cell over a single-absorber solar cell lies in its ability to capture
a broader spectrum of sunlight, thereby achieving a higher power conversion eﬃciency (PCE). As
illustrated in the ﬁgure 3, a single-absorber solar cell can only utilize a portion of the incident light,
limited by the intrinsic properties of the absorber material. In contrast, a tandem architecture employs
a high band gap absorber in the front, allowing lower-energy photons to pass through and be absorbed
by a second lower band gap material positioned behind.
This sequential absorption enables more
eﬃcient utilization of the solar spectrum compared to single-junction designs.
Figure 3: Scheme of a tandem solar cell with two photo-absorbers
In Section 2.2.3, it will be demonstrated that the theoretical eﬃciency of a tandem solar cell can exceed
the one of a single absorber device by up to 35%.
There is three main tandem-junction architectures that di↵er in how subcells are interconnected and
illuminated. In a two-terminal monolithic tandem, the wide and narrow bandgap layers are deposited
directly on top of one another and share a single electrical circuit, which simpliﬁes module integration
but requires current matching between the two cells. In a four-terminal mechanically stacked tandem,
two fully independent cells are fabricated separately and then stacked so that each subcell can operate
at its own optimum current and voltage. This maximizes eﬃciency but adds complexity and cost.
Finally, in a four terminal spectrally split design, an optical element (a prism for example) divides the
incoming sunlight by wavelength and directs each spectral band to its ideal single junction cell, allowing
completely independent optimization of each absorber without electrical interconnection constraints.

1.3.2
Major steps in the literature
The ﬁrst tandem solar cell recorded was the one made by Woodal and Hovel in 1972 [23].
They

with a tunnel junction to maintain electrical continuity and avoid a voltage drop at the p-n junction

After these ﬁrst demonstrations in the early 1970’s in the 1980’s and 1990s’, the monolithic cells
became more common and some groups tried to optimize the band gap matching. That is the case
of Lamorte et al. in 1980 who reported the feasability of a theoretical 29% AlGaAs-GaAs cascade
solar cell under AM0 [25]. A bit after, in 1994, with the optimization of window layers, anti-reﬂection
coatings, and graded band gap front emitters, King et al. [26] produced GaInP/GaAs/Ge triple-
junction devices, culminating in a 27.3% eﬃciency under AM0 illumination.
These achievements
underscored the importance of precise band gap engineering (Eg1⇡1.9 eV, Eg2⇡1.4 eV, Eg3⇡0.67 eV)
for close to ideal current matching under AM1.5 conditions.
The next step in the development of tandem solar cells is the use of monolithic structures and con-
centrators to gain eﬃciency. Indeed, in the 2000’s and more precisely in 2007, King et al. reported
a GaInP/GaAs/InGaAs triple-junction with a 1.0 eV bottom cell grown on a relaxed InGaAs bu↵er,
achieving 40.7 percent eﬃciency under 240 times the normal sun concentration [27].
During the 2010’s and 2020’s, silicon maintained its dominance in the photovoltaic industry, while the
emergence of perovskite materials led to a growing number of perovskite/silicon tandem solar cells.
In 2016, Albrecht et al. measured an eﬃcient monolithic tandem cell 18% formed by a bottom silicon
heterojunction and a top perovskite cell allowing for a very high open circuit voltage of 1.78 V [28].
In 2020, Xiao et al. reported a certiﬁed 24.2% eﬃciency perovskite tandem solar cells under one sun
illumination and (≥1cm2), enabling scalable tandem integration [29].
Today, the absolute record for a two terminals cell without concentrator is detained by Longi with a
monolithic perovskite-silicon tandem with a 34.6%. This is not yet published but this value has been
certiﬁed by the European Solar Test Installation (ESTI) [30].
1.4
Selenium Thin ﬁlm Solar Cells - State of the art
Selenium (Se) has long been recognized in the ﬁeld of photovoltaics, primarily due to its historical
signiﬁcance as the active material in the ﬁrst solar cell, developed by Charles Fritts in 1883 [18].
Despite this early prominence, selenium has received considerably less attention in recent decades,
particularly with the rise of high-eﬃciency silicon and perovskite-based technologies. This is largely
due to the relatively high band gap of selenium (⇡1.95 eV), which limits its ability to absorb a broad
portion of the solar spectrum, thereby constraining the maximum theoretical eﬃciency achievable.
In addition to its performance limitations, selenium is also considered a relatively rare element, with
global extractable reserves estimated at approximately 95,000 tonnes [31]. Although selenium occurs
naturally and does not require synthetic production, it is rarely mined directly from the earth. Instead,
it is typically obtained as a byproduct of copper reﬁning. Furthermore, selenium is known to be toxic
when accumulated in excessive amounts in biological systems [32], which poses environmental and
health concerns but its chemical stability remains an asset, supporting its continued consideration as
a potential material for solar cells that are therefore not toxic.

1.5
Polymers integration in Solar Cells
In recent years, several research groups have attempted to integrate organic compounds (mainly poly-
mers) into their solar cells, most often as electron transport layers (ETLs) for n-type polymers and
hole transport layers (HTLs) for p-type polymers.
A polymer is a large molecule composed of repeating structural units, connected by covalent bonds
to form long molecular chains. In the ﬁeld of photovoltaics, certain polymers known as conjugated
polymers exhibit semiconducting properties due to the presence of alternating single and double bonds
along their backbone. This conjugated structure allows ⇡-electrons to become delocalized across the
chain, facilitating charge transport. Although the intrinsic charge mobility of polymers is generally
lower than the one of inorganic semiconductors, they o↵er several advantages such as solution pro-
cessability, mechanical ﬂexibility, and tunable electronic properties through molecular design. These
features make polymers (and most of the organic layers such as PCBM) well suited for use in solar
cells, particularly as active layers or as interfacial materials for charge extraction and transport.
Moreover, organic layers often exhibit advantageous mechanical properties, such as ﬂexibility and op-
tical transparency.
These characteristics enable the fabrication of lightweight, bendable, and even
semi-transparent solar cells, thereby expanding the range of potential applications. Flexible organic
photovoltaics can be integrated into wearable electronics, rollable panels, building-integrated photo-
voltaics (BIPV), and other unconventional surfaces where rigid, traditional silicon-based solar cells
would be impractical. This versatility highlights one of the key beneﬁts of using organic materials in
next-generation solar technologies. Optical transparency is also a feature that serve the creation of
invert solar cells. That is why in 2020 W. Liu et al. introduced PCBM as an Electron transport layer
and PEDOT:PSS as a Hole transport layer in an Se solar cell with invert structure [38]. This device
reached a power eﬃciency of 3.9%, demonstrating that the introduction of organic layers into a Se
solar cell was feasible.

2.1
From photon energy to electrical current
To understand how solar cells operate, it is ﬁrst essential to have a solid understanding of semiconductor
physics, as these physical principles form the foundation of solar cell functionality.
2.1.1
Photon energy to chemical potential
When considering an isolated atom, solving the Schr¨odinger equation provides the discrete energy
levels occupied by its electrons. However, in a system composed of a large number of atoms, these
energy levels broaden and form continuous energy bands, with some energy ranges being allowed and
others forbidden, leading to the formation of energy gaps. The energy bands of primary interest are
the valence band and the higher energy conduction band. If these bands are very close or overlap,
the material behaves as a conductor. If they are far apart, the material is classiﬁed as an insulator.
However, there exists a category of materials in which the energy gap is too large to be fully bridged
at T =0K, yet too small for the material to behave as a true insulator at room temperature. In this
case, the material can be identiﬁed as a semiconductor.
The case of semiconductors is quite similar to that of insulators. At T = 0 K, the electrons will also
occupy all states of an allowed band (valence band). In this case, electrical conduction is impossible,
as in the case of an insulator. A semiconductor is therefore insulating at zero temperature. However,
at ﬁnite temperature, electrons will acquire the necessary energy to occupy states in the upper allowed
band (conduction band), according to the Fermi-Dirac statistics. This is only possible if the gap energy
is suﬃciently small.
When a photon with energy greater than or equal to the band gap strikes the material, it can excite
an electron from the valence band to the conduction band.
This band gap, typically ranging between 0.5 and 3 eV, is suﬃciently small to allow optical excitation
of an electron from the valence band to the conduction band. This process is illustrated in the ﬁgure
4 below. When such an excitation occurs, an electron is promoted to the conduction band, leaving
behind a hole in the valence band.
These electron-hole pairs are the fundamental charge carriers
responsible for photovoltaic processes.

In semiconductors, the Fermi energy (EF ) is also an important concept to describe the probability of
an energy level being occupied by an electron at thermal equilibrium. It corresponds to the energy
level for which the probability of an electron being present is exactly 50%. In a semiconductor (and
that is what deﬁne it), the Fermi energy is approximately halfway between the valence band and the
conduction band, as we can see in the Figure 4.
However, when the semiconductor is doped, the position of the Fermi energy shifts. In an n-type
semiconductor (negatively doped), the Fermi level moves closer to the conduction band, reﬂecting an
increased probability of free electrons. In contrast, in a p-type semiconductor (positively doped), the
Fermi energy level approaches the valence band, indicating a higher concentration of holes.
Figure 5: Excitation of an electron by a photon, transitioning from the valence band to the conduction
band, then creating a chemical potential for the electron/hole pair
Thus, when electrons jump in the conduction band, excited by photons, they can sometimes go to
energy states higher than the lowest energy level in the conduction band. When this happens, they
fall back to the lowest energy level in the conduction band, a phenomenon known as thermalization.
The same phenomenon occurs with holes, which return to the highest energy state in the valence
band. Hence, after thermalization, when electrons are present in the conduction band (and holes in
the valence band), the Fermi energy splits into two parts, and the di↵erence between these two parts is

energy of the valence band and will be called ✏F V . This is how a semiconductor converts photon
energy into chemical energy.
2.1.2
From chemical potential to current ﬂow
However, these charge carriers are not permanent. Recombination mechanisms play a central role
in device performance. Recombination can be radiative, when an electron falls back into the valence
band and emits a photon; Auger, when the recombination energy is transferred to a third carrier (often
another electron); or Shockley-Read-Hall (SRH), when it occurs via electronic states added into the
band gap. These electronic states are caused by impurities or defects in the crystal. These mechanisms
compete with charge collection at the electrodes, so minimizing them is crucial to improving device
performance.
Once generated, the carriers must be transported to the electrodes to be collected in the form of an
electric current. The transport of charge carriers in a semiconductor depends on their mobility and
lifetime. Transport can take place by di↵usion, under the e↵ect of a concentration gradient, or by
drift, under the inﬂuence of an electric ﬁeld. It is at this moment that the energy is transformed from
chemical to electrical.

2.2
Photovoltaic devices
Now that it is easier to understand how a semiconductor captures the energy of a photon, transforms
it into an electrochemical potential, and then how it can turn it into an electric current, it is time to
show how a solar cell uses these physical phenomena and how they can be optimized to make a good
solar cell.
2.2.1
The heterostructure and Invert cell structure
The main principle is to separate electron/hole pairs and transport them in di↵erent directions. It is
therefore essential to have a layer with high conductivity for electrons and very low conductivity for
holes (n-type semiconductors), and the same for holes (p-type semiconductors). However, to create
these semiconductors, a crystalline structure is doped with electron donors or acceptors. But as it
has been shown earlier, when imperfections are present, they induce recombinations. To avoid this,
a photoabsorber (Se for example) is inserted between these positively and negatively doped layers,
and these n-type and p-type semiconductors are used to transfer charges (electrons or holes) to the
contacts. The n-type semeconductor that transport electrons is in this case called an ETL that stand
for ”Electron Transport Layer”. Same for the p-type semiconductor that is called a HTL that stand
for ”Hole Transport Layer”. The ﬁgure 6 shows this structure. Because this structure contains several
semiconductors, it is called an heterostructure. The invert cell structure showed below is consequently
also a type of heterostructure.

being below 8%). However, due to its relatively high band gap, selenium is well-suited as the high
band gap component in a tandem solar cell. When paired with silicon, such a tandem could potentially
achieve an eﬃciency of around 35%.

3.1
Substrate cleaning
The ﬁrst step when designing solar cells, or more simply any experiment using ITO, FTO or Si
substrate, is to cut the samples (through the coated side) to the right size using a diamond blade. The
size of these samples throughout this report is ﬁxed at 13.5mm by 15.5mm. This is the standard size
used by most of the substrate holders.
The next very important step in the process is the cleaning of the substrate. The ﬁrst step is to scratch
the edges of the ITO or FTO substrates, thus reducing the probability of small glass dust detaching
from the sample and interfering with the various thin-layer deposition processes. Once this step has
been completed, the various substrates (whether ITO, FTO, Si (double polished or not)) will be placed
in di↵erent solution baths in the sonicator (ultrasonic bath) to be considered clean.
Figure 14: Di↵erent cleaning baths
As shown in the Figure 14, these three baths last 15 minutes. The ﬁrst is Milli-Q water (ultrapure
water produced by a Milli-Q puriﬁcation system) to remove the remaining particles (glass, dust...).
The second is the Acetone bath that is used to remove all the organic trace that could be on the
substrate. Finally, the last one in IPA is used to dissolve all the possible traces of Acetone on the
substrate. In between each bath, the substrates were dipped in Milli-Q water.
When all these steps are performed, the substrates are dried with nitrogen gun and placed directly in
the UV-Ozone chamber, or the vacuum chamber if a longer conservation time is needed.
The ﬁnal step before using the clean substrates is UV-Ozone treatment.
This treatment has two
main characteristics: it breaks down organic contaminants and removes carbon-based residues from
the sample surface. In addition, it increases hydrophilicity, improving the spreading and adhesion
of aqueous or polar solutions (e.g., PEDOT:PSS, PCBM). Preferably, the clean substrates are used
directly.

Thin Film deposition
Thin ﬁlms can be obtained using a variety of deposition techniques. This section will provide a brief
description of the di↵erent methods used and how they work. As each type of deposition has its own
advantages and disadvantages, it is worth using certain types of deposition depending on the desired

3.2.1
Spin coating and solution preparation
Spin coating is a widely used technique for depositing thin-ﬁlm layers, particularly in the manufacture
of solar cells. The process consists in pouring a small quantity of liquid (usually composed of the
molecules of interest in a solvent) into the center of the substrate. The substrate is then rotated,
typically at speeds ranging from 2,000 to 10,000 RPM. The centrifugal force generated in this way
evenly spreads the liquid over the substrate. During this rotation phase, the air ﬂow generated by the
rotation also helps to evaporate the solvent, allowing a solid ﬁlm composed of the molecules of interest
to ﬁnally be observed on the substrate.
Figure 15: Spin coating main steps
Typical spin-coating steps therefore include 1) deposition of the solution, 2) spreading by rotation,
then 3) evaporation of the solvent (into the air during rotation or during annealing of the created solid
ﬁlm) as shown in Figure 15. The main advantages of this technique include its simplicity, low cost,
and ability to produce very homogeneous ﬁlms on ﬂat surfaces with good thickness control (often on
the nanometer scale). It is also compatible with many organic and hybrid materials. However, spin
coating also has its limitations: low material utilization eﬃciency (most of the material is ejected),
diﬃculties in coating non-planar substrates, and lack of uniformity on large surfaces. Despite these
drawbacks, the method is still widely used because of its reproducibility, accessibility, and speed.
Finally, depending on the viscosity, the RPM, the evaporation rate of the solvent, the concentration
of the solution, the density and the thickness of the ﬂuid layer, the ﬁlm will be thicker or thinner.

3.2.3
Sputtering
In the context of this master’s thesis, only gold and silver were sputtered as contact materials for testing
solar cells. Although sputtering is generally used more extensively, particularly for the deposition of
ETLs and HTLs, spin coating was preferred here due to the focus on organic compounds. A brief
explanation is provided in this section, accompanied by a schematic representation of the simpliﬁed
design of the magnetron sputtering machine.
Figure 18: Sputtering design
As well as the thermal evaporation, the sputtering process is a physical vapor deposition (PVD)
technique used to deposit thin ﬁlms by ejecting material from a solid target onto a substrate. The
system operates under high vacuum (below 10−7 mbars) and involves the introduction of an inert
gas, usually argon, into the chamber. An electric ﬁeld is applied between the target material and the
substrate, which ionizes the argon gas and creates a plasma. Then the positively charged argon ions
Ar+ are accelerated toward the negatively charged target, causing particles of the target material to
be ejected due to momentum transfer. These ejected particles travel through the vacuum and then
condense onto the substrate to form a uniform thin ﬁlm (around 200nm for Ag).
Page 23 of 76
==PAGE:31==
Another component of the system is the magnetron, which consists of magnets positioned behind the
target. The objective of these magnets is to trap electrons near the target surface, increasing the
ionization eﬃciency of the plasma and thereby enhancing the deposition rate. The system can operate
in direct current (DC) mode for conductive targets or in radio-frequency (RF) mode for insulating
materials. However, RF sputtering provides better ﬁlm quality and uniformity, and it generally results
in lower deposition rates compared to DC sputtering. Gold (Au) and Silver (Ag) were deposited using
DC mode as they are highly conductive materials.

3.3.3
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and Cross section SEM
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is a widely used technique for imaging the surface of materials
with nanometer-scale resolution. Unlike conventional optical microscopes, which use visible light and
glass lenses to magnify features, SEM employs a focused beam of high-energy electrons. These electrons
are generated in an electron gun and accelerated toward the sample under high vacuum. The electron
beam is focused by a series of electromagnetic lenses into a very ﬁne probe and scanned across the
surface of the sample.
As the beam interacts with the sample, various signals are generated due to collisions between the
incident electrons and atoms in the material. One of the most important signals used in SEM imaging
is the secondary electron (SE) emission. Secondary electrons are low-energy electrons (typically less
than 50eV) that are ejected from the atoms in the sample as a result of inelastic scattering events.
Because they originate very close to the surface (within a few nanometers), secondary electrons are
highly sensitive to surface topography. Regions with steep edges or ﬁne textures emit more secondary
electrons due to the angle of incidence and proximity to the detector, leading to the high-contrast,
3D-like images for which SEM is known.
The reason SEM provides much higher resolution than optical microscopes lies in the wavelength of
the electron beam. The resolving power of any microscope is fundamentally limited by the wavelength
of the radiation it uses. Visible light has wavelengths in the range of 400–700nm, which limits the
resolution of optical microscopes to about 200nm (the di↵raction limit). In contrast, electrons accel-
erated at high voltages (e.g., 5–30kV) have de Broglie wavelengths on the order of picometers, which
Page 25 of 76
==PAGE:33==
enables SEM to achieve resolution below 1nm under ideal conditions.
However, because electrons are easily scattered by gas molecules, SEM must operate in a high vacuum.
Additionally, non-conductive samples can accumulate charge when exposed to the electron beam, which
distorts the image. To avoid this, such samples are often coated with a thin conductive layer of gold,
platinum, or carbon before imaging.

3.3.5
Atomic force Microscopy (AFM)
Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) is a high-resolution scanning probe technique that enables imaging
of surface topography at the nanometer scale. In contrast to Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM),
which relies on electron beams, AFM uses a mechanical probe to map the surface.
The system consists of a very sharp tip (with a radius of curvature typically below 10nm) mounted on
a ﬂexible cantilever. As the tip is scanned across the sample surface, interatomic forces between the
tip and the sample cause deﬂections of the cantilever. These deﬂections are detected via a laser beam
reﬂected from the top surface of the cantilever into a position-sensitive photodetector. The measured
deﬂection is used to generate a topographical map of the sample in real time.
AFM operates in di↵erent modes:
• Contact mode: the tip is in continuous contact with the sample surface, useful for hard surfaces,
but it can damage soft materials.
• Tapping mode: the cantilever oscillates and intermittently touches the surface, minimizing
lateral forces and reducing sample damage.
• Non-contact mode: the tip hovers above the surface and senses attractive van der Waals
forces, suitable for extremely soft or delicate surfaces.
In this work the AFM data are collected with the non-contact mode.

This section presents the main results obtained during this master’s thesis. The results are organized

The ﬁrst part concerns the development of a solar cell using the inverted cell structure and incorporating

The second part focuses on the gluing approach, aiming to replicate the results obtained in the publi-

4.1
Invert Se cell with organic layers integration
As previously discussed, the inverted structure is particularly advantageous for future integration into
tandem solar cells as well as for simplifying fabrication processes. Since this conﬁguration has already
been validated within the DTU laboratories [47], the main focus now lies in the incorporation of organic
compounds, primarily polymers. However, as demonstrated by B. petretis et al. [48], the growth of
amorphous selenium ﬁlms is strongly inﬂuenced by the nature of the underlying substrate, particularly
its electrical conductivity, which a↵ects the resulting crystalinity and overall ﬁlm quality. Consequently,
introducing new materials implies that the optimal parameters for selenium crystallization must be
re-evaluated.
4.1.1
Selenium crystallization/annealing methods
This ﬁrst subsection focuses on the crystallization of selenium on new substrates (ITO and ITO/PE-
DOT:PSS). The substrates are cleaned as described in the previous Section 3.1, then placed in the
UV-ozone chamber for 20 minutes.
A thin layer of PEDOT:PSS (reference: CleviosTM P VP AI
4083) is subsequently spin-coated on top at 6000 RPM, targeting a ﬁnal thickness of approximately
30nm. Further details regarding the rationale for this thickness and the spin-coating parameters will
be provided in the section dedicated to the characterization of the organic layers later in this thesis.
Once the PEDOT:PSS layer is deposited, the PEDOT:PSS layer is annealed on a hot plate at 130°C
for 10 minutes and then cool down to room temperature. Then, the samples are directly transferred
to the Mercedes thermal evaporator for selenium (and often tellurium) deposition (see Section 3.2.2).
This subsection discusses the various post-deposition treatments applied to the samples following their
removal from the thermal evaporator.
The desired output of this section is to ﬁnd the main characteristics of temperature, time and annealing
methods that shows the best results both in terms of crystallization (grain size and orientation of the
grains) and ﬁlm quality (presence or not of pin holes, roughness).
Annealing methods
After removing the samples from the thermal evaporator, the selenium ﬁlm is in an amorphous state.
In order to convert it into a crystalline phase, it must be annealed. Several methods can be employed
Page 29 of 76
==PAGE:37==
for this purpose, but they all share a common principle: the crystallization occurs when the amorphous
selenium receives energy, either thermal or optical, causing it to increase in temperature and transition
into a crystalline structure.

The following subsection aims to present an overview of the di↵erent annealing methods used during

Hot plate
The ﬁrst and most obvious way to anneal amorphous Se samples is the hot plate.
Although this
technique looks very simple, it is actually widely used because it is very simple and available. However,
its major drawback (considering the models available) is its very poor temperature accuracy. Indeed,
to crystallize Selenium and be consistent in the results, as well as to increase reproducibility, every
degree counts, and temperature accuracy is a very important factor.
Pizza Oven
The pizza oven is a sort of hot plate with a home-made design. It consists on a block of temperature
conductive metal with a slot inside to accommodate samples. A thermocouple is integrated for better
temperature control. The general design is shown in ﬁgure. 22
Page 30 of 76
==PAGE:38==
Figure 22: Scheme of the Pizza Oven with a sample
However, although this design enables more precise temperatures to be maintained, it has a longer
inertia than a simple hot plate. As a result, heating time is much slower, making it impossible to
achieve certain temperature ramps. Heating this pizza oven is not complicated: simply place it on a
hot plate. However, the temperature set as the goal on the hot plate is not necessarily the one that
will be reached in the heart of the pizza oven. Finally, to cool down the design if temperatures are too
high, a small ﬂow from a N2 gun (Nitrogen gun) can be used to reduce the temperature of the entire
structure by a few tenths of a degree.
Finally, in this design the temperature does not only come from the back of the sample, but also from
the top. This is why this method can potentially lead to di↵erent crystallizations of Selenium.
Laser pre-annealing
Another approach to crystallizing amorphous selenium ﬁlms involves the use of a laser, carefully
selecting a wavelength that is absorbed by the target material (selenium in this case) to initiate
crystallization. This technique creates a thin layer of high-quality crystalline grains at the rear side
of the amorphous ﬁlm, which then serve as a template for grain growth during subsequent thermal
annealing. This method has been shown to produce high-quality ﬁlms, as demonstrated by R. Nielsen
et al. in the study [49].
Although this approach was not extensively used in the present work, preliminary tests were conducted
on ITO/PEDOT:PSS/Te/Se and ITO/PEDOT:PSS/Te/Se samples to identify optimal laser param-
eters. However, the research focus shifted toward substrates based on FTO, which prevented further
optimization of this technique within the current project timeline. Nonetheless, the only selenium
solar cell results showing promising performance during this six-month project originated from the
bachelor’s thesis of Tom´as Cortes. His work involved implementing a new laser annealing setup for
selenium solar cells and evaluating its key processing parameters.
For this reason, laser annealing is included in this section.
Encapsulation
One of the major problems encountered in various attempts to anneal Selenium layers was their
evaporation, leaving samples with highly irregular Se layers. This phenomenon was ampliﬁed when
the Te layer was not present before the Se. To overcome this problem, and as the aim of this project
was to integrate organic compound layers by spin coating, it was possible to encapsulate the amorphous
Selenium between two organic layers prior to crystallization to prevent evaporation. This method will
be referred to as encapsulation annealing in this report.

==PAGE:39==
O2 treatment
As Kai Shen et al. showed [36], introducing Oxygen can lead to better orientation of the t-Se (Trigonal
Selenium) grains, leading to better ﬁlms and carrier transport. In order to achieve that, some of the
ﬁlms were treated with O2 before annealing. In the case of this master’s thesis, treating a sample with
O2 means that this sample has been under an O2 ﬂow for 1 minute.
Optimization and characterization of Se ﬁlms
Before introducing organic layers into Se solar cells, one of the major challenges of this master thesis
was to achieve a good crystallized Se layer. Selenium is an element that does not stick very well to
other materials, so it is quite complex to crystallize it on di↵erent substrates. Tests were therefore
carried out on both ITO and PEDOT:PSS to determine the optimum parameters for obtaining the
highest-quality Selenium ﬁlms. The points of interest are as follows ordered by importance level:
• Crystallinity:
As seen previously, selenium crystallizes in its trigonal form. For a completely
random orientation, the most intense peaks are (100) present at 2✓⇡24 and (101) at 2✓⇡30,
their ratio will be compared to determine the best possible orientation.
Charge transport across the ﬁlm plane in the (hk0) directions perpendicular to the c axis is
poor because bonds between adjacent selenium ribbons are weak. In ﬁlms where most grains are
oriented along (hk0), the ribbons lie ﬂat on the substrate surface, which reduces the electronic
performance. Many studies agree that reducing the proportion of (hk0) aligned grains leads to
higher eﬃciency solar cells [50]. By contrast, transport along the c axis is more e↵ective thanks
to strong covalent bonds within each ribbon. Films with higher proportion of vertical ribbons
therefore exhibit better charge mobility. The best results occur in (001) oriented ﬁlms and, more
generally in (hk1), where vertical ribbon orientation maximizes charge conduction [51]. In this
case the preferential peak will be the (101).
To quantify the relative strength of the (100) and (101) peaks, the ratio R is used:

Crystallinity appears to be the most critical factor, as it is directly linked to charge transport in
selenium and can thus signiﬁcantly improve the eﬃciency of Se solar cells. Many studies have
aimed to optimize this key parameter (see [35]). The grain size is also an very important factor
of the crystallinity of a material. Indeed with larger grains, the crystalline material more closely
approaches a perfect crystal (i.e. a single, uniﬁed grain), thereby eliminating defect sites and
grain-boundary interfaces.
• Roughness :
An excessive roughness of the t-Se ﬁlm can lead to poor contact with the ETL
or HTL, voids, or localized high ﬁelds that can cause leakage. Two main characteristics can
be calculated, the average roughness (Ra) and the root mean square roughness (Rq). The root
mean square is more sensitive to pronounced local defects (spikes, dust particles, micro-craters)
when the average roughness smooths out those extremes and reﬂects the overall roughness.
• Pin holes : A pin-hole in a selenium ﬁlm is a tiny, localized defect where the deposited layer
fails to fully cover the substrate, leaving a microscopic void or thin spot.
These gaps arise
when individual Se islands do not coalesce seamlessly during growth or retract during thermal
processing, and manifest as tiny craters visible under optical microscopy, SEM or AFM. In
devices, pin-holes act as pathways that bridge the ﬁlm’s electrodes, leading to increased leakage
currents and reduced performance, as well as serving as initiation sites for degradation. The
picture 24 below shows a pin hole (red spot) of an SEM picture.

Results
Thus, after investigating the main factors a↵ecting the annealing of amorphous selenium (temperature,
duration, and technique, while keeping the atmosphere constant) it appears that the optimal procedure
(found in this thesis) for achieving the desired ﬁlm properties (whether on bare ITO or on ITO with
PEDOT:PSS) is to pre-treat the sample with O2 and then anneal it in air at 190°C for four minutes
on top of the pizza oven, before spin coating any layers on top.
The principal sources of uncertainty in these measurements relate to the annealing temperatures them-
selves. Although a thermocouple is present in the pizza oven, there remains some doubt as to the precise
temperature to which the sample is exposed.

4.1.4
Result of the section
To conclude this ﬁrst section on the inverted Se cell structure with integrated organic layers, a large
number of experiments were carried out to characterize Se crystallization under varied conditions.
Systematic parameters variation like O2 pretreatment, temperature, duration and annealing methods
showed that the optimal protocol for this master thesis is an O2 pretreatment followed by four minutes
of annealing at 190°C on the pizza oven. The ETL and HTL were likewise examined. Incorporating
these layers into test devices demonstrated that, although they inﬂuence Se crystallization, they do
not alter the optimal Se annealing parameters.
However, the properties of the Se ﬁlms prepared under these conditions proved poorly reproducible,
as reﬂected in the experimental results. Only a small number of complete devices yielded conclusive
performance data.
Other tests were also conducted using the other annealing methods described earlier, but as expected,
they did not yield conclusive results.

• Temperature choice

• Setting the desired temperature on the two hot plates and adjusting the pressure during the heat-

• Wait ten minutes at the desired temperature. During this time the pressure should be stable.

• When the last step is done, the pellet press can be removed from the Hot press and the samples

4.2.3
Integration and Characterization of Hot pressed PEDOT:PSS and PCBM
When the organic compounds were characterized in the previous section, the pressure was simply
atmospheric. However, here, since both pressure and temperature are higher, their properties may
change.
This section is based on existing literature and new experiments, its aim is to describe the behavior of
these organic materials under elevated pressure and temperature.
First, for PEDOT:PSS, Liu et al.
showed in 2022 [13] that increasing temperature and pressure
improves the conductivity of PEDOT:PSS ﬁlms up to 230°C and 8MPa. Beyond that temperature,
the ﬁlm’s conductivity begins to decline.
In our setup we use only 1.2MPa and temperatures up to 250°C, which theoretically does not maximize
ﬁlm conductivity. However, referring to the table shown before, when the hot plate setpoint is 250°C,
the actual sample temperature is approximately 239°C, which is quite close to the 230°C optimum.
Next, PCBM exhibits a similar response to pressure and temperature. Oyewole et al. demonstrated in
2021 [55] that, when doped with P3HT, PCBM ﬁlms show improved carrier mobility and lifetime as
pressure and temperature increase up to 150°C and 8MPa. Above 200°C, however, PCBM no longer
responds favorably and its properties deteriorate.
In this section on incorporating organic layers into a glued cell, it will therefore be necessary to ﬁnd a
way to reduce the gluing temperature, which is currently between 225°C and 250°C. To that end, as
in the ﬁrst part of the Result and Discussion, the crystallization processes of Se on PEDOT:PSS and
on PCBM will be compared with those on bare ITO.
Characterization of Se “sandwiched” between a layer of PEDOT:PSS and PCBM

PCBM layer, when the cells are separated the selenium properties on the PEDOT:PSS side and on the
PCBM side may di↵er. This study is carried out below using one sample hot pressed at 225°C between
two ITO substrates and one sample hot pressed at 225°C between an ITO/PEDOT:PSS substrate and
an ITO/PCBM substrate.

4.2.4
Attempt to stick PEDOT:PSS and Sorbitol
The primary aim of this master’s thesis was ﬁrst to explore the use of organic compounds as a glue
between the top cell and the bottom cell. This strategy would simplify the fabrication of tandem Si/Se
solar cells and, more generally, any tandem solar cell incorporating organic layers.
Two testing approaches were then envisaged: bonding the two cells via the HTL layer (PEDOT:PSS)
or via the ETL layer (PCBM). The latter seems more promising on paper, since it would allow selenium
to crystallize without direct contact with PCBM, which appears to enhance its crystallinity. However,
a survey of the literature reveals that similar experiments have already been carried out using mixtures
of PEDOT:PSS and d-sorbitol [56] [57] [58]. The reason to use d-sorbitol in this work is its low melting
point and glue properties that has been shown in these papers.
In these studies, d-sorbitol and PEDOT:PSS functioned as a conductive glue.
To investigate this
approach here, the tests listed below were performed on clean ITO substrates in air, without selenium.

The PEDOT:PSS solution was prepared exactly as described earlier in this report.
The two ITO
substrates were then spin coated with PEDOT:PSS under the same conditions as before (6000 RPM
for 1min, acceleration 3000 RPM/s). The ﬁlms were subsequently annealed for 10min at 130°C.
One of the ﬁlms was then coated with d-sorbitol. Unless otherwise speciﬁed, the d-sorbitol layer was
spin coated at 2000 RPM to maximize its thickness. The d-sorbitol solution consisted of isopropyl
alcohol (IPA) containing 6% d-sorbitol by mass. This mixture was stirred for several hours, since

easily in water.
All tests were performed under the same setup conditions as before (hot press and pellet press with
graphite sheets).
Unfortunately, none of these tests proved conclusive. One possible explanation is that the d-sorbitol
concentration was too low to allow e↵ective bonding. Although tests were carried out in that direction
(by increasing the thickness of the d-sorbitol layer), it is quite possible that the layer still was not thick
enough due to the very low concentration of d-sorbitol in the solvent.
This section presents only preliminary tests, representing a ﬁrst step toward optimizing cell gluing.
Further experiments will need to be carried out in the future, as the results obtained highlight a need
of improvement.

4.2.6
Result of the section
In this second part of the Result and Discussion, systematic tests varying temperature and substrate
composition (ITO alone or ITO with PEDOT PSS and ITO with PCBM) were conducted to determine
the optimal conditions for both bonding the substrates and producing the best possible selenium in
terms of crystallographic orientation and grain size. The optimal parameters identiﬁed in this study
are hot pressing at 1200kPa and 250°C using the standard procedure described earlier. Under these
conditions, exceptionally large grains (up to 4 micrometers) form and the ﬁlm achieves a preferential
orientation with R = 0.22.
Regarding cell gluing, the tests performed in this section are a crucial step towards the fabrication
of practical Si/Se tandem cells. In particular, the ability to hot-press selenium-based cells directly
against silicon cells is truly exciting. This gluing approach could be applied to any tandem architecture
compatible with a selenium top cell, opening up new possibilities. However, the Selenium thin ﬁlms
still exhibit imperfections that preclude high-quality solar cells, pin holes and poor crystallinity when
annealed with PCBM present challenges for future work. To address these issues, some tests have
been carried out using alternative materials or methods. For example, replacing PCBM, which hinders
selenium crystallization, with TiO2. A brief description of the implementation of a TiO2 layer to replace
PCBM is provided in Appendix 6. In addition, hot pressing experiments have been conducted with
pre-crystallized selenium under more optimal conditions in order to avoid non preferential orientation,
pin holes and excessively small grains. These additional experiments have not yet succeeded, but too
little time was devoted to them. They therefore warrant further investigation to yield more conclusive
results. A way to improve these characteristics would be to be able to investigate what happens inside
cells when gluing is successful. Indeed, for now, the cells must be split in two, which prevents perfect
observation of the selenium structure.

5
Conclusion and Future Work
In this master’s thesis, two complementary strategies were pursued to advance the ﬁeld of high-band-gap
selenium (Se) photovoltaics and to explore their integration into tandem architectures with silicon (Si).
The ﬁrst strategy involved the fabrication and detailed characterization of inverted Se solar cells incor-
porating PEDOT:PSS as a hole transport layer and PCBM as an electron transport layer. A systematic
study of the annealing parameters protocols was carried out. It was found that depositing amorphous
Se onto clean ITO and ITO/PEDOT:PSS substrates, followed by pre treating with O2 and annealing at
190 °C for four minutes in an air atmosphere on top of the pizza oven, consistently produced densely
packed grains of approximately 500nm in size and an R-ratio around 0.42–0.44, indicating a (101)
preferential orientation. Despite these structural improvements conﬁrmed by SEM, AFM, and XRD
analyses, electrical characterization revealed very low open-circuit voltages (0.2 V) and short-circuit
currents (0.1 mA.cm2), largely due to pinhole formation and insuﬃcient shunt resistance.
The second strategy was inspired by gluing techniques used in tandem cell assembly and employed
hot-press bonding to attach selenium ﬁlms directly to various substrates. Initially, thin amorphous Se
layers hot-pressed at 250°C under 1200kPa between two ITO substrates adhered strongly and crystal-
lized into large, well-oriented grains (2 µm in average) that remained intact during manual handling.
Building on this success, the same hot-press conditions to bond an ITO/PEDOT:PSS/Te/Se sample
to an ITO/PCBM/Te/Se sample were applied in an e↵ort to create a device incorporating organic
transport layers. In this case, selenium crystallization became more random showing the need of more
experiments to build a proper device. A ﬁnal experiment in this series involved gluing an ITO/Te/Se
substrate to a silicon wafer. Despite the brittleness of the silicon, the substrate did not fracture, and
the Se/Si interface formed a continuous ﬁlm with few pinholes and grain sizes of approximately 500nm.
XRD analysis revealed an R-ratio of 0.72, indicating a more random orientation, yet mechanical ad-
hesion was excellent. These results demonstrate that selenium itself can serve as an e↵ective adhesive
glue, potentially simplifying the fabrication of monolithic Se/Si tandem cells by eliminating the need
for complex interconnect layers. Concerning the organic layers as conductive glue, the ﬁrst tests were
not conclusive, more experiments are needed in future work to see if that could be an interesting
solution.
Important challenges must be addressed before practical devices can be realized. First, the inverted
Se cells with PEDOT:PSS and PCBM interlayers did not exhibit convincing photovoltage and cur-
rent characteristics in this study. Pinholes and interface defects continue to dominate their electrical
behavior, and the thermal stability of the organic layers under hot-press conditions remains a critical
concern.
Second, the hot press bonding process, while promising, lacks of process control. Non uniform pres-
sure and temperature gradients within the pellet press lead to uneven ﬁlms and patterned pin hole
formation.
Future work should focus on mitigating these issues through a combination of materials engineering
and process optimization. First, other organic layers should be tested. PEDOT:PSS is an excellent
candidate for both the inverted cell structure and the gluing step, as it performs well under pressure
and temperature and its spin-coating process is highly reproducible and yields uniform, high-quality
ﬁlms. PCBM, on the other hand, should be reconsidered or at least replaced by another polymer
since it is unstable outside an argon atmosphere, its spin-coated layers can be inconsistent, and it is
relatively expensive. For example, PNDI(20D)2T, a common n-type organic semiconductor in solar
cells, could be evaluated as an alternative.
Second, studies should aim to reduce the density of pinholes in the Se ﬁlms. Pinholes undermine
the electronic and optical properties of the layers and may arise from suboptimal Te concentrations
Page 73 of 76
==PAGE:81==
before Se deposition or from inadequate annealing conditions, particularly the annealing atmosphere.
Adjusting these parameters could greatly improve ﬁlm quality.
For the gluing process, it would be valuable to correct the planarity of the hot press to guarantee
uniform pressure over the entire sample surface. It may also be worthwhile to explore conductive glues
based on PEDOT:PSS/sorbitol blends with higher d-sorbitol concentrations. Finally, one could con-
sider using the n-type layer itself as the adhesive: by crystallizing the Se ﬁrst under optimal conditions
without contact with the n-type layer, it might be possible to avoid the undesirable (100) preferential
orientation observed when Se is annealed directly against that layer.
Finally, once interface quality is assured, e↵orts should shift toward full device integration: fabricating
and characterizing complete Se/Si tandem cells under one sun illumination, mapping current-voltage
and external quantum eﬃciency for each subcell, and conducting accelerated aging tests to evaluate
mechanical and environmental stability.
Although fully functional tandem devices were not achieved within the scope of this work, the com-
bination of optimized Se crystallinity and a novel hot press gluing approach lays a solid foundation.
By systematically reﬁning material interfaces and process parameters, it should be possible to un-
lock eﬃcient, scalable monolithic Se/Si photovoltaics, thereby opening new avenues for low cost, high
eﬃciency solar energy conversion.
