Abstract
The construction sector plays a major role in creating tomorrow’s sustainable society. The
increased awareness of resource consumption, environmental damage, and especially climate
change has led to increasing demands for sustainability in construction. The need to build more
sustainable is widely accepted, however, there is a lack of good examples and guidance on how
this can be translated into speciﬁc actions. Renovation of dwellings seems to be particularly
relevant in this context, as the relatively high, building-related energy and resource consumption
allows for savings of greenhouse gas emissions.
In this assignment, the focus is on the use of life cycle assessments to prepare a sustainable
building design. It was desired to ﬁnd out, whether it is possible to increase the level of
sustainability in the built environment by replacing the existing detached houses from the 60s
and 70s with houses based on prefabricated modules. The prefabricated building design is
optimised by minimising environmental impacts and to determine the level of sustainability, the
environmental footprint of the house is normalised with the bearing capacity of the globe, called
absolute sustainability.
The analyses show that it is easier to achieve a low energy consumption by constructing new
buildings, rather than renovating an existing building, and it can be an economic advantage to
construct new houses based on prefabricated volume modules. There is thus a potential for the
construction sector to contribute to the sustainable transformation by demolishing the current
energy-consuming housing stock and constructing new prefabricated module-based buildings.
Moreover, the result shows that with the current limits for absolute sustainability it is very difﬁcult
to build an absolute sustainable single-family house. This is even though the ﬁnal building design
ends up having a greenhouse gas emission that is 60% lower than the median for single-family
houses in the industry reference SBi report Klimapåvirkning fra 60 bygninger.
To reach the political targets of a 70% CO2 reduction by 2030, and globally being CO2-neutral
by 2050, the assessment shows that there is a critical need for implementation of solutions. By
reorganising the Danish energy system to being emission-free by 2045, and replacing all the
energy-consuming detached single-family houses built in the years 1960-1979, these types of
buildings will be able to become CO2-neutral by 2045. This is made possible by constructing
new low-energy buildings with a low-cost construction phase by off-site produced building
components.
The conclusion of the thesis is that prefabricated detached houses have a great potential for
promoting sustainable construction, even though the complexity is constantly increasing and the
deﬁnition of the concept of sustainability is subject to constant change.

Nomenclature
Absolute sustainability The sustainability level of a product concerning the carrying capacity
of the globe.
AP
Acidiﬁcation Potential. Reacts with water and falls as "acid rain", which i.a. results in
the break down of root systems and leaching of plant nutrients.
CC
Carrying Capacity. "The maximum sustained environmental intervention a natural
system can withstand without experiencing negative changes in structure or functioning
that are difﬁcult or impossible to revert. The size of the environmental capacity is per.
deﬁnition limited and is also (partially) quantiﬁable" from A. Bjørn & M.Z. Hauschild,
2015.
CCS
Carbon Capture and Utilisation. Carbon capture and utilisation is the process of
collecting carbon dioxide that needs to be recycled for further use.
CML
Centrum voor Milieukunde Leiden. A characterisation method for life cycle assessments
based on a problem-oriented approach, which means that quantitative modeling is
limited to using midpoint categories.
DGNB
Deutsche Gesellschaft für Nachhaltiges Bauen. Certiﬁcation scheme for sustainable
construction.
EoL
End of Life. The last construction phase, where the building is demolished and the
building parts abolished.
EP
Eutrophication Potential. Excessive nutrient inputs promote unwanted plant growth in
delicate ecosystems, e.g. algae growth resulting in ﬁsh death.
EPD
Environmental Product Declaration. Documentation that expresses the aggregated data
of the environmental emissions produced by a product life cycle.
GDP
Gross domestic product is a measure of a country’s value added, i.e. the value of a
country’s total production of goods and services minus the value of the raw materials
used in a given period, typically one year.
GHG
Greenhouse gases. Greenhouse gas emissions are the emission of greenhouse gas that
causes climate change by creating a greenhouse effect in the Earth’s atmosphere.
GWP
Global Warming Potential. Impact category that indicates the total emission of a
greenhouse gas relative to one unit of the reference gas, carbon dioxide.
ILCD
International Life Cycle Data system. A recommendation of characterisation methods
to be used to calculate different impact categories.

1.1
Background
The main theme of this report concerns the optimisation of the way the resources in the building
industry are utilised. Our planet is currently in a climate crisis, where the Earth’s surface
temperature is rising every year (NASA, 2021). The Earth’s climate is changing, the ecosystem is
getting out of balance, and it is only getting harder to bring it back to the state of balance at baseline
(Stockholm Resilience Centre, 2021). Human activity is increasingly causing greenhouse gas
emissions, such as CO2, methane, CFCs, and nitrogen oxides, which have with 95% probability
lead to the warming of our planet over the last 50 years (NASA, 2021).
The construction industry contributes greatly to this development, from which greenhouse gas
emissions doubled in the period 1970-2010, and in 2014 the industry accounted for 25% of the
total greenhouse gas emissions (IPCC, 2015). In 2010, the construction industry also accounted
for 32% of total global energy consumption, of which 24% came from residential and 8% from
commercial buildings (IPCC, 2015).
About 38% of all detached houses in Denmark are built in the years from 1960 to 1979 (Danmarks
Statistik, 2021a). These houses were built at a time without requirements for energy efﬁciency,
and they are therefore not very well insulated. These houses will thus have to be renovated in the
coming years to reduce their energy consumption.
Renovation of these older houses can be very costly as they will typically be expensive to maintain
- even after a major renovation. Expenses that will not appear in the construction of a new house,
and new construction will thus in the long run be the cheapest solution (Dagens Byggeri, 2020b).
However, with construction as a signiﬁcant factor in the climate challenges, solutions to the large
emissions and consumption must be found. These solutions cannot stand alone, but require that
the construction industry is set on a change of norms and traditions, which is why this project seeks
to help the designer of the construction well on its way to replace unhealthy energy-consuming
buildings with innovative and sustainable low-cost modular construction.
1.2
Objectives
Through research, this project aims to develop a new sustainable building design of prefabricated
modules. Making it possible to increase the level of sustainability in the built environment by
replacing the existing detached houses from the 60s and 70s with houses based on prefabricated
modules.
The main thesis of this report is to examine the possibility of designing an absolute sustainable
detached house by the use of prefabricated modules and using it as a solution instead of renovating
existing detached houses from the 60s and 70s.
The main thesis is divided into three sub-theses:
• What does it mean that a building is sustainable, and what considerations for implementing
sustainability are thus relevant?
• Can the construction of new prefabricated module-based buildings as a replacement for
existing detached houses from the 60s and 70s be a sustainable solution compared to
typical renovation solutions?
• Can the design of a detached house built as a modular wooden building be optimised and
thus made absolute sustainable?

Chapter 1. Introduction
Sustainability in the construction industry and the current tools for quantifying and documenting
sustainability are examined through a limited empirical study. The preliminary study will
moreover look into the possibilities of using the ﬁndings to realise the political sustainability
targets and will be used to determine an appropriate method for further investigations.
A Life Cycle Assessment is used to determine the environmental impact of the construction of a
prefabricated building design of a detached house. The prefabricated building design used is a
result of the course 11116 Sustainable Buildings at DTU carried out during Fall 2020. A Life
Cycle Assessment (LCA) tool is used to determine the environmental impact of the construction
in comparison to typical renovation solutions, and the level of sustainability is determined by
relating the environmental footprint to the bearing capacity of the globe.
The subsequent thesis concerning optimisation of the building design is developed on the same
building design from the course 11116 Sustainable Buildings.
The found results will be evaluated and put into perspective through a discussion, and based on
the conclusions of this, the ﬁndings will be able to inspire the actors who set the direction and
framework conditions for construction in Denmark, and thus promote the current sustainable
transition.

Chapter 2. Context
2.1
Political sustainability initiatives
By 2030, we must reduce our greenhouse gas emissions by 70% compared to 1990 (UN base
year), and by 2050 at the latest, Denmark must be climate-neutral (Klima-, Energi- og Forsyn-
ingsministeriet, 2020). Over the past year, decisions have been made in Denmark that ensures
greenhouse gas reductions of approx. 5 million ton CO2-eq. This corresponds to a quarter of
what must be achieved in 2030. This reduction is mainly driven by developments in the energy
sector, where emissions have fallen by 65% from 1990 to 2018.
As much as 40% of the energy consumption in Denmark is consumed in the built environment.
Primarily for heating or cooling. And 30% of the CO2 emissions are related to buildings. Either
in the form of energy used for heating or the CO2 that is embedded in building materials and
consumed in the construction process. Thus, buildings are an important piece in the ambitions to
reduce CO2 emissions, and at the same time the answer to how we get a cost-effective transition
to a future where the energy supply must be green and fossil-free.
In December 2020, the Danish government presented a new climate proposal with a strategy
for sustainable construction. The goal of the strategy is to ensure a more sustainable and
quality-conscious construction that is also ﬁnancially responsible (TRM, 2020). The strategy
is fundamentally about setting the long-term framework for a transformation of the Danish
construction sector that encompasses all the dimensions of sustainability: the social, the economic,
and the environmental. The strategy is divided into ﬁve focus areas, of which Focus area 1: More
climate-friendly construction, includes CO2 regulation in construction and further development
of LCA and LCC for design tools, and it is this focus area that this report will primarily focus on.
In March 2021, a broad majority in the Danish parliament agreed on this new national strategy
for sustainable construction in Denmark (The Danish Government, 2021). This means that, in
the coming years, the new strategy will try to ensure quality-conscious, ﬁnancially responsible,
and more sustainable construction by, among other things, adding requirements in the building
regulations for the carbon footprint of buildings. These requirements will be based on the
criterion from the voluntary sustainability standards (VSS) on life cycle assessment (LCA), which
calculates the building’s CO2 emissions. The requirement is introduced in the building regulations
in the same way as the building regulations’ other technical requirements.
Experience with the voluntary sustainability standards must be built up before the requirements of
the standard can be introduced in the building regulations. This is ensured through a two-year test
phase in 2020-22, which i.a. must cover the environmental, the indoor climate, and the economic
effects of the standard. After the phasing-in of the emission requirement in 2023, the parties to
the agreement will meet every second year with the agenda of setting a limit value for the coming
two years, so that this can be determined based on the latest knowledge and data. The initiative
will ensure the introduction of requirements for LCA calculation in 2023 for all construction
and the introduction of a limit value for new construction over 1000 m2 from 2023 of 12 kg
CO2 eq./m2/year and other new construction from 2025 with an expected limit of 10.5 kg CO2
eq./m2/year. The limit in the voluntary sustainability standards for 2023 and 2025 are 8 and 7 kg
CO2 eq./m2/year, respectively.

2.2
Sustainability in the industry
Today, the construction industry has not worked intensively with the calculation of CO2 emissions
on construction projects, as there have not been any actual limit values before, and thus no
incentive to lower embedded CO2 in the building materials. Moreover, the limit values given in
the new national sustainability strategy for 2023 corresponds to the current emissions of most
new construction in Denmark and do therefore not seem like an ambitious goal. It can thus be
said, that the strategy seems like it will not make a real difference in the building sector before
the new and more ambitious requirements in 2025.
Nevertheless, there are signs that sustainability - both concerning construction and operation - is
an increasing trend in the construction industry. This is driven by more stringent requirements for
resource and energy efﬁciency in buildings, changing consumer preferences, and the economic
savings of using less energy and fewer resources. This can among other things be seen by several
major players in the Danish construction market intensively working with sustainability in their
strategies, such as Rockwool, which on January 1st, 2021 switched their Danish production to
biogas, which has reduced their CO2 emissions by 70% (Pedersen M., 2021). But to reach the
climate goals and have a green transition in the entire construction sector, there is a need for the
development of tools and ambitious requirements for emissions as well as energy consumption
and supply with an economic basis.
2.2.1
Sustainable Development Goals
"Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present without compro-
mising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs." is the deﬁnition of sustainable
development from the Brundtland Report (Brundtland Commission, 1987: 37). The very concept
of sustainability covers many areas, and can broadly be divided into social, economic, and envi-
ronmental sustainability, also called the Triple Bottom Line. When working with sustainability, it
is important to keep all three bottom lines in mind.
Until 2015, the UN was responsible for organising extensive work to design the 17 world goals
through an inclusive process with companies, governments, and NGOs from around the world.
The 17 world goals and the associated 169 sub-goals represent the world’s most important
challenges, which we as a society must work to solve towards 2030. The SDGs are an ambitious
plan for the world and place new demands on all countries to take responsibility and contribute to
a sustainable transition at all levels. It calls for a holistic and strategic effort, while the individual
organisations at the same time need concrete and measurable tools to work with the world goals.
By working strategically with the SDGs, new opportunities and dynamics are created that put
construction into a new and broader societal understanding framework - a framework where
responsibility and effects of decisions go hand in hand.
Dansk Byggeri has chosen to focus on 9 of the 17 goals in which the Danish construction industry
can make a concrete difference: #4 Quality Education, #5 Gender Equality, #6 Clean Water
and Sanitation, #7 Affordable and Clean Energy, #8 Decent Work and Economic Growth, #9
Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure, #11 Sustainable cities and communities, #12 Responsible
Consumption and Production as well as #13 Climate Action (Dansk Industri, 2021).
A survey conducted by Deloitte (Deloitte, 2019) showed that 75% of companies in the Danish
construction industry know about sustainability and the SDGs. However, many of the companies
in the construction industry do not use the goals in their day-to-day work. Thus, there is great
potential for improvement in the industry, which, seems to evolve from the economic aspect of
the contemporary sustainable solutions. To create a greater focus on the sustainability goals, there

2.2.2
Life Cycle Assessments
Life Cycle Assessments (LCA)1 are increasingly used in Denmark to assess the environmental
sustainability of buildings. With LCA on a building, both environmental impacts and resource
consumption from operations as well as from building materials can be calculated and evaluated
over a selected calculation period (called consideration period). By using an LCA, the environ-
mental impacts of a given building can be quantiﬁed and compared with similar buildings to be
able to assess how the environmental impacts can be reduced.
LCA is an important part of both the new Voluntary Sustainability Standard and of various
environmental certiﬁcates such as DGNB. In addition, DGNB does not yet include detached
houses, which have a signiﬁcant share of the construction activities in Denmark. In 2015,
LCAbyg, which is a free Danish LCA tool for construction, was launched. The use of LCAbyg
has gradually grown over the last ﬁve years, where there are now almost 4500 registered users
and between 400-600 active users of the tool every month (LCAbyg.dk, n.d.).
The Voluntary Sustainability Standards was launched in May 2020 (TBST, 2020) and it will with
the new national strategy lead to further focus on the use of LCA to reduce the environmental
impact of buildings in Denmark, including the climate impact of buildings.
Absolute Sustainability
Life cycle assessments provide answers regarding the relative sustainability of buildings by
comparing the results and indicating which solution is most sustainable. Thus, it does not tell
how sustainable the buildings are. Therefore, a new phenomenon has emerged, called Absolute
Sustainability, which aims to investigate whether the environmental impact of a building is low
enough, concerning what our planet can carry. Thus, the concept of absolute sustainability refers
exclusively to environmental sustainability.
In a study by A. Bjørn & M.Z. Hauschild, 2015, the need for an "absolute" evaluation criterion
instead of current practice in the form of traditional comparative environmental impact assessment
(LCA) of buildings was identiﬁed. The evaluation criterion is based on a scientiﬁc understanding
of the environment’s capacity to cope with anthropogenic perturbations/emissions and is aligned
with the fundamental principle of life cycle thinking in ISO 14040 (DS/EN ISO 14040:2008,
n.d.).
Absolute sustainability means that the environmental impact is within the safe operating space.
For example, a building is deﬁned as absolutely sustainable if the results from the LCA for
the different impact categories remain within the allocated threshold. Thereby, an absolute
sustainability assessment can indicate whether a building’s environmental performance is "good
enough" in regards to the carrying capacity of the globe.

2.2.3
Planetary Boundaries and the Carrying Capacity of the Environment
The concept of planetary boundaries was proposed in 2009 by a group of environmental re-
searchers led by Johan Rockström from the Stockholm Resilience Center and Will Steffen from
the Australian National University (Stockholm Resilience Centre, 2021). The concept is based
on the idea that the state of the environment changes together with the environmental impacts
to which the earth is exposed. Figure 2.1 shows a curve of the global environmental response.
The curve will remain fairly stable until a certain limit, after which a drastic drop in the state of
the environment will occur. Such a decrease will occur, for example, due to large emissions of
greenhouse gases, whereby climate change will be extremely affected. The original state will be
difﬁcult to restore, and the control variable will have to be reduced a lot more than what it has
increased, to return to the starting point. Therefore, reaching the critical point of environmental
impact where the dive of the curve takes place should be avoided. The critical point is called the
carrying capacity of the environment. The area under this point is difﬁcult to deﬁne precisely
as it has not yet been reached, and therefore a range has been deﬁned where it is thought that
the carrying capacity of the environment may lie. This is the yellow ﬁeld, which in Figure 2.1
is called the zone of uncertainty. The red ﬁeld is thus beyond the zone of uncertainty, which
means that the limit is exceeded. The planetary boundary is thus given by the value of the Earth’s
carrying capacity and is estimated by scientists within the ﬁeld. This means that the Earth will not
be able to carry a larger emission than the established boundary without unrecoverable changes
in the climate. In the ﬁgure, the boundary is seen as the line dividing green and yellow. The
green area illustrates how large the environmental impact can be before it becomes critical. This
emission limit must be shared between humans and nature’s background level, and the share
allocated to humans is called the Share of Safe Operating Space (SoSOS) for humanity.
Figure 2.1 illustrates how the global response is affected by several local and regional impacts.
Locally, the impacts assume a soft curve, but when all the local impacts are seen together on a
global level, a critical point (marked with a dotted line in the zone of uncertainty) emerges where
the curve will take a drastic dive.
Figure 2.1: The conceptual framework for the planetary boundary approach, showing the safe operating space, the
zone of uncertainty, the position of the threshold (where it is likely to exist), and the area of high risk.
Source: Stockholm Resilience Centre, 2021.

been deﬁned within 9 impact categories (climate change, ocean acidiﬁcation, stratospheric ozone
depletion, biogeochemical ﬂows, freshwater use, land use, biodiversity loss, atmospheric aerosol

2.3
Renovation vs. construction
Almost 65 percent of the built environment in Denmark was built before 1980 (Danmarks
Statistik, 2021a). That is before requirements were made for the insulation capacity and energy
consumption of buildings. This results in leaks of heat from doors and windows in many buildings,
which is conﬁrmed by looking at the many buildings that have been energy labeled. 59% of all
single-family houses that are energy-labeled were labeled from D-G in 2020, and it is assumed
that almost all of the single-family houses without an energy label (about 40%) also have an
extensive energy consumption (Dagens Byggeri, 2020a). There are simply too many buildings
with a bad energy label. So there are plenty of CO2 savings to reap if we want to reduce energy
consumption in our buildings. Insulation of ceilings and walls, new windows and doors, or new
systems for controlling energy consumption are just examples of solutions that can reduce energy
consumption in our buildings. There is thus great potential for CO2 savings by introducing
solutions that can reduce the energy consumption in older buildings. And by combining the
solution with the replacement of fossil energy sources such as oil and natural gas boilers, then
great CO2 reductions can be achieved.
In the Climate Partnership’s report with recommendations to the government from March 2020
(Klimapartnerskabet, 2020) the assumption that renovation is better than demolition and con-
struction in terms of reducing CO2 emissions is expressed, but without profound evidence. And
even though there are several examples of renovation projects that have resulted in both value
creation in the buildings in question and have provided a more climate-friendly solution, the
renovation solution is still often downgraded by the house-owners in favor of demolition and new
construction, as this solution makes it easier to personalise the home.
2.3.1
Development of energy demand & supply
With the goal of Denmark being independent of coal, oil, and gas by 2050, the entire Danish
energy supply must be restructured so that it is based on renewable energy sources, such as wind,
solar, biomass, and geothermal energy. Energy-efﬁcient buildings are essential in a cost-effective
restructuring of the Danish energy supply. This means that the energy supply to the building sector
must be converted to new energy sources, primarily consisting of solar heating and geothermal
energy (Mathiesen et al., 2016). Biomass boilers should not be supported, as it does not appear to
be an appropriate supply solution for heating of buildings, either in terms of fuel efﬁciency or
from an economic point of view but will be used to a lesser extent than today.
Traditionally, the energy used for the operation (especially heating) of buildings exceeded the
amount of energy used in construction by far. But that is not the case anymore. Since the
ﬁrst Building Regulations were published in 1961 and began to set requirements for how much
energy new construction may use, energy consumption in new construction has fallen drastically
(Energistyrelsen, 2014).
In Figure 2.2 the developments are illustrated. Over the past 60 years, the need for newly built
buildings for operating energy for heating, ventilation, and cooling has been greatly reduced,

about 1.25 million tons of CO2/year (Klimapartnerskabet, 2020) can be reached in 2030 by energy
efﬁciency improvements and further 1.8 million tons of CO2/year by replacing the fossil energy
sources, if the Danish politicians act now. There is thus a great potential for mitigating Denmark’s
greenhouse gas emissions, which can be achieved cost-effectively e.g. energy renovation in
buildings.
The development in energy consumption does however also entail a large potential for reducing
both resource and energy consumption in the construction phase. The production, transport,
disposal, and recycling of building materials account for an increasing share of the construction’s
total resource and environmental effects. Therefore, attention is now focused on the building ma-
terials’ embedded energy consumption and environmental impacts as an opportunity to continue
to increase the sustainability of the construction sector in the coming years.

Prefabricated volume modules
Buildings based on prefabricated volume modules are typically constructed with wood and have
in connection with the focus on the green transition in recent years become a more popular
construction method. Prefabricated modules are based on elements of wood produced at the
factory and are thus independent of the weather. The elements and modules are thus produced
with e.g. a much greater certainty that moisture does not accumulate in the structure during
construction. Moreover, the modules have advantages concerning faster construction time, greater
security for quality, minimisation of mold damage, easier transport, assembly, and reusability.
These advantages mean that several aspects of the construction process can be completed simul-
taneously, rather than sequentially as required in traditional construction, resulting in off-site
construction being able to reduce project construction time by approximately 40 to 50 percent
(McKinsey & Company, 2019). These are important elements on the transition to sustainable
construction and the possibilities for minimising CO2 emissions. Moreover, it is a fairly low-cost
construction method - making the sustainable solution economically advantageous.
Prefabricated wooden modules can also be produced with pre-assembled roof and facade cladding,
windows, doors, interior cladding, and electrical installations, etc. The work with the production
of elements and modules at the factory can ensure that insulation work and installation of the
vapor barrier in the elements can take place under good conditions, which ensures a correct
execution. This is an advantage concerning sustainable construction, as the complexity of
sustainable construction has become very large and can be expected to increase. Many therefore
ﬁnd it difﬁcult to navigate in this area, but by producing sustainable building modules off-site the
complexity can be reduced. Modular construction offers an integrated solution to the challenges,
as it provides an opportunity to integrate the required knowledge and create an incentive for

Methodology
The methodology of this project is based on a study by Brejnrod
et al., 2017 which developed a method for assessing the absolute
sustainability of buildings. Thus, this project will ﬁrst perform
a quantitative assessment of the environmental impacts of three
different building scenarios: a complete renovation scenario, con-

based on prefabricated volume modules. The impacts found in this

3.1
LCA methodological background
Life cycle assessment is a standardised method for assessing products’ resource consumption
and environmental impacts throughout their life cycle. The assessments in this thesis have been
performed in accordance with the European standard DS/EN 15978:2012, n.d. and DS/EN ISO
14040:2008, n.d. The main steps in the LCA are shown in Figure 3.1. The assessment represents
a repetitive analysis process. In each iteration, goals, and scope are updated as the inventory
analysis, which increases the accuracy of the available data and their representatives.
Figure 3.1: Illustration of the general phases of a life cycle assessment, as described by DS/EN ISO 14040:2008, n.d.
Although the method is standardised, some freedom of method and several other conditions
(e.g. data) that inﬂuence the results of an LCA are allowed. There are several manuals and
guides on LCA that describe how LCA can be performed. The most important to highlight is the
ILCD Handbook, developed by the Institute for Environment and Sustainability, the European
Commission Joint Research Center (JRC), in collaboration with the DG Environment (European
Commission, 2010). The ILCD Handbook consists of a series of technical documents that provide
detailed guidelines for all the steps required to complete an LCA. The purpose of the handbook is
to ensure the quality of life cycle data, methods, and assessments, and the analysis in this report
is performed based on this handbook.
As LCAbyg is the most commonly used LCA software in Danish construction companies, this
software has been chosen as a starting point in this project. The assessments are performed
in LCAbyg version 5 based on available quantity statements. A comprehensive overview of
quantities, materials, and lifetimes can be found in Appendix A.
3.1.1
Goal and Scope deﬁnition
This study aims to compare the environmental impacts of different building scenarios. Therefore,
this study is a comparative case study where the results will be used as guidance for the ongoing
design of a prefabricated building design with an optimised energy consumption developed in
course 11116 at DTU. Overall, the aims of the Life Cycle Assessment are to:
• Quantify the environmental impact from the prefabricated building design
• Compare quantiﬁed impacts with the impacts of a renovation and type house scenario
Based on these assessments, a further design of the prefabricated building design for environmen-
tal improvements of the house will be performed.
Functional unit
To ensure comparability between the different scenarios, the function, geometry, and size of the
buildings are the same for the three different scenarios.

Consideration period
The requirement for the calculation period of the calculations is currently 50 years in the VSS.
This requirement has been chosen due to international harmonisation, as 50 years is used in a
majority of European countries. However, the building’s consideration period is set at 120 years
in this report to consider longer-term investments. The service life of the building materials is
based on SBi instruction 2013:30, which contains a service life table for building materials (SBi,
2013:30), which is also the service life that is integrated into the calculation tool LCAbyg.
3.1.2
Life Cycle Inventory
The assessment will be made in LCAbyg, which is a tool for calculating life cycle assessments
of buildings, developed by the National Building Research Institute, SBi. Generic data from
Ökobaudat (ÖKOBAUDAT, 2021), which is speciﬁcally designed to support the construction
sectors, as well as product-speciﬁc environmental product declarations (EPDs) will be used for
the analysis.
System boundaries
The calculations include the life cycle phases shown in Figure 3.2. The included life cycle phases
are the same as recommended in EN15978.
Figure 3.2: Construction life cycle and the deﬁned system boundaries.
Thus, processes from production, the use phase, and the end of service life are included. Pro-
duction (A1-A3) includes extraction of raw materials, transport to the factory, and production of
building materials. In the use phase, the replacement of materials (B4) is included, which is based
on the materials’ expected lifetimes and the energy consumption during operation (B6). And at
the end of the service life, the waste treatment of the materials (C3 and C4) is calculated. These
steps include processing before recycling, incineration, and disposal of materials. The remaining
steps not highlighted in ﬁgure 3.2 are not included in the assessment.
It should be noted that according to the standard (DS/EN 15978:2012, n.d.), demolition of the
existing building is not included in the new construction scenario, and also that for renovation,
impacts from the existing building are not included.
Energy consumption
As energy consumption during operation has a relatively large impact on a building’s overall
potential environmental impacts, the assessment must be based on updated emission factors for
the Danish electricity and district heating.
The operating energy consumption is calculated based on data sets for projection of energy
production for the period 2020-2040, ie. the energy scenario which presupposes an increased
share of renewable energy for Denmark’s energy production until the year 2040 (COWI, 2020).

Characterisation methods and impact categories
To explain the potential environmental impacts as well as resource consumption, environmental
impact categories and indicator units are used for the calculations. LCAbyg performs LCAs
with the characterisation method CML. The planetary boundaries developed by A. Bjørn & M.Z.
Hauschild, 2015, which is used as normalised references in this project, has been prepared with
ILCD. For the two methods, there is a difference both in the way calculations are made, what is
included, and in the units that characterise the impact categories. This means that the results of
an LCA cannot be compared directly between the two methods, and conversion between them is
therefore needed.
CML was developed by the Institute of Environmental Sciences, Leiden University, and was ﬁrst
described in 1992 by Heijungs et al., and most recently in 2002 by Guinée et al. (Guinée et al.,
2002). In addition, it is possible to download an excel spreadsheet from Leiden University’s web-
site, which contains more than 1700 different characterisation factors (Department of Industrial
Ecology, 2016). CML is based on a problem-oriented approach, which means that quantitative
modeling is limited to using midpoint categories. Midpoint refers to the early stages of a chain
reaction for example CO2 emissions. Where endpoints refer to the later stages, i.e. what the
discharge will result in, such as the impact on human health.
The normalisation factors are calculated based on emissions from various substances and charac-
terisation factors. They are available both to the Netherlands alone, the EU, and the world. CML
is deﬁned for both baseline indicators and non-baseline indicators. Baseline contains 11 impact
categories, which are the ones used in LCAbyg, whereas non-baseline contains about 50 impact
categories (Acero, Rodríguez & Ciroth, 2016). The baseline impact categories can be found in

3.3
Estimation of Life Cycle Cost
In the total economic calculation, the building’s costs are estimated over the entire life cycle of the
building. In this report, this means that procurement costs, maintenance costs incl. replacement of
building parts and supply of electricity and heating are included. The total economic calculation
will also be made for 120 years and the result will be stated in DKK.
3.3.1
LCC tool and data
The analysis is carried out in the LCC tool LCCbyg developed by the Statens Byggeforskn-
ingsinstitut, SBi. The calculations with the tool provide a quick estimate of the expected costs
for carrying out a construction project and the subsequent operation thereof over the 120-year
consideration period. The calculations are based on generic key ﬁgures, which are not necessarily
accurate for the speciﬁc task. Costs are based on indicative estimates from Molio Price Data,
whereas costs used for the supply of electricity and heat are based on LCCByg’s standard costs
of DKK 1.77 per kWh electricity and DKK 0.52 per kWh district heating. Life expectancies
are processed from the lifetime table in the report SBi, 2013:30. The uncertainty of the results
depends on the calculation assumptions used and the data for the calculations. Changes to these
can have a big impact on the results. In this report, the results are used exclusively for comparison
between different scenarios, for which the assumptions are similar. The results must therefore be
interpreted with caution by comparing results found elsewhere.
The result of the life cycle costs is calculated in the present value. The present value is an
expression of how much money must be set aside today to be able to bear all future costs during
the calculation period with the chosen discount rate. The discount rate is the term for the interest
rate or conversion factor used in the calculation of the present value. The higher the interest rate,
the lower the present value today of the costs and revenues that lie in the future.

Assessment of impact potentials
In this study, the assessment of the prospective scenarios focuses exclusively on the scenarios’
global warming potential (GWP) measured in kg CO2 equivalents. Results expressed as CO2
equivalents are an expression of the fact that greenhouse gases such as methane and nitrous oxide
are also included and converted into a common unit: CO2 equivalents. Other environmental
impact potentials were not the focus of this part of the study, which is why they are not included
in this report. When making decisions, the other environmental impact potentials should also be
analysed and included.
Bio-based materials can absorb, store and release carbon throughout their lifetime. This carbon
is also referred to as biogenic carbon. As a result of LCAByg’s database, it is not possible to
separate the biogenic carbon and the carbon that relates to e.g. fossil fuels in bio-based materials.
According to the updated product standard (EN 15804: 2012 + A2: 2019), the stored biogenic
carbon should be reported separately, but as data are not yet available, this has not been possible
in this report.
3.4.2
The geometry of the house
The geometry of the house is in principle completely free in this step, but to achieve the lowest
possible material consumption, and at the same time avoid too many thermal bridges in the
construction, a square house is assumed. In addition, it is desired that the ﬁnal building design
should consist of prefabricated elements, to ensure a fast and cheap construction process. The
modules are transported on a block trolley, which places a limit on the size of the modules. In
Denmark, the limitation is 4.5 m wide, 15 m long, and 4.05 m high (Realdania, 2014). These
dimensions must therefore not be exceeded in the prospective scenario.
The living area2 for a sustainable building design is essential, as the key to a sustainable city is
to avoid constructing empty houses, and the sustainability of the house is thus highly reliable
on the users, norms, and thereby social sustainability. Therefore, reduction of the current living
area should be considered in the light of the current living area trends, where the average living
area is currently increasing. In 1970 the average total area in newly built detached houses was
149 m², which has increased to 205 m² in 2020 (Danmarks Statistik, 2021b). This corresponds
to the average living area per person was 57.3 m² which then has increased to around 78.8 m²
per person, with an average of 2.6 residents, which has not changed signiﬁcantly over the same
number of years.
However, it is estimated that the trend of minimising the amount of physical property and having
fewer things and simple, high-quality ﬂexible furniture will spread, while proximity to social
activities and cultural experiences will be weighted higher than a large living space in the near
future (Arkitema Architects, 2018). The use of indoor and outdoor public areas for living, work,
and social activities has become much more widespread over the past ten years and has made the
immediate surroundings an increasingly important part of the value of the home. The housing
quality thus consists of the home’s inherent qualities and to a greater extent of attractions in
the immediate surroundings. As an example, common facilities in the neighborhood and the
immediate area could provide the opportunity to meet and thus reduce the need to be able to have
many people over to social activities in the house.
To minimise the individual environmental footprint, it seems obvious to assess the framework
conditions for housing with a focus on examining the possibilities of establishing smaller family
homes with greater housing qualities. Applicable to all homes are requirements for indoor climate

3.4.3
Energy consumption during the use phase
In this step, it must be considered which energy solutions are most efﬁcient concerning the
consumption of heat and electricity for the building. As the focus of this project is not on energy
efﬁciency incl. heating and electrical installations, this step in this report will only include
an estimate of the prospective scenario’s energy consumption. It is chosen to use the same
installations as for the current building scenario, as it is estimated that these will be necessary for
the operation of the building.
For the estimation of the energy consumption of the building design the dynamic building
performance simulation software IDA Indoor Climate and Energy 4.9.9 (IDA ICE) will be used.
IDA ICE is a dynamic simulation program, developed by the Swedish company EQUA Simulation
AB. In connection with the simulation, a weather data ﬁle is used as input. In Denmark, there
are generally 3 valid weather data ﬁles: TRY, DRY, and DRY2013. This project is based on
DRY2013, which is the latest weather data based on data from the years 2001 to 2010. In the
DRY2013 ﬁle, there are measurements for parameters such as solar radiation, wind, temperatures,
and relative humidity, which together strengthen the reliability of the local weather conditions.
In the weather data, which is made by DMI, the country has been divided into different zones,
where each zone forms part of the country with the same climatology. When performing the
energy simulation, the building is assumed to be located on the DTU Campus in Lyngby.

4.3
Sensitivity analysis
LCA is a robust and well-established method for quantifying environmental impacts from a
system’s life cycle, e.g. a building. With a high-quality database, robust and valid results can be
obtained, but since life cycle analyses often try to quantify future environmental impacts, parts
of the analysis are based on assumptions and hence uncertainty in the results and conclusions
drawn from them. Therefore, it is important to test the most critical assumptions using sensitivity
analyses. Sensitive input variables are characterised by a relatively large inﬂuence, which can
thus lead to a greater uncertainty of the result. Sensitive input data can be related to the building’s
design, choice of materials, dimensions, and areas or factors such as the choice of environmental
data and lifetimes, which have an impact on the number of replacements during the consideration
period. This section, therefore, maps and assesses selected variable sensitivities in the life cycle
assessment.

environmental impacts from the demolition of existing construction in the new construction
scenario have not been included, and the existing construction is also not maintained and disposed
of in the renovation scenarios. This system boundary is extended in this section so that the
analyses include the demolition and replacement of the existing building parts. The new system

kWh emitted in 2050. The projection is based on the projection integrated into LCAbyg. The
projection method is moreover in accordance with the guidelines for life cycle assessments made
for DGNB certiﬁcations. Nevertheless, this assumption has a major impact on the buildings’ total
climate footprint over 120 years as illustrated in Figure 4.9.

construction scenarios, as the operating energy is higher in older buildings and the result will
therefore be affected relatively more by a static energy assumption. Likewise, an assumption
that the energy supply will turn climate-neutral faster than the projection assumes would lead to
greater favoritism of the renovation scenarios than this analysis shows. It is therefore pointed out
that the results and conclusions in this report are to that extent sensitive to assumptions for the
energy projection, however, this sensitivity analysis shows that the conclusions remain unchanged
about the prefabricated scenario that is the focal point in this report regardless of the assumption
about projection or not.

choices between several solutions, which can have different ﬁnancial consequences in the short
and long term, respectively. However, it can be difﬁcult to compare and assess the ﬁnancial
consequences of differences in, for example, service life and maintenance needs between different
scenarios. To support the assessment of the most advantageous scenario over the service life of
120 years an analysis of the different scenarios life cycle cost have been performed.
For the life cycle cost calculation, some costs have been excluded, as they are not assumed to
have a relevant inﬂuence on the conclusion. Inventory and equipment, as well as Management,

for the acquisition and maintenance of all usual building parts are included in all scenarios. For
Supply, costs for heat and electricity are included, as these are different for the three scenarios.
Finally, costs for cleaning are not included in the calculation, as these are assumed to be the same
for all scenarios.

4.5
Interpretation
The interpretation is the ﬁnal stage of the sustainability assessment. Here the results of the
previous sections will be discussed in light of the uncertainties of the applied data and the
assumptions that have been made and documented throughout this study. Finally, solutions to
achieve an absolute sustainable building design based on the prefabricated scenario will be listed.
The overall result of the comparative LCA has shown that the prefabricated scenario performs
best. The prefabricated scenario performs best in 3 out of the 5 midpoint impact categories
and does therefore seems to be preferable compared to the renovation and type house scenarios
keeping in mind the limitations of the study.
The highest environmental burdens were identiﬁed in the building component contribution
analysis. It was found that the concrete-based components (foundation and terrain deck), the
roof and the windows, especially the skylights, is the main driver of environmental impacts in
the prefabricated scenario. The global warming potential was further investigated, where the
production of the concrete-based elements as well as replacements of the building components
with a low service life was found to contribute signiﬁcantly highest.
The global warming impact category is particularly affected by substances such as CO, CO2, CH4,
and N2O, which act as greenhouse gases and exceeding the limits for these, thus causing global
warming. These emissions occur mainly during the production of the building components, and it
is thus this phase that one must particularly be aware of to reduce the global warming impacts.
By normalising the results with the available carrying capacity, global warming is seen as being
the biggest emission problem in the various scenarios, as the remaining impact categories stay
within the sustainability target. Based on this, the analysis shows that the most advantageous

It may seem simple to choose the materials and compositions with the lowest environmental
impact. However, materials are produced in different ways and are associated with different types
of environmental impacts that can be difﬁcult to compare. At the same time factors such as service
life, building site, the quantity of material, cost, and load-bearing capacity has to be considered,
which makes it more difﬁcult to determine whether solution A is better in environmental terms
than solution B. The following optimisation of the building design is based on both life cycle
assessments and life cycle cost. This provides a better basis for decision-making, as it makes
it easier to recognise and choose the most appropriate solutions in the long term based on
considerations of both climate and economy.

from the Ökobau database. This means that the zinc cladding roof has 3 times longer a lifetime
than the rooﬁng felt roof. The service life of rooﬁng materials also depends on environmental
factors, and different materials are recommended for use in different environments. Zinc is a
natural and living material and it is corrosion-resistant. Within a year after assembly, the material
will have a natural patination process. Patina is a protective and self-healing layer that is formed
on the surface of zinc by atmospheric exposure. This results in a low need for maintenance, as
well as a low environmental impact. In addition, zinc cladding is potentially 100% recyclable,
and very easy to disassemble, resulting in a large recycling potential. In Europe, 95% of old
zinc is recycled. By using recycled zinc, the production phase can achieve up to 96% less CO2
emissions.
In the comparative life cycle assessment, the tiled roof structure at the studied type house scenario
was found to have a lower impact on global warming than the ﬂat roof with rooﬁng felt. This
lower emission is a result of the three times the longer service life of the tiled roof, although
tiles are a heavy cladding material with an energy-heavy production. As an alternative to tile
cladding, ﬁber cement can be used, which has many of the same qualities, but is signiﬁcantly
lighter and has a less energy-intensive production. Fiber cement mainly consists of cement, silica
sand, cellulose ﬁbers and is used in coated form mainly in facade applications. The sheet is
made up of many thin, compressed layers, each of which is a reinforced plate. This construction
in combination with the waveform gives the plate strength and rigidity. There is therefore no
requirement for the substrate on ﬁber cement roofs, as the boards can keep completely tight. This
is also the reason why ﬁber cement sheets can be laid on roofs with a slope of up to 14 degrees.
Slate is a type of stone and has been used for facade cladding and rooﬁng for centuries. It is
a natural material with long service life and therefore a low need for maintenance and is in an
environmental perspective a better solution than a tiled roof due to the long service life. However,
as seen from Figure 5.2 this solution has the highest emission over the 120 years - except for the
current solution. This is probably due to the extraction and milling of the raw material. Moreover,
rainwater can be forced into the construction under the slate plates, and substrate material is
therefore needed for this type of rooﬁng, resulting in a very heavy roof construction with high
material consumption. The raw material for the slate plates is picked up from the ground in a
quarry, which is resource-intensive and leaves holes in the landscape, and as slate cannot be
mined in Denmark, the heavy material must be shipped, which also affects the environmental
impact negatively, and results in signiﬁcantly higher material cost.

The most frequently used insulation material in Denmark is mineral wool formed with melted
glass, stone, or slag (industrial waste) that is spun into a ﬁber-like structure. This has a very
insulating effect, but requires large amounts of energy to produce, as large parts of the production
take place under very high temperatures. There are two types of mineral wool products: glass
wool and stone wool, whereas the environmental data for the mineral wool used in this report
consists of 50% of each.
To investigate the two different insulation types separately, product-speciﬁc environmental data,
based on EPDs have been used. ROCKWOOL is the world’s largest producer of stone wool, and
data for the ROCKWOOL stone wool thermal insulation (ROCKWOOL International A/S, 2019),
for use in building applications, have been used in this assessment. It is made from volcanic
rock typically basalt or dolomite, an increasing proportion of recycled material in the form of
briquettes, and a low percentage of the resin binder. This product has the same thermal properties
as the current mineral wool insulation. The glass wool investigated has a slightly lower insulating
ability, but not of a signiﬁcant nature. The data of ISOVER Batts λ37 from Saint-Gobain Denmark
A/S (Saint-Gobain Denmark A/S, ISOVER, 2021) have been used in this investigation. This
product is formed of a high share of recycled glass cullets (more than 50% of external glass
cullets) and a small amount of natural and abundant raw materials.
In walls and roofs, it is crucial that all materials are good for transporting moisture, so that
moisture from the indoor climate can transfer through the materials. In recent years, several
insulation products have been developed which, in some assessments, are stated to be more
suitable than mineral wool, as they are good for handling moisture. Organic materials, such as
paper, ﬂax, and wood ﬁber, can absorb and release moisture - a property that mineral wool does
not have. This means that there is no need to incorporate a vapor barrier in the construction, as
the moisture can pass through the entire wall and roof construction without causing problems.
Moreover, the vapor barrier can make the construction vulnerable, like leaks in the vapor barrier
- along with thermal bridges - are the main causes of moisture damage and problems with rot
and mold in buildings. The basic material in paper wool is recycled newspapers and similar. In
other words, an extension of CO2 storage, and the product, therefore, has a positive impact on

Cellular glass is a porous glass foam material that is produced by foaming liquid glass, which
then solidiﬁes in foam form. Glass foam can be supplied both as solid plates and granules. It
is both pressure-resistant, light, a very stable substrate, capillary-breaking, water-resistant, and
is not attacked by fungus, which means that it can be used almost everywhere. However, a
relatively high amount of energy is spent on production, as it is made from a mixture of crushed
or granulated glass and a blowing agent, such as carbon or limestone, but in return, the material
can be recycled in new production.

for roof cladding are recycled. Here, too, it is ﬁrst the building that uses the recycled materials
that beneﬁts from the CO2 savings.

5.1.7
Partial conclusion
The roof is the highest contributor to global warming from the building construction. The
bituminous rooﬁng cladding is replaced with zinc sheets, which with an emission of 28.54 kg
CO2 eq./m2 reduces the impact from the roof construction by 36%.
For the concrete-based building components (foundation and deck), a higher CO2 emission is
seen in the production phases. This is because the current production methods of cement are
energy-intensive, and that CO2 is emitted in the chemical process itself in cement production.
All wood products are assumed to be incinerated at the time of disposal, which means that the
absorbed CO2 in the wood is released again. This also means that emissions can be postponed
by extending the life of the products either in the building in question or by recycling in a new
building. To reduce the CO2 emission as much as possible, the concrete deck solution is therefore
replaced with wooden beams spanning between the foundation. This solution also makes it
possible to replace the expanded polystyrene insulation, which accounted for the largest share of
the decks’ environmental impact, with low-density mineral wool insulation.
It was found that glass wool insulation from ISOVER had the lowest GWP impact over the
120-year consideration period. All insulation types in the wall and roof construction are therefore
replaced with glass wool insulation, which reduces the overall impact on the GWP from the
insulation material by 57%.
The skylights in the building design are removed and instead, a clerestory solution is implemented
in the building design, providing daylight for the center part of the building. This results in an
emission of 17.2 kg CO2 eq. per m2 for the windows and doors corresponding to a reduction of
28%, and a life cycle cost reduction of 46%.
By implementing all the assessed optimal solutions the CO2 emissions from the materials are
2.94 kg CO2 eq./m2/year, which is 60% lower than the median for single-family houses in the
SBi report Klimapåvirkning fra 60 bygninger, and a total GWP reduction from the basis scenario
of 28%. This reduction means that the building with the current energy consumption ends up
having a total global warming impact of 4.53 kg CO2 eq./m2/year, which is below the assumed
requirement in the VSS for a CO2 limit value in 2029. However, the emission from the building
construction is still far from the target of an absolute sustainable building design.
The implementation of fossil-free energy-based materials can reduce the emission of the building
by a further 1.86 kg CO2 eq./m2/year, resulting in a total of 155% of the allocated safe operating
space for the dwelling. And even with the assumption of a static energy supply from a fossil-free
energy system over the entire 120-year consideration period (step 6), the absolute sustainability
target is still exceeded with 0.64 kg CO2 eq./m2/year.

5.2
Reducing the living area
To achieve an absolute sustainable building design, a further reduction in GHG emissions is
needed. This can be achieved by reducing the living space per person by reducing the built-up
area or by increasing the number of residents per dwelling unit. In this report, the number of
residents is assumed to be 2.6, as this is the current average number of residents per dwelling in
Denmark, and the building itself is therefore reduced to achieve an absolute sustainable design.
With the assumption that the environmental impact is proportional to the gross area of the building,
a reduction of 63% of the living area gives a target of 82.8 m2 (gross area), corresponding to a
building size of 9.1 x 9.1 m. This corresponds to a reduction of 60% of the current average living
area per person, and even though the current trends seem to move towards smaller dwellings,
this reduction is considered culturally challenging and thereby unrealistic in connection with a
future implementation of the building as a solution for replacement of the current 70s dwellings.
Therefore, it is decided to take the future scenario with fossil-free energy for the material
production and the supply during the use phase into account when deciding on the reduction of
the building size.
When implementing the most advantageous building components in terms of CO2 eq. emission as
well as fossil-free based materials, the emission from the building parts ended up being reduced
by 73%. This means that the living area would have to be reduced by 58%, corresponding to a
building size of 9.8 x 9.8 m.
Moreover, if the energy supply is assumed to correspond to a static fossil-free energy supply, the
emission from the energy consumption is reduced by 55%. This means that with an assumption
of a fossil-free energy supply for the building and the production of building parts the living area
will have to be reduced by 37%, giving an area of 54.1 m2 per person. As the building is designed
to be able to accommodate 2.6 people, the gross area will be 140 m2, which is assumed durable
without cultural challenges.

5.3
Energy consumption during the use phase
As described in Context there has been a great development in the energy consumption for building
operation over the past 60 years. Generally, there has been a great focus on the construction of
energy-efﬁcient buildings, as it has been easy to relate to and make requirements for. However,
it can be more difﬁcult to get hold of sustainability, as it is a more complex size that requires a
broader approach to qualities in the construction. This section examines that the change in the
geometry of the dwelling does not constitute a signiﬁcant difference for the energy consumption
of the building during the use phase, which is assumed to have a nearly zero energy consumption.
About installations, it is important to think about both the amount of resources to materials, but
also the energy efﬁciency in the long term. The dwelling is constructed with an electrical domestic
hot water tank of 160 L, electrical radiators and ﬂoor heating, and an air handling unit (AHU)
without a heat pump. The installations are all electrical and not based on low-temperature heat
sources because the heat demand has been reduced so drastically with e.g. increased insulation,
lowered U-values, and the use of frameless windows. This is the most advantageous solution
for energy-efﬁcient construction, based on assessments of both energy consumption, the future
restructuring of the energy grid, implementation of energy sources, and the construction cost
(Report from 11116).
About ventilation, there are also some considerations to be made in connection with the environ-
mental impact and energy efﬁciency. The environmental impact from materials will be minimised
by the use of natural ventilation, but as natural ventilation creates a greater heating demand, there
will be a need for more energy to be used on heat than if mechanical ventilation had been installed.
This is because heat recovery can be utilised by mechanical ventilation. And thereby you can
minimise the emissions from the operating energy, but in return, the environmental impact from
the materials will be higher. As the building in this project consists of a highly insulated thermal
envelope with low energy demand, it is constructed with natural ventilation in all rooms in the
form of venting hatches. The ventilation system has a total airﬂow of 60 L/s corresponding to the
minimum exhaust airﬂow requirements in the building regulations.

7.1
Use of absolute sustainability limits
Based on the analysis, it can be concluded that with the current technologies and possibilities,
it is difﬁcult to build an absolute sustainable single-family house. Even though the operating
energy consumption of the building is optimised (result from course 11116) and signiﬁcantly
lower than the current requirements in the building regulations, as well as constructed from
materials emitting less GHGs than for the building constructed with the common practice (type
house scenario), the analysis shows that the building is still far from the absolute sustainability
target. From this, it can be seen how great a need there is for both materials, building components,
geometries and energy to be rethought in alternative solutions to achieve absolute sustainability
for single-family houses.
7.1.1
The allocation method and global perspective
As the absolute sustainability limits are allocated based on statistics on the share of GDP
represented by total household expenditure and the classiﬁcation of individual consumption, it
is assumed that the consumption pattern will remain the same as that seen today. The share
of GDP, which constitutes the total expenditure for a household, is calculated based on the
total expenditure from GDP in Denmark, and the classiﬁcation of individual consumption is
determined on the basis that consumption is as it is today. Consumption will be very different
from household to household and will depend on parameters such as wealth, culture, and location.
Moreover, this project will look very different globally, depending on which country it is de-
veloped for or whether it is made so that it is internationally comprehensive. Worldwide, very
different economic, cultural, and climatic situations are seen, and the needs will vary. One can
i.a. imagine that the need for living space will be different, which may make it more difﬁcult or
easier to introduce a smaller living area per. person.
However, there will be additional other needs and norms in relation to building materials globally,
and especially the amount of insulation materials will be different in countries with a different
climate, which will be able to reduce emissions. On the other hand, in many cultures, you
will see a large use of cooling/heating system, which in operation will use more energy. As
previously mentioned, there is a big difference in the environmental impact of using different
energy sources, and the sources for energy production can vary greatly from country to country.
For example, Norway’s energy supply is already fossil-free and consists of 91.5% hydropower
(Statistics Norway, 2021). All this will mean that a project like this will give very different results,
depending on which country it is carried out for.
If there was interest in introducing absolute limits on environmental impacts globally, it was
conceivable that consumption patterns would generally be regulated, and perhaps become more
evenly distributed. If the consumption pattern were regulated in such a way that some consumption
items are reduced or completely removed, it is conceivable that there would be a greater safe
operation space for the dwellings than is seen in this project. In addition, the used normalisation
references for the absolute sustainability limit used in this thesis follow an "egalitarian" orientation.
This means that the size of the safe operating space is distributed with the same amount of space
for everyone. The normalised references were expressed as the carrying capacity of a reference
region divided by its population and thus describes the annual personal share of the carrying
capacity (A. Bjørn & M.Z. Hauschild, 2015). This results in the European reference limit
generally being lower than the global reference limit, mainly due to a relatively high population
density in Europe. This way of distributing the carrying capacity of the globe creates certain
challenges for compliance with the limit in Denmark and other industrialised countries with

countries with an existing emission below the limit with a possibility to start emitting more.

7.2
Social sustainability and terraced housing
Houses are built to meet some social needs, comfort needs, and partly also to meet requirements
and needs in the environment around the construction. In other words, there is a great focus on
social sustainability in the construction industry. These can be aspects such as indoor climate, light
incidence, and social spaces. What is rarely taken into account is environmental sustainability.
This is however expected to change shortly as a result of the new agreement on sustainable
construction (The Danish Government, 2021).
Social sustainability involves a lot of cultural elements. The social culture concerning housing is
very much centered around that it should be large and presentable for guests, and there is usually
also a "fashion trend" to follow with housing types, decor, etc. All this is something that can mean
replacement and renovation based on trends and lifestyle, more than that the items in question no
longer work. In addition, there is also, as previously mentioned, a trend for increasing living space
per. person in Denmark. This creates some challenges in minimising the living area per resident,
despite the current trend of small living, as previously discussed in Section 5.2. A way to address
the issue of a smaller living area per person could be by building terraced houses rather than
single-family houses. This could reduce the amount of the building’s thermal envelope, which
are some of the building components with the highest impact on global warming. In addition to
the emissions from the building components, this will also reduce the energy consumption of the
dwellings further, due to the thermal envelope/living area ratio, and thus minimise cold bridges in
the building design. This has not been investigated further, as this change will change the social
qualities of the dwelling and thus the functional unit used in the assessment. However, assuming a
reduction of between 5-10% of the emissions from the building parts by merging three dwellings
into terraced houses, the dwellings will constitute 147-155% of the safe operating space without
including the emission from the energy consumption, and 229-237% with emissions from energy
consumption. By including the assumption on a fossil-free supply in both the production and use
phases, this is reduced to 92-95% compared to the previous 98%.
There is thus a potential for further emission reductions in the construction of terraced houses
as a replacement for the detached single-family houses by an aggregation of the houses and the
inclusion of the associated reduction in energy consumption. In this way, there is a possibility to
have a slightly higher living area per person.

7.3
Sustainability in relation to legislation
The new National Agreement on Sustainable Construction, which sets climate requirements for
new construction from 2023 and introduces mandatory calculation of the environmental impact of
all new construction, will be a crucial factor for Denmark to achieve the climate goal in 2030 and
at the same time progress at a pace where the industry has time to adapt, provide opportunities
for innovation and export of sustainable solutions. Without laws and regulations, the industry
will continue to avoid the expense and hassle of life cycle assessments, and continue with the
burdensome standards. However, based on this study, it seems that that the CO2 requirements for
new buildings, etc. have to be phased in faster than by 2023 and tightened to achieve the goal of
climate neutrality in 2050.
Moreover, it must be ensured that the requirements promote the use of wood and other climate-
friendly building materials and solutions and ensure that the construction sites become fossil-free
as soon as possible. As described in Section 6.2.1, reaching a CO2-neutral state requires a
potential for continuous CO2 reduction, and cellulosic building material is a way to promote this.
However, the construction industry tends to be very conservative in its approach and prefers to use
what it is familiar with. Bio-derived materials are often perceived as perishable, ﬂammable, and
with short service life. However, these perceptions are not supported by facts. The government
should recognise this alternative storage of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere and provide an
economic beneﬁt to the guardians of it. It is a way of storing atmospheric carbon dioxide that can
be easily achieved and without economic sanctions (as opposed to carbon capture and storage).
This is something that can be done for the beneﬁt of society and the environment. Anthropogenic
carbon emissions will undoubtedly change the climate, and these will have enormous economic
consequences.
In addition, there is a lack of focus on renovation in the new agreement. The new agreement
focuses primarily on new construction, which already has relatively low emissions compared
to the requirements. As mentioned, the construction sector is one of the biggest culprits when
it comes to CO2 emissions. Therefore, the political agreement should focus more on how to
make the already built houses more sustainable. It should be taken into account overall that a
considerable amount of energy and CO2 has already been spent on the construction of an existing
property and that more should be done from a political point of view to ensure an agreement on
the renovation of existing buildings so that they become more energy optimal.
As explained in IDA’s climate response (Ingeniørforeningen IDA, 2021), it is economically
advantageous to reduce heat consumption in connection with the renovation of existing homes.
The best and cheapest way to implement these savings ongoing is by transforming the existing
energy-consuming building stock into low-energy buildings.
The energy renovation is crucial not only to reduce the heat demand but also to prepare the
buildings for low-temperature district heating and thus the transition to the new supply system.
The new supply system reduces net losses in the district heating lines due to lower temperatures,
but even more importantly, this measure makes the district heating supply signiﬁcantly more
efﬁcient. Lower temperatures increase the efﬁciency of the future renewable energy sources
such as solar, heat pumps, geothermal and surplus heat from industry and data centers. In this
connection, it is therefore crucial that the energy renovation of the dwellings focuses on both
reducing the heat demand and lowering the requirement for what temperature the district heating
water or the heat pump must supply.

Chapter 7. Discussion
7.4
Further Studies & Perspectives
The renovation scenario used in this report is a complete renovation with the preservation of
relatively few load-bearing building parts (foundation, terrain deck, and the load-bearing part
of the exterior wall). This form of renovation has been chosen as this is the scenario for the
majority of the buildings we see today due to the alarming conditions of the current 70s dwellings.
However, it could be interesting to investigate whether more building parts could be retained from
the original construction, to reduce any unnecessary emissions from the production of materials
as well as the procurement costs. This could be done by examining the emissions from the
production of new materials versus the emissions from the energy reduction corresponding to
change.
In this project, the type of housing observed has been narrowed down to exclusively deal with
single-family houses from the 60s and 70s. To be able to carry out a more accurate implementation
plan for the renovation of homes throughout Denmark, it will require that other housing types
are also included. An interesting further study could therefore be the inclusion of all types of
housing used in Denmark so that the nationwide reduction for the construction sector would be
more comprehensive. Moreover, it is worth remembering that we will have to look at the global
perspective. Solutions for Denmark are a good and important step along the way, but in the big
picture, it is not what solves the climate problems.

Chapter 8. Conclusion
This thesis aimed to develop a new sustainable building design of prefabricated modules. Making
it possible to increase the level of sustainability in the built environment by replacing the existing
detached houses from the 60s and 70s with houses based on prefabricated modules.
The results of this thesis show that it is much easier to achieve low energy consumption by
constructing new buildings, rather than renovating an existing building. As the analysis in
Chapter 4 shows, it can also be an economic advantage to construct new houses based on wooden
prefabricated volume modules. There is thus a potential for the construction sector to contribute
to the sustainable transformation by demolishing the current energy-consuming housing stock
and constructing new prefabricated module-based buildings. This can be concluded based on the
comparative life cycle analysis that examined whether it is environmentally sound to build new
prefabricated modular buildings as a replacement for the existing dwellings constructed in the 60s
and 70s compared to typical renovation solutions. The environmental impacts and construction
costs were compared, and the level of sustainability was determined by relating the environmental
footprint to the bearing capacity of the globe. The main conclusions of this thesis are summarised
as follows.
From the comparative life cycle assessment in Chapter 4:
• By comparison with a renovation and type house scenario, the construction of new prefab-
ricated module-based buildings as a replacement for existing detached houses from the 60s
and 70s has the lowest environmental impact over 120 years.
• The wood-based prefabricated building is economically advantageous compared to conven-
tional construction types over the 120 years.
From the prospective scenario development in Chapter 5:
• By implementing environmentally sound building components in the design of the prefab-
ricated building from course 11116 it was possible to reduce the GHG emission from the
building parts to be 60% lower than the median for single-family houses in the SBi report
Klimapåvirkning fra 60 bygninger.
• With the current technologies and opportunities, it is very difﬁcult to build an absolute
sustainable single-family house. However, by assuming a fossil-free production of mate-
rials and energy supply it is possible to stay just below the absolute sustainability limits
developed by A. Bjørn & M.Z. Hauschild, 2015.
From the impacts, mitigation, and adaptation assessment in Chapter 6:
• For the implementation of the prefabricated buildings, the technical adaptation to the cli-
mate objectives has to be taken into consideration in a socio-economically sound way. This
is made possible by constructing new low-energy buildings with a low-cost construction
phase by off-site produced building components.
• To reach the political targets of a 70% CO2 reduction by 2030, and globally being CO2
neutral by 2050, national-based sustainability targets for reaching a zero-emission instead
of the more ethical egalitarian-oriented distribution are preferable. Reaching the absolute
sustainability target will require a replacement of all the energy-consuming dwellings within
10-11 years. These signiﬁcant energy savings in buildings and industrial processes is found
to be impossible in such a short time and will lead to the extra cost of the transformation.

Chapter 8. Conclusion
80
• In the short term, from 2021 to 2030, a total of 4.03 million tonnes of CO2 eq. calculated
as the accumulated net climate impact can be saved. This corresponds to a replacement of
6% (26,854 buildings) of the building types of detached single-family houses built in the
years 1960-1979 with the new prefabricated buildings, every year until 2030. This means
that by 2045, all the energy-consuming dwellings will have been replaced with the more
sustainable prefabricated dwellings, and this housing segment will then be able to become
CO2 neutral by reorganising the energy system.
• CO2 reductions in the form of biochar and potential for Carbon Capture and Utilisation
have to be taken into consideration to become climate-neutral by 2050. This results in the
large potential for the construction of prefabricated houses based on wooden structures.
It can thus be concluded that a sustainable sound solution for managing the older and energy-
consuming housing stock in Denmark will be to tear down the houses and replace them with
sustainable prefabricated homes. Based on the conclusions, the result of this report will be able
to inspire the actors who set the direction and framework conditions for construction in Denmark
and thus promote the sustainable transition of the construction sector.
