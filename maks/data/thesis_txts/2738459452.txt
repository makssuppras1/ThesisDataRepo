Abstract 
This study proposes employing Bayesian optimization to design spatially and temporally variable blank holding 
forces for a forming process of aluminum pouch films. The material is used for packaging of a pouch-type lithium-
ion battery cell. To meet the growing demand for higher capacity of a cell, applying variable blank holding forces 
has been tried to achieve deeper forming depth beyond the current limit. A proper variable blank holding forces 
can be found some trial and error by experiments or Finite Element (FE) simulations. However, the high cost of 
experiments and FE simulation make it challenging to find a solution. In this scenario, Bayesian optimization 
emerges as a viable strategy because it is a specialized optimization algorithm to find a design point which has 
the maximum performance while minimizing the number of experiments or simulations. In this study, variable 
blank holding force is determined by using Bayesian optimization for achieving the target depth in pouch forming. 
 
Keywords Bayesian optimization, Variable blank holding forces, Pouch forming, Stamping process

Chapter 1.
Introduction
Stamping processes play a pivotal role in the manufacturing of various components, and factors
influencing their formability have garnered considerable attention. Lubrication, blank shape, and blank
holding forces are recognized as crucial elements affecting the success of stamping operations [1–3]. While
the uniform application of blank holding forces across the entire blank has been a traditional approach,
the consideration for varying forces in time [4], position, or both [5, 6] has been a consistent focus of
research endeavors to enhance the formability.
Numerous studies have delved into the optimization of variable blank holding forces.
However,
practical applicability and efficiency have been significant challenges, especially when Finite Element
(FE) simulations consume substantial time.
Some proposed solutions are idealized and not readily
applicable in real-world forming machines. This paper aims to address these challenges by presenting a
pragmatic approach to optimizing blank holding forces in the industry.
The specific problem addressed in this study is on the formability of a distinctive material known
as aluminum pouch film. Developed for packaging lithium-ion battery cells used in electric vehicles,
pouch film has become widely utilized in the battery packaging industry. This material has a layered
structure, comprising polypropylene, aluminum, PET, and nylon, with a thickness ranging from 150
to 180 micrometers. To encase a battery cell, the film undergoes a stamping process, resulting in two
rectangular cups. Industries strive to deepen the stamping to increase the volume of a single cell, but
encounter limitations with uniform and constant blank holding forces.
Alternative methods to enhance formability, such as employing lubrication between the blank and
tools or optimizing the blank shape to regulate material inflow, exist. However, their application is
restricted due to constraints imposed by subsequent manufacturing processes of packaging battery cells.
Furthermore the asymmetric punch shape and the anisotropic properties of the pouch film have been
identified as obstacles to improving formability. This has prompted a demand for varying forces in both
time and position, paving the way for the exploration of enhanced formability in pouch film stamping
processes.
To apply different forces to various positions on the blank, a specialized forming machine has been

Chapter 2.
Background
2.1
Formability in Stamping Processes
Formability in the stamping process refers to the material’s ability to deform and take on the desired
shape without defects or failures. It is a crucial aspect of stamping or deep drawing, where a flat sheet
is transformed into a three-dimensional part through various processes such as bending, drawing, and
stretching.
Wrinkles and fractures are two common defects that can occur during the stamping process and
are inversely related to each other. Wrinkles happen when the material undergoes excessive compressive
force. They are characterized by undesired folds or buckling in the blank. Wrinkling is a typical result
of insufficient blank holding force.
On the other hand, fractures occur when the material fails due to excessive tensile stress, especially
in areas with severe deformation. Fractures can take the form of cracks or splits in the material. High
blank holding forces, improper lubrication, or material defects can contribute to fractures during the
stamping process.
Factors that can affect the formability of the stamping process include [1–3]:
• Shape of the blank: when the blank is fed with the punch, the amount of material flow varies
depending on the shape of the punch. Adequate material flow is needed in regions requiring it to
prevent excessive stretching, while fewer material is needed in regions where excessive stretching
may cause wrinkles. Therefore, designing an appropriate blank shape contributes to enhancing
formability.
• Lubrication: friction between the blank and punch, or between the blank and die, significantly
affects formability. Localized high friction can impede material flow, leading to excessive stretching
around the affected area. Lubricating the interface between the blank and tool facilitates proper
material flow, thereby improving formability.
• Draw bead: draw bead can be employed in cases where material flow needs to be restricted. As
the blank passes through the draw bead, bending and localized friction occur, hindering material
3
==PAGE:10==
flow.
• Blank holding force: blank holding force plays a crucial role in pressing the blank and significantly
influences formability. Adequate force is required to prevent excessive compression on the plane,
thereby suppressing wrinkles. However, excessive blank holding force in regions requiring material
flow may prevent proper material flow, leading to excessive tension and potential fracture. If the
punch shape is axisymmetric and material anisotropy is low, applying uniform blank holding force
across the entire blank may suffice. However, in other cases, research on varying blank holding
force according to the blank’s position has been extensively conducted.
A common theme identified in previous studies is the significance of maintaining minimum blank
holding forces to prevent wrinkles, as fractures tend to occur with higher blank holding forces [6].
Striking a balance between preventing wrinkles while avoiding fractures is crucial for successful
stamping or deep drawing.

2.3
Bayesian Optimization
Bayesian optimization (BO) is a robust optimization technique widely applied across diverse do-
mains, including engineering, machine learning, and complex manufacturing processes [14]. Particularly
relevant in scenarios where the objective function is computationally expensive, such as FE simulations,
Bayesian optimization offers an effective methodology to maximize or minimize the objective function.
At its core, Bayesian optimization employs a probabilistic surrogate model, often a Gaussian Process
(GP), to approximate the true objective function. The Gaussian Process serves as a statistical model that
6
==PAGE:13==
not only captures the underlying trend of the objective function but also provides uncertainty estimates
at each point in the solution space.
Figure 2.2: A flowchart of Bayesian optimization process
Gaussian Processes are advantageous in Bayesian optimization due to their flexibility and ability
to model complex, non-linear relationships. A Gaussian Process essentially represents a distribution
over functions, allowing it to adapt to varying complexities and uncertainties in the objective function.
As Bayesian optimization proceeds iteratively, the GP is updated with new data points, refining its
understanding of the objective function and guiding the search towards promising regions. This iter-
ative process of updating the surrogate model, combined with the acquisition function that balances
exploration and exploitation, enables Bayesian optimization to efficiently converge towards the optimal
solution, even in scenarios with limited evaluations of the expensive objective function.
The overall explanation about Bayesian optimization in this section is referred from a book written
by Garnett [15]. Especially, the sections detailing Gaussian processes and Gaussian process regression are
based on the foundational work by Rasmussen [16]. In addition, the discussion on acquisition functions
draws extensively from the articles by Frazier, Wu, Poloczek and Balandat [17–20].

2.3.5
Decision Policies and Acquisition Functions
The optimization process involves making several decisions, such as where to gather data and when
to stop the optimization. These decisions are difficult because of incomplete and changing information
about the objective function.
Gaussian process regression offers a way to understand the unknown
objective function by updating our knowledge with new data.
This approach enables us to forecast
potential observations using the posterior predictive distribution.
To apply these beliefs in decision-making, Bayesian decision theory can be employed. This approach
connects Gaussian process regression with decision-making under uncertainty in the optimization context.
12
==PAGE:19==
Beginning with an initial dataset, new observations are collected iteratively according to the optimization
policy. This policy leverages existing data to determine the next observation point.
An effective method to create an optimization policy is to start by defining an intermediate compo-
nent called an acquisition function. This function evaluates each potential observation point, assigning
a score based on its potential contribution to the optimization process. By choosing the point with the
highest score from the acquisition function, an optimization policy can be developed. This technique
is commonly used in Bayesian optimization, where the term ’acquisition function’ is often synonymous
with ’policy’, with the underlying goal of maximizing the acquisition function.
In a mathematical expression, an acquisition function, α : X →R where X is a design space, maps
a score to every design point in the design space X based on the preferences regarding where the next
observation should be made, taking into account the data already observed. To explicitly account for
this dependency on existing data, the notation α (x; D), where x ∈X and D is a set of observed data,
will be used for acquisition functions, meaning that the observed data act as parameters influencing the
preferences.
Once the acquisition function is defined, the next investigation design point can be determined by
finding:

It might appear inefficient that solving another maximization problem to address the objective
maximization problem. However, commonly used acquisition functions possess characteristics that make
their optimization significantly more manageable compared to the original problem in hand. Acquisition
functions are typically inexpensive to evaluate and analytically differentiable, enabling the utilization of
readily available optimizers when computing the policy. In contrast, the objective function is presumed
to be costly to evaluate, with its gradient often unavailable. Hence, the approach allows us to transform
a challenging and expensive problem into a sequence of simpler, more affordable problems.

Unlike Expected Improvement, which considers only the posterior at the sampled point, the Knowl-
edge Gradient acquisition function evaluates the posterior over the entire domain and how the sample
will alter that posterior. KG assigns value to measurements that enhance the maximum of the posterior
mean, even if the sampled point’s value is not an improvement over the previous best. This can provide
minor performance benefits in standard Bayesian optimization with noise-free evaluations and significant
improvements in scenarios involving noise, multi-fidelity observations, derivative observations [17]. In
such cases, the benefit of sampling lies not in finding a better value at the sampled point but in improving
the maximum of the posterior mean across all feasible solutions. For instance, a derivative observation
might indicate that the function is increasing near the sampled point, suggesting a higher posterior
mean maximum than previously observed. When these effects are first-order, KG often outperforms EI
significantly [18,19].

Material Characterization of the Pouch Film
Figure 3.1: Cross-section of the pouch film
To simulate the forming process of the pouch film, the plastic properties of the pouch film must first
be modeled. As shown in Figure 3.1, the film is a composite material consisting of four different layers,
PET, nylon, aluminum and polypropylene (PP).
Following the work done by Jang [23], properties of layered materials can be modeled in two ap-
proaches: with and without the homogenization assumption. For the former case, the cross-section is
assumed to be a single homogeneous layer. Thus, the equivalent properties was obtained across the
cross-section (1-layer model). On the other hand, without the homogenization assumption, properties of
all layers was obtained considering force equilibrium between two layers (4-layer model).

4.1
Problem Circumstances
Figure 4.1: Pouch Film after Forming and the Structure of the Forming Machine
From Figure 4.1, it can be seen that the pouch film after forming has two rectangular cups. Also, 1/4

Chapter 7.
Conclusion
This paper proposes a methodology for optimizing conditions of a process incurring significant time
costs in FE simulations. It specifically describes this methodology by applying it to a real-world problem,
the pouch forming process.
As the optimization solution, the blank holding force combination which forces almost all elements
in the target area set on the forming limit diagram. Unfortunately, the solution has not been verified by
performing the actual forming test. However, it can be said that the solution has a potential to make
successful forming because another forming test, by the author, with BHF which has higher objective
value than the optimized one led to successful actual forming.
As a future work, the actual forming test with the optimized BHF should be done to verify it. In
addition, the optimized blank holding forces in this study will not be the unique solution. There would
be several solutions which makes the forming process successful. By studying the physical meaning of the
current solution and giving a more proper initial dataset to the optimization process, the optimization
process would be more efficient and find better solutions.
