Abstract
In order to investigate the diﬀerent ﬁre properties that aﬀect the intumescent coatings
performance and their behaviour under diﬀerent ﬁre conditions, three sets of experi-
ments representing diﬀerent types of heating exposure were conducted for two diﬀerent
intumescent coating - the water-based PaintA and the solvent-based PaintB. In the
ﬁrst set of experiments, in an electric oven, steel samples were exposed to several non-
standard ﬁre curves with heating rates lower than the ISO834 ﬁre curve. The steel
specimens were IPE400 steel proﬁle sections protected by 1.00 mm dry ﬁlm thickness
(DFT) of intumescent coating. The same samples were used also in the second set of
experiments, where the steel specimens were exposed to the ISO834 cellulosic standard
ﬁre curve in a gas furnace. In the third set of experiments, steel plate samples pro-
tected by 3 diﬀerent intumescent paint DFTs (0.50, 1.00, 1.50 mm) were tested in a
mass loss cone. The steel specimens were exposed to diﬀerent heat ﬂuxes, which pro-
vided temperature-time curves with heating rates similar to the ISO834 standard ﬁre
curve.
In all the sets of experiments, the intumescent coating behaviour was assessed by
means a qualitative analysis based on visual inspection: colour, compactness, cracks,
expansion, density, ﬂammability and smoke production.
Moreover, the intumescent
coating performance was quantitatively assessed according to two diﬀerent parameters:
the thermal resistance based on the Eurocode formula for insulated steel sections and an
eﬃciency parameter, deﬁned as the diﬀerence between the absolute temperature of the
unprotected and protected steel divided by the absolute temperature of the unprotected
steel.
The results underlined that many intumescent coatings characteristics, such as the
paint expansion and the smoke production, are strongly dependent on the ﬁre proper-
ties. However, other aspects like the paint activation temperature are intrinsic charac-
teristics and they are independent of the ﬁre conditions.
Nevertheless, many diﬀerences were highlighted on the behaviour of the two diﬀerent
intumescent coatings under various ﬁre exposures. The thermal resistance developments
had a similar trend, but their performances were strongly dependent on the heating rate.
In particular, it was found that PaintA works better in terms of thermal resistance
than PaintB. Moreover, the performance of the solvent-based PaintA improved with
an increasing heating rate, while the performance of the water-based PaintB decreased
with increasing heating rate. Similarly, diﬀerent eﬃciencies of the two paints were also
registered: this discrepancy was more pronounced at low heat ﬂuxes and for thinner
paint coatings. Furthermore, the intumescent paint eﬃciency improved with higher dry
ﬁlm thicknesses, but the intumescent coatings were not very sensible to their applied
thicknesses and the performances with diﬀerent DFTs were comparable. In addition, it
==PAGE:8==
was found that also the nature of the ﬁre condition inﬂuenced the intumescent coatings
behaviour with similar temperatures and heating rates.
The study conﬁrmed that the current procedure for the design of intumescent coat-
ings has certain limitations, as diﬀerent paints have diﬀerent performances according
to their compositions and their performance is also a function of the ﬁre scenarios. In
particular, the water-based paint had better performances at high heating rates, while
the solvent-based paint had better performances at slow heating rates.
Therefore, the intumescent coatings design should be assessed according to a wider
range of ﬁre scenarios with diﬀerent properties, e.g. heating rate. In fact, in the case
of solvent-based paints, the design according to the standard ﬁre exposure generates to
an overestimation of the intumescent coating performance for slow ﬁres. This leads to
a mistaken design on the unsafe side.

Introduction
The discipline of ﬁre protection engineering can be deﬁned as the application of science
and engineering principles to protect people and their environment from destructive
ﬁres. The history of ﬁre protection engineering is based on a series of catastrophic
conﬂagrations.
The ﬁrst recorded example can be found in the huge ﬁre in 64 AD which devastated
Rome (Fig. 1.1). Emperor Nero, in response to a catastrophic ﬁre, ordered to rebuild
the city implementing passive ﬁre protection techniques, such as ﬁreproof materials for
external walls. After the collapse of the Roman Empire and the onset of the Middle
Age, a new technical approach for ﬁre protection emerged after the Great London Fire
of 1666 (Fig. 1.1). The English city adopted its ﬁrst building regulations requiring
ﬁre-resisting wall separations and design ﬁre-suppression equipment [1][2].
Figure 1.1:
Two of the greatest destructive ﬁres in history: on the left "The Fire
of Rome", Hubert Robert (ca. 1770-1790); on the right "The Great Fire of London",
unknown artist
During the Industrial Revolution, the introduction of huge factories and material
storage practices resulted in a greater ﬁre risks and a number of disastrous building ﬁres
occurred during this period. Once again, this pointed out the need for structural ﬁre
solutions and the necessity of new ﬁre-ﬁghting measurements, manual up to that time.
In this period, the installation of manually operated perforated pipes at the ceiling
represents the ﬁrst ﬁxed ﬁre-suppression system in the history. Also, the number of
conﬂagrations dropped as conventional timber structures were generally replaced by
masonry [1][2].
During the 19th century, many organisations and insurance companies were estab-
lished to reduce the loss of life and property from destructive ﬁre.
Fire protection
engineering became a profession and principles of science and engineering were applied
==PAGE:24==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
to develop research aimed to ﬁnd new structural ﬁre solution and ﬁre-extinguishing
systems. Anyway, many of the advances in ﬁre protection were brought about as a
reason to catastrophic ﬁres with consequent loss of life and property [3].
During the 20th century, building and ﬁre codes and standards became primary
means of applying ﬁre protection engineering for life safety and property protection.
Lessons learned from catastrophic ﬁres, especially in new tall steel-framed buildings,
were applied to revise codes and standards and improve the ﬁre regulations [4].
Subsequently, a number of new ﬁre protection systems were developed for use by
the engineers: new ﬁre-suppression, smoke control and insulation systems were created,
also thanks to a new research approach, mainly based on real ﬁre experiments and
full-scale ﬁre testing [5].
Finally, the computational power of modern computers has resulted in computa-
tional methods for determining the quantitative evaluation of ﬁre protection and ﬁre
resistance. The performed-based design is currently used primarily for unique structures
that cannot be adequately protected utilising the existing prescriptive building and ﬁre
codes, or to determine engineering alternatives to prescriptive code requirements [2].
Still the fear of uncontrolled ﬁres and the desire to avoid their consequences have
remained as a primary human reaction and as an important human objective: ﬁres
cause indirect and consequential losses arising from loss of production, of proﬁts, of
employment and of exports and thus destroy a signiﬁcant percentage of the economic
wealth of a country. Because the consequences of a ﬁre are disastrous and the risk is
always high, ﬁre protection measures have to be adopted. Fire protection involves the
study of behaviour, suppression and investigation of ﬁre and related emergencies. It
also includes research and development, production, testing and application of suitable
ﬁre protection systems [6]. The primary goal of ﬁre protection is to limit the levels of
casualties and injuries and of property losses in a ﬁre event [7].
1.1
Fire protection systems
Fire protection engineering is usually divided into two big groups: active and passive
ﬁre protection systems. This division is based on how the systems work in order to
contrast the ﬁre and guarantee a good level of safety.
Active ﬁre protection systems involve automatic devices and human direct actions
to control and extinguish the ﬁre [8]. These systems are divided into two categories
according to their purpose: ﬁre control and ﬁre suppression. Striking examples of such
active systems are automatic sprinkler systems and ﬁre ventilation systems.
On the other hand, passive ﬁre protection systems are built as a part of the whole
building. They do not require operation by people or automatic controls, but they
can be considered as an on-site ﬁre resistance measure to prolong the longevity of
load-bearing structures. The primary reason is life safety and this goal is mainly ac-
complished by maintaining structural integrity during the ﬁre and limiting the spread
of ﬁre and its damaging eﬀects [8]. Passive ﬁre protection systems are usually divided
into two types, non-reactive, of which the most common types are boards and sprays,
and reactive, of which thin ﬁlm intumescent coatings are the most common example.
In turn, thin intumescent coatings can be either divided into on-site or oﬀ-site applied
paints.

1.1.1
Passive ﬁre protection materials
The consequences of a ﬁre are usually disastrous and the risk is always high for this kind
of dangerous events. The risk is equal to the product of two variables, probability and
consequences. The division between active and passive ﬁre protection is also related to
the inﬂuence that these systems have on the frequency and the consequences of a ﬁre
event. The probability of occurrence of a ﬁre is already low and diﬃcult to decrease
further. As a consequence, it is fundamental to limit the consequences. Passive ﬁre
protection systems have a key role on the limitation of human and material losses. For
example, structural ﬁre protection materials are applied to avoid catastrophic conse-
quences, such as the building collapse during a conﬂagration.
The application of insulation materials is one of the most common measures to
protect the structure from direct ﬁre exposure. Traditionally the most adopted way of
achieving a good level of structural ﬁre safety is to cover or wrap the steel elements
with conventional ﬁre protection products. The most common passive ﬁre insulation
materials (boards, sprays, ﬂexible blankets and concrete encasement) are shown in Fig.
1.2.
Figure 1.2: Common passive ﬁre protection materials: boards, sprays, ﬂexible blankets
and concrete encasement, respectively from left to right [9]
Board systems are one of the most popular type of ﬁre protection in the United
Kingdom. They are usually based on ex-foliated vermiculite, which is a hydrous silicate
material characterised by non-combustibility and low conductivity. They are widely
used both where the protection system is in full view and where it is hidden. They
oﬀer the speciﬁer a clean, boxed appearance and have the additional advantage that
application is a dry trade and may not have a signiﬁcant impact on other activities.
Also, boards are factory manufactured and their thicknesses can be guaranteed. Fur-
thermore, boards can be applied on unpainted steelwork. On the other hand, a board
system could be relatively expensive because of its diﬃculty of ﬁtting around complex
details and its long application procedure. Moreover, they have durability problem in
a corrosive environment and they are subjected to loss of integrity in long-term service.
Spray applied ﬁreprooﬁng materials, such as cementitious formulations or mineral
ﬁbre products, are extensively used in the United States. Despite being one of the
cheapest forms of ﬁre protection in terms of application costs and applicability to com-
plex shapes and details, they have decreased in popularity in the last decades. Mainly
it is because they are not suitable for aesthetic purposes and their application is a wet
trade: drying time needs to be accounted for in the construction program and this
may have impacts on other site operations. Furthermore, some of the earliest spray

protections contained asbestos, which is no longer allowed due to health issues.
Another solution for the passive ﬁre protection of steelworks is the application of
ﬂexible blanket systems: the steel structure is wrapped with low conductivity material,
usually made by mineral wool. They have been developed as a response to the need
for a cheap and easily applied ﬁre protection material which can be applied with a dry
trade on complex shapes and details.
Until the late 1970s, concrete was by far the most common form of ﬁre protection
for structural steelwork. However, the introduction of lightweight, conventional systems
such as boards and sprays have seen a dramatic reduction in their use. Nevertheless,
concrete encasement still has its place and it continues to have a small percentage of
the ﬁre protection market with other traditional methods. The principal advantage
of concrete is an excellent durability in corrosive conditions: it performs well where
resistance to impacts, abrasion, weather exposure and corrosive agents is signiﬁcant.
On the other hand, concrete is heavy and expensive compared to the lightweight systems
and it often requires large space utilisation and time-consuming application procedure
[9].
1.2
Steel structures and ﬁre protection
All commonly used structural materials lose their designed strength when exposed to
ﬁre: concrete cracks, timber chars and steel loses its bearing capacity. Therefore, it is
important to apply some ﬁreprooﬁng system onto these structural members [10].
In particular, steel is becoming increasingly popular as a main structural material
in the construction industry. Its high strength and ductility enable the possibility of
designing slim and light buildings, the fascinating structures of the modern world (Fig.
1.3). Starting from high-rise buildings, this architecture feature is often pursued when
designing new airports terminals, big exhibition halls and sports stadiums [9].
Another reason of its success is the ability to use pre-fabricated steel structures
with consequent reduction of construction time and costs. Steel is not only an eﬃcient
technology, but it is also environmentally friendly: metal structures are very durable in
time, they can be fully recycled and they do not release pollutants during their design
life. Steel has also many advantageous properties for ﬁre protection engineering, for
example it is a non-combustible material [9].
However, steel is a thermally high conductive material and it structural and physical
properties degrade with increasing temperatures, as most materials. While steel has
a particularly high melting point, its structural stability and strength can be heavily
compromised in a ﬁre scenario. In particular, the strength of steel at high temperatures
has been deﬁned in great detail and it is known that at temperatures of 550oC structural
steel retains only 60% of its room temperature strength, as shown in Fig. 1.4 [9][11].
This type of material deterioration can cause the collapse of building structures and it
can lead to many fatalities and property damages.
Therefore, the ﬁre protection of steel structures has become an increasingly im-
portant issue in the construction industry over the past two decades.
The ongoing
construction of low self-weight and slim steel structures demands a proper level of pro-
tection and safety against ﬁre-related damages and fatalities. Moreover, slenderer and
lighter structures are required to have a longer ﬁre resistance and they must withstand

1.3
Intumescent coatings
The previously described conventional methods for ﬁre protection engineering are rela-
tively cheap and they can be easily installed in many diﬀerent situations. Nevertheless,
they are usually deemed to be aesthetically unpleasant and, therefore, they do not rep-
resent the best desirable choice for slender and light buildings with visible steelwork.
For this reason, the trend in structural ﬁre protection for steel over the past two decades
has been the rise in popularity of thin intumescent coatings. This option deﬁnitely seems
a preferable alternative for ﬁre protection of visible steel structures.
Intumescent coatings are paint-like substances which are inert at low temperatures.
Under thermal exposure, the coating swells up when it reaches a temperature threshold
(about 250oC). The swelling reactive layer keeps growing until the underlying ablative
paint is totally consumed, but it stops if heating is interrupted. Finally, if the aggression
process is maintained, a solidiﬁed char layer appears on top of the system. The ﬁnal
structure is a porous stratiﬁed layer with high insulating properties [13].
The ﬁrst commercial patent for a foaming ﬁre retardant system was issued to H.
Tramm in 1938 [14]. A variety of coating formulations, in the forms of paints and mas-
tics, have been developed and put into commercial use starting in the 1950s. However,
the implementation of intumescent products in the building industry remained very
limited until the mid-1980s [15]. Over the past decades intumescent coatings have been
considerably improved and now they dominate the ﬁre protection market. Their great
success is due to the possibility to easily cover complex details with an attractive and
decorative ﬁnishes: thin coating allows to express the shape of underlying steel (Fig.
1.5).

Apart from the attractive architectural appearance, intumescent coatings are thin
and lightweight and they do not change the intrinsic properties of the protected mate-
rial. On the other hand, intumescent coating application costs are usually higher than
sprays, although costs have decreased in recent years. Furthermore, their application
is a wet trade which requires suitable atmospheric conditions during application and
precautions against over-spray. Another drawback of adopting this ﬁre protection sys-
tem is the limited ﬁre resistance period: most intumescent coatings can traditionally
provide up to 60 minutes ﬁre resistance in an economic way. Improvements in the tech-
nology of application and production in recent years have reduced coating thicknesses
considerably and intumescent paints are competitive in the 90 minutes market as well.
Nevertheless, nowadays an increasing number of intumescent coatings can achieve 120
minutes ﬁre resistance [9]. This aspect has revolutionised ﬁre protection engineering
because intumescent coatings are now designed for structures with 120 minutes ﬁre
resistance requirements, such as high-rise buildings.
Intumescent coatings can be divided into two broad families: thin ﬁlm and thick
ﬁlm. Thin ﬁlm intumescent coatings are either solvent-based or water-based and are
mainly used for cellulosic ﬁre conditions. Thick ﬁlm intumescent coatings were originally
developed for the oﬀ-shore and hydrocarbon industries, but have been modiﬁed for use
in buildings.
Still, in recent years, intumescent coatings have become the dominant passive ﬁre
protection materials used in industrial and public buildings because of their advantages
such as light weight, attractive appearance, potential for oﬀ-shore application and prac-
tically taking no space [17]. This concept is also conﬁrmed by the market trends for
ﬁre protection of steel frames shown in Fig. 1.6.
Today, intumescent coatings are not used only as ﬁre protection in the building
industry, but they are applied and adopted for several purposes.

1.3.1
The intumescent process
The intumescent coatings are thermally reactive ﬁre protection materials and they are
usually composed of a combination of three diﬀerent substances: an inorganic acid
source, a carbon-rich polyhydric compound and a blowing agent. Also, some pigments
are present in the intumescent coatings: not only for its decorative properties, but they
provide better weather prooﬁng ability and improved mechanical stability [22].
All
these ingredients are bound together in a polymer matrix [24][23].
Commonly used inorganic acid sources are ammonium salts, amine/amide phos-
phate and organophosphorous compounds. Compounds such urea, melanine and de-
cyandiamide of urea-formaldehyde resins are often introduced into the intumescent
formulations to act as blowing agent. Ammonium polyphosphate (APP) and pentaery-
thritol (PER) are the most typical carbon-rich polyhydric compounds, the main cause of
formation of black carbonaceous compact foam [25] [10]. Finally, titanium dioxide and
silica are the most commonly used inorganic pigment ﬁllers in the intumescent coatings
When the paint is exposed to ﬂames or to a thermal radiation and it reaches a crit-
ical temperature, usually around 250oC, the inorganic acid source undergoes a thermal
decomposition, yielding acid. Then the blowing agent is activated at a temperature of
300oC-350oC, causing an endothermic reaction which absorbs heat from the substrate

==PAGE:30==

and decomposes to release a large amount of gaseous products. Thus, gas production
is triggered by this reaction: the gas bubbles are trapped in the coating to cause the
molten carbon matrix to swell, forming an expanding multi-cellular system. The ex-
pansion continues until the blowing agent is ﬁnished or the carbon matrix is not viscous
enough; moreover, as the temperature increases, the mixture is solidiﬁed. Finally, the
carbon char further oxidises leaving a residual protective multi-cellular insulation char
structure, characterised by a high porosity and negligible self-weight. It appears as a
soft white foam and it behaves as a low-conductivity thermal barrier, which decreases
the speed of temperature penetration inside the substrate [26][27][28][29].
In this way, the intumescent process causes the coating to bubble, foam and swell up
to 100 times its original thickness to produce a porous charred thermal barrier against
heat transmission and oxygen diﬀusion [30].
Figure 1.7: Intumescent process
As shown in Fig. 1.7, four reaction steps can describe the intumescent process when
the coating is exposed to ﬂames or to a thermal radiation:
1. In the early stage of heating, an endothermic process starts and a large amount
of thermal energy is absorbed by the intumescent coating, whose temperature
increases quickly;
2. At a critical temperature, the polymer matrix melts and degrades to form a vis-
cous liquid: the inorganic acid source in the coating undergoes normally thermal
decomposition at temperatures between 100oC and 250oC;
3. At temperatures between 280oC and 350oC, the blowing agent with the coating
decomposes to release a large amount of gas, a fraction of which is trapped within
the molten matrix;
4. the molten ﬂuid hardens and releases residual volatile to form a char structure.
These four reaction steps could happen in sequence or together and with diﬀerent
time intervals: this fact mainly depends on the composition of the chemical and their
mixture. In an intumescent paint, it is fundamental that the diﬀerent chemical sources
in the mixture show a suitable matching thermal behaviour. Moreover, the order and

appropriate sequence. For instance, if gasiﬁcation takes place when the molten polymer
is too viscous, bubble growth will be strongly opposed and the gas will tend to diﬀuse

1.4
Design ﬁres for structural ﬁre safety
During the years, the necessity of compatibility between ﬁre tests conducted in diﬀerent
laboratories scattered around the world pointed out the need of implementing standard
procedures to test the performance of ﬁre protection materials. The modern ﬁre per-
formance assessment method in the standard EN 13381-4 [31] is based on the standard
ﬁre resistance test, which represents a prescribed time-temperature ﬁre scenario [32].
However, ﬁres can occur anywhere with diﬀerent characteristics and results in diﬀerent
severity, depending on diﬀerent periods of exposure. Modern ﬁre engineering is moving
towards more realistic ﬁre conditions [10].
1.4.1
Standard ﬁre curves
Nowadays the standard ﬁre curves are widely adopted to unify the ﬁre test procedures,
both in testing of building materials and in ﬁre protection design of structures. In the
Eurocode [33], two nominal ﬁre curves are reported and they can be used for diﬀerent
ﬁre scenarios, depending on the type of fuel. The graph in Fig. 1.8 shows the two main
nominal ﬁre curves provided by the Eurocode [33].

curve and Hydrocarbon Temperature-time ﬁre curve

terial, the hydrocarbon standard temperature-time ﬁre curve [33] is commonly used: an

However, the standard ﬁre curve does not reﬂect realistic heat exposures: it is ﬁxed
and the temperature always increases in time. Therefore, it must be used for a given
duration, which is given by the code on the basis of the occupancy, but it cannot
reproduce a plausible ﬁre scenario for the most design applications. The temperature-
time relationship of a real ﬁre has deﬁned phases, such as growth, ﬂashover and decay
[34]. Despite the limitations, the standard ﬁre exposures have been universally adopted
in many countries because of its simplicity and universality.
1.4.2
Parametric ﬁre curves
The main feature of the standard ﬁre is that it is independent of various parameters
known to aﬀect the ﬁre intensity. The structural ﬁre engineering community has been
asking for a more ﬂexible temperature-time relationship based on several parameters
related to real scenarios, such as the available ventilation, the amount of combustible
material, the characteristics of the compartment geometry and boundaries, etc. This
opens up a number of new design possibilities, the so-called parametric temperature-time
ﬁre curves. This is a mechanism of calculating the actual time-temperature relationship
in a compartment of known characteristics and represents an alternative to the standard
ﬁre curve.
Recognising that the standard ﬁre curve was not physically reasonable, researchers
in Sweden in the 1970s (Pettersson et al. 1976 [35]) developed a method of predicting
compartment ﬁre gas temperatures by considering heat balances in a ﬁre compartment.
In this way Pettersson et al. [35] introduced temperature-time curves that relate the
gas temperature within the compartment to its characteristics: the model considered
the thermal properties of the compartment boundaries, the available ventilation and the
fuel load [36]. This was the ﬁrst example of parametric temperature-time ﬁre curves.
Like the standard ﬁres, parametric ﬁres consist of temperature-time relationships,
but these relationships contain parameters that represent particular aspects of the re-
ality. Normally, three parameters are included in these models: the ﬁre load present in
the compartment, the openings in the walls or in the roofs and the type and nature of
the diﬀerent walls of the compartment [10]. The graph in Fig. 1.9 shows an example of
parametric temperature-time curves from Eurocode [33] with varying opening factors.

The above described approach represents the foundations of a new design concept for
the coming years based on the development of performance-based methods: more ﬂexible
methods aim to enable safer and more economical design. The implementation of this
new design manner has been facilitated by the improvements in the available compu-
tational methods. Performance-based design methods utilise computational modelling
tools, such as CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics), in order to analyse the impacts
of ﬁre in more complex compartment geometries. Additionally, real ﬁre eﬀects can be
estimated based on the worst case design scenario by implementing parametric ﬁres
in performance-based designs. In fact, diﬀerent ﬁre scenarios have diﬀerent levels of
severity on engineering design aspects. For examples, fast ﬁres with high temperatures
usually constitute the most critical cases for evacuation purposes. On the other hand,
slow and long ﬁres represent the most dangerous case for the load-bearing capacity
of structures because the heat penetration through a cross-section is a time-lasting
procedure and a longer duration of ﬁre exposure increases the possibility of material
degradation and failure [37]. This leads engineers to choose from a much wider set of
design tools and solutions; additionally, architects are left with more freedom in the
design of building geometries and interior arrangements, since the ﬁre hazards can be
assessed for any particular compartment [38].
1.5
Current design practice for intumescent coatings
In the current design practice, intumescent coatings manufactures usually provide de-
sign tables to structural engineers. These tables report the minimum required dry ﬁlm
thickness (DFT) of intumescent coating needed to ensure that the temperature of a
structural steel member is kept under a speciﬁed design temperature in a speciﬁed
amount of time during which the structural bearing capacity must be ensured. In or-
der to design a proper ﬁre protection, structural engineers choose the best intumescent
coating thickness from the provided design tables, based on many input parameters:

they have to fulﬁl the ﬁre resistance class (R30, R45, R60...)
according to the de-
sign temperature, the section factor, the proﬁle type (open or closed sections) and the
element type (mainly beams or columns).
However, this method of designing a proper passive ﬁre protection is problematic
for several reasons.
Firstly these design thicknesses are typically determined on the basis of standard
ﬁre conditions, in particular on results achieved by a cellulosic standard ﬁre exposure
[31][32]. Then standard ﬁre results, which correspond to unrealistic ﬁre exposure, are
extended to all the other ﬁre scenarios without deeply investigating the behaviour of
the intumescent coatings under diﬀerent ﬁre conditions. This fact leads to the incorrect
assumption that the thermal properties of the intumescent coatings are independent
of the ﬁre condition. On the contrary, intumescent coatings are reactive materials and
their thermal properties are strongly dependent not only on temperature, but also on
the heating condition and the type of ﬁre exposure. Therefore, the thermal properties
of intumescent coatings, particularly thermal resistance and thermal conductivity, ob-
tained by standard ﬁre tests are not consistent and reliable under diﬀerent sets of ﬁre
conditions [28]. The thermal properties of other conventional ﬁre protection materi-
als are mainly temperature dependent and they can be described by simple empirical
property-temperature relationships. On the contrary, the performance of intumescent
paints is complex and the coatings behave diﬀerently according to the applied heating
condition, coating thickness and protected structure [28][29][39].
Moreover, the design tables provided by the intumescent coating producer companies
only clearly state the ﬁre resistance class and the design temperature: mainly they
deﬁne the time necessary for a structural element subjected to standard ﬁre exposure
to reach a certain temperature. This leaves out completely the temperature evolution
in the structural element during the ﬁre exposure. As a consequence, the only possible
methods which can be adopted for the structural ﬁre design according to Eurocode
[11] are simpliﬁed procedures, such as the method of critical temperatures (MCT). In
this method, the structural ﬁre design is performed member by member, rather than
considering the entire structural system. Thus, the design of structural ﬁre protection
does not investigate the overall stability of the structure during the ﬁre event and this
leads to an unsafe and dangerous simpliﬁcation [40].
Finally, many diﬀerent types of intumescent coatings are nowadays produced world-
wide: there are many mixtures of ingredients which provide really diﬀerent performances
in the diﬀerent ﬁre scenarios. Therefore, in recent years researchers have been mainly
concerned with developing new intumescent coatings products. However, there is a se-
vere shortcoming in these research studies based on the expected performance and the
testing procedures. Intumescent coating products are usually tested according to stan-
dard ﬁre exposures and standard ﬁre resistance tests and consequently industry aims at
developing new intumescent coating products to pass these types of tests, in which the
ﬁre exposure is ﬁxed. As already stated, the ﬁre protection performance of intumescent
coatings is unfortunately highly dependent on the heating condition and hence the type
of ﬁre exposure. Therefore, since the frontier of structural ﬁre engineering is pushed
to include ﬁre conditions diﬀerent from the standard ones, it is crucial to characterise
the ﬁre performance of intumescent coatings in diﬀerent ﬁre exposures and structural
design [10].

1.6
Relevant studies
During the last decades, many researchers have been involved in the study of intumes-
cent coatings and their behaviour. Some of them were interested in the chemical com-
position and they tried to deﬁne the best mixture for an eﬃcient intumescent paint.
Vandersall [41] ﬁrstly reviewed intumescent chemistry in 1971 and he described the
general categories of its chemical components. In the 1980s, Camino et al. [25][42][43]
worked on a detailed mechanism of intumescence and published a series of papers to give
a general overview of the chemical aspects. More recently, Le Bras [44][45] and Bour-
bigot [46][47] have carried out more investigations regarding the intumescence chemistry
and physics. Their studies were focused on how intumescent coatings behave with dif-
ferent chemical components and how these components inﬂuence the diﬀerent aspects
of the intumescent behaviour.
Beyond the chemical composition, many numerical and mathematical models of
various complexities have been developed in order to study the intumescent process and
the heat transfer behaviour of intumescent systems. An innovative model was developed
by Cagliostro et al. [48]: the model took into account the fundamental chemical reaction
kinetics, mass and heat transfer proﬁles in order to evaluate the temperature of the
substrate material. The model estimates the insulating eﬃciency by considering the
eﬀective thermal conductivity, evaluated as the ratio between the thermal conductivity
and the expansion factor of the intumescent layer. Anderson et al. [49] developed a
procedure to estimate the eﬀective thermal conductivity of the intumescent char and,
in particular, he focused on the eﬀect of material expansion on the thermal response of
an intumescent coating. Many others models have been developed, but most of them
were based on simpliﬁed one-dimensional heat transfer through the intumescent layer
and on semi-empirical formulas. On the contrary, Butler et all. [15][50] have developed
several models at a very complex level, including 1-dimensional and 3-dimensional heat
transfers in order to study intumescence in great detail.
In the literature, many research studies based on these intumescent models can be
found. Various approaches and methodologies have been proposed to analyse the per-
formance of intumescent coatings exposed to diﬀerent ﬁre conditions. Anderson et al.
[49] adopted his own model to evaluate the eﬀective thermal conductivity of the intu-
mescent layer. The procedure was based on a heat transfer analysis of temperature-time
data from one-dimensionally designed experiments of coated steel coupons exposed to
aviation-type fuel ﬁres exposures. Jimenez et al. [23] studied the performance of an
intumescent coating subjected to a hydrocarbon ﬁre. Both aviation-type fuel ﬁres and
hydrocarbon ﬁres are more severe than standard ﬁres in that they will reach very high
temperatures with rather rapid growth. Li et al. [27] proposed a simple approach to
assess the equivalent thermal resistance of intumescent coatings subjected to the ISO
834 cellulosic ﬁre [32]. Bartholmai et al. [51] investigated the inﬂuence of external heat
ﬂux and coating thickness on the thermal insulating properties of intumescent coatings.
In a mass loss cone heater set-up, the intumescent paint was exposed to several radiant
heat ﬂuxes, providing temperature-time curves similar to the standard ﬁre one [32]. Dai
et al. [52] carried out some experiments on steel joints partially protected by intumes-
cent coatings and subjected to standard ﬁres. The eﬃciency of the ﬁre protection was
evaluated through the eﬀecting thermal conductivity by inverting formula 4.2.5.2 re-

Wang et al. [28] performed some furnace tests on steel plates protected by intumescent

1.7
Project objective
Based on the literature study exposed in the previous pages, this section provides a
brief description of the project objective.
Intumescent coatings are nowadays dominant passive ﬁre protection materials used
in industrial and public buildings and, in particular, in steel constructions. However,
current design procedures for assessing the amount of intumescent coating on steel
proﬁles exposed to ﬁre are typically based on the assumption of an equivalent constant
value of thermal resistance of the coating, determined on the basis of standard ﬁre
tests. However, several studies have highlighted how the behaviour of the intumescent
coatings does not depend only on temperature, but is also aﬀected by other properties
of the ﬁre, such as ﬂame exposure, heat ﬂux and ﬁre growth rate. In particular, it has
been demonstrated that the current procedure of assessing intumescent coating thermal
properties under standard ﬁre condition can not be applied to other ﬁre conditions due
to the ﬁre-dependent nature of organic materials such as intumescent coatings.
The current study went beyond the results of a previous DTU MSc project [26] and
aimed at further investigating the diﬀerent aspects that aﬀect the thermal resistance of
the intumescent coatings and their behaviour under diﬀerent ﬁre exposures. A better
understanding of the diﬀerent phases in the variation of the thermal resistance of the
coatings was achieved, by investigating the role played by several characteristics, such
as the activation temperature of the paint and the ﬁre heating rate.
Attention was also paid to the inﬂuence of the dry ﬁlm thickness (DFT) of the
intumescent coating and the type of steel proﬁle – two aspects that had not been
investigated in the previous DTU study, as well to other relevant parameters of the
intumescent coating such as expansion ratio, char structure characteristics and smoke
production. Furthermore, a speciﬁc analysis was performed in order to deﬁne the ideal
thickness of the coatings, both regards eﬃciency and the cost optimisation.
In order to fulﬁl the project objective, three diﬀerent sets of experiments were con-
ducted, representing diﬀerent types of heating exposure. Moreover, two diﬀerent in-
tumescent coatings - one water-based and one solvent-based - were tested in order to

Theory: heat transfer models
This chapter provides a theoretical description of the heat transfer models adopted in
the diﬀerent experiments in order to investigate the intumescent coatings behaviour
under diﬀerent ﬁre scenarios.
Mathematical modelling of heat transfer in structural members under ﬁre conditions
is an essential part of a standard procedure in ﬁre resistance assessment. The main
advantage of mathematical models is that they are simple to use and can be easily
elaborated using spreadsheets.
Moreover, they conﬁrm if the experiment has been
executed in a proper way: in particular, they can validate the tests if the mathematical
model can predict the experimental results with a minimal error.
2.1
Heat transfer models of unprotected steel
Firstly, heat transfer models are established for the experimental set-ups where the steel
is unprotected and subjected to a heat exposure.
2.1.1

2.2
Heat transfer models of protected steel
Secondly, heat transfer models are established for the experimental set-ups where the
steel is protected by intumescent coatings: they react in case of high temperatures and
they develop thermal insulating properties.
2.2.1
Electric oven and gas furnace to protected steel
This one-dimensional model is well-known in the literature [35] and it is valid for two
sets of experiments: the electric oven and the gas furnace. The heat transfer mechanism
between the hot gasses and the protected steel can be simpliﬁed as the one in Fig. 2.4:
once again, the samples are subjected to both convective heat transfer from the hot air
present in the chamber and radiative heat transfer from the heating source. However,
in this case the presence of an insulation material (intumescent coating) decreases the
speed of temperature penetration through the steel substrate. At the surface, the heat
transfer is simpliﬁed by two parallel heat transfer coeﬃcients, which stand for convection
αc and radiation αr, placed in series to a conduction thermal resistance dp/λp, which
represents the insulation material.
This model is based on the assumption that the temperature gradient is linear and
the quantity of energy needed to heat the insulation is negligible. The intumescent
coating has usually a small thickness (about few millimetres) and thus this assumption
is usually valid. The derivation of this heat transfer model is based also on other two
assumptions. The heat ﬂow in the steel and the insulation material is unidimensional
and the validity of this assumption is greater when there are minimal corner eﬀects.
Also, at every point of time the steel temperature is uniformly distributed over the cross
section of the steel member: this means that the thinner the parts of cross-section, the
greater validity of this assumption [35].

coating is very small quantity when the intumescent paint has reached its full expan-

when the steel plate has reached a quasi-steady state. Thus, it can be stated that after
achieving quasi-state conditions, the intumescent coating surface temperature should
be approaching a constant value, mainly depending on the level of radiation. As a
consequence, the cone heater incident irradiance is much greater than the other three
terms and they can be neglected.
Nevertheless, the intumescent coating surface temperature Tp is really diﬃcult to
evaluate and it can be estimated only by adopting sophisticated equipment, for instance
a thermographic phosphor technique. Thus, without this information it is not possible
to derive the intumescent coating thermal resistance for this speciﬁc experimental set-
up.
Therefore, a more intuitive approach was proposed in order to evaluate the intu-
mescent coating performance. In particular, the ability of the intumescent paint to
reduce the heat penetration and the steel substrate temperature was assessed by eﬃ-
ciency considerations. The intumescent coating eﬃciency was related to the ability of
the passive ﬁre protection system to lower the protected steel temperature relative to
the unprotected steel temperature. Many diﬀerent formulations were proposed, but it
was selected the one with the highest physical value.
Regarding the mass loss cone heater experiment, the protected and unprotected
steel plate temperatures increase during the test due to the radiation exposure. After a
certain amount of time, which depends mainly on the heat ﬂux, the two temperatures
reach a constant value till the end of the experiment. This aspect is related to the fact
that in such short amount of time the intumescent paint develops its char structure, but
it is diﬃcult to highlight degradation phenomena. Thus, the best intumescent coating
eﬃciency should be represented by an increasing function which reaches a constant
value within the experiment: ﬁrstly the paint expands increasing its thermal resistance
and secondly it keeps its own thermal insulating properties.

Experimental set-up
This chapter provides a description of the experimental set-ups arranged during the
tests aimed to understand several characteristics of intumescent coatings. The three
diﬀerent sets of experiments took place at two diﬀerent locations: the electric oven and
the mass loss cone heater tests at DTU Fire Laboratory (building 122, DTU Campus,
2800 Kongens Lyngby, Denmark), while the gas furnace test at DBI Fire Laboratory
(DBI - Danish Institute of Fire and Security Technology, Jernholmen 12, 2650 Hvidovre,
Denmark).
3.1
Preliminary experiments
During the initial phase of the project, a range of preliminary experiments was con-
ducted before the main ones. This practice is always advisable in order to identify the

Types of intumescent coatings used
In order to highlight common aspects in the behaviour of these passive ﬁre protection
materials, two diﬀerent types of intumescent coatings were investigated. Both of them
are commercial products available in the market and they are designed for applications
on structural steel members. In this project they are identiﬁed as PaintA and PaintB.
PaintA is a solvent-based emulsion and in the technical speciﬁcation it is described
as highly ﬂammable when it is liquid and before application. On the contrary, PaintB is

about the intumescent coatings were found in the technical data sheets provided by the

3.5
Thermocouples
The control and the measurements of the temperature of the diﬀerent examined com-
ponents had a key importance in these sets of experiments. An easy and inexpensive
way of measuring temperature is by using thermocouples, devices which consist on two
diﬀerent conductors that contact each other at one or more spots, where a temperature
diﬀerential is experienced by the diﬀerent conductors. It also produces a voltage when
the temperature of one of the contact points diﬀers from the reference temperature of
the other, in a process known as the thermoelectric eﬀect. The diﬀerent conductivity
of the two conductors induces a current in this circuit and a special device is able to
evaluate the temperature starting from a potential diﬀerence. The main limitation with
thermocouples is the accuracy: system errors of less than one degree Celsius (oC) can be
diﬃcult to register, but they are really useful for big temperature gradient, for instance
in a ﬁre scenario.
In these sets of experiments, temperature measurements were executed by using
type K thermocouples, composed of conductor wires of nickel and chromium (diameter
33
==PAGE:56==
CHAPTER 3. EXPERIMENTAL SET-UP
0.5 mm) with a combined outer diameter of 1.4 mm and a theoretical maximum service
temperature of 1250 oC. The two conductors (Ni and Cr) were coated by an insulation
material (double glass braids) which prevents short circuits in the thermocouple and so
systematic errors.
The temperature was evaluated by connecting the thermocouples to a data logger,
able to translate the potential diﬀerence of the two conductors into a temperature
gradient. All the data were also collected by the software called Agilent BenchLink
Data Logger 3.
Before the experiments, each thermocouple was also tested in order to be sure to
have performed a proper work during its realisation and also to make reasonable the
measurements obtained during the experiments.

3.6.1
Electric oven at DTU Fire Laboratory
The electric oven at DTU Fire Laboratory is a Scandia Oven IW500 and has a theoret-

10oC/min. The electric oven heating chamber has inner dimensions equal to 72x82x97
cm and it can be accessed by the front door (Fig. 3.6).
The oven is heated by electrical energy which increases the temperature of steel
spirals located throughout the internal surface of the heating chamber. The steel spirals
are electrical resistances and they produce radiative heat inside the heating chamber:

oven inner surfaces, and the objects placed inside. The electric oven produces a clean
environment in which the main mechanism of heat transfer is radiation from the steel
spirals and porous bricks at high temperatures and convection from the hot air to the
test specimens at low temperatures.
Furthermore, the oven is equipped with a cylindrical exhaust hood which can remove
the excessive heat and smoke coming out from the oven top opening. It also helps and
facilitates the oven cooling procedure which always follows each experiment.
The steel temperature and the oven temperature were evaluated using thermocou-

will be described below. All the temperatures estimated by the thermocouples were

Experimental set-up in the electric oven
Because of the temperature diﬀerences across width and depth of the oven, the steel
proﬁle sample was placed in the centre of the oven for testing: this provision was decided

For this reason, the sample was placed at the half distance along the three main

parts.
In order to locate the sample centrally with respect the height of the heating chamber

the sample ﬂanges in order to sustain the steel specimen. Moreover, an insulation board
was placed between the specimen and the brick columns in order to avoid conduction
between the lower oven heating surface and the steel sample through the bricks. The
insulation board was made of vitreous wool and it was manufactured mainly for thermal
insulation: in this set of experiments it was really useful due to its endurance to high

3.6.6
Temperature-time curves and heating rates
The common design method for testing ﬁre protection systems applied to structural
steel members are typically based on the standard temperature-time ﬁre curve [32][33].
Since it is characterised by high heating rates, especially in the ﬁrst 30 minutes, and
the available electric oven can reproduce a maximum heating rate of approximately
10oC/min, the steel specimens were exposed to non-standard ﬁre curves. One of the
goals of this project was also to understand the relationship between the intumescent
coating characteristics and the ﬁre heating rate.
According to the suggestion given in a previous study by Andersen [26], a total
of four diﬀerent heating rates were implemented for the experiments conducted in the
electric oven chamber. The desired temperature-time curves were programmed in the
oven controlled, which allows the tester to deﬁne the heating curve in terms of three
parameters: a theoretical constant heating rate expressed in degree Celsius per hour;
a target temperature which the oven has to reach; a hold time which represents the
amount of time that the oven has to hold the ﬁnal target temperature.

experiments conducted in the electric oven: they were qualitatively denoted as "fast",
"medium", "slow" and "very slow", according to their heating rates. Furthermore, this

3.7.1
Mass loss cone heater at DTU Fire Laboratory
The mass loss cone heater is an established device for measuring the heat release rate
of combustible materials in respect to diﬀerent ﬁre impacts. In particular, it has the
ability to create a heat ﬂux on a small area (about 100 cm2) at a particular distance
from the heat generation device. The radiation is provided by a steel spiral located
in the cone above the sample. Samples can be exposed to diﬀerent heat ﬂuxes: by
adjusting the temperature of the spiral, a desired level of radiation is provided to the
specimens at a deﬁned distance. All the dimension of the mass loss cone heater are the
ones according to the standard ISO 5660-1 [61].
The cone heater generates radiant heat ﬂuxes up to 100 kW/m2 at 25 mm distance
corresponding to a post-ﬂashover room ﬁre at a very high level. The cone heater was
used to simulate radiant heat ﬂuxes typical for room ﬁres [62].
Moreover, the mass loss cone heater is equipped with a weighting device which has
the ability to evaluate the sample mass during the experiment. The weighting device
has an accuracy of ± 1g and it is really useful to estimate the mass loss during the test
or during any particular experiment phase.

Experiments in the gas furnace
The third set of experiments in this project was performed at DBI Fire Laboratory
in a gas furnace where the steel proﬁle samples were exposed to the ISO834 cellulosic
standard ﬁre curve [32].
The main diﬀerence with the electric oven test is that the gas furnace can generate
higher heating rates and in this way it was possible to reproduce the standard ﬁre curve
described in the standard IS0 834-1:1999, which demands a very high heating rate and,
in particular, during the ﬁrst 30 minutes [32].
Once again, the main aim of this set of experiments was to better understand the
behaviour of intumescent coatings subjected to another diﬀerent ﬁre scenario, in par-
ticular exposed to the ISO834 cellulosic standard temperature-time ﬁre curve.
3.8.1
Gas furnace at DBI Fire Laboratory
The gas furnace is a device used for performing ﬁre tests on several materials. Its pe-
culiarity is to reproduce really severe ﬁre scenarios, characterised by high temperatures
and high heating rates. The gas furnace at DBI (Danish Institute of Fire and Security
Technology) is from Krom Schroder and it can reach maximum temperatures around
1400oC and maximum heating rates around 100oC/min. The gas furnace heating cham-
ber has inner dimensions equal to 150x150x150 cm (Fig. 3.14).
The gas ﬁred furnace produces a clean environment in which the main mechanism
of heat transfer is radiation from the furnace inner walls at high temperatures and
convection from the hot air to the test specimens at low temperatures.
The furnace is heated by gas. The fossil fuel is burned and the heat created is then
pushed into the heat exchanger, where the air is heated up. Thus, the newly heated air
is forced to move into the furnace chamber through four diﬀerent duct-works, placed at
diﬀerent furnace sides and heights. By adjusting the amount of air+gas mixture and the
ﬂow, it is possible to control the furnace temperature, both manually and automatically.
The furnace temperature is monitored by 8 plate thermocouples placed throughout the
heating chamber. In this way, the gas furnace can reproduce the standard ﬁre curves
described in the standard IS0 834 [32] with a great accuracy.
Moreover, the gas furnace is placed under a large chimney which can remove the
excessive heat and smoke produced during the experiment and during the cooling down
phase. The gas furnace is also equipped with an internal ventilation system, a funda-
mental component to control the pressure and remove the excessive smoke and ﬂying
particles inside the heating chamber.
The steel temperature and the furnace temperature were evaluated using thermo-
couples, placed at speciﬁc locations and ﬁxed according to an attachment method which
will be described below. All the temperatures estimated by the thermocouples were col-
lected by a data logger, which was linked to a PC for data collection and elaboration.

Experimental set-up in the gas furnace
The 5 steel proﬁle samples were placed in a symmetrical conﬁguration inside the gas

In order to raise the samples position respect to the height of the heating chamber it
was decided to lift them up and support them by columns made of lightweight concrete
blocks. This material is really useful for this kind of experiments due its low thermal
conductivity and its capability to maintain its bearing resistance at high temperatures.
Each column was composed of 2 lightweight concrete blocks with dimensions 50x30x15
cm.
Moreover, an insulation board was placed between the specimens and the columns
in order to avoid conduction through the lightweight concrete. The insulation board
was made of mineral wool and it is usually manufactured for thermal insulation. Its
thickness was equal to 1 cm.
As in the case of the electric oven, the IPE steel proﬁle samples were placed in hor-
izontal position with the ﬂanges orthogonal to the insulation board. The main reasons
were to have continuity between the diﬀerent experiments and the ones explained for
the electric oven experimental set-up.

Standard temperature-time curve
The main goal of this project was to investigate the diﬀerent aspects that aﬀect the
thermal resistance of intumescent coatings and their behaviour under diﬀerent ﬁre ex-
posures.
In the ﬁrst two experimental set-ups the steel specimens were exposed to
non-standard ﬁre curves, while in this case the ISO834 ﬁre curve was reproduced in
order to implement the standard ﬁre scenario. Unlike the previous two cases, the gas
furnace can reproduce the standard ﬁre curve described in the standard ISO 834-1:1999,
which demands high temperatures and high heating rates, in particular during the ﬁrst
30 minutes [32].
The steel proﬁle samples were exposed to the cellulosic standard ﬁre curve (equation
1.1), the common design method for testing ﬁre protection systems applied to structural
steel members in buildings.

The standard ﬁre test is the main tool to deﬁne the ﬁre resistance class of ﬁre
insulation materials. The higher resistance class is the R120 and thus the materials
are usually exposed to the standard ﬁre for 120 minutes. In this way, according to the
design temperature and the section factor, it is possible to deﬁne the ﬁre resistance class.
Regarding intumescent coatings, they can achieve 120 minutes ﬁre resistance for only
few cases. Most intumescent coatings can traditionally provide up to 60 minutes ﬁre
resistance and nowadays, after many improvements, they are increasingly competitive
in the 90 minutes market as well [9]. In this particular experimental set-up the standard
ﬁre test lasted for 60 minutes because the main aim of this project is to understand the
thermal resistance development at temperatures up to 1000oC. The cellulosic standard
ﬁre curve reaches about 950oC in 60 minutes and about 1050oC in 120 minutes. Thus,
the second test hour with a diﬀerence of 100oC was not very relevant for this study.

Results and discussion
This chapter presents and discusses the results achieved during the experiments con-
ducted for each of the described experimental set-ups.
4.1
Visual and physical comparison between diﬀerent
intumescent coatings
This section treats the intumescent coating physical characteristics, which were high-
lighted by a visual inspection during the diﬀerent experiments. In this project two
diﬀerent types of intumescent coatings (PaintA and PaintB) were considered in order
to underline common aspects in the behaviour of this passive ﬁre protection material.
Interestingly, the various sets of experiments have underlined more diﬀerences than
shared characteristics between the two products.
4.1.1
Applied coating characteristics
Regarding the IPE steel samples used in the electric oven and the gas furnace ex-
perimental set-ups, the specimens were coated by professional painters. The samples
protected by the two diﬀerent paints looked exactly the same, characterised by a bright
white colour and a smooth surface. The only registered diﬀerence was the one related
to the intumescent coating average dry ﬁlm thickness: this topic has been already faced
in the section 3.3.
Regarding the cone heater experimental set-up, I coated the steel plate samples with
the two diﬀerent intumescent paints, applied in 3 diﬀerent DFTs. Once the coatings
were dried out, some diﬀerences between the two products were highlighted. PaintA,
a solvent-based intumescent paint, is more viscous than PaintB and consequently more
diﬃcult to apply in a precise dry ﬁlm thickness. The PaintA ﬁlm is more tender and it
can be more easily removed, once the paint has completely dried out. On the contrary,
PaintB, a water-based intumescent paint, creates a compact and hard ﬁlm on the applied
material: this fact is also conﬁrmed by the fact that the coating is water-based and an
initial steel oxidation takes place during the drying procedure. This leads to a better
cohesion between the dried coating and the protected steel substrate. On the other
hand, no diﬀerences were highlighted in the colour and the surface roughness of the two
applied intumescent coatings.

4.1.2
Char structure characteristics
The three experimental set-ups have produced diﬀerent intumescent coating char struc-
tures at the end of the heat exposures. This aspect is related to the diﬀerent ﬁre sce-
narios: the maximum temperature, the heat ﬂux, the heating rate and the test duration
have inﬂuenced the intumescent process and the char structure formation. This leads
in many diﬀerent char structures with various characteristics.
The main char peculiarities for the diﬀerent ﬁre scenarios and the two intumescent
coatings can be highlighted in the corresponding appendices: electric oven in Appendix
D, mass loss cone heater in Appendix E and gas furnace in Appendix F.
Colour and compactness
The ﬁrst main diﬀerence is related to the char structure colour and compactness. Re-
garding the electric oven and the gas furnace experimental set-ups, a huge amount of
energy was provided to the steel samples during the tests. Peculiarities of these exper-
iments are long exposures and high temperatures. As a result, the paint completely
expanded and degradation phenomena were recorded during the second part of the
tests. At the end of the intumescent chemical reaction, the gradual combustion of the
carbon binder took place. This component has a dark colour and it is the main respon-
sible for the cohesion of the char. Consequently, the char structures produced by the
electric oven and gas furnace tests looked like a white brittle dusty foam.
On the other hand, the cone heater submitted the protected steel samples to high
heat ﬂuxes for a short period. During the tests, the two paints had the chance to develop
their expanded char structures. However, during this short duration, the carbon binder
did not have the possibility to burn out. As a result, the resulting char structures were
characterised by a black/grey colour and a better cohesion.
Char structure development and cracks
Moreover, the tests conducted in the electric oven and in the gas furnace have high-
lighted another important diﬀerence in the characteristics of the char structure.
Regarding PaintB, a relevant expansion of the intumescent coating was registered
for all the diﬀerent temperature-time curves with various heating rates.
The white
dusty foam was continuous and homogeneous along its thickness and it seemed really
compact and well attached to the steel substrate. This aspect was conﬁrmed by the
diﬃculty of removing it completely from the steel specimens after the tests. Only small
cracks were observed and, in particular, along the inner edges where the ﬂanges are
connected to the web. In Fig. 4.1 the main characteristics of PaintB char structure are
shown.
On the contrary, the experiments have shown diﬀerent characteristics for PaintA.
Firstly a reasonable expansion of the intumescent coating was observed for all the
tests with temperature-time curves with high heating rates. On the other hand, an
insigniﬁcant expansion was recorded for low heating rates, such as the "very slow"
temperature-time curves. The white dusty foam was registered with the same charac-
teristics of PaintB one (continuous and homogeneous along its thickness). However, this
speciﬁc paint has always developed a black crust between the char structure and the

Another signiﬁcant aspect emerged from several experiments is the diﬀerent expansion

In the electric oven and gas furnace tests, it was really arduous to evaluate the
expansion of the intumescent paint because of several reasons. The absence of com-
pactness of the dusty foam char structure, the diﬀerent expansion at diﬀerent locations
and the loss of material from the specimens are the main challenges faced to measure
the paint expanded thickness.

However, the intumescent paint expansion was monitored and recorded in the mass
loss cone heater experiments. Each ﬁnal char structure thickness was measured carefully
and the values turned to be included in a range between 10 and 60 millimetres. In
addition, PaintA registered slightly higher expansions than PaintB, for the same DFT
and heat ﬂux. Furthermore, the ﬁnal maximum expansion of the char structure was
divided by the initial dry ﬁlm thickness in order to estimate the expansion factor. The
values resulted to be included between 10 and 80 times the initial dry ﬁlm thickness.

As expected, higher expansion factors were recorded for higher heat ﬂuxes, thus for
a higher amount of energy received. Interestingly, the highest expansion factors per
each heat ﬂux were obtained for the samples protected by the thinnest dry paint layer,
0.50 mm. This aspect underlined the ability of intumescent coatings to expand and
develop their char structures in a better way when the paint layer is thin and the heat
can penetrate through all its thickness. This is also conﬁrmed by the other two DFTs:
the thicker paint layer, 1.50 mm, registered the lowest expansion factors.
However, the highest maximum expansion thickness was always reached by the
specimens protected by DFTs equal to 1.00 mm: this paint layer is composed of the
right amount of material in order to be penetrated by the heat and to generate a
complete expansion. As a result, this value represents the optimal compromise between
expansion and eﬃciency for an intumescent coating. These products are commonly
applied on the surfaces of interest in dry ﬁlm thicknesses of about 1 millimetre.
Density
Nevertheless, a common aspect between PaintA and PaintB was found in the evaluation
of the density of the intumescent paint char structure.
By selecting small samples
extracted by the char structure, it was possible to estimate its density as the ratio
between the mass and the volume. Curiously it was found that the density was mainly
constant for all the char structures obtained by the three diﬀerent experimental set-
ups. A mean value of 0.02 g/cm3 was evaluated. Considering that the declared density
of the paint dry ﬁlm thickness is 1.30 g/cm3, the char structure density represents
less than 2% of the virgin value. Nevertheless, it was highlighted the fact that the char

Ignition
Surprisingly the experiments conducted in the electric oven have shown a really unex-
pected feature regarding one of the two intumescent coatings. During PaintA tests it
has been observed for a certain temperature range that the paint was burning and there
were small and discontinuous ﬂames on the surface of the intumescent char structure.

4.2
Phases of intumescent coating thermal resistance
development
In this section the insulating properties of intumescent coatings are investigated by
considering their thermal resistance. Moreover, the general trend in the development
of the thermal resistance curve is explained by deﬁning the diﬀerent phases.
4.2.1
Thermal resistance of the intumescent coatings
Apart from the externally visible properties, the main results obtained by heating the
IPE steel proﬁle specimens in the electric oven and gas furnace are series of tempera-
tures, evaluated by using thermocouples placed at particular locations. Using this data,
it is possible to analyse the eﬃciency of the intumescent coatings and their character-
istics.
In a more detailed research focused on setting up precise numerical models of the
intumescent process, the temperature of the intumescent paint and char structure is of
higher signiﬁcance. However, since this project focuses on the eﬃciency of the intu-
mescent coatings under diﬀerent ﬁre conditions and from the structural point of view,
measurements of the steel temperature were made, inasmuch the temperature of the
steelwork beneath the intumescent coating is of main practical signiﬁcance.
The temperature measurements were used to calculate the corresponding thermal
resistance of the intumescent paint according to equation 2.16. The average surrounding
temperature was evaluated as the average between the oven/furnace thermocouples
temperatures.
On the other hand, the average steel temperature was computed as
the average between the mean web thermocouple temperature and the mean ﬂange
thermocouples temperature: the main reason of this choice is the fact that, with good
approximation, the volume of the IPE steel proﬁle sample is composed of 50% of web
and 50% of ﬂanges. Therefore, by using the equation 2.16, the thermal resistance of
the intumescent coating was obtained, point by point. Considering that the thermal
resistance expression provided by equation 2.18 relates directly the increment in steel
temperature to the diﬀerence between the steel and the surrounding temperature, it
was used to quantify the eﬃciency of the intumescent coatings in the electric oven and
gas furnace experimental set-ups.
By collecting all the punctual values of thermal resistance throughout the time, a
common trend was observed for both the two intumescent paints (PaintA and PaintB):
the general shape is shown in Fig. 4.8.
As already proposed by Andersen [26], the development of the thermal resistance
throughout the time can be divided into four general phases. However, the previous
term "decay phase" suggested by Andersen [26] was changed in "post-austenitization
phase" since the intumescent coating behaviour at really high temperatures is still not
completely known. Further experiments should be performed in order to understand
better this characteristic.
Thus the four phases of the development of the thermal
resistance throughout the time are inert phase, transient phase, steady phase and post-
austenitization phase.

4.2.2
Critical points
The four phases are identiﬁed according to some critical points that can be recognised
on the thermal resistance development curve throughout the time.
The ﬁrst one is the "activation point". It is identiﬁed as the minimum value of
the thermal resistance before the intumescent process of the paint. This point refers to
when the intumescent chemical process begins and dry ﬁlm thickness starts swelling and
creating the char structure, which oppose a thermal resistance to the heat penetration.
The so-called "end of reaction" point refers to the end of the procedure when the
paint absorbs heat for the intumescent chemical reaction. The exhaustion of this en-
dothermic reaction is the only reason for such a distinct change in the thermal resistance.
Therefore, the peak indicates the point where the chemical reaction stops and the end
of the intumescence of the char. It is identiﬁed as the maximum value of the thermal
resistance within the transient phase.
The "steady point" represents the instant when the gradual decline of the thermal
resistance during the transient phase reaches a steady value, which will be approxi-
mately kept constant during the steady phase. Mathematically, it was identiﬁed as
the maximum curvature point (third derivative equal to zero) of the hypothetical trend
curve of the thermal resistance during the decreasing branch of the transient phase and
the steady phase.
Finally, the "austenitization point" refers to a particular temperature phenomenon
which takes place in the steel at 735oC. At this temperature, a molecular transformation
of steel occurs and, in particular, its structures changes from ferrite to the face-centred
cubic conﬁguration of iron, also know as austenite.
This point is also well-known
as eutectoid temperature and usually it is about 730-735oC [63]. This endothermic
transformation is taken into account by adopting the expression of the thermal capacity
of the steel (equation 2.6) suggested by the Eurocode [11]. Since at the austenitization
point the thermal capacity of the steel is almost 10 times respect to its common value,
61
==PAGE:84==

there is a drop in the derivation of the thermal resistance: the main reason is the fact
that the majority of the heat absorption, and so the thermal insulating properties, are
addressed to the really high thermal capacity of the steel.
4.2.3
Inert phase
Before the intumescent paint reaches its reaction temperature, the dry coating is inert
to the temperature and its thickness is kept to the initial value. During the inert phase
the intumescent coating is slowly melting and increasing its viscosity considerably.
The thermal resistance provided to the steel is minimal and the measured protected
temperature is similar to the unprotected one. However, there a small diﬀerence and it
produces a little insulation to the steel. Unlike the unprotected case, the paint provides
to the steel a thin layer of material characterised by a thermal capacity and thermal
resistance, even if they are really small. Moreover, the intumescent paint changes the
colour of the steel proﬁle specimen and consequently it changes completely its radiative
characteristics, e.g. emissivity.
4.2.4
Transient phase
The transient phase is mainly composed by two branches: a growing branch and a
declining branch, which can be separated at the "end of reaction" critical point.
The ﬁrst branch begins when the intumescent paint reaches its activation tem-
perature: the blowing agent present in the coating is activated and an endothermic
gas-producing reaction starts. At this point the melted layer of intumescent coating
is viscous enough to be penetrated by the gas bubbles produced during the reaction.
The intumescent paint starts swelling and rapidly increases its volume. When all the
virgin material is consumed and the blowing agent is exhausted, the paint looks like
an expanded black multicellular char structure. This point corresponds to the peak of
thermal resistance within the transient phase and the intumescent char is considered
as fully developed. In addition, at this point the char structure is characterised by a
black colour due to the high concentration of carbon binder.
Afterwards, the gradual decline of the thermal resistance is related to the gradual
consumption of the carbon binder, which is the main component constituting the char
structure. During this phase also the char colour changes, starting from black/grey and
becoming white. Furthermore, the consistency of the char structure changes during
this stage: from a cohesive and compact material towards a brittle and lightweight
material. This aspect conﬁrms the fact that the main responsible for the cohesion and
the dark colour is the carbon binder, which is combusted during the declining branch
of the transient phase.
4.2.5
Steady phase
When the endothermic reaction is exhausted because all the combustibles have been
burned, the intumescent coating enters into the so-called steady phase. At this stage
the only contribution to the thermal resistance is provided by the white expanded
char structure. During this phase the thermal resistance is kept to a constant value.
62
==PAGE:85==
CHAPTER 4. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
The steel and the oven/furnace temperature curves are parallel: this means that the
intumescent char is able to keep the thermal increment in the steel equal to the one in
the surrounding environment.
4.2.6
Post-austenitization phase
After the austenitization point the char structure looks like an extremely brittle and
inconsistent material, characterised by a light white colour. At this stage the carbon
binder is completely combusted and with that the char structure is degraded and, in
particular, it has lost its cohesion. During the post-austenitization phase and at high
temperatures, the cracks begins to occur in the char structure. The thermal resistance
starts decreasing from the steady value and an increment in the steel temperature is
recorded. The cracks act as thermal bridges and the char structure does not provide a
good insulation to the steel anymore.
The main crack patterns recorded during the tests are cracks parallel to the ﬂanges
and on the internal portion of the proﬁle, where the ﬂange is attached to the web.
Cracks were also recorded parallel to the web along the proﬁle cut. Furthermore, the
particular crust bubbles in PaintA char structure represent a weakness and one of the
reasons of reduction of thermal resistance.

Inﬂuence of the heating rate on the paint activation tem-

Inﬂuence of the heating rate on diﬀerent thermal resis-

Current design procedures for assessing the amount of intumescent coating on steel
structures exposed to ﬁre are typically determined on the basis of standard ﬁre tests.
The data obtained from these tests are elaborated and design tables are usually provided
to structural engineers by the manufacturers. These tables are usually deﬁned based
on the assumption of an equivalent constant value of thermal resistance of the coating.
The equivalent constant thermal resistance is an intumescent coating characteristic and
it can be estimated implicitly from the design tables.
In this project, the equivalent constant thermal resistance according to the standards
was evaluated for the two intumescent paints. The design tables provided by the two

manufacturers represented the starting point of the analysis. The whole procedure is
explained in detail in Appendix C. The resulting standard equivalent constant thermal
resistances for the two intumescent paints (PaintA and PaintB) are:
- PaintA equivalent constant thermal resistance: RISO,A = 0.117 m2K/W
- PaintB equivalent constant thermal resistance: RISO,B = 0.113 m2K/W
In this section the equivalent constant thermal resistances for the two intumescent
coatings are compared to signiﬁcant and representative values obtained in the elec-
tric oven and gas furnace experiments. The comparison terms are four and they are
described in the followings.
The ﬁrst term of comparison is the total mean value. This term was obtained as the
average value of the intumescent paint thermal resistance with the main three phases:
inert phase, transient phase and steady phase. Also in this case, the post-austenitization
phase was omitted due to its uncertain deﬁnition.
The second term of comparison is the transient phase mean value. This term was
evaluated as the average value of intumescent paint thermal resistance within the tran-
sient phases. It represents an overestimation of the insulating performance, since in this
phase the thermal resistance values are much higher than the rest of the experiment.
The third term of comparison is the steady phase mean value. This term was cal-
culated as the average value of the thermal resistance within the steady phase. At this
point the performance of the intumescent coating has reached a steady state, thus this
gives a safe estimation of its performance.
The last term of comparison is the most frequent mean value. This term was obtained

Conclusions
The current study investigated the diﬀerent aspects that aﬀect the performance of
intumescent coatings and their behaviour in diﬀerent ﬁre scenarios. Three diﬀerent
experimental set-ups representing diﬀerent types of heating exposures were conducted
for two diﬀerent intumescent paints: the solvent-based PaintA and the water-based
PaintB. Interestingly, the various sets of experiments have underlined more diﬀerences
than shared characteristics between the two products.
First of all, the visual physical characteristics of the intumescent char structures
were analysed. Both paints produced a white dusty foam, continuous and homogeneous
along its thickness with a density approximately 2% of the original dry virgin paint one.
The water-based PaintB coating generated a compact and well attached to the steel
substrate char structure with only minimal cracks. On the contrary, the solvent-based
PaintA coating produced brittle char structures characterized by a black crust between
the white foam and the steel substrate. This created open cavities, which represented
weaknesses in the insulation material and the main reason of cracks.
As expected, the two intumescent paints increased their thicknesses up to 80 times
the initial dry ﬁlm thickness. Higher expansion factors were registered for higher heat
ﬂuxes and heating rates and PaintA usually produced slightly higher expansions than
PaintB. The highest expansion factors were obtained for the thinnest dry paint layer
and the lowest expansion factors for the thickest paint layers.
This conﬁrmed the
ability of intumescent coatings to expand and develop their char structures in a better
way when the paint layer is thin and the heat can penetrate through all its thickness.
However, the maximum expansions were recorded for the specimens protected by 1.00
mm of intumescent paint. This thickness represented the best compromise between
performance and eﬃciency for both intumescent coatings.
Surprisingly, ignition phenomena and ﬂames were observed during some experi-
ments. For this reason, ignition tests were conducted on the two products. The results
outlined diﬀerent ﬂammability characteristics of the two intumescent paints. PaintB
was classiﬁed as non-combustible, while PaintA as combustible. The main cause of this
diﬀerence was related to the diﬀerent nature of two products: PaintA was solvent-based,
while PaintB was water-based paint. However, the release of combustible gasses during
the intumescent process was registered for both products. Relating to the ﬂammability
aspect, also a diﬀerent smoke production was recorded.
Regarding the electric oven and the gas furnace set-ups, the insulating performance
of intumescent coatings was investigated by considering the thermal resistance. By
collecting all the punctual values throughout the time, the general trend in the de-

velopment of the thermal resistance was easily recognized in both intumescent paints
subjected to the diﬀerent ﬁre curves. As already proposed by Andersen, the trend was
divided into four general phases, deﬁned according to four critical points. By deﬁning
the activation point, it was found that the activation temperature of the two intumes-
cent coatings was constant in the diﬀerent ﬁre scenarios. In particular, PaintA activated
at slightly higher temperatures than PaintB: 300-320oC for the solvent-based paint and
270-290oC for the water-based one.
Furthermore, it was found that PaintB had better insulating properties than PaintA
for most of the ﬁre exposures. Only for ﬁre curves characterized by really high heating
rates the two performances were comparable. Indeed, the current study surprisingly
underlined a diﬀerent inﬂuence of the heating rate on the two intumescent paints per-
formances. In particular, the performance of PaintA (solved-based) improved with an
increasing heating rate, while the performance of PaintB (water-based) worsened with
increasing heating rate. The heating rate also inﬂuenced the transient phase duration:
shorter transient phases were registered for higher heating rates.
In addition, the equivalent constant thermal resistance according to the ISO stan-
dard test was evaluated for the two intumescent paints. These values were compared to
signiﬁcant and representative values of thermal resistance. It was found that the cur-
rent procedure for the design of intumescent coatings has certain limitations, as diﬀerent
paints have diﬀerent performances according to the composition and the ﬁre scenario.
Nevertheless, the two intumescent coatings were designed according to the same com-
mon practice, which is based on the cellulosic standard ﬁre curve. However, according
to the results obtained during this research study, this exposure characterized by really
high heating rates produced the worst scenario for PaintB insulating performance and,
therefore, the best design case. On the contrary, the standard ﬁre curve represented
the best scenario for PaintA insulating performance and, as a result, PaintA thermal
resistance turned out as overestimated, representing a mistaken design on the unsafe
side.
Regarding the cone heater set-up, the insulating performance of intumescent coat-
ings was investigated by considering an eﬃciency parameter, deﬁned as the temperature
diﬀerence between the unprotected and protected steel divided by the absolute temper-
ature of the unprotected steel.
As expected, it was found that the intumescent coating eﬃciency increased with
increasing heat ﬂuxes and increasing dry ﬁlm thicknesses, both for PaintA and PaintB.
Furthermore, in accordance to the thermal resistance considerations, the results con-
ﬁrmed that in general PaintB eﬃciency is higher than PaintA and this diﬀerence is
more signiﬁcant at low heat ﬂuxes. Moreover, the performance of the steel samples
protected by 1.00 mm and 1.50 mm of dry paint layer were very similar, while the
performance of the thinner layer, 0.50 mm, was slightly lower than the other two. This
aspect highlighted that the intumescent coatings are not very sensible to their applied
thicknesses and the performances with diﬀerent DFTs are usually comparable. In addi-
tion, at low heat ﬂuxes it was possible to better observe the activation of the paints. In
particular, it was observed that the two intumescent coatings have diﬀerent activation
temperatures and PaintA requires more energy than PaintB to initiate the intumescent
process.

Finally, the performance of the two intumescent coatings was compared for all the
cases of the three diﬀerent ﬁre scenarios through eﬃciency considerations.
Firstly,
PaintB intumescent coating had higher eﬃciency values than PaintA in the electric
oven and cone heater set-ups, while for higher heating rates in the gas furnace PaintA
performance was better than PaintB. This aspect agrees with the previous thermal re-
sistance considerations. However, at really high heating rates the previous statement
was not veriﬁed. It was found that the two paints behave diﬀerently for similar high
heatings rates, but diﬀerent ﬁre exposure. The results indicated that the paint per-
formances are inﬂuenced also by the nature of the ﬁre exposure, such as radiative or
convective heat transfer and not only by the temperature gradient.
Lastly, it was highlighted that the paint performance increased with increasing heat-
ing rates, but each intumescent coating has a diﬀerent sensitivity to low heating rates.
In particular, while PaintB performance decreased gradually with decreasing heating
rates, the paint still developed reasonable insulating properties at really low heating
rates. On the contrary, at really low heating rates PaintA did not activate at all. Actu-
ally, it degraded and developed insulating properties worse than the non-reactive virgin
paint layer.
As an overall conclusion, the current study has conﬁrmed that the intumescent
coating behaviour is strongly inﬂuenced by many characteristics of the exposing ﬁre
scenario. In addition, the performance of these passive ﬁre protection materials is also
dependent on the chemical composition of the paint and, in particular, if the coating is
composed of a water or a solvent based mixture.

Future studies
The research presented in this thesis represents an attempt at understanding the diﬀer-
ent aspects inﬂuencing intumescent coatings performance under various ﬁre conditions.
The project focused on many features of intumescent paints and ﬁre exposures, but
future research will be necessary to improve understanding as well as to expand the
results to all relevant ﬁre scenarios.
First of all, the same experiments should be conducted on other intumescent prod-
ucts available in the market with diﬀerent compositions and produced by diﬀerent
manufacturers. The performances of other water-based and solvent-based intumescent
coatings should be tested and compared in order to conﬁrm or contradict the theory
exposed in the current study. It is recommended that further research should also fo-
cus on the analysis of the chemical composition. In particular, the future work should
aim at understanding which components and quantities can inﬂuence the intumescent
coatings performance under diﬀerent ﬁre conditions.
In addition, a future work should concentrate on ﬁnding a technique aimed to eval-
uate the intumescent coating surface temperature in the mass loss cone heater set-up,
for instance through the use of thermographic phosphor equipment. In this way, inter-
esting conclusions could be drawn from the direct comparison of the thermal resistances
obtained in diﬀerent experimental set-ups.
Moreover, in this study, intumescent coatings were exposed to several ﬁre curves with
diﬀerent heating rates or heat ﬂuxes. The unprotected steel temperature curves for all

the ﬁre exposures implemented in the three diﬀerent experimental set-ups are shown in
Fig. 4.30. Regarding the tests in heating chambers, the "ISO curve" implemented in
the gas furnace is much faster and more extreme than the "fast" curve in the electric
oven. It is advisable that a future work should expose steel samples to temperature
time-curves between the previously described ones. In this way, it should be possible
to obtain a general understanding of the intumescent coating performance for a huge
variety of heating rates, from the cellulosic standard ﬁre curve to the much slower ones.
Further studies are also needed to investigate the behaviour of intumescent coatings
under diﬀerent types of heating. For instance, real ﬁres with ﬂames can be very far from
these simulated conditions. Therefore, new sets of experiments should be conducted,
in which coated steel specimens are exposed to real ﬁre conditions. The importance
of ﬂame characteristics and the real ﬁre properties can be studied generating small
real ﬁres, for example by burning wooden sticks in a ﬂashover room.
In this way,
the inﬂuence of other aspects, such as the ﬂame exposure, the ﬁre source distance
and the steel proﬁle position (parallel or orthogonal to the ﬂame development) can be
investigated more accurately.
The common practice for testing the ﬁre protection materials and, in particular,
intumescent coatings is through an external ﬁre exposure, for instance in a heating
chamber or in front of a radiant panel. However, an interesting and unique ﬁre condition
is represented by an internal exposure. The steel element can be heated up through
conduction from the adjacent components, which are directly subjected to a real ﬁre
exposure. Therefore, further experimental investigations are needed to examine this
unique type of ﬁre exposure.
Future studies on the current topic are also required in order to clarify the charac-
teristics of the "post-austenitization phase", since the intumescent coating behaviour
at really high temperatures is still not completely understood.
In addition, special
attention should be paid to the crack development during this phase. Furthermore,
future work should concentrate in analysing the diﬀerent aspects that could inﬂuence
the crack development of the intumescent coating char structure, e.g. the steel proﬁle
type (open or closed section).
Furthermore, all these considerations should be also conﬁrmed by full-scale tests:
large beams, columns or steel frames protected by intumescent coatings should be ex-
posed to several ﬁre scenarios.
Lastly, two other aspects related to the intumescent coatings should be investigated.
The ﬁrst one is the inﬂuence of loads and resulting steel deformations on the paint per-
formance. These factors are expected to create weaknesses in the insulation and aﬀect
the crack development in the char structure formation. Secondly, future research should
deal with the smoke produced by the charring of the intumescent coatings, which of-
ten produces toxic or non-environmentally friendly components during the intumescent
process. The latter aspect can increase the safe application of the intumescent coatings
and extend their applicability in the industry.

Appendix C
Nomogramma
It is quite uncommon for the intumescent coating producer companies to provide to
the ﬁre safety engineers an equivalent constant thermal resistance for the design. The
equivalent constant resistance evaluated according to the standard ﬁre test [32] is usually
an intermediate result which is used to elaborate design tables.
Nowadays manufactures usually provide design tables to structural engineers. In
these tables the minimum required dry ﬁlm thicknesses (DFT) of intumescent coatings
are presented according to the ﬁre resistance class (R30, R45, R60...), the design tem-
perature, the section factor, the proﬁle type (open or closed sections) and the element
type (mainly beams or columns).
The so-called "Nomogramma", developed by the "Commissione per la Sicurezza
delle Costruzioni in Acciaio in caso d’Incendio" [64] represents a very useful and easy
graphical method aimed to assess the ﬁre resistance of steel structures (Fig. C.1). This
graph relates the ﬁre resistance class of a steel component to its design temperature:
starting from these two parameters, it is possible to evaluate the section factor As/Vs for
unprotected sections or the product between the section factor As/Vs and the thermal
conductivity λp/dp of the insulation material for protected sections. This method can
also be overturned in order to obtain other parameters, starting from diﬀerent known
characteristics.
In this project, the "Nomogramma" was used to evaluate the equivalent thermal
resistance of intumescent coatings, starting from a well-known ﬁre resistance class,
design temperature, section factor and minimum required dry ﬁlm thickness.
The ISO equivalent constant thermal resistance for the two diﬀerent intumescent
coatings was evaluated by using the "Nomogramma" and the proper design tables [Fire
Resistance Classes R30, R60 and R90 - beams and columns, open sections (H and I);
section factor: As/Vs=175m−1; required dry ﬁlm thickness: dp ≃1000µm].
The resulting values for the two intumescent coating paints (PaintA and PaintB)
are:
- PaintA: RISO,A = 0.117 m2K/W
- PaintB: RISO,B = 0.113 m2K/W
