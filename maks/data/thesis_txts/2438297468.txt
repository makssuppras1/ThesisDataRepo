Abstract
Coiled tubing CT is a widely used technology in the oil and gas industry. The employment
of this technique for the exploration of long horizontal wells has highlighted limitations on
the currently HSLA steel used for the tubing. The need of resistant but at the same time
light materials arises. Composite materials due to their good Young’s modulus/density ratio
could be considered as a valid alternative to steel for this application.
A feasibility study has been started by the ”Danish Hydrocarbon research and Technology
Centre” of Denmark Tekniske Universitet DTU on behalf of Maersk Oil. This project starts
from the results obtained in [1] and wants to proceed in a deeper analysis of composite coiled
tubing. Due to the possibility of composite materials to be tailor-made to exhibit particular
mechanical properties, analysis tools need to be developed to test the behaviour of CCT
under internal/external loading as well as bending. FEM models in ANSYS APDL are built
to attain this purposes.
After an initial analysis of the composite lay-ups proposed in [1], a CCT design process
is undertaken to ﬁnd a suitable conﬁguration that satisﬁes the project prerequisites: total
distance along the whole depth of 25000 ft, sustain burst (45 MPa) and collapse (20.7 MPa)
pressure and capability to be spooled in 4 m diameter reel. The accomplishment of the
requirements is assessed through the evaluation of material failure. Maximum Stress and
Tsai-Wu failure criteria are applied.
Four conﬁgurations made by Carbon/PEEK are suggested for the analysis. All of them
succeeded in the reach and loading requirements but failed on the bending test. The best
conﬁguration considered is [30/-30/15/-15/10/10/-15/15/-30/30] able to travel for 25.750 ft
and to be bent on a 7 m diameter reel. The high longitudinal stiﬀness Ex of the laminate
lay-up necessary to reach long horizontal distances considerably reduces the ﬂexibility of the
tubing, requiring the application of a bigger dimension spooling reel.
On the basis of the stress results of the bending analysis, a fatigue life estimation is performed
on the selected CCT conﬁguration.
Due to the impossibility to retriever relevant data
regarding the S-N curves of a compression-compression test, strong assumptions are made for
the construction of the curves. Approximately 600-700 bending cycles are estimated using
this approach. This outcome is not in line with the expectations of enhanced fatigue life
given by composite materials. Experimental tests should be performed to get more reliable
results.
Despite the conﬁgurations studied have only partially achieved the requirements, the FEM
tool developed could be reused and improved in the future for further analysis on CCT.
A ﬁnal attempt to model the behaviour on a full scale well model is performed. The high
number of elements generated by the mesh has prevented the continuation of the testing.

1
Introduction
Despite the intensive worldwide exploration eﬀorts during the last several decades, most of
the petroleum basins are approaching or have already reached their maturity. More than
three-quarters of current worldwide hydrocarbon production is obtained by optimising the
recovery from existing ﬁelds [2]. The actual challenge for the oil and gas industry is to iden-
tify and develop strategies that would maximise the value of established existing reserves.
Many methods have been developed to enhance the wellbore intervention in particular for
the exploitation of horizontal wells. One of the most famous and frequently used methods
is the Coiled Tubing (CT) intervention technique.
Coiled tubing is a technology developed in the 1950s for the purpose of service and work-over
applications in the oil and gas industry. Whilst these operations still account for the 75%
of the coiled tubing use, several technical advancements had opened its utilisation also to
drilling and completion applications.
The operational concept of the coiled tubing system involves the deployment of a continuous
pipeline that can run for kilometers into the wellbore. These tubular structures are used to
perform diﬀerent tasks. From a reel, the tubing is bent over a tubing guide arch and driven
into a tubing injector. Here a hydraulic drive tubing injector takes the tubing and pushes
it into the wellbore. When the service is completed, the small diameter tubing is retrieved
from the wellbore, spooled and stored on a large diameter reel. An example of coiled tubing
system is shown in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Coiled tubing system
In the late 1980s, researches and experiments have shown the possibility to exploit oil and
gas reserves otherwise inaccessible through the use of the coiled tubing (CT) technology on
horizontal wells. According to [3], in 2001 commercial databases were contained records of
about more than 34.000 horizontal wells not completely exploited in 72 countries around the
Page 1 of 106
==PAGE:12==
Master thesis DTU
1. INTRODUCTION
world, most of which in Canada (18.005) and U.S.A (111.344). By the way, the long dis-
tances reached needed in horizontal wells imposes some limitation to the CT technology used.
The conventional coiled tuning is made mainly by HSLA steel (High Strength Low Alloy),
a type of alloy steel that provides better mechanical performances compared to carbon steel
and good resistance to corrosion. In spite of the technological improvements in the HSLA
steel for coiled tubing applications, weaknesses still persist in its operational functionality,
especially into horizontal wells.
The major issues are represented by the large axial compressive load and the pressures ex-
perienced into the well. As the tubing goes deeper, the forces and pressures increase rapidly
leading to a possible mechanical failure of the system. In addition to this, the heaviness of
the steel CT accentuates considerably the friction forces between the tube and wellbore wall
in the horizontal sections, causing early buckling.
An attempt to overcome the problems showed out with the use of CT steel is represented by
the application of composite materials for the realisation of coiled tubular. The necessity of
light but at the same time, resistant materials leads to the employment of composites with
coiled tubing purposes. In fact composite materials can have good mechanical properties
in terms of Young’s modulus E but at a lower density, as shown in Figure 1.2. This is a
considerable advantage considering the horizontal well applications.
Figure 1.2: Relation between Young’s modulus and density in materials - CES Edupack 2017
In addition to the common advantages attributed to composite materials, composite coiled
tubing (CCT) could provide important advantages in terms of working conditions, produc-
tivity and operational safety, as listed in the following.
• Pressure service
During wellbore service operations, the HSLA steel tubing is subjected to plastic de-
formation events that limits its pressure-cycled lifetime. Due to the anisotropic nature

of composite materials, the tubing can be tailored to sustain certain critical loads
(burst and collapse pressure) without operating on the geometry as usually performed
with isotropic metals. Simply changing the orientation of the ﬁbres, the mechanical
properties of the tube can be considerably enhanced for the purpose of the application.
• Corrosion resistance
During wellbore operations, service ﬂuids are pumped into the well through the tubing.
The acid composition of these ﬂuids is very dangerous because it induces progressive
damage to the steel tubing. CCT can be constructed of corrosion resistant materials,
reducing or even eliminating these tenacious chemical inhibitions.
• Design for purpose
In general, composite materials are constructed with multiple layers of materials ac-
cording to the speciﬁc strength requirements of the tube. However, not all the layers
may have a structural purpose. For example, an external coating with high-wear re-
sistance can be used in order to protect the CT and enhance its lifetime. Another
interesting implementation is the so-called ”Smart CCT” string.
Because CCT is
made of non-metallic materials, conductive wires can be integrated into the laminate
along the length of the tubing, improving signiﬁcantly the logging and monitoring of
downhole operations.
• Tube weight
As previously mentioned, HSLA steel coiled tubing has limitations regarding the total
distance travelled in horizontal wells due to the frictional drag generated by the tubing
weight. Depending on the composite material used, the weight per foot of CCT can
be from 25% to 40% of that of equivalent sizes of steel tubing [4].
• Service life
During its working life, CT has to withstand to a great number of bending cycles. In
Figure 1.3 are shown the bending events that the coiled tubing must endure.

HSLA steel coiled tubing has shown in diﬀerent studies to be particularly inﬂuenced
by the plastic deformations imposed upon the string by these bending events [4], [5].
The employment of composite materials for coiled tubing has shown the capability of
exceeding the service life of conventional steel coiled tubing by a factor of ten or more
[4]. In fact composites behave diﬀerently from HSLA steel when subjected to bending
events. The stresses/strains generated by bending do not result in plastic deformation
with the same degree as isotropic materials. Only minimal deformations can be seen
after extensive use of the tubing.
The ﬁrst attempt of implementation of CCT is represented by the Anaconda system in the
late 1990s [6]. In this project, a carbon/epoxy tubular has been exploited for inaccessible
reserves on the North Sea. The system failed due to bending problems between the Epoxy
and the inner thermoplastic liner. Micro-cracking and fractures appear because of the brittle
nature of the Epoxy matrix. Subsequently, other tests were performed as reported in [7],
obtaining good results for what concern the fatigue life and working pressures.
The composite material with a CT application represents still a very wide and shady ﬁeld.
The great variety of ﬁbres and matrices that can be used, in addition to the conﬁgurations
that the laminate can assume (in terms of ﬁbre angles and thicknesses), allows a huge margin
of optimization with CT purposes.
From the beginning of its development, Maersk Oil has been involved in the research of
composite coiled tubing.
Recently a feasibility study has been initiated by the ”Danish
Hydrocarbon research and Technology Centre” of Denmark Tekniske Universitet DTU on
behalf of Maersk Oil. A preliminary study has been undertaken by Andrei Costache and
Christian Berggreen from the DTU Mechanical Department [1], [8] .
Tha aim of this project is to run through the design process described in the paper ”Compos-
ite Coiled Tubing for extended reach in horizontal oil wells” [1] proposed by Andrei Costache
and Christian Berggreen and bring some considerations in particular with regard to the load-
ing conditions. The main purpose of this paper is to highlight and discuss some inﬂuential
parameters on the design of CCT with the aim of horizontal long distances.

3.1
Classical Laminate Theory
The overall mechanical behaviour of an orthotropic laminate is a function of the material
properties and stacking sequence of the individual plies. Classical Laminate Theory (CLT)
is the most common method applied to analyse it. A brief description of the CLT is depicted
below.
Classical Laminate Theory is based on the following assumptions:
• The laminate consists of a perfectly bonded layers: there is no slip between the adjacent
layers;
• Each lamina is quasi-homogeneous and orthotropic;
• Each lamina is in a state of plane stress;
• All the displacements are small compared to the thickness of the laminate.
The laminate can be made of several layers, each with its ﬁbre orientation. A local coordinate
system (LCS) describes the direction of the ﬁbres in each layer, whilst a global coordinate
system (GCS) is used for all the layers of the laminate. The ﬁbre angle θ deﬁnes the rotation
around the z-3 axis of the local coordinate system from the global coordinate system. When
θ = 0, the LCS coincides with the GCS. These coordinate systems are shown in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Local coordinate system (1,2,3) and global coordinate system (x,y,z).
Each layer needs four independent material constants that specify its structural response.
These parameters are the Young’s modulus in the longitudinal and transverse direction E1
and E2, the Poisson’s ratio µ12 and the shear modulus G12. Using these constants for a 0◦
lamina, the generalised Hook’s law gives the stress-strain relationship in the local coordinate
system (1,2,3) of the lamina:

3.3
Failure criteria
The suitability of the CCT conﬁguration is assessed by determining the eﬀect of the stresses
inside the thickness of the tube. In the case of composite materials, the stresses do not vary
linearly as for an isotropic material but are purely dependent on the ﬁbre orientation. Here
comes the importance of applying a failure criteria to control that the stresses do not exceed
the limit strengths given by the material properties.
In this project, a static material failure is assessed using two failure criteria already imple-
mented into the software ANSYS APDL. The two criteria are:
• Maximum Stress failure criterion
• Tsai-Wu failure criterion
The failure evaluation is made through the calculation of the failure index, an useful tool to
understand the material stress condition. The usual approach to evaluate it consists on the
calculation of stresses/strains in the local coordinate system of each lamina and then apply
the appropriate failure criteria to compare the stresses/strains found with the allowable val-
ues from the material. If the failure index IF > 1, it means that failure has occurred on the
material. This approach is only capable to determine the ﬁrst-ply failure, therefore even if
the index failure of one ply exceeds 1, the whole laminate is assumed to have failed. In the
real case, a coeﬃcient bigger than 1 represents only the starting of material failure and not
the ultimate rupture of the component.

3.4
Fatigue
The evaluation of the fatigue life of classical engineering materials is a diﬃcult role, but
even more diﬃcult is to make predictions regarding the working like of composite materials.
A composite lamina or laminate has many failure modes and fracture mechanics that are
dependent on the material of ﬁbre/matrix used and ﬁbre orientations.
Diﬀerent techniques can be used to predict the durability of a material. In this thesis, the
stress life approach is applied at composite coiled tubing. This is a widespread method based
on the evaluation of the so called ”W¨ohler curve” or ”S-N curve”.
Test specimens are cyclically loaded between a maximum and minimum stress level, σmax and
σmin until failure occurs. The stress amplitude σa and the number of cycles N to failure are
then represented in the S-N curve. In the plot, the total life of the specimen is represented,
where total life implies the total number of cycles that the dominant fatigue crack needs to
bring failure. In Figure 3.5 is shown the nomenclature for constant stress amplitude loading.

3.4.1
Fatigue on composites
Diﬀerently from isotropic materials, composites are aﬀected by other parameters in addition
to the above mentioned [11],[13]:
• Fibre property eﬀects
For unidirectional ﬁber composites, high strength and high stiﬀness composites result
also in great fatigue strength. The high stiﬀness ﬁbers limit the strains in the matrix
preventing the initiation of matrix cracks that could initiate the failure. Usually a high
static strength is a prerequisite of high fatigue strength;
• Matrix property eﬀects
The resin used for bonding the ﬁbres plays an important role on fatigue strength. Ma-
trix materials that have high failure strains improve considerably the fatigue resistance.
Important with this regard it is also the adherence of the matrix to the ﬁbres.
• Fibre fraction eﬀects
Decreasing the ﬁbre percentage, the fatigue life can be drastically reduced due to the
reduction of the material stiﬀness.

4.2
Element type
The composite tube model is developed employing only one type element, namely SOLSH190.
This is a combination of solid and shell elements perfectly suitable for composite material
modelling. It is designed for structures with a wide range of thicknesses (from thin to thick).
SOLSH190 element possesses the continuum solid element topology and features 8-node
connectivity. The element has 3 degrees of freedom DOFs for each node: translations in the
x, y and z directions. The shell properties permit the layered applications, such as modelling
laminates, storing the data for all the layers. The element can store up to 250 layers inside,
and the accuracy of the solution can be enhanced using an increased number of integration
point through the plies. By default, the software considers 3 of them, and this is also the
option used for the calculations.

2. Recall element solution: ”RSYS,SOLU”

The cause of this marked diﬀerence between Maximum Stress and Tsai-Wu failure criteria
could rely on the way how the methods calculate the failure index. While Maximum Stress
considers the stresses singularly to evaluate the failure ratio, Tsai-Wu uses all the stress
components as shown in Eq 3.17.
In this way, the calculation of IF is highly dependent on the sign of the stresses created by
the loading case and the orientation of the ﬁbres. Considering a unidirectional 0◦lamina
on the cylinder thickness, under collapse pressure, the load generates a stress compression
on the hoop and radial direction while tension in the axial direction. Burst pressure causes
instead stress in tension along the hoop direction whilst the radial and axial stress are in
compression.
Under pressure loading, the hoop stress represents the highest contributor to the calculation
of the IF and a negative value drops the coeﬃcient for the Tsai-Wu criterion, determining a
big reduction compared to Maximum Stress.
Moreover, also the angle orientation can have a big inﬂuence with this regard. Changing the
ﬁbre orientation, the hoop stress contribution generated is distributed diﬀerently in the plies
as already seen in [20]. The calculation of the B coeﬃcient in Eq 3.19 is therefore highly
inﬂuenced by the rotation of the local stresses.

Observation regarding the results from failure criteria
From the description of the failure criteria and other analysis found in literature [21], it was
expected bigger values of the failure indices in Tsai-Wu than Maximum Stress, due to its
full consideration of the stresses in the calculation. Only in two cases (burst pressure - case
3 and 11) this correspondence has been seen. For the rest of the cases, Maximum Stress
failure criterion has registered higher values.
The reason for this outcome could rely on the ﬁbre orientation and stacking sequence of
the laminate.
In a study regarding the failure analysis of a composite cylinder [20], it
is evidenced the dependence of Tsai-Wu failure index on the stacking sequence and angle
orientation. Considering a symmetrical [θ, −θ]s and an anti-symmetrical [θ, −θ]2 lay-up, they
studied the behaviour of the two failure criteria varying the angle θ under the application of
an internal pressure. Whilst Maximum Stress is almost unaﬀected by the stacking sequence
utilised, in Tsai-Wu variations are seen rotating the ﬁbres between 40◦and 75◦. In addition
to this, for angles between 0◦and 40◦the two criteria are almost in accordance, at 40◦-60◦
Tsai-Wu is higher and then at 60◦-90◦Maximum Stress calculates higher values.
This study shows that due to its complex nature, composite materials can behave diﬀerently
according to their composition. Therefore, it is not possible to state clearly which failure
criteria could predict the highest failure index.
5.5
Conclusions
It is clear from the results of the pressure tests that the burst pressure has a great inﬂuence
on the CCT design process. The composite tube has to be able to carry a higher internal
than external pressure limiting the design choices of the composite lay-up. In fact, the cases
that did not pass the test failed because the layers closest to the inner radius were not able
to sustain the load in the hoop direction. To avoid this stress concentration a 90◦initial layer
was suggested in the ﬁrst four conﬁgurations. The tests have shown that the assumption
that the ﬁrst layer carries most of the stresses cannot be considered enough on the design of a
composite tube. If also the subsequent layers in the laminate are not thick enough to reduce
the stress, the tube can fail as demonstrated by Case 1.
The stacking sequence applied in
cases 5-12 has also shown a dependence on the layer thickness.
Discrepancies are seen through the evaluation of the two failure criteria, in particular with
the application of an external load.
Due to the impossibility to retriever experimental data regarding the materials during this
project, it is hard to argue on which failure criteria is better to rely. For the further analysis
both the methods of failure estimation are used and the highest values are considered to
maintain a certain margin of safety.

6.4
Conclusions
The analysis has shown that none of the cases analysed has failed under combined axial and
pressure loading. Diﬀerent and opposite trends of the failure indices are seen at the incre-
ment of the axial force under the addition of pressure. Combination of compressive axial
load and burst pressure is seen to be the worst case due to the high hoop stresses generated
by the superposition of the two loading eﬀects.

Bending simulation
Into the well, the tubing could also experience bending events given by the formation of si-
nusoidal or/and helical buckling. However, during the CCT design process, this information
cannot be taken into account. What can be considered are the bending events that charac-
terised the use of CT. As shown in Figure 7.1, the tubing is spooled oﬀand on from the reel
at each wellbore operation. Six bending events can be identiﬁed during the deployment of
the tubing, but two of these can be seen as more relevant in terms of strains generated.
Figure 7.1: Coiled tubing bending events
In this thesis, it is studied the bending event that characterised the tubing when spooled
on the reel (bending event 6). Laying on the reel, the tube has the side in contact with the
drum in a compressive stress state, while the other side is in tension. This stress distribution
generated by the curvature of the reel can also be altered by the application of other loads.
As previously mentioned in Section 6, during the tubing deployment, a tensile axial load is
also applied by the system to tighten the tubing, whilst an additional tensile hoop stress is
given by the internal ﬂuid pressure.
In CCT design, a test is needed to evaluate the material failure under bending condition and
also combined loading. It is elementary thinking that using big reel dimensions would solve
the problems related to the stresses generated by the curvature. However, there are practi-
cal needs to limit the maximum diameter of the spooling reel due to onshore transportation
constrains such as bridges and tunnels. With this regard, the aim of be able to spool the
tubing on a 4 m reel is inserted in the requirements of the study.

posite cylinder, it is added a curved surface to represent the reel where the tube is bent.
The radius of curvature of the surface is a parametric value and it is varied during the anal-
ysis according to the results from the failure criteria. Between the tube and reel model, it
is deﬁned as a contact surface in order to consider the contact pressure generated by the

7.3
FEM bending simulation
The FEM model built is now used to simulate the bending behaviour of composite tubes. The
selected cases from the previous analysis are tested on a 4 m diameter reel. This simulation
is performed to verify the ﬂexibility of the composite conﬁguration under a certain radius of
curvature. In this stage, failure criteria are applied to the bent tube to check material failure.
The study can be divided into three test stages according to the outcome of the analysis:
1. Bending
2. Bending + axial tightening
3. Bending + axial tightening + internal pressure
The ﬁrst test is a pure bending simulation. If the outcome form both the failure criteria
is positive, a second test is run adding axial tightening on the composite tube. A tensile
axial force is applied on the left edge of the tube, in opposite direction to the rotation of
the reel. If also the results of this test are positive, a combined loading is applied to the tube.
Due to it is not known the magnitude of the tensile force applied by the reel system, it is
assumed in the analysis a 70 kN load. The average internal pressure is instead around 3000
psi (≈20.7 MPa) according to [22]. To consider the worst pressure case, a 45 MPa internal
pressure is applied.

CCT design
The previous tests have shown limitations regarding the actual performances of the con-
ﬁgurations proposed in [1]. None of the cases analysed has succeeded in the simulations
performed.
According to the results obtained, the most critical part in the design process is represented
by the capability of the CCT to be spooled on a 4 m diameter reel. The high longitudinal
modulus Ex required for the horizontal reaching limits the ﬂexibility of the tubing, making
necessary the use of bigger spooling reels. In all the cases, at least one of the failure indices of
the laminate has exceeded the limit value 1. Using Maximum Stress failure criteria, mostly
in the outer layers of the tube, values above 2 are registered, meaning that the stresses gen-
erated by the curvature are twice of the ones allowed. Increasing the bending radius until
both the criteria indicate safety, reels with 8.5-9 m diameter should be used to store the

In this Section, a study of new possible conﬁgurations that could withstand the requirements
presented in Section 2, is performed using the ANSYS model built.
The design process starts with the evaluation of the maximum distance obtainable with the
selected laminate conﬁguration. With this purpose, the Classical Laminate Theory has been
integrated into the ReachCalculation MATLAB code provided for the project. Using this
tool, the parameters that deﬁne the composite tube can be easily changed to perform studies
of the tube geometry. In the previous version of the ReachCalculation script, the bending
stiﬀness EI, submerged weight We and tube outer diameter OD had to be calculated sepa-
rately, converted to Imperial units and inserted in the MATLAB script.
Firstly, using the new MATLAB code, several conﬁgurations are manually selected and tested
to verify the fulﬁlment of the requirement of total distance travelled along the whole depth.
According to the wellbore information provided, the CCT has to travel at least a distance
of 18000 ft in the horizontal section to overcome the minimum distance requested. The ﬁrst
concern is thus to ﬁnd a lay-up conﬁguration that is capable to attain this distance on the
horizontal sector.
Once this condition is satisﬁed, the conﬁguration is applied to the composite tube and sim-
ulated with burst and collapse pressures as well as axial loads in ANSYS. Lastly, if the
previous tests have succeeded, the conﬁguration is bent with a curvature of 2 m to check
that the stresses do not exceed the limit values imposed by the material speciﬁcs.

9.2
Conclusions
In this Section, it is shown how the stress results from the bending simulations can be used to
obtain additional information about the CCT designed. Knowing the unidirectional fatigue
S-N curves of the composite material used, the number of cycles of the tubing bent on the
reel can be easily obtained. Here, the estimation of the fatigue life of conﬁguration T4 is
performed very roughly. Due to the lack of relevant information, strong assumptions have
been made in order to create a compression-compression S-N curve for Carbon/PEEK.
The life estimation for the tubing spooled on a 7 m diameter reel shows that the CCT is
going to fail at a low number of cycles (600-700). On one side, this results could be considered
poorly reliable due to it is known as the good fatigue performances of composite materials.
According to [4], a CCT in Kevlar with 1.5” OD and 6.5’ diameter, under varied pressure,
can be bent over a 6.5-foot diameter reel for 20000-80000 cycles. In the previous analysis,
this order of cycles is obtained only using a reel 1.50 m bigger than the one designed. On the
other side, the necessity to orient the ﬁbres mostly with angles between 0◦and 30◦may have
highly reduced the fatigue performances of the CCT. Using strong longitudinal layers, the
bending stiﬀness of the tubing is enhanced, but at the cost of a lower ﬂexibility. A natural
consequence of this, as seen in the study above, it the failure of the ﬁbres in the longitudinal
direction due to the high compressive/tensile stresses generated by the curvature.
An real experimental fatigue test on the conﬁguration should be performed to gather reliable
information about the performances of the composite tube.

10.1
Introduction
Well simulation has a fundamental role on the oil gas drilling. Before its employment, the
tubing is usually tested with particular software such as Cerberus, developed by National
Oilwell Varco NOV. Cerberus modelling software can accurately predict and analyse cumu-
lative forces at each stage of the coiled tubing deployment into the well, helping the user in
the CT design.
In this thesis, an attempt to model the behaviour of the coiled tubing inside the well is
performed, to create an useful tool also for the composite employment in this ﬁeld. As the
rest of the simulations, the model has been studied with the ﬁnite element software ANSYS
The study obtained poor results, but the methodology applied and the issues found are
however reported.
10.2
Modelling description
Inside the well, the tubing is permanently immersed into a ﬂuid that could be mud either
seawater. After a preliminary study regarding the possibilities that ANSYS APDL oﬀers
for the interaction solid-ﬂuid elements, it is concluded that the software do not oﬀer enough
options to perform it. A solid-solid approach is therefore chosen.
The idea is to built two concentric cylinders, one representing the tubing and the other the
space between the tubing and the well wall. Between the two volumes there is contact with
a friction coeﬃcient of ν = 0.2. An initial clearance is deﬁned between the two cylinders
in order to not have always friction, especially in the vertical part. One cylinder is made
of composite material as deﬁned in the previous Sections, while the other is made with a
material with low stiﬀness (E = 0.1 GPa) so that it can be easily deformed with the contact.
The external cylinder is clamped on its outer surface. The geometry has the dimensions
reported in Section 2, as shown in Figure 10.4.

Through a displacement control, the tubing is inserted into the well. The composite tube
is built parallel to the vertical section with an initial penetration into it. It has a length

Issues during the development
After deﬁning the geometry/elements/materials, a rough mesh is generated in the model.
Due to the diﬀerences in terms of dimensions between the composite tube geometry (outer
diameter OD = 60 mm) and a total length of the well (7.64 km), more than 1 million of
elements are created. The program took more than 1 hour to generate the mesh, meaning
Page 85 of 106
==PAGE:96==
Master thesis DTU

that the time needed later to solve the system would have been too long for any purpose.
A possibility to reduce the total number of elements is seen with the employment of super-
elements. This is a particular feature of ANSYS that permits to group several elements in
only one, saving computational heaviness. For each super-element, the nodes for which the
results have to be calculated need to be deﬁned. For example, in the cylinder that represents
the wellbore, only the internal nodes of the model are activated due to the necessity of cal-
culating friction. The external ones that are clamped are not considered into the calculation
of the super-element matrix.
Implementing also this technique, some problems with the deﬁnition of the super-elements
have emerged. Apparently, the operation of creation of these elements is too heavy for the
computer used that stopped the ANSYS program. Other attempts were performed with a
more powerful machine but the same result is seen.
Due to the issues found during the development of this test, it is concluded that the AN-
SYS version used cannot perform simulations on big scales that employ a huge number of
elements. However, it is not excluded that there is the possibility to make it works. The
limited time of this master thesis did not permit to ﬁnd a solution that could be instead
developed in future works.
The possibility to reduce the dimension of the well were not taken into account during the
study due to the necessity to see the possible buckling behaviour of the tubing.

Conclusions and recommendations
11.1
Conclusions
Using the FEM model built in ANSYS on the conﬁguration proposed in [1], it has brought
to several outcomes.
Firstly, it is emerged that burst pressure represents a major issue on the CCT design. While
all the cases considered easily carried the collapse pressure, many of them failed in a burst
scenario. The combination of burst and collapse pressure brings to a reduction of the total
hoop stresses due to the superposition of the two diﬀerent stress eﬀects. To avoid burst
failure, the application of an inner 90◦oriented layer was suggested in Case 1-2-3-4. Case 1
failed because the circumferential stress experienced on the second layer was too high. The
inner layer did not manage to reduce enough the stress and avoid the material failure of the
0◦in the transverse direction. The safety of the composite laminate under internal pressure
cannot be assessed using only this assumption, but of course, it could help to reduce the
circumferential stresses through the thickness. While under burst pressure are quite small,
relevant discrepancies are seen evaluating failure criteria with Maximum Stress and Tsai-Wu
in a collapse scenario. This is most likely given by the diverse formulation of the two failure
criteria and the direction of the stresses generated.
The application of axial loading on the composite tube has shown diﬀerent behaviours in
the calculation of the material failure, depending on direction of the load applied. At the
increment of a tensile axial load, the burst pressure reduces the maximum IF calculated
whereas opposite tend is seen under collapse pressure. Applying a compressive axial loading
instead, the burst pressure increases the IF while collapse pressure decreases it.
These
diﬀerent trends could be given by the superposition of the stress states induced by the
loading.
Using the FEM model to simulate the bending event of the tubing on the reel, all the cases
failed with a 2 m radius of curvature.
With a MATLAB script that combines Classical Laminate Theory and ”ReachCalculation”
analysis, new lay-up proposals are made. The material chosen is Carbon/PEEK because
more performing for long horizontal reach purposes. The conﬁguration found that performs
better in terms of ﬂexibility is [30/-30/15/-15/10/10/-15/15/-30/30] with OD = 60 mm and
ID = 25 mm. However, the minimum bending radius of the conﬁguration is 3.5 m, more than
the 2 m wanted. The high longitudinal stiﬀness of the laminate considerably reduces the
ﬂexibility, as shown in a study performed varying the ﬁbre angle in [θ, −θ]4 and [(θ, −θ)2]s
lay-ups. The optimal angle for bending is around 45◦, whereas to fulﬁl the reach requirement
angles under 25◦are needed.
Based on the results of the bending simulation, an estimation of the fatigue life of the tubing
is performed. Around 600-700 are expected for T4 conﬁguration under 3.5 m radius bend-
ing. Due to the strong assumptions made to do this study, the results obtained could be
not considered reliable. Using proper data of the S-N curves, the estimation could be more
accurate and the assumptions made could be also veriﬁed.
The attempt to model the behaviour on a full scale well mode did not bring any result. The
high number of elements generated in the model (1200000) prevented a further development.

for its application. On the other hand, the high cost of Carbon/PEEK could discourage
this eventuality. In case, other cheaper but suitable materials should be selected and
tested.
• Development of a well simulation in ANSYS
Other attempts should be made in the future trying to simulate the behaviour of the
tubing into a well model. An eﬀective solution to reduce the number of elements should
be found. After that, the implementation of more complex loads is needed.
