Abstract
The frequency response of pressure gradient microphones depends on the distance to
the source as well as the type of source (radiation pattern), leading to a boost in the
low frequency range when the microphone is placed close the source. This is called the
proximity eﬀect and it is a unique property of pressure gradient microphones when exposed
to a spherical wave. However, conventional loudspeakers exhibit a spherical wave behavior
only in the far ﬁeld, where proximity eﬀects are less prominent. The reason of deviation
from this behavior in the near ﬁeld mainly has to do with the dimensions of the loudspeaker
enclosure and driver that are comparable to the wavelength and therefore aﬀect the sound
ﬁeld.
In this sense, this project investigates a possible solution to spherical acoustic radiation
in the near ﬁeld through known analytical models, numerical simulations and advanced
techniques. The study is restricted to the on-axis generated sound ﬁeld with special focus
on distances under 15 cm from the source in the frequency range of 20 to 5000 Hz.
The results of this work suggest a loudspeaker design that achieves the decay of a point
source in its proximity and validates the model through anechoic measurements of a 3D-
printed mock-up of the design.

1
Introduction, Pressure-Gradient
Microphones and Proximity Effect
This project has as a goal to design a physical sound source (loudspeaker) that has the
purpose of measuring accurately the response of pressure-gradient microphones in its near-
ﬁeld. It will be shown that pressure-gradient microphones are devices that react to the
diﬀerential of pressure and therefore their response gets altered under diﬀerent decaying
ﬁelds and distances.
This property introduces a concept called proximity eﬀect, that
is distinct to pressure-gradient microphones and is usually characterised for spherically
decaying ﬁelds. Diﬀerent loudspeakers generate diﬀerent decaying ﬁelds, mainly due to
their physical sizes being large compared to the wavelength, which causes diﬀraction and
interference with the direct decaying ﬁeld. It is therefore of interest not only to design a
spherically radiating sound source, but to understand where the deviations one experiences
when changing measurement loudspeakers come from.
1.1
Structure
This thesis is structured as follows: it will be this introductory chapter’s goal to explore the
diﬀerent aspects of pressure-gradient microphones and understand the design requirements
of the prospective sound source. This chapter will later explore sound propagation inside
horns as a ﬁrst attempt for spherical propagation and establish a measure of sphericity
to assess any design explored in the project.
Chapter 2 will explore spherical sound
sources that are analytically promising candidates for spherical propagation. It will also
introduce the concept of the acoustic centre, which is crucial for this project, and explore
its inﬂuence on the models implemented. Chapter 3 will focus on the inner characteristics
of the loudspeaker cabinet to be designed, and assess a way of excitation for the envisioned
model. Chapter 4 will explore an optimization scheme that parameterizes the geometry
of the loudspeaker cabinet and that through a deﬁned cost function that models the
measure of sphericity chosen for the project, reshapes the geometry in the aim of achieving
the speciﬁed design goals.
Chapter 5 will be dedicated to validate the work done in
chapter 4 through the realization of a prototype and its assessment against other interesting
loudspeakers through measurements. Chapter 6 will provide a short conclusion on the
ﬁndings of this thesis along with thoughts about future steps for this project.
1.2
Pressure-Gradient Microphones
Microphones can be classiﬁed in two main categories: cavity and free-ﬁeld. Cavity mi-
crophones exhibit an electrical signal that is proportional to the acoustic pressure at its
diaphragm, whereas free-ﬁeld ones exhibit an electrical signal that represents the acoustic
pressure that would exist at the membrane in the absence of the microphone. [1]
A pressure-gradient microphone is a type of free-ﬁeld microphone that is sensitive to
diﬀerences in sound pressure levels between two adjacent points, usually referred to as
the front and back of the diaphragm. Typically, this type of microphone consists of a
diaphragm with both surfaces exposed to the sound ﬁeld. As the sound wave impinges on
the diaphragm, a pressure diﬀerential is established between the two sides, which generates

1.4
Lumped Element Method
Usually, mechanics and vibrations problems are represented entirely by means of diﬀeren-
tial equations. Using a schematic diagram can help simplify the complexity of assembling
those equations. Electro-mechanic and electro-acoustic analogies come from the idea of
using the equations of electrical circuit theory to the solution of mechanical and acoustical
problems. The main idea is to represent acoustical and mechanical elements (e.g. springs,
mass and dampeners) in electrical circuit elements, to then have the equations assembled
by the circuit theory. This method of assembling the diﬀerential equations by the analo-
gies of lumped elements and following electrical circuit theory to solve them is labeled as
the lumped element method.
The model has only time as an independent variable and can only be used when the
wavelength greatly exceeds the dimensions of the component and when the distributive
parameters are homogeneous (e.g. density, elasticity, resistivity). [3]
In acoustic systems, an analogy is established with electrical circuits as illustrated in Fig.
1.3. Within this analogy, one can model various elements or systems, such as acoustic
cavities or compliances, which are represented with capacitors in electrical circuits. The

underlying concept revolves around recognizing that enclosed cavities exhibit compliance-
like behavior when subjected to volume velocity or pressure inputs. In such cases, the air
conﬁned within the cavity compresses and emulates the characteristics of a spring in a
mechanical system, for instance.

Another element in this analogy is the acoustic mass, which represents a tube containing
a volume of air that oscillates when stimulated by a source. In this context, the compress-
ibility of air is disregarded, and the modeled behavior reﬂects the movement of air as a
solid block, akin to a mass. In the lumped analogy, the acoustic mass is modeled as an
inductor.
Acoustic losses refer to the dissipation of energy in a narrow tube caused by viscosity
eﬀects. These losses can be represented by resistors within the electrical circuit analogy.
Moreover, the sources within the system are categorized as across-variable sources and
through-variable sources. A voltage source is used to represent a pressure source, while a
volume velocity source is depicted by a current source.
The mechanical elements are represented in Fig. 1.4. They are mass, compliance and
dampening and overall they can be represented in two ways.
One of these approaches is known as the impedance analogy, which stems from the ob-
servation that the impedance in electrical systems bears similarity to the impedance in
mechanical systems. In this context, both forms of impedance are computed as the ratio
between the across-variable and the through-variable. Consequently, force corresponds
to the across-variable (analogous to voltage), while velocity corresponds to the through-
variable (analogous to current). The construction of the impedance analogy is shown in
table 1.1. In this analogy, the mechanical mass is represented by an inductor, the damper
by a resistor, and the compliance by a capacitor. Moreover, the force source is linked to
a voltage source, while the velocity is associated with a current source.
The second approach involves examining each variable and its interaction with the re-
spective elements. When force is analyzed as a variable applied to a body, it essentially
interacts through that body. Therefore, in this reference frame, force becomes a through-
variable and is consequently represented by a current source. On the other hand, velocity,
in this case, acts as an across-variable and assumes the role of a voltage source. With this
perspective in mind, another analogy known as the mechanical mobility analogy can be
established, as illustrated in table 1.2. Within this analogy, once the force and velocity
sources are deﬁned, the mechanical mass can be equated to a capacitor, the mechanical loss
or dampener can be represented by a conductance G and the compliance by an inductor.

The physics applied on and inferred from the electrical domain of a lumped element
model are analogous within a range of frequencies to those in the acoustic domain. The
assumptions are that no transversal sound waves propagate, therefore the wavelength must
be large compared to the dimensions of the acoustic elements. The length of tubes must be
smaller than one sixteenth of the studied wavelength, otherwise axial pressure uctuations
have to be considered. Additionally, the radius of tubes has to be smaller than one-twenty
ﬁfth of the wavelength, otherwise one must consider lateral pressure ﬂuctuations. The
elements are assumed to have an idealized behavior (incompressibility for instance) and the
material or medium density distributions are assumed to be uniformly and homogeneously
distributed across the components. Additionally, nonlinearities at higher sound levels (such

4
Optimization
Through a boundary element method (BEM) model, one can set up a loudspeaker design
similar to the one discussed in chapter C and through trial and error, vary the geometry
until a better or satisfactory performance is achieved. However, the design variable con-
sidered in this project (decay of sound) is more complex for one to visualize and predict
possible model variations that would help achieve the design goals. Optimization methods
can aid this process if deﬁned properly, and their complexity is entirely determined by
the formulation of the problem, inﬂuencing the time it takes the algorithm to converge
to a solution, varying from minutes to days. The publication by [12] presents a thorough
demonstration of the potential of optimization by employing a three-dimensional shape
optimization technique on a loudspeaker cabinet, with the objective of attaining a uniform
directivity. A simpliﬁed approach of this publication is developed in this chapter, with a
design goal that concerns the decay of sound.

design variables. Again, this acoustic centre study here assumes that the deviation of the

close the behavior of a source is to that of a point source. The design variable chosen

frequencies k, that range in 1/6 octave bands from 20 Hz to 10 000 Hz. The cost function

5.3.3
Proximity Effect Measurement
Proximity eﬀect measurements are done to visualize the deviation of a pressure-gradient
microphone’s response from that of a normal pressure-sensitive microphone. Since proximity-
eﬀect is frequency and distance dependent, this measurement is performed at various dis-
tances from the source for both a pressure-gradient microphone and another comparable
pressure microphone, where the response of the pressure-gradient microphone is normal-
ized by that of the pressure microphone at every distance.
This type of measurement is more transparent compared to the double distance decay
measurement, which basically replicates the decay ﬁeld based on a ﬁnite diﬀerence method.
As long as the double distance decay measurements are not corrected for the true acoustic
centre, the response they show is biased and does not show the true behavior of the
gradient ﬁeld of the pressure. Pressure-gradient microphones on the other hand do not
require prior knowledge of the acoustic centre position to respond in a certain way; if they
are placed in a slightly oﬀposition, they will still react to the true pressure-gradient ﬁeld
rather than adapt to the uncorrected decay plots observed in this report. In other words,
only the knowledge of the microphone’s response at the true 1 cm distance, for example,
is distorted.
This measurement is done using the prototype and the B&K Mouth Simulator as well, to
show the dependence of these measurements on the type of source. Fig. 5.16 shows part
of the measurement setup for the proximity measurements.

6
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the inﬂuence of the size and shape of loudspeaker cabinets on
the decay experienced in the near and far ﬁeld of the loudspeaker. Diﬀerent geometry
parametrization approaches were explored and showed bias to converge to elongated mod-
els. A method to calculate the near-ﬁeld acoustic centre is developed and shows almost no
need for correction with the prototype simulations. However, after prototyping and per-
forming measurements, it is clear that one can get away with wider cabinets (Bruel & Kjær
Mouth Simulator) when correcting for the acoustic centre position. A future step for this
project is to include an acoustic centre study inside the optimization scheme and therefore
allowing for potentially less problematic features of the design (e.g. the tube). Moreover,
it has been shown that the dimensions of the microphone to be measured should also be
accounted for at close measurement distances to avoid pressure build-up or standing wave
eﬀects. It has also been made clear that the response captured of a pressure-gradient
microphone relies highly to the source it is being measured with, drastically changing fun-
damental characteristics in the observed response whenever the sound ﬁeld deviates from
sphericity (e.g. cut-oﬀfrequency observed). Further improvements also should include
low-frequency lumped models of the inner cabinet of the loudspeaker for the problem to
be deﬁned completely, and potentially phase response, which has not been explored in this
thesis and might reveal other important information. All in all, the results are consid-
ered satisfactory as a ﬁrst iteration and proof of concept, and the study raised important
aspects of this challenge that the loudspeaker design could have beneﬁted from.
