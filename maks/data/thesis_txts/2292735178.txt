Abstract 
 
It is expected a mobile traffic growth of 1000 times during the next decade. This 
growth is the result of the increasing popularity of the mobile devices, the increment 
of mobile subscribers and the evolution in the services offered by the mobile 
networks.  This growth in data traffic implies that the mobile networks will have to 
experience a 1000 fold improvement of the capacity to be able to support it.  
Furthermore, many new scenarios will come out of this evolution in the mobile 
networks (i.e. increasing presence of sensors and machine type devices) and they will 
imply new and different technical requirements. An overall study of the network 
state, the future requirements and technical solutions to fulfill these requirements is 
already under performance by many of the telecommunication industry players. 
In this report, a study of the evolution in the requirements and the technical solutions 
to match them is perform. As a result of the analysis of two of the main scenarios in 
the future system, rural environment and ultra-dense network, a recommendation of 
a heterogeneous network with flexible air interface is given. In rural environments a 
macro cell solution with frequencies below 6 GHz is the most suitable, while in the 
ultra-dense network scenario, it will be a small cell deployment, with working 
frequencies in the mmWaves range.

The scope of this thesis is to study the impact in the future mobile systems of the evolution of the 
data rates and capacity requirements by the users, as well as the scenarios created through this 
requirements. The analysis will be performed in an Excel spreadsheet with the goal of get to know 
how these future requirements will affect the dimensioning of a mobile network in some scenarios. 
The approach to the problem and the structure of the report will be the following one: 
Chapter 2 covers an evolution of the mobile network systems since their first appearance to the 
ongoing LTE and LTE-A networks. The main drivers that will make necessary a new mobile system 
by 2020 are also covered, as well as how the industry is approaching the problem. 
Chapter 3 approaches some of the requirements of the future 5G systems and studies the technical 
solutions offered to satisfy this requirements. In this report the main focus will be put in the capacity 
requirements, the situation about the availability of the spectrum and its usage, and the air interface 
solutions.  
During chapter 4, the scenarios found as key use cases in the development of the 5G systems are 
presented. Two of them will be analyzed in terms of network requirements for their deployment 
and a combined solution will be given. 
Finally, in chapter 5 the conclusions obtained from the analysis performed will be presented in the 
report.

2. Evolution of Mobile Network 
Technologies 
 
This report will focus on the future mobile system foreseen for 2020. However, it is important to 
understand how the mobile systems have evolved since the first time they appear to their current 
state and how are they going to evolve. All the growth of the mobile communications that the 
society is experiencing nowadays is not something new. Since the appearance of the first mobile 
system, the number of subscribers has just grow continuously, until the point that almost each ten 
years, a new mobile system has appeared. Thus, in this section, a description of the previous systems 
and their main technical features will be covered, and a roadmap of the projected work for the next 
generation system as well. 
2.1 First Generation  
First Generation Systems, also known as 1G, emerged in early 1980’s. This systems were deployed 
entirely for voice traffic. FM was the modulation used and traffic was multiplexed using FDMA. Thus, 
these systems were based in analog communications. The major breakthrough of this mobile 
systems was the introduction of the cellular network concept. The idea consisted on the division of 
the geographical coverage area to serve into cells, where each one of the cells is served by a base 
station. These systems introduced concepts like handover or roaming. 
However, there were major security issues within these systems. Analogue phones were very 
insecure. Anyone who owned an all-band radio receiver was able to listen any conversation and 
could collect many information which could be used to reprogram and resell stolen phones. 
Furthermore, these systems had a low capacity, low speeds and poor voice links. 
The first operational 1G system was deployed in 1979 in Japan by the company Nippon Telephone 
and Telegraph (NTT). It was soon followed by other rollouts like the deployment performed in 1981 
in Norway, or in following years in the United States or the United Kingdom. [41] 
In Europe, the most common 1G systems were Nordic Mobile Telephones (NMT), mainly established 
in the Nordic countries as well as in Switzerland or Netherlands, and Total Access Communication 
Systems (TACS), mainly used in the United Kingdom. In the United States, the Advanced Mobile 
Phone System (AMPS) became operational in 1982 
 
2.2 Second Generation  
First Generation systems soon became insufficient for the users’ demands. Thus, early in 1990’s, the 
next mobile system emerged. The main standard is GSM, which stands for Global System for Mobile.

Despite the following mobile systems, GSM still has over 2 billion users spread over more than 100 
countries. [6] 
While it was still designed mainly for voice services, the big difference between 2G systems and 
their predecessors is that this new system was digital. The development of digital communications, 
made them not only more useful, but more profitable for future mobile systems too.  
GSM offers voice services over circuit switching, with TDMA and FDMA as access methods. It uses 
200 KHz channels with 8 timeslots each. Voice rates of 13 Kbps in full rate are offered, while data is 
limited to 14.4 Kbps. Short Messaging Service (SMS) is also a service covered by GSM. 
Other improvements that GSM presents compared to previous mobile systems are mobility 
management services or security improvements. In GSM, the handover and location area update 
concepts are more developed. Regarding security issues, encryption and ciphering is introduced in 
order to avoid eavesdropping. Furthermore, authentication methods are employed to verify that a 
user is allowed to register in the network. [6] 
The main entities of a GSM network are the Base Station Subsystem (BSS), Network & Switching 
Subsystem (BSS) and Operation Subsystem (OSS). Figure 1 shows the topology of a GSM network. 
 
Figure  5 - GSM Network Architecture 
In Europe, GSM have been deployed in the bands of 900 MHz and 1800 MHz, while in the US they 
have been deployed in the 1900 MHz and 850 MHz bands. 
However, both voice and data limitations caused the appearance of new mobile systems still 
considered as part as the Second Generation of mobile systems. HSCSD, while maintaining circuit 
switching, combines timeslots in order to achieve higher data rates up to 57.6 Kbps. 
However, in order to increment data rates, packet switching was more suitable. GPRS, which stands 
for General Packet Radio Service, makes a more efficient use of the resources by being the first 
mobile system that manages data services through packet switching. It is considered as 2.5 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
Generation because, while it enhances GSM, it is deployed as an evolution, everything valid for GSM 
it is valid for GPRS. 
In GPRS network capacity is shared between voice and data. The network architecture presents 
some differences compared to the GSM architecture. The BSS evolves in order to be able to manage 
packets, it is called Packet Control Unit (PCU) and there are two new nodes, the Serving for GPRS 
Support Node (SGSN) and the Gateway for GPRS Support Node (GGSN). GPRS allows for theoretical 
maximum average bit rates of 160 Kbps. 
The Enhanced Data rates for GSM Evolution system, also known as EDGE, uses a new modulation 
format, 8-PSK instead of GMSK, and new coding schemes, and enhances GPRS to the point of being 
able to obtain realistic bit rates up to 200 Kbps. This system is considered as the 2.75G. 
2.3 Third Generation 
This generation of systems is made of a set of systems that fulfil the suggestion made by the 
International Telecommunication Union in the IMT-2000, in the beginning of the 21st century. The 
main 3G standard is the Universal Mobile Telecommunication System (UMTS), which is a 3GPP 
standard. There are many other 3G systems like CDMA2000 or TD-SCDMA.  
The main difference of UMTS compared to GSM or other previous systems is that UMTS has been 
deployed for both, voice and data, since the beginning.  It uses circuit switching for voice 
transmission and packet switching for data transmission. Instead of TDMA and FDMA like in the 2G 
systems, UMTS employs CDMA as the new radio access method, which allows all the users to 
transmit simultaneously. Each cell in UMTS has a bandwidth of 5 MHz, although more capacity can 
be added by allocating more frequencies.   UMTS enables data rates up to 384 Kbps 
UMTS presents a new network architecture, whose main three elements are the UE (mobile phone), 
the UMTS Terrestrial Radio Access Network (UTRAN), which includes the UMTS base stations, known 
as nodeBs, and the UMTS Core network. [6] It also brings enhanced techniques in terms of mobility 
management, as well as security authentication and encryption improved methods. The figure 
below shows a diagram of the main entities of an UMTS network. 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
 
Figure  6 - UMTS Network Architecture [6] 
However, as a need of higher bit rates and lower latency values, an enhancement of UMTS was 
introduced before 2010. It is known as High-Speed Packet Access, HSPA, which is a combination of 
its downlink and uplink versions, HSDPA and HSUPA. This enhanced system is able to provide higher 
data rates by taking advantage of new modulation schemes, like 16-QAM, or adaptive modulation 
and coding (AMC). 
2.4 Fourth Generation  
Fourth Generation Systems were introduced around 2010. This generation of networks includes 
Long Term Evolution (LTE) and LTE Advanced.  
LTE, which stands for Long Term Evolution, was, like other previous system, the result of the need 
of higher bit rates alongside lower latencies, and the need of making available many new services 
on mobile devices. It first appear as a 3GPP standard in the Release 8. Although this new mobile 
system meant a complete redesign of the core and the radio network, it is not considered as true 
4G, because it does not fulfill IMT-Advanced requirements. It is considered as a 3.9G mobile system.  
One of the novelties that LTE brings is in terms of radio access methods. In the downlink, it uses 
Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access (OFDMA), while in the uplink, the method employed 
is Single Carrier-FDMA (SC-FDMA). It allows theoretical peak bit rates of 300 Mbps and 75 Mbps, in 
the downlink and the uplink respectively.   
The main feature of OFDMA is that is composed by a lot of narrow orthogonal subcarriers with a 15 
kHz spacing. Increasing the bit rate can be made by adding more carriers. However, for devices such 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
dependent on battery life as smartphones are, OFDMA is not the most suitable technique in the 
uplink. Thus, in this link SC-FDMA is employed. 
LTE has been defined in 22 frequency bands, like the 700, 850, 1800, 2600 MHz bands. In Denmark, 
for example, it is being deployed in the 1800 and 2600 MHz bands, being the second one the main 
option. 
Multiple Input Multiple Output, known as MIMO, is another of the new features that LTE brings. In 
LTE is employed as single user MIMO in the downlink and multi user MIMO in the uplink. This 
technique will be explained deeply in further sections. 
In terms of network architecture, LTE shows a clear separation between the control plane and the 
user plane. It also supposed a clear simplification of the core network. A LTE network is 
characterized by three big blocks, with their own entities each block. These blocks are the UE, the 
Evolved UTRAN (E-UTRAN) and the Evolved Packet Core (EPC). In the E-UTRAN is located the 
eNodeB, which is an enhancement of the UMTS nodeB, now gathering all the radio functionalities. 
Figure [7] shows the main entities of a LTE network. 
 
Figure  7 - LTE Network Architecture [6] 
LTE-Advanced, known as LTE-A, is a major enhancement of LTE that appeared around 2010 in the 
3GPP Release 10. It is considered the first true 4G mobile system. It allows to reach much higher 
data rates by combining LTE carriers up to 100 MHz of bandwidth, technique known as carrier 
aggregation LTE-A peak bit rates are 1Gbps in the downlink, and 500 Mbps in the uplink. 
LTE-A also improves some features of LTE like MIMO by elevating the number of transmission 
antennas up to 4 in the uplink and 8 in the downlink. It also brings new features like Coordinated 
MultiPoint, CoMP, key feature in terms of mitigating interference, or the use of relays in order to 
extend cell coverage or capacity. CoMP, like MIMO, will be deeply explained in further sections.

2.5.3 Devices Connected and Machine-Type Communications 
Global demand rise for mobile wireless networks is not a result only of more people getting more 
connected with much more frequency. The enormous growth of the number of devices connected 
to the network is one of the most important drivers for the need of the 5G mobile network. 
It is believed that in 10 years there might be up to 10 times more machines and devices connected 
to the network than human users. [11] It is expected by 2020 the coexistence of around 50 billion 
devices connected to broadband Internet services. [8] 
Among these connected devices it will be possible to find currently common devices like 
smartphones, tablets or surveillance cameras, and some other machine-type devices which will 
grow as the future network grow. Some of them will be medical and care equipment, cars, 
wearables, home automation elements, industrial equipment, smart cities sensors and any type of 
embed sensor.  For example, it is expected that emergency professionals will be able to handle their 
communications always in public networks instead of through dedicated networks like it happens 
nowadays. [8] 
The main idea is that everything and everyone able to be connected is expected to be connected to 
the network. 
This new type of network users require a secure, efficient and reliable interaction with the network. 
This, alongside with the very low battery consumption needs is why their penetration into the 
network introduce some new scenarios and requirements for the future mobile networks. Satisfying 
all these scenarios with all the different requirements between each other lay out one of the biggest 
challenges for the deployment of the future 5G mobile systems, and, deploy a network able to fulfill 
them will be one of the things that will add value to the future network. 
As it is stated in [3], actual trends indicate that sensors will become very pervasive in cities and will 
start to be also part of rural environments. Some of them will interact continuously between each 
other creating an intercommunication framework. Similar situations will be included in the more 
common IoT and they will require ultra-low energy, battery consumption and latency. 
Following with the example given earlier in this section, some machine-type devices will be part of 
safety and emergency systems and services, for example, emergency lines with authorities or 
emergency infrastructure in case of natural disaster. In these situations these devices require very 
robust communications, with a high efficiency and energy consumption. Reliability will also be a key 
factor in this scenario. 
Furthermore, there is a big rise in electronic and telecommunications penetration in life matters. 
Technology is being more used to manipulate instruments, for example, in remote surgery. Vehicles 
and traffic related elements bring incorporated more technology every day trying to enhance user 
driving experience; and eHealth is one of the applications that will grow most during the next 
decade. Using eHealth devices is a very effective way to add value to users’ network experience.

2.7 Summary 
1G, emerged around 1980, was intended for voice traffic and based on analogue communications. 
Many networks coexisted without standardization. The major breakthrough of 1G was the 
introduction of the cellular networks concept. 
2G was the first digital generation of digital mobile systems. Still widely used, GSM is the main 
standard of 2G systems. It offered higher speeds alongside with other new services like SMS or 
rudimentary data communication. GPRS and EDGE are enhancements of GSM that use packet 
switching to manage data traffic for the first time, allowing to reach realistic bit rates up to 200 kbps. 
As a result of a 3GPP standardization, UMTS emerged as a 3G system.. New concepts like CDMA are 
introduced. It allows bit rates up to 384 kbps. HSPA is a system that appeared to provide higher data 
rates by introducing new concepts like AMC or new modulation schemes. 
Around 2010, LTE supposed a redesign of the core and radio networks. It employs different access 
methods in the downlink, OFDMA, and in the uplink, SC-FDMA. LTE brings some novel features like 
MIMO, and allows data rates up to 300 Mbps in the downlink and 75 Mbps in the uplink.  
LTE-A applies Carrier Aggregation to reach up to 1 Gbps in the DL and 500 Mbps in the UL. It also 
employs new techniques like CoMP, intended to mitigate interferences. 
The need to provide much more network capacity, reach higher data rates no matter the coverage 
and the new scenarios emerged from the machine-type communications have led the urge of a new 
mobile system by 2020. 
With LTE and LTE-A deployments still ongoing, several projects working on the development of the 
5G systems have emerged as a result of the collaboration between the Industry and the Academia. 
The goal for 5G is to be able to connect anyone or anything that can be connected, and everywhere 
and every time they want to be connected.

3.2.1 Heterogeneous Networks  
Heterogeneous Network is the term used to refer to a network composed by cells of different sizes, 
radio access technologies (RATs), transmission solutions and base stations. [13] A Heterogeneous 
Network usually is a compound of regular macro cells complemented by smaller cell like micro, pico, 
femto or even personal cells. [12] 
 
Figure  13 - Illustration of a Heterogeneous Network [6] 
First small cells appearances were in GSM and HSPA times. However, in those technologies small 
cells just played a role as temporary or substitute solution. This role is changing drastically and it is 
expected that small cells and heterogeneous network will be one of the most important elements 
in future network deployments in order to the addition of capacity. According to [14], by 2020 
around 78% of the traffic will be driven through small cells, devices will be served by a combination 
of these different cells. 
The role they could play in supporting capacity and increasing user performance in indoor situations 
and hot zones (very crowded areas) were the main drivers for the need of HetNets deployment. The 
evolution of these multi-layer networks depends on several factors like legacy technologies 
infrastructure and their use, spectrum availability, traffic distribution and users tendencies within 
an area and, of course, issues related to the network deployment  and management cost. 
Three steps in multi-layer networks deployment will be studied now, the use and evolution of macro 
cells and the use of small cells for indoor and outdoor deployment.  
Macro cells evolution 
All the operators have almost global GSM coverage, an effective HSPA deployment and a mature 
rollout of LTE networks in dense areas.

management of the legacy systems continuously.  This is one of the reasons behind the Single RAN 
technology, which allows to support several mobile communications standards in the same base 
station. It is expected that the base stations of macro cells in the future 5G mobile deployment will 
have to support not only this future mobile technology but also LTE, HSPA and even GSM, which will

Outdoor small cells deployment 
However, as it has been stated earlier, capacity and coverage in hot zones is one of the key issues 
for 5G and one of the main drivers for HetNets deployment. This former hotspots with non-uniform 
traffic have now become larger zones, like stadiums or main squares. Very crowded areas might 
cause a mobile traffic peak in periods of time that the network has not been deployed to support. 
In this situations, macro cells are not capable to provide the required capacity, and this outdoor 
small cells offer a good solutions. 
Density of traffic and the available spectrum define the relation between macro and micro cells 
efficient deployment. For example, in urban areas pico cells will be more used due to the higher 
importance of mobile broadband capacity versus coverage. Cell coverage area usually shrinks when 
the data rates increase.  During this section different issues in the small cell deployment will be 
studied. 
==PAGE:34==
33 
 
One of the first ones is to decide if the small cells will be deployed using dedicated spectrum or 
shared with macro cells. On the one hand, due to network transparency, it can be thought at the 
beginning that a shared spectrum will improve coverage and mobility. On the other hand, 
interference between macro and small cells will deteriorate user performance when the traffic 
grows. For this reason, the operator will have to decide between the use of dedicated spectrum for 
small cells and the introduction of interference coordination techniques. This decision also impacts 
in the transmission power that should be used. While for high traffic a high transmission power 
might congest these micro cells, reducing this power is not a good option because it is expected to 
appear coverage holes. 
Another option for these small cells deployment is the use RRH. According to [15], a remote radio 
head is a radio transceiver that remotely connects to a control panel of a network. This can 
connection can be done wirelessly or, due to the latency and bandwidth requirements, via dark 
fiber. 
Moreover, both downlink and uplink traffic must be studied. According to [12] a typical ratio 
between both links’ traffic is 1:5 (UL:DL). Depending on the limiter link, the rollout should focus on 
the addition of more macro cells (DL limited network) or in the micro cell deployment (UL limited 
network). 
Finally, network performance and backhaul requirements can be improved if these small cells are 
deployed in clusters, which will be typically located at street level. A zone controller and the access 
points form this topology. As it is stated in [12], an effective method for this street level deployment 
is the use of NLOS wireless backhaul, which allows to deploy the access points inside the hot spots 
and connect up to 100 AP through only one connection to the EPC. 
Indoor small cells deployment 
The enhancement of macro layer along with the deployment of outdoor small cells provides a very 
cost-effective method to improve capacity and coverage in high density outdoor areas. However, 
indoor small cells deployment will be required in locations like shopping malls, enterprise buildings 
or airports. In this situations three solutions are being considered: DAS, the deployment of femto 
and pico cells and the usage of Wi-Fi for indoor offload. 
1. DAS: this technique is based on providing cellular coverage inside a building through the 
distribution of RF to a network of antennas [16]. Figure 8 shows an example of an in-building 
DAS. The purpose is to create a fully integrated layer together with the macro cells in order 
to manage and handle all the in-building traffic and offloading the required capacity from 
these macro cells. Depending on the method to distribute the RF signal from the radio source 
through the building, there can be three types of DAS.  
- 
Active: the RF signal is transmitted over coax cable or fiber after its conversion to a digital 
signal. This digital signal is again converted to a RF signal by an antenna. 
- 
Passive: the distribution from the RF signal to the antennas is done through passive 
components without amplification. 
==PAGE:35==
34 
 
- 
Hybrid: a combination of both systems above mentioned is used. An active DAS is used 
between the head end and the remote units, while a passive DAS is used from the remote 
units to the antennas. 
For operators, this is a good solution for large buildings where it exists the possibility of 
sharing the costs amongst the operators.

3.2 Spectrum  
Spectrum availability is one of the key issues the development of the next generation of mobile 
networks will face, as well as the efficiency in its usage will affect directly to the capacity of the 
network and its performance.  The current situation is that the most common mobile technologies 
now (3G and 4G), have their usage bands overloaded. Additional spectrum is crucial for 5G mobile 
networks in order to be able to manage the expected traffic growth. 
During this section, first an overview of the target frequency bands and access management option 
will be given. Finally, a more detailed study will be assessed. 
Nowadays, two options are being evaluated when thinking about the additional spectrum allocation 
requirements: 
 The first one consist on exploring the frequency bands which are already in use or those 
whose licenses have recently been acquired by the network operators. These frequency 
==PAGE:37==
36 
 
bands are composed of those frequencies below 6.5 GHz. According to [1], during November 
2015, in the ITU World Radiocommunication Conference these bands will be studied in order 
to identify the ones available for the 5G mobile networks usage. There are many bands 
suitable of being used for 5G spectrum, but it needs to be studied because of the different 
properties of each band. For example, part of the L-band works well in providing good 
coverage; while the band between 470-790 MHz is appropriate for indoor and outdoor 
coverage. 
 Due to these bands of frequencies’ congestion, frequencies above 6 GHz are being 
considered as suitable spectrum. So the second option consists on exploring from 30 GHz up 
to some hundreds of gigahertz. This range of frequencies is known as mmWaves, because of 
their wavelengths of just some millimeters. This frequency bands are an item for future WRC 
agendas, waiting for the results and decisions of the WRC 15. 
When deciding between frequencies above or below 6.5 GHz, some issues have to be taken into 
account. One of the most obvious, it is that the propagation properties of each band is not the same,  
so there will be many differences between choosing one or other option. 
For example, to give coverage in rural areas or indoor locations, it is very suitable the usage of 
frequencies below 1GHz [3]. Furthermore, up to 6.5-10GHz, it is feasible that the radio interface 
could follow some of the characteristics of the current cellular deployments [4]. 
However, mmWaves are more suitable for shorter-range connections and when higher speed is 
required; making them the best option for ultra-dense deployments (being high speed in very 
crowed places one of the 5G requirements and use cases).  
Another issue regarding the spectrum is its management. Today cellular mobile networks are based 
on the exclusive and dedicated usage of spectrum. While this is expected to keep being the main 
way to access to spectrum, other options have to be explored. There is an important amount of 
radio spectrum available under many other authorization modes, being some of the most well-
known unlicensed usage or Licensed Shared Access (LSA). [31] 
Some applications will require additional spectrum due to the capacity required. In a briefly 
description, two possible options regarding spectrum access are the following ones: 
 Sharing licensed spectrum by network operators within a determined time or area in order 
to being able to guarantee a quality of service proper to what these future application 
require. 
 Make use of unlicensed spectrum. However, using unlicensed spectrum has some technical 
limitations that make very difficult to consider it as a full substitute for 5G spectrum. It should 
be consider as an exception that has to be fully integrated into the 5G networks.

3.3.1 Authorization Usage and Spectrum Access  
It has been stated earlier than the spectrum state-of-the-art is based on exclusive and individual 
exploitation of frequency bands by each mobile network operator (MNO). However, the future 
landscape is expected to be much more complex. It is expected to coexist many frequency bands 
with different access regulation approaches, including both exclusive and shared access. It also 
might be necessary to develop techniques that enable this regulatory landscape. 
Broadly speaking, it can be identified two different spectrum usages: Individual Authorization 
(Licensed) and General Authorization (Licensed Exempt/Unlicensed). 
3.3.1.1 
Individual Authorization 
This authorization regime is also known as Licensed Access. In this situation, the National Regulatory 
Authority (NRA) grants the exclusive usage rights of a spectrum band to the MNOs. These rights are 
valid over a fixed time and for a block of frequencies. It can be nationwide or more limited 
geographically. 
Within this group, there are different levels of spectrum access, which are the following: 
==PAGE:39==
38 
 
a. Exclusive Access. Nowadays the most common method employed by the NRAs to grant 
licenses is through an auction. However, there are other ways to obtain a license like a 
beauty contest procedure or by a direct application from a MNO. During the period of the 
license, the MNO is the only user of that block of frequencies over a region or nationwide. 
There is no need to share communication resources with any other service. Possible 
interferences coming from adjacent band are managed through the definition of regulatory 
requirements or through the assumption of the compliance with the actual specifications. 
b. Co-Primary Shared Access. A joint use of a primary license holder spectrum is agreed with 
other operator. If both of the parties have the same regulatory status the sharing has to be 
made by a mutual agreement. All the agreement conditions are subject to the approval of 
the NRA. Two of the most suitable schemes for this type of access are: 
- 
Mutual renting: in order to use the spectrum resources in a more dynamic way, an 
operator can rent to other operator blocks of unused resources. These unused resourced 
can be rented from many different operators. However, the primary owner remains the 
priority usage over the rented resources. 
- 
Spectrum pooling: the corresponding NRA issues authorizations to a limited number of 
user to access a determined band of the spectrum. There is no guarantee of availability 
of access or granted spectrum. However, both authorized users and pool rules are known 
in advance, giving some predictability in terms of capacity expected. 
c. Licensed Shared Access. In this access model there are three differentiated participants: 
- 
Incumbent: is the participant that, according to the LSA rules stated by the regulator, 
would offer its unused spectrum to the LSA licensees. 
- 
LSA Licensee: is the participant that would make usage of the shared spectrum thanks to 
a sharing agreement and a LSA spectrum usage license given by the Regulator. 
- 
Regulator: it is the party of the model in charge of the definition of the LSA framework 
and the issue of the LSA use rights. This role is usually performed by the NRA. 
Thus, in a LSA model, the incumbent would give rights to access the spectrum to a limited 
number of LSA licensees under the supervision of the agreement by a Regulator, typically 
the corresponding NRA.  By doing this, it is possible to maintain a proper QoS, while the LSA 
licensees must protect the service provided by the incumbent. However, this last point adds 
complexity to the sharing. 
d. Light-licensing. It is a simplified method in which authorizations are issued in situations 
where there is not any concern about possible interferences. However, it is important to 
review if there might be problems with already existing services. The two schemes in this 
method are also mutual renting and spectrum pooling, already explained. 
3.3.1.2 
General Authorization 
Under this regime fall some terms like “License exempt”, “license free” or “unlicensed”.  The main 
idea of this option is that, although there are some technical conditions, the access to the spectrum 
use is granted without an individual authorization for the user. Thus, there is not any guaranty 
against interferences, and the spectrum is shared amongst many operators using their assigned 
==PAGE:40==
39 
 
resources for many different scenarios, making more difficult to provide an expected QoS. However, 
the cost is lower than in Individual Authorization (License exempt access usually costs 0). 
The spectrum access schemes in this regime are the following ones: 
a. Unlicensed Shared Access. This scheme involves the unprotected against interferences 
access to a frequency band in which there is not assigned any primary service. 
b. Unlicensed Primary Access. In this situation there is a primary services allocated in that band 
of frequencies. However, every user is authorized to access this service. Regulator does not 
stablish a required protection but the one that the primary service implies. 
c. Secondary Horizontal Shared Access. It is a scenario similar to the Unlicensed Shared Access, 
except for the requirement of protection of primary users. The spectrum resources left by 
the primary users can be shared amongst many secondary users, which translates into 
almost no guarantee of performance and QoS.

3.3.2 Sharing technologies 
In order to allow the sharing of the spectrum between several MBB systems from different 
operators it might be required to develop technical solutions. This solutions would allow the 
coexistence between those several systems, creating an efficient regulatory environment. 
One of the biggest risks of sharing the spectrum is the ability to efficiently manage the possible 
interference between each system, since there is a large potential of it due to the open nature of 
the spectrum.  This management can be done in several domains: 
- 
Frequency: orthogonal frequency resource blocks are used by each system. 
- 
Time: the same spectrum is used by each system but in different intervals of time. 
- 
Spatial: interferences are managed via orthogonal spatial resources. 
- 
Code: orthogonal code resources are used to manage interferences. 
In a sharing environment, coordination activities to manage these interferences can be performed 
through two different solutions, distributed and centralized solutions. 
a. Distributed solutions. In this type of solutions, coordination takes place between the 
systems involved, allowing the coordination to be done locally. Commercial agreements 
might be unnecessary since there is the possibility of integration into the standards. Some 
of the solutions included in this family are: 
- 
Peer-to-peer coexistence protocol: coordination is done over a well-defined interface, 
which is used for a direct exchange of messages between the sharing systems. 
- 
Coexistence beacon: systems regularly send known signals which information about 
presence and other aspects, which can be used by other systems in order to obtain a fair and 
successful sharing access environment. 
- 
Spectrum sensing and dynamic frequency/channel selection: frequency of operation 
is selected on a dynamic basis thanks to the results of measurements (energy/feature 
detection). 
- 
MAC behaviour based schemes. 
b. Centralized solutions. In this type of solutions there is an absence of direct contact between 
the sharing systems. They interact with a central entity that manages the sharing access. 
This solutions imply some benefits like a major control, predictability and reliability of the 
resources. However, the set up time is much slower, making this option much more suitable 
for longer time scales. Nowadays, these are the solutions preferred by the operators when 
the spectrum sharing implies monetary transactions. Two approaches within this solutions 
are the followings: 
- 
Spectrum coordinator: a central management entity grants access in an exclusive basis 
for short periods of time. 
- 
Geo-location database approach (GLDB): a database is queried by the sharing systems 
in order to find available spectrum at a certain location.

3.3 Air Interface 
In previous section it has been mentioned that LTE and LTE-A deployments will continue as an 
important player in the future mobile network ecosystem. Furthermore, many GSM and UMTS 
networks will remain as legacy systems.  
To be able to support a quality experience in such a diverse environment like this, flexibility is one 
of the main requirements for the future 5G system air interface. The future concept will not be a 
unique air interface for 5G, but a combination of many air interfaces and their flexible management. 
Some of the last improvements experienced in the air interface area are the use of OFDM by LTE 
systems, or the incorporation of low orders of MIMO. However, for the future mobile system, some 
techniques are under study in order to be able to provide the enhancements needed for the 
required technical improvements such as higher data rates or higher efficiency. 
During this section, some of this techniques, like massive MIMO or CoMP, alongside with the 
existence of possible new waveforms, will be studied. 
3.4.1 Massive MIMO 
Massive MIMO includes all the systems that use antenna arrays with a few hundred antennas that 
serve many dozens of UE simultaneously in the same time-frequency resource. [29]  
Massive MIMO is based on a multiplicity of physically small, active and individually controlled 
antennas that serve a multiplicity of active users, using directly measured channel characteristics. 
[28] This technique is an enhancement and evolution of previous versions like Point-to-Point MIMO 
or Multi-User MIMO. During this section, both previous techniques will be overviewed and Massive 
MIMO will be explained. 
==PAGE:46==
45 
 
a. Point-to-Point MIMO.  A link of this type is composed by a concentrated array of M antennas 
transmitting data to a concentrated array of K antennas located in the UE. Every signal at the 
receptor is a linear combination of the transmitted signals and the coefficients determined 
by the end-to-end link propagation. 
The base stations send a vector-valued signal where each component includes different 
information. To generate the vector of received signals, it has to be multiplied by the channel 
matrix. This matrix is learned by the receptor through pilots (training signals) sent by the 
transmitter. [28] 
This technique is not scalable beyond 8x8 arrays, due to some factors like the susceptibility 
to propagation environment, the rise of the matrix learning time, the complication of the 
UE, etc. 
The following figure illustrates a Point-to-Point MIMO link. 
 
Figure  19 - Point-to-Point MIMO link [28] 
b. Multi-User MIMO. This technique can be understood as a Point-to-Point MIMO link where 
the concentrated array of K antennas has been broken into K individual users.  Multi-User 
MIMO has several advantages compared to the previous version of MIMO, like the UE only 
require a single antenna or less vulnerability to propagation environment.  
However, the growth in the amount of time required to obtain the CSI and the complexity 
of the coding/decoding scheme, make this technique also not scalable. 
Massive MIMO takes advantage of the spatial-division multiplexing in order to be able to use same 
frequencies and times for the data transmitted. It is also distinguished because the channel 
characteristics used are directly measured, instead of assumed, giving the base station good enough 
channel knowledge in both uplink and downlink.  
In [29], benefits and drawbacks of the usage of MIMO are explained. On the one hand, some of the 
benefits are the following: 
- 
Increase capacity over 10 times and 100 times improvement in radiated energy efficiency. 
The capacity improvement is obtained from the extreme spatial multiplexing performed. 
Energy efficiency comes from the possibility of each antenna to focus in smaller regions. 
==PAGE:47==
46 
 
Base stations can assure that the constructive interference of all the waveforms sent by the 
antennas takes places only at the intended location. 
- 
Massive MIMO antennas are made by hundreds of low cost amplifiers whose output power 
is in the order of mili-Watts. Furthermore, performance in terms of fading or noise only are 
important in a combined way. 
- 
Simplifies the multiple access layer. Each terminal can be assigned with the whole 
bandwidth, rendering most of the physical layer control signaling redundant. 
- 
Increases the robustness against intentional jamming. Signal coming from intentional 
jammers can be canceled using excess degrees of freedom of the antennas. 
- 
Enables a reduction of the air interface latency, which in wireless communications is typically 
determined by fading. 
On the other hand, these are some of the limiting factor of Massive MIMO: 
- 
Channel reciprocity. There is a reasonable consensus that the propagation channel is 
reciprocal. However, the hardware chains for the uplink and downlink might not be 
reciprocal in the base station or terminal transceivers. 
- 
Pilot contamination.  
- 
Radio propagation and orthogonality of channel responses.

3.4.3 New Waveforms  
One of the key features for the air interface of the future mobile system is going to be its flexibility 
and dynamism in order to be able to support the diversity of foreseen scenarios and their specific 
requirements. It is very important that the design of this new and enhanced radio air interface 
improves vastly the spectral efficiency and capacity of the mobile system.  
During this section, some of the enablers to fulfill those required improvements will be covered. 
3.4.3.1 FTN SIGNALING 
FTN stands for Faster than Nyquist.  It is a signaling technology that was first proposed in 1975 by 
James Mazo. It is able to make a more efficient use of the spectrum and double the bitrate compared 
to actual systems, without worsening the BER vs. SNR. [35] [34] 
==PAGE:51==
50 
 
In this technology, symbols are transmitted in a more dense manner, making a better use of the 
spectrum. However, this turns adjacent symbols into non-orthogonal symbols, resulting in a more 
complex system. Transmitting faster than the Nyquist limit implies an interference problem. 
Some of the main features of FTN are: 
- 
It is possible to transmit sinc pulses a 25% faster without decreasing the Euclidean distance 
between symbols. 
- 
MCMs (multi carrier modulations) offer the possibility for higher gains in the systems. 
Interference management is a primordial issue when deploying practical FTN systems. Thus, the 
increasing complexity of the FTN system is inevitable. This can be handled in several ways: 
- 
When using OFDM, a maximum likelihood based detector can be used. 
- 
In single carrier systems, throughput can be improved by using an FTN multi-rate equalizer. 
- 
FTN systems can develop fast implementation modems when using other technologies like 
FBMC or OQAM. 
Multi carrier modulation systems can add FTN signaling and easily benefit from it. However, in [35] 
is stated that, while FTN systems can increase up to twice the bit rate when the transmission 
atmosphere is favorable, there are some topics where more research is required, like finding the 
boundaries of FTN in terms of mobility and delay, or the combination of FTN and MIMO. 
3.4.3.2 Waveform Candidates 
An environment like the one envisioned for the future mobile system will cover many different 
services and situations. Current mobile systems use OFDM, a multi-carrier scheme. In order to fulfill 
future needs, it would be very useful if it is possible to transform a band into independent sub-bands 
which can be fully configured independently depending on the service required.  
This situation is feasible with some new waveforms that use filtering components. In [35] two 
possible new waveforms are introduced, Filter-Bank Multi-Carrier (FBMC) and Universal Filtered 
MultiCarrier (UFMC).  FBMC maintains the overall structure of an OFDM system, and a minimum 
number of sub-carriers are filtered. However, UFMC requires the redefinition of some procedures 
due to the individual filtering of each sub-carrier that this new waveform introduces. 
a. UFMC. This new waveform, also known as Universal-Filtered OFDM (UF-OFDM), is an 
enhancement of the current OFDM used in 4G networks. It improves the capability of 
separation each sub-band independently by applying a per sub-band filtering. That way, each 
sub-band can be set up independently depending on each radio link situation, being able to 
provide high spectral or energy efficiency, depending on the tier of the device. The following 
figure shows a comparison between OFDM and UFMC in terms of power spectral density, 
and it is possible to see the big difference between both waveforms. 
==PAGE:52==
51 
 
 
Figure  24 - Comparison between OFDM and UFMC in terms of Power Spectral Density [36] 
In the time domain, it has been proved that the overhead grows with the number of symbols per 
burst, offering gains compared to both OFDM (around 10%) and FBMC (depending of the size of the 
burst). [37] 
It is a big improvement that a system is able to provide the required quality to their users by the 
possibility of adjusting the link properties to the individual needs. Furthermore, any system working 
with UFMC should be able to manage any scheme of OFDM. 
b. FBMC based waveform 
In multi-carrier systems using FBMC, it is possible to individually filter a single subcarrier by using a 
prototype pulse. [35] It is very useful regarding to the future requirement of a flexible air interface. 
An appropriate selection of this prototype pulse allows to manage each of the sub-bands in an 
optimal way, without creating interference to the other sub-bands adjacent.  
FBMC requires less guard bands at the edges, leading to an improvement of the spectral efficiency, 
compared to OFDM.  In [38] it is shown that this improvement is around 13%. 
Some of the features that make FBMC an interesting option for the 5G air interface are: 
- 
Capability to adapt the shape of the pulse depending on the radio link conditions, in order 
to obtain a proper channel configuration for the users. 
- 
Robustness against synchronization impairments. Regarding the possibility of pulse shape 
adaption, an appropriate configuration allows several users located in adjacent bands to 
transmit asynchronously. 
However, in terms of a practical implementation of systems based on FBMC, some issues have to 
be taken into account: 
- 
When estimating the channel, FBMC is susceptible to degradation in long delay channels and 
there is a problem with the power of the precoding symbols transmitted. 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
- 
In MIMO situations, it may appear inter-block interference when two transmission blocks 
have different channel configuration. MIMO with FBMC can achieve lover BER than MIMO 
with OFDM. 
- 
Any radio link suffers from radio frequency imperfections, which are linked to the waveform 
chosen for that link. 
In conclusion, by using both, FBMC and UFMC, it is easier to fulfill the needs of the different 5G radio 
services due to the individual configuration of each sub-band partitioned. They also provide higher 
energy and spectral efficiency. 
3.4 Summary 
The increasing data traffic will lead to the need of a similar improvement of the network capacity 
to support this growth. One of the most promising techniques to fulfill this capacity requirement is 
the HetNet concept.  
There are three differentiated steps and ways to approach the HetNet problem depending of the 
specific situation: macro cell evolution, outdoor small cells deployment and indoor small cells 
deployment. 
Regarding the spectrum usage, there are currently two trends. The first one is, if possible, continue 
using the range of spectrum below 6 GHz. However, these bands are currently overloaded and the 
availability of enough bandwidth will be studied in the WRC 15. The other trend is to move future 
systems to the frequencies over 30 GHz, the mmWaves.  
The spectrum landscape will be characterized by several authorization usages and ways to access 
the spectrum, with increasing importance of sharing schemes through either centralized or 
distributed solutions. 
Flexibility is going to be the main characteristic of the new air radio interface, being Massive MIMO 
and CoMP some of the key drivers. 
Massive MIMO enhances the 4G MIMO versions, taking advantage of the spatial-division 
multiplexing to be able to improve vastly network capacity, users’ data rates and network efficiency. 
CoMP will be a key feature in interference-limited scenarios, being able to transform interference 
into useful signals for the users. There are two main approaches of this problem, Joint Processing 
and Coordinated Beamforming/Scheduling.  
In order to achieve the expected improvement in capacity and spectrum efficiency, it might be 
required the employment of new waveforms. In this area of study, the main aspects studied will be 
the use of Faster than Nyquist Signaling and FBMC and UFMC as candidate waveforms. While FTN 
makes a better use of the spectrum by transmitting symbols in a more dense manner, UFMC and 
FBMC enables multi carrier systems to be much more flexible by being able to individually 
configuring each sub-carrier depending on the service required.

4. 5G Use Cases Analysis 
4.1 Use Cases Presentation 
As it has been stated in Chapter 3, the requirements for the 5G mobile systems remain unclear and 
diverse. Since this new mobile system will be a lot more than just higher data rates for smartphones, 
very different situations will affect the establishment of these requirements.  
Therefore, all these different situations that must be taken into account bring in a problem in order 
to establish the pre-requisites to fulfill by this new system. For example, the requirements must be 
fulfilled for a great variety of devices. In addition, there will emerge a very high variety of 
applications and variability of the performance attributes. From low-latency applications to delay 
sensitive ones.   From best effort applications to ultra-reliable ones. 
In order to be able to provide a reasonable and proper requirements, some of the previously 
mentioned projects have worked in the definition of several use cases with similar requirements.

4.4 Scenario 2. Ultra-Dense Network 
4.4.1 Description 
This second scenario will also match one of the use cases proposed in [3] and [8]. It will face the 
challenge of ultra-dense networks. In [3] this scenario is presented as “Broadband access in dense 
areas”, while in [8] it is called “Dense urban information society”.  
The main purpose of this scenario is to provide very high speed in very crowded places. This is one 
of the key issues in current deployments, where the user experienced data rates is clearly lowered 
in locations highly populated.  
The scenario would span an area in a big city where the users’ density is very high, resulting in a very 
crowded and high traffic area. It could match some situations like commercial areas or business 
locations, where it is expected that a crowd will be continuously using their mobile devices. It has 
been selected a total area of 5 km². 
According to the methodology followed in the first scenario, some target requirements for this 
situation have been given in both [3] and [8] and will be followed during the analysis of it. Some of 
the most important are: 
- 
User density:  10000 users/km². 
- 
User experienced data rate: 300 Mbps for the downlink and 50 Mbps for the uplink. 
These values should be matched also in cell edges. 
- 
E2E latency: 10 ms. 
- 
Mobility: low, pedestrian. Vehicles will not be considered. 
- 
Traffic volume per subscriber: 500 Gb/month/subscriber  
- 
Availability: 95% in space and time.

4.5 Real Case Situation 
In the previous sections two scenarios have been presented and analyzed. The first one takes place 
in a rural environment, a low density area with low traffic volume. The second one represents an 
ultra-dense deployment. This would match with some areas in big cities where traffic volume is even 
bigger than the average for the cities. 
However, in a real network deployment undertaken by a mobile network operator, the situation is 
not either one scenario or another, but both situations take place and have to be combined and 
taken into account. 
In a real case situation like the one proposed in this section, it will be crucial to use another one of 
the techniques presented as a key enabler of the future mobile network systems, the HetNets. 
It has been analyzed how all the different situations and scenarios propose many different 
challenges in technical requirements and different techniques and features have to be applied by 
the mobile operator in order to satisfy all these different needs in an efficient way. 
The solution proposed would be a combination of both solutions offered for the two scenarios 
analyzed. The cities would be covered by smaller cells, up to several hundreds of meters, and will 
work on the range of mmWaves. The implementation of massive MIMO will also be required, since 
it has been proved that it allows to increase the spectral efficiency widely. 
Between the cities, there would be areas with low traffic density which would represent the rural 
environment scenario. This regions would be covered by macro cells of several kilometers of radius 
and served by frequencies below 6 GHz. This situations would require the implementations of higher 
order modulations to reach higher spectral efficiency, along with an increasing of the bandwidth of 
between 50 and 100 MHz. Some level of MIMO would be required especially in the edge situations 
to be able to provide at least 100 Mbps. AMC would be also required to match the goals within the 
whole coverage area. 
The overall result would be a heterogeneous network with a flexible and dynamic air interface. It 
would be formed by cells of different sizes, different working frequencies and different modulation 
schemes.  
Furthermore, the differences in spectrum requirements will demand a dynamic and flexible 
spectrum, with the capability of any kind of sharing spectrum technique.

4.6 Summary 
During this chapter, two key scenarios for the future generation of mobile systems have been 
studied and analyzed in order to achieve an understanding of the needs to fulfill the technical 
requirements that these scenarios present. Both scenarios are connected to the ones proposed in 
[3] and [8]. 
The first scenario represents a rural environment where the key challenges come in terms of 
providing high capacity and rates to a very wide area. The second one is an ultra-dense network 
scenario. It challenges the capability of a network to be able to provide enough capacity and very 
high data rates to a considerable amount of users located in the same area.  
Link budgets of several ranges of frequencies have been studied in order to understanding of the 
propagation properties of those bands and the manner that they affect the sizes of the cells 
deployed. It has been proved that lower frequencies imply much higher cell radius. In fact, while 
frequencies below 6 GHz are able to provide cell radiuses of several km, the range known as 
mmWaves is only able to provide cells with a radius of a few hundreds of km. 
Based on the traffic projection over the first scenario and the users’ density, a provided capacity 
goal has been set and the requirements from the network to fulfill these needs have been analyzed. 
A reasonable amount of extra bandwidth is required, up to 140-160 MHz, with an increase of the 
spectral efficiency up to 7 bps/Hz. This spectral efficiency might be reachable by spatial multiplexing

5. Conclusions 
 
It has been a long journey since the appearance of the First Generation of mobile systems, around 
1980, until the point of being nowadays using and deploying the Fourth Generation of systems.  
People and society needs and evolution have been the major driver for mobile networks evolution, 
making necessary to develop a mobile system almost every ten years. 
1G was an analog system that introduced the cellular network concept. GSM was the first digital 
system and each generation of mobile systems has moved towards a data communications 
detrimental to the voice communications, being GPRS and EDGE the first systems to use data 
packets communications. Each mobile system has been able to provide higher data rates and offer 
more capacity to the users. UMTS, a 3G system, was able to provide data rates up to 384 kbps by 
introducing new concepts like CDMA, new modulation schemes or AMC. 
LTE employs different access methods in the downlink, OFDMA, and in the uplink, SC-FDMA, and 
allows data rates up to 300 Mbps in the downlink and 75 Mbps in the uplink. LTE-A applies Carrier 
Aggregation to reach up to 1 Gbps in the DL and 500 Mbps in the UL. CoMP is introduced to mitigate 
interferences. 
The need to provide much more network capacity, reach higher data rates no matter the coverage 
and the new scenarios emerged from the machine-type communications have led the urge of a new 
mobile system by 2020. The goal for 5G is to be able to connect anyone or anything that can be 
connected, and everywhere and every time they want to be connected. 
The increasing data traffic will lead to the need of a similar improvement of the network capacity 
to support this growth. One of the most promising techniques to fulfill this capacity requirement is 
the HetNet concept, which has three differentiated steps and ways to approach the problem: 
macro cell evolution, outdoor small cells deployment and indoor small cells deployment. 
Regarding the spectrum usage, there are currently two trends. The first one is, if possible, continue 
using the range of spectrum below 6 GHz, which is subject to the availability of enough bandwidth. 
The other trend is to move future systems to the frequencies over 30 GHz, the mmWaves.  
The spectrum landscape will be characterized by several authorization usages and ways to access 
the spectrum, with increasing importance of sharing schemes through either centralized or 
distributed solutions. 
The new air radio interface will require high flexibility, being Massive MIMO and CoMP some of the 
key drivers. Massive MIMO takes advantage of the spatial-division multiplexing to be able to 
improve vastly network capacity, users’ data rates and network efficiency. 
CoMP will be able to transform interference into useful signals for the users in interference-limited 
scenarios. There are two main approaches of this problem, Joint Processing and Coordinated 
Beamforming/Scheduling. 
==PAGE:83==
82 
 
The possibility of needing new waveforms is also under study. While FTN signaling makes a better 
use of the spectrum by transmitting symbols in a more dense manner, UFMC and FBMC based 
waveforms enable multi carrier systems to be much more flexible by being able to individually 
configuring each sub-carrier depending on the service required. 
Two of the presented scenarios have been studied and analyzed in order to achieve an 
understanding of the needs to fulfill the technical requirements faced.  
The first scenario represents a rural environment where the key challenges come in terms of 
providing high capacity and rates to a very wide area.  
Link budgets of several ranges of frequencies have been studied in order to understanding of the 
propagation properties and their influence in the sizes of the cells deployed. Lower frequencies 
allow much higher cell radiuses. In fact, while frequencies below 6 GHz are able to provide cell 
radiuses of several km, the range known as mmWaves is only able to provide cells with a radius of 
a few hundreds of km. 
Based on the traffic projection over the first scenario and the users’ density, a provided capacity 
goal has been set and the requirements from the network to fulfill these needs have been analyzed. 
A reasonable amount of extra bandwidth is required, up to 140-160 MHz, with an increase of the 
spectral efficiency up to 7 bps/Hz. This spectral efficiency might be reachable by spatial multiplexing 
of by introducing 256-QAM in the modulation scheme (its maximum theoretical spectral efficiency 
is 8 bps/Hz). 
In edge situation, it has been proved that it will be necessary to employ MIMO, at least 2x2 or 4x2, 
to reach the target of 100 Mbps. If 8x8 MIMO is deployed, almost no variation in spectral efficiency 
in the edge is required. 
The second scenario represents an ultra-dense deployment scenario, where a high traffic area is 
presented. Here it has been proved that, given the vast amount of spectrum that might be needed, 
the most suitable range of frequencies for this deployment is the mmWaves. The result would be a 
much denser network with cells of up to few hundred meters. 
A theoretical approach has also shown that the application of MIMO above 12 transmission 
antennas, will lower deeply the growth required in terms of spectral efficiency or bandwidth. 
UE enhancements are also required to make effective the network improvements. Supporting 
higher order modulations or higher order of MIMO will be required. 
Finally, a real case scenario is proposed, where a combination of both first and second scenario 
takes place. A HetNet with a flexible and dynamic air interface with the possibility of access to 
shared spectrum techniques will be the result. In the rural areas, a solution based on macro cells 
and frequencies below 6 GHz would be implemented, while in the high traffic areas, a solution 
based on small cells working on the mmWaves range would be the most suitable. MIMO would be 
required until some extent in both situations.
