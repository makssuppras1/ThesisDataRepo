Abstract
Physiological monitoring during magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is typically performed
using contact-based sensors such as respiratory bellows and pulse oximeters. While effec-
tive, these systems can complicate setup, introduce artifacts, and reduce patient comfort.
This project explores whether respiratory and cardiac phase information can be inferred
non-invasively from head motion captured by a markerless optical tracking system.
Head motion was acquired using the TracInnovations Tracoline system during MRI ses-
sions, alongside reference physiological signals from standard clinical sensors. The system
recorded head motion in six degrees of freedom, which served as input to machine learning
models trained for both classification and regression. Support vector machines (SVMs),
temporal convolutional networks (TCNs), and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks
were evaluated across both tasks.
LSTM models consistently achieved the best performance, particularly in respiratory phase
estimation, where they produced accurate and smooth predictions across most sequences.
Cardiac phase estimation proved more diﬀicult due to the lower amplitude of pulse-induced
motion and interference from noise and respiration, though some sequences exhibited
encouraging trends.
These results demonstrate that respiratory and, to a lesser extent, cardiac phase informa-
tion is encoded in head motion captured during MRI. This approach offers a promising
non-contact alternative for physiological monitoring, with potential to simplify workflows
and enhance patient comfort in clinical settings.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation and Context
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is an essential tool in both clinical diagnostics and
neuroscience research. However, image quality is highly sensitive to subject motion, par-
ticularly in brain MRI, where even small head movements can introduce artifacts that
compromise diagnostic reliability and research outcomes.
To address this, various motion correction strategies have been developed. One promising
approach is optical motion tracking, which enables real-time estimation of subject move-
ment without interfering with image acquisition. This study focuses on data acquired
using a markerless optical tracking system developed for the MRI environment by TracIn-
novations A/S (Ballerup, Denmark).
The system provides continuous, high-resolution
head motion data throughout the scan and is primarily used for motion correction. Be-
yond this application, the signal may also reflect subtle physiological patterns—such as
those caused by respiration or cardiac activity.
These physiological processes play a key role in MRI acquisition and analysis. They are
typically recorded using contact-based devices, like respiratory belts or pulse oximeters, to
reduce physiological noise or enable synchronization. However, such sensors can be prone
to placement errors, patient discomfort, and increased setup complexity.
Recent research has explored contact-free alternatives for physiological monitoring, includ-
ing Pilot Tone (PT) [1], remote photoplethysmography (rPPG) [2], and Doppler radar [3].
This raises the question of whether optical motion tracking data might also contain embed-
ded physiological signals that could be exploited without requiring additional hardware.
1.2
Problem Statement
Although the tracking system used in this study continuously records head motion dur-
ing MRI, it is not currently used for physiological monitoring. Preliminary observations
suggest that periodic components related to respiration and cardiac activity may be em-
bedded within the motion signal. However, it remains unclear whether these physiological
patterns can be reliably isolated from voluntary movement and system noise.
The key challenge is to determine whether the motion signal contains suﬀicient physiolog-
ical information to allow for accurate, robust, and generalizable estimation of respiratory
and cardiac cycles. This involves separating physiology-driven components from unrelated
motion, validating the extracted signals against ground-truth measurements, and assessing
performance across subjects and scanning conditions.
This leads to the central research question of this study: Can respiratory and cardiac
signals be accurately and reliably estimated from optical head motion data recorded during
MRI, using a markerless tracking system as a standalone, sensor-free source of physiological
information?
1.3
Objectives
This project investigates whether motion data acquired from a markerless optical tracking
system during MRI can serve as an effective source of physiological information.
The
primary goal is to estimate respiratory and cardiac cycles from the motion signal without
relying on conventional contact-based sensors.
1
==PAGE:10==
To achieve this, machine learning models are developed to infer physiological signals from
motion features, with their performance validated against ground-truth recordings ob-
tained from a pulse oximeter and respiratory cushion. A successful outcome could sim-
plify MRI workflows, reduce setup time, and eliminate the need for separate physiological
sensors—since both motion and physiological information would be available from the
same data source.

2
State of the Art
2.1
Motion Artifacts in MRI
MRI is an advanced imaging technique widely used in clinical diagnostics and neuroscience
research due to its ability to generate high-resolution images of soft tissues, including the
brain, organs, and musculoskeletal structures. Its non-invasive nature and flexibility in
imaging contrast mechanisms make it particularly valuable in longitudinal and functional
studies. However, a significant drawback of MRI is its susceptibility to motion-related
artifacts, which remain a significant challenge despite decades of technical advancement.
Unlike modalities such as computerized tomography (CT) or X-ray, MRI involves relatively
long acquisition times. The signal is acquired sequentially over time, which makes it highly
vulnerable to any motion occurring during the scan. Even minor movements can disrupt
the spatial encoding process, resulting in degraded image quality. These motion-induced
artifacts typically appear as blurring, ghosting, signal dropout, or spatial distortion, which
can obscure anatomical details or mimic pathological conditions. In clinical contexts, this
can reduce diagnostic confidence, often requiring repeat scans and thereby increasing both
scan time and healthcare costs. In research settings, such artifacts can significantly impact
quantitative analyses, resulting in data misinterpretation or loss of statistical reliability.
Motion sources in MRI are varied and frequently physiological. These include periodic
involuntary movements, such as those associated with respiration and cardiac activity;
sudden involuntary actions like swallowing, sneezing, yawning, or blinking; and voluntary
or conscious movements, often triggered by discomfort or anxiety experienced during the
scan [4].
2.1.1
Physiological Artifacts
Physiological processes, particularly cardiac pulsation and respiration, are major sources
of motion artifacts in MRI, particularly in the context of brain imaging. Although in-
voluntary and often subtle, these motions can introduce signal fluctuations that degrade
image quality.
Cardiac-induced motion arises from the pulsatile nature of cerebral blood flow. During
the systolic phase, the rapid influx of blood into the brain elevates intracranial pressure,
which in turn causes arterial expansion and displacement of brain tissue and cerebrospinal
fluid (CSF). This dynamic interaction causes bulk motion of brain regions—especially
central structures like the brainstem and diencephalon—as well as microvascular shifts at
the capillary level [5]. These movements can induce phase shifts and signal variations,
resulting in artifacts, especially in sequences sensitive to flow or phase, such as phase-
contrast MRI.
Respiratory motion similarly affects brain MRI. The movement of the chest and diaphragm
during breathing can induce slight head displacement, leading to spatial misalignments in
the acquired data. In addition, respiration alters the magnetic field homogeneity, resulting
in image shifts in the phase-encoding direction and increased susceptibility to artifacts [6].
In functional MRI (fMRI), these physiological movements pose significant challenges. The
blood-oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD) signal utilized in fMRI is sensitive not only to neu-
ral activity but also to fluctuations related to cardiac and respiratory cycles. These varia-
tions can introduce low-frequency noise that mimics or obscures neural signals, potentially
complicating the interpretation of functional data [7].
3
==PAGE:12==
Beyond brain imaging, physiological motion is also a major consideration in cardiac and
abdominal MRI. In cardiac applications, the inherent motion of the beating heart needs
precise synchronization between image acquisition and the cardiac cycle to reduce mo-
tion blur and spatial misregistration [8].
Similarly, in abdominal imaging, respiratory
motion can cause substantial displacement of organs such as the liver and kidneys, intro-
ducing artifacts and reducing diagnostic quality [9]. In both cases, accurate monitoring
and correction of physiological motion are essential to ensure image fidelity and clinical
interpretation.
To address these issues, several correction strategies are employed. Retrospective methods
use physiological recordings (e.g., ECG and respiratory belts) to model and eliminate
physiological noise from the data. In contrast, prospective techniques synchronize image
acquisition with specific phases of the cardiac or respiratory cycles, known as gating, which
helps minimize motion-related artifacts by controlling when the images are captured.
2.2
Physiological Signal Acquisition in MRI
Accurate acquisition of physiological signals during MRI is essential for synchronizing
image acquisition with cardiac and respiratory cycles, thereby minimizing motion arti-
facts.
Traditionally, this has relied on dedicated hardware sensors physically attached
to the subject. However, these approaches come with several limitations, motivating the
development of sensor-free alternatives.
2.2.1
Traditional Techniques and Limitations
ECG and pulse oximetry are standard methods for monitoring cardiac activity.
ECG
involves attaching electrodes to the patient’s skin to detect electrical signals of the heart,
while pulse oximetry uses light absorption through a fingertip sensor to estimate blood oxy-
gen saturation and pulse rate. The latter method provides a delayed but less interference-
prone cardiac signal compared to ECG in some contexts.
For respiratory monitoring,
respiratory belts are typically placed around the chest or abdomen to track variations in
thoracic or abdominal circumference; alternatively, respiratory cushions or pressure sen-
sors can be situated beneath the back or around the torso to detect pressure fluctuations
caused by breathing.
While these sensors are widely used and can offer good signal quality under ideal condi-
tions, they present several practical limitations:
• Patient discomfort: The placement of electrodes, belts, or sensors can be uncom-
fortable, especially in long scans or in sensitive populations like pediatric or elderly
patients.
• Subjectivity of fit: The correct placement and tension of belts and sensors are
operator-dependent and can vary between sessions and technicians, leading to vari-
ability in signal quality.
• Sensitivity to motion and scanner environment: These devices are prone to displace-
ment, signal degradation, or even disconnection due to subject motion, sweating,
or magnetic field interference, especially during long acquisitions or in high-field
scanners, resulting in data loss and failed gating or correction.
• Extended setup times: Proper placement and testing of sensors increase the prepa-
ration time prior to scanning, which is particularly critical in clinical workflows.
These challenges have prompted the exploration of alternative methods that reduce re-
liance on physical sensors.

2.2.2
Sensor-Free Alternatives
The motivation to reduce patient discomfort, shorten setup time and maintain signal qual-
ity in the face of motion has stimulated a new generation of “sensor-free” physiological
monitoring methods. Instead of attaching electrodes or belts, these approaches exploit
intrinsic MRI signals, built-in hardware or external non-contact modalities to derive car-
diac or respiratory waveforms suitable for either prospective triggering or retrospective
correction.
Navigator and Self-navigator Echoes
Navigators in MRI are brief, low-resolution data acquisitions integrated into the imaging
sequence to monitor subject motion in real time [10]. The earliest and simplest imple-
mentation, the one-dimensional (1D) pencil-beam navigator, employs a narrow excitation
beam placed over a specific anatomical structure—typically the diaphragm or heart. By
measuring displacement along a single axis, the navigator enables respiratory gating using
a predefined acceptance window. This helps reduce motion-related artifacts by selectively
acquiring data when the anatomy is within the desired position. While effective, data
acquisition is suspended when the target moves outside the window, which may extend
exam duration [11].
To overcome this limitation, image-based navigators (iNAVs) have been introduced. These
acquire low-resolution 2D or 3D images just before each high-resolution imaging seg-
ment. iNAVs offer a broader spatial context, allowing real-time estimation of both rigid-
body translations and minor deformations. This motion information can be used either
prospectively—to adjust imaging parameters during acquisition—or retrospectively, by
sorting data into motion-resolved bins [12].
More recently, self-navigated approaches have emerged, particularly in radial and golden-
angle sampling schemes. These methods exploit the intrinsic repeated sampling of central
k-space, allowing physiological motion signals to be extracted directly from the imaging
data itself. This method achieves 100% scan eﬀiciency and eliminates the need for external
navigators, although it increases reconstruction complexity [13].
Pilot-Tone Radiofrequency Tracking
A Pilot-Tone (PT) system transmits a very low-power, narrow-band radiofrequency (RF)
signal from a small antenna mounted inside the scanner bore. This signal is positioned well
outside the imaging bandwidth, ensuring that it does not interfere with the acquisition of
diagnostic images. As the patient’s thorax or heart moves—due to respiratory or cardiac
activity—the standing-wave pattern of this reference tone is subtly modulated. These
modulations, reflected as frequency and phase shifts, are detected by the receiver array
and demodulated in real time to obtain clean respiratory and, at higher harmonics, cardiac
waveforms [1].
Since the PT operates continuously and independently of the imaging pulse sequence, it
can be superimposed on any MRI protocol without increasing scan time. This enables
real-time motion tracking and correction, including prospective slice adjustments and ret-
rospective gating, with latencies on the order of milliseconds [14]. A recent clinical study
demonstrated that PT can reliably capture both respiratory and cardiac motion dur-
ing cardiovascular MRI, showing strong agreement with image-derived respiratory traces
(correlation coeﬀicient r > 0.95) and ECG-based cardiac triggers (median jitter < 22 ms),
supporting its potential for accurate physiological monitoring [15].
BioMatrix Respiratory Sensor
Some MRI systems integrate respiratory monitoring hardware directly into the scanner
table to streamline physiological data acquisition. One example is the BioMatrix Respi-
ratory Sensor developed by Siemens Healthineers, which is embedded within the patient
5
==PAGE:14==
table and spine coil.
It detects respiration by measuring changes in the loading of a
dedicated sensing coil as the chest expands and contracts.
This coil operates at a distinct frequency from the Larmor frequency used for imaging,
allowing respiratory signals to be acquired independently of the imaging data. A small
input current is applied, and variations in the output current correspond to the respiratory
cycle, increasing during inhalation and decreasing during exhalation [16].
Since the sensor is pre-installed and calibrated by the manufacturer, it does not require
additional placement or setup by the operator.
Compared to conventional respiratory
belts, the fixed sensor reduces variability caused by positioning errors or loose fittings.
A recent study on a 3T MAGNETOM Vida system showed that the embedded sensor
produced respiratory traces comparable to those from external belts during resting-state
fMRI and demonstrated improved consistency during task-based imaging [17].
Remote Photoplethysmography
Remote Photoplethysmography (rPPG) is a non-contact optical technique for extracting
physiological signals—primarily cardiac and respiratory waveforms—by analyzing subtle,
periodic variations in skin reflectance captured through video imaging. These variations
are induced by changes in blood volume within the superficial vasculature, which modulate
the intensity of reflected light, particularly in the green channel of RGB video due to its
sensitivity to hemoglobin absorption. The method involves recording video of the subject’s
face or other exposed skin regions using a camera positioned outside the bore. Temporal
fluctuations in pixel intensity are then processed using signal decomposition and filtering
techniques to isolate the physiological components from noise sources such as motion
artifacts, lighting variability, and scanner-induced interference [2].
The feasibility of rPPG in MRI environments has been demonstrated in recent studies,
which validated the technique against standard physiological monitoring tools. These in-
vestigations showed that rPPG-derived signals were comparable to those obtained from
finger-based PPG and ECG, supporting its potential for retrospective gating and physio-
logical noise correction in fMRI applications [18, 19].
Continuous-Wave Doppler Radar
Continuous-wave (CW) Doppler radar enables non-contact monitoring of physiological
motion by continuously transmitting a RF signal—typically in the 2–5 GHz range—and
analyzing phase shifts in the reflected signal caused by thoracic movements associated
with respiration and cardiac activity. Positioned outside the MRI bore and directed at
the chest, the radar captures these subtle displacements and demodulates the signal to
extract real-time respiratory and cardiac waveforms. Since the radar operates continuously
and data acquisition occurs concurrently with standard MR readouts, it does not extend
scan duration or introduce significant additional RF energy.
Proof-of-concept studies at 3T have demonstrated the feasibility of using CW Doppler
radar for non-contact acquisition of respiratory and cardiac signals during MRI. Operated
at 2.4 GHz, the system caused minimal interference with MR image quality, supporting
its potential for retrospective gating applications [3].
Ultra-Wideband Radar
Ultra-Wideband (UWB) radar is a contactless sensing technology that uses very short
electromagnetic pulses across a wide frequency range of several GHz to detect motion.
In MRI applications, a UWB radar system transmits a pseudorandom waveform—often
an M-sequence—and measures the time delay and amplitude of reflected signals from
internal anatomical structures.
These reflections vary with physiological motion, such
as the expansion of the lungs or the beating of the heart, allowing the radar to detect
6
==PAGE:15==
respiratory and cardiac activity without any physical contact or interference with the
MRI scanner.
This technique has been validated in a combined MRI/UWB prototype operating at 3T,
where researchers demonstrated that the radar system could monitor myocardial and tho-
racic motion without disrupting MR image acquisition. The physiological signals captured
by UWB radar were compared with simultaneous MR-based measurements, confirming the
radar’s accuracy in tracking internal organ motion [20].
2.3
Optical Motion Tracking in MRI
MR navigators (§2.2.2) can measure subject motion without the need for external hard-
ware; however, they consume additional sequence time and may not effectively capture
rapid or complex head movements [21].
External optical tracking systems overcome these limitations by continuously monitoring
the subject from outside the magnet bore, delivering a six-degree-of-freedom (6-DOF) pose
stream that is fully independent of the MRI’s RF and gradient sequences. When operated
with suﬀiciently high frame rates and submillimeter spatial resolution, these systems can
resolve both voluntary and involuntary motion components.
2.3.1
TracInnovations Optical Tracker, Tracoline
The Tracoline system by TracInnovations is a markerless optical motion tracking solution
developed for MRI and hybrid PET/MRI environments. It utilizes a non-contact 3D stereo
vision approach that relies on invisible infrared structured light to continuously acquire
high-resolution surface data of the patient’s face. From these data, the system estimates
rigid head motion in 6-DOF in real time.
To ensure full MRI compatibility, the system architecture eliminates in-bore electronics.
A passive vision probe is positioned near the patient’s head inside the scanner bore and
connected via optical fibers to a camera and infrared light source housed in an RF-shielded
enclosure located behind the scanner, outside the bore.
This configuration minimizes
electromagnetic interference and ensures safe operation in the high magnetic field. Power
is supplied from the adjacent control room through a standard RF wall filter, while data are
transmitted via an optical cable routed through a waveguide to the TracSuite processing
workstation located in the control room. This setup is illustrated in Figure 2.1.
The system operates at 30 Hz, generating dense 3D point clouds of the facial surface.
Motion estimation is performed by aligning each new point cloud to a reference frame
using the Iterative Closest Point (ICP) algorithm. Each pose estimate is accompanied by
a tracking validity parameter, which quantifies confidence in the measurement. If tracking
is compromised due to occlusion, rapid motion, or poor visibility, the system discards the
unreliable estimate and retains the last valid pose [22].
This design enables robust, real-time motion tracking suitable for prospective and ret-
rospective motion correction in MRI, significantly enhancing image quality and reducing
motion-related artifacts in clinical neuroimaging.
2.4
Applications of Physiological Signals in MRI
2.4.1
Physiological Gating
Physiological gating is a method used to mitigate motion artifacts by synchronizing image
acquisition with specific phases of the cardiac or respiratory cycles. The goal is to acquire
data when the body, or specific anatomy, is relatively still. This can be done through
either prospective or retrospective gating methods.
7
==PAGE:16==
Figure 2.1: Schematic overview of the Tracoline optical tracking system. A vision probe
inside the MRI bore captures head motion and transmits it to the external TracSuite
workstation [22].
Prospective Gating
In prospective gating, MRI data acquisition is actively synchronized with a selected phase
of the cardiac or respiratory cycle. Physiological monitoring devices—such as ECG for
heart activity or respiratory bellows for breathing—are employed to generate real-time
triggers.
These triggers prompt the scanner to initiate data collection during periods
when physiological motion is minimal.
In the case of cardiac gating, data acquisition is synchronized with the cardiac cycle,
typically using the R-wave of the ECG as a temporal reference. This approach ensures
that data is collected during diastole, when the heart is in a relaxed state and motion
is minimal [23]. Similarly, respiratory gating aims to capture data at consistent points
within the respiratory cycle, often during end-expiration, when chest and head motion
are reduced [24]. This technique is highly effective in reducing motion artifacts; however,
it necessitates very precise timing and may extend scan time due to inactivity periods
between acquisitions. Furthermore, in the context of cardiac gating, it may be vulnerable
to arrhythmias.
Retrospective Gating
Retrospective gating, by contrast, involves continuous acquisition of MRI data alongside
simultaneous recording of physiological signals. After the scan, the physiological traces
are used to retrospectively assign each acquired data point to a corresponding phase of the
cardiac or respiratory cycle. This process allows a more flexible and robust reconstruction
of images or time series that are temporally aligned with specific physiological states.
Both gating approaches are widely used in cardiac, abdominal, and neuroimaging appli-
cations, where physiological motion poses a significant challenge. By synchronizing image
acquisition with specific phases of the respiratory or cardiac cycles, these techniques help
reduce artifacts and improve the reliability of anatomical and functional measurements.
2.4.2
Physiological Noise Regression in fMRI
fMRI relies on the BOLD signal to infer neuronal activity. However, the BOLD signal
is not only sensitive to neuronal fluctuations but is also significantly affected by physio-
logical processes such as cardiac pulsation and respiration. These processes can introduce
structured, low-frequency fluctuations and aliasing artifacts into the fMRI time series that
mimic or obscure neural signals, ultimately reducing statistical power and interpretability
if not accounted for.
Physiological noise regression aims to isolate and remove these non-neuronal fluctuations
from fMRI data. A common approach involves recording physiological signals—typically
8
==PAGE:17==
cardiac and respiratory traces—during the scan and using these signals to model their
corresponding variance in the BOLD time series. This correction is especially important
in resting-state fMRI, where structured physiological noise can dominate low-frequency
bands.
One of the most widely used methods is RETROICOR (RETROspective Image COR-
rection), which uses concurrently recorded ECG and respiratory waveforms to generate
phase-based regressors that account for periodic physiological fluctuations [25].
These
regressors are incorporated into a general linear model (GLM) to remove corresponding
variance from the fMRI signal.
More advanced approaches, such as DRIFTER, also rely on external physiological record-
ings but model them using Bayesian filtering. This allows for more flexible estimation of
physiological noise, accommodating nonstationary heart and breathing rates throughout
the scan [26].
In contrast, other techniques operate without any external physiological measurements.
For example, CompCor identifies noise components directly from the fMRI data by ap-
plying principal component analysis (PCA) to signal fluctuations in non-neuronal regions,
which are then used to account for noise in gray matter signals [27]. Similarly, PESTICA
infers physiological signals from the fMRI time series itself using independent component
analysis (ICA) to estimate respiratory and cardiac rhythms [28].

3
Background
The goal of this project is to estimate respiratory and cardiac activity from head motion
data acquired during MRI using a markerless optical tracking system. This motion data
evolves over time and is inherently sequential. Accordingly, the learning tasks are designed
to reflect the cyclical structure of physiological signals.
Two supervised tasks are defined:
• Phase classification: assigning each time point to a discrete phase of the respiratory
or cardiac cycle.
• Phase regression: predicting a continuous phase value that captures the signal’s
progression through the cycle.
To address these tasks, three machine learning architectures are explored. Support Vector
Machines (SVMs) serve as a baseline, relying on static, hand-crafted features without
modeling temporal dynamics explicitly. In contrast, Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
networks and Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs) are designed to learn temporal
dependencies directly from the sequential input.
The following sections provide a theoretical overview of these models and explain their
relevance for modeling physiological sequences.
3.1
Support Vector Machine
SVMs are supervised learning models used primarily for classification tasks, although
they can also be extended to regression. The fundamental goal of an SVM is to identify a
hyperplane in an N-dimensional feature space (where N is the number of input features)
that best separates the classes by maximizing the margin — the distance between the
hyperplane and the nearest data points of each class, known as support vectors [29]. This
concept is illustrated in Figure 3.1.

optimal separating hyperplane, while the dashed lines represent the decision boundaries
that define the margin width. The circled data points lying exactly on these margins are
the support vectors, which determine the position of the hyperplane [30].

3.2
Long Short-Term Memory
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are designed to process sequential data by maintaining
a hidden state that evolves over time. At each time step, the hidden state is updated based
on the current input and the previous state, allowing the network to capture temporal
dependencies. However, standard RNNs suffer from the vanishing gradient problem, which
hampers their ability to learn long-term dependencies, especially over lengthy sequences.
LSTM networks, were introduced to mitigate this issue by incorporating a memory cell
and three gates: the input gate, forget gate, and output gate. These gates control the flow
of information into and out of the memory cell, allowing the network to retain or discard
information as needed [32].
At each time step t, the LSTM performs the following computations. The forget gate
determines which elements of the previous cell state should be retained:
ft = σ(Wfxt + Ufht−1 + bf)

The input gate controls how much new information is incorporated into the cell state:
it = σ(Wixt + Uiht−1 + bi)
A candidate update to the cell state is then computed as:
ˆCt = tanh(Wcxt + Ucht−1 + bc)
These components are combined to update the cell state:
Ct = ft ⊙ct−1 + it ⊙˜ct
Finally, the output gate determines the next hidden state based on the updated cell state:
ot = σ(Woxt + Uoht−1 + bo)
ht = ot ⊙tanh(ct)
In these equations, xt is the input vector at time t, ht−1 is the hidden state from the
previous time step, and Ct−1 is the previous cell state. The terms W∗and U∗are weight
matrices, and b∗are bias vectors, all of which are learned during training. The functions
σ and tanh denote the sigmoid and hyperbolic tangent activation functions, respectively,
and ⊙represents element-wise multiplication.
The key component of the LSTM is its cell state Ct, which acts as an internal memory that
can carry information across time steps. This structure helps preserve gradients during
backpropagation through time, thereby allowing the network to learn long-range patterns.

LSTMs are especially effective in modeling temporal dependencies over moderate to long
sequences. Bidirectional variants, which process sequences in both forward and backward
directions, can further enhance performance when the complete sequence is accessible.
These properties make LSTMs a strong candidate for tasks involving time-dependent
physiological signals, where understanding the evolution of motion over time is key to
accurate phase classification.

3.3
Temporal Convolutional Network
TCNs are a class of convolutional neural networks adapted for sequence modeling tasks.
Unlike recurrent models such as LSTMs, TCNs use 1D convolutions to capture tempo-
ral dependencies, which enables parallel computation and generally leads to more stable
gradient propagation during training.
A defining characteristic of TCNs is the use of causal convolutions, which ensure that the
output at time step t depends only on current and past inputs, not future ones. This
property is essential for applications such as real-time prediction or forecasting, where
future data is not yet available.
To eﬀiciently model long-range dependencies, TCNs incorporate dilated convolutions,
which introduce fixed gaps between the elements of the convolution kernel.

By stacking layers with exponentially increasing dilation factors (e.g., d = 1, 2, 4, 8), the
receptive field of the network grows rapidly, as illustrated in Figure 3.3a. This enables
TCNs to model long-term temporal patterns while keeping the architecture relatively
shallow and computationally eﬀicient.
Another important component is the use of residual connections, inspired by ResNet, which
help mitigate vanishing gradients and allow deeper networks to be trained effectively. A
typical TCN block consists of a dilated causal convolution, non-linearity (e.g., ReLU),
dropout, and a residual connection that either passes input directly or applies a 1 × 1

4.2
Preprocessing
The preprocessing pipeline was designed to prepare both the optical motion tracking data
and the physiological ground truth signals for subsequent signal analysis and machine
learning tasks. Given the multimodal nature of the data and the variability in acquisition
formats and quality, careful preprocessing was necessary to ensure alignment, compara-
bility, and robustness.
4.2.1
Resampling
The physiological data acquired from the MRI scanner (respiratory cushion and pulse
oximeter) was recorded at a fixed sampling rate of 400 Hz. In contrast, the Tracoline
18
==PAGE:27==
optical motion tracking system produces data at a variable sampling rate of approximately
30 Hz.
To enable temporal alignment between modalities, the tracking data was resampled to a
uniform time grid using interpolation. Specifically, a linear interpolation method was ap-
plied independently to each of the 6-DOF, as well as the 3D motion signal. The resampled
output matched the time resolution of the physiological signals, ensuring consistent input
across all downstream analysis and modeling pipelines.
While linear interpolation provides a simple and computationally eﬀicient solution, it
assumes that the signal between known samples changes smoothly. As such, it does not
recover high-frequency components that may have been under-sampled or lost due to the
motion tracking system’s lower sampling rate.
Rapid movements cannot be faithfully
reconstructed and may instead result in aliasing or temporal blurring. However, since
physiological signals like respiration and cardiac cycles operate within frequency ranges
that are well below the tracking system’s sampling limit, this interpolation strategy is
expected to be adequate without introducing significant distortion.
4.2.2
Temporal Alignment
To synchronize the optical motion tracking and physiological data streams, a triggering
mechanism was implemented during acquisition, enabling the MRI scanner and the Tra-
coline system to exchange timing signals. While this provided precise timing for aligning
the tracking data with the imaging sequences, the physiological recordings did not capture
these triggers. As a result, direct temporal alignment between the physiological signals
and the other data streams was not possible.
To overcome this, semi-manual alignment was performed by identifying common features—
such as periodic respiratory patterns—present in both the motion and physiological signals.
The 3D motion trace was particularly useful for this purpose, as it aggregates head move-
ment across all axes, making it easier to visually match with the respiratory waveform.
Figure 4.2 illustrates an example of this alignment, showing the correspondence between
the respiratory signal and the 3D motion trace over time.
Figure 4.2: Temporal alignment of motion and respiration signals. The blue curve repre-
sents 3D motion data from the tracking system, while the pink curve shows the respiration
signal from the respiratory cushion.

some cases, physiological recordings were excessively noisy or incomplete, often due to
physical limitations of the sensor setup, such as poor contact or signal saturation during
motion. These segments could not be reliably labeled and were therefore removed.

4.3
Label Generation for Supervised Learning
To enable supervised machine learning approaches for physiological phase classification
and regression, ground truth labels were derived from respiratory and cardiac waveforms
acquired during MRI scanning. The labeling process was designed to provide accurate
temporal references, using both discrete and continuous representations of physiological
cycles, depending on the task.
4.3.1
Classification Labels: Discrete Phase Segmentation
The classification labeling pipeline involved four main steps: signal filtering, peak detec-
tion, phase segmentation, and final visual validation. Each stage was essential to mitigate
the effects of noise and artifacts inherent to physiological recordings in the MRI environ-
ment.
The raw respiratory and pulse signals were initially filtered to suppress noise while preserv-
ing the characteristic oscillatory patterns. Respiratory signals were filtered using low-pass
filters with cutoffs between 0.2–0.5 Hz, tailored to individual breathing rates. Pulse sig-
nals were band-pass filtered using a combination of high-pass (0.5–0.7 Hz) and low-pass
(1.3–2.5 Hz) filters to isolate heartbeats. These filter parameters were selected empirically
per subject.
From the cleaned signals, a peak detection algorithm identified local maxima and minima
that defined individual physiological cycles. Each cycle was segmented between two suc-
cessive minima, with the maximum in between serving as the cycle’s peak. A midpoint
20
==PAGE:29==
value—halfway between the minimum and maximum amplitudes—was used to divide the
cycle into four temporal segments:
• Phase 0 – Rise from minimum to midpoint
• Phase 1 – Rise from midpoint to maximum
• Phase 2 – Fall from maximum to midpoint
• Phase 3 – Fall from midpoint to next minimum
Each time point was assigned a label from 0 to 3 based on its position within this structure.
This labeling approach offered a physiologically meaningful and cyclic representation of
the waveform, as illustrated in Figure 4.4.

4.3.2
Regression Labels: Continuous Phase Representation
In addition to discrete classification, a regression-based approach was also developed to
capture the continuous nature of physiological cycles.
Instead of assigning categorical
phase labels, each time point in the waveform was mapped to a continuous phase angle on
the unit circle, representing its relative position within the cycle, as shown in Figure 4.5.
Each physiological cycle was defined as the interval between two successive zero-crossings
of the waveform, with one maximum and one minimum in between. The cycle was linearly
parameterized from 0 to 2π, such that the first zero-crossing corresponded to 0, the peak
to π/2, the second zero-crossing to π, and the trough to 3π/2. The resulting continu-
ous phase θ(t) was then encoded as a two-dimensional vector (cos θ(t), sin θ(t)), avoiding
discontinuities between cycle boundaries and enabling smooth regression targets.
Figure 4.5: Continuous labeling for regression. Each physiological cycle is mapped to a
circular phase angle and encoded as (cos θ, sin θ), preserving temporal continuity across
cycle boundaries.
4.4
Sliding Window Segmentation
To enable supervised learning from continuous motion tracking data, a sliding-window
segmentation strategy was employed. Each training sample corresponded to a fixed-length
segment of input data, and the associated label (discrete phase or continuous angle) was
taken from the final time point in the window. This setup reflects a causal, real-time
prediction scenario, where only past and present information is available at inference
time.
Different window lengths were used depending on the target physiological signal: 1.5
seconds (600 samples at 400 Hz) for respiratory phase modeling, and 1.0 second (400
samples) for cardiac phase modeling. In both cases, windows were extracted with 90%
overlap to ensure dense sampling and smooth phase predictions across time.
To reduce boundary artifacts during filtering, each window was temporarily padded with
0.5 seconds of real data before and after its range. The full padded segment was used
exclusively during filtering, and the padding was removed afterward to recover the clean,
filtered window.
Filtering was performed in two stages: a low-pass filter was first applied to suppress high-
frequency noise, followed by a high-pass filter to remove low-frequency drift. The exact
filter parameters were chosen based on the target physiological signal. For respiratory
signals, cutoffs were set between 0.08 and 0.4 Hz, while for cardiac signals, higher cutoff
22
==PAGE:31==
ranges were used to reflect the faster heart rate (e.g., 0.7 to 1.6 Hz). These filters helped
isolate the relevant physiological components while minimizing motion-related noise and
baseline fluctuations.
Although this approach introduces an additional 500 ms delay in a real-time prediction
scenario—due to the use of 0.5 seconds of future data—it was necessary to ensure stable
filtering and accurate prediction. Removing this padding resulted in a notable performance
drop, with accuracy decreasing by more than 10
These preprocessing steps—windowing, padding, and frequency-selective filtering—were
applied uniformly across all modeling pipelines to produce consistent, clean motion seg-
ments. Depending on the model, either features selected based on their predictive value
were extracted from these segments (SVM), or the filtered sequences were used directly
as input (neural networks).
4.5
Modeling Approaches
To extract respiratory and cardiac phase information from optical motion tracking data,
several modeling strategies were developed. These ranged from traditional signal process-
ing baselines to deep learning models trained in both classification and regression settings.
4.5.1
Discrete Phase Classification
The classification task involved assigning each windowed motion segment to one of four
discrete phase labels (0–3), as described in Section 4.3.1. Three classification models were
evaluated: a SVM as a traditional baseline, and two sequential neural networks—LSTM
and TCN.
Support Vector Machine
The SVM used handcrafted feature vectors derived from the filtered motion windows.
Each channel (3 translations, 3 rotations, and 3D motion; 7 total) was summarized using
a subset of time-domain statistics: mean, standard deviation, min, max, median, skewness,
and the position of the maximum value (argmax). These features were selected based on
a feature importance analysis, which identified them as the most informative for phase
classification. This resulted in a 49-dimensional input vector per sample (7 features × 7
channels).
Feature vectors were standardized using z-score normalization based on the training data.
The SVM was implemented with a radial basis function (RBF) kernel, regularization
parameter C = 1.5, and kernel coeﬀicient γ = scale, which automatically adapts based
on the input feature variance.
These hyperparameters were selected empirically using
grid search with cross-validation on the training set to balance model complexity and
generalization.
Neural Network Models
Both deep learning models (LSTM and TCN) used the filtered motion sequences directly
as input. To enrich the temporal signal, each sequence was expanded by appending its first
and second derivatives (velocity and acceleration), producing a 21-channel input tensor (7
motion channels × 3).
Each model was trained to output a softmax distribution over the four phase classes, us-
ing the same training configuration: Adam optimizer, focal loss to handle class imbalance,
batch size of 64, and early stopping with a patience of 3 epochs. Input data was stan-
dardized using z-score normalization per channel, based on statistics from the training set.

4.5.2
Continuous Phase Regression
To better represent the smooth, cyclical nature of physiological signals, a regression-based
modeling approach was implemented. Instead of predicting discrete class labels, the models
were trained to directly regress the continuous phase trajectory over time, based on the
unit circle representation described in Section 4.3.2.
Support Vector Regression (SVR) was used as a baseline method for this task. A multi-
output strategy was adopted, in which separate SVR models were trained to predict the
cosine and sine components of the phase. This was implemented using a MultiOutputRe-
gressor wrapper around an SVR with a RBF kernel and hyperparameters C = 1.5 and
ϵ = 0.15. This approach allows the model to predict smooth, continuous values while
maintaining robustness on small datasets.
The same deep learning architectures used for classification (LSTM and TCN) were
adapted for this task by modifying the output layer to predict two continuous values
corresponding to the cosine and sine of the phase angle.
In this setting, the classification loss function was replaced with mean squared error (MSE),
but the rest of the training configuration—such as the optimizer, batch size, early stopping,
and input normalization—remained the same.
The models were implemented in Python, using scikit-learn for both SVC and SVR models
and TensorFlow/Keras for the LSTM and TCN architectures.
4.6
Model Evaluation
Model performance was evaluated using a leave-one-group-out (LOGO) cross-validation
strategy, where each group corresponded to a distinct motion tracking sequence. This
approach tested the model’s ability to generalize across different acquisitions, simulating
realistic scenarios where model predictions must transfer across scanning conditions and
sessions.
For classification tasks, the main evaluation metric was accuracy — the proportion of
25
==PAGE:34==
correct phase predictions out of all predictions.
In addition, confusion matrices were
computed to assess how often each class was correctly or incorrectly predicted.
For regression models, performance was assessed using the mean angular error between
the predicted and ground-truth phase, computed as the signed angle between their cor-
responding unit vectors. This angle, defined in the range (−π, π], captures the smallest
rotational difference between the two phases and naturally accounts for their cyclic nature.
All neural models were trained using early stopping, with 10% of the training set held
out as a validation set. Training stopped if the validation loss failed to improve for three
consecutive epochs.

6
Discussion
6.1
Respiration Phase Estimation
The results demonstrate that respiratory phase estimation from head motion is a fea-
sible task, with models achieving reliable classification accuracy and low angular error
in regression across most sequences. Sequential architectures such as LSTM and TCN
slightly outperformed the baseline SVM, with LSTM generally yielding the best perfor-
mance across both tasks. While the performance gains were modest, they consistently
reflected the advantage of incorporating temporal information. The limited size and vari-
ability of the dataset likely constrained the models’ ability to demonstrate more substantial
improvements over the baseline. Importantly, regression results were particularly encour-
aging, showing smooth and consistent phase trajectories in several cases. These findings
suggest that head motion contains suﬀicient information to support accurate respiratory
phase estimation using machine learning methods.
Despite the encouraging results, several limitations affect the robustness and generaliz-
ability of the models. Most notably, the dataset is both limited in size and imbalanced
across subjects. In particular, one participant (Subject 5) contributes a disproportion-
ately large portion of the data, which can lead to biased learning increasing the risk of
overfitting to that individual’s respiratory patterns. This subject’s recordings feature rel-
atively clean, high-amplitude cycles, which may partly explain the consistently higher
model performance observed on those sequences.
Variability in respiratory frequency across subjects presents an additional challenge for
model generalization. Subjects 2, 3, and 5 exhibit faster breathing rates (approximately
0.24–0.27 Hz), whereas Subjects 1 and 4 show slower rhythms (around 0.08–0.10 Hz).
Models trained predominantly on faster cycles may struggle to learn features representa-
tive of slower patterns, which likely contributes to the reduced performance observed in
sequences from slower-breathing subjects.
The current manual alignment procedure, based on visual inspection of waveform corre-
spondence, is inherently approximate and susceptible to error. This limitation becomes
particularly relevant when the physiological signal is noisy, low in amplitude, or irreg-
ular, as observed in some sequences. Even small misalignments—on the order of a few
hundred milliseconds—can distort the temporal relationship between motion and phase
labels, ultimately constraining model performance. While coarse alignment is generally
achieved, the lack of a precise synchronization mechanism likely contributes to prediction
inaccuracies in challenging cases.
Despite these limitations, the overall results are promising. In particular, the regression-
based estimates of respiratory phase perform well across most sequences, aligning closely
with the expected respiratory dynamics. The achieved performance may already be com-
parable to that of conventional sensors such as respiratory belts or cushions, which are
themselves prone to inaccuracies—especially during subject motion, where they may shift,
lose contact, or saturate. Optical motion tracking, by contrast, provides a non-contact
method that is less susceptible to such issues and can be seamlessly integrated into existing
MRI environments, making it a compelling approach for respiratory monitoring.

==PAGE:46==
6.2
Cardiac Phase Estimation
In contrast to respiration, cardiac phase estimation proved substantially more challenging.
Classification performance was generally low, often approaching chance levels, indicating
that models struggled to identify consistent class boundaries based on head motion data.
Nonetheless, the regression-based models—particularly the LSTM—produced smoother,
cyclic trajectories that were more consistent with the underlying physiological waveform.
While the mean angular error remained high (exceeding 60° on average), the presence
of these periodic patterns suggests that the models were able to capture aspects of the
underlying rhythm, even if precise alignment with the ground truth was limited.
The primary diﬀiculty arises from the inherently low amplitude of cardiac-induced head
displacements.
These movements are significantly smaller than those associated with
respiration and are often obscured by larger sources of variation, including spontaneous
head motion, residual tracking noise, and overlapping respiratory components.
In addition, the method relies on rigid head motion to reflect physiological signals, which
are not always clearly expressed in the recorded data.
Whether these signals appear
depends not only on the subject’s physiology but also on external factors, including head
position in the scanner, the level of head support, and individual anatomical differences.
These aspects influence how strongly internal physiological motion—especially cardiac
activity, but also respiration—is translated into detectable head motion. This leads to
considerable variation across sequences, with some exhibiting clear periodic patterns and
others showing much weaker signals. Such variability places a fundamental constraint on
the consistency and generalizability of the models.
Nevertheless, the regression outputs were not entirely uninformative. In several sequences,
predicted phase trajectories displayed regular, cyclic patterns that broadly aligned with
the cardiac waveform. In some cases, however, these trajectories appeared temporally
offset from the ground truth, suggesting possible phase misalignment. This likely reflects
residual synchronization errors in the manual alignment procedure, which can have a
more pronounced effect when dealing with high-frequency, low-amplitude signals such as
those associated with the cardiac cycle. Additionally, some degree of offset may reflect
true physiological delays between the arrival of the cardiac pulse at the head and at
the finger, where the reference signal was recorded.
Although more precise alignment
would be necessary for accurate gating, the estimated trajectories may still be useful for
applications such as continuous pulse rate monitoring, where capturing overall periodicity
may be suﬀicient.
To better understand whether the models captured structure beyond noise, regression
performance was compared to a random baseline. Although angular errors were generally
high—and in some sequences approached or even exceeded baseline levels—the aggregated
error distributions showed a consistent shift toward lower values for the model predictions.
This suggests that the network was not simply outputting unstructured noise but was
extracting weak, periodic information from the motion signal. These differences, while
modest, indicate that relevant physiological features are present in the data and are at
least partially captured by the models.
Overall, while the current results do not establish cardiac phase estimation as robustly
feasible under the present approach, they indicate that relevant physiological information
may still be embedded in the motion data. Improving preprocessing, alignment, and signal
separation techniques may reveal more consistent cardiac signatures in future work.

7
Conclusion and Future Work
This thesis explored the potential of estimating physiological phase, focusing on both
respiration and cardiac cycles, from optical head motion tracking data acquired during
MRI scanning. Both classification and regression approaches were implemented and eval-
uated using a LOGO cross-validation framework, with model performance assessed using
standard metrics such as classification accuracy and mean angular error.
The results demonstrate that respiratory phase estimation is a viable task. Sequential
models such as LSTMs consistently outperformed the non-temporal baseline, indicating
the benefit of leveraging temporal dependencies in the data. Notably, regression-based pre-
dictions achieved low angular errors and closely followed the expected respiratory pattern
across most sequences. These findings confirm that head motion encodes useful respiratory
information, and suggest that non-contact phase estimation using optical tracking could
offer a promising alternative to conventional belt- or cushion-based sensors, particularly
in settings where those methods are prone to error or inconvenient to use.
Estimating the cardiac phase remains significantly more diﬀicult. Classification models
often performed near chance, and regression predictions showed higher angular errors. This
is likely due to the lower amplitude of cardiac-induced motion, as well as interference from
larger sources of variation, like spontaneous head motion or residual noise. Nonetheless,
the presence of cyclic patterns in some sequences suggests that meaningful cardiac-related
information is being captured, and that performance could be further improved with
refined preprocessing and modeling approaches.
In summary, this work establishes a solid foundation for non-contact respiratory phase
monitoring using markerless optical tracking in MRI settings. Although cardiac tracking
presents greater challenges under current conditions, the findings highlight the potential
for integrating motion-based physiological monitoring into clinical workflows—particularly
in scenarios where conventional sensors are unreliable or impractical.
Future Work
While this study establishes the feasibility of extracting physiological phase information
from head motion tracking data, several directions remain open for future improvement
and further exploration.
A major limitation of the current work is the scale and diversity of the dataset. The small
number of subjects, combined with the dominance of data from a single participant, likely
constrained model generalizability and limited the benefits of more complex architectures.
Expanding the dataset to include a broader range of subjects, breathing styles, and motion
conditions would help create more robust models and reduce subject-specific biases. With
a more representative dataset, more advanced architectures—such as transformer-based
models or attention mechanisms—could also be explored, as these typically require larger
training sets to perform well.
Another priority is the improvement of the signal alignment process. The current manual
synchronization method, based on visual inspection of waveform correspondence, is inher-
ently imprecise. Developing an automated, systematic approach to align the motion data
with physiological signals would reduce human error and improve reproducibility. This
39
==PAGE:48==
could involve re-evaluating the use of synchronization triggers captured during acquisi-
tion, or developing signal-based alignment strategies that automatically infer the correct
temporal offset by analyzing similarities between the motion and respiration waveforms.
Moreover, the ground truth signals used in this study—obtained via pulse oximetry and
respiratory cushion—can themselves be affected by noise, displacement, or signal dropout,
introducing potential inaccuracies into the training and evaluation labels. Exploring alter-
native sensing methods or more reliable acquisition setups may help improve label quality
and, in turn, model robustness.
The filtering pipeline also offers room for refinement. In this work, each input window was
extended with 0.5 seconds of additional data before and after the prediction interval to
minimize boundary artifacts during filtering. Although this padding significantly enhanced
signal quality and improved model performance, it introduced a delay of 0.5 seconds in
the predictions. While such a delay is acceptable in offline settings, it poses a limitation
for real-time applications where immediate phase estimates are required. Future research
should explore adaptive and causal filtering approaches that operate without relying on
future samples, thereby reducing latency while maintaining signal fidelity.
Cardiac phase estimation, in particular, would benefit from improved signal separation
techniques. The subtle motion caused by the cardiac cycle is easily obscured by respiration
or other sources of noise. Methods such as blind source separation (e.g., ICA) or denoising
strategies (e.g., wavelet-based filtering) may help to isolate the relevant components more
effectively. While some of these techniques were briefly tested, further investigation is
needed to assess their full potential in this context.
Future efforts could also focus on investigating additional signals provided by the optical
tracking system beyond rigid head motion. These include the 3D point clouds, where local
surface deformations may reveal subtle physiological movements not captured by global
head motion estimates. Another promising input is the raw PSI (Projected Structured
Infrared) images, which encode infrared intensity patterns across the facial surface. Vari-
ations in these intensity values may reflect physiological changes such as blood volume
fluctuations, similar to those used in video-based pulse detection methods. Incorporating
these complementary signals could improve sensitivity to physiological dynamics, par-
ticularly for cardiac phase estimation, where rigid motion alone often provides limited
information.
Once more robust models are developed, evaluating their performance on new, independent
subjects will be essential to assess their generalizability across different respiratory and
cardiac patterns.
Beyond validation, translating this approach into practical use will
require real-time implementation with minimal latency. Future work should explore how
motion-based phase estimation can be integrated into MRI acquisition workflows, for
example, to support prospective gating. This could be particularly useful in scenarios
where traditional physiological sensors are diﬀicult to apply or prone to failure—such as
in abdominal or thoracic imaging, pediatric populations, or high-motion environments.

LSTM Model Outputs
This appendix provides sequence-wise visualizations of the LSTM model’s outputs for both
respiratory and cardiac phase estimation. For each test sequence and task, the following
plots are shown:
• Discrete phase predictions over time, compared to ground-truth labels
• Confusion matrix for the classification output
• Continuous phase predictions from the regression model, plotted against the true
phase
• Angular error over time between predicted and true continuous phases
• Angular error histogram comparing model predictions with a random baseline, in-
cluding mean error markers
These visualizations provide insight into the variability in model performance across sub-
jects and sequences, complementing the aggregate metrics reported in Chapter 5.
