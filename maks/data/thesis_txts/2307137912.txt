Abstract 
Half of the world’s camel population lives in the North East Africa. There is a great 
potential for developing a camel dairy industry. Available starter cultures have 
showed stunted growth in pure camel milk, why there is a need to identify and 
characterize new potential starter cultures. Twenty-seven strains of Lactococcus 
lactis were inoculated in camel milk and bovine milk. The strains were a mix of 
well characterized strains, commercial starter cultures and newly isolated in camel 
milk. The acidification was monitored and the strains were divided in four 
acidification groups dependent on their ability to ferment the milk. The milk 
derived peptides after hydrolysis in camel milk and bovine milk were identified by 
HPLC-MS and through bioinformatics linked to the relevant milk proteins. The 
main part of the peptides came from caseins, mainly β-casein. Most of the camel 
milk isolated strains contained a PIII-type cell envelope proteinase (CEP) and 
acidified bovine milk better than camel milk. Two Lc. lactis strains isolated in 
camel milk showed really good acidification of camel milk, and were not able to 
acidify bovine milk. Their cleavage specificity of ß-casein was similar to a PI-type 
CEP, but since they could not ferment bovine milk, it was assumed that a new CEP 
type had been discovered.

1. Introduction 
The arid areas of Africa in the North East Africa hold 11.5 million camels. This is 
half of the worlds camel population, which amounts to around 23 million camels. 
Ethiopia has almost 1.1 million camels (“FAOSTAT,” 2013). The camels in North 
East Africa are of the one-humped species Camelus dromedarius; the two-humped 
species Camelus bactrianus lives in Asia. When the term “camel” is used, it refers to 
the species Camelus dromedarius. Nomad pastoralists hold most of the camels. 
Camels are good milk producers with a lactation period of 9-18 month with 
production of around 5 kg milk per day under pastoralist conditions, and the 
camels can survive up to 30 days without water. The pastoralists primarily use the 
camels for milk sale, but they are also used for transportation and meat. Most of 
the milk is consumed raw or as spontaneous fermented milk, as fermentation is an 
easy way to preserve the milk under warm conditions. 
The camel population in East Africa makes a significant contribution to the 
national economies, but the development of a dairy industry has been limited 
because it has not been possible to produce butter and cheese from camel milk 
(Farah & Fischer, 2004). However, due to modern biotechnology the dairy enzyme 
camel chymosin is commercially available, which provides new opportunities for a 
conventional dairy industry based on camel milk (Kappeler et al., 2006). The 
Haramaya Camel Dairy is a 5-years Danida supported research project started in 
2013 with the purpose of conducting the necessary research for product 
development of camel dairy products (DANIDA, 2012). The present study is 
connected to the Haramaya Camel Dairy project. 
 
1.2. Camel milk composition 
The composition of camel milk is similar to bovine milk with moisture content at 
86-88%, and almost the same level of fat, lactose, proteins, and ash (Table 1). The 
color is opaque white, and the taste is sweet and sharp and a bit salty. The taste 
and composition vary dependent on the time of year and the feeding. The pH of 
fresh milk ranges from 6.2 – 6.5 (Farah & Fischer, 2004), which is lower than the 
pH of bovine milk at 6.63±0.08 (Tsioulpas, Lewis, & Grandison, 2007).

Although the chemical composition seems similar, there is variance in-between the 
individual constituents. The main protein types are casein and whey (Farah & 
Fischer, 2004). They caseins are defined as the groups of proteins that precipitates 
on acidification to pH 4.6 at around 30°C. For camel milk the caseins account for 72 
– 76 % of the proteins, which is less than for bovine milk containing around 80% 
(Fox, Uniacke-Lowe, Mcsweeney, & O’Mahony, 2015). The whey protein content in 
camel milk is 22 – 28 % of total protein, which is slightly higher than in bovine 
milk (Farah & Fischer, 2004). Camel milk does not contain the whey protein ß-
lactoglobulin, which is found in bovine milk (Hinz, O’Connor, Huppertz, Ross, & 
Kelly, 2012). The relative amount of caseins in bovine and camel milk is shown in 
Table 2. Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) added to camel milk do not grow as rapidly as in 
cow milk, the lag-phase is longer and the yield is low. The different composition of 
caseins can possibly explain some of the diverse behavior of camel milk during 
processing. Further, the calf chymosin, which is a proteolytic enzyme present in 
the stomach of newborn calves responsible for coagulation of the milk solids and 
used for cheese making in the industry, is not effective in camel milk (Farah & 
Fischer, 2004).

1.3. Caseins 
Caseins are synthesized in the mammary gland, which is the only place in nature 
where they are present. Their molecular mass is from 20 – 25 kDa. They are 
phosphoproteins containing around 0.85% phosphorus. The phosphate groups 
bind large amount of calcium, which makes it to a very nutritionally valuable 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
protein. Caseins are very heat stable and can withstand heating at 140°C for 20 
min without coagulation. The caseins contain a high content of apolar amino acids 
(Val, Leu, Ile, Phe, Tyr, Pro), which would make them insoluble in water, but the 
high content of phosphate groups, low level of sulphur-containing amino acids and 
a high content of carbohydrates, make the caseins soluble. The molecular structure 
of casein is quaternary and it is present in milk as large colloidal aggregates called 
micelles consisting of α s1-casein, α s2-casein, β-casein, and κ-casein. The quaternary 
structure makes the caseins readily susceptible to proteolysis (Fox et al., 2015). 
The role of κ-casein is to protect the micelle from coagulation. κ-casein has 
negatively charged sugars pointing out from the micelle surface repelling the 
micelles apart. The main function of chymosin is to cleave the negatively charged 
part of κ-casein. The cleavage makes the micelles insoluble in water and they start 
forming a tight network, where other solids are entrapped and coagulate. The 
caseins in camel milk do not coagulate to same extent as in bovine milk. This might 
be due to the relative lower content of κ-casein. The cleavage site of κ-casein and 
structural differences around this site is different in camel and bovine milk, which 
also might explain why calf chymosin is not effective in camel milk (Farah & 
Fischer, 2004). 
 
1.4. Redox potential of milk 
Oxidation-reduction (redox) reactions involve the transfer of en electron from an 
electron donor to an electron acceptor. The compound loosing an electron is 
oxidized and the one accepting an electron is reduced. It is a simultaneously 
reaction. Redox potentials (Eh) are measured in volt. At 25°C and pH 6.6-6.7 and 
equilibrium with air the redox potential of bovine milk is in the range of +0.25 to 
+0.35 V. The principal factor affecting the redox potential of milk is the 
concentration of dissolved oxygen. LAB fermentation of lactose in milk has a huge 
impact on the milks redox potential. When the LAB has utilized the available O2, a 
rapid decrease in Eh occurs causing a negative redox potential in fermented milk 
and cheese (Fox et al., 2015). The redox potential affects the metabolic pathway of 
LAB and production of aroma compounds in cheese and fermented dairy products 
(Martin et al., 2011).

1.5. Lactic acid bacteria 
LAB are a group of gram-positive lactic acid producing bacteria. LAB are divided in 
two fermentation groups depending on their metabolism of glucose. Homolactic 
LAB use the glycolysis pathway, and heterolactic LAB use the phosphoketolase 
pathway. The homolactic group contains following families: Enterococcaceae, 
Lactobacillaceae and Streptococcaceae except for one group in the genus 
Lactobacillus. In the glycolytic pathway glucose is metabolized to lactic acid in ratio 
two molecules of lactic acid from one molecule of glucose. Fructose 1,6-
diphosphatase is the key enzyme in this pathway. From one molecule of glucose, 
two molecules of ATP are generated through phosphorylation. The heterolactic 
group includes members of the family Leuconostocaceae and species in 
Lactobacillus genus. The phosphoketolase pathway generates one molecule of 
lactic acid, CO2, and ethanol from one molecule of glucose and the net gain of ATP 
is one molecule per glucose molecule. Some LAB are able to metabolize 
disaccharides such as lactose. The disaccharides are transported across the cell 
membrane as free sugars or phosphorylated. In the cell they are hydrolyzed to 
monosaccharides such as glucose and galactose for lactose and hereafter 
metabolized by one of the pathways. LAB are either obligately homofermentative, 
facultatively heterofermentative or obligately heterofermentative. The metabolism 
of LAB is dependent on electron acceptors. Most species use oxygen and pyruvate. 
The electron acceptors contribute to oxidation of NADH to NAD+, which are 
important coenzymes in metabolic pathways (Endo & Dicks, 2014). 
LAB are widely used in the food industry in fermented dairy products such as 
yoghurt, cheese and quarg (Siezen, 1999). They are very fastidious and require 
certain amino acids (AA) to grow (Chopin, 1993). As milk contains only a relatively 
small amount of free AA, the dairy LAB are dependent on a proteolytic system that 
can break down caseins in milk to peptides and AA (Børsting et al., 2015). 
 
1.5.1. Lactococcus lactis 
The genus Lactococcus belongs to the family Streptococcaceae. Lactococcus is 
homofermentative and ferment glucose to lactic acid by glycolysis (Endo & Dicks, 
2014). The genus is divided into seven species, where Lactococcus lactis (Lc. lactis) 
is the only species of the genus used for dairy technology.  Lc. lactis is further 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
divided into four subspecies: Lc. lactis ssp. lactis (Lister 1873), Lc. lactis ssp. 
cremoris (Orla-Jensen 1919), Lc. lactis ssp. hordniae (Latorre- Guzmán et al. 1977), 
and Lc. lactis ssp. tructae (Pérez et al. 2011) (Kim, 2014). 
Lactococci are facultative anaerobe and mesophilic. They are fastidious and 
require complex culture media for optimal growth (Kim, 2014). M17 is a 
recommended medium containing peptone, yeast extract, beef extract, lactose, 
ascorbic acid, MgSO4, and ß-disodium glycerophosphate (Terzaghi & Sandine, 
1975). 
The great industrial potential of L. lactis is due to its metabolism of protein, lactose 
and citrate contributing to flavors and textures in the dairy industry. Lc. lactis ssp. 
lactis and Lc. lactis ssp. cremoris are commercially important for their homolactic 
ability to ferment lactose. Their metabolisms differ from other LAB by having a 
simultaneous catabolism of galactose and glucose. Lc. lactis ssp. lactis var. 
diacetylactis is the only Lactococcus able to metabolize citrate in milk, yielding in 
flavor-rich metabolic end-products (diacetyl, acetoin, 2,3-butylene glycol, 
acetaldehyde, ethanol, and lactic acid) (Kim, 2014). 
 
1.5.1.1. Proteolytic system in Lc. lactis 
The proteolytic system consists of three major components: 
1. A cell wall-associated proteinase that initiates the degradation of extra 
cellular casein into medium-sized peptides at a length of maximum 30 
residues (Kunji et al., 1995). 
2. Peptide transporters that carry the peptides across the cell membrane. 
3. Various intracellular peptidases that hydrolyze the medium-sized peptides 
into smaller peptides and AA (Liu, Bayjanov, Renckens, Nauta, & Siezen, 
2010) (Figure 1). Flavor compounds such as aldehydes, alcohol, and esters 
can be formed by further conversion of AA (Liu, Nauta, Francke, & Siezen, 
2008).  
The cell wall-associated proteinase is attached to the cells via a membrane anchor 
present at the extreme C-terminal end of the molecule (Kok, 1990). 
The extracellular proteinase in Lc. lactis is a cell-envelope serine proteinase (CEP) 
called lactocepin or PrtP (Siezen, 1999). It was first identified in Lc. lactis ssp. 
cremoris HP (Exterkate, 1975). The gene coding for lactocepin is called prtP 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
(Christensson, Pillidge, Ward, & O’Toole, 2001). The prtP gene in Lc. lactis is 
plasmid located (Kok, 1990) and a loss of these plasmids results in a lactocepin-
negative (Prt-) phenotype, which will grow slow in milk (Pearce, Skipper, & Jarvis, 
1974). The size of the lactocepin plasmid might be important for determining the 
level of proteolytic activity (Christensson et al., 2001). 
 
 
Figure 1. A simplified model of the proteolytic system in Lc. lactis on casein breakdown. 
PrtM = Membrane bound lipoprotein, PrtP = Cell wall proteinase (CEP), Opp = 
Oligopeptide transport system, Dpp = Peptide binding proteins, DtpT = Di/tripeptide 
transporter. PepN, PepO, PepF, PepX, PepP, PepC, PepL, PepQ, PepI, PepT, PepV, PepR, 
and PepD are intracellular peptidases (Pinto et al., 2012). 
CEP is the key enzyme in proteolysis as it is responsible for cleaving more than 
40% of the peptide bonds (Kim, 2014). Caseins are the main source of AA and 
account for around 90% of the growth of Lc. lactis in milk (Flambard, Helinck, 
Richard, & Juillard, 1998). There are two different types of proteinases, organized 
by their specificity for caseins. The PI-type proteinase, which mainly hydrolyze ß-
casein, and PIII-type proteinase, which hydrolyze both αs1-, ß- and κ-CN (Vos et al., 
1991). Then, there is an intermediate type PI/PIII that shows PI-type specificity for 
ß-CN, but hydrolyze the αs1-CN with PIII-type specificity (Monnet, Ley, & González, 
1992). The two types of proteinases cleave ß-CN at significantly different sites 
(Reid, Ng, Moore, Coolbear, & Pritchard, 1991). The proteolytic system within 
same species is diverse; having different cell wall bound proteinase, peptidase and 
peptide transporters (Bayjanov et al., 2009; Liu et al., 2010). 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
Three types of membrane located peptide transport systems have been identified: 
a di-tripeptide carrier (DtpT) driven by a electrochemical proton gradient and 
oligopeptide transport system (OPP) and dipeptide transport system (DPP), which 
are members of the ABC (ATP-binding cassette) transporter family and ATP-
driven. Opp transports peptides in the range of 4 to 35 AA, and DPP can transport 
peptides up to 9 AA across the membrane (Picon, García-Casado, & Nunez, 2010).   
 
1.5.1.2. Classification of Lc. lactis based on CEP 
The cleavage specificity of CEPs toward fragment 1 to 23 of αs1-casein (αs1-CN(f1-
23)) for 16 Lc. lactis strains was compared by Exterkate et al. (1993). From this 
comparison the strains were divided in seven groups (a-g), which caused the 
foundation of a new classification system of CEPs; referred to as Exterkate’s 
classification system. Group a to d had PIII-type proteinases, group g had a PI-type 
proteinase, and group e and f had an intermediate type proteinase (F. A. Exterkate, 
Alting, & Bruinenberg, 1993). The classification system was later extended by one 
group; h (Broadbent, Strickland, Weimer, Johnson, & Steele, 1998). Børsting et al. 
(2015) genome sequenced 34 different strains of Lc. lactis ssp. cremoris and Lc. 
lactis ssp. lactis to identify the CEP AA sequences. Further, the same strains 
including four additional were grouped by their identified peptides after 
hydrolysis in milk. A principal component analysis (PCA) of the positions of the 
first 800 AA of the N-terminal end of CEP could explain 68.6% of the AA variation 
with four principal components (PCs) and 46.9% robustness. Another PCA model 
based on identified peptides released from αs1-CN and ß-CN used five PCs and 
could explain 67.2% of the variation with 42.5% robustness. Both PCA models had 
a similar grouping of two large groups (X and D), suggested as new classification 
groups in Exterkate’s classification system. Exterkate’s classification group d was 
divided in group X and D (Børsting et al., 2015). 
Additionally, by combining the sequence analysis and a three dimensional model of 
CEP structure of subtilisin and Lc. lactis ssp. cremoris SK11 CEP, Børsting et al. 
(2015) suggested five new CEP AA positions (272, 290, 291, 366, and 381) located 
opposite the substrate binding region as important for substrate-binding 
specificity. The AA positions were not conserved in group X, but it was possible to 
distinguish between subgroups in D from these AA positions. The finding 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
correlates with Bruinenberg et al. (1994) who found that removal of residues 238-
388 altered the caseinolytic specificity of the enzyme and therefore had an 
influence on substrate specificity. 
 
The aim of the present study was to identify and characterize suitable starter 
cultures in camel milk. A previous study found that starter cultures had stunted 
growth in pure camel milk, most likely explained by difficulties in satisfying their 
nutritional needs. This could be explained by a proteolytic system of LAB with 
reduced efficiency on camel milk proteins (Berhe et al., 2015). The present study 
attempts to categorize strains of Lc. lactis after their ability to hydrolyze milk 
based on their acidification curves and the derived peptides from proteolysis of 
milk. Different strains of Lc. lactis have been used for hydrolysis experiments in 
camel milk and bovine milk. The strains were carefully selected, containing a 
reference set representing each of Exterkate’s classification groups, strains from 
commercial starter cultures (R-707, R-704) used for cheese production (Chr. 
Hansen A/S), and newly isolated and identified strains from camel milk (Fugl, 
2016; Kiran & Hussain, 2015).

2.2. Bacteria preparation  
Twenty-seven different Lc. lactis were transported from Denmark to Ethiopia on 
M17 0.2 % lactose agar in 2 mL tubes. All strains were streaked on M17 0.5% 
lactose agar on arrival to ensure survival from transportation. The M17 agars were 
made according to the instructions with respectively 0.2% and 0.5% lactose added 
(Difco TM & BBL TM, n.d.). The method for stock preparations was derived from 
Børsting et al., 2015. Strains isolated in bovine milk were inoculated in 10 mL fresh 
autoclaved (15 min, 121°C) bovine milk. Strains isolated in camel milk were 
prepared in 10 mL fresh (double) boiled (100°, 30 min) camel milk. Stocks were 
incubated overnight at 30°C, transferred to 1.8mL vials and were stored at -20°C 
until use. Figure 2 shows a flow chart of the experimental procedure carried out in 
Ethiopia.

simultaneously. The acidified milk was centrifuged (3,400 x g, 10 min, 5°C), the 
supernatant was heated (85°C, 10 min) to stop potential enzyme activity. 1% 
(vol/vol) formic acid was added to precipitate the caseins. Centrifugation was

until transportation to Denmark. The experimental work was carried out in 
duplicate over a period of three weeks at Haramaya University, Ethiopia. 
After transportation to Denmark were samples immediately stored at -20°C until 
use. 
 
2.4. HPLC-MS  
The analysis was carried out at University of Copenhagen, Department of Food 
Science. The supernatants were thawed and centrifuged (10,000 x g, 10 min, 20°C) 
and filtered through a 1 mL Omnifix®-F syringe (B.Braun, Denmark) attached with 
a 13 mm Q-max® PR syringe filter with a 0.22 μm cellulose acetate membrane 
(Frisenette, Denmark). The filtered supernatants were analyzed by high 
performance liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (HPLC-MS) using a 
Dionex Ultimate 3000 LC system coupled to a Q ExactiveTM MS system (Thermo 
Scientific). Ten µL of each sample was injected onto the column (Aeris TEM 2.6 µM, 
Peptide XB-C18, 250 x 4.6 mm, Phenomenex). The peptides were eluted with 
eluent A (0.1% formic acid in water) and eluent B (0.1% formic acid in 20/80 
water/acetonitrile (V/V)) using a 250 µL min-1 flow rate and a gradient of 100% A 
for 5 min, after 10 min 15% B, after 65 min 55% B and after 66 min 100% B for 5 
min, from 72 min to 80 min the gradient was 100 % A. The MS was operated in 
positive mode and mass spectra were collected by running a full MS scan from 200 
to 2000 m/z followed by a data dependent MS/MS of selected top ten ions. MS 
resolution was 70,000 with automatic gain control (AGC) target at 3 x 106 for the c-
trap in the Orbitrap analyzer. The MS/MS resolution was 35,000 with AGC target at 
106 and a dynamic exclusion of 2 s. 
The MS data was analyzed with Proteome Discoverer (version 1.4, Thermofisher) 
using the SEQUIST searching algorithm. For bovine milk samples the Taurus 
proteome fasta Uniprot database was used, and for camel milk samples the 
Camelus ferus (Bactrian camel) Uniprot database was used. Peptide length was 
minimum 4 and maximum 144, and the precursor tolerance was 10 ppm. The 
search results were filtered in Proteome Discover with the integrated Target decoy 
PSM validator algorithm to a q-value of <0.01, which ensures a peptide-spectrum 
match false discovery rate less than 0.01 (Figure 3).

2.5. Data Analysis 
CINAC software (Alliance Instruments, France) was used for data processing of the 
pH, temperature and redox measurements. The data from HPLC-MS analysis was 
in the format .xlsx. It was a huge data set that needed further processing. Data 
processing was made with the program package pepi (Peptide Protein 
Investigation), developed in the language Python 2.7 by Egon Bech Hansen, 2016 
(Appendix A). The program pepigN.py was used to extract the selected peptide 
data from .xlsx files originating from the HPLC-MS analysis. pepigN.py used the 
input files as arguments and wrote the outputs on standard output (Appendix B). 
The program pepiTable.py was used to make a summary of the distribution of 
peptides on proteins in each sample. The input file was the file made from 
pepigN.py. The output was tab-separated lines on standard output, which was 
converted to an .xlsx file (Appendix ). The program pepiSites.py found top 50 most 
abundant N- and C-terminal ends in each of the samples included in the input file. 
The input was the file generated in pepigN.py. The output gave a list of 50 cleaving 
sites and the sequence of 20 amino acids before and after the cleaving site. The 
output was tab-separated lines in standard output and was converted to .xlsx files 
(Appendix ). The program pepiplot.py was used to make plots of the position and 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
abundance of peptides relative to the position in the protein. The file generated in 
pepigN.py was used as input file (Appendix E). 
Multivariate data analysis was made with the software LatentiX (Copenhagen, 
Denmark) used for Principal Component Analysis (PCA). The input files were 
adjusted .xlsx files from HPLC-MS analysis. All isoforms of a peptide were joined 
together. Due to high variation in the data, the logarithm was taken to all data used 
for PCA. Other preprocessing method could have been used, but according to 
Nature, the log transformation are default approach (Enot et al., 2008). Before 
preprocessing, all zeroes were converted into ones in order to make it possible to 
take the logarithm. In LatentiX “mean centering” was chosen as following 
preprocessing. Data output from pepiTable.py, which was analyzed in LatentiX, 
was not log transformed, but autoscaled, which is a combination of mean centering 
and scaling by 1/SDev (Esbensen, Guyot, Westad, & Houmoller, 2002).

3.2.1. Milk derived peptides in camel milk 
In total 3035 different peptides were identified from 38 proteins in fermented 
camel milk. Most peptides came from the caseins. The highest amount of peptides 
came from ß-CN, where 1206 peptides were discovered from which 1171 were 
unique. The second highest amount of peptides came from short isoform of αs1-CN, 
where 704 peptides were identified but only 14 unique peptides. The third highest 
contributor to peptides was αs1-CN having 673 identified peptides. The best 
represented whey protein was Glycosylation dependent cell adhesion molecule 1 
(GlyCAM1) and the isoform of GlyCAM1. 673 peptides from GlyCAM1 were 
identified, of which 34 peptides were unique. From the isoform 315 peptides and 
14 unique peptides were detected. Only nine peptides (5 unique) were discovered 
from α-lactalbumin. The measured peak area for the samples were in the range 
from zero to 1010, but no measurements between zero and 106. This indicates that 
there was a detection limit around 106, and it is very likely that peptides below the 
detection limit were present in the samples. The reason for low content of peptides 
derived from whey proteins was probably due to their globular, compact protein 
structure giving strong resistance towards bacterial proteolysis. 
 
3.2.2. Milk derived peptides in bovine milk 
In total 1383 different peptides were identified from 267 proteins in fermented 
bovine milk. As for camel milk most of the peptides came from caseins. Best 
represented was ß-CN with 580 peptides (579 unique). From αs1-CN 413 peptides 
were identified of which all were unique, and from αs2-CN were 299 identified. 78 
peptides were identified from GlyCAM1, which was the best representing whey 
protein. ß-lactoglobulin was the second best represented whey protein having 11 
peptides identified. There was only one identified peptide from α-lactalbumin in 
bovine milk. Camel milk samples had the highest content of total peptides

be explained by the differences in databases, where the proteomic data base for

3.4.1. Amino acids at cleavage sites 
The CEP cleavage specifity were investigated by comparison of matrices containing 
twenty amino acids before and after the cleavage sites. The positions before the 
cleavage sites were numbered Pn, where P stands for position and n is the position 
number from the cleavage site starting with 1 for the amino acids just before the 
site. The amino acids after the cleavage sites were numbered Pn’, where P1’ 
describes the position right after the cleavage site. The CEP cleavage specificity of 
top 50 peptides of both C-terminal and N-terminal was investigated, although 
there was a risk of aminopeptidases present in the milk, which would cause 
further cleavage of the peptides N-terminal. The purpose was to analyze the 
proteolytic cleavage sites and investigate if CEP had specific preferences. The 
matrices were visually analyzed and an example of data output is attached in 
Appendix K. The analysis was based on previously defined acidification groups and 
divided in camel milk and bovine milk. Characteristics of the matrices did not 
apply to all samples in a group, but were general trends. Bovine milk samples were 
more consistent than camel milk samples. The terms “high” and “low” refer to the 
digit in the matrices. High, which was defined as minimum seven, refers to that 
minimum seven of top 50 peptides had a certain amino acid at the specific 
position. Low, which was defined as maximum two, refers to that maximum two 
peptides had a certain amino acid at the specific site (Table 6).

have a PIII-type CEP. Strain 87 looked more similar to strain 101 as the average 
peptide length was 26 and 22 amino acids, and between 14% to 31% of the 
peptides derived from αs1-CN. Peptides from ß-CN made up 35% and 66 % of the 
peptides. Those similarities could indicate that strain 87 and most of the other 
strains isolated in camel milk contained a PIII-type CEP.

Strain 88 had a cleavage pattern from proteolysis of ß-CN similar to strain 110. 
When comparing the peptide statistics in Table 8 for the two strains, some 
differences appeared. Both strains had approximately the same average peptide 
length around 16 amino acids, but about 20% of the peptides in strain 88 were 
shorter than 10 peptides, where it was about 10% for strain 110. Further, sample 
88aC almost had twice the amount of peptides derived from αs1-CN compared to 
both 110 samples. Because strain 88 was placed close to strain 89 in the PCA plot 
in Figure 9, the cleavage pattern of ß-CN by strain 89 (Figure 21) were compared 
with the one of strain 88. The cleavage patterns looked very similar, and the same 
was valid for the peptide statistics, where most of the numbers were similar except 
for the percentage of peptides from αs1-CN for sample 89aC, which were double as 
high than the other samples. This indicates that both strain 88 and 89 had a PI-type 
CEP. However, they differentiated a lot by the fact that strain 88 was not able to 
ferment bovine milk which clearly shows that the proteinases could not be similar. 
To summarize, none of strain 81 or 88 had a PIII-type CEP or PI-type CEP, and both 
strains were good to ferment camel milk, and could not ferment bovine milk. Both 
strains showed potential as suitable starter cultures in camel milk, although more 
research is needed. Their unique characteristics could indicate that a new type of 
proteinase was discovered. The new proteinase showed the same cleavage 
specificity for ß-CN as a PI-type CEP, but had different behavior in bovine milk. 
==PAGE:40==

There is a need for further research in order to conclude the findings of a new CEP. 
It is recommended to perform a HPLC-MS analysis in triplicate to get more reliable 
data for the samples. Further, it is suggested to genome sequence the CEP of Lc. 
lactis isolated in camel milk for a comparison of the CEP AA sequence as Børsting 
et al., 2015 did. It would also be interesting to look further into the behavior of the 
camel milk isolates in boiled camel milk, as especially strain 81 had a remarkable 
behavior in boiled milk.

4. Conclusion 
The present study has shown that acidification of twenty-seven different strains of 
Lc. lactis in camel milk and bovine milk could be divided in four acidification 
groups dependent on their acidification rate and pH after incubation ON. The main 
part of the derived peptides after hydrolysis came from caseins, where peptides 
from ß-CN had highest abundance. In general, the peptides were rich in proline, 
but the group with the best acidifying strains were absent of proline at the position 
before cleavage site and almost absent of proline in the two positions after 
cleavage site. The main part of Lc. lactis isolated in camel milk contained a PIII-type 
CEP and acidified bovine milk better than camel milk, although any of the milks 
==PAGE:41==
40 
 
were acidified as good as with commercial strains. Strain 81 and 88, which were 
camel milk isolates, were not able to ferment bovine milk, but acidified camel milk 
really good, thus showing potential as suitable starter cultures in camel milk. They 
showed same cleavage specificity for ß-CN as a PI-type CEP, but because they could 
not acidify bovine milk, it was assumed that a new type of CEP was discovered.
