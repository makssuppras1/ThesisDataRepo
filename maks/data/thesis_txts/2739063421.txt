Abstract
This thesis investigates the AI transparency and reliability of Spiking Neural Networks
(SNNs) for analyzing EEG signals in the context of stress detection. Spiking Neural Net-
works represent the third generation of Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), leveraging the
timing of spikes to process information more similarly to the behavior of biological neu-
rons. This approach offers potential advantages in efficiency and robustness, particularly
for tasks involving temporal data such as EEG signals. Explainability is critical for building
trust in these models, especially in critical applications like healthcare, where understand-
ing the model’s decisions can significantly impact patient outcomes.
To address these issues, three models were developed: a simple SNN, an EEGNET
converted to an SNN, and a convolutional SNN based on EEGNET. To enhance the in-
terpretability of these models, internal states such as membrane potential, firing rate,
spike trains, and trained weights were examined. Additionally, two existing explainability
methods, SAM and TSA, were implemented. Notably, the TSA method showed promis-
ing results, demonstrating significant overlap with another study in identifying the most
relevant EEG channels.
Explainable AI (XAI) techniques, such as SAM and TSA, play a crucial role in providing
insights into the internal workings of SNNs. By explaining how these networks process
and respond to input data, XAI enhances our ability to trust and effectively utilize SNNs
in critical applications like stress detection in healthcare.

Introduction
Electroencephalography (EEG) signals are one of the most common methods of obtaining
spontaneous electrical activity associated with various brain states. The acquiring method
is usually non-invasive to the patient [1]. The psychiatrist Hans Bergen detected the first
rhythmic electrical activity from the scalps of human volunteers in Jena, Germany, in 1924.
[2].
Advancements in EEG acquisition devices have facilitated the collection of useful informa-
tion regarding brain functions, with the potential to apply it to a wide range of applications.
EEG signals detect changes in brain activity and they can be used to diagnose conditions
such as dementia, brain tumors, Alzheimer’s, encephalitis (inflammation of the brain), or
stress [3].
Since the early 20th century, researchers have proposed several advanced preprocessing
and feature extraction techniques, such as frequency distributions (TFD), fast Fourier
transform (FFT), eigenvector methods (EM), wavelet transform (WT), and autoregressive
method (ARM) methods to analyze the EEG signals [4]. A century after the EEG signals
were gathered for the first time, complex tools such as machine learning (Support Vector
Machine (SVM), Artificial Neural Network (ANN)) [5, 6, 7] and deep learning algorithms
(CNN, RNN) [8, 9] are common methods used to decode and interpret EEG data.
The growth in the deep learning field has not only improved the classification accuracy
of EEG signals but also facilitated the integration of the preprocessing phases, feature
engineering, and selection in the learning process, utilizing raw signals as input data.
These powerful algorithms have been applied to various EEG classification tasks, such
as emotion recognition, motor imagery, mental workload, seizure detection, event-related
potentials, and sleep stage scoring [10]. Despite its benefits, deep learning methods come
with significant drawbacks. These include high cost, computational capacity, and ethical
and security concerns due to a lack of transparency.
Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs), the third generation of ANN, mimic the behavior of bi-
ological neurons and overcome the complex computational problems and the black-box
behavior faced by deep learning methods through temporal and spatial information pro-
cessing mechanisms. Another advantage of SNNs is energy efficiency since binary spikes
are fed into SNNs instead of analog values in traditional ANN[11]. Even though this field
is still in its early stages, it has already generated significant interest in both the Artificial
Intelligence (AI) and neuroscience communities.
However, there are many relevant but still open questions in the field of SNNs, such as
interpretability and explainability of SNNs, learning algorithms for very deep and large-
scale SNNs, SNN benchmarking framework, acceleration of SNN computation both for
friendly and efficient SNN programming tools for both training and inference [12].
This master thesis project explores the interpretability and explainability of spiking neural
networks for stress classification tasks using EEG signals. It involves developing an SNN

1.1
Problem statement / Research Questions
Stress detection is critical in healthcare, mental well-being, and human-computer inter-
action. According to the World Health Organization, depression will affect around 322
million individuals globally by 2030, perhaps leading to other physiological issues [13].
EEG signals have been used to provide valuable information on patterns of neural activity
associated with stress. Recent advancements in SNNs have shown promise in accu-
rately identifying stress patterns from EEG signals. However, deploying SNNs for stress
assessment introduces challenges related to transparency and reliability that raise ethical
concerns.
The objective of this project is to explore the biological approximations inherent in SNNs by
designing accurate and transparent neural networks that can address the characteristics
and security issues associated with the ”black box” nature of deep learning. By doing
so, the project aims to improve the interpretability and transparency of these complex
systems.
The purpose of this research is to address the following key questions:
• What SNN architectures can be used to provide transparent and interpretable rep-
resentations of stress-related patterns in EEG signals?
• What are the trade-offs between model complexity and interpretability in SNNs for
stress detection?
• Can the current visualization techniques be adapted to explain SNN decisions to
end-users, especially in the context of stress detection?
1.2
Research goals and methods
The research aims to provide an interpretable and transparent system capable of stress
detection using EEG signal analysis. The system will highlight the benefits of SNNs by
generating local explanations of SNN models that provide explicit explanations of the
decision-making process. This approach ensures a higher degree of transparency and
accountability in system operations.
Thus the goals are:
• Determine an effective encoding algorithm for converting real-valued signals into
spikes while preserving task-relevant information.
• Explore the data to understand the characteristics of EEG data(its distribution, noise
level, potential outliers), identify the potential challenges(non-stationarity, high di-
mensionality, low signal-to-noise ratio).
• Select and train the SNN architectures adapted to successfully address the task of
stress detection, utilizing EEG datasets.
• Conduct a comprehensive evaluation of the trained models, and their performance
based on metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and other relevant indicators.

Background
This chapter will provide a general overview of the state-of-the-art studies related to the
transparency and reliability of AI systems using SNN to decode EEG signals. Firstly, a
background of stress, EEG signals, SNNs, and explainable artificial intelligence methods
will be reviewed. Next, different machine learning approaches for decoding EEG signals
will be covered, starting with classic ML algorithms (SVM and ANN) to the most innovative
SNN algorithms. Finally, an overview of transparency and interpretability for deep learning
as proposed by several research papers is offered.
2.1
Stress
According to the Oxford dictionary [14], stress is a noun defined as a psychological and
physical strain or tension generated by physical, emotional, social, or occupational cir-
cumstances, events, or experiences that are difficult to manage or endure. In psycho-
logical terms, stress was described by Selyo [15] who proposed the General Adaptation
Syndrome (GAS) process as a general physiological reaction to a wide range of stressors
comprising alarm, resistance, and exhaustion stages. When an individual experiences
stress, they will activate the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis and sympathetic-
adrenal medullary (SAM) axis in response to the stressors. Once activated, these axes
will secrete the stress hormone known as cortisol. The accumulation of cortisol will cause
the body to respond to stress [16].
Most people feel stress throughout their lives because of the complexity of the human
social and personal environment, various and concurrent interactions with surrounding
situations, and variability in stress manifestation. Stress is a widespread phenomenon,
which has recently come to the attention of researchers in psychology, neuroscience,
medicine, and related fields, such as affective computing. Stress can affect individuals
positively or negatively depending on how the situation is handled. Good stress, or eu-
stress, is present in the short term and acts as a motivator, inspires, helps focus energy,
and enhances performance. On the other side, bad stress (distress) can cause anxi-
ety, disorientation, poor attention, impaired performance, and even more serious health
concerns.
The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5) [17] defines acute
stress disorder as the beginning of particular phobic symptoms within three days to one
month following a stressful experience. People may suffer an immediate stress reaction
to a trauma, but long-term stress can develop into post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).
The researchers assessed the economic loss based on the kind of stressors: heat stress
on labor activity will affect roughly 60% of the worldwide working hours of 940 million per-
sons involved in agriculture by 2030 and 19% of the global working hours in construction.
The overall cost of these losses is estimated to be $2.4 trillion each year. [18]. The impact
of mental health in the workplace is estimated to be 12 billion working days lost each year
due to sadness and anxiety. Globally, the cost of lost productivity is around $1 trillion per

2.2
EEG signals
The electrical activity of the brain measured at the scalp level is represented by the EEG
data. Electrodes record voltage changes in the ionic currents produced by neurons [19].
Electrodes are physical sensors used to perform analog recording (the voltage difference
between any two electrodes), usually made of metals that are good electron conductors.
They are connected to amplifiers that enhance and filter the EEG activity. EEG data is
represented by channels for each electrode, with voltages represented on the x-axis and
time represented on the y-axis. Event codes are located at the bottom of the data plot
along the y-axis (Acheson, 2019).
Standardization and regulations for collecting data from electrodes have been developed
and applied. Herbert Jasper proposed the 10-20 system at the Brussels IV International
EEG Congress in 1957 to standardize the EEG placement method [20]. Today, this is
the most widely used system. The numerals ”10” and ”20” refer to the distances between
adjacent electrodes, which are either 10% or 20% of the entire distance (front-back or
right-left) of the skull. Figure 2.1 displays an example of the 10-20 system with electrode
labeling. Each letter indicates the general area of the brain that the electrode covers, as
follows: pre-frontal (Fp), frontal (F), temporal (T), parietal (P), occipital (O), and central
(C).
Figure 2.1: 10-20 system [21]
After data collection, the next phase involves analyzing the signals and filtering the noise.
According to [22], brain waves comprise positive frequencies between 0.5 and 40 Hz.
The first step in preprocessing is to eliminate any abnormal data at frequencies above or
below the experimental interest range. In addition, the power spectrum is divided into five
distinct frequency bands: delta, theta, alpha, beta, and gamma.
The subsequent phase in EEG preprocessing is feature extraction, which aims to reduce
dimensionality and compact data. This step is crucial for addressing several inherent
limitations of EEG signals, such as low signal-to-noise ratio, non-linearity, non-stationarity,
and inter-individual variability. Feature extraction techniques are primarily employed in
classical machine learning algorithms like (SVM, ANN), while deep learning methods do
not rely on hand-crafted features. The feature extraction method used for an ML algorithm

depends on the specific application or problem being tackled.
There are various feature extraction methods used on EEG signals across different do-
mains, including the time domain, frequency domain, decomposition domain, time-frequency
domain, and spatial domain. Among these methods are Fast Fourier Transform (FFT),
Wavelet Transform (WT), Time-Frequency Distribution (TFD), EigenVector Methods (EM),
and Autoregressive Methods (ARM) [4]. In time domain feature extraction, the signal is an-
alyzed as a function of time, quantifying how a signal changes over time. In the frequency
domain, methods like FFT extract features from the sinusoids obtained by converting the
signal from the time domain to the frequency domain. Decomposition feature extraction
filters the desired components, resulting in data compaction. The wavelet transform (WT)
is a popular decomposition method because it allows efficient use with non-stationary
signals (Ieracitano et al., 2020)
In this study, the time-frequency analysis technique for EEG signals called discrete wavelet
transform (DWT) analysis has been applied to filter the clean data and decompose the
signal into the following EEG bands [23]:
• Delta (0-4 Hz): The delta band includes all waves that are less than 4 Hz and re-
flect synchronized brain activity, being located in the frontal lobe in adults and the
posterior lobe in children. These waves are commonly recognized during sleep or
in significant organic brain diseases.
• Theta (4-7 Hz): Theta frequencies, ranging from 4 to 7 Hz, predominantly occur in
the parietal and temporal regions of children’s brains. They remain unnoticed or
absent in healthy, alert adults. However, theta waves can emerge during periods of
emotional stress, such as disappointment or frustration, and certain stages of sleep.
• Alpha (8-13 Hz): Alpha rhythms are neural oscillations in the frequency range of
8–13 Hz, predominantly recorded from the occipital region of the brain during states
of wakeful relaxation with closed eyes. They are usually seen in calm and balanced
mental states, with low stress levels.
• Beta (13-30 Hz): Beta waves, ranging from 13 to 30 Hz, are associated with active,
alert states and cognitive functions such as concentration, problem-solving, and
decision-making. Beta waves are located in the frontal and parietal lobes and are
more prominent during intense mental activity. Excessive beta activity can be linked
to stress, anxiety, and various mental health issues.
• Gamma (>30 Hz): Frequency above 30 Hz is associated with hyperactivity or in-
formation processing in different areas of the brain including the prefrontal cortex,
hippocampus, and amygdala.
2.3
Spiking Neural Networks
SNNs are the third generation of neural networks and emerged to address some of the
limitations of traditional neural networks (NNs), such as high computational cost, lack of
temporal dynamics, and difficulty in spatiotemporal data modeling. SNNs also aim to
provide a more realistic and biologically plausible model of neural computation inspired
by neuroscience findings [11]. The main difference between ANNs and SNNs is how they

LIF is the most widely used model for constructing SNNs due to its simplicity and compu-

Since the information received and sent by the LIF neuron is represented by spikes, it
is crucial to convert the original data into a set of spikes (Richmond, 2009). This spike
coding is required in two different parts of the network:
1. Input encoding: the input data is converted into spikes which are fed into the neu-
ral network. The conversion can be accomplished through various mechanisms.
Rate coding converts amplitudes into a neuron’s spike rate or firing rate. Temporal
coding translates input intensity into the timing of peaks. Delta modulation converts
temporal changes in input intensity into spikes, remaining silent otherwise.
2. Output decoding: the output of the network should be represented by meaningful
and informative spikes. This can be achieved through various coding mechanisms.
Rate coding selects the output neuron with the highest firing rate, enhancing error
tolerance. Temporal coding identifies the first neuron to fire, making the predicted
class energy efficient. Population coding relies on multiple neurons per class, pro-
viding a robust representation.
The development of efficient learning algorithms for SNNs is still under research. The pri-
mary objective of the training loop is to effectively learn features from the available obser-
vations. The SNNs have been trained using supervised, unsupervised learning (STDP)
and reinforcement learning. The most frequently used algorithms are STDP learning,
ANN-SNN Conversion, and backpropagation in SNN (surrogate gradient) [24].
STDP (Spike-time dependent plasticity) learning is an unsupervised learning algorithm
that updates the strength of connections between neurons based on the precise timing
of their spikes. Synapse weights increase if the postsynaptic spike occurs within a cer-
tain time window after a presynaptic spike. Conversely, synapse weights decrease if the
postsynaptic spike occurs before the presynaptic spike [25]. Some advantages of STDP
Investigating AI Transparency and Reliability in EEG-Based Stress Detection with SNN
9
==PAGE:24==
include its ability to process and encode brain information by leveraging temporal patterns
and correlations in input data.Additionally, STDP can reduce latency and power consump-
tion in SNN by minimizing rise time and peak frequency. However, STDP is sensitive to
noise and variations in input data and requires complex decoding and synchronization
mechanisms to extract the encoded information.
The non-differentiability of spiking signals does not allow the use of supervised learn-
ing algorithms of traditional ANNs, such as the error backpropagation algorithm. The re-
searchers have explored various methods to address this issue. One notable approach is
ANN-SNN conversion or shadow training which involves training a standard ANN model
with ReLU neurons offline and subsequently converting it to an SNN with IF neurons us-
ing the learned synaptic weights for initialization. The conversion relies on the principle
that ReLU neurons can be mapped to IF neurons with minimal loss for SNNs operating
on rate-encoded inputs or direct input encoding. The challenge of this method is deter-
mining the firing threshold of IF neurons that can balance the accuracy-latency trade-off.
The main advantage of this learning algorithm is that SNNs can inherit the high perfor-
mance and accuracy of ANNs. However, a significant drawback is the absence of timing
information, which can lead to higher inference latency.
The third category of learning algorithms is the backpropagation of errors in SNN algo-
rithms. The classic backpropagation algorithm requires the gradient to be calculated in
advance, so it needs a continuous derivative, but the derivative of the spike function is
undefined at the time of the spike and ”0” otherwise. One of the most successful methods
to perform gradient-based training in SNNs is a gradient surrogate which approximates
the gradient of the spike function using a smooth continuous function that serves as a
surrogate for the real gradient. This allows SNNs to be trained using gradient-based opti-
mization without losing the peak nature of the network. SNNs trained with this technique
perform backpropagation in time (BPTT) to achieve high and low accuracy latency (100-
time steps) [25], but training is very computationally and memory intensive in terms of
total training iterations compared to conversion techniques.
2.4
Explainable artificial intelligence (XAI)
Explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) emerged as a necessity to explain to humans the
decisions and predictions made by machine learning models. Transparency is needed
especially in medical applications, where the motivation is to provide trust in decisions
such that patients are comfortable with the AI outcome. [26]
Transparency in neural networks can include interpretability, explainability, and under-
standing black box. In this thesis, ”transparency” is used to: 1) explain the decisions
made by algorithms; 2) expose the patterns within the inner mechanism of the algorithm
and 3) uncover the model architecture and hyper-parameters during the training phase.
An ML algorithm is considered transparent if the model parameters from training data
and the labels generated by the testing data are described and motivated by the sys-
tem. The transparency of an ML method is achieved through the overall model structure,
the individual model components, the learning algorithm, and the output generated by
the model. However, achieving complete transparency in ML approaches remains chal-
lenging. Instead, systems strive for varying degrees of transparency, focusing on model

==PAGE:25==
transparency, design transparency, and algorithmic transparency.
The term ”interpretability” in a machine learning model refers to the ability to be understood
by a human. In other words, interpretability is defined by how much a model’s behavior
and decisions make sense to a human being. It is worth noting that domain expertize is an
important component of explainability, as understanding/reading EEG signals is often per-
formed by qualified doctors. When performing different tasks, the brain activity changes
a certain range of frequencies. According to [27], the degree of stress experienced is as-
sessed by the difference in the ratio of beta and alpha waves in the brain. These waves
cannot be seen with the naked eye, thus some pre-processing is necessary.
The initial goal of training machine learning models wass to obtain a high accuracy in learn-
ing relevant features, but there is a recent and ongoing high demand for understanding
the way a specific model operates and the underlying reasons for the decisions made by
the algorithm. On the one hand, simple networks such as linear regression, logistic re-
gression, and decision trees are intrinsically interpretable because they can explain how
the model learns and how the decisions are made (the least squares method is used to
make the decision). On the other hand, deep learning methods are considered black box
models because they compute gradients through a network with millions of weights. The
goal of training is usually to optimize an objective function that cannot accurately capture
the real costs of a model’s decisions. The costs related to ethics or fairness are difficult to
be specified in an objective function, and researchers may not know or be able to observe
these costs in theory.
Spiking neurons are designed to mimic biological neurons by emitting pulses at specific
times to transmit information. Their discrete growth behavior provides an advantage in
the design of transparent neural networks, as it allows researchers to gain insight into the
temporal dynamics of neural computation. However, achieving full transparency remains
an ongoing challenge, especially when dealing with large-scale SNNs. Even though SNNs
are in the early stages of research, it would be beneficial to already have interpretability
methods for these models. Transparency and explainability requirements (explainable
data, explainable predictions, and explainable algorithms) will likely be required of SNNs
in the same way as current ANNs.
In recent years, more and more articles focused on different measures and frameworks
to capture transparency and interpretability. The concern about the black-box behavior of
machine learning models has led to the implementation of new laws such EU AI Act [28] to
ensure better conditions for the development and use of this innovative technology. The
Act aims to ensure that AI systems used within the EU are trustworthy, safe, and respect
fundamental rights. As a consequence, new tools such as Lime, and SHAP have been
published, while popular DL libraries integrated their own XAI libraries, such as Pytorch
Captum and tensorflow tf-explain. These tools are capable of helping researchers detect
erroneous reasoning in classification problems that many previous researchers possibly
have missed and protect the end-user from possible misclassification.
Interpreting and explaining ML models is facilitated by tools like SHAP [29], LIME [30],
and other visual approaches, providing model-agnostic insights.

3.1
Machine learning classification algorithms
In recent decades, many researchers have used machine learning and deep learning
techniques to analyze EEG signals due to the wide range of applications of EEG, such as
emotion, language, attention, and learning. In classical machine learning, SVM and ANN
are the most common algorithms used in EEG classification for stress tasks.
In [5], the authors performed a statistical analysis using a SVM classification algorithm to
capture human stress. Data processing was performed offline and PCA was applied for
data reduction. The SVM classifier was shown to be able to distinguish whether a person
was stressed or not (output value was in the range 1-3, where 1 is relaxed, 2 - normal,
and 3 - stressed) with performances summed in 84.0% Sensitivity, 90.0% Specificity, and
86.0% Overall Accuracy. Even though these algorithms achieved good accuracies, they
encountered two major drawbacks: high-dimensional data, particularly when the number
of features significantly exceeds the number of observations, and overfitting on the training
data, especially in cases where the number of features is high compared to the number
of samples.
In [9], six hybrid combinations of BiLSTM, RNN, LSTM, and GRU models were developed
to classify human stress levels into two classes: stressed and relaxed. A convolutional
neural network (CNN) was used for automatic feature extraction to achieve better predic-
tion performance and, together with the RNN architecture, achieved the lowest accuracy
of 89.91%, while the CBGG deep hybrid model achieved the highest high accuracy of
98.10%. The best performance (99.20%) in detecting human stress levels was achieved
in the [8] study that exploited a hybrid deep learning model based on DWT, Convolu-
tional Neural Network, and Bidirectional Memory on the long-term (CNN-BLSTM, online
neuroplasticity spiking neural network (O-NSNN), O-RSNN and B-RSNN).
Deep learning models are very sophisticated and perform very well on large and complex
data sets, but their disadvantage is that the information encoded in the NN weights is
difficult to interpret. Experts must be able to reason about the model’s behavior relative to
the domain, otherwise they cannot trust it as a decision-making tool. This is particularly
important in areas where ethics (neuroscience) are involved or the key stakeholders need
explanations. Furthermore, the training loop requires significant computational resources,
making them expensive to train and implement. To overcome this problem, researchers
have begun to explore EEG analysis using SNNs.
The limitations of accuracy loss and long inference time for the ANN to SNN conversion
have been explored in this study [34]. Their results showed near lossless conversion with
VGG-16, PreActResNet-18, and deeper structures. The methods have been tested on
three datasets: MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. The best performance was achieved
on MNIST and CIFAR-100 datasets, for which the conversion loss was negative or very
low. In [35], the authors proposed a framework for direct training for SNN architectures

4.1.1
Data Collection
The data availability is considered crucial to constructing a machine learning model or
data-driven real-world systems, including spiking neural networks. However, acquiring a
high-quality EEG dataset encounters several challenges. These include the lack of stan-
dardization across EEG devices, the need for specialized knowledge, and the requirement
for high-quality capture techniques. Moreover, EEG signals are directly affected by body
movement, eye movements, muscle electrical interference, or even heartbeats.

The Poisson encoder transforms input data at a spike rate. To ensure that the firing
probability is properly scaled, the analog signal given as an input is normalized to the
range [0, 1]. Then, it creates an empty spike train, which is essentially a timeline that
records whether a spike occurs (1) or not (0) at each time step. For each time step,
a random number between 0 and 1 is generated which is compared to the value of the
signal at the current time step, multiplied by the length of the time step. This product gives
a threshold that determines the likelihood of a spike occurring. If the random number is
less than this threshold, it records a spike (1) in the spike train, otherwise records no

The following layer performs depth-wise convolution with a kernel size of (8, 16). This
layer learns spatial filters for each temporal filter and decreases the number of trainable
parameters without relying on previous feature maps. Batch Normalization is used on
the feature map dimension before applying ReLU nonlinearity. Since EEGNet is a very

ensuring that no units are codependent with one another. The output of block 1 generates
frequency-specific spatial feature maps.
Block 2 implements separable convolution, beginning with a depth-wise convolutional
layer and progressing to a point-wise convolutional layer. Separable convolutions offer
two key benefits: (1) fewer parameters to fit and (2) explicit decoupling of relationships
across feature maps. This is achieved by learning a kernel summarizing each feature
map individually and then merging the outputs ideally. Average Pooling 2D is also used
to decrease the number of parameters and the representation of each channel’s spatial
size. This layer takes the input data array and calculates the average value of all the
values in each channel. This produces a smaller tensor than the matching input data.
The final block is the classification block, which sends the features to a dense layer of N
units, where N is the number of classes. In the original EEGNet architecture, the SoftMax
function is the last layer which computes a complicated exponential function intended
to allow error back-propagation learning when the goal function has cross-entropy. The
predicted class should be the label of the output neuron with the highest activation. In
contrast, the SNN model predicts the neuron based on the spike trains. As a result,
a simple linear function is employed to identify the most representative spike trains in
EEGNet, which has no effect on accuracy but improves conversion.
The original EEGNet uses the ELU activation function, which requires computing an ex-
ponential function when the input activation is negative in order to achieve high accuracy.
Existing conversion approaches [52], [53] employ ReLU activation to establish a connec-
tion with the transfer function of a spiking neuron. The findings show that the LIF neuron’s
input current is proportional to the activity of a rectified linear unit (ReLU) within a defined
time window. As a result, to successfully convert EEGNet to spiking neural networks while
reducing complexity, the activation function of ELU is changed to ReLU.
The ANN-SNN conversion has been proven to be a promising method to maintain the
performance of trained feedforward ANN. As stated in chapter 2.3, the main distinction
between ANN and SNN is that SNN generates discrete pulses while the output of the ANN
neurons is a continuous real value.
The goal is to convert the real base propagation in EEGNet to the spiking-based by pre-
serving the accuracy of the model. This involves developing spiking implementations of
max-pooling layers, softmax activation, neuron biases, and batch normalization, but also
making the resource calculation more efficient and reducing energy consumption.

Convolutional SNN - Model 3
The third model (Model 3) is more complex as it aims to exploit the advantages of the
EEGNet network and the SNN behavior (these networks can process time-series data
while preserving the temporal accuracy of input signals). The implemented model is a
spiking feed-forwarded neural network based on the EEGNet model described in the pre-
vious section 4.2.2, but replacing the activation functions with spiking neurons and the
average pooling 2d with max pooling 2d layer. This type of architecture ensures that the
data is transmitted strictly in one direction – from inputs to outputs, there are no cycles,
and processing can take place over many hidden layers.
The SNN EEGNet was adapted from the original EEGNet architecture, incorporating spik-
ing neurons to leverage the efficiency and biological plausibility of SNNs. The architecture
included:
• Convolutional Layers: To capture spatial and temporal features of the EEG data.
• Spiking Neurons: Replaced traditional neurons to simulate the spiking behavior
observed in biological neurons.
The performance of SNN allows to sending raw signal as input while the spike encoding
is represented by the first five layers of the network, which are {Conv2d-BatchNorm2d-
Conv2d-BatchNorm2d-LIFNode}
The library used to define the network was Spiking Jelly [50] because it offers the spik-
ingjelly.activation_based.layer package to create the connections between neurons, facil-
itating the implementation and use of the LIF neurons. For the LIF module, the surrogate
function ATan and different thresholds for each layer have been configured.
An additional time-step dimension is added during the forward function, and the input is
repeated T times to create a sequence with shape=[T, N, 1, C, Ts]. The output is defined
by the firing rate of the last spiking neuron layer.
4.3
Training and Validating SNNs
Although the amount of studies on SNNs has recently expanded, developing an effective
and spiking-specific training approach for these networks remains a challenge due to
their unique characteristics. While impressive advances in deep learning are enabled by
training non-spiking neural networks using the backpropagation algorithm, applying this
method to spiking neural networks has been hindered by the existence of discrete spiking
events and discontinuities.
On the other hand, the complexity of the temporal dynamics of SNNs allows for a mul-
titude of interpretations of a neuron’s firing pattern. Naturally, this means that there are
numerous ways of experiments to train SNNs depending on the network architecture and
the encoding method. During this project, the following groups of methods have been
applied to improve the accuracy:
• Shadow training: A non-spiking ANN is trained and converted into an SNN by inter-
preting the activations as a firing rate or spike time.

The surrogate gradient method was used to train the SNN EEGNet. This method ad-
dresses the challenge of non-differentiability in spiking neurons by approximating the gra-
dient of the spiking function with a surrogate gradient during backpropagation [50]. This
allows the use of gradient-based optimization techniques, such as AdamW or Adam, to
train the SNN effectively.
The surrogate gradient method involves the following steps:
1. Forward Pass: Compute the membrane potential and generate spikes based on
the input data (windowed EEG signals).
2. Surrogate Gradient Calculation: During the backward pass, replace the non-differentiable
spike function with a smooth surrogate function to compute gradients.
3. Parameter Update: Use the computed gradients to update the model parameters,
similar to traditional backpropagation.
For the training procedure, the optimal number of epochs found was 50, with a batch
size of 16, consistent with previous models to ensure comparability. The model achieved
high accuracy within the low number of epochs, significantly faster than the ANN EEG-
Net, which required ten times more epochs. This rapid convergence is attributed to the
efficient learning dynamics of spiking neurons and the effective optimization techniques
used. Another parameter was the inference time steps defined to ensure accurate sim-
ulation of spiking behavior. The number of time steps (T) determines how long the SNN
simulates to produce an output, chosen to balance between computational efficiency and
accuracy.
To prevent overfitting, a dropout rate of 0.5 was applied after Block 2 and 0.6 after Block
3 of the network. Dropout helps in regularizing the model by randomly setting a fraction
of input units to zero at each update during training, which prevents the network from
becoming too reliant on any particular neurons.
AdamW was chosen as an optimizer for its ability to handle large datasets and sparse
gradients. It combines the benefits of Adam (adaptive learning rates) with weight de-
cay regularization, which helps in preventing overfitting. A CosineAnnealingLR learning
rate scheduler was employed to adjust the learning rate dynamically. This scheduler re-
duces the learning rate following a cosine annealing schedule, which helps in achieving a
smoother and more efficient learning curve. Another regularization technique applied was
the weight decay applied to penalize large weights, encouraging the model to maintain
smaller, more generalizable weights.

4.3.4
Benefits of Spiking Neural Networks
SNNs and ANNs are both powerful tools in the field of machine learning and neural com-
putation. However, SNNs offer several unique advantages over traditional ANNs, partic-
ularly in terms of efficiency, biological plausibility, and real-time processing capabilities.
One of the most significant advantages of SNNs is their efficiency in terms of energy con-
sumption. SNNs are inherently more energy-efficient than ANNs because they process
information using discrete spikes rather than continuous activations. This event-driven
processing means that neurons only consume energy when they spike, making SNNs
particularly suitable for low-power applications and neuromorphic hardware. [54]
SNNs excel at handling temporal dynamics as they inherent the ability to process infor-
mation over time. This makes them particularly well-suited for tasks involving sequential
data, such as brain data, speech recognition, dynamic image processing, and real-time
decision-making.
Another strength of SNNs is the robustness to noise and variations in input data due to
their event-driven nature. This robustness makes them suitable for real-world applications
where data can be noisy or incomplete such as EEG signals. Additionally, SNNs can
adapt to new information more efficiently, as they can continue to learn and update their
parameters even during training.
4.3.5

Training SNNs is quite challenging, and several specific issues have arisen. The data en-
coding method, spiking behavior, choosing the right hyperparameters, the limited number
of frameworks, everything can be an obstacle to getting good network training.
Even though encoding continuous EEG data into spikes is non-trivial, the choice of en-
coding scheme (rate coding, temporal coding) can significantly impact the network’s per-

is crucial. This adds complexity to the training process, as the network must learn both
spatial and temporal patterns. Finding the optimal encoding method and ensuring the
network captures these dynamics accurately can be challenging.
The most common limitation is related to the presence of the discrete spikes of the spik-

Another drawback is the limited number of tools and frameworks designed for SNNs com-

4.4
Interpretability tools
Explainability in AI refers to the ability to understand and interpret the decisions made by
a model. While various explainability methods have been developed for ANNs, such as
saliency map, Grad-CAM, SHAP, and LIME, their direct application to SNNs is limited due
to the differences in gradient computation, temporal dynamics, and sparse activity.
The surrogate gradient method smooths the gradients to approximate the non-differentiable
spike function. This smoothing process can obscure the precise contributions of individ-
ual spikes, making it difficult to apply methods like Grad-CAM that rely on exact gradient
values. The sparse firing of neurons in SNNs means that only a subset of neurons are
active at any given time. This sparsity can make it challenging to generate comprehensive
activation maps, as the contributions of inactive neurons are not represented.
Given these challenges, there is a need for specialized explainability methods tailored to
the unique characteristics of SNNs. Currently, there is little attention given to the inter-
nal spike behavior of SNNs in the literature. SNNs are designed to mimic the behavior
of biological neurons more closely and offer several unique advantages in terms of ex-
plainability compared to traditional ANNs due to their biologically inspired architecture,
temporal dynamics, and sparse activity. In SNNs, neurons communicate through dis-
crete spikes, or action potentials, which are generated when the membrane potential of

==PAGE:52==
a neuron exceeds a certain threshold. This event-driven nature of SNNs allows them to
process information in a temporally dynamic and energy-efficient manner.
For this thesis, the temporal dynamics have been explored by examining the spiking ac-
tivity at each time step, and the most influential neurons and connections have been iden-
tified, providing insights into which parts of the network are critical for specific decisions
by using the sparse activity. To enhance the explainability of SNNs, two visualization
tools were adapted and implemented: SAM [44] and TSA [45] which highlights the most
influential spikes during the inference.
Visualizing the synaptic membrane potentials and spiking activity of neurons in SNNs
provides deep insights into the network’s functioning. These visualizations help in under-
standing how the network processes information, makes decisions, and adapts to new
data. The spiking activity of neurons can be visualized to provide a clear picture of how
the model processes information. The spike plots show the timing of spikes for each
neuron, providing a detailed view of the network’s activity over time, while visualizing the
membrane potential of neurons can help understand how inputs are integrated and lead
to spikes.
SAM is a bio-plausible visualization approach designed to analyze and explain the inter-
nal spike behavior of temporal deep SNNs. This concept provides a visual representation
of spike activations, helping to understand how the network processes information and
makes predictions. SAM helps in identifying which neurons are most active during spe-
cific time intervals and how their activity correlates with the input data and the network’s
predictions. The method involves visualizing spiking activity of neurons over time, high-
lighting the key neurons and connections involved in the decision-making process. This
can be represented as a heatmap or a series of spike raster plots, showing the intensity
and timing of spikes.
TSA is another visualization technique used to understand the temporal dynamics of spik-
ing neurons. It focuses on the timing and sequence of spikes, providing insights into
how the network processes temporal information. TSA helps in analyzing the temporal
patterns of spikes, revealing how the network integrates information over time to make
predictions. The output presents the sequence of spikes for each neuron, showing how
the spiking activity evolves. This can be represented as a series of membrane potential
traces or spike trains, illustrating the temporal relationships between spikes.
The TSA method had as inspiration SAM [44], both visualization methods relying on the
attribution elements and do not require class labels or performing backpropagation to
calculate gradients. While SAM uses only spike activity in the forward propagation to
compute the heatmap, the TSA map uses spike activity, output membrane potential, and
weights in the input domain.

Discussion
This chapter aims to reflect on the work presented previously, providing a comprehensive
analysis of the findings. The explainability results of the SNN models will be compared
with relevant findings to contextualize our results within the broader field of neuroscience.
Additionally, the research question will be addressed. Lastly, the limitations of this study
and suggestions for future research will be discussed.
6.1
Overall Result Analysis
The adoption of explainability for deep learning models in research involving EEG is still
very rare. In the literature, a majority of the model explanations for ANN are based on
the visualization method using the backpropagation approach. However, these methods
cannot be applied to the SNN models. This thesis represents the first study to integrate
explainability methods specifically adapted for SNNs with EEG signals. One of the ma-
jor problems when working with EEG signals is that interpreting raw time series data is
inherently less intuitive compared to visualizing images.
To explore the explainability of SNNs for EEG-based stress detection, a series of models
with increasing architectural complexity have been developed. The first model was a
simple SNN consisting of three linear layers with LIF neurons. This model used encoded
spikes using the Poisson encoder and it has obtained an accuracy of only 56% on the test
set. Despite the relatively low accuracy, it provided valuable insights into the fluctuations
of membrane potential and the generation of spikes, as these represent the starting point
for implementing explainable methods. Building on this foundation, the next approach
was to train an ANN and convert it into an SNN. This model obtained a higher accuracy
of 80% on the test set, but the complexity and training time increased significantly.
Leveraging the knowledge gained from these experiments, a final model that achieved
the best accuracy of 83% on the test set was developed by effectively integrating and
learning both temporal and spatial features. Model 3 required only 50 epochs for training,
compared to 500 epochs for the traditional EEGNET model. This demonstrates that train-
ing an SNN model is more energy-efficient but demands more computational resources
due to the increased model size because of the new time dimension.
TSA and SAM methods have been applied for the simple SNN to experiment and explore
how these methods work and how effective they are. Working on a simple model allows to
check and validate the connections of the neurons. On a more complex network such as
Model 3, all combinations of layers and neurons complicate the process. TSA can work as
a channel selection for stress detection because it can indicate the influence of specific
EEG channels for prediction as it was illustrated in the results section by figure 5.14.
These results represent the two classes predicted correctly (stressed: 1, and nonstressed
:0). By comparing the TSA maps for both classes side by side, the distinct patterns of
neural activity that the model uses to differentiate between stress and non-stress states
can be evidentiated. When the stressed class was detected, it was possible to see how
the TSA map was predominantly red, that is, certain EEG signals positively influenced the

6.2
Addressing the Research questions
To answer the first two research questions (see section 1.1) which concern the model
complexity and interpretability, various SNN architectures have been explored to provide
transparent and interpretable representations of stress-related patterns in EEG signals.
To enhance the interpretability of these models, explainability methods such as SAM and
TSA have been employed, allowing visualization of the contributions of individual neurons
and spikes to the network’s predictions.
Simple SNNs with linear layers (Model 1) have straightforward architectures, making it
easier to trace the flow of information and understand how inputs are transformed into
outputs. Each neuron’s contribution can be directly observed and interpreted. In sim-
ple SNNs, explainability methods can be applied more directly, providing clear visual-
izations of neuron contributions. On the other hand, Model 3, while powerful, involves
more complex operations such as convolutional layers and pooling, which can obscure
the interpretability of individual neuron contributions. The interactions between numerous
parameters can make isolating the impact of individual neurons or connections challeng-
ing. Therefore, applying explainability methods becomes a challenge.
While Model 3 can capture more complex patterns and potentially offer better perfor-
mance, simple SNNs with linear layers can be more explainable due to their straight-
forward architecture, fewer parameters, and easier application of explainability methods.
The choice between these models depends on the specific needs of the application, bal-
ancing the trade-offs between accuracy and interpretability.
Most visualizations for ANN such as saliency maps, and grad-CAM rely on gradient meth-
ods. Unlike traditional neural networks, SNNs process information over time, making
it more complex to visualize their decision-making process. Adapting techniques like
saliency maps and Grad-CAM would require capturing the temporal evolution of neuron
activations, making it a challenging task. This study [44] showed applying crafted grad-
cam provided smoothed results, so special visualization techniques need to be developed
for SNN.
To address the third research question, this thesis implemented two already existing ap-
proaches. TSA succeeded in selecting the most influential EEG channels by showing
the positive and negative contributions. On the other hand, even though the SAM map
showed promising results for images, when implemented for EEG signals did not bring
enough information. Therefore, depending on the context, the application, and the dataset
different approaches can be implemented to offer interpretability visualization for stress
detection.
6.3
Limitations and Future work
This research provides significant insights into the behavior and performance of SNNs
and their mathematical models. However, there are several limitations and possible future
directions that require improvements.

Conclusion
This thesis explored the explainability of SNNs in the context of EEG-based stress detec-
tion. Three models were developed: a simple SNN with three linear layers of LIF neurons,
an ANN EEGNet model converted to an SNN model, and a more complex convolutional
SNN adapted from the EEGNET model. The study implemented various explainability
methods, including the visualization of internal states of LIF neurons, SAM, and TSA.
The simple SNN provided fundamental insights into the behavior of SNNs, achieving 56%
accuracy on the test set. Despite its modest performance, it highlighted the importance of
understanding membrane potential fluctuations and spike generation. The convolutional
spiking neural network, adapted from EEGNET, demonstrated superior performance by
effectively integrating temporal and spatial features, requiring only 50 epochs for training
compared to 500 epochs for the traditional EEGNET model. A comparative analysis with
other studies revealed significant overlaps and differences in the identified EEG channels
for stress detection. The TSA map in this study identified F7, P3, P7, P09, Oz, and F8
as representative channels, aligning with known neural findings in the field of stress and
emotional processing. These results were compared with another study that identified
F3, FC5, F8, Fp1, F4, P7, Fp2, and F7 as universally optimal channels. The overlap
of channels such as F7, F8, and P7 emphasize their importance, while the differences
highlight the need for further research.
This research represents the first study that integrates explainability methods specifically
adapted for SNNs with EEG signals. By applying SAM and TSA to the SNN models, this
study bridges a significant gap in the field, enhancing the interpretability and transparency
of SNN-based EEG analysis. The findings contribute valuable insights into the behavior
and performance of SNNs and their mathematical models.
One limitation of this study is the SAM and TSA methods were applied to the simple
SNN, which achieved a lower accuracy compared to the convolutional SNN. This suggests
that further exploration of explainability methods on more complex and higher-performing
models, such as the convolutional spiking EEGNET, could provide deeper insights and
more accurate interpretations.
In conclusion, this thesis demonstrates the feasibility and potential of using SNNs for EEG-
based stress detection. The integration of explainability methods not only increases the
transparency of these models, but also makes them reliable for their wider acceptance and
application in real-world scenarios such as healthcare. By addressing identified limitations
and exploring future research directions, the field can continue to advance, contributing to
more efficient and interpretable stress detection systems that can be used with confidence
by clinicians.
