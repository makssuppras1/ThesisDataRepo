Abstract
The offshore wind energy market has developed at a rapid pace in recent years with am-
bitions to employ high-wind areas in deeper waters. Since an offshore wind farm consists
of multiple wind turbines, an individual mooring and anchorage system for each turbine is
not the most efficient design and a coupled mooring system could reduce costs [1]. This
thesis investigates the dynamics of the floaters and the loads acting on the mooring lines
in a case with two turbines with a shared vertical mooring line.
The assessed designs are based on the model built in HAWC2 in an earlier study [2]. The
turbine configuration consists of two 15MW IEA turbines supported by a spar-buoy floater
substructure. A base case with two turbines each connected to a vertical shared line is
considered, and three alternatives: a case with increased load on the shared line (a 5 MN
buoy), reduced load on the shared line (a 1 MN weight), and a case where the individual
mooring lines are at a 35 degree angle with the seabed. In addition to this a single turbine
model is used for understanding the influence of the shared line on the mooring line loads
and floater motions. Since mooring line fatigue failure is one of the failure mechanisms
with highest probability for a floating turbine according to [3], the design assessment is
made for DLC 1.2 [4], consisting of normal sea states with turbulent wind and wind speeds
ranging from cut-in to cut-out combined with a corresponding wave height and wave pe-
riod. The environmental conditions are based on Morro Bay site, with 870m water depth
and sea state characterized by waves with long periods. Four wind directions are con-
sidered in the simulations: 0, 30, 60 and 90 degrees. In addition to this, a turbine yaw
misalignment of +/- 10 degrees is simulated, as well as a wind and wave misalignment of
15, 25 and 35 degrees.
The comparison between the single turbine and two turbine cases shows notable differ-
ences. In a single turbine configuration the mooring line load is evenly distributed amongst
the lines regardless of wind direction, while for the two turbine configurations a significant
load increase is observed on the upwind line when the wind is parallel to the lines ex-
tending to the shared line. The wind and wave misalignment results in less governing
mooring line loads for the single turbine, while for the two turbine configuration the mis-
aligned conditions cause greater line loading for 90 degree wind direction. In addition to
this, the surge displacement of the upwind turbine in a two turbine configuration is 30%
higher compared to the single turbine case. The pitch motion of the single turbine is higher
for wind speeds exceeding 11m/s, while it peaks at 10m/s for the two turbine case.
The behavior of all configurations with two turbines is similar. The average mooring line
loads are governed by wind speed and thrust force on the turbines for all cases. They do
not show significant sensitivity to the yaw misalignment scenario. Orienting the shared
mooring line and connected lines at 90 degrees to the predominant wind direction reduces
the loading with 65% compared to a case where they are aligned with the wind. The case
where the tension load on the shared line is increased results in improved mooring line
load distribution, and a 4% reduction in the fatigue load on the highest loaded line, while
the surge displacement of the upwind turbine is reduced. The opposite is observed for
the case with reduced tension line load - increased fatigue loads on the shared line and
connected lines and a greater surge displacement for the upwind line, making it least
favorable. The case with mooring lines at 35 degrees angle with the seabed does not re-
sult in significant improvements compared to the base case. It was observed that for the
different configurations, the wind and wave misaligned case result in governing damage
equivalent loads. For future assessment it would be worthwhile to review ultimate limit
states together with misaligned wind and wave condition.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The significant growth of offshore wind energy in the past decade has caused a renewed
interest in floating wind opportunities. According to an assessment by Ørsted [5], there
are large markets globally that can benefit from floating wind.
Figure 1.1: Global offshore wind potential, Source:Ørsted [5]
In USA alone, the potential for floating wind is 2.8 TW [6]. Offshore sites at greater depths
typically offer stronger winds with better production availability which makes them an at-
tractive investment for developers. While the fixed offshore turbines are most often sup-
ported by a monopile or a jacket substructure, some of the typical substructures for floating
turbines include a spar buoy, a semi-submersible or a tension leg platform. All of these
structures come with different challenges and while they have been used in the offshore
oil and gas industry before, there is little experience in their use for floating wind.
The first commercial offshore floating wind farm, Hywind Scotland, consists of 5 turbines
with a spar substructure [7]. This type of substructure is considered to be one of the
promising options for large scale projects due to the ease of procurement as the structure
is relatively simple in shape. The complexities include maintaining stability during opera-
tion, as well as transport and installation offshore, but there is also a lack of experience
with its performance for floating wind. In fact, an optimization study [8] concluded that the
spar buoy diameter for the Hywind project, which was commissioned in 2017, could be
reduced by 25 percent to achieve the same turbine performance and operational life.
With the growing plans to develop offshore floating wind and increased number of tur-
bines in a wind farm, there is a need to optimize not just individual turbines but also the
wind farm configurations and layout. Sharing mooring lines and anchors in a wind farm is
one of the opportunities for cost reductions. However, by connecting adjacent turbines,
new coupling behaviors may be introduced in the system and further investigations are

required to gain better understanding of the additional design challenges, as well as the
implications of these on the mooring line loads.
1.2
Scope of Work
The implementation of spar buoy substructures in floating wind demands further investi-
gations in mooring line behavior and potential for reducing costs by making use of shared
mooring lines between turbines. This parameter study intends to provide insight into the
behavior and loading of the design concept of two wind turbines connected to a shared
mooring line.
1.2.1
Design Cases
The design that has been assessed is based on an earlier study, see [2]. It consists of two
15MW IEA turbines with a spar buoy substructure, fixed to the seabed with taut mooring
lines and connected to a vertical shared line. The turbines and substructure have not
been modified, while the mooring lines have been adjusted for a base case and three
alternatives as follows:
1. Two turbines with a shared mooring line - base case, originated from [2]
2. Two turbines with a shared mooring line with increased tension force on the shared
line
3. Two turbines with a shared mooring line with reduced tension force on the shared
line
4. Two turbines with a modified angle to the seabed (35 degrees)
In addition to the above, a single turbine model has been used to determine the influence
of the shared mooring line on the surge and pitch behavior of the floaters. Two cases
with modified tension load on the shared mooring line are used in order to gain insight
into the influence of the shared line force on the load distribution amongst the remaining
lines; an alternative with an increased mooring line footprint is assessed to observe the
implications on the accelerations and displacement of the turbines. The site used as a
basis for the environmental loading is Morro Bay located on the West coast of the USA,
California State. The site depth is 870 m which has been used for all design cases. The
wave climate is characterized by waves with long periods.
According to [3] mooring line failure is one of the high risk elements in a floating tur-
bine configuration, and mooring line fatigue is determined to be one of the leading failure
causes. Therefore, this study is focused on investigating design loads according to DLC
1.2 as described in IEC 61400-3-2 Design requirements for floating offshore wind turbines
[4], which represents fatigue loads on the turbine in operational condition (cut-in to cut-out
wind speeds). DLC 1.2 is based on normal sea state conditions (NSS). The considered
wind speed range includes: 4, 6, 8,10, 10.59 (rated wind speed), 11, 13,15,17,19,21,23
and 25 m/s. A joint probability has been implemented, such that each wind speed is con-
sidered along with a corresponding significant wave height and wave period. This takes
into account four aligned wind/wave directions (0, 30, 60 and 90 degrees), a +/-10 degree
turbine yaw misalignment and three cases with misalignment between wind and wave
with an offset of 15, 25 and 35 degrees between the two.
One of the limitations on the performed simulations and the presented results is that no
wake effect has been included in the HAWC2 simulation. That means that the wind ob-
served at the upwind turbine is also observed at the downwind turbine without any addi-
tional turbulence. Accounting for the wake would result in a more turbulent wind observed
2

for the downwind turbine, which would affect the mooring line loading and the floater mo-
tions. Furthermore, the implementation of wave theory is made using a linear wave theory

focused on determining the fatigue load on the polyester sections. This is because the

Numerical Modelling
This section provides background information on the numerical modelling used for the
turbine, floater and mooring lines. The technical specifications of each component, as
well as each design configuration, have been described in Chapter 5.
2.1
Turbine Numerical Model
This study consists of design combinations which have been simulated in HAWC2. HAWC2
is an aeroelastic code which performs wind turbine simulations in time domain [9]. The
principles of working of this code have been briefly described in the following sections.
2.1.1
Aerodynamic Calculations
The fundamental method behind the calculations of aerodynamic loads on the wind tur-
bine blades in HAWC2 is the unsteady Blade Element Momentum Method. The basis for
this method was developed by Glauert (1935) [10], originally for airplane propellers, and
is also commonly employed for calculating wind-induced loads on a turbine, the power of
the rotor, and thrust force on the turbine. Its unsteady adaptation takes into account the
irregular nature of the wind load and load response.
Here a brief overview of relevant steps for calculation and formulas relevant for calcula-
tions are presented, further details are available in [11].
In order to determine the loads acting on the turbine, each turbine blade is split into several
elements with a specified section length. The blade elements are each assigned airfoil
characteristics, which are pre-generated either via practical testing in a wind tunnel, or
through CFD simulations. The airfoil is subject to the wind flow, which generates a lift and
a drag force on the blade section.

Water Kinematics and Hydrodynamic Model
2.2.1
Wave Kinematics
The water kinematics in HAWC2 are implemented in a Dynamic Linked Library (DLL) and
coupled to HAWC2 via an interface. The relevant wave theory used for the performed
simulations is irregular airy theory [13], which is in line with DNV-RP-C205 [14]. Airy the-
ory has been used as it is suitable for deep water waves, see figure below.
Figure 2.2: Wave Theory - Application based on wave steepness and water depth
The applicable theory is based on the relationship between the water depth and apparent
wave period squared on the horizontal axis and the wave height over the apparent wave
period squared on the vertical axis. For the site that is considered in the simulations, see
further description in Chapter 4, characterized by a water depth of 870m and relatively
long periods, ranging from 11 to 16 seconds. It is relevant to note that this is a site-
specific condition which affects the mooring line loads, and for sites with intermediate
water depth which may also be suitable for floating turbines, a Stokes or Stream function
may be applied and alter the floater behavior. The Pierson-Moskowitz spectrum [15] is
applied for the wave generation; this spectrum is used to describe the wave frequency

2.3
Mooring lines - Practical Application and Modelling
2.3.1
Practical Application
Offshore floating structures have various means of mooring. In terms of geometry, there
are three main types: catenary, tension-leg (vertical) and taut mooring lines [19]. The cur-
rently commissioned offshore wind farms all have a catenary mooring system. Catenary
mooring lines do not arrive at an angle with the seabed which is beneficial as they do not
induce vertical loads on the anchor.
Figure 2.5: Catenary Mooring Lines
Figure 2.6: Tension-Leg Mooring Lines
Figure 2.7: Taut Mooring Lines
They restrain the motion of a floating body by the increase in the suspended line weight
– as the line stretches, more of it is suspended which increases the weight acting in re-
sistance to the body motion. This also influences the stiffness behaviour of the cate-
nary mooring lines, as their stiffness is increased progressively once the line is subject to
greater tension. This may be quite suitable for a floating production storage and offloading
(FPSO) designs where only a single floating body is restrained and the seabed footprint
is not a big concern. In the case of floating wind turbines, however, over a hundred wind
turbines may be moored in the same field, resulting in a significant seabed footprint.
Tension-Leg mooring systems have a small seabed footprint and provide high stability of
Assessment of dynamic loads on floating wind turbines with shared mooring lines: parameter study
11
==PAGE:20==
the restrained floating body. In this configuration all lines are vertical and require strong
pre-tension. Investigations show that a single line failure may not be critical for the sys-
tem [20]; however, they require a more complex installation method which increases the
overall costs.
Taut mooring lines are mooring lines that are always in tension, which have a smaller
footprint than catenary lines. Because of this the line length is shorter and the seabed
footprint is smaller. A scale study [21] showed that the radius to the anchor may be re-
duced by 2/3 when using taut mooring lines. The same study suggests, however, that
they may also result in higher accelerations in the tower. This is because taut mooring
lines have to absorb the motion loading from the attached floating body which may re-
sult in higher pitch amplitudes. Furthermore, they provide less stability than tension-leg
configurations but require less pre-tensioning which makes the installation method less
complex and more cost efficient. For the current study, taut mooring lines have been re-
viewed as they provide a good trade-off between platform stability and installation costs
compared to catenary and tension-leg lines.

Fatigue Assessment
The focus of this parameter study is the fatigue loading on the mooring lines according to
DLC 1.2 [4]. In a recent study [3], it was concluded that the probability of fatigue mooring
line failure is considered to be one of the top three failure modes and also leads to signif-
icant consequences in extreme events as it may even result in capsizing of the turbine.
The fatigue damage for the described model set-up and analyses has been reviewed
for several components. Of main interest is the shared mooring line which is made up
of polyester section only. In addition to the shared mooring line, the polyester sections
connecting the two turbines (indicated as line 1 and line 7 on the figures in Chapter 5) to
the shared mooring line have been addressed, as well as the line which us upwind (Line
5) in the case where the wind is parallel to the lines connecting the two turbines.
3.1
Fatigue Calculation Approach
The fatigue loading on taut mooring lines is assessed with a S-N curve which represents
on the vertical axis the stress range and on the horizontal the number of cycles. In the
current designs, three of the mooring lines consist of both chain sections and polyester
sections, while the shared line is made of polyester only. While the intent here is to estab-
lish a basis for comparison between the mooring lines, it has to be noted that following a
study [22] it was concluded that in fact in a shared set up of polyester and chain sections,
the chain sections show worse performance and are thus governing in fatigue failure.
While DNV-OS E-301 [23] proposes a T-N curve for steel mooring lines, such as chain,
six-strand rope and spiral strand rope, the performance of polyester sections is not in-
cluded. API RP 2SM [24] suggests an alternative T-N curve for polyester mooring lines,
yet [22] argues that this one was based on conservative assumptions and did not account
for latest industry practice which produces higher quality products. The referenced T-N

Post Processing
7.1
Quality Assurance
In order to ensure that the single turbine configuration, base model with two turbines and
alternatives run as expected, each of them was ran for cases without wind and with steady
wind (excluding turbulence), as well as for still water, regular and irregular waves. The
turbulence box generation was reviewed in the log files to verify that it was successfully
generated. Furthermore, the turbulence seeds, as well as the irregular wave seeds were
compared to confirm that for each environmental condition, a new seed is used for both
wind and wave. The spar buoy behavior was reviewed for several directions to ensure
that the coordinate systems between ESYSMooring and WAMIT have been implemented
correctly. In order to have representative results, simulations were ran for 1200 seconds
(20 minutes) and results for the second 600 seconds (second 10 minutes) were consid-
ered for the analyses.
After running the simulations, these were also assessed for quality. Due to the large vol-
ume of data generated, it was necessary to adopt a more practical approach on ensuring
the quality of data. The following properties were reviewed to verify and filter data:
• File size
The file sizes for each configuration were reviewed. Large log files were an indi-
cator of poor convergence and these simulations were reviewed individually. Small
sizes of output were an indicator of incomplete simulation (terminated due to time
restraint). In such cases the simulations were ran with an extended run time.
• Statistical extremes
Data extremes were reviewed on a global basis. The average and extreme values
were compared and any files that generated notable outliers were reviewed individ-
ually.
Outside of this, spot checks were performed to verify the behavior of the controller, power
output and turbine motion. By applying this quality assurance approach it was possible
to ensure that all simulations were implemented correctly and therefore the results could
be used for further post processing.
7.2
Post Processing Method
The post processing has been done in Python. The HAWC2 simulations output was saved
as compressed binary file and processed for the sensors of interest, in particular:
• Mooring Line loads (all polyester sections of each mooring line) - 9 sensors
• Floater Surge - 1 sensor per turbine
• Floater Pitch Angle - 1 sensor per turbine
From these, the extremes and average values for each sensor were extracted in order to
observe trends, split by various conditions: wind speed, wind direction, wind-wave mis-
alignment offset, turbine yaw misalignment. The mooring line loads were reported, as
well as the floater motions and tower accelerations.
The fatigue calculation was performed using the WETB Github toolbox [31] and was cal-
culated for 1e7 equivalent load cycles for comparison. The ASTM cycle counting method
was applied according to ASTM E 1049 standard [32] implemented in Python. First, a

9.1
Conclusion
In this study, a configuration of two turbines with a shared mooring line is assessed for
fatigue loading on the mooring lines as well as surge and pitch floater behavior. A single
turbine configuration is used to compare the loads when there are no shared elements
between the turbines.
Simulations of these models are made in HAWC2 for 13 wind speeds taking into account
4 wind directions, turbine yaw misalignment, and wind and wave misalignment. Each of
these configurations is reviewed for these conditions and the load trends remain similar
for all design cases with two turbines. The four wind directions considered show that the
90 degree wind direction results in the smallest loads on Line 1, 5, 7 and the shared line,
with 65% reduction in load compared to the 0 degree wind case.
The peak loads are observed for wind speeds between 10 and 13m/s, following the thrust
curve trends. The load combinations with yaw misalignment do not show significant dif-
ference to the cases without yaw misalignment. Yet, they are relevant when assessing
fatigue as they produce higher load ranges. For the considered designs no relationship
between yaw misalignment and wind speed could be observed.
The cases with wind and wave misalignment have varying influence on the loads on Line
1, 5, 7 and the shared line. For the 0 degree wind direction they do not result in significant
load differences, however, for 90 degree cases the misaligned cases are governing. This
is because at 90 degrees these lines do not take on maximum loads as they are oriented
perpendicular to the wind direction, however, due to the wave misalignment there is a
load redistribution amongst all mooring lines. This shows that wave loading influences
the floater motion even if the thrust force on the turbine is more dominant. The load dif-
ferences for normal sea states for aligned and misaligned design combinations are within
4%, however, for more extreme wave conditions they could become more pronounced.
The single turbine case shows smaller surge displacement compared to both turbines in
the base case with two turbines. The surge displacement of the upwind turbine is higher
than the one of the downwind turbine for the two turbine cases and exceeds the 15m ex-
cursion limit set in the Basis of Design [29]. The single turbine case shows higher pitch
motion at wind speeds exceeding 17m/s, while for the considered two turbine set-ups it
is higher for the downwind turbine and is observed at the rated wind speed. This is at-
tributed to the less stable mooring line connected to the vertical shared line compared to
the upwind turbine which is supported by a line connected to an anchor.
The review of fatigue loading on the mooring lines shows that the shared mooring line
is not as sensitive to fatigue damage, whereas Line 5 (the upwind line) is most highly
loaded. The single turbine case has a 9.9% lower DEL on the upwind line compared to
the base case. The most optimal DEL is observed for the case with a buoy, which results
in 4% lower damage equivalent load for the upwind line. Smaller improvements are seen
with the other two cases - 1% reduction in load for the case with a reduced shared line
tension force, and 2% for the case with mooring lines at 35 degree angle. Lines 1 and 7
share similar loads and their damage equivalent load is 60% lower than the one for Line
5.
From the assessed configurations, the most optimal design with regards to mooring line
load distribution and fatigue performance is the case with a buoyancy force acting on the
shared mooring line, which ensures a better engagement of the shared line and reduced
loading in the upwind line for a zero degree wind condition. This has the disadvantage

of increasing the vertical force on the anchor supporting the shared line. The cases with
added weight or modified mooring line angle showed marginal differences to the base
design. The original configuration would also be suitable, however the outer lines of the
turbines (in the current case, Line 5 and Line 3) would likely wear out faster and a tension
line with higher stiffness would be an appropriate design optimization.
9.2
Discussion and Future work
The reviewed design cases are used to assess the critical loads on the shared mooring
line and the connected elements. The configurations considered are based on [2] and
focus on modified load in the shared tension line and different mooring line angle with
the seabed. However, alternative configurations with shared lines as well as anchors
are possible which may also result in reduced costs. Furthermore, the shared mooring
line concept has the main advantage of reducing the number of necessary anchors. On
the other hand, it introduces a greater risk when there is a line failure. For this study
no risk assessment has been performed but would certainly be relevant for a realistic
design case. It is important to note that site-specific sea states have been applied in
the analyses. The Morro Bay site is swell dominated, whereas a site with shorter wave
periods may result in a different motion response from the floaters, affecting the mooring
line loads as well.
Following from the assessments performed here, reviewing an ultimate state condition
would be beneficial for gaining insight in the motions and mooring line loading. Further
study of the original controller response and possible optimization would be beneficial for
improvement of the two turbine set up response.
