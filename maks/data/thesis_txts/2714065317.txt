Abstract
Predictive maintenance is a technique used to predict when maintenance of a given sys-
tem should be performed. Today, many predictive maintenance procedures are based
on cloud computing, where this study analyses a completely offline solution using an ac-
celerometer and a microcontroller. Machine learning models using Random Forest, Neu-
ral Network and Support Vector Machine algorithms are trained and evaluated in terms
of model accuracy, size and execution time. Examination of these results showed that
all algorithms achieved model accuracy of 99% on the collected datasets, and based on
advantages in regards of size and execution time, Support Vector Machine was chosen
as the preferred model. Several optimizations in terms of power consumption, execution
time and memory usage are proposed, resulting in 93% reduction in power consumption
and 40% reduction of running time, while maintaining the model accuracy. Lastly a sim-
ple interface using two LEDs are implemented resulting in a successful proof of concept
solution.

Introduction
In this chapter an introduction to traditional maintenance strategies and why predictive
maintenance is interesting will be given. Furthermore the reasoning behind the choices
of hardware used to solve the problem will be described.
1.1
Maintenance Strategies
In the industry, the two traditional maintenance strategies are Corrective maintenance
(CM) and Preventive Maintenance (PM). Corrective maintenance, also known as “run-to-
failure”, is a strategy where the system runs until failure, for then to be repaired. CM leads
to high level of system downtime and maintenance costs due to sudden failure [1].
For PM, one of the main objectives is to reduce failure rate, or failure frequency, by main-
taining the system prior to failure. In the industry the PM strategy is often based on rec-
ommendations from the original equipment manufacturer (OEM), which is most likely on a
set interval (e.g. every 10.000 hours or once a year). This strategy has three main draw-
backs. First, the environment varies from machine to machine, and would therefore in
all probability, need different time intervals. Second, machine manufactories do, in most
cases, not experience machine failures, and therefore the persons operating the machine
on a daily basis, will have more practical knowledge of failure prevention. Finally, one has
to keep in mind that the OEMs are businesses that might want to maximize spare parts
replacement through frequent PMs, in order to make more money [2].
To optimize the effectiveness of PM decision making, Condition-based maintenance (CBM)
were introduced. CBM, also known as predictive maintenance, is a strategy that recom-
mends maintenance decisions based on the data collected through condition monitoring.
A solid argument for CBM is that 99% of equipment failures are preceded by certain signs,
conditions, or indications that a failure is going to occur [3]. The goal of CBM is that main-
tenance activities are carried out when needed, instead of on the basis of a scheduled
interval. This implies a reduction in downtime due to failures being detected before they
happen, and a reduction in maintenance costs, hence mechanics and replacement parts
are only payed for when necessary.
The most common way to perform predictive maintenance today, is by sending live data
(e.g. from sensors) to the cloud, and perform the computations there. A lot of data is
transferred online, and problems may arise, if e.g. the system is not placed near a solid
internet connection.
For this project, a completely offline predictive maintenance procedure will be implemented
on a microcontroller. The goal is to create an optimal solution in terms of machine learning
(ML) model performance, microcontroller performance and energy consumption. Several
decisions regarding the ML model, hardware configuration and code structure will be dis-
cussed, based on analysis of the system.
A physical simulation of system wear over time is constructed by an electric fan attached
to a plywood board with a screw, which is loosened over time. By loosening the screw
progressively we simulate the system getting more and more worn, until the screw is
completely loose, resulting in system failure. An image of the constructed system can be
seen in fig. 1.1.

Machine Learning Background
Knowledge
In a literature review of machine learning methods applied to predictive maintenance con-
sisting of 54 papers published between 2009 and 2018 concludes, that the most frequently
used supervised machine learning methods for predictive maintenance are Artificial Neu-
ral Network, Random Forest and Support Vector Machine[4]. All these have different
characteristics, that will be touched upon in this chapter.
Before we go into details about the different methods, some general background knowl-
edge of machine learning will be explained in the following section.
2.1
Model selection and evaluation
The generalization performance of any machine learning model relates to its ability to
predict independent test data. In practice, estimating and evaluating this performance is
essential, since it helps us to choose the right model, and gives a measure of quality for
the final chosen model.
The relevant aspects of model selection and evaluation for this project will be explained
in this chapter.
The first important concept to understand in the bias-variance tradeoff.
2.1.1
Bias-Variance Tradeoff
When discussing prediction error of a machine learning algorithm, we generally distinguish
between two kind of errors, namely bias (underfitting) and variance (overfitting). Bias can
be expressed as the inability of the model to capture the underlying structure of the data,
meaning that the model is too simple. On the other hand, variance is when the model
is too sensitive to small fluctuations in the training data, which will result in inability to
generalize to unseen data.[5]. The bias-variance tradeoff is illustrated in fig. 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Bias-variance tradeoff
For every machine learning model, we need find a good balance between bias and vari-
ance, in order to minimize the total error. The amount of bias and variance is controlled
by the model complexity, and probably the most common way of finding a good model
complexity, which is also used for this project, is cross-validation.

2.2.1
Random Forest
Random Forest (RF) is an ensemble method for regression and classification build on
tree-based methods. RF is an extension over bagging, which is commonly used to reduce
variance within a noisy dataset. A common approach for bagging is using bootstrapping,
which is a random sampling with replacement of the training data — meaning that individ-
ual data points can be chosen more than once [7]. Bagging is then building a classifier on
each bootstrapped dataset, for then to classify new data points as the majority vote off all
the classifiers. This concept is illustrated on fig. 2.5, in which each little square represents
an individual data point. We see how the models are build on different subsets of the data,
while each individual data point might appear in several subsets.

The point of bagging is to average many approximately unbiased but noisy models, and
thereby reduce the variance. Trees are ideal for bagging since if grown large enough, they
are able to capture very complex structures in the data, i.e. they have very low bias, at the
cost of high variance. In other words, very large trees will most likely overfit the training
data. This is where bagging can show its worth, hence each individual tree generated in
bagging is identically distributed, and therefore the expectation of any individual tree is
the same as the expectation of an average of B bootstrapped trees. Meaning, that the
bias is unaffected by bagging, hence the only improvement is through variance reduction.
The variance of the average of identically distributed random variables, each with variance
σ2 and with positive pairwise correlation, ρ is:

The idea of Random Forest is to reduce the correlation between bagged trees, and thereby
reduce variance even more than plain bagging. This is done with random selection of
variables in the tree-growing process. More specifically, only a random subset of input
variables are considered for each split. So on top of the slightly different training sets
(bootstrap samples), the trees will also have different variables as candidates for each

As mentioned earlier, the LIS2DTW12 accelerometer can be configured to different output
data rates (ODR), also referred to as sample rate. From the datasheet for the accelerome-

Values stated in the datasheet can be seen in table 3.1. It might seem tempting to se-

form of undersampling, that occurs when the sampling rate is too low compared to the
frequency of the actual data. This can lead to misidentification of the true signal, mean-
ing that the collected data does not correctly represent the actual behavior of the system.
This concept can easily be illustrated with the sine wave.
The minimum sample rate needed for any signal in order to be able to later reproduce the
original waveform faithfully is usually called the Nyquist rate. The Nyquist rate is defined
as the sampling rate equal to twice the highest frequency of a given signal [14]. As we
see on fig. 3.1, the minimum sampling rate for correctly identifying the sine wave is two
samples per period, which is exactly the Nyquist rate.
This is due to the fact, that each period for the sine wave has one peak and one trough,
and thereby two critical points. Therefore if we get two samples per period, we obtain a
loose interpretation of the true waveform. Note here, that the samples are not necessarily
taken at the peaks, meaning that even though the captured frequency is close to accurate,
the amplitude might be underestimated. This is showed on fig. 3.2, in which samples are
offset by a constant, to avoid sampling at the peaks. By increasing the sample rate, we
enhance the chance for sampling near the peaks, resulting in better identification of the
true amplitude.
In practice, the sample rate in chosen to be higher that the Nyquist rate in order to avoid
sampling at unfortunate times.

vibration signal expressed as a Fourier series.

There is no right or wrong when choosing how many folds to use for cross validation,
since this depends on the objective. Small values of k imply that a great portion of the
data is used for test in each split, which also means that a relative small portion is used
for training and can therefore lead to bias in the estimate of the true prediction error. On
the other hand, very large values for k have low bias but can introduce variance due to
the high portion of data used for training versus the small part used for testing[6]. We also
note that as k increases we also increase computational efforts hence more models will
need to be trained. As we will see in the following, many models will be trained for this
analysis, and therefore 3 outer folds and 5 inner folds are chosen as a good compromise
between bias, variance and computational demand. I.e. for each combination of model
type and sample rate, we will use 3 outer folds and 5 inner folds as demonstrated on

During timing of data collection of 1600 samples with 1600 Hz ODR, another drawback of
this setting unveiled. It was expected that collecting 1600 samples would take roughly 1
second (1600 Hz = 1600 samples per second), but it turned out to take almost 2.5 seconds.
Research of the collected data showed that even though the accelerometer was correctly
configured for 1600 Hz ODR, the true ODR was roughly 800 Hz. Numerous experiments
were carried out, leading to the conclusion that the I2C communication was the limiting
factor. From the datasheet for the accelerometer we see that the I2C interface is compliant
with fast mode standards, which increases the I2C speed frequency from 100 KHz to 400
KHz, at the cost of extra energy consumption. After fast mode was enabled, we see data
collection time around the expected one second. One might wonder how data collection is
happening faster than the actual ODR, i.e. how are 25 samples collected in 40 ms with 50
Hz ODR, when it should take half a second (50 Hz = 50 samples per second). The reason
for this is, that the accelerometer has a 32-samples FIFO buffer. That is, even when we
are not reading from the accelerometer, it is filling the FIFO queue. This implies that when
we actually are reading from the accelerometer, the samples are already collected and
stored in the FIFO, and therefore we do not have to “wait” for the data to be sampled.

6.2

Calculation of the descriptive statistics includes two loops, one for accumulating the sum
used to find the mean, and one for accumulate sum of squared variance used to get
standard deviation. A timing of only the two loops show that they take 6.7 ms, which is
roughly half of the time spend in the data preprocessing phase.
Note here that in order to calculate the variance, we are accumulating the squared devia-
tions of each data point. Calculations of the squared deviations are done with the function
pow() from the C standard library header file math.h. Loops containing function calls gen-

Choice of Microcontroller
After spending time on this project it is clear that the chosen microcontroller is a bit over the
top for this problem. First of all the MCU has two cores, namely a Cortex®-M7 core, that
operates at up to 480 MHz and the Cortex®-M4 core that operates at up to 240 MHz, in

peripherals and clock configurations with so many options was overwhelming. Thereby, if
the project was to be restarted, a less powerful and more simple MCU would be chosen.

Related Work
According to research done by McKinsey, companies in heavy industries have the oppor-
tunity to potentially increase asset availability by 5 to 15 percent, and reduce maintenance
costs by 18 to 25 percent by utilizing predictive maintenance [24]. This clearly shows the
advantages of predictive maintenance, and why this is an interesting topic to explore even
further.
At the time of writing, no common best practice for embedded predictive maintenance ex-
ists, and various studies have tried different solutions. Unsupervised learning is a method
that is used repeatedly. The goal of unsupervised learning, is that the model tries to sep-
arate machine behavior into different clusters. This method has proven to introduce con-
fusion about different states of the machine, hence multiple different clusters are present
for both “normal” and anomaly machine behavior. For simplicity, as seen in [25], all clus-
ters are merged into two clusters, one for normal behavior and one for anomaly behavior.
The downside of merging clusters is, that it takes away the possibility of distinguishing
between slightly and vastly anomaly behavior, which in some cases can be very useful.
Another study used supervised learning to implement a classifier for detecting gas leakage
and capacitor malfunction in an air condition system[26]. Data was collected from various
sensors, of which a training and test data set were created. Different models were trained
and evaluated, and showed that a decision tree classifier were the superior model for the
given problem. This study used sensors and a microcontroller to collect a given data set.
Models were trained and evaluated using MATLAB and were never deployed, executed
and analyzed on the microcontroller as we did for this project.
An out-of-the-box solution called Bob Assistant1 that is using predictive maintenance to
monitor machines has been created by the french company Cartesiam, which was later
acquired by STMicroelectronics. Instead of creating custom tailored machine learning
models for each problem, Bob Assistant is an IoT2 device that work with generalized
pre-build models. In contrary, for this project we developed and optimized a predictive
maintenance solution for a specific problem, in order to achieve best possible performance
in terms of model prediction capability, memory usage and processor load.
Tsang describes how vibration monitoring can be used to detect fatigue, wear, imbalance,
misalignment, loosened assemblies, turbulence, etc. in systems with rotational or recip-
rocating parts [1]. It is stated, how the amplitude of each distinct vibrating component
will remain constant, unless there is a change in the operating dynamics of the system.
Therefore, vibration monitoring using the accelerometer described in section 1.2 is used
for this project.

Bad internet connection
First of all the decoupling of the internet enables numerous opportunities that otherwise
would have been impossible. According to a report on the state of broadband in the world
by UNESCO, as of 2019 only just above 55% of households around the globe have an
internet connection [27], that means 3.7 billion people are without internet connection.
Furthermore the report states that in some regions in Africa, one gigabyte (GB) of data
cost nearly 40% of the average monthly wage. In these poor areas where an online
solution is not an option, and the whole community can be reliant on a given machinery,
a solution like the one implemented in this project can be crucial. This could include
systems with various essential purposes such as supplying people with drinking water
or other critical infrastructure. A research of “COVID-19 and food insecurity in Africa”
showed that proportion of Africa’s population affected by food stress (all income used in
purchasing food) and food crisis (selling core assets for food) increased after COVID-19
hit the world[28]. Especially the non-industrialized regions of Africa saw larger impact of
the disruptions of supply chains due to the pandemic. It is expected that this is due to
the high necessity for manual work, and therefore when labour force drops due to a crisis
like COVID-19, the food production in these areas follow. Frédéric Baudron, who is a
senior scientist and systems agronomist states that one way to ensure the resilience of
food reserves for these areas is to mechanize the critical tasks in the production[29]. This
would imply more machines, hence more demand of predictive maintenance in areas with
potentially bad or expensive internet connections.
9.2
Battery usage
Key challenges of the 21st century is human impact on global warming due to CO2 emis-
sions, and one could argue that minimum energy consumption should be high priority for
every system, even though it is directly connected to the power grid. Of cause we need
systems that function as needed, and therefore minimum is meant as “as low as possi-
ble without compromising the functionality”. In an ideal world in which all systems run
on green energy, this would be immaterial, but unfortunately this is not the current state
of world. If a system is not connected to the power grid, but rather powered by battery,
several benefits emerge when the energy consumption is reduced. First of all, mining of
lithium and other materials that are used for most batteries emits large amounts of CO2,
requires chemicals, enormous amounts of water and can leave contaminants and toxic
waste behind[30]. Despite the environmental footprint for producing batteries, they can
still be more climate-friendly than the alternatives if charged with green energy. When
that is said, if we can cut down on the number and size of the batteries, while still uti-
lizing the full potential of them, that is the optimal solution. Therefore, if we can reduce
the energy consumption for a system, we can avoid changing our batteries to often and/or
use smaller batteries depending on the need for the given situation. Powering a predictive
maintenance system on battery furthermore enables opportunities for machines operating
on the move, such as agricultural machinery.

9.3
Healthcare
Not only does the approach for this project apply for predictive maintenance, but rather
embedded machine learning in general. Artificial intelligence and machine learning tech-
nology have made huge advancements the recent years, hence before unseen oppor-
tunities extending to the healthcare sector have appeared [31]. A study from Kakatiya
Institute of Technology and Science trained machine learning models with the purpose
of predicting heart and diabetes diseases based on pre-collected data from UCI Machine
Learning Repository1[32]. They conclude that the models were able to correctly predict
heart and diabetes diseases with 85 and 78 percent accuracy respectively. It is clear that,
if these diseases can be detected real time by e.g. monitoring blood or other parameters,
many severe consequences or even death can be avoided. One can imagine a small
device either mounted on the body or placed strategically by surgery can be used for run-
ning such a machine learning model. In cases like this, energy consumption is of great
concern since if a device is placed with a surgery, maximum battery life is crucial in order
to avoid recurring surgeries just for replacing the battery. Likewise, if a device is to be
carried around, a large battery would be a hassle, and therefore a battery as small as
possible would often be high priority for such devices, and referring to previous section,
small batteries often imply lower power consumption.

Conclusion
A proof of concept completely offline predictive maintenance solution running on a mi-
crocontroller was implemented. Support Vector Machine, Random Forest and Neural
Network models were trained on various data sets collected with different configurations
of sample rate, sample size and two different types of input vector, namely raw data sam-
ples and descriptive statistics of a collection of data points. It was found that that several
different machine learning algorithms were able to solve the problem with model accu-
racy as high as 99%. The Support Vector Machine models were struggling for the raw
data, but showed equally good performance as the other two methods for the statistical
input vector. The models varied a lot in size, and especially some of the RF models were
extremely large compared the the others, while the SVMs were superior in terms of size
being between 7 and almost 27000 times smaller than the other models. We saw how
model size were highly correlated with execution time on the microcontroller, and there-
fore, since all the models achieved the same accuracy, SVM were chosen as the best
model for our problem.
After settling on a model, a high power configuration with short execution time and a low
power configuration with longer execution time were compared. The low-power configura-
tion reduced the energy consumption with over 90% at the cost of roughly 70% increase
in running time. Several optimizations were proposed to the low power configuration in
order investigate how close we could bring the running time to that of the high power
configuration.
Great improvements were found in reducing I2C communication overhead when read-
ing data from the accelerometer, resulting in a reduction of the complete running time
of almost 50%. A significant part of the preprocessing phase were spent on sorting lists
of samples for x-, y- and z-axis, and therefore different sorting algorithms were imple-
mented and evaluated. Evaluations showed, that due to the relatively short lists, insertion
sort were preferred over both bubble sort and merge sort. Lastly in order to reduce model
size, feature selection was carried out, reducing the input space from 21 features to just
4 without suffering any drop in model accuracy. With just the remaining 4 features, plenty
of computations in the data preprocessing phase were eliminated having great impact on
the execution time. Furthermore, none of the 4 features were related to the x-axis, and
therefore time could saved in the data collection phase as well by only reading data from
y- and z-axis. With all these optimizations we were able to bring the total execution time of
the low power configuration down to 40% faster than the original high power configuration
while still maintaining the enormous reduction of energy consumption.
A very simple interface consisting of two LEDs located on the microcontroller is imple-
mented to illustrate the different states of the machine while operating. The solution pro-
posed in this project consists of three states, namely fully functional, service needed and
critical.
As intended with the given time frame this project works well as a proof of concept. Future
work should include extension of this project to a problem from the “real word”, in which
the observations and consideration stated in the thesis would also be valid, but may result
in a different outcome.
