Abstract
Biodiesel is a renewable fuel that could reduce societies dependency on fossil fuels while reducing
carbon emissions. The enzymatic synthesis of biodiesel is an established process that has gained much
popularity recently. Enzymes allow for a cleaner and more efﬁcient process that generates less waste and can
operate with low quality feedstocks. One problem with this is the presence of FFA in the substrate oils. FFAs
can be converted to FAME, but normally they are eliminated via resins, acid treatment or saponiﬁcation.
This reduces the yield of reaction and increases costs. Very little focus has been put in biodiesel processing
by enzymatic ways. The demanding speciﬁcations on biodiesel composition has made it so that FFA levels
have to be kept below 0.25%. The enzyme Candida antartica lipase B is able to esterify FFA in low water
concentrations, which are ideal to remove the remaining FFA in crude oil. Water is a by-product of the
reaction, so it affects the reaction rate if it accumulates in the Here we combine the enzyme CAL-B with
a bubble column reactor with an agitator, where water is continuously eliminated from the system into a
methanol reservoir by air bubbling. We reach within speciﬁcation levels of biodiesel within 6 hours of
processing at a enzyme concentration of 0.8% and close to underspeciﬁcation at enzymes concentration of
0.4%. Agitation was found to have a big impact in the reaction rate, specially at low FFA concentrations.
The reaction was found to be mass transfer limiting, as the reaction rate increased with increasing agitation
at the same enzyme dosage. These process can be used as an initial point to drive the future research on
processing of biodiesel with enzymes, and some conﬁgurations that could let to success are discussed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The ever-increasing need for additional energy, the depletion of fossil fuel reservois, and envinromental
concerns have stimulated a growing interest for renewable energy sources[6]. Fossil fuels are the main
source of energy currently used for transportation purposes. Fossil fuels are non renewable, are sources of
pollution through CO2 and cause serious econeomic concerns, makes countries energy dependant from one
another. To solve this environmental problems, Biodiesel has emerged as a promising fuel obtained from
renewable sources. Biodiesel is biodegradable, non-toxic and exhibits a higher cetane number as well as a
favourable emission proﬁle. It can also be used in the same engines as diesel, making it a suitable alternative
without the need of changing the current engines. In contrast, production costs are still high and the recent
reduction of fossil fuel prices has put emphasis on optimization of biodiesel processes.
Biodiesel is the mono alkyl ester of long fatty acid chains derived from vegetable oils or animal fats,
to be used in compression-ignition engines. Already in the 19th Century, Rudolf Diesel suggested the use
of plant oils as fuel for engines. These could not be used due to high viscosity, low power output, and low
volatility which will leave carbon deposits in the engine. Trans-esteriﬁcation or esteriﬁcation is the simplest
way to circumvent this problems. The free fatty acids (FFA) and tryacilglycerides (TAG) in animal and plant
oils are chemically reacted with a small chain alcohol, such as methanol, to produce alkyl esters, such as
Fatty Acid Methyl Ester (FAME). The reactions can be seen in ﬁgure 1 [15].
Figure 1.1: General esteriﬁcation reaction scheme of FFA with an alcohol to yield FAME
In order to be sold, biodiesel needs to meet retailer speciﬁcation demandsA. One such demand is that
commercial Biodiesel needs to have a FFA content below 0.25wt%. Current processes can attain a biodiesel
with up to 2% FFA left. This FFA needs to reduced and so far methods have included acid treatment or

1.1.1
Chemical Biodiesel production
As of today, Biodiesel is most commonly produced by chemical means[1]. Alkaline-catalyzed process
are the most widespread. In this reaction, high purity raw materials, such as vetable oils, are necessary. The
most commonly used catalyst are KOH, NaOH and sodium methoxide because of their relatively cheap
prise[? ]. Although KOH is preferred due to its easier downstream processing. These process presents a
very high conversion yield under mild conditions of temperature and pressure. The main drawback of the
alkaline process is its sensitivity to high FFA and water content. Fatty acids react with the alkaline catalyst
forming soaps as side products. One molecule of catalyst takes up to three molecules of FFA, which could
otherwise end up being FAME, reducing the overall yield. Alkaline catalysts are also very hygroscopic,
which decreases their activity if too much water is present. A level of FFA bellow 0.5% wt and a water
content of 0.1% wt is desirable for alkaline reaction. These makes it so that raw materials such as tallow,
greases or cooking oils are not suitable for alkaline production and will need pretreatment to esterify the
FFA and remove the water content. Both acid catalyst and enzymes can be used for pretreatment, in 1.1.1 an
example of a chemical process with acid pretreatment is shown. Due to soap formation, alkaline catalyst
cannot process FFA, so they are less desirable to handle low purity processes.
The second most commonly used process is the acid catalysis. Acids such as sulfuric, hydrocloric acid
and sulfonic acid are the most commonly employed. Acid is normally added in 0.5% to 1% molar ratio,
although some authors have used higher[8]. In spite of their insensitivity to to FFA content, acids are ignored
for the most part in industrial application due to their slower reaction rates and the corrosion to the processing
equipment. These two factors increase the cost of operation and are thus undesirable.

1.1.3
Lipase-catalyzed reaction
Lipases are carboxilic ester esterases (E.C. 3.1.1.3) with the capacity to catalyse both hydrolitic cleavage
and synthesis of ester bonds in glycerol esters. They are widespread enzymes found in many different species
and play a major role in lipid digestion and metabolism. Under physiological conditions, they catayse the
hydrolysis of carboxilic esters. Lipases are often found acting in oil-water interfases, and their functionality
has adapted to that[9].
There are two major kinds of lipases, interfacially and non-interfacially activated lipases. The former
present an increase of activity when attached to a water-oil interface. The latter do not present and increase,
which means they can be activated in solution.[3]. Lipases can also present regioselectivity in their activity,
which means that they will most likely catalyse a reaction in one of the chemical bonds more pronely than
in others. Non-speciﬁc lipases show no preference towards which esters bonds they attack, whereas most
lipases present a sn-1,3 speciﬁcicty.

Liquid CAL-B properties
Candida Antartica lipase B is an alkyl ester hydrolase (E.C. 3.1.1.3). CAL-B has no signiﬁcant homology
to any other known lipase and deviates from the consensus sequence of serine active site of other lipases [? ].
This could explain the different behaviour of this lipase, as it has a Ser-His-Asp catalytic triad and a quite
narrow entrance to the active site, which explain its great speciﬁcity. Nonetheless, Lipase B shows and αβ
structure motif, which is similar to other lipases.
3.1
Key parameters affecting lipase activity
There are several factors affecting the reaction rate of lipases and the yield obtained thorugh the reaction.
In this section the most important parameters are discussed, but there are several others that may affect the
reaction rate, such as soaps or impurities coming from the reaction. The focus of the report is on the usage
of a liquid lipase, but most of the literature studies have been performed in immobilized lipases, so some of
those investigations will be used. Liquid and immobilized lipases react differently to the reaction parameters,
but they have the same molecular structure, so some similarities can be expected. Also, it is important to
note that in the studied system, a factor can affect both the reaction conditions and the reactor set up, which
will be discussed further down in the report.
3.1.1
Temperature
Temperature has a double fold effect. On the one hand, it accelerates the reaction rate, as it increases
the rate constant and lowers the mass transfer limitations. On the other hand, as the temperature keeps on
increasing, a sharp decrease in the reaction rate is observed. Enzymes are proteins and are thus subject to
denaturalization. Furthermore, the presence of inactive enzymes will further decrease the reaction rate, as
the presense of the inactive enzyme blocks the interface to the active enzyme[16].
It is thus important to maintain the temperature within an optimum that maximizes reaction rate while
keeping denaturation low.

3.1.2
Water content
Water concentration is an integral factor in the esterﬁcation of of FFA. Water has an impact in both the
activity of the lipase and the thermodynamic equilibrium of the esteriﬁcation reaction[17].
While using a soluble form of lipase, it is important that a minimun of water is surrounding the enzyme to
keep its three dimensional structure. Given the case of a too-low water content, the lipase would denaturazi
and enzymatic activity will be lost. Additionally, the water content of the reaction will greatly affect the
yield of the reaction. When trying to eliminate an already small percentage of FFA, water content of the
reaction will have to be kept within a minimum, as water is a product of the reaction. Finally, it is important
to consider that soluble forms of enzymes come with a very high water dosage, and thus the addition of
additional enzyme will have an effect on the water content of the reaction media.
All in all, water content is a complex compromise between having as little water as possible to push the
reaction to its thermodynamic limit, and having enough water to maintain the enzyme structure.

3.1.3
Methanol concentration
The molar ratio of Methanol has an important effect of the reaction yield. In theory, 1 mol of Methanol
sufﬁces to react with 1 mol of FFA, to produce 1 mol of FAME and 1 mol of Water. Despite this, an excess
of methanol is sometimes needed in order to drive the reaction equilibrium towards products. A molar ratio
of 3 to 1 has been suggested[18].
An excess of methanol, however, can have a detrimental effect on the reaction yield, as it can deactivate
the enzyme. The alteration of the enzymatic structure due to short chain alcohols can be explained by the
strip off of of water molecules from the hydratation shells of proteins. The distance between lipases is thus
reduced, as consequences of the reduction of the hydration shell. Lipases may precipitate due to higher
intermolecular interactions, such as Van der Waals or polar atraction. This could mean that higher enzyme
concentrations are more susceptible to alcohols than lower ones [18].
Overall, a high Methanol concentration is important to improve the yield of the reaction, but its concen-
tration has to be kept as low as possible to prevent denaturalization.
3.1.4
Enzyme concentration
The reaction rate and the yield increase with increasing enzyme concentration until a certain amount of
enzyme is reached, in which case the reaction rate remains constant. As a consequence, there is an optimal
reaction concentration. These optimal concentration can be explained by the absorption of lipase into the
aqueous-oily interface. In the beginning, the reaction rate will increase linearly, as no lipase is initially bound
to the interface[18]. As the absorption progresses, the concentration of lipase in the bulk of the reaction and
on the interface will reach an equilibrium. A further increase in the enzyme concentration of the bulk has no
effect on the reaction rate, because it cannot be absorbed in the interface. It can be argued that enhanced
mixing will make the oil drops in the system smaller, thus increasing the interfacial area of the reaction. In
this case, mixing capacity and enzyme concentration will be linked, as the optimal concentration of enzyme
will increase with better mixing[19].

3.2.1
System description
The bubble column in this report is where the esteriﬁcation of free fatty acids (FFA) takes place. The
esteriﬁcation occurs in a media which is mostly crude fatty acid methyl esters (FAME) with a low water(<1%)
and enzyme content. The reaction mixture forms a two phase liquid-liquid dispersion system with an aqueous
phase distributed in a hydrophobic oily phase. Gas in the form of dry methanol-saturated nitrogen is dispersed
in the liquid-liquid mixture, which effectively it means that the system under study is a triple phase system.
The system can be seen in Figure 3.2.
Nitrogen gas is pumped from the methanol reservoir to the reaction bulk. There, gas bubbles transfer
methanol towards the aqueous phase while water is removed from the water/glycerol/methanol mixture. This
air is recirculated towards the methanol reservoir, which will entrap the water from the nitrogen and will
resaturate the gas with methanol the nitrogen. Meanwhile, in the reactor, methanol will be transferred from
the aqueous phase to the oily phase, as it will be consumed by the reaction.

CHAPTER 6
Final discussion
In light of the results shown in the previous section, it is clear that there is an advantage to using this
setup for biodiesel reﬁning. Temperature affects both the reaction rate and the drying rate but, in this case,
45ºC were always selected because it was the only setup that allowed for enough water to be dried from
the system. Approximately 4000 ppm of water has to be removed from the system, from the reaction and
the set-up. The water amount in the FAME can change drastically depending on handling, as biodiesel is a
hygroscopic substance. It is important to make sure that the water content of the biodiesel is known when
calculating drying times. Temperature also has an effect on the equilibrium of methanol within the reactor.
At lower temperatures, it is expected that more methanol stays in the tank, while boosting the reaction rate.
These is quite a big problem of this set-up: the methanol content is always low because of the drying system.
An alternative would be to add pulsed feeding of methanol or use another drying system that did not force
evaporation of methanol, such as molecular sieve.
Methanol can also have a negative effect in the reaction, if it reaches critical deactivation concentrations.
The reaction rate is bound to improve at higher methanol concentrations. Deactivation can also happen by
other means than methanol. Deactivation of lipases in air-liquid interfaces has been reported before [25]. At
high air ratios, the enzyme is more prone to be deactivated by bursting of bubbles, specially if foams are
formed. This might discourage the implementation of high air ﬂows and agitations, as a somewhat gentle
conditions seem more beneﬁcial towards the reaction rate. Pulsed aeration might be an alternative. Allowing
the reaction to react while water accumulates and the removing the water with a high air ﬂow could be a
beneﬁcial approach to safe air. Agitation has been shown to be a key parameter in the esteriﬁcation reaction.
With agitation, the usage of up to ten times less air ﬂow has been shown. Agitation also provides better
mixing to the reaction components, which has been shown to positively correlate with reaction rate, specially
at low FFA concentrations.
For industrial application, there are mainly three different applications possible. The ﬁrst is the usage of
the current set-up. The drawback of this set-up is that the methanol concentration is low and there is more
air usage than necessary. Nonetheless, its application is simple and no manual handling of the apparatus is
needed during operation. Secondly, the usage of an alternative way of drying could be used. Molecular sieve
and vacuum drying could be used. It is important to take into account that these new drying techniques will
have an impat also in the reaction, so some study will be needed before implementation. Lastly, a 6 tank
approach could be used, as seen in Figure 6.1. The concept plays with the aforementioned pulsed feeding
of methanol and pulsed drying. In one reactor, methanol could be added, allow the reaction to progress,
and drying next. These cycle of addition of methanol, reaction and drying could be repeated and done as a
continuous process where the biodiesel would be continuously processed.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
The present report focuses on the development of a process that can cost efﬁciently reﬁne crude biodiesel
into within speciﬁcation biodiesel. First, a setup was built. The setup included bafﬂes, which allow for a
better mixing as they prevent axial ﬂow. The setup also included an agitator in the form of a propeller, which
has been shown to increase the mixing of the reactants greatly. Finally, the reactor setup had a inlet in the
bottom, which was used to pump air through. For the methanol reservoir, it was necessary to install a sparger
in order to meet the necessary mass transfer between nitrogen gas and liquid methanol. Temperature was
also measured, as the difference in temperature in both tanks affects the equilibrium of water and methanol
of both systems.
Once the system was build, the ﬁrst necessary step to solve the problem was to understand the reaction
rates of liquid CAL-B. The ﬁrst thing that was noticed was the huge effect that water content had in the
reaction. So big was this effect, that the system initially conceived to understand the reaction rates was
deemed inefﬁcient. Water from the moisturised air around the water bath was entering the reaction as
samples were taken, which greatly affected the outcome of the reaction. In spite of this, the 20 ml reaction
were not a complete failure, as they could underline the effects of different reaction parameters in the reaction
rate, albeit there was a lot of lack of ﬁt present in the model. 4 mL plastic tubes were used instead. The main
goal of the reaction in 4 mL tubes was to understand the effect of the methanol concentration in the reaction,
as it was found to be not signiﬁcant in the 20 ml reaction. The effect of methanol was quite large at different
enzyme concentrations, and thus methanol was decided to be investigated in the 1 litre reactor. 4 ml tubes
are not ideal models for the reaction, as the mixing between oil and water phases is not very good.
Drying was an important part of the report. The system was shown to be able to remove enough water
from the bulk reaction. That is, both the water generated from the reaction and the water added to the system
from the reactants could be removed in a shorter time than the reaction time. Drying was not a limiting
factor, but it is clear that it is then a waste of energy to continuously dry. Other systems to dry or a pulsed
drying system could have been used instead. Air ﬂow rate also has an impact on mixing, though, so that is a
not straightforward approach. The parameters of operation for the reaction were calculated from empirical
correlations found in the literature. One big assumption was assuming that the system would be turbulent.
These let do underestimating the power input necessary to mix the reactor properly. It was shown in the
discussion that with a bigger reactor with the same proportions, the system would indeed be turbulent, so
most likely the P/V relation will hold true while scaling up.
The reactions in the 1 litre vessel showed that at 0.8vv% of enzyme, the conversion goal could be attained.
The system could be either mass transfer limiting or enzyme kinetic limiting. In this work, it was shown that
it was indeed mass transfer that holds a greater effect in the reaction. The reaction rate was improved when
using higher mixing rates, at all enzyme concentrations. On top of this, the effects model shows a greater

inﬂuence of mixing rate when the FFA content is low. At low FFA concentrations, the enzyme has it harder
to ﬁnd the reactant, while at higher FFA mixing is relatively less important because molecules can meet each
other more easily.

CHAPTER 8
Future work
A better understanding of the mechanism of the Lipase CAL-B is necessary in order to tackle better
the problems that can be faced during operation. If the mechanism of action of CAL-B is understood, then
it would be easier to predict its behaviour based on the different phases compositions’. For instance, the
knowledge of whether the methanol is taken from the aqueous or the oily phase could help workout better
the amount of methanol necessary for the most efﬁcient reaction.
Similarly, a better understanding of the operational limits of CAL-B could play a key role in optimizing
its performance. Denaturalization of the enzyme by temperature or methanol are known deactivation factors,
but very little data is known on how much methanol is necessary to deactivate the enzyme, at what ratio
will it be deactivated and the interaction of different factors in the deactivation, such as high methanol
concentration and high temperature. In the same line of this suggestion, performing experiments at higher
temperatures such as 50ºC could increase the rate of reaction and shed some light on CAL-B’s operational
limits.
So far, all the mathematical models available for biodiesel synthesis have been focusing on the ﬁrst part
of the reaction, the conversion of oils into FAME. Very little focus has been put into optimizing a model
for the lowest FFA and glycerides conversion. The knowledge that has to be gathered in order to properly
build a model will deﬁnitely help further the understanding of the process. A simple model for the different
parameters could help set up the most optimal reaction for each incoming biodiesel batch. With different
raw materials, the properties of biodiesel change slightly and the ﬂexibility to cope with different process
demands will be key in industrial application.
For industrial application, a better understanding of the drying process is instrumental. It was out of
the scope of the project to do a lot of experimentation with variation within the methanol reservoir. The
reason for this was that both of the systems (methanol reservoir and reactor) where affecting each other, and
by focusing on one at a time, optimization is easier. Regardless, it would be a very important step in the
development of the process to have data on how the state of the methanol reservoir affects the reaction. Data
on MeOH and H2O concentrations in the tank and their effect on reaction rate and yield is very helpful,
as the timing for methanol replacement could be assessed. The drying efﬁciency of the system could be
plotted against its water concentration, which make it simpler to determine the behaviour of the drying
system. Alternatively, other ways of drying could be implemented. Vacuum drying or molecular sieve could
be potential alternatives, as using a methanol reservoir has its risks because it is highly toxic and ﬂammable.
All in all, the direction of the research should head towards understanding the gaps in knowledge of the
reaction system, while trying to implement the most efﬁcient and well-understood drying system.

Water determination by automated Coloumetric Karl Fischer  
 
Operation procedure of the Coulometric Karl Fischer (KF) system equipped 
with an automatic Oven Sample Processor.   
 
Bioenergy Opportunities RD dept. 433 (NZBA). 
 
The KF equipment determines the water content in a sample by the principle of 
an oven method. Heat is applied to release the moisture in a sample as water 
vapour. The water vapour is then transferred to a measuring cell in a steam of 
gas. Here, the moisture determination is carried out coulometrically. The cou-
lometric titration quantifies the water content based on a chemical reaction in-
volving iodine stoichiometrically equal to the water amount. The iodine needed 
for titration is generated directly in the electrolyte by an electric current (gen-
erator electrode). A dramatically lowered voltage difference in the presence of 
minimal quantities of free iodine is used to determine the endpoint of the titra-
tion (indicator electrode). 
 
The Coloumetric KF system is ideal for analyzing liquid or solid samples that 
release their moisture only reluctantly or by heat. Typically examples are crude 
oils and oil products, plastics, pharmaceuticals, food stuffs and freeze-dried 
products. The KF is implemented to analyse fats and oils samples as well as 
immobilized lipase products but can also be used for other applications.  
 
Please note: Samples that contain aldehydes, ketones or large amounts of 
solvents can harm the Oven sample processor unit and must be injected di-
rectly into the columetric cell.

Free Fatty Acid determination by NaOH titration 
 
To determine the Free Fatty Acid (FFA) content in oils and fats samples.   
 
Bioenergy opportunities RD dept. 433 (NZBA).  
 
The method can be used for quantification of FFA in oils and fats samples 
such as crude and refined vegetable oils, animal fats and biodiesel. The 
method has been optimized to quantify FFA content (0-100%) during the con-
densation reaction of FFA (C18:1) + glycerol → Triglyceride (TG) + H2O.  
 
The method is based on the AOCS Ca 5a-40 Official Method. Reagent and 
sample volumes are down scaled compared to the original method and alkali 
concentrations adjusted accordingly. 
A known mass of oil is dissolved in 2-propanol and phenolphthalein is added 
as indicator. The free fatty acids are then neutralized by titration with a NaOH-
solution until the first pale permanent pink color. 
Note: Be aware that ‘all acids’ represented in the oil samples are included in 
the titration result. Hence, the calculated FFA content can be affected by the 
oil quality (determined by the processing it has been exposed to). Citric acid 
may for instance be present in gums in noticeable amounts (originating from 
the acidic degumming process).

APPENDIX C
Central composite design
During the initial preparation of the experimentation, it was clear by the huge amount of variables that
were present in the system, that it would provide too much data to be studied individually. Of all the variables
presented before, four variables were selected: glycerol concentration, enzyme concentration, Temperature
and initial water level. These variables were set between two levels and a Central Composite Design was
applied . The Central Composite Design is based on the full factorial design and adds central and axial
points for more consistency in the results. For a number of variables k and m central points, the total number
of runs is set to be n = 2k + 2k + m, this amount of runs is generally lower than a 3k that would be needed
to obtain a full factorial design.
In the case of our experiment:
CCD runs:n = 24 + 2 ∗4 + 2 = 26runsFull factorial design:n = 34 = 81runs
(C.1)
It is thus that CCD is used, to reduce the number of runs while still getting enough data to get a second order
equation that can predict which of the variables have the bigger effect on the conversion of the FFA. It is
worth noting, that the purpose of this experiment was to screen the variables and see which ones should
be the focus on the research in order to obtain the desired speciﬁcations in biodiesel. as estimated to be
around 5 to 10 mins. Or as little as it was detectable. The program JMP (registered) was used with the
aforementioned variables, levels and number of runs.
