Abstract
This project revolves around the topic of hearing aid microphone inlet design. It is an
important topic because there is currently a transition in the industry from electret
microphones towards MEMS microphones. With this transition the risk of hearing aids
being overdriven by ultrasound is increased, due to the reason that MEMS microphones
have a higher resonance frequency.
MEMS microphone is a standard design.
Hearing aid manufacturers have to work
around it. Therefore, the issue should be ﬁxed before sound reaches the microphone,
by modifying the inlet.
As such, the aim is to explore diﬀerent designs that attenuate ultrasounds and explore
methods that can simulate these designs.
These methods are lumped element method, transmission matrix method and ﬁnite
element method. Additionally, optimisations will be done on lumped element models
and on transmission matrix models.
These modeled methods are compared between themselves and the advantages and
limitations of each are discussed with examples. A few optimised designs were used to
compare between the ﬁnite element method and the transmission matrix method with
expressions that include losses, which is called Stinson’s model throughout the project.
Some of the ﬁnal optimised designs are candidates that have the potential of ﬁxing the
issue.
Additionally, comparisons are made between the FEM and Stinson’s model where the
results show that Stinson’s models are under-calculating the damping in the systems
when comparing the peaks of resonances, with roughly 10 dB across a range of 3 diﬀerent
geometries. Taking this into consideration, Stinson’s model is still a good method as it
is able to calculate the frequencies of the resonances with very low errors.

0.1
Ultrasound issue
Introduction
This project focuses on the Frequency Response (FR) of acoustic systems coupled to Micro-
electromechanical System (MEMS) microphones used in hearing aids. The purpose of the
project is to study the FR of the microphone combined with its acoustic inlet/duct in order
to be able to understand how the inlet design is aﬀecting the performance of a hearing
aid device. With the gained knowledge, a method for ﬁnding an optimal design shall be
developed. The tools used in the study will range from Lumped Element Method, Two-Port
models to Numerical Methods.
0.1
Ultrasound issue
Hearing Instrument / Hearing Aids (HIs) have become progressively smaller along the time-
line.
A few of the challenges are size reduction and increasing the Signal to noise ratio
(SNR) while not compromising on other characteristics of the devices. MEMS microphones
have been recently introduced in HI because of their small dimensions compared to previ-
ously used Electret Condenser Microphone (ECM). MEMS microphones have a light weight
diaphragm which results in a larger resonance peak that also occurs at higher frequencies
compared to ECM. The MEMS microphones do output a larger SNR compared to the ECM
counterpart. Still, the compromise is that the frequency response of the MEMS microphone
is larger at higher frequencies, as well as at ultrasound frequencies. Thus, there is a larger
chance to over-saturate the ampliﬁer of the HI with a loud ultrasound (f.x. automatic doors
or cars that use ultrasound sources for detecting objects in proximity) such that Intermod-
ulation Distortion (IMD) would occur. Due to IMD the ultrasound can fold down into the
audible range such that noise can be perceived by HI users. Therefore, there is an interest in
modeling the system, in order to ﬁnd how diﬀerent design parameters do inﬂuence the FR.
Not much can be done to the MEMS microphone as it is an industry standard, except for
programming the in-built digital ﬁlter, but this is out of the scope of this project. Thus, the
only left area of study remains to be the inlet, that is guiding the sound waves towards the
MEMS microphone.
0.2
MEMS microphone market
The MEMS microphone market is growing and by year 2024 it is expected to reach 2.8 billion
US dollars. The demand is expected to grow to approximately 13.3 billion units per year

0.3
Delimitation
The scope of this project is to reduce the FR in the range of 20 kHz < f < 50 kHz and keep
as ﬂat of a response below 10 kHz as possible. A bigger priority is given to frequencies in the
range of 30 kHz < f < 40 kHz, as these frequencies proved to be more problematic, based on
Demant’s experience. There are also some set limits in terms of FR used by Demant which
were used in this project as guidelines, see Figure 51 in Appendix. Another delimitation
is that the MEMS microphone was modeled using Lumped elements.
The model was a
simpliﬁed version, based on a standard design provided by Demant. In order to ﬁnd out
which inlet design would be best suitable, some physical limits had been set. The total
physical straight tube length had to not exceed 2 mm and the total volume of cavities had
to not exceed 10−9 m3 (10 mm3), because these limits could have helped at determining
based on the limited space which system design performs best. These limits were enforced
during the design phases. However, during the analysis, these limits were broken for study
purposes.

1.3.1
Basics of the method
An acoustic Lumped Element Model (LEM) can be represented using electrical components.
The basic passive resistor, capacitor and inductor components that connect with ideal wires
into an idealised network can be considered as analogies to speciﬁc acoustic components.
An analogy in this case can be explained as 2 diﬀerent components from diﬀerent ﬁelds, (f.x.
electronics and acoustics) that show same behaviour across a range of frequencies. Analogies
are not limited only to components. The basic electric variables that drive and ﬂow through
the components are the Voltage and Current, which have Pressure and Volume ﬂow as their
respective acoustic analogies. [3, ch.3] For example, an inductor is analogous to an acoustic
mass, which can be seen in an acoustic tube. For other analogies see Table 1.

1.4.1
Assumptions on using Transfer Matrix Method
• The system is considered one dimensional, meaning that no planar modes are con-
sidered (planar modes are in the plane perpendicular to the propagation direction of
waves). One way of verifying if a duct is not in the one-dimensional frequency region
is by using Equation 5.
• The system is considered adiabatic (due to disregard of losses).
• The shape of the cavity does not inﬂuence the response of the system, only it’s volume
does.
1.5
Stinson’s model - expressions of acoustic losses in narrow tubes
As this project is focused around small sized HIs, it is decided to use Stinson’s visco-thermal
losses model which can cover a large range of frequencies and tube radii. The method is
described in Michael R. Stinson’s paper called "The propagation of plane sound waves in nar-
row and wide circular tubes, and generalization to uniform tubes of arbitrary cross-sectional
shape" [6]. It is important to consider visco-thermal losses as they signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the
acoustic response in narrow gap regions, where the duct dimensions are comparable to the
thickness of a viscous boundary layer, which can be found using [7, Eq.2.13]
dν =

where µ is the dynamic viscosity, ρ is the mass density of air and ω is the angular frequency.
As an example, at 4 kHz with room conditions dν = 3.5.10−2 mm, which is 35% of a 0.1 mm
tube radius.

1.6
MATLAB Solver-Based Nonlinear Optimization
As mentioned in MATLAB’s documentation, solver-based nonlinear optimisation can be
used to "Solve nonlinear minimization and semi-inﬁnite programming problems in serial or
parallel using the solver-based approach". [9]
Setting up an optimiser requires a few steps. First is choosing the solver of the optimiser.
As an example, "fmincon", is a nonlinear programming solver. It is able to ﬁnd the local
minimum, of a function that can contain multiple variables and constraints.
The next step is the creation of an objective function, that shall be minimised by fmincon.
The objective function, shall contain all of the important aspects that are wished to be opti-
mised. If there are multiple objectives to be included in the objective function, it is possible
to include them by summing the objectives into the single objective function. Generally, it
is recommended to normalise the expressions, as it gives each objective equal weighting. On
the other hand it is also possible to give more weighting to individual objectives.
The constraints, can be deﬁned as equality and/or as inequality expressions. Also, it is
possible to deﬁne lower and upper boundary for each of the parameters.
Finding a global minimum can be quite challenging if done manually. In MATLAB exists a
tool called ’multistart’, which is designed for calculating multiple minimums, which output
the set of parameters with the most minimised objective function. ’fmincon’ can be used
as a solver as well. It does so by randomising the initial parameters within the constrained
boundaries, to a number that the user can deﬁne. Thus, it is possible to have f.x. 10000

initial starting conditions with randomised parameters.
To make this process more eﬃcient, it is possible to use parallel computing.
It is also
possible to deﬁne the limit of the step size to be bigger in the ’multistart’ process. Step size,
is the numerical amount that is changed in parameters with each iteration. The closer the
optimiser gets to a minimum solution, the smaller the step size becomes. Once the limit of
the step size is reached the optimiser stops.
It is recommended to normalise the parameters as well, especially when there is a big nu-
merical diﬀerence between the parameters. Having a large limit for step size makes it fast to
ﬁnd the global lowest. Small step size makes possible to solve for more accurate solutions.
After, the solution of the ’multistart’, can be fed as initial parameters into ’fmincon’. A
much lower step size limit can be deﬁned and solved with ’fmincon’ in order to obtain an
accurate global minimum.

Finite element method
Finite Element Method (FEM) is a numerical method that can be used for ﬁnite element
analyses on multiple physical phenomenons. One of the phenomenon that is related to the
topic of this project is the wave propagation, which can mathematically be described by a
partial diﬀerential equation. The wave equation is [10]
∇2p = 1
c2
∂2p
∂t2 ,
(34)
where p is the instantaneous pressure, c is the speed of sound, t is the time, and ∇2 is the
Laplacian expression responsible for the coordinate system.
The partial diﬀerential wave equation is the strong form, also called the diﬀerential form.
This form states strict conditions that are to be obtained at every domain location. On the
other hand, the weak form, also called the integral form states conditions that are to be
obtained by approximations. [10]
The weak form is what is solved numerically. It is done so by discretizing the weak form
into sets of matrices. More speciﬁcally, discretisation implies the act of dividing the domain
into elements and nodes. Also, it involves approximating the solution within the element
using shape functions. After which the element matrices and vectors deﬁning material prop-
erties and boundary conditions are assembled, which then are solved using matrix algebra.
Generally the more reﬁned the mesh is, the more accurate results are obtained. [10]

1.7
Finite element method
When working with small scaled geometries, it is non-trivial to consider the thermo-viscous
eﬀect and the conductivity of heat in the medium.
The acoustic ﬁeld, creates thermal
and viscous boundary layers next to the walls. Within the boundary layers the losses are
signiﬁcantly large, due to shear and thermal conduction. The thickness of boundary layers in
air medium varies depending on the frequency. See f.x. the expression for viscous boundary
layer thickness in Equation 23. [10]
COMSOL is a solver and multi-physics simulation software used for ﬁnite element analy-
sis. Two modules that are the most relevant to the topic of this project are going to be
further discussed. One of them, called "Thermoviscous Acousics", solves the full linearized
Navier-Stokes, continuity and energy conservation equations simultaneously. This module
can provide the most accurate results but at the cost of being extremely computationally
demanding. The second module called "Pressure acoustics", contains a boundary condition
called "Thermoviscous Boundary layer impedance (BLI)". This boundary condition is con-
tributing to the model with considerations of the thermal and viscous eﬀects next to the
wall. It should be applied only in cases when the boundary layers are not overlapping. The
biggest advantage of using this module is because it requires signiﬁcantly less computational
resources. [11]
Due to the large gradient of particle velocity in the boundary layer, it is important to allocate
enough elements such that the losses can be accurately calculated. Comsol recommends to
use in between 5 to 10 elements within the boundary layer thickness. [11]

Stinson’s models
Stinson’s model was based on the transfer matrix method, with additional equations that
accounted for losses. If the validity boundaries are respected (see sub-chapter 1.5.1), Stin-

be included without end corrections, as the model calculated the end corrections diﬀerently,
based on Equation 31 and Equation 33. The inﬁnitely baﬄed end correction was calculated
the same way as in previous models (see Equation 9). Another diﬀerence (compared to
TMM) was in the input parameters of the Stinson’s model, where an additional parameter
that deﬁnes the radius of the cavity in the HR (that is if the model includes a HR at all)
was included.
Next, the model calculates the eﬀective density and bulk modulus of each sub-system inde-
pendently, as described in chapter 1.5.2.
As an example, the same set of parameters as in Table 2 were used. Because this system
contains a HR, the eﬀective input impedance of it was implemented as described in chapter

159 series) of solutions, large enough, such that no diﬀerence can be observed with a larger
number of solutions.

way as in the TMM, see Equation 37.

in the objective function responsible for the target of HR’s resonance has less weighting.
Thus, there is a higher chance for the resonance to deviate from the target. It is possible
to tune the resonance to a large range of frequencies, however, the higher the resonance of
HR is, the higher the chance is to obtain a very short tube, or small cavity, where the end
corrections are dominant. Because the methods of calculating the end corrections are not
accurate, it is important to aim for designs that have larger components compared to the
end corrections, such that the end corrections cannot dominate the response.

Discussion
Throughout this project diﬀerent methods of calculating the FR of acoustic systems com-
bined with a lumped element model of a MEMS microphone have been explored. Starting
with the simplest method which was Lumped element method and progressing to more ad-
vanced models which were Transmission matrix method and Finite element method, gave a
great insight into some of the existing tools’ capabilities. Additionally, Stinson’s model was
implemented on top the the transmission matrix method. Transmission matrix method is
essentially the engine, while Stinson’s expressions (see theory chapter 1.5) are including the
damping in narrow acoustic systems.
When a new method was studied, it was compared to the previously studied, less accurate
method. Thus, a trial to validate the methods was made. At the same time it was possible to
exploit the methods to the point where the less accurate models would show their limitations.
For example, the lumped element method is limited to simulate models at frequencies the
wavelength of which should be much larger than the physical dimensions of the components.
Also, another limitation of the lumped element method is the incapability to obtain the
resonances of a stand-alone tube (as can be seen in the results shown in chapter 3.1).
Another example is the transmission matrix method which on its own is limited due to it
not accounting for the losses in a system. Still, by combining it with Stinson’s expressions
it is anyways limited to geometries that can be assembled from simpliﬁed shapes like tubes.
Also, during the comparison against the FEM model (see chapter 6.4) it was possible to
observe another limitation that the transmission matrix has which is due to it being one
dimensional.
Comparing FEM results to Stinson’s models results it was concluded that Stinson’s models
are under-calculating the damping in the systems when comparing the peaks of resonances
with roughly 10 dB across a range of 3 diﬀerent geometries. On the other side it is a quite
good method for calculating the resonances with very low errors.
Final method, the FEM, is the top accurate method used in this project.
Still, it does
have limitations as well. These limitations are based on how accurately the problem can be
deﬁned. Softwares like Comsol, containing multiphysics modules makes it easier to tackle
this kind of issue. Another big limitation is the computational cost. It is a luxury to be able
to solve the full linearised Navier-Stokes equation throughout a multitude of frequencies.
This is one of the challenges engineers have to face, and use this tool in a limited amount.
Page 63 of 67
==PAGE:70==
This limitation is also the main reason why engineers are motivated to look for alternatives
like analytic expressions.
During the FEM modelling part of the project it was quite diﬃcult to obtain high quality
elements in the mesh, while using the Comsol’s "Boundary Layers" tool, especially around
the discontinuous surfaces, f.x. around the tube to HR saddle like connection. It could be
interesting to explore diﬀerent meshing techniques for obtaining accurate boundary layer
calculations in more complex geometries.
The obvious way to test the validity of an FEM would be to compare the FEM results to a
measurement. This comes with another set of challenges. One typical challenge is trying to
avoid as much noise as possible. Also, test subjects have imperfections which are important
to identify and are most of the times impossible to implement in FEM.
One remark has to be mentioned about the microphone inclusion in the FEM models. In
this project, it was chosen to simply attach the "Lumped port" boundary condition to the
entire output face of the acoustic system. For more accurate results, a coupling would have
to be modelled with the size matching to the port size of the MEMS microphone.
Besides that, the aforementioned models were combined with optimisation tools which were
set-up to look for better designs.
This gave as well an insight into the limitations and
advantages of the studied systems (see the systems in Figure 9(a) to Figure 13(a)). It was
found out that systems consisting of series of tubes connected to cavities do perform well,
as they act as low pass ﬁlters, but they also do take the most amount of space. Also, it
was deduced that there must be an optimal amount of resonators to get a good response
and increasing the amount of resonators does not always improve the response in conﬁned
spaces.
During the optimisation process for each of the models, the weights had to be adjusted in an
iterative manner, to guide the optimiser to obtain better responses. This is a cumbersome
part in the process because the weights are adjusted based on guessing and feedback from
the results.
At the same time it was important to make sure that the constraints are working as they
should and that they are not over-constraining the model. Over-constraining the model
means applying too many constraints that can conﬂict with each other and not give enough
freedom for the optimiser to reach the minimised solution. An example of an over-constraint
would be for a cylindrical cavity that is set to have a larger radius than a neighbouring
component, while the length is restricted based on the lengths of end corrections and while
Page 64 of 67
==PAGE:71==
the volume has to be under a certain limit. In this case there is one degree of freedom only,
the radius of the cavity. In case the volume of the cavity is constrained to be too low, the
minimum radius constraint would conﬂict and as such the model would be over-constrained.
After it was decided to stop the optimisations, the designs were analysed and compared.
Some designs were better than others, but there was not a clear winner, due to the reason that
compromises had to be made when choosing a design over another. For example Stinson’s
model 1 and 2 optimised designs have better response in the high range of the ultrasound
region compared to Stinson’s model 3 optimised result which has a better response in the
lower ultrasound range, while also taking less space. Last but not least, Stinson’s model 4
which has the best response in the focused (30 - 40 kHz) ultrasound range while having the
worst response in the range between 1 kHz to 10 kHz.
Regarding the manufacturing of the designs encountered in this project, it can be said that
it might be a challenging task depending on the methods used. Simple shapes like the 3rd
and 4th order designs could be possible to manufacture with the current methods used by
Demant. But, the saddle like shapes, where 2 tube connect in parallel, would probably be
best to be 3D printed with SLA method for example. SLA method provides smooth surface
and would ensure high tolerances with ±0.15% dimensional tolerance and with a lower limit
of ±0.01 mm. [13]
Also, manufacturing the design with quarter-wave resonators could be quite challenging to
achieve with methods like even 3D-printing (due to tube radii of 0.1 mm), considering that
space is limited and the resonators have to ﬁll as little volume in most probably some kind
of spiralling way.
At the late stage of reporting it was found out that in the FEM model discussed in chapter 6.4,
was an error. The lumped port responsible for coupling the acoustic part to the microphone
was not set correctly.

Conclusion
Throughout the project, the author got to learn in more depth about the lumped element
method, transmission matrix method, transmission matrix method with Stinson’s expres-
sions that account for losses and ﬁnite element method. Tackling these methods in this
manner did leave a good impression of the advantages and the limitations of the aforemen-
tioned methods, as they were used in a progressive manner from more simple and limited
methods to more complex and accurate ones. The most accurate method that was used
as a reference in order to validate the results of the other methods, was the ﬁnite element
method. Also, the author got for the ﬁrst time to learn how to use COMSOL.
The project helped to get a good impression of how companies are currently dealing with
issues similar to the ones that were tackled in the project.
This project was an opportunity for the author to use advanced optimisation algorithms and
to learn how to apply them on actual problems present in the hearing aid industry.
For future works, it can be recommended to explore more the design with quarter-wave
resonators. One idea would be to investigate the optimal amount of resonators.
Another suggestion for future works would be to investigate the HR connected to a main
wave-guide tube, and explore the possibility of setting up the model to obtain a longer neck
and smaller cavity, such that the resonance of the HR could be kept at the desired target,
and such that the length corrections would have less impact on the response. If transfer
matrix method with Stinson’s expressions would be used to calculate the response of such a
design, it could be interesting to explore how far away from the microphone the HR should
be placed, such that Stinson’s model could accurately calculate the response.
Another idea relevant would be to replicate the optimised geometries and measure their
response. Probably the most feasible way would be to 3D-print in resin, for example using
the SLA method. Also, it would have to be taken into account that resin is not a completely
sound hard material, therefore, the material properties would have to be implemented in the
FEM for proper comparison.
Another future works suggestion would be to make use of the shape optimisation tool avail-
able in Comsol and explore more complex shapes of acoustic systems.
Finally, the purpose of the project was to study the FR of the microphone combined with
acoustic inlet in order to be able to understand how the inlet design is aﬀecting the perfor-

an electro-acoustic system by optimising the inlet design.
