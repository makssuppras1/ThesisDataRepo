I
Summary
This work investigates the fabrication of strain-induced, site-controlled quantum dot structures
using pre-patterning via high-resolution electron beam lithography, followed by the controlled
self-assembly of quantum dots within the Stranski-Krastanov growth regime using Metalorganic
Vapour-Phase Epitaxy (MOVPE). The objective was to achieve deterministic quantum dot place-
ment with optimised uniformity and reproducibility, crucial for scalable quantum- and optoelec-
tronic applications.
The first part of this thesis establishes the theoretical framework necessary to understand quantum
dot behavior and formation. It begins with an introduction to quantum confinement principles
and the effects of strain in epitaxial systems. We present the fundemental quantum effects that
make quantum dots viable for photonics, mechanisms guiding self-assembly, particularly within
the Stranski-Krastanov growth mode, and provide insight into how strain relaxation drives quan-
tum dot nucleation. The thesis then explores deterministic site-controlled growth approaches,
highlighting the challenges of transitioning from stochastic self-assembly to precise spatial con-
trol.
The second part of this work focuses on the experimental realisation of site-controlled quan-
tum dots through nanofabrication and epitaxial growth. The process flow is detailed, including
resist-coating, electron beam lithography exposure, etching and epitaxial growth using MOVPE.
A series of growth experiments were conducted to refine the fabrication strategy, with specific
attention given to precursor dynamics during the MOVPE growth. Finally, the fabricated struc-
tures were characterised using optical microscopy, scanning electron microscopy, and atomic
force microscopy to evaluate quantum dot morphology and spatial distribution for various pat-
tern geometries.
The findings of this work contribute to the development of scalable site-controlled quantum dots,
essential for applications in quantum photonics.
While site-control was achieved to some extent, further optimisation of planarisation and etching
processes is required to enhance reproducibility. The insights gained from this research provide
a foundation for refining growth techniques and improving fabrication methodologies for future
quantum dot technologies.

1
Introduction
The progressive refinement of microelectronic devices has allowed for the proliferation of com-
puting power, transforming industries and daily life. Continuous improvements in device per-
formance, scalability, and energy efficiency have driven the miniaturization of electronic com-
ponents to the point where the fundamental limits of classical physics are being challenged. As
feature sizes shrink towards the atomic scale, quantum mechanical effects take precedence, plac-
ing constraints on conventional semiconductor architectures and driving the development of new
innovations that harness these effects.
Among these emerging technologies, quantum light sources play a crucial role in the advance-
ment of quantum information science. In particular, single-photon emitters are essential for
applications such as quantum key distribution, quantum computing, and quantum metrology
[1]. Among the various quantum emitters explored, self-assembled quantum dots (QDs) have
emerged as a promising platform due to their ability to provide high-purity, indistinguishable,
and entangled photon pairs [2].
A particularly important spectral region for quantum communications is the telecom C-band
(1530-1565 nm), which aligns with the minimum attenuation window of silica optical fibers:
These fibers exhibit exceptionally low loss at infrared wavelengths, around 0.2 dB/km [3], mak-
ing them the backbone of long-distance optical communication networks. Over 95% of the
world’s data traffic is transmitted through undersea fiber-optic cables operating in this spectral
region [4]. Moreover, the telecom C-band enables Dense Wavelength Division Multiplexing
(DWDM), where multiple data channels are transmitted simultaneously, significantly increasing
optical carrier capacity [5]. While extending transmission into the telecom-L band (1565-1625
nm) can allow for even more data channels, the increased chromatic dispersion at higher wave-
lengths can introduce pulse broadening effects [6]. Thus, the C-band remains the optimal choice
for high-capacity optical communication systems.
Despite the efficiency of silicon-based photonic platforms for guiding light, they suffer from a
critical limitation: the inability to generate coherent photons. This issue arises from the cen-
trosymmetric crystal structure of silicon, which forbids second-order nonlinear optical processes
such as spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC) [7]. Consequently, alternative mate-
rial platforms are required for quantum light generation. Among these, InAs/InP quantum dots
have demonstrated significant potential as robust single-photon sources, capable of operating in
the telecom wavelength range with high efficiency and coherence [8] [9] [10].
A fundamental property of QDs is their ability to confine charge carriers in all three spatial di-
mensions, leading to discrete electronic states. This quantum confinement effect significantly
modifies the density of states, shifting from a continuous spectrum in bulk materials to dis-
crete, atomic-like levels in QDs [11]. Self-assembled QDs are typically formed via the Stranski-
6
==PAGE:8==
Krastanov growth mode [9], where a few monolayers of material initially grow pseudomorphi-
cally as a strained wetting layer before spontaneously forming three-dimensional islands [12]
[13]. This self-organization process results in defect-free QDs embedded within a semiconduc-
tor matrix, crucial for high-performance optoelectronics [14].
While self-assembly enables spontaneous QD formation, precise control over QD positioning
and emission wavelength remains a challenge.
Despite the significant potential of quantum dots as single-photon emitters, achieving high spa-
tial precision in their positioning remains an ongoing challenge in the field. The self-assembly
process results in randomly distributed QDs, leading to difficulties in integrating them into scal-
able photonic architectures. For practical quantum technologies, the deterministic placement of
QDs with predefined emission properties is essential.
Strain engineering presents a promising solution to this challenge by providing an effective
means of site-controlled QD nucleation. This method could allow for precise control over both
the spatial positioning and the optical properties of the QDs, offering a scalable approach to
deterministic QD fabrication [15]. Compared to other site-control techniques, such as selective
area growth [16], strain engineering minimises material defects and preserves the high optical
quality of QDs required for quantum applications.
This project is dedicated to the advancement of epitaxially grown, self-assembled InAs/InP quan-
tum dots, using strain engineering for precise site control. By controlling the intrinsic strain fields
that arise during epitaxial growth, the spatial positioning and of these QDs can be finely tuned,
offering a scalable approach to deterministic quantum dot fabrication. This methodology is criti-
cal for integrating high-quality single-photon emitters into quantum photonic circuits, paving the
way for enhanced performance in quantum communication and computation applications.

Theoretical Framework
A thorough understanding of the physics governing quantum dots is essential, both conceptually
to understand why these nanostructures are viable in the optoelectronics landscape, but also to
understand the fabrication process behind them. This section provides the necessary theoreti-
cal foundation, outlining the principles of quantum confinement, and strain effects in epitaxial
systems.
2.1
Density of States in Quantum Confined Nanostructures
The limitations of traditional silicon-based photonic platforms, as outlined in the previous sec-
tion, highlight the necessity of exploring alternative materials and fabrication approaches for ad-
vanced photonic and quantum communication systems. Since the 1990s [11], QDs have shown
promise as the next-generation photonic technologies, owing to their unique quantum confine-
ment effects. These effects enable discrete energy levels and tunable optical properties, making
QDs highly versatile for various applications.
Quantum dots are semiconductor nanostructures capable of confining charge carriers in all three
spatial dimensions, resulting in atom-like discrete energy states -a concept we will explore mo-
mentarily.
2.1.1

2.1.2
Energy Bands and Bandgaps in Bulk Semiconductors
The continuous nature of the DOS leads to the formation of well-defined energy bands. These
bands are separated by forbidden energy regions, bandgaps, where no electronic states exist.

rial at the equilibrium interatomic separation [18] Figure 12.3.

The bandgap energy Eg represents the minimum energy required to excite an electron from the
valence band to the conduction band. The formation of the bandgap can be understood through
the periodic potential of the crystal lattice, which governs the behavior of electrons in a crystalline
solid. As electrons propagate through the periodic potential, their wavefunctions experience con-
structive and destructive interference due to the periodic arrangement of atoms. This interference
causes the splitting of energy levels into continuous energy bands and the opening of bandgaps
at certain points in k-space. This is formally described by Bloch’s theorem [19]:
In a crystal with a periodic potential V (¯r) that satisfies V (¯r + ¯R) = V (¯r) for any lattice vector
¯R, the electron wavefunctions can be expressed as:
ψn¯k(¯r) = ei¯k·¯run¯k(¯r),
(7)
where un¯k(¯r) is a periodic function with the same periodicity as the crystal lattice, ψn¯k(¯r) is the
electron wavefunction, ¯k is the crystal momentum, and n denotes the band index.
According to this theorem, electron wavefunctions in a crystal can be described as plane waves
modulated by a periodic function. At specific points in k-space, known as Brillouin zone bound-
aries, these wavefunctions undergo Bragg reflection, leading to gaps in the energy spectrum—the
bandgaps. The width of a bandgap depends on the strength of the periodic potential and the over-
lap of atomic orbitals in the crystal, making it an intrinsic material property in bulk systems.
Within these allowed energy bands, the DOS describes how the available electronic states are
distributed. The continuous nature of the DOS ensures that electrons can always occupy states
within the bands, enabling broad absorption and emission spectra. Unlike discrete atomic energy
levels, electronic transitions in solids can occur over a wide range of energies, spanning the
bandgap and extending throughout the bands.
2.1.3
Direct- and Indirect Bandgaps in Semiconductors
The bandgap of a semiconductor can be classified as either direct or indirect, depending on the
relative positions of the conduction band minimum and the valence band maximum in k-space.
This distinction determines the efficiency of interband transitions, where an electron is excited
from the valence band to the conduction band through the absorption of a photon, provided
that the final state in the conduction band is unoccupied, in accordance with Pauli’s exclusion
principle [20][21].
In a direct bandgap material, the conduction band minimum and the valence band maximum
occur at the same value of crystal momentum, generally at the center of the Brillouin zone,
k = 0. An interband transition in such a material occurs when an electron absorbs a photon with
energy ℏω that matches or exceeds the bandgap energy Eg, causing the electron to be excited
from the valence band to the conduction band:
ℏω ≥Eg.
(8)

Since this transition requires no change in crystal momentum, interband transitions can occur
efficiently through the absorption or emission of a photon.
The bandgap energy for a direct bandgap material can be expressed as:
Eg = Ec(k = 0) −Ev(k = 0),
(9)
where Ec(k) and Ev(k) represent the energy levels of the conduction and valence bands, respec-

In an indirect bandgap material, such as silicon, the conduction band minimum and the valence
band maximum occur at different points in k-space. For example, the conduction band minimum
may occur near the X-point in the Brillouin zone, while the valence band maximum remains at
k = 0. As a result, a direct interband transition is not possible without a change in momentum. In
this case, the transition requires the involvement of a phonon to provide the necessary momentum
transfer:
Eg = ℏωphoton + ℏωphonon = Ec(kc) −Ev(kv),
(10)
where ℏωphoton is the photon energy, and ℏωphonon is the phonon energy.
The need for a phonon-assisted transition makes radiative recombination in indirect bandgap ma-
terials much less efficient compared to direct bandgap materials, as the simultaneous involvement
of both a photon and a phonon significantly reduces the probability of the transition occurring at
all.

2.1.5

materials.

Effects of Discrete Density of States in Quantum Dots
We have now derived the density of states (DOS) for both bulk semiconductors and quantum-
confined nanostructures, demonstrating the fundamental differences between these systems. As
illustrated in Figure 3, this dimensional dependence of the DOS follows a clear progression:
in bulk materials, the DOS is continuous, whereas in lower-dimensional systems, increasing
confinement leads to greater quantization. Ultimately, in zero-dimensional quantum dots, the
DOS becomes entirely discrete, consisting of a series of Dirac delta functions, with no continuous
states.
This transition from a continuous to a discrete DOS fundamentally alters the material’s optical
properties. In bulk materials, the continuous DOS enables electronic transitions between any
occupied state in the valence band and any unoccupied state in the conduction band. As a result,
absorption occurs over a broad range of energies, leading to wide and featureless absorption
spectra. In contrast, the discrete DOS of quantum dots restricts electronic transitions to well-
defined energy levels, producing narrow, sharply defined spectral features.

2.2
Quantum Dot Growth Dynamics and Self-Assembly
Fabricating quantum dots with controlled size and distribution is crucial for performance and
properties. A widely used approach is to exploit certain reactions and surface physics that pro-
mote spontaneous aggregation of materials into well-defined structures.
This process is known as quantum dot self-assembly, and is specifically a process, where elastic
strain relaxation leads to spontaneous generation of three-dimensional islands on the nanometer
scale [27]. Self-assembly occurs during epitaxial growth, wherein the deposited material forms
a thin film on a substrate, with the lattice parameters of the thin film closely matching those of
the underlying substrate.
2.2.1
Principles of Epitaxial Growth
Epitaxial growth is a process in which a crystalline material is deposited onto a crystalline sub-
strate in such a way that the deposited layer adopts a well-defined crystallographic orientation
that is determined by the underlying substrate [28]. The introduced atoms diffuse across the
substrate surface and integrate into the underlying crystal lattice, and thus the process requires
that the lattice parameters of the deposited material to closely resemble the substrate’s and for
the crystal orientations to be aligned. When these conditions are met, the result is a high-quality,
monocrystalline thin film with precisely controlled dimensions, down to individual atomic lay-
ers, depending on the deposition technique used.
The introduced, unbound, atoms, called adatoms, undergo a three-step process during epitaxial
growth [27][28][29], which can be seen in Figure 4: The process begins with the deposition of
adatoms onto the substrate surface. At this stage, the adatoms are not yet chemically or phys-
ically incorporated into the substrate and it’s crystalline lattice. Instead, they adhere weakly to
the surface through van der Waals forces or ionic interactions, a process known as adsorption.
These interactions allow the adatoms to remain mobile in the plane, enabling mobility across the
surface, while still sticking to the surface.
After adsorption, the adatoms undergo surface diffusion, migrating across the substrate in search
sites that minimises the free energy (see Figure 4). These sites are typically associated with
topology changes such as steps, defects, or seeds, which offer more bonding opportunities. By
settling at these locations, the adatoms reduce their free energy by minimizing the number of un-
saturated bonds. As adatoms accumulate at these favorable sites, small clusters form, which act
as nucleation centers. These nuclei then become seeds that promote further growth by attracting
more adatoms to join them.
In the final stage, the clusters formed during nucleation grow larger as additional adatoms attach
to them. Over time, the clusters expand and begin to merge with one another, leading to the
18
==PAGE:20==
formation of larger, connected structures. This process continues until the clusters coalesce to
form a continuous and uniform thin film across the substrate surface.

settle into a nearly global free energy minimum. It should be mentioned that although metal-

On the left-hand side, γBg represents the surface energy of the bare substrate in contact with the
surrounding gas before deposition occurs.
On the right-hand side, the first term, γAg cos(θ), corresponds to the surface energy of the de-

material, as the film adapts its shape based on its interaction with the substrate.
The second term, −γAB, represents the interface energy between the deposited film and the sub-
strate, which influences adhesion and wetting properties.
The final term, CkBT · ln(ρ), accounts for the elastic strain energy, where C is the elastic mod-

deposition.

2.2.3
Considerations of III-V Material Systems
We have now established the principles of self-assembled epitaxial growth and the different
growth modes governed by lattice-mismatch induced strain, and we can therefore turn to more
holistic properties of strain in heterostructures. Besides influencing growth dynamics, strain also
has an impact on the electronic structure of the material, band alignments, carrier mobility, and
optical properties, by physically deforming the crystal lattice.
For quantum dot fabrication and thin film heterostructures, the most common class of materials
are the III-V semiconductor family, in which elements from groups III and V of the periodic
table form alloys [29][34][35].
Unlike silicon, which is a group-IV semiconductor that forms stable covalent bonds with itself,
III-V materials consist of elements from different groups of the periodic table. Despite this com-
positional difference, both types of semiconductors rely on strong covalent bonding within their
crystal lattice to achieve structural stability. Bulk single crystals of semiconductors, whether
group-IV or III-V, are typically grown using the Liquid Encapsulated Czochralski method [29],
in which a seed crystal is submerged into a molten solution of the material and then slowly pulled
out, allowing the material to crystallize with the same orientation as the seed.
This process yields highly ordered covalent mono-crystalline structures with high atomic coor-
dination, resulting in strong interatomic bonding and relatively low defect densities.
Regardless of whether the material is a group-IV, III-V, or even II-VI semiconductor, the atoms

manium, or the zinc blende structure in GaAs and InP. The periodic arrangement of atoms in

The morphology of the bands and bandgaps, the positions of the valence band maximum and

bandgap.
The character of the bandgap stems from the atomic orbital contributions to the electronic states
at the band edges. In group-IV semiconductors like silicon, both the conduction and valence
bands originate from the same atomic species, resulting in highly symmetric bonding interac-
tions. The electronic states near the band edges primarily involve sp3 hybridized orbitals, form-
ing an extended covalent network with significant orbital overlap. However, this symmetry in
bonding leads to a conduction band minimum that is not located at the center of the Brillouin
zone (k = 0, also known as the Γ-point), as the valence band maximum, but instead at a different
momentum point, such as the X, or L point in silicon [29].
This displacement of the conduction band minimum arises because at Γ, the conduction band
primarily consists of s-like orbitals, which do not strongly hybridize with the valence band
states, leading to a relatively high energy at this point. In contrast, at X and L, the conduction
band states experience stronger orbital mixing, particularly with p-like and higher-order orbitals,
which lowers the energy of these states [36][37]. As a result, silicon and germanium exhibit an
indirect bandgap, where the lowest-energy optical transitions require a phonon to assist in mo-
mentum conservation.
In contrast, for III-V materials like GaAs and InP, the valence band maximum is predominantly
derived from the p-orbitals of the group-V element, while the conduction band minimum orig-
inates from the s-orbitals of the group-III element. This difference in atomic species breaks
the symmetry present in group-IV semiconductors, allowing for stronger s-p hybridization at Γ

2.3
Deterministic Site-Control Growth of Quantum Dots
The quantum dot growth explored in the previous section is inherently stochastic, relying on
strain-driven self-assembly to spontaneously nucleate nanostructures at energetically favorable
sites, which, as mentioned, can be at defect sites, foreign particles, or simply from a random
seeding adatom.
While this process is highly efficient and widely used, the random spatial distribution of self-
assembled quantum dots presents challenges for applications requiring precise positioning and
uniformity. The placement of quantum dots directly influences their optical and electrical inter-
actions, impacting coupling efficiency and leading to variations in size, shape, and composition,
which in turn contribute to inhomogeneous broadening of optical emission and inconsistencies
in electronic properties.
To overcome these limitations, deterministic site-controlled growth offers an alternative ap-
proach, in which lithographically pre-patterned substrates are used to guide nucleation at pre-
defined locations [45].
2.3.1
Strain and the Energy Landscape
In conventional Stranski-Krastanov growth, a thin wetting layer initially forms coherently on the
substrate. However, as the lattice mismatch strain builds up, the system transitions from two-
dimensional layer growth to three-dimensional island formation to minimize its elastic energy
[46], a process we covered in detail in the previous section.
In the case of deterministic site-controlled growth, pre-patterning of the substrate modifies the
local strain distribution, creating sites that promote nucleation. The introduction of pits, recesses,
or mesas alters the strain energy landscape, by providing regions of local strain accumulation or
relaxation [47] [15].

2.3.2
Spacing and Patterning Effects
The spatial arrangement and geometry of nucleation sites significantly influence QD formation
by modifying both the local and global strain field, and diffusion dynamics. While local strain
near or on top of a filled hole governs the individual nucleation events, the collective interaction
of neighbouring strain fields dictates whether QDs form in strain-wise isolation or exhibit strain-
mediated coupling effects [51].
The shape and dimensions of predefined nucleation sites influence adatom mobility, thereby af-
fecting dot morphology and size distribution. Larger or deeper pits provide an extended surface
for adatom accumulation, increasing the likelihood of multiple QD nucleation per site. This has
been observed in structured GaAs substrates, where hole depth and diameter directly correlate
with QD occupation numbers [52]. In contrast, asymmetric site geometries, such as elongated
trenches or anisotropic etch profiles, induce directional strain relaxation, leading to the formation
of elongated quantum dots [53]. This phenomenon arises because strain relaxation is anisotropic,
occurring preferentially along certain crystallographic directions, thereby influencing both the
shape and alignment of QDs [54, 15, 48].
The spacing between nucleation sites determines the degree of strain field overlap, directly af-
fecting QD size uniformity and ordering. When sites are closely spaced, the overlapping elastic
interactions modify the local strain energy density, leading to strain-assisted coalescence or or-
dering effects, where dots preferentially nucleate in clusters rather than as isolated structures
[51, 55]. This effect has been experimentally observed in site-selective growth on GaAs sub-
strates, where the local accumulation of adatoms within patterned pits is highly dependent on
both spacing and hole shape [52].
At large separations, QDs nucleate with greater independence, as strain fields remain localized to
individual pits. This reduces collective strain effects, leading to enhanced spatial uniformity and
better-defined dot sizes. Conversely, when nucleation sites are too closely packed, merging strain
fields redistribute material, disrupting the self-assembly process and promoting the formation of
elongated or clustered structures instead of isolated dots [52].

Litterature Review
3.1
Self-Assembly and Quantum Dot Heterostructures
Strain-induced self-assembly has been an observed phenomenon in epitaxial growth since the
mid-1980s. Initially, it was seen as an inconvenience in the fabrication of thin films due to
its tendency to spontaneously disrupt uniform layer growth. However, as highlighted by Kuech,
Bhattacharya, and Bansal [27], this effect was later harnessed as a method for creating defect-free
self-assembled quantum dots. Their review provided a holistic discussion on the physics gov-
erning self-assembly in semiconductor epitaxy, particularly emphasizing the role of elastic strain
relaxation in controlling QD size and uniformity. Through detailed experimental and theoreti-
cal analysis, they demonstrated that beyond a critical thickness defined by the Blakeslee model
(42), the initially uniform two-dimensional film undergoes a spontaneous transition into three-
dimensional islands. This transition, now classified as the Stranski-Krastanov growth mode, has
since become a cornerstone of QD fabrication in strained heteroepitaxial systems.
The formation of self-assembled QDs has been extensively studied across various semicon-
ductor material systems, with InAs/GaAs and InAs/InP emerging as some of the most well-
characterized platforms. Further work in the spontaneous ordering of coherently strained QDs
was investigated in the review by Ledentsov et al. [56]. In the review, they covered island nucle-
ation and size distribution are dictated by a competition between strain-driven thermodynamic
minimization and kinetic growth constraints.
The work by Kuech, Bhattacharya, and Bansal [27] further explored how growth parameters
such as substrate temperature, deposition rate, and alloy composition influence the self-assembly
process. By tuning these conditions, researchers have achieved significant control over QD mor-
phology, directly leading to control of emission wavelengths across a broad spectral range rang-
ing from 1033 nm (0.6 eV) to 2066 nm (1.2 eV).
The impact of substrate temperature on QD formation is particularly significant.
For InAs
QDs grown on GaAs substrates, it was been observed that the density of self-assembled is-
lands decreases with increasing temperature, ranging from approximately 1012 cm−2 at 400oC to
0.5 × 1010 cm−2 at 500oC, using MBE. This trend is attributed to the increased surface diffu-
sion length of adatoms at higher temperatures, which reduces nucleation density and favors the
formation of larger QDs. However, excessive temperatures can lead to desorption of adatoms,
adversely affecting the QD size distribution.
Besides the temperature, it also showed taht the deposition rate plays a crucial role in determining
the QD morphology. Higher deposition rates lead to an increased island density due to a higher
adatom flux, which shortens the diffusion length and promotes nucleation at multiple sites.
A similar behavior was covered for InAs QDs grown using MOVPE on InP substrates, in the
same review. The study demonstrated a strong correlation between QD morphology and pho-
toluminescence spectra, showing that larger QDs correspond to lower transition energies. AFM
30
==PAGE:32==
histograms revealed that at a QD height of just below 5 nm, which was produced by MOVPE
growth at 6.5 monolayers per second, the QD density could reach values as high as 1010 cm−2,
with a narrow size dispersion.
As the study increased the growth rate to 9s−1, the density decreased significantly by an order of
magnitude, which proposes a scaling relation, where larger islands form at the expense of smaller
ones. This trend is consistent with the established strain-driven mechanism, wherein surface dif-
fusion promotes the redistribution of material from smaller QDs into larger structures.
Also in the review, a direct correspondence was observed between the QD size distribution and
the photoluminescence emission peaks, with smaller dots emitting at higher energies of 1.03
eV, while larger QDs exhibited redshifted transitions near 1.10 eV [27]. This aligns well with
theoretical band-structure calculations for InAs/InP QDs, which predict a shift in ground-state
transition energies due to size-dependent quantum confinement effects.
3.1.1
Alternative Growth Techniques for Quantum Dot Fabrication
While the SK growth mode remains the dominant approach in the fabrication of self-assembled
QDs, alternative growth methods such as Droplet Epitaxy, DE, and Molecular Beam Epitaxy,
MBE, have been explored to avoid some of the limitations inherent to strain-driven self-assembly.
This was demonstrated in the work by Holewa et al. [57], which investigates the synthesis of
InAs/InP QDs using DE, yielding highly symmetric quantum dots with a low fine-structure split-
ting of approximately (50 ± 5) µeV.
This is fundementally different to the SK-grown QDs that has been outlined in this thesis, where
the inherent strain-induced anisotropy typically results in a much larger FSS, leading to signif-
icant energy level splitting, which makes the energy levels non-degenerate, which could be a
desirable property for single-photon sources [8]. Additionally, their reported QD densities are
significantly lower than those observed in SK growth, with a surface density of 2.8 × 108 cm−2.
This is approximately two orders of magnitude lower than the QD densities observed in MOVPE-
grown SK QDs, where densities can reach values on the order of 1010 cm−2 [27].
Another approach is MBE-based growth of InAs/InP QDs, as demonstrated by Miyazawa et al.
[8], which also demonstrates yet another method of succesful fabrication for highly pure single-
photon sources with exceptionally low multi-photon emission probabilities, with their study
acheiving a low second-order correlation function, reporting a g(2)(0) value of (4.4±0.2)×10−4.
Additionally, the MBE-grown QDs emit single photons also at 1.5 µm (0.83 eV), making MBE
another well-suited method for quantum communication over distances beyond 200 km.
From the litterature, it is evident that substrate temperature is a major factor in QD growth and
morphology, irrespective of which of the two strain-driven epitaxy methods are used. For SK-
grown QDs, the density of self-assembled islands has been observed to decrease with increasing
temperature, ranging from approximately 1012 cm−2 at 400◦C to 0.5 × 1010 cm−2 at 500◦C [27].
These increased temperature essentially increases the diffusion length of adatoms, which reduces
the nucleation density, and instead favouring the formation of larger QDs, making size-control
possible by tuning the substrate temperature. However, excessive temperatures can lead to the
31
==PAGE:33==
desorption of adatoms, thereby degrading the QD size uniformity.
A comparison of the photoluminescence properties across different growth techniques further
highlights the trade-offs between methods. For SK-grown InAs/InP QDs, PL peaks range from
1.03 eV to 1.10 eV, depending on QD size distribution [27]. In contrast, the DE-grown QDs
in [57] exhibit a PL peak centred at 0.85 eV (1500 nm), which is in accordance with general
theory, as a common property of SK-grown QDs is the characteristic red-shifted emission spectra.
However, it is possible to subvert this, as has been done in the MBE-grown QDs in [8], which
demonstrate emission in the same spectral range but with a substantially lower fine-structure
splitting of 100 µeV, albeit higher than DE-grown QDs. Additionally, the single-photon purity,
as determined by g(2)(0) measurements, reveals a stark contrast between the techniques: DE-
grown QDs achieve a single-photon purity of approximately 92.5%, whereas MBE-grown QDs
demonstrate extremely high purity of 99.96% [8].
3.1.2
Strain effects in deterministic site-controlled quantum dot growth
The stochastic nature of self-assembled SK growth often leads to random spatial distributions of
QDs, posing challenges for applications requiring precise positioning and uniformity. To address
these limitations, variations of deterministic site-controlled growth techniques have been devel-
oped, enabling the fabrication of QDs at predefined locations with controlled properties.
Among these approaches, the buried stressor method and nanohole template technique have
emerged as leading strategies due to their ability to influence strain fields and achieve spatially
controlled QD formation [15]. The buried stressor method, for example, utilizes a buried AlAs
layer that undergoes partial oxidation to form Al2O3, generating localized strain fields that direct
QD nucleation [15].
The resulting strain modulation facilitates the deterministic alignment of QDs with oxide aper-
tures, minimizing inhomogeneous broadening while maintaining optical purity.
Experimental demonstrations have achieved highly uniform QD positioning in 28 x 28 mesa
arrays, with inhomogeneous broadening reduced to 15.8 meV, a notable improvement over the
18.4 meV broadening observed in non-positioned QDs [15], showing the importance of deter-
ministic growth. Additionally, linewidth distribution statistics from a 10 x 10 subarray indicate
that site-controlled QDs exhibit an average linewidth of 120 µeV, compared to 160 µeV for non-
positioned QDs.
An alternative approach to deterministic site-controlled QD growth is the incorporation of quan-
tum dots within position-controlled InP nanowires, grown via a bottom-up selective-area vapour-
liquid-solid epitaxy [58]. This method has demonstrated a fabrication yield of nearly 100%,
overcoming one of the persistent limitations in site-controlled QD growth, where unintended
nucleation events often degrade the ratio of succesful growths on predefined sites. The InAsP
QDs are embedded within tapered InP photonic nanowires, enabling efficient single-photon ex-
traction with collection efficiencies reaching up to 83% and near-transform-limited linewidths of
880 MHz [58]. A key advantage of this approach is the absence of the two-dimensional wetting
32
==PAGE:34==
layer commonly present in self-assembled QDs, which reduces spectral pollution from nearby
QDs and eliminates hybridization effects that broaden emission linewidths [58].
Optical characterization of the 10 x 10 array of nanowires confirmed that every single QD ex-
hibited bright emission, with an average exciton peak energy centered at 1264 meV and a stan-
dard deviation of 6 meV, demonstrating exceptional uniformity compared to conventional SK
growth [58]. The single-photon purity measurements revealed multiphoton emission probabili-
ties as low as 0.6%, highlighting the robustness of this approach for quantum photonic applica-
tions [58]. While this technique provides near-unity yield, a remaining challenge is mitigating
phonon-mediated pure dephasing, which broadens linewidths beyond the transform limit due to
carrier-phonon interactions under above-band excitation [58].
3.1.3
Tuning Electronic and Optical Properties of Quantum Dots
Strain also plays a defining role in the optical properties of QDs, influencing both excitonic tran-
sitions and emission characteristics. As described in [10], authored by Holewa and others, the
SK growth mode enables the formation of QDs with discrete energy levels, akin to atomic-like
transitions, as described in the first section of the thesis. This quantum confinement effect is pro-
nounced in InAs QDs on InP wetting layers, which emit in the telecom bands at 1460-1625 nm.
In the study, it was investigated if such QDs could serve as high-purity single-photon sources:
The primary measure of quality for single-photon sources is the second-order correlation func-
tion g(2)(0), which quantifies the probability of detecting more than a single photon at a time.
For an ideal single-photon source, the value of g(2)(0) should be zero, indicating that only single
photon events occur.
Holewa et al. demonstrated that InAs QDs, when grown under well-controlled strain conditions,
exhibit highly pure single-photon emission, characterized by a second-order correlation function
g(2)(0) < 0.02, confirming their suitability as indistinguishable photon sources. This result will
be compared to alternative growth methods in the comming section.

4.2.1
Initial growth of Epitaxial Layers
Unlike MBE, which is governed by physical vapor deposition under ultra-high vacuum condi-
tions, MOVPE is a type of chemical vapor deposition, where precursor gases decompose at the
heated substrate, forming epitaxial layers through surface reactions, with a rate of deposition in
the range of 0.1 to 1 monolayers per second (ML/s) [29].
One of the defining characteristics of MOVPE is its inherently non-equilibrium nature, which is
a little different from the simpler equilibrium system that we discussed earlier in Section 2.2.
Fundementally, the same actions are taking place, and the same principles apply, however, in our
thermal equilibrium model, we assumed that adatoms had sufficient time to diffuse across the
substrate surface, settle into thermodynamically favorable sites, and reach a near-global free en-
ergy minimum before incorporation into the lattice. But in MOVPE, growth is limited by kinetic
constraints such as mass transport, surface reaction rates, and precursor depletion, meaning that
adatom diffusion is often interrupted before reaching an equilibrium state, which makes MOVPE
deviate from the ideal layer-by-layer growth. Instead, metastable structures, step-bunching, and
alloy segregation can emerge. This deviation from equilibrium is particularly interesting in the
Stranski-Krastanov growth mode, where quantum dot self-assembly is driven by strain relax-
ation, but is simultaneously influenced by growth rate, supersaturation, and adatom mobility.
Unlike equilibrium-based models where the critical thickness for island formation is solely dic-
tated by elastic energy minimization, in MOVPE the nucleation and evolution of quantum dots
are strongly dependent on the V/III precursor ratio, -amount, and growth temperature[27], mak-
ing growth parameter adjustment and optimisation important, as it can be used to compensate for
the limited kinetics.
One way to counteract these kinetic limitations is by increasing adatom mobility, which can be
achieved through temperature control.
This is particularly critical in Frank-van der Merwe growth, the mode responsible for forming
the underlying epitaxial layers. Since this process relies on layer-by-layer deposition, adatoms
must diffuse across the surface to reach stable lattice sites rather than forming clusters. Higher
temperatures can support this diffusion, which is why growth typically exceeds 600oC. Without
sufficient thermal energy, adatoms risk becoming trapped in metastable configurations, leading
to surface roughness and departures from ideal layer-by-layer growth.
In the fabrication first approach, an InGaAs layer grown on an InP substrate, followed by lithog-
raphy and etching steps, after which a subsequent InP layer with InAs quantum dots was grown
on top. In the second approach, the InP top-layer was grown on InGaAs from the onset, and
lithography and etching were performed on both layers.

strates used were 2-inch (001)-oriented InP wafers, pre-treated by the manufacturer to eliminate
native oxides. The epitaxial deposition began with the growth of a 16 nm In0.53Ga0.47As layer,
initiated by ramping the temperature to 660oC over 30 seconds, followed by a stabilization phase

4.2.2
Electron Beam Lithography
EBL is similar to traditional photolithography, but instead of using light, it employs a focused
beam of electrons to expose a resist. Unlike photolithography, which relies on optical masks and
is diffraction-limited, EBL uses direct writing at single-digit nanoscale resolution, achieving fea-
ture sizes well below the diffraction limit of light-based techniques. This makes EBL particularly
suited for high-precision nanofabrication where flexibility and resolution are critical.
The process involves scanning a tightly focused electron beam across a resist-coated substrate,
selectively exposing predefined regions. The exposure dose is defined by the user and determines
the amount of energy delivered to the resist. The system calculates the beam dwell time required
for each pixel to achieve the requested dose, adjusting it dynamically based on the defined area
dose. Exposure is performed in a serial manner, where each feature is fractured into smaller
writing fields, and within each field, the pattern is decomposed into discrete beam shots, placed
according to an exposure grid.
To achieve uniform exposure, the shot pitch, the spacing between consecutive beam shots, must
be optimised in with beam current and dose. A finer pitch results in a higher number of beam
shots, increasing exposure time but improving resolution and feature uniformity. For example,
at a 2 nA beam current with a dose of 250 µC/cm2, a 4 nm shot pitch would yield a dwell time
of 20 ns, while reducing the shot pitch to 2 nm would lead to a dwell time of 80 ns.
Another critical aspect of EBL is pattern alignment across multiple writing fields. The JEOL
system employs an automated write-field alignment procedure that ensures sub-15 nm stitching
accuracy. Misalignment between neighbouring fields can lead to stitching errors, causing dis-
continuities in features that span multiple fields. The JEOL system utilises an internal alignment
sequence to correct for these effects, improving pattern fidelity and ensuring smooth feature tran-
sitions.
The lithographic procedure followed a standardised DTU NanoLab workflow.
Prior to exposure, the samples underwent a heat treatment step to eliminate any condensation
and surface contamination that could interfere with resist adhesion. Even minor amounts of sur-
face moisture can impact resist uniformity, leading to defects such as dewetting or inconsistent
exposure outcomes, and heating the wafer drives off adsorbed water molecules and organic con-
taminants, ensuring a clean and dry surface before resist application.
Following heat treatment, the wafers were coated with Surpass 3000, which is a priming agent
designed to enhance resist adhesion. Traditional adhesion promoters such as hexamethyldisi-
lazane (HMDS) form a hydrophobic silane layer to improve adhesion, but Surpass 3000 instead
uses a heteroatom resonance bonding process, where functional groups within the Surpass 3000
molecule selectively bind to electrostatic contaminants on the substrate, neutralizing their charge
and allowing them to be rinsed away, while also forming a thin film of functional groups that
promote adhesion [63]. This Surpass application is then followed by a 30 second Deionised
water rinse to rinse away contaminants, and finalise the adhesion promotion process.

4.2.4
Resist Stripping
After etching the sample, the photoresist must be removed to expose the underlying material. For
this project 2 different approaches were used: a simple dip in solvent for an extended period of
time, and a more elaborate process heated solvent dips and an ultra-sonic bath [68]. After having
performed wet resist removal, the samples were subject to a final dry clean using a plasma asher.
The wet chemistry process involves submerging the sample in Remover 1165 [69], a solvent
designed to dissolve photoresist without damaging the underlying material. Remover 1165 is an
organic solvent that breaks down the polymer chains of the resist through solvation, allowing it
to be removed off the substrate.
It should be noted that the original recipe for the advanced resist removal process included sev-
eral steps, where the Remover 1165 was heated beyond its flash point of 88◦C [69], which is
prohibted in the NanoLab clean room due to safety regulations.
The process was therefore slightly modified, such that the temperature never exceeded 80◦C.
Furthermore, the original process also calls for sonication in propan-2-ol at room temperature,
however, the fumehood is only equipped with a single ultra-sonic cleaner, and as the water bath
in the ultra-sonic cleaner, prior to the propan-2-ol bath, has been heated to 80◦C, far exceeding
the boiling point of propan-2-ol, the propan-2-ol ultra-sonic treatment was omitted, instead re-
placed by simply mechanically agitating the sample in room temperature propan-2-ol every 30
seconds for 10 minutes.
Following either of the wet resist removal processes, the sample is rinsed with isopropanol (IPA),
as IPA disolves organic contaminants while being miscible with water. A final rinse with deion-
ized water (DIW) ensures the complete removal of any remaining impurities.
The plasma asher process, a descumming process, is necessary to remove any residual resist
that may remain after the solvent-based removal. This step is particularly important for ensuring
clean surfaces for subsequent processing.
Plasma ashing relies on reactive oxygen species to break down and volatilize the remaining or-
ganic photoresist material. The oxygen plasma generates high-energy radicals that interact with
the carbon of the resist, converting it into volatile byproducts such as CO2 and H2O, which are
then evacuated from the chamber. This step is performed to make sure that the surface is com-
pletely clean and free of organic contamination.

4.2.5
Quantum Dot Growth
After having completed the resist removal process, the samples were ready for the epitaxial
growth of the quantum dots.
The process starts out by cleaning the samples in a (NH4OH (25%): H2O) ammonium hydroxide
solution, in a 1:5 ratio for 60 seconds. This wet clean is weakly basic, and is commonly used to
dissolve native oxides on III-V materials, since it easily froms hydroxides complexes with group
III oxides [70][71]. The samples were then submerged in DIW, while in transport to the MOVPE
reactor, to ensure that no re-oxidation occured by exposing them to ambient conditions.
Just before loading the samples into the reactor, they are moved from the DIW bath to a nitrogen
bubble bath, to agitate the surface and remove any remaining particles. This process takes a
couple of minutes and is done to ensure a clean surface before the epitaxial growth.
The epitaxial deposition process can be separated into 3 different steps: The conditioning of the
chamber, the growth of the InP toplayer, and the growth of the InAs quantum dots.
Figure 17: Visual representation of the temperature profile during InP toplayer / InAs QD growth
on InGaAs. Different growth phases are distinguished by color.
As in the previous MOVPE growth, the reactor was first conditioned by overshooting the tem-
perature, this time, to a target of 720oC, before the PID controller stabilized the temperature at
650oC, as seen in Figure 17. This process takes approximately 500 seconds to complete. During
this phase phosphine is introduced at a flow rate of 200 sccm to provide a stable growth environ-
ment for the InP layer, as the surface is prone to desorption, unless a high chemical pressure of
phosphorous is maintained. The flow of phosphine is maintained for the two first phases.
The temperature is then lowered to 510 degrees over 1.5 minutes (Figure 17), and indium is
48
==PAGE:50==
introduced into the reactor in the form of trimethylindium (TMIn) at a flow rate of 200 sccm
approximately 23 minutes into the process, and is maintained for 80 seconds. After the indium
flow is terminated, the phosphine flow is maintained for another 7 minutes, to prevent InP de-
composition.
After this phase, the growth of the InAs quantum dots is initiated by lowering the temperature
further to 494◦, see Figure 17, and introducing arsenic into the reactor, at a flow rate of 1.51
sccm, maintained for 500 seconds, with a short 2 second window of Indium at the beginning of
the phase, at a flow rate of 30 sccm.
The growth of the InAs quantum dots is concluded by a controlled temperature ramp-down to
room temperature over 650 seconds.

5.2.1
Epitaxial Growth of InGaAs and InP
The new sample iteration also used an InP (011) substrate, similar to the previous sample, but
with a key distinction in the epitaxial growth process. Previously, the InP layer was intended to
be grown alongside InAs quantum dots, as outlined in Section 4.2.1 and Table 1, on top of the
InGaAs layer. In this iteration, however, the 10 nm InP layer was grown immediately after the
16 nm InGaAs layer during the same growth sequence.
Compared to an InGaAs-terminated surface, an InP top-layer provides a more stable growth en-
vironment by mediating strain relaxation and suppressing supersaturation-driven fluctuations..
Without an InP cap, uncontrolled precursor supersaturation leads to non-uniform nucleation,
where different regions of the wetting layer reach the critical thickness ( 1.5 ML) at varying
rates, resulting in inconsistent QD densities and size distributions. By maintaining a chemically
stable and structurally coherent surface, the InP cap moderates local strain energy, ensuring a
more uniform transition from 2D to 3D growth [9].
One of the driving mechanisms behind the regulation is that the InP cap suppresses As/P inter-
mixing, which a major source of wetting layer instability in InAs QD growth. During MOVPE,
exposure to AsH3 induces self-limited As/P exchange, leading to composition variations in the
wetting layer, ranging from InAs0.18P0.82 (2 ML thick) to InAs0.75P0.25 (4 ML thick). This vari-
ability affects the critical thickness for QD nucleation, making growth conditions unpredictable.
With an InP cap in place, the surface remains phosphorus-terminated, minimising As/P intermix-
ing and maintaining a well-defined wetting layer [9].
Furthermore, the phosphorus-rich environment of the InP cap prevents premature strain relax-
ation, which would otherwise lead to dislocations and degrade QD quality. When InP is present
before InAs deposition, strain remains coherently distributed, allowing for controlled nucleation
and uniform QD formation. Without the cap, strain relaxes unevenly, resulting in high clustering
and broad size dispersion [9].
After the growth, the surface was inspected using OM, and no visible defects were observed.

5.2.3
Etching of the InGaAs/InP epitaxial Layers
While the addition of the InP capping layer improves the overall quality of the epitaxial structure,
it also introduces additional complexity to the etching process. In the previous iteration, etching
was straightforward, as it only required the selective removal of the InGaAs, however, with the
presence of the 10 nm InP cap, a sequential etching process was required: First, etching the InP
top-layer, followed by the InGaAs layer.
This additional step requires precise etching control to selectively remove each layer while pre-
serving pattern fidelity and resist integrity. While this modification enhances QD growth unifor-
mity in later stages, it increases the overall fabrication complexity.
The etching process is outlined in Table 5 in Section 4.2.3. Similar to the previous wet etch,
the InGaAs was etched using a sulfuric acid-hydrogen peroxide solution, while the InP was first
etched using a hydrochloric-phosphoric acid solution. Multiple samples were etched with slight
variations in InP etching time to assess consistency. As shown in Figure 15, the etch rate for the
InP layer was approximated at 2.85 nm/s with an uncertainty of ±0.33 nm/s.
However, the validity of this estimate is questionable due to significant variability in the data
points. Specifically, while an etch rate of 2.85 nm/s would predict a 10 nm removal in just 3-4
seconds, the actual etching test revealed a depth of 12 nm in 8 seconds, significantly lower than
expected. This discrepancy suggests that the etch rate may be overestimated, potentially due to
the high viscosity of the etchant, which could slow down the effective material removal. Given
this uncertainty, etching times were systematically varied between 3 and 10 seconds in 1-second
increments to better characterise the actual rate and ensure process reproducibility.
The effects of the varied etch times on the InP layer showed tremendous variability, as can be
readily seen in Figure 27.
Figure 27: Left: Sample A1, InP-layer etch duration of 5 seconds. Right: Sample A3, InP-layer
etch duration of 7 seconds. Both samples were subsequently etched with the solution for InGaAs
for 16 seconds. 10x magnification.
60
==PAGE:62==
The etching results showed significant resist degradation across all samples, although distinct
variations in morphology and pattern retention were observed. The right sample in Figure 27,
which underwent a longer H3PO4:HCl etch (7 s versus 5 s for the left sample) prior to the sub-
sequent H2O2:H2SO4:H2O etch, exhibited nearly complete pattern degradation, whereas the left
sample retained faint but discernible labels.
Resist island formation was observed in both cases, but in the right sample these islands were
strangely absent near the patterned regions. This suggests an interplay between etchant viscos-
ity, surface conditioning effects, and resist interactions with the etchants, which has also been
observed and discussed in other studies [74][75].
Due to the unusual thickness of the etchant, we immediately considered the role of etching kinet-
ics, as investigated in [76], which asserts that when the reaction rate is limited by mass transport,
a concentration gradient of the active etching species can develop at the solid/solution interface.
In a viscous solution like our etchant, where the mass transport of reactants and by-products is
inherently slow, the efficacy of the etching process may become unpredictable. A highly vis-
cous etchant, such as H3PO4, may not diffuse away from the surface in a proper manner, for
instance during the rinse step. As a result, remnants of the etchant could persist locally, leading
to unintended effects even after the sample has been immersed in a new solution. This raises
the hypothesis that etchant retention is an indirect consequence of the high viscosity, as slower
diffusion rates hinder the transport of reactive species away from the surface.
The unintended effects of etchant retention could potentially be twofold:
First, the formation of resist islands near the patterned regions in both of the samples in Fig-
ure 27 suggests that residual H3PO4 may have acted as a buffer layer between the resist and
the second etchant. This buffering effect could have altered the interaction between the resist
and the subsequent H2O2:H2SO4:H2O etch, preventing immediate peeling and instead leading
to a more gradual degradation process. Rather than complete delamination, it is possible that
retained phosphoric acid partially dissolved or weakened the resist, leading to a redistribution of
smaller resist fragments instead of large-scale peeling. This is somewhat consistent with previ-
ous observations, Poulin et al. [74] observed that residual etchants can catalyse the dissolution
of photoresists when phosphoric acid interacts with organics.
However, this buffering effect does not explain why the resist islands form away from the pat-
terned regions in the sample shown to the right in Figure 27, with the pattern itself being almost
entirely removed. Since the phosphoric-hydrochloric acid etch-duration was longer in this case,
it would stand to reason that the buffer layer would be at least equivalent or perhaps greater than
in the left sample.
Instead, it is possible that the patterned region facilitated a more extensive dissolution of the
resist. Since the pattern contained openings in the resist, the etchant may have diffused more ef-
fectively into and underneath the resist during the extended etching period, leading to increased
degradation. Following this heightened weakening, compared to the other sample, exposure to
the second etchant could have completely removed the resist in these areas, as the resist was
61
==PAGE:63==
already compromised.
This hypothesis, however, is not as compelling as the buffering effect explanation, as it seems
unlikely that an increase of just two seconds in etch time alone could induce such a dramatic dif-
ference in local resist degradation. Although the viscosity of the etchant is high, the additional
etching duration should not have significantly altered the penetration depth of the etchant to such
a pronounced extent.
It is likely that the observed degradation during etching is a combination of several factors, in-
cluding the viscosity of the etchant, the resist composition and integrity, etch duration, rinse
efficiency, resist-etchant interactions, and possibly inherently stochastic effects. Furthermore,
H3PO4-based etching is a common wet-chemical method for InP, typically employing higher
H3PO4 to HCl ratios, such as 3:1 [65] [67]. In this context, etch rates around 1000 nm/min have
been reported for InP using a 3:1 H3PO4:HCl solution.
Given that 10 nm is a relatively thin layer for a wet-chemical etching process that is typically used
for much thicker layers, the standard etchant compositions may not be optimal for this specific
application. Therefore, further studies into the etching dynamics and concentration adjustments
for this specific process should be conducted to fully understand the mechanisms and to achieve
a successful etch on thin InP epitaxial layers.
Since resist degradation was observed across all samples, independent of etchant composition,
dilution, or material system, the most plausible explanation is that the resist itself was compro-
mised. In particular, the finding that the resist began to peel when exposed solely to a DIW dip
(Figure 25), despite treatment with an adhesion promoter, was a strong indicator.
The automatic spin-coater used in the resist application process has previously experienced sol-
vent evaporation issues, resulting in unintended variations in resist thickness. Instead of adhering
to the target value of 180 nm, the resist layer often exceeds this thickness, approaching approxi-
mately 200 nm.
Given the prior malfunctions of the equipment, we opted to attempt the etching again, using
a sample that had been coated prior to the project. This allowed us to assess whether the pre-
existing coating exhibited greater resistance to the etching process, thereby providing a definitive
evaluation of the current resist’s performance, and potentially allowing us to move forward with
the fabrication process.

To address the issue of poor planarisation, we modified our growth sequence by introducing ar-

and promote uniform nucleation sites for InAs QDs, leading to improved layer planarisation and
more consistent QD morphology, than observed in the previous sample.
The change is based on previous observations [77], which demonstrated that an optimised As/P
exchange process significantly improves QD height distribution and overall surface morphology.
By introducing AsH3 before the growth of InAs QDs, a stabilised arsenic-rich surface is estab-
lished, effectively limiting the diffusion of indium adatoms and suppressing their desorption.
This should result in a more uniform and well-integrated InP capping layer, reducing the height
variations and roughness observed in previous growth attempts.

Conclusion
The goal of this project was to fabricate strain-induced, site-controlled quantum dot structures
through pre-patterning with high-resolution electron beam lithography, followed by the con-
trolled self-assembly of quantum dots within the Stranski-Krastanov growth regime using Met-
alorganic Vapour-Phase Epitaxy. By applying these fabrication techniques, we aimed to achieve
deterministic quantum dot placement with optimised uniformity and reproducibility.
This work explores the physics of quantum confinement effects, the self-assembly process, and
the various epitaxial growth regimes, applying these principles to develop uniform fields of in-
dium arsenide quantum dots at predefined locations on a III-V material system.
Experimentally, multiple growth and processing iterations were conducted to address challenges,
particularly resist peeling during etching and poor planarisation during QD growth. The initial
approach, using an InGaAs-on-InP epitaxial layer, failed before the final InP/InAs growth due to
etching issues. A second attempt, in which the InP top layer was grown in the same sequence
to improve planarisation also failed at the etching stage, leading to the conclusion that the issue
was related to the resist. To resolve this, a third pre-coated sample of InGaAs on InP, was used,
which saw successful etching and subsequent InP and InAs quantum dot growth. Two growths
were performed, as the first growth suffered from poor planarisation and stochastic dot forma-
tion, while the second attempt, with earlier arsine introduction, improved growth dynamics and
yielded hyperlocalised quantum dot arrays, though planarisation issues persisted. The samples
were characterised using optical-, atomic force- and scanning electron microscopy.
The results demonstrated that growth conditions, particularly precursor introduction during growth,
significantly influence quantum dot formation. However, despite successfully producing quan-
tum dots, the site-control aspect showed varying degrees of success. As discussed in the results,
this likely had to do with poor planarisation, causing disruptions to adatom surface diffusion, and
consequently, to the uniformity of quantum dot formation.
Future work should focus on improving planarisation by refining the growth sequence. Grow-
ing an additional InP layer directly on InGaAs during the initial stage before the final InP/InAs
growth could help reduce lattice mismatch effects and enhance surface uniformity, leading to
more controlled quantum dot formation. Additionally, optimisation of the etching process for
InP is necessary: The excessive viscosity of the current phosphoric-hydrochloric acid etch likely
impeded mass transport and process control. Exploring alternative dilution strategies, such as
using water instead of just increasing the concentration of phosphoric acid, would reduce vis-
cosity, possibly making the process more sustainable and reproducible. Alternatively, an InP
etching solution without phosphoric acid, like H2O:HCl could be employed, which also exhibits
selectivity toward InGaAs, although to a lesser extent [67, 65].
In conclusion, this research has demonstrated a structured approach to quantum dot fabrication,
highlighting key challenges in site-control and planarisation in MOVPE epitaxial growths. The
findings highlight the critical role of growth sequence optimisation and precursor introduction in
achieving deterministic quantum dot placement.

7.1
Exposure Mask Topology Optimisation
In wafer design, a commonly encountered problem is optimising the layout of ones design, in
this case the number of chips, that can be fitted onto a circular wafer. Maximising the chip-per-
wafer (also known as die-per-wafer (DPW)) can have a substantial impact on the overall yield,
costs, and resource usage.
Some standard tools and resources to calculate the DPW number is freely available on the internet
12, however these are often limited to the most common wafer dimensions such as 4, 6 and 8 inch
wafers, or are restricted in some dimension, most commonly inter-chip spacing.
To combat this problem, we created a custom topological optimaisation procedure, employing
the widespread probabilistic optimisation technique simulated annealing, to maximise the yield
of each wafer produced.
7.1.1
Problem Definition
The main objective is to determine the optimal arrangement of square chips (or dies) on a circular
wafer with a diameter d = 50.8 mm. Each chip has a fixed side length s = 1 mm, and there is
a specified spacing g = 8 mm between adjacent chips. Due to the circular shape of the wafer,
some chips near the periphery may fall outside the usable area and therefore need to be excluded
from the count.
Furthermore, an ”edge exclusion zone” must also be considered: this region is deliberately left
unpatterned or unused due to various process limitations, such as resist adhesion issues, edge
bead formation, and the challenges in maintaining uniformity near the wafer’s edge during litho-
graphic processes. We estimate the edge exclusion zone to be 2 mm in width, extending inwards
from the perimeter, which reduces the effective diameter to 46.8 mm.
Thus, calculation of the maximum Die Per Wafer (DPW) is constrained by:
• The wafer diameter, d.
• The effective diameter, de.
• The chip sidelength, s.
• A fixed spacing, g, between chips.

7.1.3
Optimisation Using Greedy and Simulated Annealing Algorithms
However, there is an optimisation strategy, which produces a more unstable configuration, ie. is
not guaranteed to be better than the two previous methods, but which can maximise the DPW
significantly under certain circumstances. The optimisation process begins with a greedy algo-
rithm, which generates an initial solution. The greedy approach systematically places chips on
the wafer by iterating over potential grid positions and selecting valid placements that maximise
the DPW. This is achieved by ensuring that each chip fits within the usable radius of the wafer
and satisfies minimum spacing constraints. The greedy algorithm is deterministic, and produces
a stable, replicable starting configuration, which is particularly advantageous as it provides a
well-structured base for further refinement.
Once the initial greedy solution is obtained, the optimisation transitions to the simulated anneal-
ing phase. Simulated annealing is inspired by the physical process of annealing in materials,
where materials are heated and then gradually cooled to reach a stable, low-energy state. In the
context of die placement, simulated annealing enables a probabilistic exploration of configura-
tions by introducing controlled randomness, allowing the algorithm to escape local maxima and
explore a broader solution space. This phase refines the initial greedy configuration, aiming to
identify an arrangement that yields a higher DPW.
The annealing process involves gradually lowering the temperature parameter, which controls
the level of randomness in chip placement. Higher temperatures allow for greater variability and
exploration, while lower temperatures focus on refining the solution by reducing randomness.
At each iteration, a new configuration is generated, and if the new configuration improves the
DPW, it is retained. Suboptimal configurations are also accepted probabilistically to avoid being
trapped in local optima, following the probability:

Initializes the ShapefieldGenerator for creating a field with
specified shapes.

def create_shape(self, x, y):
"""
Generates the specified shape at given (x, y) based on shape type.
"""
radius = self.diameter / 2
if self.shape == ’circle’:
return gdspy.Round((x, y), radius, number_of_points=16)
elif self.shape == ’square’:
return gdspy.Rectangle((x - radius, y - radius), (x + radius, y
+ radius))
elif self.shape == ’diamond’:
return gdspy.Polygon([
(x, y - radius), (x + radius, y), (x, y + radius), (x -
radius, y)
])
def create_field(self):
"""
Generates a layout for a single field filled with the specified
shape type.
"""
# Determine max shapes per field
shapes_per_side = int((self.field_size - self.diameter) // self.
pitch) + 1
# Calculate the offset to centre the field on the given position
offset_x = -self.field_size / 2 + self.diameter / 2
offset_y = -self.field_size / 2 + self.diameter / 2
# Add additional global shifts
global_shift_x = 100
# Shift the field 100 um to the right
global_shift_y = 50
# Shift the field 50 um up
# Create a unique cell for the field
field_cell_name = f"{self.shape[0].upper()}_x{self.position[0]}_y{
self.position[1]}"

7.4
Critical Thickness Script
This Python script implements calculations related to lattice properties in semiconductor materi-
als, specifically using Vegard’s Law and critical thickness estimation. The code consists of three
main functions:
• vegards law(X): Applies Vegard’s Law to estimate the lattice constant of an alloy as
a function of the composition fraction X.
• lattice mismatch(X): Calculates the lattice mismatch between the estimated Veg-
ard’s Law lattice constant and a reference material.
• critical thickness(h c guess, b, denominator, tol, max iter): It-
erative function that computes the critical thickness of a strained layer using a convergence
approach.
The script executes the critical thickness calculation by calling critical thickness()
with predefined parameters, yielding the final computed thickness value h c final. This im-
plementation is crucial in material science and epitaxial layer design, helping to determine strain
limitations and growth conditions for semiconductor heterostructures.
# Define a function to calculate Vegard’s Law
def vegards_law(X):
# Apply Vegard’s law
a = X * a_InAs + (1 - X) * a_GaAs
return a
def lattice_mismatch(X):
return (vegards_law(X) - a_InP)/a_InP
# tol: Tolerance level for convergence (default is 1e-3,
def critical_thickness(h_c_guess, b, denominator, tol=1e-3, max_iter
=100):
for _ in range(max_iter):
# Right-hand side (rhs) of the equation: This is the updated
value of h_c based on the current guess
rhs = (b / denominator) * (np.log(h_c_guess / b) + 1)
# Check if the difference between the current guess and the rhs
is smaller than the tolerance (convergence)
if abs(h_c_guess - rhs) < tol:
break
# Update h_c
h_c_guess = rhs
return h_c_guess
# Perform the calculation
h_c_final = critical_thickness(h_c_guess, b, denominator)
