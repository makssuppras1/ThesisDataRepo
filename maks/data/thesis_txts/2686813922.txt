Abstract
In recent years, it has come to the attention of the scienti๏ฌc community that corona emission from
top of thunder clouds are far more prevalent than previously thought. These events, such as blue
jets, sprites, and elves, highly a๏ฌect the radiation balance in the earths atmosphere - and such
have a signi๏ฌcant impact on ionization and perturbation of water vapor and green house gasses.
It is therefore of interest - not only for climate scientists, but for space physicists alike to be able
to best resolve these events. Currently, the best mission for these events is the ASIM module, on
the International Space Station. ASIM observes the earths atmosphere in a nadir con๏ฌguration,
and can as such directly measure large discharge events from thunder clouds. However, ASIM
is not capable of fully resolving these events, as the actual height and vertical structure of these
discharges simply cannot be found directly via ASIM observations. Therefore TOTEM is proposed
as a successor mission to ASIM. TOTEM will observe the clouds and atmosphere - just like ASIM,
but in a slanted con๏ฌguration, meaning, TOTEM will be able to fully resolve the discharge events
from thunder clouds, which ASIM canโt. This report uses simulated scenarios to optimize di๏ฌerent
mission critical parameters - namely the ๏ฌeld of view, and slant angle, to best lay a foundation upon
which these parameters shall be chosen for TOTEM. This is done by preparing di๏ฌerent methods
to determine the "top of cloud height" (TCH) for simulated scenarios - and also a real data case.
The methods proved to do well in resolve the TCH under some assumptions, with the estimated
TCH being in the range of ยฑ0.1 [km] of the input cloud height with an standard deviation of 0.5
[km] for a weighted result, and 0.5โ1.5 [km] for the 2 methods on their own. These results should
make a good foundation upon which to choose the parameters for TOTEM, although it is of the
belief of the author that further analysis should be conducted.

1.
Introduction
The Atmospheric Interactions Monitor (ASIM) on board the International Space Station (ISS)
have in the past few years since its installation in 2018, been key to quantify the profuse activity of
electrical discharges and high energy events which takes place above thunderclouds [5]. A multitude
of discharge events were discovered throughout the 1980s and 1990s [1], originating from thunder-
clouds. This encouraged science communities to actively look for those events, by pointing their
cameras above large thunderstorms, as well as space scientists pointing their instruments down
towards earth. As a result, the ๏ฌrst dedicated mission coming in 2004 on board the Taiwanese
satellite FORMOSAT-2[5].
ASIM discovered corona activity is more common than previously thought.
Corona emission
streamers, also known as blue jets, was thought rare and only associated with large and high
altitude thunderstorms. Corona streamers are large electrical discharges, best observed in narrow
bands below 777.4 nm - which is usually used by lightning detectors - hence why they have prior to
ASIM been hard to detect. Clouds "block" the view of the emission as the emission is absorbed and
scattered by water vapour and gasses. But images by Andreas Mogensen in the THOR experiment
suggest a rather abrupt rise in emission from the clouds - meaning the emission starts high up in
the clouds structure, see ๏ฌgure 1.1.

Frame 1 serves as a reference frame, and frames 2-8 shows a pulsating blue jet. Image
credit [1].

4.1.
Estimation of TCH from ISS Perspective
The goal was to estimate the Top Of Cloud Height (TCH) of clouds - both from a simulated data
case, and from real ASIM data, in order to best lay a foundation upon which mission critical pa-
rameters for the proposed TOTEM mission would be selected. TOTEM is thought the successor to
ASIM, since several experiments, among them the THOR [1] experiment, have assured the need for
slanted observations of thunderclouds, to best resolve the high energy events and corona emission
from thunder clouds. These events, previously thought rare events has been proved to be both more
abundant and severe - even stretching up into the stratosphere, with great impact on greenhouse
gas perturbations and the atmospheres radiation balance. It is therefore desirable to better resolve
these events, via slanted observations as to directly image and measure these events.
In particular TOTEM will be mounted in a slanted con๏ฌguration, opposed to the nadir con๏ฌg-
uration of ASIM, which means the viewing parameters would need to be optimised. The slanted
view proved to highly impact how the cameras would observe the clouds due to the earths curva-
ture. Should the TOTEM team just copy the FOV from ASIM (54 [deg]) and mount TOTEM with
the theoretical optimal slant, they would indeed su๏ฌer tremendously under poor spatial resolution.
As such, the parameters investigated in this report was the Field of View (FOV) and slant angle ฮธ0
in particular. This was illustrated directly in ๏ฌgure 2.7, as TOTEM with a slant of 45 [deg] and a
FOV of 54 [deg] would have a enormous ground track and even look beyond the horizon. In ๏ฌgure
4.1 we take another look at the ground projection for various FOV and slants.
Results was achieved by developing methods to estimate the TCH from images taken with an
optical camera, using various principles and analysis. 2 methods were developed: The Lightning
Method (LM), and The Delta X Method (DXM). The direct LM utilized how a clouds coordinates
can be approximated with the lightning which happens inside the cloud - which meant this method
really estimates the height of the lightning instead of cloud, so we had to assume the calculated

5.
Conclusion
The goal was to develop methods such that the Top of Cloud Height (TCH) could be estimated
for the proposed TOTEM mission. TOTEM is thought a successor to the successful ESA mission,
ASIM, which observes atmosphere and space interactions in a nadir con๏ฌguration on the Columbus
module on ISS. TOTEM will also make atmosphere and space interaction observations, although
it will appear in a slanted view, where scientists hope to directly resolve streamer emissions from
top of thunderstorms as well as the emission height (TCH) - an important parameter for corona
activity. To determine the TCH we conducted a simulated study, using developed methods, which
was derived as functions of mission critical parameters.
The 2 methods developed proved to estimate the TCH down to a minimum error of ยฑ1 [km],
which is in all fairness a good result. The weighted method yields a STD of โ0.5 [km] for a
slant of 45 [deg] - for a large range of observation times โT. The DXM delivered good STDโs for
relatively high angles, but for a slant of 45 [deg], the STDโs were in the range 1 to 1.5 [km] for a
slant of 45 [deg], and a โT between 30 โ60 [s]. However these results will vastly improve should a
more narrow FOV be opted for. This however, in particular for the weighted results and the LM,
was only reliable as long as we could assume 1 method, the Lightning Method, could make su๏ฌ-
cient estimations from 2 images, with an neglectable error on the cloud position. In reality more
images are needed, as this method is very inaccurate, with a 2 [km] median error on the geographic
position of the lightning strike as a best case scenario. This error will directly propagate into the
TCH result, but it could be signi๏ฌcantly lowered with multiple images instead of only 2. Multiple
images would require a longer observation time which in turn limits the FOV and slant choices -
this however directly con๏ฌrms the need for a slanted view - coupled with the bonus of the ability
to directly resolve the TLEโs, corona emissions, jets etc. on images. The other method, the Delta
X Method, did a good job as well - also on its own. The results were all weighted in a weighted
average which took both methods and all estimations into consideration, and proved the errors
associated with TCH determination were in an acceptable range.
Tests on real ASIM data were conducted.
As both methods needed either multiple images of
same clouds, or observations of the same cloud for a long time - up to a minute - it quickly became
clear it is hard to ๏ฌnd such data. ASIM were able to deliver images which, to some degree, ful๏ฌlled
these demands, thus a small test on real data could be conducted, but unfortunately the real data
test proved to be unsuccessful, with a (very) unreliable result. The chosen data set did give some

observations, which were spread out over half a minute, but it was far too di๏ฌcult to actually select

To summarize, this means for FOV and slant to be chosen such that we may get observations
of clouds up to close to 1 minute, while also achieving a spatial resolution which at the very least
rivals that of ASIM - if not better, as this will have a huge positive impact on any errors associated
with the observations.

Future Work
For the future, we suggest a small study be conducted on the feasibility of introducing multiple
images for the LM, and develop a new way to properly weight the many images, such a more true
representation of the TCH can be obtained, using the methods developed in this report. We proved
our methods actually work and function as intended, but the large uncertainty associated with the
LM methods is an issue which needs to be addressed.
If possible, a study, which in likeliness would approach THOR, could be conducted aboard ISS
- but with known slant angle, so to properly verify TOTEM. Slanted observations are di๏ฌcult to
conduct on - ground, and this would be a relatively low cost mission. Essentially all which is needed
is a gimbal, an astronaut with some spare time and a high resolution digital camera.
