Abstract
This manuscript presents a user­oriented Matlab tool designed to analyze fracture tests of
DCB­UBM specimens. The tool integrates a graphical user interface (GUI) that facilitates
the computation of fracture resistance curves, allows parametric studies, and includes
a design module supporting the DCB specimens design phase. It accommodates com­
plex geometries, multilayer material distributions, and diverse loading conditions, includ­
ing bending moments, normal forces, and residual thermal stresses. Several analytical J
integral solutions have been implemented, enabling direct evaluation from experimental
data. The tool has been validated against existing solutions and applied to generate the
results discussed herein. Key findings are summarized, and recommendations for future
developments are outlined.

Introduction
1.1
Problem statement and motivation
Climate change is already affecting both major global economies and developing na­
tions, prompting an urgent call for action from policymakers and stakeholders. Among
the many studies and publications available on the matter, the 2023 edition of IRENA’s
World Energy Transitions Outlook [1], puts emphasis on the fact that transitioning to a
renewable­based energy system remains the most effective strategy to combat climate
change. However, the current pace of this transition is falling short of what is needed [2, 1].
Achieving the goal of limiting global temperature rise to 1.5°C necessitates a reduction
of approximately 37 gigatonnes of CO2 emissions from 2022 levels, aiming for net­zero
emissions in the energy sector by 2050. This ambitious target demands a fundamen­
tal overhaul of global energy systems, particularly through the large­scale expansion of
renewable energy capacity. According to IRENA, renewables contributed 83% of new
power generation capacity added in 2022, totaling 295 gigawatts (GW) and bringing the
global share of renewables to 40% of total installed capacity.
Under IRENA’s 1.5°C pathway, renewable energy capacity must exceed 33,000 GW by
2050. Wind energy—both onshore and offshore—will play a pivotal role in this transition.
From a current capacity of 900 GW, wind power is projected to grow to over 10,000 GW
by mid­century, accounting for nearly one­third of all renewable capacity. Offshore wind,
in particular, is expected to expand dramatically, reaching nearly 2,500 GW by 2050—
a forty­fold increase from the 63 GW installed in 2022—positioning it as a cornerstone
technology in the global effort to meet climate goals. However, the expansion of wind
energy presents its own set of challenges.
One of the key hurdle in wind energy development is the precise evaluation of structural
integrity and the expected lifespan of wind turbines, especially their blades. In recent
years, the wind energy sector has witnessed a significant increase in wind turbine blade
lengths, driven by the need to capture more energy and improve efficiency, particularly in
offshore environments. Modern blades now exceed 100 meters in length, a substantial
leap from earlier designs. This growth has been supported by innovations documented
in e.g. in patent literature [2], especially in the field of modular blades which enable the
assembly of ultra­long blades from smaller segments to ease transportation and manufac­
turing [2]. Additionally, insights from [2] highlight a surge in patent filings related to blade
aerodynamics, materials, and structural optimization, reflecting the industry’s focus on
scaling up turbine size while maintaining reliability and cost­effectiveness. As discussed
by Erives [3], maintaining reliability necessarily requires addressing the significant con­
cern associated with blade failure which in the case of blades mainly comes from fracture.
Mastering the understanding of blade fracture mechanisms starts with understanding the
fracture behavior of composite blades which remains still a challenging field of research.
Failing to get the full picture, has led to adopting a pragmatic engineering approaches
based on conservative design practices. While extensively used, this design philosophy
has significant limitations. Composite blades are often manufactured with (un­intended)
pre­existing defects, and they can absorb substantial energy once damage begins. As a
result, designs that are too conservative may lead to overly heavy structures, which can
actually increase the loads experienced by the blades.
1
==PAGE:9==
An alternative approach is the damage­tolerant design philosophy, which permits damage
initiation and controlled propagation, as long as it remains stable and progresses slowly.
This method offers the potential for lighter, more efficient blades and enables a broader
design space to meet specific performance goals [4]. However, despite these advan­
tages, the use of damage­tolerant design remains limited—mainly due to the absence of
reliable and efficient tools for accurately predicting damage progression.
The failure tests addressed in this project play a key role in the understanding and design
of damage­tolerant structures using damage tolerant composite materials. These tests
are particularly useful in determining the fracture mechanics properties of fiber­reinforced
composite materials used in modern blades as further discussed in Chapter 2 of Erives [3].
There is currently a need to improve the analysis and modeling of tests such as those ad­
dressed herein. In this context, the tools developed in the framework of this project will
help gaining valuable insights into composite material fracture mechanics and as such will
be of help during the design phase of specimens to be used during experimental testing.
In addition, the tools described in the following chapter are specifically designed for ana­
lyzing experimental data. They are user­friendly, robust, and capable of handling a wide
range of multi­layered double­beam specimens with heterogeneous material distributions.
1.2
Main focus
This study focuses on the type of fracture tests known in the literature as Double Can­
tilever Beam (DCB) loaded with Uneven Bending Moments (UBM) tests [5, 6, 7]. Several
references are available in the literature, as highlighted in the next section, where a com­
prehensive analysis is presented.
A specific subset of the available literature has been selected to perform the calculations
presented in this study. Focus has been put on developing a tool which can be used to
analyze efficiently experimental data, perform a parametric analysis and provide a DCB
specimen design tool in the context of the experimental set­up available at DTU.
Among other facilities, the DCB­UBM experimental setup is available at DTU which allows
for the performance of tests where a given DCB specimen, with a specific geometry and
multilayer composite material composition, is subjected to external actions (e.g. applied
loads and/or moments).
As a result of the applications of external loads, crack initiation and propagation occurs
in the specimen. During the first part of the test, the specimen initially resists the loads
and as times elapses, a crack starts to be observed. The length of the fracture process
zone usually increases with time until it reaches a fully­developed state, where the frac­
ture resistance attains a steady­stated value until the sample failure occurs. This facture
creation and growth process is influenced by many factors including the specimen ma­
terial and geometry as well as the load history. A well­recognized approach aiming at
assessing the sample resistance to fracture is based on the calculation of the J integral.
The J integral, described more in details in Sec. 2.2.1, is a measure of the energy (per
unit crack­area) associated with the crack growth and hence the increase of its length
during the DCB sample test.
In the remainder of this document, the approach followed to calculate the J integral by
means of an ad­hoc user friendly Graphical User Interface (GUI) will be described.
Multiple GUIs have been developed to address specific geometrical and load type cases.
The results obtained with these GUIs have been validated and then presented and ana­
lyzed.
Each GUI comprises two components. The first is GUI design (Sec. 1.6), i.e. the view
that the user experiences including the set of buttons, input and output fields. Fig. 1.1.
provides an illustration of one of the GUIs developed in the framework of this project.

The second is the calculation module (Sec. e.g. 1.7). Such module is conceived to per­

it has been verified that the J integral results obtained in [6] are in complete analytical

agreement with those obtained in the following publications:

• A symmetric DCB specimen made from a single material loaded by uneven bending

Following this approach, it was possible to reproduce already existing results for valida­

In addition, a novel tool has been developed to perform a parametric analysis and to pro­
vide the opportunity to simplify the specimen design phase. With the parametric analysis,
the user can decide which parameter to vary among a predetermined list and analyze the
effect on the J integral. With the design tool, the user can build a multiple line design chart

design parameter, e.g. layer height or layer width. This design chart can support the
user in deciding which layer height or width to chose for a given material distribution in or­

the extra two main goals
• familiarizing with the field of DCB­UBM testing facilities and methodologies, and
• understanding the analytical and computational models used to simulate fracture

1.3.1
Fracture mechanics testing methodologies
Several tests methods are described in the literature. Among the many different studies
the four below are of particular interest for the current project.
Mixed­Mode Fracture Mechanics with the DCB­UBM Configuration
Sørensen et al. [5] introduced a novel double cantilever beam specimen subjected to
uneven bending moments (DCB­UBM) for characterizing mixed­mode fracture in layered
materials and adhesive joints. The test setup enables continuous variation of mode mixity
from pure Mode I to Mode II by adjusting the ratio between two applied bending moments,
M1 and M2. Closed­form solutions for the energy release rate, G, and the mode mixity
phase angle, ψ, are derived analytically for both isotropic and orthotropic materials using
linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) and J integral approaches.
Mixed Mode Cohesive and Bridging Laws for Fibre Composites
Sørensen and Jacobsen [15] presented a detailed experimental methodology to extract
mixed­mode cohesive and bridging laws in unidirectional glass fibre/polyester composites
using a J integral­based formulation. Their approach distinctly separated the fracture
process into a crack tip region characterized by J0, and a bridging zone described through
bridging laws. Assuming that the cohesive laws are derived from a potential function, as
proposed initially by Suo et al. [9], the J integral equals the potential function evaluated at
the end­opening and end­sliding. Fitting the measured fracture resistance (J as a function
of end­opening and end­sliding) as a sum of polynomials, cohesive laws were obtained
by partial differentiation. Mixed­mode tests revealed a strong coupling between normal
and shear cohesive stresses and displacements, which were extracted using polynomial
fitting from J integral data.
Mixed­Mode Fatigue Crack Growth in Composites with Large­Scale Bridging
Holmes et al. [16] developed an experimental methodology using DCB­UBM specimens
to investigate fatigue delamination crack growth under mixed­mode conditions with large­
scale bridging. Steady­state crack growth (where the crack tip advanced with a constant
growth rate, much lower than the initial growth rate of the unbridged crack) and fully­
developed bridging zones was observed in Mode I and mixed­mode loading, while Mode
II failed to achieve steady propagation. Using J integral formulations, cyclic traction­
separation laws were proposed to characterize the fatigue behavior under various mode
mixities.
Fracture Toughness Characterization in Sandwich Composites Using DCB­UBM
Berggreen et al. [17] introduced a modified DCB­UBM test rig utilizing torsional actua­
tors to apply pure moments at the cracked specimen ends, facilitating fracture toughness
evaluation of face sheet/core interfaces in sandwich composites. Digital Image Corre­
lation (DIC) and numerical modeling were used to characterize effective kinematics and
improve the data reduction scheme. The test rig supports a wide range of mode mixities
and provides reliable toughness measurements for foam­core sandwich panels.
1.3.2
Analytical and computational modeling
The analytical and numerical models used in this project are based on the equations
implemented and described in the two following main references. Their content, which
is briefly summarized here below, has been analyzed and has served as a basis for the
results presented in this manuscript.
Extraction of Mixed­Mode Cohesive Laws from R­Curves
Erives et al. [7] proposed an experimental and analytical methodology for determining
mixed­mode cohesive laws in unidirectional (UD) composite laminates exhibiting large­
scale fibre bridging. The approach is based on the assumption that cohesive traction can
be derived from a potential function. Using a series of double cantilever beam specimens
5
==PAGE:13==
loaded with uneven bending moments (DCB­UBM), the authors calculated R­curves from
corresponding end­openings across a wide range of mixed­mode conditions. The poten­
tial function was constructed piecewise to capture both the crack­tip and bridging regions,
and cohesive traction were obtained via partial differentiation. The resulting cohesive laws
were fully coupled, with both normal and shear traction depending on the combined open­
ing parameters. Notably, the peak traction occurred at intermediate phase angles, and
the bridging traction accounted for a significant portion of the total fracture energy. This
method provides an alternative to idealized cohesive laws and enables accurate modeling
of delamination in composites under complex loading conditions.
General J integral solution for specimens loaded by moments, axial forces and
residual stresses
Toftegaard et al. [6] presented a comprehensive analytical framework for evaluating the J
integral in cracked double cantilever beam (DCB) specimens subjected to combined axial
forces, bending moments, and residual stresses. Their formulation is general in scope, ac­
commodating both functionally graded materials and multilayered laminates with arbitrary
variation of elastic properties in the height direction. The formulation remains valid under
large­scale bridging conditions and includes a treatment for residual stresses by decom­
posing the problem into thermal and mechanical contributions. The proposed stiffness­
based approach unifies and extends existing solutions, offering a conceptually simple yet
robust method for calculating Jext across a wide range of specimen configurations. This
generalization is particularly valuable for experimental determination of mixed­mode co­
hesive laws, where accurate evaluation of fracture resistance is essential.
1.4
Problem limitation
The primary aim of this project is to develop a general­purpose analysis tool capable of
handling a wide variety of laminated and layered specimens subjected to different loading
conditions, including axial forces, bending moments, and thermal residual stresses. The
tool is intended to support the calculation of key fracture parameters such as the external
energy release rate, Jext, as well as end­opening and end­sliding displacements. These
quantities are essential for the subsequent determination of cohesive laws governing de­
lamination and interfacial fracture behavior.
1.5
Programming language choice
An important choice made at the beginning of the project relates to the selection of the
appropriate programming language used to calculate and display the relevant physical
quantities associated with the tests to be considered. The main criteria taken into consid­
eration for this choice were as follows
1 GUI creation complexity,
2 language ability to perform time consuming calculations,
3 level of knowledge of the language within the project team.
Regarding item 3, two main languages were mastered by the student and supervisor team,
i.e. Phyton [18] and Matlab [19]. Using a language known by the team was considered
important to ensure a smooth transition and knowledge transfer after the completion of
project, so that the tools can be handed over and used in analysis of test data. Regarding
item 1 and 2, the choice among the two has been triggered mainly by the presence in
Matlab of an App Designer Module which facilitates the User Graphical Interface (GUI)
creation. Indeed, the Matlab App Designer simplifies the writing of the scripts needed
to create an interface that a user can utilize to load data, specify input parameters and

2.2
Test configuration
The test configuration corresponding to the double cantilever beam loaded with uneven
bending moments (DCB­UBM) is considered in the framework of this project. Sørensen
et a. [5]1 propose this test configuration which has attracted a lot of interest in the field
of fracture mechanical testing of composite materials of application in wind turbines as
described in Erives [22]. Indeed, one of the advantages of this test setup comes from the
fact that the test configuration is such that the solution of the J integral does not depend
on the crack length [5] and hence provides a stable crack growth. Such a stable growth
simplifies the measurements of fracture data and the analysis of the fracture evolution.
This allows for experimental campaigns on a wide variety of DCB samples to be carried
out, which together with the analytical and computational modeling of the fracture evo­
lution phenomena, positively contributes to the understanding of how to determine the
fracture resistance properties of composite materials.
The purpose of this chapter is not to repeat the description of the test configuration in its
entirety as several references are available and provide a comprehensive overview of the
topic, see e.g. [5], [3]. However, the main features will be recalled to support the reader
in understating the work carried out in this project without the need to deeply analyze the
content of all references.
In the DTU DCB­UBM test facility, a specimen is placed in a vertical configuration. Fig. 2.1
shows two exemplary tests carried out in the facility. There, a vertical specimen is visi­
ble. The test shown on left shows a specimen loaded with symmetric moments where a
crack is visible at the top. The one on the right, shows a still a vertical sample loaded with
asymmetric moments where a crack is also visible at the top of the sample. However, the
second sample is clearly bended towards the left compared to the other one. The load
scenarios and the materials are different from each other in the two cases, but, without
going too much into the details of the test carried out, these two scenarios are useful to
visualize the dependency of the fracture growth phenomena on the specimen geometry

The connection between the experimental set up and the analytical modeling of the phys­
ical phenomena determining the crack growth process starts with the modeling of the test
facility. This is illustrated by Fig. 2.1 reproduced from Erives [3]. The moments (M1 and
M2) are applied at the top ends of the beams and, when thinking at the free­body diagram
for the specimen, a resulting moment is applied to the specimen at its bottom from the
constrains which blocks the bottom part of the vertical specimen beam thereby ensuring
its equilibrium during the test.
The cables are put into tension by the lower beam which can follow a displacement move­
ment along the vertical direction as indicated in Fig. 2.3. A cable runs from the top to the
bottom and up again via low­friction rollers, effectively creating the same force everywhere
in the cable. As the length of the cable is constant, the tension in the cable is uniform,
supporting the validity of the assumption that both arms are loaded by the same load.
Hence, the load P, measured by two load cells on each of the arms, is first measured and
then averaged to define the input used in the computation of the moments M1 and M2.
Another advantage of the present test configuration is represented by the possibility to
measure the end­openings using an extensometer and linear variable differential trans­
ducers (LVDTs) located near the initial crack tip to record displacement data to claculate
the end­opening and end­sliding for use to calculate cohesive laws. Furthermore, acous­
tic emission and high­quality video of the tested specimens can be used to measure crack
onset time and monitor the overall crack growth process.
The load is applied from the beginning of the experiment until and end loading point is
reached, after which the unloading phase starts (see e.g. Sec. 4.2.1 of [3] for more de­
tails).
In summary, a time variable load is applied to both top parts of the specimen which, as
loads increases, starts to develop a fracture process zone in its central part which grows
with time until the end of the loading phase. During the experiment, for the given geomet­
rical and material configuration, the loads are measured together with the displacements
near the initial crack tip. These quantities are then used as input for the calculation of
fracture mechanics related physical variables and parameters that are useful to derive
fracture resistance properties of the tested material as further discussed in the next sec­
tion.

Function Purpose
This function generates a new Excel file structured to accept and organize material and
geometric properties for a layered specimen configuration, as the DCB specimens used in
the fracture mechanics tests under investigation in this project. The layers are categorized
as either lower or upper, with each layer requiring multiple physical descriptors.
This function exemplifies a comprehensive approach to structured data preparation. By
combining user input validation, dynamic file generation, and rigorous formatting con­
trols, it ensures that data related to layered specimens are collected in a consistent, user­
friendly, and analysis­ready format.
Input Parameters and Validation
The function begins by retrieving the number of lower layers (NL) and upper layers (NU)
from the GUI input fields. These values are rounded and validated to ensure they are
numeric and greater than or equal to one. If validation fails, an alert dialog is shown to
the user, and the function exits prematurely. This validation step prevents downstream
errors associated with invalid spreadsheet dimensions or operations.
File Selection and Header Generation
Upon successful input validation, the user is prompted to specify the filename and destina­
tion for the output Excel file. The total number of columns in the spreadsheet is computed
as:
N = 3(NL + NU) + 3
This expression accounts for three physical parameters (width B, height H, and Young’s
modulus E) per layer and three additional global parameters: ℓL (arm of moment M1), ℓU
(arm of moment M2), and P (applied load). For each of the inputs, the units are specified
in the corresponding header.
The column headers are then dynamically generated and categorized into seven blocks:
• Block 1: Width of lower layers B(Li) [mm], for i = 1, . . . , NL
• Block 2: Width of upper layers B(Ui) [mm], for i = 1, . . . , NU
• Block 3: Height of lower layers H(Li) [mm], for i = 1, . . . , NL
• Block 4: Height of upper layers H(Ui) [mm], for i = 1, . . . , NU
• Block 5: Young’s modulus of lower layers E(Li) [MPa], for i = 1, . . . , NL
• Block 6: Young’s modulus of upper layers E(Ui) [MPa], for i = 1, . . . , NU
• Block 7: Moment arms and load ℓL [mm], ℓU [mm], and P [kN]
Excel File Creation and Initial Formatting
A data matrix is constructed according to the previously described dynamically generated
7­blocks structure with one row for headers and a second row initialized for user input.
30
==PAGE:38==
This matrix is then written to the specified Excel file using writecell().
Advanced Formatting with ActiveX
To ensure clarity and enforce data integrity, the spreadsheet undergoes extensive format­
ting via ActiveX automation [26]:
• Cell Protection: All cells are locked by default, with only the second row (intended
for data entry) unlocked.
• Color Coding: Each of the seven blocks is visually distinguished using alternat­
ing colors—light yellow for lower­layer properties and dark yellow for upper­layer
properties. The default sheet background is set to a neutral grey.
• Borders and Gridlines: Thin black borders are applied throughout, with bold bor­
ders delineating each block’s boundaries. This enhances visual separation and re­
duces user error during data entry.
• Sheet Protection: After formatting, the worksheet is locked to prevent unintended
modifications.
Error Handling
All Excel automation is wrapped in a try-catch block to intercept and report formatting
errors, typically due to file access permissions or ActiveX server issues. If an error occurs,
a user alert dialog displays the exception message.

• The Excel file is opened and displayed using ActiveX server automation [26].
• Cells in the header row and the user input row are formatted with alternating

• Input cells (second row only) are unlocked to permit user modifications, while

• Grid lines and bold outer borders are applied to each block to enhance read­

6.1
Introduction
GUI­4 represents the evolution of all previously described GUI­4 towards the main deliv­
erable of this project. As such, both the user interface and the GUI’s calculation modules
of this GUI have been designed thanks to the experience gained by implementing the
previous ones. In addition, as all previous GUIs have been tested over already existing
tools and results, the confidence level in the implementation of GUI­4 is reinforced by the
previous analysis presented in Sec. 3.5, Sec. 4.8 and Sec. 5.8.
The goals of GUI­4 are three­fold:
• the analysis of the tests results for a DCB­UBM fracture test, and
• the generation of parametric analysis J integral surfaces
• the generation of a design tool.
First, the user of GUI­4 will be able to benefit from the dynamic excel file input generation
to define the geometry and material distribution of the two DCB specimen beams including
the thermal properties where required.
Second, the user will be able to load experimental data resulting from a DCB­UBM test
(see e.g. Fig. 3.3). After having specified all required input, the user will be in a position
to generate the fracture mechanics related curves (e.g. J vs time, J vs opening).
Third, the opportunity of creating J surfaces will be offered to the user to analyze the
impact of varying some parameters within a ±25% range.
Finally, with the aim at supporting the user with useful information in the context of the
DCB­UBM specimens manufacturing, a design tool is offered to GUI­4 users where ranges
of J as a function of the applied loads are provided as a function of design parameters
such as layer widths (i.e. bLk, bUk) and heights (i.e. HLk, HUk).
The geometry and material properties of the DCB specimen dealt with in this GUI are

7.1.1
Impact of layers’ Young modulus on J integral
The results shown in Fig. 7.1 and 7.2 are obtained for the DCB sample described in
Tab. 6.1­6.2. In Fig. 7.1 the Young module of the three layers is changed within a range
of ±25% with respect to the nominal value specified in Tab. 6.1. The layers labeling is
defined as follows:
• L1: layer number 1 of the lower beam,
• U1: layer number 1 of the upper beam,
• U2:layer number 2 of the upper beam.
As visible from that figure the J integral, among the three Young moduli for the layers L1,
U1 and U2, the one which has the strongest impact on the J integral is the one of layer
L1. This appears to be coherent with the fact that, when changing either the one of U1 or
U2, only one of the two materials of the upper beam is changed while keeping the rest of
the specimen the same. On the other hand, when changing the young module of L1, in
the present scenario, corresponds to changing completely the lower beam.
As further discussed in Sec. 8.1, the Performance Analysis can be used to assess the
impact of the uncertainty on the value of the Young module used to characterize the ma­
terial used in the DCB. In the present case, following the results shown in Fig. 7.1, the
J integral appears to be more sensitive to Young moduli variation for layer L1 rather that
layers U1 and U2. As a result, uncertainty on the measurement of the Young modulus of
layer L1 would have a higher impact on the J integral than the uncertainties associated
with the Young modulus of layers U1 or U2.
7.1.2
Impact of layers’ width on J integral
In Fig. 7.2 the width of the three layers is changed within a range of ±25% with respect to
the nominal value specified in Tab. 6.1. From the analysis of Fig. 7.2 a higher impact on
the J integral is observed for the layer widths than Young modulus. This impact increases
when the width decreases below its nominal value for each of the two layers above and
below the fracture plane in line with the fact that the J integral, [Eq. (2.5), (4.5)], scales2
with 1/Bk. As for the case discussed in Sec. 7.1.1, the results shown in Fig. 7.2 confirm
that it would be preferable to have a lower uncertainty on the measurement of the layer
width for L1 rather than for U1 or U2. Indeed, the J integral appears to be more sensible
to the variation of the width of layer L1 rather than U1 and U2.

In the previous chapters a detailed analysis of the main achievements of this project was
presented. In this section, a summary of these contributions is given in relation to the
objectives of this Master project.
GUI­1: a graphical user interface has been developed allowing users to simulate
fracture behavior of symmetric homogeneous DCB samples with un­even bending.
The J integral and the crack opening are calculated from a set of geometrical and
time dependent load input data. This objective was defined in the low­risk objectives
of the project.
GUI­2: a graphical user interface has been developed allowing users to simulate
fracture behaviors of asymmetric heterogeneous multi­layers DCB with un­even
bending for a single time instant load scenario. The J integral is calculated from
a set of geometrical and measured load and displacement input data. This objec­
tive was defined in the high­risk objectives of the project.
GUI­3: a graphical user interface has been developed allowing users to simulate
fracture behaviors of asymmetric heterogeneous multi­layers DCB with un­even
bending, normal loads and thermal residual stresses for a single time instant load
scenario. The J integral and other stress and geometrical parameters, such as layer
stress and curvature, are calculated from a set of geometrical and load input data.
This was defined in the high­risk objectives of the project.
GUI­4: a graphical user interface has been developed allowing users to simulate
fracture behaviors of asymmetric heterogeneous multi­layers DCB with un­even
bending and thermal residual stresses. The J integral and the crack opening, as
well as other stress and geometrical parameters, are calculated from a set of geo­
metrical and load input data. This objective was defined in the high­risk objectives
of the project.
Parametric Analysis: together with GUI­4, comes a module for performing a para­
metric analysis. Its importance is particularly appreciated in the context of the eval­
uation of the impact of the uncertainty intrinsically present in the measuring of test
input parameters. Indeed, every test starts with the choice of a DCB geometry and
material distribution among the different layers. The geometry is defined by the
layer widths and thicknesses. These dimensions are measured with e.g. a caliper
and as such come with an intrinsic measurement uncertainty. The J integral, via
the extensional stiffness coefficients [Eq. (2.5), (4.5)], scales1 with 1/Bk. Hence,
performing a parametric analysis of J as a function of layers’ width provides useful
insights on the impact of layer’s width uncertainty on J integral calculations since
it shows how J varies with a varying Bk. The same applies to the uncertainty on
layers’ height and Young’s moduli. Regarding the latter one, an interesting case is
provided by wood. Indeed, as further described in [29], the standard deviation of
Young module experimentally determined data, is high leading to a high uncertainty
on the values to be used for testing DCB specimens comprising wooden layers.

2. the interfacial strength2, and
3. the load that the testing machine is able to produce.
The interfacial strength and related properties represent the primary parameters of
interest in experimental testing. The applied load is predefined and constrained by
the capabilities of the testing equipment. Fracture resistance is influenced by both
the material properties and the geometry of the sample. The influence of these two
factors on the J integral can be assessed using the tool, which operates indepen­
dently of physical experiments, enabling evaluation during the design phase and
reducing reliance on trial­and­error testing.
For example, when facing the choice of selecting the values of different layers
heights, it is known that the J initegral, via the bending stiffness coefficients [Eq. (2.5),
(4.7)], scales3 in a non linear manner with 1/H3

the J lines for several values of different layers’ helps understanding the impact of
this non linear dependency on J thereby simplifying the selection of specific height
values. This objective was defined during the progress of the project and was not
foreseen at project initiation stage.
8.2
Conclusions
”This Master’s project focuses on wind turbine blade technology, with a central emphasis
on the fracture behavior of DCB (Double Cantilever Beam) specimens. All developed tools
are unified by this common theme. The cases examined involve fractures with large­scale
fibre bridging, a phenomenon whose understanding is critical to the effective design of
wind turbine blades. As described in Ch. 1, the lifespan and failure resistance of several
wind turbine blades’ components are affected by delamination. Indeed, delamination can
occur in several key components, including the spar caps, shear webs, and skin layers. It
typically arises at the interfaces between different composite layers or between the core
and face sheets in sandwich structures, especially under fatigue loading, manufacturing
defects, or impact damage. Hence, the tools developed in this project can be used in the
assessment of delamination in one of the above mentioned components. In addition, the
parametric analysis, and more specifically the design tool developed in the framework of
this master project, can be used as a support during the DBC­UBM samples design phase
by reducing reliance on trial­and­error testing.

9.1
Further testing
This project has analyzed a limited set of test cases, which constrains the current un­
derstanding of how various parameters influence the measurement fracture resistance

needed—beginning with the collection of existing experimental data, and followed by the

9.2
Design tool : stress lines
Among the assumptions made in the derivation of the general solution for the J integral
presented in [6] and recalled in Eq. (2.5), the elastic behavior of the material in the different
layers of each beam is a key one. This ensures that the sample failure is dominated
by the fracture initiation and growth mechanisms described in Ch. 2 instead of plastic
deformation. However, experience tells that sometime one of the layers of the upper
or lower beams fails before the DCB sample itself. This layer failure is induced by the
local stress level being higher than the yield stress for the specific layer material. As
a result, the test carried out under such circumstances is of little use and the invested
resources for its performance are wasted. To reduce the risk of such an unwished failure,
an improved version of the design tool could be implemented, or the current tool could be
applied differently, according to the following logic. Instead of plotting a set of J lines as
a function of the applied moment M2, a set of σ lines could be plotted for each layer of
the upper and lower beams. As it is assumed that σ varies linearly within each layer, the
actual version of GUI­4 already computes the σ at the bottom and the top of each layer
and saves the corresponding values in the output file. Hence, at least two options are
available.
• Option 1: the routine used to generate the graphs depicted in e.g. Fig. 6.6 can be
modified to plot the σ at the top and the bottom of a specific layer to be input by
the user, or for all layers in multiple tabs. This can be done as the values of σ are
already stored in the output variables to be written in the output file.
• Option 2: the current tool can be used for different load scenarios to produce several
values of σ, calculated via Eq. (5.15), for the bottom and top of each layers and
then use the obtained data to plot a graph comprising several σ lines as the one
obtainable by modifying the routine according to Option 1.

visible in Fig. 9.1, the stress level will vary with the applied moment for a given set of

width by e.g. ±10% of the nominal value, the σ curve will be displaced as schematically
represented in Fig. 9.1. It is worth noticing that the schematic representation is really

A.3.1
Description of the openNewExcel function
The openNewExcel function is designed to automate the generation of a structured input
Excel file.
Input Parameters
• N_L: Number of lower layers (input via GUI element edtColumnsA)
• N_U: Number of upper layers (input via GUI element edtColumnsB)
• isThermalStressPresent: Boolean flag indicating whether thermal stress parame­

Main Functionalities
1. Input Validation: Ensures that the number of upper and lower layers is a valid

2. Header Generation: Based on the number of layers and the presence of thermal

• Elastic moduli (ELi, EUi)
• Poisson’s ratios (νLi, νUi)
• Thermal expansion coefficients (αLi, αUi), if applicable
• Load data: axial loads (N1, N2) and moments (M1, M2)
• Temperature difference term (∆T), if thermal stress is considered
3. File Saving: Prompts the user to save the Excel file through a file dialog interface.
4. Excel Sheet Creation:
• Initializes the Excel file using writecell with headers and a placeholder empty
row.
• Opens the Excel application and loads the saved file via ActiveX.
• Inserts an illustrative image (if available) for reference.
• Applies lock/unlock rules: all cells are locked except the second row, where
the user can enter data.
• Applies alternating color formatting (light and dark yellow) for each data block
to improve readability.
• Draws grid lines and applies bold borders between blocks to visually segment
different parameter groups.

Description of the Parametric Analysis Function
1 Function Workflow
The PerformParametricAnalysisButtonPushed function is a callback (Fig. A.9) executed
when the user initiates a parametric analysis via the corresponding button. The function
proceeds through the following logical stages:
1. Input Initialization: All relevant input fields (inputValue1 to inputValue4) are reset
to NaN to clear any residual data from previous runs.
2. Option Selection: The function invokes a dialog box (showOptionDialog_PerfAnal)
to allow the user to select a specific geometric parameter for analysis [Fig. 6.4]. If
no selection is made, an alert is issued and the function exits.
3. Input Validation: Based on the selected option, the function validates the corre­
sponding input value to ensure it is a positive integer within defined bounds (either
NL or NU, depending on the type of parameter).
4. Index Calculation: An index idx is calculated to locate the appropriate parameter
in the GeomMat_Data matrix. The calculation logic varies depending on the selected
option.
5. Parametric Sweep Setup: The center value of the selected geometric parameter
is varied by ±25%, generating a set of 10 evenly spaced values. These represent
the parametric variation for which the analysis is carried out.
6. Computation Loop: For each parametric value and for each time step, the function
computes the J integral using the ComputeJIntegral routine. Results are stored in
