Abstract
Wind energy is set to play a vital role in the green transition, yet the deployment of existing technology
is constrained by limited available locations. Floating wind energy addresses this issue by unlocking the
rich potential of deep sea-waters. However, the current cost of this technology remains high. At the same
time, concerns arise regarding the impact of wind farms on the touristic activity of coastal areas. The aim
of this thesis is to develop a methodology for optimizing the layout of floating wind farms by balancing
two critical factors: Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE) and Visual Impact (VI). This multi-objective
optimization utilizes a Vector Evaluated Particle Swarm Optimization (VEPSO) algorithm to maximize
the economic feasibility of floating wind farms while preserving the natural beauty and touristic activity
of coastal regions. The proposed methodology incorporates criteria that reflect local touristic activity to
assess the visual impact of offshore wind farms, departing from the principles of the Spanish method. In
parallel, an LCOE calculation algorithm is used to evaluate site-specific techno-economic parameters such
as local wind conditions, depth, length of electrical cables, and mooring lines. The layout-specific results
of the VI and LCOE analysis are combined through the VEPSO for a multi-objective optimization of the
layout.
Two of the recently announced offshore wind locations in Greece, Crete 1 (East of Crete) and Crete 2b
(North of Elounda), are explored as case studies. The method effectively balances the trade-off between
economic and visual concerns. The results underline the potential of the method to support the sustainable
development of floating wind farms and increase local support through optimal design.
Keywords: Wind Energy, Offshore Wind, Floating Wind, VEPSO, Layout Optimization, Visual Impact,
LCOE minimization, Multi-Objective Optimization, Spanish Method

Introduction
The climate crisis stands as one of the most pressing challenges of our era, with the use of fossil fuels for
energy production being one of the roots of this problem. The consequences of climate crisis make the
transition from fossil fuels to renewable energy sources more urgent than ever before. Wind energy is set to
play a vital role in this transition. The plan of the EU includes 60GW of offshore wind energy installed by
2030 and at least 300GW by 2050 [1], however, at the end of 2023 the installed capacity was only 19 GW
[2]. At the same time the share of offshore wind in the power mix was only 2% in 2023 with wind energy
in total, covering 19% of EU’s electricity demand, when the EU target for 2030 is 35 % [1]. Achieving this
target requires a significant acceleration in wind energy deployment, but the available locations onshore
are limited. Offshore wind resources are much greater allowing for higher energy production, however the
deployment of fixed-bottom offshore wind foundations is constrained to waters shallower than 60 meters
due to high foundation costs [2], significantly limiting the available locations for such installations.
Floating wind energy is a new technology in the renewable sector, that has the potential to harness the
reach wind resource of deep sea waters. This novel technology uses the experience gained by the oil
industry in the development of floating foundations in order to accelerate the green transition. Unlike
fixed-bottom offshore wind, floating wind uses a floating platform that is anchored to the seabed as
foundation, with the turbine placed on top. This technology comes to surpass the depth obstacle opening
up vast new areas for development, particularly in locations with deep waters such as the Mediterranean
sea. Floating wind allows for the exploitation of better and more consistent wind resources that are located
far from the coastline. However, it presents also significant challenges. The increased complexity and the
immaturity of the supply chain, results in a higher cost compared to fixed-bottom foundations. According
to WindEurope and ETIPWind[3], the cost of floating wind in 2020 was around 184 e/MWh, while at
the same time the cost of bottom-fixed offshore wind was 86 e/MWh. Following the trend presented in
figure 1 the LCOE of floating offshore wind is expected to reach the same levels as bottom-fixed only in
2050.
Figure 1: Offshore Wind Cost Projection [3]
The development of a strong supply chain is necessary to decrease the high cost of floating wind [4], since
the shallow water locations available for fixed-bottom installations are limited.

Layout optimization techniques can mitigate the problems associated with the development of floating
wind farms. An optimized layout can have a 3% lower LCOE according to the Corewind project [13].
In addition layout optimization can help reduce also the visual impact of the wind farm by placing the
turbines in locations where they are less visible from the coast. In this study a heuristic algorithm called
Vector Evaluated Particle Swarm Optimization (VEPSO) is employed in order to identify an optimzal
layout. VEPSO departs from the principles of simple Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) it was developed
by Parsopoulos, Vrahatis [14] on the basis of Vector Evaluated Genetic Algorithm (VEGA) [15]. This
algorithm optimizes multiple swarms at the same time following the methodology of the simple PSO [16]
as it will be explained in the next paragraphs.
The problem to be explored in this thesis is the multi-objective optimization of floating wind farms, in
order to balance the need for clean renewable energy with the preservation of the natural and visual
beauty of coastal areas. This problem is subject to the following Research Questions:
1. How can layout optimization reduce the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) of floating
wind farms to enhance economic feasibility?
2. What methods can be developed to quantify the visual impact of floating wind farms
in coastal areas?
3. How can an optimization approach be implemented to simultaneously minimize the
LCOE and visual impact of floating wind farms?
This optimization includes two main objectives that will be co-optimized in order to achieve favorable
results in both aspects. The first objective is the cost minimization. In this case the cost is expressed
through the LCOE. Floating wind farms present high costs due their immature supply chain and complex
installation [17]. Therefore, there is a need to develop a method that will minimize the LCOE of the wind
farm to make the completion of the project economically feasible, unlock the full potential of offshore
wind and provide low-cost clean energy. The second objective of this optimization is the minimization of
the visual impact. Offshore wind farms are often located in sites close to touristic areas and may have an
impact to the local touristic activity due to their visual impact [18]. As a result the visual impact of the
wind farms must be minimized to maintain the aesthetic appeal and attractiveness of the coastal areas.

1.1
Motivation
Greece is one of the countries with the highest wind resource potential in the Mediterranean. With the
areas having the highest potential often located offshore; one can easily understand the development of
wind projects in those areas could come in contrast with the vibrant touristic activity of the Greek shores.
In addition, the deep waters of the Mediterranean Sea make the development of offshore wind farms more
challenging and expensive, since in most cases the depth exceeds 100m. The motivation of this thesis is to
unlock the rich offshore wind potential of the Greek Seas and other locations with similar characteristics
by developing a methodology that will minimize the cost of deep-water offshore wind projects, while at
the same time reducing the visual impact, in a way that the intense touristic activity of the Greek shores
will not be affected by the realization of those projects.
1.2
Innovation
The methodology developed during this thesis will introduce a novel approach to the current layout
optimization techniques.
This approach introduces a combined optimization of both cost and visual
impact aspects of an offshore wind farm, unlike conventional methods that tend to approach cost and
visual impact as separate entities often leading to sub-optimal results in both aspects. Moreover, this
research presents a new visual impact assessment point of view. This method departs from the parameters
and the logic behind the Spanish Method, applied in a more holistic approach with multiple viewpoints,
making it more suitable to offshore wind farms. It takes into account aspects such as the population and
the touristic activity of an area, the distance to the wind farm and the portion of a turbine visible from
each viewpoint.

2
Literature Review
After the completion of the literature review the key findings are presented below.
2.1
Visual Impact Methodology
The first part of the developed methodology is the assessment of wind farm’s visual impact. The visual
impact of wind farms is highly subjective.
Quantifying it is challenging, as it depends on personal
perceptions, preferences, and various individual factors. Although the importance of wind farm’s impact
on the shore is recognized in the literature, current research around the topic is still limited [20].
The literature related to the assessment of visual impact includes different approaches to the topic. One of
the approaches includes 3D visualisations of the wind farm. This method is combined with questionnaires
filled out by the local population [21]. The values of nuisance are calculated based on the replies of the
local inhabitants for different layouts and distances from the viewpoints. This approach is the most widely
used in the literature as it directly involves the opinions of the locals in the design process, ensuring that
the visual impact of the wind farm is minimized according to local preferences [22]. The combination
of 3D visualizations with questionnaires is a method that has been used in both onshore and offshore
locations.
Another approach applied in the literature for the assessment of visual impact is the Spanish Method [23].
This method was developed by Hurtado et. al [23] and attempts to quantify the visual impact through
different coefficients. Those coefficients are related to distance from a settlement, number of turbines
visible, population of the settlement and others. The values of the coefficients suggested by Hurtado
have been reviewed by more modern research and more modern approaches have been suggested [24] [20].
Those suggestions manage to improve the performance of the method and adapt it to more modern times
often through participation of local populations through questionnaires [20]
Finally, the third approach followed in the literature to follow the guidelines of the state legislation and
then compare it to the opinion of the locals again through questionnaires. This method is proposed by
Gkeka-Serpetsidaki [25]. The location examined is the same as the one presented in this thesis (Lasithi,
Crete). The guidelines of the Greek legislation are compared against the opinion of the locals for the
visual impact of an offshore wind farm.
2.2
Optimization Algorithm
Multiple approaches have been chosen in the literature in order to find the optimum wind farm layout
using the coordinates of wind turbines as decision variables.
The Gradient-based algorithms are based on the evaluation of derivatives of the objective function in
every position on the search space [26]. In other words, the algorithm moves towards the direction of the
steepest descent, to find the global minimum. At each iteration, the gradient is computed at the current
parameter values, and the parameters are updated to move closer to a minimum of the objective function
[27]. This procedure continues until the changes in the objective function value fall below a predefined
threshold, meaning that the algorithm has reached an optimum solution.
The Genetic Algorithm (GA) is a heuristic algorithm inspired by natural selection. The algorithm uses
multiple generations to find the optimal solution. Each generation consists of individuals who are updated
based on their fitness to the objective function [26]. Each individual represents a position in the search
Page 5 of 67
==PAGE:17==
space and a solution. In each generation of a GA, a selection process occurs, pairing individuals from
the current population based on the quality of their solutions to contribute genetic material for the next
generation. These pairs are combined to produce new solutions, known as child solutions. This blend
aims to retain good characteristics from the parent solutions while introducing randomness to prevent
stagnation in local optima. This iterative process of selection continues until a significant portion of the
population is replaced, and the overall quality of solutions improves. Typically, the GA iterates for a
number of generations or until sufficient diversity is achieved within the population [13].
The PSO is also a heuristic optimization algorithm inspired by the behaviour of bird swarms[16]. In this
algorithm each individual inside the swarm, known as particle, explores the search space to find a solution
for the objective function. If a particle finds a better solution than the current best of the swarm then
it updates the best solution making the rest of the swarm move in this direction. The position of each
particle is adjusted at the end of every iteration based on the current best-known solution. This iterative
process continues for multiple iterations. Under this structure all particles collaborate with each other in
finding the best possible solution[15].
The random search (RS) algorithm is the simplest algorithm from the ones used in the literature. This
algorithm uses an initial feasible layout and starts moving the turbines randomly in the search space
[28]. At each step the algorithm chooses one turbine randomly and changes its position randomly. If the
objective value is improve the chosen turbine moves again randomly to another position. If the objective
value is not improved a different turbine is chosen [29].
Variations of the presented methods are used for multi-objective optimization. Although the cases of
multi-objective layout optimization are rare in the literature, two are the main techniques used to adjust
the presented methods to multi-objective optimization in general. The first is the modification of the
objective function to include both objectives. In this case, all objectives are weighted and summed to
one objective function. Then the algorithm is used as it would be used for single-objective optimization
[30]. The second method is a Pareto-based approach, this approach includes the separate calculation of all
objective functions, then a Pareto front of the non-dominated solutions is created. The literature following
this technique includes variations of widely used algorithms such as the PSO and Genetic Algorithm. One
of those variations is the vector-evaluated particle swarm optimization (VEPSO)[16]. This method is very
similar to the traditional PSO. The main difference is that all objectives are evaluated separately by a
separate swarm and its position update is affected by the results of the other objectives [14]. A similar
approach is followed by the vector-evaluated genetic algorithm (VEGA). Finally, the Non-dominated
Sorting Genetic Algorithm (NSGA) can also be used. In NSGA, instead of picking individuals randomly,
they are grouped into fronts based on how they compare to each other. This means that not everyone in
a front will move on to the next stage. This sorting helps keep a wide range of options open and helps
the algorithm make better choices [31].
2.3

literature include among others CoE/LCOE minimization, AEP maximization and NPV maximization.
Annual Energy Production (AEP) maximization is used as the objective in the optimization process
[26]. This objective although is not the most widespread is utilized by some commercial software such
as WindFarmer[32], OpenWind[33] and WindPro[34]. AEP maximization is also used in research papers
combined with gradient-based optimization algorithms [35] [36] or Particle Swarm Optimization [37].

2.5
Overview
The main methods to assess the visual impact of a wind farm presented in the literature are the Spanish
method and 3D Visualizations combined with questionnaires. However both of those methods cannot be
applied to a multi-objective layout optimization process since they require a fixed layout and not a layout
that changes dynamically inside the optimization process. For this purpose a novel methodology will be
developed, making use of the coefficients employed by the Spanish method as they were reviewed by more
modern literature [24]. In addition a more holistic visual impact analysis including a larger number of
viewpoints will be conducted as dictated by the increased size of offshore wind farms.
Most of the methods presented take into consideration only the visual impact on a number of settlements.
Tsarknias [46] highlighted the importance of adding more viewpoint categories than settlements, those
categories could have an important impact on the touristic activity of the coastal areas.
As recent
events around the development of floating wind farms in the island of Crete showed [47]; the existence
of an important monument or increased number of tourists in the bathing shores significantly affect the
perception of the locals about the visual impact of a wind farm. This is the reason it has been decided
to include multiple viewpoint categories and not only settlements in this visual impact assessment as
suggested by the literature [46].

The majority of the literature for wind farms layout optimization uses heuristic and meta-heuristic algo-
rithms to find the most optimal solution. The two methods mostly used are Particle Swarm Optimization
(PSO) and Genetic Algorithms (GA). When those two methods are compared one can notice that PSO
requires fewer parameters and computational power. In PSO particles adjust their positions based on the
current known global best (GBEST) that could be found by any particle, this way particles collaborate to-
wards finding the best solution [13]. However, one of the known downsides of PSO is that it could converge
prematurely. In contrast GA could explore a large space better but require more time and parameters,
since each generation competes with the previous one to find the best solution. In general GA requires
more parameter tuning making it more difficult to calibrate. A third approach includes Gradient-based
algorithms that could be a simple method to the optimal solution. However, those algorithms include
downsides such as the fact that they could very easily converge to a local optimum potentially disregard-
ing superior solutions elsewhere [27]. Finally, less complex methods such as the random search algorithm
(RS) are considered, although the randomness of those methods is significantly increased lowering the
probability of finding the global optimum solution [28].

Methodology
The multi-objective optimization presented bellow covers the full area defined for the placement of the
wind farm known as the wind farm polygon, or polygon, in order to find the best trade-off between the
two objectives. The methodology of a combined LCOE and Visual Impact (VI) minimization is presented
in three main parts in this section.
3.1
Visual Impact Minimization
The novel methodology of the visual impact minimization presented in this thesis departs from the
principles and the logic behind the Spanish method [23]. This same approach is used on a more macro-
scale context, since the method was originally used in a limited number of villages. The coefficients of
the Spanish Method are adjusted according to more modern studies that take into account the increased
size of wind turbines during the more recent years, including the 2nd Spanish Method [24].
The increased size of offshore wind farms creates the need for a standardized method of evaluating the
visual impact. Due to their size, offshore wind farms are visible from multiple points that, in contrast to
the original Spanish method [23] addressing only settlement viewpoints, may include important cultural
spots, sites of natural beauty and others.
Therefore, the coefficients of the Spanish Method used in
this study serve the purpose of expressing the visual impact in both settlements and sites with touristic
activity. Under this reasoning three main categories of viewpoints are considered: Settlements, Bathing
Waters and Archaeological Sites-Monuments. In addition, the large size of offshore wind farms makes
impossible the calculation of coefficients (a),(b) and (c) as they are presented in the original Spanish
Method, since the increased number of viewpoints increases the complexity in calculating what portion
of the wind farm is visible from each village (Coefficient a), what portion of the houses of each village is
visible from the wind farm (Coefficient b) and what is the view angle (Frontal,Diagonal or Longitudinal)
from each village to the wind farm (Coefficient c). As a result, the portion of the wind farm visible from
each viewpoint will be expressed in a different way that will be presented in the next paragraphs, along
with a new approach to coefficient b. Finally Coefficient c will not be considered since it assumes a cuboid
shape, that is not relevant to an optimized layout, at the same time the method of calculation used would
significantly increase the complexity for an offshore wind farm. The thresholds for the population (e)
and distance (d) coefficients of the original Spanish Method will be adjusted according to more modern
literature and they will be used in the presented approach.

3.1.1
Viewpoint Categories
In order to examine the impact of the wind farm’s visual impact in the touristic activities three main
categories will be considered: Settlements, Bathing Waters and Archaeological Sites-Monuments. The
selection of those categories are based on the criteria used to assess the visual impact for the suitable
sitting of offshore wind farms in the same areas to those studied during the case studies of this thesis [46]
[59].
The settlements considered for the layout optimization include all local settlements located within the
Viewshed of the defined wind farm polygon. Each local settlement corresponds to one viewpoint. The
data for each settlement and its population are extracted from the latest population census [60].
The bathing waters considered for the analysis are the ones located within the Viewshed of the polygon.
For EU countries the bathing shores that are monitored by the European Marine Observation and Data
Network (EMODnet) [61] for the quality of their waters are considered relevant for the analysis according
to the literature [46].
The third viewpoint category used for the assessment of the visual impact is the Archaeological sites
- Monuments of the area, as defined by the Archaeological Cadastre [62]. In this case all the known
archaeological sites and monuments located within the Viewshed are used for the visual impact assessment.
3.1.2
Coefficients
As already mentioned the Visual Impact analysis of this thesis is based on the principals of the original
Spanish Method [23]. In this paragraph all coefficients used in the Spanish Method will be presented
and adjusted based on the literature in order to serve the purpose of quantifying the visual impact of an
offshore wind farm.
Coefficient (a) expresses the visibility of the wind farm to each village. This coefficient is calculated as the
number of turbines that are visible from the village divided by the total number of wind turbines of the
wind farm [23] [24]. In this methodology the portion of visible wind turbines will not be expressed under
a coefficient since the nature of offshore wind farms that are visible by multiple viewpoints along with
the increased size of the wind turbines used would increase the complexity in extreme levels. However in
the developed methodology the visual impact of each wind turbine is calculated individually based on its
location. Subsequently the visual impact of all wind turbines is summed and then divided by the total
number of turbines in the wind farm. As a result turbines that are not visible by the viewpoints have a
zero VI reducing the total visual impact of the layout in a similar way to Coefficient (a). In contrast, the
Page 11 of 67
==PAGE:23==
original Spanish method calculates the visual impact for the wind farm as a whole, creating the need for
Coefficient (a).
In the original Spanish Method Coefficient (b) is used to express the visibility of the village from the wind
farm, by the ratio of number of buildings that are visible from the park and total buildings [24]. Again
this approach is not implementable to the presented methodology since the method not only involves
multiple settlements instead of only one examined by SPM, but also includes multiple viewpoints such as
bathing waters and monuments that are not applicable to this approach. In this research Coefficient (b)
will be used to illustrate what portion of each turbine is visible from each viewpoint following the visual
exposure as described in the literature [63]. According to this approach the visual impact decreases with
the reduction of visible turbine part. This reduction could be attributed to obstacles such as small hills

from the viewpoint. The reduction is linear and Coefficient (b) is the ratio of Size visible to tip height as

3.1.3
Visual Impact (VI) Analysis
For the purpose of the visual impact minimization a raster is created. This raster covers the considered
polygon with a resolution of 25mX25m. The raster is created in order to calculate the values of the Visual
Impact Coefficients for each point within the polygon. In contrast to the SPM where the calculation
takes places in a static environment where the placement of each turbine is fixed, during the optimization
process the placement of the turbines is dynamic with multiple relocations. Since the calculation of the
coefficients takes place before the optimization process, instead of calculating the VI coefficients based on
the positions of the turbines, one value is calculated for each point located at the center of each 25mX25m
cell. Those points will be called target points. Finally during the optimization process the VI value for
each turbine is calculated based on its position in the polygon.
As mentioned, the first step of the Visual Impact analysis is the creation of the Viewshed based on the
polygon of the wind farm. This analysis takes as input the Digital Elevation model of the area (DEM),
the observer height and the target height. For the analysis the tip height of the turbine is defined as
target height, along with a an observer height of 1.7 meters [67]. The tip height is selected as target
height according to [63] since for distances within the radius, even a part of the blade could have a visual
impact. In case only a small part of the turbine is visible, the impact will later be adjusted based on
coefficient (b). The radius of the analysis is limited to 45km as explained in the previous paragraph.
Finally the earth’s curvature is taken into account, with an atmospheric refraction coefficient of 0.13 [68].
Page 13 of 67
==PAGE:25==
The result of the Viewshed is the areas where at least one point of the 25X25m grid is visible from.
Subsequently the viewpoints that are located within the boundaries of the Viewshed are identified for the
next step of the analysis, those points will be called observer points. The Intervisibility network GIS tool
will be then used to identify if there is visual contact between the observer and target points. The inputs
of this tool are the observer and target points, the DEM and the atmospheric refraction coefficient, while
earth’s curvature is again taken into account. The height of target points is defined at the tip height of the
turbine while the observer height is again 1.7m. The result is a matrix that presents which observer points
are visible from each target point along with the target size that is visible. Finally the distances between
observer and target points are calculated through the distance matrix tool. This analysis takes place three
times, one for each set of viewpoints (Settlements, Bathing Waters, Archaeological Sites-Monuments).
The results of the GIS Processing will be used to calculate the values of VI. Coefficient (b) will be
calculated by dividing the target size with the tip height of the turbine as mentioned in the previous
paragraph. Coefficient (b) is calculated for each observer point that is visible from a target point, if an
observer point is not visible from the target point the value of coefficient (b) is zero. The values for all
observer points are then summed to result in one value for each target point. For example if we have five
viewpoints and a target point is visible from only three of them with a coefficient (b) of 0.3 for each one
of them then the value of coefficient (b) for this target point is 0.9. Coefficient (d) is calculated based
on the thresholds selected (in this case study as displayed in figure 5) and coefficient (e) is calculated
based on the population coefficient function developed for each case, (table 4). In a similar way one
value is calculated for each observer point visible from a target point, then those values are summed.
Finally coefficients (b) and (d) are multiplied to calculate the VI for Bathing Waters and Archaeological
Sites-Monuments in each target point (i) according to equation 7

Mooring System
The length of mooring lines and the mooring footprint are important aspects of the optimization process.
The increased cost of mooring lines for deeper sites may force the algorithm to move to more shallow
areas to reduce the LCOE. At the same the ”mooring footprint”—the circle around the turbine defined
by the maximum reach of the mooring lines—serves as a boundary to prevent overlapping mooring lines.
It is a common practice to avoid overlapping mooring lines for floating wind farms, in order to minimize
any risk of entanglement or uneven load distribution that could lead to failure [29] [79].

The methods of Visual Impact Analysis and LCOE calculation are combined through the VEPSO algo-
rithm as the two objectives of the multi-objective optimization.
3.4.1
Problem Formulation
As mentioned the aim of the presented methodology is the optimal placement of the wind turbines in
order to find a solution that serves both objectives of LCOE and Visual Impact minimization. For this
reason the decision variables of the optimization problem are:
Page 21 of 67
==PAGE:33==
• Turbine Positions (Latitude and Longitude)
• Offshore Substation Position (Latitude and Longitude)
As will explained in detail in case study presentation, the position and the capacity of the onshore
substation are already defined by the state; as a result they are not variables. The available capacity of
the substation dictates the capacity of the wind farm. For the case studies presented a 15 MW turbine
is used, therefore the number of turbines is pre-defined (not decided by the algorithm) by dividing the
capacity of the wind farm with the capacity of the turbine. The material and the configuration of mooring
lines (catenary) and electrical cables (lazy wave) is also defined in advance, along with the type of anchor.
Finally an initial layout is imported to the VEPSO before the first iteration. This layout serves the
purpose of guiding the VEPSO to feasible solutions, since a random initial distribution led to unfeasible
results. This initial layout consists of a 8RD Downwind X 5RD Crossing, turbine grid according to the
distances used in the literature [99]. The connection grid (turbine connection in each string and turbines
connected to the substation) is also defined by the initial layout and the connection pattern is not possible
to be changed by the algorithm. However the length of the connecting cables is possible to change. All
those variables are fixed to decrease the complexity of the optimization and allow to find optimal results in
reasonable time. The introduction of more decision variables in the optimization process would increase
exponentially the complexity and possible combinations of variables, and most probably decrease the
efficiency of the method.

3.4.2
VEPSO algorithm
The vector-evaluated particle swarm optimization (VEPSO) was introduced by Parsopoulos [14]. The
algorithm uses the same concept as the vector-evaluated genetic algorithm (VEGA). The only change
compared to the original PSO is introduced during the step of velocity update. Velocity is the difference
in the position of a particle between two concecutive iterations [16]. The VEPSO is able to optimize
multiple objectives using equal number of swarms. In the case of LCOE and VI minimization two swarms
are used, one for each objective. A swarm is a set of particles that moves in the search space to find
an optimal solution. In this case the search space is the area covered by the polygon of the wind farm.
The algorithm evolves around two variables the global best (GBEST), that is the best solution currently

==PAGE:35==

The first step of the algorithm is to find an initial set of turbine positions as mentioned in the previous
paragraph; then the two objective functions are calculated for all particles of the two swarms. In case the
selected positions do not satisfy the constraints the solution is greatly penalized with a very high value
of LCOE and VI, so the unfeasible solution is not chosen (Penalty Function). The execution then stops
and the algorithm updates the positions again.
If the solution found is better than the particle’s PBEST then the PBEST is updated. If the solution is
better than the GBEST of the Swarm then the GBEST is updated. It is noteworthy that although both
objective values are calculated, the GBEST value of a Swarm are updated only by the Particles of the
same Swarm. For example if the Swarm optimizing the VI finds a Solution with LCOE lower than the
GBEST of the Swarm optimizing the LCOE the GBEST will not be updated.
The next step is the one that differentiates VEPSO to the rest of optimization methods, the velocity
update [14].
The velocity of each Swarm is updated using the GBEST of the other Swarm and the
PBEST that is optimizing the objective of the current Swarm .This way the particles don’t move fast to
the GBEST as they are pulled by both their PBEST and the GBEST of the other Swarm, finding more
solutions in the area between the two optima. This leads to higher probability of finding a good balance
between both objectives. The velocity is updated after each iteration (t), based on the previous velocity

3.4.3
Modifications on the VEPSO
A number of modifications were introduced to the standard VEPSO algorithm in order to improve its
performance and make it more suitable to the problem explored.
It was noticed that by starting the algorithm with a random distribution of the turbines it was very
rare to start with a feasible solution, due to the constraints of the problem.
As a result, it would
take a lot of iterations just to come up with a purely performing layout that a layout drawn manually
would outperform. The solution was to import an initial layout to the algorithm with a relatively good
performance, then the algorithm could use it as a driver and improve upon [13].
The second modification is related to one of the weaknesses of the PSO, which is converging to local
instead of global optima. To address this issue the particles of the algorithm as scattered uniformly across
the full search space for the first iteration instead of being randomly allocated. This technique allows for
better exploration of more possible possible solutions across the full search space and reduces the risk of
converging prematurely to a local optimum [104].
According to the original VEPSO the GBEST n is updated unless there is a solution with better fitness
to the n-objective. This means that in case there is a solution with equally good fitness to the n-objective
and better fitness in the n+1-objective, the GBEST n is not updated. The algorithm has been modified
to update the GBEST also in this case as suggested in the literature [16].
The final modification is to run the optimization based on a maximum run-time and not a maximum
number of iterations. The reason to do that is to reduce the effect of non-feasible solutions in the process.
Since non-feasible solutions take only a fraction of a second to run, the simulation duration depends only
on the feasible solutions. In contrast if a maximum number of iterations was used, the unfeasible solutions
would take a large part of the total iterations and the feasible solutions would be only a small fraction of
the total iterations [13].

Case Study
The developed methodology is applied in two different case studies in collaboration with the company,
iWind Renewables. The development of the case studies is based on the recently announced areas for
development of offshore wind farms in Greece [105]. In 2023 HEREMA announced the first tenders for
the first ever offshore wind farms in Greece. The locations are pre-defined by HEREMA and the first
projects are expected to be completed by 2032 [106]. Due to the high water depth of the Greek seas
most of the locations are deeper than 60m. Therefore, only 4 out of 23 announced locations are expected
to be fixed-bottom [105]. The examined locations present a significant wind potential [88] but at the
same time they are located in the deep sea-waters of the Mediterranean sea, making the development of
fixed-bottom foundations infeasible and floating platforms the only solution.
The main reasoning behind the selection of Greece for the case study is the proximity of the announced
locations to the coast, combined with the high touristic activity of the same shores. The Greek state has
decided to not consider its EEZ for the initial phase of offshore wind farm deployment, opting instead to
locate them within its territorial waters [105]. However, as of the time this thesis is being written, Greece’s
territorial waters extend only up to 6 nautical miles [107]. Consequently, the announced locations are
relatively close to the shore, where economic activities often conflict with the development of large-scale
wind farms. This situation highlights the relevance of the described methodology, in order to reduce the
high cost of floating wind farms while minimizing the visual impact from key local viewpoints.

The turbine considered for the case study is the 15MW IEA Wind Turbine [54]. A turbine of this size

Optimization
In the optimization process, two swarms are utilized to optimize the two objectives. Each swarm consists
of 30 particles, based on similar studies [118]. Usually the wmax value ranges from 1.2 to 0.7, while the
wmin ranges from 0.4 to 0.1 [13] [15]. For this study, values of 0.9 and 0.4 were selected for wmax and
wmin, based on literature addressing similar problems [15]. A sensitivity analysis is conducted for the
values of C1 and C2. However for VEPSO the literature suggests a value of 0.2 for both constants [119]
[15]. Finally, an execution time of 1 hour is selected for Crete 1 and 2 hours for Crete 2b, based on the

A sensitivity analysis takes place regarding the relative weights used in the creation of the VI index.

are presented in figure 39.
Figure 39: Pareto fronts for different VI relative weight scenarios
The key to understand the different Pareto fronts lies in the results of the VI analysis (Figures 66, 19 and
20). To examine the two extremes, the VI of Settlements is relatively low in the north part of the polygon
where the wind speed is higher. This means that when the weight of settlements is 0.9, the VI index is
low in the high wind speed areas. As a result the two objectives ”agree”, allowing the algorithm to find
optimal solutions for both objectives at the same time, creating a less spread Pareto front, close to the
ideal point. On the other hand the VI of Archaeological sites is high in the north part of the polygon
as appears in figure 66, causing the two objectives to contradict each other in Scenario 3 (A=0.9). The
result is a more spread Pareto than does not manage to find satisfying solutions in both aspects. This
happens because VI index and LCOE reduce almost linearly in opposite directions of the search space
making it extremely difficult to find a satisfying solution in both aspects. However, the micro-sitting of
the wind farm reduces the LCOE by placing the turbines in positions where the wake losses are not as
significant and the cable and mooring costs are reduced due to bathymetry.

confirm that the limited optimization space makes it difficult to achieve significant differences between
scenarios, with differences being more apparent in Crete 1 than in Crete 2b.
Distance also plays a
significant role in the final result, as expected.
In the case of Kserokampos, Crete 1, the algorithm
places the layout further north, increasing the distance from the viewpoint in the high settlements weight
scenario. However, this increased distance does not have a significant effect. A better calibration of
coefficient (d) through local participation could probably improve the results. The minimal impact of
bathing waters is also confirmed in the visualizations, as there is no difference between the equal weight
and high bathing weight scenarios for the coast of Kato Zakros. Finally, the method presents differences
compared to the quantitative Spanish method, which will be explained in the discussion.

6
Discussion
The methodology presented approaches the problem of multi-objective layout optimization of floating
wind farms, aiming in combined LCOE and Visual Impact minimization.
Part of this optimization was also the Visual impact methodology developed.
The Spanish method
employed is a common approach in the literature with successfully applications in the examined study
area [20]. As mentioned this method was developed many years age and was initially used for onshore
applications and for limited number of settlements. The increased size of turbines today makes them
visible from large areas that may include sites with unique natural beauty or increased touristic activity.
A study that aims in quantifying the visual impact and reflect in the opinion of the locals should include
also those viewpoints. Although, Archaeological sites, Bathing Waters and Settlements are examined
as viewpoints in the specific case study, due to their relevance to the examined area and their touristic
profile; other viewpoint categories could be also examined based on the characteristics and the examined
area and the concerns of the locals. Those new viewpoint categories could for example be national parks,
recreational areas or viewpoints from key transportation routes. The selection of the relevant viewpoint
categories depends only in the selected area and should be decided based on the area located inside the
viewshed of the defined polygon.
In the methodology presented, coefficient (e) is used to express different importance between viewpoints
within the same category. In this case study coefficient (e) is only used for the category of settlements(S)
and it uses the population to reflect varying significance for different viewpoints. The viewpoints within
archaeological sites (A) and bathing waters (B) are considered all of equal importance, since coefficient
(e) is not included, due to lack of data related to number of visitors per site. However, this fact would not
be true in a real case. For a example an archaeological site with a high number of visitors would be more
important to keep away from the wind farm as it was proven in the case of Spinalonga [47] mentioned
in the case study description. This fact creates the need for an introduction of coefficient (e) across all
viewpoint categories, to quantify the importance of each separate viewpoint. The most direct method to
do this would be again through questionnaires to the local stakeholders, if data related to the number
of visitors are available or relevant legislation is available it can be also used to form the the values of
coefficient (e) for all viewpoints.
It is important to keep in mind that every community has different characteristics. Since the coefficients
proposed by the Spanish method were initially developed for a case in Spain, they may reflect well the
opinion of the public on the visual impact for this case, but it is not entirely sure if the same coefficients will
be equally relevant in a different position or country. Each population has different perception of visual
impact, different cultural and social characteristics, different economy and different opinion on renewable
energy sources. All those aspects increase the uncertainty of the Spanish method as it was proposed
initially.
The need to readjust the method to modern times has been highlighted by the literature.
However, the modification of the coefficients needs to include also the opinion of the local population.
In a real case scenario this would be done through surveys filled by the local stakeholders. The answers
given would be used to calibrate the Spanish method in order to express accurately the visual impact as
experienced by the locals.
In the context of the original Spanish method coefficient (c) was used to assess the view angle from the
viewpoint to the wind farm; due to the high number of viewpoints and the possibly irregular shape of the
layout this coefficient was not possible to be implemented to the presented methodology. Coefficient (c)
could be implemented in the method by being employed for only a specific number of viewpoints with
Page 61 of 67
==PAGE:73==
higher importance. In this case the coefficient would have to be implemented inside the optimization
algorithm to follow the dynamic change of the layout and be multiplied by the total visual impact of
the layout. Another issue expressed by the revised Spanish method [24] is the reduction of visual impact
with distance. The visual impact is maximum until the first threshold distance and then reduces. In the
original Spanish method as well as in this study this reduction is linear. However, the revised Spanish
method suggests a polynomial curve, at the same time surveys contacted on real case studies by Sullivan
et. al [64] present an exponential decay of the visual impact with distance. The reduction of visual
impact is something that can depend on more than one factors, the size of the turbine, the atmospheric
conditions or personal opinions can all play a role. In addition, the first threshold of 16km selected for
the IEA 15MW turbine was chosen based on estimations of visual impact for turbines of difference size.
The extrapolation conducted was necessary since there is no relevant literature for turbines of this size
but increases the uncertainties of the result. Both the first threshold and the visual impact reduction
can depend on multiple factors and the only way to decrease their uncertainty is to perform multiple
visualizations in different distances combined with questionnaires to the stakeholders.
Another limitation of the presented visual impact assessment methodology is that it does not evaluate
the visual impact of a wind turbine in relation to other turbines. For example, a turbine might be located
behind or very close to another turbine from the perspective of a viewpoint. In this case, the impact of that
turbine would likely differ from if they were placed further away. However it is difficult to quantify this
difference in visual impact, and the only way to explore it is through visualizations making it challenging
to implement in a layout optimization algorithm. At the same time the perspective of the wind farm is
different for each viewpoint further increasing the complexity. In addition the shape of each array can
play a role in the visual impact. The Danish approach to visual impact, suggests regulated turbine arrays
following the shape of a line or a curve. According to this approach the nuisance is reduced when there is
an organized pattern in the layout and not an irregular placement, since the human eye is more used to
visual order and predictability. This principle has been employed in wind farms around the world, such
as the Middelgrunden offshore wind farm outside Copenhagen.
Two approaches to the assessment of visual impact have been presented in this thesis; the Spanish method,
a quantitative approach, and visualizations, a qualitative approach. The Spanish method expresses the
visual impact through the VI index, allowing for its direct implementation in the layout optimization
process. In addition, it has the ability the reflect the impact of multiple different viewpoints in one index.
One disadvantage of the Spanish method is that it does not account for obstacles, between the viewpoint
and the wind farm, considering only the terrain. This can result in analyses based on viewpoints that
cannot actually see the wind farm, especially for viewpoints within settlements. Therefore, it is crucial to
place viewpoints in spots that have a clear line of sight to the wind farm to ensure accurate assessments.
On the other visualizations, while less applicable to engineering models, are more useful in public com-
munication by providing easily understandable images. However, they require significant resources and
cannot efficiently assess multiple viewpoints simultaneously. Visualizations also incorporate qualitative
factors such as surrounding landscapes and obstacles like houses and vegetation, overcoming some limita-
tions of the Spanish method. Combining both methods can lead to better outcomes. Visualizations can
incorporate the opinion of the public in the optimization through the proper calibration of the Spanish
method coefficients, while after the optimization visualizations can be presented to the public. This ap-
proach promotes stakeholder participation and transparency, potentially increasing local support for the
wind farm project.
The costs considered for the LCOE calculation were derived from the NREL report for offshore wind
Page 62 of 67
==PAGE:74==
costs of 2022 [69]. The result is an LCOE close to 100 e/MWh for Crete 1 and 120 e/MWh Crete 2b,
this difference is attributed mostly to the higher wind speed of Crete 1 and not to decreased cost since
the Crete 1 is the deepest of two locations. The results of LCOE completely agree with the results of the
Floating wind LCOE map developed by IREC [121] validating the proper implementation of the costs
and the LCOE calculation algorithm. The first wind farms are expected to be operational between 2030
and 2035, however today’s costs were used for the analysis. This approach was selected in order to avoid
the uncertainty of future prices and be able to compare the result with current projects. After all, the
objective of this research is not the exact definition of the LCOE when the project is operational but the
optimization of the layout. The result of the analysis will not be affected by different costs in the future
since they will equally compact every layout.
This thesis evolves around the combined minimization of visual impact and LCOE. However the presented
methodology could use different objectives to optimize the layout of a wind farm. Those objectives depend
on the activities that are most important to the local population. The visual impact was selected in this
case, due to the increased touristic activity around the island of Crete and the concerns that the view of a
wind farm in the horizon would reduce that touristic activity. In a different example, an objective related
to the impact of the wind farm on the local fishermen such as mooring footprint and cabling minimization
could have been employed instead. Other objectives may also include the impact on the local ecosystem
etc. The selection of the objectives relies on the characteristics of the area that is examined.
The selection of the turbine and the platform is one of the parameters that affect significantly the result.
In a scenario where equal capacity is considered using a smaller, lower capacity turbines would lead to
the use of more turbines in total. In this case the LCOE would increase since more turbines require
more floating platforms, the most expensive part of the structure, while any economy of scale achieved
by large turbines would be lost. At the same time, the wake losses would increase and cost of mooring
lines and electrical cabling would also increase. The effect of more turbines on visual impact depends
mostly on the distance from each viewpoint. Looking at Crete 1 the polygon is located 6 - 10 km away
from the viewpoints. Comparing a smaller turbine with a first threshold distance of 10 km with the IEA
turbine having a first threshold at 16km, coefficient (d) would be 1 (maximum) for both. In addition more
turbines are used in the first case, as a result the visual impact will be higher for smaller turbines in this
scenario. Taking the same turbines to Crete 2b the result would probably be different. Along reducing
the size of the turbine would reduce also the maximum threshold distance since the hub height is smaller.
If we assume a maximum threshold distance of 35 km a number of the now relevant viewpoints would
be excluded. In addition, most of the viewpoints are located relatively far from the polygon leading to a
lower coefficient (d) compared to the IEA turbine. Finally coefficient (b) would probably be also lower
since a larger part of the small turbine would be hidden by obstacles or the horizon.
A change in the platform type change will may have an impact in the results for the considered objectives
and locations. The depth of of both locations is larger than 100 m, so all floating platforms presented can
be used. Any change in the results is related to the mooring footprint of each platform. Platforms with
smaller footprint, such as the TLP, will allow the algorithm to place the turbines closer utilizing better
places with lower visual impact, while it will reduce the length of electrical cabling. The cost of mooring
lines and platforms will be different for other platform types, however this difference in the cost does not
affect the final result.
The wind speed appears to be the main driver of the LCOE optimization swarm. Other influential factors
include bathymetry, distance to the onshore substation and wake losses.
Locations with large wind
speed variations within the polygon, increase the effect of wind speed in the final result. AEP is heavily
Page 63 of 67
==PAGE:75==
dependent on the wind speed driving the turbines to the sites with higher wind resource. Nevertheless,
bathymetry will still drive the placement to more shallow sites reducing mooring and cabling cost, but
this will take place in sites where the wind resource is high. Wake losses will regulate the distance between
the turbines, based also on the wind speed direction. Finally, the distance to the onshore substation does
not seem to affect the result significantly, since the difference in the cost of export cable is not enough
to drive the layout closer to the substation, if the rest of drivers lead the layout to a different direction.
The final result of the VEPSO is driven equally by both LCOE and VI. This one of the advantages of the
VEPSO, since the method updating the position of the particles leads to this result.
The initial layout imported plays a role in the final result. This effect is more apparent in Crete 2b, where
the polygon is smaller, and the number of turbines almost double than in Crete 1. The limited space
available for optimization restricts the particles’ movement, preventing them from ”flying” far from the
initial layout, while the wake effect creates local maxima in the search space, further reducing movement.
The turbine connection grid, defined as an input to the algorithm, can also affect the final layout. The
algorithm follows the connection pattern in the final layout, as major changes to this pattern would
probably lead to an infeasible solution due to cable crossing. Therefore, the algorithm tends to place
connected turbines close to each other, as interventions from other arrays often result in cable crossings.
The N.O. Jensen near wake model, commonly used in wind farm layout optimization, is known for its
simplicity and efficiency. However, its performance in accurately predicting near wake behavior, 2-3RD
downstream of a turbine, has certain limitations [42]. The near wake region, which is the area immediately
downstream of the turbine, is characterized by complex turbulence and flow interactions. The NO Jensen
model tends to oversimplify these interactions, leading to less accurate predictions in this region. The
N.O. Jensen model has been employed in this thesis, since it provides a good balance between accuracy
and computational efficiency. Any near wake limitations of the N.O. Jensen model are limited in this
methodology as presented. This is due to the spacing constraint implemented in the process that does
not allow the mooring footprints of turbines to overlap. If we take as an example two turbines placed on a
depth of 200m, each of them would have a mooring footprint with a 372 m radius, meaning that they can’t
be placed closer than 744 m apart. Considering the depth of the case studies only a limited number of sites
in the polygons allow placement in the near wake of another turbine, this is the reason the uncertainty
related to the performance of the N.O. Jensen model is limited. Another limitation of the wake model
is the fact that it does not model the direction perpendicular to the turbine, this results in a ”cone”
behind the turbine where the wake decay is applied only in the x-direction (downwind), and the velocity
deficit is considered constant across the y-direction (crosswind) for any given downstream distance. This
simplification makes more challenging for VEPSO particles to ”fly” over the wake of another turbine since
only a few millimeters can have a high impact in the LCOE and prevent the particle form moving in this
direction.
One of the inputs of the VEPSO are the maximum and minimum inertia values (wmax and wmin). Larger
values allow the particles maintain most of their current velocity and direction. The values used in this
thesis follow guidelines proposed in the literature. The minimum value used, (wmin = 0.4), is on the
upper side of the values suggested in the literature, which range from 0.1 to 0.4. This value was chosen
in order to allow the particles to move sufficiently to find new solutions, especially since the values of C1
and C2 are already much lower than the values of PSO. The reduction of inertia is linear, starting from
(wmax at the beginning of the simulation, and decreasing to wmin) at the end of run time. This method
is enhances exploration at the start of the simulation and exploitation at the end, allowing the algorithm
to explore the full polygon for suitable sites and then fine-tune the layout to improve the result.

However, the optimal constants are influenced by the visual impact index throughout the polygon. Ideally,
numerous simulations should be conducted with varying constants to identify the pair of C1 and C2

Conclusion
The research conducted in this thesis presents a novel approach to the multi-objective layout optimization
of floating wind farms, balancing the need to minimize the Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE) and the
Visual Impact (VI), through the VEPSO algorithm. Crete is one of the most touristic locations in the
Mediterranean Sea, but at the same time the island’s wind resource is rich. The results indicate that it
is possible to achieve economically viable wind energy solutions while preserving the visual integrity of
coastal areas, which is essential for maintaining local tourism and community support.
How can layout optimization reduce the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) of floating wind
farms to enhance economic feasibility?
The developed methodology employs the Vector Evaluated Particle Swarm Optimization (VEPSO) al-
gorithm to optimize the layout of floating wind farms. The VEPSO uses the positions of wind turbines
and offshore substation as decision variables. This problem is subject to a number of constraints ensuring
that the final result is feasible and realistic. Additionally, a sensitivity analysis on the VEPSO constants
was conducted before integrating them into the algorithm to ensure optimum results. The optimization
process reduced the LCOE by considering local wind conditions and minimizing wake losses and costs
associated with cables and mooring through strategic turbine placement. By enhancing the efficiency of
energy production and reducing associated costs, the methodology improves the economic feasibility of
floating wind farms.
What methods can be developed to quantify the visual impact of floating wind farms in
coastal areas?
The thesis introduces a novel methodology for quantifying the visual impact that departs from the building
upon the principles of the Spanish method. The method has been expanded into a more holistic approach
by incorporating different categories of viewpoints. In this application settlements, archaeological sites
and bathing waters were used in the analysis to reflect the visual impact of wind farms in the touristic
activity. The method takes into account various factors like the population and touristic activity of an
area, the distance to the wind farm, and the portion of the turbine visible from each viewpoint. The
relative weights included allow for the simulation of different VI scenarios depending on the interests of
the local stakeholders. The coefficients used in the method can be tailored based on the interests of local
stakeholders, providing an accurate quantification of the visual impact of floating wind farms.
How can an optimization approach be implemented to simultaneously minimize the LCOE
and visual impact of floating wind farms?
The VEPSO multi-objective optimization incorporates two objective functions, the LCOE is minimized
simultaneously with the visual impact.
This approach considers both optimization objectives to find
the optimal trade-off between them.
The methodology demonstrates the ability to achieve favorable
outcomes in both economic and aesthetic dimensions. In cases where LCOE and VI align, the algorithm
can easily find optimal solutions for both objectives simultaneously, the result is a Pareto front close to
the ideal point. On the other hand, when the two objectives conflict, finding an optimal solution for both
aspects becomes much more challenging, leading to more spread Pareto front. However, the algorithm still
manages to reduce LCOE by minimising wake losses and cable and mooring costs through micro-siting,
even in this complex scenario. The size of the search space also affects the complexity of finding optimal
results, as a limited search space increases the difficulty of achieving optimal outcomes.
In addition, the inclusion of layout visualizations in the analysis highlights the differences between this
Page 66 of 67
==PAGE:78==
method and the visual impact quantification method presented. Visualizations can be a great means of
involving local stakeholders in the planning process, while the quantification method is suitable for the
layout optimization process. Combining both approaches can lead to better results, as the visualizations
can be used to calibrate the coefficients of the Spanish method before the optimization process, thereby
increasing local participation.
Overall, this thesis highlights the importance of different aspects in the development of floating wind
farms. These factors, while diverse, can hold equal significance and can be co-optimized to address all
concerns effectively. The methodology presented and its findings underscore the potential of innovative
approaches to harmonize technological advancement with ecological and social values, paving the way for
a more sustainable future.
8
Future Research
The methodology and findings presented in this thesis open several opportunities for future research. As
already highlighted, the participation of local stakeholders in the visual impact assessment is very impor-
tant for the correct implementation of the method. A future study could calibrate the Spanish method
coefficients based on questionnaires to assess different importance between viewpoints, different threshold
distances and other aspects presented in the discussion like social characteristics of each community. The
method of visualizations could also be used to support the questionnaires in quantifying correctly the
visual impact to the explored viewpoints.
On the foundations laid by this thesis, the methodology could be expanded to other objectives, according
to the characteristics of the area examined and the priorities of the local population. A different objective
could be the minimization of the wind farms impact to local fishermen’s activity.
A different approach could be followed also in the optimization algorithm. In a future scenario additional
variables, like the number of wind turbines or the connection pattern, could be added. The algorithm
would then be able to decide not only the positioning of the turbines but also the capacity of the wind farm
and how the turbines will be connected to each other. Finally, the structure of the layout optimization
could also be redefined. Instead of changing the position of turbines, the algorithm could use the shape
of each turbine array and the distances between turbines within each array as decision variables. This
approach ensures that turbines are placed in a regulated pattern, following curved lines with varying
curvatures, avoiding a disorganized final layout.
This concept is inspired by the Danish approach to
visual impact, which suggests regulated layouts that the human eye is accustomed to, thereby reducing
visual nuisance.
