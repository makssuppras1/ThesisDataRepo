Abstract
The master thesis at hand Microstructure characterization of deformed supermarten-
sitic stainless steel is a supplementary study to a broad study on the deformation
behaviour of three steels by Frank Niessen regarding phase transformations in super-
martensitic stainless steels. Two inter-critical annealing treatments had been given to
the steels, leading the martensitic microstructure to consist of varying initial fractions
of reverted austenite with diﬀerent mechanical stabilities. In-situ X-ray diﬀraction
study during tensile loading had showed the transformation of reverted austenite to
strain-induced martensite, lead to an increased ductility due to the volume expan-
sion associated with the change in crystal structure. Further, it had been found that
strain-induced martensite formed preferentially with ⟨220⟩α′ parallel to the tensile
direction.
This postmortem investigation of the deformed tensile test specimens, focused on char-
acterizing textural development with increasing strain levels. Orientation mapping
was conducted by electron backscatter diﬀraction and transmission Kikuchi diﬀrac-
tion.
The EBSD measurements indicated vague alignments of the strain-induced
martensite, [110]α′ parallel to the tensile direction for some measurements.However,
conclusive representative textural trends were not observed. TKD investigations sug-
gested that strain-induced martensite may nucleate, depending on the applied tensile
direction, with a new orientation diﬀerent to the Kurdjumov-Sachs orientation rela-
tionship between tempered martensite and reverted austenite.

Resumé
Denne afhandling er et supplerende studie til en bred undersøgelse af deformation-
sopførslen af tre stål udført af Frank Niessen, vedrørende faseomdannelser i super-
martensitisk rustfrit stål. De tre stål er blevet givet to inter-kritiske varmebehan-
dlinger, hvilket resulterede i en martensitisk mikrostruktur bestående af varierende ini-
tiale fraktioner af reverteret austenit med forskellig mekanisk stabilitet. In-situ rønt-
gendiﬀraktionsundersøgelser, under trækbelastning, havde vist at reverteret austenit
undergik faseomdannelse til tøjrings-induceret martensit, hvilket medførte en øget
duktilitet grundet volumenekspansionen associeret med forandring i krystalstruk-
turen. Det har endvidere vist sig, at tøjrings-induceret martensit fortrinsvist dannes
med ⟨220⟩α′ parallel med trækretningen.
Denne postmortem-undersøgelse af de deformerede trækprøver, fokuserede på at karak-
terisere teksturudviklingen ved stigende tøjringsniveauer.
Orienteringskortlægning
blev udført ved electron backscatter diﬀraction (EBSD) og transmission Kikuchi
diﬀraction (TKD). EBSD-målingerne indikerede en svag tilpasning af den tøjrings-
inducerede martensit, [110]α′ parallel med trækretningen for nogle målinger. Der blev
imidlertid ikke observeret entydige representative teksturtendenser. TKD-undersøgelsen
antydede at tøjrings-induceret martensit kan nukleare, afhængigt af den pålagte trækret-
ning, med en ny orientering der er forskellig fra Kurdjumov-Sachs orienteringsrelatio-
nen mellem tempereret martensit og reverterede austenit.

Reading guide
This master thesis is initiated with Chapter 1, which introduces the Super 13 Cr
steel grade and brieﬂy touches upon the in-situ synchrotron X-ray diﬀraction studies,
performed by Niessen et al, investigating the deformation and transformation induced
plasticity exhibited by the Super 13 Cr grade. Finally, the motivation and scope are
presented.
Chapter 2 explains the material scientiﬁc background, summarizes a state of the art
review of phase transformations in supermartensitic stainless steel and explains the
fundamentals of the characterization techniques, which have been employed through-
out the experimental investigations.
Chapter 3 describes the details of the experimental procedures, including sample
preparation, experimental setups and parameter studies. Further, the conducted ex-
aminations are summarized for EBSD and TKD.
Chapter 4 exhibits the obtained results for the LR-, HR EBSD and TKD measure-
ments of the three, AH, ST and DT, sample conditions. Inverse pole-ﬁgure maps and
inverse pole-ﬁgure plots are the measures by which the texture, of the tensile test
specimens, has been evaluated.
Chapter 5 discusses the ﬁndings of the texture characterization of the ST- and DT
condition tensile test specimens.
Considerations related to the limitations of the
applied imaging techniques are put forth, which may explain the observed textural
developments.
Chapter 6 presents the conclusion in which the entire project is summarized.

Introduction 1
In the gas- and oil industry duplex steel is widely used as it poses attractive prop-
erties which are required for the application of pipelines, high strength, weldability
and corrosion resistance. However, duplex is expensive compared to specially tai-
lored transformation-induced plasticity (TRIP) steels, speciﬁcally the supermarten-
sitic stainless steel grade based on the Fe-Cr-Ni ternary system [1].
The supermartensitic stainless steel exhibits a microstructure constituent of marten-
site and varying fractions of reverted austenite, depending on the applied heat treat-
ment [1, 2, 3, 4]. Due to plastic deformation during uniaxial tensile loading it has
been reported in [5, 6] that reverted austenite transforms into strain-induced marten-
site. Leading the microstructure to consist of tempered martensite, newly formed
strain-induced martensite.
The Super 13 Cr grade steel has been investigated from a mechanical point of view,
where the strain behaviour has been examined in order to understand the TRIP
behaviour. In a prior study [5], Niessen et al. have performed in-situ synchrotron
X-ray diﬀraction (XRD), under tensile loading, to study the TRIP exhibited by the
Super 13 Cr grade steel.
The objective of the prior studies, conducted by Niessen, was to examine the me-
chanical stability of the reverted austenite during elastic and plastic deformation of
the supermartensitic stainless steel. The in-situ XRD measurements showed that the
intensity of the reﬂection of ⟨220⟩α′ planes increased, for both the ST- and DT condi-
tions, as the fraction of reverted austenite decreased implying that the strain-induced
martensite preferentially forms with the ⟨220⟩α′ aligned parallel to the applied tensile
direction.
Thus, it is of interest to investigate: 1) whether the newly formed martensite forms
with a certain orientation to the applied stress axis, 2) whether there is a certain
orientation of austenite grains which transform more readily into martensite than
others, either by grain shape or crystallographic orientation and 3) whether the strain-
induced martensite forms inheriting the orientation of the tempered martensite or
nucleates with a diﬀerent orientation altogether.
1.1 Scope
The scope for the present work, is to expand upon the prior study by characterizing
the deformed microstructure of Super 13 Cr at varying strain levels by means of

and to interpret the microstructural characterization in conjunction to the in-situ

2.1 Metallurgy
In this section the fundamentals of martensite formation, supermartensitic stainless
steel, austenite reversion, strain induced martensite as well as the transformation
induced plasticity in stainless steels will be presented. The fundamental background
given below is necessary to establish, as the aim of the project at hand is to in-depth
characterize the deformed microstructure of the supermartensitic stainless steel.
Finally, the principles of the characterization techniques EBSD and TKD, will be

2.1.1 Martensitic steel
In Fe alloys a face centered cubic (fcc) austenite (γ) phase may transform to lath
martensite (α
′), undergoing the following transformation γ →α′, where the resulting
martensite forms as either body centered cubic (bcc) or body centered tetragonal
(bct). This is the most common type of martensite, which forms in e.g. Fe-C and
Fe-Ni alloys [7].
The dimensions of the martensite laths are quite small in the range of 0.2 −0.5
µm. The characteristic microstructure of lath martensite is believed to be due to
the tendency of the individual martensite laths to align themselves parallel to one
another. This alignment gives rise to the substructure of the lath martensite [7].
Consequently, an austenite grain is divided into packets, consisting of parallel laths
sharing the same habit plane, which can further be divided into blocks consisting of
laths with the same orientation, corresponding to a variant of the Kurdjumov-Sachs
(K-S) orientation relationship (OR), see Figure 2.1a [7, 8].
Previously it was thought that the blocks consist of only a single variant, however it
was discovered, by use of EBSD, that a block in reality consists of laths of two speciﬁc
variant groups (sub-blocks), which are misoriented by an angle of approx. 10◦, see
Figure 2.1b [7].
It is rather well established that for a range of steels, the Ms and C content are
the two most inﬂuential factors which aﬀect the morphology and substructure of the
martensite. Generally, martensite exhibiting lath morphology is associated to a rather
dislocated substructure. As the martensite in the investigated stainless steel has a
very low C content, 0.02 wt.%, its structure is essentially bcc rather than bct [2].

Lath martensite forms fulﬁlling certain OR with the parent austenite. The habit plane
for lath martensite is close to {111}γ or {557}γ [7]. An OR is a relation between cer-
tain planes or directions of two crystals. For many phase transformations there exist
certain favoured OR between a parent- and product phase, which minimize the inter-
facial energy between the two crystals. OR are signiﬁcant as they can provide insight
regarding: the morphology of precipitates during phase transformations, nucleation
mechanisms and interfaces [9].
In steels there are four major OR, namely the Pitsch, Nishiyama-Wassermann, Kurdjumov-
Sachs and Greninger-Troiano, of which the K-S O-R is of interest, see Table 2.1, a
schematic sketch is given in Figure 2.2. The OR can be expressed as a pair of par-
allel directions in a pair of parallel planes. This description is typically applied for
coherent or semi-coherent precipitate-matrix OR [10]. In the case of K-S O-R, there
are 24 variants in total. Laths sharing the sample plane parallel relationship, e.g.
V1-V6, have the same habit plane, namely (111)γ [7, 8]. A packet consists of parallel
laths with the same habit plane, thus also the same plane parallel relationship, see
Figure 2.1a. Since there are four equivalent {111}γ planes, namely the (111), (1¯11),
(¯111) and (11¯1), there are four crystallographically diﬀerent packets in any given
austenite grain. In a single packet, there are six variants, e.g. V1-V6, with diﬀerent
direction parallel relationships on the same adjoining parallel close-packed plane, e.g.
(111)γ ∥(011)α′, the sum of the combinations equals 24 total variants [7, 8].

2.1.2 Supermartensitic stainless steel
Supermartensitic stainless steel is a type of stainless steel with lath martensite mor-
phology and exceptionally low content of the interstitial elements C and N. The low
content of interstitial elements limits the tetragonal distortion of the martensitic unit
cell, rendering the martensite bcc. The low interstitial content leads to only a small
contribution of solid solution strengthening compared to bct martensite.
Rather,
martensite laths contribute to hardness through their small size, essentially increas-
ing hardness by grain reﬁnement [6].

2.1.3 Reverted austenite
During inter-critical annealing the formation of a two phase lamellar microstructure,
consisting of reverted austenite and tempered martensite, can be promoted in super-
martensitic stainless steels. Inter-critical annealing refers to annealing in the region
where austenite and ferrite are in thermodynamic equilibrium [6].
Reverted austenite is distinguished from retained austenite, the former is formed
during inter-critical annealing whereas the latter is the untransformed austenite during
a cooling process.
The reverted austenite is stabilized due to the partitioning of
austenite stabilizing alloying elements by diﬀusion. Ni diﬀuses into austenite while
Cr diﬀuses into martensite [6].
Lee et al [3] studied the eﬀect of the reverse transformation mechanism of martensite
to austenite and the amount of reverted austenite after the reverse transformation in
Fe-13 wt.% Cr-7 wt.% Ni-3 wt.% Si martensitic stainless steel. The stainless steel type
is similar to Super 13 Cr, for which the chemical composition is given in Table 2.2.
The reverse transformation temperatures applied in [3] were 625 ◦C and 675 ◦C and
similar to those applied by Niessen, 630 ◦C and 670 ◦C, in [6].
It has been stated in [3], that applying a reversion treatment at 625 ◦C resulted in
a ﬁlm-like reverted austenite, which preferentially forms along α
′ lath boundaries; A
small amount of granular austenite forms within the laths. After cooling, austenite
has a thickness of ∼0.3 µm, which is retained at the lath boundaries as well as within
the laths [3].
Applying a reversion treatment at 675 ◦C leads to the formation of more reverted
austenite inside the α
′ laths, compared with 625 ◦C, due to a higher driving force for
reverse transformation. After cooling, the majority of the ﬁlm-like reverted austenite
at the lath boundaries has transformed into martensite.
On the other hand, the
granular austenite within the laths remains untransformed at room temperature. This
may be caused by the fewer martensite nucleation sites in the granular austenite
compared to the ﬁlm-like austenite, which is directly related to the size of the grains
[3].
Similarly, Bilmes et al [14] have shown that the microstructure of 13 wt.% Cr–Ni-
Mo low carbon martensitic stainless steel, after single- and double heat treatment,
consists of tempered martensite and reversed austenite with a lath-like morphology,
which is ﬁnely dispersed along the martensite laths.
2.1.4 Strain induced martensite formation and transformation
induced plasticity
Strain induced martensite formation means, as the wording implies, that an applied
strain induces the martensite formation. Strain is here plastic strain, i.e. slip systems
must be active. The idea is that the plastic deformation will lead to local sites of high
deformation in the microstructure in which the amount and potency of nucleation sites
==PAGE:23==
2 Background
10
for martensite formation is signiﬁcantly increased which aids the martensite formation
[15].
The strain hardening capacity with strain in TRIP steels can be attributed to the
deformation-induced transformation of retained austenite that causes gradual forma-
tion of martensite throughout the deformation regime. Numerous dislocations are
generated to accommodate the volume expansion and shear deformation accompany-
ing the transformation [16].
On straining the austenite is transformed to martensite.
This is associated with
a strain hardening eﬀect, which essentially is a further increase of the yield stress.
At the same time the transformation guarantees ductility, i.e. elongation, because
the deformation is not entirely accomplished by dislocation movement but a phase
transformation that involved a volume increase [17].
TRIP is a mere consequence of strain induced martensite formation. It describes
the eﬀect that a transformation in the material, which is virtually always martensite
formation, may be used intentionally to increase the ductility of a material, while
maintaining similar strength.
For Super 13 Cr, the grade of investigation, it corresponds to a gain in elongation by
the sole transformation of austenite to martensite which does not require dislocation
slip. This transformation will generally occur in a way that the elongation is along
the tensile axis in order to reduce the energy in the system, resulting in a very direc-
tional elongation. Hence, strain can be obtained without exhausting the conventional
deformation mechanisms, which virtually always leads to extended ductility.
As austenite grains progressively transform to martensite the hardness of the material
increases, eventually obtaining similar strength as the as hardened (AH) condition
just before material fracture. This eﬀect is clearly visible in Figure 2.10b, in which
the single annealing treatment condition (ST) and double annealing treatment (DT)
condition achieve the same strength as the AH condition.
In summary, transformation induced plasticity is the gain in ductility at similar
strength with regard to the untempered condition, which can be achieved by tuning
the austenite stability of a material to provoke strain-induced martensite formation.

2.3 Introduction to characterization techniques
The following section will present the fundamentals of the two characterization tech-
niques, EBSD and TKD, which have been applied to examine the microstructure,
identify phases and characterize any textural developments in the tensile test speci-
mens.
2.3.1 Electron backscatter diﬀraction
The EBSD technique has been widely applied for orientation mapping, which is an
important step in materials characterization. Orientation mapping can be used to de-
termine the crystal orientation at each scanning point within a material. The obtained
information can be used to determine grain size distribution, grain misorientation and
crystallographic texture.
EBSD is a micro analytic technique developed to obtain crystallographic information
from specimens in a scanning electron microscope (SEM) down to the nanoscale. The
proposed line of thought behind the EBSD technique is directing an electron beam on
a strongly tilted crystalline sample, usually to 60◦or 70◦from the horizontal, which
allows for a high yield of the diﬀracted backscattered electrons to be detected on a
ﬂuorescence screen, see Figure 2.17b.
Figure 2.17: Schematic illustration of the EBSD system [18].
The mechanism behind the observed diﬀraction patterns is letting the incoming elec-
trons strike the sample surface, which are elastically backscattered, with small loss of
energy, by the atoms of the material and resulting in formation of a divergent source
of electrons close to the surface of the sample [19]. An illustration of this phenomenon
is shown in Figure 2.17a. Some electrons are incident at angle which satisfy Bragg’s
equation:
==PAGE:34==
21

nλ = 2d sin θ
(2.4)
where n is an integer, λ is the wavelength of the electrons, d is the spacing between
the planes and θ is the angle of incidence of the electrons on the diﬀracting plane. At
small Bragg angles θ, the diﬀracted electrons form a set of paired very open cones,
almost approximated to a disc, each corresponding to a diﬀracting plane.
In order to detect these diﬀracted electrons, the screen of the EBSD detector is set
to intercept the diﬀraction cones which results in a pair of conic parallel sections on
the screen. Since the angles of the cones are rather large, the conic sections appear
as straight lines. These lines are called Kikuchi lines and each pair of parallel lines is
known as Kikuchi band.

Even if the EBSD patterns are generated by backscattered electrons, the signal does
not come from the entire interaction volume. The diﬀraction signal eﬀectively emerges
from the created electron source (also called point) in the top few nm (∼10 −50 nm)
from the surface of the sample, which means that the EBSD technique is very surface
sensitive. This means that the surface of the sample must be free of preparation
artefacts before the EBSD characterization [20].
2.3.2 Transmission Kikuchi diﬀraction
Historically, EBSD has been used to characterize materials with grain sizes in the
order of microns. The spatial resolution can be as high as roughly 20 nm for dense
materials and around 50 nm for lighter materials, such as aluminum. However, due
==PAGE:35==
2 Background
22
to the gross limitations in the spatial resolution the need arose for a technique with
an improved spatial resolution to be able to characterize <10 nm particles [21].
TKD, also known as transmission-EBSD, makes this possible. The main diﬀerences in
TKD and EBSD are that; the sample is electron transparent, the collected diﬀracted
signal consists of transmitted (forescattered) electrons rather than backscattered elec-
tron and samples are mounted horizontally in relation to the EBSD detector [21]. In
the following section the fundamentals of the TKD will be elaborated upon.
TKD is essentially utilizing the same experimental setup as EBSD, albeit with two
main diﬀerences. A thin electron transparent sample is mounted instead of a bulky
sample and the sample tilt angle is around 20◦or 0◦from horizontal, compared to the
60◦- 70◦tilt in EBSD. Two diﬀerent tilt conﬁgurations of TKD are applied: oﬀ-axis
TKD corresponding to an applied tilt angle of ∼20◦and on-axis TKD with a tilt
angle of 0◦. The two conﬁgurations are illustrated in Figures 2.19 and 2.20.
Figure 2.19: Schematic sketch of the experimental set up
for oﬀ-axis TKD [22].
Figure 2.20: Schematic sketch of the experimental set up
for on-axis TKD [23].
In the conventional oﬀ-axis setup, the detector is placed vertically on the side of the
sample. In this conﬁguration the Kikuchi pattern is generated by the transmitted
electrons.
For the on-axis setup the scintillator is placed horizontally underneath the sample,
such that the electron beam is incident perpendicularly to the sample surface. Ac-
cordingly, the electrons contributing to the diﬀraction pattern are low angle scattered,
whereas they are high angle scattered in the conventional setup. This leads to shorter
acquisition times, as the intensity of the transmitted electrons is higher for low an-
gle scattered electrons than for high angle scattered electrons [24]. Additionally, the
on-axis set-up enables in-situ measurements due to shorter acquisition times, which
is diﬃcult with the oﬀ-axis setup.

In TKD the diﬀraction point source signals emerge from the bottom surface, which
is diﬀerent from EBSD where they are backscattered and exit the top surface.
Despite its many advantages, TKD has certain disadvantages: the samples need to be
electron transparent in order to be compatible with this technique, for bulk materials
this implies laborious sample preparation. Further, the area of investigation is smaller
compared to EBSD investigations [21].
2.4 Automated indexing
The two main analyses that can be performed in an EBSD experiment are point anal-
ysis and crystal orientation maps. Conducting a point analysis experiment results in a
diﬀraction pattern from which the crystal orientation(s) can be calculated. Applying
this in a grid of points enables one to generate crystal orientation maps. The acquired
maps are constructed on the basis of the sample normal- (ND), transverse- (TD) and
rolling direction (RD).
By calibrating the EBSD system, the crystal orientation can automatically be calcu-
lated from the positions of several Kikuchi bands obtained from the diﬀraction pattern
collected from the EBSD detector. The position of a Kikuchi band is calculated using
the Hough transform [19]. The Hough transform converts an image captured with the
EBSD detector, with (x, y) coordinates, into a representation in the so-called Hough
space with (ρ, θ) coordinates. This conversion is done using the following expression:
ρ = x cos θ + y sin θ
(2.5)
The Hough transform is designed to detect lines of high intensity from the EBSD
image. A straight line in the image space (x, y) is characterized by ρ, the perpen-
dicular distance from the origin and θ the angle made with the x-axis, which can be
represented by a single point (ρ, θ) in the Hough space [20]. The Hough transform
processing steps are illustrated in Figure 2.21.
To identify the phase from the detected bands in the Hough space a so-called triplet
method (sets of three bands) is generally used. This method is based on the calculation
of a conﬁdence index (CI) which is performed using a voting system and is given by:
CI = (V1 −V2)/Videal
(2.6)
where V1 and V2 are the number of votes for diﬀerent solutions of indexing and Videal
is the total number of votes from the detected bands. This voting system considers
the angular ﬁt-value, where the lower values are assigned to be the best ﬁts [20].
The CI is used to measure the reliability of the phase identiﬁcation. However the
Bruker system, which is used for the measurements, is a black box in regards to
the phase identiﬁcation as no information about the calculation of the CI is readily
available.

2.4.1 Inverse pole ﬁgures
An inverse pole ﬁgure can be used to represent textural data, by plotting the orien-
tation distribution function (ODF) of a selected sample direction, may be RD, TD
or ND, in relation to the crystal axes [25]. Sample directions are projected into the
crystal frame and the projection plane for such an inverse pole ﬁgure is a standard
projection of the crystal, of which only the unit stereographic triangle need to be
presented. For cubic crystal symmetry there are 24 unit triangles in the stereographic
projection, all of which are crystallographically similar [25, 26]. It is clear from Fig-
ure 2.23, that a single unit triangle represents the full textural information of the
complete inverse pole-ﬁgure.
Figure 2.23: A complete inverse pole-ﬁgure for the sample x direction and the corresponding unit stereographic triangle.
The ODF method of texture representation is based on a mathematical function
which describes the frequency of occurrences of speciﬁc crystal orientations in a three-
dimensional space, Euler space. Coordinates of the Euler space are deﬁned by three
so-called Euler angles, which emerge from the three rotations required to bring the
crystallographic axes of a crystal, in the specimen, into coincidence with the specimen
axes [25, 26]. The outlined formulation is applied for all crystal in the specimen,
==PAGE:40==
27
2.4 Automated indexing
ascribing a set of Euler angles to each crystal.
Figure 2.24: Schematic sketch of Euler angles, as deﬁned by Bunge [26].
Bunge [26] used generalized spherical harmonic functions to calculate the ODF by the
three independent parameter, the Euler angles. For a rolled material the specimen
geometry can be denoted by three mutually perpendicular directions, RD, TD and
ND, also known as the specimen reference frame. Similarly, for a crystal in a specimen
a crystal frame of reference may be deﬁned, having three mutually perpendicular axes,
[100], [010] and [001]. The orientation of the given crystal will then be determined by
a set of three rotations required to transform the specimen reference frame into the
crystal reference frame [25, 26]. The set of three rotations are given as follows:
1) A ﬁrst rotation by an angle φ1 around ND, which will take RD to the RD’ position
and TD to the TD’ position. The value of the φ1 angle should be such that RD’
is perpendicular to the plane formed by ND and [001].
2) A second rotation by an angle Φ around RD’, which will take TD’ to the TD”
position. The value of the Φ angle should be such that ND coincides with [001].
3) A third rotation by an angle φ2 around ND=[001], the value of the φ1 angle should
be exactly such that RD’ goes to [100] and TD” goes to [010].
According to the formulation proposed by Bunge, a set of (φ1, Φ, φ2) values can be
related to the orientation of a given crystal [25, 26]. The set of Euler angles can be
used as three cartesian coordinates to deﬁne a three-dimensional orientation space.
Each point in this space will have a unique coordinate (φ1, Φ, φ2) and will represent
an crystal orientation with respect to the specimen reference frame. The presence of
==PAGE:41==
2 Background
28
clusters in orientation space will indicate texture in a material, the absence of said
clusters will indicate that the material is textureless [25, 26].
2.4.2 Kernel halfwidth
In order to evaluate the texture IPFx plots are computed. The IPFx plots are vi-
sualizations of the corresponding ODF, which are calculated for a speciﬁed kernel
halfwidth (HW). Varying the kernel HW determines how sensitive or insensitive the
visualization should be in resolving smaller orientation clusters when ﬁtting a contour
over the orientation distribution. Identifying a suitable kernel HW is fundamental in
order to generate consistent IPFx plots, such that direct comparison of IPFx plots is
possible.
The kernel HW was studied for the AH condition map 9, as it had a high indexing
rate of martensite leading to a reliable visualization of the orientation distributions
from diﬀerent orientations, instead of only showing certain orientation distribution
which may have been the case if a map with lower indexing potentially could lead to.
A low indexing rate could potentially be due to certain crystallographic orientations
being indexed more readily compared to other crystallographic orientations. Thus,
the indexed points may not present the true distribution of orientations, instead being
biased towards certain orientations. Hence, a high indexing rate may better present
the true orientation distribution. In Figure 2.25 the eﬀect of varying the kernel HW,
from 1◦to 10◦(except for 8◦), is shown.
Considering the IPFx calculated for a kernel HW of 1◦, Figure 2.25a, there appears
to be two strong orientation distribution clusters and a third faint localized cluster
near [110]. Additionally, many "noisy" clusters are visualized, hence a kernel HW of
1◦seems inappropriate. Comparing this to the IPFx calculated for a kernel HW of 5◦,
Figure 2.25e, the contours do no longer distinguish between the initial two clusters.
Hence, a kernel HW > 5◦is considered too large. A kernel HW of 3◦, Figure 2.25c,
seems reasonable and is applied for every IPFx shown in the results section, as the
initial two strong clusters are still distinguishable while maintaining their intensity.

2.4.3 Slip systems and Schmid factor
Plastic deformation of crystalline materials usually occurs by slip, which is the sliding
of atom planes over one another by dislocation movements. Dislocation movements
do not occur with the same ease on all crystallographic atomic planes and in all
crystallographic directions.
The preferred crystallographic plane along which the
==PAGE:43==
2 Background
30
dislocation traverses is the slip plane and the movement direction of the shear is the
slip direction.
This pair constitutes a slip system, which are characteristic of the
crystal structure of materials [27].
The active slip system for any metal or phase, depends on the crystal structure and
occurs in such a fashion that the atomic distortion due to the dislocation movement
is a minimum. For a particular crystal structure, the slip plane is the closest packed
plane, i.e. the highest planar density. The slip direction corresponds to the direction,
in this plane, which is closest packed with atoms, i.e. the highest linear density [28].
Austenite forms in a fcc crystal structure, a schematic sketch indicating the slip system
is given in Figure 2.26, whereas tempered- and strain-induced martensite form in a bcc
crystal structure, the associated slip systems for both phases are given in Table 2.3. In
a fcc crystal structure slip occurs along ⟨110⟩directions within the {111} planes, for a
bcc crystal structure the slip system would be {110}⟨111⟩. As shown in Figure 2.26,
a slip system may consist of more than a single slip direction. For fcc there are 12
possible slip systems due to four {111} planes and three ⟨110⟩directions within each
plane, respectively [28]. There is no particular closest packed plane in a bcc crystal
structure, slip is hence possible on more than one family of planes, e.g. {110}, {211} or
{321}. Common for metals/phases with fcc or bcc crystal structure is their ductility,
due to plastic deformation being possible along the various slip systems [27, 28].

3.1.3 Parameter optimization
A study of the acquisition parameters was performed, where the eﬀect of beam current
(BC) and map step size (SS) was investigated to determine the optimum parameters
for EBSD measurements. The eﬀect was assessed qualitatively, by comparing the
indexation degree of the acquired EBSD inverse pole-ﬁgure maps. The parameter
study was done for the DT condition tensile test specimen. Fixed SEM parameters
were: acceleration voltage of 20 kV and aperture diameter of 40 µm. Varied SEM
parameters: step size varied from 50, 30 to 20 nm, beam current varied between 2.1
and 8.5 nA (spot size varies with applied BC thus not stated separately).
The eﬀect of varying the SS on the resolution and the degree of pattern indexation
was investigated qualitatively for step sizes of 50 nm, 30 nm and 20 nm, respectively,
see Figures 3.4a to 3.4c. The applied BC was 2.1 nA. The inverse pole-ﬁgure maps
acquired were for the DT condition, as the microstructure was very ﬁne grained due
to the reverted γ. The two areas mapped for 30 and 20 nm maps, in Figures 3.4b
and 3.4c, are both approx. from the area which is marked in Figure 3.4a. There is a
higher degree of indexation for the 20 nm SS map compared to the 30 nm SS map.
However, taking into account the increase in measurement time, which is not stated,
the corresponding increase in indexation is not considered reasonable to apply a SS

of 20 nm. Thus, the optimum value for SS considering both pattern indexation and
measurement time was determined to be a SS of 30 nm.
Further, the eﬀect of the applied BC was investigated, by acquiring three inverse pole-
ﬁgure maps, varying the it from 2.1, 4.5 to 8.5 nA, respectively. The applied map SS
was 30 nm. It was found that applying a BC of 2.1, 4.5 and 8.5 nA, lead to pattern
indexing rates of 33, 39 and 69 frames/s. Hence, the optimum value seemed to be a
BC of 8.5 nA. In Figures 3.5a and 3.5b the corresponding inverse pole-ﬁgure maps

non-indexed points seems roughly equal for the two maps. Thus, a BC of 8.5 nA was
considered the optimum choice as it the highest corresponding indexing rate.
The optimum values investigated above, were optimized early in the experimental
work and were not necessarily applied for all subsequent EBSD measurements. Ap-
plied parameters were determined depending on sample condition and signal-to-noise
ratio and were adjusted to ensure optimum mapping conditions.

3.2.3 Parameter optimization
A parameter study was conducted for TKD measurements, as for EBSD measure-

The parameter study was performed on a 17-4 PH SS thin foil, as it is a grade with
similar chemical composition to Super 13 Cr and as no Super 13 Cr thin foils were
prepared and available at that time. The investigated thin foil was in as-annealed
condition.
During parameter optimization applied BC and SS were varied and optimum values
found, while values for acceleration voltage, aperture size and detector distance were
found in [6] and applied.
The eﬀect was assessed qualitatively by comparing the
indexation degree of the acquired TKD inverse pole-ﬁgure maps. An overview inverse
pole-ﬁgure map was acquired to locate areas with ﬁne structures, two such areas were
located in Figure B.36. Inverse pole-ﬁgure maps were acquired for site of interest
(SOI) 1 and SOI 2 to determine the optimum values for BC and SS, see Figures 3.12c
to 3.12f.
Fixed SEM parameters were: acceleration voltage of 30 kV and aperture diameter of
30 µm. Varied SEM parameters: applied SS were 10 and 3 nm, BC varied between
1.7 and 6.7 nA. In Figures 3.12c and 3.12e the SS was constant at 3 nm and BC
varied.
Comparing the two inverse pole-ﬁgure maps, Figure 3.12e is qualitatively
estimated to have a lower fraction of non-indexed points. Thus, a BC of 6.7 nA seems
the optimum value of the two. In Figures 3.12d and 3.12f the BC is ﬁxed at 6.7 nA,
as it was determined to be the optimum, while the SS is varied. The corresponding
measurement times were approx. 5 and 55 min, respectively. Considering the fraction
of non-indexed points and measurement time, a SS of 10 nm was determined to be
the optimum value.

For the acquired LR EBSD scans the phase fractions were plotted as function of the
distance to the fracture surface for the ST condition, see Figure 4.15.
The highest
volume fraction measured for reverted austenite was approx. 18 vol.%, at a true strain
level of < 10 %, which is in good agreement with the reverted austenite fraction estimate,
in Figure 2.15e, measured by in-situ XRD by Niessen in [5]. Approx. 28 mm from
the fracture surface the strain level is suﬃciently high to initiate the strain-induced
martensitic transformation. At approx. 2 mm from the fracture the austenite fraction
reaches a minimum measured value, approx. 0.8 vol.%, indicating the termination of
the strain-induced martensitic transformation.
This could be interpret as approx.
17 vol.% reverted austenite transforming during
tensile testing. However, this should be interpreted in conjunction to the fraction of
non-indexed points, which seems to be within a range of approx. 15-30 vol.%, except for
the measurement made at 0.55 mm from the fracture surface, reaching a peak of approx.
60 vol.%. As electron diﬀraction is based on Bragg’s law for constructive interference
from atoms in a crystalline system, it is sensitive to the deformation introduced in the
material at high strain levels. Thus, high levels of deformation have a crippling eﬀect
on the indexation rate.
It is unclear how reverted austenite and martensite contribute to the fraction of non-
indexed points. It is conceivable that austenite is more easily indexed than the strain-
induced martensite. Further, the scanned areas for the EBSD measurements are quite
small, rendering interpretation about macroscopic trends futile. In addition, it is possible
that measurements were acquired from areas with locally higher fractions of reverted
austenite.

pendix B.3, Figure B.7. The area fraction is plotted as function of γ grain area. The

in the γ grain area is visible. This compared to the γ phase fraction in Figure 4.15 a
decrease of approx. 5 vol.% is seen. This may suggest that the largest reverted γ grains
readily undergo strain-induced transformation.
Scatter plots of the Schmid factor as function of γ grain area were also plotted, these are
presented in Appendix B.2, in Figures B.1 to B.3. The general observed trend suggests
that large grains readily undergo strain-induced transformation, this is evident when

4.2.3 TKD
In order to characterize the deformed microstructure in detail TKD measurements were
performed for both ST and DT conditions. TKD enables for very detailed observations,
albeit very localized. In the following results obtained for ST condition sample C3 are
presented in Figures 4.29 to 4.31. An overview of the microstructure of sample C3 ROI 0
is shown in Figure 4.29a, where the dark central region in the perforation caused by the
double-jet EP during sample preparation. The electron transparent region, is what has
been indexed during measuring while the surrounding dark area is non-indexed points.
The regions of investigation (ROI) which have been imaged are shown in Figure B.37d.
The corresponding phase map, Figure 4.29b, shows a microstructure consisting of strain-
induced- and tempered martensite and few untransformed reverted γ grains. The IPFx
plot for γ indicates the alignment of [221]γ, [211]γ and [411]γ parallel to the tensile
direction for the few untransformed γ grains. For martensite there are no strong OD
clusters, indicating no particular texture. The interpretation of IPFx plots measured
by TKD may not be taken as a measure of the macroscopic texture of the deformed
tensile specimens. Rather, it may be used to gain insight in crystallographic directions
preferred by the reverted austenite, in the investigated prior γ grains.
In Figure 4.29e, the tempered martensite is colorized according to the highest Schmid
factor per grain. The bulk of the tempered martensite has associated Schmid factors >
0.4, while few localized grains have Schmid factors of approx. 0.35. The relatively high
Schmid factors indicate that the tempered martensite is likely to have active slip system
along the tensile direction, resulting in plastic deformation. A high Schmid factor for
reverted austenite grains, indicates the likelihood martensite nucleation due to plastic
deformation.
In Figures 4.30a and 4.30b the IPFx map and associated phase map for sample C3 ROI
1 are given, in both of which some untransformed reverted austenite can be seen. The
IPFx plot for reverted austenite and tempered martensite, in Figures 4.30c and 4.30d,
indicate relatively strong textures for the two phases. The most intense OD clusters
seem to show parallel alignment of [211]γ and [110]α′ with the tensile direction.
In Figures 4.30e and 4.30f the reverted austenite and tempered martensite are colourized
according to associated Schmid factors. For both the reverted austenite and tempered
martensite the Schmid factors range from approx. 0.4 to 0.5, which suggest that plastic
deformation is very likely to appear. The tempered martensite has the majority of grains
aligned such that the angle between slip planes and tensile axis is close to 45◦.
In Figures 4.31a and 4.31b the IPFx map and associated phase map for sample C3 ROI
2 are presented, both untransformed reverted austenite and tempered martensite can
be seen. The fraction of non-indexed points is higher, compared to C3 ROI 1 due to
the increasing thin foil thickness hindering electron transmission. The IPFx plot for
reverted austenite and tempered martensite, in Figures 4.31c and 4.31d, indicate no
clear textures. The associated Schmid factors, given in Figures 4.31e and 4.31f, once
again indicate the probability of slip system activation.

In the following, TKD results obtained for DT condition sample F2 are presented in

induced- and tempered martensite and some untransformed reverted γ grains. The IPFx
plot for γ, Figure 4.46c, and α
′, Figure 4.46d, indicate vague OD clusters according to
the color scale. Hence, it does not seem reasonable to interpret any alignment of grains.
In Figure 4.46e, the tempered martensite has been colorized according to the highest
Schmid factor per grain. Similar to ST sample C3, DT sample F2 exhibits relatively high
Schmid factors, approx. >0.4, which suggest a high probability of plastic deformation.
In Figures 4.47a and 4.47b the IPFx map and associated phase map for sample F2 ROI
1 are given, relatively many untransformed reverted γ grains are visible in the upper
left prior γ grain. Due to austenite memory, the reverted γ grains more or less have the
same orientation. This is visible in the IPFx plot for austenite, Figure 4.47c, where the
intensity is centered around [320]γ. For the tempered martensite the OD clusters are
much less intense, indicating a vague texture.
In Figures 4.47e and 4.47f the reverted austenite and tempered martensite are colourized
according to associated Schmid factors. For the reverted austenite the Schmid factors
seem to be >0.45, for the majority of grains. For the tempered martensite, the same
seems to be the case except for a few grains with associated Schmid factors of approx.
0.35. As the true strain level for sample C3 is relatively low, ∼15 %, the slip planes are
not aligned uniformly with respect to the tensile axis.
In Figures 4.48a and 4.48b the IPFx map and associated phase map for sample F2 ROI 2
are presented, investigating the untransformed reverted γ grains which were seen in ROI
1. The IPFx map reveals γ grains which are undergoing strain-induced transformation.
Nucleation of strain-induced martensite with a orientation diﬀerent from the orientation
of the tempered martensite is seen. This may imply that strain-induced martensite can
nucleate, depending on the applied tensile direction, with a new orientation which diﬀers
from the K-S O-R between tempered martensite and reverted austenite. Examples of
such strain-induced martensite grains are highlighted in Figure 4.48a with unbroken
lines, while dotted lines mark strain-induced martensite grains partially in ﬁeld of view.
The IPFx plot for reverted austenite and tempered martensite, in Figures 4.48c and 4.48d,
show very intense localized OD clusters, which stem from the single and two orientations
of austenite and martensite imaged, respectively. The associated Schmid factors, given
in Figures 4.48e and 4.48f, assume relatively high values. For reverted austenite and
tempered martensite, the associated Schmid factors are > 0.45, hence plastic deforma-
tion is likely to occur. For the strain-induced martensite grains marked in Figure 4.48a,
the corresponding Schmid factors seem to be noticeable smaller, around 0.35. It may be
that, depending on the orientation of tempered martensite grains relative to the tensile
direction, a new martensite nucleates fulﬁlling a diﬀerent K-S O-R.

Discussion 5
The aim of the study at hand was to investigate whether strain-induced martensite
in Super 13 Cr forms with a certain direction, ⟨220⟩α′, parallel to the applied tensile
direction as suggested by the in-situ XRD results for the ST- and DT conditions, in
Figure 2.15b and Figure 2.16b, measured by Niessen in [5].
EBSD was applied to investigate the textural development, in the tensile test specimens
with increasing strain levels. LR- and HR EBSD IPFx maps were acquired for the AH-,
ST- and DT- conditions, of which the ST- and DT- conditions were of particular interest
as they contained varying fractions of reverted γ with diﬀerent stabilities. The IPFx
plots for the LR EBSD series for both γ and α
′, presented in Figures 4.18 and 4.19
and Figures 4.35 and 4.36 for the ST- and DT condition, respectively, did not indicate
any clear textural development for the reverted γ and the strain-induced α
′. Only for
speciﬁc maps a vague alignment of [110]α′ parallel to the tensile direction can be seen,
such as Figures 4.19h and 4.36h, however no consistent clear trends were found for the
macroscopic texture of the specimens. The same is the case for the IPFx plots generated
for the HR EBSD series. There may be several reasons as to why this was the case, of
which a few are elaborated upon in the following.
The measurement areas in the LR EBSD series of the ST condition were consistent for all
maps in the series, while the measurement areas for the DT condition were inconsistent
throughout the measurement series. For the ST condition all maps in the series had a
constant measurement area of ∼75×65 µm2, whereas for the DT condition measurement
areas varied from ∼299 × 262 µm2 to ∼75 × 65 µm2. The measurement area in the
ST condition measurements was comparable to the size of some prior γ grains, with
diameters of ∼60 µm. Hence, the texture evaluation is not representative of the overall
macro texture of the ST condition specimen, rather it is a localized snapshot depicting
the texture of a few prior γ grains, at best.
For the DT condition measurements,
the inconsistency in measurement areas directly aﬀects the amount of prior γ grains
mapped, which in return aﬀects the statistical basis the texture is evaluated on. The
above-mentioned reasons also apply for the measurement areas for the two HR EBSD
series.
The main issue is that the very ﬁne grained supermartensitic microstructure has a very
coarse texture, due to the prior γ grain size, which for many maps was comparable to
the measurement areas. It is simply not feasible to image large areas, which ensure a
statistical valid basis for evaluating the texture, while applying a resolution suﬃciently
high to resolve the very ﬁne reverted γ grains.
==PAGE:113==
5 Discussion
100
In addition, evaluation of the texture is further complicated by the non-indexed points
for three main reasons: First, the high degree of deformation in the specimens lead to
relatively large fractions of non-indexed points. This is eﬀectively lost crystallographic
information, which could contribute to a more complete understanding of the texture.
Second, it is plausible that reverted γ and tempered α
′ are readily indexed compared
to the strain-induced α
′, which may be highly deformed due to shearing nature of the
strain-induced transformation. This probably further diminishes the crystallographic
information which can be acquired and indexed. Further, any information obtained will
not represent the true fractions of tempered- and strain-induced α
′. The OD clusters
related to the strain-induced α
′ may, relative to the tempered α
′, be severely underesti-
mated.
Third, it may be the case that some crystallographic orientations are, similarly, readily
indexed relative to others. Consequently, this bias may enhance certain OD clusters,
while diminishing others. Further, it is not known for which phases and orientations
this bias is strongest, as such no compensation can be performed to establish the true
information. This may make it even more challenging to obtain true texture information,
which can be taken to be representative of the microstructure.

Conclusion 6
The microstructure of a supermartensitic stainless steel, Super 13 Cr, was studied with
EBSD and TKD at increasing strain levels to identify and characterize textural de-
velopments. Three sample conditions, AH, ST and DT, each given speciﬁc annealing
treatments were investigated. The ST- and DT conditions had varying initial fractions
of reverted γ with diﬀerent stabilities.
At increasing strain levels the reverted γ transformed into strain-induced α
′. Speciﬁc γ
grains were transforming, a study of the grain size distribution with increasing strain lev-
els indicated that larger grains with high associated Schmid factors transformed readily
compared to smaller grains. It was not unequivocally observed that the strain-induced α
′
formed with the ⟨220⟩α′ parallel to the tensile direction for the ST- and DT conditions,
only for certain IPFx maps.
There are several points which may explain why more clear textural trends could not be
identiﬁed, of which the main issue is the very ﬁne grained microstructure which inherits
a very coarse textures due to the prior γ grain size.
To be able to resolve the ﬁne
grained reverted γ and newly formed strain-induced α
′ a high resolution is required.
The combination of a ﬁeld of view suﬃciently large to image several prior γ grains, to
be representative of the microstructure, whilst resolving the ﬁne grained microstructure
is deemed impractical.
Additional possible explanations are: The high degree of deformation introduced into the
material during tensile testing, distorts the crystal structure and decreases the yield of
diﬀracted electrons. Thus, resulting in a higher fraction of non-indexed points. Further
substantiating the previous point, there may also be a negative bias in the fraction of
non-indexed points towards indexation of the strain-induced α
′, which may be highly
deformed due to the shearing nature of the strain-induced transformation.
Detailed TKD measurements of DT condition sample F2, with ∼15 % true strain
level, suggested that strain-induced α
′ may, depending on the applied tensile direction,
nucleate with a new orientation which diﬀers from the K-S O-R between tempered α
′
and reverted γ. This was observed for the DT condition and at this particular strain
level, solely.
The strain-induced martensitic transformation of reverted γ may be further investigated
by hard X-ray microscopy performed at a synchrotron facility. It is possible to probe
volumes in the order of mm3. Further, it is possible to identify and selective probe the
reverted γ to investigate the texture development during tensile testing. An advantage
of using this technique is the large probe volume, which provides a better statistical
basis for interpreting a representative textural development.
