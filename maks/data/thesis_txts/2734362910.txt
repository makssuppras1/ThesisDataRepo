Abstract 
 
Nitrous oxide is a potent greenhouse gas, an ozone scavenger, and the biggest contributor to 
the carbon footprint of wastewater facilities. Within biological water treatment, it is mainly 
generated in activated sludge reactors as an undesired product of nitrogen removal. 
Tabulated emission factors have been proposed for estimation of emissions based on 
influent nitrogen, however direct monitoring provides more reliable results and valuable 
insights into nitrous oxide production. This thesis focuses on nitrous oxide online 
monitoring at the Novozymes wastewater treatment plant in Kalundborg, the biggest of its 
kind in Northern Europe. The study revealed different patterns in nitrous oxide generation 
and accumulation in the water phase, responding to varying conditions in the tanks. 
Nitrifiers nitrification was identified as the predominant pathway during aeration of the 
tanks, thus being the main contributor to its total emissions. Daily emissions were also 
compared with influent conditions and a correlation between certain events in the 
operation of the plant and nitrous oxide emissions was established. In this regard, high 
Chemical Oxygen Demand to Total Nitrogen ratios were found to be inversely correlated to 
nitrous oxide emissions due to assimilation. With the collected data, an average emission 
factor of 1.55% for the whole plant was estimated. Additionally, experiments to reduce 
emissions based on changes in the control of the plant were performed. Promising results 
were obtained when operating under continuous aeration (56% emission factor reduction) 
and with lowered dissolved oxygen (25% emission factor reduction), although risks and 
limitations of operating under such conditions were also identified. This research 
underscores the importance of monitoring nitrous oxide emissions for accurate assessment 
and suggests potential methods for reducing emissions.

1 Introduction 
 
Climate change is one of the most pressing global challenges of our time, which has been 
raising concerns on anthropogenic Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions for decades. Nitrous 
oxide (N2O) is one of the main contributors to this growing problem, together with carbon 
dioxide (CO2) and methane (CH4). In fact, despite its net emissions being the lowest of the 
three, N2O has a heat trapping ability 273 folds that of CO2 (Smith et al., n.d.). Furthermore, 
it is also connected to other environmental issues, being a major stratospheric ozone 
scavenger and pollutant with adverse effects on air quality (Ravishankara et al., 2009). 
Anthropogenic sources are responsible for 43% of the total N2O emissions, being associated 
with various activities, such as, agriculture, stationary fuel combustion and wastewater 
treatment, among others. In the field of wastewater, 0.7 Tg-N of N2O are produced per year, 
corresponding to almost 200 Tg in CO2 equivalents. This is the same as taking 400 million 
transatlantic flights in one year, making water treatment the third biggest human related 
source of this gas (Tian et al., 2020).  
Carbon neutrality is a common aspiration in wastewater treatment plants (WWTP). As a 
result, N2O production has gained special attention in recent years. Reviews show that N2O 
is often the single biggest contributor to the carbon footprint of water utilities. However, 
these emissions greatly vary depending on plant design, operating conditions and 
wastewater composition, with the contribution of N2O ranging from 14% to 48% of the total 
emissions in Scandinavian plants (Delre et al., 2019; Gustavsson & Tumlin, 2013). Even 
within a single facility, the mechanisms of N2O formation are a combination of biological and 
physical processes that are complex and inherently variable. This highlights the need for 
emission monitoring programmes to find mitigation strategies tailored to every plant (Delre 
et al., 2019; Gustavsson & Tumlin, 2013). 
Despite these variations, N2O emissions are always associated with the removal of other 
nitrogen compounds, such as ammonia (NH₃), nitrate (NO₃⁻), and nitrite (NO₂⁻). This is a 
key step in wastewater treatment because excessive discharge of such compounds can be 
hazardous for human health and the environment (Ahmed et al., 2022; Ward et al., 2018). 
Nitrogen is most commonly removed from wastewater biologically, in activated sludge 
reactors (ASR), where around 80% of the total N2O emissions of the plant are generated 
(Gruber et al., 2021). In these tanks, N2O is produced within the liquid phase, where the 
biochemical reactions take place, then transferred into the gas phase and discharged into 
the atmosphere. Biological nitrogen removal is performed through two processes: 
nitrification and denitrification.  
In wastewater, nitrogen is mainly present as NH₃, or its ionic form, ammonium (NH4+). 
During nitrification, readily available NH₃ is oxidized into NO₂⁻ by ammonium oxidizing 
bacteria (AOB) and then, NO₂⁻ is further oxidized into NO3⁻ by nitrite oxidizing bacteria 
(NOB) under aerobic conditions. During denitrification, NO3⁻ is reduced to dinitrogen (N2) 
by heterotrophic bacteria (HB) using organic carbon as an electron donor under anoxic 
conditions (Winkler & Straka, 2019). All these transformations are part of the nitrogen cycle 
shown in Figure 1.

Although nitrification-denitrification is the most conventional process, other novel 
strategies like partial nitritation or Anammox are also gaining importance. In partial 
nitritation, NH3 is oxidized only to NO₂⁻ without NO3⁻ production, reducing oxygen demand 
from AOBs and organic carbon demand from HBs (Antileo et al., 2013; Duan et al., 2019). On 
the other hand, anammox bacteria are used due to their capacity to convert NH4+ and NO₂⁻ 
into N2, also saving conversion steps and resources (Antileo et al., 2013; Duan et al., 2019).  
Regardless of what the chosen nitrogen removal process is, N2O production is undesirable 
and tends to be the result of operating under sub-optimal conditions (Kemmou & 
Amanatidou, 2023). Three different pathways have been found to be responsible for N2O 
production: AOB nitrification, AOB denitrification or HB denitrification (Figure 2). 
During AOB nitrification or nitrifier nitrification (NN), N2O production can occur in two ways 
as part of the NH₃ to NO₂⁻ oxidation. The first possible pathway takes places when oxidation 
is incomplete, and after NH₃ is transformed into the intermediate NH2OH, which is then 
converted into NO, which further reacts into N2O. The second and most commonly observed 
option is the abiotic pathway, where NH2OH reacts with already present NO₂⁻ to produce 
N2O (Chen et al., 2020). Since in both cases N2O is a side product of NH₃ oxidation, high 
concentrations of NH₃ and high dissolved oxygen (DO) promote N2O production through this 
path (Duan et al., 2021).   
During AOB denitrification or nitrifier denitrification (ND), N2O is produced when, instead 
of using O₂ as the electron acceptor, NO₂⁻ is used. This results in its reduction to N2O. This 
path is regarded as the main cause of N2O generation, because nitrifiers lack the enzyme to 
further reduce N2O to N2, causing its accumulation (Chen et al., 2020). Since in this pathway 
N2O is the result of a competition between NO₂⁻ and O₂ for being the electron acceptor, N2O 
production increases at low DO or high NO₂⁻ concentration (Duan et al., 2021). 
During HB denitrification or heterotrophic denitrification (HD), N2O is generated as a 
necessary intermediate in the transformation from NO3⁻ or NO₂⁻ to N2 as seen in Figure 1 
==PAGE:11==

(Chen et al., 2020). Here N2O production increases when there is an incomplete 
transformation, which can occur when there is low chemical oxygen demand (COD), low 
temperature or under high DO and high NO₂⁻ because they are both inhibitors for N2O 
reductase (Duan et al., 2021). However, this pathway is mostly regarded as a sink rather than 
a source, since it can also deplete accumulated N2O.

Identifying N2O generation mechanisms and what triggers them is an important step in 
reducing GHG emissions in a WWTP. However, despite the advances in the comprehension 
of N2O production, it can still be hard to establish the predominant pathway in a system. 
This is especially true for full scale, where there are more factors to consider, experiments 
are difficult to run and there is less control over the reactor parameters. Furthermore, N2O 
emissions monitoring is far from being standardized. Different measuring technologies and 
protocols exist, with different levels of complexity and reliability. 
This lack of unity has motivated entities such as the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate 
Change (IPCC) to create emission factors (EF) that allow discharged N2O to be estimated 
from more commonly measured parameters, such as incoming TN. In their latest guideline, 
the IPCC proposes an EF of 1.6% N2O per mg-N of TN (IPCC, 2019). Some countries have also 
established region specific EFs based on their own reports. In the case of Denmark an EF of 
0.84% N2O per mg-N of TN has been proposed (MUDP, 2020).  
However, despite the convenience of calculating emissions from EFs, they should be used 
cautiously. Both previously mentioned EFs were obtained using data from urban water 
plants only. This raises the question of whether they may be representative of other sorts of 
WWTP. Therefore, when possible, direct measurements should be the preferred and most 
reliable way of quantifying N2O. Furthermore, with Denmark being the first country 
planning to tax N2O emissions from 2025 on municipal WWTPs, N2O monitoring is becoming 
more crucial than ever.  
The aim of this project is to monitor and do a comprehensive study of N2O emissions at the 
industrial WWTP of Novozymes, in Kalundborg. The obtained data will be used to assess 
N2O emission and production patterns and to calculate an approximated EF of the plant. 
Furthermore, mitigation strategies to reduce such emissions will be proposed and tested in 
full scale experiments.

1.1. 
Background 
The Novozymes wastewater treatment plant in Kalundborg is remarkable for being the 
largest of its kind in Northern Europe. It receives industrial wastewater from the production 
plants of both Novozymes and Novo Nordisk. Organic loadings of up to 2.5 million person-
equivalents in only 10,000 m3 are treated every day. Moreover, the influent is highly variable 
in terms of pH, loading and composition; rich in biological solid waste; and at a temperature 
of around 35℃ all year long. All these characteristics make this plant unique and difficult to 
compare to most WWTPs (Monje, Junicke, et al., 2022). 
Moreover, the industrial sector in Kalundborg is thriving, which is the reason why the plant 
is undergoing an expansion. While the expansion aims to accommodate the ever-growing 
wastewater flow, a bigger capacity may come at the expense of a bigger environmental 
impact in terms of total N2O emissions. That is the reason why now is a crucial moment to 
evaluate current weak points and improve upon them.  
N2O emissions in the ASR tanks were identified as one of the major contributors to the 
carbon footprint of the Novozymes WWTP in an environmental evaluation based on a full 
plant model of the plant (Monje, Owsianiak, et al., 2022). In this study, an N2O EF of 2.5% 
was estimated, hence way above the IPCC estimated EF of 1.6%. This has drawn attention to 
this substance and proven N2O mitigation to be an important milestone for the future 
improvement in the GHG emissions of water utilities.  
Monitoring N2O emissions is, thus, one of the measures taken by Novozymes in their 
compromise with sustainability to reduce their GHG emissions. To achieve this, a N2O 
Wastewater System from Unisense was first installed in April 2023. However, no 
trustworthy data on the actual emissions was obtained before the start of this project in 
September 2023. Therefore, this thesis constitutes important tangible attempt to 
understand and reduce N2O emissions at the Novozymes WWTP.  
It should also be mentioned that the data obtained in this project will not only be used for 
direct evaluation of the current emissions, which is the scope of this thesis; it will also be 
implemented in an updated version of the full plant model currently under development. 
This improved version will allow for accurate emission calculation, expanding on previous 
efforts made in the study by Monje et al. (Monje, Junicke, et al., 2022; Monje, Owsianiak, et 
al., 2022). 
 
1.2. 
Project Description 
This MSc thesis deals with the monitoring of N2O emissions in one of the activated sludge 
tanks in the WWTP of Novozymes, in Kalundborg. To do so, two N2O sensors from Unisense 
a/s were used to obtain on line N2O concentration data. The sensors were installed in the 
same tank but in different phases, one being in the liquid phase and one in the gas phase. 
The purpose of having a sensor in each phase was to obtain complimentary information 
from them. Following N2O concentrations in the liquid phase gives insight into the reactions 
taking place inside the tank. Additionally, emissions can be indirectly calculated from this 
data. On the other hand, the gas phase sensor gives direct data, which could potentially be 
more valuable for emission calculations. Each sensor was calibrated according to the phase 
in which it is installed. Whenever a sensor was unavailable, monitoring of the liquid phase 
was prioritized as it is considered the most relevant and accurate of the two. Real time 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
emissions were calculated from the obtained concentration information and used to 
calculate total daily emissions and to approximate the EF of the plant.  
The obtained data was then analysed and contrasted with other monitored parameters, such 
as dissolved oxygen (DO), NH4+, NO2 and NO3 concentrations, total suspended solids (TSS) 
and total nitrogen (TN) and COD in the influent. The gathered information was used for N2O 
and emissions pattern identification. Concentration patterns were employed in an attempt 
to identify the main N2O production pathways observed in the ASR. Emission patterns were 
used to outline the most relevant parameters and operational conditions influencing total 
emissions. Analysis of the data was done both visually, through graphic representations; and 
mathematically, through Principal Component Analysis (PCA). PCA is a statistical method 
that decreases the complexity of a collection of data points by reducing their variables into 
their essential features called principal components. Representing these components in a 
coordinate system allows for variables to be more easily interpreted and correlated 
(Greenacre et al., 2022).  
Finally, the gained knowledge of the system was used to propose mitigation strategies. Some 
of these strategies were evaluated through experiments conducted in the monitored tank 
only. The level of success of each experiment was assessed based on the EF obtained under 
each condition. Plausibility and risks of a potential full plant implementation were also 
addressed.  
 
1.3. 
Objectives 
This project aims to monitor N2O concentrations and emissions at the WWTP of Novozymes 
in Kalundborg and propose potential improvements to the current operation of the plant in 
order to reduce its environmental impact. The main research objectives are listed below: 
• 
To install and calibrate two N2O Unisense sensors. 
• 
To continuously collect data of N2O concentration and other relevant parameters in 
the evaluated tank. 
• 
To calculate N2O emissions and estimate the EF of the plant. 
• 
To represent and analyze the obtained data graphically and through PCA. 
• 
To identify patterns and the main N2O production pathway. 
• 
To propose strategies for N2O emission mitigation. 
• 
To conduct full scale experiments of the proposed strategies. 
• 
To evaluate the results of the experiments and their viability.

2 Materials and Methods 
 
2.1. 
Novozymes Industrial Wastewater Treatment Plant 
The Novozymes WWTP treats industrial wastewater originating from the production plants 
of Novozymes and Novo Nordisk. This loading comes through different Process Waste Water 
influents (PWW1-4) and one high-solid content “Spent BioMass” (SBM) stream, as shown in 
the flow diagram in Figure 3. The influents include wastewater from enzyme and pharma 
production based on fermentation and recovery processes, high strength process water 
from the enzyme recovery filtration process and high strength ethanol process water 
streams from the pharmaceutical recovery process, among others. The SBM stream contains 
spend biomass from enzyme (95%) and pharmaceutical (5%) recovery processes (Monje, 
Junicke, et al., 2022). 
The plant layout consists of one buffer tank for liquid streams (BTL), one primary clarifier 
(PRIM), one pre-acidification tank (PAT), two anaerobic granular sludge reactors (AnGSR), 
one biogas desulfurization (BDS), seven activated sludge reactors (ASR) with 
aerobic/anoxic cycles, five secondary clarifiers (SEC), three dissolved air flotation units 
(DAF), two buffer tanks for biomass (BTB), two inactivation tanks (IT) and one dewatering 
station (DEW) comprising nine decanters. The plant can be divided int three sections: an 
anaerobic water line (BTL, PRIM, PAT, AnGSR), an aerobic water line (ASR, SEC, DAF) and a 
biogas line (BTB, IT, DEW). The liquid streams are usually treated first through the anaerobic 
line and then through the aerobic line, but can also be bypassed directly to the aerobic line. 
The SBM and the sludge from PRIM, SEC and DAF are inactivated and separated into a 
dewatered cake which is used for biogas production and a reject water that is sent back to 
the water lines (Monje, Junicke, et al., 2022). 
The section of the plant where the N2O monitoring will be conducted is one of the ASRs. 
These are closed tanks with an active volume of around 3,600 m3 with bottom aeration and 
a biomass concentrations ranging from 8-11 kg MLSS/ m3, with only 60% VSS content. As 
shown in the flow diagram, there are several influent streams to these reactors. The most 
frequent and constant influent is the combined outflow from the two AnGSRs (Stream 1). 
This flow contains most of the received nitrogen but lower COD due to its consumption by 
anaerobic microorganisms growing in the previous tanks, resulting in a COD/TN ratio of 
around 2-3. Another important influent to the ASRs is the reject stream from dewatering, 
which is rich in lime content and has a COD/TN ratio of 10-12 (Stream 2). A less constant 
stream but with great impact on the performance of the ASRs is the overflow from the PRIM, 
which is bypassed to the ASRs before going through the AnGSRs (Stream 3). Overflow is 
generally not the main influent but it occurs frequently and intermittently for varying 
periods of time and it contains a high COD concentration and a COD/TN ratio of 14-16 that 
can affect microbial growth. Overflow becomes the main influent to the ASRs when the 
AnGSRs are not in operation. Apart from those, other less crucial influent streams are the 
sludge recirculation from the SEC that ensures high microbial concentration (Stream 4), the 
underflow from the DAF (Stream 5) and the bypass from PWW1-4 (Stream 6). The ASRs are 
operated with intermittent aeration. Nitrogen removal is performed through partial 
nitritation-denitritation.

2.2.3. Control and Automation 
As stated before, the ASRs work under intermittent aeration and they perform partial 
nitritation and denitritation. To automate this process, a control strategy that calculates a 
dynamic DO setpoint based on NH4+ and NO2- concentrations is used. This strategy is 
designed to allow AOBs to perform nitritation during aeration periods and HBs to perform 
denitritation during non-aeration periods, while maintaining low NH4+ and NO2- 
concentrations in the effluent.   
On the one hand, NH4+ concentration is used to calculate a dynamic DO setpoint, which itself 
determines the airflow. Figure 4 shows how the DO setpoint is calculated. A lower limit 
(NH4_1) and an upper limit (NH4_2) are established for NH4+ and a lower DO setpoint 
(SP_O2_1) and upper setpoint (SP_O2_2) are assigned. When the concentration is below 
NH4_1, aeration is stopped. While the NH4+ concentration is within this range, DO is 
calculated based on those values to allow nitritation. When NH4_2 is exceeded, the DO 
setpoint directly switches SP_O2_2.  
 
Figure 4. Principal diagram for the NH4+ control system (SP_O2_1: Minimum oxygen setpoint defined by the 
operator, SP_O2_2: Maximum oxygen setpoint defined by the operator, NH4_1: Actual ammonia concentration at 
SP_O2_1 and NH4_2; Actual ammonia concentration at SP_O2_2). 
On the other hand, if the NO2- concentration is above an established upper limit 
(SP_NO2_MAX) aeration will be turned off, even if NH4_1 is not achieved, until the 
concentration goes below an established NO2- lower limit (SP_NO2_MIN). Then aeration will 
start, until NH4_1 is achieved or the NO2- concentration rises to SP_NO2_MAX again. 
Furthermore, an additional control based on NO3- is used in case accumulation of this 
component occurred, despite it not being present under normal conditions. All the 
previously mentioned limits, setpoints and their assigned tags and values are summarized

2.2.4. N2O sensors 
To monitor real time N2O concentration, the N2O Wastewater System sensor and its 
corresponding control box from Unisense Environment A/S was used during the project, 
shown in Figure 5. The Unisense sensor is an electrochemical sensor based on a Clark 
electrode. Clark-type sensors create a voltage when O2 reaches its cathode and is 
electrolytically reduced. In order to detect a certain substance, a membrane enriched with 
an enzyme capable of reacting to that substance and releasing the O2 that will react with the 
cathode is used. The voltage can then be correlated to the substance concentration based on 
the diffusion rate of O2 through the membrane (Law et al., 2012).  
The Unisense Wastewater System consists of a sensor head with the Clark electrode 
protected by an aluminum tube and connected to the sensor body that amplifies the signal 
and sends it to the control box. Apart from concentration, the sensor is also sensitive to 
temperature, so different sensor heads exist depending on the working concentration and 
temperature ranges and they include a thermometer in the sensor body to correct for slight 
temperature changes. The amplified voltage signal from the sensor is transmitted to the 
control box and reinterpreted as a signal percentage which is used as raw value. A 
calibration is used for the control box to calculate the actual concentration from the raw 
signal. Unisense sensors need to be calibrated every two months or after big temperature 
changes and the sensor heads need to be replaced every 6 months or earlier if the tip is 
mechanically damaged. This sensor is designed to be used in liquid phase, but theoretically 
Clark type sensors are able to work also in the gas phase.

2.2.5. Sensor calibration 
Gas Phase Calibration 
Since the used sensors are not specifically designed for the gas phase, no calibration method 
is provided by the vendor for this purpose. Therefore, different calibration methods were 
tested, each improving on the previous attempt through a process of trial and error. In all 
cases a two-point calibration was planned using N2O-free air for the first point and a 
100ppm pressurized N2O gas bottle for the second point, following a stabilization period to 
ensure that the baseline signal was below 2%. As the controller is also not designed for this 
purpose, the calculation of the baseline and the slope, as well as the conversion of raw data 
to N2O concentration were done in R.  
• 
Method 1: the first point was measured out in the air far enough from the upper 
vent of the tank to prevent any emissions from affecting the signal. The second point 
was obtained by placing the sensor in a plastic bottle where N2O gas was being 
released from the pressurized bottle. The plastic bottle was sealed to prevent N2O 
from escaping and when the signal stabilized the concentration was assumed to be 
100ppm.  
• 
Method 2: the general method was the same as in the previous attempt but with the 
sensor placed on top of the vent at all times so that the measuring temperature 
would be close to that inside the tank.  
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
• 
Method 3: the first point was measured in the air far from the vent as in method 1. 
The second point was measured inside the plastic bottle but with the sensor on top 
of the vent as in method 2 and taking the measurement during a non-aeration 
period.  
• 
Method 4: this calibration was performed at a time when almost no N2O was being 
produced inside the tank. The sensor was placed in a bottle and sealed to prevent 
gas from getting in contact with the sensor head. Then it was introduced inside the 
tank for a prolonged period of time (around a day) to stabilize. For the second point 
the N2O gas was released inside the sealed bottle and the sensor hung inside the tank 
until the signal stabilized. In this way both measurements were performed at the 
actual tank temperature.  
 
Liquid Phase Calibration 
A two-point calibration was performed on the controller following the method stated in the 
Unisense N2O Wastewater System User Manual and using Unisense Calibration Kits. Before 
starting, when a new sensor body or sensor head was used, the sensor was let to stabilize in 
a bucket with tap water until the raw sensor signal was below 2%. The first point was then 
obtained by submerging the sensor in a 4L bucket with tap water at a temperature close to 
that of the tank. When the signal was stable, a value of 0 mg N2O-N/L was assigned to it in 
the controller. The second point was obtained by adding 5mL of a N2O solution contained in 
an ampule as part of the Unisense Calibration Kit. After mixing, the sensor was inserted in 
the bucket again and a value of 0.94 mg N2O-N/L was introduced in the controller once the 
signal stabilized. The controller then calculates the zero point, which should be close to 0 
and the Slope, which should be between 15% and 30%. The temperature of the water in the 
bucket was measured with a thermometer and manually inserted in the controller, since the 
integrated thermometer is too high up in the sensor body to be inserted in the bucket.

2.4. 
Principle Component Analysis 
Two sets of PCAs were performed to better understand the collected data and as a tool to 
support the identification of patterns in N2O production and emissions. The first PCA used 
the continuously obtained data from the on-line and at-line sensors (NH4+, NO2-, DO and N2O 
in liquid phase) together with the airflow and the calculated live emissions. For this set of 
data, a special attention was paid to the varying delays in NH4+ and NO2- concentrations, due 
to their big impacts in the final results. This was done by first representing the data and then 
applying a delay to make the peaks of NH4+ and NO2- match the beginning and the end of the 
aeration period, respectively. The lag applied to the graphs was then also applied to the data 
for PCA analysis. 
The second PCA used the daily values for influent conditions (COD and TN), as well as the 
daily observed TSS, the calculated emissions, EFs and daily COD/TN ratios. Since TN values 
were needed both for the EFs and for the COD/TN, only data from days when the full influent 
information was available could be used.  
 
2.5. 
Operation Strategy Experiments 
A series of experiments were run to evaluate the performance of the bioreactor using 
different operation strategies based on the findings on what the main factors influencing 
N2O emissions were. Both nitrogen removal and N2O emissions were evaluated for each 
experiment.  
Experiment 1: Continuous aeration  
This experiment aimed to test whether the ASR could operate with simultaneous nitritation 
and denitritation (SNdN) and if that would reduce N2O emissions. To do so, the control 
strategy was switched from the NH4+ and NO2- concentration strategy to the O2 strategy. A 
constant DO setpoint of 0.5 mg/L was established for 100% of the time. This setting should 
provide enough oxygen for nitritation to be carried out by nitrifiers, but low enough to allow 
denitritation. By working with constant concentrations instead of high peaks both N2O 
production from both NN due to high NH4+ concentration and ND due to high NO2- should be 
prevented.  
Experiment 2: Decreased DO setpoint 
The purpose of this experiment was to challenge the established DO setpoint aiming to 
reduce aeration cost, NO2- accumulation and NH4+ oxidation rate. To do so the current control 
strategy with intermittent aeration was maintained but the upper DO setpoint was set to 2.0 
mg/L during aeration periods. All other setpoints and limits were maintained. This setting 
should lower nitritation rate, consequently reducing the effect of the NN pathway. 
Experiment 3: Decreased NO2-, NH4+ and DO setpoints 
The objective of this experiment was to evaluate emissions when NH4+ and NO2- were kept 
at lower concentrations by changing their concentration limits, but maintaining the current 
control system. This was achieved by establishing a maximum NH4+ and NO2- setpoint of 4 
mg/L. When NH4+ surpassed that limit aeration was started with a lower DO setpoint of only 
0.1 mg/L and an upper DO setpoint of 0.5 mg/L. When NO2- exceeded the limit, aeration 
stopped. This setting should avoid high NH4+ and NO2- peaks that trigger N2O production.

Regardless of the accuracy of the gas phase concentration based on the calibration Method 
3, important information on the dynamics of N2O concentration in the gas phase can be 
extracted from the pattern shown in Figure 4. It can be observed that during aeration, N2O 
concentration goes from being very low to slowly increasing. This is due to stripping of the 
N2O from the liquid phase caused by aeration, with the maximum being achieved right 
before aeration stops. During non-aeration the N2O concentration in the gas phase slowly 
decreases. This is due to the design of the ASRs. Since they are closed tanks with a small 
opening at the top, the air in the headspace is being exchanged very slowly, causing the 
concentration to also drop slowly. When aeration starts again, the big volume of new air 
replaces the N2O rich air in the headspace, causing the sudden drop in the signal. This also 
reveals that N2O is unlikely to be accumulating during non-aeration but produced during 
aeration instead, as no stripping can be seen at the very beginning of the aeration periods, 
but it increases as aeration continues. 
Measuring of N2O concentrations in the gas phase was a milestone in this study because it 
was initially regarded as the most direct way to monitor emissions. However, calibration of 
the Unisense N2O Sensor in the gas phase and validation of the results were complicated. 
Since the sensor is not intended for gas phase use, there was no standardized calibration 
procedure. Thus, the different methods described in Section 2.3.1. were attempted to find 
an appropriate one, the main challenge being emulating the temperature conditions inside 
the tank.  
According to the User Manual, Unisense Sensors should undergo calibration at a 
temperature within a 3-degree range of the operational temperature. In the ASR the 
temperature of the gas phase in the headspace of the tank is around 30℃, thus not being 
reproducible in the outside. Methods 2 and 3 attempted to solve this by performing some of 
the measurements for the calibration on top of the upper opening of the tank, and Method 
4 directly inside the tank. However, when the ASR was under operation N2O emissions from 
the tank would interfere with the measurements for the first calibration point. That is the 
reason why for Method 3 the first point was made outside and the second point on top of 
the lid. The uncertainty was then whether measuring the first point at a lower temperature 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
would significantly affect the calibration curve. Method 4 is the most adequate method 
because it measures both points inside the tank at the operating temperature. This last 
calibration was only possible to make during a full plant shutdown at the end of the 
monitoring period. At this time the liquid phase sensor could confirm that N2O was not being 
generated and there was no risk of interference from emissions. While method 4 is not easily 
reproduceable, it serves as a validation for Method 3, since the obtained equations for both 
methods were similar enough for the results to be comparable. Nevertheless, all the 
previously mentioned calibration methods can be considered rudimental and 
untrustworthy compared to those seen in literature. 
In a study by Marques et al. where Unisense sensors were used in the gas phase for full-scale 
N2O emission monitoring, a minimum of six calibration points were required to account for 
the concentration and temperature variability observed in the gas phase. In this study four 
different commercial N2O gas mixtures (100% N2O, 422.3, 104.3 and 83.7 ppm N2O from 
Linde) together with a mass flow controller were used to test different N2O concentrations. 
To adjust the temperature to the desired set-point, all measurements were taken in a vessel 
inside a water bath. Additionally, to validate the tested measurements a commercial N2O 
online gas analyser and a gas chromatograph coupled to an electron capture detector were 
used (Marques et al., 2016). Such level of complexity could not be matched and goes beyond 
the scope of what was aimed for in this study.  
In addition to the uncertainty of the validity of the employed calibration methods and the 
obtained concentration values, converting such values into emissions would also be a 
challenge. To do so, the obtained concentration would need to be multiplied by the flow of 
air exiting the tank. During aeration it can be assumed that this is equal to the airflow. 
However, during non-aeration the rate at which the gas in the headspace is being renovated 
cannot be calculated.  
In the case of Marques et al., the Unisense sensor is mounted inside a hood that floats on top 
of the liquid phase. This hood has a pump that removes the air at a constant flowrate. This 
flow is then used to calculate emissions from the concentration in the hood, which are 
extrapolated to the whole tank, taking into account the area of the hood and the area of the 
tank (Marques et al., 2016). In general, the use of a hood is the most common approach for 
mounting of N2O sensors in tanks with bottom aeration (Law et al., 2012; Ye et al., 2014).  
All things considered, the current solution should not directly be used as the main method 
of N2O emission monitoring. To employ Unisense sensors a validated calibration method 
would be necessary to measure N2O concentration in the gas phase, coupled with a hood 
mounting or an outflow gas measurement for emissions calculation. Alternatively, a sensor 
specifically designed for emission monitoring could be used. Infrared analysers are probably 
the most commonly used (Ahn et al., 2010; Desloover et al., 2011), but chemiluminescence 
(Kampschreur et al., 2008) and mass spectrometry analysers have also been used for this 
purpose (Law et al., 2012).

While the sensor was intended to capture concentration values continuously, interruptions 
occurred for various reasons, including sensor breakage or malfunction, and due to 
experiments being conducted in the tank. This led to the liquid phase data during operation

Within the recorded data, there are periods when certain parts of the plant were undergoing

concentration and emissions were evaluated equally under all circumstances.  
The events concerning the performance of the plant will be discussed when analysing each

majority of this period, causing high overflow between December 9th – 15th. It should be 
clarified that the conducted experiments are not included in any of these periods so as to 
analyse them separately.

size of the peaks and when the biggest peak was occurring. These three scenarios were:

Finally, Figures 11 and 12 represent one of the periods when N2O peaks are minimal. Very

tons/day/tank and TN was 0.44 tons/day/tank, resulting in a COD/TN ratio of 11.49. Other 
than that no abnormal conditions were observed in the plant. 
To understand what is causing the different patterns, the maximum concentrations of NH4+ 
and NO2-, the slope of NH4+ as well as the influent conditions should be observed. When 
looking at the NH4+ slope, values are considerably lower in the day with no peaks than in the 
days with peaks. This points to a lower nitrogen loading in the influent or less nitrogen 
availability. This former is corroborated by the influent TN in each of the represented days. 
TN is lowest during the day with no peaks, intermediate on the day with only a peak during 
aeration and highest on the day with peaks during non-aeration phases. 
Regarding the concentrations of NH4+ and NO2-, peaks of these substances in the day with no 
N2O peaks are around 4 mg/L and 6 mg/L, the day with peaks during aeration they are 
around 5 mg/L and 8 mg/L respectively and the day with peaks during non-aeration around 
6 mg/L and 10 mg/L respectively. It should be noted that the NH4+ upper limit in the control 
is the same for all these days. It is, therefore, a matter of the signal delay causing the control 
to not reacting quickly enough to the higher influent nitrogen, thus allowing higher NH4+ 
concentrations to be achieved, which also results in higher NO2- peaks. In fact, the varying 
delay in the NH4+ sensor signal could be accentuating this difference depending on the day. 
To sum up, if the NH4+ concentration peaks are used to explain the different patterns, in the 
case of the no-N2O-peak pattern, concentrations are kept low enough for no N2O to be 
produced. In the peak during aeration pattern, the NH4+ concentration is enough to trigger 
N2O production through NN. In the peak during non-aeration pattern, this is also true, but 
additionally the NO2- concentration is high enough to trigger ND or HD when DO drops as 
aeration stops.  
However, the achieved COD and COD/TN ratio may actually be a better explanation for why 
peaks during non-aeration are not always observed, since high COD/TN causes assimilation 
and improves heterotrophic denitrification (Phanwilai et al., 2020). The COD/TN ratio was 
7.02 the day with peaks during non-aeration, whereas the day with a peak during aeration 
it was 16.49. The higher COD/TN on the day with only a peak during aeration would increase 
denitrification speed, preventing NO2- accumulation and the consequent N2O production 
during non-aeration. This could be the ultimate justification for the differences between 
those two patterns. Unfortunately, COD/TN does not explain the low-peak pattern on the 
represented day, since a value of 11.49 is observed, which is not too far from the average 
COD/TN ratio of 10. Nonetheless, other days with low peaks do appear to have a high 
COD/TN, as will be discussed in Section 3.4.2., suggesting assimilation can be one of the 
reasons explaining this pattern, but not the only one. 
In general, the information on N2O concentration in the liquid phase obtained with the 
Unisense sensors together with the online data from the other sensors gives a good 
understanding of the processes taking place inside the reactors. The insights that these 
sensors provide us with allow us to find patterns in N2O production, to identify the relevant 
production pathways in our system and under what circumstances they are most prevalent. 
Ultimately, this understanding sets the basis for future improvements and the design of 
control strategies that minimize the carbon footprint of the plant while assuring an 
adequate water quality.

3.4. 
Emissions 
 
3.4.1. Real time emissions 
Emissions were calculated following the equations previously described in Section 2.5. This 
allowed for an approximated real time emission value to be estimated and represented 
together with the other continuously recorded data (DO, NO2-, NH4+ and N2O 
concentrations). Figures 7, 9 and 11 also show the real time emissions in kg N2O/day as 
black dots.  
These figures reveal when a bigger amount of N2O is being released into the atmosphere and 
how this relates to the observed concentration patterns. As expected, the biggest emissions 
always occur during the aeration periods. Even in periods when the concentration curves 
are starkly different as is the case for Figures 7 and 9, the emission pattern is very similar. 
This also shows that the high peak during non-aeration eventually has a very low impact on 
the carbon footprint of the plant. Therefore, efforts should be put in reducing the peak 
during aeration caused by the nitrifiers.  
Despite the already highlighted importance of monitoring N2O concentrations in the liquid 
phase, emission monitoring is the main focus of this study. Concentration alone does not 
give an idea of the environmental impact of the ASRs and the plant in general. The way 
emissions are calculated is therefore crucial to have a veridic idea of how and when N2O is 
being released into the atmosphere. Since the use of gas phase measurements was 
disregarded due to the lack of confidence in the methodology and the available data, 
emissions were calculated using KLa equations and superficial gas velocity. This is also the 
solution proposed by the sensor manufacturer and the way they intended their product to 
be used. However, instead of eploying the calculations integrated in the control box of the 
Unisense system, the attempt of improving upon them was made by using as many real time 
data from the sensors in the ASR tank as possible. This results in a very accurate KLa that 
ensures trustworthy real time emission values.  
Using Clark-type sensors in the liquid phase for emission calculation is a well-established 
strategy. Though it comes with limitations such as being susceptible to interferences and a 
difficulty accounting for special variability, it is also one of the technologies with the lowest 
detection limit (Law et al., 2012). As for the calculation method, a study by Marques et al. 
showed that results calculated using KLa and superficial gas velocity agreed by 87% with 
results obtained through gas phase sensors. However, the KLa used by them was not 
dynamically calculated an adjusted as it is in this study. Still, the most accurate methodology 
when using liquid phase sensors is calculating the KLa based on the oxygen transfer rate 
(OTR) of the reactor integrating fouling, salinity and impurity factors in the estimation. 
Results obtained with this method agreed by a 98% with the gas phase sensor (Marques et 
al., 2016). In the future, the possibility of applying the OTR strategy should be considered to 
obtain even more precise live emissions

always perfectly correlated with cell concentration, but it is usually a good approximation.

3.4.3. Emission Factor 
EFs are a useful tool that allows for fair comparisons of the emissions between plants since 
the emissions are divided by the influent TN. They are also the way in which authorities like 
the IPCC give their approximations of how much N2O water utilities emit. Table 4 shows the 
EFs of the Novozymes plant for the days for which their calculation was possible. It also 
includes COD, TN, COD/TN ratio to the ASRs, TSS and total emissions for those same days. 
This data will be later used in a PCA to try to find correlations between the emissions and 
the other daily obtained parameters. 
A large variability can be seen in the EFs, ranging from 0.28% to 3.57%, with the average EF 
for all the calculated days within the monitoring period being 1.55%. In the context of the 
Novozymes WWTP, which receives a yearly average influent TN of 6 tons/day, this EF of 
1.55% translates to 93 kg/d of N2O being emitted in the whole plant. In a year, this adds up 
to 34 tons of N2O being released into the atmosphere, which in CO2 equivalents corresponds 
to almost 10,000 tons. To put these numbers into perspective, that is comparable to taking 
20,000 transatlantic flights. These results urge us to find improved operation strategies that 
minimize future emissions. 
As alarming as these results might be, they are just below the 1.6% EF suggested by the IPCC 
(IPCC, 2019). It is also considerably lower than the 2.5% estimated in the sustainability 
analysis of the plant based on the full plant model (Monje, Owsianiak, et al., 2022). 
Nevertheless, it is still almost twice as big as the regional EF of 0.84% given by the Danish 
authorities (MUDP, 2020). It should also be acknowledged that EF was only obtained for a 
limited number of days and a longer monitoring period would allow for a more accurate 
estimation. These facts together with the high values observed during certain days, 
demonstrate that there is room for improvement. With a future mitigation plan such 
emissions could be greatly decreased. Potential strategies and their plausibility based on 
experiments will be further discussed in Section 3.6.

are able to perform SNdN. With SNdN concentrations of both NO2-, NH4+ and DO are allowed 
to be kept low. This has a double benefit: firstly, by keeping NO2- and NH4+ low the conditions 
that trigger N2O production are being prevented; secondly, a lower DO may result in lower 
aeration requirements. 
This same strategy of changing from intermittent to continuous aeration with a DO setpoint 
of 0.5 was previously applied by Duan et al. in a WWTP with similar characteristics (influent 
partly orignating from industry, treated first anaerobically and then in SBRs with 
intermittent aeration). In that study a 35% emission reduction with EFs going from 0.89% 
to 0.58% was achieved. Additionally, airflow was reduced by 33%. The main observed 
downside of continuous aeration was a hindered sludge settleability (Duan et al., 2020).  
In our study, during the first 12 hours of the experiment shown in Figure 20, very promising 
results were observed. Low NO2- and NH4+ indicated good nitrogen removal. In fact, NH4+ 
concentrations were lower than NO2- ones, suggesting that the DO setpoint could be set even 
lower. N2O concentrations and emissions were also minimal. Slight peaks were only 
observed at the same time as DO peaks, which are most likely due to difficulties of the 
aeration equipment of the plant to maintain a constantly low aeration. Even though constant 
aeration means constant N2O stripping, the EF was 0.68%, which is very low for our plant 
compared to the estimated average EF of 1.55%. The average airflow was also reduced by 
23% when compared to October 31st, a day with a similar COD and TN, going from 65.2 
Nm3/min to 50.0 Nm3/min. 
==PAGE:42==
42 
 
After the interruption of the experiment, during the last 24 hours, represented in Figure 21, 
there was still a period of good performance. However, around the beginning of November 
30th NO2- started to accumulate, resulting in great N2O peaks and consequent emissions. 
These big peaks originated at relatively low NO2- concentrations, compared to the peaks 
observed during normal operation. This suggest that N2O is being produced through a 
different pathway from the usually observed one. Since the peaks originated due to NO2- 
accumulation at low DO, ND is likely to be the pathway causing the emissions (Duan et al., 
2021).  
The cause of NO2- accumulation is unclear. The shutdown of the anaerobic tank might have 
caused stress in the microbial population, since, while this happened, the F/M ratio must 
have been very high. Another possible explanation is an increase in influent nitrogen or a 
decrease in COD that would negatively affect HD. Unfortunately, COD and TN values were 
not recorded during the first day of the experiment and cannot be compared to the situation 
during the last day. If this is the cause, however, there would be room for improvement. NH4+ 
concentrations remained almost non-existent, indicating that nitritation is performing well. 
By setting an even lower DO, or a dynamic one that decreases based on the NO2- 
concentration, accumulation could be prevented.  
The results of this experiment remain promising due to the very low EF obtained during the 
initial 12 hours. Still, the NO2- accumulation issue showcases the increased sensitivity that 
comes with operating under continuous aeration. This highlights the need for an improved 
control system, since, at the moment, when running under continuous aeration the DO 
setpoint remains fixed. The design of a control strategy with a dynamically calculated DO 
that reacts to NH4+, NO2-, and maybe even N2O is a requirement that should be met if the 
continuous aeration solution is further explored in the future. 
One more consideration should be made that also applies to the other experiments. Since all 
experiments have only been run in one tank and during a short period of time, changes in 
the microbial population cannot be assessed. The good NH4+ removal values observed in this 
experiment may partly be due to “healthy” microorganisms from the remaining six tanks 
entering the monitored one through the sludge recirculation. Furthermore, the impact of the 
high HRT of the ASRs to the microbiome of the sludge can take long to be visible. Before 
implementation of any strategy, prolonged experiments in all tanks would be necessary. The 
full plant model currently under development may also help predict the effects of these 
changes in the operation of the ASRs.  
 
3.6.2. Decreased DO setpoint 
The decreased DO setpoint experiment started December 1st and was run until December 
4th, with profiles not varying significantly throughout the days. Figure 22 represents DO, 
NO2-, NH4+ and N2O concentration and emission profiles for a 12-hour period on December 
3rd. During this period DO peaks reached a maximum of 4.27 mg/L, despite the established 
setpoint of 2.5 mg/L. Aeration was working 38.61% of the time with an approximate airflow 
of 105.9 Nm3/min during aeration. Both peaks for NH4+ and NO2- were close to 5 mg/L, with 
the maximum peaks being 5.05 mg/L NH4+ and 4.69 mg/L for NO2-. Influent COD and TN for 
that day were 7.73 ton/day/tank and 0.67 ton/day respectively. The emission factor for the 
represented period was 1.17%.  
==PAGE:43==
43 
 
 
Figure 22.  DO, NO2-, NH4+ and N2O concentration and emission profiles during 12 hours of Experiment 2. 
This experiment aimed to assess if nitritation could be performed with lower aeration. This 
would reduce the NH4+ oxidation rate, the pathway that, from previous results in this study, 
is thought to be the main contributor to N2O production at the Novozymes WWTP. This 
would also extend the duration of the periods, reducing the number of switches from 
aeration to non-aeration, which is a moment often correlated to higher emissions (Duan et 
al., 2021). A reduction in aeration would also be expected. 
Although reducing DO can trigger ND, studies show that this pathway is not observed when 
DO is above 1 mg/L (Peng et al., 2014). This strategy is thus a popular approach that has 
been proven to effectively reduce N2O emissions both in models (Duan et al., 2020) and at 
full scale in different sorts of plants like intermittently aerated bioreactors (Chen et al., 
2019) and membrane bioreactors (Wen et al., 2020). 
Figure 22 shows the general performance of the tank during the experiment. N2O peaks 
were only observed during aeration and were relatively low, resulting in an emission factor 
of 1.17%, which is also a considerable improvement from the estimated plant EF of 1.55%. 
Other than that, a good comparison is hard to make because there were no days with a 
similar influent COD and TN during the monitoring period. On November 21st the influent 
TN was similar, and the EF was 2.16%, although the average airflow was only improved by 
2.4% with the lowered DO, going from 41.5 Nm3/min to 40.5 Nm3/min. On November 4th 
the COD/TN ratio was similar but that day the EF was 1.15%, so actually better than during 
the experiment. Aeration did improve by 39.7% being 67.0 Nm3/min on average for that day. 
However, as already addressed in Section 3.5.2., comparing days based on the influent 
conditions only might not be very informative of the actual improvement.  
Another change seen in Figure 22 is that NH4+ peaks are now higher than NO2- peaks, which 
was not the case during normal operation. This points at nitritation performing slower that 
denitritation, which was somewhat expected. Although comparison with the other ASRs is 
unfair due to each reactor performing differently under normal conditions, the monitored 
reactor, where the experiment was run, did go from being the one with the second-best 
==PAGE:44==
44 
 
nitritation rate to being the worst performing. This raises the question of whether a high 
influent would be able to be handled if all the reactors were run with a DO setpoint of 2 
mg/L. In fact, the current setpoint of 5 mg/L was established by engineers from Novozymes 
based on their observed requirements during the highest loads. 
Although the results from this experiment are less exciting than those of Experiment 1, it 
does look like there is still some room for improvement when operating under the current 
control strategy. Although a 2 mg/L DO setpoint might not be enough for high loadings, it 
does appear to be able to handle average ones. Therefore, the equation that currently 
calculates the operating DO could be more finely adjusted to minimize aeration when a high 
DO is not necessary potentially also slightly improving N2O emissions.  
 
3.6.3. Decreased DO, NO2- and NH4+ setpoints 
The decreased DO, NO2- and NH4+ setpoints experiment was run between December 18th and 
December 21st. However, the normal operation of the tank was interrupted several times 
during the experiment due to a malfunction of the NO2- sensor. The sensor registered 
increasing concentrations leading the signal surpassing the upper NO2- limit, causing 
aeration to stop and NH4+ to accumulate. Figure 23 represents a 12-hour period between 
December 20th and December 21st when results were not affected by the sensor problem. 
During this period, DO reached peaks of up to 1.81 mg/L. Aeration was working 44.2% of 
the time with an average airflow of 39.78 Nm3/min. NO2- and NH4+ peaks reached a 
maximum value of 4.30 mg/L and 4.35 mg/L respectively. The influent COD and TN were 
1.72 ton/day/tank and 0.24 ton/day/tank respectively, hence very low. The emission factor 
of the period was 1.87%. It should be noted that during this experiment the Novozymes 
production plant was preparing for shutdown and due to the lower loading two of the ASRs 
were not in operation to save resources.  
 
Figure 23.  DO, NO2-, NH4+ and N2O concentration and emission profiles during 12 hours of Experiment 3. 
==PAGE:45==
45 
 
The purpose of this experiment was to maintain low concentrations of the substances that 
trigger N2O production. Also, to emulate the conditions met in Experiment 1 but maintaining 
the current control strategy, which allows for a dynamic DO setpoint. The focus of this 
experiment was not to apply a common mitigation strategy to the plant, but rather to test 
the limitations of the current control system.  
Regarding the N2O concentration pattern in Figure 23, it can be seen that there is a peak 
during aeration but also a certain peak at the beginning of the non-aeration period. However, 
it is only the peak during aeration that significantly affects emissions. The already stated EF 
of 1.87% is actually higher than in other experiments and the previously estimated average 
EF of the plant. Thus, the tested strategy does not appear to be beneficial in terms of N2O 
emissions. As for NO2- and NH4+ concentrations it appears that removal is successful and 
occurs rapidly. The slope of NH4+ accumulation is very low which is logical given the very 
low influent COD and TN concentrations. The reason why the influent was so low at the time 
of this experiment is that the Novozymes production plant had already shut down for the 
holiday period. The low TN is also responsible for the relatively high EF that contrasts with 
what the real time emissions appear to show. Due to the minimum influent at which the 
plant was operating, the success in nitrogen removal cannot be extrapolated to more normal 
conditions.  
Nevertheless, the experiment does reveal some valuable information about the system. Even 
though the DO setpoint was set at a maximum of 0.5 mg/L, the DO peaks reach values of up 
to 1.81 mg/L. This points to either the control or the aeration system not being able to 
maintain low DO. This contrasts with the continuous aeration experiment, where the 
desired DO setpoint of 0.5 mg/L was achieved with reasonable accuracy. When examining 
the airflow used in experiments 1 and 3 an average airflow of around 30 Nm3/min is 
observed in both, yet the DO varies greatly. This shows that the reason why DO is higher in 
experiment 3 is actually that the influent is very low and the microorganisms do not get to 
use so much O2. Therefore, it is the aeration system that is limited with 30 Nm3/min being 
the minimum airflow it can provide.  
 
All things considered, the experiments reveal great potential for improvement when it 
comes to N2O emissions reduction. However, before implementation prolonged tests in all 
the ASRs must be conducted to verify the obtained results. The experiments also show the 
limitations of the current aeration system when providing very low airflows. This should be 
taken into consideration in future experiments. For example, as an improvement to 
Experiment 1, it was proposed to reduce the DO below 0.5 mg/L, to promote denitritation 
against nitritation, but this might not be possible if airflow cannot be further decreased. 
Limitations were also met when using the control strategies just as they are at the moment. 
Therefore, an even more precise control could be the key to operating the ASRs at their full 
potential. 
In the future, apart from further experimenting with the control of the tanks, other 
strategies could be considered. Given the correlation between high COD/TN and low 
emissions due to assimilation, the possibility of dosing COD directly into the ASRs could be 
of great interest. Upcoming experiments could also be focused on this type of solitons.

4 Conclusions 
The conclusions drawn from this N2O monitoring project at the Novozymes WWTP in 
Kalundborg are summarized here: 
- 
The Unisense N2O sensor is not adequate for gas phase monitoring without a 
validated calibration method and an emission calculation strategy. 
- 
Three N2O peak patterns were identified from the liquid phase measurements: 
highest peak during non-aeration, highest peak during aeration and low peaks. 
- 
Using a real time calculated kLa provides accurate live emissions using data from the 
liquid phase sensor. 
- 
Only N2O peaks during aeration have a relevant impact on the overall emissions of 
the plant. 
- 
Nitrifiers nitrification is the pathway responsible for high N2O concentrations during 
aeration and, thus, for the general emissions. 
- 
Some changes in the daily emissions of the ASRs can be explained based on the 
performance of other sections of the plant. 
- 
A high COD/TN ratio can trigger assimilation and reduce N2O emissions. 
- 
The estimated emission factor of the Novozymes WWTP is 1.55%. 
- 
Principal Component Analysis is successful at representing patterns and identifying 
correlations between N2O and other monitored parameters. 
- 
Alternative control strategies such as SNdN through continuous aeration or 
operating with a lowered DO are plausible and have the potential to reduce 
emissions. 
- 
Improved control strategies could be developed to minimize N2O emissions, taking 
into consideration the limitations of the airflow system.
