| Abstract
Mixing is an important step in the chemical and biochemical industry that reduces
segregation of components in order to achieve a homogeneous state. Speciﬁcally, mixing
of ﬂuids is vital for a majority of industrial operations dealing with chemical, biochemical
or biological processes that can be accomplished through the application of static mixers.
However, the mixing eﬃciency of static mixers is not widely investigated, and the research
that has been conducted resulted in diﬀerent conclusions depending on the parameter
being investigated. Conclusions that state eﬀective distributive mixing is experienced with
increasing mixing elements, under turbulent conditions or in some cases under creeping
conditions are some examples of what has been contemplated. All previous studies that
have been conducted used numerical and experimental methods to analyze the mixing
eﬃciency. Speciﬁcally, computational ﬂuid dynamic (CFD) as a tool has been used to
investigate the mixing eﬃciency of static mixers, which leads to the objective of this thesis.
The scope is to numerically and experimentally analyze the mixing eﬃciency of a static
mixer containing six mixing elements,a pipe length of 500mm and diameter of 25.4mm.
The numerical analysis was executed using ANSYS CFX 18.1 that consisted of applying
two modelling approaches one being a homogeneous mixture model (HM model), and the
other was a multicomponent model that was deﬁned by specifying that the composition of
the mixture should vary through space and time. This was referred to as the VCM model,
or variable composition mixture model. The parameters that were investigated consisted
of varying the initial linear velocity using 0.005 m/s, 0.05 m/s, 0.2 m/s, 1 m/s and 1.5 m/s
as the selected speeds in order to capture creeping, laminar and turbulent ﬂow regimes.
Two length to diameter ratios of the helical mixing elements was also investigated that
consisted of a 1:1 and 2:1 ratio. The working ﬂuids that were used for the investigation
was glycerol and water at ambient temperature.
The experimental analysis consisted
of 2:1 length to diameter ratio, and using a OX-EDDY-911006 micro-sensor to measure
oxygen levels near the wall and center after the sixth mixing element with a TP-200-
911981 temperature sensor for compensation. 0.05 m/s, 0.2 m/s and 1 m/s were the only
linear speeds tested because of pumping diﬃculties caused by high viscosity of glycerol,
and to inconsistency of oxygen readings.
To quantify the mixing performance, the coeﬃcient of variation (CoV) was used with nu-
merical data obtained through cross-sectional planes placed after each mixing element con-
taining glycerol fractions. It was concluded that both models produced identical trends,
but the VCM model at 0.005 m/s resulted in the lowest CoV value of 0.039 after the
sixth element in contrast to 0.042 obtained through the HM model. The CoV value had
a decreasing trend with increasing mixing elements for all initial velocities, but 1 m/s
and 1.5 m/s resulted in an approximate 90% change in CoV value between ﬁrst and sixth
mixing elements. Despite questionable results, it was overall concluded that both mod-
els were identical, and that increasing the velocity and number of mixing elements had
a positive inﬂuence on the distributive mixing. For case 2, both models also generated
identical trends with the HM model having the lowest CoV of 0.207 at 1.5 m/s in contrast
to 0.221 obtained by the VCM model. 0.005 m/s, 0.05 m/s and 0.2 m/s appeared to have
no substantial impact on the CoV value with increasing mixing elements. In both cases,
the shear stress proﬁle was also investigated, and it was concluded that areas where high
levels of shear stress occurred were around the intersections between mixing elements.
When the two cases, it was found that a 1:1 L/D ratio resulted in an overall lower CoV

| Introduction
Mixing quality has a signiﬁcant impact on the success or failure of industrial pro-
cesses, speciﬁcally in relation to the chemical and biochemical industry. The mechanisms
behind mixing are detailed, and cover macroscopic and microscopic phenomena, but the
general understanding is taking two or more components and bringing them into a homoge-
neous state through physical means. In other words, it is the reduction of in-homogeneity
to achieve a desired result such as obtaining the same temperature, concentration or
achieving the same phase. This is either done through conventional mixers that apply ro-
tational movement with impellers and stirrers also known as dynamic mixing, or by static
mixing that does not require any rotational power. Static mixers come in diﬀerent types
such as Kenics, SMX and SMV that vary in the geometrical shape of the mixing elements,
but the focus of this project is on a helical structured static mixer. Static mixers utilize
the structure of the blades that consists of a number of elements mounted inside a pipe.
These elements are positioned such that the leading element is perpendicular to the next
element resulting in a splitting and reuniting of the process stream. As the process stream
is split and reunited, dissipation, convection and turbulence of the ﬂow all contribute to
mixing the components as they advance through the pipe. [Soman, 2016][Thakur et al.,
2003].
Poor mixing can lead to various undesired results depending on a speciﬁc process such
as wide particle size distributions, undesired chemical/biochemical pathways that lead to
overproduction of impurities, large concentration gradients or reduction in mass trans-
fer[Nienow et al., 1997]. Therefore, it is highly desired to understand the inﬂuence of
speciﬁc parameters on the outcome of the distributive mixing inside a static mixer, with
the intention that such issues can be avoided or minimized. This leads to the purpose
of this study, which is to investigate the mixing behavior of a mixture containing glyc-
erol and water, which will resemble an industrial scenario where a polymer ﬂuid and
a process stream containing biomass passes through a static mixer. In this study, the
polymer ﬂuid will be glycerol, and the process stream will be water. The polymer ﬂuid
acts as a ﬂocculant triggering agglomeration of the diluted process stream creating ﬂocs
and aggregates in order to separate unwanted material from the product. It is assumed
that, under certain conditions, poor mixing in the static mixer occur that results in a
heterogeneous mixture, which this work aims to investigate. This will be accomplished
by ﬁrst conducting an numerical analysis with the help of ANSYS CFX 18.1, which is a
Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) program. The static mixer to be modelled has six
helical mixing elements with 180◦alternating blades, a total pipe length of 500 mm and a
pipe diameter of 25.4 mm. Two diﬀerent length to diameter ratios for the mixing elements
was investigated that consisted of a 1:1 an 2:1 ratio. Five diﬀerent initial speeds were
tested to determine what inﬂuence this had on the mixing eﬃciency. This consisted of
0.005 m/s, 0.05 m/s, 0.2 m/s, 1 m/s and 1.5 m/s that covered creeping, laminar and tur-
bulent ﬂow regimes. The selection of initial velocities were based on literature study, and
to ensure the diﬀerent ﬂow regimes were simulated. Two diﬀerent modelling approaches
were applied that consisted of a multicomponent model based on mass fractions, and
the other being a homogeneous mixture model that was volume based. The descriptive
analysis consisted of constructing cross-sectional planes after each mixing element, and
placing three monitoring points after the sixth mixing element with two close to the wall,
and one being in the center. Contour plots containing the concentration and shear stress

2.1
| Fundamentals on static mixers
Static mixers, also referred to as motionless mixers, in the process industry have
been frequently applied because of their attractive characteristics.
Process industries
such as chemical, pharmaceutical, food processing, polymer synthesis, water treatment
and petrochemical utilize import characteristics that static mixers provide such as lower
capital costs, lower energy consumption, easily cleanable parts and respectably shorter
residence time compared to traditional Continuous Stirred Tank Reactor (CSTR) or batch
reactors.
Static mixers can either be constructed into a hollow tube or channel with
diﬀerent geometries, depending on the speciﬁc process, that inﬂuences the ﬂow to promote
or enhance mass and heat transfer. Other types of static mixers consist of inserting a series
of identical and stationary parts referred to as elements. The shape of the mixing elements
determines the behaviour of ﬂuid ﬂow, which indirectly inﬂuences the eﬀectiveness of the
mixer [Thakur et al., 2003][Ghanem et al., 2014][Jones et al., 2002]. Static mixers rely on
external pumps to drive process streams across the elements, which is why the pressure
drop is an important factor to take into consideration when deciding on what type of static
mixer, and the number of elements. High pressure drops will lead to higher operational
and equipment cost in order to achieve the necessary pressure to drive the ﬂuid through
the piping system [Soman, 2016]. The general motive for applying static mixers is to
obtain stable dispersions, and to increase interfacial area for mass transfer [Thakur et al.,
2003]. A common static mixer applied in the industry, speciﬁcally for viscous ﬂuids, is the
Kenics helical element structure that consists of several alternating right and left-hand
helices that are placed at a 90◦angle to each other. The helical elements are the more
attractive geometrical shapes to use in static mixers when conducting numerical analysis
on ﬂuid ﬂow because its simple design compared to other static mixing elements. Each
element twists at an angle of 180◦(around 0.5 revolutions)[Jones et al., 2002]. A picture
of a helical static mixer can be seen in ﬁgure 1. This speciﬁc static mixer can be used
in both laminar and turbulent ﬂow regimes under diﬀerent areas of application such as
thermal homogenization of polymer melt, Gas-Liquid dispersion, mixing of high viscosity
ﬂuids and dispersion of viscous liquids[Thakur et al., 2003].

The basic principles involving static mixers consist of a series of division, conversion and
inversion of the process streams. Starting with the division step, the process streams are
divided (split) as the ﬂuids pass over a mixing element. Layers are created as a result of the
process stream being split, and the number of layers can be determined by raising 2 to the
power of element number (Layers = 2n). Flow conversion occurs when the process stream
is forced to the next element causing the ﬂuid to move radially. This is dominated by
radial mixing caused by cross-sectional displacements because of the geometrical structure
of the mixing elements that convert the direction of the process streams, and results in a
speed diﬀerence between the molecules causing shearing. As the process streams change
direction when passing through each mixing element, rapid inversion of inertial forces
take place, which directly agitates the streams. Thus, ideally leading to homogeneity
after passing through a certain number of mixing elements depending on the speciﬁc
process. For laminar ﬂow regimes, it is understood that the main mixing mechanism is the
dividing of the process streams. On the contrary, for high turbulent ﬂow a combination of
conversion and inversion dominates [Soman, 2016][Thakur et al., 2003]. These mechanisms
have been analyzed using computational ﬂuid dynamics that is used as a tool to determine
the mixing eﬃciency of static mixers, and to help understand the ﬂow behaviour. A more
thorough explanation will be given in section 2.2.2 that goes into more detail on the
governing equations for ﬂuid ﬂow analysis.

2.1.1
| Flow characterisation
The ﬂow of liquids through a pipe can occur in two ways referred to as laminar and
turbulent. There is also a transitional region between laminar and turbulent ﬂow regimes,
but the general ﬂow is either classiﬁed as being laminar or turbulent. In laminar ﬂow,
the velocity proﬁle is parabolic with the maximum velocity at the center, and a non-slip
condition can be used at the wall where the velocity is zero if the wall is stationary. For
turbulent ﬂow conditions, the ﬂow is in a swirling motion which results in a smoothing
velocity proﬁle across pipe cross section. However, the swirling motion caused by eddies
is found at the center away from the pipe wall. The outer layer of the velocity proﬁle
will still ﬂow laminar that sometimes is referred to as the "Laminar Sub-Layer" because
the viscosity of the ﬂuid creates a boundary that suppresses the turbulence. Therefore, a
no-slip condition can still be assumed. A illustrative representation of the two ﬂow types
can be found in ﬁgure 3[Clement et al., 2004].

turbulent (right ﬁgure) ﬂow regimes [Clement et al., 2004].

behaviour. It was discovered by Osborne Reynolds that inside an empty pipe, the di-

behaviour[Clement et al., 2004].

2.1.2
| Studies involving mixing quality of static mixers
Since the late 1970s, where static mixers became more frequent in the process indus-
try, there have been few studies involving mixing behaviour and ﬂuid dynamics in relation
to static mixers. The reason for this is the complex geometry that is associated with static
mixers making it impractical to use analytical solutions compared to traditional rotational
mixers [Thakur et al., 2003]. Thus, the application of Computational Fluid Dynamics has
been a vital tool that applies a numerical approach. A more thorough explanation of the
Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) as a tool will be given in section 2.2.1. The stud-
ies that have been conducted involved the investigation of diﬀerent Reynolds numbers,
preferably in the area where creeping and laminar ﬂows occur (10−5 ≤Re ≤1000) using
a Kenics helical static mixer [Hobbs et al., 1998][Byrde and Sawley, 1999]. The reason for
operating at low Reynolds numbers is due to the simplicity associated in modelling the
ﬂuid ﬂow when applying dynamic models such as the Navier Stokes equation. Complexity
of the modelling is reduced for creeping ﬂow because the acceleration term, presented in
section 2.2.2, is neglected resulting in a simpliﬁed Navier-Stokes equation. For laminar
ﬂow, it is easier to numerically predict the ﬂow compared to turbulent conditions. The
proﬁle is smooth with regular paths in contrast with turbulent ﬂow regimes that has ir-
regular movement of particles of the ﬂuid. In addition, for laminar ﬂow the ﬂuid ﬂows in
parallel layers with no disruption compared to very high lateral mixing under turbulent
ﬂow. Not only is the modelling less complex, but the computational time needed to solve
the ﬂuid models is reduced, which has been the main reason for operating under creeping
and laminar conditions [Jones et al., 2002][Larsson et al., 2015][CFX][ANSYS-Modeling,
2011].
In an article by [Hobbs et al., 1998], it was found that for creeping ﬂow involving New-
tonian ﬂuids (Re ≤10), the ﬂow through a Kenics static mixer is globally chaotic and
that the mixing performance is independent on the Reynolds number. However, under
laminar conditions the mixing performance is dependent on the Reynolds number. This
was obtained using a CFD software package (FLUENT/UNS) to analyze velocity pro-

increased. Through this it was also concluded that there were areas where mass transfer

eﬃcient mixing, and is postulated to be a direct result of few parameters investigated in
both experiments. However, both did conclude that the most stretching, and therefore
the most mixing occurs at the intersection between elements under creeping ﬂow con-

pressure drop per unit element in a Kenics static mixer increases with increasing Reynolds

2.1.3
| Shear stress and rate for Newtonian ﬂuids
A majority of the mentioned instances where static mixers were investigated involved
Newtonian ﬂuids even though the application of static mixers are highly used in the
industry involving Non-Newtonian ﬂuids. A Newtonian ﬂuid is a ﬂuid where the viscous
stresses in the ﬂow are linearly proportional to the strain rate at every point. In order
words, the viscosity is independent of the strain rate, and the ﬂuid is incompressible
meaning the density and viscosity is constant. On the contrary, a Non-Newtonian ﬂuid
does not satisfy Newton’s law of viscosity and therefore is dependent on the strain rate
[Bird et al.]. Shear strain rate is also referred to as the velocity gradient, which is the ratio
between the velocity of a moving ﬂuid to the distance from a wall. It is an indication of the
rate the ﬂuid layers move along a given geometry. When multiplying the viscosity with
the shear rate, the shear stress is obtained. Shear stress is the force acting tangentially
to a surface of a ﬂuid causing deformation. In context with ﬂuid ﬂow, the forces are the
viscous forces that cause friction and resistance when the ﬂuid is moving along a wall. For
incompressible Newtonian ﬂuids, the shear stress is directly proportional to the shear rate.
However, this is not the case for Non-Newtonian ﬂuids since the viscosity is changing[Bird
et al.]. Shear stress is a tensor that is expressed in the following when using Cartesian

For laminar conditions, shear rate is an important parameter that can be used to quantify
dispersive mixing since the boundary layers vary in diﬀerent ﬂow velocities in contrast to
turbulent ﬂow patterns. Increasing shear rate and stress promotes more dispersive mixing,
which can be seen in ﬂuid ﬂowing through mixing elements compared to an empty pipe.
However, higher shear stress indirectly means more energy of the ﬂuid will be lost in
terms of pressure, since there is a huge pressure drop when using static mixers. Previous
studies have analyzed shear stress and rate distributions at diﬀerent cross-sectional planes
across a static mixer at Reynolds number of 30. It was observed, using contour plots,
that the shear rate distribution was non-uniform, and that low shear rates occur when
the blades are spares (thin). On the contrary, high shear stress areas were located around
the junction areas between mixing elements. It was postulated that the areas with high
shear rate is where more stretching and dispersion of the ﬂuid occur. With variations in
shear stress and shear rate at diﬀerent cross sectional planes, indicated that there was
discrepancies in the dispersive mixing across the static mixer[Soman, 2016].

2.2.1.1
Mesh construction
Meshing is an important pre-processing step before simulating a ﬂow problem. The
principle behind meshing is to discretize a domain into nodes and elements converting con-
tinuous functions/models into discrete counterparts. This is speciﬁcally eﬀective in solving
partial diﬀerential equations by converting them into algebraic expressions [Sadrehaghighi,

nodes, edges and cell centers are represented [Bakker].
Each cell represents a control volume into which the domain is broken into smaller sub-
domains. Nodes are speciﬁc points depicting the point in the geometrical structure that
could represent independent variables such as time or space. Faces and edges are the
boundaries of a speciﬁc cell that have a physical representation (e.g., inlets, outlets, walls,
ﬂuid regions). Cell centers contain data, also referred as a variable, for each speciﬁc cell
that the CFD software uses[Ferziger and Peric, 2012].
There are diﬀerent types of geometrical structures for meshes that fall into either unstruc-
tured or structured meshes. Depending on the speciﬁc geometrical domain, either unstruc-
tured or structured meshes can be chosen. Unstructured meshes incorporate tetrhedrals
for 3D or triangles for 2D because of the geometrical complexity of a given structure that
has irregularities in the shape. Furthermore, unstructured meshes are easily generated
by using the built in "mesh generation" function in a given software such as ICEM, AN-
SYS Workbench or ANSYS Fluent. For structured meshes, the structured grids do not
have irregularities, therefore a square like geometry can be used for the meshing such as
hexahedrals for 3D and quadrilaterals for 2D. Structured meshes are time consuming to
generate, and require a more manual approach compared to structured meshes[Ferziger
and Peric, 2012]. With respect to this project, a unstructured mesh will used because of
the geometrical complexity of the static mixer.

As stated before, a mesh grid contains elements and nodes that split the domain into
smaller cells. Determining the right number of elements and nodes can inﬂuence the ac-

element quality. With regards to orthogonal quality, it is the angle between the vector

place. To justify a good mesh, a value close to 1 should be used to determine the correct
mesh size with an accepted value above 0.20. Skewness explains how well the element

ideally this should be close to 0. Aspect ratio is the relationship between the longest to
the shortest side in a cell, and ideally should be 1. A maximum value should not exceed
100. Lastly, the element quality should ideally be close to 1[Hybschmann, 2017][ICEM].
The criteria is dependent on the software where the mesh is constructed, and the criteria
provided here is relevant for ANSYS Workbench 18.1.

2.2.3
| Modeling turbulent ﬂow
Modeling turbulent regimes using CFD is a diﬃcult task because of certain char-
acteristics such as eddy formation. The occurrence of eddy formation results in more
complex models required in order to try and predict ﬂuid behaviour inside a pipe be-
cause of the chaotic nature eddies bring. These eddies carry a wide range of vorticities
diﬀering in scale that contribute to the random chaotic nature. Therefore, to predict
a particle moving in a ﬂuid under turbulent conditions or ﬂuid behaviour in general is
a diﬃcult task that involves immense computational power[Larsson et al., 2015][CFX].
Turbulent ﬂows are characterized by their ﬂuctuating velocity ﬁelds that consist of mass,
energy and momentum transport phenomena. Modeling of such problems depends on the
physics occurring in a given ﬂow problem, level of accuracy and of course the availability
of computational resources capable of solving the complex mathematical problems. There
are three basic approaches in ANSYS CFX to solve turbulent problems. This consist of
the Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS), Large-Eddy Simulation (LES) and Reynolds Av-
eraged Navier-Stokes Simulation (RANS). DNS fully solves the unsteady Navier-Stokes
equations without any turbulence model. This means no modeling is required, and that
all spatial scales present in the turbulence must be resolved in the mesh using a very
small step size. The step size can be determined by calculating the Courant number
that provides a good indication for stability. Too high Courant number leads to accuracy
problems. The initial time-step was determined by calculating the Courant-Friedrichslevy
number (CFL), which is a condition used to ensure convergence when solving partial
diﬀerential equations.

2.2.4
| Modeling a two ﬂuid system
There are two approaches in ANSYS CFX 18.1 to model a situation containing two
separate ﬂuids. One approach is referred to as the multicomponent ﬂow method, which
assumes that the components in the mixture are mixed at a molecular level, and one
single phase is present. This could be represented by specifying density, viscosity or even
mass fractions. The components share a mean velocity, pressure and temperature ﬁelds
resulting in only one set of the governing equations being solved. It is also assumed that
mass transfer is governed by convection and diﬀusion.
If mass fractions are speciﬁed
then the multicomponent model approach is a Fixed Composition Mixture. On the other
hand, if fractions are not known then a Variable Composition Mixture can be used that
states both components vary in space and time regarding their proportions. This could
be done by specifying mass fractions at diﬀerent boundaries. Overall, this approach takes
the physical proprieties and speciﬁed conditions to determine an average value for the
governing equations, which means that the average is highly dependent on what exactly
is speciﬁed[ANSYS-Modeling, 2011].
The other approach deals with applying methods from multi-phase ﬂow modeling where
more than one ﬂuid is present. A ﬂuid in ANSYS CFX context can refer to a liquid,
gas or solid.
This approach does not assume that the components (ﬂuids) mix at a
microscopic scale, and that the materials can either possess their own or common ﬂow
ﬁeld. Instead of micro mixing, it is assumed that the components mix on a macro scale
with a distinguishable interface between the ﬂuids. Typically, this approach is used to
simulate multiple ﬂuid streams, bubbles, droplets, solid particles or even free surface
ﬂows. There are two distinct models (methods) provided in ANSYS CFX that consist
of a Eulerian-Eulerian and Lagrangian Particle Tracking for a multi-phase model. From
a theoretical perspective, the Eulerian-Eulerian method solves the ﬂuid ﬂow problem at
a ﬁxed point at which the ﬂuid passes through. This could be a exact coordinate, or a
domain where a control volume is established to analyze the ﬂuid with respect to time and
space. On the contrary, the Lagrangian Particle Tracking principle solves the ﬂuid ﬂow by
following the ﬂuid using particles[Ferziger and Peric, 2012]. Within each distinctive multi-
phase model consists of several simpler implementations such as inhomogenous multi-
phase ﬂow, homogeneous multi-phase ﬂow and multicomponent multi-phase ﬂow. The
inhomogeneous method involves separate velocity and other ﬁelds for each ﬂuid that is
speciﬁed, but the pressure ﬁeld is common for all components. Furthermore, the ﬂuids
18
==PAGE:35==
interact at the inter-phase. Homogeneous is a much simpliﬁed version of the Eulerian-
Eulerian method where all ﬂuids share the same velocity, pressure and other gradient
ﬁelds. Multi-component multi-phase ﬂow, as the name depicts, is a combination of both
approaches.
Overall, the general distinction between the two approaches is that one method imple-
ments the volume fraction of a given ﬂuid, and uses this to solve the governing equations,
while the other applies the mass fraction. In other words, one is mass fraction based, and
the other is volume fraction based. The choice between what is most appropriate is highly
dependent on a given situation, and of course the information available regarding weight
fractions and thermodynamic properties. Validating the most eﬀective and eﬃcient model
to a given situation should come from experimental data that will help determine which
model best represents the mixing phenomena inside a static mixer[Thakur et al., 2003].

| Results and Discussion
This section will present results from the numerical and experimental investigations
that were conducted under the period of this project. Results will not only be presented,
but a discussion will be provided followed by the comparison between numerical and
experimental results.
First, the numerical analysis will be discussed for case 1 and 2
with individual discussions followed by a comparison between the two cases. Then, the
experimental analysis will be presented. Lastly, a comparison will be given to validate
how eﬃcient the CFD model is in describing the mixing behaviour inside the helical static
mixer. A illustrative ﬂow chart is provided in ﬁgure 21 that givens an overview of the
methodology during numerical and experimental investigations conducted in this context.
The numerical analysis started with sketching, followed by meshing and setting the model
up for simulating the ﬂuid ﬂow problem. A mesh analysis was then conducted until the
optimal mesh was found. This optimal mesh was then implemented under the respective
case study. The ﬂow problem was simulated, and convergence was checked. If this was
not satisﬁed, then diﬀerent model speciﬁcations were changed. If the system converged,
then the results were evaluated. If the data was reasonable with respect to literature,
then the model is validated using the experimental data.

There has been documented studies with regards to the inﬂuence changes in the
geometry of the mixing elements has on the distributive mixing. Speciﬁcally, the length
to the diameter ratio has been investigated using ratios of 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1, 1.25, 1.5 ,1.75
and 2 under creeping conditions using a working ﬂuid viscosity of 500cP, pipe diameter
of 5.08 cm and length of 10.16 cm. It was concluded that the stretching per unit length,
which was used to quantify the mixing, was greatest at lower length to diameter ratios
of the mixing elements, which implied that low L/D ratios could provide a given amount
of mixing in a shorter residence time. However, when normalizing the stretching rate
with the pressure drop, which was the energy input of the system, it was found that the
mixing eﬃciency was independent of the L/D ratio of the mixing elements[Hobbs and
Muzzio, 1998]. This is why it is interesting to compare case 1 and case 2 to determine if
this is valid even though this study applied only two diﬀerent length to diameter ratios.
The results for both modelling approaches were plotted separately for each case, and the
results can be found in ﬁgure 48. In this context, the CoV is not normalized with the
pressure drop because it is not a direct measurement of stretching, which was relevant
for [Hobbs and Muzzio, 1998]. Furthermore, the stretching intensity was plotted against
the L/D ratio in contrast to this study where the CoV is plotted against the number of
mixing elements.

| Conclusion
This research aimed to analyze the mixing eﬃciency of an inline static mixer incor-
porated with six helical mixing elements with glycerol and water at ambient temperature
as the working ﬂuids. The analysis consisted of a numerical study accomplished by using
ANSYS CFX 18.1, which is a commercial Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) software,
in order to simulate the ﬂuid ﬂow problem. Here, two case studies were analyzed that
consisted of case 1 with a 1:1 ratio and case 2 that had 2:1 length to diameter ratio of
the helical mixing elements where two diﬀerent modelling approaches were tested using
the initial velocity as the manipulating variable consisting of ﬁve diﬀerent linear speeds.
This consisted of 0.005 m/s, 0.05 m/s, 0.2 m/s, 1 m/s and 1.5 m/s in order to cover
creeping, laminar and turbulent ﬂow regimes. A statistical approach that consisted of
calculating the coeﬃcient of variation (CoV) was used as a parameter to quantify the
mixing eﬃciency. One approach was referred to as HM, which was a homogeneous mix-
ture model that was based on specifying volume fractions, and the other was the VCM
model (variable composition mixture) that is classiﬁed as a multicomponent method where
mass fractions were speciﬁed. Based on a quantitative and descriptive analysis using each
model to capture the mixing eﬃciency of the static mixer, it can ﬁrst be concluded that
both modelling approaches resulted in identical trends. For case 1, the trend observed was
a decreasing coeﬃcient of variation value with increasing mixing elements for all of the
ﬁve initial velocities tested. The results had discrepancies with documented studies, and
did not satisfy expectations. These discrepancies were seen at laminar ﬂow that resulted
in a lower CoV value after the sixth mixing element compared to turbulent conditions. It
was not expected to see that both turbulent and laminar ﬂows would have approximately
the same decreasing rate with increasing mixing elements. Instead, it was anticipated
that laminar ﬂows would have a gradual decrease in CoV values with increasing mix-
ing elements, and turbulent conditions would have steeper decreasing trend. For case 2,
both modelling approaches resulted in no substantial inﬂuence in the CoV value when
increasing the number of mixing elements for 0.005 m/s, 0.05 m/s and 0.2 m/s linear
initial velocities. 1 m/s and 1.5 m/s did have a decreasing CoV value with increasing
mixing elements, which did satisfy expectations. Overall, it was concluded that having a
1:1 length to diameter ratio improved the distributive mixing, but furthermore analysis is
required using more ratios to strengthen this conclusion. Lower CoV values were achieved
with 1:1 ratio, but the pressure drop was higher.
The second analysis consisted of an experimental study with a static mixer consisted of six
helical mixing elements that had a 2:1 length to diameter ratio. An oxygen micro-sensor
and temperature probe for compensation was used to measure oxygen signals at diﬀerent
positions after the sixth mixing element. Under the experimentation, operating problems
were experienced that lead to inconsistent results, and only 0.05 m/s, 0.2 m/s and 1 m/s
linear velocities tested. The purpose of the experiments were to validate the CFD, but
because of questionable results, the validation was inconclusive.

| Future work
The investigations carried out under this project period have generated conclusive
results regarding the mixing eﬃciency of a six helical blade static mixer. However, there
are many diﬀerent tests and adaptations that were not looked into because of time short-
age. The focus here has mainly been on establishing a CFD model to describe the mixing
behaviour between two simple ﬂuids, and to validate with experimental results. Future
work would be of course to investigate more complex ﬂuids, such as Non-Newtonian ﬂuids
since static mixers are highly applicable in this area. ANSYS CFX and Workbench have
built in general Non-Newtonian models such as power law index, Carreau and Herschel-
Bulkley model for direct use, but all require speciﬁc parameters that are dependent on
the ﬂuids being analyzed. This of course would mean that experiments would be needed
in order to obtain the speciﬁc parameters depending on what model is being applied.
The information obtained through simulations would provide a better understanding of
the mixing eﬃciency when operating with Non-Newtonian ﬂuids even though it is at this
point suitable to assume that the mixing eﬃciency would decrease as the viscosity in-
creases based on the results obtained during this project. However, it would be more
conclusive to actually investigate and obtain results for Non-Newtonian ﬂuids instead of
speculating the mixing eﬃciency on behalf of results obtained from Newtonian investiga-
tions.
Diﬀerent length to diameter ratios would also be interesting to investigate to strengthen
the claim that low ratios result in a more eﬃcient static mixer. Under this study, only
two ratios were used, and both cases generated substantially diﬀerent results. Without
more conclusive data on other ratios, it is diﬃcult to justify the results, and therefore is
inconclusive on whether low ratios does in fact have a positive inﬂuence on the mixing
eﬃciency.

analyze the mixing such as planar laser-induced ﬂuorescence. This particular method has
been applied in numerous case studies involving static mixers, and has been documented
to be very eﬀective in analyzing the mixing eﬃciency. It is however a costly procedure
compared to the measuring technique used in under this project, but the fast and eﬃcient
results obtained will be beneﬁcial for validating and understanding how well computa-
tional ﬂuid dynamics can be used as a tool for assessing mixing quality. This technique
would also have less operational complications that were encountered when using a oxygen
micro-sensor that resulted in inconsistent signals.
Under this project, the inﬂuence diﬀerent turbulent models have on the results was not
investigated, and could be interesting for future studies. The SST and k-ϵ was inter-
changeably applied depending on the speciﬁc linear initial velocity. What could have
been done is using both turbulent models on 1 m/s and 1.5 m/s to compare the diﬀerence
this has on the concentration proﬁle, shear stress gradient and CoV. For the other initial
speeds tested, this is not possible since 0.2 m/s was suspected to be a transitional regime,

A.2
| Method to solve governing equations
Partial diﬀerential equations can not directly be solved in the same manner as ordi-
nary diﬀerential equations since there are more independent variables such as space and
time. One common approach is to discretize the partial diﬀerential equations (PDE) in
the necessary independent variables. In order words, it is necessary to discretize in space
and time. Discretization is an approximation, as stated before, that turns continuous
PDE into discrete algebraic equations. Some of the more common discretization methods
include ﬁnite volume (FV), ﬁnite diﬀerence (FD) and ﬁnite element (FE)[Ferziger and
Peric, 2012]. In the context of ANSYS CFX 18.1, the ﬁnite volume is applied. This works
by creating control volumes, that can be seen as sub-domains, around nodes (points). The
principle behind the FV method is that when dividing the domain into ﬁnite sub-domains
creating a grid-like structure, as shown in ﬁgure 6, containing smaller control volumes are
created and transport equations are integrated over each ﬁnite volume.
64
==PAGE:81==
Figure 52: Illustration of FV method where the shaded area represents the control volume to
which integration is applied with P being the parameter used[Tu et al., 2013].
There are two types of numerical methods that ANSYS CFX 18.1 provide for ﬂow prob-
lems, which is a pressure and density based solver. It is also possible to couple the two
if necessary. Both solver types divide the domain into discrete control volumes, as stated
numerous times. Then, integration of the governing equation on the individual control
volumes is done resulting in a system of algebraic equations for the desired unknown
variables. The solver then guesses a value for the unknown variables to the algebraic
equations that results in sequentially solving the system of equations. If convergence is
achieved then the solution is found, however if this not the case then the algorithm is re-
peated. It is understood by the term convergence that this, in the context of CFD, refers
to when a speciﬁc parameter has reached steady-state[Larsson et al., 2015]. Convergence
is evaluated by one of the two statistical methods that act as constraints for the solver.
This consist of a Residual Means Squared Error (RMS), and a MAX residual. If the
diﬀerence between the obtained solution (Xsol) and the estimated solution satisﬁes the
constraint < 1 · 10−4Xsol if RMS is used, or Xsol < 1 · 10−3, then convergence is achieved
and the solution is obtained[CFX][Hybschmann, 2017]. In practice, obtaining a converged
system can be an issue, and ﬂuctuation of a speciﬁc parameter is occurring. This indi-
cates that the solver cannot ﬁnd a solution because of several reasons. One reason could
be because of the actual model of choice, which is not suitable for the given problem.
Other reasons could be because of insuﬃcient meshing, a transient steady-state is valid
instead of steady-state or could be because of the conditions (initial and boundary) need
correction[Larsson et al., 2015].
A.2.1
| Boundary and initial conditions
To solve the governing equations, it is necessary that the correct initial and bound-
ary conditions are chosen accordingly to the ﬂow problem. As stated, the FV method
integrates over a control volume, and to make this possible it is necessary to deﬁne a
boundary to which the solver can ﬁnd a numerical solution to a given ﬂow problem. For
example, a typical boundary condition is usually given in the inlet and outlet of a pipe
since this should be known, or in some cases assumed. Two well known boundary condi-
tions are the Dirchlet and Neumann. The Dirchlet boundary condition is a given value
of a variable at a boundary. This could be that a variable is constant such as a velocity.
One particular well known boundary condition is a "no-slip" condition. This states that
65
==PAGE:82==
Technical University of Denmark
the ﬂuid’s layer in contact with a wall will have the same velocity as the wall, which in
order implies that the velocity is zero if the wall is stationary. This is general for fully-
developed velocity proﬁles that have no velocity at the wall. Other types of boundary
conditions that refer to variables could also deal with stress continuity. This implies that
the stress is zero at the interface between a viscous ﬂuid, and another ﬂuid that has very
low viscosity. If the case is that both liquids are very viscous, then the shear stress will
be the same for liquid one as for liquid two. With regards to cases with two ﬂuids, there
is also another well known boundary condition that assumes velocity continuity at the
interface resulting in the velocity of ﬂuid one being equal to the velocity of liquid two.
The other known boundary condition category deals with gradients, and is referred to
as Neumann boundary conditions. Here, it is assumed that the gradient normal to the
boundary (perpendicular) of a variable is constant. Some examples of this could be tem-
perature, pressure or ﬂux. One in particular deals with symmetry, and states that the
ﬂux is the same on either side of a plane (in both directions), thus resulting in the ﬁrst
order derivative of the ﬂux being zero[Bird et al.][Ferziger and Peric, 2012].

B.2.2
| Velocity ﬁeld for non-ideal
It was concluded that assuming an ideal mixture between glycerol and water is not valid,
and therefore the velocity ﬁeld is not presented in the core of this report, but instead
the vector plots can be found in section B.2.1. On the contrary, the velocity ﬁeld for
a non-ideal assumption is shown in this section in order to investigate the inﬂuence the
length to diameter ratio has on the ﬂow. Since the diﬀerence between 0.005 m/s and 0.05
m/s are not substantial, according to what has been seen during the investigations, the
velocity ﬁeld for 0.005 m/s and 1.5 m/s will only be presented. Furthermore, for spatial
reasons regarding this section, the vector plots after the ﬁrst and sixth will only be shown.
