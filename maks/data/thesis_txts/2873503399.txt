Abstract
Modern manufacturing increasingly relies on automated data collection and analysis. Still,
scalable computer vision solutions remain difficult to deploy across diverse production
environments. This thesis, conducted in partnership with Factbird ApS, addresses the
challenge of developing visual counting systems that operate autonomously with minimal
manual annotation and oversight.
To achieve this, an integrated visual counting pipeline was developed, combining auto-
mated annotation with few-shot learning, uncertainty quantification, and anomaly detec-
tion. The system was validated across four distinct manufacturing domains within food
production and packaging, each with unique visual challenges.
The pipeline achieved substantial annotation time savings, reducing manual effort by up
to 68.6% on average, while requiring as few as five training images for near-optimal per-
formance. The Histogram of Gradients embeddings provided the best tra de-off between
computational speed and accuracy, while ResNet18 embeddings achieved superior clas-
sification performance at a higher computational cost. The counting system demonstrated
100% accuracy on unseen data across all evaluated domains.
Uncertainty analysis revealed that frame confidence variance was the most effective pre-
dictor of localized failure modes, with marginal evidence of negative correlation to counting
accuracy. Both Bayesian and Maximum Likelihood anomaly detection methods success-
fully identified degraded sequences, particularly those affected by occlusions and blurring
of the lens. The Bayesian approach additionally offered practical threshold flexibility with-
out compromising decision stability.
These findings demonstrate the feasibility of scalable, self-monitoring visual counting sys-
tems capable of rapid deployment and adaptation across diverse manufacturing environ-
ments.

Introduction
Modern manufacturing generates vast amounts of data, driving advances in automation,
quality control, and process optimization. Among the emerging technologies, computer
vision stands out for its potential to enable more flexible and data-driven monitoring and
control. Despite these advances, deploying computer vision systems across diverse man-
ufacturing environments remains a complex, costly, and resource-intensive challenge.
Industrial Partnership with Factbird ApS
This thesis was written in partnership with Factbird ApS, a Danish software company that
provides edge computing solutions for their manufacturing intelligence system. Factbird
operates across manufacturing facilities throughout the world, deploying sensor networks
that connect to their analytics platform to monitor production performance in real-time.
Through their deployment experience, Factbird has encountered the scalability limitations
of current computer vision approaches first-hand. Although pilot installations often demon-
strate impressive accuracy, scaling these solutions across multiple sites and production
lines reveals fundamental challenges in data requirements, system reliability, and main-
tenance overhead. Each deployment requires hours of manual annotation and ongoing
technical support to maintain performance as conditions change.
This partnership provided both the industrial context and the real-world validation data
necessary to develop and test solutions that address genuine deployment challenges.
Research Question
This thesis addresses a critical question for industrial computer vision: Can visual count-
ing systems be designed for autonomous deployment across diverse manufacturing envi-
ronments while maintaining high accuracy and reliability with minimal site-specific tuning
and expert oversight?
The question arises from a practical observation: successful computer vision deployments
in manufacturing are almost always custom engineering efforts. Each new production line,
facility, or product variant typically requires significant manual work to collect training data,
configure models, and establish monitoring protocols. This project-by-project approach
limits scalability and prevents the technology from achieving the cost-effectiveness nec-
essary for widespread adoption across the manufacturing sector.
Thesis Approach and Scope
Rather than optimizing individual components in isolation, this work aims to develop an
integrated system that simultaneously addresses three interconnected challenges: mini-
mizing manual annotation requirements, ensuring reliable performance assessment, and
enabling autonomous deployment across diverse environments.
The proposed approach combines automated annotation, high-performance counting mod-
els, and adaptive monitoring frameworks. The system is designed to bootstrap itself with
minimal human input, continuously monitor its own performance and uncertainty, and en-
able future autonomous interventions when reliability degrades.
The system is evaluated across four distinct manufacturing domains within food produc-
tion and packaging, each presenting unique visual challenges. These environments are
specifically chosen to test the ability of the system to generalize across diverse conditions,
rather than merely adapt to variations of a single use case.

Background
2.1
Industrial Context and Motivation
There are two primary goals in modern manufacturing: To optimize production time and
reduce waste. Therefore, the Overall Equipment Efficiency (OEE) has become the gold
standard for measuring the effectiveness of the manufacturing process.[1] This metric
consists of three different components:
Availability = run time
total time
Performance = actual performance
ideal performance
Quality = good pieces
total pieces
Availability measures the time the equipment is running of the time the equipment should
be running. Performance measures the current production rate, i.e. pieces per minute
compared to what should be possible in the production time. Quality measure the good
pieces produced (i.e., not including product wasted due to poor quality) of the total pieces.
These three components then combine to form a measure of how well the equipment is
being utilized:
OEE = Availability × Performance × Quality
The key insights are not just how well the production is doing, but also why it is not doing
better.
Two of the three components, Performance and Quality, come down to simple counting.
Many solutions have been suggested to replace manual counting; some of the most widely
used include photoelectric sensors and mechanical counters, both of which have distinct
advantages and limitations. Photoelectric sensors, which include laser and other types of
light emissions, work by measuring the reflection or interruption of the light emitted by the
sensor. [2] These tend to have extremely high precision and are quite cost effective, which
is why they are the most widely used of the two types. [3] Furthermore, they are robust
to most environmental disturbances, such as moderate light changes. The key limitation
of photoelectric sensors is that they are only able to handle single file production lines
such as simple conveyors. [3] Additionally, photoelectric sensors require the counted
product to be nontransparent and not too reflective. In theory, mechanical counters solve
both of these issues, but are often too impractical for real-world applications. Mechanical
counters tend to have shorter lifespans and require physical interaction with the product,[4]
which in many cases is undesirable. Although there are more types of sensors, they
are often highly specialized and expensive. Most of them are also just variations of the
principle used by photoelectric sensors and mechanical counters, and none of them really
solves the issue of disorganized production and when product interaction is not desired.
This is often the case, for example, in food and beverages which account for 16.8% of
sales and 15.4% of employees in US manufacturing (2021 data). [5]
However, there are many more examples where manufacturing complexity and product
diversity have outpaced traditional sensing capabilities. With these specific challenges in
mind, the appeal of visual sensing becomes clear. Camera-based sensing has superior
flexibility, non-contact operation, affordability, and the ability to extend sensing beyond
simple counts.

2.2
Computer Vision for Industrial Counting
2.2.1
Classical Methods
Several methods for camera-based counting exist. One of the earliest and simplest meth-
ods is template matching. The algorithm slides a template across the image, computing
the correlation coefficients at each position. [6] Objects are then counted when the cor-
relation exceeds a threshold. This approach has very limited use cases in practice as it
struggles with scale, rotation, and lighting variations. An advanced extension of this is to
use Scale-Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT) and Speeded-Up Robust Features (SURF)
which are invariant to changes in scale, rotation, and lighting. [7] This tends to become
computationally expensive and requires that objects have distinctive features. Other ad-
vanced methods like Blob detection, including the Laplacian of Gaussians (LoG), work by
finding patches of similar pixels that are different from its surroundings. [7] Blob detection
works well for circular objects but tends to merge overlapping objects into a single patch
and split a single object into multiple patches. An extension of this idea are Edge detec-
tors such as Canny, Sobel, or Prewitt which detect object boundaries for further contour
analysis. [8] Although Edge detectors work well for objects with clear boundaries, they
can be quite sensitive to noise and false edges, such as shadows. A conceptually more
advanced approach to shape detection is Hough Transform. In short, Hough Transform
takes the image space and transforms it into parameter space, where the edge pixels
then vote for shape parameters. [9] This is often done using the Random Sample Con-
sensus (RANSAC) algorithm as it is less sensitive to noise. Hough Transform works well
for circular objects and is robust to partial occlusions, but is computationally expensive. A
conceptually simple approach that works well in controlled environments is Background
Subtraction. Background Subtraction is an umbrella term that covers multiple techniques
that work by detecting textures different from the known background. [10]
Although all of the above methods have their own use cases, they share a common limita-
tion: They do not scale well with the magnitude of modern manufacturing. The challenge
is to extend beyond single production lines to enterprise-wide deployments across mul-
tiple facilities, product types, and manufacturing processes. Here, these systems simply
have too long a set-up time and are not robust enough, making manual configuration and
maintenance too expensive.
2.2.2
Introducing Deep Learning
The required level of flexibility and performance has only been achieved with the advance-
ment of Deep Learning. Starting with Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), object de-
tection evolved through several paradigm shifts that made accurate counting applications
possible. Early approaches relied on the ”sliding window technique”, where a classifier
would exhaustively scan across different locations and scales in an image. [11] Although
conceptually simple, this approach was computationally expensive and often produced
redundant detections that complicated counting tasks. The introduction of two-stage de-
tectors marked a significant breakthrough. Regional-CNN (R-CNN) [12] introduced the
use of region proposals, identifying potential object locations, and then classifying each
region separately. This was refined through Fast R-CNN [13] and Faster R-CNN [14],
which improved efficiency by sharing computational features and introducing learnable
regional proposal networks. However, the multistage pipeline remained complex and rel-
atively slow for real-time counting applications.
2.2.3
Detection-Based Counting and YOLO
The emergence of single-stage detectors such as You Only Look Once (YOLO) revolu-
tionized the field by reformulating object detection as a direct regression problem. Instead

abilities simultaneously in a single forward pass. This unified architecture dramatically
improved the inference speed and provided a more consistent detection result. [15]
Following this initial breakthrough, subsequent versions brought significant improvements
that enhanced both detection accuracy and count reliability, culminating in the revolution-
ary integration of attention mechanisms. YOLOv3 (2018) introduced multi-scale detection
through Feature Pyramid Networks (FPN), enabling the model to detect objects at three
different scales simultaneously. Precise localization helps to distinguish closely packed
objects during counting. [15] YOLOv5 (2020) represents the first major commitment to-

extra-large) to balance speed and accuracy based on application requirements.
[16]
YOLOv8 (2023) included further architectural refinements. The decoupled detection head
separated classification and localization tasks, improving performance on small objects,
which was a big challenge in object detection. [17] YOLOv11 (2024) marked the first sig-
nificant integration of attention mechanisms, the foundational work used in transformers
[18], into the main YOLO architecture. [19] YOLOv12 (2025) represents a paradigm shift
towards an attention-centric architecture. Unlike previous versions that relied primarily on
CNNs, YOLOv12 successfully incorporates Area Attention Mechanisms while maintaining
real-time performance. [20]
YOLO models represent the detection-based counting paradigm, and the real advantage
of these models is the inference speed that makes them suitable for real-time counting
applications. Examples include traffic counting [21], marine applications [22], and agri-
culture [23].
2.2.4
Density-Based Counting
Deep learning has also proven to be superior in crowd counting using density-estimation
models. Density-estimation models generate density maps where the intensity of the pix-
els corresponds to the object density, with the total count obtained by integrating across
the entire map. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) such as Multi-Column CNN (MCNN)
[24] and Congested Scene Recognition Network (CSRNet) [25] dominated this field, par-
ticularly for crowd-counting applications.
MCNN uses multiple columns with different
receptive fields to handle scale variations, while CSRNet employs dilated convolutions
to capture multi-scale contextual information without losing resolution. More advanced
density models include Context-Aware Network (CAN) [26], which incorporates attention
mechanisms to focus on relevant regions, and Distribution Matching Counting (DM-Count)
[27], which uses dynamic receptive fields to adapt to varying object densities. These ap-
proaches excel in scenarios with extremely dense object distributions in which individual
object boundaries become indistinguishable, such as crowd-counting or cell-counting in
microscopy images. However, density-based methods have fundamental limitations, as
they provide only approximate counts through integration, struggle with discrete object
boundaries, offer no individual object localization, and are sensitive to annotation quality
in density map generation.
2.2.5
Regression-Based Direct Counting
An alternative paradigm, regression-based methods, treats counting as a direct regres-
sion problem, where networks predict count values without explicit density maps or object
localization. These methods typically use CNN backbones (ResNet, VGG) with global
average pooling layers that compress spatial information into count predictions. Count-
ception introduced the concept of using redundant counting for improved accuracy. [28]
Instead of predicting a single density value per pixel, redundant counting deliberately cre-

ates overlapping predictions where each pixel is counted multiple times by different recep-
tive fields. Regression-based methods are computationally efficient and work well when
only total counts matter, such as inventory management. However, they provide no spa-
tial information about object locations and struggle with complex spatial distributions or
occlusions.
2.2.6
Few-Shot and Zero-Shot Counting
Few-shot counting methods learn to count new object categories from just a few exam-
ples, thereby addressing the limitation of requiring large annotated datasets for each ob-
ject type. Few-Shot Adaptation and Matching Network (FamNet) uses a feature extraction
module and a density prediction module, along with an adaptation scheme that leverages
provided exemplars to adapt the network at test time through gradient descent updates.
[29] The most recent advance involves zero-shot counting, eliminating the need for task-
specific training data. This approach represents a fundamental change from traditional
counting methods by leveraging foundation models and visual-language understanding.
Objects are specified through natural-language descriptions or visual prompts without
prior training in the target object category. CLIP-Count represents the first end-to-end
pipeline for text-guided zero-shot object counting, leveraging the powerful vision-language
capabilities of CLIP. [30] The method addresses the fundamental challenge of adapting
vision-language models to dense prediction tasks such as counting. To align text embed-
dings with dense visual features, CLIP-Count introduces a patch-text contrastive loss that
guides the model to learn informative patch-level visual representations. An alternative
zero-shot paradigm uses visual prompts rather than textual descriptions. [31] One pro-
posed method identifies cropped objects from the input image itself to serve as examples
for counting, thus eliminating the need for human-annotated examples. Their approach
tackles the dual challenge of identifying patches that contain objects of interest while en-
suring that these patches are visually representative of all instances in the image. These
methods offer unprecedented flexibility but often sacrifice accuracy for generalization and
require significant computational resources for inference.
2.2.7
Practical Limitations
Modern manufacturing intelligence runs on Edge Devices and, while accelerator modules
keep evolving, most Deep Learning models cannot run on such hardware. The com-
putational gap between state-of-the-art research models and deployable edge solutions
remains substantial. Many sophisticated counting approaches are designed for offline
image-level analysis rather than real-time inference, making them computationally infea-
sible for deployment on typical industrial hardware. Edge devices in industrial settings
must balance competing demands for accuracy, speed, and resource efficiency while
maintaining consistent performance under varying environmental conditions. These in-
clude temperature fluctuations, vibrations, and electromagnetic interference, all of which
are common in manufacturing environments. Manufacturing environments require count-
ing systems that can process streams of data in real-time while operating within strict
power and thermal constraints. In addition, these systems must demonstrate long-term
reliability with minimal maintenance, as industrial deployments often operate continuously
for months or years without human intervention. The ability to perform inference locally
eliminates the dependency on network connectivity, which is critical for some applica-
tions, and the need for expensive cloud or server-side computing. These are some of
the main reasons why the YOLO family remains the preferred choice for most industrial
counting applications. YOLO’s architecture offers multiple deployment pathways, includ-
ing quantized models, pruned networks, and specialized inference engines. All of which
helps achieve real-time performance on ARM processors and embedded GPUs com-

2.3
The Scalability Challenge in Industrial Vision Systems
Although YOLO-based detection counting has established itself as the most viable so-
lution for industrial deployment, there are fundamental limitations in scalability. These
limitations include the data annotation bottleneck that hinders rapid deployment and the
absence of robust mechanisms that ensure continued reliability. The scalability chal-
lenges emerge from a fundamental mismatch between the static nature of trained models
and the dynamic realities of industrial environments. YOLO’s effectiveness depends not
only on comprehensive training datasets but also on the assumption that deployment con-
ditions will remain relatively consistent with training conditions. However, industrial set-
tings are characterized by continuous variation. Lighting conditions change throughout
shifts and seasons, production equipment introduces new visual artifacts, product speci-
fications evolve, and camera positions may drift over time. Importantly, traditional YOLO
deployments provide no mechanisms for detecting when these changes have compro-
mised model performance, leading to silent degradation that can persist undetected until
manual inspection reveals systematic counting errors. This reliability challenge is com-
pounded by the black-box nature of deep learning models, which provide point estimates
without uncertainty quantification. Industrial decision makers require not just counts, but
confidence in those counts. Especially when the accuracy of the count directly impacts
production planning, inventory management, and quality control processes. The absence
of uncertainty estimates and automated performance monitoring limits the trust in the
model and adoption of counting systems in mission-critical applications, where incorrect
counts can have significant economic consequences. Furthermore, the effort required
to continuously monitor, validate, and retrain YOLO-based systems across multiple pro-
duction lines, facilities, and product variants often exceeds the available expertise and
resources of manufacturing organizations. The result is a fragmented landscape where
successful computer vision deployments remain isolated successes rather than scalable
solutions that can be systematically deployed and maintained across diverse industrial
environments.
2.3.1
Data Annotation Bottleneck
The creation of high-quality training datasets for YOLO-based counting systems repre-
sents one of the most significant barriers to scalable industrial deployment. The annota-
tion process requires substantial time investment and often specialized domain expertise,
which is often scarce. Creating production-ready datasets for industrial counting appli-
cations requires substantially more effort than typically acknowledged in the academic
literature. Even with AI-assisted annotation tools that reduce manual effort to single-click
annotations per object, practical experience from industrial annotation tasks reveals sig-
nificant time requirements. For example, annotating the four datasets used in this thesis
using such AI-tools took between 38.4 seconds per image for the fastest dataset and
73.8 seconds for the slowest dataset. For a typical YOLO domain adaption dataset with
approximately 200 images, annotation translates to approximately 2 and 4 hours of pure

annotation time, per deployment, assuming that there is no iteration of the set-up, which
is very unlikely for real-world applications. Even in the rare cases where datasets can be
reused through data augmentation to deploy on new production lines, it is generally unde-
sirable to have no domain-specific examples. Therefore, the problem becomes exceed-
ingly unmanageable with scale. This well-established limitation of deep learning models
imposed by manual annotation has driven significant research into automated annotation
approaches that can reduce or eliminate the need for human labeling. These techniques
aim to bootstrap model training with minimal supervision while achieving performance
comparable to fully supervised approaches. The two primary directions in this field are
auto-annotation techniques that generate labels automatically from existing data or mod-
els, and weak supervision methods that extract maximum learning value from minimal
human input.
Auto-annotation and Weak Supervisions
Recent breakthroughs in auto-labeling have demonstrated substantial efficiency gains
that fundamentally challenge traditional annotation paradigms. Zero-shot foundation mod-
els such as YOLO-World, Grounding DINO, and YOLOE can achieve up to 95% of human-
level performance while cutting labeling costs by up to 100,000 times. [32] These systems
leverage large-scale vision-language models trained on massive multi-modal datasets to
generate application-specific pseudo-”ground truth” labels without requiring any manual
annotations for the target domain.
The technical foundation of these approaches is vision-language foundation models that
have been trained on billions of image-text pairs, enabling them to understand complex
visual concepts and their linguistic descriptions. When configured for object detection,
these models can process natural language queries describing target objects and gener-
ate bounding-box annotations with confidence scores. Critically, empirical studies reveal
that models trained solely on autogenerated labels often match the performance of those
trained on human annotations. Griffin et al. (2025) achieved mAP50 scores of 0.768
on PASCAL VOC compared to 0.817 for human-labeled data and 0.538 versus 0.588 on
COCO. [33] This counterintuitive result suggests that foundation models, trained on di-
verse internet-scale data, can sometimes provide more consistent and comprehensive
labeling than human annotators.
However, foundation models face fundamental limitations that constrain their applicabil-
ity in specialized industrial contexts. Foundation models struggle with completely novel
object categories that lack representation in their training data, domain-specific industrial
components with unique visual characteristics, and manufacturing scenarios involving
proprietary equipment or processes. Additionally, the black-box nature of these systems
provides limited interpretability for understanding labeling decisions, which can be prob-
lematic in quality-critical manufacturing environments where annotation errors have down-
stream consequences. Performance degradation is particularly pronounced when dealing
with highly specialized industrial objects, fine-grained defect detection, or scenarios re-
quiring domain expertise that extends beyond general visual understanding captured in
internet-scale training data.
Pseudo-labeling represents one of the most straightforward approaches to reduce an-
notation requirements. Lee (2013) introduced the foundational pseudo-labeling method
where a model trained on limited labeled data generates ”pseudo-labels” for unlabeled
samples based on high-confidence predictions. [34] These pseudo-labels are then incor-
porated into the training, effectively expanding the labeled dataset. The method assigns
the maximum confidence class as the pseudo-label for unlabeled data and trains using
a weighted combination of supervised loss on labeled data and pseudo-supervised loss

on unlabeled data. A critical component is the gradual increase of the unlabeled loss
weight to prevent early overfitting to incorrect pseudo-labels. However, pseudo-labeling
faces significant practical challenges. Low-quality pseudo-labels can create confirmation
bias where the model reinforces its own errors. This is particularly problematic in indus-
trial settings where object appearance varies due to lighting changes, wear patterns, or
production variations not represented in the initial labeled dataset. The success of the
method depends heavily on the initial model quality and the similarity between labeled
and unlabeled data distributions.
Few-shot and Transfer Learning
Few-Shot object detection (FSOD) has emerged as a critical research area that aims to
detect novel objects from only a few annotated samples. [35] Modern frameworks inte-
grate metric learning and meta-learning to create class-agnostic detection models capable
of accurately recognizing new categories not appearing in training samples. [35] The most
effective FSOD approaches follow a two-stage training paradigm consisting of an initial
base training phase using abundant data to acquire general prior knowledge, followed by
fine-tuning with few-shot target data.
Transfer learning serves as the foundation for most FSOD methods, utilizing pre-trained
models as starting points for new detection tasks. The fine-tuning approach involves
base-training phases in which entire models are trained on large, labeled object detection
datasets, followed by fine-tuning phases targeting smaller domain-specific datasets. Do-
main gap mitigation strategies include domain randomization techniques that train models
on synthetic images before fine-tuning with real data. This has proven particularly effec-
tive when semantic fidelity is favored over visual fidelity. Optimization requires collections
of models pre-trained on domain-specific datasets, where each fine-tuning task should uti-
lize the model with the base domain most closely corresponding to the target dataset.[35]
Commercial platforms have emerged to address annotation scalability, though with im-
portant limitations. Roboflow’s primary auto-annotation offering is Roboflow 3.0, which
provides AI-assisted labeling capabilities using sparse data. Scale AI provides annota-
tion services through both AI-assisted tools and human annotators using their Remotasks
platform. Roboflow and Scale AI’s approach combines automated pre-labeling with hu-
man verification, though the quality and speed depend heavily on the specific use case
and domain. These commercial solutions require ongoing subscription costs and are
largely based on proprietary models.

Model Reliability and Trust
Quantification of uncertainty has become increasingly critical for the deployment of AI sys-

ranging from financial losses to safety hazards. In computer vision, traditional neural
networks often provide confidence estimates that do not adequately reflect the true un-
certainty of the prediction. Thus, making it difficult for practitioners to reliably distinguish
between confident predictions and those that require human intervention. This limitation is
particularly problematic in manufacturing environments, where automated object-counting
systems using models such as YOLO must operate reliably under varying conditions.

Statistical Methods for Distribution Shift Detection
Anomaly and drift detection address the critical challenge of identifying when visual con-
ditions have shifted sufficiently to compromise model performance in deployment scenar-
ios. This becomes essential for manufacturing counting systems where environmental
changes, equipment wear, or new product variants can silently degrade accuracy without
explicit failure signals. Multiple complementary approaches operating at different scales
and leverage distinct assumptions about data distribution and model behavior exist.
Statistical approaches monitor the distributional properties of the input data to detect when
new samples deviate significantly from the training distribution. [44] Maximum Mean Dis-
crepancy (MMD) and related kernel-based methods compare feature distributions be-
tween training and production data by computing distances in reproducing kernel Hilbert
spaces. This provides non-parametric tests for distribution equality with theoretical guar-
antees. The Kolmogorov-Smirnov test offers a classical parametric approach for univari-
ate distributions, while the Population Stability Index (PSI) provides a practical metric for
monitoring categorical variable distributions over time. [45] For multivariate data, these
techniques often operate on learned feature representations rather than raw pixels, using
embeddings from pre-trained networks or intermediate layers of deployed models.
Hypothesis testing frameworks provide formal statistical guarantees about the change
in distribution. Two-sample tests can detect when the marginal distribution P(X) has
changed, while conditional distribution tests assess whether the relationships P(Y |X)
have changed. [46] The Page-Hinkley method represents a sequential approach to de-
tecting changes in data stream means over time, commonly used for real-time monitoring
of model performance indicators. [47] However, hypothesis testing frameworks and se-
quential change detection methods such as Page-Hinkley face fundamental limitations.
They often require sufficient sample sizes to achieve statistical power, may not capture
subtle changes that impact model performance, and struggle with high-dimensional data
where the curse of dimensionality affects test sensitivity.
Influence Functions for Anomaly Detection
Influence functions provide a principled approach to anomaly detection by quantifying how
individual training examples affect model predictions, enabling the identification of sam-
ples that would significantly alter model behavior if removed from the training set. Influ-
ence functions were originally developed for robust statistics [48], but have been adapted
for deep learning to measure the impact of training data on model parameters and pre-
dictions. [49]
However, influence functions face fundamental limitations when applied to object detec-
tion tasks, such as YOLO-based counting systems. Unlike simple classification prob-
lems, where influence functions have been successfully demonstrated, object detection
presents several intractable challenges. The multi-component loss functions that com-
bine classification, localization, and objectness terms make it unclear how to compute
meaningful influence scores between training and test examples. Additionally, the vari-
able number of detections per image breaks the sample-to-sample correspondence that

influence functions require. Despite extensive research in the influence function literature,
no studies have successfully demonstrated their application to object detection models.
Reconstruction-Based Anomaly Detection
Autoencoder-based detection trains reconstruction models on normal operating data. The
reconstruction error is then monitored as an anomaly signal under the assumption that
autoencoders will poorly reconstruct inputs that differ significantly from training data. [50]
The reconstruction error,

serves as the primary anomaly score, where x represents the input and ˆx the recon-
structed output. Variational autoencoders (VAEs) extend this approach by probabilisti-

reconstruction-based anomaly score is given by:
Anomaly Score(x) = −Eqϕ(z|x)[log pθ(x|z)]
where qϕ(z|x) is the encoder network that maps input x to a latent representation z, and
pθ(x|z) is the decoder network that reconstructs x from z. This score reflects how well
the model can reconstruct the input, with higher values indicating a greater likelihood of
anomalies.
Similarly, generative model approaches using Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs),
normalizing flows, or diffusion models attempt to model training data distributions explic-
itly. The anomalies are then detected when the samples exhibit low likelihood under
learned generative models. [52]
However, reconstruction-based approaches are fundamentally misaligned with the goals
of counting applications. These methods flag inputs that deviate from the training distri-
bution, but counting errors can occur even when images appear visually normal. What
matters in industrial counting is detecting when the performance of the model has de-
graded, not when the input images look unusual. For example, a subtle blurring on the
lens might yield low reconstruction error but lead to substantial counting errors. Con-
versely, an image with shadows or reflections may be poorly reconstructed while still being
counted correctly. Furthermore, reconstruction-based methods require training separate
autoencoder models on large amounts of “normal” production data for each deployment.
This reintroduces many of the same annotation and training bottlenecks that hinder the
scalability of YOLO-based solutions. It also doubles the maintenance burden, since prac-
titioners must train, validate, and update both the counting and anomaly detection models
for every deployment.
Practical Limitations and Challenges
Despite their theoretical foundations, anomaly and drift detection methods face signifi-
cant practical limitations. [53] False positive rates remain problematically high in most
approaches, limiting their deployment in production environments where human interven-
tion costs are substantial. [54] Ground truth availability poses another critical challenge,
as many applications lack immediate access to labels for validation, making it difficult
to distinguish between consequential drift requiring intervention and benign distributional
changes. [55]
Inconsequential drift represents a fundamental challenge. Large feature shifts may be
statistically significant but operationally irrelevant if they occur in regions that do not af-
fect model behavior. In contrast, subtle changes in critical decision boundaries may not

trigger statistical tests but significantly impact performance. The curse of dimensional-
ity affects many detection methods, as the joint monitoring of multiple features becomes
computationally intractable and statistically unreliable.
Real-world medical imaging studies demonstrate that monitoring performance alone is
insufficient to detect data drift and drift detection is highly dependent on the sample size
and demographics of the patient. [56] Temporal considerations further complicate de-
ployment. Distinguishing between transient anomalies (temporary lighting changes, dust
on sensors) and systematic drift requiring model updates requires sophisticated temporal
modeling and domain expertise. However, for the purpose of counting these issues are
equally important to detect of proper accuracy assessment.
Manufacturing environments require additional considerations, including adaptive thresh-
olds that adjust the sensitivity based on the operational context. Multi-modal monitoring
combines visual features with metadata, and hierarchical detection operating at multiple
scales from individual samples to batch-level patterns. The ultimate challenge remains to
balance sensitivity against false positive rates while maintaining computational efficiency
suitable for real-time deployment constraints.
Human-in-the-Loop
Human-in-the-loop (HITL) systems have emerged as a practical approach to address the
limitations of fully automated computer vision systems in industrial environments, where
the consequences of incorrect predictions can have significant operational and economic
impact. These systems strategically combine human expertise with machine learning
capabilities to achieve performance levels that neither component independently could
attain. [57] Of course, this is only the case when the collaboration is properly designed
and the human and AI capabilities are complementary. While also managing the costs
and scalability challenges associated with continuous human involvement.
The foundational principle of HITL systems lies in selective human intervention, where
automated systems handle routine decisions while escalating uncertain or critical cases
to human operators. In industrial counting applications, this typically manifests through
confidence-based routing, where predictions below a specified threshold trigger human
review. Additionally, exception handling mechanisms identify anomalous patterns requir-
ing expert interpretation. [57] Modern HITL frameworks incorporate active learning princi-
ples to maximize the value of human input by prioritizing samples that provide the greatest
information gain for model improvement, rather than simply reviewing low-confidence pre-
dictions.
However, HITL systems face several limitations that constrain their scalability and effec-
tiveness. HITL operation often requires operators to understand both the domain-specific
context and the machine learning system’s behavior patterns. This dual expertise re-
quirement can be difficult to maintain between multiple sites and as personnel change
over time. Additionally, the feedback loop between human corrections and model updates
requires careful design to prevent degradation. Inconsistent human input can introduce
noise that reduces rather than improves the performance of the model. The challenge lies
in identifying the optimal balance point where human participation provides sufficient value
to justify its ongoing costs while maintaining scalability across enterprise deployments.

Research Gaps and Thesis Positioning
Machine learning research shows significant fragmentation, with related areas develop-
ing separately despite being interconnected in real-world applications. Data efficiency
research has achieved meaningful progress in individual techniques such as few-shot
learning, active learning, and synthetic data generation, often demonstrating substantial
reductions in annotation requirements on benchmark datasets. However, these studies
typically operate within controlled experimental settings and may not fully address the re-
liability concerns that emerge when models are trained on limited data in production envi-
ronments. In contrast, reliability research focusing on uncertainty quantification and model
monitoring generally assumes access to substantial labeled data and stable deployment
conditions, requiring validation datasets that can be impractical to obtain across diverse
industrial contexts. Therefore, there is a notable gap between component-level optimiza-
tions and system-level performance. The integration of individually effective techniques
may produce unexpected interactions that current research does not systematically ad-

dress. Practitioners lack guidance on trade-offs when deploying data-efficient methods in
environments where annotation budgets and performance guarantees both matter.
Most current research uses established benchmark datasets that are practical for a direct
comparison of methods. However, these datasets tend to be highly curated with unreal-
istic labels and data quality. Furthermore, benchmark datasets often exhibit character-
istics that poorly reflect real manufacturing environments. Academic datasets frequently
present excessive diversity that spans multiple domains and object categories. This sce-
nario does not accurately represent typical manufacturing, where products maintain con-
sistent specifications and appearance while environmental factors introduce the primary
sources of variability. In practice, manufacturing counting systems must handle changes
in lighting conditions, conveyor positioning, camera angles, and surface reflections, while
the objects themselves remain relatively standardized. This mismatch between bench-
mark characteristics and deployment realities limits the practical applicability of research
findings when transitioning from laboratory validation to industrial implementation. In ad-
dition, most studies optimize for individual use cases without systematically investigating
transferability across different products, manufacturing environments, or facility config-
urations. Research assumptions about available computational resources, annotation
budgets, and expert availability are reasonable for controlled studies. However, these
often break down when systems are scaled across multiple industrial sites with varying
technical capabilities and constraints. The configuration and maintenance overhead re-
quired for current solutions frequently demands extensive per-deployment customization
and ongoing expert involvement. This creates cost structures that become prohibitive on
an enterprise scale. Additionally, academic frameworks typically focus on either horizontal
scaling challenges (deploying across geographically distributed facilities) or vertical scal-
ing challenges (adapting to new product lines) in isolation, rather than addressing both
simultaneously. The temporal dimension of scaling, maintaining system performance as
manufacturing processes evolve and product designs change over months and years,
receives limited attention in research that emphasizes snapshot performance metrics.
Furthermore, current deep learning approaches tend toward monolithic model designs
that, while achieving impressive accuracy, create systems that are difficult to update, de-
bug, or partially retrain when deployed across diverse environments with different failure
modes and performance requirements.
This thesis addresses identified scalability challenges through a unified framework that
treats data efficiency, model reliability, and deployment scalability as interdependent de-
sign considerations. This design philosophy begins with considering authentic industrial
counting requirements and constraints gathered through collaboration with industry. The

system specifically designed for counting tasks that leverages uncertainty estimation not
only to minimize annotation requirements, but simultaneously to enhance counting relia-
bility. Creating natural synergies between the objectives of data efficiency and reliability
in counting scenarios. The system architecture employs a modular design that enables
incremental deployment of counting systems across manufacturing sites with minimal per-
site customization, incorporating automated configuration mechanisms that adapt to local
environmental conditions while maintaining consistent counting performance standards.
A continuous learning framework maintains counting accuracy over time through observa-
tions of uncertainty patterns and human-in-the-loop feedback, ensuring that the systems
adapt appropriately as manufacturing processes change. The validation approach lever-

Methodology
The methodology of this thesis aims to build a scalable end-to-end visual counting pipeline.
The three critical challenges of counting in production environments are addressed: 1) the
data annotation bottleneck, 2) counting accuracy, and 3) system performance monitoring.
The system is built to run on edge devices and cloud infrastructure with easy integration
into manufacturing environments. This allows initial hardware deployment and data col-

taneously streaming both video and counting data to cloud-based monitoring systems.
The uncertainty monitoring component continuously samples from these data and eval-

flagged by model monitoring, which can then trigger retraining. This architecture ensures
scalability by minimizing human intervention through automated annotation and intelligent
monitoring, while maintaining reliability through uncertainty-driven quality control. Figure

Data Degradation
Four types of data augmentation were applied to evaluate the robustness of the anomaly
detection solution under realistic operational conditions. The four types of data augmenta-
tion included: partial local blurring (Figure 3.3a), occlusions (Figure 3.3d), local and global
brightness changes (Figure 3.3c), and contrast changes (Figure 3.3b). The selection of
these degradation types was motivated by both observed and anticipated challenges in
industrial counting environments. Blurring and contrast modifications directly mimic actual
problems encountered in the production line, including grease accumulation on camera
lenses and recipe changes that alter product appearance. Occlusion and illumination
variations represent expected operational challenges, such as camera obstruction and
lighting fluctuations, that commonly occur in industrial settings.
The degradations were applied exclusively to the meatball dataset, as this is the only pro-
duction line where operational issues have been empirically observed and documented.
Even though the degradations are meant to mimic actual problems, they are still synthet-
ically constructed. Therefore, little is gained by extending them to other data domains.
Extending these artificial degradations to other datasets would not provide meaningful
insight, as specific failure modes are inherently tied to the means by which they were
made. The purpose of this analysis was to establish the limitations of the uncertainty
quantification methods and to identify which types of degradation were reliably detected
and predicted. Thus, the analysis was exploratory to provide practical guidance for de-
ployment in real-world industrial counting applications.

The Segment Anything in Images and Videos Model (SAM2) is a visual transformer foun-

mentation (PVS). SAM2 uses a transformer architecture with streaming memory designed
specifically for real-time processing of both images and videos. The model accepts var-
ious prompt types, including points and bounding boxes, enabling interactive segmen-
tation of objects of interest. In this thesis, the official implementation available through
Meta AI’s GitHub repository was used. [58] The automatic mask generator function in
SAM2 transforms complex object detection and localization tasks into simpler binary im-
age classification problems by separating object localization from identification. SAM2
excels at finding precise boundaries regardless of object type, while classification be-
comes a straightforward secondary step. This approach is particularly suitable for envi-
ronments with consistent backgrounds, where only the objects of interest change position
across images. In such controlled settings, background examples maintain high similarity
throughout the dataset, improving classification efficiency. The exact model used is the
”small” model (checkpoint 2.1) as it offers similar performance at much cheaper memory
and computation than the ”base+” or ”large” models.

3.2.2
Feature Extraction
Features are needed to filter the masks that contain the object-of-interest from the back-
ground masks. It is possible to do image classification solely based on RGB values, but it
is very inefficient and the results are often poor. There are many alternative approaches.
In this project, three different feature embeddings were chosen, each from a different era
of computer vision methods to assess task difficulty and computational requirements.
Histogram of Gradients
The first approach used was Histogram of Gradients (HoG). HoG is the simplest and
oldest of the three approaches used. [59] This classic approach works by dividing the
image into cells and binning the gradients for each of these cells. Therefore, it is good at
detecting shapes in an image, but may not provide enough information in general.
ResNET18
The second approach used was ResNET18. [60] In ResNET18 the extracted features
are learned features based on the ImageNet dataset, which contains more than 1.2 mil-
lion images in 1000 different image classes. [61] Thus, these features generalize well.
Compared to newer and bigger architectures, ResNET18 has a shorter inference time. It
is important to note that different layers capture different types of features. Therefore, in
this project, layers are sampled at multiple depths of the network to get a better feature

3.2.3
Classification Methods for Mask Filtering
Once features have been extracted from a masked region, they were classified into back-
ground masks and object-of-interest masks using four different models. As the purpose
of auto annotation is to have as little human intervention as possible, the four models
chosen for the task were chosen for their simplicity. Although classic ML methods are
generally outperformed by modern Deep Learning approaches, they have the advantage
of requiring much less data.
K Nearest Neighbors
The first model used was K Nearest Neighbors (KNN). KNN is a distance-based algorithm
that can be used for both regression and classification. [63] The algorithm, as its name
implies, works by comparing the k number of nearest data points to the proposed data
point and deciding based on distance. This approach is quite intuitive and in theory works
with only a k number of training points without actual training time. This implies that new
classified examples can be added without retraining. However, the cost comes at test
time, where the distance from the new point to all existing points must be calculated. The
limitations of this algorithm include the curse of dimensionality, the scale of features, and
the sensitivity of the chosen distance measure. In the implementation for this project the
distance measure was treated as a hyperparameter along with the number of neighbors
k.
Support Vector Machine
The second model used was Support Vector Machines (SVM). SVM works by finding an
optimal hyperplane to separate classes (for classification) or approximate a function (for
regression). SVMs excel at creating clear decision boundaries by maximizing the mar-
gin between classes. Although computationally intensive during training, SVMs offer effi-
cient test-time performance, making them historically valuable for real-time applications in
robotics and computer vision. SVMs handle non-linearity through the ”kernel trick”, which
implicitly maps data to higher dimensional spaces without explicitly computing the trans-
formation. Though generally more resilient to high dimensionality than many algorithms,
SVMs can still suffer from decreased performance in extremely high-dimensional spaces
due to the concentration of distances between points. [64] In the implementation for this
project, the choice of kernel and regularization (C) were treated as hyperparameters.
Random Forest
The third model used was Random Forest (RF). RF belongs to the family of tree methods.
The basic principle can be derived from a simple decision tree where a single parent
node splits into child nodes that again split until purity is reached. The strength of RF
is that instead of using one tree, it uses the agreement of many trees made on subsets
of data and/or features. This approach falls under the category of ensemble methods.
RF shows excellent empirical performance in many domains. [65] In the implementation
for this project, the hyperparmeters tuned were the number of estimators (trees) and the
minimum number of samples required for split.

Scalable Visual Counting

Logistic Regression
The fourth model used was Logistic Regression (LR). LR is a statistical supervised learn-
ing method commonly used for binary classification. [66] This method works similarly to
linear regression by fitting a set of coefficients through minimization of a loss function.
However, unlike regular linear regression, the linear (or non-linear) combination is fed
through the logistic function, bounding the input to the interval of [0, 1]. The output is typi-
cally interpreted as the probability that the data point belongs to class 1. Although this can
be easily adjusted to specific needs, common practice is to use 0.5 as a decision bound-
ary. Logistic regression works with both L1- and L2-type regularization, which aids feature
selection and overfitting, respectively. In the implementation for this thesis regularization
type and strength are treated as hyperparameters.
3.2.4
Principal Component Analysis
Principal Component Analysis (PCA), can reduce the number of features while preserv-
ing most of the information in them. The proposed feature extraction methods create
feature vectors of dimensionality D > 500. RF and LR can handle regularization/feature
selection, but this is not the case for KNN and SVM. For the KNN algorithm the issue is
the classic ’Curse of dimensionality’, where the algorithm does not scale well. Addition-
ally, in a high enough dimensionality, any distance measure loses quantitative meaning,
although some distance measures are more sensitive than others. [67] SVMs face signif-
icant challenges in high dimensional spaces when sample sizes are limited. Specifically,
they often suffer from ”data piling,” where multiple training points have identical projections
onto the normal vector of the separating hyperplane. This phenomenon, most prominent
in high-dimension, low-sample-size scenarios, occurs because SVMs focus exclusively
on support vectors at the margin boundary. Consequently, the model becomes overly
sensitive to particular aspects of the training data, potentially capturing noise rather than
meaningful patterns.[68]
Due to the limitations described above, KNN and SVM were implemented with the use
of PCA as a binary hyperparameter. [69] PCA works by re-projecting the data onto new
dimensions along the directions of maximum variance. A key limitation is that each new
dimension must be orthogonal to the rest in the original space. These new dimensions are
called principal components. The number of principal components represents a trade-off
between dimensionality and preserved variation. Thus, the cost of reducing the dimen-
sionality is the loss of information. Typically, preserving 95% of the variance can signifi-
cantly reduce the dimensionality of the feature space. A particular strength of this method
is that the principal components can be found efficiently simply by using Singular Value
Decomposition. For this project, the preserved variance was considered a discrete hy-
perparameter (90, 95, and 99 percent).
3.2.5
Non-Maximum Suppression
Overlapping bounding boxes for the same object are common issues with bare-boned ob-
ject detectors. A similar issue occurred in this thesis with the proposed regions from SAM2
masks, when background object masks overlap with objects-of-interest masks. Similarly,
some objects may have multiple segmentation masks large enough to be classified cor-
rectly. A simple post-processing solution to this problem is Non-Maximum Suppression
(NMS). NMS takes all bounding boxes with significant overlap and only keeps the one that
the model is most confident in. In this project, a common measure of overlap adjusted for
the actual sizes of masks, called Intersect over Union (IoU), was used.

3.3.1
Object Detection Framework
This thesis uses YOLOv8 (You Only Look Once version 8) [17] as the primary object de-
tection framework. Developed by Ultralytics and released in January 2023. The selection
of YOLOv8 was motivated by hardware constraints, balancing both accuracy and compu-
tational efficiency. The YOLOv8 architecture employed can be divided into the following
3 modules.
• The backbone extracts multi-scale features from input images, capturing hierar-
chical feature maps that represent both low-level textures and high-level semantic
information.
• The neck refines and fuses the multi-scale features extracted by the backbone. This
enhances information flow across different feature levels to enable the detection of
objects at varying sizes and scales.
• The head generates the final predictions, including the bounding-box coordinates,
the object confidence scores, and the class labels from the refined features.
3.3.2
Tracking
In this project based on counting with video, it was necessary to avoid double counting,
i.e., keep track of which objects between frames and not just count the objects within
each frame. YOLOv8 has an implemented tracking algorithm, called BoT-SORT (Boxes
of Tracks - Simple Online and Real-time Tracking) [70], which was used in this project.
This multi-object tracking algorithm combines detection-based tracking with motion mod-
eling to maintain consistent object identities across video frames. BoT-SORT integrates
object detection with Kalman filtering for motion prediction and uses deep learning-based
appearance features to associate data between frames. BoT-SORT enhances previous
tracking approaches like SORT and ByteTrack by incorporating improved appearance
modeling, camera motion compensation, and sophisticated track management strategies
that handle occlusions, appearance changes, and crowded scenes. The algorithm is op-
timized for real-time performance while maintaining robust tracking accuracy, making it
suitable for applications in surveillance, autonomous driving, and video analysis where
the consistent preservation of object identity is critical.
3.4
Uncertainty Monitoring
Uncertainty estimation is essential for scalable visual counting systems to minimize hu-
man intervention while maintaining operational reliability. Although deep learning models
including YOLO produce confidence scores, these are typically poorly calibrated and do
not accurately reflect the reliability of the prediction. Overconfident predictions on out-
of-distribution samples or degraded inputs can lead to silent failures where incorrect re-
sults are produced with high confidence. Proper uncertainty quantification provides well-
calibrated confidence estimates that enable a reliable identification of problematic video
sequences that require human review. This capability is critical for scalability as it al-
lows the system to operate across varying conditions without extensive manual oversight
based on unreliable confidence scores. Additionally, well-calibrated uncertainty estima-
tion enables systematic identification of video sequences for training data augmentation,
creating a continuous learning mechanism that improves system robustness over time.

3.4.1
Sample-Based Uncertainty Quantification
Although real-time uncertainty quantification would be ideal, focusing uncertainty analysis
on selected samples provide significant practical advantages. This approach enables the
deployment of computationally intensive methods, which would be prohibitive for contin-
uous real-time processing. More importantly, this selective strategy allows for strategic
sampling during periods of high data streaming when counting accuracy is the most critical
and at highest risk of degradation. During these high-throughput periods, environmental
factors, sensor limitations, or data quality issues can significantly impact model perfor-
mance. Thus, making uncertainty quantification most valuable precisely when computa-
tional resources are most constrained. By concentrating uncertainty analysis on these
critical samples, computational efficiency is maintained while ensuring robust uncertainty
estimates when they matter most.
3.4.2

MCDropout as Pseudo-Calibration
In this thesis, the use of MCDropout as a ’pseudo-calibration’ is examined. MCDropout
originally, is a proposed method to quantify aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty. [37] By
leveraging the dropout, normally used for regularization during training, it is possible to
get stochastic forward passes from otherwise deterministic Neural Networks.
However, YOLOv8 is built without dropout, and simply implementing dropout for CNN’s
at inference time has unique challenges. CNN’s rely on spatial relations in features that
are lost by using traditional dropout. In spite of this, for YOLO models in particular, MC-
Dropout has been shown to enable quantification of epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty.
Zhao et al. propose to simply add a dropout layer between the neck and head of the
model[71], which creates stochasticity. However, the exact relation between dropout rate
and quantified uncertainties is not specified. Ghiasi et al. propose DropBlock[72] which
implements dropout regionally on feature maps rather than completely at random. They
show mathematically that this is equivalent to Variational Inference, a state-of-the-art ap-
proach to posterior estimation in Bayesian machine learning.
By averaging confidence scores across multiple stochastic forward passes, MCDropout
provides a form of ’pseudo-calibration’ that yields better calibrated uncertainty estimates

For this thesis monitoring framework, a direct likelihood-based approach was used. Specif-

tribution. This direct likelihood-based approach was chosen because it provides an in-
tuitive measure of how typical new sequences are relative to the baseline distribution.
[71] The average likelihood also provides a metric that is robust to variations in sequence
length, making it suitable for comparing sequences of different durations. By establishing
a threshold on this average likelihood score, an interpretable anomaly detection mech-
anism was created which effectively identified when the uncertainty patterns of a model
deviated significantly from expected behavior, warranting further investigation. Using the
average likelihood of sequences also only requires us to make distributional assumptions
about the good sequences. This is particularly convenient in anomaly detection, where it
is difficult to know what potential anomalies may look like in advance.

The simplest and most common approach to parameter estimation in statistics is Maxi-

results in probabilistic machine learning that tie the MLE solution to traditional methods
such as least squares regression. However, a critical limitation arises from the hard binary
decision boundary and the challenge of interpreting raw likelihood values, which lack intu-
itive meaning and are difficult to threshold consistently across different datasets or model
configurations.

3.5.1
K-Fold Cross Validation
To ensure that the results are reliable when testing annotation models, K-fold cross-
validation was used. The principle of K-fold cross-validation is to have K different train and
test split subsets on the same data. Then, the test is performed K times and the results
are averaged at the end. In this project, one of these splits was used for hyperparameter
tuning to balance computational cost and accuracy.
In addition to standard K-fold cross-validation for model training, a specialized cross-
validation approach was employed to evaluate the performance monitoring system. Due
to limited data availability, the selection of baseline sequences significantly affects the
monitoring performance. To ensure robust evaluation and assess decision stability, all
combinations of baseline sequences were systematically tested. For each baseline size
configuration, every combination of clean video sequences serves as baseline references,
with the remaining sequences used for testing. Performance metrics were calculated on
aggregated predictions across all baseline combinations to account for selection variabil-
ity.

4.3.1
Metrics of Uncertainty
Reliable uncertainty quantification is crucial in this context for two main reasons. First,
to develop a metric that can predict when counting results are likely to be inaccurate,
providing a safeguard against silent failures. Second, to enable detection of degraded
video sequences, which is essential to identify anomalies and trigger appropriate system
responses. Without effective uncertainty measures, the system cannot recognize when it
is operating beyond its reliable limits, undermining both scalability and robustness. This
section explores and evaluates several candidate metrics to support both predictive ac-
curacy monitoring and anomaly detection in visual counting tasks.
The first two uncertainty measures are count variance and mean confidence.
These
showed insufficient separation between good and degraded video sequences to serve
as reliable quality indicators (Figure 4.10). Count variance, which measures the variabil-
ity in detection counts across MCruns, exhibited overlapping distributions between good
sequences (mean ≈1.53) and most degradation types (1.57-1.81). Only contrast degra-
dation showed elevated variance (≈3.28). Similarly, mean confidence demonstrated mini-
mal discrimination capability, with good sequences averaging 0.802 confidence compared
to degraded sequences ranging from 0.788-0.801. The small magnitude of these differ-
ences, combined with substantial overlap between distributions, makes both measures

5
Discussion
5.1
Strengths
Automated Annotation
The presented approach for automated annotation is supported by strong and thorough
experimental validation. Best practices for handling scarce data, including cross-validation,
were applied to ensure the reliability and robustness of the results. Unlike studies limited
to a single data domain, this approach demonstrates usefulness across four distinct do-
mains from different industries. Viable performance was achieved in all cases, suggesting
that the approach is not only functional but also generalizable across diverse application
areas.
Alternative strategies were also considered during the development process. One may
wonder why methods such as similarity-based classification or zero-shot learning were
not pursued further. Early prototyping showed that direct similarity-based classification
using CLIP produced promising results, even with as few as two annotations. However,
this method required setting a similarity threshold, which proved to be highly arbitrary.
Even after applying a sigmoid transformation, the optimal threshold often required tuning
at four to five decimal places, making the approach impractical and overly sensitive to
minor adjustments.
Zero-shot models were also briefly explored, but consistently underperformed in manufacturing-
related domains. This was probably due to the limited representation of industrial settings
in their training data. Although prompt engineering could potentially improve performance
in this context, such adjustments would effectively loose zero-shot capabilities.
For comparison, the automated annotation pipeline was evaluated against Roboflow 3.0,
a widely used industry leading tool. Across all domains tested, the presented pipeline
offers a viable and competitive alternative that supports scalable annotation with minimal
human input.
Visual Counting
The counting task established a strong baseline with 100% accuracy under the tested
conditions. This confirms that, when the system operates within known and controlled
settings, counting can be performed with perfect reliability. The key challenges moving
forward are to improve robustness by pushing the limits to which counting remains accu-
rate and to enhance sensitivity to detect early degradation in conditions without compro-
mising precision. Balancing these factors offers a theoretical path to a complete solution.
Uncertainty Monitoring
The research identified frame confidence variance as a potentially valuable uncertainty
measure that appears to correlate better with counting accuracy compared to the tradi-
tional entropy-based approach. This finding is particularly interesting because variance-
based monitoring exhibits sensitivity to localized failure modes such as camera lens con-
tamination and occlusions, while entropy-based measures show greater responsiveness
to global distributional changes. The complementary behavior of these measures sug-
gests opportunities to develop comprehensive monitoring systems that could address dif-
ferent types of performance degradation.
Despite working with limited data availability, the analysis employed systematic cross-
validation approaches to maximize the reliability of the findings. By testing all combi-
nations of baseline sequences and aggregating predictions, the evaluation attempted to
Scalable Visual Counting
57
==PAGE:66==
account for selection variability and provide more stable estimates of monitoring effective-
ness. This approach provides better insights into the potential generalization of results
across different operational scenarios.
The monitoring system employs a threshold-based decision mechanism that can be ad-
justed based on operational requirements.
The Bayesian approach provides intuitive
thresholding, with scores naturally interpreted as probabilities within the [0,1] interval.
The threshold analysis suggests relatively stable performance across a range of decision
boundaries, potentially offering operators flexibility to balance false-positive and false-
negative rates according to specific industrial contexts.
The framework is based on established principles of Bayesian inference and maximum
likelihood estimation. The Gamma distribution assumptions for modeling baseline uncer-
tainty patterns appear to fit the observed data well. The consistency between Bayesian
and MLE approaches across experimental conditions suggests that the probabilistic mod-
eling choices may be reasonable and provides some confidence in the approach.
Initial prototyping explored alternative monitoring approaches using external factors such
as production rates and operational metrics, which offered significant computational ad-
vantages over prediction-based uncertainty quantification. However, these approaches
proved impractical due to the unpredictable variability inherent in manufacturing environ-
ments. This exploration highlighted the potential value of focusing on model-intrinsic un-
certainty measures that remain relatively stable regardless of external production condi-
tions.
Scalability
The framework design considers the requirements of autonomous and human-in-the-loop
workflows for industrial deployment. Inclusion of both variance- and entropy-based un-
certainty measures provides complementary capabilities. Although variance shows bet-
ter performance for anomaly detection, entropy offers potential to identify data drift and
selecting useful examples for future annotation. This dual approach establishes compo-
nents that could support active learning workflows, where uncertainty guides the selection
of samples for system improvement.
5.2
Limitations and Challenges
Automated Annotation
The current automated annotation pipeline offers significant potential for computational
optimization. Mask-based feature extraction processes individual image crops sequen-
tially, limiting GPU batch processing. This design results in noticeable performance differ-
ences between the embedding methods, with ResNet18 taking two to three times longer
than HoG or CLIP.
These differences are primarily due to resolution requirements, not fundamental algorith-
mic inefficiencies. HOG features can be efficiently extracted at low resolutions, whereas
ResNet18 is architecturally designed for high-resolution inputs (224×224), resulting in
significantly more operations per forward pass. This often seems excessive, as the im-
age crops in this application are typically much smaller. A more efficient solution would
process the full image once through the backbone and extract features directly from the
spatial locations of detected masks, enabling proper batching and avoiding redundant,
high-cost forward passes.
The evaluation also highlights the need for domain-specific hyperparameter tuning for
segmentation. The systematic recall limitations observed in the dough dataset are not
58
Scalable Visual Counting
==PAGE:67==
due to flaws in the segmentation approach but to the cross-domain parameter tuning.
The segmentation parameters for SAM2 were deliberately optimized across all datasets
to help minimize the need for future domain-specific annotation. However, the few-shot
learning analysis showed that approximately five manually annotated images per domain
are already required for optimal classification. This makes it both practical and efficient
to use these same annotations for domain-specific SAM2 tuning, which would resolve
under-segmentation in complex scenes while preserving the system’s few-shot learning
capabilities.
Visual Counting
The current evaluation uses 200 training images per domain without investigating mini-
mum viable dataset sizes, which likely vary significantly across object types and probably
require fewer than 200 images in many cases. However, this optimization becomes ir-
relevant as the automated annotation becomes sufficiently cheap. Rather than pursuing
marginal gains through dataset size optimization, the focus was on making annotation
cost-effective enough to support larger, more robust datasets.
A more critical unanswered question is whether manual curation of auto-generated labels
provides meaningful benefit. When automated annotation achieves high accuracy rates,
only a small percentage of labels will contain errors. The robustness of YOLO training
to these occasional mislabeled examples remains unexplored. If YOLO proves to be
sufficiently robust to imperfect labels, then annotation pipelines could be fully automated
without human verification, further reducing deployment barriers.
Two domains (meatballs and bottles) used test sequences from the same recording ses-
sions as their training data, providing limited insight into real-world deployment perfor-
mance. Only the cans and doughs datasets were collected on different days, demon-
strating genuine temporal generalization. Although these cross-temporal results show
perfect accuracy, they represent controlled conditions rather than dynamic industrial en-
vironments. However, the bottle dataset was specifically chosen as a challenging case,
but still achieved 100% counting accuracy. This could suggest that the robustness of the
approach may extend beyond what these limited tests can demonstrate.
Uncertainty Monitoring
The uncertainty monitoring evaluation suffers from severely constrained data availability,
limiting the analysis to hypothesis generation rather than definitive validation. Although
the synthetic degradations (blur, occlusion, brightness, contrast) were designed to mimic
empirically observed failure modes from actual production lines, their artificial construction
means findings may not generalize to real-world failure patterns. However, it was delib-
erately chosen not to expand this synthetic evaluation to the remaining datasets. Doing
so would only show consistency between artificial scenarios, rather than providing mean-
ingful evidence of real-world performance. The analysis remains valuable for establishing
proof-of-concept and identifying promising approaches, but requires validation on larger
datasets with genuine operational failures.
The striking similarity between the Bayesian and MLE approaches reveals a fundamental
issue with temporal correlation in video data. Consecutive frames are extremely highly
correlated, dramatically reducing the effective sample size far below the total frame count.
While 15 seconds of footage represents a genuinely low-data scenario where Bayesian
methods are expected to differ from the MLE solution, the high temporal correlation cre-
ates the illusion of abundant data. This negates the Bayesian approach’s advantage in
low-data settings, causing the methods to behave similarly. More critically, this over-
confidence in uncertainty estimates could lead to missed anomalies and reduced system
sensitivity.

Traditional distribution shift detection methods, such as the Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests,
failed to provide reliable anomaly detection, flagging nearly everything as anomalous.
Similarly, theoretically superior Bayesian approaches, including posterior predictive checks
and Bayes factors, showed poor performance in preliminary testing. Although there is no
definitive evidence for why these methods failed, the temporal correlation structure in
video data likely plays a significant role. Frame variances captured at 30 FPS exhibit
temporal dependencies that violate the independence assumptions underlying classical
statistical tests and Bayesian model comparison methods. The current uncertainty-based
approach is at least intuitive as it asks ’how likely is what I’m seeing now compared to what
I have seen, given everything I have seen?’ However, this creates a problem in validation:
The threshold is set using the least likely sample from the same data used to fit the distri-
bution, making the threshold inherently too strict. However, this would not be an issue in
real-world deployment where baseline data can be continuously collected. Still, achieving
statistically sound performance requires addressing the underlying temporal correlation
structure.
Although MCDropout provided better calibrated uncertainty estimates, its performance
characteristics raise concerns about generalization. Given how little data it was tested
on, dropout rates cannot be confidently optimized, nor can a general optimum be claimed
for future applications. Additionally, it remains unclear how dropout rates affect the results,
making parameter tuning difficult. This makes MCDropout seem less optimal compared to
alternatives such as using DropBlock or ensemble methods. However, it may be the most
practical option since only one layer uses dropout, meaning that clever optimization of
forward passes could make the approach computationally viable for real-time monitoring.
Scalability
An important limitation is that the gap between these component-level results and fully
autonomous end-to-end pipeline deployment remains unquantified. Testing the complete
pipeline would have provided valuable insights into how substantial the remaining inte-
gration and deployment challenges are.
5.3
Future Work
Automated Annotation
The current approach relies on extracting features from cropped mask regions, which
introduces a significant computational bottleneck. Future work could explore two promis-
ing directions to improve efficiency: (1) leveraging native SAM2 tokens for each gener-
ated mask, or (2) applying mask-pooling operations directly on SAM2’s encoder features
to aggregate relevant information per mask. However, given the nature of the task of
SAM2, predicting accurate mask boundaries, there is some concern that its feature rep-
resentations may primarily encode spatial information rather than rich semantic content.
Nevertheless, both approaches hold potential to eliminate the cropping and re-processing
workload, which currently constitutes a substantial portion of the overall computation time.
The results revealed performance variation across domains, most notably the poor recall
observed in the dough dataset. A comparison with an ideal pipeline further highlighted
the importance of domain-specific tuning to achieve reliable segmentation. Additionally,
the training size analysis indicated that approximately five manually annotated images
per domain are sufficient to train the classifier effectively. Since these annotations are
already required, they could also be used to optimize the SAM2 parameters for each
domain. This targeted tuning may help address systematic segmentation failures, such
as the under-segmentation issues observed in the dough domain. Future work should
investigate the extent to which domain-specific tuning improves performance. Although it

is not expected to fully match the ideal pipeline, it is reasonable to anticipate measurable
performance gains.
The current binary classification framework (object vs. background) could be extended to
multi-class scenarios. The KNN classifier would be a natural choice, potentially enabling
the simultaneous annotation of multiple object types within the same production line. Im-
portantly, this extension should preserve the few-shot learning advantages demonstrated
in binary experiments.
Visual Counting
The current system maintains object tracking throughout the entire video sequence, in-
creasing the likelihood of tracking failures over long distances. A more robust approach
could implement zone-based counting that relies only on confirming whether the ID of
the object appears on both sides of a counting line or in a small area of the image. In
this setup, continuous tracking of each ID across the whole frame is not required. In-
stead, it only matters if the same ID is detected just before and just after crossing the line.
This reduces tracking complexity and limits the impact of ID switches, since an object may
change IDs multiple times but will still be counted only once if it crosses the line. However,
this method may require more computation than simply counting unique IDs, representing
a trade-off between robustness and processing cost. Such an approach could still signif-
icantly reduce cumulative errors and instability in cases where long tracking sequences
are prone to failure.
Uncertainty Monitoring
The current approach uses all available frames for uncertainty estimation. However, the
high temporal correlation between consecutive frames leads to overconfident predictions
and limits the choice of detection methods. An easy way to mitigate this is to apply block
averaging or systematic subsampling to reduce temporal correlation. A more robust but
also more complex approach involves explicitly modeling the correlation structure using
autocorrelation functions. It should be noted that both strategies typically require substan-
tially more data than was available for this thesis. These changes may also necessitate
transitioning to non-parametric methods for anomaly detection to better accommodate the
modified data characteristics.
Although MCDropout offers practical advantages for uncertainty estimation, ensemble
methods could provide more robust uncertainty estimates at the cost of increased com-
putational overhead. Additionally, implementing DropBlock, which applies dropout re-
gionally on feature maps rather than at random, may offer a more theoretically grounded
approach to uncertainty quantification that better respects the spatial structure of CNN
features. A comparative evaluation of MCDropout, DropBlock, and ensemble methods
would help to establish the optimal balance between computational efficiency and uncer-
tainty estimation quality for monitoring applications.
Scalability
The current framework provides the foundational components for scalable deployment,
but still requires validation of fully autonomous end-to-end operation. Future work should
focus on implementing and evaluating a complete Autonomous Visual Counting pipeline,
where the initial setup includes automated video capture and intelligent selection of five
representative frames for manual annotation. After this minimal human input, the system
would operate independently. Entropy-based triggers would enable an automatic expan-
sion of the dataset without requiring human correction. Human intervention would only
be necessary when counting uncertainty alarms suggesting potential hardware failures or
environmental disturbances. Although the current work does not fully realize this vision,
Autonomous Visual Counting represents the most promising and practically relevant di-

Conclusion
Visual counting systems can be designed for scalable deployment in diverse manufactur-
ing environments with minimal expert oversight. The integrated framework combining au-
tomated annotation, visual counting, and uncertainty monitoring demonstrates that such
deployment is technically feasible while maintaining operational reliability.
The results were based on careful experimental evaluation across four real industrial do-
mains, supported by rigorous cross-validation and systematic comparison against com-
mercial baselines, suggesting that the findings can be generalized beyond controlled lab-
oratory conditions.
The automated annotation approach was competitive with commercial baselines while
requiring minimal training data, demonstrating that the approach can effectively replace
extensive manual annotation in industrial settings. The counting system achieved perfect
accuracy across all domains, proving that YOLO-based detection can maintain reliability
across diverse manufacturing contexts when conditions are maintained.
The anomaly detection approach was capable of identifying synthetic degradations. The
successful detection highlights the potential of model-intrinsic uncertainty signals as early
warnings of emerging reliability issues. Together, these components establish a founda-
tion for self-monitoring visual systems that can trigger human intervention only when it is
truly necessary.
The results were mainly limited by the scarce data available for the evaluation of uncer-
tainty monitoring, which restricted the analysis to synthetic degradations that only simulate
real-world failure modes. As a result, the availability of the monitoring system to detect
the full range of unforeseen anomalies remains unproven.
Future work should focus on deploying the full end-to-end pipeline for extended periods
to assess robustness over time. In these deployments, entropy-based uncertainty should
guide annotation, allowing the automated system to gradually replace human involvement
entirely. The long-term goal is to develop visual counting systems that can run indepen-
dently after a minimal setup, only involving human operators when the system detects
signs of hardware problems or changing environmental conditions.
