Abstract
This study set out to comprehensively analyze the water and element balances within willow zero-
discharge facilities, which are systems designed for efficient water management. The objective was
to unravel the complex interactions within these facilities.
A key area of focus was examining the accumulation of salt in constructed wetlands. However,
the findings in this regard are not definitive and should be approached cautiously. In an attempt
to understand the water dynamics, a model was developed. While useful, it became evident that
the model could have been more comprehensive.
In terms of assessing the balance of different elements, particularly sodium, the study faced limita-
tions in data availability. The sampling strategy did not yield sufficient information, and there was
a scarcity of supporting literature.
As the study progressed, the scope expanded due to the company’s growing interest in additional
elements that were not initially considered. This shift was primarily influenced by input from the
industry partner.
Furthermore, the study highlighted the challenges associated with scaling experimental models to
real-world applications, underscoring the complexity of translating theoretical models into practical
solutions.
It is important to note that the level of support anticipated from the partnering company was
not fully realized, which hindered the execution of the optimal plan for the study.
In conclusion, this study serves as an initial exploration into the water and element balances of
willow zero-discharge facilities. While it provides valuable insights, it is foundational and neces-
sitates further research.
Future studies should take into account the challenges and limitations
encountered in this study and strive for more robust data collection and analysis methods.

1
Introduction
In this study, we delve into the intriguing question: "Are salts accumulating in constructed wet-
lands ?" Specifically, we focus on zero-discharge constructed wetlands that are employed as domestic
wastewater treatment facilities. Imagine having a swimming pool in your garden, but instead of
being filled with water, it is filled with soil and willows are growing in it. The domestic wastewater
is mixed and channeled into this facility, where the willows play a crucial role. They not only help
in evaporating the water but also potentially absorb some of the elements present in the wastewater.
To thoroughly investigate this, we have designed a comprehensive experimental setup. This includes
the installation of monitoring equipment and the execution of a meticulously planned sampling strat-
egy. The study incorporates both chemical analyses and physical measurements, which are carried
out across different facilities.
One of the key aspects we are looking into is the behavior of salts within these wetlands. Un-
derstanding whether there is an accumulation of salts is vital as it can have implications on the
efficiency and sustainability of these wetlands as a wastewater treatment solution. Moreover, it’s
essential to understand how the willows contribute to the evaporation process and the uptake of
elements, and how this affects the overall balance of substances within the system.
Furthermore, this study aims to shed light on the complex interactions and processes taking place
within these constructed wetlands. This includes understanding how various factors such as envi-
ronmental conditions, wastewater composition, and plant characteristics can influence the behavior
of salts and other elements.
In essence, this work is not just about answering the question of salt accumulation; it’s about
gaining a deeper understanding of the intricacies of constructed wetlands as a wastewater treat-
ment option. The insights derived from this study could be invaluable for improving the design and
management of constructed wetlands, ensuring their effectiveness and sustainability in the long run.
2
Literature review
Both natural and constructed wetlands have been scientifically investigated since the early eighties
for their potential to clean wastewater (e.g Gersberg et al. (1985)). In the case of the facilities
built by Salix Greentech, the constructed wetland is completely covered with soil and willows (Salix
viminalis) are planted in rows, the bottom is covered by an impermeable layer.
2.1
Input of the system
The input for our system is strictly domestic wastewater as every facility is linked to one household.
Domestic wastewater refers to the water that is generated from households, including bathrooms,
kitchens, laundry, and other activities such as cleaning. The composition of domestic wastewater
can vary widely depending on several factors such as the number of occupants, their lifestyles,the
type of plumbing system in use or the water supply mineral content. Typically, domestic wastewater
contains a mixture of organic and inorganic compounds, including human waste, food waste, soaps,
detergents, and cleaning agents. Domestic wastewater also contains pathogens, such as bacteria
and viruses, that can pose a health risk to humans and the environment. Effective treatment of
domestic wastewater is essential to protect public health and environmental preservation.
5
==PAGE:12==
A good overlook for domestic wastewater can be the investigation of general quality parameters for
the water such as total suspended solids (TSS), biological oxygen demand in five days (BOD5) and
total nitrogen (TN) and phosphorus (TP). A review of the existing data: Obarska-Pempkowiak et al.
(2015), reported values for wastewater of European household connected to constructed wetlands
between 1990 and 1998. Average values for the general quality parameters were: 98.6mg/L for TSS,
97.0mg/L for BOD5, 28.5mg/L for TN, and 8.6mg/L for TP. While COD5 values are not available
in this dataset, it is often used as an indicator of the organic pollution load in wastewater. COD5
measures of the amount of oxygen required to chemically oxidize the organic matter in a sample
of wastewater. The spatial, time and habits variability produces big differences in the content of
domestic wastewater, researchers in Japan have documented as early as in the eighties, differences
in domestic wastewater quality according to the type of cooking taking place in the kitchen (Ukita
et al. (1986)).

2.2
Cleaning capacity of wetlands
Constructed wetlands are an effective and eco-friendly method for treating various types of wastew-
ater, including domestic and industrial effluent. Among the different types of constructed wetlands,
those planted with willow species have shown great potential for phytoremediation of heavy metals
and organic pollutants due to their fast growth, high biomass production, and adaptability to differ-
ent soil and climate conditions (Yıldırım & Çıtır Kasım (2018)). Furthermore, integrated household
constructed wetlands (IHCW) systems, which combine anaerobic and aerobic treatment processes,
have been developed as a sustainable and cost-effective alternative for treating domestic wastewater
in rural areas (Wu et al. (2011)). In this literature review, we will explore the use of constructed
wetlands planted with willows for domestic wastewater treatment in rural areas.
Constructed wetlands are a suitable technology for treating domestic wastewater, particularly in
rural and remote areas where conventional treatment systems are not available. The integrated
household constructed wetland system, which typically consists of a sedimentation tank followed by
a constructed wetland bed planted with willows, has been shown to effectively remove various pol-
lutants from domestic wastewater. High removal rates of biochemical oxygen demand (BOD5) and
6
==PAGE:13==
total suspended solids (TSS) have been reported. Pretreatment in the sedimentation tank also plays
an important role in reducing BOD5 and TSS concentrations, as well as buffering the treatment
system against excessive particulate or organic loading (Wu et al. (2011)). The anaerobic degra-
dation process in the sedimentation tank contributes to treatment, particularly in reducing BOD5
and TSS concentrations (Ávila et al. (2015)). However, removal of ammonium-nitrogen (NH4-N)
and total phosphorus (TP) is limited in the sedimentation tank and in anaerobic environments due
to low dissolved oxygen concentrations (Wu et al. (2011)).
The cleaning capacities of constructed wetlands vary based on the type.
The book Treatment
wetlands, Second edition by Kadlec & Wallace (2009) describes 5 different categories of Treatment
wetlands, found in Figure 1. While the figure describes 5 types of treatment wetlands, it is not
uncommon to combine any of the elements in the scope of achieving higher quality water treatment.
Such hybrid systems have in fact proven to be more effective than their peers (Tran et al. (2019),
Ávila et al. (2015)).
Figure 1: Pyramid of treatment wetlands copyright Kadlec & Wallace (2009)

straw bale (7) where microbial activity can occur.
The straw bale provides support for micro-

(3,2m2×1m/Person) (Brix & Arias (2005)). Reactors made of straw have been proven to enhance
denitrification through organic carbon release beneficial to microbial utilisation Guan et al. (2019).
The vertical flow systems analysed in the study from Ávila et al. (2015) have demonstrated high

of 98-99% for conventional pollutants and above 80% for various pharmaceuticals, personal care
products, and endocrine disruptors. Additionally the study found denitrification occurs within the

Willows planted on the constructed wetland provide additional treatment through their root system,
which supports biofilm growth, enhances nutrient uptake, and increases oxygen transfer to the
subsurface environment. Willows have been found to have a high potential for phytoremediation
through phytostabilization, phytotransformation, and phytovolatilization mechanisms (Yıldırım &
Çıtır Kasım (2018)). The use of willow species, particularly Salix alba, Salix babylonica, and Populus
alba, had the highest boron (B) removal performance in constructed wetlands. These species have
been found to have a high phytostabilization capacity attributed to their ability to secrete boron-
chelating compounds and mucilage from their roots into the rhizosphere (Yıldırım & Çıtır Kasım
(2018)). As for the willow species used in this thesis : Salix viminalis, it has been found to be effective
in the removal of cadmium, copper, and zinc from contaminated soils.
The same study found
other willow species such as Salix purpurea capable of removing high levels of polycyclic aromatic
hydrocarbons (PAHs) from contaminated soils. While more data is available for toxicity values for
Salix viminalis than for other species, S. viminalis has proven to be more effective than S. Alba at
effectively accumulating cadmium in its root apical region Touati et al. (2019). While some willow
species are better at targeting specific pollutants as previously seen, all willows share a common
genus: Salix and therefore common traits that characterise this genus.
Other members of the
Salicaceae family such as Poplars are also capable of similar phytoextraction and phytostabilisation
(e.g. Khudolieieva & Kutsokon (2018), Yıldırım & Çıtır Kasım (2018), Martínez-Hernández et al.
(2018)).
Pertaining to Salix viminalis, a study: Brzezinska et al. (2016) focusing on a system composed of
a septic tank and a constructed wetland with common osier planted in it revealed that enzyme
activity was twice as high in the rhizosphere (soil influenced by root secretion) compared to the
bulk soil. The highest enzyme activity was observed during summer (July and August) and the
lowest during autumn (October). In terms of specific enzyme activity, a-glucosidase, b-glucosidase
and phosphatase activity was higher in rhizosphere samples than bulk soil samples (Figure 2). The
study concluded that the presence of common osier roots significantly enhanced enzyme activity,
which in turn led to more efficient wastewater treatment. These enzymes are particularly relevant
for the field of wastewater treatment as they are all naturally occurring enzymes that are produced
by microorganisms in the soil. These enzymes play a crucial role in the biogeochemical cycles of
carbon and nitrogen, breaking down complex organic molecules into simpler forms that can be taken
up by plants and other organisms. In the context of this thesis, the enzymes should be present in
the soil of constructed wetlands used to treat wastewater from single households. The roots of the

common osier, planted on the wetland are therefore supposed to stimulate the activity of these
enzymes, contributing to the breakdown of pollutants in the wastewater.

The constructed wetland bed planted with willows is particularly effective in removing total Phos-

The same study from Wu et al. (2011) conducted on the performance of an integrated household
constructed wetland (IHCW) system planted with willows showed high removal rates of biochemical
oxygen demand (BOD5) and total suspended solids (TSS) in domestic wastewater.
2.3
Toxicity and willow resilience
Willows are chosen for their ability to resist waterlogging hence providing the facility with an op-
erational exchange matrix both above and below the water table. Constructed wetlands often opt
for plants categorised as halophytes (Fountoulakis et al. (2017)). While willows are not categorised
as halophytes, their resilience to salt is higher to those of other trees. The study from Khudolieieva
& Kutsokon (2018) found that a hybrid willow species between alba x fragilis was not influenced
by any increase of sodium chloride concentration during an experiment with concentrations up to
100mM. At similar concentrations, a hybrid Aspen showed significant decrease of the intensity of
shoots growth up to 94.3% after two months.
Available toxicity values for willows in the literature can be found in Table 29, willows can display
different values based on the duration of the test, in fact the EC50 for sodium chloride showed the
longer the experiment would last the lower the EC50 would be (Clausen & Trapp (2017)).
The resilience of willows to waterlogging makes them a good candidate for constructed wetlands
wastewater treatment facilities. Back in 1996 a study (Jackson & Attwood (1996)) showed how wil-
9
==PAGE:16==
low roots waterlogged for more than 20 days slowed their deep root expansion but increased shoot
growth in their upper part after only 7 days, particularly specialising in aerenchyma. Aerenchyma
is a specialised type of tissue found in some plants, particularly in aquatic and wetland plants, as
well as in some plants that grow in waterlogged or poorly aerated soils. The primary function of
aerenchyma is to facilitate gas exchange and transport oxygen from the aerial parts of the plant
to the submerged or waterlogged roots. Aerenchyma is characterised by large air spaces or cavities
that are formed within the plant tissue. These air spaces create a low-resistance pathway for the
movement of gases, such as oxygen and carbon dioxide, between the plant’s above-ground parts and
its roots. This allows the roots to receive adequate oxygen for respiration, even in environments
with low oxygen availability, and helps the plant to survive under waterlogged conditions.
Aerenchyma is not the only adaptation willow trees were able to develop to avoid anoxic stress,
Grosse et al. (1996) found willows use a pressurised ventilation system which increases the oxygen
supply of the roots and rhizomes. The same study showed that while the pressurised system was
able to provide necessary oxygen to the organs in anoxic environments, excess oxygen could be
used by the plants to regulate the redox potential of the rhizosphere protecting the roots against
phytotoxins uptake (Grosse et al. (1996)).
While the development of specialised tissues help with the survival of willows in waterlogged en-
vironment, Yang et al. (2019) observed in their experiment non-flooded willows produced more
biomass and accumulated more cadmium than their non flooded peers.
2.4
Water transpiration from willows
As previously explained willows are well adapted to wet environments and distribute themselves
close to water.
Their evolutionary advantages have made them good wetland colonisers.
As a
result willows can be found colonising Australian streams (NSWGovernment (2021)). The study
Doody & Benyon (2011) characterised the water loss due to willows transpiration for Australian
streams. Willows with permanent access to water were able to transpire at most 15.2 mm.day−1
and those with semi-permanent access to water 2.3 mm.day−1 (used for estimations using crown
projected area). On the other side of the world in Florida, Salix caroliniana is gradually taking
over graminoids wetlands and contributing to water losses through low water efficiency (Budny
& Benscoter (2016)).
As stated in the same study: "Willow had higher average rates of leaf
stomatal conductance and net photosynthesis rate than sawgrass. However, willow had lower water
use efficiency (WUE) and greater LAI, resulting in greater loss of water through transpiration
by willow populations and diminishing projected landscape water availability despite marginally
increased Carbon assimilation." hence showing the potential of the Salix genus to evaporate water
in greater amounts than a lot of other wetland species.
2.5
Conclusion
The constructed wetland is a viable option but no literature fully covers the potential accumula-
tion of salts in a zero discharge system. The bachelor thesis from Kirk (2013) found significant
differences in NaCl concentrations between different systems but no correlation between concentra-
tion and system age. While there are a lot of different factors and input to consider, zero-discharge
wastewater treatment constructed wetlands benefit significantly from the inclusion of willow species.
The root system of willows, such as Salix viminalis or Salix alba promotes biofilm growth, enhances
nutrient uptake, and increases oxygen transfer, leading to improved treatment processes. These
willow species have shown a high potential for phytoremediation through phytostabilisation, phy-
totransformation, and phytovolatilisation mechanisms, effectively removing pollutants like boron,
cadmium, copper, zinc, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) from contaminated soils and
10
==PAGE:17==
water.
Constructed wetlands with willows have been found to achieve high removal rates of biochemical
oxygen demand (BOD5), total suspended solids (TSS), and total phosphorus from wastewater.
The presence of willow roots in the rhizosphere stimulates enzyme activity, which is essential for
the biogeochemical cycles of carbon and nitrogen, ultimately contributing to the breakdown of
pollutants in the wastewater.
Willows are resilient to waterlogging and saline conditions, making them suitable candidates for
constructed wetlands wastewater treatment facilities. The development of specialized tissues like
aerenchyma and pressurized ventilation systems allows willows to survive in waterlogged environ-
ments and protect themselves against phytotoxin uptake.
Moreover, willows can effectively transpire water, contributing to the overall water balance within
the wetland system. However, it is important to consider the potential water loss through transpi-
ration when designing a constructed wetland system.
Overall, the integration of willow species into zero-discharge wastewater treatment constructed wet-
lands enhances pollutant removal, improves treatment efficiency, and leverages the natural resilience
and adaptability of willows to optimize the system’s performance.

3.6
Sample preparation and analysis
The water samples from the first campaign went through pH and conductivity measurement before
going the analysis laboratory, the strategy for other samplings was adapted and water samples were
measured for these parameters plus oxygen on site using a flow-cell.
Total Organic Carbon (TOC) Analysis
For the analysis of Total Organic Carbon (TOC), samples were subjected to combustion at tem-
peratures ranging between 750 °C and 800 °C using O2 as the carrier gas at a flow rate of 160
ml/min. The analysis was performed using a multi N/C 3100 flow injection TOC analyzer man-
ufactured by Analytik Jena. The Total Carbon (TC) and Inorganic Carbon (IC) were analyzed
using a Non-dispersive Infrared Spectroscopy (NDIR) detector. The TOC was determined using
the differential method, where TOC = TC - TIC. Total Nitrogen bound (TNb) was analyzed using
a Chemiluminescence detector (CLD). Data processing was carried out using the multiWin 4.13
software.
ICP-MS Analysis for Trace Metals
For the analysis of trace metals such as Cr, Fe, Co, Ni, Zn, As, Cd, Hg, and Pb, samples were ana-
lyzed using an ICP-MS (7850x, Agilent Technologies) with Yttrium serving as an internal standard.
Before analysis, samples were acidified to 2% HNO3 and 1% HCl (v/v). The ICP-MS instrument
was equipped with Platinum tipped skimmer and sample cones, a double pass Scott spray chamber
operated at 2 °C, and a Micromist nebulizer. The elements were analyzed in He collision mode with
a Helium flow of 5 ml/min for Cr, Fe, Co, Ni, Zn, and As, and in no gas mode for Cd, Hg, and Pb.
Data processing was performed using the MassHunter 5.2 Workstation Software (v. D.01.02).
Solid samples for metal analysis were prepared using microwave digestion with a mixture of 65%
Nitric Acid, 37% Hydrochloric Acid, and 30% Hydrogen Peroxide in a ratio of 3:1:1. The final
concentration for analysis was 2% Nitric Acid.
ICP-OES Analysis for Sodium
For the analysis of sodium (Na), samples were analyzed using an ICP-OES (Avio 200, PerkinElmer)
with Yttrium as an internal standard. The detector was operated in Radial mode, and detection
was achieved at wavelengths of 589.592 nm for sodium. Prior to analysis, samples were acidified
to 2% HNO3 and 1% HCl (v/v). The ICP-OES instrument was equipped with a baffled, cyclonic
spray chamber and a Micromist nebulizer. Data processing was carried out using the SyngisticTM
for ICP Software version 2.0 from PerkinElmer.

Generally, a higher soil density suggests a more tightly packed soil structure with reduced pore
spaces, which could intuitively indicate a decreased overall pore space and, consequently, a reduced

often have smaller pores, can retain water more efficiently against gravitational forces, potentially
resulting in increased water preservation within the soil profile.
The observed differences in soil density and water content can be linked to the soil texture noted

were characterized as more clay-like soils. As such, the clayish soils at Karise and Dalmose would be
expected to have higher densities and smaller, more numerous pore spaces compared to the sandy
soil at Jægerspris, and this can account for their differences in water content.

Water balance
The water balance in the facilities in a function of Equation 32. Using the daily evapotranspiration
and precipitation rates obtained in subsection 3.3. As well as the daily water consumption values
from Table 4. A conceptual model can be built to try reconstructing the water balance of the
facilities incorporating a few parameters:
KC, the crop coefficient, is a dimensionless coefficient used in agricultural water management,
specifically in calculating evapotranspiration. It is a ratio that reflects the relationship between
the evapotranspiration rate from a specific crop and the evapotranspiration rate from a reference
surface. It is expressed as:
KC = Observed evapotranspiration
Potential evapotranspiration
(1)
In the first conceptual model, the Kc alone was not providing accurate results (the water storage
was negative in summer) as it is a coefficient that can change with time e.g. presence of leaves
on the trees, water availability or diseases affecting the trees. Two KCs were therefore introduced,
one when water is easily available (KCw) and another when water is scarce (KCn) (relative to the
facilities). The KCn prevents the model from going negative in summer when there is little to no
water to evaporate.

Plant evaporation is a multifaceted process involving numerous physical and biological mecha-
nisms within the soil, plant organisms, and atmosphere.
These processes are often modeled in
soil-vegetation-atmosphere transport (SVAT) models. Running these simulations requires consis-
tent access to evaporation driver data and a thorough understanding of model parameters and how
they change with the seasons.
However, the necessary data for these sites under investigation and parameters for willow in these
facilities are not readily available. We also lack evaporation test data and continuous water level
monitoring.
Despite these challenges, evaporation remains a vital element for the proper functioning of the
facility, given its relation to maintaining the zero discharge condition and managing the salt accu-
mulation, distribution, and concentration levels. Therefore, a conceptual model is created, which
relies solely on available data: daily precipitation and potential evaporation predicted for the near-
est grid point by DMI, physical dimensions of the facility (including soil, reactor, plants), estimated
wastewater loading, plant canopy establishment and management, and the water table values mea-
sured at three separate points in Spring 2023.
As previously mentioned, a model based solely on KCbar provided negative volumes in summer.
Meaning the KC could not be assumed stable throughout the year.
Regarding KC’s behavior in relation to water, it is tied to the availability of water.
In ample
water conditions, Kc is expected to be high, representing the plant’s ability to transpire water
effectively. However, in conditions of water scarcity or drought, the plant’s transpiration rate is
inhibited, and the value of Kc drops. It’s important to note that this relationship between Kc and
water availability is a simplified approximation and may not capture all the complex interactions
in real-world scenarios.

In the previous model all precipitation marked P was assumed to contribute to the water volume
below ground. In reality the above ground soil acts as a buffer and recharges its water content up
to field capacity before letting the water infiltrate the below ground part of the facility (Figure 23.
This water content stored in the above ground is available to the vegetation growing on it including
the willows.
A new model is therefore necessary, one that takes this field capacity into account. While this plant-

of soil above the facilities and this information is not available, a new parameter is included in the
model. This new parameter is called above ground soil moisture retention. This new parameter
noted SMR is a ratio of how much water from precipitation after runoff is staying above ground and
leaving it with evaporation and transpiration from willows and how much precipitation percolates
to the facility.

if it did, the water volume measured in the facility is still less than what the model predicts,

discharge facility. One hypothesis is the zero-discharge aspect of the facility being compromised. The

more correct should be built.
A third model more correct with regards to the theory and processes happening in the facilities
should incorporate the following elements:
• Water flows in the unsaturated soil: The model should simulate the water flows in the
unsaturated soil, which is all soil above the water table. The assumption is that precipitation
(P) enters the soil, while facility wastewater (WW) enters the reactor directly. However, there
might be some cases where P enters the bottom of the facility.
• Water storage at field capacity (FC) and permanent wilting point (PWP): The
model should consider the water storage at FC (Vwsoil,FC) and PWP (Vwsoil,PWP). These
values can be determined at the facility scale using the pF curve (soil water retention curve),
given the total volume of the soil and the soil texture (here previously assumed).
• Volume of unsaturated soil: The model should calculate the volume of unsaturated soil
from the total soil volume and the water level, neglecting capillary rise. This will help deter-
mine how much water can be stored in this compartment. The volume of soil above ground
is unfortunately not available in this thesis.
• Transpiration and root water uptake: The model should account for transpiration and
root water uptake from the unsaturated soil and from the bottom. However, partitioning the
total evaporation (KC ×Epot) into evaporation (E) from soil, interception (I) from wet canopy,
and transpiration (T) is a challenge.
• Potential root water uptake rates: The model should consider the potential root water
uptake rates from the two compartments (unsaturated soil and bottom). Partitioning the two
is again a challenge, where known transpiration rates from similar willows could be used.
• Variable fraction of P entering the bottom: The model should consider that the fraction
of P entering the bottom is likely not constant. It is very likely that there is no percolation
at all in summer and only in winter there is, after the soil is at field capacity. The conceptual
second model simplifies it as a constant called SMR (Figure 24).
In summary, a third model should be a more complex two-compartment model that simulates water
flows in the unsaturated soil and considers various factors such as water storage at Field Capacity
and Permanent Wilting Point, volume of unsaturated soil, transpiration and root water uptake for
each compartment, and variable fractions of Precipitation entering the bottom.

does not seem to be above critical toxicity values especially considering the supply of urea the

Hypothesis 5: The concentrations follow specific distribution patterns in the facilities
Here only sodium is assessed as it is the highest concentrated element and all results were available.

not the same size).
Hypothesis 5: The sodium concentrations within the constructed wetland do not exhibit a sta-

Discussion and points of interests
5.1
Higher Zinc concentrations in Karise
The mass balance gave Karise as a strong Zinc accumulator, with a mass balance being positive of
131g of Zinc. The tap-water also featured zinc concentrations of 1587 µg/L compared to 199 for
Dalmose and a concentration below quantification limit for Jægerspris. The wood in Karise also
had a higher Zinc concentration than in the other two facilities. The leaves however did not follow
the pattern with a concentration lower than in Jægerspris but 4 times higher than in Dalmose. The
soil Zinc concentration is also higher in Karise than everywhere else.
The most realistic hypothesis that would explain higher Zinc concentrations in Karise is the following
one: as Karise tap-water is self pumped from the local groundwater, the many buildings on the
property contaminate the local groundwater with their Zinc roofs.
When rainwater comes into
contact with roofing materials, it can dissolve or dislodge metals and other substances present on
the roof surface. As the rainwater runs off the roof, it carries these substances with it. When this
runoff water reaches the ground, it can infiltrate the soil and potentially contaminate groundwater
sources.
Additionally the pipework could contain zinc pipes. The below picture shows one of the building of
the property with what appears to be a Zinc roof.

5.5
Mass balance robustness
The mass balance has been shown to not be robust enough as:
• Samples from the grinder well did not represent the typical wastewater going in the facility.
While initially the water was supposed to be sampled after the grinder pump.
• The Canopy was variable and the LAI was estimated instead of being measured making the
leaf export inaccurate.
• The resolution of the leaf and wood export being yearly gives margin for a lot of variability.
• The soil samples were not taken deep enough to see any up concentration effect from the
bottom liquid. It is therefore unsure how much of each element binds to the soil and how
much stays in the solution when the bottom liquid level goes down. The estimation of the
element mass in the facilities can be underestimated.
• The atmospheric deposition was not taken into account.
Looking at the results it provided, it can be observed that copper is being accumulated in Karise
and Dalmose, this is a plausible explanation as plants can benefit from high copper supply but are
able to discriminate copper when the levels are too high in the environment, as stated in Marschner
(1988), "even at high supply, up to 60% of the total copper in roots might be bound to the cell wall
fraction and the cell wall-plasma membrane interface".

6
Conclusion
This study has shed light on the intriguing possibility of sodium accumulation within the facilities,
particularly in a compartment that was not subjected to sampling. It is noteworthy that, with the
exception of sodium, the balance of the other assessed elements appears to be maintained or not
critical, as evidenced by the congruence between the quantities entering and exiting the facilities.
This suggests that there is no significant accumulation of these elements.
In the context of toxicity, the soil in which the willows are cultivated did not exhibit any signs of
toxicity. However, it is imperative to mention that the liquid at the bottom of the facilities occasion-
ally approached or even exceeded the toxicity thresholds for sodium chloride (with the assumption
that all chloride forms sodium), specifically in the straw bale. This is a critical observation that
warrants further investigation. Conversely, all other elements remained well within the safe limits,
showing no signs of toxicity.
Another aspect that merits attention is the toxicity in the willow organs.
It was found that a
direct comparison with benchmark values was not feasible due to the scarcity of relevant data in
the existing literature. This highlights a gap in the research and underscores the need for more
comprehensive studies in this domain.
Furthermore, this study has brought to the forefront the disparities between different facilities. Not
only does this accentuate the inherent variability, but it also raises pertinent questions regarding
the underlying factors that contribute to these differences. The study ventured into formulating
hypotheses to elucidate the discrepancies and incoherence observed among the facilities.
Based on the findings of this study, several recommendations can be made to ensure the optimal
functioning of these facilities. Firstly, it is crucial to conduct further investigations into the deepest
layers of the facilities. This will provide a more comprehensive understanding of the concentrations
at these depths, which could significantly influence the overall results of such studies.
Secondly, monitoring of sodium chloride concentrations in Jægerspris is recommended.
This is
to ensure that the soil does not reach similar concentrations as those observed in the straw bale in
the foreseeable future. High concentrations of sodium chloride could potentially disrupt the balance
of the system and negatively impact its performance.
Lastly, it is recommended to thoroughly investigate any potential breaches in the Dalmose fa-
cility. Any breach in the system could lead to the discharge of wastewater into the environment,
which could have significant environmental implications. Therefore, it is crucial to identify and
address any potential breaches promptly to prevent environmental contamination and ensure the
sustainability of the system.
In conclusion, this work serves as a stepping stone in understanding the dynamics of element ac-
cumulation and toxicity in the context of facilities housing willows. It opens avenues for further
research, particularly in the realm of sodium accumulation and toxicity thresholds, and calls for a
more in-depth exploration of the factors that account for the variations among different facilities.
