Summary (English)
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the inﬂuence of ambient displays on
sleeping habits by implementing an interactive system. The system is designed
for improving users’ sleep quality. The requirements for the system have been
established based on interviews conducted with potential users. Prototypes of
the display were presented to users in a study consisting of two phases. During
the ﬁrst stage of the study the users were wearing Jawbone bracelets in order
to provide data feedback generation. In the second stage, the users were pre-
sented with the research prototype for evaluation of its potential in terms of
behavior change by ﬁlling out Positive and Negative Aﬀect Scale questionnaires
and taking part in semi-structured interviews. The outcome of the thesis is the
ﬁnal version of the prototype visualizing color-based feedback on users’ sleep
duration followed by the study based on PANAS questionnaires to evaluate the
aﬀect on potential users.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Sleep is one of the most important physiological processes — a basic need which
core function is regeneration of mind and body overnight. Numerous papers
show the importance of quality and quantity of sleep by revealing their inﬂu-
ence on diﬀerent aspects of health. Insuﬃcient amount of sleep, called sleep
deprivation, causes a wide spectrum of health-related issues such as e.g. day-
time sleepiness, fatigue [TLA+04], impairment of cognitive functions [AP07] or
adverse aﬀection of metabolism [TLA+04] and the immune system [MK05]. If
the sleep deprivation is of a long-standing and chronic nature, the consequences
to the organism can be much worse, as it can cause a broad range of cardiovas-
cular diseases [MHT+09, AWM+03] or several metabolic issues including e.g.
diabetes [KSPVC07].
Sleep deprivation problem being an eﬀect of a continuous increase of responsi-
bilities, both professional (which often results in working overtime) and personal
ones, has also its social dimension. It has been proved that there is a correla-
tion between sleep deprivation and motor vehicle crash rates [Tef16]. Insuﬃcient
amount of sleep has also an adverse inﬂuence on human relationships. For in-
stance, suﬀering from sleep deprivation by at least one partner in a couple may
have a signiﬁcant impact on the relationship, as well as may increase the prob-
ability of having a conﬂict [GC14]. Last but not least, people who sleep less are
perceived as more tired and less attractive to others [ASI+10].
==PAGE:22==
2
Introduction
The motivation for tackling with the problem of sleep deprivation in a form of a
master thesis is personal as I have observed myself several symptoms that have
been caused by a lack of sleep during the night: daytime fatigue, sleepiness,
lack of concentration at work, getting irritated easily.
Having programming
background, I have decided to attempt to ﬁnd a countermeasure that could
unobtrusively suggest people put stronger focus on their sleep schedule with a
reference to the ICT technology.
The research on design and use of computer technology to interact with users is
known as Human-Computer Interaction. One of the recent topics of this research
are ambient displays. Unlike standard types of displays, their main functionality
is to provide a message without requiring the user’s focus. Potential users may
get information from a source which is not located in the foreground. Numerous
past implementations of this concept have proved that ambient displays can work
as systems for behavioral change.
Throughout the past three decades ambient displays have been constructed
for a wide range of purposes. One of the earliest examples is the
Live Wire
created by Natalie Jeremijenko in 1995 [WB97] reﬂecting the Internet traﬃc
through whirling motion. With time, diﬀerent ways of presenting information
have been investigated, which resulted in a broad spectrum of solutions being
used both in public and private areas. More detailed elaboration concerning
relevant examples of ambient displays will be mentioned in Chapter 2.
Although ambient displays can be identiﬁed as a speciﬁc branch of ambient
user interfaces, they should not be treated as such, as they do not allow users
to provide their input.
A typical ambient inﬂuence system usually uses an
inference engine or an unobtrusive sensing device to collect the data necessary
to transform them into feedback. [Qui10]
Humans can notice various types of changes happening in the background en-
vironment, such as movement, sound, light, color, change of form or even smell
[Qui10]. Therefore, alongside with being informative, ambient displays can also
function as decorations ﬁlling up various types of spaces.
The following chapters contain details regarding the development of the research
prototype of an interactive system which aims to foster healthy sleep patterns
of potential users. Chapter 2 presents related work within the area of ambient
displays preceded by historical background. Furthermore, the chapter contains
two other subsections in which the author analyses the current understanding
of quantiﬁed self and investigates recent development in sensing. System design
consisting of qualitative and quantitative interviews together with the outcomes
of the implementation is described in Chapter 3. The next part of the document
is devoted to the technical details regarding the implementation of the proto-
==PAGE:23==

type and is followed by a chapter documenting a ﬁeld study conducted on six
participants grouped into three couples.
The thesis contains design and implementation of the research prototype of an
ambient display that visualizes feedback regarding duration of user sleep with
the use of diﬀerent light colors. A study has been conducted with help of six
participants who initially were asked to wear Jawbone bracelets, thanks to which
it was possible to collect the data necessary for the second stage of the study —
a semi-structured interview conducted during the presentation of six diﬀerent
types of visualisation with simultaneous ﬁlling out of Positive And Negative
Aﬀect Scale (PANAS) questionnaires.
This part of the project is followed by evaluation of the correctness of several
research hypotheses which treated as a whole give an answer to a general ques-
tion: what set of attributes provides feedback that can be most informative,
suggestive and eﬀective in terms of inﬂuencing people’s sleeping patterns with
the use of ambient displays. Furthermore, the thesis aims to investigate the
eﬀectiveness of reﬂection as a trigger for behavioral change when it comes to
ambient displays. The next parts of the thesis discuss the outcomes of the study.
The document ends up with conclusions and future work regarding the project.
The following sections of the thesis as a whole explain the novel approach to
mitigate the problem of sleep deprivation by trying to trigger reﬂection on the
past sleep of users based on the light-color feedback produced by the ambient
display prototype. Another contribution to the research on light ambient dis-
plays is investigation on the inﬂuence of the period from which the data are
collected on the aﬀect on users.
The thesis contributes the following: a) the design of an ambient color-based
visualization b) set of the equations that convert accumulated sleep amount
into color-based temporal visualization patterns in the RGB spectrum c) the
implementation of the research prototype utilizing usage of personal tracker and
an interactive light bulb d) a user study to evaluate the positive and negative
aﬀect produced by the system e) insights into the design of future color-based
temporal visualization of sleep patterns .

Chapter 2
Related Work
This chapter aims at presenting related work in the ﬁelds being explored in
this master project, i.e. ambient inﬂuence, quantiﬁed self and sensing. Each of
the examples mentioned in this part of the document will be compared to the
research prototype in order to determine appropriate area for contribution to
the topic of ambient displays acting as inﬂuential devices.
2.1
Ambient displays
As the amount of information surrounding people in everyday life grows rapidly,
there is also a proportionally growing need for such informational systems that
are unobtrusive to potential users. The following subsections explain the history
of ambient displays presenting several projects that have become milestones
when it comes to understanding of this topic in HCI area.
2.1.1
History
One of the earliest and most known examples of an ambient display is the Live
Wire (also known as Dangling String) which has been introduced in 1995 by

Figure 2.1: The Live Wire designed by Natalie Jeremijenko in 1995. [WB97]
Natalie Jeremijenko (in Weiser and Brown [WB97]). As the name suggests, it
was a display in a shape of an eight feet long wire hanging from the ceiling.
The purpose of the wire, which was located in Xerox Palo Alto Research Center,
was to reﬂect the Internet traﬃc using three diﬀerent channels — whirling mo-
tion, sound, and touch. Being installed in a public hallway, the device presented
its feedback unobtrusively. It could be seen and heard by every person passing
it by in the oﬃce. The Live Wire has been considered by Weiser and Brown as
a novel approach to the ﬁeld of ambient inﬂuence.

a suggestion when it was appropriate to start a connection that requires heavy
download or upload of the data.

Ambient displays for triggering behavior change
With a growing number of diﬀerent ambient displays being created, it has been
observed that such installations might have a potential when it comes to impos-
ing a behavioral change on users. This has been thoroughly analyzed in 2010
by Rogers et al. [RHM+10] who created and installed a persuasive ambient
system at Jennie Lee Building located in the Open University (Milton Keynes,
United Kingdom) that was consisting of three independently acting modules —
Follow-the-Lights, The Clouds and The History.

between the entrance to the building and the stairs leading to higher ﬂoors. The
most visible component of the whole system were The Clouds represented by
two groups of spheres moving up and down grouped by colors. The ﬁrst group

Nakajima and Lehdonvirta who created several interfaces for research purposes.

one, which has already been mentioned, but also an immediate one. At any
time users were brushing their teeth, the ﬁsh were responding with dancing and
the aquarium was cleaned oﬀthe algae with a scrub. The study conducted by
the authors has shown emotional connection between users and the installation.
The participants did not want ﬁsh to disappear, that is why they responded
with a desired activity. In consequence, the mirror caused an improvement of
habits among the users as the average time spent on brushing daily increased
visibly.

2.1.3
Persuasion vs reﬂection
All previously mentioned examples of ambient displays presented feedback in
the real-time, trying to persuade potential users to change some aspect of their
behavior according to the assumption stated in the design process. Apart from
concerns regarding the selection of a proper feedback type to address the prob-
==PAGE:31==
2.1 Ambient displays
11
lem that is supposed to be tackled by a given ambient system, more important
is the observation that persuasive systems tend to focus on narrowing down the
complex problem set in a broader context into a smaller frame [BHP+12]. As
a consequence, the system that is supposed to solve a smaller part of a wider
problem situated in an artiﬁcially constructed environment might be susceptible
to failure when it is placed in the real world.
Brynjarsdottir et al. [BHP+12] have reviewed 36 diﬀerent papers that described
persuasive systems pertaining to behavioral change. They have pointed out that
very little number of the papers provided evidence regarding long-term study
that could conﬁrm an eﬀective long-lasting change of behavior. Therefore, it is
unclear how potential outer factors can have an inﬂuence on the eﬀect that has
been achieved in an isolated framed environment. Furthermore, the majority of
reviewed papers focused on increasing users’ awareness rather than aiming at
the improvement of a given habit or encouraging to take up a desired activity
[BHP+12].
Another critical comment on persuasive systems stated by Brynjarsdottir et al.
[BHP+12] is that they are often designed to act in an authoritarian way by
judging which user activity is desired and which is not. This kind of approach
might be problematic for varying local environments, where the understanding
of sustainability might be diﬀerent and dependent on various factors [BHP+12].
It is important to notice that real-time persuasion does not work in every situ-
ation. Sleep-related issues are examples of problems which are not possible to
be mitigated with immediate feedback, as potential users of the system would
not be aware of it due to their limited consciousness. Therefore, the information
conveyed by the system in such case should be based on an alternative approach
[BHP+12].
One of the suggestions proposed by Brynjarsdottir et al.
is to use reﬂec-
tion rather than persuasion as a means of triggering behavioral change. Such
methodology could help overcome the problem of narrow-framing by resigning
from deﬁnition of desired behavior in favor of provoking users to think over what
actually should be improved in order to beneﬁt in a long term. This proposal has
been followed by an introduction of another indication — shifting from behavior
to practice, which means that although the sustainability of eﬀect produced by
a given system is visible within the framed reality, it should not be analyzed
in isolation, but rather placed in a broader context including e.g. sociocultural
conditions.
In comparison to persuasion, research about reﬂection in HCI seems to be at a
relatively early stage. This area has been explored in the context of amateur
runners. In 2015, Knaving et al. conducted a two-year complex study consist-

2.1.4
Understanding light patterns
With the growing number of ambient light displays, there appeared a need to
derive design guidelines that can be followed during development of future de-
vices. Matviienko et al. proposed a categorization of ambient light displays into
four information classes: progress, status, spatial and notiﬁcation [MCM+15].
Progress information class, which is supposed to assume goal reachability as a
purpose, groups such displays whose feedback presents a relative point on the
path to achieve the desired aim by continuous value change in both directions —
increase or decrease. When it comes to Status information class, it deﬁnes such
ambient light displays that, opposed to progress class, show the absolute value
indicating current state not considering goal achievement. Spatial information
class categorizes systems that direct users to speciﬁc places or point-of-interests.
The last information class, Notiﬁcation, includes displays attracting primary at-
tention of potential users.
At the moment of deﬁnition check, it appears that Spatial and Notiﬁcation
classes aim at diﬀerent scopes of feedback. Hence, they have been eliminated
from my considerations at the beginning of the research prototype design. Hav-
==PAGE:34==
14

ing come to the comparison of remaining classes, which are Progress versus
Status classes, my conclusion was that the research prototype could be classi-
ﬁed as either the ﬁrst mentioned group or the second one, depending on the
circumstances.
If feedback regarding the user’s sleep is scoped out to the last night only, then,
in my opinion, the notion of a goal reachability vanishes as the user is presented
with diﬀerent feedback every single day. For such a condition, it can be assumed
that the system could belong to the Status information class. The situation
might change when the time span of visualizing feedback based is extended
to more consecutive days. Although the user might still be presented with new
feedback once he gets up in the morning, the change is relative as the system still
takes into account those days that have been considered previously. Therefore,
what I reckoned was that if the research prototype visualized feedback based
on the information gathered from a set of days, then it could be labeled as a
display belonging to the Progress information class.
Matviienko et al. [MCM+15] investigated various light patterns with respect to
speciﬁc information classes. During their study consisting of two stages includ-
ing searching for appropriate categorization of patterns into speciﬁc information
classes and further evaluation of them depending on various scenarios, major-
ity of participants identiﬁed green color as positive information and red as a
negative one. Furthermore, another interesting observation was that red color
stopped being associated as bad information if it was opposed to a color diﬀer-
ent than green, e.g. blue. In such scenario, most participants started to think
of a red color in terms of its warmth.
Considering light patterns with respect to sleep, one must remember that se-

inﬂuence of speciﬁc color on humans’ mood. In 2013, Randy Nelson and Tracy
Bedrosian conducted a comprehensive study during which they exposed mice to
changing colors light [BN13]. One of the conclusions was that the blue light was
the worst in terms of mood-related aspects, while red light reduced occurrences
of depressive-like symptoms.

between) and red-blue light pattern into further considerations while designing
and implementing the research prototype in order to further investigate the cor-

was to include not only such light patterns that are suggested as eﬀective e.g.
in terms of presenting a progress, but also a pattern that contained a light color
not recommended to be used in the sleeping room. The reason behind this de-

2.2
The Quantiﬁed Self
Recent development of electronic and computational technologies combined with
exponential growth of data storage and data processing revealed new opportuni-
ties in several aspects of lives. At present, people are able to monitor their body
processes or activities that (in the best-case scenario) could be measured with
complex dedicated equipments. In consequence, a global movement called Quan-
tiﬁed Self has appeared, gathering people interested in collection of personal
data from various types of mobile devices to deepen their knowledge about ev-
eryday activities and physiological processes [Lee13]. The term has been coined
by Gary Wolf and Kevin Kelly in reference to a local group of users and tool
constructors exploring the topic of data collection automation.
Three years
later the movement became widely known thanks to relevant articles published
in the American press (Wired Magazine and New York Times), followed by a
TED talk given by Gary Wolf in June 2010 [Wol10].
The Quantiﬁed Self movement acts within a speciﬁc area of human-computer
interaction research called personal informatics, which initially has been deﬁned
as a class of systems that enable users to gather personal data regarding diﬀerent
aspects, so they can reﬂect on and evaluate it from a time perspective [LDF10].
One year later Li et. al. [LDF11] extended this deﬁnition by describing personal
informatics not as a mere classiﬁcation of speciﬁc systems but as an activity that
can be tracked, quantiﬁed, and reﬂected on in order to better understand the
user’s behavior.
The number of activities or options oﬀered by various devices is so high that it
can confuse users striving to focus on too many diﬀerent issues at once. This
can result in ceasing to use the device due to the loss of interest or having
signiﬁcant problems with analyzing data provided in various formats [CLL+14].
This can be a valid argument for a need to frame a speciﬁc problem by correctly
choosing what should be measured in order to achieve relevant and meaningful
information.
It has been observed that personal informatics systems might have a strong
potential in changing particular user behavior since they produce data trigger-
ing reﬂection. However, there exist numerous diﬀerent approaches to reﬂective
learning, which makes it diﬃcult to create a uniﬁed framework having common
features for the majority of techniques.

The problem has been tackled by Rivera-Pelayo et al. [RPZMB12] who based
their proposal on the reﬂection process model built by Boud et al. which takes
into account not only all stages of cognitive process but it is also generic and
applicable to various cases (Fig. 2.7). The reﬂection process proposed by Boud
et al. consists of three stages. The ﬁrst one are experiences such as behaviors,
ideas and feelings at the time of an experiment, study or during system usage.
This stage is followed by the reﬂective process which is a time for returning to
the previously sensed experience along with utilization of positive feelings and
removal of uncomfortable ones. The reﬂective process also involves repeated
evaluation process of the experience. The last phase of the process are activities
grouped within a set labeled as outcomes. At this moment, users should look at
the experience from a diﬀerent perspective, having some more information after
the reﬂective process. The outcomes of the process also involve a change in the
studied behavior and, what is also important, readiness for its application that
is tightly linked to a commitment to acting.
The framework proposed by Rivera-Pelayo consists of three distinguishable as-
pects: tracking cues, triggering, recalling and revisiting experiences. When it
comes to the ﬁrst dimension, it can be done using diﬀerent types of sensors;
it can also target diﬀerent aspects of life or activities, and can be performed
for various purposes. One of the tracked aspects that was identiﬁed by Rivera-
Pelayo et al. are physiological data that also include information concerning
sleep. Considering triggering, two diﬀerent types have been recognized: active,
where the system explicitly notiﬁes the user, and passive, where it unobtrusively
provides data. Passive triggering is in line with the nature of ambient displays
as both pertain to presenting information and omit to attract the user’s primary
attention.
Although the issues regarding persuasion have been raised and alternative ways,
such as reﬂection, to trigger behavior change have been proposed there are still

Is it

2.2.1
Recent developments in sensing
It can be observed that despite the growing number of tracking devices, the
users’ needs together with their usage patterns still are not given primary focus,
which leads to a situation in which the devices fail to satisfy the users’ needs
instead of being cut out for them. In consequence, dissatisﬁed users tend to pick
the devices irregularly and with lower frequency, to end up with totally losing
their interest in activity trackers after approximately six months of usage [GS17].
At this point it should also be noted that the investigation over the needs of
users with respect to sleeping patterns is also limited and often neglected in HCI
literature [CCWK11].
Ledger and McCaﬀrey stated that in order to maintain a long-term engagement,
three factors should be considered: habit formation, social motivation, and goal
reinforcement [LM16]. In terms of the ﬁrstly mentioned factor, the best ap-
proaches to maintain user engagement adhere to features of the habit loop, that
are: cue, behavior and reward, rather than presentation of the personal data
that are collected by sensor. Although social connections can be a powerful
factor driving the quality of behavior change, this factor of long-term engage-
ment should be thoroughly investigated while constructing a system returning
feedback, as sleep is an intimate aspect of life, hence the related data might
reveal information that the users might not want to publish [Di114].
According to Ledger and McCaﬀrey, it is also vital to ensure that the user is able
to feel the progress being done in order to achieve a long-term goal while using
a system aiming at achievement of engagement sustainability. With properly
selected feedback it is possible to make people achieve next steps or milestones
on their path to the desired ﬁnish [LM16]. Having embedded this statement
into the context of sleep, a question arises — can feedback from just one sleep
event be useful in terms of progress evaluation? If not, then how long should be
the period of investigation to become an informational basis for visualization of
feedback that could support goal reinforcement?
Those questions have been taken into consideration while designing and con-

2.4
Summary
Summarizing literature review done in the previous section of this chapter, sev-
eral decisions aﬀecting further design of the research prototype have been made.
Having pointed out issues related with persuasion as a technique of triggering
behavioral change, my decision was to base the feedback produced by the pro-
totype on Rivera-Pelayo et al. framework that originates from reﬂection process
model speciﬁed by Boud et al. Another outcome of related work analysis was
to consider pairs of participants for the purpose of study in order to investigate
the aspect of togetherness. Studies conducted by Bedrosian and Nelson, and
Matviienko et al. were the point of departure for choosing light patterns for
the following design consideration in terms of feedback visualization. Further-
more, based on three aspects of long-term engagement introduced by Ledger
and McCaﬀrey, I have decided to investigate whether period over which data
are acquired is important in terms of feedback informativeness and triggering
behavioral change.
All the decisions mentioned above have been taken into
account in the design phase which is documented in Chapter 3.

Chapter 3
Design
The purpose of this chapter is to present a step-by-step design process ran for
the purpose of creating a research prototype that is one of the outcomes of the
master project. The aim of the prototype was to visualize light feedback to the
user based on the amount of sleep he or she took in the previous day or over
the last week.
The main inspiration while thinking over design process is the four-staged design
cycle proposed for HCI systems by Harper et al. [HRRS08] that consisted of
the following phases: research, study, design, build. Therefore, I have started
the process by trying to deeply understand the problem of sleep deprivation
with respect to various aspects of life — both private and professional ones.
The design outcomes that have been taken into account in the implementation
phase being documented in Chapter 4 are the result of literature review (which
is presented in Chapter 2) combined with conducting a set of interviews with
twelve people who expressed their motivation to improve their sleeping habits.
In the following sections, the details regarding the step-by-step process of gath-
ering valuable guidelines from the interviews are presented. Section 3.1 outlines

The aim of the semi-structured interviews conducted with twelve volunteers
was to gather information regarding the sleeping habits in order to formulate
guidelines for the system design and confront them with the knowledge gained
at the stage of literature review.
3.1.1
Participant proﬁle
My decision was to focus on participants that share common features — when
choosing potential candidates for the interview I was interested in people who
were employed and led an active lifestyle, had multiple responsibilities, hobbies
or other non-work activities. Simultaneously, the diﬀerentiating features of my
interviewees were: profession, education, age and gender.
The reason for such desired proﬁle was my conviction that people who are
active on many dimensions and, in consequence, have a tight schedule, need
to thoroughly organize their day plans in order to have time for every activity.
Therefore, I wanted to investigate my interlocutors’ priorities and the place of
sleep in their hierarchy of life aspects.
At ﬁrst, each of interviewees was given a background questionnaire containing
general questions about age, gender, education, work type, amount of working
hours. The questionnaires contained also two additional Likert questions about
participants’ lifestyles and stressfulness of their work conditions. The partici-
pants were provided a 1-7 scale, where 1 represented a strongly positive answer,
whereas 7 — a strongly negative one.
There are many opinions as to the optimal amount of choices representing re-
sponses in Likert-scale questions. In 2011, Pearson performed a comprehensive
review of various approaches in terms of his research regarding 21-point Likert
scale [Pea11]. What can be concluded from his paper is that there is not a
single answer to the question which scale width is the most appropriate one,
because numerous papers in which the problem was addressed to seem to con-
tradict each other. Diefenbach et. al [DWO93] investigated scales in terms of
health hazard risk, which is a context related to questions about lifestyle and

work stress included in my questionnaire. In his research, Diefenbach concluded
that seven-point scale is the most eﬀective one. The scales of higher order were
not as accurate as the seven-point one becoming more diﬃcult to use. On the
other hand, ﬁve-point scale produced slightly worse results on all criteria taken
into account by Diefenbach et. al and was judged signiﬁcantly poorer when it
comes to subjective comments of participants. Based on this, my decision was
to choose seven-point scale as, in my opinion, it provides opportunity to give a
more precise response to the stated question than the ﬁve-point one.

3.4.1
Research prototype design
Each green note that has been chosen for ﬁnal considerations in terms of propos-
ing the idea for the research prototype contained a grain of usefulness. The BJ6
note mentioned using light sensor and wearable device as a combination of the
inputs for the potential system.
However, there are arguments against light
sensing as a source of information for the prototype. The ﬁrst one is that the
daylight period is dependent on the year season. Furthermore, it does not solve
the problem in the situation where a user goes to bed at the right hour, but
still sleeps for short amount of time waking up very early in the morning or, on
contrary, sleeps too long. Finally, light intensity is a piece of information that
could be useful only at the phase before going to sleep. Therefore, reﬂection as
a method of triggering a behavior change does not ﬁt in this context. Based on
all mentioned arguments, my decision was to drop the idea of light sensor and
put a stronger focus on a wearable device, that could be able to track the user
sleep returning its duration after it was completed.
The BJ6 / J2 note suggested a feedback type that could be used to visualize
the amount of sleep which is supposed to be collected using a wearable device
mentioned in BJ6 green note — changing of light colors. The conﬁgurations
of feedback color types that were taken into account for the development of
the research prototype have been already mentioned in Section 2.4 and are
based on the research done by Matviienko et al. [MCM+15], and Bedrosian and
Nelson [BN13] described in section 2.1.4. When it comes to colors’ friendliness
depending on user pulse rate, that part of the idea does not correspond to the
concept of using reﬂection rather than persuasion as a behavior change.
Finally, the A13 gave a valuable proposal what should be the source of the
visualization — a lamp that could present the colors based on the input coming
from a wearable device regarding the amount of sleep the user had last night.
Such a prototype conforms to the framework proposed by Rivera-Pelayo et al.
[RPZMB12] which consisted of three aspects: tracking cues, triggering and re-
visiting the experience. The ﬁrst would be realized by collection of the data
coming from the sport tracker into the application that would interpret it and
send a relevant information regarding the feedback type to the output part of
the system — an interactive light-bulb mounted plugged into a lamp.

4.1.1
Sleep tracker
As mentioned at the beginning of Section 2.2, the growth of the Quantiﬁed Self
community and development of personal informatics research would have been
much more diﬃcult without a contribution to the area of sensing. Over the
last years numerous devices that provide various types of information on a wide
range of life aspects have been created and studied. Furthermore, the size of
sensing devices becomes smaller, so more wearable sensors are being introduced
with time making sensing technologies present in our everyday lives.
When it comes to sleep, it can be tracked using sensors or manual input and there
already exist such devices that can log information about speciﬁc stages of sleep
including light, REM and deep phases [CCWK11]. One of the most popular
products that can monitor sleep are wearable devices: FitBit [Fit], Jawbone
UP [Jawa], or SleepCycle [Sleb] [ECB+14] which tracks body movement in bed.
The ﬁrst two mentioned devices track a wide range of other diﬀerent activities,
whereas the SleepCycle is strictly focused on a sleep only.
All of them use
accelerometers in order to collect the relevant data. Additionally the SleepCycle
gathers information by analyzing sound in order to identify speciﬁc sleep states.
There are such devices on the market that do not require any kind of a physical
contact in order to collect sleep data. An example of such system is S+ by
ResMed [SPl]. This solution is based on a radio frequency technology and it
monitors the breathing and movements of the user while having sleep. It can
also analyze the bedroom environment — light intensity, noise and temperature.
Some of the available sleep monitoring devices collect the data thanks to the
sensing thin stripe being placed under the bed sheet. The examples of solutions
in real life are Beddit [Bed] and Sleepace RestOn [Slea]. There is a risk however,
that those devices might not produce accurate sleep information if the user sleeps
in bed with another person as his/her movement can aﬀect the data coming from
sensors.
Although there are numerous sleep tracking devices introduced, only a subset of
them provide a RESTful API through which it is possible to integrate with it and
obtain a data that can be further processed. FitBit, Jawbone and Beddit provide
well-documented open APIs for third party developers that enable to read all
possible kinds of data being produced by those systems. At the opposite side
are Beddit and Sleepace RestOn and SleepCycle that do not present an interface
which can be accessed by the external application.

track activity and sleep. The reason behind this decision is that although in this

The API (Application Programming Interface) provided by Jawbone enables to

account at the oﬃcial Jawbone website [Jawc] and then register the application
that is going to be created. The ﬁnal result of this action should be obtaining the
client ID accompanied by the application secret, that are necessary to initiate
the OAuth2 handshake.

cation of several URLs that are required for establishing a connection:

Chapter 5
Evaluation
The purpose of this chapter is to present the full documentation of the study
whose aim was to verify the correctness of several research hypotheses that
treated as a whole constitute an answer to a general question of this study – what
set of attributes forms a feedback that can be most informative, suggestive and
eﬀective in terms of inﬂuencing people’s sleeping patterns with use of ambient
displays.
5.1
Hypotheses
The purpose of this section is to present research hypotheses which have been
veriﬁed in research prototype evaluation:
Hypothesis H1: The red-yellow-green visualization based on the sleep data
from one week causes the least negative aﬀect of perception among users.
Hypothesis H2: Blue color as a positive feedback on the amount of sleep
over the week is distressing to the users.
==PAGE:86==
66
Evaluation
The reason for statement of H1 is a conviction that red-yellow-green setting is
the widely known color pattern of presenting information where green means
positive information and red — the negative one. Considering a one week of
sleep data acquisition creates the space for compensation of the days in which
the amount of sleep was insuﬃcient. My assumptions are based on Matviienko
et al. study which revealed that the red-yellow-green color information setting
is appropriate for Progress information class. [MCM+15]
When it comes to the H2 hypothesis, I have based it on the research of Bedrosian
and Nelson [BN13], outcomes of which revealed that blue color causes negative
eﬀect on mood-related measures.
Thus, my suspicion is that the blue color
repeated over the week as a positive feedback might distress potential users.

5.3
Measures
Because the relevant study requires inter alia a quantitative analysis after it was
conducted, one of the task was to choose the evaluation method that can be
relevant in terms of the study and is based on the use of questionnaire, because
the study focuses on subjective feelings of each participant. The All About UX
(www.allaboutux.org) [All] webpage provides a detailed explanation on wide
range of various evaluation methods. In order to ﬁnd the most appropriate one,
in my opinion, I started the elimination process in an iterative way.
==PAGE:88==
68
Evaluation
During the ﬁrst iteration I have eliminated those methods that are not based
on questionnaire.
Out of 20 remaining evaluation methods that passed the
ﬁrst round, 12 have been not been considered anymore after the second phase.
At this stage, I have eliminated those methods that are technology-speciﬁc or
not relevant for evaluation of functional prototypes and, thus, are not applica-
ble for the purpose of this study. After two iterations, only eight evaluations
were left. Because the round robin comparison could take excessive amount of
time, my decision was to choose the evaluation method arranging them in the
single-elimination "tournament". The pairs have been created by placing all the
remaining methods in alphabetical order and taking a top-bottom approach.
After the third stage of elimination, four methods have been eliminated —
Geneva Emotion Wheel, SUMI, Product Semantics Analysis and QSA GQM
questionnaires. Mostly the reason behind such a choice was that those method,
in my opinion, required relatively too high amount of eﬀort comparing it with
the scale of the study. The outcome of the fourth iteration was elimination of
Product Semantic Analysis and QSA GSM questionnaires as they appeared to
be used for marketing and commercial purposes rather than for the research
ones.
My ﬁnal choice was the Positive and Negative Aﬀect Scale (PANAS)
questionnaire although Hedonic Utility scale seemed similarly reliable and valid
when it comes to performance in various tests. The ﬁnal argument in favor of
PANAS was that it was initially used in clinical settings which is more related to
the problem of sleep than marketing research from which Hedonic Utility scale
originates. [WCT88, VSG03]
The Positive And Negative Aﬀect Scale questionnaire contains a set of positive
and negative adjectives with which it is possible to evaluate mood of the user
being put in a speciﬁc condition. The evaluation is done with use of ﬁve-point
Likert scale. Once the questionnaire is ﬁlled out, the ﬁnal step in its scope is to
calculate overall positive and negative scores by suming up scores regarding all
positive and negative adjectives respectively. [WCT88]
My inspiration, when it comes to use of a speciﬁc PANAS questionnaire instance,
was the version introduced in the Therapist’s Guide to Positive Psychological
Interventions book by Magyar-Moe [MM09] which is based on the questionnaire
from an Appendix of the article written by Watson and Tellegen [WCT88].

Chapter 6
Discussion
The main outcome of the study is that there is a signiﬁcant inﬂuence of light
feedback type and period of data acquisition on the overall negative aﬀect on
users’ perception. The red-yellow-green light feedback on a weekly basis (Con-
dition RYG-W) has been identiﬁed as the one which caused the least overall
negative aﬀect on users. The reason for such a ﬁnding might be the presence of
in-between states that might increase the understanding of the feedback. This
was conﬁrmed by the qualitative analysis which revealed several comments on
the need of a yellow color to avoid being presented with negative feedback even
after small insuﬃciency in sleep. It is also in line with comments regarding
weekly feedback where part of the users admitted that one worse night in terms
of sleep amount still could be masked by the rest of the nights of the seven-day
period. Therefore, all the facts stated above suggest that the H1 hypothesis of
the study may be true.
The weekly red-blue feedback (RB-W) seems to be the visualization that caused
a lot of controversies and contrary opinions. The study revealed signiﬁcant dif-
ference between this condition and conditions labeled as RB-D and RG-D in
terms of the Distressed measure. Paradoxically, the reason for such an outcome
might be the fact that it was diﬃcult for most of the users to understand the
message behind the blue color. Hence, the red-blue feedback on weekly amount
of sleep seemed not to represent an amount of sleep to users. My understanding
of the situation is that this condition did not trigger any emotion due to ambigu-
==PAGE:108==
88
Discussion
ity of the information. That could be the reason why the users did not perceive
that conﬁguration as distressing them in general. Another possible reason could
be the fact that the red color might not have been necessarily treated as nega-
tive in conﬁguration with blue as a positive one, despite unanimous opinion on
red light of all participants who identiﬁed this color as a symbol of bad infor-
mation. Therefore, even if the system produced negative feedback it might not
have been understood as such. Such statement could be justiﬁed by the research
of Matviienko et. al whose study revealed that red means negative information
only when green is the representation of the opposite message. Therefore, based
on the outcome of the study the H2 hypothesis was rejected.
In terms of the Scared measure, a signiﬁcant inﬂuence of light type feedback
combined with period of data acquisition has been also observed. Furthermore
a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between RB-D and RG-W has been revealed in this
matter with RG-W having the lowest least mean square for the Scared measure.
The reason behind this outcome might be again the fact that the blue as a
representation of daily positive feedback might mislead users, as a visible part
of them did not understand the message of the color during the study. When
it comes to weekly red-green feedback, it has a potential to reduce the risk of
watching the negative feedback regarding the amount of sleep, as the user can
compensate the shortage and have a control over the status during the week. In
that case the bulb would show the green light which has been identiﬁed by users
as a strong meaning of positive information.
Such feedback could be rather
motivational than scary.
Another interesting outcome from the study is that some of the users preferred
not to see the negative feedback at all as they found it a root cause of stress which
adversely aﬀects the sleep quality. The other participant perceived a red light
as a strong motivational alert that in their opinion would cause them to improve
their amount of sleep in order to prevent from watching negative feedback again.
Therefore, further studies should also take into account a condition where only
the positive part of the feedback is presented whereas for the negative scenario
the prototype would not project any kind of light feedback.
Considering the period of sleep data acquisition, the study participants ad-
mitted that they found the seven-day feedback useful, however some of them
simultaneously stated that they could be stressed and thus not motivated, as
the information about the past week is able to show the sleep trend as opposed
to the daily information which can be instantly improved in the next night. On
the other hand, the positive information regarding the seven-day period seemed
to be a strong motivation for users to keep up the regularity of sleep.
Discussing informational aspect of the light feedback, majority of users admitted
that the colors are proper indicators of progress or status of their sleep. During
==PAGE:109==
89
the study I was able to note down several remarks which conﬁrmed that the
prototype feedback reﬂected the actual situation from a certain night. Further-
more, the prototype triggered reﬂection process in a group of users regarding
their previous sleep during the study. This, in my opinion, is a solid premise
that the system has a potential to encourage people to reﬂect on their past sleep
activities or habits.
On the other hand, however, a visible group of users mentioned their need to
see the root causes of their feedback arguing that light color is an insuﬃcient
information, when it comes to diversity of sleep aspects. This could be a strong
argument for combining the research prototype with the interface that could
allow users to access more details regarding the sleep. In other words, light
ambient displays might have a potential as a part of a complex system being
the initial catalyst of refection process triggered by the user who sees the light
feedback.
The possibility of maintaining potential behavior change in long term cannot be
assessed based on the study. However, there is a concern that the system might
not serve users after the longer period. The reason behind such a statement are
comments I have collected from the participants of the study who did not believe
in sustainability of their behavioral change.
In my opinion, such comments
have been stated due to the fact that according to my observations most of the
participants treated the prototype as a gadget rather than as a useful device that
can help in everyday life. Furthermore, the prototype required regular wearing
of the Jawbone bracelet in order to collect the data which might be a tedious
routine in a long term causing a drop of interest on the system. Therefore, it is
necessary to carefully think of the system setup and interaction design, provided
that the further research on the prototype will be continued.
In order to validate the eﬀectiveness of the research prototype, more exten-
sive study should be performed, as the results of quantitative analysis indicate
statistical insigniﬁcance for majority of PANAS measures including all positive
ones.
There exist also several user-experience issues regarding the research prototype
that could be improved in the future. From the user perspective, the choice of
colors’ depth and light intensity should be revisited as during the study several
comments that the bulb produced a feedback that might be tiring to the eyes
after some time have been stated. The prototype could also be parametrized in
terms of the desired amount of sleep in order to adjust its performance to the
need of the speciﬁc user.

6.1
Limitations
The study was limited due to several factors. First of all, I have managed to
encourage only six people to accept my invitation to take part in the study.
Secondly, the limited set of Jawbone bracelets was also an inﬂuential factor.
Furthermore, one of the users reported technical issues with one of the bracelets,
which imposed a need for the replacement.
At the time of writing the document, however, the seventh person decided to
take part in the study collecting the sleep data based on which I will present
several feedback modes with use of the research prototype to him. I believe that
the larger scale of similar study like mine could provide stronger evidences and
more conclusions that would be signiﬁcant in terms of the topic of light ambient
displays aiming at mitigation of sleep deprivation problems.

Future work
Future work regarding this project will be focused on diﬀerent aspects of light
ambient displays addressing the issue of sleep deprivation. First of all, the re-
search prototype requires additional deployment conﬁguration in order to make
it easy to install at homes. I am also aiming at creation of the Dev Tools mod-
ule that could enable the developer to adjust several light settings for the need
of users such as e.g. intensity, hue and brightness, by using GUI rather than
sending raw HTTP request to speciﬁc endpoints. The plan is to deploy the

transforming the system into being fully autonomous.
Apart from aiming at higher amount of participants for the user study, my goal
is to follow my investigations by conducting also the in-the-wild study once the
prototype is autonomous in order to verify its eﬀectiveness, informativeness and
aﬀect on user in the real environment. This should imposed another extension
of the research prototype so it could log the statistical data that could be used
in the statistical analysis of the study.
I believe that still on-going user studies can bring more guidelines about the
place where the research prototype could be installed so it would be unobtrusive
from the user perspective.

Chapter 7
Conclusions
The thesis presented the design of an ambient color-based visualization including
derivation of equations converting accumulated amount of sleep to color-based
temporal visualization pattern in RGB format. The design is followed by the
implementation of the research prototype utilizing usage of personal tracker
and an interactive light bulb, and a user study to evaluate the positive and
negative aﬀect produced by the system. The document explains the contribution
to the research on light ambient displays by comparison with several systems
mentioned in the Related Work that have been introduced into the real world
and presents insights into the design of future color-based temporal visualization
of sleep patterns.
The novel approach of the research prototype is providing the color-based feed-
back that informs about the amount of time spent on sleep in the past and thus
might cause the users reﬂect on their sleep-related activities. The information
can be based on the data input regarding one night or one week. I believe that
my thesis might be a reference for further examination of the research on light
ambient displays in terms of sleep deprivation problem.
The ﬁeld study was conducted with help from six participants, majority of whom
expressed their will to improve their sleeping patterns in the future. The results
obtained in this process have been analyzed statistically and qualitatively.

The analysis has shown that the visualization types had a signiﬁcant inﬂuence
on users’ aﬀect in terms of being distressed, scared and cumulative negative

the in-wild-study which aim would be to verify the eﬀectiveness of the system in
the real environment. I hope that my work will be a small step in understanding
of the design of the technologies that support healthy active living and contribute
to the wellbeing of the society.
