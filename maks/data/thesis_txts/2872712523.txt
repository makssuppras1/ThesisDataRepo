Abstract
Denmark has set some ambitious goals for the development of renewable en-
ergy projects before 2030 to meet their climate goals. With this there is a big
focus on land based solar and wind energy. However, the expansion of these
technologies faces a plethora of hurdles regarding economy, regulatory, environ-
mental and social challenges. This thesis investigates some of the key barriers
to the development of green energy in Denmark through literature review, stake-
holder interviews, as well as a case study of the project ”Fjaldenen Sol og Vind”
from Momentum Energy Group’s portfolio, to see how these challenges affect the
overall possibility of finishing such projects, and how difficult it is becoming with
new regulation. Through interviews with relevant parties, there has been identi-
fied critical issues such as unpredictability of municipal approval, financial burden
with regards to grid connection, as well as local opposition and rigidity of the EU
habitat directive. The study further investigates how implementation of national
policies varies from municipality to municipality and how a lack of a streamlined
planning process delays projects and increase the uncertainty for developers and
investors. By comparing key project costs from 2024 to 2025 for ”Fjaldene Sol og
Vind,” and examining additional barriers such as newly introduced bat protection
regulations, this thesis demonstrates that the current trajectory is unsustainable.
Without significant reforms, Denmark will fall short of its target to quadruple re-
newable energy capacity by 2030.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Denmark has long been considered a frontrunner in the global energy transition.
With strong political ambitions, the country has set out to achieve a fourfold in-
crease in land-based wind and solar energy production by 2030. This expansion
is seen as critical for meeting national climate targets, reducing fossil fuel depen-
dency, and enabling broader electrification of the economy.
However, despite this strong ambition, the development of land-based renewable
energy projects, particularly wind and solar, has encountered growing resistance
and planning bottlenecks. A combination of regulatory complexity, grid capacity
limitations, municipal approval delays, and local opposition has slowed project
implementation, calling into question whether the 2030 goals are achievable under
the current system.
1.2
Motivation
This thesis is motivated by the growing disconnect between Denmark’s ambitious
renewable energy targets and the increasingly complex realities of project devel-
opment on the ground. Although political goals and policy frameworks are evolv-
ing in favor of accelerating the green transition, many developers experience the
opposite in practice, rising costs, regulatory burdens, and growing uncertainty as
major challenges.
Another persistent challenges is the issue of local acceptance. While support for
renewable energy remains strong at the national level, gaining approval at the
local level has become more difficult. Public resistance, and opposition to both
wind turbines and solar installations contribute to project stagnation, adding time
and financial risk for developers.
This raises a broader economic and policy paradox: how can Denmark acceler-
ate renewable energy deployment while simultaneously increasing the financial
burden on those trying to build it? As former U.S. President Ronald Reagan once
famously remarked:
“When you tax something, you get less of it, and when you subsidize
something, you get more of it.”
– Ronald Reagan, June 17, 1988
In the Danish context, this principle appears to be inverted. While there is clear
political intent to expand renewable energy, the cumulative effect of rising grid
fees, compensation schemes, etc. may have the unintended consequence of
slowing down exactly the kind of projects Denmark is aiming to promote.

1.3
Research Question
To better understand this contradiction, this thesis investigates the barriers to
large-scale land-based renewable energy development in Denmark and explores
how these can be mitigated. It is guided by the following main research question:
Is a fourfold increase in renewable energy in Denmark by 2030 realistic?
Sub-questions include:
• What are the key barriers developers face in the planning and permitting
process?
• How does changes in compensations schemes and grid costs influence
project feasibility?
• What role does local opposition play?
• What can be done to help accelerate the deployment of renewable energy
in the future?
1.4
Introduction To This Report
The thesis is structured as follows:
• Chapter 2 describes the methodology and scope of the research. It out-
lines the methods used in the thesis, including literature review, qualitative
interviews, field observation, and webinars.
• Chapter 3 outlines the current state of renewable energy in Denmark and
the major policy goals. It provides historical context for Denmark’s energy
transition and presents national targets for 2030.
• Chapter 4 presents insights from interviews with key stakeholders. These
include developers, municipal planners, and industry representatives. The
chapter identifies recurring themes that will later be analyzed further.
• Chapter 5 explores economic barriers, including support schemes and grid
connection costs. It analyzes how evolving tariff structures as well as in-
creasing compensation schemes, impact the price of a project.
• Chapter 6 analyzes local opposition and public acceptance mechanisms. It
looks at the dynamics of NIMBYism and considers why it is not a useful term,
as well as assessing role of compensation schemes and local ownership
models in addressing resistance.
• Chapter 7 reviews potential acceleration strategies such as streamlining
municipal approval processes, adapting the EU habitat directive, and im-
proving EIA procedures could reduce bottlenecks.
• Chapter 8 provides a case study to illustrate how these challenges affect a
real-world project. The “Fjaldene Sol og Vind” project serves as a concrete
example of how local concerns, and shifting policy incentives converge in
practice. The chapter includes technical and economic analysis to quantify
the project’s evolution over time.

1.5
Scope
This thesis focuses specifically on land-based wind and solar energy projects
in Denmark, and does not cover offshore wind or other renewable technologies
such as biomass or hydrogen. While some findings may be relevant in a broader
context, the analysis is limited to the Danish policy context and draws primarily on
national-level legislation, municipal planning practices, and real-life implementa-
tion challenges faced by developers.
The case study of ”Fjaldene Sol og Vind” is used to explore these barriers in more
detail. It is not intended to be representative of all projects in Denmark, but
it provides a concrete example of how regulation, economic changes, and local
reactions interact in a real project. The thesis does not aim to deliver a full techni-
cal feasibility study, but rather examines the project through the lens of planning,
policy, and social acceptance.

Methodology
Literature Review
The literature review in this thesis draws on two main types of sources. For the
conceptual and theoretical foundation, regarding social acceptance and NIMBY
(Not In My Back Yard) peer-reviewed academic articles were used. These pro-
vided validated frameworks and empirical studies to understand how local oppo-
sition to renewable energy projects emerges and evolves.
In addition to academic literature, this thesis also draws on key policy and in-
dustry reports to understand the current efforts being made to support renewable
energy expansion in Denmark. Before initiating interviews and the case study
analysis, reports such as “Mere sol og vind på land”[1] from NEKST (Nationalt
Energi-Krisestab) were reviewed. NEKST is a Danish government task force es-
tablished to help accelerate renewable energy. It brings together actors from min-
istries, agencies, municipalities, and the energy industry to identify and remove
barriers in the planning and permitting process for wind and solar projects.
In contrast, for the regulatory and economic context of wind and solar development
in Denmark, the review included official reports, policy documents, and technical
publications from relevant institutions such as NEKST, Energinet, and the Dan-
ish Energy Agency. Additionally, relevant national legislation (e.g., VE-loven and
Planloven) was reviewed to understand the planning and approval procedures
that directly impact project development.
Interviews
To understand the key barriers slowing down the development of renewable en-
ergy projects in Denmark, a series of semi-structured interviews were conducted
during the initial research phase of this thesis. The aim was to capture a diverse
set of perspectives from stakeholders directly involved in planning, policy, and
implementation. Semi-structured interviews were chosen as the primary method
of data collection because they offer a balance between structure and flexibility,
which was essential given the exploratory nature of this research. At the outset,
the full extent of the challenges and dynamics within renewable energy project
development in Denmark was not entirely known. Using a highly structured inter-
view format would have risked overlooking important insights that only emerged
during open conversation. Instead, a semi-structured approach allowed for a set
of guiding questions while enabling the conversation to evolve naturally [2]. In-
terviews were held with project developers from Momentum Energy Group, with
Anders Storgaard from the industry association Green Power Denmark, as well
as with a municipal planner from Holbæk and Municipality. These interviewees
were selected to purposely represent different points in the project development
value chain — from private sector execution to public sector regulation.
The interviews typically lasted between 30 to 60 minutes and the interview guide

3.1
Historical Development and Current Status
Denmark has long been considered a global pioneer in the integration of renew-
able energy, particularly wind. However, recent trends indicate a slowdown in the
development of new renewable energy projects raising concerns about Denmark’s
ability to meet its ambitious 2030 climate goals.
By the end of 2024, the total combined installed capacity of wind and solar power
in Denmark reached a combination of 4.761 MW from onshore wind installations,
and 4.114 MW from solar photovoltaic (PV) systems. Due to the lower full-load
hours of solar installations compared to wind turbines, the effective contribution
of solar power is lower. To normalize this, the solar capacity has been converted
to ”onshore wind equivalent” units, resulting in approximately 1.371 MW of equiv-
alent capacity. This means that in terms of full-load adjusted capacity, the actual
combined renewable power in 2024 was 6.133 MW[3]. only a 4% increase from
2023. The increase for wind power was a mere 1%, while solar grew by 16%.
Although a 16% rise in solar capacity appears significant, it is relatively modest
when compared to 2022, when solar PV capacity nearly doubled from 535 MW to
1.052 MW a growth of over 90%. This clearly reflects a slowdown in renewable
energy expansion.
The stagnation is also evident in electricity production figures. Despite modest
capacity additions, total electricity generated from land-based wind and solar in
2024 remained roughly the same as in 2023. This is partially attributed to lower
wind and solar irradiation that year, as well as the decommissioning of older wind
turbines. This trend is shown in Figure 3.1.

not align with the targets set for 2030. Unless something changes it is unlikely to
accelerate at the required rate.

3.2
Political Goals and Reality
In 2022, the Danish government reached a landmark climate agreement, the Kli-
maaftale for grøn strøm og varme 2022, setting an ambitious target to quadruple
onshore renewable energy capacity primarily from wind and solar by 2030. This
goal entails increasing annual production from roughly 12 billion kWh to 50 bil-
lion kWh of renewable electricity, a transformative shift that requires significant
acceleration in development rates and overcoming a range of socio-political and
regulatory challenges [4].
To facilitate this expansion, the government introduced the concept of state en-
ergy parks in December 2023. Given that the land area required for renewable
energy development is projected to increase from approximately 0.5% to 1.3% of
Denmark’s territory, locating suitable sites has become a critical bottleneck. The
state energy parks framework aims to streamline approval processes for large-
scale projects by allowing municipalities to classify qualifying projects as part of
this scheme. This classification potentially enables developers to seek dispensa-
tions from certain natural and environmental restrictions that would otherwise limit
or prevent development [5].
However, the process for granting such dispensations remains stringent. Each
protected natural area impacted by a proposed project must undergo an individ-
ual, detailed assessment to determine if a dispensation is justified. Importantly,
dispensations can only be granted if no alternative, less sensitive locations are
available within the region[6], ensuring adherence to the precautionary princi-
ple embedded in EU nature protection legislation, such as the Habitats Directive
[7] and Birds Directive[8]. This dual requirement promoting renewable expansion
while safeguarding biodiversity reflects the political balancing act between climate
ambition and environmental protection.
Local Acceptance and Incentive Schemes
Recognizing that political and regulatory solutions alone are insufficient, the Dan-
ish government has emphasized the importance of local acceptance for renew-
able energy projects. To foster community support, financial incentives have been
strengthened:
• The Green Pool (Grøn Pulje) funding allocated to municipalities hosting re-
newable projects to invest in local nature restoration, biodiversity enhance-
ment, and community development has been increased by 150%.
• The VE-Bonus, a direct compensation scheme providing monetary benefits
to residents living near renewable installations, has been increased by 50%
[9].
Despite these initiatives, the gap between political goals and reality remains sig-
nificant. Key challenges that hinders the minimizing of this gap will be presented
in the next chapter.

Interview Findings
4.1
Key findings
This chapter presents the empirical findings from the interviews conducted during
the initial research phase of this study. The purpose of this chapter is to outline
the key themes that emerged from the interviews. These findings reflect the lived
experiences and insights of professionals working at different levels of the en-
ergy system, and they highlight the multifaceted challenges currently hindering
the deployment of renewable energy projects. The findings are presented without
interpretation or evaluation; a more detailed analysis and discussion will follow in
Chapter 5.
Themes were identified through thematic coding of interview transcripts and are
organized around the most frequently cited barriers to renewable energy deploy-
ment. Each subsection presents one major theme, supported by direct quotations
from the interviews to illustrate the perspectives of participants in their own words.
Regulatory Complexity
A recurring theme that emerged was the challenge posed by the municipalities
and the varying approaches they take to processing applications for VE (renew-
able energy) projects. With 98 municipalities in Denmark, each operating with
its own procedures and requirements, developers face significant complexity and
uncertainty. This lack of uniformity creates a difficult landscape to navigate when
initiating potential projects.
”To achieve this goal, more specific and standardized requirements
need to be issued to municipalities from the government.”
- Magnus, Momentum Energy Group
Currently, municipalities have the authority to impose additional requirements on
developers seeking to advance their projects. These requirements are often un-
predictable and can vary widely, adding another layer of uncertainty to the pro-
cess. Furthermore, local political dynamics frequently influence decision-making,
further complicating the landscape for developers.
”Local elections have a significant influence, as many are reluctant to
process applications in the lead-up to an election, fearing it may cause
controversy and result in a loss of voter support.”
- Magnus, Momentum Energy Group
As a result, even when developers comply fully with national legal requirements,
this may still prove insufficient.
”At times, it feels like it’s not enough to know the law and understand
the municipal plan. And that’s fundamentally a problem if we can’t rely
on the very documents that decisions are based on. It’s puzzling that
even when all legal requirements are met and no mistakes are made,

a project can still be rejected due to local political considerations. At
the same time, we have to acknowledge the role of local politics —
because otherwise, we’re not really accepting how the country is gov-
erned.”
- Andreas, Momentum Energy Group
The complexity of the Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) was another recur-
ring concern. Although the EIA is a crucial component of projects of this nature, it
is often perceived as disproportionately demanding in terms of time and resources
relative to its scope or impact. This adds further strain to the development process
and may hinder project progress unnecessarily.
”The Environmental Impact Assessment process is quite extensive.
New guidelines have been introduced for bat monitoring, requiring two
full seasons of data collection and observation before any conclusions

This lengthy approval process was also acknowledged by the planning depart-
ment in Holbæk municipality.
”The approval process takes a long time, but this is beyond our control
as planners. Much of the delay is due to the environmental impact
assessment, which can take several years to complete. If any issues
arise or corrections are needed, the process has to be revisited. The
same applies to Energinet when they need to lay new power lines.”
- Anders, Planner for Holbæk Municipality
Economic Barriers
The economic dimension of these projects is also a significant factor, primarily
affecting the developers. As previously mentioned, the high level of uncertainty
throughout the project lifecycle extends to the financial aspects as well. This is
further compounded by recent legislative changes, particularly the substantial in-
creases to the Green Pool (Grøn Pulje) and the VE-Bonus in 2024. While these
initiatives aim to support local communities, they also place additional financial
burdens on developers, thereby negatively impacting overall project feasibility
”The green pool (Grøn Pulje) allocated to municipalities has been in-
creased by 150%, significantly raising costs for renewable energy projects.
Additionally, the renewable energy bonus for neighboring areas has
been raised by 50%, contributing to a notable impact. Furthermore, the
costs associated with the grid and tariff systems have also changed.
Notably, Energinet, the state-owned operator of the transmission net-
work, has raised its tariffs by 70% between 2024 and 2025.”
- Anders, Green Power Denmark
The introduction of grid zones and additional tariffs for grid connection has also
influenced how developers assess the optimal size of a project. Geographical

location has become an increasingly important factor, as connection costs and
availability now vary significantly depending on where the project is situated.
”Many areas are classified into red, green, and yellow zones, with
much of the country falling into the red zone - and these areas are
often where there is available space for more renewable energy and
where projects already exist. These zones are typically in municipal-

Local Resistance and Municipal Approval
One of the most consistently mentioned challenges across all stakeholders was
the increasing resistance from local communities. Several interviewees stressed
that public opposition can significantly delay or even halt development efforts,
even in cases where projects fully comply with all regulatory and planning re-
quirements. As one municipal planner noted:
”First of all, local resistance is definitely the biggest barrier we see
when it comes to getting renewable energy projects approved.”
- Anders, Holbæk municipality
Interviewees also observed a broader cultural shift in how individuals perceive
their right to participate in land use decisions—even when those decisions do not
directly impact them. This shift was identified as a key factor contributing to the
growing levels of public engagement and opposition, particularly from residents

Market Uncertainties and Demand
The renewable energy transition is often seen as a supply-side challenge—centered
on permitting, grid access, and local opposition. However, an equally urgent issue
lies on the demand side: the slow pace of electrification across sectors. Without
faster adoption of electricity as the main energy carrier, even the most ambitious
renewable build-out may not align with actual energy use.
”Right now, a large part of our heating still relies on biomass, we
still have a lot of fossil-fueled cars, and few heat pumps. We’re also
still using oil and gas boilers to handle peak demand in winter. We
have ambitious goals to transition to electric solutions across sectors—
transport, heating, heavy industry—but we’re not moving fast enough.
As we electrify more of society, the demand for electricity will surge,
especially for green electricity. But unless the shift in these sectors ac-
celerates, we won’t create the necessary demand pull to drive large-
scale renewable energy expansion.”
— Anders, Green Power Denmark
Rather than being a matter of technology alone, the challenge of electrification
points to the need for coordinated sectoral policies and targeted infrastructure
investments, to ensure thatrenewable supply and electric demand follow the same
curve.

Economic Barriers
5.1
Support schemes
5.1.1
VE-Bonus
The VE-Bonus is a compensation scheme designed to offer annual payments to
households located near renewable energy projects. It aims to provide financial
recognition to those living close to wind turbines or solar parks. For projects that
receive a building permit after July 1st, 2024, the bonus is calculated based on
the estimated electricity revenue from 9.75 kW of the project’s total capacity. This
results in an approximate yearly payment of around 16,000 DKK for households
near wind turbines and 4,500 DKK for those near solar PV installations. These
figures can vary from year to year, depending on fluctuations in electricity prices.
For projects approved before July 1st, 2024, the compensation is lower, calculated
from only 6.5 kW of capacity. Projects permitted before January 1st, 2021, fall
under an older version of the scheme, which limits the calculation to 5 kW [10].
Over time, the VE-Bonus scheme has become more generous, reflecting a grow-
ing emphasis on local compensation, but also increasing overall costs for devel-
opers. Still, there is a cap for how much the combined payouts to the VE-Bonus
can be: only 1.5% of the total installed capacity can be used as the basis for
VE-Bonus payments.
To illustrate, if a project has a total installed capacity of 80,000 kW, the maximum
number of eligible households would be:
80, 000 kW · 1.5%
9.75 kW
≈123 households
(5.1)
If more households qualify, say 150, the share per household will decrease ac-
cordingly. In that case, the compensation would be based on:
80, 000 kW · 1.5%
150 households = 8 kW
(5.2)
This means that in situations where more than 1.5% of the total installed capacity
would be necessary to payout the full amount to neighbors, the monetary com-
pensation will be lowered in order to not exceed the cap.
But it is not everyone who can apply for VE-Bonus. Only households situated
within a defined proximity to the project are eligible for VE-Bonus payments. For
wind turbines, eligibility includes residences located within a radius of eight times
the total height of a turbine. For solar parks, the eligibility radius is 200 meters
[11].
Although 1.5% may seem like a small portion of total capacity, it typically covers
a relatively high number of households. For large-scale solar projects, it is less
Planning for solar and wind in Denmark: Is a fourfold increase in renewable energy realistic? 15
==PAGE:21==
common to have over 123 residences within 200 meters, which limits the total
financial exposure. Additionally, homes near solar parks, less than 200 meters,
also qualify for put options, giving the residents the option to to sell their property
to the developer, who in turn has to buy it, which can result in significant extra
costs. This is less likely in wind projects, where only residents within six times
the turbine height are eligible for put options, while VE-Bonus eligibility extends
slightly farther to eight times the height. As a result, more homes may qualify for
the VE-Bonus in wind projects compared to solar, but the financial risk is more
manageable.
Finally, while the payment per household is set each year, the actual amount de-
pends on electricity prices, and may vary accordingly [10]

5.2
Grid Connection
5.2.1
Grid Costs
In order to deliver electricity from a renewable energy project to consumers, devel-
opers must secure a grid connection either to Energinet, the Transmission System
Operator (TSO), or to one of the regional Distribution System Operators (DSO).
The distinction depends on the voltage level of the connection: projects connect-
ing at 30–60 kV typically link to the DSO level, while those requiring 132–150 kV
must connect directly to the transmission grid via Energinet.
While this structure may seem logical and technically sound, it introduces signif-
icant economic complexity. The size of the project and its geographic location
especially its placement within Denmark’s ”geozones” can substantially influence
the cost of grid connection. This adds a layer of strategic planning for developers,
who must weigh wind or solar potential against escalating infrastructure fees.
Geozones
To manage grid capacity and encourage development where electricity demand is
highest, Denmark categorizes areas into three geozones based on grid balance:
• Red zones – Production dominated areas where electricity supply already
exceeds local demand.
• Yellow zones – Transitional areas with more balanced production and con-
sumption.
• Green zones – Consumption dominated areas where new production is
most beneficial to the grid.
Most geozones are typically revised once a year to help guide decisions on project
placement. However, starting from January 1st, 2025, some zones have changed
to a dynamic model instead of only doing annual updates. With this approach, the
zones are updated on a weekly basis, offering a more accurate and up-to-date
overview of local grid capacity. For zones using this dynamic system, develop-
ers and other stakeholders are also provided with a table showing the remaining
available capacity to better help keeping track of when an area may shift into a
different geozone category[12].

Local Opposition
6.1
Understanding NIMBY
It’s no surprise that one of the most critical factors in developing renewable en-
ergy projects is securing the support of local citizens. As Anders from Holbæk
Municipality put it:
First of all, local resistance is definitely the biggest barrier we see when
it comes to getting renewable energy projects approved. - Anders,
Holbæk municipality
The so-called NIMBY (Not In My Backyard) effect is often cited in this context.
However, while the term captures a general sentiment, it is not particularly useful
for understanding or addressing the root causes of opposition. Simply labeling
opponents as ”NIMBYs” does little to move the conversation forward. To truly
understand local resistance, we need to be more specific about its underlying
motivations.
Adding to the challenge is the widespread circulation of myths surrounding var-
ious renewable energy technologies. Many of these claims are factually incor-
rect, yet they persist and spread from one project to another—undermining trust
and stalling development. To combat this, the NEKST taskforce has produced in-
formational videos aimed at correcting misinformation and sharing facts through
social media. The goal is to foster a more informed public dialogue and reduce
resistance based on false claims.
Some of the most common myths are outlined below:
[1]
Myth: ”Turbines and PV ruins the biodiversity and nature”
Nature areas in Denmark is getting smaller and smaller a lot of places, so
it’s vital that we take care of it. By setting up PV or turbines is in no way the
same as ruining the nature. It is possible to cooperate the two together and
not only benefit the green production, but also the nature.
Myth: ”There is no need for additional renewable energy in Denmark”
In 2022 Denmark produced 21,2 terawatt hours of green energy, equivilent
to 60% of the danish electricity consumption, so we are not quite there yet.
Myth: ”It’s much cheaper to produce renewable energy offshore compared
to onshore”
As the market and technology is at the moment, LCOE calculations from The
Danish Energy Agency shows that the productions cost of onshore turbines
towards 2030 is 30% cheaper than offshore production.
Myth: ”Most people in Denmark are against erecting more turbines”
A survey done by Aalborg university shows that 74% of the 4.000 people
Planning for solar and wind in Denmark: Is a fourfold increase in renewable energy realistic? 23
==PAGE:29==
asked was supporting of further development of turbines in Denmark. Only
3,6% and 4,3% did disagree and completely disagreed.
While Denmark is often praised for its commitment to renewable energy, including
its ambitious rollout of wind power, local opposition continues to pose challenges
for specific energy projects. This opposition is frequently labeled as NIMBYism,
a term that suggests local residents support renewable energy in general, but
reject it when developments are proposed near their homes. However, such a
label often oversimplifies and discredits more complex and legitimate concerns
rooted in how people relate to places[17].
Rather than viewing opposition as irrational or selfish, it is more accurate and con-
structive to interpret it as a form of place-protective action. According to Devine-
Wright (2009)[17], resistance to local energy projects can be understood through
two key psychological concepts: place attachment and place identity. Place at-
tachment refers to the emotional bonds people form with specific locations, while
place identity relates to how these places contribute to one’s sense of self. These
attachments are shaped by social relationships, cultural meaning, and physical
characteristics of the environment.
When energy developments are introduced, particularly without meaningful local
engagement, they can be perceived as threats to both the emotional value and
the identity of a place. This perception can trigger emotional responses such as
anxiety, grief, or anger—particularly if individuals feel excluded from the planning
process or powerless to influence outcomes. Devine-Wright notes that disruption
to place can unfold gradually, even before any physical changes occur, as individ-
uals begin to imagine or anticipate negative outcomes for places they value.[17]
Importantly, opposition does not always come from ignorance or lack of knowl-
edge, as the NIMBY stereotype suggests, if so, that would be an easy fix. On
the contrary, residents may be well-informed and motivated by deeply held place-
based values. For example, people may oppose developments they see as harm-
ing the local community or see it as a thread to their ability to control and influence
what happens in their community—all of which has nothing to do with the facts of
the project.
Understanding NIMBYism as a form of place-based coping offers more than just
an explanation — it also points to better planning practices. If public opposition is a
reaction to perceived threats to place, then early and inclusive engagement, trans-
parent communication, and shared decision-making become essential. Rather
than dismissing opposition, project developers and planners should treat it as a
sign of strong local ties and work collaboratively to align renewable energy goals
with local values.
6.2
Local Acceptance
Local acceptance is often highlighted as a key factor in the development and ap-
proval of renewable energy projects in Denmark. Policymakers frequently stress
that public support is essential for ensuring a smooth implementation process.
While the idea is sound in principle, it remains vague and inconsistently applied
Planning for solar and wind in Denmark: Is a fourfold increase in renewable energy realistic? 24
==PAGE:30==
in practice. At present, there is no agreed-upon definition or metric for what con-
stitutes local acceptance. Nor is there a clear framework for how it should be
measured or evaluated in the permitting process.
This lack of clarity creates a difficult situation for developers. They are expected to
demonstrate acceptance from the local community, but without clear benchmarks
or procedural guidelines, it becomes nearly impossible to know what “acceptance”
actually means in operational terms. When asked to define it, many political actors
offer only general statements, and there is little agreement on what level of support
is sufficient or how it should be demonstrated.
More concerning is the way this ambiguity can influence decision-making at the
municipal level. In some cases, local authorities can use the perceived absence
of local support to stop or delay projects,even when all technical and legal require-
ments have been met. This opens the door to discretionary and potentially politi-
cized decision-making, where personal or local political interests may override
national policy goals. For developers who have invested in fulfilling the require-
ments of environmental assessments, stakeholder consultations, and regulatory
compliance, the risk of rejection based on undefined social criteria introduces sig-
nificant uncertainty. This was highlighted by a developer who said the following:
”At times, it feels like it’s not enough to know the law and understand
the municipal plan. And that’s fundamentally a problem if we can’t rely
on the very documents that decisions are based on. It’s puzzling that
even when all legal requirements are met and no mistakes are made,
a project can still be rejected due to local political considerations.”
- Andreas, Momentum Energy Group
Without clear rules or tools, he argues, the concept of local acceptance risks be-
coming a political instrument rather than a constructive part of the planning pro-
cess.
6.3
Compensations Schemes
Currently, the VE-compensation schemes are viewed as a baseline or minimum
requirement, which often leads municipalities to impose additional local demands
on developers. These conditions can slow down the approval process and cre-
ate uncertainty — not only for developers but also for local residents. Without a
consistent framework, no one can be certain which rules apply in a given munic-
ipality, making the development environment unpredictable and uneven across
the country.
To address this, a uniform national rule set should apply equally to all munici-
palities. Local authorities should not be permitted to impose extra demands be-
yond the standardized compensation schemes. This would not only streamline
the process but also reduce the risk of perceived favoritism or informal negotia-
tions, where a developer might feel pressured to agree to special conditions in
exchange for local approval, even if another project may have been a better fit.
The VE-Bonus and Green Pool schemes were designed to avoid precisely this
Planning for solar and wind in Denmark: Is a fourfold increase in renewable energy realistic? 25
==PAGE:31==
situation — ensuring that compensation is split fairly, with direct payments to af-
fected neighbors (VE-Bonus) and a broader sum to the municipality (Green Pool).
Those who benefit from VE-compensation typically fall into two groups. The first
includes residents who either support the project or are indifferent — they tend to
accept increased payments without issue. The second group includes those who
are strongly opposed to the project. For them, increased compensation is unlikely
to change their stance, unless the amounts become unrealistically high — which
would be neither practical nor economically viable. As a result, some residents
interpret rising compensation levels not as fair mitigation, but as an attempt by de-
velopers to ”buy” acceptance — a perception that undermines trust, even though
it may not reflect the developer’s intent.
6.4
Local Ambassadors
Citizen meetings in municipalities often carry a tense or even hostile atmosphere,
particularly toward developers, due to opposition from parts of the local population.
In small villages—where many renewable energy project sites are located—there
are residents who quietly support the projects, apart from the landowners directly
involved. However, many of these individuals are reluctant to express their views
publicly. In such small and close communities, people often fear being publicly
shamed or making ”enemies” by speaking out in favor of a project.
This dynamic has been observed repeatedly in citizen meetings conducted by
Momentum Energy Group, where supportive individuals often choose to voice
their opinions only in private, one-on-one conversations after the meeting1. As a
result, negative voices tend to dominate the public discussion, creating a skewed
perception of community sentiment.
Currently, the prevailing narrative in the media suggests that most people are
opposed to renewable energy projects. However, an analysis conducted by Andel
and Norstat challenges this perception. While NIMBYism is indeed identified as a
significant hurdle, the study revealed that actual opposition comes from a small,
though vocal and well-organized, minority—only 3% of respondents. In contrast,
90% of citizens expressed support for renewable energy projects[18].
This highlights the need for visible local ambassadors who are willing to publicly
support these initiatives. Their presence could encourage others to do the same,
helping to correct the distorted public perception. The influence of negative me-
dia coverage and persistent rumors further exacerbates the issue. When misin-
formation dominates both public meetings and local news, it spreads quickly and
undermines progress. This concern was also raised by the municipal planner in
Holbæk, who commented on the harmful impact of unchecked misinformation.
”I often hear that the same concerns and misunderstandings repeat
themselves from one renewable energy project to the next. It’s like
someone thinks: ”Last time, someone mentioned leaching toxic liquids
from solar panels, and that hindered the project — so let’s just use that

this pattern consistently occurred.

argument again here.”
- Anders, Holbæk Municipality
Mindsets like these are often no longer driven by genuine concerns—such as
potential contamination or other specific issues—but rather serve as a means to
halt the project altogether, using any available argument. In such cases, accurate
information becomes crucial. It is essential that citizens are well-informed, so they
can distinguish between valid concerns and misinformation.
For this reason, it is essential to actively combat misinformation and raise public
awareness. Several advertising campaigns have already made a positive impact
in this area.
One such example is Andel’s 2022 campaign, Giv Plads (“Give Space”), which
used visuals and real-life stories to illustrate how little land is actually required to
secure a green energy future. As shown in Figure A.5, the campaign featured
people who were initially opposed to wind turbines. The result was a significant
shift in public opinion: 23% of respondents reported increased support for tur-
bines, 24% became more open to the idea of new installations, and 25% reported
contributing more actively to the green transition as a result of the campaign[19].
In addition, the campaign included an interactive Instagram filter that allowed
users to place a virtual wind turbine into a photo of their local landscape. More
than 12,000 unique locations were submitted with the message: ”I want to give
space to turbines here.”[19]
Another successful initiative by Andel was the two-part campaign Windy Retreats[20].
It focused on collecting testimonials from people already living near wind turbines.
These residents generally reported that the concerns often raised—such as noise
or visual disruption—were greatly exaggerated. To bridge the gap between per-
ception and reality, they invited skeptical citizens to stay in their homes and ex-
perience the conditions firsthand. The second part of the campaign documented
these visits, where many skeptics concluded that the impact of turbines was far
less intrusive than they had expected.

lights the gap between perception and actual experience:
• 85% of those opposed to wind turbines in their area cite noise pollution as
a concern.
• 43% believe turbines may cause sleep problems.

7.1
Concise Municipal Requirements
A recurring theme in several interviews is the lack of a clear and unified approach
across municipalities for handling renewable energy projects. Despite national cli-
mate goals, there is no standardized framework guiding local approval and plan-
ning processes. Instead, each municipality applies its own procedures, require-
ments, and interpretations of national legislation.
To achieve this goal, more specific and standardized requirements
need to be issued to municipalities from the government.
- Magnus, Momentum Energy Group
This inconsistency creates significant uncertainty for developers, who must nav-
igate a new and often unfamiliar administrative process in each location. Even
small differences—such as in documentation or consultation practices—can im-
pact project timelines and costs. As a result, developers must spend considerable
time understanding local conditions and adapting to varying expectations, which
can be especially challenging for smaller firms.
This fragmented system also makes it harder to scale renewable energy efforts.
What works in one municipality may face delays or rejection in another, adding to
the perception of risk and reducing investor confidence.
It is worth noting that while this issue is widely acknowledged in interviews and
anecdotal accounts, it is surprisingly difficult to find formal or publicly available
sources that document the extent of these inconsistencies. Most of the avail-
able insight is based on firsthand experience working with project developers at
Momentum Energy Group, informal discussions, and comments made during in-
terviews with various parties.
National Guideline
To reduce inconsistency between municipalities, a mandatory national framework
should be introduced to standardize how renewable energy projects are planned,
evaluated, and approved. While allowing room for local adaptation, the framework
would ensure a consistent and predictable process across the country.
Key elements could include:
• A clear, step-by-step approval process with defined phases and deadlines.
• Standardized documentation requirements, such as environmental assess-
ments and stakeholder engagement plans.
• Unified evaluation criteria to ensure fair and consistent decisions.

Such a framework would make the planning process more transparent and effi-
cient, lowering project risk and administrative delays. It would benefit developers
through predictability and municipalities by offering a solid legal foundation for
decision-making.
7.2
Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)
Streamlining the Environmental Impact Assessment Process
The Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) is a key element in responsible re-
newable energy development. It ensures that potential effects on biodiversity,
landscape, human health, and cultural heritage are thoroughly evaluated before
projects are approved. However, in Denmark, the EIA process has increasingly
become a bottleneck, delaying progress and discouraging investment.
”[...] Much of the delay is due to the environmental impact assessment,
which can take several years to complete.”
– Anders, Planner for Holbæk Municipality
When a project is awaiting EIA approval, the developer is often left in limbo. With-
out clarity on the outcome, they are reluctant to invest further in development
activities due to the risk of project rejection. This creates long idle periods where
projects simply wait for administrative procedures to catch up—slowing the pace
of the green transition.
The EU EIA Exemption
In response to the energy crisis triggered by Russia’s invasion of Ukraine and the
subsequent reduction in natural gas supply to Europe, the EU adopted emergency
measures to accelerate renewable energy deployment. One of these measures
was a new framework to accelerate the deployment of renewable energy[6] Article
6 of the regulation allows EU member states to temporarily bypass full EIA pro-
cedures for renewable energy and grid infrastructure projects, as long as these
are located in areas designated for renewable development. It also provides lim-
ited exemptions from certain protections on wildlife, with the goal of speeding up
permitting processes.
This exemption was originally set to expire in June 2024, but has since been ex-
tended to June 30, 2025. It represents a significant opportunity for EU countries to
accelerate renewable energy deployment with legal backing from the EU. Despite
this, Denmark has not made use of the exemption — and, according to current
policy, does not plan to do so before it expires.
Instead, Denmark is working to incorporate selected elements of the EU exemp-
tion into permanent national legislation through the upcoming VEIII Directive. The
following statement was included in the legislative proposal:
”A faster transition to green energy is the way out of both the climate
and energy crises. Lengthy administrative permitting procedures — in-
cluding the complexity and duration of Environmental Impact Assess-
ments (EIAs) — are among the biggest obstacles to investment in re-
newable energy projects and the related infrastructure.”[21]

– Minister for Climate, Energy and Utilities, Lars Aagaard
While the original EU exemption was optional, the new law aims to embed stream-
lined permitting into Danish regulation. Whether this will effectively reduce EIA-
related delays remains to be seen.
The following sections highlight two specific and increasingly important challenges
within the EIA process that have generated considerable debate and uncertainty
for developers.
Bats
New challenges have emerged in relation to environmental and biodiversity pro-
tection. One notable development is the updated report published by DCE – the
Danish Centre for Environment and Energy – titled “Handbook on Animal Species
Listed in Annex IV of the Habitats Directive” [22]. Commissioned by the Danish
Environmental Protection Agency, this report is now regarded as the authoritative
source of guidance for public authorities, NGOs, and ministries concerning Den-
mark’s obligations under the EU Habitats Directive, particularly in regard to the
protection of bats.
Several renewable energy projects have already been impacted, as authorities
have informed developers that the findings in the report may lead to significantly
stricter requirements [23]. A central issue is the limited scientific understanding
of bat behavior, including how their migration patterns and flight activity vary sea-
sonally and geographically. Due to this lack of data, the DCE now recommends
that bat activity be monitored in and around a project site for at least two years
before any conclusions can be drawn.
The report also references research from Germany, which suggests that avoid-
ing collisions between bats and turbines requires stopping turbine operation al-
together during periods of high bat activity. While a full stop is not practical for
energy production, a more feasible mitigation strategy is to suspend turbine oper-
ation during key times—typically at night or during specific migration periods.
However, the recommendations are highly precautionary due to the lack of data.
One of the more controversial suggestions is to set a minimum cut-in wind speed
of 8–10 m/s to reduce collision risk [22]. This has sparked significant concern
among developers, as it would drastically reduce the operational hours of wind
turbines. As shown in Figure A.6, the mean wind speed in Denmark at 100 meters
above ground level is only about 8.2 m/s. This would have a profound effect on the
annual energy production. Even in the windiest 10% of the country, the average
wind speed is only 9.07 m/s — still below the upper end of the proposed threshold.
As a result, this new interpretation of bat protection requirements may render
many planned projects economically unviable, prompting calls for more balanced,
data-driven regulation if these suggestions is to be implemented.

Outsourcing
Another possible solution is to facilitate the temporary lending of staff between
municipalities. The number of renewable energy projects varies greatly across the
country, meaning some municipalities are overwhelmed with applications while
others have little or no project activity. This imbalance creates staffing challenges,
especially because experienced planners and environmental specialists tend to
move toward municipalities with active development pipelines, where their skills
are in high demand.
This creates a paradox: the municipalities that are already more advanced in the
green transition continue to attract skilled professionals, further reinforcing their
capacity, while the municipalities that are falling behind are often left short-staffed
and unable to build the necessary in-house expertise. As a result, the gap be-

Political Changes
The Green Tripartite Agreement (Den Grønne Trepartsaftale)
In 2024, the Danish government entered into a major agreement with agricultural
and environmental stakeholders known as the Green Tripartite Agreement. The
purpose of this agreement is to reduce emissions from agriculture by restoring
biodiversity and reshaping Denmark’s land use. One of its key goals is to restore
250,000 hectares of agricultural land and convert it into new forests and areas
of wild nature before 2045. In addition, the agreement aims to remove 140,000
hectares of agricultural wetlands from production to reduce CO2 emissions from
those areas.
To support this transition, the government has set aside 9.4 billion DKK to com-
pensate farmers who own land classified as lowland.[24] This financial support
enables them to cease farming activities on those plots and instead allow nature
to reclaim the land, helping bind CO2 in the soil.
While the agreement is rooted in environmental concerns, it unintentionally intro-
duces further constraints for the development of renewable energy. The farm-
land being taken out of production is no longer available for other uses and is
categorised as unsuitible for renewable energy due to monitary concers as land
sutable for renewable energy has a higher value. This reduces the already limited
supply of suitable land for VE projects.
A possible solution would be to align the goals of the Green Tripartite Agreement
with Denmark’s climate targets by allowing certain areas to serve both purposes:
restoring nature and supporting renewable energy infrastructure. For example,
placing solar panels on wetland areas could simultaneously reduce agricultural
emissions and generate clean energy. Denmark only needs to increase land use
for VE from 0.5% to 1.3% to reach its 2030 climate goals, a relatively modest ad-
justment. On the other hand, the Green Tripartite Agreement is aiming to rewild
approximately 10% of the country’s land area. It seems reasonable that a small
share of that land could be used to support renewable energy without undermining
its environmental goals. However, the Green Tripartite Agreement does not em-
phasize this combination of renewables and nature restoration as much as some

==PAGE:39==
might hope. Attending a webinar on the Agreement and its implications for land
use led to disappointing insights. Although several speakers presented slides
featuring images of both solar PV parks and wind turbines, the topic of integrating
renewables with nature restoration was not mentioned even once.
Some developers have explored the possibility of installing solar PV systems on
wetland areas but found the high cost of building stable foundations in saturated
soils to be too expensive. Another thing in the current compensation structure:
farmers who withdraw agricultural activity from wetlands are eligible for govern-
ment compensation. However, if they instead lease their land to renewable energy
developers and receive private payments, they become ineligible for that support.
This makes sense from a regulatory perspective, it prevents double compensa-
tion for the same land. But what if the government redirected the funds already
earmarked for compensation to instead support renewable energy developers by
subsidizing the additional costs of building in wet conditions?
• The farmland would still be removed from agricultural use, meeting the orig-
inal environmental goal.
• CO2 would still be bound in the soil.
• The farmer would be compensated, likely at a higher rate, by the developer.
• The developer would receive support to cover the additional cost of building
on wetlands.
• The state would not incur any additional cost, just redirect the existing com-
pensation pool.
• The country would accelerate its green transition, alongside reducing CO2
emissions.
Such a policy shift would represent a net win for all parties involved: the environ-
ment, landowners, renewable energy developers, and the Danish state.
Electrification
One of the key challenges hindering the expansion of renewable energy (VE) in
Denmark is insufficient demand. In any well-functioning market, supply and de-
mand must evolve in tandem. Without a significant increase in demand, renew-
able energy projects risk becoming economically unviable, thereby discouraging
investment in new developments. To meet its ambitious climate and energy tar-
gets, Denmark must accelerate its electrification efforts substantially.
Currently, electricity accounts for only about 20% of Denmark’s total energy con-
sumption. This relatively low level of electrification ranks Denmark 23rd among
European countries in terms of electricity’s share of overall energy use [25]. How-
ever, according to the Danish Energy Agency (Energistyrelsen), it is technically
feasible to electrify more than 90% of the energy consumption in Danish busi-
nesses [25]. This reveals a substantial, yet largely untapped, potential for electri-
fication, one that could significantly boost demand for renewable energy. There

8.1
Case Presentation
This section presents a real-world case study from Momentum Energy Group to
illustrate how the challenges and barriers discussed in previous sections manifest
in an ongoing renewable energy project. The aim is to assess how these issues
have influenced both the economic and planning aspects over the last years.
To support the analysis, a map will be provided to visualize the project area, rel-

and nearby residences. Production estimates will be calculated using WindPro to

regulations and the potential impact on nearby residents.
The case focuses on a repowering wind project called ”Fjaldene Sol og Vind”.
The original plan involved dismantling 18 existing smaller turbines and replacing
them with four new turbines, each with a total height of 180 meters, alongside a
96-hectare solar PV installation.

component was removed. The project is now strictly a wind repowering project.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to investigate whether Denmark can realistically achieve a four-
fold increase in land-based renewable energy by 2030. The research combined
interviews, a literature review, and a real-world case study to better understand
the regulatory, economic, and social obstacles involved. While the ambition is
clear, the path forward appears far less certain.
Although Denmark possesses both the technical capabilities and investment in-
terest needed to expand wind and solar power, several key barriers continue to
stand in the way of progress.
What are the key barriers developers face in the planning and permitting
process? Interviews with project developers and municipal planners revealed
widespread frustration with inconsistent and unpredictable municipal planning pro-
cedures. These local differences create uncertainty, delay project timelines, and
demand extra administrative resources from developers. In addition, complex
Environmental Impact Assessments (EIAs) — particularly with the introduction of
new regulations on protected species like bats — further complicate the permitting
process,
How do changes in compensation schemes and grid costs influence project
feasibility? Increasing financial burdens — such as rising grid connection fees,
transformer contributions, and new compensation schemes — are affecting the
economic viability of renewable energy projects. As demonstrated in the “Fjaldene
Sol og Vind” case study, these escalating costs can significantly alter a project’s
budget and reduce investor confidence. In particular, uncertainty about future
tariffs and compensation rules makes long-term planning and financial forecasting
more difficult.
What role does local opposition play? Local opposition remains a significant
barrier to renewable energy deployment. While national support for the green
transition is strong, individual projects often face pushback from nearby commu-
nities. In the case study, strong resistance led to the complete removal of the PV
component and a scaling down of the overall project. Although such concerns —
like noise or visual impact — are often dismissed under the label of “NIMBYism”,
they are rarely rooted in ignorance or outright rejection of climate goals. Instead,
they often stem from a deep attachment to the local landscape, fear of irreversible
change, and a perceived lack of influence in the decision-making process. These
factors highlight how local perception can strongly influence renewable energy
projects.
What can be done to help accelerate the deployment of renewable energy
in the future? Several practical steps could help address the current barriers.
Fixing grid tariffs over multiple years would offer greater financial predictability for
developers and investors. Municipal planning procedures should be streamlined
and harmonized to align more closely with national climate goals, reducing delays
Planning for solar and wind in Denmark: Is a fourfold increase in renewable energy realistic? 41
==PAGE:47==
caused by inconsistent local approaches. Denmark should also make full use of
the upcoming VEIII Directive, especially in light of the missed opportunity to apply
the EU exemption period. In the longer term, a more integrated strategy that
connects the Green Tripartite Agreement with national energy targets could create
synergies between biodiversity restoration and renewable energy development,
rather than treating them as competing land uses.
What emerges from the analysis is not a lack of technical capability, but a lack
of prioritization. Many projects are ready to proceed, yet they are held back by
administrative complexity, political caution, and local resistance. Despite ambi-
tious national targets, renewable energy development often takes a backseat to
competing land-use interests.
In the end, the critical question is not whether Denmark can meet its 2030 renew-
able energy targets — but whether it is willing to make this goal a top political and
societal priority. Without stronger commitment at both national and local levels,
the current pace of development will not match the ambition of the targets. As
long as renewable energy is not treated as an urgent priority, there will always be
something new that cuts ahead in the queue — leaving the green transition stalled
once again.

A.1
Interview Guide
Introduction
1. Presentation of myself and the purpose of the interview:
• The purpose of this interview is to gain your insights into which factors
are most critical for ensuring that these projects are carried out effec-
tively and can help Denmark meet its 2030 targets.
2. Background questions:
• What is your role in the company, and what experience do you have
with renewable energy development in Denmark?
• What is your specific experience with solar and wind projects?
Main Part
3. Challenges in project development:
• What are the biggest challenges you currently see for solar and wind
projects in Denmark?
• How do political decision-making processes impact the development
of these projects? Are there specific regulations that cause delays or
issues?
• Are there particular environmental or biodiversity-related regulations
that are difficult to navigate? How do they affect project development?
• What about the economics — what financial challenges are typically
encountered? (e.g., rising costs, financing opportunities)
4. Specific issues:
• What challenges have you observed in relation to the authorities’ ap-
proval processes? Have there been instances where delays had a sig-
nificant negative impact?
• How are local communities and stakeholders handled during project
development? What are the typical challenges regarding public en-
gagement?
• What about technical challenges such as grid connection and grid ca-
pacity — how do these affect projects?
5. Possible solutions:
• What solutions do you see as most effective in addressing these chal-
lenges?

• Are there specific initiatives that could help increase local community
acceptance and ease the approval process by authorities?
• Do you/your company have experience with solutions implemented in
other countries such as Germany or the US (e.g., related to bat protec-
tion or grid connection), and could they be applied in Denmark?
6. Future predictions:
• What do you think will be the biggest obstacles to solar and wind energy
