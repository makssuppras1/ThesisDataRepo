Introduction
This section will give a brief introduction to the project. It contains the inspiration and
motivation for taking on this project, along with a problem statement discussing the overall
goals for the project and its limitations.
1.1
Motivation
In recent years, the consumer-grade 3D printer market has experienced unprecedented
growth, driven by technological advancements, decreasing costs, and a rising demand
for personalized manufacturing solutions. As 3D printing moves from a niche domain
into mainstream consciousness, manufacturers are fiercely competing to capture a larger
share of this expanding market.
The increased adoption of 3D printing technology has ushered in a new era of creativ-
ity and innovation, enabling individuals and businesses to bring their ideas to life with
unprecedented ease, affordability, and speed. From hobbyists tinkering in their garages
to small-scale enterprises revolutionizing prototyping and production processes, the ac-
cessibility of consumer-grade 3D printers has drastically changed the way we conceive,
design, and manufacture physical objects.
Although 3D printing is a rapid method of manufacturing, printing jobs can still take hours
to complete. Without error detection, if a problem arises midway through, the entire print
will need to be scrapped, resulting in significant material waste and lost time.
This paper explores the application of AI Object Detection Models to detect errors during
the printing in order to pause all processes. One easily identifiable error is the uncontrolled
spooling of filament onto the printer bed, which, if left undetected, can lead to consider-
able filament waste. Factors contributing to this failure include incorrect filament tension,
clogged nozzles, improper printer calibration, and printer speed. Early detection of these
issues can salvage the print or at least reduce material waste.

1.2
Problem Statement
This master’s thesis project aims to develop a real-time failure detection system for 3D
printers. The system will be designed to monitor 3D printing processes continuously and
autonomously detect any anomalies or failures that may occur during printing. By utilizing
a web camera, the system will capture live video feeds of the printing process, while a
disconnected computing architecture ensures that the monitoring and detection process
remains independent of the 3D printer’s operating system, thus enhancing compatibility
and flexibility.
The proposed real-time failure detection system addresses a critical need in the 3D print-
ing industry by providing users with an advanced tool for monitoring and troubleshooting
printing processes. By leveraging web camera technology and disconnected computing
architecture, the system offers a versatile and platform independent solution that can be
easily integrated with existing 3D printer setups. Ultimately, this research aims to enhance
the reliability, efficiency, and user experience of 3D printing workflows, thereby accelerat-
ing the adoption of additive manufacturing technologies across various applications.
1.2.1
Project Goals
1. Develop a real-time monitoring system using a web camera, capable of capturing
video feeds and tracking progress of 3D printing processes.
2. Implement computer vision algorithms to analyse the live video streams and detect
various types of failures or anomalies during the printing process, such as print bed
adhesion issues, filament jams, or extruder malfunctions.
3. Optimise computer vision algorithms on a per print basis, by developing a method
for training image recognition models virtually on the 3D parts.
4. Design and implement a solution that allows for the monitoring and detection system
to operate independently of the 3D printer’s operating system, ensuring compatibility
with a wide range of printer models and configurations.
5. Conduct extensive testing and validation of the system’s performance using a variety
of printing materials, and failure scenarios to assess its accuracy, reliability, and
effectiveness.
1.3
Limitations
In this paper development and testing of an AI Object Detection Model for detecting 3D
printer errors, was limited to only detect the uncontrollable spooling of filament, commonly
referred to as ”spaghetti”.
Due to the lack of access to a personal 3D printer, the testing of the Object Detection Mod-
els was carried out on recordings of 3D prints. These recordings are meant to simulate
the feed of a web camera, and will allow for greater control of the different parameters
that can affect the results, such as different resolutions and frame rates. This will ensure
that the print will always be the same, allowing for more precise comparisons of different
solutions.

2.1
Bambu Lab X1 Series
Bambu Lab, a Chinese 3D printer manufacturer founded in 2020, is headquartered in
Shenzhen, China. Its most popular model is the X1 series, particularly the X1-Carbon.
This model features a USB camera installed in the corner of the printer chamber, which
allows for various functions such as live video streaming, timelapse recording, and real-
time monitoring of the printing process. One notable feature of the printer’s monitoring
system is Spaghetti Detection, which helps identify and address printing issues.
2.1.1
How it works
The system uses a powerful on-chip Neural Processor Unit (NPU), to do most of the
heavy lifting. The system takes a frame from the Chamber Camera every T seconds,
and feeds it to the AI detector running on the NPU. The detector is a state-of-the-art
object detection neural network, which finds the bounding box of the potential spaghetti
with a confidence value. If the confidence is high enough (the confidence threshold is
determined by the sensitivity you choose), it is considered a positive candidate. After
processing the detection result, the system makes an accumulation of the results for the

latest 10 frames. If there are enough positive candidates, and they are spatially consistent,
then a spaghetti failure is detected. [1]
To have the detector work properly, there are a few requirements:
1. The detector needs to be enabled in the “Print Options”. The sensitivity can be
adjusted from low, medium, high. If the printer is left unattended for longer periods,
the sensitivity can be set to “low”, so it is less likely for smaller defects to pause the
printing.
2. The chamber light needs to be on. The spaghetti detector needs proper lighting
conditions in order to capture the details of the prints. By default, the light is switched
on when the first layer starts to print. If the light is shut off manually, it will not be
turned on again.
The X1 series can detect failures/spaghetti based on the processing power it has. The sys-
tem uses a machine-learning algorithm and all the data processing is done locally. When
the printer is used in LAN mode, the printer behaves just like when it is not connected to
the internet and uses local processing to provide this feature.
If the printer is not connected to the cloud, and the user improvement option is not enabled,
the printer will rely only on the information it has in the latest firmware for failure detection,
which might be outdated compared to the cloud-connected experience where the AI model

Prusa Research AI Detection
As of the writing of this paper Prusa have not released any failure detection software to
the public, but they are currently in development of “AI Detection” for “Prusa Connect”.
Prusa Connect allows the user to manage G-codes and control 3D printers remotely, no
matter their location.
Their AI solution works by processing a camera image that is sent to their neural network
that has been trained on their in house 3D printers. In their model, they have defined mul-
tiple classes for different error types, so they are not focusing on just spaghetti detection
like Bambu Lab. Some of the classes they mentioned in their 3D printing podcast are:
spaghetti Detection, empty sheet detection or if the camera has suddenly changed angle.
[2]
Since Prusa Research 3D-printers don’t have any built in web cameras, there solution is
more flexible than Bambu Lab. Their AI detection works with any USB camera, that can be
placed anywhere. Currently there is no surveillance mode implemented to continuously
monitor the print and check if an error has been detected. Their solution is manually
operated by pressing a button in the interface, which will analyse the current frame and
outputs a confidence score of what it has detected, it then prompts the user to give a
thumbs up or down, to further train the model.

4.1
Model Training
When training a machine learning model, several critical factors must be carefully consid-
ered. These include the methodology for determining the contents of an image, the size
and diversity of the dataset used for training, and the selection of an appropriate baseline
model. Each of these components plays a pivotal role in the overall performance and
accuracy of the machine learning model.
4.1.1
Types of Object Detection Models
Within the field of Computer Vision the three primary tasks are Image Classification,
Object Detection and Image Segmentation.[12]
Image Classification
Figure 4.1: An example of Image Classification to detect cats and dogs.
Image classification is a task in computer vision that involves assigning a label or category
to an image based on its visual content along with confidence score. This process enables
machines to recognize and categorize objects, scenes, or features within an image.
Real-Time Failure Detection System for 3D Printers

==PAGE:20==
For instance, if an image classification model is trained to detect images containing cats
or dogs, it employs the classifier labels ”Cat” and ”Dog.” Upon being presented with an
image, the model outputs a probability distribution indicating the likelihood of the image
belonging to each of these categories, as seen in figure 4.1.
Image classification is most applicable in scenarios where the primary objective is to iden-
tify the content of an image without concern for the specific locations of objects within
the image. However, this approach falls short in more complex scenes involving multiple
subjects, as it cannot detect multiple objects of the same or different classes within the
same frame.
For instance, in the context of detecting spaghetti-like failures within a 3D print, image
classification proves impractical. It would be nearly impossible to ascertain which specific
parts of the print contain the failure, thus rendering image classification insufficient for
such detailed analysis.
Object Detection
Object detection is a computer vision task that involves identifying and locating objects
within an image. Unlike image classification, which only assigns a label to an image, ob-
ject detection draws bounding boxes around each detected object and classifies them.
This technique allows for the identification of multiple objects within a single image, along
with their precise positions. Object detection is widely used in applications where under-
standing both the presence and the location of objects is crucial.
Using the same classes as before, figure 4.2 shows an example of how Object Detection
can be used.
Figure 4.2: Side by side comparison of using Image Classification and Object Detection
to find the classes ”Cat” and ”Dog”.
These examples illustrate the utility of object detection for identifying print failures, pro-
viding users with an intuitive method for understanding which specific parts of the print
the model has determined to contain spaghetti-like failures. This capability enhances
the user’s ability to diagnose and address print issues effectively by clearly indicating the
locations of detected failures within the 3D print.
Image Segmentation
Image segmentation represents a more advanced form of object detection. Instead of
producing a rough estimate through bounding boxes, image segmentation generates pre-
cise outlines of the pixels corresponding to the classified object. This granular approach
is particularly beneficial when the application requires an exact delineation of the object’s
shape. For instance, consider a scenario where the user needs to process a series of
images containing multiple dogs and the task is to order the dogs by size. In this case,
pixel-by-pixel classification provided by image segmentation can accurately accomplish
this task.

4.1.2
Generating Datasets For Different Models
Part of this thesis will also look into the optimal method for creating a dataset to be used
in training an Object Detection model. This project will analyse the effect of the following
two parameters, the size of the dataset and the variety of the contents of the dataset.
Generic Model With Small Sample Size
When training an object detection model, it is generally recommended to have a sample
size of at least one hundred images per classification. The initial model in this study will
be trained on a series of images of 3D print failures, specifically those exhibiting signs of
spaghetti, sourced from the internet. In object detection, the quantity of data is determined
not just by the number of images in the dataset, but also the quantity of individual object
instances in each image.
This dataset will contain the minimum recommended size of 107 images, enabling a com-
parative analysis with a substantially larger dataset to evaluate the impact of dataset size
on model performance. The images in this dataset will vary in resolution and clarity and
will be manually labeled using Label Studio.
Generic Model With Large Sample Size
For the model trained on a larger dataset, the images were sourced from RoboFlow.com[13].
This dataset contains 8,853 images, providing a significantly larger sample size compared
to the smaller dataset. Both datasets comprise images of failed 3D prints exhibiting vary-
ing degrees of spaghetti-like failures.
Specialised Model
This study will also investigate the effectiveness of a smaller sample size comprising im-
ages of spaghetti failures on a single 3D model. If successful, this type of model could be
highly beneficial for the mass production of a specific part, as it should be faster to train
due to the smaller dataset size.
4.1.3

In order to evaluate a model for detecting 3D print failures, a 3D model needs to be de-
signed that would have a high rate of failure. The most typical causes for spaghetti failures
are[14]:
• Too high nozzle temperatures that turn the filament into a fluid.

4.3
Evaluation Criteria
The performance of the models will be evaluated by referencing a video recording of the
print process. This recording is annotated with the frame number, enabling manual deter-
mination of the earliest frame at which a spaghetti failure can be detected. By comparing
this reference frame to the detection results from the different models, it will be possible to
identify false positives, as any detected print failure should only occur after the manually
determined frame of failure.
For each model, various parameters can be adjusted, including recording resolution, con-
fidence threshold for detecting failures, and the number of consecutive frames with detec-
tions required to confirm a failure. The results from all combinations of these parameters
will be compared to identify the configuration that delivers the best performance.
