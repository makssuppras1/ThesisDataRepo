Abstract
Abstract
Global warming is causing the frequency of extreme weather events to increase, conse-
quently the number of rain storms is projected to increase 40% over the next 50 years. At
the same time the earth’s population is rapidly urbanizing and it is therefore vital that
cities are able to manage the increasing rain fall and prevent ﬂooding. The key lies in
improving the sewer systems by means of physical upgrades and eﬃcient control.
This thesis explores modeling and control of sewer systems based on the Virtual Tank mod-
eling method, which enables simpliﬁcations of sewer dynamics and enhances the viability
of real-time control. A subsystem of Barcelona’s sewer was modeled and a Model Predic-
tive Controller with hard and soft constraints was designed and implemented. The results
from simulating a 100-year return period storm showed the controller was able to reduce
ﬂoods. Additionally simulations with a 1-year return period storm showed the controllers
ability to reduce ocean pollution. A Gain Scheduled control approach was then imple-
mented to change the control priorities for speciﬁc subsystems and this further increased
the performance.

CHAPTER 1

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
We live in a world with a changing climate, where global average temperatures are rising
and extreme weather is becoming more frequent. Most scientist agree that global warming
is human caused [4], and even the more optimistic projection predict the trend to persist
for the next 50 years. At that point the globe will see an average temperate increase of 2
degrees, accompanied by a huge increase in extreme weather [5]. The intergovernmental
panel on climate change expects an increase of 40% in extreme precipitation and a 140%
increase in heat waves. These eﬀects are potentially already aﬀecting Denmark. In July
2011 a heavy storm caused serious ﬂooding in many parts of the country, amounting to
damages around 6 billion danish kroner (DKK). The storm amplitude ranked it as a 1000-
year return period event, but only two years later in October 2013 the most powerful
storm ever recorded in Denmark hit the country. The storms do not only cause material
damages, environmental damage is just as big a part of the problem. Flooding usually
causes untreated sewage water to ﬂow into inhabited areas, spreading dangerous bacteria
and harmful chemicals. Furthermore it causes contamination to the surrounding nature
such as lakes, oceans and rivers, which can have a huge eﬀect on the ecosystem. A lot of
the damage from extreme weather events can be prevented if countries invest in proper
preventive measures. With more than 66% of the worlds people living in cites withing 30
years [6], urban ﬂood-prevention is one of the most important areas to improve. Urban
ﬂoods happen when the urban drainage systems are not able to keep up with the incoming
rain. Improving the sever systems ability to handle rain storms is therefore a key factor in
ﬂood preventions.

1.2
Context
Sewage is disposed in sewage systems, a network of pipes which transport sewage away
from residential and business areas. Most sewers are made with branched pipelines from
individual homes into sewer mains transporting it to treatment plants. Most urban sewer
networks consist of thousands of kilometers of pipes, all connected in a huge fractal like
structure. In the big sewer mains between city zones, redirection gates are often placed
to control the ﬂow of water. Most cities also have retention reservoirs which can function
as a buﬀer which temporarily stores water, ﬁgure 1.2 shows pictures of the sewer system
elements.
Figure 1.2: Image showing a retension resevoir, sewer main, waste-water treatment plant
and a sewer gate.

A sewer can either be combined which means it carries both waste water and rainwater
or it can be separate where the two are kept apart. No matter which system, all waste
water is carried to a treatment plant where it is treated and recycled back into use or
into nature. The rainwater is instead directly recycled which means the separate system
has to treat less water than the combined system. Furthermore, when a combined system
ﬂoods it will carry both rainwater and waste water inside the city, where the separate
will only ﬂood with rainwater. This means that combined sewer ﬂoods are much more
damaging, because the waste water has a signiﬁcantly higher content of harmful chemicals
and bacteria. Most older cities like Copenhagen, Barcelona and Paris are unfortunately
equipped with the inferior combined sewer as they were built in a diﬀerent time period. The
process of minimizing the overﬂow is thus as important as ever. Another source of pollution
is when the maximum inﬂow of the treatment plants is exceeded. The excess waste water
will then be poured directly into the surrounding nature via an emergency pipe, be it a
river or an ocean. Sewer networks are therefore tasked with multiple objectives, which can
be hard to meet with the increase in extreme weather events.
1.3
Sewer system control
There are two main ways to improve sewer networks; the ﬁrst is to physically upgrade the
system, the second is to improve its eﬃciency by optimizing how it is controlled. Optimizing
the control scheme is the approach taken in this thesis. Today most sewage networks are
either manually controlled or controlled by simple local PID controllers [3]. This in not
ideal, as sewer networks are considered a complex system with many challenging features:
• A distributed system with sometimes hundreds or thousands of sensors, actuators
and controllers.
• Actuator and storage elements with limited operating range and physical constraints.
• Nonlinear dynamics with high dependency on the type of modeling used.
• Subsystems with many unknown parameters such as pipe diameters and slope angles.
All these behaviors would have to be taken into account, in order to control a sewer
system optimally. It is therefore not a trivial task to implement sewer network controllers,
as it is associated with both great diﬃculty and great risk. A huge emphasis is therefore
put into modeling and simulation of such systems, as real world test are non trivial. Despite
the challenge posed in implementing a sewer network controller, there exists a few good
examples. One example is the Barcelona sewer network where Carlos A. Ocampo Mar-
tinez [1] used a hybrid linear model approach and a model predictive control method to
successful implemented a controller. Another is done by Magdalene Marinaki and Markos
Papageoriguiu [3], who used a hydro-linkage model with a nonlinear optimal controller.
The controller was optimised by a feasible direction algorithm and implemented in Obere
Iller (in Bavaria, Germany).

1.4
Aim of the Thesis
The work carried out in this thesis aimed to model and control a sewer network, in order to
minimize the negative eﬀects of extreme weather events. The negative eﬀects include ﬂood-
ing of residential and business areas and pollution caused by waste water ﬂowing untreated
into the environment. Additionally it was attempted to minimize the damage in cases
where ﬂooding can not be avoided. The system performance was therefore benchmarked
on the following criteria:
• Minimization of ﬂooding.
• Minimization of untreated waste-water ﬂow into the environment.
• Minimize the damage in case of ﬂooding.
The basis for the modeling and control solutions developed in this work was the Barcelona
sewer. The choice was motivated by the fact that the Barcelona system has been widely
studied and physical parameters existed and was ready to use. The work presented in this
thesis has drawn a lot of inspiration from earlier work done on the Barcelona system. First
and foremost Martinez [2], but also from the more recent work by Einar and Hannes [20].
First a study of sewer system modeling methods was done in order to choose the ideal
approach. The Virtual Tank model introduced by [22] was then selected, the model uses a
linear modeling approach which is generalised for all points of operation. One beneﬁt of the
Virtual Tank model is that it enforces a signiﬁcant simpliﬁcation, which is desired when
dealing with complex systems. A discrete time linear system model was then created based
on a subsystem of the Barcelona sewer and its sewer elements. The model was further used
to create a discrete state space representation which was subsequently implemented into
Simulink. The Model Predictive Control (MPC) method was then chosen as the control
strategy for the waste-water system. The MPC method is well suited for sewer system
because it enables the controller to anticipate future weather events and output control
actions accordingly. Furthermore it allows for the inclusion of constraints, enabling the
system to work closer to its boundary conditions. Finally MPC’s work well on systems
with multiple inputs and multiple outputs (MIMO), because its control action is computed
by solving an optimization problem subject to weighting factors. The control strategy was
therefore formulated for an MPC with both hard and soft constraints. Having formulated
the controller, it was implemented and tested by gradually adding complexity. From there
the controller was put to use on the Barcelona sewer model, where it was tuned in order to
optimally solve the control objectives. Diﬀerent optimization schemes were explored, and
the resulting Model Predictive Controllers were compared to a simple PID control scheme.
Finally, a Gain Scheduled control method was then introduced to enable diﬀerent opera-
tion points to have separate control priorities. This allowed the system to minimize the
nonlinear cost associated with ﬂooding. The results found where very promising for the
Virtual Tank modeling method on sewer systems, as well as the control of such systems by
Gain Scheduled Model Predictive Controllers.

Modeling Approach
This chapter will give an overview of the modeling methods used to describe waste-water
systems. The Saint Venant equations, Virtual Tank model, Hybrid Linear Delayed model
and channel water modeling are all brieﬂy described. The Virtual Tank model is then
selected as the best suited method for the objectives of this thesis. The choice was motivated
by the Virtual Tank models ability to reduce the complexity of the sewer system, together
with the feature that the linear modeling approach is generalised for all areas of operation.
2.1
Saint Venant equations
The Saint Venant equations describe the ﬂow of ﬂuid under a constant pressure.
The
equations are derived from the equations of conservation of mass and the conservation of
linear momentum1. When modeling water systems such as pipes and rivers, where the
water only ﬂows in one direction, the 1-D form of the equation is commonly used. This
form of the equation has 5 assumptions which are [9]:
i The ﬂow is one-dimensional.
ii The distribution of pressure is hydrostatic2.
iii The velocity is uniform over a channel section.
iv The average channel bed slope is small.3
v The ﬂow is incompressible.

The Virtual Tank model (VT) is a control oriented model developed by Gelormino and

the equation of mass conservation. The Virtual Tank model introduces an element based
approach, where a sewer network is described as a series of interconnected catchment areas,
with a catchment area being the area from which rainfall ﬂows into a sewer. This means
that the model describes both the sewer and the above area collecting the rain and waste

volume of sewage inside the sewer mains associated with a determined subcatchment
of a given sewer network. The sewage volume is computed via the mass balance of
the stored volume, the inﬂows and the outﬂows related to the sewage mains, and the
equivalent inﬂow associated with rainwater [22].

The Hybrid Linear Delayed Model (HLD) is an extension of the VT model which adds
additional modeling dynamic options to the VT approach. The main addition is its ability

2.4
Channel water modeling
Here 3 channel water modeling types will be brieﬂy introduced, the Integrator-delay model
the Integrator-delay with zero and the Distributed Muskingum model. These models are
designed to describe ﬂow in channels and pools, and are therefore less suited as a singular
modeling approach, however combined with other methods they could be useful in systems
containing channels. The Integrator-Delay (ID) model uses ﬂow changes in the upstream
of a channel and relates this to the water level downstream. The model has two parts, a
delay time describing the propagation time for changes in water levels, and an integrator
which describes the volume of water in the channel. One downside to the ID model is that
it does not take oscillations made by ﬂow changes into account. Litrico and Fromion [8]
therefore proposed an expansion to the ID model by adding a zero. This zero would account
for the missing frequency response caused by changes in water level. This model is called
Integrator-Delay with zero (IDZ). The last model is called the Distributed Muskingum (DM)
[19] model, after the Muskingum River where it was ﬁrst proposed as a hydrological model.
This model is based on similar principles of inﬂow upstream and outﬂow downstream
determining the total volume, and a lag constant determining the propagation time. One
diﬀerence lies in the method by which the constants are determined, which in the end has
an eﬀect on how the system behaves in a control scenario. The other diﬀerence is that
DM allows for channel segmentation, such that one pool can be segmented into smaller
parts. In practice the IDZ model was found to be more responsive and energy eﬃcient,
but the DM model produced better results when working with a predictive control law [7].
The IDZ model would therefore be a suitable addition if the modeled system happened to
include a river or a channel.
2.5
Modeling choice
In this chapter, 3 diﬀerent modeling approaches for sewer systems have been introduced, as
well as 3 models for water channels. The Saint Venant method was excluded initially, as its
complexity makes it unsuited for a real-time control system. The choice for the sewer model
therefore fell between the VT model or the HLD model. At ﬁrst the HLD model seemed
the best choice, as it is an expansion of the VT, and consequently is a more precise method.
With the consideration of computation time the beneﬁts were rather vague, as even though

the HLD does add additional complexity, it has also been proven to function for real time
sever network applications. The choice in the end became the Virtual Tank model because
of its simplicity, the reason being that, if the simple solution solves the problem then there
is no reason to use the more complicated solution. The simpler solution should therefore be
tested to see if it is suﬃcient, if not, the more complex solution could then be used. Finally,
an argument can be made that companies might prefer a simpler solution over a complex
solution, even if the complex solution is more eﬃcient. The priorities for a company is
not solely based on eﬃciency, and usually includes concerns about the resources needed
to implement such systems and risk of failure, which would arguably be more for a HLD

channels should be included. As a priority for the thesis was to save time, it was decided not
to include a channel, as the most ideal sewer system to model at the time was a subsystem
of Barcelona sewer for which parameters already existed. This model did not contain any
rivers or channels, and the choice was therefore not to include channel modeling. The
conclusion was therefore to use the Virtual Tank model as the sole approach for modeling
sewer systems in this thesis.

Sensors
Sensors are necessary in order to observe what happens inside the waste network. Sensors
are therefore quite commonly found throughout the sewer network, with the most common
sensor [2] being level gauge sensors (Limnimeters). Level gauge sensors measure the volume
of water inside a pipe or water reservoir. The water level can then be converted into an
estimation of ﬂow in a given pipe.
Treatment plants
Waste-water treatment plants (WWTP) are used to clean sewage water in order to make
it reusable and to minimize damage to the environment. The plants are able to remove
physical debris, chemicals and biological contamination from the waste water. A big part
of the treatment is done by bacterial cultures, which are able to process the unwanted
contaminants. The disadvantage is that this makes the plant sensitive to the water ﬂow
into the plant.
The inﬂow to a WWTP is therefore limited to the highest amount of
water the plant can handle without damaging its bacterial culture. Any water exceeding
this amount will be directed though a bypass to the sea or a river.
A sewer network
should therefore strive to process as much waste water as possible while staying below this
threshold.

The rainfall used in this thesis will be based on two diﬀerent rain scenarios, one with a 100-
year return period and another with a 1-year return period. Return period is a probabilistic
value which expresses the chance of seeing an event in a given year. A single year can
expect a probability of 1% of having 100-year storm, or in a period of 20 years there is
a 1.6% chance to have two 100-year storms [18]. In contrast to what the name implies
there is no periodic relationship, only a probabilistic. The reason for choosing these two
types of storms was that, to know the real performance of a sewer controller, it should be
tested both in regular weather and in an intense storm. Rainfall is commonly categorized
by its intensity-duration-frequency (IDF) relationship [14]. In ﬁgure 3.5 Barcelona’s IDF
curves are plotted [15], where each curve shows the intensity-duration ratio for a given
return period. This clearly illustrates the common perception that intense storms tend to
be short while a mild drizzle can go on for long periods. The frequency aspect shows that
storms with higher intensities are rarer. From ﬁgure 3.5 the intensity of a 3-hour 100-year
rainfall is found to be 63 mm/h and for a 1-year it would be 31 mm/h. These values can

Control Formulation and Analysis
4.1
Control methodology
The management of a waste-water network proposes many challenges to the control system.
The system has to handle physical constraints, such as the limited capacity in a sewage
pipe or mass conservation of water ﬂowing into and out of a pipe junction. Additionally the
controller should be able to react on the weather forecast such that all known information
is utilized. The controller would have to handle multivariable system as sewers are always
made up of a network of pipes, tanks, sensors and actuators.
4.2
Model Predictive Control
Within sewer network control one particular strategy is well suited to solve the challenges
mentioned above. This is known as Model Predictive Control (MPC). This method func-
tions by solving a optimisation problem subject to constraints and future input signals.
One thing this allows for is control much closer to the physical constraints than what is
possible with regular linear regulators [11]. It also allows the system to handle weather
forecast as these are a estimation of future input signals. The controller is constructed
according to the model of the plant and can therefore be used in almost all systems [10].
The MPC controller is similar to Linear-Quadratic Regulators (LQR), as both try to
minimise a cost function over a given time horizon9. A LQR does not allow for speciﬁc pole
placement10 but instead relies on design of weighting matrix for which an optimal solution
will be found [12]. The weights will determine how “important” each system parameter is
in the optimisation process, the parameters could be the control eﬀort, state value, etc..
LQR design is therefore usually an iterative process where a control engineer goes through
multiple version of the controller before ﬁnding the ideal one. These principles also apply to
MPC controllers with the exception that LQR allows for an inﬁnite control horizon which

for ﬁnite horizons

MPC does not. It should be noted however that MPC is not just one control method but
includes a wide ﬁeld of control methods which are all developed around the same basic
elements. The basic elements are as follows:
• A prediction model, which incorporates the system’s dynamics such that future be-
havior of the system can be predicted.
• A cost function, which serves as an objective for the system by taxing any derivation
from set trajectories. Minimisation of such function will therefore give a controller
which behaves optimally in regard to set trajectory and weights.
• A control signal computation, which is produced by solving a set of equations and in
turn used as input into the system.
The prediction of the system behavior is most commonly done over a certain time
referred to as the ﬁnite horizon. This time period is then split into a number of discrete
intervals for which the input signal is held constant. An MPC is therefore almost always
implemented in discrete time, with a prediction horizon Np corresponding to the number
of future samples it predicts the system output. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 4.1 with the
black arrow representing the prediction horizon Np, it can be seen that samples k to k +Np
is being predicted at time k, corresponding to the predicted output marked in brown. An
MPC implementation can choose to calculate fewer input values than the number of states
it predicts. The number of input values it calculates is equal the to control horizon Nc,
also marked by an arrow. It should be noted that the control horizon is limited to values
less than the prediction horizon, Np ≥Nc [11]. If the Np > Nc the MPC assumes the last
input value to be constant in the rest of the prediction horizon, this is also shown in ﬁgure
4.1. Having Np > Nc would reduce the number of equations in the quadratic problem
and therefore also the calculation time, the downside is that this limits the precision of
the prediction. For this thesis the prediction horizon Np will be kept equal to the control
horizon Nc because the focus is not to minimize the computation eﬀort but rather on the
systems performance.

Quadratic Programming
The objective of Quadratic Programming is to ﬁnd an n-dimensional vector u that
will minimize f(u) given as:
f(u) = 1
2uTHu + gTu
(20)
subject to Fu ≤b.
Where H is real symmetric matrix called the Hessian, g is vector called the Gramian,
F and b are the constraints matrices. This Quadratic programming problem is convex
therefore only one minima exist, which guarantees termination of the optimization problem.
The structure of this algorithm enables users to calculate the number of operations needed
to solve the problem and therefore also the computation time. These two facts are extremely
important for real-time application and is one of the reasons MPC is widely used. One issue
that can occur is that the problem does not have a solution within the given constraints.
This will make most algorithm stop and return an error message. This is of cause not ideal
and any implementation should avoid these cases by either using soft constraints or having
some kind of back up method. Soft constraints is a type of constraint which if exceeded
adds a cost to the cost function. Soft constraints are explained further in chapter 4.4. In
this thesis the Matlab function quadprog from the optimization toolbox will be used to
solve the QP problem11.

5.2

To verify whether the unconstrained MPC implementation is functioning correctly, the
MPC will be compared to an Linear Quadratic Regulator. An MPC controller without
constraints functions identically as a LQR. Both controllers try to minimise a cost function
over a given time horizon, so given the same cost function and weights, the controllers
should act identically. The only diﬀerence being the horizon from which it optimizes, the
LQR is able to have an inﬁnite horizon in contrast to the MPC which only includes ﬁnite
horizons. A inﬁnite horizon LQR was implemented in Simulink with the same weights
and cost function as the unconstrained MPC based on equation 42. The Simulink model
can be seen in Appendix 8.3. As the MPC can only have a ﬁnite prediction horizon, the
behavior of the MPC should gradually get closer that of the LQR as the prediction horizon
of the MPC is increased. The MPC is therefore plotted with three diﬀerent prediction
horizons, Hp = 5, Hp = 10 and Hp = 20. To compare the two controllers in detail, a plot
of the volume and control signals for the ﬁrst tank is seen in ﬁgure 5.4, the full control
and state plots can be seen in Appendix 8.4. Looking at the control signal, it is apparent

The input constraints are implemented to simulate four types of physical limitations on
the sewer gate model. The ﬁrst is that the maximum ﬂow out of a gate is the ﬂow into
it. u1 is therefore limited to an input amplitude of the disturbance d1, denoted u1,max,d1.
u2 to the outﬂow of tank 1, giving u2,max,qout,1 and u3 to the outﬂow of tank 2 u2,max,qout.
The second limit is enforced by the pipes which all have a maximum ﬂow capacity. A
redirection gate therefore has two limits enforced on it, one by each of its outﬂow pipes.
This is realised as an upper umax,pipe and lower bound umin,pipe. The third constraint needs
a bit more explanation. The key is to notice that the ﬁrst column of the disturbance matrix
only aﬀects tank 3, which means that the model assumes that all water coming in through
redirection gate 1 ﬂows through to tank 3. This is not physically correct, however it is a
reasonable approximation of what happens if the retention gate is modeled correctly. The
explanation for why it works, despite it being physically incorrect, lies in the input matrix
B and the constraints on the input u1. The ﬁrst column of B, corresponding to actuator u1,
can move water from tank 1 to 3 and vise versa. This is not correct unless u1 is constrained
such that the actuator newer lets water ﬂow from tank 1 to tank 3, as the water is already
assumed to initially ﬂow into tank 3. The third constraint is therefore u1 ≥0. A similar
limitation acts on u2 and u3 that are therefore also constrained to positive values.

6
Controller tuning
In this chapter, the MPC controller will be tuned to solve the control objectives of the
Barcelona sewer. The tuning will focus on two objectives in this chapter, the ﬁrst objective
is to minimize overﬂow which can cause huge damages to the city, but which only happens
during intense storm events.
The second is to have a steady ﬂow to the WWTP, not
exceeding its maximum inﬂow capacity which would result in pollution of the ocean. To
achieve the ﬁrst objective, the controller will ﬁrst be optimized to minimize overﬂow by
benchmarking it on a 100-year return period storm. Next another controller is optimized
for optimal ﬂow to the WWTP, by benchmarking it with a 1-year return period rain
storm. The two controllers are compared throughout the chapter to decide which approach
performs best.
6.1
Optimal predictions
The eﬀects of the prediction horizon Np on the system is analysed in order to ﬁnd the
optimal number of samples the MPC predicts. The analysis here tries to ﬁnd the smallest
horizon which still gives an optimal or almost optimal performance. The reason is that
control horizon is a major factor in regards to the computation time involved in solving
the QP problem. The eﬀect of having diﬀerent horizons is studied by making controllers
with varying Np and simulating them react to a short storm event. The controllers have
Np = 3, 10, 40, and the resulting state and input values are plotted in ﬁgures 6.1 and 6.2.
Looking at the state values of tank 1 it is observed that the controllers with the longest
prediction horizon react ﬁrst to the incoming rain. It is also observed that the controller
with Np = 40 seems to have the smallest error, however Np = 10 only has a bit more. For
the Hp = 3 controller, the error is bigger as it fails to ”prepare” for the incoming storm.
The two other controllers on the other hand seems to ”prepare” tank 2 and tank 3 before
the rain arrives at t = 275 min. This is a good indication that prediction is being done by
the controller. Similar behavior can be seen in the input signals, where Np = 10 and Np =
40 react a bit earlier than Np = 3. For tank 3 the prediction only seems to have a minor
eﬀect resulting in roughly the same behavior seen from all three controllers. Overall this
56

DTU

seems to be a good indicator that a higher prediction horizon is better. On the contrary,
not much improvement is seen going from Np = 10 to Np = 40, which indicates that
increases have diminishing returns at a certain point. The rest of this thesis will therefore
use MPC controllers with Np = 40, with the reservation that Np = 80 will be tested and
if no signiﬁcant performance diﬀerence exists, the value will be kept at Np = 40. In a real
world system the prediction horizon should be adapted to the time it takes the given sewer
to empty itself, as this would prepare it for an incoming storm. This period is more than
24 hours for some systems, which would mean a prediction horizon of 288 samples, which
is quite high for a system with maybe 50 tanks and a sample time of less than 10 min.
A solution to this would be to use a Move Blocking [25] methodology in the QP problem.
This method allows the controller to skip any given samples, and could therefore produce a
system with many samples in the ﬁrst two hours and fewer samples in the next 22 hours19.
This method is however outside the scope of this thesis and prediction horizon will thus be
Np = 40. With a speciﬁed prediction horizon the next step is to tune weights to minimize
overﬂow which will be described in the next section.

7
Gain Scheduling Controller
In Chapter 6 it was shown that an MPC is an eﬀective tool at solving the control objec-
tives of a sewer system. However, it was also shown that no controller performed best
in all scenarios and some compromises had to be made. This chapter strives to combine
the advantages of diﬀerently tuned controllers by introducing a Gain Scheduled approach.
Moreover, this chapter tries to solve an additional control objective which is to minimize
the damage caused in case of ﬂooding.
7.1
Motivation
There are two reasons Gain Scheduling will improve the performance of the MPC controller.
The ﬁrst is that diﬀerent controllers perform better depending on which conditions they
are used under. One example of this was shown in chapter 6, where Ko was shown to
perform better at high water levels, while Kn was shown to perform better around nominal
conditions. The second reason lies in the nonlinearities of the damages caused by ﬂooding.
Under most circumstances it causes more damage to ﬂood two areas by a small amount
than it costs to ﬂood one area by that amount combined. According to DHI [24], the cost
of ﬂooding an area is initially very high and falls as the amount increases. It is therefore
a highly desired feature of sewer control systems to be able to adjust the cost (inside the
cost function) of ﬂooding areas as the storm is happening.
7.2
Gain scheduling methods
Gain Scheduling is widely used technique for the design of nonlinear controllers [16]. The
most common method with gain scheduling is a kind related to divide and conquer approach
which divides a nonlinear problem into linear sub-problems. This method is useful because
it enables the use of linear control design methods applied to nonlinear problems. The
approach used in this thesis will be based on the divide conquer approach, where the
system will be subsystem depending on the state values. However the scheduling will be
based on the predicted value of the state which allows the linear controller more time to
react and should result in better performance.

Two subsystem Gain Scheduled controllers
A gain scheduling controller is implemented to combine Kn and Ko into one single con-
troller. The system will therefore be divided into two subsystem, one is around nominal
conditions, another represents the system when it gets close to overﬂow. The controller
schedule will be based on the predicted trajectory, such that the system can change the
controller preemptively. The threshold for the two states are deﬁned as follows:
Nominal Water Level (NWL) using Kn:
max(xp,1) < x1,max · 0.75
max(xp,2) < x2,max · 0.75
max(xp,3) < x3,max · 0.75
(106)
High Water Level (HWL) using Ko:
max(xp,1) ≥x1,max · 0.75
max(xp,2) ≥x2,max · 0.75
max(xp,3) ≥x3,max · 0.75
(107)
If a state value at any point is predicted to be larger than 75% of the overﬂow threshold,
the high water level (HWL) state will take over and Ko will be the control parameters
used. Conversely, if the state is currently in the HWL state and all values in the prediction
horizon is smaller than 75%, the state will change back to the nominal state (NWL) and
use Kn parameters. In practice this is implemented as a two-state state-machine, with
NWL corresponding to state-machine value (SMV) 0 and HWL corresponding to SMV 1.
A delay mechanism is implemented to only allow a state change every 10 samples, which
ensures that the system does not switch back and forth, the reason being that changing the
controller also changes the predicted state values, from which the scheduling is decided.

Concluding Remarks
8.1
Conclusion
In this thesis a versatile modeling and control method for waste-water systems has been
presented. A Virtual Tank modeling approach have been used to create a simpliﬁed model
of Barcelonas sewer. A Model Predictive Control method was then applied with the main
objective of minimizing the damage from intense rain storms.
First a study on a number of sewer system modeling methods was done in order to select
the ideal approach. The Saint Venant and Hybrid Linear Delayed modeling approaches
where discarded because of their complexity, while the Channel modeling method was
unﬁtting because the 3TS did not include channels or rivers. The Virtual Tank modeling
method was then selected because of its ability to reduce the complexity of sewer system,
as it presents a linear modeling approach which is generalised for all areas of operation. A
discrete-time linear-system model was then created, based on a subsystem of the Barcelona
sewer and its sewer elements. A disturbance model was created speciﬁcally for both a 1-
year and a 100-year return period storm in Barcelona. The models were then manipulated
to create a discrete state-space representation, which is subsequently implemented into
Simulink.
The Model Predictive Control (MPC) method was then used as the control
strategy for this thesis.
The MPC method enables the controller to anticipate future
weather events, and output control actions accordingly. Furthermore it allows the inclusion
of constraints. The control strategy is then implemented for an MPC, ﬁrst by including
disturbance prediction, then by adding hard constraints to the input, input rate and output.
The MPC is then altered to have soft output constraints, enabling the controller to work
even in cases of ﬂooding. The controller was tuned for two diﬀerent weather scenarios. The
tuning process yielded preliminary result on selecting an eﬀective prediction horizon, as well
as methods for tuning the weights on the error, input and slack variables. The resulting
controller tuned for overﬂow Ko was seen to reduce the overﬂow 10 fold compared to a
simple PID controller, and half that of the nominally tuned controller Kn. The nominally
tuned controller Kn was however found to reduce ocean pollution by about 60% compared
76
==PAGE:89==
Modeling and Control of Wastewater systems
DTU
20-02-2017
to the simple PID controller and Ko. The conclusion is that a single regular MPC can
not perform optimally in both scenarios.
This result led to a Gain Scheduled control
approach, where diﬀerent controllers where combined by giving them separate subsystems
of operation. This method yielded a controller KGS which was able to combine some of the
advantages of both Ko and Kn. The Gain Scheduled controller was then further expanded
to have diﬀerent subsystems for each possible overﬂow, resulting in a versatile controller
which takes the nonlinear costs associated with overﬂow into account. Overall, the results
shows a great potential for the Virtual Tank modeling method for sewer systems, as well
as the control of such systems by Gain Scheduled model predictive controllers.
8.2
Outlook
Some ideas for continuing the work presented in this thesis are outlined below.
• The methods presented in this thesis should be applied to a full sewer system in order
to prove the viability for a much more complex system.
• Tuning a full sewer system controller would require some kind of optimization algo-
rithm in order to ﬁnd the optimal parameters. It would therefore be an advantage to
test optimization algorithms such as the Gradient decent algorithm or the Evolution
algorithm.
• After an optimization algorithm is implemented, the system could be used for plan-
ning purposes. While planning the placement of additional pipelines or reservoirs, the
system could be used to test out the system performance of a potential placement.
• The methods should be tested in a non-real-time simulation environment like Mike
Urban [26], which is based on the Saint Venant equations. As this would further
verify the viability of the method before implementing it on a real sewer system.
• More complex weather models, based on real world data and with added stochastic
elements such that the rain is not predicted with 100% accuracy.
• The inclusion of a more accurate model of the waste water contributed from housing
and businesses, which could also be based on real world data with stochastic elements.
The waste water is most likely correlated with the time of day which could also be
modeled.
• A more realistic comparison to the control strategies in use in sewer systems today,
preferably a direct comparison with an existing local PID control scheme. Or al-
ternatively a human operators control-abilities could be compared to an MPC-based
system by running a real-time environment simulating a storm situation.
• To enable the controller to have both a small sample time and a long prediction
horizon, the controller could use Move Blocking [25]. This would reduce the compu-
tational eﬀort for long prediction horizons by ﬁxing the input or its derivatives to be

• Hybrid Linear Delayed modeling could be implemented in order to compare the per-
