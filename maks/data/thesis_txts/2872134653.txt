Abstract
The transient community has over time been dealing with larger amounts of data, the main
focus of this report is searching for Tidal disruption events (TDEs), Supernovae (SNe) Ia
and Fast blue optical transients (FBOTs) that have only been observed photometrically,
and do not have spectroscopic observations used for classification. TDE is a rare tran­
sient type that have slow change in brightness and constant temperature in addition to
being located in the center of the host galaxy, which makes them stand out from other
transients photometrically. SN Ia is common and have a large population with high qual­
ity light curves (LCs). FBOT is rare, very fast evolving and hot that lie in short and difficult
to observe timeframes. FBOT surveys rely on the photometric properties for identification.
The transient search is done in the Zwicky Transient Facility (ZTF) archive using LC
extracted features and host distance, all obtained using the Lasair broker. We show 5
promising TDEs and 5 SNe Ia using a machine learning based Random Forest (RF) clas­
sifier trained on confirmed transient LCs. The RF classifier prediction is tested on spectro­
scopically confirmed transients resulting in a 0.667 F1 score and 0.98 ROC AUC for TDEs
and a 0.918 F1 score and 0.93 ROC AUC for SNe Ia. We provide a list of 26 potential
FBOTs with a mixture of SNe and CV, with 11 of them having no spectroscopic observa­
tions, found by restricting the color and duration of LCs to only include well­sampled fast
and blue transients.

1
Introduction
The astronomical community is facing a Big Data challenge, with the prospect of the
Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST), with 20 terabyte of data every night and ex­
pected 10 times the amount of alerts than the currently largest ZTF survey [1][2]. These
large amounts of alerts require a selection process that does not rely on follow­up spec­
troscopy, as more transients will only have photometric data available.
This amount of data is too large to be reasonably distributed to science users directly, but
instead sent to community brokers. ALeRCE and Lasair are among the brokers tasked
to overcome the challenge of processing alerts, using ZTF and soon LSST to identify su­
pernovae (SNe) and other transients alerts early, and to notify science teams who want
to make follow­up observations. Brokers also provide archives for past transient events,
along with filters to search for specific transient types based on observational criteria [3][4].
The wide surveys contain many different types of transients, for this report we will focus
on TDE, FBOT and SN type Ia.
A TDE is the accretion of matter into supermassive black hole from a star in approach,
producing a flare of radiation. TDEs are rare, and less studied than more common tran­
sients, they have shown to have a distinct and constant blue continuum observable in the
optical spectrum [5]. TDEs are an interesting type of event as they enable the study of
black holes in the center of galaxies, which are otherwise invisible.
An FBOT is a fast evolving type of transients which can reach peak luminosities in a mat­
ter of days. These transients are a varied group containing members of many different
transient types, including but not limited to cataclysmic variables (CVs), SNe Ia [6], as well
as more rare transient types such as SNIbn [7]. FBOTs are very fast and rapidly changing
events, which makes especially spectroscopic follow­up difficult to achieve consistently.
More FBOTs are wanted, to confirm physical models at shorter timescales and help obtain
a new understanding of SN evolution.
A SN Ia is an explosion of a white dwarf that has become too massive to sustain its own
weight, producing a light with a similar absolute brightness. SNe Ia is the most common
SN type, with the separation of SNe Ia from other SN being an extensively studied problem
due the usefulness of SNe Ia as a distance measurement tool from their peak brightness.
There is an increasing pool of transient objects >19 magnitude in the ZTF survey that has
only been observed photometrically as reported by the Bright Transient Survey [8], . It
is possible to find a significant number of TDEs and FBOTs using photometric selection
methods, with the opportunity to learn more about their populations. Machine Learning
(ML) is a good fit for this kind of problem as large number of transients can be classified
photometrically using a smaller sample of transients, with a combination of photometric
data and a spectroscopic classification, giving us LC data along with a ground truth based
on spectra. Multiple classifiers have already been developed using this concept, including
TDE classifier tdescore [9] as well as SN Ia classifier based on the open SN catalog [10]
The goal for this project is to build a RF classifier trained on spectroscopically confirmed
TDEs, SNe Ia and other transients which are confirmed as neither, and compare with
other classifiers. The classifier will be used to classify a large number of spectroscopi­
cally unconfirmed transients in the ZTF archive. The photometric features is also used to
search our sample for FBOT candidates which do not have a well­defined classification,
by instead restricting feature values based on selection criteria used in FBOT surveys
[11].

ZTF survey, data brokers and Transient

2.1
ZTF
The ZTF survey is an optical wide­field survey of the night sky in search of transient
events. ZTF uses red and green optical bands for observation, and provide position and
point spread function (PSF) fitted magnitudes of alerts. Each night ZTF provides over
1 million alerts which are sent to community brokers for further distribution to science
users. ZTF provides further contextual information on alerts such as a real­bogus score
to measure alert quality and a star­galaxy classification scores to help filter alerts [1].
2.2
AleRCE
AleRCE is a Chile based community broker. AleRCE provides an alert pipeline taken from
the ZTF alert with the purpose of alert filtering, crossmatching and classification. Some
challenges AleRCE and other brokers are facing with classification, as described by the
AleRCE team [3] include:
• Balancing purity and completeness metrics.
• Training models on unbalanced datasets, which might not represent transients as a
whole.
• Dealing with spectroscopically labelled data, which is biased towards brighter sam­
ples.
• Combining data from different telescopes with different cadences and filters.
• Connecting with other brokers and follow­up infrastructure.
The ALeRCE LC filter is based on a RF with multiple layers, the first layer determines the
parent classes, transient, stochastic or periodic, the next layer uses a classifier special­
ized for each parent class with a total of 15 child classes [12] , an overview of the classes
can be seen in Figure 2.1.
ALeRCE uses a combiniation of metrics for model validation, an overall assessment is
made using averaged completeness and F1 scores obtained from the test set. A label
specific assessment is done using the confusion matrix to find certain overrepresented
classes. A check for biases is made for each class to see if it matches the expected
abundance and position in the night sky.

2.3
Lasair
Lasair is a UK­based community broker, providing the ZTF alert stream to users with LCs
and context maps. Lasair has a built­in transient and alert filter creator, to make cus­
tomised lists of transient alerts based on user requirements.
Lasair also has an in­built contextual transient classifier called Sherlock. Sherlock is a
boosted decision tree algorithm with 7 possible transient labels based on angular sepa­
ration from various source catalogs[13].
The sherlock classes are:
• Variable Star:
If it lies within 1.5” of a catalogued point source,
• Cataclysmic Variable:
if it lies within 1.5” of a catalogued cataclysmic variable,
• Bright Star:
if it is below the lower magnitude limit and separation is below the magnitude de­

• Active Galactic Nucleus:
If it lies within 1.5” of a catalogued AGN or QSO,
• Nuclear Transient:
It it lies within 1.5” of the core of a resolved galaxy
• Supernova:
If it is not classified as a nuclear transient, but any of the following is true
– within 2.4 times the semi­major axis of the galaxy
– within 50 Kpc of the galaxy core
• Orphan:
If it is not any of the other classes

3
Machine Learning
ML is a field focusing on data­driven decision making algorithms, which can be trained to
detect patterns in large datasets. The type of algorithm used here is supervised ML [16],
which is divided into stages.
• Training: The input data, with predefined targets is divided into a test set and train­
ing set. The algorithm then constructs the ML model that best predicts the targets
in the training set.
• Testing: The model predicts the targets of the test set, and its performance is mea­
sured by comparing the predicions with the actual targets.
• Real use: Once the performance is satisfactory the ML model can be used to predict
targets on new data, and compared with predictions from other sources.
3.1
Random forest
A RF is a method based on the decision tree algorithm, where multiple decision trees
take part in the decision making process. Each tree is constructed by a subset of the
original training data, and the percentage of trees outputting a given target is the target
probability.
Using Multiple decision trees has the advantage of reducing variance, as the target is
decided from many trees and changing the output of one tree does not change the overall
output. RFs also introduces versatility in the prediction as the certainty can be measured
from the amount of trees that agree with the majority decision.

3.2
Metrics
During testing the model will make various correct and incorrect predictions on the test
set. The possible predictions for any target m are:
• True positives (TPm): Supposed to predict the target and does so correctly
• False positives (FPm): Supposed to predict another target, but predicts this target
incorrectly.
• True negative (TNm): Supposed to predict another target and does so correctly
• False negative (FNm): Supposed to predict the target, but predicts another target
incorrectly.

4
Transient Photometric Classification
Here is described the process of constructing a photometric LC classifier, able to distin­
guish TDEs and SNe Ia from other types of transient objects.
4.1
Data selection
The TDE sample LC data and auxiliary information such as angular distance to host galaxy
and galactic latitude coordinates is retrieved using the Lasair API SQL select query. The
ZTF objects queried must have crossmatched TNS object names, and a TNS classifica­
tion matching with the four TNS classes related to TDEs, ”TDE”, ”TDE­H”, ”TDE­H­He”
and ”TDE­He”.
The remaining data sample is also retrieved from the Lasair API, limited to the previous
three years of ZTF objects, named ZTF22­, ZTF23­ and ZTF24­ with more than five con­
firmed transient observations using the lasair object feature ”ncandgp”, meaning observed
magnitude above reference image, and ZTF real­bogus score is above 0.75 [22]. These
extra limitations limit the data size to an acceptable level and helps filter away unusable
transients before processing the LC data.
The non­TDE sample is partioned into three categories. SN Ia, other consisting of TNS
classes not related to SNe Ia or TDEs, and the unlabeled category containing spectro­
scopically unconfirmed sources.
4.1.1
Labelling SN Ia subcategories
SNe Ia are determined from the TNS­classes ”SN Ia”, including the subtypes ”SN Ia­91T­
like” and ”SN Ia­91bg­like” as these are still fundamentally type SN Ia LCs with some
differences. ­91T being a more luminous but otherwise similar lightcurve [23], and ­91bg
being fainter with a faster decline [24]. The SN Ia subtypes ”[”SN Ia­pec”,”SN Ia­SC”]”
is expunged from the data, as these are defined as more exotic SN with inconclusive
behaviour that could hurt our effort to classify.

used by Lasairs Sherlock classifier [13]. The final value is chosen as the lower of the two
options, given it is a valid distance.

Model training
The RF model is trained using a bagging method, first 30% of the data for each target is
reserved for testing, with the remaining 70% used for training. A total of 1000 trees are
trained, each using 80% of the training sample, ensuring uniqueness among trees and

reduces the effect of outliers during training.
Each decision tree is restricted to a maximum depth of 10 splits, and a minimum of 2

A Tree is finished once it has classified the sample it has been given correctly in its entirety,
or has either reached maximum depth or is unable to separate more than 1 object from

ready to be used on the test sample for evaluation.

5
Transient Filtering of FBOTs
In this chapter we search for FBOTs in our sample, by passing certain criteria as described
by [11] and [26]. FBOTs do not have a well defined classification, which means that a RF
classifier cannot be easily trained to find FBOTs, a different approach is to limit our sample
to LCs with characteristics typically found in the FBOT population. Our first restriction is
to ensure only fast transients, we use the time above half maximum t½ as a standardised
method of measuring the evolution of transients, ensuring transients are only counted with
a reasonable amount of change in brightness. t½ is only known if at least one observation
on either side of the peak is below half maximum, in addition we also want to calculate
g­r color so we want both g­ and r­band for the period which immediately disqualifies 75%
of our starting sample from 10366 to 2596. Even the known FBOT classified by TNS,
ZTF23ableqsp does not pass this requirement due to its low number of observations.
Our next requirement is to only count transients that are blue, our set criteria is that the
transient is blue meaning g­r is below 0 across the whole half maximum period, ensuring
consistently hot transients. This removes an additional 80% of the sample leaving 497
transients. with the additional requirement of t½ below 12 days, removing slower transiets
leaves 50 transients on our list.
We want to make sure that the rise measured is blue due to, so to ensure that the rise is
genuine we only use transients with a rise in both g­ and r­band preciding peak brightness,
which keeps 41 FBOT candidates.
Our last requirement restricts our position in the sky, as FBOT searches are interested in
extragalactic transients we remove transients within 15 degrees galactic latitude removing
15 transients which are most likely not SN and restricting our list to 26 FBOT candidates.
In summary we restrict our sample in the following ways:
• well defined half maximum rise and decay.
• A blue color from half maximum rise to half maximum decay, to ensure only blue
objects.
• A t½ below 12 days, to ensure only fast objects.
• A well defined rise in both g­ and r­band.
• An absolute galactic latitude above 15, to remove most stellar transients found in
the Milky Way.
These criteria ensures that our list contains fast, blue transients, with as few as possible
non­supernovae.

RF decision making process
The decisions of the RF trees are supposedly easier to interpret compared to other ML
models, due to their simple structure. Ideally the decisions made by the tree creates
branches dominated by a single label, which is split into smaller and smaller sub cat­
egories. A breakdown of an example tree shown in figure 6.4 can help highlight this
decision making process.
Each node is designated a color based on the concentration of a specific label, green for
TDE, purple for SN Ia and orange for other labels. This tree has been limited to a depth
of 6 to keep the size to a minimum. Some notable properties for each label shown in this
tree:
• TDE are found in two concentration on the tree.
The first and largest group has a slow decay rate <0.02 in the r­band and <0.04 in
the g­band, similar to a large number of other labeled object. Unlike the other slowly
decaying transients the majority of TDEs in this are found close to the host galaxy
center, with separation distances <0.4.
The second group has a higher decay rate >0.02 in the r­band similar to most SN
Ia, what differentiates them is the lack of reddening with g­r change being <0.013
combined with a generally blue color as g­r is <­0.21.
• SNe Ia can be found in most of the decision tree, the distinctive property of SN Ia is
their fast LC changes, particularly the higher g­band decay rate and g­r color change
due to reddening over time.
• other labeled objects are more diverse than the rest, the features which are able
to separate about half of these objects is the fact that they are rising and decaying
slower than SNe Ia but are still reddening over time unlike TDEs.

Sample differences
The spectrocopically unconfirmed sample has some noticable differences, because spec­

being detected and classified, even if the LC is dimmer as the demand for samples of rare
transients is much higher.

8
Conclusion
We present a photometric RF transient classifier, with the purpose of searching for TDEs
and SNe Ia in ZTF archival data, along with a search method for finding FBOTs.
The sample is based on ZTF LC data that is interpolated using gaussian process regres­
sion, along with galactic latitude and angular galaxy seperation as additional contextual
information. From the interpolated LC a number of features, used to discern between tran­
sient types. The RF classifier is trained and tested on LC data which includes spectroscop­
ically confirmed transients from TNS. The search for objects without spectroscopic clas­
sification has led to a number of promising TDEs, namely ZTF22aajnyhg, ZTF23aaerviz
and ZTF22abzajwl, as well as SNe Ia, ZTF22abjgawo, ZTF22abdfnyp, ZTF24abacbwh,
ZTF24aaiwefo and ZTF22aamaabh.
The RF classifier has been shown to falsly classifiy some SLSN and AGN as TDE during
testing, and during the search for unclassified TDEs showed likely AGN with high confi­
dence as TDE. Classifiers such as tdescore has shown that there is much to improve on
the TDE RF classifier in order to obtain a larger varity of TDEs.
For the SN Ia classification there were SN II which were classified as SN Ia with >80%
certainty during testing, which will certainly contaminate the SN Ia sample found in the
unclassified search.
The FBOT search resulted in a mix of 26 SNe and CVs which have to be discerned man­
ually. Using galactic latitude as a way to separate CVs as stellar objects was not as
effective as anticipated. There is also clear limits on the effectiveness of gaussian pro­
cess interpolation when used on rapidly changing objects such as FBOTs.
Improving the search for transients is possible in two main ways. Using a larger sample
that provides opportunity to determine the properties of outliers, combined with the use
of more LC features that better describe specific transient types. Both of these improve­
ments are possible to implement using LSST data which will provide more transients than
ever, and also provide more ways to discern transients with 6 different color bands from
UV to IR color.
