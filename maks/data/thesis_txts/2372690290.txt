II
Abstract 
Major depressive disorder is a highly debilitating disorder affecting approximately 
16% of the world population. Though the neurobiological mechanisms are not fully 
understood, combinations of stress, the immune system and glutamatergic signaling 
may play a key role. The mechanisms underlying the beneficial effects of 
electroconvulsive therapy (ECT), is also poorly understood. Ketamine an antagonist 
of the NMDA receptor and an agonist of the AMPA receptor has been shown to 
induce a fast acting anti-depressive effect mediated through glutamatergic signaling. 
Previous studies show that ketamine increases BDNF mRNA and protein expression 
as well as GluA1 (AMPA receptor subunit) insertion in the synapse, through a 
decrease in the phosphorylation of eukaryotic elongation factor 2 (eEF2). 
Consequently, the aim of the present study was to examine the effects of 
electroconvulsive stimulation (ECS – an animal equivalent to ECT) in an animal 
model of depression-like behavior (chronic restraint stress) on the protein levels of the 
AMPA receptor subunits GluA1 (AMPAR1) and GluA2 (AMPAR2) as well as 
BDNF, eEF2, eEF2-P and mTOR.  
 
The protein levels of BDNF, eEF2, eEF2-P and mTOR were investigated by western 
blots using protein homogenate of hippocampal and cortical brain tissue from rats 
subjected to CRS, ECS and a combination of CRS+ECS. AMPAR1 and AMPAR2 
immunereactivity in the subgranular zone were examined by immunohistochemistry. 
Our results show no significant effects of CRS on any of the examined proteins, 
decreased protein levels of BDNF in animals treated with ECS and CRS+ECS in 
combination, and an increase in eEF2-P and AMPAR2 in rats subjected to the 
combination treatment.  
 
Our results show no significant changes in CRS while the combination of CRS+ECS 
induces more potent stress related changes than CRS and ECS individually. It is 
possible that the beneficial effects of ECS happen immediately after the treatment or 
through other mechanisms. This study could not confirm any similarity between the 
changes in glutamatergic signaling induced by ECS and ketamine; this needs to be 
investigated in future studies, as the mechanisms of ECT is still unknown.

Resumé (abstract in Danish) 
Depression er en stærkt svækkende lidelse, som forventes at ramme 16% af verden 
befolkning. På trods af at de neurobiologiske mekanismer endnu ikke er fuldt forstået, 
menes det at kombination af stress, immunesystemet, glutamat signalinger, alle spiller 
vigtige roller. Den bagvedliggende mekanisme af den gavnlige effekt af elektrochok 
(ECT) er også uklar. Ketamin, som er en antagonist af NMDA-receptoren of en 
agonist af AMPA-receptoren, har vist sig at have en hurtigtvirkende anti-depressiv 
effekt gennem påvirkning af glutamat signalering. Tidligere studier har vist, at 
ketamin øger BDNF mRNA og protein ekspressionen såvel som GluA1 (AMPA-
receptor subunit) inkorporering i synapsen gennem sænket fosforylering af 
eukaryotisk elongation factor 2 (eEF2). Formålet med dette studie var at undersøge 
effekten af elektrochok stimulering (ECS – dyreækvivalent til ECT) i en dyremodel af 
depressions-lignende adfærd (kronisk fastholdt stress) på proteinniveauer af AMPA-
receptoren subunits GluA1 (AMPAR1) og GluA2 (AMPAR2) samt BDNF, eEF2, 
eEF2-P og mTOR. 
 
Proteinniveauerne af BDNF, eEF2, eEF2-P og mTOR blev undersøgt ved western 
blot af proteinhomogenat af hjernevæv fra hippocampus og cortex fra rotter udsat for 
CRS, ECS og CRS+ECS. Immunoreaktiviteten af AMPAR1 og AMPAR2 blev i den 
subgranulære zone undersøgt via immunohistokemi. Vores resultater viste ingen 
signifikante ændringer af CRS ved de undersøgte proteiner, sænkning af BDNF var 
observeret i ECS og i CRS+ECS kombinationen, og en stigning af eEF2-P og 
AMPAR2 var kun observeret i kombinationsbehandlingen. 
 
Vores resultater viser ingen signifikante ændringer i CRS, mens kombinationen af 
CRS+ECS fremkalder mere potente stressrelaterede ændringer, end CRS og ECS gør 
hver især. Det er muligt, at de gavnlige effekter af ECS sker umiddelbart efter 
behandlingen eller gennem andre mekanismer. Vores undersøgelse fandt ingen 
sammenhæng mellem ændringerne i glutamat systemet induceret af ECS og ketamin. 
Dette skal undersøges i fremtiden, da mekanismerne bag ECT stadigvæk er ukendte.

Introduction 
 
Depression 
Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) is a highly pervasive disorder, ranked by the 
World Health Organization (WHO) as the 4th leading cause of disability worldwide. 
In 2020 it is estimated to be ranked 2nd after diabetes and cardiovascular diseases (1, 
2). The lifetime prevalence of having a major depressive episode (MDE) is estimated 
to be 16%, with an estimated burden of 210.5 billion dollars in 2010 in the US alone 
(3). The prevalence of MDE is not uniformly distributed throughout the human 
lifespan. Consequently, young people have a higher expected life-time prevalence of 
MDE (4), coupled with suicide being the second leading cause of death among young 
people. The onset of MDD is typically observed in the late teens into the mid-
twenties, affecting women more than men in a 2:1 ratio (2). Between 33-50% of 
patients suffering from MDD will have recurrent episodes in a given year (1, 5).

MDD is associated with both social and economical burdens such as increased risk of 
leaving an education, martial dysfunction, loss of ability to work and lower income as 
well as increasing the risk of suicide. Furthermore, MDD has been associated with 
high comorbidity of anxiety, chronic pain, cancer, diabetes and many more illnesses 
(1). Despite that antidepressant treatment is beneficial in most MDD cases, remission 
does not always provide complete removal of all symptoms, especially the social 
dysfunction and comorbidities remain which can cause further distress in the patient 
(2). 
 
MDD also affects the brain. Postmortem studies of severely depressed patients show 
decreased volume of the prefrontal cortex (PFC) and the hippocampus, impaired 
neurogenesis in the hippocampal dentate gyrus and an increased atrophy of dendrites 
in the hippocampus following depression (10–12). Contrary, magnetic resonance 
imaging (MRI) shows that the loss of hippocampal volume is reversed in patients 
treated with electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) and other antidepressants (13, 14).  
 
One setback in the diagnosis of MDD is the lack of biomarkers. Consequently the 
diagnosis of MDD is still entirely based on questioners. A study examining 
biomarkers in both melancholic and atypical depression found that the most 
promising biomarkers are involved with inflammation and metabolism (15). 
Furthermore, brain derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) was previously investigated 
as a potential biomarker, but turned out unreliable (16–18).  
 
Depression and Stress 
Depression is a highly heterogeneous disorder, with a broad array of symptoms (table 
1) and a complex etiology. Genes, neurobiology, and environment all influence the 
development and progression of the disorder (19, 20). In this study we have used an 
animal model of stress-induced depression wherefore the main focus is on stress and 
depression in the following paragraphs.  
 
Stressful, traumatic early life experiences (ELE) have a major impact on not only the 
risk of developing MDD but also dysfunction of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal 
(HPA)-axis (21). In animal models of early life stress like maternal separation 
==PAGE:11==
 
3
previous studies have shown changes in brain regions such as amygdala, 
hippocampus and the PFC which in turn may affect cognition, emotional behavior and 
neuroendocrine functions (22). These changes can be seen through oxidative stress, 
inflammation and decrease in functional connectivity. Individuals subjected to 
negative stressors in early childhood (parental deprivation, neglect, abuse, or exposure 
to threats) do not only have an earlier onset of psychiatric disorders, but also a higher 
risk of self-harm as well as a poorer response to treatment in several disorders (22, 
23). In a follow-up study, childhood maltreatment was shown correlated with 
increased levels of awakening cortisol after 25 years (21), and stressful ELE and 
parental stress, have been linked to variations in methylation of the glucocorticoid 
receptors (GR) and other genes involved in the HPA-axis, (24). Overall variations in 
DNA methylation in MMD patients have been observed in brain regions such as the 
hippocampus (25–27).  
 
Not only traumatic ELE but also stress caused by social ranking increases the risk of 
depression. In primates, low social ranking causes elevated cortisol levels, which may 
lead to an increased immune activation, hypertension, cardiovascular diseases as well 
as reproductive dysfunction (28, 29). Subordinate monkeys tend to show resemblance 
of human anxiety disorder with hyper-vigilance, constant scanning of the environment 
for threats, depressive-like behavior, and decrease food intake (28). In humans, the 
stress associated with lower socio-economical status show adverse effects in many 
realms of health and an increased risk of psychiatric disorders (28).  
Social isolation and loneliness have even been linked to consistent changes in 
leukocytes and associated with gene expression of inflammatory genes (30). These 
immune regulation can in turn lead to increased activation of the amygdala, deficient 
hippocampal neuroplasticity and changes in hippocampal pyramidal neurons (30). 
 
As mentioned, the HPA-axis is a central systems involved in the regulation of stress. 
In humans, fear recognition is mediated through the amygdala, which in turn activates 
the HPA-axis. This activation results in release of corticotrophin-releasing hormone 
(CRH) from the hypothalamus. CRH signals the pituitary gland to secrete 
adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH), which causes the release of glucocorticoids 
(cortisol in humans, corticosterone in rodents) into the blood from the adrenal cortex. 
Glucocorticoids have a negative feedback loop on the hypothalamus and pituitary 
==PAGE:12==
 
4
gland that is mediated through the GR lowering the secretion of glucocorticoids from 
the adrenal cortex (fig. 1) (31). As half of MDD patients have hypersecretion of 
cortisol (6, 11, 32), and MDD have been linked to elevated CRH in cerebrospinal 
fluid (20), a maladaptive regulation of the HPA-axis is one theory behind MDD. 
Elevated levels of glucocorticoids impair hippocampal long-term potentiation (LTP) 
and induce long-term depression (LTD), and enhances amygdala dependent fear 
conditioning and anxiety-like behavior, which is also observed in MDD (33, 34).

Both the hippocampus and PFC contain neurons that are glucocorticoid receptor 
enriched, and chronic exposure of cortisol in the CNS act as an inflammatory 
stimulant (32). This effect can be facilitated by decrease in glutamate reuptake, 
altering free cytosolic calcium as well as oxygen radicals. It is therefore suggested 
that the increased immune activation by glucocorticoids increase hippocampal cell 
death (32). Prolonged periods of chronic stress impairs the PFC and hippocampal

dependent cognitive functions and cause impairment of memory (33–36). Chronic 
stress induces atrophy, suppresses neurogenesis and impairs hippocampal functions.  
 
The immune system under stress 
Elevated levels of glucocorticoids are able to increase the pro-inflammatory activity 
of the immune system (6, 30, 37, 38), and patients treated with pro-inflammatory 
cytokines such as INF-α display side effects of depressive behavior indistinguishable 
from MDD (39). Non-steroid anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), affecting GRs have 
a better antidepressant effect than placebo (39, 40). Increased immune activation can 
lead to increased oxidative stress, and a meta-analysis of patients suffering from 
MDD show an increased in markers of oxidative stress (41).  
The activation of the immune system affects the signaling of synapses, consequently 
interfering with serotonin and glutamate release, binding and reuptake. Furthermore, 
pro-inflammatory cytokines induce the enzyme indolamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO) 
which can affect the serotonin metabolism (30, 39, 40, 42). IDO in microglia increase 
production of quinolinic acid (QA) which is an agonist of the NMDA receptor (22, 
30, 40) and inhibits the reuptake of glutamate through disruption of the excitatory 
amino acid receptor 1 and 2 (EAAT1-2) and inhibition of glutamine synthetase (30, 
34, 43). Polymorphisms in the genes of cytokines IL-1b, IL-6, IL-10, and C-reactive 
protein are all linked to lowered response to antidepressant medication (44, 45), and 
one third of all depressed patients also have elevated C-reactive protein in their serum 
(21). Finally, stressful ELE is able to induce a dysfunction between inhibitory and 
excitatory signaling in the PFC, and studies of depressed patients have shown that 
dysfunction of glutamate signaling is coupled with inflammatory mechanisms. This 
suggests that overactive microglia increases the release of glutamate (22, 46).  
 
MDD is a complex disorder that spans a broad array of possible biological 
mechanisms. It is however established that interconnectivity of stress, the immune 
system, the environment and social behavior is part of the etiology of the disorder.

Glutamate hypothesis of Depression 
Excessive amount of glutamate is one potential mechanism behind MDD pathology. 
 
Glutamatergic signaling 
Glutamate is the major excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain binding to 3 different 
ionotropic receptors: NMDA (NMDAR), AMPA (AMPAR), and the kainite receptor. 
Glutamate is synthesized from glucose in astrocytes that plays a crucial role in 
removing and recycling glutamate from the extracellular space of the synapse. 
Glutamatergic receptors are expressed on the surface of postsynaptic neurons and the 
number is tightly controlled, as this determines the excitatory synaptic efficacy (34).  
 
The AMPAR is responsible for the primary depolarization in glutamate-mediated 
neurotransmission. After a depolarization, Mg2+ is removed from the voltage-
dependent NMDAR making it permeable to Na+ and Ca+. This causes a slow and 
long-term excitatory postsynaptic modulations (47). Most AMPAR are 
heterotetrameric assemblies of different combination of 4 receptor subunits: GluA1, 
GluA2, GluA3 and GluA4. The typical constellation is 2 dimers of 2 subunits (48). In 
e.g. the hippocampus the receptor is mainly comprised of a GluA1-GluA2 or GluA2-
GluA3 heteromers, while GluA4 is rarely expressed in the adult brain (48). The 
turnover of the receptor is between 10 hours and 2 days (48). The recruitment of the 
receptor is dominated by phosphorylation of GluA1, and GluA2 is then later 
incorporated into the receptor. The phosphorylation of GluA1 seems pivotal to LTP of 
the synapse and the lack of this receptor causes LTD, by affecting the availability of 
synaptic Ca2+ - permeable AMPARs. 
 
The NMDAR is also comprised of four subunits, with 2 GluN1 subunits in 
combination with 2 additional subunits (GluN2A-D and GluN3A-B) in any 
combination. Most NMDA receptors are predominantly comprised of a homodimer of 
either GluN2A or GluN2B or a heterodimer of GluN2A and GluN2B (49). Studies 
suggest that NMDAR containing GluN2A are located synaptic and GluN2B 
containing NMDAR are extrasynaptic. While signaling through synaptic GluN2A 
promotes cell survival through increase in ERK1/2 and BDNF signaling, 
extrasynaptic GluN2B is involved with cell death (49–51). Excessive concentrations 
==PAGE:15==
 
7
of extrasynaptic glutamate can thereby impair LTP via signaling through 
extrasynaptic NMDARs. Antagonist of NMDAR reverses this effect. Furthermore, 
excessive concentrations of extrasynaptic glutamate mediated by stress and MDD are 
neurotoxic through stimulation of these GluN2B containing NMDAR (34). 
 
BDNF is released in the glutamatergic synapse binding to the tropomyosin-related 
kinase receptor (TrkB) in both the pre-and postsynaptic neuron. BDNF modulates 
growth, dendritic complexity and changes the density and morphology of the spines 
(47). BDNF is furthermore linked to LTP and consequently contributing to 
mechanisms involving in learning and memory. BDNF increases phosphorylation of 
GluN1, GluN2B and GluA1 (52), mediates GluN1, GluN2A and 2B and GluA1 
integration into the membrane and alters protein expression of GluA1-3.  
 
Alterations of the glutamate system in MDD 
Elevated glutamate release and serum levels have been linked to MDD and lowered 
expression of EAAT1/2 and glutamine synthetase have been shown in postmortem 
PFC, of patients with MDD (53, 54). Not only MDD in human patients but also rat 
studies of learned helplessness and stress display rapid changes in the release of 
glutamate (34). This is in agreement with reports showing that inhibition of the 
glutamate transporter in astrocytes is able to induce depressive-like behavior in mice, 
increase susceptibility to chronic stress and disinhibition of rapid eye movement 
during sleep, characteristic of patients with MDD (55). Taken together, this suggests 
that inhibition of glutamate clearance may facilitate the depressive symptoms and 
sleep disruption observed in MDD.  
Downregulation of BDNF mRNA, low concentrations of BDNF in the serum and 
genetic polymorphisms has all been linked to MDD. Patients treated with 
antidepressants and ECT show reversal of these effects (6, 56, 57). A disruption of 
BNDF signaling in stress or MDD is central for the translation, incorporation and 
phosphorylation of the AMPAR. Previous studies show that monoaminergic 
antidepressants increase the expression of BDNF mRNA in rats. Furthermore, the 
localization of AMPAR subunits (GluA1 and GluA2/3) is changed in the 
hippocampus of rats up to 72 hours after chronic administration of classic 
antidepressant drugs (58, 59). Finally both selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors 
==PAGE:16==
 
8
(SSRIs) and tricyclic antidepressants (TCA) affect the NMDAR and AMPAR 
providing further evidence for a link between glutamatergic signaling and anxiety and 
MDD (60–62). 
 
It therefore seems reasonable to assume that the glutamatergic system plays a pivotal 
role in the etiology of MDD as glutamatergic dysfunctions can lead to depressive-like 
behavior or increase the risk of developing MDD. 
 
Glutamate, stress and the immune system 
Both AMPAR and NMDAR are affected by stress, and while acute stress enhances 
the incorporation of both these receptors into the membrane, chronic stress reduce the 
expression of GluA1 and GluN1 (33, 34, 63). Mediated by GR activation, 
ubiquitination of GluA1 and GluN1 is increased in PFC, during chronic stress. 
Chronic stress also decreases mRNA expression of GluA1 whereas it enhances 
synaptic currents through the NMDARs in the hippocampus (10), and this is also 
observed in studies of rodents exposed to stress where GluN1, GluN2A and GluN2B 
all are increased (64, 65). 
Elevated levels of cortisol alters hippocampal neurogenesis and modifies the function 
of BDNF (20, 45). BDNF as well as the downstream MAPK/ERK pathway are 
inhibited by glucocorticoids, inducing oxidative stress (66, 67). Finally, one study 
reported that the number of stressful event and the severity of childhood neglect 
contributes to decreased mRNA expression of BDNF (45).  
The immune system is also able to influence glutamatergic signaling. Thus, pro-
inflammatory cytokines increase glutamate release which causes excitotoxicity in 
post-synaptic neurons (30, 46). As mentioned earlier, the NMDAR, EAAT1 and 2 as 
well as glutamine synthetase is affected by increased immune activation. The pro-
inflammatory cytokine IL-1β is able to lower the mRNA expression of BDNF (45), 
and during stress it has been shown that 70% of the observed hippocampal shrinkage 
was caused by 3 variables: BDNF, IL-6 and cortisol levels (45). The BDNF Met allele 
is also linked to a risk in inflammation-induced depression (68) (fig. 2).

Treatment 
Treatment of depression is conventionally performed using inhibitors of the 
monoamines serotonin, norepinephrine and dopamine (monoamine-deficiency 
hypothesis) (54). Though this type of antidepressant treatment rapidly increases the 
level of these neurotransmitters in the brain, the antidepressant effect is generally 
observed 4-6 weeks after initial mediation (20, 69). Furthermore, the rate of remission 
is rather low with some studies showing remission rates as low as 27% within the first 
12 weeks (17, 70). Monoamine inhibitors may also cause extensive side effects like 
drowsiness, insomnia, agitation, sexual dysfunction and even increased risk of suicide 
(9). Recent studies have additionally shown increased activation of the anxiety 
pathway through the CRH receptor 1 (CRHR1) in rodents (71). It is therefore possible 
that targeting monoamines primarily modulates downstream signaling pathways that 
are responsible for the therapeutic effect, but not the core mechanism underlying 
depression (69). It should be mentioned that depletion of serotonin, norepinephrine 
and dopamine does not necessarily lead to depression (54) 
==PAGE:18==
 
10
Patients in severely risk of self-harm including suicide, do not necessarily have the 
time to wait for the beneficial effects of traditional antidepressants, and a more fast 
acting approach would be desirable. Consequently, ECT is an option whereas 
ketamine recently has drawn attention, as it shows potential as a fast acting anti-
depressant.  
 
Electroconvulsive therapy 
ECT has been part of antidepressant treatment in psychiatry for many decades, but 
stigma still shrouds it. Despite this, ECT is still the most effective treatments in 
regards to MDD and treatment resistant depression (TRD) (72). Today ECT is very 
far from its origin, providing anesthesia and muscle relaxant before treatment (72). 
Though temporal memory impairment is a risk, in very rare cases the effect is 
permanent (73). Further, currently no other antidepressant treatments are as efficient 
as ECT with improvements of depressive symptoms in 70-90% cases (38, 72, 74). 
Typically, patients offered ECT have previously been treated with other types of 
antidepressants without significant beneficial effects (75). The standard treatment is 
comprised of 6-12 sessions (3 times a week) and most commonly there is 
symptomatic improvements within the first week, and full remission after 3-4 weeks 
(74, 75). Typically the “objective symptoms” such as: appetite, sleep, psychomotor 
retardation and overall energy are relieved in the beginning while mood, guilt and 
self-esteem improve at a later time during treatment. (75). However the exact 
mechanisms of the beneficial effects of ECT is far from understood. 
 
Effects of ECT on the glutamatergic signaling 
ECT and electroconvulsive stimulation (ECS – the animal equivalent to ECT) can 
reverse some of the changes induced by stress or depression on the glutamatergic 
system. First off all ECS lowers the ratio of the NMDAR to AMPAR (62), and 
increase in the levels of BDNF (38, 76–78). 
In the hippocampus ECS increases the expression of GluA1 and GluA2 mRNA 
expression causing the receptor to be more Ca2+ permeable, which facilitate synaptic 
plasticity (77). These results are supported by a study by Naylor et al. (1996) who also 
showed increased GluA1 mRNA expression, however they did not find any changes 
of the GluA2 or any of the NMDAR subunits (79). This is in contrast to a study by 
==PAGE:19==
 
11
Park et al. (2014) showing downregulation of the mRNA expression of NMDAR 
subunits GluN2A and GluN2B in the PFC after 10 days of ECS. This effect was 
shown to be mediated by histone deacetylase (HDCA2) (80). Further, a study reports 
increased GluN2A and GluN2B mRNA expression by ECS up until 48 hours after the 
last treatment (81). Thus no consensus of the effects of ECS on NMDAR is present. 
Increased potentiation of the AMPAR and reduced activity of NMDAR, presumably 
through the uncoupling of CaMKII from the receptor, is proposed as the mechanism 
(82). 
 
Ketamine 
Ketamine is a non-competitive antagonist of the NMDA receptor and an agonist for 
AMPA receptors (83). It has previously been used as an anesthetic but recent studies 
has proven that ketamine has significant antidepressant effects in sub-anesthetic doses 
(5, 70, 83–85). However, the clinical use of ketamine raises several concerns 
especially for patients who have shown tendencies of substance abuse and because 
chronic use of ketamine has been shown to change integrity and connectivity of the 
white matter in the brain (86). Preclinical studies show increased anxiogenic-like 
behavior in rats following ketamine treatment, which could be a problematic issue in 
general and in MDD patients with comorbidity of anxiety (87). Ketamine also 
increase mTOR signaling, which has been implicated in cancer development and 
consequently raising major concern for the treatment of MDD (83).  
Eventually there has been a major interest in unraveling the anti-depressant effect of 
ketamine. Ketamine rescues rats from chronic stress-induced depressive-like behavior 
as measured in the forced swim test (animal model of depression) (88). Interestingly, 
ketamine improves depressive-like behavior in rats subjected to chronic restraint but 
do not alter corticostrone or monoamine levels (70). Additionally ketamine reduces 
pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β and TNF-α) dose-dependently as well as increases 
neurogenesis, an effect also observed following ECT/ECS. Taken together, this 
implies that the effects of ketamine includes processes involved in inflammation and 
neuroplasticity instead of hormones and monoamines (89).  
Both ECT and ketamine are fast acting anti-depressant, affecting glutamatergic 
signaling and increases neurogenesis. It is therefore proposed that both treatments 
work through the same mechanisms.  
==PAGE:20==
 
12
Effects of ketamine and glutamatergic signaling 
Interestingly ketamine, like ECT, increases the ratio of AMPAR to NMDAR in the 
hippocampus both acutely and chronically (90). The antidepressant effect of ketamine 
is blocked by AMPAR antagonists, implying that AMPAR signaling is part of the 
anti-depressant effect. A study using AMPA as an agonist and ketamine in 
combination presented a better antidepressant effect than AMPA and ketamine alone 
(91). Furthermore, this treatment lead to an increase in BDNF protein levels. When 
ketamine binds to AMPAR the receptor is phosphorylated which causes plasma 
membrane translocation (88, 92). The phosphorylation of the NMDAR and increase 
of GluN1 mRNA expression is mediated by ketamine. Ketamine also increases 
protein levels of mTOR and BDNF (93), and inhibition of either mTOR and BDNF 
counteracts the anti-depressive effect (94). Moreover, expression of genes involved in 
neuroprotection, neuroplasticity and antidepressant effects (BDNF, TrkB and MAP 
kinase) have been shown to increase in rats following ketamine injections and ECS 
(54, 95).  
While ketamine is an antagonist of the NMDAR, complete blockage of the receptor is 
not favorable. 50% blockage might be the best concentration, as the anti-depressant 
effect of ketamine in rodents is not achieved using higher doses. This suggests that 
specific composition of NMDAR is needed for an efficient effect since the subunits 
have varied affinity for ketamine, or that a regulatory ratio between NMDAR activity 
and other receptors are responsible for the antidepressant effect (96). GluN2B or 2C 
seems to be the target of ketamine as deletion of these subunits mimic the 
antidepressant effect (96). Other studies suggest that the GluN2C has the highest 
affinity for ketamine (97). However the effect of ketamine does seem to have the 
highest inhibitory effect on the GluN2C and GluN2D subunits (98). GluN2C acts 
neuroprotective in ischemia, and this suggests that this subunits have some function in 
protecting the neurons from glutamatergic excitotoxicity (99). GluN2C has also been 
associated with excitatory postsynaptic current (EPSC). This is shown in GluN2C 
knockout mice that have a lowered EPSC frequency and a higher inhibitory 
postsynaptic current (IPSC), and a reduction in dendritic spine density (100). 
Consequently, it is not unlikely that the subunit GluN2C has a major role in the anti-
depressant effects of ketamine (fig. 3). 
Despite these findings, studies in mice using the ketamine metabolite (2R,6R)-HNK 
show a similar antidepressant effect as ketamine – independently of NMDAR 
==PAGE:21==
 
13
inhibition (101, 102). (2R,6R)-HNK does not only increases GluA1 and GluA2 but 
also BDNF in the hippocampus, while it decreases eEF2-P. This, in combination with 
an increase in EPSP and the lack of ketamine side effects, clearly suggests the HNK 
could prove to be an efficient antidepressant (101).

Aim 
Based on results from the literature of the effects of ECT, ECS and ketamine 
treatment showing increased BDNF as well as decrease of eEF2-P protein levels in 
the hippocampus we hypothesized that animals treated with ECS and CRS+ECS in 
combination would show similar results, while rats treated with CRS alone would 
display opposite changes. Furthermore we expected an increase of GluA1 and GluA2 
in both ECS and CRS+ECS treated rats, as ECT may affects AMPA receptors in a 
comparable way as the proposed metabolite of ketamine HNK. 
Thus, the aim of the present study was to estimate the protein levels of BDNF, eEF2, 
eEF2-P and mTOR in the hippocampus and cortex in rats subjected to CRS, ECS or 
CRS+ECS. Furthermore, we estimated the immunoreactivity of AMPAR subunit 
GluA1 and GluA2 in the subgranular zone in the hippocampus.

Materials and Methods 
The handling of the rats, experimental procedures and sectioning of the brains were 
performed by Mikkel V. Olesen and Johanne L. Alemu.  
 
Animals 
All the animal procedures were performed in accordance with guidelines from the 
Danish Animal Experimentation Inspectorate and approved by the local ethical 
committee for Experimental Animals (License no. 2003/561-644).  
Forty-eight male Wistar rats (Taconic M&B, Denmark) weighing 220-250 g, 7-8 
weeks old, were used in this study. Pairs of rats lived in cages with a 12-hour 
day/night cycles and free access to tap water and standard rodent chow ad libitum, 
except when they were partaking in the animal experiments (ECS, CRS and FST).  
 
Experimental grouping 
After 1 week of habituation in the animal facility, the rats were randomly divided into 
4 treatment groups of 12 individuals: The first group of rats were subjected to ECS 3 
times per week for 3 weeks (ECS); the second group was exposed to chronic restraint 
6 hours each day for 3 weeks (CRS) in combination with sham-ECS 3 days per week; 
the third group was subjected to both chronic restraint 6 hours each day for 3 weeks 
and ECS 3 times per week for 3 weeks (CRS+ECS) and the fourth group received 
sham-ECS 3 times per week for 3 weeks (control, Ctrl) (fig. 4). During the 
experiments 3 rats died.

Electroconvulsive stimulation 
ECS is the animal equivalent of the human applied electroconvulsive therapy (104). 
During ECS, the rats were transcranially exposed to a constant current (50 mA, 0.5 s, 
50 Hz square wave pulse) delivered by a PSCC-10 pulse stimulator (DCM 
electronics, Denmark) using metal forceps. A properly application of ECS caused a 
generalized tonic-clonic seizure lasting 20-30 seconds (105). Control rats receiving 
sham-ECS, were subjected to the same treatment but without passing of a current. In 
order to resemble the clinical schedule of ECT, ECS was applied 3 times per week 
(Monday, Wednesday and Friday) between 8-9 AM. Rats receiving ECS and CRS in 
combination were subjected to chronic restraint one hour after the ECS session in 
order to avoid any post-seizure behavioral effects. Following treatment, the rats were 
returned to their home cages.  
 
Chronic Restraint Stress 
Chronic restraint stress has been shown to induce depressive-like behavior in rats as 
seen in (106, 107). The rats were individually places in a flexible wire mesh restrainer 
that was closed in both ends to minimize the space around the animal. This, limit their 
freedom to move but allows free breathing and unnecessary heating of the animal. 
The rats were placed in the restrainers for 6 hour each day (10 AM to 4 PM) in their 
home cage together with their cage mates. The process was repeated for 21 
consecutive days. During the chronic restraint sessions, the rats were placed in a 
distinct room to ensure that non-stressed rats were not affected by ultrasonic 
vocalizations.  
 
Forced Swim Test 
Depressive-like behavior in rats can be assessed through the forced swim test (FST) 
(106, 108). Each rat was individually placed in an upright Plexiglas cylinder 
(diameter 19 cm, height 50 cm) containing 28 cm of water (25°C ± 0.5). The first day 
of testing (experimental day 22) was a pre-test of 15 minutes habituation to the water-
cylinder before the rats were dried and returned to the home cage. 24 hours later, the 
rats were subjected to a 5-minute test. The pre-test and the test were recorded on 
video and a blinded tester scored the total duration of immobility for each rat in the 
==PAGE:25==
 
17
test and the last 5 min of the pre-test. As treatments preceded both tests, the difference 
in %-immobility between the test and pre-test was used in order to exclude the 
variability of behavioral responsiveness in the individual rat (109). The criteria for the 
rats being immobile was when they were floating in an upright position only making 
minimal movements to keep their head above water (110). 
 
Tissue processing  
24 hours after the behavioral assessment the rats were deeply anesthetized with 
hypnorm/midazolam and perfused transcranially with phosphate-buffered saline 
(PBS) followed by 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA). Adrenal glands and brains were 
quickly removed and weighed before the brains were fixed in 4% PFA overnight. 
Then the brains were transferred to 30% sucrose diluted in 0.1 M phosphate buffer for 
3 days, dried using a paper towel and frozen on powdered dry ice. The two 
hemispheres were separated and one hemisphere was selected randomly in the first 
animal (in the next animal the other side was chosen and so forth) before being 
mounted on an ULTRApro 5000 cryostat (Vibratome, USA). Then the hippocampus 
was exhaustively sectioned (Bregma -1.80 to -7.04 (111)) into 15-µm-thick sections 
in the coronal plane and mounted consecutively in 20 series on Superfrost+ slides. 
Consequently, each series had a sampling frequency of 20, i.e. the first series 
consisted of section 1-21-41etc. and the second series of 2-22-42 etc. Finally, the 
sections were stored at -80°C until further use.

Data analysis 
 
Western Blot: The intensity of each band on the membrane was measured in Image 
Studio (version. 5.2.5, LI-COR Bioscience, USA). As housekeeping proteins often are 
used to normalize protein intensity in western blots, we used GAPDH in this setup. 
GAPDH however, proved not to be stable between the groups, prompting the use of 
the total protein concentrations (REVERT stain) as suggested by several papers (115, 
116). Consequently, the protein intensity was normalized to the total protein 
concentration using REVERT Total Protein Stain. 
 
Immunohistochemistry: Measurements of immunoreactivity in the hippocampal sub-
granular zone (SGZ) was performed using an Olympus BX50 microscope (Olympus, 
Denmark) connected to a camera (Basler, Germany), a digital microcator (Heidenhal, 
Germany), a motorized stage (Märzhäuser Wetzlar, Germany) and equipped with a 
20x and a 100x oil-immersion objective. The SGZ was delineated as a two-cell wide 
layer along the inner border of the dentate granula cell layer (GCL) using the 20x 
objective. The quantification of immunoreactivity was done at 100x, applying a 
systematic random sampling design to select the fields of view in which the 
==PAGE:31==
 
23
immunoreactivity was quantified. We used a step length of 0.25µm x 0.25µm and a 
frame/field of view of 0.0625 µm2, resulting in an average of 52 fields of view per 
animal. Using the AuthorTM program (Visiopharm, Denmark), a customized APP was 
created for the estimation of AMPAR 1 and 2 subunits through immunoprecipitation 
in the SGZ. The APP was trained in differentiating between 4 variables: Receptor, 
background, cytoplasm and nucleus. Capturing and calculation of the total area and 
receptor density was done by the APP. The procedure was carried out with NewCast 
software (version. 6.5.0.2303, Visiopharm, Denmark). The total volume of 
immunereactivity was calculated by multiplying the area fraction of immunereactivity 
with the total volume of the SGZ, which was estimated by Cavalieri’s principle by 
Mikkel V. Olesen.  
Statistics 
One-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s post-hoc test was used to analyse the data 
derived from Western Blots and immunohistochemistry. Data were analysed for 
normal distribution using D’agostino & Pearson normality test and outliers were 
identified using the ROUT method with a Q-value of 1%. All statistical analysis was 
performed in Graphad Prism 7 (version 7.02 GraphPad Software Inc., La Jolla, CA, 
USA). The Principal Component analysis (PCA) was performed in R-studio (version. 
1.0.136, RStudio Inc.).

Discussion 
Primary findings 
In the present study we observed significant changes in the levels of several proteins 
in the hippocampus of rats subjected to ECS and ECS+CRS. Specifically the level of 
BDNF was significantly decreased in ECS and ECS+CRS treated rats compared to 
controls whereas AMPAR2 and eEF2-P were significantly increased in ECS+CRS. 
Thus, our initial hypothesis, that CRS would induce changes in the glutamate system, 
which would be normalized by ECS, was not confirmed. Instead these observations 
indicate that CRS and ECS have synergistic effects on the glutamate system as our 
data show (not significant) increased fold changes of eEF2-P and decreased fold 
changes of BDNF in CRS and ECS treated animals as compared to the control. This 
effect was significant in the ECS+CRS treated rats (fig. 9). The negative correlation 
observed in the hippocampus-PCA between BDNF and eEF2-P is in agreement with 
previous studies showing that increased eEF2-P levels silences the translation of 
BDNF (83, 117) and vice versa (118). We did not observe significant changes of 
eEF2, mTOR and AMPAR1 protein levels in the hippocampus or BDNF, mTOR, 
eEF2, eEF2-P, AMPAR1 and AMPAR2 in the cortex. 
 
Methodological limitations  
The large variation observed in the presented data, especially visualized in the PCA, 
should be considered when interpreting the data. First, the step of DAB staining 
during the IHC protocol is very sensitive. If not executed in a precise manner, small 
variation in incubation time may affect the results of DAB staining. Despite exact 
timing of the procedure, we observed tissue that was not homogeneously stained 
through all the sections, possibly caused by slight difference in thickness and irregular 
shrinkage. In order to obtain an optimal segmentation of immunoreactivity this 
uneven staining required customization of several APPs to separate AMPAR1 and 
AMPAR2 receptors from sections with high, medium and low background staining. 
Thus, the variation seen in the IHC could potentially arise from the distinct 
differences of the staining, the variation of each APP developed, and from the 
biological variance in the animals. Despite these challenge we observed a significant 
==PAGE:40==
 
32
increase in the protein level of AMPAR2 in the hippocampus of ECS+CRS treated 
rats. 
Another obstacle was presented by the instability of the reference protein, GAPDH. 
While this was solved by using to the total protein levels, one should be aware of 
unstable reference proteins in future studies applying ECS and CRS. As anticipated, 
no changes in any of the proteins examined were found in the cortex. Changes in the 
PFC have been observed with MDD and ECT, however when sectioning these rat 
brains, most of the PFC was not sampled, leaving us with a region more generally 
termed cortex. Thus we cannot rule out the possibility that CRS and/or ECS in this 
setup induce changes within cortical sub-regions such as PFC.  
 
Stress induced depression (CRS) 
In the present study depressive-like behavior was induced by CRS. Others have 
previously shown that CRS results in depression-like behavior such as increased 
immobility in FST and tail suspension test, lower sucrose preference and longer 
latency of feeding, showing that CRS is capable of inducing a broad range of 
depressive-like symptoms (106, 119–121). In addition to a decreased FST 
performance the CRS treated animals had a significant increase in weight of the 
adrenal gland, a finding that is usually a hallmark of stress (122).  
Despite the behavioral and physical signs of depressive-like behavior and stress in the 
animals, we did not find any significant changes at the protein levels by CRS alone. 
This is in contrast to previous studies showing that CRS significantly decrease 
hippocampal BDNF in rats (107, 123) and in MDD patients in serum and post mortem 
studies (6, 38, 56, 124, 125). 
 
An important issue to address is the time at which the effects of CRS and ECS are 
measured. Many animal models of depression that tests the effect of ECS or ketamine 
are typically examining the effects hours or one day after the last administration. 
Compared to the rats in the present study, this is very different as the rats received the 
last ECS 5 days prior their sacrifice. More so, this setup focused on a more clinic-like 
approach, with ECS distributed 3 times per week for 3 weeks, where most studies of 
ketamine administration typically are based on a single dose. While many studies 
have not had the same stress setup as in this study, the shorter 2-day interval between 
==PAGE:41==
 
33
the CRS treatment and sacrifice makes the results from the stress more reliable than 
the ECS. Studies using FST typically also have the same interval as our study. 
 
ECS and BDNF 
We showed that ECS was capable of reversing CRS-induced depressive-like 
behavior, which is in agreement with previous studies of rodents (76, 106, 126) and 
similar with anti-depressive effects of ECT in humans (72, 127–129). However, rats 
subjected to ECS had increased weight of the adrenal gland suggesting that ECS 
induces a peripheral reaction, without affecting the FST score. Therefore it is not 
unlikely that ECS alone or in combination with CRS is a potential stressor. We have 
previously shown that ECS increases neurogenesis in the SGZ by 260% with 50% of 
the newly formed neurons surviving up to 12 months after end treatment (106, 121). 
Despite the increase in neurogenesis, these new neurons were not the cause of 
improved performance in the FST, indicating that the clinical effects of ECT are 
caused at least in combination with other mechanisms. The study therefore aimed to 
explore the molecular changes induced by ECS and their potential antidepressant 
effects.  
 
Multiple studies have found an effect of ECS on BDNF both at the protein and 
mRNA level in rodents. CRS-induced alterations of BDNF can be reversed by ECS 
applied 10 days prior to a 45 min restraint stress session (107). 
BNDF have been shown to increase 9 days after 7 days of treatment with ECS in rats 
that, 25 days prior to their euthanizing, were subjected to 28 days of chronic 
unpredictable mild stress (76). This effect is also observed in flinders sensitive line 
rats (genetic rat model of depression) showing increased levels of BNDF 7 days after 
a 5-day period of ECS (126). 
In a study using microarrays it was shown that the BNDF/TrkB/MAP kinase pathway 
in the hippocampus was upregulated 4 hours after ECS (95), and BDNF mRNA 
expression was also increased 12 hours after the last ECS in another study (77). At 5 
days (120 hours) after ECS we observed a down-regulation of BDNF in response to 
ECS; this was true both for rats treated only with ECS and rats subjected to 
CRS+ECS. The above studies exemplify the importance of the temporal timing when 
investigating the effects of ECS. Furthermore, the effects of ECT on BDNF in 
==PAGE:42==
 
34
humans have also been studied. Levels of BDNF has been shown to be upregulated 
following ECT (6, 38, 56, 124, 125). 
 
Interestingly, a study in human patients comparing serum BDNF levels after ketamine 
or ECT shows that ketamine increases serum BDNF levels in responders of the 
treatment one week after administration. However, this is not observed in patients 
treated with ECT, suggesting that the effects of the two treatments on BDNF levels 
are caused by different mechanisms (17). Contrary, plasma levels of BDNF are 
increased by ECT treatment in another study, but this increased BDNF levels could 
not be link directly to the depressive symptoms (127). Finally, in two studies of TRD, 
no significant increase in serum BDNF was found following ECT and the clinical 
improvements observed after ECT did not correlate with BDNF serum levels (128, 
130). Whereas the anti-depressant effect of ketamine is absent in BDNF knocked-out 
mice (5), it is unsure if this is similar in ECT. Ketamine and ECT could possibly 
affect BDNF differently as the literature does not provide evidence of similar 
regulation. Even within the realm of ECT the influence on BNDF is still unclear.  
 
AMPAR, mTOR and eEF2 
The regulation of AMPAR, eEF2 and mTOR has not been directly investigated in 
depressed humans or animals displaying depressive-like behavior, however studies of 
the effect of antidepressant treatment have been performed in numerous studies. 
NBQX, an antagonist of the AMPAR, has been shown to inhibit the anti-depressant 
effect of ketamine; Mice subjected to ketamine treatment in a social defeat setup 
showed that NBQX inhibits the anti-depressant effect of ketamine (101, 131) and 
attenuates the phosphorylation of GluA1 (132), implying the necessity of AMPAR 
activation in the antidepressant effect of ketamine. Phosphorylation of GluA1 was 
also observed after 10 days of ECS in rat hippocampus (82) and two separate studies 
confirm the effects of ECS on GluA1 as mRNA expression was upregulated in the 
hippocampus of rats 24 hours and 72 hours after ECS (77, 79). Furthermore, in the 
study by Nordgren et al. (2013) GluA2 was downregulation up to 12 hours after the 
last ECS.  
Zanos et al. (2016) did also observe an increase of both AMPAR subunits GluA1 and 
GluA2 after administration with ketamine (101). Studies investigating the 
==PAGE:43==
 
35
antidepressant effect of ketamine, AMPA and in combination, in rats show the highest 
increase of mobility in FST and higher sucrose preference in combination of AMPA 
and ketamine. This simultaneously upregulated BDNF and mTOR-P levels 24 hours 
after administration (91). Ketamine also increases the ratio between 
AMPAR/NMDAR (90).  
It is not implausible that the effects seen with ketamine, could be similar in the rats in 
this study, however during the 5 day period between ECS and brain fixation these 
changes could be reduced.  
 
Rapamycin is also effective in blocking the antidepressant effect of ketamine by 
inhibiting mTOR and preventing the induction of GluA1 into the synapse (133). 
Interestingly, the mTOR-containing complex mTORC1, a regulator in cell growth and 
regulation, has been reported to significantly changes the components in the 
mTORC1 complex in rats following 10 days of ECS, suggesting that the function of 
mTORC1 could be involved in anti-depressant effect of ECT (134). However HNK 
does not affect the protein levels of mTOR or the phosphorylation of it (101).  
 
With regard to eEF2 and eEF2-P they have only been indirectly investigated in the 
antidepressant mechanism of ketamine. Through blocking of NMDAR in rat 
hippocampal cultures, eEF2 kinase is inactivated decreasing the phosphorylation of 
eEF2 – thereby initiating translation, which is the core mechanism of ketamine (135). 
Lowered levels of eEF2-P results in an increase of BDNF protein levels (136). These 
results are also inferred in rats subjected to a maternal deprivation model which is 
reversed with ketamine (137). In the previously mentioned study by Zanos et al. 
(2016) the metabolite of ketamine HNK decreases the levels of eEF2-P following 
ketamine treatment in mice, without the inhibition of NMDAR (101). 
 
Does ketamine and ECT have the same mode of action?  
Based on the results from our study we cannot confirm that ECS works through a 
similar mechanism as ketamine in regards to glutamatergic signaling. However the 
literature of ECT has yet to describe the function of eEF2, mTOR and AMPAR2 in 
sufficient detail. This leaves a gap between the findings in the present study and the 
literature. The distinct setup of this study and studies with ketamine makes the 
==PAGE:44==
 
36
comparison of the methods an approximation at best. A study comparing ketamine 
and ECS directly would be preferable, possibly alluding to altered mechanisms of 
each treatment, and the progression of protein levels following treatment. A recent 
study did not find any positive effect of ketamine as the anesthetics used in 
combination with ECT, further implying the complexity of the two different 
treatments (138). 
 
Compiled stress 
The results from this study seem to indicate that ECS and CRS compound in the 
ECS+CRS group. BDNF in this group is decreased compared to controls and this can 
possibly be explained by the fact that both treatments are stressful for the rats. This 
would end up in a higher stress burden in this group as compared to CRS and ECS 
alone. This could in turn lead to the observed decrease in BDNF levels and increase 
of eEF2-P levels. Further, the levels or expression of BDNF and eEF2-P could 
possible be different few hours after the last ECS treatment. The results of this study 
may be an effect of a stressful period, suggesting, that the beneficial effects of ECS is 
rooted in changes immediately after the ECS session. BDNF having a short half-life, 
could well within reason be back to the levels seen in stressed animals. 
Stress has a negative effect on BDNF, and glucocorticoids mediate a downregulation 
of BDNF mRNA expression through the GR (139). Further, chronic administration of 
corticosterone in rats (30 days) show mRNA increases of EAAT1 and 2 but decreases 
GluA1 and GluA2 (140). However, those rats did experience maternal separation 
from day 2 after birth, possibly extending the stress stimulation in a manner that 
cannot be compared to this study. Also mice subjected to 7 weeks of chronic social 
stress show increased mRNA expression of GluA2 in the hippocampus (141). The 
paper comments on the possibility that a decreased GluA1/GluA2 ratio is involved in 
the risk of depression, which we also observed a tendency of in the present study. 
This decreased ratio of GluA1/GluA2 is also observed induced by stress in the 
amygdala (142). 
Our results suggest that the ECS+CRS treated rats are more potently stressed, 
however ECS somehow keeps the rats “afloat” as observed in the FST score. This 
implies that additional factors are at play in the complicated intricacies of depression-
like behavior and that lowered BDNF levels in combination with higher eEF2-P 
==PAGE:45==
 
37
levels are not enough to cause the depressive-like behavior. ECS could possible 
stabilize other pathways or systems in the brain keeping the ECS+CRS rats from the 
depressive-like behavior despite the increased impact of stress.  
 
Stabilizing effect of ECS 
The method of ECT has caused controversies through its lifetime.  
While it has been described as a “reboot” of the brain, suggesting all signaling 
pathways are activated, we don’t see this in the present study. We find that ECS 
increases the mobility in the FST and increase neurogenesis but on molecular basis, 
we don’t observe great changes induced by ECS alone. Previous studies using the 
exact same rats show that the volume of hippocampus or its sub-regions are not 
affected by CRS or ECS and that only the number of newly formed neurons in the 
SGZ is increased (Johanne L. Alemu – Unpublished study).  
Maybe ECT should be regarded as a stabilizer, correcting the damage done by MDD 
and keeping systems functioning in the brain during prolonged stress. While ECT is 
not a risk free endeavor the positive effects cannot be ignored. Electric vagus nerve 
stimulation is investigated in the regulating of the immune system and depression 
(143). It seems that simple electric stimulation has a positive effect on depressed 
patients and this potentially could be the same effect seen in ECS and ECT (144). 
Only the future will show which direction this therapy will take, but the idea of ECT 
as a stabilizer could be a new way of thinking about the treatment, however the 
involved mechanisms are still to this day far from understood.

Conclusion and perspectives 
In conclusion, the present findings show that CRS, contrary to our hypothesis, does 
not result in significant changes of BDNF, eEF2, eEF2-P, mTOR, AMPAR1 and 
AMPAR2 protein levels in the hippocampus and cortex. Furthermore, ECS alone 
decreases BDNF levels in the hippocampus, without any other significant alterations. 
This is in contrast to previous studies. Treatment of rats using the combination of ECS 
and CRS decreased BNDF levels in the hippocampus even further when compared to 
ECS alone, as well as increases hippocampal eEF2-P and AMPAR2 levels. No effect 
of CRS + ECS treatment was observed in the cortex.  
Our results do not provide evidence for the antidepressant effect of ECS, as all the 
changes observed in CRS+ECS treated rats most likely were associated with an 
increased stress exposure caused by both treatments. The beneficial effects of ECS 
may arise from other mechanisms or in a short time period after the end of the 
treatment. Our data is ambiguous of whether these effects are directly linked to the 
glutamatergic pathways. As preliminary data suggests that ketamine and ECT act on 
glutamatergic signaling through distinct mechanisms, future studies comparing the 
antidepressant and fast-acting effects of both treatments are needed. Additional 
studies of ECS with blockage of AMPAR, BDNF or mTOR could provide further 
inside of the regulatory effects ECS has on glutamatergic signaling. Patients with 
previous MDE have an increased risk of developing a second one. Further assessment 
of the animals through several cycles of the experiment could possibly induce more 
pronounced changes in each treatment group.
