Abstract 
 
 
The quest for sustainable and efficient ammonia production methods has led to a 
surge in interest in electrochemical synthesis processes. This master's thesis delves into the 
innovative realm of electrochemical ammonia synthesis, with a particular emphasis on 
lithium-mediated processes as a promising alternative to conventional methodologies like 
the energy-intensive Haber-Bosch process. The primary objective of this research is to 
advance electrochemical techniques for ammonia synthesis by exploring and optimizing 
organic electrolytes under ambient conditions to enhance production rates and Faradaic 
efficiencies. Key aspects of the study include the selection and optimization of organic 
electrolytes based on tetrahydrofuran (THF) and diglyme solvents, as well as evaluating 
the energy and mass balance of the process by utilizing advanced techniques such as 
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy and Distribution Relaxation Time analysis. 
Through well-established experimental design and analysis, this thesis establishes 
a framework for optimizing the Nitrogen Reduction Reaction (NRR) process. The strategic 
choice of solvents, electrolytes, and ethanol concentrations plays a pivotal role in creating 
a conducive environment for efficient ammonia synthesis. Electrochemical Impedance 
Spectroscopy (EIS) and Distribution Relaxation Time (DRT) analyses provide valuable 
insights into system impedance characteristics and potential loss mechanisms, guiding the 
path towards process optimization and enhanced performance. 
The findings of this research underscore the critical need for innovative approaches 
in electrochemical ammonia synthesis to address the energy and environmental challenges 
associated with traditional production methods. By contributing to the development of 
sustainable and efficient ammonia production techniques, this study aims to pave the way 
for future advancements in the field of chemical engineering, offering a promising 
alternative to the current ammonia production landscape.

1. 
Introduction 
1.1. 
Background and Context  
Ammonia: From Green Fields to Green Fuels 
The industrial synthesis of ammonia, a cornerstone of modern industry and agriculture, 
has been a subject of intense debate and research, given its pivotal role in supporting global 
food security and its emerging potential in renewable energy sectors. Ammonia, with 
annual production rates exceeding 170 million metric tons by 2015 [1][2], has been a 
central player in sustaining the growing world population, with estimates suggesting that 
nitrogen fertilizers contributed to 42% of the overall birth rates over the 20th century [3], 
discernible in Figure 1, and today, they support half of the earth’s population [4].   
 
Figure 1: Exponential increase in world population due to large scale ammonia production 
[3]. 
 
The primary use of ammonia has traditionally been in producing nitrogen-rich 
fertilizers, a vital component in boosting agricultural productivity. This role has been 
crucial in feeding an increasingly populated planet, with around 80% of synthesized 
ammonia used in the fertilizer industry [5]. Beyond its traditional applications, ammonia 
is also gaining recognition as a potential sustainable fuel. Its high hydrogen content and 
energy density make it an attractive carrier of renewable energy, suitable for heavy 
transport power generation and distributed energy storage [6]. 
==PAGE:11==
  
 
2 
 
Despite its widespread use and benefits, ammonia production predominantly relies 
on fossil fuels, raising concerns about sustainability. This reliance has spurred efforts to 
develop more sustainable ammonia production methods, particularly those harnessing 
renewable energy sources [7]. Looking to the future, the demand for ammonia is expected 
to rise significantly, with projections estimating a demand of 350 million metric tons per 
year by 2050 [8], nearly double the amount of the current production rate. This increase is 
not only due to the increasing world population and the corresponding rise in food demand 
but also the burgeoning interest in ammonia as an energy carrier. The potential for 
ammonia to replace fossil fuels as a carbon-free energy option is particularly intriguing, 
offering a more sustainable energy pathway [5]. 
 
However, the journey towards more sustainable ammonia production has its 
challenges. Current experimental procedures for nitrogen electroreduction, a key process 
in ammonia synthesis, require critical reassessment and improvements [9][10]. As we 
reflect on the Haber-Bosch (HB) process and its impact on the industry, the urgency to 
develop environmentally friendly methods required for increased consumption is further 
emphasized. Considering the historical significance and current reliance on the HB process, 
ammonia’s future direction is not just a scientific or industrial challenge but a necessity for 
the continued growth and well-being of human society. 
 
 
The Heart of Synthesis: HB Process 
 
The HB process, a pivotal invention in modern chemistry, revolutionized ammonia 
production by catalytically synthesizing ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen. This 
century-old method remains the primary source of the world's ammonia, contributing to 
about 90% of its annual production [7] the remaining is produced through sustainable 
methods like electrocatalysis, photocatalysis, molecular catalysis, plasma-enabled 
synthesis, and organic nitrogen fixation through microorganisms[11]. The HB process 
operates under rigorous conditions, typically at high temperatures (300-550°C) and 
pressures (200-350 atm), necessitating large centralized plants and substantial capital 
investment [12]. This high-pressure environment, essential for achieving reasonable 
ammonia concentrations, leads to significant financial outlays [13]. 
 
Despite its efficiency at scale, the HB process is notably energy-intensive. It 
consumes approximately 1-2% of the world's annual energy output and is responsible for 
a similar percentage of global CO₂ emissions due to its reliance on steam reforming of 
fossil fuels for hydrogen production [14][5]. This reliance on fossil fuels, primarily natural 
gas, for hydrogen generation results in CO₂ emissions, almost twice the amount of 
ammonia produced [14], accounting for roughly 1.8% of global CO₂ emissions [8]. 
 
The centralized nature of the HB process, necessitated by extreme reaction 
conditions, leads to additional costs for long-distance transportation of ammonia [12]. This 
==PAGE:12==
  
 
3 
system, which is mismatched with the distributed nature of ammonia utilization as a 
fertilizer, presents a logistical challenge [13]. Moreover, the HB reactor typically operates 
continuously to prevent damage to the catalyst, making it incompatible with the nature of 
renewable electricity supplies, which are increasingly important in a carbon-constrained 
world [8]. 
 
The Imperative for a Sustainable ammonia Cycle 
 
In light of these challenges, there is a growing recognition of the need for 
alternative, more sustainable ammonia production technologies. Efforts are underway to 
develop processes that allow ammonia to be produced at distributed sources under more 
benign conditions [14]. The imperative for a sustainable ammonia cycle is unmistakable. 
The transition to greener ammonia production technologies is not only a response to 
environmental concerns, but also a forward-looking approach to meet global ammonia 
demands sustainably.  
 
The push towards greener ammonia production is not just an environmental 
imperative but also a technological one. Recent advancements in electrochemical methods 
demonstrate significant strides in developing efficient and environmentally friendly 
ammonia synthesis techniques [15], [16]. These methods, which leverage advancements in 
catalysis and reaction engineering, promise to reduce the carbon footprint associated with 
traditional ammonia production processes. 
 
Furthermore, beyond the realm of electrochemical synthesis, a broader spectrum of 
sustainable methods is being explored. Various innovative approaches, including 
photocatalytic processes and the use of novel catalysts like ruthenium and molybdenum 
[11], [17], highlight a paradigm shift in ammonia production, moving away from the 
energy-intensive Haber-Bosch process towards more eco-friendly alternatives. 
 
While the progress is promising, challenges remain in scaling these technologies 
for industrial application. There is an increasing need for continued innovation in 
electrochemical and plasma-activated reactions, emphasizing integrating these methods 
into existing industrial frameworks [18], [19]. The future of sustainable ammonia synthesis 
lies in the successful amalgamation of these novel methods with economic viability and 
scalability. 
 
Green Potential Alternatives  
 
In the quest for sustainable ammonia production, various alternatives to the 
traditional (HB) process are being explored, leveraging advancements in electrochemistry 
and renewable energy sources. These alternatives aim to reduce the carbon footprint of 
ammonia production and enable distributed production models. 
==PAGE:13==
  
 
4 
 
Recent developments in metal-mediated ammonia synthesis have shown potential 
for ambient condition operations, indicating a promising direction for research and 
development [13]. For instance, lithium-mediated cycling processes have been developed 
for synthesizing ammonia from N₂ and H₂O at ambient pressures, offering an effective 
strategy for green ammonia production [20].  
 
However, the development of an efficient and selective catalytic system for a metal-
mediated electrochemical nitrogen reduction reaction (eNRR), capable of splitting the N₂ 
triple bond and stabilizing hydrogen evolution reaction, remains a major challenge, 
hindering its scalability and efficiency [12], [14]. Continuous processes utilizing lithium 
salts in specific electrolytes have shown some potential in overcoming these limitations, 
but further research and development are required to improve efficiency and scalability 
[21]. 
 
Ammonia synthesis, powered by renewable energy sources such as wind or solar 
power as opposed to methane-driven hydrogen-fueled synthesis, offers a pathway to reduce 
CO₂ emissions and aligns with the broader goal of decarbonizing industrial processes[8], 
[5], [22]. Furthermore, this approach is compatible with small-scale, distributed production 
facilities, addressing the logistical challenges of centralized ammonia production and long-
distance transportation [12].  
 
Another promising avenue is high-temperature solid oxide electrolysis (SOE), 
which can utilize waste heat from HB plants [6]. This method could integrate with existing 
infrastructures, potentially enhancing the efficiency of ammonia production while reducing 
its environmental impact. 
 
1.2. 
Lithium-mediated Ammonia Synthesis 
Advancements and Limitations 
 
Lithium-mediated ammonia synthesis represents an innovative approach in the 
field of chemical engineering, offering a promising alternative to traditional methods. The 
lithium-mediated ammonia production cycle comprises electrochemical and thermal-based 
reactions, aiming to achieve high selectivity in the conversion process [23].  
 
The first method involves an electrochemical nitrogen reduction process. It utilizes 
solvents like dimethoxyethane and tetrahydrofuran (THF) for effective ammonia synthesis, 
with the latter achieving a Faradaic efficiency (FE) of 58.5 ± 6.1% at ambient pressure 
[24]. Studies have shown that electrodeposited lithium in solvents like THF can overcome 
selectivity challenges in this process [25]. Moreover, electrocatalyst advancements, such 
as Au-Cu2-xSe, have significantly enhanced ammonia yield and FE in nonaqueous 
electrolytes [26]. Despite its potential, one of the main inefficiencies in this process is the 
==PAGE:14==
  
 
5 
overpotential required to obtain metallic lithium, which sets a clear target for future 
improvements [23]. 
In contrast, the thermal-based methods refer to the chemical reactions that occur due 
to heat application. As shown in Figure 2, after the electrochemical step of molten salt 
electrolysis, powered by electricity, the lithium produced reacts with nitrogen gas at 
elevated temperatures to form lithium nitride (Li₃N) in a process known as direct 
nitridation. This reaction is typically endothermic, requiring heat input to proceed [20]. 
 
Techno-economic electricity cost analysis and energy input considerations reveal 
promise for this process, especially considering its ability to utilize renewable resources, 
mitigate CO₂ emissions, and be readily decentralized compared to the conventional 
centralized process methods [20]. The electrical energy cost of LiOH electrolysis in a 
lithium-mediated electro-thermochemical cycling process is estimated to be around 14 
kWh/kg NH3, which is a highly beneficial result considering that the overall average energy 
cost for the HB process is approximately 10 kWh per kilogram of ammonia [20]. 
 
Figure 2: Schematic of a lithium-mediated cycling process for sustainable NH3 production 
from N₂ and H₂O driven by renewable energy sources [20]. 
 
In the realm of lithium-mediated ammonia synthesis, recent advancements in 
electrochemical processes, particularly within flow cells, offer intriguing prospects. The 
transition from batch reactors to flow reactors in lithium-mediated nitrogen reduction for 
electrochemical ammonia synthesis is a significant development. This shift involves a 
deeper understanding of the mechanical aspects, advancements in reactor design, and 
exploring key variables, assessing the benefits and challenges of both reactor types for 
scaling up production [27]. 
 
One intriguing advancement is using a membrane-free electrochemical cell for 
lithium-mediated ammonia synthesis. This design enhances the efficiency and scalability 
==PAGE:15==
  
 
6 
of the process [28]. Moreover, adding small quantities of oxygen into the reaction 
atmosphere has positively impacted lithium-mediated ammonia synthesis, increasing FE 
[29]. This discovery opens new avenues for optimizing the electrochemical nitrogen 
reduction process. Furthermore, integrating electrodialysis and electrocatalysis for 
ammonia synthesis from waste nitrogen sources, along with using green ammonia to 
capture CO₂ and convert it to ammonium formate, is a promising approach in this field 
[30]. 
 
 
Effects of Electrolyte Optimization 
The optimization of electrolytes is a critical factor in enhancing the efficiency of 
electrosynthesis, especially in processes like nitrogen reduction to ammonia. This 
aspect of chemical engineering has seen significant advancements in recent years, with 
various strategies being employed to improve both the FE and the rate of ammonia 
production. 
One approach involves using proton-conducting ceramic oxides as electrodes for 
nitrogen reduction. Operating these electrodes, specifically oxide or molten chloride 
electrolytes, at higher temperatures has increased the rate and FE, with efficiencies 
exceeding 50% and rates ranging from 10−9 to 10−8 mol NH3 per square centimeter per 
second. Nevertheless, the rate is achieved at high temperatures above 673 K, which 
involves additional costs for applying this method compared to the HB process [14]. 
It’s worth mentioning that without rigorous contamination controls, skepticism about 
the actual rates of ammonia production and the viability of this method for substantial 
ammonia synthesis is plausible. Similarly, using less viscous ionic liquids (ILs) mixed 
with aprotic solvents, along with high-surface area iron-based electrodes, has 
demonstrated an almost tenfold increase in yield rate, though at a lower FE of 32% [7]. 
The implementation of less energy-demanding redox mediators is also seen as a 
key direction for improving the mediated electrochemical nitrogen reduction reaction 
(eNRR) approach [6]. The continuous redox-mediated pathway, first demonstrated 
using a Li+/0 mediator, provides promising rates and selectivity at elevated pressures 
[6]. 
In the field of lithium-mediated nitrogen reduction (LiNR), the solid electrolyte 
interface (SEI) formed from the decomposition products of an organic electrolyte 
during lithium deposition on the cathode plays an important role [22]. Studies suggest 
that a fluorine-based electrolyte, in combination with a highly porous copper electrode, 
can achieve a remarkable 95% ± 3% FE at certain current densities under elevated 
nitrogen pressure [5].

process in organic electrolytes for nitrogen reduction to ammonia is the most efficient in 
converting N₂ to NH3. However, they also noted challenges such as low yield rates, FE, 
and system instability, underscoring the need for optimized electrolytes [31].

1.3. 
Research Objectives 
Organic Electrolytes in eNRR 
 
The use of organic electrolytes in electrochemical ammonia synthesis has garnered 
significant interest in recent years, offering a promising approach to improve the efficiency 
and feasibility of this process. Various studies have explored different aspects of using 
organic electrolytes, focusing on achieving higher reproducibility, selectivity, and FE 
under ambient conditions. 
 
Tetrahydrofuran (THF)- based solvents, mainly when used in lithium-mediated 
strategies, have reported some of the highest reproducible efficiencies for electrochemical 
NH3 synthesis to date [1]. For instance, using a phosphonium-based ionic liquid (IL), which 
notably does not contain nitrogen, a maximum FE of 66% was obtained on an iron-based 
cathode [7]. This approach highlights the importance of rational electrode–electrolyte 
design for efficient ammonia electrosynthesis under ambient conditions [6]. 
 
However, non-aqueous routes, while offering higher selectivity, typically operate 
at potentials that considerably increase the energy cost [6]. This underscores the need for a 
balanced approach where the benefits of using organic electrolytes are weighed against the 
potential increase in energy costs. 
 
An important aspect of using organic electrolytes is forming the solid electrolyte 
interface (SEI) from the decomposition products of the electrolyte during lithium 
deposition on the cathode. The SEI plays a crucial role in facilitating homogeneous lithium 
==PAGE:18==
  
 
9 
plating, suppressing undesired hydrogen evolution and electrolyte decomposition, and 
enhancing nitrogen reduction [5]. 
 
The type of lithium salt used in the electrolyte also significantly influences the 
distribution of synthesized ammonia. For example, using LiClO4 results in a higher 
percentage of ammonia trapped in the deposited layer compared to LiPF6 and LiBF4 [5]. 
This finding highlights the importance of carefully selecting the lithium salt for optimal 
ammonia production and distribution. 
 
The electrolyte composition, including the concentration of ethanol as a proton 
source, is another critical factor. For instance, electrolysis conducted in an N₂ saturated 
THF solution containing 1 M LiBF4 and 0.11 M EtOH has been a part of experimental 
setups to assess ammonia synthesis efficiency [12]. 
 
Scope of the Research 
 
 
In this master's thesis, the main goals and objectives are centered around the 
innovative realm of electrochemical ammonia synthesis, with a particular emphasis on the 
exploration and optimization of organic electrolytes. The research is driven by a vision to 
revolutionize the current ammonia production methodologies, which largely depend on the 
HB process, thereby addressing environmental concerns and energy efficiency. 
 
The foremost objective of this research is to delve into the advancement of 
electrochemical techniques for ammonia synthesis. This includes developing and testing 
different electrolytes capable of operating under ambient conditions to achieve higher 
ammonia production rates with improved FEs. Understanding the underlying mechanisms 
and optimizing the conditions for effective electrochemical reactions will be a pivotal part 
of this objective. 
 
A significant focus of the research is laid on the use and optimization of organic 
electrolytes based on tetrahydrofuran (THF, Sigma, CAS: 109-99-9) and diglyme (Sigma, 
CAS: 111-96-6) solvents in the electrochemical synthesis process. The research also aims 
to evaluate the energy and mass balance analysis of the electrochemical synthesis of 
ammonia. This evaluation has a particular focus on Electrochemical Impedance 
Spectroscopy (EIS) to identify losses in the system. 
 
Improving the selectivity and reproducibility of ammonia production within 
organic electrolytes is another key objective, aiming to develop more controlled and 
consistent synthesis methods. 
 
By achieving these objectives, the research intends to significantly contribute to the 
field of chemical engineering by providing a sustainable, efficient alternative to the 
==PAGE:19==
  
 
10 
traditional methods of ammonia production, paving the way for future innovations in this 
critical area.

2. 
Methodology 
2.1. 
Experimental Design 
Experimental Setup Overview 
 
Figure 4: Experimental Setup Overview [35] 
 
The experimental setup shown in figure 4 for lithium-mediated ammonia synthesis 
is designed to investigate the efficiency and feasibility of using lithium as an 
electrochemical mediator in the nitrogen reduction reaction (NRR). The apparatus is 
constructed to provide a controlled environment for the response, capture evolved gases 
for analysis, and ensure safety throughout the process. 
 
 
Key Components and Analytical Instruments 
 
 
Electrolyte Tank: The electrolyte tank contains the organic electrolyte solution, a 
diglyme, or THF used as solvent, mixed with lithium tetrafluoroborate (LiBF4). The 
concentration of the lithium salt and any additives like ethanol (EtOH) are precisely 
measured to optimize the NRR. This tank is connected to the electrochemical cell, ensuring 
a continuous electrolyte supply to the electrodes. 
 
Electrochemical Cell: An electrochemical cell used in lithium-mediated eNRR 
comprises a cathode made from stainless steel and an anode. The anode is also made of 
stainless steel covered with platinum or platinum gold. Nitrogen gas is introduced as the 
reactant at the cathode. The cell's design also includes current collectors and a housing 
structure to maintain the integrity of the cell and ensure efficient reactant distribution and 
product removal.

3.1. 
Introduction to Mass and Energy Balance in 
Electrochemical Systems 
The study of mass and energy balance in electrochemical systems is crucial, especially for 
innovative processes like lithium-mediated electrochemical ammonia synthesis. Ensuring the 
conservation of mass and energy, this principle is foundational for achieving equilibrium 
between inputs, outputs, and internal transformations. Such balances are pivotal for the 
efficiency and sustainability of lithium-mediated ammonia synthesis, a highlighted method for 
decentralized ammonia production [38]. 
Mass balance tracks material flow through the system, which is crucial for optimizing the 
ammonia synthesis process in lithium-mediated systems. Energy balance, conversely, accounts 
for all energy forms entering, leaving, or being transformed within the system, essential for 
evaluating system efficiency and potential improvements [39]. 
 
3.2. 
Significance and Methodology 
The application of mass and energy balances in electrochemical systems like lithium-
mediated ammonia synthesis is significantly important. It aids in identifying process 
inefficiencies and optimization opportunities. The focus on the solid-electrolyte interphase (SEI) 
in such systems underlines the necessity of these considerations for process stability and 
efficiency enhancement [40]. Addressing challenges like dinitrogen splitting and hydrogen 
evolution reaction competition underscores the critical role of mass and energy balance [39]. 
The methodology for assessing mass and energy balances starts with fundamental 
quantification steps. Basic techniques include stoichiometry for mass balance and 
thermodynamics for energy balance, which are crucial for initial analyses. Simple and effective 
methods provide a basis for sizing electrochemical energy storage systems based on energy 
balance, showcasing the application of straightforward calculations in achieving system 
optimization [41]. 
Following basic quantification, advanced analytical techniques like Electrochemical 
Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) offer deeper insights into mass and energy dynamics. EIS is 
pivotal for identifying loss mechanisms and understanding the system's impedance 
characteristics. Distribution of Relaxation Times (DRT) analysis further enhances EIS data 
interpretation, providing a detailed view of electrochemical processes and aiding in the 
optimization of mass and energy flows [42] [43].

3.4. 
Loss Analysis  
 
 
In the preceding analysis of the electrochemical synthesis of ammonia, the theoretical 
framework—based on idealized assumptions—predicts a significantly higher efficiency than 
what has been observed in empirical outcomes. This notable discrepancy between theoretical 
and actual efficiencies necessitates a thorough investigation into the factors contributing to 
energy loss within the system, a critical endeavor that forms the core of our analysis. 
     
Delving into the mechanisms and phenomena responsible for the observed losses. The 
imperfect Faraday efficiency, which in practice never reaches the idealized 100%, directly 
impacts the system's overall energy conversion efficacy. Similarly, deviations from ideal 
mixing conditions, the presence of impurities, and the practical challenges of maintaining a 
100% charge selective membrane further contribute to the efficiency gap. These factors, 
coupled with the real-world complexities of mass transport limitations and the potential for ion 
accumulation within the membrane, underscore the multifaceted nature of the losses 
encountered. 
    To address and elucidate these inefficiencies, a detailed analysis is imperative. Here, the role 
of resistive elements within the system becomes paramount, and Electrochemical Impedance 
Spectroscopy (EIS) offers a tool for precisely identifying and quantifying these resistive 
elements within the electrochemical system. Factors such as internal resistances, which hinder 
current flow, and charge transfer limitations, which impede the reaction kinetics at the 
electrode surfaces, are pivotal in understanding the overall energy loss. Additionally, other 
electrochemical impedances, including the effects of ion transport barriers and the dynamics 
of the electrochemical interface, further complicate the system's efficiency. 
 
3.5. 
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) Analysis 
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) is an analytical technique that offers a non-
destructive means to probe the electrochemical dynamics of various systems, including 
corrosion mechanisms, sensor functionality, battery efficiency, and lithium-mediated 
electrochemical ammonia synthesis. This method entails the application of an alternating 
current (AC) voltage of small amplitude across an electrochemical cell and measuring the 
resultant current to derive an impedance spectrum. This spectrum, covering a broad frequency 
range, provides a comprehensive view of the kinetic and mass transport processes occurring 
within the system. Such detailed insights make EIS an indispensable tool in the field of 
electrochemical research [44]. 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:31==
  
 
22 
 
Role of EIS in Identifying Loss Mechanisms 
The primary utility of EIS in the context of lithium-mediated electrochemical 
ammonia synthesis, and indeed in many electrochemical systems, lies in its ability to 
identify and quantify loss mechanisms. These mechanisms, which can significantly impact 
the efficiency and performance of electrochemical processes, manifest as resistive losses, 
capacitive behaviors, and inductive phenomena within the impedance spectrum. By 
decomposing the impedance spectrum into its constituent elements, researchers can 
identify specific processes such as charge transfer resistances, mass transport limitations, 
double-layer capacitance, and adsorption/desorption kinetics, among others. 
The impedance data, when plotted as Nyquist or Bode diagrams, allows researchers 
to separate and analyze the processes by their time constants, offering insights into 
overlapping electrochemical reactions. For instance, charge transfer processes are typically 
represented by semicircles in Nyquist plots, whereas linear portions at low frequencies 
indicate diffusion-controlled processes, known as Warburg impedance. This granular level 
of analysis provided by EIS enables pinpointing precise loss mechanisms, ranging from 
electrode kinetics and electrolyte resistance to diffusive barriers [45] [46]. 
Recent studies highlight the technique's nondestructive nature and ability to yield 
valuable data for characterizing electrochemical systems, proving its efficacy in identifying 
loss mechanisms across various applications [45]. Additionally, introducing enhanced 
tools that simplify the EIS analysis has enabled researchers to conduct impedance studies 
without needing an exhaustive understanding of the underlying electrochemical 
mechanisms [46]. 
In lithium-mediated electrochemical ammonia synthesis, discerning these loss 
mechanisms is pivotal for enhancing system efficiency. Factors such as the electrochemical 
surface area, the interface properties between electrode and electrolyte, and the kinetics of 
the lithium-mediated reactions play significant roles in the process efficiency. By isolating 
and measuring the impact of these factors through EIS, researchers can fine-tune the system 
toward higher productivity and reduced energy demands.

Data Interpretation and Model Fitting 
In analyzing electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) data for a lithium-
mediated ammonia synthesis system, the ZView software was employed to fit the 
experimental data using two different equivalent electrical circuits (EECs). The first EEC 
comprised a solution resistance (Rs) in series with a parallel combination of a constant 
phase element (CPE) and a charge transfer resistance (Rct), representing the double-layer 
capacitance and charge transfer kinetics at the electrode-electrolyte interface, respectively. 
The second EEC extended the first by including a Warburg impedance (W), symbolizing 
the diffusion-controlled process. The choice of EEC was guided by the physicochemical 
processes believed to be prevalent in the system under specific experimental conditions. 
These fits allowed for the quantification of parameters that characterize the electrochemical 
behavior of the system, contributing to a deeper understanding of the mechanisms at play 
within the lithium-mediated ammonia synthesis process. 
 
Comparing the EIS data across all samples before running the electrochemistry 
provides insights into how the initial conditions may affect the subsequent electrochemical 
behavior. Given that the experiments for samples 28, 29, and 30 are identical, one would

high-frequency intercept with the real axis, reflecting the electrolyte’s conductivity, while

reactions at the electrode surface. The CPE accounts for the non-ideal capacitive behavior

3.6. 
Distribution of Relaxation Times (DRT) Analysis 
 
The Distribution of Relaxation Times (DRT) Analysis is an advanced methodological 
approach in electrochemical research, primarily used to dissect the complex impedance spectra 
of electrochemical systems into a more interpretable and analytically useful form. This 
technique is particularly significant for its ability to differentiate between overlapping 
electrochemical processes by identifying their unique time constants, thus offering a refined 
understanding of the system's electrochemical dynamics [47] [48]. The DRT analysis has 
found extensive application across various domains, including the characterization of lithium-
==PAGE:40==
  
 
31 
ion batteries, fuel cells, and corrosion processes, among others. Its adoption is driven by the 
need for a more detailed and accurate analysis of impedance data, moving beyond the 
limitations of traditional equivalent circuit modeling to a more direct interpretation of 
electrochemical phenomena. 
 
Theoretical Basis and Importance 
 
The theoretical foundation of DRT analysis is deeply rooted in the principles of 
electrochemistry and physics, particularly the concept that each electrochemical reaction 
or process within a system is characterized by a specific relaxation time. This theoretical 
basis allows for the deconvolution of impedance spectra, facilitating the identification and 
separation of simultaneous processes based on their distinct temporal behaviors. The 
importance of understanding and applying DRT analysis lies in its ability to provide 
detailed insights into the electrochemical mechanisms at play, including identifying rate-
limiting steps, differentiating capacitive and resistive elements, and the assessment of 
material properties [49]. By leveraging the DRT approach, researchers can achieve a higher 
spectral resolution and process separability, significantly enhancing the material and 
system characterization for improved performance and efficiency in electrochemical 
applications. 
The importance of DRT analysis in electrochemistry cannot be overstated. It has 
been instrumental in tackling the ill-posed problem of interpreting impedance spectra, 
which traditionally required sophisticated mathematical regularizations to resolve [50]. 
Through DRT, researchers can now access a model-free approach that circumvents the 
limitations of equivalent circuit models, providing a direct, more accurate representation 
of the system's electrochemical behavior [51]. This analytical prowess not only enhances 
the accuracy of electrochemical diagnostics but also paves the way for developing more 
efficient and durable energy storage and conversion devices. 
 
Application of DRT in Electrochemical Systems 
The Distribution of Relaxation Times (DRT) application in electrochemical 
systems spans a wide array of technologies, including lithium-ion batteries, fuel cells, and 
electrochemical sensors. This analytical technique has proven invaluable in dissecting 
complex impedance spectra, thereby enhancing the understanding of internal mechanisms 
and processes. For instance, the DRT has been instrumental in the rapid characterization 
and high-throughput screening of electrocatalysts, significantly accelerating the 
development of novel oxide electrodes for fuel cells and electrolyzers [52]. Similarly, 
Gaussian process regression, as applied to DRT analysis, optimizes kernel hyperparameters 
==PAGE:41==
  
 
32 
to resolve the ill-posed problem of traditional electrochemical impedance spectroscopy 
(EIS), allowing for a detailed investigation of resistive and capacitive processes within 
systems like solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) electrodes [53]. 
Further demonstrating the versatility of DRT, it has facilitated the distinction 
between bulk and grain boundary resistances in studies of proton-conducting electrolyte 
materials [54]. This differentiation is crucial for optimizing the performance of 
electrochemical devices by targeting material properties for improvement. In lithium-ion 
batteries, the DRT analysis extends beyond traditional EIS, enabling the assessment of 
relaxation processes through pulse evaluation techniques. This approach enhances the 
resolution of processes associated with large time constants and offers a novel avenue for 
diagnosing and understanding degradation mechanisms [55]. 
 
Advantages of DRT over Traditional EIS Analysis 
The advantages of DRT analysis over traditional EIS are manifold, particularly its 
ability to provide a more detailed and accurate depiction of electrochemical processes. The 
Bayesian approach to DRT estimation exemplifies this by incorporating uncertainties and 
observational errors in EIS data, offering a robust framework for estimating the state of 
health in lithium-ion batteries [56]. Another significant advantage is the development of 
EIS2MOD, a novel modeling framework based on DRT, which enhances the predictive 
accuracy of Li-ion cell behavior under various operational conditions [57]. 
DRT analysis also excels in its capacity to resolve closely occurring 
electrochemical processes, which might be indistinguishable from traditional EIS due to 
overlapping spectral features. By deconvolving the impedance spectrum into a distribution 
of relaxation times, DRT facilitates a more precise separation and identification of these 
processes, thus enabling a more nuanced understanding of the system's electrochemical 
dynamics. This attribute is particularly beneficial in applications like water management 
in proton exchange membrane fuel cells (PEMFCs) and the evaluation of electrode 
degradation mechanisms under various operational stresses [58] [59].

4.1. 
Introduction to Electrolyte Engineering 
 
Electrolyte engineering and system development play critical roles in enhancing the 
performance of lithium-mediated electrochemical ammonia synthesis (LiMEAS). This 
emerging field has seen considerable advancements through the optimization of electrolytes 
and system designs, aiming to improve ammonia production efficiency, selectivity, and 
stability under ambient conditions. 
 
A systematic investigation into ether-based electrolytes for LiMEAS highlighted their 
impact on conductivity, product distribution, parasitic reactions, and FE  [24]. Key factors 
influencing the performance of electrochemical nitrogen reduction (e-NRR) systems include 
the intrinsic properties of the catalyst, the gas-solid-liquid interface, and the specific design of 
the reaction system, underscoring the complexity of achieving efficient ammonia synthesis 
[62]. 
 
The introduction of oxygen to the reaction atmosphere, at conditions like 20 bar N2 pressure 
with 0.8 mol. % O2 has been shown to significantly boost the FE, reaching up to 79%, 
demonstrating the potential for operational enhancements in LiMEAS [29]. Furthermore, 
adopting strategies such as ring-chain coupling law in electrolyte engineering has led to 
remarkable increases in FE, with values reported up to 54.78±1.60% [63]. 
 
The role of the Solid Electrolyte Interphase (SEI) in both LiMEAS and lithium-ion batteries 
(LIBs) is crucial for stabilizing electrode surfaces, controlling reactant access, and promoting 
selectivity. This highlights the importance of SEI in enhancing performance across different 
systems [36]. Investigations into the SEI layer dynamics within the electrochemical Li-
mediated ammonia synthesis process using various electrolytes, such as LiClO4 and LiBF4, 
have shown their critical influence on mass transport to the electrode and overall system 
performance [37]. 
 
Electrolyte engineering strategies to boost faradaic Efficiency include modulating 
electrolyte pH, improving proton transport velocity, adjusting water activity, and utilizing 
hybrid, water-in-salt, ionic liquids, and non-aqueous electrolytes [64]. Novel cell designs and 
electrolytes have significantly improved the selectivity and productivity of LiMEAS, marking 
a step forward in addressing the technological challenges for its industrial application [65]. 
 
Electrolyte engineering is pivotal for advancing LiMEAS towards practical applications. 
By addressing challenges such as enhancing FE, optimizing system stability, and improving 
selectivity through innovative electrolyte formulations and system configurations, significant 
progress can be made toward sustainable and efficient ammonia production.

Ethanol Concentration 
The impact of ethanol (EtOH) concentration on the electrochemical synthesis of 
ammonia was extensively studied using two different solvents, THF and diglyme, under 
various experimental conditions. The ethanol concentration in the electrolyte was varied to 
find the optimum condition that would yield the highest FE for ammonia production. 
Ethanol's role as a proton shuttle in the electrochemical system is pivotal for 
enhancing ammonia synthesis [66]. Ethanol facilitates efficient proton transfer to the 
nitrogen adsorbed on the electrode surface, significantly influencing the reaction kinetics 
and optimizing the FE observed in our experiments. This role underscores the importance 
of ethanol beyond its effects on conductivity and solvent properties, marking it as a critical 
component for achieving high Faradaic efficiencies in lithium-mediated ammonia 
synthesis. 
To ensure consistency across our experiments, we fixed several parameters. 
Lithium salt (LiBF4) concentration was maintained at 1M, gas flow rate at 15 ml/min, and 
the electrochemical cell remained unchanged. We also kept the current density of 6 
mA/cm2, charge throughput of 306 C and a constant electrolyte circulation speed. These 
measures were vital to isolate the impact of ethanol concentration on the FE in ammonia 
synthesis. 
THF showed an optimal FE of 79% at an ethanol concentration of 0.15 vol%, with 
efficiency dropping to 60% and 59% at 0.25 vol% and 0.35 vol%, respectively, and 
plummeting to 40% at 0.5 vol%. This pattern suggests that increasing ethanol concentration 
beyond a certain point detrimentally affects the electrochemical environment or reaction 
kinetics, with THF's optimal balance at a lower ethanol concentration indicating a more 
favorable reaction environment for ammonia synthesis. 
In terms of diglyme, maintaining LiBF4 at 1 M and adjusting ethanol concentrations 
showed an optimal FE of 60% at an ethanol concentration of 0.10 vol% emphasizing a 
decline in FE as the ethanol concentration increased, as demonstrated in the table below. 
This trend underscores the sensitivity of ammonia synthesis efficiency to ethanol 
concentration in diglyme, with a more notable decrease at higher concentrations. 
The experimental data collectively highlight the critical role of ethanol 
concentration in influencing the electrochemical synthesis of ammonia. Optimal ethanol 
concentration varies with the choice of solvent; however, both THF and diglyme 
experiments suggest that lower ethanol concentrations tend to favor higher Faradaic 
efficiencies. This could be attributed to the balance between solvent dielectric properties, 
ethanol's role in the electrolyte's conductivity, and its interaction with the active species 
within the electrochemical environment. Understanding these interactions is crucial for 
optimizing the conditions for efficient ammonia synthesis.

LSV Effect 
 
Linear Sweep Voltammetry (LSV) is a powerful electroanalytical technique 
increasingly employed in advanced materials research, particularly in the context of 
lithium-ion batteries and electrochemical synthesis processes. This technique involves the 
linear variation of an electrode's potential over time, allowing for the detailed analysis of 
electrochemical reactions by observing the current response. LSV's recent application 
highlights its significance in exploring the electrochemical stability of potential current 
collectors for aqueous zinc ion batteries in a near-neutral ZnSO4 electrolyte, demonstrating 
its utility in understanding the intricacies of electrochemical systems relevant to energy 
storage technologies [67]. Moreover, the technique has been pivotal in characterizing the 
charge storage capacity of ionogels with varying electrolyte solution concentrations [68], 
evaluating the performance of ionic liquids as electrolytes, revealing specific discharge 
capacities in experimental batteries [69], and assessing the performance of novel 
electrolyte additives in forming protective SEI layer [70].  
In this project, the LSV step is omitted in two experiments to evaluate its impact on 
the synthesis process. The experiments revealed that skipping the LSV phase did not 
significantly affect the FE, which was an essential finding given future plans to transition 
from using potentiostat to power supplies, where conducting LSV is impossible. This 
approach aimed to assess the potential implications on system efficiency without

our methodology in the face of changing technical constraints. 
 
 
 
5. 
Future Scope and Conclusions 
 
This thesis has embarked on an in-depth exploration of electrochemical ammonia 
synthesis through lithium-mediated processes, tackling the dual challenges of sustainable 
chemical production and the inherent limitations within existing methodologies. Through 
a meticulous review, it has underscored the critical energy and environmental drawbacks 
associated with conventional ammonia synthesis techniques, particularly the Haber-Bosch 
process. This examination has underscored the urgent need for innovative approaches 
within this field. 
The experimental framework established in this study was meticulously designed to 
optimize the Nitrogen Reduction Reaction (NRR) process. The strategic selection of 
diglyme and tetrahydrofuran (THF) as solvents, in conjunction with lithium 
tetrafluoroborate (LiBF4), and the precise modulation of ethanol concentrations, were 
pivotal in fostering a conducive environment for the NRR. This setup enabled a 
comprehensive series of experiments that scrutinized the impacts of electrolyte 
composition, solvent selection, and ethanol concentration on the Faradaic efficiency (FE) 
of ammonia synthesis. Additionally, the deployment of Electrochemical Impedance 
Spectroscopy (EIS) and Distribution Relaxation Time (DRT) analyses furnished profound 
insights into the impedance characteristics of the system and illuminated potential loss 
mechanisms, charting a course towards optimization. 
The incorporation of Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) and Distribution 
of Relaxation Times (DRT) analyses was instrumental in dissecting the electrochemical 
dynamics at play. The EIS analysis, through the application of an alternating current (AC) 
voltage across the cell to generate an impedance spectrum, revealed essential loss 
mechanisms by shedding light on kinetic and mass transport processes. This analysis 
facilitated the identification of pivotal factors such as charge transfer resistances and mass 
transport limitations that directly impact electrochemical efficiency. Concurrently, the 
DRT analysis offered a nuanced understanding by differentiating overlapping processes 
via their distinct time constants, thereby identifying rate-limiting steps and differentiating 
between capacitive and resistive elements. Collectively, these analyses provided a holistic 
view of the system's electrochemical behavior, significantly enhancing our comprehension 
of its operational intricacies. 
==PAGE:56==
  
 
47 
Experimental outcomes have illustrated that variations in ethanol concentrations and 
gas flow rates markedly affect the FE and overall efficiency of the system. Optimal 
conditions were identified, with ethanol concentrations set at 0.15 vol% for THF, achieving 
an FE of 79%, and a peak FE of 60% at an ethanol concentration of 0.10 vol% in diglyme, 
highlighting THF's superior Faradaic efficiency under optimized conditions. Furthermore, 
an optimal gas flow rate was pinpointed to harmonize ammonia production with efficient 
gas utilization, while specific current densities were determined to maximize FE without 
undermining system stability. This research not only contributes to the advancement of 
sustainable ammonia synthesis but also lays the groundwork for future investigations into 
lithium-mediated electrochemical processes. 
For further exploration. Future research should prioritize enhancing the system's 
efficiency and selectivity. This could involve the development of new catalysts that lower 
the activation energy for nitrogen reduction and improve Faradaic efficiency. Additionally, 
integrating renewable energy sources to power the electrochemical process could further 
align the technology with sustainable production goals. Investigating the long-term 
stability and durability of the system under continuous operation will also be essential to 
move from laboratory-scale demonstrations to industrial applications.
