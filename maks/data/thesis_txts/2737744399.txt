Abstract
This master’s thesis investigates how design features of autoinjector devices can be
specified to minimize use errors related to premature lift, aiming to develop a more
universal autoinjector.
Through co-creation sessions with both technical autoinjector
experts and autoinjector users, critical design features such as audible feedback and hold
force were identified and investigated. It was thus hypothesized that continuous clicks
and a lower hold force would significantly reduce the number of premature lift. To test the
hypotheses, two prototypes with varying audible feedback (continuous clicks vs. start-
of-dose/end-of-dose clicks) and two prototypes with varying hold force (3.6 N and 8.2 N)
were developed. A within-subjects user study was conducted with 24 participants from
the general population, 12 of whom were injection-experienced and 12 injection-naive,
comparing the two types of audible feedback and the two levels of hold force. Study
results demonstrated that continuous clicks and a low hold force significantly reduces
the number of premature lifts. Furthermore, an analysis of force application suggested
an optimal hold force range between 3.6 N and 5.75 N. However, given that vulnerable
individuals are among autoinjector users, further research involving these populations is
necessary to confirm the suitability of these findings for a universal autoinjector.

Introduction
Autoinjectors are medical devices designed to automatically deliver a dose of medica-
tion into the subcutaneous or intramuscular tissue. These devices facilitate self-injection,
enabling patients to administer medications without professional assistance. The ease
and safety of self-administration are attributed to the automatic and simplified injec-
tion process, which contrasts with manually controlled injection devices. Autoinjectors
come in mechanical and electro-mechanical variants and are available in various shapes
and forms. This master’s thesis primarily focuses on mechanical autoinjectors, which are
spring-driven and activated by applying force to the device.
Since the launch of the EpiPen autoinjector in the 1980s, many manufacturers in the
MedTech field have developed autoinjectors for treating both emergency and chronic
conditions [1]. The wide range of treatments means that autoinjector users have diverse
conditions and disabilities that may affect how they operate the device. Consequently,
autoinjectors are designed specifically for the intended user, with variations depending
on the treatment they deliver.
For environmental and manufacturing cost purposes, it would thus make sense to design
and develop a universal autoinjector that is compatible with multiple kinds of medica-
tions. However, diversity in user capabilities presents a significant challenge in develop-
ing such a device, because human capabilities are not uniform. A universal autoinjector
should be applicable to all kinds of users, including vulnerable individuals such as those
with limited hand dexterity, visual impairment, hearing impairment, and the elderly. A
universal design of an autoinjector therefore requires design features to be specified to
accommodate most individuals.
The operation of an autoinjector consists of several steps in preparation, activation, and
injection. Critical use errors often occur during the injection process, where users may
prematurely lift their device before the injection has ended, potentially causing them to
not receive the full dose. Therefore, the research question for this master’s thesis is: How
can the design of an autoinjector be specified to minimize premature lift?
Design features that are significantly involved in the injection process include the required
force to hold down the device and audible feedback that communicates the injection start
and end for the users. To answer the research question, variations of hold forces and au-
dible feedback will be assessed and compared. It will be evaluated whether continuous
clicks as audible feedback, as opposed to only a start and end click, reduce premature
lift, and likewise, whether a low hold force reduces premature lift compared to a high
hold force. Therefore, two hypotheses were assessed through user studies in this master’s
thesis:
• Continuous clicks significantly reduce the number of use errors related to premature
lift.
• Low minimum hold force significantly reduces the number of use errors related to
premature lift.
This master’s thesis is conducted in collaboration with Technolution, a consulting com-
pany specializing in product development within MedTech and Pharma. Technolution

Background
3.1
Autoinjector
Within the field of mechanical autoinjectors, there are two main types which are push-
on-button and push-on-skin. These two types of autoinjectors diverge in their activation
mechanisms. While the push-on-button requires the user to depress a button with their
thumb while holding the device against the injection site, the push-on-skin variant only
demands downward pressure of the device body onto the injection site for activation
[2]. Despite differences in activation mechanisms, both types are similar. Common to
all autoinjectors is a cartridge or a syringe to house a drug dose. A sketch of how the
spring-driven mechanism looks can be seen in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Sketches of the spring-driven mechanisms inside autoinjectors. The top
illustration depicts a push-on-button variant, while the bottom illustration shows a
push-on-skin variant. Inspiration: [2]
For button-free autoinjectors, the device is placed on the injection site and pressure is
applied. During this movement, the needle shield ascends within the device [2]. Each
device possesses a specific activation force. Once this force is met, the spring is engaged,
pushing the plunger downward through the cartridge/syringe, which simultaneously ex-
pels the drug through the needle. Once the plunger is pushed down, the injection has
ended. After the injection has ended, the user lifts the device from their skin, prompting
the needle shield to retract and lock automatically, which mitigates the risk of post-use
needle injuries.
Similarly, in push-on-button devices, the user presses the device onto the skin to ele-
vate the needle shield. However, the injection is triggered by pressing a button with the
required minimum activation force, initiating the same spring-driven process as with the
button-free design.
In addition to these mechanisms, most autoinjectors consist of a needle cap that locks the
needle shield to prevent premature activation. Furthermore, some steps in the mechan-
ical process typically generate audible and tactile feedback to aid user comprehension
and engagement. A majority of the devices are constructed to produce a click sound and
tactile feeling once the device is activated, and another click sound and tactile feeling
6
==PAGE:8==
once the plunger has reached the bottom as an end-of-dose signal. These types of clicks
are characterized as start-of-dose and end-of-dose clicks [3]. Another type of audible
feedback, which is not as common among currently available autoinjectors, is continu-
ous clicks throughout the whole injection process. Additionally, many devices feature a
viewing window to provide visual feedback, with the plunger often colored vibrantly to
enhance the visibility of observing when it has reached the bottom.
These types of injection devices were initially introduced in the 1960s with a version
serving military applications [1].
Twenty years later, the EpiPen was introduced to
the market, providing a means for easy self-administration of epinephrine in cases of
anaphylactic attacks [4]. The introduction of self-injection options has become increas-
ingly popular, leading to the release of numerous types of autoinjectors since the first
EpiPen, facilitating self-therapy for emergencies and chronic diseases. Thus, more than
20 pharmaceutical companies have developed about 80 different autoinjectors [1]. Today,
autoinjectors are used for the treatment of migraines, rheumatoid arthritis, multiple scle-
rosis, Crohn’s disease, psoriasis, obesity, anaphylactic shocks, and many more conditions.
The current market of autoinjectors reflects a diverse array of sizes, grips, feedback
mechanisms, force requirements, injection times, and more. Different autoinjectors on
the market are sketched out on Figure 2. While design choices are partly based on aes-
thetic considerations, they are mostly and carefully done to optimize usability and user
experience. Alotugh autoinjectors have always been acknowledged for being easy and
quick to use, continuous improvements consistently aim to enhance the ease of use of the
devices, which explains consistent design changes. Some autoinjectors are long and slim,
some are smaller and wider, some have a curvy shape, and some have a textured grip.
Consequently, all these design choices are carefully selected to ensure that the device can
easily be administered by the intended users.
Figure 2: A sketch of different emergency-use autoinjectors on the market. The sketch
is taken from an article by John Burke [5]
A typical series of tasks when using an autoinjector can be seen on figure 3. Operating
such devices includes needle cap removal, injection site identification, placing the device
on the injection site with a correct angle, then pushing the device down to activate it,
holding it down 5-10 seconds after hearing the second click, and finally carefully remov-
ing the autoinjector [3]. Although these steps sound rather simple, use errors frequently
7
==PAGE:9==
arise. The root causes of these use errors are among others commonly related to the
force required for activation and injection. Furthermore, some people find it diﬀicult to
remove the needle cap or even understand that it should be removed. Others misinter-
pret the meaning of the audible feedback. Such challenges underscore the importance
of addressing design limitations to mitigate use errors effectively. To do so, autoinjec-
tor manufacturers must consider ergonomic aspects that could potentially limit the user
in administering the device. Given the broad therapeutic scope of autoinjectors, users
encompass a wide range of disabilities, impacting their capabilities and needs for admin-
istering the device.
Figure 3: A series of user tasks when operating an autoinjector.
The trend of developing autoinjectors over the past few decades is expected to rise in con-
junction with the increasing prevalence of diseases and the desire to reduce healthcare
costs [6]. Self-administration options such as autoinjectors improve treatment adherence
and reduce the frequency of healthcare visits. Given that autoinjectors are often disease-
specific, it would make sense to develop a universal autoinjector that can be used for
various therapies and thereby contribute to the reduction in cost for both development
and manufacturing. Such a device would further contribute to more sustainable solu-
tions, which is a topic of increasing global importance. However, the diversity in users
presents a challenge in developing a universal autoinjector as human capabilities are not
uniform. Thus it is essential to understand human capabilities and limitations to specify
design features that accommodate most individuals.

3.2
HF Engineering and Usability Engineering
A key element in the development of medical devices, and product design in general,
is Human Factors Engineering (HFE). HFE studies the interaction between humans and
surrounding systems, focusing on understanding the human behaviour, abilities, limi-
tations, and other characteristics. This knowledge is applied to develop systems that
improve effectiveness, productivity, safety, ease of use, and overall human well-being and
quality of life [7].
Closely related to HFE is Usability Engineering (UE), defined by the American National
Standards Institute as the ”extent to which a product can be used by specified users to
achieve specified goals with effectiveness, eﬀiciency, and satisfaction in a specified context
of use”. While HFE aims to reduce use-related risks by understanding human capabilities,
usability focuses on characterizing user interfaces to ensure effectiveness, eﬀiciency, and
user satisfaction [8]. In medical device development, the Food and Drug Administration
(FDA) considers HFE and UE synonymous [9].
In the development of medical devices, authorities require adherence to human factors
and usability processes to ensure that the medical device is safe and effective to use. Key
considerations in product development include the user, the use environment, and the
device user interface, as illustrated in Figure 4. If the user interface is not designed to
fit the intended user or the intended use environment, use errors can occur, leading to
unsafe and ineffective devices. A well-designed user interface, on the other hand, facili-
tates correct user actions while preventing errors and enhancing the safety and eﬀiciency
of the device.
Figure 4: Interaction between human factors considerations and outcome. (source:
FDA [9])
Consequently, HFE and UE processes revolve around mitigating use errors, which is an
essential systematic approach. This involves identifying intended users, the use environ-
ment, and all tasks involved in using the device. Task analysis is a method that breaks
down the device use process into sequences of tasks, which are then analyzed to identify
user interface components, potential use errors, and potential results of the use errors [8].
In this process, critical tasks are identified, which for medical devices are defined as tasks
”if performed incorrectly or not performed at all, would or could cause serious harm to
the patient or user, where harm is defined to include compromised medical care”[9]. For
9
==PAGE:11==
combination products, which are products that combine drugs, devices, and/or biological
products, a critical task is defined as a task that could cause harm, thus not only serious
harm [10]. It is also crucial to understand the root cause of these use errors to determine
how to mitigate them.
Formative evaluations are studies conducted early in the design and development process
to provide additional insights into the user interface design by observing user interac-
tions, revealing unrecognized use-related hazards and errors. When use-related hazards
and use errors are identified, design modifications aim to eliminate or reduce these hazards
through risk control measures [11]:
• Inherent safety by design: Design choices that eliminate interactions leading to use
errors.
• Protective measures in the medical device itself or in the manufacturing process:
Safety mechanisms that reduce the risk of use error.
• Information for safety: Written information or training users to avoid use errors.
These control measures are prioritized in the hierarchy listed, with inherent safety by
design being the most effective.
Usability and human factors engineering follow an iterative process, where design modi-
fications are continuously made based on preliminary analysis such as task analysis and
formative evaluations. The design process is user-centered, deeply examining the user’s
interactions with the product. The user-centered design approach focuses on tasks, envi-
ronments, goals, needs, and limitations. Users are thus involved throughout development,
ensuring that the product meets their needs, often through formative studies. Results
from these studies lead to design iterations, which are tested until optimal usability is
achieved [12].
The iterative implementation of formative studies aligns with the lean build-measure-
learn cycle [13].
This framework emphasizes rapid prototyping and feedback by first
developing a prototype of a medical device on the current understanding of user needs,
tasks, use errors, and use-related hazards. Formative evaluations are then conducted to
gather data on user interactions and potential use errors. These data are then analyzed
to understand the root causes of errors, and these insights inform subsequent design iter-
ations. Therefore, the implementation of formative studies in the early stages of design
and development enhances the likelihood of identifying hazards and use errors early on,
eventually reducing costs and contributing to developing a safe and effective product.
Usability engineering activities must be documented in a Usability Engineering file for
regulatory approval. In the U.S., the FDA oversees this process, while in the EU the
documents must comply with the Medical Device Regulation (MDR) and reviewed by
notified bodies. These authorities ensure that the devices comply with regulatory re-
quirements to ensure the safety and eﬀicacy of medical devices.
Autoinjectors are classified as combination products since they combine a drug-delivery
system with a drug or biological product (depending on the therapy). Companies de-
veloping autoinjectors must therefore comply with regulatory pathways for combination
10
==PAGE:12==
products, which are more complex than those for medical devices, as they cover both
drug and device aspects. However, the HFE/UE processes are similar, with differences
in definitions (e.g. critical tasks) [10].
Applying HFE and UE systematically in the development process ensures safe and ef-
fective devices.

implemented in the framework of this master’s thesis.

Methods
4.1
Universal design
Universal design (UD) refers to the design of products, environments, programs, and
services to be usable by all people to the greatest extent possible, without the need for
adaptation or specialized design. Thus the concept of UD emphasizes around inclusivity,
ensuring that groups of people, including those with disabilities, are not excluded by the
design [14].
Seven principles for UD were developed by a group of architects, product designers,
engineers, and environmental design researchers in 1997 [14]. These principles can be
employed to evaluate existing designs and guide design processes.
1. Equitable use
2. Flexibility in use
3. Simple and intuitive use
4. Perceptible information
5. Tolerance for error
6. Low physical effort
7. Size and space for approach and use
The principle of equitable use ensures that a design is useful for people with diverse abil-
ities, not only for a niche group. Flexibility in use focuses on the design meeting a wide
range of preferences and abilities. Simple and intuitive use means designing something
that is easy to use or understand, regardless of the user’s prior experience, knowledge,
language skills, or concentration level. Perceptible information ensures that the design
effectively communicates necessary information to the user, regardless of ambient condi-
tions or sensory abilities. Tolerance for error involves minimizing hazards and adverse
consequences of use errors. Low physical effort ensures that the design can be used eﬀi-
ciently and comfortably without demanding high physical effort. Lastly, size and space
for approach and use involve designing with appropriate dimensions, regardless of the
user’s body size, posture, or mobility. By adhering to these principles, more universal
solutions can be developed.
Since products developed using the UD principles are usable by people without limi-
tations, a universal autoinjector offers the advantage of designing for human diversity.
Therefore, UD is the process of coming as close to ideal designs as possible. Although
ideal designs may not exist, striving to approach them generally benefits a broader range
of people.
An autoinjector that can be used for a wide range of treatments would fulfill the UD
principle about equitable use. Furthermore, design features such as force, grip, audible
feedback, and visual feedback should be specified to accommodate people with various
abilities. Simple and intuitive use can be achieved by minimizing the number of steps
12
==PAGE:14==
required to administer the device and incorporating feedback mechanisms that are easy
to understand. This further satisfies the principle of perceptible information, ensuring
that the users correctly perceive feedback mechanisms. Tolerance of error is addressed
through the HFE/UE processes explained in the above section, which aim to minimize
hazards. Low physical effort is fulfilled by enabling self-administration so users can avoid
frequent visits to healthcare providers, and by designing the device so that it does not
require excessive force to operate. Lastly, the size and shape of the autoinjector should
be designed for convenience both on the go and to provide a grip suitable for most users.
4.2
Double Diamond Framework
To define the framework for this research, the Double Diamond model has been em-
ployed. The double diamond is a design method developed by the British Design Council
to structure the innovation process [15]. It organizes design processes from idea to concept
in a logical manner. The method consists of two diamonds, each representing two stages
in design and development. Together, the diamonds represent a process of first exploring
and understanding an issue on a deeper level (first diamond), and then providing spe-
cific answers and taking focused action (second diamond). In other words, this approach
encourages designers to implement divergent and convergent thinking strategically into
their framework.
This method consists of four stages, the first one being the Discover stage [15]. Here, a
specific area of issues is explored by gathering as much information as possible (diverge).
This can be accomplished through desk research, interviews with relevant stakeholders,
or co-creation sessions with individuals affected by the issue (e.g. users of a product).
Once suﬀicient information has been collected to understand the issue, the Define stage is
initiated. At this point, the collected insights are converged to select and define a specific
problem. The results from this stage will lay the groundwork for the design process and
determine the scope of the solution space.
Once the problem definition is defined, the second diamond is reached, where the next
stage is Develop. Here, divergent thinking is implemented again to explore possible solu-
tions to the problem defined in the previous stage. This can be done through co-creation
with relevant individuals, such as potential users if a product is being designed. Finally,
the Deliver stage is where different solutions are tested out to eventually choose the most
optimal ones. This stage can yield a concept, prototype, or finished product, depending
on the project’s progress [15]. In practice, design teams constantly collect feedback on
their concepts and products to iteratively improve them. Therefore, the stages are not
always clearly separated as described. For instance, sometimes the output of a deliver
phase leads to returning to a define phase.
13
==PAGE:15==
Figure 5: Double diamond framework for this master’s thesis. Inspiration: [15].
Based on the Double Diamond model, a framework has been defined for this master’s
thesis, illustrated in Figure 5. As shown, the discover stage includes conducting a sys-
tematic literature review to gather knowledge regarding possible use errors occurring in
the use of injection devices. Furthermore, a Known Use Problems (KUP) analysis was
performed on oﬀicial data provided by the FDA. Finally, an expert interview with a
human factors specialist experienced with autoinjectors was conducted. All these sources
of information were collected and compared in the define stage to converge on a specific
problem statement for this study, which focuses on a specific use error.
Through co-creation with autoinjector experts from Technolution in mechanical engi-
neering, usability engineering, and user experience (UX), the defined problem statement
was expanded to brainstorm possible pain points and relevant design features within the
topic of the chosen use error. Output from this co-creation session served as input for sub-
sequent co-creation with autoinjector users, aiming to suggest possible solutions. During
the deliver stage, proposed solutions are transformed into prototypes and subjected to
user studies. Based on these studies, final solutions are either selected, or further itera-
tions and development are suugested. This final stage thus encompasses prototyping, user
study recruitment, planning and conducting studies, and finally performing data analysis.
4.3
Research method
The natural approach to research aligns with the arranged events in the double dia-

5.1.1
Literature Review
Before the initiation of this master’s thesis, a systematic literature review was con-
ducted to analyze current research on the usability of autoinjectors and the common use
errors associated with them. The review was part of a separate 5 ECTS special course,
which culminated in a report. The report is attached in Appendix M, where the system-
atic approach is thoroughly described. Thus the review will only be briefly presented in
this master’s thesis.
In total, 32 research papers were reviewed, encompassing patients from 14 different dis-
ease areas. That includes rheumatoid arthritis (RA), Crohn’s disease, psoriasis, ana-
phylaxis, diabetic retinopathy, diabetic neuropathy, migraine, asthma, multiple sclerosis,
opioid emergency, diabetes, osteoporosis, Parkinson’s disease, and arthritis.
Further-
more, healthy subjects, caregivers, and healthcare professionals (HCPs) were included in
various studies.
17 publications identified use errors related to premature lift, including studies by Hudry
(2017 [17]), Dománska (2017 [18]), Di Terlizzi (2023 [19]), and Lange et al. (2015 [3]).
Hudry et al. found that the most common use error was not holding the device until
hearing the second click end of dose click. Hudry emphasized the need to highlight the
importance of hearing the second click, as it may not be intuitive for all users. This is
supported by Dománska et al., who observed that significantly more users relied on vi-
sual feedback rather than the second click to indicate end-of-dose. Similarly, Lange et al.
observed a high incidence of premature lifts related to audible feedback, yet highlighted
that both audible feedback and visual feedback were crucial for injection success.
Dománska et al. concluded that RA patients with limited hand dexterity and no prior
injection experience were capable of performing self-injections confidently, safely, and
effectively. In contrast, Di Terlizzi et al. identified prior experience with injection de-
vices as the root cause for use-related issues. Their study revealed that both injection-
experienced and injection-naive multiple sclerosis patients committed premature lifts, but
the injection-naive group committed more and varied types of use errors. This indicates
that prior experience with injection devices might affect the likelihood of committing use
errors.
Seven publications identified cap removal related use errors, including studies by Domán-
ska and Lageat et al (2021 [20]). In their research, Laget et al. observed that almost
half of the participants failed to remove the needle cap initially. However, after reading
the IFU, all participants completed the task. The root cause was that participants as-
sumed the cap was an activation button due to negative transfer from previous experience.
Tischer et al. (2018 [21]) investigated user preferences among four different autoinjec-
tors and found that ease of self-injection and ease of grip were the most critical features.
Additionally, buttonless injection, visual feedback, and a triangular shape allowing for
easier grip were highly preferred.
16
==PAGE:18==
A rising trend within the field of autoinjectors is the injection of large-volume and viscous
drugs. Schneider et al. (2023 [22]) examined previous work addressing the limitations and
considerations for designing large-volume autoinjectors. They noted that such drug doses
result in higher pain. Extending the injection time would reduce the pain, however it
would also cause inconvenience for the users. In a previous study (2020 [20]) Schneider et
al. investigated the impact of injection duration on the user’s applied force. Their study
demonstrated that healthcare professionals, caregivers, and a diverse range of patients
could hold down an autoinjector with a minimum hold force on 4 N for approximately
30 seconds. The study further demonstrated that use errors and diﬀiculties appeared
to be independent of injection duration, supporting the feasibility of large-volume drug
implementations.
Another interesting study was done by Barbir et al.
(2016 [23]) who examined the
effect of different autoinjector form factors on the patients’ ability to apply force. Their
study included three shapes of emergency autoinjectors, namely elliptical, cylindrical,
and prismatic. The study participant’s applied force was measured and yielded results
suggesting that an elliptical form factor resulted in the most effective force application.
To conclude on findings regarding frequent use errors, this literature review suggests
that common use errors are related to premature lift and needle cap removal. These use
errors will serve as input in defining a problem statement for this thesis.
5.1.2
Known Use Problem Analysis
Another part of the pre-study attached in Appendix M involved conducting a Known
Use Problems (KUP) analysis, which is a method to discover previous use errors commit-
ted with a specific type of device [24]. Data was extracted from the FDA’s Manufacturer
and User Facility Device Experience (MAUDE) Database [25], which contains medical
device reports of adverse events over the last ten years. Thus this KUP analysis was done
based on these data, providing real-world insights on use errors committed with the use
of autoinjectors.
Drug-delivery devices that are similar to autoinjectors, are pen-injectors.
The main
difference between these two devices is that with pen injectors you usually need to dial a
dose and manually attach the needle to the pen. In that sense, an autoinjector is more
automatic as it is often more ready to use. Besides these differences, pen-injection devices
share many of the same steps as autoinjectors. Thus MAUDE data for both types were
included in the KUP analysis, to increase the likelihood of obtaining meaningful insights
on this topic. However, it should be noted that only events applicable for autoinjectors
were considered (needle attachment and dialing dose were excluded).
In total, 831 events were screened, resulting in the identification of 50 use-related events
in pen injector data and 18 events in autoinjector data. The analysis identified 13 differ-
ent types of use-related events, with the most frequent being the use of the device beyond
the recommended use-life. The root cause was often users not following recommendations
stated in the IFU. Another common use error was incorrect storage of the device, also
17
==PAGE:19==
caused by not following the IFU.
Interestingly, the KUP analysis did not identify any cap-removal use errors, which were
prevalent in the literature. However, it did reveal two events where users failed to main-
tain pressure during injection, leading to premature lift. There were no further indications
of premature lift, making it a less frequent issue compared to those documented in the
literature.
Thus use errors identified in the KUP were predominantly related to not following the
instructions regarding expiration dates, while the literature review indicated that errors
related to premature lift and cap removal were most frequent.
5.1.3
Expert interview
In deciding which types of use errors are most relevant to investigate further, it is
essential to consider not only frequency but also criticality. An error can be very frequent
but have limited criticality. Conversely, a use error can be less frequent, but highly criti-
cal. Critical use errors can also be so rare that other, more frequent errors, become more
relevant to focus on. Hence, in selecting the most relevant use errors, a balance between
frequency and criticality should be maintained.
To address the question of criticality, a written expert interview was conducted. A Senior
Human Factors Specialist from Technolution with extensive experience with autoinjectors
and their usability was interviewed to provide insights into different use errors related
to the device and their level of criticality.
The interview covered the HF specialist’s
professional experience, the most frequent use errors she has observed, her perception of
criticality, and which design features she considered the most relevant for further inves-
tigation. Four questions were formulated as follows:
1. Can you please share your experience with different types of autoinjectors/pen-
injector devices?
2. Among all use errors you have identified in your career, which ones occur most
frequently?
3. From your perspective, what would you consider the most critical use error(s)?
4. In your opinion, which design features would be most relevant to investigate to
minimize use errors and enhance the ease of injection in autoinjectors?
Full answers to the above questions can be found in Appendix A. The HF specialist
has over 15 years of experience in human factors engineering within the pharmaceuti-
cal industry, focusing primarily on combination products such as prefilled syringes and
autoinjectors. She has conducted numerous human factors studies with patients suffer-
ing from various conditions, including diabetes, obesity, growth disorders, cardiovascular
diseases, and allergic/auto-immune diseases. Insights from this expert are thus highly
valuable for this study.
The HF specialist has identified use errors during human factors evaluations, and she
notes that many use errors occur post-market, monitored through oﬀicial and company
complaints databases. She provides a list of known use errors, including incorrect storage
18
==PAGE:20==
of the device, needle sticks during handling, cap removal diﬀiculties, premature activa-
tion, premature removal, diﬀiculties activating the device, and diﬀiculties maintaining
pressure during injection. These use errors align with findings from the literature and
MAUDE data.
In discussing criticality, The HF specialist highlights the importance of evaluating the
safe and effective use of a combination product, focusing on tasks that are critical for
users to perform. The HF specialist refers to the FDA’s definition of critical tasks for
combination products and thus underscores the importance of focusing on use errors in
tasks that could cause harm to the user if performed incorrectly or not performed at all
[10]. She suggests examining design features that could mitigate use errors related to the
injection process. One such feature is audible feedback, which impacts the premature
removal of the device caused by the misinterpretation of click sounds as confirmation.
Hence the HF specialist recommends focusing on design features that support the user’s
confirmation of injection completion.
Another relevant design feature in mitigating use errors in critical tasks is the mini-
mum required force to activate and hold the device during injection. Some users may
not be capable of applying the necessary force, leading to either a failure to activate the
device or an inability to maintain the hold force during injection, resulting in premature
removal. Therefore the HF specialist suggests focusing on force specifications that allow
fragile or disabled user groups to handle autoinjectors effectively.
5.2
Problem Statement
Based on insights from the expert interview, findings from the literature review, and
the KUP analysis, it is now possible to determine the scope of this master’s thesis. The
most frequent use errors identified in the literature search were premature lifts related
to holding time and audible feedback, and cap removal-related use errors associated with
misinterpretation of the design. In the data extracted from the MAUDE database, most
use errors were related to not following the instructions.
According to the expert interview, the most critical use errors are premature lift due
to misinterpretation of audible feedback, and the inability to maintain the device during
injection due to the required forces. Hence, findings from the literature and the expert in-
terview align. The most crucial and the most frequent use errors are related to premature
lift. Thus the primary research question for this thesis is:
• How can the design of an autoinjector be specified to minimize premature lift of the
device?
This problem was addressed through co-creation sessions with experts and potential users,
which is thoroughly described in the following section.

6.1
Co-Creation
As previously mentioned, a human-centered design approach can be employed through
formative studies during design and development. However, human-centered design can
further be approached by the implementation of co-creation with potential users.
Co-creation is a way of working together, aiming to develop new solutions through con-
necting a group of people.
This term often confounds with cooperation, but there is
a distinct difference between these two approaches. Essentially, cooperation involves a
group of people with a common goal, working simultaneously to achieve their goal [26].
Co-creation on the other hand is defined as connecting people and their goals to jointly
create a solution. Thus in co-creation, stakeholders sit together and develop, whereas in
cooperation stakeholders can work on their tasks separately toward achieving the same
goal.
A great way to facilitate co-creation is by conducting a workshop. In order to design
a good workshop that ensures obtaining valuable outcomes, five core items must be con-
sidered [27]. The first one is defining the purpose of a workshop and deciding what the
output should be. Once a purpose is defined, it is easier to design the rest of the work-
shop. Next, workshop participants are important to consider. That means considering
which types of participants are relevant to obtain the desired output and whether there is
something important to know about these participants such as abilities etc. Next, an ap-
propriate workshop environment should be chosen to fit the atmosphere of the workshop.
For instance, if it is workshop with many participants, a large room should be chosen to
fit the group. Once these three elements are defined, it is easier to design the workshop
by choosing different methods and exercises that ensure maintaining the purpose of the
workshop and obtaining valuable output. To design a workshop it is advantageous to
make a detailed workshop plan that includes all the exercises, how to facilitate them, and
materials needed. Furthermore, it is important to reflect after a workshop. This involves
considering what went well, any potential aspects the facilitator should be aware of for
subsequent workshops, what was the output and whether it aligned with the purpose of
the workshop, etc.
Co-creation can be used throughout an entire project, but it can also be implemented
in specific parts of a project such as it is done in this master’s thesis. As illustrated
in Figure 5, co-creation workshop sessions were held after a problem statement was for-
mulated. In the process of defining and scoping what specific design features should be
further investigated and tested in the user studies, co-creation sessions with experts and
injection-device users have been employed. These workshops aimed to gather relevant
individuals to collectively provide valuable insights that can be used to generate solutions
for the problem statement.
The workshop plans for the two workshops facilitated for this master’s thesis can be
found in Appendix B. Methods were drawn from the Playbook for Universal Design de-
veloped by a partnership Technology Leaving No One Behind at DTU Skylab [28]. Their
playbook provides various tools and tips to facilitate workshops with accessibility and
20
==PAGE:22==
inclusion as the main focus. Thus one of the key tools utilized from their playbook is
the Ability Prompt Cards [29]. Each card presents a disability (example on Figure 7
and can thus be used to consider a diverse range of disabilities during a design process.
This enables the evaluation of inclusion and exclusivity in the design. For these work-
shops, the prompt cards have been used as boundary objects [27]. Boundary objects are
artifacts or concepts that facilitate communication and collaboration across boundaries.
Furthermore, they set the boundaries for where the conversation goes. In this context,
the prompt cards provided a common reference point, allowing workshop participants to
engage in discussions and develop mutual understanding despite diverse backgrounds and
perspectives.

The purpose of the first exercise was to let the workshop participants think of pain

through group discussion was used. As the name indicates, this method facilitates group
discussions to rank different concepts, solutions, ideas, etc. [31]. A ranking template (Ap-
pendix C) was provided with ”most critical” at the top. Participants were then tasked
with discussing and ranking the pain points based on their perception of their criticality.

The workshop was 1.5 hours long and consisted of an introduction, an icebreaker, test

During the introduction, the purpose and the agenda for the workshop were outlined.

To investigate the topic of audible feedback, two types of audible feedback indicating

an exercise can also be called bodystorming, which is a method allowing participants to

This simulation was done using an Electro Mechanical Activation Profiler (EMAP) de-

clicks (start and end of dose click) and an injection that had continuous clicks from the
activation to the end of the injection. The aim was to explore if continuous clicks, which
are not as prevalent as the other type of audible feedback, would mitigate premature
lift. Both simulations had an injection duration of 10 seconds, which is consistent with
autoinjectors available on the market [35]. Allowing participants to try different types
of audible feedback enabled them to evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of each
and consider possible solutions for reducing premature lift.
Additionally, an adjustable force prototype, 3D-printed by colleagues at Tehcnolution,

This prototype consisted of an autoinjector-shaped body with a wide grip, and a spring-
driven button, where the resistance could be modified with an adjustable screw. The
actual hold force levels adjusted by the screw were not fully accurate, however, they
provided a suﬀicient basis for discussion and evaluation within the workshop.

After the participants had interacted with variations of the design features, they discussed
what aspects worked well and which did not. These observations were then shared with
the entire group. The following exercise aimed to let the participants think of potential
problems that could occur with these design features. To do so, an assumption dump-

advantage of the brainwalking method is that a higher number and more diverse ideas

written. The workshop was concluded with a summary of the solutions, an explanation of
how they would be utilized for further research in the thesis, and an expression of grati-

Output
All workshop participants agreed that continuous clicks were more user-friendly and in-
tuitive compared to start-of-dose/end-of-dose clicks, which were found to be confusing.
During the tests of start-of-dose/end-of-dose clicks, most participants prematurely lifted
their hands before hearing the second click. It was mentioned by the participants that
they felt it was a long duration between the clicks, and one of the participants lifted their
hand because they thought they had heard a click.
In the participants’ evaluation of a prototype with adjustable force they showed a com-
mon preference for lower forces. The lupus patients found it diﬀicult to press the button
with a higher force, while the participant with experience using Wegovy had no diﬀicul-
ties yet still preferred a lower force. Furthermore, lupus patients noted that a smaller,
wider device would be more suitable for individuals with rheumatoid arthritis compared
to a thinner one. They mentioned that in the extension of holding the force prototype
in their hand, where the lupus patients agreed that such a curvy and wide device was
nicer for arthritis hands to hold on to instead of straight and thin devices. However, they
mentioned that this particular prototype was slightly too big, so something in between
this and a straight pen was preferable.
During the assumption dumption using ability prompt cards, participants shifted towards
generating solutions rather than identifying problems related to the abilities indicated on
the cards. For instance, they suggested that fewer injection steps might help those unable
to think clearly due to stress, a short and wide grip could benefit those with limited hand
dexterity, and visual feedback might be a preferable alternative to audible feedback for

individuals unable to focus. Since these suggestions were already in the solution stage,
the brainwalking exercise further developed these solutions rather than generating solu-
tions based on problems. Generally, the brainwalking yielded recommendations such as
simplifying the design, using colors instead of sounds, offering training on device use, and
enhancing grip features.
Although these solutions were not specific enough to translate into design specifications
for subsequent prototypes, the user feedback from the tests highlighted a preference for
continuous clicks over start and end of dose clicks due to their simplicity and reduced
potential for confusion. Regarding forces, participants favored lower forces, suggesting
a further investigation to determine if lower forces reduce premature lift. Additionally,
a wide grip was generally preferred among participants, which can be considered in the
design of the prototypes for the user study.
Reflections
Overall, the workshop was successful with the participants feeling that their contributions
were valued and that they were making a meaningful impact on research. However, chal-
lenges were encountered in maintaining focus on the specific topics of audible feedback
and forces. Participants started brainstorming solutions in the problem-finding brain-
storming session and frequently diverged into broader discussions, possibly due to the
use of ability prompt cards that let them consider wider topics. Despite frequent re-
minders to focus on audible feedback and forces, discussions often expanded to aspects
that were out of the workshop’s scope. Nevertheless, the participants provided valuable
user input that will inform the next steps in this thesis, including defining hypotheses
and creating prototypes for the user study.
6.2
Hypothesis
6.2.1
Definition of hypotheses
Based on the output from the co-creation sessions, hypotheses for the research were
defined. As concluded in the above section, the workshop with users yielded suggestions
that continuous clicks and lower forces could potentially reduce premature lift when ad-
ministering an autoinjector. Therefore, the objective of the user study would be to inves-
tigate whether these suggestions were true or not. To answer this question, a hypothesis
for each of the design features was formulated:
• Continuous clicks significantly reduce the number of use errors related to premature
lift.
• Low minimum hold force significantly reduces the number of use errors related to
premature lift.
As described by the research approach in Figure 6, a part of defining hypotheses is
identifying the variables that are to be measured. To do so, a clear understanding of
variables in statistics is needed.
6.2.2
Identification of data variables
In statistics, it is essential to understand your dataset. That implies identifying the
different variables your study and dataset include. The identified variables ground the
28
==PAGE:30==
basis for your choice of statistical methods to analyze your data.
Some fundamental terms that you regularly encounter in statistics are independent and
dependent variables. An independent variable can be considered as being ”the cause”
and is often a variable that the researcher manipulates to test specific things [16]. Subse-
quently, a dependent variable is affected by changes in the independent variable. Thus, a
dependent variable is a measure of human behavior, depending on the cause (independent
variable), which does not depend on other variables.
With this knowledge, it is now possible to proceed with identifying the specific vari-
ables for this particular study. As can be seen from the defined hypotheses, each explores
the impact of one independent variable on a subsequent dependent variable. One hy-
pothesis has continuous clicks as its independent variable, while the other has minimum
hold force. Regarding the dependent variables, both hypotheses share the same, namely
use errors.
With the identification of variables, it is possible to create prototypes appropriate to
measure these variables. As stated above, the independent variables are the ones that
are manipulated. Thus audible feedback and hold force are manipulated to test their
impact on the number of premature lifts occurring. Therefore, different prototypes with
different variants of audible feedback, including continuous clicks, could be utilized to
prove the hypothesis regarding clicks. Likewise, for the hypothesis regarding hold force,
comparing two prototypes with different hold force levels would be a way to test whether
the hypothesis is true or not. The process of developing these prototypes will be described
in the following section.

6.3.1
Development of audible feedback prototypes
For the user studies, I have developed two prototypes varying in types of audible feed-

These types of clicks are the most common among the autoinjectors available on the mar-
ket and are mechanically generated by the spring-driven system, as described in Section

click have been identified. Therefore, this study aimed to mitigate this use error by using
continuous clicks as audible feedback. These clicks start from activation and continue
until the plunger reaches the bottom of the cartridge. Consequently, two prototypes were
developed to compare the two types of audible feedback.
The mechanical generation of continuous clicks is more complex than that of start-of-

Study design
When comparing various device variants it must be decided whether to explore the
participants to each condition (device variant) or to assign them to separate groups each
exposed to only one condition. These types of study designs are called within-subject de-
sign and between-subject design, respectively [44]. A within-subject design involves each
participant being measured on each test condition. As can be seen in Table 3 below, two
prototypes for each design feature are tested. A within-subject design for this particu-
lar study thus means that all participants test all four prototypes. A between-subject
design, on the other hand, means that a participant is only being measured on one test
condition. If participants are only exposed to one condition, it might increase the risk of
personal bias across test conditions. A within-subject design requires fewer participants
and minimizes personal bias. However, a within-subject study introduces the risk of a
potential practice effect, where participants perform better with each prototype due to
familiarity.

Statistical methods
7.1
Categorical vs. continuous variables
Before you can choose which statistical method to use for your data, it is necessary to
define the level of measurements of the dependent and independent variables. This means
specifying how your variables are being measured and the nature of the recorded numbers
that represent your variables [16]. Essentially, variables can either be categorical or con-
tinuous, and within each of these classes, there are several different levels of measurement.
A categorical variable segments something into different categories. For instance, a sub-
ject can be pregnant or not pregnant, and it can be alive or dead. There is a clear border
between these categories, thus a subject can only fit into one of them. Such categori-
cal variables are called binary variables [16]. Another categorical variable is a nominal
variable, which is when there are more than two categories. One great example of a
nominal variable would be blood type, where you only can have one of the seven different
blood types. Lastly ordinal variables are also categorical, and they are quite close to
nominal variables in the sense of dividing data into several categories. However, with an
ordinal variable, the categories have a logical order. For instance, if you would like to
order a sample of people based on their level of education, you could have three ordered
categories such as high school, bachelor’s degree, and master’s degree. Thus, the ordinal
variable does not provide any information about the differences between values as with
the nominal variable, but instead, the order in which they occur [16].
The opposite of categorical variables is continuous variables, which can take on any value
on the measurement scale you are using [16]. An example of a continuous variable is a
5-point rating scale, which contains any value from 1-5. If the difference between 1 and 2
is equal to the difference between 2 and 3, or 4 and 5, the variables would be considered
as interval. So, an interval variable includes intervals where the differences between the
different values are equal. Another level of continuous variables is ratio variables, which
is similar to the interval variable, however it requires a true zero point. Weight is a ratio
variable, since 0.0 kg essentially means ”no weight” and 4.0 kg means that something is
twice as heavy as 2.0 kg. Hence, for a variable to be considered ratio, it further demands
meaningful ratios of values along the scale, allowing for meaningful comparisons.
In this study, independent variables are audible feedback and hold force. Both variables
are categorical. Audible feedback implies two categories, start-of-dose and end-of-dose
clicks, and continuous clicks. Hold force is divided into low minimum hold force and
high minimum hold force. Since both variables are divided into two, non-contrasting
categories, they must be specified as nominal variables.
The dependent variable in this study is use error. This variable is a categorical binary
variable as it contains only two contrasting values, use error or success.
7.2
Parametric vs. non-parametric
Additional terms that are important to know and understand in statistics are para-
metric and non-parametric test [16]. Parametric tests typically involve data distributed
41
==PAGE:43==
according to probability distributions, such as the normal distribution. In contrast, non-
parametric tests, often termed ”distribution-free,” do not make assumptions about the
data distribution. Another distinguishing factor between the two test types is that para-
metric tests are exclusively suited for ratio and interval data, while non-parametric tests
apply to all data types, with particular emphasis on nominal and ordinal data [44].
This suggests that non-parametric tests are considerably more applicable compared to
parametric tests. However, parametric tests have a higher probability of detecting a po-
tential true effect, making them superior in terms of statistical power. Hence, parametric
tests are generally preferred, but there are situations where non-parametric tests become
necessary, such as when the data is not normally distributed or when the distribution
of the data is unknown.
Given that this study involves nominal and binary data, a
non-parametric test should be employed.
7.3
McNemar’s test
To statistically analyze whether continuous clicks and a low hold force individually
reduce use errors, a non-parametric test was employed. The purpose of the statistical
test was to assess whether differences in the number of use errors between two types of
prototypes are real or due to random effects. For this purpose, the McNemar’s test was
utilized [45]. This method is the chi-square equivalent for paired nominal data, hence it
is often referred to as the McNemar’s Chi-Square test. McNemar’s test assumes that the
data is paired and nominal, that there is one independent variable with two dependent
categories, and that the two groups in the dependent variable must be mutually exclusive.
By mutually exclusive it means that the outcome variable should be binary and thus only
have exactly two possible outcomes where each participant can only be linked to one of
the outcome values (e.g. use error/success).
Generally, McNemar’s test is used to find a change in proportion for the paired data.
Related to this study it means detecting a change in use errors with one prototype com-
pared to another prototype. The foundation to run a McNemar’ test is to arrange the
data in a 2x2 contingency table, which is a table that organizes the data within two rows
and two columns. Each cell represents the number of paired observations in two related
categories. Specifically, the cells indicate how many observations fall into each combina-
tion of outcomes. For example, how many participants succeeded in using one prototype,
but failed in the other. A specific example related to this study can be seen in Table 6.

test-statistics is, the larger the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis.

Logistic regression and order effect
Since the study employs a 4x4 Latin square design to control the order of testing each
of the four prototypes, a potential practice effect should also be considered. This effect
arises because participants may gradually gain knowledge and experience as they test the
prototypes, potentially leading to improved performance with each prototype they test
[44]. This effect is also called an order effect. The study includes four prototypes: A,
B, C, and D. As shown in Table 4, each prototype appears first exactly once across four
sessions. This means that among the 24 participants, six started with testing prototype
A, six started with prototype B, six with prototype C, and six with prototype D.
To determine whether the order of testing affects outcome variables, logistic regression
can be utilized. The logistic regression predicts the probability of an outcome Y occur-
ring based on values of a predictor variable X. Hence a logistic regression with multiple
predictor values is given by:
P(Y ) =
1

(2)
Here, P(Y ) is the probability of an outcome variable Y occurring, Xn is the predictor
variable and bn is its respective regression coeﬀicient. b0 is the Y intercept, and exp is the
base of natural logarithms[16]. The value of each regression coeﬀicient must be estimated
to run the analysis, which is done using Maximum-Likeliood Estimation (MLE). Based on
the available predictors, the (MLE) method fits models to the data and selects the most
optimal model that yields predicted values closest to the observed values. This means
that MLE identifies the coeﬀicients that maximize the likelihood of the observed values
occurring [16]. Once all regression coeﬀicients are estimated in a logistic regression, the
probability of an outcome variable can be predicted by plugging in the specific values of
the predictor variables into the logistic regression equation. If the predictor variables are
binary, they are assigned either 0 or 1, representing their presence or absence. However,
if the predictor variables are continuous or have several categories, they should likewise
be assigned their specific value.
Concerning the order effect in this specific study, there would be four predictor values
for order and four predictor values for prototypes. Each of the eight values is categorical
and binary (0 or 1). The logistic regression model would therefore look like this:

Thus P(Y ) is the probability of a use error occurring (Y ), based on the values of the
predicted variables.

The argument family=binomial was specified to indicate a logistic model [51].

Binomial test
During the studies, user preference between prototypes has further been collected.
Thus it is relevant to estimate whether there is a significant difference in preference.
For this purpose, a Binomial test can be utilized, because this test assesses whether a
proportion of a binary variable is equal to a hypothesized value [55]. The test follows
a binomial distribution, which models the probability of obtaining one of two outcomes
(e.g. success and use error) based on the sample size n and probability p of specifically
obtaining one of the outcomes (e.g. success). In this context, the two binary variables
would be prefer and not prefer.
Underlying assumptions for the binomial test are that outcome variables are binary, ob-
servations are independent of one another, a fixed sample n, and all observations would
have the same probability of having a specific outcome.
Additionally, the binomial test assumes that the population proportion of one outcome
equals a specific hypothesized value [55]. In this research, the objective is to determine
whether there is a significant difference in preference for each prototype compared to an
equal distribution of preferences. Hence, the null hypothesis is defined as follows:

outcome. Thus in this example, n = 24, r = 12 (50% of 24), and p = 50%. An RStudio

9
Discussion
9.1
Discussion of results
This study aimed to evaluate how design features can be specified to minimize pre-
mature lifts by assessing the impact of different types of audible feedback and hold forces
on the number of premature lifts occurring in a user study. The analysis of whether con-
tinuous clicks reduce premature lift compared to start-of-dose/end-of-dose clicks showed
that there were significantly fewer premature lifts with continuous clicks (p = 0.023).
Furthermore, study results revealed that a low hold force significantly reduced the num-
ber of premature lifts as opposed to a high hold force (p = 0.041). Potential root causes
for these study results will be discussed in this chapter.
While there is a possibility of order effects due to the study setup, logistic regression
analysis indicated that the order of testing had no significant effect on the results. A
detailed description of the analysis results can be found in Appendix L. Furthermore, the
study results demonstrated no significant association between prior experience and the
number of premature lifts (p = 0.068 for start-of-dose/end-of-dose, p = 0.68 for continu-
ous clicks, and p = 0.64 for high hold-force. Therefore, order effect and prior experience
will not be considered as potential root causes.
9.1.1
Audible feedback
Although there was a significant difference in premature lift between the two types of
audible feedback, participants showed a nearly equal preference for both types of audible
feedback and thus no significant difference (p = 0.839). Similarly, the SUS scores for
both types of audible feedback showed no significant difference (p = 0.34). To under-
stand why preference distribution and SUS scores do not align with the reduced number
of premature lifts, insights from the participant interview are considered.
While the statistical analysis in the results revealed that continuous clicks reduced pre-
mature lifts as opposed to start-of-dose/end-of-dose clicks, five out of seven participants
who committed use errors only with start-of-dose/end-of-dose clicks have during the in-
terviews indicated a preference towards this type of audible feedback. This could be an
indication of users arguing for their satisfaction with this type of audible feedback, and
while satisfaction is an important component in usability engineering [8], it is more crucial
in this case to look at the effectiveness and eﬀiciency. These can be measured through
the participant’s performance during the tests, which yielded that continuous clicks were
significantly more effective in reducing premature lift. However, such a mitigation would
act as information for safety risk control measure, which is the least prioritized control
measure of the three described in Section 3.2. Thus continuous clicks would potentially
be less effective against negative transfer considering the tendency of experienced users
not always reading and following the instructions.
Many injection-experienced participants mentioned that they counted in their heads
rather than relying on audible feedback due to previous experience. Despite claiming
they counted to 10, they lifted their hands prematurely before the audible feedback in-
dicated the end of the injection. This suggests that participants counted too quickly,
55
==PAGE:57==
resulting in premature lifting. Participants relying on what they are used to indicates a
bias of negative transfer at their first encounter with the prototype. Should perceived
preference towards the start-of-dose/end-of-dose clicks be considered, Schmettow et al.
(2017 [57]) argue that experienced users will need to relearn existing routines associated
with similar devices as experience with predicate devices can inhibit learning the new
interface due to negative transfer. That’s a risk that needs to be cautiously evaluated as
a lack of re-training potentially can lead to premature lift. Hence, the statistical analysis
based on participant performance with the two types of audible feedback provides valu-
able insights regarding the effectiveness and eﬀiciency of the user interface.
Additionally, the participant interview revealed that while all participants understood
continuous clicks, many misunderstood start-of-dose/end-of-dose clicks. Some thought
the initial click indicated injection completion, causing them to lift their hand after a
few seconds. Other participants did not expect an end signal following the start signal.
These participant insights indicate that a potential root cause for premature lift with
start-of-dose/end-of-dose clicks is the misinterpretation of the meaning of the individual
clicks. Misinterpretation of the audible feedback could be explained by the study setup
since participants did not receive detailed instructions on how to simulate administering
an injection. As argued in Section 6.4.3, the rationale behind this was to avoid prim-
ing the participants too much into doing what they have been instructed rather than
assessing if the individual types of audible feedback were intuitive enough for users to
understand. Guerlain et al. (2010 [43]), demonstrated increased success in performing
critical tasks when participants had access to the IFU. In real-life use scenarios, the users
do have access to instructions, thus reducing misunderstandings of how to operate the
medical device assuming they read the IFU. The same observations were done in a us-
ability evaluation of an autoinjector by Hudry et al (2017 [17]), where participants were
provided an IFU without further explanation, resulting in a performance rate of ≥75%.
The performance rate increased to 100% after further explanation of the IFU. However,
despite access to the IFU, one of the most common use errors observed by Hudry et al.
was lifting the device before hearing the second click, aligning with findings from this
study. These findings indicate that despite having access to instructions, feedback mech-
anisms from a device can still be misinterpreted. Thus Hudry et al. stressed addressing
the importance of hearing the second click in order to prevent premature lifting.
Another root cause of premature lift worth touching upon in this discussion is the pos-
sibility of misinterpreting continuous clicks. Participant interviews revealed that some
participants found the continuous clicks confusing because they expected click tones to
change as the injection progressed. The prototype utilized in this study produces mono-
tone clicks, causing participants to feel uncertain about the injection’s completion. Rather
than interpreting varying click tones, as could have been the expectations for such type
of audible feedback, they put a lot of effort into concentrating until the final click. Thus
continuous clicks with varying click tones as an indication of injection progress might fur-
ther reduce the number of premature lift, optimizing the effectiveness of injection. Five
out of the seven participants who committed use errors only with start-of-dose/end-of-
dose clicks preferred this type over the continuous clicks because of the monotone sounds
of the continuous clicks.
Moreover, a notable observation is that all participants who committed a use error with
56
==PAGE:58==
continuous clicks also committed a use error with the start of dose and end of dose clicks.
Thus no participants committed a use error solely with continuous click. Among the
seven participants who committed a use error with continuous clicks, six participants
preferred continuous clicks over start-of-dose/end-of-dose clicks, as they found it easier
to focus and block out environmental noise with continuous clicks. Not only will it reduce
the potential risk of missing a second click with start-of-dose/end-of-dose clicks, partic-
ipants also stated that continuous clicks acted as a reassuring measure of the injection
progressing as expected.
Despite the distribution of user preference, these results suggest that, for enhancing the
safety and effectiveness of autoinjectors [9], continuous clicks could be a better design
choice as they minimize premature lift by reducing the risk of misinterpreting the audible
feedback.
9.1.2
Hold force
The study results demonstrated a significant preference for the low hold force pro-
totype over the high hold force prototype (p = 0.00661). Furthermore, the SUS scores
for the low hold force prototype were significantly higher than those for the high hold
force prototype (p = 0.01). These findings further align with the performance tests that
indicated a lower hold force is more optimal for minimizing premature lift.
As noted in Section 8.4, six use errors were recorded for a high hold-force, although
no participants prematurely lifted their hands. These incidents were classified as use
errors because participants failed to maintain a hold force above the threshold of 8.2 N.
Autoinjector users must maintain at least the minimum hold force to ensure injection
success. Thus, analyzing common force application patterns is relevant to defining an
optimal hold force.
The study results indicated that 8.2 N might be too high, potentially causing users to
not apply suﬀicient force. Despite statistically significant differences in force applied with
the two prototypes, participants consistently applied more force than required with the
low-force prototype. This observation suggests that participants unintentionally apply
excessive force when not encountering significant resistance, which reveals insights into
natural force application tendencies. 50% participants had a minimum force above 8.94
N, indicating that half of the participants applied forces between 8.94 N-19.41 N which
is excessively higher than the 3.6 N required by the prototype. Participant interviews
supported this statement about unconsciously applying excessive force. A lot of the par-
ticipants said that they did not initially feel a difference between the prototypes, however
when trying them again, they could easily feel that one was much lighter than the other.
On the other hand, some participants felt a clear difference and stated it even during the
test or immediately after.

limited hand dexterity.
Interestingly, the force values recorded in this study diverge from those found in Schneider
et al.’s study (2020 [40]) study, where the participants’ applied force was similarly mea-
sured using a force detector. Schneider et al. documented force across three simulated
scenarios varying in injection duration. For adult patients injecting for 15-20 seconds,
they reported minimum, mean, and maximum values of 17.54N, 19.97N, and 28.44 N,
respectively. These values are notably higher than the values observed in this study. This
deviation could be explained by differences in study setup. Schneider et al. simulated
injections on a foam cushion attached to the abdomen, closely mimicking real-world in-
jection scenarios. In contrast, this study positioned the EMAP device on a table, with
injections performed in a downward direction. Additionally, it should be noted that the
force values from Schneider et al.’s study are calculated based on six participants, whereas
58
==PAGE:60==
the values in this study are based on 24. It is thus worth noting that should the setup
of the user study in this thesis be similar to the one from Schneider et al., there is a
potential of obtaining different results.
Results from this study suggest that a low hold force is a better design choice than
a high hold force for ensuring optimal injection. However, the recorded force values in-
dicated that hold forces can exceed 3.6 N, and further research is needed to define the
maximum appropriate hold force that still ensures safe and effective injection.
9.2
Limitations
The validity of the study results is subject for discussion due to several encountered
limitations in the user study. Firstly, one limitation concerns the recruited participants.
While it is relevant to evaluate the performance of individuals from the general popu-
lation, it would have been highly beneficial to also collect data from individuals with
limited hand dexterity. This additional data could have further supported discussions
on defining an ”ideal” minimum hold force. Furthermore, due to time constraints in this
master’s thesis, the sample size was relatively small compared to other studies, where
typically 70-100 participants are recruited [18], [21]. The smaller sample size limits the
diversity within the participant population, encompassing fewer variations in age and
participant profiles (e.g. caregiver, HCP, self-injector, healthy individual).
Another notable limitation concerns the study setup for measuring force application.
Since the EMAP was placed on a table, it resulted in a simulated injection site at ap-
proximately chest height for participants. In real-world scenarios, injections typically
occur in the abdomen or thigh, differing significantly in both height and angle from the
simulated setup in this study. Thus, the measured force values may not accurately rep-
resent the forces applied during real injections.
Additionally, study findings regarding audible feedback are constrained by the primitive
prototype that was developed. Participants expressed expectations for continuous clicks
to vary gradually in tone to indicate injection duration. Therefore, the monotone click
generated by a low-quality buzzer led some participants to prefer the start-of-dose/end-
of-dose clicks over the continuous clicks. Thus, the prototype’s audible feedback may not
have effectively simulated the typical sounds of a mechanical autoinjector, limiting the
study’s insights into user preferences and experiences.
Furthermore, a limitation in the framework of this master’s thesis is how co-creation
sessions were implemented. Integrating co-creation sessions into the initial stages could
have yielded valuable insights from autoinjector experts, refining the problem statement
and deepening the exploration of relevant issues. The use of ability prompt cards during
these sessions would have further contributed to a better understanding of user needs.
Furthermore, involving actual injection-device users in the co-creation workshop with
technical autoinjector experts would have facilitated valuable and nuanced discussions,
due to their diverse perspectives.

==PAGE:61==
9.3
Further work
Future studies could benefit from video recording sessions to analyze participants’ de-
vice operation, potentially identifying underlying cases of observed behaviors and errors.
Additionally, investigating negative transfer effects, particularly with regard to audible
feedback, by comparing continuous clicks against existing autoinjectors on the market
could provide deeper insights into user behaviors.
Given the small sample size in this study, future research should aim to include a more
diverse sample, specifically targeting vulnerable user groups such as those with impaired
hearing, impaired vision, limited hand dexterity, and elderly individuals. This expanded
participant sample would provide valuable data and insights necessary for developing a
universal autoinjector that accommodates a broader range of users.
Additionally, it would be relevant to explore other physical parameters that influence
the force application, such as grip strength and viscosity of drugs. These investigations
could inform the design of autoinjectors with optimized force profiles.
Overall, findings from this master’s thesis can serve as input for future development
of autoinjectors. From a usability perspective at Technolution, these results can be in-
cluded as part of their knowledge when consulting clients on defining user interfaces. For
instance, this study provided data on forces and audible feedback, which Technolution
can use to contribute to the future development of injection devices in general.
Additionally, Technolution can use this user study as an example of how to utilize the
EMAP device for a usability study. This thesis presents the first time the EMAP has been
used for studies. Thus Technolution can draw inspiration from this setup for conducting
internal research and collaborations with clients working with injection devices.

10
Conclusion
The study demonstrated that continuous clicks during autoinjector use resulted in
significantly fewer premature lifts compared to start-of-dose/end-of-dose clicks, thus re-
jecting the null hypothesis. However, nearly half of the participants favored the latter
due to familiarity or perceived simplicity in the fewer number of clicks.
Additionally, the study revealed that a low hold force significantly reduces premature
lift as opposed to a high hold force, also rejecting the null hypothesis. Although no par-
ticipants lifted their hands prematurely when testing the force-specific prototypes, visual
inspection of the force-time curves for the high hold force prototype showed that six par-
ticipants at some point applied less than the required hold force of 8.2 N. Such incidents
were classified as premature lift, indicating that 8.2 N as a hold force may be excessive.
Furthermore, the findings suggest that participants tend to apply more force than neces-
sary with the low-force prototype, reflecting an unconscious tendency to overcompensate.
The significant preference for the low-force prototype underscores the benefit of a lower
minimum hold force in enhancing user experience and accommodating a wide range of
users, including those with limited hand dexterity. Therefore, future research should ex-
plore the maximum hold force that ensures effective and safe injections while maintaining
ease of use.
Despite the study’s limitations, including a small sample size and an artificial testing
environment, the results provide valuable insights into autoinjector usability. This under-
scores the importance of user-centered approaches in developing a universal autoinjector
that meets diverse user needs while ensuring safety and effectiveness. Further studies
with more diverse participants and realistic testing setups are recommended to validate
these findings.
To answer the research questions of how to minimize premature lift, it can thus be
concluded that continuous clicks and a low hold force are suitable design choices for
autoinjectors.

Expert interview
1. Can you please share your experience with different types of autoinjector/pen-
injector devices? I have worked with HF assignments within the pharmaceutical in-
dustry for more than 15 years. My primary focus has been on combination products that
combine a drug product with an injectable device such as e.g. a prefilled syringe or an
autoinjector.
My experience is based on assignments from different pharmaceutical companies in both
Denmark and abroad so my experience is based on evaluation of a broad range of different
prefilled syringes and autoinjectors.
I have conducted multiple HF studies with end-users of prefilled syringes or autoinjec-
tor: Patients with e.g. diabetes, obesity, growth disorders, cardiovascular diseases and
allergic/anti-immune diseases etc. as well as provided input to device development and
Instruction material.
The end-users that I have the most experience with are healthcare professionals (nurses),
patients (adolescents and adults) as well as caregivers.
2. Among the identified errors, which ones occur most frequently?
A lot of different use errors happen during HF evaluations and later on after launch.
They depend on e.g; the complexity of the combination product being evaluated; the
users evaluation of the combination product (how different is it from the users mental
model of how it should work); the user’s capabilities in combination with the combina-
tion product’s limitations, as well as the context in which the combination products is
evaluated. When we start up the development of a new combination product we therefore
look into data from similar combination products or data from previous versions of the
combination product. The data we have at hand is based on oﬀicial databases as well
as the complaints databased handled by the companies themselves i.e we do only have a
limited overview of how frequent use errors happen as we seldom know which ones are
reported but here is a small list of common use errors:
For pre-filled syringes and autoinjectors some of the more general use errors we know
of are:
• Not checking expiry date before use
• Incorrect storage of device
• Needle sticks during handling
• Diﬀiculties with cap removal (especially on pre-filled syringes with small caps)
• Premature activation
• Pre-mature removal of device
• Failure/diﬀiculties to start the device
• Diﬀiculties to maintain pressure on device during injection (especially relevant if injec-
tions take longer time e.g. for injections with 2mL or 3mL devices or with highly viscous
drugs)
• Medication mix-up (If different doses or drugs are to be taken at the same time)
Instead of talking about frequency of use errors I would like to talk about criticality of
use errors as this is what we focus on as HF engineers.

3. From your perspective, what would you consider as the most critical
use-error(s)?
As HF engineers we always strive to make the combination product as safe and effective
as possible during our development process but we will have to accept that devices in the
hands of users can always cause use errors. Therefore we focus our key attention to the
use errors that will compromise safe and effective use of the combination product.
When we evaluate the safe and effective use of a combination product we will primarily
focus on the tasks that are critical for the user to perform. The critical tasks for a com-
bination products is defined by the FDA as: “user tasks that, if performed incorrectly or
not performed at all, would or could cause harm to the patient or user, where harm is
defined to include compromised medical care. “
The definition of “harm” is described in ISO standard as: “Injury or damage to the
health of people, or damage to property or the environment.”
The definition of “compromised medical care” is not so clearly defined but FDA’s Center
for Drug Evaluation and Research (CDER) indicates that compromised medical care is
considered a type of harm that impacts dosing and administration of the product i.e,
partial and missed doses will be deemed critical, even if the drug itself might not be
considered truly critical.
4 In your opinion, which design features would be most relevant to investigate
when aiming to minimize use errors and enhance the ease and intuitiveness
of injection in autoinjectors?
To minimize use errors associated with critical tasks of handling of pre-filled syringes or
autoinjectors I would look into design features that could mitigate the use errors related
to the injection process itself:
• Premature removal of device as this causes drug underdoses and will compromise the
eﬀicacy of the treatment. Further, this is especially tricky as the user is often not aware
that they have removed the device prematurely and therefore the use error is more severe
as it will be a repetitive use error and not just a one-off use error. Today device manufac-
turers have diﬀiculties with click-sounds made from spring-activated autoinjectors (most
on-market auto-injectors incorporate audible feedback to indicate the beginning and/or
the end of the injection sequence. Typically, this sound is generated by the spring-action
that triggers the injection, or by the plunger bottoming out once the injection has com-
pleted.).
If the user misinterprets the click-sounds as confirmation of completed dose delivered
then they will not have received the complete dose. As the click-sound is unavoidable
form a spring-activated autoinjector I think it could be interesting to focus on design
features that can support the user’s confirmation of end-of-dose.
• Maintaining the device during injection as this also causes underdoses and will com-
promise the eﬀicacy of the treatment. This use error is often due to the required forces
required to activate and hold down the device are not meeting the limitations of the user’s
capabilities which indicates that the diﬀiculties with handling the device will be a contin-

uous problem. Further, more and more companies are looking into injection of 3mL doses
and this will put more focus on the hold time being designed to fit the capabilities of the
intended users. To support a more robust handling of devices during injections I think
it could be interesting to focus on design features that will allow fragile or disabled user
groups to handle autoinjectors during long injection processes (more than 12 seconds).

1
Introduction
Autoinjectors are medical devices designed to automatically deliver a dose of drug, sim-
plifying the injection process compared to manually controlled injection devices. Through
usability studies it has been proven that the automatic process steps (activate, maintain
device on injection site, remove autoinjector after complete injection) associated with use
of an autoinjector is commonly known to generate use errors due to inappropriate design of
technical attributes such as activation force, shape and grip of the device, and feedback cues
that allows the user to unsertanding when injection has been completed. It is therefore rele-
vant to explore the usability of autoinjectors and further investigate potential root causes for
use errors associated with using an autoinjector. To do so, this study consists of systematic
literature study with the purpose of exploring the topic of the usability of autoinjectors and
specific design features. Furthermore, already existing data about autoinjectors and pen-
injectors will be analyzed to identify common use-related problems associated with these
devices. Lastly, the knowledge gained from literature studies and data analysis will be inte-
grated to formulate problem statement for related to specific design features of autoinjectors
that would be relevant to further investigate in future work.
2
Literature Study
This chapter describes a literature study that was conducted with the purpose of collect-
ing present knowledge within the field of Usability of Autoinjectors. The primary outcome
measure of interest was to identify use errors related to the use of autoinjectors. This litera-
ture study did not focus on any kinds of specific diseases nor brands of autoinjector devices.
Therefore a generic search was conducted on two relevant literature databases, PubMed.gov
[21] and DTU Findit [5]. Pubmed.gov contains millions of biomedical literature while DTU
Findit holds plenty of scientific literature relevant the Technical University of Denmark.
Hence these two databases together forms a good basis for finding relevant articles.
2.1
Systematic Search Strategy
The first step in the this systematic review was to define relevant key words for searching.
This study contains two focus areas, namely autoinjectors and usability. The key words
for autoinjector were a combination of different acronyms. Using acronyms and all possible
spelling options for a word will increase the chances of finding more relevant articles.
Three different search strings with different purposes were defined. To find generic articles
related to the usability or use of an autoinjector, the following string was defined:
(“Usability” OR “User”) AND (“Autoinjector” OR “Auto-injector” OR “Auto injector”)
2
==PAGE:90==
To specify the search above to also involve safety and eﬀicacy, the following string was
defined:
(“Usability” OR “User”) AND (“Autoinjector” OR “Auto-injector” OR “Auto injector”)
AND (“safety” OR “safe and effective” OR “eﬀicacy”)
Lastly, to find generic articles related to use-related errors in the use of autoinjectors, the
following string was defined:
(“Use error” OR “User error” OR “Use-related error” OR “User-related error”) AND
(“Autoinjector” OR “Auto-injector” OR “Auto injector”)
These three search strings were applied on PubMed.gov and DTU Findit, respectively. On
PubMed.gov, the three searches collectively resulted in 140 hits while it resulted in 252 hits
on DTU Findit. Since these numbers are the sum of each of the three searches, they do in
fact include duplicates of publications. To identify possible relevant articles, a screening of
article titles was done. The purpose of this screening was to recognize articles that, based on
their titles, dealt with the usability of autoinjectors and potential use errors related to the
devices. The analytical framework for this systematic search strategy can be seen on figure 1.
Figure 1: Analytical framework for this systematic review
Specific inclusion criteria for this screening round was predefined. Only english-written pa-
pers that dealt with mechanical pen injectors and autoinjectors were included. Moreover,
the titles of the papers should also indicate that their focal points are either usability and
use errors of these injection devices, patient adherence and preference, or design features.
Since the scope of this study is use errors related to mechanical autoinjectors, it would not
3
==PAGE:91==
be relevant to investigate the usability of other types of injection devices. However, pen
injectors are quite similar to autoinjectors in terms of both design and user tasks. The only
major difference between these two types of devices is that the pen injector requires the user
to manually attach the needle to the pen, where an autoinjector is ready-to-use. Therefore,
this study also includes papers dealing with pen injectors.
While a majority of titles fulfilled the inclusion criteria, many articles were excluded from
the review because they either dealt with drugs rather than device, needle-free autoinjec-
tors, software products, packaging, or did simply not reveal relevance to the usability of
mechanical pen injectors and injectors. Among the many titles, a total of 52 articles from
PubMed.gov and 65 articles from DTU Findit were selected for an abstract screening round.
It should be noted that many of these articles are duplicates from each of the databases.
Without duplicates, a total of 78 articles were selected. After screening the abstracts, 24
articles were excluded because they did not meet the expectations of revealing relevance to
the scope of this study. One paper was excluded because it was written in a non-englsih
language. Overall, 53 publications were included for a full article read, while 28 of these
articles were relevant and selected for this study. While reading the 28 articles, 4 other
relevant articles were found in the references of these papers. This is a very meaningful step
in a literature review as it allows you to find articles that were not identified in the searches.
Overall, 32 articles were found relevant to gain knowledge about the usability of autoin-
jectors. 6 articles dealt with patient preference ([4], [1], [13], [35], [22], [11], 25 dealt with
the usability of the injection devices ([4], [36], [24], [13], [40], [14], [41], [19], [33], [2], [7], [34],
[23], [12], [17], [37], [6], [29], [15], [20], [25], [32], [22], [18], [18]), 5 dealt with specific technical
attributes of the devices ([1], [28], [27], [11], [26]), and 2 papers dealt with analyzing adverse
events reported to the authorities ([31], [15]. 4 papers were based on literature review studies
([4], [30], [11], [39], [26]) while the rest were based on investigational test studies.

only 12 participants in this group were included. The U.S Food and Drug Administration’s
(FDA) recommendations for a human factors study is at there should be least 15 study partic-
ipants in each group.[38]. Therefore the small sample size is in fact a limitation of this study.
A subsequent study that assessed the usability of a new autoinjector among RA patients
was Dománska et al. (2017 [6]). In this study, 76 patients were recruited, making it a quite
larger sample size than the one in Hudry et al.. During this usability study, 10 out of 76 pa-
tients claimed that they had diﬀiculties removing the needle cap and 6 out of 76 prematurely
removed the autoinjector before hearing the the second click. Although both of these use
errors could be correlated with the possible poor hand condition of RA patients, the study
concluded that the results demonstrated that patients with limited hand dexterity and no
prior experience with autoinjector devices were capable of self-administration confidently,
safely, and effectively.

one summative validation study of a disposable autoinjector among a broad user population
including MS and RA patients. In their studies there was a dominating tendency of partici-
pants who had not read the IFU failed to remove the needle cap (47% participants). However
after reading the IFU, no use errors were detected. The study claims that root causes were
that participants assumed the cap was an activation button due to negative transfer from
previous experience with an injection device. Lageat et al. therefore concludes that these
use errors were not caused by physical disability. In order to minimize the risk of negative
transfer, manufacturers should consider mitigations on either device design, packaging, or
IFU to make to ensure a more intuitive use of the device. For instance, it could be changing
the color of the needle cap or emphasizing in the IFU the importance of holding the device
against injection site for a specific amount of seconds.
While Lageat et al. stated that the needle cap removal errors were not associated with
hand disabilities, Schneider et al. (2021 [28]) did conduct a study that specifically revolved
around investigating the ability of users to remove an autoinjector cap. Based on 29 patients
with chronic rheumatoid arthritis, the authors studied the effects of the cap-removal force
and user characteristics on the perceived ease of decapping the device. Participants were
asked to decap four non-functional autoinjectors with different target cap-removal forces
(25N, 35N, 45N, and 55N). The primary outcome of the study was effective completion of
cap removal and the secondary outcome was the users perceived ease of decapping. The
perceived ease of decapping was assessed using a 5-point Likert scale. The results revealed
that all participants were successfully able to decap all autoinjectors regardless of sex, age,
hand dexterity, and previous experience with autoinjectors. Hence the study claimed that
cap removal forces up to 55N would enable effective device usage, regardless of dexterity
impairment. However, the patient-reported perceived ease of use did gradually decrease as
the target cap removal force increased. Additionally, dexterity impairment seemed to neg-
atively influence the perceived ease of use, which could potentially impact patients’ device
preference. This is why Schneider et al. stated that human factor-induced perspectives on
the characterization of the technical attributes of a device is necessary to consider.
As previously mentionied, multiple sclerosis is a disease that can negatively affect the hand
dexterity. Hence Terlizzi et al. (2023 [34]) conducted a usability validation of an injection
pen among 17 injection experienced patients with relapsing MS and 15 injection naive pa-
tients. Use errors detected among the injection experienced patients were not identifying the
appropriate injection site and premature removal of the device. These use errors were also
detected among the injection naive patients, however in this group there were further inci-
dents of improper storage of device, contamination of needle while recapping, touching the
needle guard while investigation, not maintaining pressure during injection, and not reading
the IFU prior to administering the device. Hence this indicates that experienced users have a
tendency to perform less use-related errors. Terlizzi et al. claimed that potential root causes
for use-related issues was overlooking or misinterpreting info in the IFU, negative transfer

Autoinjectors for the treatment of anaphylaxis
4 articles ([41], [31], [30], [11]) in this literature review studied adrenaline/epinephrine au-
toinjectors used for the treatment of anaphylaxis, one of them being the review study by A.J
Frew (2011 [11]). The aim of A.J. Frew’s study was to identify the ”ideal” features of an
adrenaline autoinjector for the treatment of anaphylactic attacks. The study addressed a list
of problems with the use of adrenaline autoinjectors, such as needle phobia especially among
children experiencing recurrent anaphylactic reactions. A questionnaire survey of children
prescribed with an EpiPen autoinjector revealed that they had only used their device in 29%
of the anaphylactic attacks they experienced. Therefore, A.J. Frew suggested that hiding
the needle in the design of an autoinjector would potentially increase the likelihood of it
being used. Furthermore, the study claimed that incorrect self-injection techniques were
concerningly frequent and only 30-44% of patients who had been prescribed autoinjectors
were able to demonstrate correct self-administration. Incorrect administration also applies
for healthcare professionals, where a high proportion (79%) did not demonstrate correct self-
administration. Considering that healthcare professionals have the job of training patients in
the use of their injection devices, this evidence is quite disconcerting. Based on his findings,
A.J. Frew defined a list of criteria for an ”ideal” autoinjector, including correct delivery of
the correct dose, the device must be robust and reliable enough to withstand real-life use,
and the device must be easy and intuitive to use. Specifically the simplicity of use is quite
relevant for self-administration while having an anaphylactic attack since the circumstances
are often stressful in these cases. Since the adrenaline/epinephrine medication can be con-
sidered ”lifesaving” in very severe cases, there is no room for injection failure. Therefore A.J.
Frew suggested considering easy needle cap removal, minimal activation steps, and intuitive
and clear instructions to simplify the use of the device.
Simons et al. (2010, [31]) subsequently dealt wit self-administration of autoinjectors in the
treatment of anaphylaxis. This paper estimated the number of intentional injections from
epinephrine autoinjectors that had happened in the United States. A total of 15190 unin-
tentional injections were reported to the US poison Control Centers in the time frame from
1994-2007. The median age of those who unintentionally injected was 14 years and 85% of
the cases were at home or other residence. The number of unintentional injections increases
anually. Therefore, Simons and colleagues claimed that there should be increased focus on
vigilance in training the trainers and regularly coaching of those who carry epinephrine au-
toinjectors. There should also be increased public awareness of the role of autoinjectors in
the first aid treatment of anaphylaxis.
Patient preference of design features
Among the 6 articles that evaluated patient preference regarding autoinjector features, ease
of use, ease of grip, visual feedback, and audible feedback were the most common. In their
study, Tischer and colleagues (2018 [35]) investigated the patients and nurses preferences for
8

autoinjectors used for rheumatoid arthritis medication. 200 RA patients and 100 nurses were
interviewed and asked to rate the importance of 11 autoinjector attributes. The outcome of
these interviews where that ease of self-injection and ease of grip were the two most impor-
tant attributes for both patients and nurses. However, nurses valued buttonless injection,
ease of performance, and intuitive to use higher than patients did.
An interesting usability study by Bernstein et al.
(2019 [2]) compared the use of two
autoinjectors that differed only in the labeling.
One autoinjector was labeled with pic-
tograms helping the patient understand how to use the device, while the other autoin-
jector was not labeled with pictograms.
The study was conducted among patients with
severe eosinophilic asthma, where 100 patients used the autoinjector with pictograms and
50 used the one without. The results of this usability study revealed that 95% using the
device with pictograms and 89% using the one without pictograms demonstrated success-
ful self-administration. However there was a high preference for using an autoinjector with
pictograms rather than without. Therefore the implementation of pictograms as a design
feature could potentially contribute to the patients feeling more confident and safe while ad-
ministering their device. Especially for autoinjectors that are intended to inject ”life-saving”
medication such as adrenaline/epinephrine, it would be appropriate to consider pictogram
labeling to make the device more intuitive to use.

FDA data analysis (Known Use Problems
Another way to study use errors in the use of autoinjectors is to perform a Known Use
Problems (KUP) analysis [3]. The goal of a KUP analysis is to identify use errors with other
medical devices of the same type that you are studying. This procedure is a part of the
Human Factors Engineering report that is included in a premarket submission to the FDA
[38]. However, this procedure is also advantageous to employ in this study since it is relevant
to identify use errors that have happened in real life situations under real life circumstances.
This differs from the papers that have been read in the literature study since these papers
are typically based on simulated use usability studies that can never fully imitate a real-life
situation which could sometimes be under stressful circumstances (e.g. under an anaphylac-
tic attack).
The first step in this KUP analysis is to gather existing information. A source of informa-
tion about medical devices is the FDA’s public databases. The FDA offers a list of relevant
databases for the purpose of e.g. surveillance of reported events and medical device recalls.
Specifically, the Manufacturer and User Facility Device Experience (MAUDE) offers Medical
Device Reports (MDR) of suspected device-associated deaths, serious injuries and malfunc-
tions within the past 10 years. These MDRs are submitted to the FDA by the manufacturers,
device user facilities, and voluntary reporters such as health care professionals, patients, and
consumers. Therefore, it is possible to obtain a wide range of reported events through this
9
==PAGE:97==
database. [8]
There are several ways to make a search on the MAUDE database [10]. The database offers
a number of different search input such as product class, event type, manufacturer, model
number, report number, brand name, and product code. For searching you can choose to
fill all the fields, some of them, or only one. This allows for very specific and more generic
searches. For the scope of this study, it is desired to obtain as much information as possible
about all kinds of pen injectors and autoinjectors.
In the FDA system, all medical devices are assigned a product code depending on the type
of device.
To gather information about pen injectors and autoinjectors, their respective
product codes were looked up on the FDA website [9]. The common product code for pen
injectors is NSC and for autoinjectors it is KZH. Two searches were done for each of the
product codes in the time frame 1/12/2014-1/12/2024 (U.S. date format) For autoinjectors,
500 events were obtained from this search, while 351 events were obtained for pen injectors.
The data was extracted as a .CSV file, and opened in Excel where each row represented
information about each reported event.
To identify any possible use-related events, a screening process of each event was done.
Row by row, event descriptions were read with the purpose of recognizing any event that
indicated that it could be related to use errors. Among the 851 reported events, a majority
was related to device malfunctioning rather than the user’s perception and handling of the
device. For instance, many reports were about the injection button being stuck, uninten-
tional ejection, and drug leakage without any indication that these events had anything to
do with the user. The focal point of this screening was root causes behind reported events
that were only use-related, therefore device malfunctioning was not considered. Beyond that,
a lot of the events reported about the pen injectors were related to the application of the
needle. Since the scope of this study was autoninjectors, which is a ready-to-use device with
a needle, these types of events were also not considered.
Identified use-related errors
Overall, 50 use-related events were identified among the pen injector data, and 18 events
among the autoinjector data.
This makes a total of 68 use-related events found on the
MAUDE database from the past 10 years. As can be seen on figure 2, 13 different types of
use-related events were identified. The most frequent event was using the device beyond the
recommended use-life stated in the IFU (25 events). Many users used their non-disposable
devices for 7-10 years or even longer. However, in the IFU it was stated that such devices
should not be used for more than 3 years. This led to the users, experiencing that their
device had stopped working. This is a use-error since they should have switched out the
devices after three years of using it.

Another type of use error that was caused by not following the IFU was improper storage

Relevance for future work
Findings from the literature study underscored the importance of a user-centric approach
when defining design specifications for an autoinjector. In general, the most frequent use
errors were related to premature lifting of device and failed needle cap removal. These use
errors were observed to not being correlated with hand impairment. Instead, these events
were related to improper handling of the device. Root causes were, among others, that the
users misinterpreting the importance of specific steps such as counting while injecting or
waiting till audible feedback from the device. This was subsequently supported by results
from the KUP analysis conducted on reported events regarding injection devices. A major-
ity of the identified use-related events were too associated with the user’s not following the
instructions for their device.
Furthermore, the literature study recognized common design feature preferences among pa-
tients and healthcare providers. Ease of use and ease of grip were both highly valued for
administration of autoinjector devices. Additionally, button-less injection and intuitive use
of the autoinjector were frequent preferences.
From these findings it can be concluded that the activation and injection steps of an autoin-
jector are quite interesting for further investigation since many use errors derive from these
steps. For future work it would therefore be relevant to design an autoinjector that allows
for more intuitive and easy injection, focusing on the activation and audible feedback of the
device.
