Abstract
The electric price is a hot topic nowadays, as depending on it, some heavy electric de-
manding tasks should be done in lower price regions. A couple of years ago this difference
was not as noticeable, but with the opening of more renewables to the electricity mix and
increase on fossil fuel prices this difference is becoming more relevant in daily life tasks.
The electricity price is not a random value that is generated, it is affected by several fac-
tors such as the weather, the time period or the energy sources available. On this paper,
an in depth-analysis of the electricity market of the region of Oslo was done, with the
goal of studying if by adding external inputs such as the previously mentioned factors can
increase the accuracy of the electricity price.
The analysis will be done using R as main coding language, using the packages of
and
and comparing the results obtain between them.
As the conclusion of this study, it is demonstrated that depending on the stability of the
electricity market, the forecasts could improve if the correct external inputs are used. In
the case of the Oslo region, the hydro production seemed to be the factor that when
included, showed the best results.
With respect to the models, ARIMAX models show promising results when the electricity
market manifest stable trends. If the market exhibits an erratic behaviour, other methods
that are not ARIMAs are recommended to be used, such as Fourier forecasts.

Introduction
1.1
Objective
The electricity price is highly volatile, and on markets that possess a high share of vari-
able renewable energy sources the variation in the electricity price is even greater when
compared to electric systems relying on conventional technologies [1]. From businesses
to average consumers, until a certain extent everyone is interested in knowing what is the
electricity price going to be in the nearby future.
On this thesis, through different forecast models, and adding extra inputs (specially in-
formation coming from renewable resources), a forecast of the electricity prices will be
done. The country to be analysed will be Norway, to be specific the region of Oslo (NO1).
For achieving this, the coding language that will be used will be R, as the community has
already developed multiple packages that are open source and allows the user to easily
create tools for generating forecasts.
Apart from comparing different forecasts, two packages will be used and compared the
obtained results between them, in order to establish which model is more suitable de-
pending on the scenario. The packages are the
and
. Lastly,
a brief analysis on future work will be done.
This thesis will be using already existing mathematical algorithms, with the main focus on
proving the hypothesis that extra inputs can improve the forecast of the electricity price.
All the mathematical equations that have been used on this project can be found on the
cited bibliography such as [2]. The formula notation that has been used in the equations
that are included on this report follow the guidelines explained on the online document of
”onlineforecast” available at
[3].
1.2
State of the art
As the interest in knowing the electricity price has been constantly increasing for the past
years, the research regarding this topic has also increased. One example of it, is the
paper by Werona and Misiorekb [4] in which they try forecasting the electricity price using
autoregressive models with exogenous variables such as the electricity consumption of
the system as well as the temperature, having better results in some forecasts scenarios
thanks to the use of these extra inputs. Their approach will be used as main inspiration
for this thesis.
More modern papers such as the one written by Qiao and Yang [5], propose the use
of neural networks with the use of preprocessing algorithms to forecast the electricity
price on the US market. The conclusion of this paper shows promising results, having
forecasts extremely precise. The downside of this paper is that the variability factor due
to renewable energies is missing, as the impact of renewables is very low on the US when
compared to Nordic countries such as Denmark or specially Norway,
Another example is the idea proposed by Pordaryaei, Mokhlis and Illias [6] which pro-
ceeds to forecast the deregulated market by applying a hybrid method of artificial neutral
networks and artificial cooperative search algorithms. They also develop a methodology
to select which inputs to use to improve the final result of the forecast. The main source
of data used in this case comes from the Ontario electricity market in 2017, obtaining

More classic methodologies of forecast like ARIMA are still being used. In the paper of
Karabiber and Xydis [7], different forecast methodologies; ARIMA, ANN and Trigonomet-

The overall conclusion of the forecast on the electricity price is that there are a wide
range of possible methods that can be applied to the forecast. Though based on the last
year period (2021-2022), the electricity price has suffered an important increase in the
European region, being this span the months with the greatest electricity price variation
in its history [8], making the use of forecasts a very important tool. The use of forecast
has become more and more popular and the demand for more precise forecasts will be
increasing in the following years.

Case study
2.1
Norway analysis
The main country to be analysed is Norway. The main reason for doing Norway is their
available online resources, mainly precise weather forecasts that can be accessed via
API. Additionally, they count on a high dependency on renewable resources, largely hydro.
88.16% of the electricity demand of Oslo for the past 3 years (from 01-01-2019 until 11-
01-2022) has been covered by hydro energy [8]. On some other regions of Norway this
percentage is even larger, as the region of Oslo is the main consumer of Norway and
this area does not possess as many hydrological resources as other areas [9]. Part of
the electricity they consume is provided by neighbouring countries such as Sweden that
provide the Norwegian electricity grid with nuclear energy or wind energy.
Regarding the data, it was provided by ”Electricity Map”. It consists of multiple parameters
such as: electricity consumption, electricity production, origin of this electricity, electricity
imports, electricity exports, etc. These variables are refreshed on an hourly basis, with
the starting date being the 1st of January 2015 and ending on the end of February 2022.
Whenever there is a missing value due to the malfunction of the system, a spline will be
used between the last and next known value on the dataset.
Norway electric system is divided into five different regions, called as NO1, NO2, NO3,
NO4 and NO5. The region to be studied will be the NO1 region, which correspond to Oslo.
This region has the highest amount of electricity consumers (due to Oslo being the largest
city in Norway) when compared to the other Norwegian areas. In terms of production, NO1
is able to produce approximately half of its energy, while the rest is imported from other
Norwegian regions or neighbouring countries (mainly Sweden).

Norway counts with a total of 1681 hydropower plants in Norway with a capacity of 33055

thirty largest ones account for half of the water storage capacity and satisfy 70% of the
total of the Norwegian electricity consumption. By having large reservoirs, the Norwegian
system to store energy and control electricity production from renewable sources [9].
As other renewable energy sources, the most dominant after hydro is wind energy. Wind
energy started in 2002 with the construction of the Smøla wind farm, with 40 MW. Since
then, the technology has become more popular, reaching a total of approximately 5000
MW by the end of 2021 [10]. Additionally, it is expected that the wind energy capacity will
keep increasing with the installation of new wind farms. These wind farms will be onshore
and offshore. The first offshore project was announced by the Norwegian government at
the beginning of 2022. It auctioned two large areas that consisted of 3 GW of bottom fixed
foundations and 1.5 GW of floating. Norway already counts with the vital infrastructure for
the offshore development, making the offshore technology the most promising renewable
technology for the next years. Lastly, solar energy. In the past 2021 a total of 65MW of
PV capacity. For the past years the technology has been growing constantly, establish-
ing in a total of 225 MW. This technology is becoming extremely popular with the average
household, where several subsidies can be applied [11]. This type of politics have incen-
tivized the adoption of this technology by the common public, increasing drastically the
instalment rate of solar panels.

It is clear that the incidence of the fossil fuels is minimal in their electric generation mix,
though they still have an impact on the electricity price of this region. As stated in the
introduction, nowadays (February 2022) the price of fossil fuel is at a peak record. In the
specific case of Norway, the high fluctuations in the electricity price account for periods
in which the hydro reservoirs and other renewable sources can not fulfil the electricity
demand and there is a demand for gas or other non-renewable energies for the production
of electricity. Even though the gas Norway uses is a national import, the price changes of
gas prices in the European region has ended up affecting the Norwegian market due to
the interconnectivity of the energy economic markets of Europe.
This energy price crisis spike situation is mainly due to a general shortage of gas supply
at worldwide level. It could be a transition period and after some period of time the supply
of gas would be stabilized again, bringing steadiness to the electricity market. Another
possibility is that this transition period stays longer and brings a new regime with big spikes
in electricity prices due to the gas price.
Also, in February 2022, the European Union and different international entities have im-
posed several sanctions in response to Russia’s actions against Ukraine. One of the main
affected sectors is the gas supply to Europe. Several European countries, specially Italy
and Germany ([13]), firmly rely on Russian gas supply for their generation mix. This crisis
has caused an increase in natural gas that is already affecting the electricity prices across
Europe. Experts have estimated that prices won’t go down until 2023 ([14]).

By analyzing on specific the electricity price situation of Norway from the past 3 years,
multiple periods can be distinguished:
• 2019-2020: price fluctuates according to demand, the hydro reservoirs were on
nominal levels. Spikes coincide with high demand periods. Average price of 39.28
[€/MWh].
• 2020-2021: low price period due to an excess of precipitation during winter, causing
an abnormal increase on the hydro reservoirs level. Average price of 9.29 [€/MWh]
[15].
• 2021-2022: increase of prices due to instability of the gas supply. In contrast with
the previous period, there has been a lack of rain that has caused the hydro system
to not be able to supply as much electricity as it is used to. Average price of 74.12
[€/MWh].
The period that wants to be forecasted (starting in 2022) is a period of high uncertainties.
On this analysis it was defined as ”period of high price” the periods in which the price was
above the 90% quantile of the average price on that respective month. The high price
periods of December 2015 and 2021 were compared on figures 2.3 and 2.4 respectively.

2.2
Interest for Monta
This thesis has been done in collaboration with Monta, obtaining the information of Elec-
tricity Map thanks to them.
Monta is a Danish company that is specializing on the management of the charging of
electric vehicles (EVs), from smart charging to the managing of the infrastructure itself.
One of the main options of the Monta app is to be able to smart charge the vehicle when the
price is at the cheapest or when the least CO2 is being produced. This is done using the
information available at the day-ahead market of Nord Pool and estimates from Electricity
Map respectively. The day-ahead market of Nord Pool, as its name indicates, one can

only know the electricity price up to the next 24 hours. Being able to forecast the electricity
price with a wider range could improve the satisfaction of the client, as well as an extra
edge when compared to other services that provide smart charging.
Smart charging is an option that has proven to be vital for Monta users, being this one of
the most used features on their app. In order to enable the smart charging, the charging
station must be connected to the cloud services to be able to receive the updated data of
the demand. This cloud services are usually managed through an interface that is either
on the charger itself or through an app that allows the charger to manage the charging of
the vehicle.
An example of a possible application of this thesis could be a user that has the weekend to

2.3
Understanding the European Energy Market and the
Norwegian Market
The liberalization of the European utility market, mainly electricity and gas, started around
20 years ago (late 1990s). Before this, most of the European markets would consist
of national monopolies controlling the prices of each country. In order to improve the
electric market by means of competition, a progressive liberalization of these markets was
agreed, starting gradually from 1996 in the European Union. But, due to the particular
nature of electricity, meaning that it can not be stored and that certain properties need
to be maintained such as a constant frequency along the grid, requiring a third party
as ”controlling agent”. These problems are solved by keeping a balance between the
consumption and generation of electricity [17].
This ”extra agent”, called power exchanges (such as Nord Pool), come in and keep in
balance the power grid system. They keep the demand and supply of electricity in check
by generating a trading system in which the different players of the market can sell and buy
electricity for a given specific time. Based on the price given by the different generators
for their electricity and with the law of demand and supply, a market priced is fixed. This
price corresponds to the price of the last technology being incorporated into the energy
system. The order of incorporation is created based on how expensive it is for a generator
to produce a unit of energy. This means that renewable energies have always a priority
due to their marginal production cost being almost 0[€/MWh] while other technologies
such as gas and coal will have higher costs. There are special cases such as for nuclear
energy, that as they need to follow a stable regime of generation (their ramp up and ramp
down of energy is extremely low), are given preference and are always the first technology
to be incorporated into the generation mix [18].
Depending on the adjustments that need to be done in the balancing of supply and de-
mand, the electricity market is divided into two types:
• Day-ahead Market (Spot Market): it consists of an open auction that occurs once
per day all year round. All the hours for the next day are traded at the same time.

==PAGE:22==
Before this, participants must submit their generation capacity (selling) as well as
buying order. A curve based on the supply and the demand is generated for each
hour, finishing with a fixed price for each hour of the next day.
• Intraday Market: due to needing more precision and flexibility, the intraday market
allows for a greater resilience for matching the supply and the demand. Trades can
be done an hour, half-hour, fifteen or five minutes before the delivery. This ensures
the right amount of electricity being disposed at the correct time.
These markets are not exclusively nationals, as participants from neighbouring countries
can participate in different markets, as long as the international connections allows the
trading. This is one of the key aspects for achieving the climate goals of the European
Union. The interconnection target set for 2030 is of 15%, meaning that each country
should be able to export at least 15% of their electricity at full capacity. This would allow
the electricity grid to use more renewable energy when a surplus of it is generated by a
country, moving that extra generation to a country with available space for this renewable
energy [19].
On top of the day ahead price, taxes and other charges need to be applied to the end
consumer. In the case of Norway, a rent due to the electric grid being used as well as
taxes depending on the energy consumed are enforced. As a result of the high prices
being achieved at the end of 2021 and 2022, the Norwegian government has implemented
a support package in order to ease the final price by paying part of the bill as well as
reducing the taxes being applied to the bill ([20]).

3.1
Time series analysis
The world is constantly changing. For example in the meteorological field, figures such as
temperature, wind speed, precipitation are constantly recorded. Another example could
be the economics, from company stocks, to interest rates or the GDP of a country. All of
these are examples of sequential values that get updated after a certain amount of time.
The main objective of the time series analysis is to try to find possible trends and forecast
the possible future based on the historic data as well as correlate possible factors that
may affect the trend of this data [21].
3.2
Time series forecasting
An overview of some of the most common forecasting models being used nowadays are:
• Autoregressive (AR): one of the most basic model for forecasting. Taking into ac-
count the historical tries to predict future values. The first autoregressive models
came from Yule on 1927, applying an AR model to identify sunspots [22].
• Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA): follows the same approach as
the AR but includes the correction of previous error between the model and previous
observations. It is important to note that the data can not have any seasonal pattern
(stationary).
• Seasonal Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (SARIMA): includes the pos-
sibility of adding seasonality patterns to the ARIMA models.
• Exponential Smoothing: follows a similar approach to ARIMA models with the added
condition that present values rely on more in recent data than older. This is done
by attributing weights to each observation following a systematic decreasing weight
system, having recent historic data a greater value compared to older data.
• Long Short Term Memory (LSTM): consists of a series of neural networks that have
feedback connections between each other such that it analyses the most important
patterns and forgets the lesser ones.
On this thesis, it will be focus mostly on ARIMA models as it has been proven useful for
achieving similar tasks to forecasting commodity prices, in this case electricity spot prices
[23].

4.2
Possible models
Once a proper input is provided a model can be started to be generated. There are
multiple models being proposed each one with their advantages and drawbacks.
• ARIMA: called autoregressive integrated moving average, is one of the most com-
mon methods for forecasting. ARIMA are widely used for trying to predict future
prices using as basis the historic data. Due to being autoregressive, it assumes
that the future is going to follow similar trends to the past, which makes it non-ideal
for very unstable situations.
Getting into detail with the ARIMA modelling, it can be divided in 3 main components
[29]:
– Autoregressive: the model relies on using dependent relationships between an
observation and lagged observations.
– Integrated: the time series needs to be stationary, meaning that no clear trend
can be observed over time. The series is differentiated in order to obtain a time
series that does not change with respect to time. This process may be done
several times until a stationary series is finally obtained. The main advantage
of using stationary series is that no trend nor seasonality is represented and a
stable mean and variance can be observed throughout different periods of the
time series.
– Moving average: takes into account the dependency from the observation to
the residual error of the moving average of the model with respect to previous
observations.
The ARIMA model is usually written as ARIMA(p, d, q), with each element meaning:
– p: number of lag observations being included in the model.
– d: number of times the time series has been differentiated for it to be stationary.
– q: size of the moving average window.

Using simple ARIMA models got discarded as the data being used is hourly and
the ARIMA model has been proven to obtain better results on lower frequency data,
specially yearly, quarterly or monthly. Apart from this, the computational time used
when training the data set it is considerably longer when compared to the other
methods.
• STL: called seasonal and trend decomposition using Loess. Has as main advantage
to ARIMA the acceptance of a larger set of seasonal data, not only quarterly or
monthly data. The main advantage of STL is that it can adapt to varying periods
of seasonality. On the case of the electricity price, it follows the same periodicity
constantly, so it is not an ideal model.
• ARIMA + Fourier Series: by combining the ARIMA with a Fourier series, we are able
to utilize as many seasonalities as needed in our time series while keeping the time
series stationary. On the case of electricity prices, a daily, and weekly seasonality
will be used, with possibilities of expanding them to quarterly or annually trends if
needed. The Fourier series, will consist of a series of sines and cosines functions
of k parameters, called harmonics, that imitate the values given by a time series.

RMSE algorithm
Finding the right method for calculating the RMSE is important in order to obtain a fair com-

price follows a more predictable pattern than others. Multiple ways of doing this compar-
ison were tried until an efficient algorithm that would calculate the RMSE in a reasonable
time was coded.
The final method that was selected at the end was to calculate the first forecast 168 hours
before the first hour of comparison, being the only valuable value of the forecast the last

4.6
Final model comparison regressor structure
The final model for deciding which regressors are the most suitable to improve the fore-
casts follows:
1. Input: the data that will be inputted into the forecasts will be the smoothed price
data, following the adaptive smoothing that was explained in section 4.1.1. The
information that will be used on the regressors will be raw, with no smoothing applied.
Additional correction such as possible blank spots due to data not being received is
also corrected. These corrections are done through splines.
2. Loop and indexing: a loop is initiated, making sure the forecasts are calculated for
the desired length of time. As time advances with the loop, the historic data being
used will be advancing as well, that is why a general index is kept in addition to the
hours that will be forecasted.
3. Forecast: in the case that regressors are being used, one must add the future pre-
dictions of these regressors previous to the final forecast of the price. As the data
being used is historic data and the values are known, the regressors use the historic
data as if they were forecasts.

Using RMSE and MAE
In order to establish a valid comparison between forecasts, the most commonly used
methods are the root-mean-square error (RMSE) and the mean absolute error (MAE).
Both of them come with advantages and disadvantages, and depending on which method
is used for comparison, the results of which forecast performs the best will vary.
5.1.1
RMSE
The root mean squared error is commonly used to for evaluating forecast results by mea-
suring how far do the predictions fall from the true value by applying Euclidean distance.
The equation of the RMSE was previously described in equation 4.3.
One of the main disadvantages of using the RMSE is that as the error are squared and
then the average done, having only a couple predictions way off when compared to the
rest of the series, will bring the RMSE values up considerably. Additionally, as the norm
is being used, it does not distinguish if the model is overestimating or underestimating
with respect to the real value, though on the case of this thesis, this does not pose an
impediment.
The RMSE will be the main method for evaluating which regressors to use.

5.4.3
Other countries analysis
The initial idea of this thesis was not only analyzing the Oslo region, but the whole coun-
try and extrapolate this model to other countries by doing modifications. This initial idea
changed rapidly once I realized the amount of work that it was just analyzing one zone.
Depending on the country, the regressors being used in the model will be changing. An
example of this is Denmark, Denmark would use as an important regressor the wind en-
ergy consumption, as it one of its main sources of energy, though as disadvantage of this,
is the unpredictability of the wind energy consumption when compared to hydro energy
[31] [32] [33]. This means that the price will be harder to forecast for these countries.
An additional problem is the use of exogenous variables such as the temperature. The
advantage of NO1 is that most electricity consumers are located in a narrow area around
Oslo, meaning that they are under the same weather conditions. This case can not be
extrapolated to electricity areas corresponding to large areas such as Germany or Spain.
An additional interesting feature would be forecasting the most stable countries and see
if the forecast of those countries could be used as possible regressors for less stable
markets. And example of these areas are the northern regions of Norway, which show
a more detached electricity price market, probably due to a lower consumption and an
excess of renewable energy [34].

6
Conclusion
The main point of this thesis was studying if by feeding to a forecasting model extra ex-
ogenous information it could increase the accuracy of the electricity price forecast of NO1.
It has been demonstrated that this hypothesis, in general, is true. The results will improve,
though one most do a previous study of which parameters influence the electricity price
for different regions. For identifying these parameters, it is extremely helpful the use of the
functions
from the package
which helps generate figures such as figure
2.3.
If the right parameters are introduced into the model, and a reliable forecast can be gen-
erated for these additional inputs, the model will benefit from having this extra data.
Another factor that must be taken into consideration is the state of the electricity market.
If the market is suffering from big price spikes (a high standard deviation), the model
will improve if only the most relevant parameter is being fed into the system. In this
type of situation it is also really beneficial to implement forgetting factors that give more
importance to the recent data.
If the electricity market is going through the opposite scenario, a very stable period, with
relatively low standard deviation and clearly defined patterns, the models show an in-
crease on the precision of the RMSE. The forgetting factor is not recommended in these
scenarios as the model benefits from all the available extra information.
In the case of NO1, the best overall results were achieved by transforming the electricity
price into a Fourier series, smoothing the peak prices and introducing information about
the hydro production. The results with this regressor improved the outcome by approx-
imately 13% when compared to the base case scenario of no regressors when using
ARIMA models from
package.
With respect to the possible forecast models to be used, ARIMA models showed good
results for periods of relatively calm (no peaks) with more defined patterns (the February
2022 period); while it is advised to use other methodologies for more erratic months such
as December 2021. For that month, better results were obtained by forecasting using
Fourier series than by ARIMA models in these scenarios.
As summary, electricity price forecasts in NO1 can benefit of using additional information,
but only from the right parameters. This difference is specially noticeable if long horizons
are being forecasted.
