Check-In Counter Allocation at CPH
Thesis – Mathematical Modelling and Computing
Jesper Jæger Henriksen
s103247
DTU - Technical University of Denmark
Abstract
This thesis suggests a tool to solve the problem of allocating check-in counters to airlines
in Copenhagen Airport, CPH. The tool has been developed in cooperation with supervising
Professor Jesper Larsen, head of management engineering division at DTU. The objective
is to fulﬁl the counter demand and preference of all airlines, while not exceeding queue size
and check-in baggage belt limitations. The suggested tool consists of two parts. First an
algorithm is run to produce the possible counter allocations for all airlines. These possible
allocations are then used as input to a mixed integer programming(MIP) model, which is
then solved. Four diﬀerent MIP models will be suggested and tested. These test have been
conducted on real world datasets provided by CPH to ensure real life application.
Two
models are suggested and both solved the provided datasets to optimality.

INTRODUCTION

Introduction
Air traﬃc has become a regular part of many people’s life. The reasons to board an airplane are
many, however common for them all is that they require you to check-in. Ensuring that check-in
is handled as smoothly as possible is essential to any airport. Even though self-check-in and
online check-in has been introduced, the demand for staﬀed check-in counters is still growing.
It is the job of the airport to make sure these counters are available to all airlines located at the
airport. Allocating these counters can be very diﬃcult, as it is a very complex problem to solve.
The topic of check-in counter allocation at airports is becoming increasingly relevant. An
increase in passengers at many of the busiest airports around the world raises the demand for
check-in counters. Recent numbers show an average passenger growth of 4.41% from 2013 to
2014 in all airports in the EU (Eurostat, 2015). In most cases, adding new counters is simply
not a possibility due to practical limitations such as space available. Instead, many airports
have turned to the development of check-in counter allocation tools, and Copenhagen Airport,
or CPH, is no diﬀerent.
CPH has seen a steady increase in passengers over the last several years. In 2014, CPH
sat a record by having the third year in a row with more then 24.1 million passengers (CPH,
2014). Until 2010, the check-in counter allocation was done manually at CPH. This lead to high
congestions in some areas of the airport, as the problem was too complex to solve optimally by
hand. Therefore, CPH issued a request for an allocation tool to handle their increasing counter
demands.
The purpose of the thesis is to develop a tool that can ﬁnd an optimal solution to the
check-in counter allocation issues at CPH. The tool should use real data provided by CPH as
input and yield a full and optimal solution containing both schedules and layout drawings of
the obtained solution. These layout drawings should contain all relevant information for all
airlines and check-in counters. In addition, the tool should be ﬂexible and eﬃcient enough to
solve problems as new situations arise. To ensure this, CPH has demanded a solution should be
produced in no less than 900 seconds equivalent to 15 minutes.
To CPH it is very important to ensure an optimal passenger and baggage ﬂow throughout
the airport. Therefore, limitations has been set on how much baggage certain baggage belts
can handle along with how many people may queue in certain areas of the airport. If these
capacity limitations are not met, an optimal ﬂow throughout the airport can not be ensured,
and it may result in delays. To monitor if these capacities are violated, graphs of the queue size
and check-in baggage inﬂux are requested for each area throughout the airport.
1.1
Previous work
The airport check-in counter allocation is a very well known problem that has been solved in
many diﬀerent ways. Most of the previous work has included a simulation of passenger arrival
data, which is used to determine the necessary check-in counters for each airline or individual
ﬂight. This stands in contrast to what is done at CPH. At CPH, the amount of counters needed
per airline is given as hard constraints before the model is solved.
A good example of using simulation to predict passenger arrivals and counter need is done
by van Dijk and van der Sluis (Dijk and Sluis, 2006). They propose a model that consists of
two steps. The ﬁrst step tries to predict the amount of counters needed based on a stochastic
simulation of the arrival time of passengers as well as the amount. The simulation uses data
from 96 ﬂights with between 50-300 passengers in 1-4 hour time spans. The simulated counter
needs are converted to ’blocks’. These blocks are two-dimensional matrices dictating how many
counters are needed in each time interval for a single ﬂight. When this is done, a deterministic
model is run to optimize the created blocks. These blocks are then used as input for step 2,

The approach of van Dijk and van der Sluis is somewhat similar to the approach that we
will attempt, however some key diﬀerence exists. First of all, they consider individual ﬂights not
entire airlines. From a modelling perspective, this is not signiﬁcant. The main diﬀerence is that
we do not attempt to minimize the amount of counters. In most cases, our model will try to
use as many counters as possible. At CPH, the amount of counters used is of little importance.
The main issues are to allocate the airlines in a way that satisfy their demands while keeping an
optimal ﬂow throughout the airport. To do this, the model will most likely spread the allocation
as much as possible, thus not making the solution compact.
Another key diﬀerence is that we know the arrival rate and amount of counters needed per
airline. Because of this we do not simulate the counter demand. We do, however, convert the
data from CPH into similar ’blocks’ by intelligently creating what we will call patterns for each
airline.
In 1995, Bryan Wang (Wang, 1995) introduced a diﬀerent approach to the airport check-in
allocation problem: A genetic algorithm using Darwinian models. The algorithm was developed
to solve the situation at a non-disclosed international airport, however, it is stated that the
check-in counters are centrally located. His algorithm consists of three rather simple steps. Step
1: Create initial allocation plans by randomly assigning ﬂights to counters. Step 2: Iteratively
measure the ﬁtness of each allocation plan and randomly merge the best. Step 3: Pick the best
allocation plan when the algorithm terminates. Wang looks a three diﬀerent versions where only
one is suitable. The models allow for ﬂight overlaps on counters but seeks to minimize this. It
is noteworthy that the models treat each ﬂight separately, thus not grouping airlines together
when assigning counters. This means that some overlap can happen within the same airline
which is considered okay.

Hon Wai Chun (Chun, 1996) suggested an algorithm he called the MDA-2 to solve the
check-in counter allocation in the Hong Kong Airport. Chun uses real data provided by the
Hong Kong airport. This means that his models included data from 410 ﬂights during a 24 hour
period divided into 15 min intervals. These 410 ﬂights can be allocated to 300 diﬀerent counters.
The MDA-2 algorithm creates 3-dimensional counter proﬁles that take time, counters and queue
length into consideration. The general ﬂow of the MDA-2 algorithm is roughly: Eliminate all
illegal counter proﬁles as dictated by a set of constraints - Select a legal counter - Place counter
proﬁle in a Gantt chart - Check constraints - Place if legal, if it is not, change the proﬁle slightly
and try again. If the model cannot place a legal counter proﬁle it is discarded and a new one is
selected. The counter proﬁles are simulated based on knowledge from previous ﬂights and the
current ﬂight schedule.
In 1999, Chun followed his MDA-2 model with a knowledge based heuristic he calls IRSS
(Chun, 1999).
The aim of the IRSS heuristic model is to predict the minimum number of
counters needed to satisfy a number of demands. As for his previous model, arrival rates and
check-in times are predicted based on previous experiences. The approach of the heuristic is
to ﬁrst simulate proﬁles for all ﬂights. When these proﬁles have been found for all ﬂights, all
check-in groups are merged. A check-in group is a collection of ﬂights run by the same airline.
The last step is to run a simulation for the entire airport based on the previous simulation results.
A more recent heuristic approach was suggested by Shangyao Yan in 2004 (S. Yan et al.,
2004). Yan considers the Taiwan international airport CKS. His model considers the allocation of
75 ﬂights to 20 counters on a weekly basis. The model was designed to assign check-in counters
while minimizing the total passenger walking distance and what Yan calls the AIV (Actual
Inconsistency Value). The AIV is a measure of the consistency of ﬂight location over time,
i.e. ﬂights with the same ﬂight number should be placed at the same counters. The suggested
heuristic method consists of 3 binary IP models solved by the simplex method. Originally Yan
created one big model, but due to the size and the solve time, this model was not a feasible
solution. Therefore, he split the model into three models.
The ﬁrst model solves the assignment problem, i.e. makes sure that all ﬂights are accounted
for and no counter is double booked while minimizing the total walking distance for passengers.
Between the ﬁrst and second model the AIV or HIV (Heuristic Inconsistency Value) is calculated
for the solution of the ﬁrst model.
The HIV is then used in the objective function of the second model. This model tries to
minimize the total sum of HIV throughout the entire planning week. The minimum HIV for
each ﬂight and block combination is saved and used as input to the third model.
The last model is a merger of the two previous models. The entire ﬁrst model is reused and
the objective function of the second model is used as a constraint. The right-hand side of this
constraint is the minimal HIV found in the second model multiplied by some factor. This factor
can be regulated as seen ﬁt by the airport

Problem Description
We seek to develop a tool that is to be able to deliver an optimal check-in counter allocation that
allocates airlines according to their wishes, while evenly distributing queue sizes and securing
optimal baggage ﬂow.
To make this optimal allocation, the tool needs to uphold a set of
requirements given in Section 2.3.1 while optimizing the solution according to the ”Nice to
have” conditions given in Section 2.3.2. In contrast to the approaches mentioned in Section 1.1
the tool does not have to simulate or predict the necessary counters or the arrival rate of the
passengers, since this has been provided by CPH.
2.1
Terminal Layout
The check-in area at CPH is divided into two terminals. As we can see in Figure 1 each terminal
is divided into check-in clusters, queue areas and baggage areas. Each check-in cluster aligns
with a queue area, while baggage areas usually encompasses multiple check-in clusters. A check-
in cluster consists of multiple check-in counters, the range of which is deﬁned on each cluster in
Figure 1.

Terminal 3 is almost exclusive used by SAS, however some other Star Alliance members may

is the most congested terminal. According to COO of Copenhagen Airports A/S Henrik Peter

or metro. In addition to these departing passengers Terminal 3 also has to handle all arriving
passengers with CPH as ﬁnal destination.
Due to the limited amount of airlines in Terminal 3 and the high rate of passengers arriving
by metro and train, there is a lot of traﬃc through Terminal 3 towards Terminal 2. Queue area
4 in particular is a critical point since this acts as bottle neck for the walkway to Terminal 2.
Often times departing passengers arriving at Terminal 3 have to squeeze between welcoming
relatives and queueing passengers at Q4 to get to their designated check-in counter in Terminal
2.
The critical point in Terminal 2 is not surprisingly Q5, however, since the traﬃc in the
walkway primarily ﬂows one way, the congestion is not a major issue.

2.3
Check-In Counter Allocation Patterns
To visualize the counter proﬁles in Table 1 we can convert them to bar charts. An example of
this is shown for the airline KLM in Figure 2. The check-in counter proﬁle bar chart shows the
counter demand as a function of time. This visual representation of a check-in counter proﬁle
could be constructed several diﬀerent ways. Each version would represent a diﬀerent counter
allocations of the same proﬁle. We will refer to diﬀerent allocations of the same counter proﬁle
as patterns. These patterns are created for each proﬁle and is a 1:1 reﬂection of the proﬁle
counter demand for each time period.
Figure 2: KLM check-in counter proﬁle example. The bar chart shows the check-in counter demand of
KLM as a function of time.
When these patterns are created we ensure that all check-in counters are closed in the reverse
order they were opened in. This means that the ﬁrst counter to open is the last to close. In
addition, whenever an additional counter is opened it has to be a counter that has already been
utilized if possible. This is done to ensure consistency. Figure 3 shows some of the unfortunate
patterns that can be created if this is not ensured.

The patterns in Figure 3 are highly irregular, which will make then unnecessarily hard
to place together. Especially the middle pattern is undesirable, as it occupies more check-in
counters than necessary. We would like the constrain our patterns to only use as many diﬀerent
counters as needed to meet the airline demands. CPH has not speciﬁed that the pattern types
illustrated in Figure 3 are invalid. However, because the patterns are clearly not desirable, we
have decided to make these types of patterns illegal.
With these restrictions, only four diﬀerent pattern types can be create. The ﬁrst type is
created by always adding additional counters to the left of the ﬁrst opened counter. This type
is what we call Left in Figure 4. We recognise this pattern as the counter proﬁle in Figure 2.
The second pattern type is created by ﬂipping the Left pattern and is called Right. The third
type is what we call Center. The Center pattern is created by adding additional counters to
alternating sides of the initial counter. Even though the ﬂipped version is technically legal, we
do not include it as a legal pattern in the tool. The reason for this is that each pattern is very
space consuming and the ﬂipped Center pattern is too similar to the original to add any value
to the model.
Figure 4: All legal pattern types: Left, right and center. The ﬁgures show the amount of counters needed
at speciﬁc time periods.
Some airlines demand counters in multiple none-adjacent groups of time periods. For exam-
ple, counters could be demanded between 06.00-08.40 and then again between 14.00-18.05. An
idea could be to use diﬀerent pattern types in each group of time periods. This is not possible
however, because only previously opened counters are opened when possible as mentioned ear-
lier. Thus we only include three pattern types in the tool.
In some cases, the counter demand is constant in all time periods where counters are de-
manded. An example is the demand of Air Berlin in Table 1 which is either 0 or 2. In this
case the Left, Right and Center patterns would all be identical. Figure 5 makes this completely
clear. Throughout this thesis, these types of patterns will be referred to as uniform patterns. If

2.3.1
Requirements
The requirements can be divided into two subcategories: Layout and airline. The layout require-
ments relate to the physical appearance of the airport terminals. The ﬁrst layout requirement
is that all proﬁles must be placed within a single check-in cluster. Check-in cluster are sets
of check-in counters often divided by physical obstacles like staircases or because they are in
separate terminals. The second layout requirement says, that counters allocated to the same
airline must be adjacent at all time.
The airline requirement dictates that all requested check-in counters must be allocated. To
make this requirement feasible, CPH must ensure that no more than 90 counters are promised
in any time period.
Further, it is important, that CPH takes the cluster requirement into
account when granting the counter demands. If CPH is not able to ensure this, then the airline
requirement will not be feasible.
2.3.2
’Nice to have’ Conditions
The ’nice to have’ conditions can also be divided into layout and airline categories. The layout
conditions concern the queue sizes and check-in baggage inﬂux. As explained in Section 2.1,
the terminals at CPH are subdivided into queue and baggage areas. These areas have been
given a maximum capacity that must not be exceeded. To ensure an optimal ﬂow in both the
terminals and on the baggage handling belt, the tool must seek to minimize any violation of
these capacities.
In addition to the amount of counters required by each airline, a set of preferred counters
is given to CPH. All airlines should be allocated to these counters if possible. If an airline is
not allocated to a preferred set of check-in counters, it is said to be misplaced. The reasoning
behind the selection of these check-in counters are usually historical or otherwise personal to
each airline and has little to do with the day-to-day quality of operations. Therefore this will
be viewed as the least important condition, which will be reﬂected in the objective function.

Solution Proposals
3.1
Solution Description
The proposed solution will be a tool consisting of two parts.
The ﬁrst part is the instance
generation method according to the speciﬁcations explained in Section 2.3. The instance gener-
ator is implemented in Java. By following the speciﬁcations in Section 2.3, we ensure that the
second layout requirement is always met. By design, the instance generator also insures that all
counters in any instance are placed within a single check-in cluster.
The second part is an allocation model that places the generated patterns within the CPH
layout.
The allocation model is formed as an MIP model and is solved using GAMS. The
allocation model ensures that all requested counters are allocated, if possible. The objective of
the MIP model is to minimize the violation of queue and baggage area capacities while placing
as many airlines as possible on their preferred check-in counters.
Each part is run separately. First, the instance generation method will be executed using the
scenario data as input. The output will only be scenario speciﬁc parameters including passenger
arrival rates, baggage inﬂux and naturally the instances for all airlines. These instances and
parameters will then be used as input to the allocation model. By doing this, the allocation model
will be independent of the scenarios making it very versatile and applicable to any scenario.
With the input provided by the instance generator, the allocation model is able to ﬁnd
an optimal solution to any scenario. Each solution will include a check-in counter allocation
schedule, visual layout schemes for the entire airport and each individual check-in counter cluster.
In addition, graphs and statistics will be provided of queue sizes and baggage inﬂux in each area.
From these graphs it will be easy to verify if a violation has occurred.
3.2
Instance Generator Method
The purpose of the instanceGenerator algorithm given as Algorithm 1 is to create the input
for the allocation model. The algorithm utilizes the preferred counters deﬁned by the airlines
at CPH to create possible instances for all airlines. The amount of instances created is deﬁned
by the totalInstances parameter. If an airline has enough preferred counter combinations to
create totalInstances instances, only preferred instances will be created. A preferred instance
is an instance where only preferred counters are included. If this is not the case, Algorithm 1
adds additional counters to the allowedCounters set. The allowedCounters set deﬁnes which
counters an airline can be allocated to. The amount of counters that can be added is controlled by
the availableInstances parameter, which is initialized as the diﬀerence between totalInstances
and the amount preferred instances. availableInstances deﬁnes how many instances that can
be created before the totalInstances limit is reached.

by only including the most promising instances.
3.3
Allocation Models
In an eﬀort to ﬁnd the best possible approach, four diﬀerent allocation models have been created.
All four models uses the same core model. The diﬀerence between the allocation models is their
instance inclusion method. From the previous work described in Section 1.1, it seems that the
biggest obstacle when using a deterministic method is the size of the solution space. Therefore,
we will try to limit the solution space to ensure a faster runtime while still obtaining a good
solution. To compare the results obtained with the solution space reducing methods, we will
also consider some more pure deterministic approaches.
3.3.1
Objective Function
The objective of the model is to ﬁnd the best possible allocation of the check-in counters. As
we have seen, CPH has stated some requirements that should be fulﬁlled for a solution to be
valid. As explained in Section 3.1, the two layout requirements have already been met during
the instance generation. The generated instances will be used as input. The three ’Nice to
have’ conditions provided by CPH will be used to deﬁne the goodness a solution. Throughout
this thesis z will be referred to as the objective function value or solution value which is the
numerical value used to measure the goodness of a solution. The objective function have been
constructed so that the best possible solution will have a solution value of 0. All solutions with
z = 0 are considered as equally good.
The ﬁrst part of the objective function does not address a condition but a requirement.
The airline requirement states that a valid solution must allocate all demanded counters for all
airlines. However, the data provided could cause this to be infeasible. In diﬃcult scenarios the
allocation model may not be able to obtain a solution that satisﬁes this constraint before the
time limit is reached. We want to make sure that a solution is always returned in all cases.
Therefore we include this requirement in the objective function as the variable θa,t. θa,t takes

3.3.3
Breadth First Instance Inclusion Model
Due to the size of the instances, including too many will cause the model to either run out of
memory or exceed the time limit. To avoid this, an iterative approach to the inclusion of the
instances is introduced. The idea is that the Core model is run several times with a smaller
subset of the allowed instances. Between each Core iteration additional instances will be added.
The instances are added solely based on their placement in the instanceOutput set. This mean
that the instances in the instanceOutput set are added chronologically. The instance to be
added is the instance with the lowest index number that has not already been added.
This model is meant to be very simple and ﬁnd an acceptable solution in a short runtime.
This is why the approach to the instance inclusion functionality is very simple. The proposed
model should be seen as a ﬁrst step to test the instance inclusion concept.
To accommodate the instance inclusion functionality, we need to include Equation 30 in
Model 1. We will call this model Model 1*. The ﬁrst iteration of Model 1* only includes all

Modiﬁed Dataset Experiments
CPH has seen a continuous increase in passengers the recent years (CPH, 2014). Therefore,
it would be very interesting to examine, how the models will perform with increased pressure
on both baggage and queue areas. To simulate this increase in a simple way, the maximum
capacities at each queue and baggage area are decrease instead of increasing the inﬂux for each
airline. This will be true throughout the experiments of this type for both models.
We will concentrate on one dataset at a time when we do our modiﬁed experiments. The
approach to these experiments will be simple: First, we will decrease the queue area capacities
only, then we will decrease the baggage area capacities only and lastly we will decrease both
at the same time. Decreasing both the queue area and baggage area capacities simultaneously
seems to be the most realistic scenario, however we would like to decrease them separately ﬁrst
to see how the models reacts.
We would like to ﬁnd two decrease factors for each dataset. The ﬁrst should be the smallest
decrease that causes a violation in any queue or baggage area. The second should be selected
to put maximum pressure on the models without rendering them without any possibility of
mitigation. This will happen if the area capacities are lowered to such a degree that violations
are unavoidable.
In the following experiments datasets 2010-06-26, 2010-09-16 and 2011-02-11 will not be
considered. Datasets 2010-06-26 and 2010-09-16 are omitted from these experiments because

Final Observations
The ﬁrst and most important observation is: Given the set-up of the models, four of the orig-
inal datasets are solved with a solution value of 0 by both models. The remaining dataset is
impossible to solve without having some queue violations, as was shown in Section 5.1.3, which
means that even though a solution value of 0 was not obtained, the dataset was still solved to
optimality. Thus, both models have succeeded in providing the best possible solutions for all
original datasets.
When we modify the datasets to make them harder to solve, we generally see two types of
violations: Large violations caused by a single airline and smaller violations caused by multiple
airlines.
The ﬁrst violation type is impossible to mitigate, as we have already discussed at
41
==PAGE:45==
9
CONCLUSION
lengths. The second is more complex and was ﬁrst believed to occur, if the misplacement penalty
was too high for the models to mitigate eﬀectively. However, after running experiments without
misplacement penalties, we saw many solutions remained identical. This again could mean two
things. Either we found the best solution and moving any of the airlines anywhere else would
cause a higher violation, or we did not create enough instances. The ﬁrst case is possible, however
we have seen many solutions with vacant counters in terminal 3 which make this explanation
unlikely. Instead, it strongly suggests that we did not create enough instances to make such
a misplacement possible. Arguing against this is the fact that we often see high baggage and
queue violations in terminal 3, which would only be worsened by allocating additional airlines.
Examining the runtime results of all experiments, it does not seem that adding additional
instances will help the models’ overall performance, especially if we want to consider a model
without misplacement penalties. In the case without these penalties, we see very long runtimes
with very few improved results. Most experiments yielded either the same solutions as found
with misplacement penalties or failed to terminate before the 900 second limit was reached.
Only three experiments showed improved solutions.
A general observation from all experiments is that the biggest challenge for CPH going for-
ward is the queue sizes. Throughout all experiments, we generally see higher violations in the
queue sizes than we did for the baggage inﬂux, in particular when they were increased simul-
taneously. The most daunting task seems to be with single airlines that cause high violations
within their own queue area.
The upside of Model 3 is that it has the possibility of checking all known solutions within
the solution space created by the instances. However, whenever you have a deterministic model,
which this can be viewed as, memory and runtime are often issues. Therefore, it is not surprising
that the biggest weakness of Model 3 is memory issues. What is a little surprising, is that the
runtimes are often relatively good, if the model does not run out of memory.
Model 4 was designed to have better runtime but slightly worse solution values than Model
3. However, what we see are very mixed performances. Model 4 has generally slower runtimes
than Model 3 when considering dataset 2010-05-07, but faster runtimes for dataset 2011-01-13.
The reason for this could be the value of the stopping criteria parameter. If the experiment
does not have a solution value less than the stopping criteria, the runtime is guaranteed to be
900 seconds. After running all experiments, a more suitable value could be 100. In harder cases,
where Model 3 runs out of memory, Model 4 clearly outperforms Model 3 by always obtaining
a solution.
Another way to improve the performance of Model 4 would be to convert it to a true heuristic,
making it exclusively in Java. The compile time between each run in GAMS is very high as it
takes about two minutes between each iteration. This make GAMS very unsuitable for this kind
of heuristic. It would be very interesting in future work to convert Model 4 into a true heuristic
and compare its performance to the deterministic Model 3.
9
Conclusion
In this thesis, we have tried to solve the check-in counter allocation problem at CPH. The
problems at CPH are caused by an increase in passengers. This increase leads to larger queues
and an increased pressure on the baggage belts. To mitigate this pressure, CPH would like a
tool to allocate their check-in counters in an optimal way while still granting the requests of the
airlines. To aid the creation of such a tool, CPH has created and provided all necessary input
data.
To solve this problem, a tool consisting of a heuristic method and a MIP model is suggested.
The heuristic method converts the data provided by CPH into input data for the MIP model.

The MIP model then generates an optimal solution to the check-in counter allocation problem.
To diversify the suggested tool, two diﬀerent MIP models are suggested.
Layout drawings,
schedules, queue size and baggage inﬂux graphs are generated as output.
We have shown that our tool successfully optimizes the check-out counter allocation prob-
lem using real scenarios provided by CPH. The tool developed consist of a instance generator
algorithm and a MIP allocation model. We have suggested two diﬀerent MIP allocation models.
Both models succeeded in solving all ﬁve scenarios in the allowed 900 seconds. Model 3 solved
all ﬁve scenarios to optimality given the setup of the tool. Model 4 found optimal solutions too
all scenario except one.
To emulate an increase in passengers and baggage the queue and baggage areas capacities
were lowered. Two datasets were used to conduct additional experiments using the decreased
capacities. These experiments revealed the diﬀerences in the characteristics of the two models.
By design, Model 3 will always either yield the optimal solution or none at all. The increased
diﬃculty of the modiﬁed scenarios caused the model to run out of memory, before it could
be completed on three occasions. In addition, one experiment could not be solved within the
allowed time limit.
The instance inclusion functionality of Model 4 allows it to ﬁnd a solution in all experiments,
except one. However, because the instances are added iteratively, an optimal solution cannot
be guaranteed. The solutions found are all either worse or equal to the solution found by Model
3 in experiments where Model 3 does not run out of memory. Model 4 is extremely sensitive to
the parameter tuning of the stopping criteria parameter. If the model is to succeed in ﬁnding a
good solution quickly, this parameter must be set correctly. Our experiments suggest that this
is could be problem speciﬁc. Our experiments show that a value of 10 is very suitable for most
datasets, while for others a higher value is required to obtain a good solution in a short runtime.
In an attempt to encourage the misplacement of airlines to mitigate the queue and baggage
area violations, the misplacement penalties were removed from the objective function of both
models. This did not provide the desired result. Instead, it caused an increase in runtime due
to a presumed worsening of the branch-and-bound functionality in GAMS. In a few cases the
removal of the misplacement punishment did yield solutions with lower queue and baggage area
violation, however the amount of airlines misplaced were extreme and did not merit the decreased
violations. The most interesting insight gained by these experiment was that some solution
were identical to the previously obtained solutions that did include misplacement penalties.
This showed that an optimal solution with respect to the queue and baggage area violations
is obtainable with misplacement penalties included. These two types of results shows that the
objective function has been constructed perfectly to obtain the desired solutions.
In conclusion, both Model 3 and Model 4 solved the original datasets to optimality. Model
3 ensures optimality if a solution is found, while Model 4 ensures a solution.
It is hard to
proclaim one model as better than the other. However, as long as the datasets resemble the
ones provided by CPH, Model 3 should be used since it does solve all scenarios within the time
limit and ensures optimality. If the complexity of the scenarios increase, Model 4 should be
utilized. A solution containing both of them could be suggested. The approach would be to use
Model 3 for pre-planning, if time is not an issue, and then to use Model 4 if unforeseen situations
arise that demands a quick solution.
9.1
Future Work
Upon completion of the models presented in this thesis, some additional interesting development
remains. The obvious question that remains is whether the amount of instances that can be
created is satisfying. We have shown that the datasets provided by CPH can be solved with
the current number of instances. However, if the amount of airlines, counters or passengers all

increase, further patterns may be necessary. As we have explained, the reason only 57 patterns
are created is due to a space issue. To work around this issue, we either need to utilize improved
hardware or rethink the way instances are represented.
One way to change the representation of the instances is to adopt the method of van Dijk
and van der Sluis (Dijk and Sluis, 2006). They only create patterns that are not counter speciﬁc.
This would lead to a great reduction in amount of necessary space, as we could simply use the
patterns instead of instances. The challenge would be to include the layout constraints, that are
handled in the instanceGeneration algorithm, in the allocation models.
The danger of creating patterns this way, is that it could lead to increased runtimes for the
allocation model and a possibility of running out of memory. Even though the space consumption
of the pattern input would be decreased, the amount of constraints would increase. The reason
for this is that each pattern type would have the possibility of being placed on any range of
counters.
Another way to increase the amount of generated patterns would be to convert Model 4 to
a true heuristic exclusively in Java. By doing this, patterns could be generated and discarded
between each iteration based on the solution obtained. Another approach would be to program
Model 4 in Java and connect it to a MIP solver. By doing this, the instance inclusion could be
replaced by an actual column generation.
In some of the solutions we have found, the counters have been allocated in such a way that
the airlines do not have any time periods to handover the counters. In some cases the check-in
counters are allocated to two diﬀerent airlines in two adjacent time periods. In reality, such
a handover might not be possible without losing some minutes. Further, if an airline needs
additional time periods, due to some unexpected event, this would be very hard to handle.
Therefore, an interesting expansion would be to add a cost to the objective function, if a certain
amount of time periods does not pass between two airlines using the same counter.
Another interesting addition to model would be to add a cost to the objective function, if
two airlines are placed directly next to each other. In some cases, airlines may need additional
counters due to unforeseen events. Opening additional counters is obviously only possible, if
adjacent counters are unallocated in the given time period. Increasing the amount of unallocated
counters next to each airline would make it possible to expand the counter demand in certain
time periods. Allocating additional check-in counters could also be seen as a way to mitigate
queue and baggage violations.
