Abstract
This Thesis presents the design process of the analogue part of an X
band Frequency Modulated Continuous Wave(FMCW) radar to introduce
high school students into the fundamentals of the radar technology. A de-
tailed presentation of the radar types used today is made and the choice of
the FMCW technology over the rest is elaborated. Then the formulation
of speciﬁcations is explained and based on that, the choice of components
that will be used in the design of the transmitter and receiver is justiﬁed.
Moreover, all the ﬁlters used in the system are presented and their design
process is explianed thoroughly The ﬁnal design is tested and the results are
evaluated, while future steps to complete the radar design are deﬁned.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The aim of this thesis is the design and construction of a Continuous Wave
(CW) radar in the X band for demonstration purposes to high school stu-
dents and introduce them to the theory of radar systems.
The X band refers to frequencies between 8 and 12 GHz as is shown in
table 1.1. The X band was chosen as the operating frequency band on as it
will lead to low power consumption and small system size making it portable
, which will be really useful as it will make its transport easier, especially
in the case of the antenna.
Moreover, it is possible to ﬁnd components
on the market for free as samples or in reasonable prices, that cover the
required range of frequencies. Going to higher bands will lead to even smaller
component sizes and less power consumption, however components in these
frequencies are diﬃcult to ﬁnd and expensive.

2.2
The transmission line
A transmission line is a structure consisting of two conductors separated
by a dielectric material acting as an isolator, designed to conduct alter-
nating current. The signiﬁcant characteristic of a transmission line is that
the voltage and the current varies in phase and amplitude along its length
while in lumped components the voltage and current are constant along any
dimension of the element.

3.1
History of Radar
Radar is a deduction system that uses radio waves to determine the
range,cangle and/or velocity of objects [5] . The transmitting waves travel
in space and when reﬂected on an object returns to the its receiver. From
these echoes’ phase, frequency and moment of arrival, the range and the
speed of the target can be determined.
Since the German physicist Heinrich Herz proved the existence of electro-
magnetic waves the world changed dramatically due to the technologies that
emerged, most importantly in the ﬁeld of telecommunications that made dis-
tances meaningless for transfer of information. On 21 November 1903 , the
German physicist Christian H¨ulsmeyer made a patent application for an ap-
paratus of his invention called Telemobiloscope. The Telemobiloscope, that
was supposed to be installed on a ship, was sending electromagnetic pulses
from an array of Hertzian dipoles. If some of those pulses were reﬂected
by another metallic object ,i.e. another ship, they were received by its re-
ﬂective parabolic antenna, a relay was activated and a bell started to ring
alerting the crew of the ship for a possible collision. The patent was accepted
in March of 1904 and was demonstrated in Germany and the Netherlands
later that year. The Telemobiloscope despite being able to detect only the
presence of an metallic object and not providing any information about its
range and velocity, is considered the ﬁrst radar system and opened the way
for the development of the modern radar [6].

8.2
PCB Design considerations
PCB design might be seem an easy task, however for high frequencies
there are many thinks that should be taken into consideration as high fre-
quency signals are more prone to reﬂections and sensitive to noise. The most
important are [20]:
• There should always be groundplane below the RF path.
• Traces with diﬀerent widths, hence diﬀerent characteristic impedances,
should not be connected directly to each other as this impedance dis-
continuity will cause reﬂections.
• traces should not be very long in order to avoid the possibility for
them to act as an antenna.
• Trace corners should not be used in order to avoid reﬂections. If they
have to be used , then ﬁg.8.1 should be consulted:

• DC ports should be as far as possible from RF ports.
• The ground plane below the RF path should be consistent and not

• There should be a minimum distance between two signal traces to

• In contrast with low frequency design, in higher frequencies all the
substrate regions not used by the RF signal should be ﬁlled with copper
to be used either as power or groundplane.

9.1
Conclusion
This thesis contained a detailed presentation of the radar and RF theory
. The most important radar technologies were outlined each with its pros
and cons. Then, the design process of all the ﬁlters used in the design as
well as the the criteria for the choice of the ready components was analysed.
Finally, a proposed PCB design was fabricated and measured. However,
due to certain ﬂaws to the design it was not possible to acquire any useful
measurements. The following sections suggest ways to improve the current
design and complete the radar system.
9.2
PCB Design
A new PCB design approach should be considered. one of them is depicted
in ﬁgures 8.11 and 8.12 where the proposed layout for each component is
used. Some other issues were ﬁxed, such as the addition of DC block capac-
itors in both outputs of the LNA. Moreover, the radar could be redesigned
without using the IF frequency components. The beat frequency can be
obtained by mixing the transmitted signal directly with the received one.
Doing so, leads to a simpler PCB as only one mixer and VCO is used.
9.3
Post Processing
The ﬁrst thing that has to be done is the generation of the modulation
signal. Fig4.10 shows diﬀerent modulation signals. The one that is mostly
used is the ramp signal as there is linear relation between the voltage and
104
==PAGE:127==

frequency. This signal can be easily generated with the aid of a microcon-
troller. The ramp signal can be generated and sampled using software, eg
Matlab. Then the samples should be stored in the microcontroller’s mem-
ory and then used as an output from one of its analogue pins. It is crucial
to use a low pass ﬁlter at the analogue output , as the output will be a
stepped ramp due to quantization. The low pass ﬁlter will smooth the out-
put and will make it more linear. Moreover the choice of the modulation
signal frequency is important as it aﬀects the range detection (see eq.4.8) ,
a modulation frequency of 25-30 Hz is a usual choice.
Converting the analogue output of the radar receiver to digital is en-
abling the digital processing of it, making it possible to extract valuable
information of it and employ various digital signal processing algorithms in
order to minimize noise and improve its SNR. The most important thing
that should be done in the post processing is the extraction of the speed
and distance of the target from the beat frequency. In order to achieve that,
the microprocessor responsible for generating the VCOs tune signal should
also generate a signal,called synchronization signal informing whether there
is an upramp or a downramp in the tune voltage. A Fast Fourier Transform
(FFT) algorithm should be implemented to estimate the beat frequency for
each case. Then by taking the mean value of the estimated frequencies to
extract the actual beat frequency , the range can be computed using eq.4.8.
Then eq.4.7 should be used to extract the Doppler frequency and thus the
targets radial velocity.
As it was mentioned in the introduction, the ﬂuctuations of the RCS
of the targets are causing issues as it would require additional output SNR
in order to achieve the highest possible detection probability.
Therefore
the method of pulse integration should be implemented.
The algorithm
should save and average n returned pulses in order to remove as much noise
as possible and therefore improve the SNR of the output. By consulting
ﬁgures 3.3 and 3.4 and assuming that the target follows the Swerling model
case 1, which is the worst possible case, it would need the integration of 200
pulses to achieve the necessary improvement to the SNR.
Moreover, there should be some care about clutter. Clutter is stationary
objects ,eg trees and buildings, that should be ignored by the radar. This is
an easy task in the case of a CW radar as they will produce a Doppler shift
close to DC and can be ﬁltered out using a simple high pass ﬁlter. In the
case of the FMCW radar, the Doppler shift is hidden in the beat frequency.
Therefore, it should be extracted as before and then if it is below a threshold
, the target is regarded as clutter and should be ignored.

9.4
Tracking
In order to show the full capabilities of the radar, the tracking feature shall
be added to the system. Given that can be used more than one antenna
for receiving and transmitting and by taking into consideration the system
complexity , the tracking scheme that is suggested to be implemented is the
monopulse tracking. An array of for antennas should be fabricated , 1 for
transmitting and 3 for receiving . This way tracking in both elevation and
azimuth axis can be achieved.
An upcoming technology that is going to be used in 5G networks can
be employed for tracking as well. Beam steering is a form of beam forming
that can change the direction of the main lobe of the antenna pattern so
that the latter always points to the target. Multiple antennas transmitting
at diﬀerent phases , thus the beams generated interfere constructively or
destructively forming the radiation pattern. By changing the phase of each
antenna element , the main lobe of the radiation pattern can be moved to
point the target at any time. By doing this the position of the antennas is
ﬁxed and the use of a servo motor and its limitations can be avoided.
9.5
Predistortion
The biggest disadvantage of the VCOs is the that for a point and onward
cease to have a linear behaviour. Therefore, despite the sweep of the tuning
voltage is linear , the change of the frequency of oscillation is not linear. The
method of predistortion distorts the tune signal in a way that the output
will be linear in the frequency range of diﬀerence.
Predistortion can be
implemented in an analogue or digital[13] manner and should be considered
in the future to improve the linearity of the transmitter VCO.

Product Overview
The PMA2-123LN5+ is a E-PHEMT based wideband, low noise MMIC amplifier with a unique 
combination of low noise, high IP3, and low current making it ideal for sensitive, high-dynamic-range

a tiny, low profile package (2 x 2 mm, 8 lead MCLP), accommodating dense circuit board layouts.

Product Overview
KAT models are a series of absorptive fixed attenuators fabricated using highly reliable and repeatable 
GaAs MMIC IPD* process. The models operate from DC up to 43.5 GHz. They have outstanding attenua-
tion accuracy and flatness while maintaining excellent VSWR throughout the entire band. The models can 
also handle input power up to around 2W, depending on each value, which makes this model series an 
ideal choice for a wide range of applications.

Supports a wide array of applications including 5G, wireless infrastructure, microwave 
communications, satellite, defense and aerospace, medical broadband and optic ap-
plications.
Small Size and simple to use
(2 mm x 2 mm)

RO4000® Series 
High Frequency Circuit Materials
RO4000® hydrocarbon ceramic laminates are designed to offer superior high frequency 
performance and low cost circuit fabrication. The result is a low loss material which 
can be fabricated using standard epoxy/glass (FR-4) processes offered at competitive 
prices.
The selection of laminates typically available to designers is signiﬁ cantly reduced once 
operational frequencies increase to 500 MHz and above. RO4000 material possesses 
the properties needed by designers of RF microwave circuits and matching networks 
and controlled impedance transmission lines. Low dielectric loss allows RO4000 series 
material to be used in many applications where higher operating frequencies limit the 
use of conventional circuit board laminates. The temperature coefﬁ cient of dielectric 
constant is among the lowest of any circuit board material (Chart 1), and the dielectric 
constant is stable over a broad frequency range (Chart 2). For reduced insertion 
loss, LoPro® foil is available (Chart 3). This makes it an ideal substrate for broadband 
applications.
RO4000 material’s thermal coefﬁ cient of expansion (CTE) provides several key beneﬁ ts 
to the circuit designer. The expansion coefﬁ cient of RO4000 material is similar to 
that of copper which allows the material to exhibit excellent dimensional stability, a 
property needed for mixed dielectric multi-layer boards constructions. The low Z-axis 
CTE of RO4000 laminates provides reliable plated through-hole quality, even in severe 
thermal shock applications. RO4000 series material has a Tg of >280°C (536°F) so 
its expansion characteristics remain stable over the entire range of circuit processing 
temperatures.
RO4000 series laminates can easily be fabricated into printed circuit boards 
using standard FR-4 circuit board processing techniques. Unlike PTFE based high 
performance materials, RO4000 series laminates do not require specialized via 
preparation processes such as sodium etch. This material is a rigid, thermoset laminate 
that is capable of being processed by automated handling systems and scrubbing 
equipment used for copper surface preparation.
RO4003C™ laminates are currently offered in various conﬁ gurations utilizing
both 1080 and 1674 glass fabric styles, with all conﬁ gurations meeting the
same laminate electrical performance speciﬁ cation. Speciﬁ cally designed
as a drop-in replacement for the RO4003C™ material, RO4350B™ laminates
utilize RoHS compliant ﬂ ame-retardant technology for applications requiring
UL 94V-0 certiﬁ cation. These materials conform to the requirements of IPC-
4103, slash sheet /10 for RO4003C, see note #1 for RO4350B slash sheet 
determination.

Prolonged exposure in an oxidative environment may cause changes to the dielectric properties of hydrocarbon based materials. The rate of change increases

applications and reports of oxidation resulting in performance problems are extremely rare, Rogers recommends that the customer evaluate each material 
and design combination to determine ﬁ tness for use over the entire life of the end product.
