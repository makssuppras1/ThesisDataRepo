Abstract
This thesis, conducted in collaboration with Team Danmark, presents an aerodynamic
analysis of a speedway motorcycle, using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simula-
tions and wind tunnel experiments with the main goal of reducing drag and enhancing
performance. Building upon the previous work of Sebastian Obwoya Gudmand-Høyer [1],
this study expands his analysis by including the rider in the geometry and performing an
extensive flow evaluation, identifying key drag contributors, investigating the effects of
wheel rotation and proposing aerodynamic modifications.
A high-fidelity CFD model was developed using STAR-CCM+, to examine high and low
pressure regions, vortex structures and flow separation. The results confirmed that the
rider is the dominant drag source, with its presence contributing over 50% of total re-
sistance.
The front fairing was identified as the primary motorcycle drag contributor.
Expanding on previous work, a new hand cover design was proposed, achieving a 9.5%
total drag reduction by minimising high-pressure zones and wake turbulence.
Wind tunnel experiments were conducted to validate the CFD methodology, with a focus
on Reynolds number dependency and yaw angle effects. The results confirmed that drag
area stabilises at higher Reynolds numbers, validating the assumption that the scaled
wind tunnel model approximately represents full-scale aerodynamic behaviour within the
limitations of scaled wind tunnel testing. A yaw study revealed an increase in CdA with
yaw angle, with asymmetries attributed to the rider’s positioning and motorcycle geom-
etry. CFD results closely matched experimental data. For the simulated flow velocities
of 30 m/s and 40 m/s, where results are considered Reynolds number independent, devi-
ations were 5.77% and 4.79%, respectively, compared to the wind tunnel tests. For the
yaw angles simulated, CFD results presented a maximum deviation of 3.05% compared to
experimental results. These results reinforce the accuracy of the numerical methodology
used in this thesis.
Mesh and time-step convergence studies ensured the accuracy and independence of sim-
ulation results from numerical resolution and temporal discretisation. The rotating vs
stationary wheels study revealed that wheel motion increased total drag by 3.87%, with
the front wheel contributing significantly due to intensified low-pressure zones. The rider
positioning study demonstrated that leg placement significantly affects wake formation
and drag, though not always in a predictable manner, highlighting the complex interac-
tion between body positioning and aerodynamic performance.
With no previous CFD studies in speedway motorcycle aerodynamics, this thesis presents
a pioneering contribution by providing new insights into drag reduction strategies for
speedway motorcycles. The findings have practical applications not only for Team Dan-
mark but also for the motorsport community, offering data-driven recommendations for
improving aerodynamic eﬀiciency in competitive racing. Future research should further
explore advanced turbulence models, conduct full-scale testing and refine the proposed
designs to optimise aerodynamic performance further.

Preface
The present thesis is a six-month conclusion of development and investigation, with the
objective of researching and improving the aerodynamic performance of speedway motor-
cycles. Performed in collaboration with Team Danmark, the work extends and refines
previous research and deepens the understanding of airflow interaction with motorcycle
and rider. What is addressed in this work is the result of CFD simulations and wind
tunnel testing, with an objective towards proposing drag reduction methodologies.
The motivation for such a study arises out of a lack of current aerodynamic work in
speedway motorcycles. In contrast, in other motorsports such as Formula 1 and MotoGP,
aerodynamics is an important consideration, but in speedway, its use has not yet been
examined. In an attempt to fill that gap, this thesis aims to present data-based information
and make recommendations for improvement in terms of aerodynamic eﬀiciency.
Throughout the period of this investigation, a variety of obstacles have been encountered,
including complications in meshing, simulation instabilities and experimental challenges
in wind tunnel testing. Each challenge provided valuable learning experiences, shaping
the methodology and findings presented in this thesis.

Introduction
Speedway is a motorcycle sport in which four to six riders compete over four laps on an oval
dirt track. Because they only have one gear and no brakes, speedway race motorcycles are
extremely different from other motorcycles. They are made to maximize power delivery
and handling. In order to get around the turns, riders use power slides, and in the straight
sections, they may reach high speeds of 110 km/h.
In Denmark, speedway has a long and successful history, between 1978 and 2014 the
Danish national team won the Speedway World Team Cup 15 times. Legendary riders
like Hans Nielsen, who helped win 11 of these wins, have solidified Denmark’s reputation as
a dominant force in the sport [2]. Despite its popularity, speedway motorcycle engineering
has mostly focused on handling and power delivery optimization, with little consideration
given to aerodynamics, which could provide additional performance advantages.
Aerodynamic optimization is essential in other motorsports such as Formula 1, MotoGP
and NASCAR, in order to increase speed, reduce drag and improve stability. The same
principles apply to speedway racing. However, there are no aerodynamic studies on speed-
way motorcycles. Drag force, which is the resistance experienced by an object moving
through a fluid (in this case, air), has a big impact on the motorcycle’s performance.
Higher top speeds, quicker acceleration and more economical fuel use can all be achieved
by reducing drag.
Therefore, increasing speedway bikes’ aerodynamic eﬀiciency offers
significant potential to improve performance in competitive environments.
In recent years, Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has become a widely used tool for
analysing and optimizing vehicle aerodynamics. CFD simulations allow for a detailed ex-
amination of airflow patterns, pressure distribution and turbulence around complex shapes
like motorcycles. By replicating real-world conditions, CFD provides valuable insights that
are not always achievable through on-track testing or wind tunnel experiments.
This thesis builds upon the work of Sebastian Obwoya Gudmand-Høyer, who conducted
extensive CFD simulations of a speedway motorcycle in straight-line conditions [1]. His
study was on locating and minimizing drag, especially from parts like the front fairing.
By investigating other aerodynamic parameters, such as the impact of the rider’s presence
and the rotation of the wheels on drag, and proposing new drag-reducing solutions like
optimised hand guards designs and adjusted rider positioning, this thesis seeks to expand
on Gudmand-Høyer’s research.
The methodology will involve CFD simulations using STAR-CCM+. Various scenarios
will be modelled to provide a detailed understanding of how the flow behaves around the
geometry and influence overall performance.
Given the absence of existing research in this area, this thesis aims to advance the subject of
speedway motorcycle aerodynamics by bringing contemporary CFD techniques to a sport
with a long history in Danish motorsports. The findings of this research are expected to not
only benefit Team Danmark but also have larger implications for motorsport engineering,
providing a framework for future aerodynamic studies.

1.1.1
Objective and scope
The previous study was motivated by the absence of aerodynamic research on speedway
motorcycles. The primary objective was to identify regions on the motorcycle that pro-
duced high drag forces and propose design modifications to reduce these forces, ultimately
improving speed and performance. His research primarily focused on the front fairing,
which was identified as a major contributor to drag force.
1.1.2
Geometry and simulation setup
In his research, Gudmand-Høyer used a 3D-scanned model of a speedway motorcycle
provided by Team Danmark. As seen in Figure 1.1, the model did not include the rider.
The geometry was cleaned up to remove unnecessary details, elements like the spokes of
the wheels were omitted to simplify the meshing process. The computational domain was
established to simulate airflow at realistic racing conditions with a flow velocity of 30 m/s,
which is typical in speedway races.
A polyhedral mesh was implemented for the simulations, refining the mesh around the
geometry and the wake, seen in Figure 1.2.

1.1.3
Key findings
A flow analysis of the speedway motorcycle was conducted in the previous study and
provided valuable insights for performance improvements. Key findings from Gudmand-
Høyer’s work that influenced the present research include the identification of major drag
contributors, new front fairing and hand cover designs, and the evaluation of the impact
of wheel rotation on drag force.
Drag contributors. New front nose designs
Performed simulations revealed several insights into the aerodynamic behaviour of the
speedway motorcycle. The front fairing was identified as a key contributor to the total
drag force acting on the motorcycle. Through multiple design iterations, Gudmand-Høyer
explored different fairing geometries to reduce this drag, ranging from minor shape ad-
justments to more radical modifications aimed at reducing the wake size and smoothing
out the flow across the surface.
One of the most significant outcomes was the identification of flow separation zones that
form behind the motorcycle’s front fairing, which created a turbulent wake, contributing
to drag. By proposing a new design of the fairing geometry, a 5.5% reduction in drag
was achieved. The results are clearly illustrated in Figure 1.3, which shows the pressure
coeﬀicient comparison between the original and modified fairing designs.

After the thesis was completed, during the completion of a special course, Gudmand-Høyer
proposed more front nose designs to further reduce drag. For these new front nose designs,
the geometry included the rider and was the same model used for the present thesis, this
model can be seen in Figure 3.2. With the goal to avoid the large vortex generated behind
the front fairing, he removed the front cover and modified the forks design to an air foiled

2
Theoretical background
The aerodynamics of motorcycles is a critical factor in determining their performance,
particularly at high speeds. Aerodynamic eﬀiciency directly impacts factors such as drag,
stability and handling. The primary aerodynamic force acting against the motorcycle is
drag, which is composed of pressure drag due to the shape of the motorcycle and skin
friction from the surface of the body interacting with the airflow. Understanding these
forces is key to reduce energy losses and improve overall speed and fuel eﬀiciency.
CFD offers a powerful way to analyse and optimize the aerodynamics of motorcycles
by simulating the airflow around the geometry.
In CFD, the complex patterns of air
movement are modelled by numerically solving the Navier-Stokes equations, which describe
the behaviour of fluid flow. These equations are based on the principles of conservation of
mass, momentum and energy, and they govern how air flows over the surfaces.
With tools like STAR-CCM+ engineers can use CFD to predict how different design
configurations affect aerodynamic forces like drag. Accurate simulations can be run to
visualize and quantify the behaviour of airflow along the studied geometry. This helps
identify regions where the flow separates or becomes turbulent, which are often major
contributors to increased drag.
In this study, drag force will be a primary focus, as reducing drag directly contributes to
improved performance. The simulation results are derived from the underlying mathemat-
ical framework of CFD, which is built on the continuity, momentum and energy equations
that describe the motion of fluids.
2.1
Governing equations
The fluid flow around a motorcycle, like all fluid dynamics problems, is governed by the
Navier-Stokes equations, which form the core of CFD. These equations describe the motion
of fluids (in this case, air) by expressing the conservation of mass, momentum and energy.
Solving these equations provides a detailed understanding of how forces like drag and lift
develop.
The Navier-Stokes equations are derived from fundamental physics principles. They are
non-linear partial differential equations that describe how the velocity field of a fluid
evolves over time. In most computational applications for aerodynamics, especially in high-
speed flows, two key assumptions are often made: the fluid is considered incompressible
(the density does not change significantly) and it behaves as a Newtonian fluid (the stress
is linearly proportional to the rate of strain).
2.1.1
Continuity equation (Conservation of mass)
The continuity equation ensures that mass is conserved within the flow field. For incom-
pressible flows, where the fluid density ρ is constant, the continuity equation simplifies to
Equation 2.1:
∇· u = 0
(2.1)
This equation implies that the net rate of flow into a given control volume must be equal
to the rate of flow out, ensuring there is no accumulation or depletion of mass.

2.1.2
Momentum equation (Conservation of momentum)
The momentum equation, derived from Newton’s second law, describes how the momen-
tum of the fluid changes due to various forces, including pressure gradients, viscous forces
and external forces like gravity. For an incompressible Newtonian fluid, the momentum
Equation 2.2 (in vector form) is:
ρ(δu
δt + u·∆u) = −∆p + µ∆2u + f
(2.2)
where: u is the velocity vector of the fluid, p is the pressure, µ is the dynamic viscosity of
the fluid, f represents body forces (like gravity), and ∆2u u represents viscous diffusion.
This equation captures the balance of forces in the flow, including inertial forces, pressure
forces and viscous effects.
This equation is crucial for calculating how air moves over
and around the geometry, which directly influences forces like drag and lift. For example,
understanding how pressure forces act on the front and sides of the motorcycle helps
engineers minimize pressure drag by optimizing the shape of the motorcycle.
2.2
Drag force
Drag force is a critical aerodynamic factor encountered by bodies moving through a fluid
medium, such as air. In the context of aerodynamics, drag refers to the resistance a body
experiences due to the relative motion between it and the surrounding air. This resistance
significantly impacts performance, energy eﬀiciency and design considerations, especially
in high-speed applications like cycling and motorsports.
Drag can be categorized into two main components: pressure drag and skin friction drag.
Pressure drag arises from the pressure differential between the front and rear surfaces
of the body, caused by flow separation and wake formation.
This component is more
pronounced in bluff bodies, where sharp geometry changes create large, turbulent wakes.
Skin friction drag, on the other hand, is attributed to the tangential forces exerted by the
fluid due to its viscosity, affecting regions within the boundary layer.
The drag coeﬀicient (CD) is a dimensionless parameter that quantifies the aerodynamic
eﬀiciency of a body and is influenced by its shape, surface roughness and Reynolds number.
It can be expressed with Equation 2.3:
CD =
FD
0.5ρU 2A
(2.3)
where FD is the drag force, ρ is the fluid density, U is the velocity of the object relative
to the fluid, and A is the frontal area. For practical applications, the product of CD and
A, known as drag area (CdA), serves as a crucial parameter in comparing aerodynamic
performances across different conditions.
Drag force impacts performance significantly by dictating the amount of power required to
maintain a specific velocity. The power required to overcome drag is given by Equation 2.4:
P = FDU
(2.4)
This relationship demonstrates the cubic dependency of power on velocity and highlights
the necessity of optimizing drag for energy eﬀiciency and enhanced performance. Stud-
ies, such as [6], demonstrate that even minor geometric refinements can yield significant
reductions in drag, especially in bluff bodies like cyclists and motorcycles.

Another essential aspect of drag is its dependency on the boundary layer behaviour. The
boundary layer, represented in Figure 2.1, is the thin region of fluid near the body surface,

separation, thereby reducing pressure drag.
In recent times, CFD has played a pivotal role in understanding and mitigating drag. It
enables detailed analyses of flow behaviour, providing insights into areas of high drag and
potential design optimizations, making it possible to develop more aerodynamic designs.

The methods used to investigate the aerodynamics of a speedway motorcycle, combining
computational and experimental approaches, are detailed in this chapter. CFD simulations
were conducted to model the flow behaviour around the motorcycle, with results validated

The scanning process was

3.3
Solver settings and turbulence models
As said before, Simcenter STAR-CCM+ was used to conduct the simulations in this study,
employing the same physics models and solver settings as in study [1].
This software
uses the Navier-Stokes equations to solve complex simulations involving fluid dynamics
and heat transfer, among other phenomena.
These equations, defined in Section 2.1,
are useful to predict the motion of fluids and are based on mass, momentum and energy
conservation. To discretize this equations over the computational grid, advanced numerical
methods, like the finite volume method (FVM), are used.
This technique divides the
computational domain into discrete control volumes (cells), and the governing equations
are integrated over each volume, ensuring conservation of mass, momentum and energy. To
couple velocity and pressure for incompressible flows, the SIMPLE (Semi-Implicit Method
for Pressure Linked Equations) algorithm was used. This method uses implicit schemes,
which are better for larger time steps, ideal for transient flows where time accuracy without
compromising stability is needed. For slow speed flows and evolving turbulent behaviour,
implicit solvers are better compared to explicit solvers [15].
To solve the fluid flow equations, the segregated flow solver was utilized.
This solver
sequentially computes the conservation equations of mass and momentum, which makes
it especially eﬀicient for incompressible flows where there are no strong interactions. A
coupled approach would offer faster convergence for complex transient phenomena, but
the segregated solver also provides a good balance between computational eﬀiciency and
robustness, making it suitable for transient simulations with moderate complexity and
limited resources [15].
In the previous study [1], the effect of turbulence modelling was analysed by comparing
different turbulence models. The Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) approach was
selected due to its balance between accuracy and computational eﬀiciency. Other meth-

4
Results
This chapter presents the results obtained from all simulation scenarios, providing insight
into the aerodynamic performance and flow characteristics analysed in the study.
4.1
Flow analysis
The contents of this section are fundamental to achieve the main objective of this thesis:
optimizing the aerodynamic performance of speedway motorcycles. To propose meaning-
ful improvements, it is crucial to have a good understanding of how airflow interacts with
the motorcycle’s geometry and to identify the key contributors to the drag force generated
during motion. Drag is one of the primary factors that limit performance in high-speed
scenarios, and breaking it down into its individual contributors provides valuable under-
standing of potential areas for improvement.
In this analysis, special attention is given to the effects of pressure distribution, velocity
gradients, wall shear stress and vorticity fields. These parameters reveal the intricate flow
behaviour around the motorcycle, including regions of high resistance, flow separation,
and turbulence. To get this results, a simulation was conducted using rotating wheels
and all the settings and conditions detailed in Chapter 3. The results of this analysis
serve as a foundation for identifying and implementing aerodynamic optimizations aimed
at reducing drag and improving performance.
4.1.1
Drag contributors
Drag force is a critical factor influencing the performance of speedway motorcycles, par-
ticularly at high speeds, where aerodynamic resistance becomes a dominant consideration.
Understanding the individual contributors to drag is essential for proposing new com-
ponents designs and minimizing resistance. As outlined in Section 2.2, drag primarily
consists of two key components: pressure drag and skin friction drag. The results of this
study reveal that at full speed, the motorcycle generates an average drag force of 257.74 N,
corresponding to a drag coeﬀicient (CD) of 0.689. In the previous thesis [1], average drag
force was 124.56 N and drag coeﬀicient 0.7848, this difference comes from the inclusion of
the rider in the geometry and the different mesh parameters and time step defined in the
present thesis. Approximately 98% of total drag force is attributed to pressure drag and
only 2% to skin friction drag. Given this distribution, the primary focus of this analy-
sis is on pressure drag, which presents the most significant opportunity for performance
improvement.
Previous research [1] identified the front fairing, front fender, front fork and front wheel
as the principal contributors to drag, which aligns with findings from [8], where these
components accounted for 60.8% of the total drag. However, unlike in [1], the current
study incorporates the rider, whose contribution to drag is expected to be significant.
By including the rider, this study provides a more comprehensive understanding of drag
contributors, facilitating more effective and close to reality aerodynamic optimizations.

contributors to aerodynamic resistance. The study [8] highlights the critical role of drag
contributor analysis in understanding the aerodynamic performance of a sport motorcycle,
because it provides the foundation for targeted design improvements to minimize drag.

Analysing the pressure coeﬀicient provides valuable understanding of the regions of high

High-pressure zones are typically observed on forward-facing surfaces such as the rider’s
chest, the front fairing and the front fork and contribute significantly to overall drag. On

wheel, the front fairing, the front shocks, the handlebar and the bottom of the motor.
As these areas significantly contribute to drag, they present opportunities for redesign or
optimization to enhance the motorcycle’s aerodynamic performance.
Examining the motorcyclist, high pressure regions are clearly visible on the legs, hands,
shoulders, chest and helmet. These areas experience significant stagnation pressure due to
their exposure to the oncoming airflow, making them main contributors to pressure drag.

be observed along the edges of the rider’s body, particularly at the hips, sides of the arms
and legs and along the helmet’s sides. Also, a low pressure wake forms behind the rider’s
back as a result of flow separation, creating suction forces that further increase drag.

Wall shear stress
Wall shear stress represents the tangential force applied by the airflow on the surface of the
geometry. This force is generated by viscous effects in the boundary layer. Although its
contribution to the total drag force is smaller compared to pressure drag, wall shear stress
is critical for understanding how flow behaves near the surface and identifying regions of
flow separation. Figure 4.5 presents the wall shear stress contour for the studied geometry.
Areas of high wall shear stress (dark blue regions) indicate strong surface shear, indicating
attached flow. In contrast, regions where wall shear stress approaches zero (white areas)
imply either flow separation or static flow.
High wall shear stress is observed on the rider’s body, where skin friction drag is most
significant. Separation zones are clearly visible on the rider’s back and legs, while a larger
separation region is present in the rear section of the motorcycle behind the left leg of
the rider in Figure 4.5a. This larger separation is primarily caused by the wake generated
by the rider’s leg, which disrupts the flow and creates chaotic behaviour around the side
fairing and wheel plate. Adjustments to the rider’s leg positioning could help reduce wake
formation, leading to reduced separation and improved performance of the motorcycle.
Focusing on the right side of the motorcycle geometry in Figure 4.5b, it is evident that the
separation region observed in the rear section on the left side is absent. This indicates that

Rider adjusted positioning
From the flow analysis, it was concluded that the rider is the primary contributor to drag,
with the legs, hands, shoulders, chest and helmet identified as the key areas generating
pressure drag. Examination of the flow behaviour around the rider’s body revealed that
the non-aerodynamic posture creates a substantial wake behind the rider, significantly in-
creasing drag. Previous studies [8, 33, 34] have demonstrated that even minor adjustments
in rider posture can influence not only localised drag forces, but also the overall flow field
and wake structures surrounding the rider.
Analysis of the flow distribution highlighted the legs as a major source of turbulence,
creating extensive high-turbulence regions. Consequently, adjustments to leg positioning
were investigated as a means to reduce drag. During the 3D scanning process used to
obtain the geometry, the rider was stabilising himself by touching the ground with his
right foot, meaning that the right leg was not in a riding position and is excluded from
this study. However, the left leg was adjusted and simulated in various configurations,
with the aim of reducing the high-turbulence region observed in the wake and streamlining
airflow around the lower sections of the geometry.
Figure 4.19 illustrates the four simulated configurations, with each one incrementally re-
ducing the angle of the leg relative to the motorcycle’s frame by 1º. Although the changes
between configurations may appear subtle, each adjustment moves the leg closer to the
motorcycle, reducing the frontal area. The rotational point was defined at the rider’s hip,
resulting in the foot being the component that moved the most while the hip remained
almost stationary.
To cover the gap created between the rider’s body and leg caused
by the leg rotation, an additional geometry was introduced along the edge of the leg to
maintain a smooth transition between the leg and the rest of the body and guide airflow
around the geometry.
These modifications aimed to reduce the frontal area of the geometry and reduce wake

frontal area for the original position and the other four positions simulated.

5
Discussion
This study aimed to provide a comprehensive aerodynamic analysis of a speedway mo-
torcycle through CFD simulations and wind tunnel experiments. Various aerodynamic
factors, such as the main drag contributors, rider positioning, wheel rotation and design
modifications were investigated.
5.1
Discussion of results
Results highlight the significant contribution of the rider to the overall aerodynamic drag,
with the rider’s presence accounting for more than 50% of the total drag force. Drag
generated by the rider comes from direct resistance generated by the rider’s body and
interference drag caused by the interaction between the rider and the motorcycle. Flow
separation around the rider’s legs, arms and helmet was identified as a primary source of
turbulence and wake formation. The pressure coeﬀicient distribution and vorticity analy-
ses confirmed that large wake regions formed downstream of the rider, increasing aerody-
namic resistance. These findings reinforce the necessity of optimising rider positioning to
minimise wake turbulence and reduce overall drag.
The mesh and time step convergence studies were conducted to ensure numerical accuracy
and stability of the simulations. A systematic refinement of the mesh revealed that beyond
a certain resolution, the average drag force remained almost constant, confirming grid
independence. Similarly, a time step sensitivity study showed that refining the time step
did not significantly alter drag force, as all the tested time steps showed similar results.
The final time step was chosen to comply to the CFL condition and ensure good a balance
between computational eﬀiciency and accuracy.
The rotating vs stationary wheels study revealed that rotating wheels resulted in a 3.87%
increase in total drag, contradicting some previous studies but aligning with [1]. However,
contrary to the results in [1, 4] the drag contribution from the wheels also increases when
wheels are rotating, suggesting that wheel rotation influences pressure distribution and
wake formation around the lower sections of the motorcycle and the rider’s feet and legs.
Notably, the front wheel exhibited a 75.39% increase in drag when rotating, largely due
to stronger low-pressure regions forming inside the wheel structure. Streamline analysis
revealed how the rotation of the wheels directs the flow to the lower part of the motorcycle
by centrifugal forces, creating a wider wake. These conclusions may not apply to real
scenarios as the wheels did not include spokes which would significantly affect the flow
inside the wheel, disturbing the flow in the low pressure region inside the wheels.
The wind tunnel validation experiments provided essential experimental validation for
the CFD simulations. The Reynolds dependency study demonstrated that CdA values
stabilised at higher Reynolds numbers, validating the assumption that the wind tunnel
tests performed accurately represent full scale conditions. Additionally, the yaw study
confirmed that CdA increased with yaw angle, with asymmetry in the results indicating
the influence of the rider’s body and motorcycle geometry. The comparison between CFD
and wind tunnel results showed variations below 6% for velocity tests and below 3% for
yaw tests, reinforcing the reliability and validity of the simulation methodology.
The newly proposed hand cover design, yielded a 9.5% reduction in drag, outperform-
ing previous iterations. The improved design minimised high-pressure zones in the front
and reduced wake turbulence by removing the front cover and introducing airfoil-shaped

forks and hand covers, as confirmed by pressure coeﬀicient contours and Lambda-2 vor-
tex structures. This highlights the potential of small geometric refinements in achieving
significant aerodynamic gains, a finding consistent with previous research in motorsports
aerodynamics. On the counterpart, the proposed designed does not come with the oppor-
tunity of incorporating advertisements in the front cover and does not protect the rider
from possible mud or pebbles that could potentially hit him. Additionally, this design is
just a conceptual design at this stage. It lacks a mounting method and further stability
and ergonomics studies must be done to validate the design.
Lastly, a new leg position was analysed by moving the leg closer to the motorcycle body.
Results revealed complex interactions between leg posture, drag force, and frontal area.
While bringing the legs closer to the motorcycle body consistently reduced frontal area,
the expected decrease in drag was observed due to secondary aerodynamic effects such
as flow separation, wake turbulence and altered pressure distribution. Some configura-
tions resulted in lower drag than others despite a smaller frontal area, suggesting that
leg positioning influences localised flow structures that may either enhance or disrupt
aerodynamics. Further analysis and simulations would be needed to find more conclusive
results.
5.2
Future work
Building on the findings of this study, several avenues for future research are proposed:
• High-resolution turbulence modelling: Following the findings in [1], the current
thesis used RANS turbulence modelling. Implementing DDES or LES could improve
wake resolution and provide more accurate predictions of transient flow structures.
These models would allow for better capturing of vortex shedding, turbulence inter-
actions and unsteady aerodynamic effects.
• Full-scale wind tunnel testing: Conducting full-scale experiments eliminates
scaling effects and enhances CFD validation. While the 1/6 scale model provided
valuable insights, full-scale testing enables direct real-world comparisons, reducing
uncertainties from Reynolds number scaling and model simplifications.
• Refinement of hand cover and fairing design: While the proposed design
achieved a 9.5% reduction in drag, further refinements could further improve the
performance of the speedway. Future works should also focus on proposing a design
with a mounting system in the speedway. Creating a real prototype of the new faring
and hand covers and testing it in full scale wind tunnel tests would provide a better
understanding of the real effect of the new design.
• Cornering aerodynamics analysis: All tests and results in this study are based
on headwind conditions. Therefore, the proposed designs were optimised for straight
line conditions. Future work should include yaw angle simulations to assess how the
new designs perform in crosswind conditions.
• Further rider positioning optimisation: Extending the rider positioning ad-
justment study by testing different postures could lead to more refined aerodynamic
recommendations. New 3D scanned geometries of the rider in different positions
would be needed.
• Optimisation of flow to the intake: Enhancing and optimising the airflow to
the engine intake could lead to potential performance gains and a higher top speed
during the straights of the race, ensuring a more stable and higher mass flow rate
into the engine.

6
Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to analyse the aerodynamic performance of a speedway
motorcycle to understand how flow behaves around the geometry and propose an aerody-
namic improvement to reduce drag force and help riders achieve a higher speed with their
speedway. This goal was accomplished with a hand guards design that resulted in a 9.5%
reduction in drag.
The flow analysis findings confirmed that the rider is the primary source of drag given the
non-aerodynamic posture. However, a drag reduction by modifying the leg positioning
was not achieved in this thesis.
Wind tunnel tests successfully validated the simulation results and confirmed the accuracy
of the CFD methodology employed in the thesis. This validation ensures that the findings
of this thesis provide a solid foundation for future studies on this field.
Overall, this study highlights that optimising aerodynamics in speedway motorcycles can
yield significant performance gains. Future work should explore higher-resolution turbu-
lence modelling, full-scale wind tunnel testing and propose new designs and rider position
to refine aerodynamic eﬀiciency further.
