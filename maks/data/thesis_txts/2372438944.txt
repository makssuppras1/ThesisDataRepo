Abstract
The mucus layer in the gastro-intestinal tract (GIT) protects the underlying
epithelium from foreign particles. The mucus consists of large glycoproteins
called mucin, which plays a signiﬁcant role in drug permeation across the
intestinal mucosa. Due to lack of in vitro models with a mucus layer and
reliable mucus sources it is diﬃcult to determine the role and function of
mucus in drug permeation. The aim of this thesis is to develop an in vitro
model to investigate the eﬀect of porcine intestinal native mucus, puriﬁed
porcine intestinal native mucus and porcine gastric mucus (PGM) on mucosal
drug delivery of amorphous sodium salt of furosemide (ASSF). The in vitro
model consisted of human epithelial colorectal adenocarcinoma cells (caco-2
cells). The caco-2 cells were cultured on semi-permeable ﬁlter which resulted
in a monolayer. The caco-2 monolayer was incubated for 3 hours with the
three mucus sources. The oCelloScope was ﬁlming the incubated caco-2 cells
to evaluate which mucus source was most compatible.
An interaction of
the mucus sources and caco-2 cells were observed, however the reason for
the interaction could not be determined. Followed by this, the caco-2 cells
were stained with alcian blue to observe if there were any attachment of
mucin ﬁbers to the surface of caco-2 cells. No siginifant diﬀerence between
the mucus sources were observed.
Hereafter, the drug permeation across
the caco-2 monolayer incubated with HBSS apical buﬀer solution and the
diﬀerent mucus sources were conducted. The apparent permeability (Papp)
for ASSF was signiﬁcantly high, when permeating across mucus sources. A
signifant diﬀerence in drug permeation of caco-2 cells incubated with HBSS
buﬀer solution and puriﬁed intestinal native mucus was also observed. In
conclusion, the in vitro results showed that drug permeation of ASSF is more
aﬀected by the presence of mucus than without mucus. Further experiments
are needed to conclude which mucus source is most suitable for caco-2 cells,
and whether they eﬀect the drug permeation.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The most common drug delivery routes are oral, pulmonary, transdermal
and intravenous drug delivery[22, 40]. Oral drug delivery is one of the pre-
ferred administration routes for both local and systemic drugs. The prefer-
ence is due to its accesibility, cost-eﬀectiveness, non-invasiveness and patient
compliance[44, 2].
The aim of using oral drug delivery is to ensure a high bioavailability to the
speciﬁc site of action such as the gastro-intestinal tract (GIT). Eventhough,
oral drug delivery is one of the accepted routes of administration, the GIT
presents some challenges. Factors such as enzymatic degradation, hepatic
ﬁrst metabolism, absorption of the compund before reaching the correct site,
solubility of the compound in GIT ﬂuid and most importantly when the
compound gets trapped/inhibited by the mucus in intestine can lead to a
low bioavailability[1, 45, 23].
Consequently, one of the main focus areas that are being studied to im-
prove the drug permeation and absorption is the mucus layer in intestine.
Mucus is a complex network of glycoproteins known as mucins. It is believed
that drug permeation is aﬀected by the interactions and entanglement with
the mucin network. It is therefore important to develop an intestinal in vitro
cell model that can imitate the function of an intestine.

==PAGE:15==
1.1
Motivation
Developing an in vitro cell model incorporating a mucus barrier is of key im-
portance when evaluating the mucosal drug permeation of oral drug delivery
systems or candidates[44]. An in vitro cell model which mimics the human
intestine greatly improves the overall understanding of the function of mucus
and mucosal permeation. Many articles regarding incorporation of a mucus
barrier to an in vitro cell model are available[30, 82], each explaining diﬀerent
methods. Most utilized in vitro cell models have either no mucus or produce
an non-homogeneous mucus layer[82, 83].
In this thesis an in vitro model of the intestine will be developed by investi-
gating diﬀerent mucus sources onto the surface of intestinal epithelial colon
adenocarcinoma cells (caco-2), which mimic the function of enterocytes[3].
Furthermore, experiments regarding attachment of mucus to the caco-2 cells
have been conducted.
These experiments formed the basis of the initial
phases of the experiment.
1.2
State of the art
Most studies use either commercially available porcine gastric mucins or pu-
riﬁed porcine mucins to create a mucus layer[82, 83, 87]. Marie Boegh et al.
have in 2013 developed a biosimilar mucus mixture to investigate the eﬀect
of mucosal drug absorption[63]. The biosimilar mixture was able to obtain a
rheological proﬁle that was comparable to porcine intestinal mucus. Further-
more, N.Navabi et al. have in 2013 used mechanical stimulation and a goblet
cell promoter to produce mucus on gastrointestinal cell lines. It showed that
a semi-wet interface with mechanical stimulation and a promoter could in-
duce a thin and partial mucus layer on the cell lines[84]. In 2013, F. Antunes
et al. examined the combination of caco-2 cells, HT29-MTX cells and Raji B
cells forming a triple co-culture. HT29-MTX diﬀerentiates into goblet cells
and Raji B cells are lymphocytes which can be turned into M-cells. The
study showed that adding the three cell lines together improved the in vitro
model and a resemblance of the human intestine was observed.

1.3
Project goal and hypothesis
The main goal of this thesis is to develop an in vitro cell model and inves-
tigating the eﬀect of diﬀerent porcine mucus sources on intestinal mucosal
drug permeation.
This has been done by examining the attachment of mucus sources to col-
orectal adenocarcinoma cells (caco-2) and determining the permeation of
amorphous sodium salt of furosemide across isolated mucus sources and the
caco-2 cells.
It was hypothesized that
• Porcine intestinal native mucus has larger impact on the drug perme-
ation compared to commercially available porcine gastric mucus and
puriﬁed porcine intestinal native mucus.
• The mucus sources can aﬀect the drug permeation of amorphous sodium
salt of furosemide
• Attachment of mucin ﬁbers to the caco-2 cell surface will be observed,
resulting in a mucus layer
1.4
Thesis overview
• Chapter 2: Theory. In this chapter, a thorough explanation of the
structure and properties of mucosa and mucus are given. The challenges
regarding the mucus such as interaction with chemical bonds and vis-
cosity are also explained. Furthermore, the evaluation of in vitro model,
the use of caco-2 cells and amorphous sodium salt of furosemide are de-
scribed. Finally, the diﬀerent detection techniques used for analysis of
drug permeation and the eﬀect of mucus sources are explained.
• Chapter 3: Materials and methods. The diﬀerent materials and meth-
ods used for the experiments are explained in this chapter
• Chapter 4: Caco-2 study. The morphology and integrity of caco-2
cells are described and analysed.

• Chapter 5: Analysis of mucus sources. The next step after studying
the caco-2 cells is the investigation of mucus sources by incubation,
visual inspection, staining and examining the viability.
• Chapter 6: Permeation study across mucus layer. The most suitable
mucus sources have been chosen in prior chapter. In this chapter the
drug permeation of amorphous sodium salt of furosemide across incu-

• Chapter 7: Conclusion. This chapter sums up the results from the
experiments and veriﬁes/rejects the hypotheses.

Chapter 2
Theory
In order to fully comprehend the challenges regarding mucus, one has to
realize the complexity of the structure and properties of the intestinal mu-
cosa, where the drug absorption occurs. The characteristics of the intestinal
mucosa and mucus will be discussed below.
2.1
Overview of the intestinal mucosa
The composition of the small intestine is displayed in (ﬁgure 2.1a). The small
intestine is a long, hollow and cylinder formed tube. It is composed of an
outer layer; serosa (connective tissue), a muscle layer; muscularis (longitu-
dinal and circular muscle layer), a thin layer; submucosa (loose connective
tissue) and the innermost layer; mucosa (cell layer). The mucosa surrounds
the lumen which contain plicae circularis (circular folds).
The circular
folds increase the surface area and contribute to the peristalsis (movement
of food)[6].
Each of these regions have their important characteristics. Since the drug
absorption occurs across the mucosa, the structure and properties of the
mucosa will be described in the following subsections.

2.1.2
Properties and structure of mucus
The intestine is covered with a viscoelastic gel called mucus (ﬁgure 2.3). Mu-
cus provides lubrication and protection of the underlying epithelium against
mechanical damage[11].
The mucus layer is two layered, closest to the lamina epithelia is a ﬁrmly
adherent mucus layer and above a loosely transient mucus layer[2]. Both
layers vary in thickness, ranging from 16µm-29µm for the ﬁrmly adherent
mucus layer, whereas the loosely adherent layer is said to have a thickness
from 123µm-480µm[5, 25]
The major constituents of mucus are water (95%-99%) by weight[4, 5, 12],
glycoproteins called mucins (1%-5%)[57, 12], smaller amounts of lipids, DNA,
other proteins, inorganic salts, enzymes, electrolytes and carbohydrates[4, 12,
16].
The mucin in mucus is secreted from the goblet cells. After being released,
the mucin ﬁbers form an entangled and crosslinked network, making mucus
semipermeable (ﬁgure 2.4)[16].
Thereby it is possible for speciﬁc molecules such as water and gases to dif-
fuse through the mucus. Mucus tends to be a barrier for larger moleules,
therefore, by looking into mucus, one can gain important information about
the absorption of drugs across mucus, which can be useful when developing
new drug candidates.
Mucin is the main component of mucus and has a molecular weight var-
ing from 0.5MDa- 50MDa[12]. They are composed of heavily glycosylated
polypeptide backbone containing high levels of serine and threonine. They
are mostly covalently linked to O-linked and to a lesser extent N-linked
oligosaccharide side chains which account for 50%-80% of the mass of the en-
tire mucin molecule[4, 12, 11]. Each oligosaccharide consists of 1-20 monosac-
charide residues[12]. Most oligosaccharides end with negatively charged sialic
acids (carboxyl group) or sulphate content[4]. This negatively charged sur-
face is typically how mucus gets into contact with for example drugs or other
compounds[58, 60, 59].

Chemical bonds
Generally, mucin ﬁbers can form chemical bonds with drugs and with them-
selves. The bonds can hold back on the compounds, which can lead to site
speciﬁc absorption. These are the chemical interactions taking place in mu-
cus.
• Covalent bonds: The glycosylated residues attached to the polypep-
tide backbone are covalently bonded.
This results in an entangled
network. Another covalent bond that occurs is when disulﬁde-bridges
attach to the mucin monomers. This forms the crosslinking and the
viscous gel[28].
• Hydrogen bonds: The glycolysated regions which are hydrophillic
can bind to other hydrophillic regions with hydrogen bonds[35, 27].
• Electrostatic interactions: The carbohydrates on the polypeptide
backbone contain negatively charged sialic acids and sulphate groups.
In many cases, these negatively charged molecules can attract positively
charged particles[55, 56]
• Hydrophobic interactions: There are hydrophobic domains on the
polypeptide backbone such as cysteine region and disulﬁde domain.
These hydrophobic domains tend to interact with lipophilic drugs[56,
27].
• Van Der Waal’s: When two similar oligosaccharades meet each other,
they are capable of interacting by van der waal forces[28].
Viscosity
The mucus has heterogeneous viscoelastic properties (non-newtonian), which
also plays an important role in the drug permeability[48, 60]. A material is
characterized as non-newtonian when it has both viscous and elastic features.
The basic principles of viscosity is that it exerts resistance and it will be per-
manantly deformed after stress has been applied. An elastic material will
store the energy when exposed to deformation and regain its original shape.
Generally when mucus has been exposed to stress, the interactions between
the mucin ﬁbers will decrease. This leads to a less entangled and liquid-like

centrifugation the high molecular weight mucins will sediment at the bottom
of the tube forming a pellet[62]. The pellet will be gel-liquid like due to the

2.2
In vitro cell model
In order to evaluate drug absorption through mucus, a proper model is crit-
ical. In this section an in vitro model will be evaluated that can be used for
intestinal drug absorption.
In vitro techniques are cost-eﬀective, oﬀer beneﬁts in terms of ethical consid-
erations and reproducible compared to in vivo models[29, 31, 46]. Generally,
in vitro models are based on human cell lines. In order to imitate the in vivo
conditions, a potential in vitro tool, cell culture models, are often used.
The main principle behind cell culturing is to culture cells on transwell plates
and examining processes like drug absorption, permeation and bioavailabil-
ity.
Cell culture models oﬀer the advantage of changing parameters and
conditions easily. This method alone provides a great opportunity to make
diﬀerent experiments, with diﬀerent complexity levels.
However, one of the limitations using in vitro model is the inﬂuence of en-
vironmental factors like temperature variation and incubation[36, 54]. Even
though this is a problem, there are lots of potential and countermeasures
that can be taken, in order to solve the inﬂuence of environmental factors[36,
47, 54].
In this thesis the focus is on intestinal mucosa. Several cell monolayer models
that imitate the human intestinal epithelia have been developed[32, 44, 30].
Caco-2 cells are the most used human cell lines to predict permeability of
drugs across the small intestine membrane[2, 39]

2.2.1
In vitro model using Caco-2 cell culture model
The caco-2 cell line originates from human epithelial colorectal adenocarci-
noma cells[30, 16]. During in vitro growth the cells tend to form polarized
and diﬀerentiated monolayers with tight junctions and brush border. These
features resemble the mature small intestine enterocytes[33, 2]. Epithelial
cells that are polarized have an apical, a basal and a lateral plasma mem-
brane. This composition allows drug to permeate through the tight junction
as well as the apical membrane.
Comparing caco-2 cells with normal epithelial cells, it seems like the caco-
2 model has limitations. As mentioned in sec. 2.2, normal epithelium is
composed of several layers and cells. Whereas, the caco-2 monolayer only
consist of enterocytes. The lack of epithelial cells is a critical parameter. In
addition to that, the diﬀerentiation period of caco-2 cells to enterocytes is
also long[29].
The central challenge of this thesis is to construct a caco-2 model with a
mucus layer lining the monolayer homogenously, as there is no mucus due
to lack of goblet cells[30]. Despite the disadvantages, the caco-2 model pro-
vides an overall understanding of the function of the intestinal mucosa. The
experiments made in this thesis are a model of what can be expected when
working with intestinal mucosa. To have something comparable and similar
to intestinal mucosa the experiments are conducted with caco-2 cells.
The integrity and viability of caco-2 cells can be measured by a MTS/PMS
assay and Transepithelial Electrical Resistance (TEER)[63]. The MTS-PMS
assay determines the sensitivity and cell toxicity by using cellular enzymatic
bioreduction of tetrazolium salt MTS to a MTS-formazan product.
The
amount of viable cells corresponds to the quantity of formazan produced[7].
The TEER is determining the integrity of the cell monolayer and is mea-
sured in Ω/cm2. TEER values are also an expression of ion transport via
extracellular and transcellular pathways. The higher the TEER value, the
more dense is the monolayer. In order to work with the caco-2 cells as a
monolayer, the TEER values should be around 300 −400Ω/cm2[51, 64].

2.3
Drug absorption across caco-2 monolayer
Drugs transported across mucosa can be transported by passive or active
transport.
It is believed that drugs across mucosa permeate by the sim-
plest form of transport namely passive diﬀusion[29]. Passive diﬀusion occurs
when the drug moves down its concentration gradient without need of en-
ergy input[65, 66, 75]. Passive diﬀusion can be divided into two categories;
transcellular and paracellular pathway. The transcellular pathway is when
the drug penetrate the plasma membrane. Paracellular pathway takes place
when the drug permeates through the tight junctions or pores between the
cells[12].
The chemical compounds that permeate through the tight junctions are
mostly large and/or hydrophillic drugs. Whereas drugs that permeate through
the plasma membrane are typically hydrophobic, lipophilic and small[67, 68].
The passive diﬀusion can be explained with Fick’s ﬁrst law
J = Papp · ∆C
(2.1)
J is the ﬂux at steady state (the linear part of the curve) also known as the
absorption rate of a drug across a barrier (
mol
cm2·min). Papp is the apparent per-
meability coeﬃcient (cm/min) and ∆C is the change of concentration [66].
The system is considered to be in steady state conditions, when the concen-
tration gradient across a barrier is constant. This will result in a constant
ﬂux and the transport will follow zero order kinetics[69]. In order to fulﬁll
the constant concentration gradient, it is assumed that there is sink condi-
tion. Sink conditon is when the concentration in the acceptor compartment
is zero, by time the concentration will increase[66] and a constant drug dif-
fusion is assumed. The acceptable increase in acceptor compartment for the
concentration gradient should not change more than 10%. The limitation
can vary depending on the experiment[69].

as a model for intestinal drug absorption[2].

2.4
Conversion of crystalline drug to
amorphous drug
The solubility of a drug is very important to obtain a good bioavailability.
A method employed to increase the solubility and dissolution rate is the
conversion of a crystalline drug to its amorphous form[70, 71]. A crystalline
solid is where the atoms are arranged in a long-range order. This means that
the crystalline structure is compact and well-deﬁned as in a lattice pattern
(ﬁgure 2.6)[72]. In relation to crystalline form, the amorphous structure is
not arranged in a lattice pattern. The atoms are randomly displaced (ﬁgure
2.6)[72, 73].
Figure 2.6: The arrangement of the molecules in crystalline and amorphous
form.
The amorphous material is in a higher energy state[72, 2] compared to the
crystalline form. This results in a higher solubility and dissolution rate. Due
to the higher energy state, the amorphous material intends to release some
of its energy in order to become stable as crystalline materials[72].
As mentioned earlier, furosemide can be used for intestinal permeation and
absorption. The drug has been converted to salt form with the purpose of
achieving a higher solubility and dissolution rate. This has been done by
L.H.Nielsen et al.[2] from the Technical University of Denmark.

2.5.1
High-Performance Liquid
Chromatography
Analysis of the amount of drug permeated through the in vitro model can be
determined with High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC). HPLC
determines the amount of drug permeated across a biological membrane as
a function of time(t)[10]. HPLC uses high pressure to force solvent through
coloumns[9]. HPLC consists of a solvent delivery system, a sample injection,
a coloumn, a detector and a computer (ﬁgure 2.9).
Figure 2.9: An image of the HPLC system. It consists of a solvent delivery
system, a sample injection, a coloumn, a detector and a computer[81]
An injection of a small volume of liquid is going into a coloumn packed with
small porous particles also known as stationary phase. The porous particles
are covered with a chemically bonded phase, which interacts with the compo-
nents to seperate them from one another[9, 10]. The individual components
of the samples are forced through the coloumn by high pressure with a solvent
called mobile phase. With the involvement of chemcial and or/physical inter-
actions between the molecules, they are separated. The separated particles

oCelloScope
The oCelloScope is a digital microscope featuring an optical magniﬁcation
4x with an optical resolution of 1.3µm[80]. The microscope is extremely use-
ful in visualizing and calculating visible attributes like cells. The equipment
consist of a movable detector, a light source and a software. The detector
can be adjusted in terms of focus and location, whereas the light source can
be adjusted in terms of illumination. The detector and light source are tilted
6.25 degress in relaion to the sample[80]. Consider a cell sample being lo-
cated horizontally in between the detector and the light source. The detector
will then move horizontally and due to the tilted composition a volume can
be scanned rather than a plane as seen in normal microscopy. As mentioned
previously the oCelloScope does also consist of a software. This software
has multiple built-in function that can be used for analysing the cells. The
oCelloScope contains for example features as segmention and aquire. The
segmentation feature allows identiﬁcation and quantiﬁcation of objects of in-
terest. Aquire is an optical inspection of the sample. It obtains raw-data as
images of the sample. These features makes it possible to create time-lapse
movies. Time-lapse movies are when the microscope repeatedly scan a spe-
ciﬁc area of interest in a certain time-interval[79].
The oCelloScope is not produced anymore and has been taken out of the

tube. In every new passage 0.7 · 106cells pr. ml. were seeded
For the cell transport study the cells were seeded in 12-well plates. Another

cells pr. ml. were seeded and cultured for 11-15 days to obtain a monolayer.

lial electrical resistance apparatus (TEER) at room temperature (Millicell R⃝
ERS (Probes),EMD millipore corporation, Billerica, USA) The TEER values
varied from 200-700Ω.

Chapter 5
Analysis of the mucus sources
In this chapter three diﬀerent mucus sources are compared and discussed.
The mucus sources have been visually compared, stained and incubated while
being recorded by the oCelloScope. These images have been used to observe if
there were any interaction or attachment of mucus to the caco-2 cell surface.
Followed by this, a discussion of the viability and attachment of the mucus
is presented.
Three mucus sources; native mucus, puriﬁed native mucus and commercially
available PGM have been used. Native mucus and the puriﬁed native mucus
have been stored in the freezer to prevent bacteria growth. Moreover, note
that the preparation data has not been available for the PGM from the
supplier.
5.1
Visual comparison of the mucus sources
The caco-2 cells can be used for modelling human intestinal epithelia. With
the purpose of making an in vitro caco-2 model with mucus for permeation
study, it is necessary to characterize the diﬀerent mucus sources. However,
in this thesis the mucus sources are only characterized in relation to its
attachment and interaction to caco-2 cellss. The aim is to quantify which of
these three mucus sources are most biocompatible and interact with caco-2
cells without damaging the monolayer. This is assessed by ﬁrst analysing the
mucus sources visually.
The mucus sources are visually compared based on the appearance. Look-
ing at ﬁgure 5.1a, one can see that the PGM is purchased in a solid dried

pellet at the bottom of the falcontube, which leaves less heavy elements (bile
salt, phosphorlipids and chyme) to ﬂow on top of the pellet. These elements
are subsequently eliminated from the pellet, which could result in the light
brownish colour.
When PGM dispersion is added to ﬂuid it swells, but it does not have the
same texture as native mucus. According to Bindu M. Boddupalli et al.[16]
the swelling could be the result of hydrogen bonding between the glycosated

Chapter 6
Permeation study across mucus
layer
Developing an in vitro model with an incorporation of a mucus barrier is
vital for evaluation of drug permeation of mucosal drug delivery systems.
In this chapter the apparent permeability (Papp) of amorphous sodium salt
of furosemide (ASSF) across the caco-2 monolayer is studied. The caco-2
cells are incubated for 2 hours with HBSS buﬀer solutions, native mucus
or puriﬁed native mucus.
Furthermore, the integrity and viability of the
incubated cells are tested with MTS/PMS assay and TEER. The permeation
study has been conducted over two passages.
6.1
Flux
In this thesis, the ﬂux determines the rate of mass that ﬂows across a surface
area. The accumulated amount of ASSF in the basolateral compartment at
diﬀerent time points have been used to determine the ﬂux(see sec.3.3). The
amount of ASSF that has permeated across the ﬁlter membrane has been
determined by the High Performance Liquid Chromotography (HPLC). The
HPLC measures the concentration of a drug that has permeated across a
biological membrane. Below, a ﬁgure of the accumulated amount of ASSF
over time is presented.

6.2
Lag time
A lag time has been observed for ASSF which has been applied on the apical
side (ﬁgure 6.1). The lag time is the linear portion of the curve to the
intersection of the x-axis. It determines when the drug permeation begins.
The lag time has been calculated for the HBSS apical buﬀer solution and the
mucus sources. The lag time for HBSS apical buﬀer solution is 7.201min.,
whereas the lag time for native mucus and puriﬁed native mucus are 3min.
and 5.965min, respectively. This indicates that it takes more time to start
the permeation of the drug across caco-2 cells with the HBSS apical buﬀer
solution than with the mucus sources. This has not been expected.
The lower lag time of the mucus sources could perhaps be due to the mucus
sources are damaging the monolayer in a very early stage of the permeation,
resulting in a higher accumulation of ASSF in the basolateral compartment
(ﬁgure 6.1). The caco-2 cells contain P-gb proteins that are on the surface
of the cell and it seems like ASSF permeate across the P-gb proteins[2, 89],
which could possibly have withheld the ASSF, resulting an increase of lag
time for the HBSS apical buﬀer solution.

6.3.1
Recovery
Recovery determines the amount of drug that has permeated to the baso-
lateral compartment at the end of the study in proportion to the initial
concentration of ASSF in the apical compartment. The recovery of ASSF for
buﬀer solution is 86±12% (n=6), native mucus had a 100±10% (n=6) recov-
ery and puriﬁed native mucus had a 80±10% (n=6) recovery. The purﬁed
native mucus has a lower recovery compared to the other two. This could
be due to some of the ASSF being trapped in the puriﬁed native mucus, due
to the high molecular weight mucins. The lower recovery for HBSS apical
buﬀer solution could be due to P-gb proteins that are on the surface of the
cell monolayer. One could assume that these proteins withhold the perme-
ation of the compound. Generally the lower recovery could possibly also be
due the ﬁlter membrane.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The overall aim in this thesis has been to investigate the eﬀect of porcine mu-
cus sources on mucosal drug delivery of amorphous sodium salt of furosemide
across caco-2 cells. It was conﬁrming that caco-2 cells could be used as an
in vitro model due to the morphology and diﬀerentiation of the cells into in-
testinal enterocytes. From the visual point of view and from the literature it
seems that native mucus has a stronger gel-forming network compared to the
purﬁed native mucus and PGM. This was due to the stickyness and stringi-
ness of native mucus, which could indicate that it had a higher viscosity.
The caco-2 cells have been incubated with diﬀerent mucus sources on top
to examine the eﬀect of the diﬀerent mucus sources on caco-2 cells. The be-
haviour of caco-2 cells with mucus sources was recorded by the oCelloScope.
A darker appearance was noticable after 3 hours of incubation for the three
mucus sources. It was therefore diﬃcult to conclude which mucus was most
suitable. Furthermore, the viability of the incubated cells were measured.
A higher absorbance was measured for the mucus sources compared to the
HBSS apical buﬀer solution. It was propbably due the an interaction between
the Mucus sources and MTS/PMS assay. In addition to that the absorbance
of the HBSS apical buﬀer solution was very low, which could have resulted
from a lower incubation time. A MTS-PMS control was conducted and it
was concluded that there was background absorbance for the mucus sources,
this was taken into consideration for future experiments. Subsequently, the
caco-2 cells incubated with HBSS buﬀer solution and mucus sources were
stained with alcian blue. It was found that mucins do attach to the cell
surface. However, it was diﬃcult to comment on the cause of the interaction
67
==PAGE:69==
of the mucins to the cell surface. Further studies are needed to evaluate the
attachment of the mucins.
Native mucus and puriﬁed native mucus were chosen as the mucus layer for
the in vitro model. It was concluded that these mucus sources were more
compatible compared to PGM due to factors like pH, rheology studies ob-
tained from the literature and resemblance of human intestinal mucus. Drug
permeation across caco-2 cells incubated with mucus sources or HBSS apical
buﬀer solution showed that the permeation of ASSF seemed to damage the
caco-2 cells. This probably led to a higher permeation of ASSF across the
mucus sources. Apart from that, the viability of caco-2 cells incubated with
mucus sources were also signiﬁcantly reduced. Further studies are needed to
identify membrane bound mucin on the tissue surface to indicate whether it
is possible to introduce a layer of mucin.

Chapter 8
Perspectives
In this thesis several questions and challenges arised during the study of
the eﬀect of mucus sources on caco-2 cells. Some of them have been solved
but there are still many aspects which could be interesting to look further at.
It is still a mystery on how the interaction of mucin and caco-2 cells oc-
curs. It would therefore be interesting to do more incubation experiments
where the caco-2 cells are incubated with mucus at various time intervals.
Followed by this the cells could be stained with alcian blue to observe if
there are any attachment of mucin to the caco-2 cell surface. Identiﬁcation
of epithelial bound mucin present on the caco-2 cell surface would help opti-
mization of the in vitro model.
Another interesting aspect could be the addition of mucus promoting pro-
teins to the caco-2 cell layer. By adding these proteins, one could assume
that the mucin ﬁbers attach to the caco-2 cell monolayer.
Generally more studies are needed for the application of mucus sources to
the cell surface. The goal would be to seek out for a thin and homogenous
mucus layer. A thin layer of mucus requires a low volume. It would therefore
also be interesting to examine the aﬀect of diﬀerent mucus volumes on caco-2
cells.
Apart from that it would also be interesting to look at the protein and lipid
content of the mucus sources, in order to see how these aﬀect the drug per-
meation.
The study of other human intestinal epithelium cells would also be interesting
to look at such as HT29-MTX cells. HT29-MTX cells are colon cancer cells,
which diﬀerentiate into goblet cells and form a monolayer when cultured on
69
==PAGE:71==
a semi-permeable ﬁlter. The HT29-MTX can produce a non-homogenous
mucus layer.
A combination of caco-2 cells and HT29-MTX cells would also be interesting
to look at. A co-culture containing HT29-MTX cells and caco-2 cells would
represent the heterogeneity of the intestinal epithelium.
