Introduction 
This study combines the department of civil engineering and the department of management engineering at 
DTU, and aims to evaluate the representativeness of current methods used in the assessment of the use-
stage of buildings, specifically pertaining to the building electricity consumption and Danish electrical grid. 
The methods used in this evaluation are based on dynamic modelling practices in the inventory data, in order 
to accommodate high-resolution energy data concerning the electrical grid composition as well as the 
building electricity consumption.  
The aim of the study is to evaluate the implications of using dynamic high-resolution inventory data on the 
environmental performance assessment of buildings, and to make recommendations for further 
development of assessment methods with a higher degree of representativeness. The study is comprised of 
two works; firstly the main article communicating the theory and methods the study is based on as well as 
the major findings and conclusions, and secondly the supporting information containing supplementary 
results and analysis to reinforce the arguments of the article, as well as detailing findings not included in the 
article due to size constraints. The combination of article and supporting information represent the main 
body of work, and serve as the final hand-in of the master thesis. 
The motivation behind this study is rooted in the documented shortcomings of conventional methods in 
practice in building life cycle assessments (LCAs), that are based on static systems models using yearly 
averages and in some cases outdated data. Recent studies have shown that utilization of dynamic system 
modelling in building LCAs provides more accurate portrayal of the impacts induced throughout a buildings 
lifetime, and can significantly alter the findings of the assessment. The chosen focal point of this study is the 
use-stage of buildings, specifically the consumption of electricity, as studies have shown this segment of the 
LCAs to be the most impactful with regards to the total environmental impact during a buildings lifetime. 
To address the inaccuracy and limited representativeness of current non-dynamic practices involved in the 
assessment of the environmental performance of buildings, this study substitutes them with dynamic system 
models constructed from more accurate high-resolution inventory data, capable of accounting for potentially 
impactful trends and variations in the production, distribution, and consumption of electricity as these 
change during the year. Upon completion of the life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) using the various system 
models, any discrepancies between them will become clear and the importance of dynamic high-resolution 
data will be quantified.  
 
==PAGE:4==
Page | 3  
 
The influence of the dynamic practices utilized in this study is highlighted by a variety of comparisons; 
between western and eastern Denmark in order to evaluate the differences in the electrical grids as well as 
overall trends in electricity consumption, as well as a comparison between the different grid resolutions in 
order to determine the ratio between accuracy of data and final findings. Finally the dynamic system models 
are compared to the conventional methods, in order to quantify the variation in impact scores results as they 
change with the chosen method for system modelling.  
The data used in the study is representative of the year 2017 and highly accurate, showing detailed 
compositions of electrical grids as well as quantities of electricity consumption, with a resolution of one 
datapoint every hour. From these data with an hourly resolution additional data resolutions are constructed, 
for every day and month. The purpose of having several iterations of dynamic system models is to compare 
the environmental impact performance between the various resolutions used, in order to assess the 
differences in the results as they are influenced by either increasing or decreasing the degree of resolution 
in the data. 
Certain parts of the data are however not publicly available at the time of the study, and could only be utilized 
through special agreement, specifically the data concerning the electricity consumption of buildings, whereas 
the electrical grid data is freely available. Furthermore the electricity consumption data of buildings in 
Denmark has only been tracked as far back as 2017, as such the data used in this study is the most up-to-
date available, and its inclusion in such a study has been unavailable until 2017. 
Scope 
The national context of the study is Denmark and focuses on the Danish electrical grid, and the buildings 
assessed in the study are all located in Denmark. As such the findings of the study with regards to the 
performances of the buildings are only representative of Denmark. The analysis of the electrical grids and 
the different resolutions observed while based solely on data collected in Denmark, may influence decision 
making contexts beyond the Danish borders. Any nation where the composition of the electrical grid changes 
throughout the year will observe a similar variation in the impacts pr. kWh, and as such the dynamic practices 
and data resolutions can significantly influence the findings of building LCAs. The Danish electricity grid being 
the focal point of the study has the benefit of being comprised of numerous different sources; fossil fuels, 
renewable sources, as well as imported electricity. The variation of the grid throughout the year shows the 
impacts pr. kWh for any given hour, and highlights which energy sources are more detrimental than others, 
or which nation being imported from has the highest environmental impact.

Page | 4  
 
The study focuses exclusively on the production, distribution, and consumption of electricity occurring during 
the use-stage of buildings, and omits the remaining stages of a buildings lifetime; such as material extraction, 
construction, or end-of-life. The decision to focus solely on this aspect of the use-stage was based on two 
factors; firstly the fact that the use-stage is the most impactful stage in terms of the overall environmental 
impact in a buildings lifetime, and secondly that the electricity production and consumption has the highest 
potential for the implementation of dynamic modelling practices, due to the markedly higher degree of 
variation in this stage when compared to the others. As seen in the analysis of the electrical grids the impacts 
pr. kWh change dramatically throughout the year, for every month, day, or even hour. As such the dynamic 
modelling practices greatly influence the impact score results of the use-stage in a given year, exponentially 
so if encompassing the entire lifetime of a building which spans a great number of years. Compared to this 
the other stages of the lifetime are rather rigid, and do not factor into the assessment every year, let alone 
every month/day/hour, given that some of the stages occur only once in a buildings lifetime i.e. construction 
or disposal. 
The focus on the use-stage allows for the assessment of newer buildings as well as older ones, since the 
electricity consumption is continuous and not dependent on decades- or even centuries old data, as would 
be the case for some buildings should construction and maintenance processes of the entire lifetime be 
considered. In this study the ages of the assessed buildings varies from 24 to 311 years, for buildings that old 
much of the inventory data is not available and would have to be assumed, instead the emphasis on the use-
stage allows for the implementation of current data and direct comparison between buildings independently 
of ages. The substantial difference in building ages is due to the selection process of the buildings aiming to 
include buildings of various sizes and ages, to assess whether or not the results of the applied methods in the 
study differ depending on the area and construction year of buildings. 
The buildings selected for the study are exclusively government-owned commercial buildings, distributed 
evenly in eastern and western Denmark. The fact that all buildings are state-owned and commercial in their 
function allows for a fair comparison between them, in so far that they all have similar occupancy and active 
times, which allows for a fair comparison. Furthermore it means that the energy data of all the buildings is 
supplied by the same source; the Danish Building and Property Agency (BYGST), ensuring that all buildings 
are measured on equal terms.

Limitation 
One of the focal points of the study is the comparison between the dynamic high-resolution grids and the 
reference electricity production process in ecoinvent, which differ in a number of significant ways. The 
main shortcomings of the database process is the inability to account for temporal variance, the amount of 
years that the process is outdated compared to the building data, and geographical representativeness of 
certain processes that represent a global production and not for Denmark. 
While the dynamic grids can rectify most of these shortcomings, the source specific unit impacts used to 
calculate the dynamic impact scores pr. kWh are found via electricity production processes found in the 
ecoinvent database, and are as old or in some cases even older than the reference electricity process. 
These processes do not include any changes in the electrical grid infrastructure between their 
establishment and the year assessed in the study (2017), the main difference is as such the general 
difference in the grid composition, and the fact that the dynamic grids account for the variations.

ABSTRACT 
The consumption of electricity in the use-stage of buildings has proven to be a factor considerably influencing 
the environmental performance of buildings. The impacts associated with use of electricity are directly 
related to the electricity grid delivering the power, and hence closely associated with the impacts induced by 
production of each kWh delivered to the grid. Contemporary life cycle inventory (LCI) data sets and hence 
contemporary life cycle assessments (LCAs) typically rely on yearly average electricity grid compositions. LCAs 
in general do not account for the variations in terms of energy sources an electricity grid undergoes every 
day, month and year. LCI data sets do therefore not account for the variations in impacts pr. kWh that follow 
these source variations. This study aims to address the dynamic nature of electricity grids, and account for 
the source variations in electricity production through the use of electricity grid data with a high temporal 
resolution. Our study focuses on the facts that grid compositions cannot be assumed static, and the study 
instead applies actual national electricity grid data. The study compares electricity composition grid inventory 
data with hourly, daily, and monthly resolutions with the conventional yearly average grid compositions as 
applied in e.g. ecoinvent. The high-resolution electricity grid inventory data are subsequently paired with 
building electricity consumption data sets with matching resolutions, in order to quantify the differences in 
terms of environmental performance resulting from the application of high-resolution grid data. Finally, the 
environmental building performance (EBP) calculated using high-resolution grids are compared to EBPs 
derived using conventional data resolutions in order to evaluate the representativeness of contemporary 
inventory data sets. The results indicate that the EBP in terms of climate burden is closely related with the 
data resolution of the grid composition and that the EBP may be overestimated by as much as a factor 2 
when compared with conventional grid inventory data with yearly data resolution.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 ELECTRICITY DEPENDENCY 
Electricity is an essential part of modern society. Our dependency on electricity has its origin in the 
industrialization of the western world, where the large-scale introduction of electricity accelerated the global 
industrialization by providing “easily available, cheap, and reliable energy from non-renewable fossil fuels, 
such as coal, oil, and natural gas” (Eldrup, Mathiesen, 2016) (Mathiesen, Nielsen, & Drysdale, 2015). The fossil 
fuels are still a major component in the global energy mix, with larger shares in emerging and developing 
economies. 
Despite the emergence of sustainable energy sources, the dependency on energy still translates into a 
dependency on fossil fuels, which has proven to be economically and environmentally unsustainable (e.g. 
the energy crisis in 1973 etc.). In the 1970s oil was one of the main sources of most countries’ energy supply. 
For example, app. 95% of the Danish energy in 1973 was produced from imported oil (Eldrup, Mathiesen, 
2016).  
The energy crisis in 1973 left Denmark and other Nordic countries in a situation that forced them to rethink 
the national energy supply. Nordic countries sought to move away not only from being solely reliant upon 
fossil fuels, but also to reduce the dependency on foreign countries’ energy resources. This resulted in the 
ambition to develop renewable domestic energy sources suitable for the political and geographical situation 
of each of the Nordic countries. In Denmark this led to a large-scale implementation of infrastructures for 
the harnessing of wind power, Norway decided for hydro power, while Sweden and Finland decided for 
biomass from their vast forests combined with nuclear power. Today, all these countries still import energy, 
but at a much lower scale than in 1970s, and mainly from neighboring countries. 
In 2016, the United Nations presented 17 global goals to be met by 2030, which are meant to guide us 
towards a sustainable development for both the people of the world, as well as the planet. The 7th goal in 
the list concerns securing the sustainability, affordability, and availability of energy for all people, and 
highlights that humanity must secure that everyone has access to reliable, sustainable, and modern energy 
at a manageable price (Eldrup, Mathiesen, 2016).  
In the current global framework, it is considered insufficient to meet the total energy demand solely by 
replacing non-renewable energy sources with renewable ones (Mathiesen et al., 2015). It is also necessary 
to drastically reduce the global energy consumption, that has been evolving in the misconception that our 
potential for the production is boundless (Brejnrod, Kalbar, Petersen, & Birkved, 2017).

1.2 EBP OPTIMIZATION THROUGH LCA 
LCAs are often used to gain understanding of the environmental impacts associated with the manufacture or 
maintenance of a given service, product or system. It is common to perform an LCA on a building covering 
impacts induced by all building life cycle stages; material extraction, construction, use, maintenance, 
refurbishment, demolition and disposal (Cuéllar-Franca & Azapagic, 2012). 
When considering the total life time of conventional buildings, the use stage carries the brunt of the negative 
environmental impacts (Brejnrod et al., 2017). In EBP assessments of various building types (e.g. residential, 
commercial, agriculture etc.) and construction materials (concrete, brick, etc.), the impacts induced during 
the use stage generally play a predominant role. The use stage of a building may represent as much as 90% 
of the aggregated impacts of buildings (Cuéllar-Franca & Azapagic, 2012)(Maslesa et al., 2017). 
While the use-stage includes maintenance and refurbishments/restorations/replacements of building 
components (Gyllenram, 2015), the main contributor to the overall environmental impacts is the 
consumption of energy during use of the building. Since energy consumption plays such a crucial part in the 
EBP, it is evidently essential that the quantification of the environmental burdens induced by energy 
consumption is as representative as possible. 
Currently, in relation to EBP of buildings, it is generally accepted to apply a static power grid that does not 
vary throughout the lifetime of the modelled building system, combined with yearly estimates for the annual 
average power consumption (Vilches, Garcia-Martinez, & Sanchez-Montañes, 2017). The combination of 
these two parameters allows for quantification of the environmental impacts resulting from the energy 
consumption of the building (Roux, Schalbart, Assoumou, & Peuportier, 2016). 
Considering how important energy consumption is for the EBP, using only static power grids and relying on 
yearly averages may, as illustrated by Sohn et al. (2016), not be the proper way to present the actual 
performance of the building in question. This article therefore studies the LCA application of high-resolution 
dynamic energy data, taking into consideration parameters like variations in the power grid composition, as 
well as the building location, age and size.  
The article at hand aims to quantify the importance of accounting for the changes in the electricity production 
throughout the year. This task is accomplished by quantifying the environmental impacts induced by 
electricity consumption in office buildings with an hourly data resolution accounting for changes in 
consumption and grid composition simultaneously. The purpose is to quantify the differences in the impacts 
pr. kWh consumed throughout the year and combine these time-dependent impacts with actual electricity 
consumption data, to achieve the most accurate EBP assessment of the building use stage as possible.

Page | 9  
 
The energy grid data and building energy consumption data are compared at different resolution scales to 
analyze the differences in the EBPs, so that recommendations can be made as to which method is most 
pertinent. 
Lastly, the high-resolution energy calculations are compared with a standard, static system assessment 
approach, where the EBPs in terms of energy consumptions are calculated using the standard method, and 
the degree to which these results differ is found.
1.3 DYNAMIC LCA 
Dynamic modelling in LCAs is a concept that has emerged over the past few years, with the intent to 
counteract some of the shortcomings inherent in traditional, static LCAs. Where traditional LCA practices are 
more rigid in their structure, a dynamic system models and/or system inventories allows for inclusion of 
parameters that changes throughout the temporal scope of the LCA, in order to more accurately reflect the 
real-life circumstances of the system being assessed (William O. Collinge, Landis, Jones, Schaefer, & Bilec, 
2013). 
Dynamic system modelling often includes temporal variations of unit processes and in some cases this 
consideration leads to results that differ from their static counter parts (Sohn et al. 2016, Beloin-Saint-Pierre 
et al., 2013).  
Dynamic LCAs differ from conventional ones by the inclusion of temporal and spatial variations in the system 
modelling (COLLINGE 2012), just as the temporal considerations have major implications on the accuracy and 
representativeness of the LCA as a whole, so can spatial variations influence the results of an LCA and thus 
influence the conclusions drawn from it. With regards to buildings LCAs, a key aspect of the overarching 
results is occupant behavior, which over the course of a buildings’ long lifespan has great potential to affect 
the EBP (Su, Li, Zhu, & Lin, 2017). 
Naturally, there are limitations to the application of dynamic modelling practices in LCAs. While the temporal 
considerations potentially yield significantly higher accuracy in the impact modelling, they also necessitate 
vast amounts of data. In most cases the data required to perform the LCAs to the highest degree (i.e. 
temporally and spatially dynamic) are not available, limiting representativeness of dynamic LCAs (W O 
Collinge, Landis, Jones, Schaefer, & Bilec, 2012). In the scenarios where the data necessary to perform 
dynamic LCAs is available, these data can alleviate the uncertainties from the static product system models 
that otherwise may arise. 
Even though dynamic LCA practices have already proven to yield informative results, the full reach of 
implementation of dynamism in LCA is not yet known, and the methods are still relatively new. 
The application of dynamic frameworks have the potential to have far reaching implications in the field of 
==PAGE:11==
Page | 10  
 
LCA and are continually being developed further. Recent studies have evaluated the applications of dynamic 
characterization, showing that the different impact categories do not react identically to temporal dynamic 
frameworks (Shimako, Tiruta-Barna, Bisinella de Faria, Ahmadi, & Spérandio, 2018). In the sensitivity analysis 
presented by Shimako et al., it is shown that for both climate and toxicity related impacts, the temporal 
considerations influence the results when compared to static systems. Furthermore, (Shimako et al., 2018) 
show that the time increment (i.e. temporal resolution) chosen has the largest effects on toxicity related 
impacts, while climate change related impacts are not affected by the temporal resolution. This observation 
is assumed to be related to the time scale of the related impacts (i.e. toxicity related impacts having a max 
time scale of months/few years, while climate change related impacts have the time scale of decades/several 
centuries depending on characterization method). 
The implementation of dynamic practices in LCA has, as illustrated above, proven to be of major significance 
for projects where such an assessment approach is feasible data wise. Applying a dynamic framework has 
hence the potential to significantly influence the final results of an LCA. The full implications of the approach 
are however yet unknown, and the practices are continuously being expanded upon. 
1.4 ELECTRICITY GRIDS 
When considering temporal variations within the LCA framework, the main objective is to divide the lifetime 
of the system into (smaller) time steps for which e.g. the supplied materials and energy also can be divided. 
When assessing the energy consumption of a building, this approach provides a more detailed impact 
assessment framework and is an obvious improvement to the conventional static system models, simply due 
to the much finer data resolution and hence variations accounted for. While it is important to account for 
the timeframe of the energy consumption of buildings, it is potentially even more important to account for 
the variation in the electricity grid composition during the same periods. Thus, when constructing an 
inventory for e.g. energy consumption, it is no longer “just” a question of quantifying the energy 
consumption, but also to resolve the energy consumption in time and address when the energy is consumed, 
thereby adding an additional dimension to the inventory analysis. Depending on the national context and 
timeframe in question, the composition of the electricity grid has the potential to fluctuate considerable, and 
the environmental impacts caused by the production can be changing in an unsystematic manner 
(Energinet.dk), meaning that the impacts per kWh consumed also may change in an unsystematic manner. 
 
A national electricity grid is a complex infrastructure, with numerous processes and components to consider 
when assessing the environmental impacts induced by electricity production. The major factors to consider 
are; extraction of materials for the production, emissions following the use of these materials, construction 
==PAGE:12==
Page | 11  
 
and maintenance of grid infrastructure, and the losses caused by transmission and transformation (Gargiulo, 
Girardi, & Temporelli, 2017). All of these factors must be considered when making recommendations for 
changes to the grid, and when combined with the scale of the system and the desire for affordable energy, 
it can be difficult to find the best solution. 
Denmark is in a unique situation when it comes to the global ambitions for the future of renewable energy, 
as the ambition in Denmark has been similar for many years, and as such much of the required infrastructure 
is already in place. However, the Danish power grid does still rely on non-renewable sources as well as import 
of energy from countries relying heavily on non-renewable energy sources. The future plans for a Danish 
power grid however aim to make it completely independent from international power and 100% reliant on 
renewable energy sources. The key component of the 2050 renewable energy grid plan (Mathiesen et al., 
2015) is flexibility of the grid. The goal is to shape the infrastructure to be able to accommodate the short 
and long term shifting nature of the renewable energy sources, in order to fully utilize the energy being 
produced, combined with an overall decrease in total energy demand. 
As such, granting the use stage and hence energy consumption a more prominent role in a building LCAs is 
only logical, and has the potential to play a major part in the development of dynamic framework of building 
LCAs in the future. 
1.5 DYNAMIC HIGH-RESOLUTION DATA 
Dynamic high-resolution data is the combination of dynamic modelling practices with the division of 
traditional data into many smaller components, allowing for an increase in the number of datapoints used to 
portray the system to a much higher degree of detail, and further highlight tendencies that would not be 
visible otherwise. 
With regards to this study, the inclusion of high-resolution data comes into play through the division of a year 
into months, days and hours, instead of a yearly average representing 1 data point aggregating 8,760 hourly 
data points into one single data point or aggregating daily 365 data points into one single data point. 
Depending on the aggregation approach, it is assumed that a simplification reducing the data resolution by 
3-4 orders of magnitude can be regarded as presentative. 
Coupling dynamic inventory analysis with dynamic system modelling, as presented here has the potential to 
substantially increase the representativeness of an LCA, and thus reduce uncertainties otherwise inherent in 
similar assessments, where the changes throughout a year are not accounted for. Especially in relation to 
assessment of the absolute EBP, as presented by e.g. Brejnrod et al. (Brejnrod et al., 2017), the 
representativeness of both the inventory and system model will play an important role in terms of the overall 
validity of the obtained results.

2 METHOD 
2.1 DATA COLLECTION  
The study is based on high-resolution inventory data sets on the energy source composition of Danish 
national electricity grid and building specific electricity consumption data on 18 office buildings located in 
eastern and western Denmark. Both data sets have an hourly resolution covering the entire year of 2017. 
National electricity grid data are provided from Energinet, the Danish national transmission system operator 
for electricity and natural gas (https://www.energidataservice.dk/dataset). These data are further 
supplemented by data from the Danish Energy Agency (https://ens.dk/en), further detailing the data on the 
energy production in Denmark. The exact purpose of supplementary data from the Danish Energy Agency 
was to increase the resolution of the data on local power production as well as central power production by 
applying data enabling the splitting of local and central power productions into their basic sources (biomass, 
biogas, natural gas, etc.). 
The Danish Building and Property Agency (Bygningsstyrelsen or BYGST) provided specific electricity 
consumption data from 18 state-owned office buildings, matching the data format and resolution of the 
electricity grid data.  
The selection of the 18 buildings was based on several parameters: building size, age and location (eastern 
and western Denmark). These parameters were chosen to estimate the variations of the implications of this 
study for buildings with different size, age and location.

REGIONALIZATION OF THE ENERGY DATA 
The data from Energinet are inherently divided into eastern and western Denmark, with the dividing line 
being the Great Belt i.e. the sea between the islands of Zealand and Funen. The reason for this regional split 
is the transference of energy between eastern and western Denmark, where the western part of Denmark 
predominantly delivers energy to the eastern part. 
While the overall import of fuels for electricity production has declined steadily since the 1970s, both eastern 
and western Denmark still import a considerable part of the energy consumed. The eastern part primarily 
imports energy from the other Nordic countries (and to some degree from western Denmark), whereas the 
western part mainly imports energy from neighbors on the European continent i.e. Germany.  
2.2 REFERENCE IMPACT VALUES (OPENLCA & ECOINVENT 3.3) 
The national electricity grid data obtained from Energinet and the Danish Energy Agency divides the energy 
mixture into the following energy sources: oil, coal, waste, solar, offshore wind, onshore wind, natural gas, 
biomass, biogas, exchange with Nordic countries, and exchange with European continent.  
Using the openLCA software (Green Delta, 2018), the Ecoinvent 3.3 inventory database (ecoinvent, 2016) in 
combination with the characterization model ReCiPe ver. 1.11 - hierarchical perspective (RIVM ,2015) the 
reference impacts production of 1 kWh of each energy source was calculated, both for midpoint scores and 
endpoint scores using ReCiPe (H). 
 
Total impacts pr. kWh were calculated using the energy source specific unit impacts (i.e. source specific 
impact per kWh produced) for each of the energy sources in the Danish grid. Combining the energy source 
specific unit impacts with the determined composition of the energy grid, the impact for each hour of the 
calendar year 2017 for each impact category could be calculated by multiplying the relative source 
contributions per hour with the corresponding energy source specific unit impacts.  
Additionally, the loss in energy due to either transformation or transmission were determined from the 
differences in gross and net electricity production. These energy source specific loss factors were later 
multiplied with the electricity consumption of the buildings to improve the precision of the actual power grid 
data. 
The same method was applied to determine the average impact potentials of every kWh per day and month. 
This procedure would later facilitate the quantification of the differences in the overall EBPs, when using 
varying degrees of resolution in the quantification of the energy grid and energy consumption combined 
impacts.  
 
==PAGE:16==
Page | 15  
 
Determining environmental performance from building energy consumption of equal resolution 
The energy consumption induced impacts on midpoint and endpoint level of the individual buildings were 
calculated on an hourly, daily, monthly levels using the existing national grid process for Denmark in the 
ecoinvent database in combination with the measured energy consumptions. The purpose of this calculation 
was to establish data resolution specific reference points allowing for quantification of the building specific 
impact changes by relying on conventional ecoinvent inventory data or actual high-resolution energy 
grid/consumption data. The goal of the comparing the two different ways of quantifying the EBP is not only 
to evaluate if higher energy grid/consumption data resolution can have significant influence on the 
overarching EBP results, but also to determine which degree of data resolution is most pertinent.

Apart from August and September, the general trend in the west follows an increase in induced impact pr. 
kWh in months where the electricity demand is high. This is caused by the current limitations in Danish 
capacity to produce energy from less impacting sources.  
The production of electricity from renewables in Denmark – mainly wind and solar power – currently has 
three main disadvantages: Firstly, the amount of energy produced by these sources is highly dependent on 
weather conditions - if no wind is blowing, or if it is cloudy, the production is limited. Secondly, we do not yet 
have the capability to meet the total energy demand during peak demand intervals and are therefore 
dependent on secondary (adjustable) energy sources during these times. Lastly, we have not yet developed 
efficient ways to store excess energy harnessed from renewable sources, and any such over production of 
electricity is wasted when produced in times of low demand. These factors mean that renewable energy 
sources are somewhat non-reliable at their current state. 
Figure 2 shows the contribution from each electricity source to national production of electricity, for the 
months June and December for both eastern and western Denmark. In all four cases the local power 
production serves as a baseline that does not vary greatly on a daily basis, while other sources, such as wind, 
displayed in green and purple, fluctuate notably. For periods where wind production is low, electricity 
production is supplemented primarily by the import of energy from other countries, or via central power 
plants. 
The difference in electricity produced from central power plants and electric boilers is large and relies 
primarily on the burning of coal and biomass. Reliance on these sources is on average 778% (central power 
plants) and 406% (electric boilers) higher in December. The substantial disparity between these two months 
is caused by the combination of higher energy demand during the winter months, coupled with the lower 
potential for renewable energy during this season.

3.3 RESULTS FROM COMBINING ENERGY CONSUMPTION AND GRID COMPOSITION 
After collecting the energy grid data and building specific electricity consumption data, the data is combined 
to determine the actual environmental performance for the electricity consumption in 17 office buildings. 
The analysis has two targets: to compare results on the 17 buildings obtained using the high-resolution data, 
and to quantify any differences between these results and those using conventional averaged data found in 
ecoinvent.  
1. The comparison is made using both midpoint- and endpoint scores. Five midpoint categories are 
highlighted in this article: Marine eutrophication, Terrestrial acidification, Photochemical oxidant 
formation, Climate Change, and Ozone depletion. All midpoint scores are presented in Appendix A of 
the supporting information section. The final comparison between different data resolutions includes all 
midpoint- and endpoint categories, displayed in section 4.4.3 of the supporting information. 
Hourly data resolution 
The hourly power grid composition and electricity consumption data have the highest resolution and are as 
such considered the most accurate. However, assessments at this data resolution level require significantly 
more data than the daily or monthly systems. Figures 6-8 in section 4.1 of the supporting information detail 
==PAGE:26==
Page | 25  
 
the hourly average distribution of both electricity consumption and impact scores pr. kWh. They show the 
progression of electricity consumption and production throughout a day based on the yearly total data. 
Data with an hourly resolution facilitates the inclusion of the small-scale deviations that occur during a day, 
such as the significant grid changes in terms of energy sources from day to night, or variations in energy 
demand between working- and non-working hours while also encompassing the large-scale changes that the 
electricity grid undergoes throughout the day and month. 
The months with the highest electricity demand are October, November, and January. In general, the

3.3.1 
Daily data resolution 
By using a daily data resolution, some insight is lost by disregarding hourly variations in the energy grid 
throughout the day, and instead assuming every hour of the day to have a uniform grid composition. On the 
other hand, the daily data resolution accounts for the variance between weekdays, which is significant for 
office buildings since the difference between working days and weekends is considerable as the electricity 
over the weekend is mainly used on “background” systems. 
 
Figure 7: Daily variations in electricity demand and environmental impacts induced by electricity production 
for March month and building W1. Left: electricity demand, Right: Daily deviation in total impact scores across 
all categories, compared to the monthly average. 
 
Figure 7 shows daily variations in electricity demand and environmental impacts. The figure shows substantial 
decrease in energy demand on every weekend or holiday. The graph also reveals an electricity consumption 
baseline for W1 representing the minimum energy consumption during days of inactivity. The average daily 
energy demand (i.e. baseline) during weekends or holidays is about 1/3 of the average of working days, with 
the weekday variations highlighted in the daily resolution section 4.2 of the supporting information. The total 
average daily deviations in electricity consumption are found in the daily grid analysis section 4.4 in the 
supporting information. 
==PAGE:28==
Page | 27  
 
The noticeable distinction between weekend and working days in the daily data resolution coupled with 
energy grid data of corresponding resolution may prove to be an effective data-resolution compromise. The 
observed trends are apparent for all buildings: all they have substantially higher electricity usage during the 
weekdays, followed by the drops over the weekend and on holidays. 
Figure 7 also shows the average daily deviations in induced impacts pr. kWh consumed compared to the 
average within the same period as the electricity consumption displayed on the left. The impacts induced pr. 
kWh do not follow the same trend as the consumption. While there are declines in the average induced 
impact pr. kWh throughout a week, consumed electricity and observable impacts peak during the working 
days, for the induced impacts it is not an as easy recognizable pattern as observed for the consumption data.  
Although the daily data resolution is not as detailed as the hourly resolution, the fact that there are significant 
differences between the individual days, both in terms of consumption and induced impacts, highlights that 
a daily data resolution still contains factors not observable applying other lower data resolutions. 
With a daily data resolution, the average impact scores are again higher for the buildings in western Denmark 
- on average 34% on both midpoint and endpoint level. 
3.3.2 
Monthly data resolution 
The monthly data resolution addresses changes occurring over the scale of one month and can hence still 
reflect the aggregated differences throughout the year, but on a monthly basis. 
The electricity usage results on a monthly data resolution basis are similar to the impacts obtained applying 
hourly and daily data resolutions. The results obtained using a monthly data resolution show once again that 
the electricity consumption of the western buildings induces larger impacts than the consumption of the 
eastern buildings. The results for western buildings are 35% higher on midpoint level and 32% higher on 
endpoint level. There is obviously a direct correlation between the electricity demand and the induced 
impacts since the western buildings on average perform worse than their eastern counterparts. Since data 
regarding specific building function and occupancy rate were not available at the time of this study, 
identification of the cause of this energy efficiency pattern is not possible. 
3.3.3 
Reference results 
The reference results show the environmental performance of buildings calculated using the reference 
process in the Ecoinvent 3.3 database. 
This system is non-dynamic as it does not take into account temporal variance of the grid- and electricity 
consumption data. This method assumes equal impact results for every kWh produced throughout the year 
and generally does not consider the variation in the building electricity consumption through different time 
intervals (hourly/daily/monthly). 
==PAGE:29==
Page | 28  
 
Since the reference impact scores pr. kWh for the basic comparison are the same all year long, the differences 
in impact results between eastern and western buildings are directly correlated to the energy results pr. m2, 
i.e. 33,4% higher on average for western buildings. 
3.3.4 
Comparison 
The differences in impact results pr. m2 between eastern and western buildings change only to a minor 
degree when changing the energy data resolution and base primarily on the consumption/m2 of each 
building. 
The value of the various data resolutions only becomes apparent when comparing the individual buildings 
and the variation in impact results stemming from the different data resolutions. 
Since the energy data resolutions change in the comparisons, while the building size, location, and total 
energy consumption remain constant, any deviation in the environmental impact scores are solely results 
from the changes in data resolution.

4 DISCUSSION 
4.1 REFERENCE DATA RESOLUTION 
One of the focus points of this study is the comparison between the impact induced pr. kWh consumed, when 
calculated relying on the reference process found in the Ecoinvent database, and when found from measured 
electricity production data, as provided by Energinet for electricity and natural gas. 
The two ways of calculating the energy consumption induced impacts differ in two major ways; firstly, in 
the energy source covered by the reference data set that are absent when calculating the impacts from 
observed and measured data, and secondly, in the temporal dynamics of the data. 
While the major contributing electricity process are the same used in this study and in the database process, 
the energy production process found in the ecoinvent database: market for electricity, low voltage | DK, 
includes several inputs that are not present in the observed electricity production in 2017, such as hydro 
power, or the inclusion of the transmission network. This is a major source of disparity between the two 
calculation methods, without being clear as to which is the most accurate. 
The second major difference is the timeframe that the two methods cover. The ecoinvent reference database 
process from openLCA is more than 6 years old (from 2012) at the time of our study, and hence 5 years older 
than the energy data used in the study (from 2017). Considering the ways in which the Danish energy grid 
has developed in the past 6 years, shifting the focus towards more sustainable electricity production, the age 
of database process from openLCA justifies it causing the impact scores to be higher. 
Furthermore, the reference process for electricity production does not take into account changes in the 
electricity grid that occur throughout the year. As the calculations from the energy grid indicate, there are 
notable changes in the impacts pr. kWh consumed depending on the time of year the electricity is produced, 
and the lack of temporal dynamics in the reference process is a major source of inaccuracy. 
4.2 BUILDING ENERGY DATA COMPLEXITY 
From the building energy consumption data analysis it has become apparent that the key component, when 
comparing the environmental performance of the buildings, is the performance indicator “energy 
consumption/building area”. The difference in their impact scores directly correlates with the difference in 
energy consumption/building area. 
A more accurate comparison between the buildings would require more complex data regarding the 
buildings, with regards to their function and occupancy rate. It is clear that the function of each building 
==PAGE:36==
Page | 35  
 
varies, as observed from the daily electricity consumption data where some buildings have a clear reduction 
during holidays, and others do not. 
Other factors that would play a major role in the comparison are data regarding number of occupants, 
working hours, activities taking place outside the regular business hours, and so forth. These are all factors 
that contribute to the energy demand of the building, but are not reflected when looking solely at the gross 
energy consumption. Should such data become more available for future studies, more suitable comparisons 
for the environmental performance of the buildings could be possible, especially since some trends in 
comparisons, such as building age – as used in this study – proved to be inconclusive. Such a comparison 
might be environmental performance pr. occupant of a given building at any given time, which is not 
identifiable from the area of a building. Or a comparison of the performance during- and outside of business 
hours, to determine which buildings could be optimized with regards to their baseline operational energy 
consumption. 
5 CONCLUSION 
The Danish electricity grid composition comprises numerous sources whose relative contributions to the sum 
change over time. Assessing this dynamic development in the study has proven to have a great influence on 
the impact scores from the electricity production, as well as the environmental performance of the buildings 
supplied by this grid. 
The energy grid analysis shows that the data resolution (month/day/hour) for electricity production is 
important, and directly influences the environmental impacts resulting from the electricity production. 
The results show that 1 kWh electricity produced in January has in some scenarios almost the impact factor 
2 of the same amount of energy produced in June. 
Both the midpoint and endpoint results show that there are important decisions to be made when calculating 
the impacts of a buildings electricity consumption. The study further shows that even without changing the 
building area or total electricity demand, the resolution of the data changes the overall results. 
The various data resolutions used resulted in different impact score results. However, the differences 
between the results were not always substantial enough to warrant the increased data volume and time 
required to perform the analysis. For the midpoint calculations, the daily resolution provided results very 
similar to those of the hourly resolution, and given the fact that the hourly data resolution requires 24 times 
more data than the daily data, the daily resolution would be the most reasonable solution. The endpoint 
scores proved to be another story, where every increment in data resolution provided significant changes in 
results, and as such the choice of resolution will impact the results for any increment used. 
==PAGE:37==
Page | 36  
 
In almost all scenarios, the impact scores calculated from the collected electricity production data differ 
greatly from the impact scores calculated from the electricity production process from the Ecoinvent 
database. The cases where the difference is in excess of 100% are most likely due to methodological 
differences and caused by certain processes or inputs not being present in both systems. 
However, the applied reference process is five years older than the research data used in the study, while 
Danish electricity grid data shows that the energy composition has changed greatly in the same timeframe. 
The results show substantial deviation across all impact categories between the different electricity grids 
used, while methodological choices may to a degree influence this deviation. The analysis of the yearly 
development of the Danish electric grid show substantial changes in the composition of the grid, which the 
reference process does not account for. 
The analysis of the progression of the electric grid composition throughout the year shows that the 
renewable energy sources dramatically reduce the environmental impact pr. kWh, but that they are still 
unreliable in their availability. The system is very much reactive based on this availability, substituting the 
grid composition with import from neighboring countries when needed. Until the technology and 
infrastructure is in place to more efficiently harness the renewable energy, and properly store it when a 
surplus is produced, the grid will likely have to remain reactive, and keep relying on less favorable means for 
electricity production. 
The high-resolution data on the building energy consumption is not publicly available at the time of this study 
and was provided through special agreement, which may not be possible for all projects or studies currently. 
The electrical grid data was however freely available and the implications from the grid analysis could 
influence established methods for the assessment of buildings, through the optimization of reference 
processes used in LCAs. The analysis shows distinct differences in the impacts stemming from the electrical 
grid depending on the month/day/hour that the production occurs, as such a static power grid will always be 
too inaccurate as it does not account for these variations. Given the results of this study, a dynamic modelling 
approach would always be recommended, but in cases where sufficient quantities or quality of data is not 
assured, a compromise could be achieved through the establishment of building-type specific electrical grid 
processes in the databases. These processes could be representative of different building functions such as 
residential, commercial, or otherwise, and correspond to a grid that more accurately portrays the impacts of 
the grid during the average times the buildings in question are active, such as standard business hours for 
commercial buildings, or the opposite for residential buildings. 
While such processes would potentially be an improvement over the outdated processes currently in use, 
and more accurately represent the composition of the electrical grid as it produces the energy consumed, 
the dynamic high-resolution grids are significantly more accurate and have proven to be impactful on the 
==PAGE:38==
Page | 37  
 
environmental performance during the use-stage of buildings. The future development of dynamic electrical 
grid modelling practices could provide real-time assessment of the building energy performance, if the data 
should become available in real-time, allowing buildings to be reactive with regards to their electricity 
consumption, by allocating more unessential electricity consuming processes to times where the grid 
composition is more favorable. Currently, the validated electricity balance data in Denmark is up to 9 days 
behind real-time, while the non-validated hourly resolution data however is only delayed by two hours. When 
these consumption data streams become available in real-time, they could have direct influence on the use-
stage of buildings.

INTRODUCTION 
 
This report is the second component of the two-part study on the effects of high-resolution energy data on 
building life cycle assessments (LCAs). The first part being the article serving as the main platform for the 
communication of the conclusions of the study, and this report serving as supporting information as well as 
a means to present any results and analysis not included in the article. 
The focus of the report is twofold; firstly to present additional information strengthening the findings of the 
article, by showing data, graphs, and tables not included in the article due to size limitations. And secondly 
to present conclusions that pertain to the variations in the buildings, and shifts the focus more from the 
electrical grid data analysis to the environmental building performances (EBP) as it shifts with building size 
and age. 
The overarching purpose of the study is to examine the effects of utilizing dynamic high-resolution 
electricity data, matched with building electricity consumption data of equal resolutions. The study 
incorporates dynamic data showing variations in the electrical grid over time, as it changes by the hour 
throughout the year 2017 in Denmark, split into eastern and western sections. This electrical grid data is 
then coupled with data concerning the electricity consumption of building supplied by the grid, also with a 
resolution of one datapoint pr. hour. The buildings chosen for the study are equally distributed in east and 
west, with nine buildings in each section for a total of 18, within each sector (east/west) the buildings are 
selected to represent differences in age and area, in order to determine if these factors change the nature 
of the findings. 
The aim of the study is to determine how representative current building LCA practices are, and if high-
resolution data coupled with dynamic modelling practices provides results that differ in a meaningful way 
compared to current standard practices. This aim can be represented as a number of research questions:  
1. How does the electrical grid, and the impacts associated with it, change throughout the year, and 
to what degree? 
2. Are there major differences between eastern and western Denmark?  
3. How does the electrical consumption of buildings vary over the course of a year? 
4. What is the impact of age and/or area on the energy performance of a building? 
5. How do the impact score results differ between various dynamic data resolutions? 
6. What is the difference in results from a dynamic high-resolution system to the standard reference 
system? 
These are factors not included in the static modelling process of EBP, it is through the answering of these 
questions that the study aims to assess the potential influence of these factors on building LCAs as a whole.

ELECTRIC GRID RESULTS 
The results follow the same procedure as the article, with the initial results covering the data from the 
measured electrical production of the power grid, in order to determine if there are significant changes in 
the impact scores pr. kWh throughout the year, and if so to quantify these. 
The results for the eastern electrical grid are not entirely similar to the western grid, while the overall trend 
is the same with the scores in general being lower during the summer, the period in which the results are 
significantly lower is longer than it is in the west. In the east the average impact pr. kWh is markedly lower 
from May to September, while this reduction in the west is limited to June and July. This is caused by two 
factors, firstly the fact that the grids are inherently different in their composition, secondly they differ in 
scale. West Denmark produces on average 75% more electricity than the east, and in some months up to 
twice the amount.  Figure 1 shows the total electricity produced in east and west Denmark, outlines the 
difference on a monthly basis, and shows that the western electrical grid supplies between 49-101% more 
electricity than the east. 
This difference in scale has great effect on the overall impact scores contributed to the electricity 
production, given the fact that the composition of the sources supplying the electricity varies as the 
demand scales up. As outlined in the article, the total electricity potential supplied from renewable sources 
within the current electrical infrastructure is limited, and any amount of electricity produced in excess of 
what the renewable sources are capable of will be supplied from import or non-renewable sources.

2.3 
ELECTRICAL GRID COMPOSITIONS 
In order for the comparison between the dynamic high-resolution electrical grids to the static reference 
grids from the database to be fair, it is important to consider the composition of both grids, in order to 
ascertain if the systems are similar enough to justify the comparison, as outlined in ISO 14044:  
“Systems shall be compared using the same functional unit and equivalent methodological considerations 
such as performance, system boundaries, data quality, allocation procedures, decision rules on evaluating 
inputs, and outputs and impact assessment. Any differences between systems regarding these parameters 
shall be identified and reported. If the study is intended to be used for a comparative assertion intended to 
be disclosed to the public, interested parties shall conduct this evaluation as a critical review” (ISO 14044, 
2006) 
In the context of this study the most critical part is the data quality, and as such it is critical to account for 
the processes included in either type of electrical grid composition, to ensure that both are representative 
of the actual grid, and any comparison between the two is accurate. 
2.3.1 
Dynamic high-resolution grid 
The dynamic high-resolution grids are determined using various resolutions, for all resolutions the basis 
reference impact scores pr. kWh for each source are unchanged, and are found using processes in the 
Ecoinvent database. 
Figure 5: Electricity demand pr. m2 for all buildings, divided into age brackets 
==PAGE:54==
 
Page 53 
For each energy source the environmental impacts caused by the production of 1 kWh are found, using 
existing processes in the Ecoinvent database, as they detail the production of electricity for Denmark. Once 
the reference impact score values are found for each energy source contributing to the electrical grid, the 
percentage of each source at any given hour is found, and the total environmental impact for a kWh at the 
chosen time step is determined by summarizing the relative contribution of each source for all impact 
categories. 
2.3.2 
Static reference grid 
The static reference grid is calculated using the process market for electricity, low voltage | electricity, low 
voltage | APOS, S. This process is comprised of a number of other processes, minor contributions from the 
distribution network, solar panels, and sulfur hexafluoride. The main component links to a process for the 
transformation from medium to low voltage, which mainly includes the process for medium voltage 
electricity production, which through the same procedure ends up linking to the production of high-voltage 
electricity in Denmark. Along the way from low to high voltage there are transmission and transformation 
losses, which means in order for the grid to supply 1 kWh of low voltage electricity, more than 1 kWh of 
high voltage electricity has to be produced. 
The process market for electricity, high voltage is comprised of a number of processes each detailing the 
electricity production from a given energy source.  The processes contributing to the production of the high 
voltage electricity are the same processes used to construct the high-resolution grid, with the exception of 
the processes concerning the transmission network construction, which are not included in the dynamic 
grids. These are however minor contributions of 6.58E-9 km and 3.17E-10 km respectively. And the 
contributions to the impact scores from the transmission network constructions pr. kWh are on average 
below 1%, with exception for natural land transformation to which they contribute 4%. 
The major components are as such the same in the two methods for determining the electrical grid, and 
the only variation being the amounts that each source contributes to the production at any given time. As 
well as the fact that the losses due to transmission and transformation are calculated according to the 
dynamic data resolution, based on the Gross and Net production, and not from static reference processes 
as with the reference system. As such the loss factor in the electricity production follows the same dynamic 
practices used on the remainder of the grid, and is calculated by the hour/day/month based on the 
resolution. The loss factor is then multiplied with the given electricity consumption, thereby representing 
the losses caused by transformation and transmission, this loss factor varies between 2-6% based on the 
electrical grid.

Table 14 shows the final midpoint comparison between the dynamic hourly grid resolution and the static 
reference system. The static system is calculated using the reference process for electricity production, and 
as such does not vary throughout the year and any kWh produced during the year is assumed to be equally 
impactful. Since the impact scores pr. kWh do not change, changing the building data resolution becomes 
redundant, and is instead calculated using the yearly total. 
The differences in the impact score results are substantial, and are higher for all but one category; 
agricultural land occupation. For the categories where the results are higher, the difference ranges 
between 26,8% and all the way up to 687,1%. The extreme categories are ionizing radiation, freshwater 
ecotoxicity, marine ecotoxicity, and water depletion, these are the categories where the differences exceed 
200%. The largest contributors to these categories are; waste, biogas, biogas, and electricity 
transformation, respectively.  
The substantial differences are caused by a combination of the simplification of the data – and by extension 
the dynamic modelling practices, the inclusion of transformation processes – that for the dynamic grids are 
calculated from the Gross and Net production, and lastly the temporal differences of the grids – where the 
dynamic grids are from 2017 while the reference grid is from 2012.
