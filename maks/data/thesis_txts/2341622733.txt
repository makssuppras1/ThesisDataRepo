Abstract 
Moldex3D with Boundary Layer Meshing (BLM) mode was adopted in this project to 
simulate the injection molding process of a hearing aid shell made of Polybutylene 
Terephthalate (PBT) with 30% glass fiber filled and predict the part warpage. Injection 
molding experiment was conducted to validate the prediction from Moldex3D. 
Injection time, injection pressure, pressure loss and warpage were treated as the main 
comparison criteria. Different parameters setting in Moldex3D were investigated to 
research their influence on the accuracy of simulation. Results showed that the injection 
molding process prediction from the simulation was relatively precise by considering 
the nozzle geometry and pressure effect on the viscosity in the simulation model and 
determine a proper heat transfer coefficient (HTC) value. But good agreement was not 
observed between the warpage of the experiment molded part and simulated part. 
Warpage were dominated by the fiber orientation but Moldex3D underestimated the 
fiber orientation effect in this project . Predicted warpage was found to be extremely 
dependent on the filling HTC and the fiber orientation model used in Moldex3D, both 
of which had significant influence on the fiber orientation.  
  
Key words: Moldex3D, injection molding process, injection time, injection pressure, 
pressure loss, warpage, fiber orientation effect

1.  Introduction to the project 
1.1.   Background  
This project was run in collaboration with Widex A/S and the Centre for Acoustic-
Mechanical Micro Systems (CAMM) and the Department of Mechanical Engineering 
(MEK-MPP) at DTU. 
 
Typical hearing aids produced by Widex A/S usually have small dimension, as shown 
in figure 1-1. The parts of the hearing aid are held together in a protective case made of 
plastic, which consists of the upper and lower shells and are assembled together at the 
final stage.  
 
Figure 1-1  Typical model of hearing aid from Widex A/S 
 
The outer shells of hearing aids are thin walled structures which call for a highly 
sophisticated molds and a lot process optimizations by trial and errors to make the 
successful parts. In these cases, the development time and expense of the hearing aid 
will be increased significantly. 
 
To reduce the time and cost for developing a new hearing aid, Widex A/S has been 
using Moldex3D, a Computer Aided Engineering (CAE) software which can simulate 
the process of injection molding, for many years to optimize the part and mold design 
and injection molding processing parameters in advance. With the advances on the 
process simulation, it is highly likely that a lot of efforts from the mold makers and 
many trial and errors steps can be reduced or even eliminated.  
 
But Widex A/S currently has the problem that Moldex3D can’t always give the accurate 
prediction, especially the warpage prediction, which just limits the application of the 
CAE simulation in Widex A/S. But as the accuracy of the simulated results is not only 
highly dependent on the capacity of the simulation software itself, but also on the users’ 
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
knowledge about the software and the injection molding process, it is possible to 
improve the accuracy of the simulation prediction with proper parameters settings in 
Moldex3D. So this project was set with the aim of validating and improving the 
prediction accuracy of Moldex3D for Widex A/S. 
 
In this project, injection molding experiment validation of the Moldex3D simulation 
was conducted for the upper shell of a hearing aid with geometry as shown in figure 1-
2. The test part is designed with a typical hearing aid shell in mind, but with all the 
micro features stripped down, to simplify the process and the numerical computation. 
The material used for the hearing aid shell was Polybutylene terephthalate (PBT), a 
thermoplastic filled with 30% glass fibers. Injection time, injection pressure, pressure 
loss in the cavity and the warpage of the part were decided as the main criteria for the 
comparison between experiment and simulation results. Different parameters setting in 
Moldex3D were investigated to check their influence on the accuracy of simulations. 
 
Figure 1-2 Simplified upper hearing aid shell investigated in this project

2.  Introduction to injection molding and 
simulation software 
2.1.   Injection molding 
Among a variety of polymer processing methods, injection molding is one of the most 
widely used processes for manufacturing plastic products because of the superior 
manufacturability and the high degree of freedom for the geometry of plastic products.  
2.1.1.   Injection molding machine 
A typical injection molding machine consists of the five major components: injection 
system, hydraulic system, clamping system, control system and mold system. Figure 2-
1 is a schematic illustration of the injection system of an injection molding machine, 
including screw, barrel, nozzle and heating bands.   
 
Figure 2-1 Injection system of a single screw injection molding machine for thermoplastics[1] 
2.1.2.   Injection molding process cycle 
Injection molding is a cyclic process. The sequence of the different stages of an 
injection molding cycle, including filling, packing, cooling, ejection and mold closing, 
are shown in figure 2-2 (a) to (f). 
 
(a) Mold closing 
Cycle starts when the mold closes and the screw begins moving forward for injection. 
==PAGE:14==
5 
 
 
(b) Filling 
As reciprocating screw moves forward, plastic melt starts filling the mold by high pressure. The filling 
phase stops when the cavity is just fully filled by volume. 
 
(c) Packing/holding 
The cavity is packed as the screw continuously moves forward. During this phase, more material is 
pushed into the cavity to compensate for the volumetric shrinkage of the frozen plastic.  
 
(d) Cooling and plasticization 
The cavity cools as the gate freezes off and the screw begins to retract to plasticize material for the next 
shot. 
 
(e) Mold open and part ejection 
When the part reaches the ejection temperature, the mold opens for part ejection. 
 
(f) Mold closing for next cycle 
The mold closes and the next cycle begins. 
Figure 2-2 Different phases of an injection molding cycle[1] 
 
To further explain the machine motion within the injection molding process cycle, 
figure 2-3 shows the cycle time and its components. 
==PAGE:15==
6 
 
 
Figure 2-3 Typical injection molding process cycle time and its components 
 
The part cooling time marked in green is actually the remaining cooling time, which 
plus the time of pack/hold marked in orange equals to the real cooling time. 
2.1.3.   Injection machine pressure and typical cavity pressure profile 
Figure 4 shows typical hydraulic pressure profile and corresponding cavity pressure 
curve over the injection molding cycle[2]. By the end of filling phase, hydraulic 
pressure reaches a peak point[3] and cavity pressure increases sharply, both of which 
are marked by red circle in figure 2-4. 
 
Figure 2-4  Typical injection molding cycle with (1) filling, (2) packing/holding and cooling, (3) mold-
opening, and (4) packing and holding phase. 
 
The period (4) in figure 2-4 can be divided into two parts: 
 
The packing (compression) phase: This phase happens from the end of filling until 
the moment when cavity pressure reaches the peak point. During this phase, 
additional plastic melt is further injected into the cavity to compensate for the 
==PAGE:16==
7 
 
shrinkage of plastics which starts to cool down and contract. 
 
 
The holding phase: Once the required amount of plastic is injected into the cavity, 
it must be held in there until the gate freezes off, which is done during holding 
phase. So the time span of stage (4) minus period (5) in figure 4 is the real holding 
phase.  
 
In most cases, molders do not differentiate between the packing phase and the holding 
phase and will typically have only one pressure setting and one time setting for both 
phases. They call this collective phase as the holding stage. 
 
V/P switch over happens before the end of filling[4], indicated by the green circle marks 
in figure 2-4. 
2.2.   Injection molding process simulation 
2.2.1.   Definition and advantages of injection molding process 
simulation 
Injection molding process simulation is conducted by numerically computing the 
solution of governing equations with boundary conditions, which now are getting more 
and more application in the injection molding industry because of the following 
benefits[5]: 
 
Process simulation allows the mold/part designer to make critical decisions about 
the design before the injection mold is manufactured to avoid potential errors. As 
re-engineering after the mold is already made can take a lot of time and cost much 
money, the process simulation in advance can shorten the development time and 
time to market for a new product and decrease the expense of design change and 
thus the cost of the product. 
 
 
It will also help select the specific material that will work with the given part and 
mold design. 
 
 
Processing parameter settings can be optimized with the simulation and thus 
decreasing the need for injection molding experiments. 
 
 
Final part quality can be predicted with process simulation as well, like the warpage, 
weld line, mechanical properties and so on.

2.2.2.   Injection molding process simulation software 
2.2.2.1. Commercially available simulation software 
Currently, there are several software packages commercially available on the market 
for injection molding process simulation. The most common ones are: 
 
Moldflow, developed by Autodesk, Inc. 
 
 
Moldex3D, developed by CoreTech System Co., Ltd. 
 
 
Sigmasoft, developed by SIGMA Engineering, Inc. 
 
In this project, Moldex3D R13 with pre-processing module—Designer Boundary Layer 
Mesh (BLM) were used for the simulation work. 
2.2.2.2. Meshing in simulation software 
In the numerical computation procedure of process simulation, the model is divided 
into many small elements with finite size, which can also be called meshing. The 
accuracy of the computation is partially dependent on the way of the modeling division 
and the size of the mesh. Besides the meshing process, the governing equations are 
discretized into a set of algebraic equations at the same time. Possibly nonlinear, and 
computational algorithms are developed to solve these equations[6]. Together with the 
corresponding boundary conditions, the discretized partial differential equations can be 
solved in splitting small elements, generating the numerical approximate solution for 
the continuous equations of the entire model[7]. Then, simulation software can 
calculate the plastic flow behavior, temperature and pressure development and so on. 
Different discretization processes and different solution algorithms form many 
numerical methods, e.g. finite difference method (FDM) and finite element method 
(FEM). Among several numerical methods, the finite element method (FEM) is a robust 
and thoroughly developed method[6], which is employed in mainstream process 
simulation software of injection molding and is also the numerical method for 
Moldex3D. 
 
Moldex3D Mesh supports various mesh types, including 2D triangular and 
quadrilateral, 3D tetrahedral, prismatic, hexagonal, voxel (brick) and pyramid meshes. 
In this project, the meshing mode of Moldex3D is BLM, which is the most powerful 
one for complex geometries. The BLM generator allows users to firstly generate inward 
one or two layers of prismatic meshes out of the triangular surface mesh of the object, 
followed by filling up the internal space with quality-controlled tetrahedral mesh, as 
shown in figure 2-5. With this unique BLM, finite element analysis (FEA) solvers can 
capture the variable distribution, such as temperature, more accurately with much less 
number of elements than the isotropic tetrahedral mesh. Moreover, the BLM model also 
==PAGE:18==
9 
 
benefits the FEA solvers by much more quality mesh than the multilayer anisotropic 
tetrahedral ones[8]. 
 
Figure 2-5  An example of meshing with Design BLM mode in Moldex3D

software

of the injection molding process. 
 
Thermodynamic relationships 
Thermodynamic information of materials is important for the computer simulation of 
injection molding, which can be reflected by Pressure-Volume-Temperature (PVT) data 
of polymers. As shown in figure 2-6, thermoplastics generally undergoes volumetric 
change over temperature and pressure[9]. Hence it’s essential to characterize PVT 
relationship in order to calculate the compressibility of material during packing phase, 
and its final part shrinkage and warpage after ejection.  
 
Different types of thermoplastic have different PVT behaviors across its transition 
temperature [10]. As shown in figure 2-7, the specific volume of semi-crystalline 
thermoplastic changes significantly and abruptly while amorphous thermoplastic only 
has change in slope in its specific volume-temperature curve.

independent of shear rate. Pure liquids with low molecular mass are good examples of 
Newtonian fluids, which usually have constant viscosity at constant temperature. 
 
However, as non-Newtonian fluids, the viscosity of polymers is a function of shear rate, 
as shown in figure 2-8 [9]. At Upper Newtonian Region where shear rate is low, the 
viscosity is a nearly constant. Polymer chains are more uniformly aligned as the shear 
rate increases, so the viscosity decreases accordingly. This is called the Shear Thinning 
Region. At Lower Newtonian Region, all polymeric chains are fully aligned and then 
the shear viscosity becomes almost insensitive to shear rate[10].

5.2.   Influence of pressure dependent viscosity 
This section employs 4 different pressure dependent viscosity parameter D to research 
its influence on the injection time, pressure and warpage. 
5.2.1.   Introduction to pressure dependent viscosity 
According to the modified Cross model mentioned in chapter 2, the viscosity of 
polymer melts is influenced by shear rate, temperature and pressure. Although its 
dependence on shear rate as well as on temperature has been thoroughly described and 
considered in formulations and predictions of the flow behavior [13][14], pressure 
effects, however, are usually an overlooked aspect of the flow properties of polymeric 
materials. In the material database of commercial simulation software, the pressure 
dependency of viscosity is commonly neglected. For example, if the Cross-Exp model 
is employed for describing viscosity, the parameter D in equation 2.10 is assumed as 0. 
And for Cross-WFL model, the parameter D3 is considered as 0. However, to avoid 
short shots, injection pressure developed during thin-wall injection molding (TWIM) is 
usually very high, which is sufficient to significantly reduce the amount of free volume 
available to molecules, resulting in an increase in intermolecular interactions and 
viscosity of melts. Therefore, neglecting the increase of viscosity induced by pressure 
in the material database can lead to a huge underestimation of pressure drops in runners 
and cavities during simulation. Actually, the prediction error in cavity pressure from 
CAE simulation for thin-wall injection molding may reach from 50% to more than 
100%, and the error increases as the parts become thinner[15].In return, incomplete 
filling of cavities, burnings and longer processing times can happen in the real injection 
molding experiment but not in the simulation if same processing condition is used.  
 
Anyway, many devices and analysis procedure have been developed to characterize the 
influence of pressure on the rheological behavior polymers by experimental methods 
since 1950s. At the beginning of this subject, Maxwell and Jung[16] used a modified 
capillary rheometer with two barrels and two independently controlled pistons, which 
enabled the melt in the capillary to be pressurized from both sides. Recently, Johanna 
==PAGE:57==
48 
 
Aho, Seppo Syrja ¨la succeeded in evaluating the pressure dependence of viscosity for 
polycarbonate, acrylonitrile-butadiene-styrene, polystyrene, polypropylene, and low-
density polyethylene by using a capillary rheometer equipped with a downstream 
pressure chamber[17]. A paper published by Cardinaels et al.[18], discussed and 
compared a superposition and a direct methods for the calculation of the pressure 
dependency of viscosity from high-pressure capillary rheometery data. 
 
As mentioned above, parameters for describing pressure dependency of viscosity are 
usually considered as 0 in current CAE simulation software in spite of a growing 
acknowledgement to the importance of the pressure effect on the melt viscosity. This is 
perhaps owing to the fact that it is not easy to conduct experimental measurements with 
high-pressure rheometry and different methods for calculating the pressure effect from 
experimental rheometry data can lead to variable results. 
 
On the other hand, besides the direct experimental approaches, indirect calculation 
method by estimating pressure dependency viscosity on the basis of PVT data were 
frequently reported. For instance, the relation between PVT and viscosity of several 
injection grade thermoplastics including polypropylene, poly(methyl methacrylate), 
polyamide, polycarbonate, polystyrene and polystyrene/polycarbonate blends was 
discussed by Mercedes Ferna´ndez et al[19]. They also validated the possibility of 
determining the pressure coefficient of the viscosity of two liquid crystalline cellulose 
derivatives, APC and HPC as well as a thermoplastic/liquid crystalline polymer blends 
(liquid crystalline polymers (LCP’s) and olefinic thermoplastics (TP’s) blends) by 
applying a combined analysis of rheological and PVT data[20].

5.4.1.   Introduction to HTC 
Figure 5-5(a) [24] is a diagram of the heat transfer between plastic melt surface and 
mold during injection molding, which is not perfect because of the difference between 
mold material and plastic as well as the existence of air gap between mold and plastic 
when the melt cools down and starts to shrink. As a result, the mold-melt interface 
temperature (Tmt) is usually higher than the set mold temperature (Tmb), especially in 
the cases with thin-walled part or melt with highly temperature-dependent viscosity, as 
shown in figure 5-5(b) during filling stage. Two terms, namely thermal Contact 
Resistance (TCR) and Heat Transfer Coefficient (HTC) are usually used to describe the 
level of such heat transferring between mold and plastic. They are the inverse of each 
other, and both depend on the material of polymer and mold metal, geometry of the 
molded part and the process conditions as well[10]. HTC is employed in Moldex3D for 
thermal boundary condition, and can be defined by expression analogous to Newton's 
law of cooling: 
                  q = hc (Tmt-Tmb)                                (5.8) 
where q is the heat transfer flux between mold and plastic melt, 
      Tmt is the mold-melt interface temperature, 
      Tmb is the set mold temperature. 
 
When using a higher hc, temperature Tmt will be closer to the mold base temperature 
Tmb, which means a good thermal contact situation and high heat transfer between 
mold and plastic melt. On the contrary, when lower hc is used, the heat transfer is small 
and this a big difference between Tmt and Tmb. 
 
 
Figure 5-5 Heat transfer between mold and polymer melt 
 
A lot of experiment work has been carried out to evaluate the nature of thermal contact 
between polymer and mold during the different stages of injection molding cycle. 
 
Bendada et al. [25] used an infrared waveguide pyrometer to monitor the temperature 
of polymer on the surface of mold (i.e. Tmt in equation 1) and two-thermocouple probe 
to determine the local heat flux (i.e. q in equation 1) crossing the melt-mold interface 
and the temperature of the mold surface touching the plastic melt (i.e. Tmb in equation 
1). With inverse algorithm, similar to equation 1 but in a more complex way, TCR (i.e. 
the reverse of HTC) was calculated in the end. They got the conclusion that the TCR of 
(a) 
(b)

polymer-cavity interface changes with time, relatively low and constant in filling stage 
and starting increasing significantly when polymer melt cools down with an interface 
air gap. Also, higher mold and melt temperatures as well as holding pressure lead to 
better heat transfer (i.e. lower TCR or higher HTC).  
 
Yao Liu et al.[24]employed similar experiment and calculation method as Bendada to 
obtain the average HTC within an injection cycle in his research, and found that HTC 
increases with rising melt temperature, surface roughness, and injection rate, especially 
with the melt temperature and the surface roughness.  
 
In Moldex3D, the HTC of the whole cycle is only divided into 3 averaged stages: filling, 
packing and detached HTC.

features. So from this perspective, HTC should not be too small. And from the aspect

trying different Ci, Cm and α values in fiber orientation calculation model. These

comparison are presented here. 
5.5.1.   Introduction to fibers in thermoplastics 
Fiber Reinforced Plastics (FRP) have been widely in injection molding industry 
because of their superior mechanical properties, high strength to weight ratio, high 
toughness to anticorrosion, and high creep resistance, compared with unreinforced 
polymers[10]. However, fibers can also cause anisotropy of mechanical and thermal 
properties to injection molded parts due to the preferential orientation of fibers. During 
injection molding, high shear rates near the mold surface aligns most fibers along the 
flow direction. As a result, injection molded parts are much stronger in flow direction 
while weaker in the direction crossing perpendicular to the major fiber alignment 
direction[26]. In addition to the mechanical strength mentioned above, fiber orientation 
also have significant influence on the dimension aspect of the injection molded parts, 
including shrinkage and warpage[27]. In order to optimize the properties of injection 
molded parts, it is necessary to predict the fiber orientation in an accurate way, which 
is however a challenge in current CAE simulation field. 
5.5.2.   Models for calculating fiber orientation in simulation 
The theory developed by Jeffery [28] first described the motion of a single ellipsoidal 
particle, e.g. fibers, suspended in a Newtonian matrix in a mathematical way. It showed 
the particle in a Stokes flow field will rotate around the vorticity axis. Even though this 
model fits very well to the dilute suspension of fibers, behavior of fibers in concentrated 
suspensions can be quite different. In concentrated suspensions, the distance between 
fibers is too short, on the order of fiber diameter or even less[29], that fibers will interact 
with each other under hydrodynamic condition, which definitely has large effect on the 
motion of fibers.  
 
As an alternative, Folgar-Tucker model [30] has been used widely to predict the fiber 
orientation during the past decades. They modified Jeffery’s equation by including a 
phenomenological term to mathematically describe the interaction between fibers. 
Folgar-Tucker model did improve the predictions of the steady state fiber orientation, 
but it over predicted the rate of fiber reorientation according to experiment observations

as a result of fiber–fiber contact in the experiment reducing the rate of fiber 
orientation[31][32].

Rotary Diffusion Model combined with Retarding Principal Rate Model (iARD-
RPR)[33][27] has been developed as well as implemented by Moldex3D recently, 
which is able to solve the problems mentioned above.

have a lower shrinkage. On the other hand, if the cooling rate is slow, then the 
macromolecule chains have enough time to relax and crystallization is easier to happen, 
thus causing a higher shrinkage

5.8.   Influence of viscoelasticity 
In this section, only the influence of viscoelasticity on the warpage is discussed as 
Moldex3D only considers viscoelasticity in warpage analysis. So it won’t change the 
filling behavior. The license of Moldex3D Viscoelasticity module was borrowed from 
CoreTech for investigation as Widex A/S doesn’t have it currently. 
5.8.1.   Introduction to viscoelasticity  
Polymers commonly exhibit viscoelastic properties and a viscoelastic material is 
usually described by the combination of both elastic and viscous behavior. Because of 
the viscoelastic behavior, polymers tend to creep under a constant load and stress relax 
under a constant strain. The latter phenomenon will occur with injection molded parts 
have residual stresses from injection molding process, and the residual stress inside the 
part can result in extra warpage after ejection.  
5.8.2.   Simulation results comparison 
A simulation with considering the flow induced residual stress and viscoelastic material 
property in warpage analysis was run and compared with the reference simulation 
which didn’t consider these two factors. As shown in figure 5-28, the simulated part 
with considering viscoelasticity has smaller dimension in the flow direction and larger 
dimension in the transverse direction than the reference simulation. But the dimension 
difference is not big, only tens of microns in each direction. This can be explained by 
that polymer molecules were partially oriented and stretched in the direction of flow 
during injection molding filling phase. These molecules would try to relax from this 
stretched condition, but they were linked together mechanically and thus constrained. 
The constrained strain then created residual stresses in the part. Part then was frozen 
and ejected from the mold with stretched molecular condition and residual stress inside. 
By considering the viscoelasticity, the residual stress will be released by polymer 
molecular chains contracting back to the randomly oriented condition after ejection, 
which can cause a shrinkage in the flow direction and then an expansion in the 
transverse direction for this specific hearing aid shell geometry. 
 
But our material PBT, with 30% glass fibers filled, should be stiff enough that molded 
parts are able to resist this extra warpage and the residual stress then will be kept before 
it was scanned by CT machine, which was done 4 days after production in this project.

5.9.   Influence of the flow rate of coolant 
In this section, simulations with different coolant flow rate were performed and 
compared. 
5.9.1.   Introduction to mold cooling 
The heat of the polymer is released and removed through heat convection of the 
coolants running in the channel, and heat conduction of the mold. This process will last 
until the whole product is fully-ejected.  
 
In Moldex3D, the cooling can be characterized by Reynold’s number Re, which is 
calculated from a flow rate of coolant. For round cooling line the value should be greater 
than 10000 to achieve turbulent flow. Turbulent flow brings better heat transfer and 
cooling efficiency [10]. A Reynolds number below 10000 can be less effective to 
remove heat from the cavity. 
5.9.2.   Simulation results comparison 
Three flow rate as listed in table 5-11 were tried in this section, which shows that flow 
rate has no influence on the injection time and pressure issues. Figure 5-29 indicates 
that the dimension of the molded part didn’t change when the flow rate changed from

5.11.   Conclusion 
With proper parameter settings, Moldex3D is able to predict the injection molding 
process in a relatively precise way. For the simulation of the injection molding process, 
the following factors have significant influence, and can be summarized as: 
 
 
It is necessary to consider the effect of pressure on the viscosity in the simulation 
as it has large influence on injection time, pressure and pressure loss. 
 
 
Including of nozzle geometry in the simulation model gives better prediction 
because it increases the compressibility of plastic melt and considers the pressure 
loss in the nozzle. 
 
 
Larger filling HTC can increase the injection time, pressure as well as the pressure 
loss. Larger packing HTC can increase the pressure loss but also bring more 
==PAGE:95==
86 
 
fluctuation to the pressure curves. 
 
The warpage prediction from Moldex3D is far from the reality, as experiment molded 
part extended in the flow direction while the simulated part shrinks in this direction. 
Warpage were dominated by the fiber orientation and Moldex3D underestimated the 
fiber orientation effect. Predicted warpage was found to be extremely dependent on the 
filling HTC and RPR model for the fiber orientation as these two parameters change 
the fiber orientation significantly. 
 
Not considering crystalline effect gives better prediction for the pressure curves and 
shrinkage. 
 
The influence of the other settings investigated in this chapter, i.e. flow solver efficiency, 
geometry boundary, IARD fiber orientation model, viscoelasticity effect and the 
coolant flow rate, is relatively less significant.

6.  Conclusions and future work 
6.1.   Conclusions 
With the pressure sensors used in this project, the real filling time can be estimated 
properly. This information is very important when CAE mode is used, where the filling 
time need to be input by users. 
 
Inconsistencies and uncertainties in the experimental data must be minimized to avoid 
introducing uncertainties in the simulation calculations.  
 
By replicating the exact processing parameters from experiment, considering the 
influence of pressure on the viscosity, including nozzle geometry in the simulation 
model and determining the proper HTC value, the simulation results of injection time, 
pressure and pressure loss are very close to the experiment, indicating that Moldex3D 
is able to predict the injection molding process correctly.  
 
The predicted warpage is not validated by the experiment in this project. For complex 
part made of fiber filled plastics, it is not suggested to use Moldex3D to accurately 
predict warpage. The warpage of the fiber filled material relies a lot on the fiber 
orientation, which can be changed greatly by the filling HTC and RPR fiber orientation 
model. 
6.2.   Future work 
Future work can start with the following points: 
1. Characterize the fiber orientation with proper CT scan machine and analysis 
software like VGStudio MAX 3.0, and compare the experiment result with the 
simulated fiber orientation from Moldex3D to validate its ability in predicting 
correct fiber orientation. 
 
2. Use a micro injection molding machine, which is more precise, to perform the 
injection molding experiment. 
 
3. Insert a proper thermometer like infrared waveguide pyrometer in the experiment 
mold to record the melt temperature and then check if Moldex3D is able to predict 
the correct temperature change inside the cavity. 
 
4. Use different mesh size and different boundary layers for cavity, runner and cooling 
channel to check if the improvement of pre-processing will increase the warpage 
prediction. 
==PAGE:97==
88 
 
 
5. Turn on fiber calculation in packing phase and then run simulations with different 
packing HTC values and fiber parameters, i.e. Ci, Cm and α, to check if there is any 
improvement in warpage prediction. The method to turn on fiber calculation in 
packing stage in shown in Appendix D.
