Abstract 
Over the last decades, liquid chromatography – Mass spectrometry (LC-MS) based 
proteomics has become the favoured method of identifying and quantifying the proteome. 
Sensitivity and depth of proteome coverage have been further improved with introduction of 
data independent acquisition (DIA) of MS data. As data of all precursor peptides are acquired 
with DIA, it is of increasing interest to develop DIA compatible labelling strategies with the 
ability to improve quantification of peptides of low abundance.  
Not only the proteome is of interest when wanting a comprehensive understanding of the 
biological mechanisms within cells. Transcriptomics reveal RNA expression within cells and 
serves as an important tool for understanding gene regulation. Combining transcriptomics and 
proteomics in a multi-omics approach offers a comprehensive view of cellular processes, 
providing more accurate insights into disease states and therapeutic targets.  
This study provides a cheap, simple and time-efficient DIA compatible labelling strategy 
with multiplexing capabilities. The labelling reaction is performed by derivatization of 
amines via reductive amination resulting in pH-driven N-termini specific dimethylation with 
over 90% of all modification located at N-termini amines.  
Additionally, this study offers a strategy for simultaneous extraction of RNA and Protein 
from same sample through one-phase extraction with the RNA isolation reagent NucleoZol. 
Through the extraction protocol it is possible to extract up to 1923 protein groups for samples 
of 50 cells.

Project aim 
This thesis aims to develop new sample preparation protocols for subsequent LC-MS/MS 
analysis. The project consists of two parts, where each part tackles different but equally 
important innovations to better our understanding of the complexity within our cells.  
First part of the thesis the focus on developing a protocol for extraction of both RNA and 
protein from same origin for combined transcriptomic and proteomic analysis. Extraction is 
performed by using the RNA isolation reagent NucleoZol which separates RNA from protein, 
DNA and carbohydrates.  
The goal of the second part of the thesis is to develop a protocol for chemical labelling with 
stable isotopes compatible with data independent acquisition by introducing dimethyl groups 
as labels. The goal of the protocol is to achieve N-terminal specific dimethyl labelling by 
utilizing the difference in pKa between N-terminal amine and amine on sidechain of lysine. 
Furthermore, the dimethyl label is suitable for multiplexing allowing for multiple samples to 
by analysed simultaneously. This project aims to introduce a method for 3plex dimethyl 
labelling.

Part 1 
1.1 Introduction 
Since Liquid chromatography – Mass spectrometry was introduced in proteomics, it has 
quickly become the preferred method for identifying and quantifying the proteome (1). With 
advances in instrumentation, data acquisition methods and software for analysis, the method 
has increased in sensitivity and opened the doors for studying the proteome down to single 
cell level and in spatial context (2), (3).  
Proteomics provide large-scale studies of proteins investigating the structure, function and 
interaction between protein as well as the effect of protein expression on cellular state and 
disease mechanisms (4). It is of interest to survey the proteome as proteins functions as the 
primary executors of cellular function. As proteins are translated from RNA, it is also of 
outmost interest to study the transcriptome to understand gene regulation and its subsequent 
effect on cellular function. Modern approaches in transcriptomics have the ability to provide 
complete sets of all transcripts as well as quantifying the transcriptome to gather insight on 
how the transcriptome changes under different cellular conditions (5).  
Despite mRNA serving as the blueprint for protein translation, mRNA levels cannot be relied 
on to accurately predict protein expression. Multiple biological processes, such as post 
transcriptional modifications and post translational modifications, affect the outcome of both 
the transcriptome and proteome (6). It is of interest to study these modification processes as 
they can reveal information on molecular changes between diseased cells and disease-free 
cells and help further our understanding of diseases progression, potential biomarkers and 
suitable drug targets (6), (7). By combining transcriptomics and proteomics into a multi-omic 
strategy, it can provide a more comprehensive understanding of biological processes than 
each of the omics can provide on their own.  
The multi-omics approach has also been extended to both single cell and spatial applications 
(8), (9). Spatial multi-omics are of particular interest when studying human diseases as it can 
provide greater insight on the complex interplay between intercellular and intracellular 
mechanism that govern pathogeneses (9), (10). Combination of transcriptomics and 
proteomics has been proven to be extremely valuable in the field of oncology (11), (12), as 
well as opening the door for future development of patient specific precision medicine (13).

1.2 Theory  
1.2.1 Sample preparation 
A critical step for successful proteomic analysis is optimal sample preparation. Proteins must 
be extracted from their origin and processed in order to be compatible with the downstream 
LC-MS analysis. For this project proteins are extracted from HEK293 cell cultures. Initial 
efforts were made in FFPE preserved pancreatic tissue samples which can be seen in 
supplementary notes S1-S5.  
1.2.1.1 Nucleozol 
NucleoZol is a reagent designed for one-phase extraction and isolation of total RNA. 
NucleoZol is compatible with a wide array of sample types including cell cultures of all 
origins as well as tissues (14).  
First step consists of lysing and homogenisation of sample in NucleoZol. Contaminants like 
DNA, polysaccharides and proteins can then be precipitated from the NucleoZol solution by 
addition of nuclease-free water and easily be removed by centrifugation as a pellet of 
contaminants forms while RNA remains in the supernatant. The RNA needs subsequent 
cleaning by addition of isopropanol and ethanol. When working with NucleoZol and RNA 
extraction in general, it is important to work under RNase-free conditions to avoid 
degradation of the RNA (14).  
The mechanism of how NucleoZol works is not published yet. NucleoZol consist of phenol 
and guanidinium thiocyanate like another RNA extraction reagent named TRIzol (15). 
NucleoZol has an advantage over TRIzol as chloroform is not needed for phase separation.  
Guanidinium thiocyanate is a chaotropic agent promoting denaturation of proteins by 
disrupting hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic interactions. The effects of guanidinium 
thiocyanate includes nucleases, thus inhibiting further degradation of RNA. As proteins are 
denatured, the hydrophobic sidechains normally hidden within the structure, will become 
exposed and decrease the hydrophilicity of the proteins. Guanidinium thiocyanate also 
possess the ability to stabilize nucleic acid structures as it disrupts the secondary and tertiary 
structures of nucleic acids which in return will increase the solubility of RNA (16), (17).  
Phenol possesses similar properties as guanidinium thiocyanate in the context of RNA 
extraction. Phenol aids in disruption of intermolecular bonds of causing denaturation of 
proteins as well as disruption of secondary structures of RNA and DNA.  
1.2.1.2 Proteolytic digestion 
Proteins are exceptionally hard to analyse by MS in their natural state as they are difficult to 
vaporise and ionize. These difficulties are circumvented by digesting the proteins into 
peptides. Bottom-up proteomics where proteolytic peptides are analysed is the most common 
approach in modern proteomics.  
Digestion can be done either by enzymatic digestion, using proteolytic enzymes, or by 
nonenzymatic digestion involving chemicals (18). The most widely applied method for 
digestion of proteins is enzymatic digestion utilizing proteases to cleave peptide bonds. 
Trypsin is considered the gold standard for enzymatic digestion. Trypsin cleaves at the C-
==PAGE:8==
Page 7 of 58 
 
terminal of lysine or arginine unless the amino acids are directly followed by a proline (19), 
(20). The cleaving sites are advantages as both lysine and protein are relatively abundant 
amino acids and typically well distributed within the amino acid sequences. As a result, 
tryptic peptides to have an average length of 14 amino acids as well as having at least two 
positive charges making it highly compatible with MS analysis (19), (18). Digestion with 
trypsin is generally performed at neutral pH at 37oC for 16-18 hours to ensure optimal 
activity of trypsin and high efficiency of digestion.  
Other commonly used proteolytic enzymes include endoproteinases such as Arg-C, Glu-C 
and Lys-C, which all cleaves from the C-terminal end (18). These proteases have high 
efficiency and specificity for cleavages at a single site. Both Arg-C and Lys-C have the same 
benefits as trypsin by cleaving at a basic amino acid it generates peptides with two positive 
charges, one at N-terminal and one on the basic amino acid. These proteolytic enzymes can 
replace trypsin for enzymatic digestion, however there is also great advantages in combining 
trypsin with an enzyme like Lys-C. By combining trypsin and Lys-C the number of missed 
cleavages will decrease resulting in higher coverage, efficiency and quality of digestion (21), 
(22).  
1.2.1.3 Single-pot, Solid-phase-enhanced sample preparation  
Efficient extraction and solubilization of proteins are a crucial first step for performing LC-
MS. To lyse cells and stabilize proteins, we utilize a variety of different buffers, salts, 
detergents and chaotropic agents. This includes reagents such as sodium dodecyl sulfate, 
guanidinium chloride or in this case NucleoZol. However, these lysing agents, detergents and 
chaotropic agents aren’t compatible with downstream analysis. The chaotropic agents are 
incompatible with the enzymes need for successful digestion of proteins into peptides as they 
can denature the enzymes. Many of the reagents will also disrupt MS analysis as they do not 
easily vaporise (23). As a result, peptide signal intensity will decrease but the instrumentation 
can also be harmed as the unvaporised reagents can clog the system increasing the need for 
maintenance and subsequently the cost.  
When applying a reagent as NucleoZol for protein extraction it is a necessity to perform 
additional sample clean up before digestion and LC-MS analysis. Single-pot, Solid-phase-
enhanced sample preparation (SP3) is a suitable method for preparation of NucleoZol 
extracted samples with the benefit of single-pot practise to help eliminate unnecessary sample 
loss (23), (24).  
SP3 workflow is based on paramagnetic beads to capturing proteins. The surface of SP3 
beads is coated with carboxylate functional groups providing a hydrophilic capture 
mechanism like the principle of hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC) (23). The 
sample is in a hydrophobic environment from addition of ethanol. The difference in polarity 
causes the proteins to be attracted to the hydrophilic surface provided by the coated beads. 
Due to the paramagnetic properties, the beads can be separated from solution by magnets 
enabling removal of original buffer and washing of the beads with minimal sample loss (23). 
Following sufficient cleaning of the beads, the sample can be eluted from the beads with a 
hydrophilic solution.

1.2.2.1 Spectrophotometry  
Spectrophotometry is an analytical technique for determining concentration of a substance in 
a sample based on the absorbance of light. The absorbance of light is directly proportional to 
the concentration as described by Lambert-Beers law (25):  
𝐴= 𝜀∗𝑏∗𝑐 
Spectrophotometry is a simple and time efficient analytic technique. The NanoDrop 
spectrophotometer has the advantage of working with low sample input of only 1-2µL.  
Spectrophotometric analysis is a common technique used in biochemistry to study the 
concentration of proteins and nucleotides. The molecules naturally contain chromophores 
through Pi conjugated systems with the ability to absorb ultraviolet radiation (25). Protein 
concentration can be determined by absorbance at 280nm due to aromatic sidechains present 
on Phe, Tyr and Trp. For nucleic acids the nitrogenous bases of purine and pyrimidine are 
responsible for an absorbance peak at 260nm (25).  
Spectrophotometric analysis of nucleotides also provides a measurement for determining the 
purity of the sample. For study of RNA the 260/280 ratio is utilized to determine purity of the 
sample, together with the 260/230 ratio which serves as a secondary measure of purity (26). 
For a sample to be deemed pure by spectrophotometric analysis it should have a 260/280 
ratio of 1.8-2.0 and a ratio of 2.0-2.2 for 260/230. A 260/280 ratio significantly lower than 
1.8 indicates contamination of molecules with absorbance at 280nm like phenol and proteins. 
260/230 ratios appreciably lower than the threshold of purity is attributed contaminants like 
carbohydrates, phenol or other molecules with absorbance at 230nm (26).  
1.2.2.2 Electrophoresis 
Electrophoresis is the practise of separating molecules based on size and charge by applying 
an electric current to move molecules through a sieving polymer gel. The electric current 
generates a voltage gradient promoting the molecules to move through the gel where smaller 
fragments sieves through the gel faster than larger molecules.  
Agilents 2100 Bioanalyzer instrument performs automated electrophoresis using a chip 
format. The chip format has the advantage of decreasing both separation time and sample 
consumption (27). The chips include sample wells, gel wells and a well for the ladder which 
serves as an internal standard. Individual analysis of DNA, RNA and protein is possible with 
different chips for each type of biomolecule. 
The molecules separated electrophoretically are detected by laser induced fluorescence. For 
RNA analysis, the samples are subjected to an intercalating dye before analysis, where the 
dye is responsible for the fluorescence signal (27). The fluorescence measured is then 
transformed into electropherograms and gel-like images.  
Traditionally RNA integrity can be estimated from electrophoresis gels by determining the 
ratio between the 28S and 18S rRNAs bands present in the gel. A ratio of 2:1 is considered 
the benchmark for intact RNA (28), (29). However, this method is error prone as the bands 
are measured through visual inspection. The 2100 Bioanalyzer software provides a RNA

1.2.3 Liquid chromatography – Mass spectrometry  
Since the development of electrospray ionization (ESI) in 1984 providing a reliable ion 
source for liquids it has allowed the coupling of liquid chromatography (LC) with mass 
spectrometry (MS) (31). By coupling the two techniques it combines the great separation 
capabilities of LC with the highly sensitive and specific detection capabilities of MS as a 
detector. Since the development of ESI there has been many great advances in the 
development of high throughput HPLC systems and increasingly sensitive mass analysers. 
Today LC-MS provides an ultra sensitive and very robust platform for proteomics.  
 
1.2.3.1 Liquid chromatography  
Chromatography is the principle of separating constituents within a sample based on each 
component’s different interaction between a stationary phase and a mobile phase.  
In LC, the stationary phase is tightly packed  with material in the column where the liquid 
mobile phase containing the analyte runs through (32). The mobile phase moves through the 
column as an elution gradient where the analytes will migrate through the column at rates 
dependent on the chemical properties of each separate component of the sample. This result 
in each component having a specific retention time and the components will elute from the 
column separated. Except for size exclusion chromatography/gel filtration, which separates 
based on size, most LC methods separate analytes based in this mechanism (32).  
The separation of analytes is highly dependent on the chemical properties of both the 
stationary phase and the mobile phase to ensure optimal interactions for the analyte and 
proper separation.  
Today the most abundant separation principle used in LC is reversed-phase (RP) 
chromatography (32). RP-LC was developed after Normal phase (NP) chromatography, with 
both methods separating based on polarity differences with the idea of components with 
similar chemical properties will be attracted to each other. However, the two methods have 
reversed elution profiles. NP-LC utilize polar silica gel for the stationary phase combined 
with a nonpolar mobile phase typically consisting of hexane or ethyl acetate (32). The 
difference in polarity between the phases promote polar constituents to interact with the polar 
stationary phase through non-covalent interactions like hydrogen bonding and dipole-dipole 
interactions. Less polar constituents will not have the same preference for the stationary 
phase as highly polar compounds and will therefor elute earlier from the column. As the 
polarity of the mobile phase is gradually increased more polar analytes will elute until the 
most polar analytes elutes last (32).   
In RPLC the chemical properties of the two phases are reversed. The stationary phase 
consists of silica alkylated with long carbon chains, with one of the most common materials 
being C18 where the silica is bonded with octadecyl alkyl chains (32). Despite the silica gel 
itself being polar, the C18 chains create a hydrophobic surface which the mobile phase 
interacts with. The mobile phase is highly polar, typically comprised of water mixed with an 
organic solvent like methanol or acetonitrile to ensure optimal separation. The chemical 
==PAGE:11==
Page 10 of 58 
 
properties of the phases promote hydrophobic interactions between the stationary phase and 
nonpolar compounds, resulting in analytes of greater hydrophobicity to have longer retention 
time, where as polar compounds wont by retained on the column and elutes faster (32). RPLC 
is ideal for analysis of peptides as they are compatible with the hydrophilic mobile phase and 
are composed of amino acids with hydrophobic sidechains allowing for hydrophobic 
interactions with the stationary phase. As a result, RPLC has stronger peptide bonding and 
sharper elution profiles compared to NPLC.  
 
1.2.3.2 Mass spectrometry 
MS is an incredibly sensitive analytical technique as it can detect analytes down to picogram 
levels. Ionized analytes are separated based on their mass-to-charge ratios (m/z) with the 
ability to distinguish changes in mass as small as different isotopic compositions (33). The 
abundance of ions for each value of m/z are displayed in mass spectrums. MS serves as the 
most powerful detector for chromatography with a major advantage being the ability to study 
analytes with similar retention times (33).  
For this project, the MS technique used start with analytes eluting from LC encountering ESI 
as the ion source. The analyte solution is converted into an aerosol of positively charged 
droplets as it moves through a capillary needle surrounded by an electrode providing a 
voltage of around 4 kV (33). The droplets are desolvated by a flow of nitrogen as drying gas. 
As the droplets become smaller their surface charge density will increase until the surface 
tension of the droplets can no longer support the charge and the analytes will transfer into a 
gaseous phase (34). ESI is a soft ionization technique as it only induces very little to no 
fragmentation as the solution-based analytes are converted into gaseous ions (35). Soft 
ionization techniques are ideal for proteomic analytes as it prevents premature fragmentation 
of analytes. Another benefit of ESI is the multiple charged ion states will facilitate ion 
dissociation MS/MS analysis.  
Before entering the mass spectrometer, the gaseous ions are filtered by High field asymmetric 
waveform ion mobility spectrometry (FAIMS). FAIMS serves as an additional separation 
method between LC and MS as it separates ions based on their behaviour in both weak and 
strong electric fields (36). Only ions with a stable flight path through the oscillating electric 
fields will enter the mass spectrometer. As a result, sensitivity increases, and noise is reduced 
by removing unwanted ions (36). As the ions enters the mass spectrometer, they are initially 
guided by the active ion beam guide. Here ionized particles move along the curved path while 
neutral particles move straight forward and are prevented from entering other compartments 
of the spectrometer (See Figure 1).  
The ions are guided into the quadrupole mass filter. The quadrupole consists of four parallel 
rods where both a constant and an oscillating voltage is applied to each rod. The ions are 
deflected by the electric field allowing only ions of a specific m/z ratio to exit the quadrupole 
depending on the oscillating voltage applied (33). The filtered ions enter the C-trap from 
where they can either enter the orbitrap or the ion-routing multipole.  
To gather the initial MS spectra the ions must first be guided to the orbitrap mass analyser. 
The orbitrap consists of an inner centre electrode surrounded by an outer electrode (37). The 
ions circle the inner electrode while also oscillating along the inner electrodes. The frequency 
==PAGE:12==
Page 11 of 58 
 
of oscillations is dependent on the m/z ratio of the ion. Fourier transform is then applied to 
the monitored waves yielding the MS spectra (37). The initial MS spectra, also termed MS1, 
shows m/z ratio of individual peptides. However, a variety of peptides of different amino acid 
compositions and length can produce identical m/z ratios. To sequence the isolated peptides 
subsequent MS spectra of fragmented peptides must be acquired.  
 
Figure 1 - Overview of Orbitrap Eclipse Tribrid Mass spectrometer (38) 
MS/MS 
Following acquirement of a MS1 spectra ions travel to the C-trap from where selected ions 
travel to the ion-routing multipole for Higher-energy collisional dissociation (HCD) with 
inert gas (See Figure 1) (39). After fragmentation by HCD, the newly formed ions are guided 
back through the C-trap and into the Orbitrap to acquire the MS2 spectra.  
By colliding precursor peptides with inert gas, the goal is to perform fragmentation at the 
peptide bonds (39). The MS2 spectra includes a series of peaks of N-termini charged amino 
acids (b-ions) and a series of C-terminal charged amino acids (y-ions)(See Figure 2) each 
with a specific m/z ratio (40), (41). Since the proteins initially are digested by a mixture of 
trypsin and Lys-C, the orientation or end of the peptide must be either C-terminal lysine or 
arginine due to the cleavage’s motifs of the proteolytic enzymes. Based on the information of 
the b- and y-ion m/z uphold against the known masses of amino acids, it becomes possible 
for advanced software to compute the amino acid sequence of the precursor ion (40).

Data acquisition methods 
Data dependent acquisition (DDA) and data independent acquisition (DIA) are two distinct 
methods for acquiring MS2 data differentiated by their method of precursor ion selection. 
DDA acts by selecting specific peptides from the MS1 scan, typically precursors of highest 
abundance, generating individual MS2 spectra for each selected precursor peptide (42). DDA 
is well suited for targeted studies, and benefits from being less time-consuming and 
expensive than performing DIA (43). By only analysing a small subset of precursor ion it 
introduces a great loss of information which is circumvented when performing DIA (43). 
DIA selects a window of m/z ratios of the MS1 scan for fragmentation (42). Since all 
peptides within the selected window are included for MS2 analysis, each peptide is 
represented in multiple overlapping spectra. Therefor, DIA is very computational demanding 
as deconvolution of spectra is necessary for sequencing of peptides (43). DIA is utilized for 
this project to gain maximized proteome coverage when working with small sample input.

1.3.2 NucleoZol extraction  
The procedure of extraction with NucleoZol is based on the user manual provided. The user 
manual is intended for cell culture samples of >1*107 cells. The project focus on significantly 
lower cell numbers hindering direct application of the user manual.  
1.3.2.1 General procedure 
The aim of the procedure was to isolate both RNA and protein from samples. When working 
with RNA it is crucial to avoid nuclease contamination to hinder degradation of RNA. The 
procedure was performed while wearing gloves and by using nuclease-free water.  
All work with NucleoZol was carried out in a fumehood while wearing gloves, safety glasses 
and a labcoat to prevent exposure as NucleoZol is hazardous. 
HEK293 Cells were measured into desired cell numbers by pipette and transferred to 
individual 0.5µL LoBind tubes. Cells were sedimented by centrifugation at 300xG for 5 
minutes and PBS buffer were removed by pipette with small amounts of buffer left to avoid 
disturbing pellet. NucleoZol was added directly to the cells to promote cell lysis. Following 
cell lysis, nuclease-free water was added to promote precipitation of protein, carbohydrates 
and DNA. Samples were vortexed for 15 seconds to ensure proper mixing and left to incubate 
at room temperature. Samples were then centrifuged for 15 min. at 12.000xG. Supernatant 
containing RNA was transferred to new 0.5 µL LoBind tubes and saved for later RNA clean-
up and analysis. Remaining pellet containing protein were resuspended in either TEAB or 
75% ethanol before protein sample clean-up and digestion.

1.3.4 Protein clean-up and digestion  
Protein pellets gathered from NucleoZol extraction was further prepared by SP3 sample 
clean-up and Trypsin/Lys-C digestion before LC-MS analysis.  
Samples from CC1 was digested prior to SP3 sample clean-up, while SP3 sample clean-up 
was not performed on samples from CC2. For CC3 and CC4 samples were digested on SP3 
beads after clean-up.  
1.3.4.1 SP3 sample clean-up 
Initially each bead solution was vigorously vortexed. The beads were extracted immediately 
after vortex as they have tendency to settle quickly. 40 µL of each solution were mixed with 
120 µL of MS-grade water in an Eppendorf tube. The tube was then placed on the DynaMag-
TM magnetic rack. The supernatant was removed after one minute and the beads was 
resuspended in 400 µL MS-grade water. This washing step was repeated three times. The 
beads were finally resuspended in 200 µL MS-grade water and ready for use.  
1 µL of washed beads were added per 20 µL of sample. 100% ethanol was added to samples 
from CC1 until a concentration of 75% ethanol was reached. A concentration of at least 70% 
is crucial to promote binding of protein to the beads. Since samples from CC3 and CC4 were 
resuspended in 75% ethanol further addition of ethanol was avoided. All samples were mixed 
gently by pipetting and samples were incubated at 25oC for 15 min. with 800rpm shaking 
using a Thermomixer. Supernatant was removed after samples were placed at DynaMag-TM 
for one minute and samples were resuspended in 50 µL 80% ethanol. After performing the 
previous step three times, the supernatant remaining was removed.  
1.3.4.2 Protein digestion 
Pierce Trypsin/Lys-C Protease Mix MS-grade was diluted in TEAB to concentration 
dependent on protein amount in the sample. 10 µL (CC1 and 2) or 20 µL (CC3 and 4) 
Trypsin/Lys-C solution was added to samples in a ratio of 1:50 Trypsin/Lys-C to protein 
amount. For CC4 the ratio was changed to 1:5 to ensure digestion was completed. Samples 
were digested in Thermomixer O/N (16-18 hours) at 37oC with 1000rpm shaking. Samples 
==PAGE:18==

were centrifuged at 21.300xG for 30 seconds and supernatant containing digested peptides 
was collected.

1.3.6 Protein analysis 
1.3.6.1 EvoTip sample loading 
20 µL 100% ACN was added to the EvoTip and centrifuged at 700xG for 1 min. (Each 
centrifugation step is performed at 700xG for 1 minute except the final centrifugation step). 
250 µL IPA was added to the wells of a 96 well plate and EvoTips were submerged for 1

column material. EvoTips were stored in a EvoTip box approximately 1/3 full of buffer A to 
prevent Evotips from drying.

conjunction with a FAIMSpro device.

1.4 Results and Discussion  
1.4.1 NucleoZol extraction of RNA and Protein  
First and foremost, it was necessary to establish if NucleoZol could be applied for protein 
extraction and not only for intended use of RNA extraction. As initial protein extraction test 
performed on FFPE tissue samples failed (see S1-S5), protein extraction by NucleoZol was 
tested HEK293 cell culture. The idea of testing on cultured cells is to avoid the extensive 
cross-linking of protein encountered in FFPE tissue. The goal of Cell culture test 1 was to 
determine if protein extraction by NucleoZol could be a possibility or if a change in strategy 
was needed for further work.  
Results of CC1 revealed it was indeed possible to extract protein from the pellet form from 
NucleoZol extraction (Figure 5). However, the number of identified protein groups was 
considerably lower than the yield expected for successful extraction RNA samples from CC1 
were discarded as extraction of protein was the sole focus of the experiment.  
 
Figure 5 - Number of identified protein groups for all conditions in CC1 
==PAGE:20==
Page 19 of 58 
 
A major problem encountered when designing the protocol for extraction with NucleoZol 
was scaling the protocol from the user manual to work for smaller sample amounts than 
NucleoZol is originally intended for. Identical volumes of NucleoZol were added to all 
samples with the exception of samples of 500 and 1000 cells. The idea was to test how the 
amount of NucleoZol would affect protein extraction. The results from testing different 
volumes of NucleoZol was inconclusive as a larger number of protein groups was identified 
with the lower amount of NucleoZol for 500 cell samples and vice versa for samples of 1000 
cells. For further analysis it would be ideal to scale the amount of NucleoZol depending on 
number of cells present in the sample to ensure all samples are treated with identical 
concentrations of NucleoZol. 
As the protein pellets from CC1 was resuspended in TEAB prior to digestion, it was observed 
that the pellets dissociated into smaller droplets rather than fully resuspend, even with 
vigorous mixing by pipette and vortex. In retrospect, the insufficient resuspension could have 
resulted in improper digestion conditions and contributed to low protein yields. The poor 
solubility observed in aqueous environment suggest the pellet is hydrophobic which could be 
a result of phenol being a major component of the pellets. As the digested peptides was 
subjected to ethanol during SP3 sample-clean up, remaining droplets of pellet was solubilized 
confirming the pellets are hydrophobic.  
However, another main factor contributing to low protein yields is the order of digestion and 
SP3 clean up. The SP3 beads are designed to attract and bind with proteins. As the samples 
were digested prior to SP3 sample clean-up, there is a potential loss of peptides due to 
improper binding interactions. If SP3 should be included in other experiments, it should be 
performed prior to digestion to ensure optimal use of SP3 beads.  
Since SP3 sample clean-up is a tedious and time-consuming process it could become a 
potential caveat if the workflow was to be implemented for experiments with large sample 
sizes. It was therefor of interest to examine how it would affect proteome coverage if SP3 
sample clean-up was omitted from the sample preparation workflow. In CC2 protein samples 
was analysed by LC-MS/MS directly after digestion without any SP3 sample clean-up (Figure 
6). Number of identified protein groups decreased for samples of 10,000, 25,000 and 50,000 
cells. The greatest decrease was seen for samples of 50,000 cells where the number of protein 
groups decreased from an average of approximately 1800 protein groups to an average below 
400 protein groups. From the observed decrease in number of protein groups it was 
confirmed that SP3 sample clean-up is indeed a crucial step to ensure proteins are released 
from the pellet formed from extraction with NucleoZol. For further experiments SP3 sample 
clean-up should be included in the workflow and occur prior to digestion to hopefully yield a 
greater number of protein groups than observed in CC1.  
As the protein pellets in CC2 were digested without SP3, it was necessary to resuspend them 
in TEAB to provide optimal environment for proteolytic enzymes used for digestion, even 
though it would be ideal to resuspend with a solution with chemical properties closer to 
phenol. Identical problems of poor resuspension were also encountered in CC2. This could 
explain some of the variability encountered between identical samples as they may not be 
resuspended to same degree. For further analysis it would be ideal to resuspend pellet with a 
less polar solution to ensure all pellets are fully resuspended to avoid sample loss and 
minimize variability between identical samples.

Another potential pitfall contributing to sample loss is removal of supernatant from pellet. 
NucleoZol contains a blue dye that ends in the protein pellet making the pellets visible just by 
examination by eyes. As the number of cells decrease, so does the size of pellet. For samples 
of 1000 and 500 cells the pellet is nearly invisible, making it difficult to ensure the pellet 
remains intact as supernatant is removed. This becomes a more prominent problem for 
samples of lower cell numbers as even small disruption of the pellet could lead to a great 
percentage of the sample being removed than the percentage of sample being removed for 
larger cell numbers.  
Disruption of pellet is not only a concern during preparation of protein samples but a concern 
for RNA samples as well. All RNA pellets are invisible when examined by eye making it 
impossible to assess if the RNA pellet is disrupted when supernatant is removed by pellet. As 
each RNA sample was subjected to multiple cleaning steps and removal of supernatant, there 
is potential for sample loss with each step of supernatant removal. This could explain the 
variability of concentration measured of identical samples (Figure 7). This is especially 
prominent for samples of 10,000 cells were RNA concentration ranges from 43.8 ng/µL to 
517.43 ng/µL. To avoid samples loss with removal of supernatant in future experiments it 
could be beneficial to introduce a different pipetting strategy.

Absorbance at 280nm and 260nm was also measured by NanoDrop to assess the purity of 
samples obtained (Figure 8). All samples from CC2 resulted in a A260/280 ratio below the 
purity criteria of a minimum ratio of 1.8. Since the ratio is below 1.8 there must be 
contamination from analytes with absorbance at 280nm. The contamination could stem from 
remaining proteins or proteins being released from pellet due to disruption. However, phenol 
contamination is highly likely as it is a main component of NucleoZol.  
The suspicion of phenol contamination was further confirmed by the measured A260/230 
ratios (Figure 8). RNA samples of 50,000, 25,000 and 10,000 cells all measure an average of 
A260/230 below 1.0. For 500 and 1000 cells samples outliers was observed. One of the 
samples of 1000 cells measured a negative A260/230 ratio. As the RNA concentration of this 
sample was below the minimum concentration limit of 16 ng/µL the measurement cannot be 
considered accurate. The A260/230 ratio of 10.8 obtained from a 500 cells sample was above 
the maximum 260/230 ratio limit of 3.0. Generally, if the ratio is above 3.0 it is due to either 
dirty sample surface when the blank measurement was performed, or the blank solution used 
was not identical to the sample solution. As the error was not encountered for other runs, the 
solution of the sample must have deviated from the solution used for blank measurement. If 
the outliers are ignored, the remaining samples have an average below 1.0 for both 500 and 
1000 cells samples.  Additional clean-up steps with ethanol could be implemented as a 
measure to limit phenol contamination. However, with additional clean-up it would also 
introduce an additional supernatant removal step. This could potentially cause greater loss of 
sample which should be avoided at all costs if the protocol should be implemented for 
samples of lower cell numbers.

A reason for the low yield could be accidental disruption of the pellets as supernatant was 
removed causing sample loss similar to previous tests. Since the supernatant removed from 
the protein pellets contained the RNA samples and must be saved, it wasn’t compatible with 
the new pipetting strategy. However, the new centrifugation strategy was still implemented to 
form protein pellets. Since the samples were placed in falcon tubes, a different rotor was 
utilized. The rotor designed for falcon tubes has a limit of 4347xG. This centrifugation speed 
was considerably lower than the speed suggested in NucleoZol’s user manual. Centrifugation 
time was increased to account for the change in speed. At first, the centrifugation time was 
increased to the double of the initial time. After centrifugation it was observed that the 
supernatant still had a blue tint which wasn’t observed in the two previous cell culture tests. 
As the blue dye previously had only been observed in the protein pellet, it was suspected that 
the pellets wasn’t fully formed yet and protein was still present in the supernatant. All 
samples were centrifuged again where the time was tripled from the initial centrifugation 
time. After the second centrifugation no blue tint was observed for samples of 10,000 cells 
and below. However, a slightly blue hue could still be observed for samples of 25,000 and 
50,000 cells but the tint had significantly decreased from the first centrifugation. Since a 
slight blue hue was still present in the supernatant, it could attribute to sample loss and the 
low yields recorded for the samples.

1.4.2 Future development  
With the insights gained from this study, there are now many strategic initiatives planned for 
the future. The first action to take would be to revisit the conditions of CC4 with additional 
NucleoZol conditions. The goal of reattempting CC4 would be to find the optimal NucleoZol 
concentration for high yield extraction of both protein and RNA. Additionally, information 
on RNA integrity in relation to NucleoZol concentration would finally be obtained by 
running samples on Bioanalyzer as samples from CC4 were of no use. All future RNA 
extraction and analysis should of course be handled with extra preventative measures to avoid 
loss of RNA by RNase contamination. It would be ideal to acquire pipette tips with filters and 
RNase decontamination solution to foster an optimal work environment.  
Once a protocol for NucleoZol extraction on cell cultures is established, it would be logical to 
extend its application to tissue samples to provide a spatial multi-omics protocol. Since cell 
cultures can’t accurately replicate the complex three-dimensional architecture of cells in 
tissue and have limited microenvironment, cell behaviour and gene-regulation can be 
drastically different compared to cells in tissue. Tissue samples preserve the spatial 
==PAGE:31==
Page 30 of 58 
 
organization of cells and allows study of the microenvironment surrounding the cells. By 
extracting both RNA and protein it will provide additional information on cell-to-cell 
interactions, tissue function and pathology than each omics technique would provide on its 
own. Initial efforts with tissue samples would ideally be performed on frozen tissue. Working 
with tissue samples will introduce an additional step in the protocol as samples must be 
homogenized in NucleoZol as a first step. Homogenization by mechanical disruption is 
recommended in the NucleoZol user manual as temperatures aren’t increase and RNA 
degradation is kept to a minimum. However, when working with small sample amounts there 
is a risk for sample loss as sample encounters the surface of the mechanical disruption tool. It 
would therefor be ideal to use sonification for homogenisation as no equipment is introduced 
to the sample. Since heat can be produced as the ultrasonic waves encounter the samples, 
there is a risk of RNA degradation. All sonication should be performed in a chilled 
environment, such as an ice-bath, to keep temperatures as close to 0oC as possible to 
minimize the risk of RNA degradation due to heat exposure.  
Tissue samples can be problematic to accurately analyse due to their inherent heterogeneity. 
FFPE tissue samples obtained through LCM circumvent the issue of heterogeneity as cells 
are visualized by microscopy and only samples with cells of interest are collected from the 
tissue. It is therefore crucial to expand the protocol to include samples from FFPE tissue to 
get the most accurate multi-omics data possible. Since initial efforts of NucleoZol extraction 
on FFPE tissue failed (see supplementary S1-S5), an alternative strategy was hypothesized. 
The alternative strategy applies SP3 beads to capture proteins released from FFPE tissue 
during digestion of the samples. Following digestion, all protein should ideally be stored on 
the SP3 beads leaving RNA in the supernatant. The supernatant containing RNA will be

2.1 Introduction 
As proteins are not just a mere translation of protein-coding genes but subjected to numerous 
processes affecting the final proteome such as post-transcriptional events, post-translational 
processes and physiological stimuli, it is important to have techniques that accurately study 
the proteome. Mass spectrometry-based proteomics have accelerated our understanding of 
complex biological system by enabling identification and quantification of the proteome of 
the studied sample (46).  
Accurate quantification is enabled through a variety of labelling strategies including SILAC 
and isobaric tags. In recent years, DIA has emerged and quickly become the preferred method 
for data acquisition as it provides proteome coverage of a greater depth and enables analysis 
identification and quantification of low abundance proteins. With the rise in DIA there is an 
increasing need for labelling strategies compatible DIA which can quantification of greater 
sensitivity.  
2.2 Theory 
2.2.1 Quantitative proteomics 
Identification of proteins is a crucial first step as it allows us to know what the proteome is 
comprised of. However, without quantification of the abundance of the proteins present we 
won’t get the full picture of the complexity within biological systems. The protein abundance 
present within cells comes as result of complex interplay of regulatory mechanism spanning 
from gene expression and all the way to degradation of proteins. The interplay of cellular 
processes ensures proteins are present in the right abundance and situated correctly within the 
cells to maintain cellular homeostasis and appropriate response to physiological stimuli. By 
quantifying the abundance of proteins from one cellular state relative to another state, like 
diseased cells and disease-free cells, it becomes possible to utilize the difference in protein 
abundance between the states to gain biological and medically relevant knowledge (46), (47). 
Quantitative proteomics of relative abundance between samples gains insight into disease 
mechanism and aids in drug discovery by enabling the study of diseased cells vs. Treated 
cells to analyse the effect of potential treatments of disease. It is also possible to perform 
absolute quantification of protein abundance primarily done by using synthetic peptides as 
internal standards for analysis (46). Absolute quantification is in important tool for accurately 
measure of protein concentration to by used for finding biomarkers for diseases.  
Generally quantitative proteomics can be divided into two broad categories: label-free or 
label-based methods.  
 
==PAGE:34==
Page 33 of 58 
 
2.2.1.1 Label-free quantitative proteomics 
With advances in high resolution mass spectrometers, it has opened the possibility for 
quantification of proteins without the need for including stable isotope labels. Label-free 
quantification is generally done either by peak intensity of LC-MS or by spectral count (48).  
For relative quantification by peak intensities the correlation between signal intensities from 
ESI and ion concentration is utilized. LC-MS/MS analysis generates precursor ion 
chromatograms for each peptide. The area under the curve of the peak can be integrated over 
retention time of the peptide to estimate protein abundance. One of the possible problems 
with quantification based on signal intensities is co-elution of separate peptides making it 
difficult to assign peaks to one specific precursor peptide (48).  
One of the most widely used methods for label-free quantification is spectral counting. The 
principle for spectral counting is that the size and amount of a protein is proportional to the 
number of MS2 spectrums generated. An increase in protein abundance will naturally lead to 
an increase in number of proteolytic peptides. By gaining more unique peptides it will result 
in an increase of MS2 spectrums for the original protein (48).   
Label-free quantification has multiple advantages compared to label-based methods. Label-
free analysis is cost-efficient by not relying on expensive reagents and is less time-consuming 
during sample preparation. With the advantage you gain by having simpler sample 
preparation, you gain a disadvantage by having more complex and convoluted data with 
label-free methods. Label-free methods will also typically require a higher number of 
replicates to be statistically significant which is negated by having internal standards in label-
based methods (49).  
2.2.1.2 Label-based quantitative proteomics 
Label-based quantification methods allow for easy comparison between different samples by 
including chemically similar but isotopically different labels to detect specific change in mass 
(49). Multiple methods exist with each having their own set of advantages and disadvantages. 
Here we will go through some of the common widely used labelling methods. 
Stable isotope labelling by amino acids in cell culture (SILAC) 
Different stable isotopes are incorporated into the proteome metabolically. Cells are grown in 
medium with essential amino acids. The amino acids can either by with light or heavy 
isotopes of hydrogen, carbon and nitrogen resulting in the isotopes being included through 
protein synthesis (50), (51). Incorporation of the heavy amino acids will result in a known 
mass shift detected by the mass spectrometer and the samples can easily be distinguished 
from one another. From the intensities of the light and heavy labelled peptides available from 
MS1 spectrums it is possible to quantify the relative abundance of proteins (50), (51).  
Since amino acids are incorporated metabolically it ensures a high labelling efficiency with 
minimal effort. However, it also drastically limits the variety of sample types the method can 
be performed on as it is only applicable with cell cultures and not e.g. Tissue samples which 
are applicable with other chemical labelling methods (50).  
 
==PAGE:35==
Page 34 of 58 
 
Reporter ion-based quantification with isobaric tags 
Different labelling strategies utilizing isobaric tags exist such as Tandem Mass tags (TMT) 
and isobaric tags for relative and absolute quantification (iTRAQ). Both methods have same 
general principle by labelling with isobaric tags having identical mass but different isotopic 
composition (52), (53). The isobaric tags are composed of three parts: a mass reporter, a mass 
balancer and a reactive group. The mass balancer ensures each tag has identical mass. Due to 
the similar mass and chemical composition the labelled peptides will generate only a single 
peak in the MS1 scan. By only generating one peak it limits the spectral complexity and 
boosts the MS1 signal (52). Through tandem MS the mass reporter group produce reporter 
ions under fragmentation. Each tag has distinct reporter ions enabling quantification of 
peptide abundance based on the intensity of the reporter ion (52). The reactive group is 
responsible for the chemical derivatization of the N-terminal amine groups and e-amine 
groups of lysine residues. The reactive group employs N-hydroxysuccinimide (NHS) esters 
which conjugate to amines by acylation where the amine groups provide nitrogen for the 
nucleophilic attack on the electrophilic carbonyl of the acyl group provided by the NHS ester 
on the tag (54), (53). By performing derivatization on amine groups, it ensures every 
proteolytic peptide have labelling sites resulting in high labelling efficiency.  
TMT labelling benefits from having the possibility to multiplex 16 samples total. By 
combining multiple samples into a single run, it automatically increases throughput but also 
reduces variability between samples. TMT is designed for data acquisition by DDA where the 
mass spectrometer selects a precursor peptide peak from MS1 to further fragment which 
TMT is ideal for as it generates a single peak of all peptides during MS1 (55), (56).  
However, with the rising interest in DIA there is a growing need for another labelling strategy 
with multiplexing capabilities compatible with DIA.  
2.2.2 Dimethyl labelling 
The scope of this project is to design a protocol for a chemical labelling strategy utilizing 
methyl groups as the tag. Derivatization of peptides with dimethyl labels provides a quick, 
simple and cost-efficient labelling strategy compatible with DIA and with the potential of 
multiplexing by introducing tags of different isotopic composition (57), (58). Furthermore, as 
labels are added to digested peptides, the method isn’t limited to certain sample types.  
Derivatization occurs through reductive amination. Formaldehyde provides a carbonyl group 
which reacts with primary amine on peptides. The carbonyl is added through nucleophilic 
addition and the amine is transformed into an imine. The imine is subsequently reduced by 
sodium cyanoborohydride, resulting in a secondary amine. The secondary amine reacts with 
formaldehyde to form another imine which is reduced to a tertiary amine with two methyl 
groups as a final product (59).  
 
==PAGE:36==
Page 35 of 58 
 
 
Figure 18 – Reaction mechanism of derivitization of amines through reductive amination 
https://www.cortecnet.com/applications/proteomics/chemical-labeling.html 
Sodium cyanoborohydride is used as reducing agent since it is selective towards imines 
whereas sodium borohydride would also reduce excess carbonyls in solution. The selectivity 
of sodium cyanoborohydride stems from reduced reactivity as the cyanide group act as an 
electron withdrawing group decreasing the nucleophilicity and hydride transfer (59).  
Both the N-termini of all peptides and lysine-sidechain provides primary amines that can be 
labelled through reductive amination. N-termini primary amines have a pKa value of 
approximately 8, whereas the primary amines on lysine-sidechain have a higher pKa value of 
approximately 10 (60). The difference in pKa value can be utilized to alter the selectivity of 
the reaction. Protonation of amines are promoted when pH is below pKa. Protonated amines 
decrease in nucleophilicity, and their reactivity decrease as the amine is responsible for acting 
as a nucleophile in the nucleophilic addition of formaldehyde. Since the pKa value is greater 
for amines at lysine sidechain, they will become protonated before N-termini amines when 
pH is lowered. As N-termini amines remains unprotonated their reactivity will be intact and 
reductive amination will occur selectively at N-termini amines (60).  
By performing reductive amination with reagents of different isotopic composition, it is 
possible to develop a multiplex strategy of three isotopologues of dimethyl labels (57), (58). 
All labels must have a minimum weight difference of 4 Da to avoid overlapping signals in the 
isotopic envelope (61). Labels formed from regular formaldehyde and cyanoborohydride 
introduce a mass shift of +28 Da per labelled peptide (Light label). Deuterium introduced as a 
stable isotope through formaldehyde results in a mass shift of +32 (Intermediate label). By 
introducing switching to 13C and deuterium in formaldehyde as well as using 
cyanoborodeuteride, a third label with a mass shift of +36 Da can be created (Heavy Label). 
When introducing deuterium as a stable isotope in labels it is important to be aware of 
changes in hydrophobicity. Since deuterium is less hydrophobic than hydrogen it will interact 
differently with the hydrophobic column material used for RP-LC.

N-terminal specific labelling provides signal accumulation on the y-ion series. The labels will 
only be observed for b-ions as they include the N-termini. B-ions of identical peptides with 
different labels will differ in their m/z ratio due to the mass shift provided by the label. All y-
ions aren’t affected by the label, hence all signals from identical peptides will accumulate 
with identical m/z ratios. The signal accumulation of y-ions provides an increase in 
sensitivity enabling identification and quantification of peptides of low abundance.

retention time shifts in future work, it is of interest to consider other labelling options. The 
effect of deuterium is easiest avoided by eliminating deuterium as a stable isotope in the 
labels. Instead, other stable isotopes should be considered. Utilizing 15N and 18O as stable 
isotopes for labelling would eliminate the deuterium effect, but application of those isotopes 
will require development of a new derivatization strategy as reductive amination requires 
either aldehydes or ketones as the carbonyl. 13C is another potential stable isotope which is 
compatible with reductive amination as carbon is added to the amines through the carbonyl 
group. Dimethyl labels with only 13C as stable isotopes will results in a mass shift of only 2 
Da compared to the light label. A mass shift of 2 Da would also negatively impact 
quantification accuracy as there would be overlaps of peaks in the isotopic envelope. When 
perform derivatization with 13C through reductive amination it would be beneficial to 
consider using aldehydes with more than one carbon as it would increase the mass shift 
between labels.

2.4.4 Future development 
First order of development must focus on decreasing the number of unmodified peptides. To 
investigate if increasing incubation time would affect completeness of reaction, a fourth test 
was performed. The test includes samples labelled with only light label, only intermediate 
label, only heavy label and a mix of all labels. Triplicates of each sample type was tested at 
different incubation times. A total of four incubation time was tested: 15 min., 30 min., 45 
min. and 60 min. The results of this test are yet to be analysed. The test will hopefully 
confirm if increased incubation time improves the number of labelled peptides and if so, what 
the ideal incubation time is. 
 If the reaction is still incomplete with increased incubation times, then the reaction 
temperature should be tested. Since increased temperature can increase kinetic energy and 
thus the reaction rate, it could decrease the number of unmodified peptides. Different 
temperatures should be tested in a similar manner as the test for incubation time, with 
triplicates of samples with different labelling conditions being tested at different elevated 
temperatures. As an extension of the temperature experiment, it could also be interesting to 
see if heating samples utilizing a thermomixer and 1000rpm shaking could improve reaction 
completeness compared to heating the samples without shaking. 
Data analysis of already performed experiments containing multiplexed samples should also 
be extended with analysis of MS2 fragmentation pattern. The main benefit of performing N-
terminal specific labelling is accumulation of y-ion signals which increase sensitivity for low 
abundance peptides. It is therefor important to assess if accumulation occurs by investigating 
MS2 spectra.  
Additionally, it could be of interest to test in-solution reductive amination with larger 
aldehydes such as acetaldehyde. By performing reductive amination with acetaldehyde 
instead of formaldehyde, the resulting labels will consist of ethyl groups. The diethyl labels 
will contain a total of four carbons, making it possible to design to different labels: a light 
label with 12C and a heavy label containing 13C. By avoiding deuterium in the labels it will 
eliminate retention time shifts and improve accuracy of quantification.

Conclusion 
The first part of the study has successfully developed a method for extraction of both RNA 
and protein from the same sample. As the method was optimized throughout the study it 
resulted in an increase in protein groups identified. For samples of 500 cells there is observed 
an increase from an average of approximately 100 protein groups identified in CC1 to a 
maximum of 5115 protein groups identified in CC5. The extraction method was also 
successfully scaled to samples of even lower cell numbers, with 1923 protein groups 
identified for a sample of 50 cells. From this study there is plenty of ideas for future 
development which could potentially increase protein yields even further along with 
increasing integrity of RNA for samples of low cell number. With future adaptation there is a 
possibility for the extraction method being implemented for spatial studies enabling further 
understanding of the complex interplay between the transcriptome and proteome.  
In the second part of the study, a new labelling strategy was successfully implemented. As 
over 90% of the labelled peptides showed demethylation only on N-terminal amines, pH-
driven N-terminal specific derivatization via reductive amination was successful. The 
labelling strategy proved to be compatible with DIA and enabling a multiplex strategy which 
can analyse multiple samples at once. With future research into the fragmentation pattern of 
the dimethyl labelled peptides, it can reveal if the labelling strategy is also able to increase 
sensitivity of low abundance peptides. As the method designed in this project provides an 
easy, cheap and time-efficient labelling strategy it is highly suitable for implementation in 
future DIA experiments.
