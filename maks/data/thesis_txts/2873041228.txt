Abstract
This study presents a multi-variable linear model for modeling waste collection rates from single-family
and multi-family homes in the Capital Region of Denmark. The model is built on the foundation of high
quality data inputs from Vestforbrændings’s waste database, and the socioeconomic data from Statis-
tik Danmark. The model was not capable of meaningfully representing waste collection rates from
seasonal residencies, and also performed poorly when applied to municipalities with a high proportion
of this housing type. Model performance improved significantly just by excluding some these mu-
nicipalities. Annual collection rates for most material fractions have changed significancy from 2022
to 2023, indicating that development and utilization of source segregation systems is still occurring
across the region. Model performance was limited for metal waste, which is attributed to low genera-
tion rates and collection at recycling drop-off centers. Recycling rates were determined in accordance
with the 2008 European Commission Waste Framework Directive, and the region was found to have a
recycling rate of 48.8%. Food waste was identified as the material fraction with the greatest potential
for improved recycling to help the region reach the future recycling targets defined by legislation. A
life cycle assessment was conducted and determined that increased plastic recycling would be the
most environmentally beneficial way to improve recycling rates. However, plastic recycling alone is
not enough to reach the recycling targets. A holistic approach focusing on better management of
plastic and food waste should be adopted to both improve recycling performance and improve system
sustainability for global warming.

Glossary
Deposit return system: The collection method in which packaging waste consisting of metal, glass,
and plastic is returned to a central collection point.
Material fraction: Visually identifiable fraction of the waste stream with common features, such as
paper, glass, plastic, etc.(Christensen, 2010b).
Material sub-fractions: A further classification of material fractions into everyday items that are typ-
ically disposed of in a household. Examples of material sub-fractions in residual waste are cat litter,
diapers, and composite packaging (Christensen, 2010b), (Freja Lerche, 2022).
Multi-family person: A person that resides in a multi-family household.
Multi-family homes: A type of household where multiple families dispose of waste in shared waste
collection bins (Edjabou et al., 2015).
Quantity collected: The mass of waste physically collected in each bin. This value does not account
for any missorting that may be present in the waste. In this report, it is represented by the following
symbols:

Recycling drop-off center: The collection method in which waste is collected at a public facility where
citizens bring their waste.
Seasonal homes: A type of household that is only occupied partially throughout the year.
Single-family person: A person that resides in a single-family household.
Single-family homes: A type of household where individual families dispose of waste in their own
waste collection bins (Edjabou et al., 2015).
Segregation efficiency: The proportion of waste properly source segregated, divided by the waste
generation, per fraction (Christensen & Matsufuji, 2010).
Source segregation: The act of separating waste at the source into different waste fractions (Chris-
tensen, 2010b).
Truck collection: The collection method in which waste is collected in bins outside of citizens’ houses
in the municipal collection scheme.
Waste generation: The starting point for a waste system, reflecting the quantities of waste that is
disposed of (Christensen, 2010b).

Introduction
1.1
Waste as a Resource
Solid waste generation is an unavoidable consequence of daily life. Waste generation is a well in-
grained facet of civilization, though modern waste management (WM) is challenged by an urbanizing
population and further industrialization. These both have acted to increase waste quantities, diversify
composition, and overall increase the economic and environmental complexity of WM over the past
century (Christensen, 2010a). The waste hierarchy presents one approach to remedy this complexity.
The waste hierarchy was originally developed as a part the European Union (EU) Waste Framework
Directive (WFD) in 2008 (Parliament & of the European Union, 2008), and is illustrated in Figure 1.1
below.
Figure 1.1: The waste hierarchy. Illustration from Axil Integrated Services (2021)
The waste hierarchy serves as a general management principle, whose actions should be followed
from the top down. The hierarchy presents three main options for WM (less waste prevention and
reduction), which are reuse/recycling, energy recovery, and disposal in a landfill or open dump. More
efficient WM benefits the environment, but also reduces material imports, fostering a more competitive
EU economy (Parliament & of the European Union, 2018). To facilitate this vision, the EU has devel-
oped the Circular Economy Action Plan, European Commission (2020). It specifies the need for better
source segregation, stronger markets for secondary materials, and a enhanced policy framework to
achieve EU circular economy (CE) goals. In DK, this plan has materialized with specific national
goals such as reducing waste incineration, increasing source segregation of waste, and increasing
the amount of waste for recycling (Ministry of the Environment, Denmark, 2021). The following sub-
section further explores the past and present legal framework that guides waste management in DK
and the EU.

1.2
Legal and Policy Framework - Past and Present
The first study to map the generation and disposal of Danish waste was conducted in 1985. At that
time, one third of all household waste was sent to landfill (European Environment Agency, 2013).
Dwindling landfill capacity motivated the Danish Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) to propose
a waste tax, which was subsequently enacted 2 years later by Danish legislators in 1987 (Andersen,
1998). One decade later in 1997, additional legislation was enacted that banned the landfilling of
combustible waste and taxed incineration plants, to further encourage waste reduction and recycling
(European Environment Agency, 2013). In 2008, the EU published the WFD and set the following
recycling targets for municipal solid waste (MSW):
• 2025: 55% of MSW recycled
• 2030: 60% of MSW recycled
• 2035: 65% of MSW recycled
Generally, these percentages are determined by the mass of waste that enters the recycling operation,
divided by the amount of waste generated. The equations for calculating these targets are presented
in guidance published by the EU Commission (European Commission, 2019).
To reach these targets, the 2008 WFD mandated source segregation and separate collection of waste.
In response to these goals, DK legislation mandated separate collection of household waste starting
from July 1st, 2021 (Ministry of the Environment and Gender Equality, Denmark, 2024). This includes
residual waste, food waste, paper, cardboard, plastic, metal, glass, food packaging, textiles, haz-
ardous waste, batteries, and small electronics. It should be noted that batteries and small electronic
waste are mandated under different legislation, and textile collection requirements were not enforced
until the following year in 2022. The pictograms shown below were developed by the Circular Industry
Association, and are ubiquitous in Danish waste collection (Cirkulær, 2024).

1.3
Current Performance - European Union and Denmark
EU member states are at various stages in working to attain the goals defined by the 2008 WFD. In
2022 member states recycling rates ranged from just over 12% to nearly 70%, with the EU average
reported as 48.7% (European Environment Agency, 2024). DKs national recycling rate was just under
the EU average, at 45.7%. In the same year, DK had of of the highest municipal solid waste generation
rate per capita of 787 kg/capita, second in the EU only to Austria (Eurostat, 2024).
Source segregation is well established and implemented in DK for some material fractions, such as
glass and metal. Other fractions, such as plastic and food waste, present significant opportunity for
improved source segregation. As such, these fractions will be key to improving recycling rates to align
DK with the recycling targets set by the 2008 WFD. (Laurent, Bakas, et al., 2014) (Laurent, Clavreul,
et al., 2014)

1.4
Environmental Assessment to Measure Sustainability
Determining progress towards recycling targets requires a detailed understanding of material flows
throughout the WM system. Material flow analysis (MFA) is a common tool used for this purpose,
and is well suited for applications in WM (Brunner & Rechberger, 2016). Whereas MFA is primarily
concerned with documenting material flows, life cycle assessment (LCA) is an established method that
associates environmental impacts (and benefits) with such flows, commonly used for WM systems
(Bakas et al., 2018). As such, MFA and LCA are tools utilized for the analysis presented in this report.
1.5
Knowledge Gaps
DK has a well established system of national waste tracking, called Affaldsdatasystemet (which trans-
lates to The Waste Data System) (Miljøstyrelsen, 2025). This system enables national level account-
ing of waste flows, from generation source to treatment method. However, detailed composition on
specific streams, especially heterogeneous ones such as household waste, is often lacking. Although
this waste stream only constitutes a relatively small proportion of overall waste generation (European
Environment Agency, 2016), its visibility and proximity to citizens necessitates proper management
(Parliament & of the European Union, 2018).
Households are often the focus of waste LCA studies (Laurent et al., 2014a), and broadly can be
divided into single-family (SF) and multi-family (MF) households (Christensen et al., 2010). This is
often the most common method of segmenting a population for waste sorting behavior, given the in-
fluence different collection systems have shown on source segregation behavior (Sewak et al., 2021).
Furthermore, the importance of local factors in an LCA cannot be understated . These include waste
composition (Bisinella et al., 2017), the background energy mix (Laurent et al., 2014a), to name a few.
Therefore, this study aims to address these uncertainties by performing a MFA and subsequent LCA,
with high quality, context specific data. Population data is utilized from Danmark Statistik, a govern-
ment agency responsible for managing various national socioeconomic statistics across DK. Waste
data from Vestforbrænding (VF), the largest municipal waste handler in DK is used in this analysis. A
waste characterization study conducted on Danish municipalities are used to assess source segrega-
tion effectiveness, and gain a better understanding of material quality when it is collected. Lastly, the
LCA will be conducted using the current Danish energy mix, highlighting the most sustainable path to
achieving the recycling targets defined by the 2008 WFD.
1.6
Research Questions, Project Scope, and Report Layout
1.6.1
Research questions
With the knowledge gaps identified in the previous section, the following research questions are de-
veloped:
1. How has the waste management system evolved over the past decade, and how does the current
performance align with the recycling targets set with the 2008 Waste Framework Directive?
2. How can the available data be utilized for representative waste modeling?
3. What are the environmental impacts of the system, and what is the most sustainable path to
reach the future recycling goals set by the 2008 Waste Framework Directive?

1.6.2
Project Scope
The scope of this report focus on household waste generated in the greater Copenhagen area. The
time frame considered is from 2014-2023, with the most recent data being utilized to calculate current
system performance. In this analysis, the focus is recyclable household waste, namely the food,
paper, cardboard, plastic, metal, glass and residual material fractions. Residual waste is included as
it often contains a high amount of recyclable material that was not properly source segregated.

Waste Data
In DK, the Danish EPA requires waste companies to report various metrics about the waste that they
handle into Affaldsdatasystemet. This includes where it was generated, what the waste is, and how it
is to be treated. Given VFs role as the primary waste management company for the Capital Region,
they are responsible for maintaining a waste database for the municipalities listed in the previous
section. Therefore, waste information from VF is used for the analysis in this report. The following
section is written to provide the reader more information on the data structure.
Waste data is provided as annual quantities of waste collected from the 18 different municipalities,
from 2014-2023. The data is further classified into ”H-Codes”, a requirement by Affaldsdatasystemet
that categorizes the waste based on what it is. There are 32 H-Codes listed, but only the 9 related
to the common material fractions found in a household waste collection scheme are included. These
H-codes were attributed to material fractions by VF, which aides in easier data presentation in waste
management reports for the municipalities they serve. These same aggregations are used in this

Gladsaxe Waste Characterization Study

segregation. Generally, these studies are an expensive undertaking, so access to them is often limited
(Lagerkvist et al., 2010). Picking analysis is a type of waste characterization with the goal of evaluating
the performance of or the introduction of existing recycling schemes (Means et al., 1995).

for the study, representing waste generation from May - September 2021. 175 MF residencies were
selected, representing waste generation from May - June of the same year. Waste was characterized

Segregation Efficiencies
In a waste collection scheme designed to collect many different fractions, quantifying how well waste
is being segregated is important. Improperly source segregated waste will not be sent to the correct
treatment facility, and can contaminate waste that is properly source segregated. To quantify how
well waste is being segregated, segregation efficiencies per fraction can be calculated. Segregation
efficiency is the quantity of a material fraction that is properly source segregated into the proper bin,
divided by the quantity of that material fraction that is put in all bins. This relationship is presented
below as an equation, from Christensen and Matsufuji (2010).
Segregation Efficiencyi = Properly source segregated wastei
Waste generationi
(2.4)

and properly source segregated quantities (highlighted cells), the segregation efficiencies for SF and
MF homes were calculated per material fraction using Equation 2.4. These are presented for the six

Multi-variable Linear Regression
To determine the proportion of waste generated from either SF or MF households (or citizens) for
TC, multi-variable linear regression (MVLR) is used (specifically ordinary least squares regression).
Although there is not a standard method for modeling waste generation, prior studies have utilized
and noted the effectiveness of this method. (Adeniyi et al., 2024), (Ghinea, 2016). The general form
of the equation is presnted below.
Waste collectedi = SF Homes * Collection Ratei,SF + MF Homes * Collection Ratei,MF + ε
(2.5)
Where the annual quantity of waste collected is provided by VF data per municipality (per material
fraction), and the annual housing information is provided by Danmarks Statistik. With enough depen-
dent variables (waste collected for different municipalities and years), the independent variables (SF
or MF collection rates) can be estimated. ε represents the error of the model, which are any influenc-
ing factors beyond population that are unmodeled, yet influence the quantity of waste collected. An
intercept of zero is assumed, as zero households would entail zero waste collected in the TC scheme.
Bootstrapping
Bootstrapping is a statistical tool developed to better understand a population from a relatively small
sample size. Bootstrapping employs random sampling with replacement, meaning that a statistical
distribution is constructed from taking repeated ”bootstrap samples”, and statistical parameters are
derived from that distribution.
Given the relatively small number of municipalities included in this study, bootstrap distributions are
used in conjunction with MVLR to determine collection rates. Further detail is provided in the ”Waste
Model” subsection.
T-test
To determine if there was a statistically significant difference between the annual quantity of waste
collected between different years, a paired t-test is utilized. An independent t-test with unequal vari-
ances was used with a significance level of 0.05. An independent t-test was selected rather than a
paired t-test due to some years including different municipalities (i.e some were excluded because
they were identified as outliers).
Waste Model
The following section details the methods used for developing the results of the waste model presented
in Table 3.4.
First, the presence of seasonal residencies in the 18 municipalities was investigated (presented in
Table 2.2). Gribskov, Halsnæs, Frederikksund, and Egedal were identified as outliers given their
proportion of seasonal residencies. These municipalities were removed from further consideration for
developing the waste model.
The annual waste collection rates for the TC scheme for the 14 municipalities were then plotted (Fig-
ure 3.4). Outliers for each material fraction and year were identified, and removed from subsequent
analysis. Next, a t-test was used to identify the first year before 2023 that differed significantly, work-
ing backwards through time. This was done to identify which material fractions were being collected
in a steady state manner, and would serve as suitable inputs for model development. This analy-
sis demonstrated that only metal and glass were being collected in fairly consistent quantities. All
other fractions were found to have statistically significant differences in collection rates from 2022 to
2023. Only 2023 data was used for these fractions. Table 3.1 summarizes the years utilized and the
municipalities excluded, per material fraction.

These exclusions result in a maximum sample size of 14 for all fractions except metal and glass.
Using a MVLR model on such a small sample size led to violating key model assumptions. This was
addressed by conducting 1000 MVLR models via bootstrapping, and generating distributions for the
annual collection rates. Interested readers can find the bootstrap distribution in the appendix of this
report. The mean value from these distributions were selected as the annual waste collection rate, and
are presented in Table 3.3. The MVLR model could not distinguish metal collection rates by housing
types, likely due to the low quantities collected. To alleviate this model limitation, metal was modeled
as the sum of metal waste collected from the TC scheme and from RDCs.
Waste generation rates were determined using the previously derived waste collection rates, and the
results from the Gladsaxe waste characterization study. The collection rates in 3.3 were multiplied by
the bin compositions found in Tables 2.7 and 2.8 respectively. The sum of each row was taken as
annual the waste generation rate, and is presented in Table 3.4.
Recycling Rate Calculations
This subsection outlines the methodology and assumptions used for calculating the recycling rates
presented in Table 3.7.
The collection rates were multiplied by the total number of SF and MF households in the region to
determine the total amount of waste collected per material fraction, per housing type. These total
collection quantities were then multiplied the bin compositions presented in Tables 2.7 and 2.8. To
account for the waste moisture content, these total collection quantities were multiplied by the per-
centages presented in Table 2.10. Moisture was then assigned as residual waste in each bin. The
dry mass collected in the proper bin was considered as the input to the recycling operation, and is
presented for SF and MF homes in Tables 3.5 and 3.6, respectively.
For the RDCs, collection rates per household were not derived. It was assumed that source segrega-
tion behavior is not nearly as variable as for the TC scheme, and that users would be guided to sort
their waste by the RDC attendants and by various signage found at these locations. Therefore, the
total quantity of waste collected at all RDCs in 2023 was used. To avoid double counting the metal, the
quantity of metal collected as RDCs was not counted here, as it was used to define the TC collection
rates. Moisture contents from Table 2.10 were applied to the respective material fraction in the same
manner as described from TC. It was assumed that of all of the waste collected for each material frac-
tion, 90% was suitable as input into the recycling operation. The other 10% was assigned to residual
waste.
Similar to the RDCs, collection rates per household for the DRS were not derived. The total quantity
of waste collected in the DRS for 2023 was used. It is assumed that any waste that could be classified
as a missorting was rejected by the sorting machines, and therefore not collected in this scheme.
Moisture content was not applied for this calculation, as it is assumed that the waste mass is measured
after the waste is compacted and most moisture is removed. According to A/S (2024), 96% of all

Life Cycle Assessment
The following subsection outlines the framework and key assumptions of the LCA used in this report.
Goal
The goal of this study was to evaluate the environmental implications of increased waste recycling and
to identify the most environmentally favorable path toward achieving the 2030 recycling target defined
by the 2008 WFD.
Modeling Framework
For this study, multi-functionality is handled via substitution. Danish electricity and heat were modeled
using average market data. Justification of this choice can be found in Appendix C of this report in the
”Modeling Framework” section.
Functional Unit
To evaluate the different treatment methods, a unitary functional unit (FU) was defined as
1 metric ton of source-segregated household solid waste, composed of food, paper, cardboard, plastic,
metal, and glass material fractions, for treatment in Denmark in 2025.
Limitations
While this study draws on Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) methodology, it does not meet all requirements
for ISO 14040 compliance and is intended as a preliminary evaluation of the environmental impacts
associated with a transition from incineration to a more circular waste management approach. Data
availability and time limitations ultimately led to a simplified approach being utilized. However, the
methodological framework for a full LCA is outlined in the appendix to support future refinement and
development.
Scenarios
For each of the 6 recyclable material fractions identified in the FU, two scenarios were defined. Sce-
narios were characterized as either 1 metric ton of waste sent for recycling, or 1 metric ton of waste
sent for incineration.
System Boundaries
The system boundaries of this study are limited to evaluating the incineration and recycling processes
(including anaerobic digestion for food waste) and their associated energy and material by-products.
Emissions related to waste collection, transport, and capital goods are excluded from the scope.
Life Cycle Inventory
For brevity, the life cycle inventory, including the waste composition, treatment technologies, EASETECH
models, and energy mixes, are presented in Appendix C of this report.
Life Cycle Impact Assessment Method
The impact assessment method used for this LCA is Environmental Footprint (EF) 3.1, without long
term emissions. EF was selected because it is the LCA methodology recommended by the European
commission (European Commission, 2022). Long-term emissions were excluded from this LCA be-
cause the model does not comprehensively model significant carbon sinks, such as landfills, which
influence the long-term carbon storage and release dynamics.

Discussion and Limitations
4.1
Discussion
Waste Trends
Total household waste generation declined by approximately 12% from 2014 to 2023, as shown in
Figure 3.1. The most substantial decrease was observed between 2021 and 2022, representing a 7%
decrease, which coincides with when the separate collection mandate began. A previous Danish study
noted a similar decrease in waste generation following the introduction of separate collection (Edjabou
et al., 2021). This suggests that source segregation is a viable waste reduction method, which is the
highest tier on the waste hierarchy and has served to increase the environmental performance of the
WM system.
From 2014 to 2023, the average collection rate for food waste tripled, as shown in Figure 3.2. Initially,
food waste collection exhibited greater variability than any other waste fraction, with some municipali-
ties collecting significantly more food waste than others, as illustrated in Figure 3.4. Although average
collection rates steadily increased throughout the late 2010s, large differences across municipalities
persisted.
For example, in 2020, some municipalities collected over 120 kg per household per year, while others
had yet to implement a food waste collection scheme. Between 2020 and 2022, food waste collection
increased by nearly 1.5 times, likely driven by national legislation. Interestingly, a previous Swedish
study observed that source segregation of food waste led to an overall increase in the collection
of other recyclable material fractions (Dahlén et al., 2007). Knowing this, and that food waste is
the largest material fraction generated (Figure 3.5), reinforces the importance of the national source
segregation policy for DK to reach the recycling targets set by the WFD.
Cardboard collection also increased substantially over the considered time period, shown in Figure
3.2. Cardboard is unique because the primary collection method shifted from RDC to TC over the 9
year time period, shown in Figure 3.3. As cardboard collection shifts from being collected at RDCs
to citizens homes, it is important to ensure adequate collection, capacity, and information. Multiple
citizens from the Gladsaxe waste characterization interviews noted cardboard containers quickly reach
capacity, which distinctiveness proper sorting (Freja Lerche, 2022).
Waste Model
Initially, the waste model was developed using waste collection data from all 18 municipalities. The
results from this initial exercise are summarized in Table B.1. For most fractions (except food waste),
model performance was characterized by wide confidence intervals and poor R2 values. To investigate
this, the collection rates were multiplied by the number of SF and MF homes in each municipality, and
compared with the actual quantity of waste collected per material fraction (presented in Tables B.2 -
B.8).
Gribskov is one such municipality, characterized by a significant proportion of seasonal residencies.
For this municipality, the model underestimates the quantity of collected residual waste by 56% (Ta-
ble B.2). For paper, cardboard, plastic and metal (glass and food waste to a lesser degree), the
model overestimates the amount of waste collected for these fractions. When comparing the under-
estimation of residual waste (3679 tons) to the sum of all the over-estimations for the recyclable frac-
tions (1622 tons), there remains a discrepancy of 2017 tons. This suggests that while some recyclable
material may be missorted into the residual bin, other factors are also likely at play, such as the uti-
lization of RDCs and the natural variability of waste generation.
Unfortunately, waste collection rates could not be modeled for seasonal residencies as was done for
SF and MF homes. However, a previous waste characterization study conducted in DK focused on
Performance and Sustainability Assessment of Household Recycling in Copenhagen, Denmark
29
==PAGE:40==
waste missortings in these dwelling types. This study distinguished between rented seasonal homes
and owner occupied homes, and noted different waste sorting behavior differences between these
two types (Høj & Karup, 2024). For the municipalities identified as outliers due to the presence of
seasonal homes (Gribskov, Frederikssund, and Halsnæs), conducting a similar study would provide
useful information for both modeling and identifying areas for improved recycling.
When comparing the model results in Table B.1 and Table 3.2, there is a noticeable improvement in
model fit. The R2 for plastic increases from 0.51 to 0.89 when removing the 4 municipalities found to
have a significant proportion of seasonal residencies.
One could argue that the increased model fit could be due to a reduction of sample size, this is not the
case with the glass material fraction. With 18 observations, the model correlation coefficient for glass
is 0.78. When increasing the sample size to 127 observations (accounting for glass collection from
2014-2023 and removing seasonal outliers), the R2 improves to 0.85. This suggests the improvement
stems from better data structure, not just a smaller sample size.
As Šomplák et al. (2023) note, there is no universal waste modeling technique or method. However,
in their review of other studies, MLVR modeling was found as the most common. Additionally, they
noted that GLMs had a tendency to provide the best results for regional level data, which is supported
by the strong model performance presented in this report. Linear regression waste models often
use socioeconomic factors, such as age, life expectancy, and population, as explanatory variables,
as demonstrated in Ghinea (2016). Although this study identified some sociodemographic variables
with significant explanatory power, a review by Rybová (2019) noted the complexity of these relation-
ships. The MVLR in this study found several sociodemographic factors significantly associated with
recyclable waste generation. However, these variables only accounted for 9% of the total variation
in waste generation. The current study benefits from access to two high quality and comprehensive
data sources: Statistik Danmark and VF waste data. These datasets provide the foundation for a ro-
bust and accurate model. However, future projects could leverage additional sociodemographic data
available from Statistik Danmark, particularly when employing different modeling approaches, such
as S-curve modeling for future waste prediction, as highlighted by Ghinea (2016).
While the model performs well and shows clear improvements from B.1 to 3.2, the metal material
fraction is an exception. The modeling of metal collection does not benefit from removal of outlying
seasonal municipalities, or from an increase of observations to account for steady state collection
behavior, as was the case with glass. The difference in collection rates between the two housing
types is significant as well, and contradicts the results from the Gladsaxe picking analysis Tables 2.4
and 2.5. Initially, it was theorized this could be the result of metal collection primarily occurring at
RDCs, demonstrated in Figure 3.3. Edjabou et al. (2019) note that those living in SF homes may be
more likely to visit an RDC than those living in MF homes, which could also help explain these results.
However, even after combining the sum of waste collected from the TC scheme and from the RDCs,
model performance for metal is still noticeably worse than most other fractions (Table 3.3. Modeling
of metal collection could be better suited for a non-linear model, however this was not explored due
to the time constraints of this project.
SF and MF homes have different total waste collection rates, as presented at the bottom of Table
3.2. This can primarily be explained by difference in the average occupants for the different housing
types, which are presented in Table A.4. Table B.9 depicts a much less drastic difference when the
total collection rates are presented per capita. Waste generation rates presented in Table 3.4 are
comparable to ones presented in a waste density analysis by Econet A/S (Claus Petersen, 2021).
However, the resulted presented in that report include additional material fractions, such as textile
waste and food packaging, which were not included in the scope of this report. Further comparison
between the two studies could be done with further information on how the generation potentials were
obtained in the Econet report.

Recycling Rate Calculations
The recycling rate of 48.8% presented in Table 3.7 is slightly higher than the 44.3% recycling calculated
by VF. This 4.5% discrepancy can likely be attributed to the different calculation methods used. VF
has calculated the recycling rate using ”loss rates” provided by a report prepared by Danish EPA
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2020). These loss rates were defined as generic estimates to determine the amount of
waste recycled, but do not differentiate generation source. For example, food waste has an estimated
loss rate of 85%. This is slightly more than the calculated segregation efficiency for SF in the Gladsaxe
picking analysis, and significantly more for MF homes, as presented in Table 2.6. However, both
calculation methods are generally in agreement with 2022 45.7% recycling rate stated by the European
Environment Agency (European Environment Agency, 2024). Regardless, all three percentages are
below the 55% target set by the WFD. More up to date waste data could provide a clearer picture of
how close the Capital Region is to achieving the 2025 WFD target, especially in light of the separate
collection mandate in 2021.
As shown in Table 3.7, the material fraction with most potential for improving the overall recycling
rate is food waste. Of the 228035 tons of waste generated, nearly one-third of that is food waste, as
shown in Figure 3.5. Of the more than 70000 tons, just over half is recycled. A 2023 publication by
the European Environment Agency also highlights the need for DK to recycle more food waste. Of the
7 countries with higher recycling rates than DK, 5 of them had a high coverage food waste collection
scheme (European Environment Agency, 2023).
Increased recycling of food waste must begin at source segregation, regardless of housing type. In
addition to the Gladsaxe study, other waste characterization studies have noted the significance of
food waste in the residual bin. Høj and Karup (2024) found that ”food waste constitutes a larger
proportion of the food waste collected in residual waste compared to the proportion of food waste
in the food waste scheme”. A separate waste sorting analysis in Herlev found the residual waste
stream contained 25% food waste for SF homes, and 39% for MF homes (Overblik: Affaldsanalyse
i Herlev Kommune 2023) (Overivew: Waste Analysis in Herlev Municipality 2023), 2024). These
studies, in parallel with the results presented from the Gladsaxe study (Tables 3.5 and 3.6), show that
improvements must be made for both SF and MF homes to reach the recycling targets set by the
WFD.
Life Cycle Assessment
The LCA results presented in 3.8 shows clear advantages for recycling when considering climate
change impacts. For recycling, the majority of the environmental benefits are derived from substi-
tution of virgin material. This is especially apparent for metal, as production of virgin aluminum and
steel are environmentally intensive processes. The net benefit from plastic recycling is the second
highest. In addition to substituting virgin plastic, recycling plastic avoids the fossil CO2 emissions that
are associated with plastic incineration. Paper and cardboard both show negative carbon emissions
from incineration, which can primarily be attributed to the energy recovery from the incineration plant.
However, as the Danish electricity and heat mix continue to shift towards more renewable energy
sources, this benefit will shift to become more of a burden. Lastly, food waste shows the smallest
net benefit from recycling. This can be attributed to two main reasons. The first is that any carbon
emissions associated with food waste incineration are considered ”biogenic”, meaning that they do
not count for climate change impacts. The second reason is the potential for emissions of GHGs
during the anaerobic digestion process. Even small leaks of methane into the atmosphere can lead
significant negative climate impacts, as methane is a potent GHG.
The results in Table 3.7 present that there is a relatively small potential for improvement for both metal
and glass. Given that plastic has such a high net benefit from recycling and that their is still approxi-
mately 40% that could still be recycled, increasing plastic recycling would be the most environmentally
friendly and likely the easiest path towards reducing emissions. However, an additional 24000 tons
of waste must be recycled to reach the 60% recycling target set by the WFD. Even if all plastic were
to be recycled, this target would still not be reached. Therefore, increased recycling of the remaining

three recyclable material fractions should be emphasized. Though there is a high net benefit from
recycling paper and there is still potential for improved recycling, this material fraction should not be
the focus. As the total quantity of paper decreases (Figure 3.2), the total savings from recycling all of
the incinerated paper will likely decrease. Therefore, it would be more feasible to increase efforts for
cardboard and food waste recycling, given that these material fractions make up a larger proportion
of the total waste stream and their generation rates do not appear to be declining.
4.2
Limitations
Waste Characterization Study
The main limitation of this study is the fact that one waste characterization study is used to represent
the source segregation behavior of the entire region. The Gladsaxe study included 105 SF homes
and 175 MF homes, sampled over the course of a few months. The entire region consists of 183991
SF homes, and 199137 MF homes. This means that the behavior of less than 1% of the total SF and
MF homes is being used to represent the behavior of the remaining 99%.
Life Cycle Assessment
Limited time and resources prohibited a full and comprehensive waste LCA. A more detailed LCA
that includes waste collection, more geographically and temporary relevant downstream treatment
technologies, and a sensitivity analysis could help further identify areas for improved sustainability.
Additionally, only the climate change impact category was considerd in the body of this report. A
future LCA would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion adn review of the additional impact
categories presented in Appendix C of this report.

Conclusion
Summary of Key Findings
This study presents a MVLR model used for estimating waste collection rates from SF and MF house-
holds in the Capital Region of Denmark. Model performance improved substantially when municipali-
ties with a high proportion of seasonal residencies were excluded, as the model could not account for
these households as proper waste generation sources. Inclusion of the municipalities with a signifi-
cant proportion of seasonal residencies led to significant over or underestimation of annual collection
quantities.
After developing collection coefficients for each of the material fractions, the results from the Glad-
saxe waste characterization study were applied to back calculate the waste generation rates for SF
and MF homes. Waste generation rates and the results of the Gladsaxe study were then used to
calculate system performance relative to the recycling targets defined by the WFD. Recycling rates
were determined as slightly higher than those calculated by VF, but still less than 2025 target. These
calculations identified food waste as the material fraction with the greatest potential to improve the
recycling rates and help DK meet future WFD targets.
An LCA was conducted to determine the net benefit of recycling 1 ton of waste rather than incineration.
In conjunction with the recycling rate calculations, this LCA demonstrated that plastic presents the
greatest opportunity for environmental savings and improving progress to increased recycling rates.
Although the net benefits of diverting food waste from incineration are less pronounced, doing so still
results in climate-related environmental savings and progress to the WFD recycling targets.
Significance and Implications
The MVLR model demonstrates that high-quality housing and waste data can significantly explain
annual waste collection quantities. However, municipalities with a large proportion of seasonal resi-
dencies can negatively impact model performance, as the model was unable to accurately represent
them as generation sources, and consistently underperformed in predicting collected waste quantities
in municipalities where this dwelling type was prevalent.
The framework used for the model is both scalable and modular. While this study focused on house-
hold waste in the Capital Region, the model could easily be expanded to model other geographic
areas. Given access to high-quality housing data, waste data, and representative waste characteri-
zation studies, the methodology used in this study could easily be scaled to encompass all of DK.
Limitations
The main limitation of this study is the use of one waste characterization study to model the source
segregation behavior of the entire Capital Region. Without other waste characterization studies to
compare this to, it is uncertain if the source segregation behavior is typical and representative of the
entire region.
Additionally, a full waste LCA was not conducted. A preliminary LCA evaluating shifting treatment
away from incineration to recycling was conducted primarily on climate change impacts. Future work
could expand this study to consider more aspects of the WM system, as well as additional impact
categories.

Recommendations and Future Work
Improvements in the WM system could stem from increased source segregation of both plastic and
food waste. Plastic waste presents the biggest opportunity for reducing the climate impact of the
WM system, though increased plastic recycling alone is not enough to reach the WFD recycling tar-
gets. Food waste has a much smaller net benefit from recycling, yet a significant proportion of food
waste could be diverted from incineration to recycling. To improve the performance, both environ-
mentally and for recycling rates, both material fractions should be targeted as areas for improved
source-segregation.
Future work should focus on further investigation of metal modeling, as the model performed poorly for
this material fraction. Additionally, a more comprehensive LCA including more of the WM system and
considering different impact categories could highlight other environmental savings besides climate
change.

Life Cycle Assessment
Modeling Framework
In LCAs, the modeling selection of the proper modeling framework is paramount for ensuring reliable
and sensible results (Laurent et al., 2014b). In LCAs, their are 4 classifications of situations, as defined
by the International Life Cycle Data (ILCD) Handbook (European Commission – Joint Research Centre
– Institute for Environment and Sustainability, 2010). Situation A is defined for studies for micro-level
decision support. Situation B is defined for larger decision support, and situations C1 ad C2 are
defined for accounting scenarios, without decision support. To facilitate the selection of a scenario,
Figure C.1 is used and discussed below.
Figure C.1: Modeling framework decision tree. Illustration from Laurent et al., 2014b
The first question to be answered is if any decisions are to be taken from the results of this LCA. Given
the objective of identifying areas for improved sustainability in the waste management system, the
answer to this question would be yes. The presence of large scale consequences in the background
system differentiates the modeling framework from either Situation A or Situation B. Though waste
incineration produces electricity and district heating, the scale at which this is produced is assumed
to be negligible to the development of future energy markets in Denmark (wind and solar energy, for
example). Additionally, this study does not argue for the construction of new incineration infrastructure
or similar measure that would drastically alter the production of waste-to-energy. Therefore, Situation
A is deemed the most suitable for this project.

EASETECH Modeling
Incineration
To model incineration in EASETECH, the EASETECH process ”Waste to energy plant, generic, DK,
2012 [with substitution]” process was selected. This process was developed based on the incinera-
tion plant that VF operates. The plant was modeled as 95% efficient, recovering 73% of the lower
heating value as heat, 22% as electricity, and the remaining 5% lost. The ”bottomash quenced with
wastewater” node was added to model the recovery of metals from the bottom ash, which were then
sent for recycling. The heat and electricity substitute the average Danish mix, which is defined later
in this subsection.

Phase 1: Goal Definition
Intended Application and Goal
The intended application of this LCA is to evaluate the environmental impacts of the waste manage-
ment system in Copenhagen, Denmark, in the year 2025. As mentioned previously in the introduction
of this report, Danish and EU legislation require the separate collection of waste, and managing waste
by the principles of the waste hierarchy and life cycling thinking (Parliament & of the European Union,
2008) (Parliament & of the European Union, 2018) (Ministry of the Environment and Gender Equality,
Denmark, 2024). Therefore, this study is intended to evaluate the separate collection and treatment
of waste, to evaluate which practices could be encouraged and which could be improved.
Limitations and Usability of Results
The results of this study should only be applied to the given region (Denmark in the present day,
given the importance of local conditions when evaluating the results of an LCA Laurent et al., 2014b.
Also, this study does not consider textile waste, hazardous waste, battery waste, or garden waste, so
the results from this study should not be extrapolated or otherwise missinterpreted to include these
fractions.
Target Audience
This LCA is primarily conducted with the target audience of VF. A secondary audience of this re-
port could be the municipalities who co-own VF, but ultimately it will be VF who facilitates whatever
recommendations come from this report.
Disclosure to the Public
This LCA study will be primarily be used for internal use at VF. As VF is the primary waste manager
for the region, it is expected that the results of this study will be shared with relevant stakeholders.
Commissioner of the Study
The commissioner of this study is VF. As previously mentioned, other influential actors include the
municipalities that co-own VF, the companies that are contracted to collect waste for the individual
municipalities, and the citizens that must properly sort and dispose of their waste in the VF managed
system.
Modeling Framework
In LCAs, the modeling selection of the proper modeling framework is paramount for ensuring reliable
and sensible results (Laurent et al., 2014b). In LCAs, their are 4 classifications of situations, as defined
by the International Life Cycle Data (ILCD) Handbook (European Commission – Joint Research Centre
– Institute for Environment and Sustainability, 2010). Situation A is defined for studies for micro-level

decision support. Situation B is defined for larger decision support, and situations C1 ad C2 are
defined for accounting scenarios, without decision support.
The first question to be answered is if any decisions are to be taken from the results of this LCA. Given
the objective of identifying areas for improved sustainability in the waste management system, the
answer to this question would be yes. The presence of large scale consequences in the background
system differentiates the modeling framework from either Situation A or Situation B. Though waste
incineration produces electricity and district heating, the scale at which this is produced is assumed
to be negligible to the development of future energy markets in Denmark (wind and solar energy, for
example). Additionally, this study does not argue for the construction of new incineration infrastructure
or similar measure that would drastically alter the production of waste-to-energy. Therefore, Situation
A is deemed the most suitable for this project.

To evaluate the waste management system, a functional unit must be defined as the basis for compar-

FU was selected for this study:
1 ton of source segregated household solid waste, composed of residual, food, paper, cardboard,
