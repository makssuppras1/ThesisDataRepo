Abstract
Nowadays, the drastic rising of energy demand motivates the investigation of alternative energy sources to substitute
conventional fossil fuels with adequate, reliable and environmentally friendly alternatives. Nevertheless, cost and
efficiency play significant role in the development of new energy technologies.
Under these circumstances, hydrogen production is gaining more and more attention. In particular, the development
of proton-conductiong ceramic (PCC) membranes for hydrogen separation has become a high priority for scientific
research. This Master Thesis focuses on PCCs application in selective H2 pumping. Specifically, it is an effort
to investigate three challenging topics referring to PCCs applications and proposes feasible and efficient ways to
addresses them.
Firstly, the formation of cracks on the electrolyte layer was examined and a crack formation mechanism was
proposed. Specifically, the cross-section of three-layered symmetrical PCC membranes was examined with Scanning
Electron Microscopy (SEM) in order to observe the formation of cracks on the electrolyte as a function of storage
duration and conditions and thermal treatment. It was concluded that reduction makes a significant difference in
maintaining the cohesion of the electrolyte regardless the storage duration and conditions.
The second part of this project concerned the sealing of the symmetrical PCC membranes for hydrogen separation,
where different types of sealing glasses were studied and a final proposal was formed. It was a preliminary effort
to seal the PCC membranes using different kinds of glass and ferritic stainless steel. The most suitable sealing
candidate was proved to be a chemical-name glass, called V11. V11 was applied in a paste form and provided good
adhesion and formed a dense and uniform sealing layer. Nevertheless, there were signs of reactivity at the interface
between the PCC membrane and the sealing glass. As a consequence, further EDS analysis was performed.
The last part of the project aimed to design a PCC membrane single repeating unit (SRU) that could facilitate and
address the challenges caused by the special properties of the symmetrical PCC membrane. The design was based
on a previous SOFC SRU stack design, developed at DTU Energy.

1.
Introduction
In recent decades, the energy demand for any kind of transportation has increased. There is an urgent need for
cheap, reliable, robust but also more environmentally friendly technologies that could solve this problem in the
near future. Under these circumstances, hydrogen cells and hydrogen production technologies earn more and more
attention.
The main goal of this Master Thesis is to introduce a symmetrical membrane for hydrogen separation able to extract
hydrogen with 100% selectivity from any conventional, H2-containing fuel. The materials used to fabricate these
symmetrical membranes are proton conducting ceramics (PCCs) [1],[2],[3],[4] which allow selective pumping of
high –purity H2 from any H2-containing gas fuel in one step. This technology is a potential breakthrough for mobile
applications, since H2 can be directly pumped and supplied to Polymer Electrolyte Membrane Fuel Cell (PEMFC)
stacks, without the need and limitation of a storage tank [5].
Figure 1: left: working principle of PCC membranes, right: PCC membrane reactor and PEMFC system [5]
Basically, high H2-content gas is produced by the PCC membrane reactor with steam reforming of a conventional
fuel. After the production step, H2 is electro-chemically pumped through the proton conducting membrane. Since
H2 selectivity is up to 100% to protons, pure hydrogen is obtained on the other side of the membrane. The demand
for steam and electric power is met by the downstream PEMFC. It is essential to stress that the whole process is
completed in one step giving the system remarkable simplicity, robustness, low-cost and efficiency [6],[7] compared
to conventional steam reforming technologies [5].
As for the conventional hydrocarbon steam reforming, it is the most well-known and economically efficient method
for hydrogen production, with numerous applications in industry. Nevertheless, it is a rather complex process
and it can be summarized in two main reactions: Steam Reforming (SR) (Eq.1) and Water Gas Shift (WGS) (Eq.2).
Steam reforming is performed under steam conditions, in a temperature range of 800-900 °C and pressure 15-30
bar. Additionally, it is supplied externally with heat and Ni-based catalysts are used. Assuming steam reforming
of natural (mainly consisting of CH4) (Eq.1) [8],[9],[10],[11],[12]:
CH4(g)+H2O(g) −−⇀
↽−−CO(g)+3H2(g)
(1)
Water Gas Shift (WGS) happens in two stages: a high temperature stage (350-400 °C) with the presence of a
CrFeO3-based catalyst [11] and a low temperature stage ( around 200 °C ) with the presence of a Cu–Zn cata-
lyst [10],[11]. This two-stage reaction can be summarized in Eq.2, following the steam reforming of CH4 (Eq.1)
[8],[9],[10],[11],[12]:
CO(g)+H2O(g) −−⇀
↽−−CO2(g)+H2(g)
(2)
Moreover, the final product (H2) needs additional purification if high purity is required [11],[12]. Although,
hydrocarbon steam reforming is highly efficient, it is energy-consuming and produces very high amounts of CO2 [8].
The material used for fabricating the PCC membranes is a PCC layer permeable only to H+ ions which al-
lows selective pumping of H2 theoretically up to 100% [5]. Y-doped solid solutions of Barium Zirconate-Cerate
1

(BaCe1–x–yZrxYyO3–δ, BCZY) are the most studied PCCs [1],[16],[18],[28],[36]. Dense BCZY membranes are ob-
tained through sintering [1],[16], [18],[28],[36]. Until now these membranes were produced in small dimension
(typically 2 to 4 cm2 [19]) and planar geometry, but transport applications require much higher power density which
entails larger planar area of PCC membranes [5].
The fabrication of planar and symmetrical PCC membranes with area size higher than 100 cm2, has already been
done at DTU Risø Laboratory. Specifically, there were produced PCC symmetrical membranes with surface area
of 135 cm2 (Figures 2 and 3).
Figure 2: Symmetrical PCC membrane with surface area equal to 135cm2 (Image provided by Stéven Pirou)
Figure 3: SEM cross-section view of reduced symmetrical PCC membrane with visible porosity on the support layers and fully dense

Specifically, the produced symmetrical PCC membranes consist of a layered structure of BCZY27
(BaCe0.2Zr0.7Y0.1O3–δ)/ NiO (50-50 %vol)//BCZY27//BCZY27/NiO (50-50 %vol)) where BCZY27/NiO is the
composition of the ceramic-metal (cermet) support and BCZY27 is the dense electrolyte. The electrode composition
was chosen based on Nickel’s notable catalytic performance in H2 production and its good electronic conductivity
[8],[14], [15]. Additionally, the composition of the electrolyte was developed after the optimization of the amount
of Y in order to enhance proton conductivity and the presence of Ce in order to ameliorate sintering. Consequently,
the PCC membranes will pump electro-chemically H2 as part of a novel application which will be manufactured
for the first time. Under this concept, PCCs can be characterized proportionately by the operational principle and
properties of the PCFCs. Specifically, they [20]:
• can operate with a wide variety of fuels in the temperature range of 500 - 700 °C
• have high electrical efficiency
2
==PAGE:11==
• perform direct oxidation of fossil fuels (anode), no intermediate step for hydrogen production (e.g. steam
reforming)
• contain solid electrolyte (no danger of drying unlike Proton Exchange Membrane Fuel Cells (PEMFCs) and
not possibly leaking unlike Phosphoric Acid Fuel Cells (PAFC))
Their main difference compared to the SOFCs is the conductivity of protons through the electrolyte layer,
while PCFCs and PCC membranes excel in faster start-up time, equipment longer lifetime and low-cost material
exploitation [20].
Figure 4: SOFC and PCFC different ionic mobility through the electrolyte [21]
It should be mentioned that it is the first time that a mobile application in the scale of 100 KW electricity demand,
could be supplied without the need of a storage unit. For instance, Toyota Mirai is an average compact electric car,
powered by a 114 KW PEMFC stack and has average H2 consumption of 1.05 kg/100 km [22]. If considering a H2
flux of 5 ml/cm2 [7] produced by a PCC membrane stack, with membrane area of 135 cm2/ PCC membrane, approx-
imately 300 PCC membranes would be required to meet the hydrogen demand of this car. It is therefore a feasible
proposal to use this technology to power electric transportation, since higher H2 flux is a realistic and achievable goal.

1.1.
Ceramic proton-conducting materials
Ceramic proton-conducting materials were discovered in 1981 by Iwahara et al. [26],[4],[3],[29],[27],[1],[30]. Since
then, a lot of work has been done on this special category of materials, because of proton’s small size and the
complexity of their physico-chemical mechanisms [4],[26],[27],[3].
Proton conducting materials are characterized by protonic conductivity (H+) in the intermediate temperature range
(300 – 600 °C). This is possible because the proton activation energy is much lower than the respective activation
energy of oxygen ions (O2–), typically from 0.6 to 0.9 eV. [1],[27],[3],[26]. Moreover, they combine high performance,
efficiency and stability at intermediate temperatures, finding application in several fields like gas separation [26],
protonic ceramic fuel cells and electrolysis [19], [45],[46],[48] or catalytic membranes for hydrogen separation [7],[6].
Some of the first proton-conducting oxides studied, had perovskite crystal structure; orthorhombic crystal structure
for Strontium Cerate (SrCeO3 –) or Barium Cerate (BaCeO3 –) and cubic structure for Barium Zirconate (BaZrO3 –)
[27],[26],[3]. In addition, other oxides have also exhibited proton conductivity like binary rare earth oxides (fluorite
structure [23]) or ternary oxides (pyrochlore structure [66]). Nevertheless, one of the highest proton conductivity
has been reached by perovskite-structured oxides, with the general composition BaCe1–x–yZrxYyO3–δ (referred to
as BCZY) [4],[3],[26],[1].
Figure 5: Indicative representation of BaCe1–xYxO3–δ crystal structure (perovskite) [52]
The indicative perovskite structure of proton-conducting ceramics is ABO3, where A represents the alkaline
earth site (Ba, Sr and Ca) where B stands for tetravalent transition metals or rare earths, with Ce or Zr being usual
candidates. Proton conductivity is enabled by doping the B sites with trivalent cations (e.g. Y, Nd, Sm, Yb, In, Eu,
Gd, etc.) as explained in the following section. This phenomenon can take place at quite low temperature (in the
range of up to 600 °C) and are characterized by low activation energy (about 0.3–0.6 eV) [27],[1],[3].
When they are used on hydrogen fuelled cells, proton-conducting electrolytes outweigh the respective oxygen-ion
conducting ones, since the water is produced at the cathode (i.e. no fuel dilution), making them suitable choice for
mobile applications. Moreover, protonic ceramic fuel cells [19],[38],[47],[48],[49],[50] and proton-conducting ceramic
membranes [6],[7] for hydrogen separation can also operate on a variety of fuels (ammonia, methane, propane,
ethanol [19]). Hydrogen is formed by steam reforming and is separated from H2O, CO2 and CO2 and CO mixture
[19],[38],[48],[6],[7].
Nevertheless, chemical stability and high proton conductivity are essential and it is challenging to combines them.
Two indicative examples are the Barium Cerates and the Barium Zirconates. One the one hand, Barium Cerates
exhibit high proton conductivity but also chemical instability, since they react with a number of gases (steam, acidic
gases) which are usually produced during the fuel cell operation. On the other hand, Barium Zirconates are found
to be chemically stable in the presence of CO2 or steam, but they require high sintering temperatures (in the range
of 1800 °C) which decreases proton conductivity because of high grain boundary resistance and Barium under
stoichiometry [1],[3],[28].
Specifically, the proton-conducting ceramic materials are characterized by high level of rather complex physico-
chemical mechanisms and thermodynamics, secondary/parasitic transport phenomena, material incompatibility,
4
==PAGE:13==
significant intolerance under different operational conditions and low fuel efficiency quite lower than the theoretical
[26],[1],[27],[3].

1.1.2.
Transport mechanisms in proton-conducting ceramics
After the first indication of proton conductivity in 1950s, several transport mechanisms have been suggested. Three
of them are briefly presented below:
• One of the first nominated mechanisms, assumed that the proton transport was achieved by hydroxyls OH–
as vehicle. This theory was based on the fact the small size of the hydroxyl ions (smaller that the oxygen ions
O2–) could lead to low activation energy [27].
• Another mechanism asserted that protons were transported by water or ammonia moving molecules in the
form of H3O+ or NH4+ [27].
• The current prevailing transport theory is the Grotthuss/free proton mechanism (T > 300 °C). According to
this mechanism, the proton acceptor sites do not move and proton conductivity appears when protons jump
among acceptor sites (oxygen vacant sites). More specifically, protons reorientate around the oxygen sites
(leading to lower activation energy) and then jump to the nearest oxygen acceptor site [27],[35],[3].

• In high-purity materials (e.g. ZrO2), it comes as an inherent material characteristic
The space charge layer model offered a new explanation of the conductivity evolution. It introduced the idea
that a grain boundary consists of a positively-charged core and two adjacent space charge layers (λ*) (Figure 9).

a consequence of oxygen vacancies’ boosting. Additionally, the grain boundary core magnitude belongs to the
atomic scale (lower or comparable to 1 nm) and can be assessed by Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM). On
the other hand, the space charge layer works as a charge balancing area where the acceptor dopant diminishes

All in all, space charge layer concept explains convincingly the conductivity decline at the grain boundaries.

1.1.4.
Fabrication challenges
BaO evaporates easily under high sintering temperature resulting in Barium understoichiometry and therefore in
poor conductivity [1],[36],[40].
After sintering at around 1800 °C, the grain size increased proportionally to the grain boundary resistance. This
happens because, since the alkaline earth sites in a perovskite-structured oxide ABO3 (A: alkaline earth (e.g. Ba,
Sr, and Ca,), B: tetravalent element (e.g. Ce, Zr)) remain vacant, the dopant substitution is enhanced, leading to
decreased oxygen vacancies [1],[28]. Additionally, the conductivity decreases with increasing the Zr content. As
a result there is need of lowering the sintering temperatures while maintaining high densification. This can be
achieved with sintering aid such as NiO [1],[28] and/or substituting some Zr4+ by Ce4+ [1],[36].
As a result high densification and conductivity should be achieved but the sintering temperature is impossible
to be drastically lowered by following conventional sintering methods [16],[28]. Additionally, high density and
conductivity should be combined with stability as well.
It is known that Y-doped Barium Zirconates show stability under CO2 and H2O conditions, while they have low
conductivity. Y-doped Barium Cerates have exactly the opposite characteristics [18],[28],[27].
Therefore, BCZY27 was used to prepare the PCC support and electrolyte tapes in order to balance stability with
conductivity.

2.2.
Fabrication of symmetrical PCC membranes
Tape-casting is a well-proven method used to produce thin-layer ceramic tapes. These tapes can be further laminated
to form multi-layered samples. In principal, the desirable suspension (slurry) is uniformly deposited on a moving
polyester film [54] while its flow is controlled by a doctor blade. The produced tapes are generally from 10 µm to
a few millimeters in dry thickness. The length of the tapes is only limited by the amount of slurry prepared. Figure
10 presents schematically the tape-casting process [53]. It can be seen that the suspension is poured in the reservoir
and as it passes under the doctor blade, the tape is casted on a polymer film [53]. Mixtures of solvent are used in
several studies to have a better control of the rheological properties and of the drying speed [57].
Figure 10: Schematic illustration of a tape-casting system [55]
Figure 11: Tape-casting facilities at DTU Risø Campus
Although, the tape-caster function seems quite simple, the characteristics of the tape depend not only on the
casting process but also on the drying and the suspension preparation/composition. Each of these parameters should
be strictly determined and controlled in order to obtain the desirable properties. Starting from the tape-casting
and drying conditions, the tape thickness depends on the gap height between the blade/blades and the film, the
tape-caster velocity and the drying conditions.
In the present project, tapes with different thicknesses were produced. Thin electrolyte tapes were casted using
a single blade, while the thicker support tapes were prepared with a dual blade system. Moreover, the tape-casting
velocity was set at 45 cm/min for the electrolyte tapes and at 20 cm/min for the support tapes in order to adapt the
casting shear rate with the viscosity of the slurries. As for the drying conditions, it was ensured that the tapes dried
in a controlled environment (ventilation chamber and controlled temperature) to avoid defects caused by unstable
drying conditions.
12
==PAGE:21==
Moving on to the slurry preparation/composition, there are several requirements that the produced suspension
should meet. It should be a colloidal suspension characterized by:
• homogeneity, uniformity and stability
• adequate mechanical robustness and flexibility (essentials for efficient shaping and further processing)
These characteristics can be achieved by mixing the raw powders with some organic additives (dispersants,
binders and plasticizers) [55].
At this point, the role of the slurry components should be further described. Powders are the main ingredients
of the slurry, the organic additives have an assisting role in maintaining an efficient network among the powder
particles to form a tape with appropriate properties that can later be sintered. The particle size of the powders has
a significant influence on the shrinkage behavior of the tapes. It is therefore very important to analyze the particle
size of powders and to keep them homogenous. The smaller the particle size, the higher the surface area, leading
to higher shrinkage and the need of a larger amount of organic additives [55].
The solvent needs to ensure the adequate dissolution of the organics (dispersants, binders and plasticizers), while
not reacting with the powders. Moreover, the solvent should evaporates with a specific speed, allowing smooth
drying and hindering the development of defects. Too fast drying leads to the formation of a skin on top of the
tape, which may induce defects like cracks, nevertheless too slow drying will also lead to such defects [56],[58],[59].
The use of solvent mixtures also increase the ability to dissolve all the organic additives of the slurry. However, to
avoid differential volatilization only azeotropic mixtures evaporating as a single liquid must be used [56].
The main role of the dispersants is to prevent the agglomeration of particles and to homogenize the slurry. On
the other hand, binders build and enhance the chemical system network by building long polymer chains during
drying. Lastly, plasticizers offer flexibility to the tapes by either breaking the long-chains created by the binder
and/or by preventing frictions in between the polymer chains [55].

1 %wt of NiO as sintering aid) surrounded by two BCZY27/NiO (50-50 %vol) support layers, were prepared by
tape-casting, lamination and co-sintering. Table 5 describes the composition of the slurries. Categorization of the

3.1.
Crack formation in the electrolyte – Origin and possible solutions
This section of the study is a consequence of the following observation: SEM analyses of symmetrical PCC mem-
branes proved that directly after sintering, the microstructure of the electrolyte is intact, nevertheless, after a few
days of storage, similar SEM analyses performed with the same samples revealed cracks on the electrolyte. This
observation represents a serious problem since it means that the PCC membranes are non-functional after only few
days due to cracked electrolyte preventing the protons mobility and causing gas leakage. Having that as a starting
point, a series of experiments were prepared in order to find out what is the cause of the cracks and how can this
challenge be solved.
Based on literature, the formation of cracks is closely related to chemical expansion due to water uptake [18]. That
happens when proton conducting materials are exposed to different water vapor conditions and their volume
expands. Consequently, stresses grow on the interfaces and eventually lead to cracks. According to some studies,
the stresses grown on the interfaces can be partially removed by reducing the dense BCZY-NiO phase in porous
BCZY-Ni support [18]. In addition, the reduction of NiO in Ni decreases the thermal expansion coefficient (TEC)
mismatch between the support layer and the electrolyte. This is accrued by the ductility of Ni that leads to plastic
deformation and not to crack formation due to thermal mismatch.
Based on these hypotheses, the following experimental procedure was performed. First, in order to find out
when the cracks appear in the electrolyte, PCC symmetrical membranes were prepared, and analyzed by SEM
directly after sintering, after 1, 3, 7 and 10 days of storage in atmospheric conditions in the laboratory. Three
PCC membranes were analyzed for each storage duration. Secondly, PCC membranes were sintered and re-
duced in 5% H2 atmosphere (balanced with N2). Three PCC membranes were analyzed by SEM directly after
reduction, and after reduction followed by 1, 3, 7 and 10 days of storage (Table 7). Lastly, the effect of controlled-
hydration was investigated by heat treatment under controlled partial pressure of water. PCC membranes were
sintered and heat-treated in moist air containing 1.5% of water. Hydration was an attempt to verify if controlled
water uptake would lead to a full hydration state that could potentially chemically stabilize the samples and
prevent cracks in the electrolyte. Three PCC membranes were analysed by SEM directly hydration, and after
hydration followed by 1, 3, 7 and 10 days of storage. After each of the three experiments detailed and named
above((i) sintered samples, (ii) sintered and reduced samples and (iii) sintered and hydrated samples) three ad-
ditional samples of each type, were stored for 10 days in a vacuum bag in order to limit the hydration caused
by the storage in atmospheric conditions in the laboratory. These nine extra samples were likewise analyzed by
SEM.
It should be mentioned that the preparation of the samples for SEM analysis was kept very simple, given the
amount of samples that should be prepared and analyzed. The samples were only polished and wiped with some
ethanol. Direct contact with water was avoided, since humidity uptake was under investigation and ethanol was
preferred to clean the cross-section area because it evaporates really fast.

3.2.
Sealing experiment
Sealing is a fundamental step to manufacture a fully functional PCC membrane stack. It is essential since it ensures
that [61]:
• the fuel and the oxidation agent (in case of a membrane the pure extracted hydrogen) will not get mixed
and/or will not react with the air surrounding the stacks
• mechanical bonding and adequate electrical insulation will be reached between the different stack parts
Nevertheless, sealing is a complicated procedure, which involves several variables to be taken into account.
To begin with, it is a priority that the PCC membrane stack manufacturing ensures good electrical contact between
the layered components regardless the changes that may occur because of the vast temperature range during the
operation of the stack. This aim can be achieved when the materials involved have similar TECs, in order to eliminate
the arising thermo-mechanical stresses between the different interfaces [62]. Specifically the thermo-mechanical
stresses can be result of [63]:
• restrained thermal expansion and/or contraction: if the axial motion of a part is constrained by another one,
thermal stresses arise. When the temperature increases, they appear as compressive stresses whereas when
the temperature decreases, they appear as tensile stresses.
• temperature gradient: heating up and/or cooling down as well as standard operation can lead to inho-
mogeneous temperature distribution, causing temperature gradients from e.g. fuel inlet to fuel outlet, or
from stack top to stack bottom. Consequently, thermal stresses are generated because of restrained free
expansion/contraction of the different elements that form the solid body.
• thermal shock of brittle materials: since ceramic materials can hardly deform plastically when thermal stresses
appear, it is most likely to develop brittle fracture and lead to extensive crack formation and propagation.
They are more susceptible in fracture during cooling down since the thermal stresses are tensile.
Moreover, significantly different TECs may lead to inconsistent adhesion or contact because of disproportionate
changes in the thickness of the different parts under intense temperature changes. The sealing materials in particular
should not only satisfy the TEC constraint but also have some inherent properties in order to be efficient for the
sealing purposes. In detail, they should be characterized by physico-chemical stability and robustness under the
operational conditions. However, except for their material properties, they should meet the requirements of competi-
tive cost and easy application in inexpensive stack assembling methods. Based on the aforementioned requirements,
sealing glasses or glass-ceramics with transition temperature Tg close or above the operation temperature seem to
be a quite satisfying [62].
Given the sealing requirements and constraints, three glass types were chosen as potential sealing materials (Table 2).
In order to assess the sealing efficiency of these glasses, four different experiments were conducted were different
glasses and metallic parts were assembled/jointed examined.
In the first set of experiments, the adhesion to the symmetrical PCC membrane and possible chemical interaction
between the PCC membrane and the glass were investigated. The second set of experiments was dedicated to
investigate the adhesion between all three joining partners, the PCC membrane the glass and the steel.
In the last two experiments, the sealing efficiency of the chosen glasses on different coated metals was investigated,
and a larger PCC membrane sample was jointed. Here the goal was to resemble a sealing geometry, which would
be used in the stack design described in Section 3.3.
All the prepared samples were embedded in epoxy, and the polished cross sections of the samples were carbon-
coated for SEM/EDS microscopy analysis. In Table 8 the TECs of the different materials and the (Tg) of the glasses

3.3.
PCC membrane stack design
The PCC membrane stack design was based on a SOFC design, internally developed at DTU Energy. This design
was chosen as a starting point, since both the PCC membrane separators and the SOFCs share many design
characteristics. In addition, an actual SOFC stack based on the prototypic design, was successfully manufactured
and tested in the past, giving the advantage of knowing which characteristics and materials should be maintained
and which need to be improved or modified.
Firstly, the structure of the Single Repeating Unit (SRU) in the SOFC design is described, followed by pointing out
the challenges that arise, when this design is applied in the case of the PCC membrane stack.
In the SOFC stack model, the cell is sealed onto a frame (Figure 61) and counter the cell are placed contact foams
(Ni for the cathode side and CuMn for the anode side), forming a ‘cassette’. The cassettes are placed between
the interconnects and the cathode and anode frames (Figure 62). The cathode and anode frames have dedicated
channels which control the flow of the fuel and the oxidant towards the cathode and the anode respectively (Figure
63). The design can be operated in counter or current flow.
Figure 61: Schematic representation of the SRU in the SOFC stack
Figure 62: Cassette structure in the SOFC SRU design
41
==PAGE:50==
Figure 63: left: SOFC cathode frame, right: SOFC anode frame
This design is adequate for the SOFC stack, since the dense electrolyte of the cell is sealed towards the metallic
cell frame. In this case, the sealing prevents gas leakage and/or mixing and the cell frame ensures that the cell will
stay in place (Figure 64 left half).
On the other hand, if this design would be applied in building a PCC membranes stack, both the electrodes would
have a porous structure after reduction which means that only the electrolyte would remain dense. That would
lead to gas leakage and/or gas mixing with consequent operational failure of the stack, since the PCC membrane
is sealed with the electrode towards the metal frame (Figure 64 right half).
Figure 64: left: SOFC sealing; right: PCC membrane potential sealing problem
In order to address this major operational challenge, some design modifications were applied.
It should be pointed out that modification were made only on the single repeating unit (SRU). They were based
on the characteristics and challenges that the symmetrical PCC membrane induces to the stack design. Specifically,
the porosity formed on both the electrodes after reduction, is a challenge, which if not taken into account, would
lead to gas mixing and/or leakages and poor electrical insulation (short circuits). It was crucial that the new design
perspective should focus on covering this weakness without compromising or substituting the already tested and
efficient parts from the original design.
For the metallic parts, Crofer 22H is considered to be used, as it has already been tested in the original SOFC stack
with satisfying behavior. One design constraint was the thickness of Crofer 22H to be used. Since Crofer 22H sheets
are only available/in stock with a thickness of 0.2 mm and 0.3 mm, only these two options were considered. The
contact foam was chosen to be Ni for both electrodes (symmetrical structure). The thickness of the foam, which
can be easily varied by pressing or rolling, was reduced from 0.2 mm down to 0.1 mm.
The footprint of the stack remained the same (140×140 mm2), but the design of the anode frame, the cathode frame,
the PCC membrane frame and the interconnect were modified. These modifications were based not only on sealing
the problematic porous areas, but also on the different dimension of the PCC membrane (Subsection: 2.2). The PCC
membrane dimension were chosen to be 100×100 mm2, since the manufacturing of membranes of this size is now
possible (Section: 1, Subsection: 2.2). Additionally, the PCC membranes have an average thickness of 0.3 mm.
Based on the testing results of the internal SOFC project, a sealing area of 0.5 mm of width is considered adequate
and robust enough. The main design difference compared to the SOFC stack can be found in the cell frame. While
the cell in the SOFC stack is sealed with the electrolyte onto the cell frame, in the PCC membrane suggested stack
design the membrane frame surrounds the membrane without being in direct contact. In this way, the frame acts
more like a spacer.
For the inner dimension of the PCC membrane frame, it was considered to have slightly larger dimension 101×101
mm2) compared to the membrane actual dimensions (100×100 mm2) so it could surround the porous PCC membrane
42
==PAGE:51==
edges, keep the PCC membrane in place and accommodate any potential dimension differentiation. Furthermore,
the thickness was changed to 0.3 mm so it is comparable to the thickness of the PCC membranes (0.3 mm).
In principle, the anode and the cathode frame maintain their efficient air/fuel hole design. The thickness of the
frames was reduced to 0.3 mm, and the area inside the frame were chosen to be 90×90 mm2. Here the idea was
to use as much active membrane area as possible, but still provide a sealing area of minimum 5 mm on each side
for sealing the frame to the PCC membrane. Based on the inner dimension of the anode/cathode frames, the foam
dimensions and the fillet area (fuel/air channels) of the interconnect were chosen to be 90×90 mm2 as well.

in manufacturing and then the new PCC membrane stack should be tested under operational conditions. Another,
practical challenges will be the robo-casting of the sealing and the complicated pattern that should be meet (Figure 65).
Robocasting or Direct Ink Writing is an additive manufacturing method, which should be suitable for applying the
complex pattern of sealing material (the gap between the membrane and the membrane frame, Figure 65). In this
method, a slurry-based ink is extruded directly from a nozzle. The nozzle mobility is controlled by a computer and
the printing follows a specific CAD model. As the object is printed layer by layer, it is built because the extruded
filament is deposited on top of the already printed part. Robocasting is correlated to rheology and not solidification
or drying as fused deposition techniques. Up to now a wide variety of inks have been successfully printed and
among them also ceramic-based inks [74], [75]. Moreover, the thermal expansion of the different parts should be
thoroughly investigated and taken into account because it may affect the dimensional characteristics of the stack.

4.
Conclusion and Outlook
To sum up, the work of this project provided some promising results and useful information were obtained, referring
to major challenges arising with the use of PCC membranes in stacks.
The first part of this Master Thesis focuses on the formation and propagation of cracks on the electrolyte layer of
symmetrical PCC membranes, related to three significant parameters: the duration of storage, the ambient humidity
and the influence of the thermal treatment applied. It was quite crucial to obtain a clear understanding of the cracks’
formation since, it drastically affects the quality of the PCC membranes and it can define the suitable storage and
stack assembling conditions that can prevent the formation of cracks. A series of SEM analyses were conducted on
sintered, reduced and hydrated samples, at different time intervals (directly after the end of the thermal cycle, after 1,
3, 7 and 10 days of storage in atmospheric conditions and after 10 days of storage in vacuum conditions) and under
different storage conditions (atmospheric conditions and vacuum conditions). The SEM results come into agreement
with literature, pointing out that water uptake causes chemical expansion of the support and stresses grow on
support/electrolyte interfaces. These stresses lead to crack formation and propagation. Even if controlled hydration
was tried as an attempt to saturate the samples in water, they still kept on getting hydrated during the storage. On
the contrary, heat-treatment in reducing atmosphere seems to provide some stress relief for the electrolyte thanks
to the increased porosity driven by the reduction of NiO to Ni. The porosity helps to accommodate the volume
expansion and the respective stresses. Moreover, the vacuum storage does not appear to assist in preserving the
electrolyte consistency. Consequently, it can be claimed that reduction is the most convenient way to hinder the crack
formation in the electrolyte. It opens a new possibility for long-term storage and stack building under non-oxidizing
conditions. Nevertheless, long-term observation of the reduced samples is needed to verify the beneficial effect of
reduction. It would be also worthy to repeat hydration with higher water percentage and/or longer dwell hydration
time, and then conclude about the effect of controlled hydration on the crack formation.
Moving on to the sealing, several different glasses and two different ferritic stainless steels were tested. It is worthy
to say that neither the experiment’s parameters nor the glass paste fabrication were completely optimized. Yet,
most of the glasses reached some level of adhesion regardless the fact that most showed cracks in the glass. In
particular, V11 glass seemed to have some desirable properties when it was applied in the form of a paste. It
ended up providing satisfying adhesion while a dense and uniform layer of glass was formed between the joining
parts. It was found that V11 had reacted with BCZY27, and EDS analysis was performed to investigate the formed
microstructures on the interface of the glass and BCZY27. SEM-EDS analysis revealed the formation of needle- and
cubic-shaped microstructures with different chemical compositions. The needles were composed of Ba, Zr and O,
while the cubes contained Ce, Zr and O and some traces of Y. The reaction mechanism is not fully understood and
further research should be done, but it seems like BCZY27 decomposed into BaZrO3 and CeO2-ZrO2 and the excess
of barium diffuses into the sealing glass. Furthermore, the expansion, the chemical and mechanical stability of the
reacted area should be tested under operational conditions.
The last part of this study was dedicated to the design of a single repeating unit (SRU) of a PCC membrane
stack. The initial inspiration came from the design of an SOFC stack as part of an internal project in DTU Energy
department. The rationale behind this idea was that the PCC membrane and the SOFC stack design share many
design characteristics. The main challenge though, is the efficient sealing of the PCC membrane since after reduction
the supports on both of its sides are porous, making the prototypic SOFC design unsuitable. As a result, a new
design was developed in order to protect the stack form electrical short circuits, and leakage/mixing of gases. The
usual requirements of good electrical contact between the PCC membrane, the contact foam and the interconnect
were satisfied in the new design as well. Additionally, the financial aspect was a major point, since the design should
have been implementable without extreme machining cost and by using specific ferritic stainless steels. By taking
into account the aforementioned parameters and the potential application of sealing by exploiting robo-casting,
the final suggested design was formed. It consists of symmetrical components (interconnect, contact foam, foam
frame) which juxtapose on both sides of the PCC membrane, which is surrounded by a frame as well. The PCC
membrane SRU design resulted in a slightly decreased height compared to the prototypic SOFC one. This is a very
encouraging progress, since lower height can be beneficial for higher power density per volume.
As a last comment, the results and the knowledge obtained through this study are meant to be a preliminary step
towards a better understanding in the relatively new field of PCC membranes and raise attention on the efforts
to deploy to effectively solve the coming challenges.
