Abstract
Projection-based Material Discrimination using Deep Learning
Machine- and especially deep learning, has been used in typical x-ray computed to-
mography applications, such as image reconstruction, image enhancement and image
feature detection. To our knowledge, only one study has performed feature detection
and analysis by directly using CT projections data without any reconstruction and
the problems that comes with it. This project presents deep learning models for de-
tection and segmentation of a list of materials in the sinogram domain avoiding any
partial volume, beam hardening or motion artifact introduced during reconstruction.
After detecting and segmenting the materials one can perform threat assessment to
evaluate if there is need to intervene. Further studies are needed for this sinogram
domain analysis approach to become practical for industrial use.
Keywords: computed tomography, deep learning, machine learning, sinogram

Introduction
It might be the radical innovations that gets remembered years down the line, but
an argument can be made that it is incremental innovation that has made us so
advances as a species. The ability to make various processes a little better, faster
or cheaper is how we in the span of a few years have signiﬁcant faster phones,
computers and more eﬃcient cars. The small upgrades are almost invisible for the
uninitiated and that can be said about this project as well. Your bags are being
CT-scanned at the airport to attempt to detect what is inside them without opening
them. CT-data has been widely used in medical, industrial and other applications
since its ability to view what is inside an object without destroying it.
Multiple projections of a patient or object are acquired over a scan in a circular
trajectory. That projection data, which is also known as sinograms, are then re-
constructed, from sinogram to the volumetric image domain, to e.g. cross-sectional
images. These reconstructed images are then passed on to various experts to inter-
pret the contents of this object or patient.
The reconstruction process has some drawbacks. It causes information loss due to
interpolation, sub-optimal statistical weighting and shading due to beam-hardening
and scattering.
If the scanned object moves between the individual projections,
it can cause inconsistencies between the projections and cause severe artifacts and
image blur, leading to inaccuracies in detection and characterization tasks. A single
projections is typically acquired in ¡ 1 ms - thus the inaccuracies are introduced in
the reconstruction process, by putting together the data from diﬀerent views.
This means that working on the raw data, could help to avoid or suppress motion-
induced errors. Another thing to consider is that reconstruction algorithms in a
wide range of CT scanners can be very computationally intensive.
So, in principle, analyzing CT data directly in the sinogram domain could help us
provide more accurate and consistent results.
This opens up a diﬀerent set of problems - the main challenge is that sinograms have
superimposed data and information related to a single feature is distributed across
the whole projection data. The beneﬁts mentioned in this section is what motivated
this project, in which we seek to demonstrate if it is possible to segment and classify
diﬀerent materials from a list directly on the raw output of the CT-scan with deep
learning techniques.
A demonstration that the techniques in this project also outperforms volumetric
image domain analysis of CT data is beyond the scope of this project and is a

1.1
Related Work
In this section we will introduce the already existing research within the ﬁeld - the
foundation of our research. We will go over the current state-of-the-art methods
and their limitations.
1.1.1
U-net architecture
The ﬁrst people to introduce the U-net architecture[1] was Olaf Ronneberger, Philipp
Fischer and Thomas Brox in their paper ”U-Net: Convolutional Networks for Biomed-
ical Image Segmentation”. They set out to design a network that required few images
to train and wanted to outperform the prior best method (a sliding window con-
volutional network) on the ISBI challenge for segmentation of neuronal structures
in electron microscopic stacks - they won that challenge. Since then the U-net has
been a popular tool for fast and precise segmentation in computer vision.
Several papers has altered the standard U-net in search for improvement. A ver-
sion called UNet++[2] consistently outperforms the baseline models for the task of
semantic segmentation across diﬀerent dataset and backbone architectures, as well
as enhances segmentation quality of varying-size objects - an improvement over the
ﬁxed-depth U-net. [3]
In 2019 the C-UNet[4] was introduced, which is a U-Net that incorporates the
Inception-like convolutional block, the recurrent convolutional block and dilated con-
volutional layers. Their experiment results show that the proposed method achieves
better accuracy and more robust segmentation results than UNet.[5]
1.1.2
Deep learning models on CT data
To our knowledge, only one other paper has performed deep learning directly on
CT projection data.[6] They present neural network methods for blood vessel de-
tection and characterization in the sinogram domain, thus avoiding the need for
reconstruction - just like we want in this project.

Figure 1: Overall workﬂow and terminology of the proposed methodology. A ﬁrst
neural network detects a blood vessel in a sinogram. Based on its centerline a vessel-
only sinogram is extracted. A second neural network characterizes the vessel-only
sinogram and estimates the vessel lumen cross-sectional area.[6]
As seen in Figure 1 they use two networks in their work ﬂow. The network that is
similar to the ones used in this project is the model shown in Figure 2 - it is not
the U-Net, but it almost has the same structure. It doesn’t use maxpool to reduce
the spatial dimensions of the image and it doesn’t use interpolation to increase the
spatial size again. It uses kernels with no padding to reduce the feature mapping
dimensions. The network also utilizes shortcut connections, where the output of
the convolutional layers are fed into the corresponding deconvolution layers. This is
the same idea as skip connection, but it doesn’t work the same way, since we have
no deconvolution layer in a U-Net. The activation functions usen in the network is
also ReLU, with the exception of the last layer - just like the U-Net. It was trained
with Adam optimizer and MSE as loss function. We also use Adam, but have also
trained models with BCE loss. The kernel size in the CNN layers are also 3 x 3
except the last layer, which has kernel size 1.

sinogram domain vessel map. Three pairs of convolution and deconvolution layers
were used in the simulation studies and ﬁve pairs of convolution and deconvolution
layers were used in the phantom experiments and the clinical data experiments.[6]

Background
2.1
Artiﬁcial Neural Networks
In this section I will go over what makes a deep learning model. Deep learning is
in part based on Artiﬁcial Neural Networks (ANN), which is inspired by the human
brain’s use of neurons to make a decision based on input information. Deep Neural
Networks (DNN) allows machines to solve complex problems by gathering informa-
tion from a potentially highly varied and unstructured data set, thus training the
model. Some of these DNN models have produced results that in some cases ex-
ceeds human performance in speech recognition, natural language processing, audio
recognition and lastly what this report is about; computer vision.
2.1.1
Fully Connected Networks
The most traditional and simple architecture for deep learning is the fully connected
network. This architecture is made is made of layers li with i ∈[1, L], all containing
neurons - the small unit that computes part of the layers output.
The input of a given layer, li, takes the output of the previous layer, li−1 and applies
a non-linear transformation to compute the output of layer li.
This non-linear
transformation is controlled by a set of parameters (often called weights) θi for each

2.2.2
Kernels
An image is a multidimensional matrix. An image has width, height and depth, like
a matrix has width, height and depth. For a standard RBG image, where we have a
channel for each color - Red, Green and Blue, respectively. We use a smaller matrix
to blur, sharpen, detect edges and perform other image processing tasks - a kernel
or ﬁlter.
We can think of the image as a big matrix and the kernel of a tiny matrix. The tiny
matrix sits ontop of the big matrix and slides from left-to-right and top-to-bottom.
With each step the tiny matrix takes on the image, it performs a mathematical
operation, convolution - performing various image processing tasks.
Diﬀerent kernels perform diﬀerent image processing tasks, such as blurring, edge
detection and sharpening. All of these operations use deﬁned kernels by the user -

speciﬁcly designed to perform the particular function. It can be average, Gaussian
or median smoothing for blurring, Laplacian, Sobel or Scharr for edge detection.
All of these are pre-deﬁned and is set from the get go. However, we are able to
automaticly deﬁne our own ﬁlters to perform tasks that help us in our deep learning
goals. By applying convolution ﬁlters, nonlinear activation functions, pooling and
backpropagation, convolutional neural networks are able to learn ﬁlters that can
detect high-level objects, such as faces and animals deep in the network. This is
done by utilizing the information the lower-levels has found, such as edges, blobs
and structures as building blocks for the high-level objects.

The output value is stored in the output image at the same (x, y)-coordinate as the

Stride
Consider Figure 8, earlier we talked about the convolution operation as
”sliding” a small kernel across an image, stopping at each pixel, computing element-

to step onto a new area in the input. If we only move one pixel to the right (and

parameter highly changes the spatial size of the input as seen in Figure 9 and 10,
which makes the concept of stride more clear.

Zero-padding
Another tool to manipulate the size of the spatial dimensions of
the input image in a CNN network is zero-padding. When applying zero-padding
we ”pad” the borders of the image with zeros to increase the spatial dimensions -
this is controlled by the parameter P. Zero-padding is used in conjunction with a
convolutional layer. When using a 3 x 3 size kernel for the convolution, the spatial
size of the output is 2 pixels smaller than the input. This can be adjusted by zero-
padding the input image with P = 1 - adding a border of zeros around the input
image.
Figure 11 visualizes the concept of zero-padding more clearly. The 5 x 5 input image
is zero-padded with parameter P = 1 to increase the spatial size to 7 x 7. We then
convolve it with a 3 x 3 sized Laplacian ﬁlter, which decreases the size of the output
by 2, thus results in the 5 x 5 output to the right.

decreasing the spatial size too quickly to be able to learn useful patterns in the

The new size of the (width) image is calculated like so:
Woutput = ((Winput −F + 2P)/S) + 1
(5)
Putting all the parameters together, we can compute the output spatial size as a
function of the input volume size, W (Width - assuming input image is square), the
ﬁlter size, F, the stride S and the amount of zero-padding, S.
The new spatial size obviously needs to be an integer, since there needs to be a
whole number of pixels, so the output of 5 needs to be an integer. If the output
isn’t an integer, then some of the parameters needs to be adjusted.

• Accepts input of size (Hinput, Winput, Dinput), since the input sizes are normally
square, it’s common to assume that Hinput = Winput.
• Requires four parameters:
1. K: The number of ﬁlters
2. F: Size of K ﬁlters - assumed square, so the size is often F x F.
3. S: Stride.
4. P: Zero-padding - number of borders of zeros added to the input image.

– Woutput = ((Winput −F + 2P)/S) + 1
– Houtput = ((Hinput −F + 2P)/S) + 1
– Doutput = K

2.3.4
Learning Rate
Each time the weights are updated the size of the change is largely dictated by the
learning rate η - therefore it can be challenging to choose a proper learning rate. If
it is signiﬁcantly smaller than the optimal value, then it will take too long to reach
the ideal weights. However, if it is signiﬁcantly larger than the optimal value, the
weights might take too large a step and overshoot the minimum we are headed to
and thus have a oscillating loss graph. A reasonable start would be to run your
model with η = 0.001 and then see how the loss graph behaves and change the
learning rate accordingly.
To summarize - the learning rate measures how much the current gradient aﬀects
the next step, while momentum aﬀects how much the past steps aﬀect the next
step.

2.3.6
Epoch and Learning Curve
When training we iterate through the training data set a number of times - this
deﬁnes an epoch. One epoch is when the algorithm has iterated one time through
the data set - one time forward and backwards through the network. To suﬃciently
minimize the training loss error the number of epochs is generally large to allow the
model to train on suﬃciently much data. The two common learning curves that are
used are:
• Training Learning Curve: This learning curve is calculated from the train-
ing data set. This is what the model is trying to minimize and thus it is a way
to gauge how well the model is learning.
• Validation Learning Curve: gives us a tool to gauge how well the model is
at generalizing, since it is calculated based on the validation data set, which
is what the model is not directly minimizing.
When training a network, the sizes of the weights change as the training goes on.
There are generally three cases of where the weights can land:
• Underﬁtting is when the model has not been training for suﬃciently long
time - a common case is that the number of epochs or the learning rate is too
low.
• Overﬁtting refers to a model that has been over-trained or needs regulariza-
tion when the output of the model is decided by a few, large weights. A model
with large weights tends to be more unstable than a model with balanced
weights. A sign of over-ﬁtting is when the training loss is very low compared
to the validation loss - thus the model is bad at generalizing.
• Optimum refers to the point of where we are satisﬁed with the state of the
weights and the model performs suﬃciently on both training and validation
data.
2.3.7
Batch Size
When training, we don’t take in all the training samples at once - we are grouping a
few samples together. We use that batch to estimate the error gradient, which is a
hyperparameter called batch size. It deﬁnes the number of samples to work through
before updating the model parameters. Batch size aﬀects how fast a model learns
and how stable the training behaves.
20
==PAGE:29==
There is a trade-oﬀbetween the accuracy of the parameter update and the compu-
tation time needed to perform an update of the models parameters. This depends
on the amount of data available in the training set. There are three options of batch
size:
• Batch mode is when batch size = size of training set, thus using all samples
to create one batch:
θt+1 = θt −η ∗∇θJ(θ)
(19)
Here we see that we have used all the training data to update θt. This means
we take a very precise step in the most correct direction to the minimum -
however, the computation time needed to go through all these training samples
can take unnecessarily long time.
• Stochastic mode is when batch size = 1, thus each batch contains one sample:
θt+1 = θt −η ∗∇θJ(θ, xi, yi)
(20)
Here we see we only use xi and yi to update thetat. This means that every
batch’s computation time is very low, but there is a high probability that each
step isn’t very generalized. This can mean we take huge steps in the wrong
direction, and thus have a very messy training curve.
• Mini-Batch mode is when batch size is larger than one and less than the
size of all the training data. (1 < batch size < size of training set):
θt+1 = θt −η ∗∇θJ(θ, xi:i+n, yi:i+n)
(21)
Here we see that we use xi:i+n and yi:i+n, where n is the batch size, to update
thetat. This we have n samples in out batches, which increases the accuracy
of the steps, while still keeping the computation time for each batch down.

• We talked brieﬂy about it in the stochastic mode above. It will be harder to

result in ﬂuctuating gradient directions compared to training with larger batch
sizes.
To summarize: The mini-batch variant draws beneﬁt from the lesser computation
time of stochastic mode and the improved gradient estimation of the batch mode.

2.3.8
Optimization Algorithm
An optimization algorithm is needed when the cost function is obtained. It is used
in an attempt to minimize the loss. In the batch size chapter earlier, we introduced
the SGD optimizer (Eq. 20).
Stochastic gradient descent has troubles with navigating in ravines, where the man-
ifold curves more steeply in one dimension that others. This can be supported by
introducing momentum to the optimizer - this helps SGD accelerate in the relevant
direction and softens oscillations. This is done by adding the fraction γ to past
update vector:
vt = γ · vt−1 −η · ∇θJ(θ, xi, yi)
(22)
We thus get the updated equation 20, which leads to:
θt+1 = θt −vt
(23)
Just like real momentum, it increases the step size if the gradient is the same di-
rection as other gradients, while it reduces updates for dimensions whose gradients
drastically changes direction. This achieves faster convergence and reduces oscilla-
tion [13]
However, if the momentum is too high the algorithm might miss the local minimum.
This is where we turn to the Adam algorithm.
It is an adaptive learning rate

3.2
Augmentation
3.2.1
Background
According to Goodfellow regularization is ”(..)any modiﬁcation we make to a learn-
ing algorithm that is intended to reduce its generalization error, but not its training
error”.[19] So regularization seeks to reduce the testing error perhaps at the cost of
the training error - but that seems like a worthwhile trade-oﬀ.
Data augmentation is another type of regularization - where we slightly change their
appearance before passing them into the network for training. The end result is a
network that consistently sees ”new” training data points that is generated from the
original data point. More data is almost always better and data augmentation is a
way to artiﬁcially generate more training data.
What is Data Augmentation
A wide range of techniques is used to generate new training samples from the original
data set. This is done by keeping the class label on the data point, but perturbing the
data point. The goal when applying data augmentation is to increase generalizability
of the model. Our network is able to learn more and more features, given it is
constantly seeing new, slightly modiﬁed versions of the input data.
When performing data augmentation in the context of computer vision, data aug-
mentation is very intuitive. We can do all kinds of diﬀerent things with volumetric
images, such as:
1. Translations
2. Rotations
3. Changes in scales
4. Shearing
5. Horizontal and vertical ﬂips
Applying small changes of these transformations will slightly change the image, but
the content of the image will still be recognizable. It is important to note that
you shouldn’t transform your data too much, so the content of the image will be
changed.
Lets assume we have a task of identifying right hands - then a horizontal ﬂip would
change a picture of a right hand, to appear as a left hand - thus we can’t keep the
”right hand” class label.

Architecture
The network architecture used in this report is illustrated in Figure 35. It consists
of a left side and a right side. The left side is the contracting path, and the right
side is an expansive path. It consists of ”blocks” of two 3x3 convolution layers,
each followed by a rectiﬁed linear unit, ReLU, and a 2x2 max pooling operation
with stride 2 and no padding. At each downsampling step we double the number of
feature channels, while with each upsampling step we halve the number of feature
channels and concatenate the corresponding feature map of the contracting path.
The expansive side also consists of convolution blocks with two 3x3 convolutional
layer, also followed by ReLU activation function. The ﬁnal layer has the number of
materials we want to detect as channels.
The original U-net [1] has four expansive and contracting blocks, but for better
memory management, we only use three in this project.

than for example the Sigmoid function, which requires exponential operations.

4.2
Hyperparameters
Hyperparameter have a high impact on the size and performance of the network.
Having the optimal hyperparameters would achieve the highest accuracy as possi-
ble, while keeping the networks size as small as possible and avoids overﬁtting the
data. Having a large amount of hyperparameters is both a blessing and a curse - it
provides a high degree of freedom and allows the network to be ﬁne-tuned. How-
ever, the hyperparameter space is exponentially increased with the number of free
hyperparameters.
Lets say you have 3 parameters we want to ﬁnd the optimum for,Nparameter, where
we try 10 diﬀerent settings per hyperparameter, lgrid. Then we would have to train:
Nmodels = lNparameter
grid
= 103 = 1000 models to train.
To keep things relatively simple and reduce the complexity of the design task, we
have set these parameters ﬁxed: activation functions, optimization algorithm, weight
initialization, kernel size, class weights. The parameters that has been changed in
this project is: batch size, cost function, learning rate and epochs.

4.2.1
My hyperparameters
When running binary data sets, the ”default” loss function is BCE and when running
non binary data sets the loss function is MSE.

6
Discussion and Future work
The aim of this project was to detect and segment materials based on CT data using
deep learning. In this section, we refer to the various challenges faced throughout
this project.
The result of this project have shown the feasibility of the U-net encoder-decoder
networks to detect and segment various materials based on CT-data. The models
performed much better when only 2 materials could be present in the container.
This makes sense, since it’s easier to keep control on 2 materials instead of 25.
6.1
Validation problems
It is diﬃcult to gauge how well a model performs - especially with spectral data.
We have used intersection over union in the project, which is normal to use in
segmentation tasks. There is however some problems with the way we calculated
IoU in this project. We have calculated IoU like this:
TP
TP+FP+FN . This requires
both the target and the output to be binary. We have blindly set everything that
is positive to 1, which can cause some discussion. What if a value in the target
is 0.001? Then that would be interpreted at a positive as much as a 0.999, which
is debatable. In an ideal world any positive-valued output would be considered as
positive, but since we are dealing with non binary targets and outputs, this can
cause some trouble.
Solution 1: Every time the model outputs something, we can attempt to apply
unsupervised clustering on that output. If the true negatives (in the output) follow
a certain distribution, can we perhaps learn that for each output and then binarize
the output with that knowledge.
Solution 2: When we are training the model, we can check each channel of the
output to see of the true positives, negatives, false positives and negatives follow a
certain distribution and then try to generalize how a certain material behaves as an
output.
The low IoU numbers should be taken with a grain of salt. It is calculated by this
formula
TP
TP+FN+FP . So if a data set has a material in 10% of all its channels, then
the maximum IoU value would be 0.1. 1 for the channel where there is something
to detect, and 0 for all the other channels. There isn’t a fair way to determine how
worse the 25 material models is compared to the 2 material models because of this
trait of IoU.
Solution: You decide to only calculate the IoU when there is something in the
77
==PAGE:86==
target - so it is possible to get a true positive. Then you could more fairly compare
the models.
6.2
Change problem instead of model
In this project we have tried diﬀerent parameters and architectures in attempt to see
how well we could segment materials based on CT-data. We could try to change the
problem instead of trying to optimize the model. In this project we have only tried
to train our models with either 2 materials or all of them. We could try to gather 2
random materials and see if there is some materials there are easier to detect than
whiskey and acetone.
Another thing we could do is to try to ﬁt a simple CNN to the data to see how well
that model classiﬁes the CT-data.
6.3
Uncertainty Quantiﬁcation
When working with machine- and deep learning it often involved working with
incomplete and imperfect data.
This uncertainty can be classiﬁed to two cate-
gories:
• Statistical uncertainty is the variance of an outcome each time the same
experiment is executed.
This type of uncertainty is hard to handle - CT
numbers as absulute values is unreliable. Aleatory uncertainty is given by the
diﬀerent manufacturers measuring CT numbers in phantoms, CT detectors
and even reconstruction kernels.
Suggestion: since aleatoric uncertainty comes from physical systems, re-
ducing it requires inﬂuence on how data is collected. This is no problem in
this project, since all the data used here is simulated. But introducing some
aleatoric uncertainty to the dataset in form of augmentation would increase
the usability of this project.
• Epistemic uncertainty is uncertainty produced by limited data also known
as systematic uncertainty, This type of uncertainty is reducible [23].
This
could be the distribution of data in our datasets, or the distribution of shapes
of the objects in the container. We know the which distribution they follow,
but they are still random each time we create a new data point.
Suggestion: We can reduce systematic uncertainty by increasing sample size.
This will probably help the 25 material data set, since it takes more data to
spread out over 25 materials compared to 2.
Unfortunately, machine- and deep learning models will always have some error.
There is a reason ”All models are wrong, but some are useful” is a saying in the
78
==PAGE:87==
community.
6.4
Performance Perspective
We don’t have something to compare the performance of the model to. It would be
a good idea to see how well a security guard would detect diﬀerent materials and
compare them with this projects models.
Another thing to do is reconstruct the output of the model and compare the volu-
metric object.

7
Conclusion
We performed a study of direct-sinogram segmentation of materials in a container.
This is based on simulated data, simpliﬁed phantoms and pure materials with no
noise. Because of this, we did not consider complicated factors such as motion blur
or high noise scenarios. Future studies could include of more realistic features, add
more materials and direct use of real CT-data. More research is needed for a model
to be usable in a real CT-scanning machine. The intention for this project was to
serve inspiration for future works and as a proof of concept.
These model doesn’t directly do threat detection, but they are primed to ﬁnd any
material in the list of materials we have available.
Incremental innovation come in many shapes and sizes and this project is close to
being one of them. We don’t know how much faster or how accurate these models
are compared to object detection on volumetric images or how well they perform
next to a security guard who looks at reconstructed images.
In conclusion, we have developed deep learning methods to detect and segment
various materials directly from CT sinograms and thus avoided the expensive re-
construction process. Using a U-net we have detected and segmented materials in
a given slice of CT projection data. We were able to achieve decent performance
of some models to segment the materials present in the CT-data - both in terms of
shape, but also intensity.
There is no doubt that the overall results shows a promising prespective of applying
deep learning model on CT-data and detecting or segmenting various materials.
Finding the optimal hyperparameters for the correct problem will improve the pipe
line of security in the air port.
