Summary (English)
This thesis consist of making a statistical shape model of skulls from polar bears. The idea
came from earlier studies that showed that there are many anatomical diﬀerences between
polar bears from east and west Greenland.
The making of a shape model consist of several steps. The main step is to align and
obtain point correspondence between two or more shapes. In this thesis two methods are
implemented and tested. The ﬁrst method is a mix between a rigid iterative closest point
transformation and a KD-tree, nearest neighbour search. The second model was intended
to be a volumetric based approach, but as problem occurred, parts of that model was
changed, and the ﬁnal alignment is instead based on a principle component analysis.
The shape model is made using a principle component analysis that gives a representation
of the deformation of a data set. These deformation informations is applied to a mean
shape. The mean shape is obtained using a generalized procrustes analysis which is a
method that removes diﬀerent features of the shapes.
The results are given in 3 sections, one for each method of aligning. The two ﬁrst results
are based on the same alignment approach, but the diﬀerence lies in how the source and
target shapes are chosen. The last part is made using the principle component alignment
procedure. Further is the results divided into 5 subsections. One where all shapes are
matched and then sections where diﬀerent combinations of the shapes are made, based on
the information known of them.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at DTU Compute in fulﬁlment of the requirements for acquiring
a M.Sc. in Engineering.
This thesis deals with making a 3D statistical shape model of skulls from polar bears. The
main goal of this thesis is to implement a shape model and test whether diﬀerences of the
skulls can be modelled. Making a shape model includes implementing diﬀerent methods
for point aligning and matching between shapes.
The thesis consist of 10 chapters. Chapter 1 is motivation and introduction to the problem.
Chapter 2 is information of data and how the data is obtained. Chapter 3 is a detailed
explanation of the implemented matching methods. Chapter 4 is a overview of the used
methods to align and match points. Chapter 5 and 6 tells how the shape model is made
throughout a procrustes analysis and a principle component analysis. Chapter 7 is a brief
overview of how the model is made. In chapter 8 is the obtained results shown and discussed.
Chapter 9 is discussion on how to improve the method and results, and ﬁnally is chapter
10 a conclusion of the thesis.

Introduction
It is seen through history that people and animal change to adapt to way the world is
changing. Polar bears are no diﬀerent. Researches has found that polar bears are somehow
related to the brown bear found in North America and Scandinavia. See Figure 1
Figure 1: Right: http://www.cell.com/cms/attachment/2035898456/2051319356/gr1.jpg,
Found in [Le] - Left: https://beautifullybony.ﬁles.wordpress.com/2014/03/polar-bear-and-
brown-bear-skull.jpg
The polar bears have made signiﬁcant genetic changes from the brown bears to be able to
adapt to the Arctic life. This has also aﬀected their anatomy. Based on the article [Le],
and the supplementary material of that article, researchers has found anatomic diﬀerences
of the polar skull from east to west Greenland.
The national museum of history has interest in looking into details of these anatomic
diﬀerences. From Figure 2 two female skulls, one from east and one from west, can be
seen. Clear diﬀerences can be seen between the two and it is easy to point where these
diﬀerences are.
Diﬀerences between two or three skulls is somehow easy to see and describe, but when the
set of shapes becomes larger it becomes more diﬃcult as many more details must be taken
into account. The concept of a shape model is a good tool to point out diﬀerences between
a large set of shapes.

Shape modelling
As mentioned before ﬁnding diﬀerences or patters between a set of shapes can be complex.
A way of making such an analysis is to make a statistical shape model. Such a model is
based on a set of landmarks that corresponds between the shapes. In Figure 3 can two
skulls be seen with two placed landmarks.
Figure 3: The two shapes from Figure 2 with two annotated landmarks.
Landmarks are annotated manually and is a very time consuming approach. In some set of
shapes this can almost be an impossible approach because of deformations of shapes or
missing parts can occur. In the sense of deformation it is meant that things can deform so
much that ﬁnding a matching spot from one skull to another is not possible. Further is
it often necessary to choose many landmarks which is why it can be a time consuming
method. This thesis therefore looks at diﬀerent ways of point matching.
When the point matching is made, the actual analysis can begin. The analysis consist
of making a procrustes analysis which is a method to removing diﬀerent features of the
shapes. Such features can be rotation, scaling, etc. After the procrustes analysis the data
is now processed such that the actual shape model can be made. The model is made using
a principle component analysis, which is a method that projects data into spaces where
most variance of the data is explained. Results from such an analysis is the concept of a
shape model.

Previously Work
Previously Work
There have been many diﬀerent works with shape models throughout the last years. The
concept of shape models are a very well know topic in computer vision.
One interesting approach to a shape model is made by Tim Cootes and Chris Taylor,
[CT04]. They developed an active shape model, which was based on a statistical shape
model which only varied in ways that was seen in a labelled training set.
Another use of shape modelling is also made by Tim Cootes et. al. [CET]. They made
an active appearance model. It is highly related to Cootes earlier work of making the
active shape model, but an AAM can be modelled such that it takes more of the original
information into account when making the model.
Project Plan - Original
At a museum in Copenhagen a collection of skulls from polar bears are gathered. One thing
that would be interesting is to make a statistical shape model of these skulls to determine
the deformation based on age, gender and location of found. Another aspect of this is to
look and determine the evolutionary change of the skull. All this is possible using a shape
model approach.
Furthermore will several methods of matching in 3 dimensions be tested and implemented.
One method that will become particularly interesting is the shape context method. This
method is commonly used in 2D but the extension into 3D is diﬃcult and time consuming.
As this is an automatic method of matching it is useful and time saving to use, when
working, this method will be experimented with.
Project Plan - Revisit
This project was from the beginning given such that a statistical shape model of polar
bears skulls was to be implemented. This includes gathering data, point matching and
shape modelling. The ﬁrst idea of point matching was to use a straight forward approach
such as simply aligning shapes and annotate points. This turned out to be a more time
consuming approach than ﬁrst thought. A more automated approach was therefore needed,
and the idea of using ICP and KD-trees came from this. Secondly was a fully automated
method, such as shape context, an idea. This idea was dropped as problems with the
equipment, the structured light scanner, occurred and took a lot time to solve. Hence a
faster implemented, but still complex method, was made. This method is a volumetric
approach where a volume is made of the shape and a transformation is estimated using an
intensity based approach. Unfortunately was there problems with the implementation of
the volumetric matching, and to solve these problem diﬀerent things was tried. One thing
was to project the data onto the space where most variance is explained using PCA. This
did not solve the problem with volumetric registration, but it turned out that using PCA
gave a good alignment and therefore is this the ﬁnal method.

Data Format
The data used for this projected is given as point clouds. A point cloud is a set of x, y
and z coordinates. Such a point cloud is in this context given as a shape. Another way to
represent point sets are by landmarks. A landmark is a set of matching points throughout
two or more shapes. In other words landmarks are needed for shapes to be analysed since
point correspondence between shapes are needed. The point correspondence problem will
be explained further in a later chapter.
Gathering of Data Using Structured Light
All data used for this problem is obtained in cooperation with the National museum of
History in Copenhagen. The data set is several scans of polar bears. The scans are obtained
using a structured light scanner which is a scanner that can record point clouds. The idea
behind the scanner is to project a certain pattern towards the object and then record
a sequence of images by two cameras while the pattern changes. By doing so a unique
binary representation of each pixel is obtained in each camera and by matching these

binary codes, correspondence throughout the cameras for each pixel is gathered. Using a
simple technique called point triangulation, the ﬁnal 3D point is obtained. This process is
repeated at diﬀerent angels of the object to get an entire 360◦image of the object. This
will result in partial scans of the object. These partial scans are then merge together using
an algorithm called Iterative Closest Point (ICP) to obtain the ﬁnal point cloud. The ICP
algorithm will be explained in details later on. The structured light scanner can be seen in
Figure 6.
Figure 6: Left: A small construction whereupon a projector, that projects the pattern,
and two cameras are attached. Right: The turning table whereon the object is placed.
Furthermore can a checker board be seen which is used to calibrate the system.
As brieﬂy explained in the caption of Figure 6 can a checker board be seen. This is simply
used to calibrate the system. The calibration consists of ﬁnding the extrinsic and intrinsic
parameters of the system. More information of calibration and in general a structured light
scanner can be found in [Aan14], [WE14] and [Wil16]. The reason to use a SL scanner is
that it uses the most of a an image sensor at every frame. A SL scanner is fast and gives
great accuracy. The accuracy depends on the scene where the scanner is, but in general it
has good accuracy. The speed somehow depends on the number of scans per angel and
which pattern that is projected onto the object. In this setup two diﬀerent patterns can
be used. A phase shift and a grey encoding pattern. In the phase shift is two sinusoidals
with diﬀerent frequency used. In the grey encoding is vertical lines with shifting width
used. To this project is the grey encoding used as this is slightly faster than the phase shift.
The grey encoding makes slightly more noise, but as each scan is processed in MeshLab,
and noise can be removed there, a faster approach was important. Furthermore was there
captured images for each 40◦. This was enough to give a detailed reconstruction.

Alignment and Matching of
Shapes
Matching or point registration is essential to make good and useful analysis of shapes.
Registration means to ﬁnd a matching points throughout shapes. The ﬁrst thing to do is
to align the shapes and secondly ﬁnd one to one correspondence between points. In other
words ﬁrst make sure that two shapes are aligned as good as possible and then ensure that
there are point-to-point correspondence, which means that taken one point on the shape,
the correspondent point should be around the same area on the other shape.
In Figure 7 can the problem of two shapes not aligned be seen.
Figure 7: Purple: shape 1, green: shape 2
The idea of matching is to aligned two shapes and ﬁnd correspondence between points.
There are diﬀerent ways of aligning two shapes. A rigid transformation includes rotation
and transformation. A rigid transformation of a vector v is deﬁned by T(v) = Rv + t,
where R is rotation and t the translation. The rotation matrix is an orthogonal matrix,
RT R = I. Further can the rotation matrix consist of reﬂection as well as rotation. This
can be excluded by forcing the rotation matrix such that det(R) = 1, [LL15]. Another
way to align shapes is using a non-rigid transformation. A non-rigid transformation can
use diﬀerent method to locally deform shapes to make alignments. A method used in a
non-rigid transformation is b-splines, [LL15]. B-splines has local support and deformation
of shapes can occur local, where a rigid transformation occurs global. In this project only
8
==PAGE:21==
rigid transformations are used.
To keep the computation time down each shape is downsampled to only consist of 100000
points each. As some shapes from the beginning consisted of over 7 million points this
was necessary to be able to compute an analysis. Furthermore is each shape recomputed
into equal sizes. This is done using a PCA analysis and then obtain the overall size of the
shape using the eigenvalues. Before this some shapes was larger than others which gave
further problems for the matching.

ICP and KD-Tree
Some information to this section is found in [LL15]
In this approach iterative closest point is used for aligning and KD-trees are used for point
matching. The idea behind the ICP is to minimize the mean square error between the ﬁxed
shape and the moving shape. If denoted fsi ﬁxed shapes i’th coordinate and msi moving
shapes i’th coordinate, where ms = Rs + t where s is the shape to be transformed and
R is a rotation matrix and t is a vector containing the transformations translation. ICP
wants to minimize the distance D which is given be the following equation,
D =

i=1
||msi −fsi||2
(1)
The basic steps of ICP are to ﬁnd closest points from the moving shape to the ﬁxed shape.
From this knowledge the algorithm then compute the transformation that minimizes the
cost function given in Equation (1). Then the transformation is applied. As the ICP is an
iterative function theses steps are continued until a convergence level is reached, which
means that D is stable at a low value. To sum up the algorithm:
• Find closest point between input shapes
• Estimate rotation and translation that align the shapes
• Transform the moving shape
• Estimate D as in Equation 1
• Continue until convergence
In general is ICP a very slow algorithm. With point cloud with a large number of points
the algorithm some how needs to be sped up. There are several method to do so. In many
implementations of the ICP algorithm a KD-tree is used to ﬁnd nearest neighbour points
between the two shapes that is to be aligned. KD-trees are a fast way of ﬁnding nearest
neighbours and are therefore useful in the case of ICP.
A KD-tree is a space partitioning data structure for organising points. It is a very useful
tool for nearest neighbour searching, which is the case that the ICP algorithm uses. When
constructing a KD-tree, in this case in three dimensions, the root of the tree will have an
x-aligned hyperplane as split. This means that at the root of the tree the data is split by
the x-variables of the data. These splits are often made at the median of the data. The
children of the root will then be split at the median of the y-variable. The children of that
new node will then be split at the z-variable. This pattern continuous. Every split consists
of a hyperplane dividing the data. Splitting in that order, a balance KD-tree is obtained,
which means that every leaf node is approximately equally far away from the root. Using a
KD-tree for nearest neighbour search can be done eﬃciently using the properties of the
tree, hence it is eﬀective to speed up the computation time of the ICP algorithm. When
performing a nearest neighbour search using a KD-tree it starts at the root node. Then
simply by looking at the x-variable the point goes either left or right in the tree. This
continuous recursively through the tree until a leaf node is reached. This node point is
then the current best. This current best is an approximation hence unwinding the tree is
necessary. At each when unwinding the following two questions are asked:

• Step one; if the current node is closer than current best, then the current node will
become current best.
• Step two; the other branch must be checked to see if any node is closer. This is done
by putting a hypersphere around the current search point with radius as the distance
to the current nearest point. If this hypersphere intersect with any of the hyperplanes
made when making the tree, the other branch must be searched. If no intersection
the entire branch at the other side can be eliminated, and the search can continue
up the tree.
In other words, a new input walks down the tree using the split computed when making
the tree. As this will only give an approximation of the nearest neighbour the search point
starts going upwards from the leaf node and asking the two above questions at each node
it hits. This continuous all way to the root node and the search is complete.
Another way to make the ICP faster and in some cases more robust is to use another
metric when minimizing the distance. In my case I have used point to point metric which
is to minimize the distance between each point. Another, sometimes, useful metric is the
point to plane metric. Here the minimizing distance is to minimize distance between a
point and the plane which is spanned be the nearest points in the other shape. Depending
on the situation and the number of points, the point to plane metric can speed up the
algorithm. In situations with planar surfaces the point to plane metric can be useful. With
the point to plane approach extra calculations are needed at each iteration, which can
cause extra computation time if the surface are complex and non planar. To speed up the
ICP further, an initial transformation can be applied. In the case of this project no such
transformation is obtained, hence this is not possible.
To this project is the ICP chosen as it is robust, i.e. will converge to a global minimum if
the number of iteration are high enough or a good initial transformation is given. As the
shapes gives no information of what a good initial transformation is no such transformation
can be used, and therefore a large number of iterations are needed. Since each shape is
equally downsampled the computation time of the ICP was kept low and could therefore
be useful for this project.

Concept of Volumetric Matching and PCA alignment
This method for matching and aligning two shapes are based on two main steps. The ﬁrst
step is to make a volume of the shape, and step two is then to use these volume to ﬁnd a
proper transformation that will align the shapes. In general the method consist of more
steps which will be given below,
• Make volume of shape
• Distance transform the volume
• Make an intensity based transformation
• Map the original data into space where transformation is valid
• Apply transformation and map back
The ﬁrst step of making the volume is ﬁrst of all to make a volume which is large enough
to contain the entire shape. The volume is of the dimensions H × W × D, width, height
and depth. The dimensions of the volume depends on how ﬁne the original data is split.
When making the volume the data’s minimum and maximum of x, y and z coordinates
are divided into ∆wide steps. Hence ∆is the parameter that decides the dimensions of
the volume. The volume will then be a grid with diﬀerent size depending on ∆. The next
step is then to make this volume of the shape by taking the original data and assign every
point to a grid point in the volume. The following equation states how every point are
assigned. This is done for all points, x, y and z.

Naive
The naive method means that shape number one is taken as reference shape and all
other shapes are aligned and point matched towards that shape. The alignment and point
matching is made using ICP and a KD-tree which is described earlier. This is very simple
and straight forward approach, and as all shapes are matched towards the same shape
one can expect a not so good aligning and matching. This method is chosen as it was a
good start and properly will give a result that is to be compared with the better and more
complex methods.
Heuristic
The heuristic method is based on the same aligning and point matching as the naive
approach. The diﬀerence lays in which skulls are matched to each other. In the naive
approach one skull was chosen to be the reference skull and all others was aligned to that.
In this method not one reference skull is chosen. Instead is it investigated to which skull the
alignment is best. This means, to begin with, one skull is taken. Then the former explained
matching approach is used from that chosen reference to all other skulls. Then it is tested
to which skull it matched best both ways. This means that two KD-trees are made, one
from each skull, reference and aligned, and then nearest neighbour search are performed
for both point set. Now it is tested how many points there are nearest neighbours from
both KD-trees. The shapes that got the highest identical point matches are then aligned
to each other. The output from the ﬁrst match is then tested to all other shapes and
procedure is repeated until all shapes is matched to another. To make this eﬃcient the
shapes was down sampled to 10000 points. In Table 0.2 can the number of points that are

The Principle component analysis is now explained using a shape approach and a summary

• Subtract mean from data
• Calculate covariance matrix of the data

It is former stated that the principle components are where most variance are explained. This
is correct, but not all components explains the same amount of variance. The eigenvalues
is a measure of the amount of explained variance and by sorting these in descending order,
and sort the eigenvectors in that order, the ﬁrst eigenvector will be the component which
explains the most variance. The second, the second most variance and so on.
To project the data with the use of the principle components, the original zero mean data
is simply multiplied with the eigenvectors, then the new data is obtained.

a set. When this is the problem it can be impossible to eﬃciently calculate the covariance
matrix since this requires to multiply two very large matrixes. Then following trick can be
useful. The trick is a way to compute a smaller matrix and from that derive the important
information of the eigenvectors and eigenvalues. The details will be given in the next

Discussion
In many cases when working with skulls, anatomic landmarks are the common choice when
aligning shapes. Anatomic landmarks are often annotated by experts in the given ﬁeld
and can therefore be diﬃcult to use when working with a thesis like this. Further is it,
as explained in chapter 1, very time consuming and in some cases impossible to use the
manually annotated landmarks. Using anatomic landmarks placed by expert can be a way
of improving the model.
When not using this manually annotated landmark approach you must ensure that the
deﬁnition of deformation regarding a rigid transformation is close to the registration that
anatomic landmarks would give. This is one reason to use the ICP algorithm as that
rigid transformation in some sense will converge towards a local optimum. This means
that no matter what the ICP algorithm will give a transformation that is useful. Other
approaches could have been used, such as the non-rigid thin plate spline approach. This is
a way of transforming point locally towards the target shape. This is often used in medical
imaging, as local deformations often occurs in that ﬁeld. Even though, it can be used to
register shapes in this context, but you should notice that making local deformations or
transformations could remove some vital information of a shape model.
Another approach to register shapes could be by using features instead of landmarks.
Feature based methods is using distinct points or extreme points on a surface, that can be
represented in certain way. Finding and matching features, and by that ﬁnding correspon-
dence between shapes, is a very complex method. As brieﬂy mentioned in the project plan
the ﬁrst idea was to look at a feature based matching, in this case shape context approach.
You can argue that the size of the set of shapes is not representative for making a shape
model because of the relatively few shapes. 27 shapes is enough to get an idea of the
possibilities of making a model, but it can be too few samples to really generalize anything.
Furthermore is the set somehow biased in the sense of more observations from east Green-
land. Further is the mix between male and female not equal. Gathering a more balanced
and larger set of shapes will make the model even better.
There are much more work that can be done in this area. Gathering more shapes will be
the ﬁrst step of making a more robust model. Next up using diﬀerent matching methods to
align and point correspond. This could hopefully give a more clear view of the distinguish
between the shapes.

Conclusion
This thesis is made in cooperation with National Museum of History in order to see if a
statistical shape model is able to model the diﬀerences that researchers has found between
skulls from polar bears from east and west Greenland.
The overall part of this thesis is based on how to align and match shapes. Here three models
are implemented in Matlab and consist of diﬀerent use of the algorithm, Iterative Closest
Point (ICP). This rigid transformation is used in two of the methods. The last method
was intended to be a volumetric method that uses a computed volume and the distance
transformation of that to make an intensity based transformation. Problems occurred while
implementing the ﬁnal method and in order to ﬁx these problems diﬀerent approaches was
used. Hereunder a principle component analysis of the data to project data into a space
with most explained variance. Studying these projections showed great alignment hence
the ﬁnal method is based on that. The Matching of points for all these models are done
using a KD-tree and the nearest neighbour search property of such trees.
The shape model is based on a standard principle component analysis which will generate
the deformation information of the data set. One of the results from the PCA analysis is
to see how many components are needed to explain a certain amount of the variance in
the data. The following Table 0.3 shows the performance of each aligning based on how
much the data can be dimension reduced to still be able to explain 90% variance.

The other analysis consist of shown the principle components behaviour while plotting them
against each other. By doing so, some clustering did appear, meaning that a distinguish
between observations was possible. The main clustering appeared when looking at either
males or females from east or west. In another ways the model was in some situation able to
show diﬀerences between skulls from east and west. As seen from Table 0.3 many diﬀerent
combinations of the data set was tested to see which one gave some information of the skulls.
As written before the main results came from looking at male and females independent
of each other. Studying males and females from the same region did not give any good
results as the main diﬀerences here would be the size and the alignment procedure included
remove that feature. In the case where all shapes are used the heuristic and the PCA
method gave the best clustering results, and diﬀerence between shapes was actually visible.
The primarily diﬀerences was on the shape and the width of the cheekbones and on the
width of the top of skulls.
The conclusion is that based on recordings of skulls using a structured light scanner,
and the implemented aligning and matching methods, diﬀerences in skulls from east and
west can be modelled using a statistical shape model. It shall be noticed that diﬀerent
deformations occurred when making diﬀerent matching approaches. Hence making such
models many things should be considered, such as, fast methods or detailed models. All
this should be considered, as diﬀerent matchings gives diﬀerent results.
