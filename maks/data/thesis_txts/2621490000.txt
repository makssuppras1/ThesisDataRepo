Abstract 
The Danish drinking water is extracted from the groundwater, but contaminated sites pose a risk to the aquifers. 
Remedial actions are performed to protect the drinking water resources and human health, however, the 
remediation technology can potentially have secondary impacts on the environment, economy, and society, 
which can be greater than the primary impact of the contaminant. The secondary impacts can be assessed 
through a sustainable remediation assessment, which applies qualitative, semi-quantitative and quantitative 
methods to evaluate the impacts. In this project, the sustainable remediation assessment was performed on a 
chlorinated ethene contaminated plume in Farum, Zealand, to evaluate the application and assessment. Five 
remediation technologies were selected for the site: three in-situ technologies with a permeable barrier, but 
with different substrates (emulsified oil and KB-1®, Provect® and KB-1®, and KMnO4), a pump and treat 
facility and monitored natural attenuation. The five technologies were assessed through a multicriteria 
assessment (MCA) and a life cycle assessment (LCA). Four different stakeholder groups participated in a 
MCA workshop to rank and score the different remediation technologies’ impacts on the indicator categories: 
environment, economy, society, and time. The stakeholder groups contributed with different perspectives, 
which were reflected in their ranks and scores during the MCA workshop. Additionally, a LCA was performed 
in the LCA tool EASETECH v. 3.3.7 to assess the technologies environmental impacts. These values 
contributed to calculate the sustainability scores for the technologies in the MCA. The results from both the 
MCA and LCA reveals that the most sustainable remediation technology is the permeable barrier with EOS 
and KB-1. However, during the remediation the formation of VC occur due to the sequential reductive 
dichlorination, which increases the risk for drinking water and human health. Therefore, the developing 
remediation technology PRB with Provect and KB-1 has potential to be applied at the Farum site, because it 
reduces the chlorinated ethenes by applying abiotic and biotic degradation. The implementation of sustainable 
remediation assessment has not gained acceptance in Denmark, due to the comprehensive efforts it takes to 
involve the stakeholders. However, the involvement of stakeholders so be considered as a supporting tool to 
select the most suitable remediation technology. A Danish framework for sustainable remediation assessment 
is therefore a necessity to make guidelines for the Danish remediation industry, which should contain 
recommendations for which methods to apply and how to select the relevant stakeholders for the specific case. 
Additionally, improvement or development of sustainable remediation technologies is essential to obtain a 
complete sustainable remediation of a contaminated site, which can be obtained by implementing LCA during 
the design of the technology. Thus, the LCA can show the hotspots of the life cycle stage for the technology 
and thereby which stage needs improvements. The LCA for the five remediation technologies for the plume 
in the Farum site, was performed in the LCA tool EASETECH v. 3.3.7., which show potential to be applied 
for modelling remediation technologies.

1 Introduction 
One of the major anthropogenic risks to human health and drinking water resources are contaminated sites. In 
Europe, the potential number of sites are 2.8 million, however the actual registered number is approximately 
650,000 of which 65,500 sites have already been remediated (Bardos et al., 2011; Hou & Al-Tabbaa, 2014; 
Pérez & Rodríguez Eugenio, 2018). The already increasing pressure on scarce resources such as drinking water 
and land for agricultural or housing purposes are further limited due to the contaminated sites and  potential 
resources are lost (European Commission, 2006a; Panagos et al., 2013; United Nations, 2015). Therefore, 
remediation of contaminated sites is necessary but costly (European Commission, 2006b). 
In the 1980s the approach of contaminated sites was to “remove all contaminations”. However, it was 
recognised that the considerable high costs could outweigh the society benefits, and the site characterisations, 
such as the geological structure of the soil, prevented the possibility for remediation. This led to the 
development of the “risk-based remediation” in the 1990s, which is applied to decide the safe and acceptable 
levels of contamination that can remain in-situ after remedial actions (Hou, 2020). 
The Danish legislation on contaminated sites was initiate in the beginning of the 1970’s, due to the concerns 
on the impacts on human health and environment from buried chemical waste deposits, and the Law on 
Environmental Protection was enacted. During the 1980’s the waste deposits gained attention, which initiated 
the mapping of oil and chemical waste deposits and the Law on waste deposits in 1990. Contaminated sites 
with pesticides, heavy metals, chlorinated ethenes and oil were increasingly registered during the 1990’s, 
which induced the Law on contaminated sites (i.e.  Jordforureningsloven LBK nr. 282 af 27/03/2017) that was 
enacted in 2000 and is still applied today. The purpose with the legislation is to protect human health and 
drinking water resources, by managing the contaminated sites systematically (i.e. mapping, investigation, 
remediation, and monitoring). Additionally, the law hold the polluter responsible for remediating a 
contamination, which means that all new contaminations from 2000 will obey to the polluter-pay-principle 
(Outzen & Andersen, 2014). 
Today, the public efforts for managing the contaminated sites in Denmark are the five Danish Regions: 
Southern Denmark, Central Jutland, Northern Jutland, Zealand, and the Capital. The Regions’ task is to map, 
conduct introductory and further investigations, remediate, and monitor the contaminated sites. The individual 
Regions are responsible to perform the tasks in each region. The mapping of contaminated sites applies two 
categories for contaminated sites: knowledge level 1 (V1) or knowledge level 2 (V2). A V1 contamination is 
identified if there have been activities on the site that can cause a contamination, while a V2 contamination is 
identified when it is documented that the site is contaminated. The V2 sites are identified through an 
introductory investigation of the sites, which will indicate the level of contamination. The further investigation 
of the site includes a characterisation and risk assessment, which will conclude is a remedial action is 
necessary. Typically, the contaminated site is management by the Regions and remediated by entrepreneurs. 
The remediated sites are monitored afterwards to ensure that contamination is remediated. The Regions have 
followed this procedure the last 13 years (Danske Regioner, 2020). 
In 2020 the Regions published a report, which estimated that 116,000 sites must be assessed and managed. 
Today, 99,400 sites have been assessed whereas several sites have been disregarded either because no 
contamination was detected, or the site has already been remediated, but 37,800 sites are registered as 
contaminated sites. In Denmark, it is estimated that 3,100 sites must be remediated to protect human health 
and drinking water resources (Danske Regioner, 2020).

Remediation of contaminated sites is performed to reduce the primary impacts a contamination pose to human 
health and drinking water resources. However, the remediation technologies can potentially have secondary 
impacts on the environment, economy, and society, in terms of emissions of greenhouse gasses, huge costs, 
and inconvenience to the local society during remediation (Hou & Al-Tabbaa, 2014; Lemming et al., 2010). 
In addition, tertiary impacts (i.e. reestablishment, reconstruction) after remedial actions also have impact on 
the brownfield (Hou, 2020). Moreover, there is also a risk of increasing the toxicity of the contamination during 
the remediation by add chemicals or enhance degradation of contaminants that makes them more toxic. The 
outcome from these considerations lead to a growing attention during the late 2000’s and sustainable 
remediation assessment was developed to apply a holistic view on the secondary impacts on the environment, 
economy, and society (Hou, 2020). The environment, economy, and society are also known as the triple bottom 
line within sustainability.  
The definition of sustainable remediation is: sustainable remediation is achieved when there is an overall 
benefit across the environmental, economic, and social aspects, while the risks associated with the 
contamination are minimised or eliminated (Bardos, 2014; Rizzo et al., 2016; Søndergaard et al., 2018). The 
environmental aspect within sustainable remediation contains management of the risks to human health and 
the wider environment (e.g. surface water, groundwater, soil and sediment, air, oceans, etc.), while minimising 
any emissions associated with the remedial actions (e.g. energy usage, greenhouse gas emissions, extraction 
of materials, etc.). These are commonly managed through a life cycle assessment (LCA) (Hou, 2020). The 
economy covers all the effects of costs and benefits that a remedial action has on a local economy, including 
workers’ wages, local employment, etc. (Hou, 2020). The society covers the impacts that the remediation has 
on people’s lives, such as the workers, the local community, vulnerable groups (kids, elder, etc.) (Hou, 2020). 
Hence, the primary impacts affect locally (e.g. ecotoxicity, human health, drinking water), while the secondary 
impacts affect both locally (e.g. production of more toxic contaminant and inconvenience during remediation), 
regionally (e.g. costs),  and globally (e.g. global warming). 
The sustainable remediation assessment is performed by selecting suitable remediation technologies for the 
specific site, and then assess these through methods that involves stakeholders (SuRF UK, 2010; Technical 
Committee ISO, 2017). The stakeholder are people how have impact on the decision or people who will be 
affected by the decision (Freeman, 1984), which is why it is important to involve the relevant stakeholders and 
have a good representation of the different stakeholder groups; key players (high interest, high influence), 
context setter (low interest, high influence), subject (high interest, low influence), and the crowd (low interest, 
low influence) (Reed et al., 2009). The stakeholders participate in the decision of a remediation technology  
for the specific site, and thereby reduce the risk of disagreement between the decision maker and stakeholders 
later in the process. Therefore, is it important to involve the stakeholders early in the assessment to ensure that 
the stakeholders gain sufficient knowledge to participate in the decision. 
The stakeholders can be involved through different methods, that are recommended for sustainable remediation 
assessment, which are qualitative and semi-quantitative methods (SuRF UK, 2010; Technical Committee ISO, 
2017). Both methods assess how sustainable a remediation technology is, by either rank or score its impacts 
on the indicator categories (environment, economy, and society) and the sustainability indicators for each 
category. The stakeholders are involved in the selection of the indicators and they rank or score the remediation 
technologies. One of the semi-quantitative methods is the multicriteria assessment (MCA), which weights the 
ranks and scores according to which indicator is most important. Hence, if the society weights highest, and a 
remediation technology has a big impact on the society, the weighted score will be big, which will imply a 
negative impact. Therefore, the remediation technology with the least negative impacts will obtain the lowest 
score. In addition to the qualitative and semi-quantitative methods, it is also possible use a quantitative method 
such as the life cycle assessment (LCA). This method does not involve stakeholders but assesses the secondary

impacts that the remediation technologies have on the environment. A combination between the MCA and 
LCA methods is recommended to support the decision-making when selecting the most sustainable 
remediation technology (Søndergaard et al., 2018). 
The secondary impacts on environment, economy, and society from remedial actions are important to consider 
because these can potentially have greater impacts than the primary impacts. To assess the secondary impacts, 
qualitative, semi-quantitative or quantitative methods can be applied, to select the most sustainable remediation 
technology. Therefore, a contaminated site has been selected to investigate the methods that are applied in a 
sustainable remediation assessment. The contaminated site is in Farum, Zealand, and is selected in cooperation 
with the Capital Region and the consultancy company COWI A/S. The site is in an urbanised area, where 
industrial activities from 1960-1990 have resulted in leakage of the trichloroethylene (TCE), which has 
degraded to the daughter product cis-DCE (Niras & Region Hovedstaden, 2017). The contamination has leched 
to the groundwater, and the contamination thus pose a risk to the groundwater, drinking water resources and 
human health. The Farum site has been selected for the sustainable remediation due to the amount of already 
existing data, which is favourable for the assessment. 
The overall aim of this thesis is to apply the sustainable remediation assessment on the contaminated plume in 
the Farum site. The objectives are: 
1. Quantify and characterise the chlorinated solvent contamination at the Farum site, as well as select 
and review the potential remediation technologies. 
2. Develop and apply a LCA model for five remediation technologies. 
3. Analyse and assess which remediation technology is most sustainable for the Farum site, through a 
MCA and LCA. 
4. Evaluate what the benefits and challenges are when applying sustainable remediation assessment.  
With respect to the LCA of the remediation technologies, the programme EASETECH that is originally 
designed to support waste systems, is applied to investigate its potential of handling contaminated sites. 
Therefore, the sub-objective is to apply and evaluate the use of the LCA tool EASETECH on the remediation 
technologies for the contaminated site.

2 Sustainable Remediation 
The awareness of implementing sustainability in today’s society have increased the last couple of decades, due 
to the concerns related to the anthropogenic activities that globally interact and change complex dynamic 
ecosystems of which has among other resulted in climate changes (Hadley & Harclerode, 2015; Weinstein, 
2010). As a response to the increasing awareness regarding sustainability, 17 Sustainable Development Goals 
were launched by the United Nations in 2015. The aim is to develop and implement sustainability within 17 
different sectors such as gender equality (goal 5), the industry (goal 9), the ecosystems (goal 15), etc., by 2030. 
Within the UN, the applied definition and understanding of sustainability is to better link economic 
development, social inclusion and environmental sustainability together and thereby obtain the most 
sustainable approach (United Nations, 2015). Balancing the environmental, economic, and social aspects 
within sustainability is also known as the triple bottom line, illustrated in Figure 2.1.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
2.1 Development towards sustainable remediation 
A sector that have been implementing sustainability even before the UN goals were launched, is the 
remediation industry (Hadley & Harclerode, 2015; Hou, 2020). As illustrated in Figure 2.2, the development 
towards sustainable remediation on a global level has evolved from the 1970’s when there was little control 
and registration on contaminated sites. In the late 1970s and early 1980s, high-profile cases such as Lekkerkerk 
in the Netherlands and the Love Canal superfund in the USA, got attention due to the risk to human health and 
drinking water resources. In both cases, buried chemical waste caused contamination of soil,  groundwater and 
surface water in populated areas (i.e. schools, houses, suburbs). Both the population and politicians demanded 
legislative restrictions on contaminated sites to protect human health and drinking water resources, which lead 
to the implementation of the “remove all contaminations” policy (Ellis & Hadley, 2009; Hou, 2020). To 
remove all contaminations, countries in Europe and the USA started to register contaminated sites to get an 
overview of how comprehensive the issues was. However, when countries such as the Netherlands started to 
register contaminated sites, the number of sites increased from 350 in 1981 to 300,000 in 1995 (Ferguson, 
1999). Hence, even though the remediation industry was growing during the late 1980s and early 1990s, and 
contaminated sites were being remediated (e.g. dig and haul, pump and treat), constrains started to occur (Hou, 
2020). Costs, accessibility and the geological structure of the soil challenged the remedial actions, and it was 
recognised in the mid-1990s that it was infeasible to remove all contaminations (Hou, 2020). This initiated the 
development of the site-specific characterisation and risk assessment of contaminated sites in the beginning of

Within the remediation industry, the interpretation of remedial actions has been assumed to be sustainable 
because the primary impacts (i.e. from the contaminants) are removed, which results in protection of health 
and drinking water resources, and facilitate brownfields,  and thereby decrease the pressure on greenfield 
(Bardos et al., 2011; Lemming et al., 2016). However, remedial actions can have higher secondary impacts 
than the primary impacts, when e.g. a heavy fuel-based remediation technology is applied on low level 
contamination (Bardos et al., 2011). Therefore, in the 1990s, life cycle assessment (LCA) started to be applied 
to estimate the carbon footprint in connection to remedial actions (Page et al., 1999). Following these efforts, 
it was recognised that there were adverse secondary impacts on both the environment, economy, and society 
associated in connection with remediation of contaminated sites. The increasing awareness of the secondary 
impacts during the last decades, demanded the remediation industry to act on these impacts. In Europe, this 
initiated the development of sustainable remediation, which focuses on balancing the environment, economy 
and society in the remediation assessment (Hou, 2020). 
2.2 Definition of sustainable remediation 
The primary impacts from a contaminated site have local impacts (e.g. ecotoxicity, human health, drinking 
water), while the secondary impacts from the remediation can potentially impact both locally (e.g. production 
of more toxic contaminant and inconvenience during remediation), regionally (e.g. costs), and globally (e.g. 
global warming) (Hauschild et al., 2018; Søndergaard et al., 2018). These secondary impacts can potentially 
have greater impacts than the primary impacts, which has initiated the development and implementation on 
sustainable remediation assessment. The most common approach on sustainable remediation is to prevent and 
reduce the secondary impacts while removing the primary impacts, which applies a holistic view on the

3 Methods within sustainable remediation assessment 
The management of a contaminated site is generally divided in different project phases, which involves actors 
for the respective processes. Regulators and the respective landowner are active in the initial phase when a 
contaminated site is identified and needs remedial action. During the evaluation phase of remedial actions, 
consultants propose the remediation options, which are selected by the regulators (Sparrevik et al., 2011), and 
the contractors perform the remedial action. Different from the current remediation practice, sustainable 
remediation assesses the secondary impacts on environment, economy, and society, and selects the remediation 
technology which is the most sustainable for the site. Different methods can be applied in a sustainable 
remediation assessment, which the following sections cover. A general approach for sustainable remediation 
is that the simplest form of assessment that provides a robust decision should be adopted regardless of the size 
of the contamination (SuRF UK, 2010; Technical Committee ISO, 2017). 
 
3.1 Stakeholder involvement 
One of the recommendations for sustainable remediation is to engage and involve stakeholders in the relevant 
processes, (Søndergaard et al., 2018; SuRF UK, 2010; Technical Committee ISO, 2017), by establishing an 
early and transparent dialogue, where knowledge, concerns and advices are shared between the stakeholders 
(Sparrevik et al., 2011). Stakeholders are either directly or indirectly affected by the contaminated site, and 
tend to be e.g. the landowner, neighbours, local businesses, or other affected citizens, organisations or groups 
(Reed, 2008; Sparrevik et al., 2011). Additionally, stakeholders can also be people who are affecting the 
decision, and people who are affected by the decision (Freeman, 1984). The types of stakeholders can be 
categorised in four different groups (Reed et al., 2009): 
- 
Key players are stakeholders with high interest and influence, e.g. regulators. 
- 
Context setters are stakeholder with high influence  but little interest, e.g. politicians. 
- 
Subjects are stakeholders with high interest but little influence, such as non-governmental 
organisations. 
- 
Crowd are stakeholder with both little interest and influence, such as non-affected citizens or 
neighbours. 
Stakeholders have the power to support and oppose a decision (Susskind et al., 1999), however, generally it is 
only during the formal hearing phase that stakeholders are involved in the project, which can potentially lead 
to disagreements and conflicts between the regulators, consultants, and stakeholders (NICOLE, 2010). The 
purpose with the stakeholder involvement is thus to reduce the potential risk and conflicts in the remediation 
process (Sparrevik et al., 2011; Technical Committee ISO, 2017). The level of engagement and involvement 
from stakeholders, was studied by Sparrevik et al. (2011) when a contaminated harbour in Bergen, Norway, 
was remediated. The study concluded that robust and transparent documentation, lateral learning and 
multicriteria decision analysis provided the knowledge the stakeholders needed to be engaged and be a part of 
the complex decision of a remediation strategy (Sparrevik et al., 2011).

weights the ranks and scores for the remediation options to obtain one score for each of the remediation options.

3.5 The quantitative method 
A quantitative method makes it possible to objectively estimate and compare the secondary impacts on the 
environment, which is why the method is often applied. However, a quantitative method is not applicable for 
all contaminations, due to the comprehensive requirements for e.g. data collection and interpretation, compared 
to the size of the contamination (Hou, 2020). Hence, it is not efficient to apply a quantitative analysis for 
remediation of a small contamination. 
The most applied quantitative method is the life cycle assessment (LCA), following the ISO Standard 14040, 
which consists of the following steps (Hauschild et al., 2018): 
1. Goal definition 
2. Scope definition 
3. Life cycle inventory analysis 
4. Life cycle impact assessment 
5. Uncertainty management and sensitivity analysis 
6. Life cycle interpretation 
The LCA is useful in terms of determining the goal and scope of a contaminated site, by using a functional 
unit that specifies what the goal for the remedial action is, for example: “Reduce 23 kg TCE in a 1,000 m3 
aquifer to achieve a level of 10 µgTCE/L”. The functional unit makes it possible to design and collect the 
inventory data that is necessary for the remediation option to achieve the goal. Additionally, to narrow down 
the area of interest, “system boundaries” specifies the objective of the project (i.e. local, regional or global 
scale), and thereby identifies which location has the greatest impacts (Page et al., 1999; Warner & Hadley, 
2012). A LCA requires representativity (i.e. time, geology, technology) and specificity of the inventory data 
(i.e. primary or secondary data) to model the life stages of the remediation option. 
Figure 3.1 illustrates the iterative process of a LCA, where changes might come along in different stages of 
the process. The uncertainty and sensitivity is both included in the inventory analysis and impact assessment 
to identify which processes are sensitive to variation (Hauschild et al., 2018). The LCA can objectively assess 
the remediation options, and both identify hot spots within the options and which option has least impacts on 
the environment.

The ISO standard 14040 for the LCA is developed to assess the environmental impacts and perspectives, does 
not access the economic and social aspects (Hou, 2020). Additionally, the LCA is not developed to involve 
stakeholders, which is why the a sustainable remediation assessment often apply semi-quantitative and 
quantitative methods to compare the results and to involve stakeholders (Søndergaard et al., 2018).  
 
3.6 Sustainable remediation frameworks 
The foundation for a sustainable remediation is the characterisation and risk assessment of the contaminated 
site, which determines whether a remediation is necessary. Several methods have been developed by 
organisations to support the selection of the most sustainable remediation option. However, the organisations 
apply different frameworks to assess the sustainable remediation (Bardos, 2014; Rizzo et al., 2016; 
Søndergaard et al., 2018). The challenge with frameworks is that they are specifically developed for individual 
countries in order to obey already existing laws and restriction (SuRF UK, 2010). Just within the Sustainable 
Remediation Forum (SuRF), frameworks have been published in the USA, Canada, Brazil, Colombia, the 
United Kingdom, Italy, the Netherlands, Taiwan, and New Zealand (Bardos, 2014; Rizzo et al., 2016). 
Therefore, it can be challenging to adapt the frameworks in other countries if there are legislative 
disagreements (SuRF UK, 2010). However, in 2017 the International Organisation for Standardisation (ISO) 
published the ISO Standard 18504:2017 on Soil Quality - Sustainable Remediation to accommodate the need 
for a flexible framework that is applicable worldwide (Technical Committee ISO, 2017). 
Currently there are no frameworks on sustainable remediation in Denmark. Therefore, the content in this 
section provides an overall elaboration of the ISO Standard 18504:2017 framework, as well as a framework 
specially developed for multi criteria decision assessment, to give an overall insight of the existing frameworks.

3.6.1 Framework with a tier assessment 
One of the sustainable remediation assessments that was developed by SuRF UK in 2010, is the tier assessment, 
which is based on the principle that the most simple and robust sustainable remediation option is selected 
(SuRF UK, 2010; Technical Committee ISO, 2017). The tier assessment uses three categories of methods: the 
qualitative, the semi-quantitative and quantitative approach, which is illustrated in Figure 3.2. The assessment 
starts with a risk-based assessment of the contaminated site to determine whether remediation is needed. 
Thereafter, the sustainable assessment begins at the simple qualitative method, and the goal is to obtain 
agreement on a remediation option at the lowest possible level as possible. However, if this fails, the next 
approach is to use the second tier, the semi-quantitative method. Finally, if no agreement is concluded in 
neither the first nor the second tier, the decision will be based on a quantitative method. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The different tiers have their benefits and drawbacks, however a higher tier provides a more accurate analysis, 
which is also equivalent to higher costs and more time (SuRF UK, 2010). The tier assessment is based on the 
necessity to have a tool that is comprehensive for both the organisation and stakeholders, and which is efficient 
in cost and resources (Bardos et al., 2016). Additionally, the simple method minimises the complexity and 
thereby give the stakeholders a manageable understanding of the sustainable remediation assessment (Bardos 
et al., 2016; SuRF UK, 2010). 
The International Organisation on Standardisation (ISO) published in 2017 the ISO Standard 18504:2017 on 
Soil Quality - Sustainable Remediation, to provide a flexible framework, which makes it possible to apply 
worldwide. The standard recommends maximising the sustainable remediation benefits by including 
sustainable factors in the early stages of a project (Technical Committee ISO, 2017).

3.6.2 Framework for a multi criteria decision analysis 
The multi criteria decision analysis (MCDA) is a tool which is often applied in the decision-making for 
environmental issues such as environmental impact assessment and selection of remediation technologies 
(Kiker et al., 2005). The tool also provides engagement of stakeholders, and ranks the remediation strategies 
using objective weights, which is why MCDA is beneficial to apply for sustainable remediation assessment.  
Figure 3.3 illustrates the framework, which is divided into three process categories: i) identification of ‘people’ 
which consists of the decision makers, experts, and stakeholders; ii) the process, which consists of six iterative 
steps and processes; and iii) the applicable tools such as semi-quantitative and quantitative methods. The 
categories do not come in a stepwise manner but interact in an iterative process. The dynamic involvement is 
illustrated in Figure 3.3, where a direct involvement is marked with the black line, and a smaller involvement 
is marked with the dotted line (Kiker et al., 2005). The framework recommends that the decision makers are 
involved during the definition of the issues and identification of remediation options, and that the sustainability 
indicators are selected in collaboration with stakeholders. Additionally, the stakeholders are involved during 
the weighting of indicators. The experts and consultants are active during the performance of the LCA, and 
lastly the decision makers are involved in the decision process. 
The framework is flexible with respect to the selection of the indicators, and depending on the project, it is 
possible to identify other needs and thereby develop other categories (Søndergaard et al., 2018). Additionally, 
the sustainable remediation tools are not predetermined. However, it is required that the MCDA is supported 
by a quantitative tool such as the CBA or LCA (Kiker et al., 2005).

3.7 Case study on sustainable remediation 
The reliability and robustness of a sustainable remediation assessment is crucial to select the remediation 
option that is most sustainable. Therefore it is recommended to assess the remediation options through both a 
semi-quantitative and a quantitative method (Chen et al., 2017; Hou, 2020). Studies conducted by Sparrevik 
et al. (2011), Chen et al. (2017), and Søndergaard et al. (2018) applied both a semi-quantitative (e.g. MCA) 
and a quantitative technique (e.g. LCA) to select a sustainable remediation option. 
The study performed by Søndergaard et al. (2018) investigated which sustainable remediation option would 
be most suitable on the contaminated site Groyne 42 in Denmark. This study applied a framework developed 
by Kiker et al. (2005), and combined a MCA and a LCA. The indicator categories for this study were 
environment, economic, society, effectiveness, and time, which were selected in collaboration with 
stakeholders (Søndergaard et al., 2018). The sustainability score for each remediation option was obtained by 
using the linear additive equation (Equation 1). The remediation options of concern were: i) continued 
encapsulation with a sheet pile wall, ii) in-situ alkaline hydrolysis, iii) in-situ thermal treatment, and iv) 
excavation with off-site treatment and disposal (Søndergaard et al., 2018). The results from the MCA revealed 
that the excavation with off-site treatment was the most preferable remediation option because it was effective 
in terms of removing the contamination, which was an important indicator for the stakeholders. Additionally, 
even though the off-site treatment had a great environmental impact, it was suggested to reduce the impacts 
by using a local treatment facility and reuse the soil (Søndergaard et al., 2018).

4 Danish legislation and remediation assessment on contaminated sites 
This project will apply a contaminated site in Denmark as a case to select the most sustainable remediation 
option. Therefore, a review on the development of legislation on contaminated sites will be presented, together 
with a description on how the sites have been and are managed today. Figure 4.1 illustrates some of the issues 
the society and environment were affected by, which with time led to the developed and enactment of 
legislations and restrictions. The following sections will go through a stepwise evaluation of the timeline. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
4.1 Legislative development on Danish contaminated sites 
In Denmark during the 1960s, the production and quantity of new chemicals increased, which resulted in 
challenges with the chemical waste disposal. The increasing challenges initiated the development and 
enactment of the first Law on Environmental Protection (i.e. Miljøbeskyttelsesloven) in 1973, which had the 
purpose to better understand the hazardous effects of the chemicals, their distribution, degradation, etc. The 
noticeable pollutions, which affected the Danish inhabitants, was firstly assessed, due to the poor air quality 
from chimneys and deoxygenation of the freshwater and ocean. However, in the late 1970’s, the concern for 
buried chemical waste increased due to the risk of leakage to the groundwater and thus the human health and 
drinking water resources (Outzen & Andersen, 2014). 
In 1980, the Danish Environmental Protection Agency initiated the mapping of oil and chemical waste 
deposits, with the purpose to compose an overview of the deposits and their potential risks. Mega sites such as 
the Groyne 42 and Kærgård Plantation in Western Jutland were identified along with thousands of other 
deposits. The work on registering the deposits induced the foundation of the Law on chemical waste deposits 
(i.e. Loven om kemiaffaldsdepoter), which added leakage and spills from point-sources to the list of sources 
to contamination of groundwater. The registration of deposits was assigned to the counties with a list of 
industrial areas (e.g. cleaning facilities, gas stations, etc.), that could potentially have chemical deposits. The 
registration of potential deposits was not based on technical field investigations, but only on the assumptions

4.2 Methodological registration of contaminated sites in Denmark 
An additional focus in the legislation on contaminated soil was to develop a method for the registration of 
contaminated sites in Denmark, and thereby assemble common guidelines for the management of contaminated 
site. In 2007, due to reorganization of the Danish municipalities and counties, the responsibility and 
management of contaminated site was handed over from the counties to the five Danish Regions (i.e. Southern 
Denmark, Central Jutland, Northern Jutland, Zealand, and the Capital Region). The current applied method is 
divided into the five following steps: mapping, introductory and further investigations, remedial actions, and 
monitoring (Danske Regioner, 2020). The Regions are the main responsible, however engineering consultancy 
companies and entrepreneurs are involved during step 3-5. The steps are elaborated in the following: 
1. Mapping of contaminated sites applies two categories: knowledge level V1 and V2 (i.e. vidensniveau 
1 and 2). V1 is applied, when there is knowledge about a current or past activity at the site, which can 
potentially result in a contamination. When a contamination is recorded, V2 is applied. The V2 
category is dominated by areas where anthropogenic activities such as industrial cleaning facilities, 
and industrial oil usage (e.g. gas stations) have been recorded, which has resulted in contaminations 
with chlorinated solvents, oil and gasoline (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2021a). 
2. Introductory investigations cover samples of soil, groundwater, surface water and indoor climate. If 
contaminants exceed the maximum allowed concentration (MAC) the site is categorised as a V2. The 
individual regions determine whether further investigations are necessary. 
3. Further investigations involve characterisation and risk assessment of the site and is performed by 
consultancy companies after request from the regions. The characterisation includes the site-specific 
geology and hydrology, and how the contaminant has mobilised into the soil layer and aquifer, which 
will determine how comprehensive the contamination is. The risk assessment determines the risks the 
contamination poses to human health, the environment and drinking water resources. Due to the 
number of contaminated sites, the risk assessment is important tool to determine which sites poses 
bigger threats than others, and thereby which needs immediate remediation (Region Hovedstaden & 
Olsen, 2020). 
4. Remediation is performed if the risk from a contamination cannot be neglected. The regions ask for 
proposals from the consultancy companies, which must include an introduction and synopsis of the 
site, the relevant remediation options and the respective costs and time requirements. The consultancy 
companies can use the excel tool Remediation Strategy for Soil and Groundwater Pollution (RemS) to 
narrow down the numbers of potential remediation options and the costs, based on the site-specific 
characterisation and information regarding the contaminants. Even though RemS can estimate the 
environmental impacts through a LCA tool and carbon footprint, it is not a requirement (Region 
Hovedstaden, 2013). Hence, based on the effectiveness, costs and time requirements, the most suitable 
remediation option is selected by the region. One approach that is applied to estimate the or set a goal 
for the remediation is to achieve the Danish groundwater quality criteria of 1 µg/L of chlorinated 
ethenes at the point of compliance (POC) 100 meters downstream. 
5. Monitoring of the site are performed after the remediation, to ensure that it has succeeded. 
Furthermore, restoration of the site is potentially needed to regain habitats. In cases where an 
immediate remediation is not required, monitoring wells can be installed to detect and measure the 
development of the contaminant, and thereby the regulators can evaluate if the risk is increasing or 
decreasing.

From 1980-1982, when the initial mapping of chemical and oil deposits in Denmark was conducted, it was 
concluded that there were approximately 3,000 deposits, of which only 500 were registered with certainty 
(Outzen & Andersen, 2014). Today, the Danish Regions are not only mapping and registering chemical and 
oil deposit, but also waste dumps, oil tanks, etc. In June 2020, the Danish Regions published the report From 
Insight to View (i.e. Fra insigt til udsyn), which estimated that 116,000 contaminated sites in Denmark must 
be assessed and managed (Danske Regioner, 2020). Today, 99,400 sites are assessed, and several sites are 
disregarded either because no contamination was detected, or the site was already remediated, but 37,800 sites 
are registered as V1 or V2 contaminated sites. For the management of the remaining 20,000 sites, which 
includes mapping, introductory and further investigations, remediation, and monitoring, it is estimated that the 
costs are approximately 27 billion. Furthermore, it is estimated that 3,100 sites need remedial action due to the 
risk towards human health and drinking water resources (Danske Regioner, 2020). 
The main purpose with the law on contaminated sites is to protect human health and drinking water resources 
from contaminated soil. To achieve the purpose of the law, the Danish counties and Regions have worked on 
registering the sites, and during the last 20 years, 83% of the mapping is completed, 65% of the initial and 56% 
of the further investigations are completed, 27% of the remedial activities are completed, 40% of monitoring 
is completed, and 55% of the cases are finished (Danske Regioner, 2020). 
The hazardous chemicals that are being managed are as mentioned pesticides, gasoline, and heavy metals. The 
most frequent chemical compound at contaminated sites is the chlorinated ethenes. These compounds are 
carcinogenic, which is why they pose a risk to human health and drinking water resources. The following 
section will elaborate the use and physio-chemical properties of the compound.  
 
4.3 Chlorinated ethenes 
Chlorinated ethenes such as tetrachloroethene (PCE) and trichloroethene (TCE) are produced in great 
quantities and have been applied in cleaning facilities, degreasing machinery, etc. (Doherty, 2000b). The 
compound’s ability to evaporate fast and effectively dissolve a wide range of organic substrates, make them 
suitable for several varieties of applications. Due to the widespread application and subsequent disposal, 
contaminations with the hazardous chlorinated ethenes are some of the most abundant contaminants at waste 
disposal sites (Doherty, 2000a). The chlorinated ethenes pose a risk to human health, drinking water resources 
and the environment due to the daughter compound vinyl chloride (VC), which has carcinogenic effects. 
 
4.3.1 Physio-chemical properties of chlorinated ethenes 
Table 4.2 contains different physio-chemical properties of the chlorinated ethenes such as the structures, which 
consists of a carbon double bond with either chlorine atoms or hydrogen atoms attached. The chlorinated 
ethenes, expect for VC, have a higher density than water, which characterise them as DNAPL (dense 
nonaqueous phase liquid). These compounds are immiscible with water and have a high viscosity and occur 
in a separate liquid phase near the subsurface in the base of an aquifer. VC is lighter than water (light 
nonaqueous phase liquid), which makes it more mobile, and will therefore occur in the top of the subsurface 
of an aquifer in a separate phase. The distribution of the contaminants in the bottom and top of the aquifer 
results in challenges and difficulties regarding the remediation. 
Chlorinated ethenes are hydrophobic (Log KOW) and will bind to the octanal phase rather than the water phase. 
Additionally, the chlorinated ethenes tend to bind to the organic carbons (Log KOC) in the aquifer rather than

4.3.2 Degradation of chlorinated ethenes 
The natural degradation of PCE and TCE occurs under strict anaerobic conditions, due to the chemical’s 
persistency in soil and groundwater. Degradation through a reductive dichlorination pathway (Figure 4.2)  is 
only possible if specific bacteria are present. The chlorinated ethenes are degraded through a bacterial 
metabolic processes which requires electron donors such as hydrogen (Bjerg et al., 2006).

Organohalide respiring bacteria represents a wide range of bacteria that reduces PCE to TCE and cis-DCE. 
However, only specific bacteria such as Dehalococcoides (Dhc) reduces cis-DCE to VC and VC to ethene due 
to specific vinyl chloride reductase genes. The presence of Dhc’s is not common and accumulation of cis-DCE 
or VC it often detected, which is of great concern because these daughter compounds are more mobile and will 
distribute further in the soil and aquifer. Additionally, VC is carcinogenic, which means that the chlorinated 
ethenes become more toxic during reductive dichlorination.  
 
4.4 Applied remediation technologies in Denmark 
Contaminated sites are remediated in Denmark to secure and protect the public health and the future drinking 
water resources from hazardous contaminants. Therefore, the sites posing highest risks are treated (Danske 
Regioner, 2020), and it is essential to select the most suitable option for the site. The point-source 
contaminations are possible to remediate because they have a source zone, whereas diffuse-sources are more 
challenging because they can origin from several sources. The following section focuses solely on remediation 
of the point-source contaminations. 
A contaminated site consists of the source zone and depending on the physio-chemical properties and the site’s 
characterisation, the contamination can mobilise into the ground and contaminate the aquifer (plume) and 
surface water or evaporate and contaminate the indoor climate in houses. Consultancy companies, such as 
COWI, applies management strategies, which are used to perform an action and management of a contaminated 
site. The management strategies are divided into six categories, which depends on the contamination. 
1. Monitoring of the contaminant’s concentration in water and air to determine the level of action. 
2. Monitoring of natural attenuation, which requires more comprehensive monitoring and solid proofs. 
3. Restrictions on application which bans establishment of estate on a contaminated site. This ban applies 
to Kærgaard Plantation in Western Jutland. 
4. Encasing the contamination with sheet pilling or stainless steel, which has been applied at Groyne 42 
in Western Jutland. 
5. Remediating the source zone to prevent further leaching of the contamination. 
6. Cutting off the contamination in the plume to prevent contamination of the pore air and further 
transportation of the contamination. 
Each strategy can be managed with different remediation technologies. The technologies are developed to 
remediate specific contaminants in either the source zone, plume or indoor, and the technologies can be divided 
into physical, chemical, biological, or combined remediations. Contaminated sites with chlorinated ethenes are 
the most frequent sites in Denmark, and an overview of the remediation technologies that remediate these 
chemicals are presented in Table 4.3. The technologies are divided into three categories: physical, chemical, 
and biological treatment, with a short description of the remediation mechanisms, whether the remediation 
takes place in-situ or ex-situ, and the operation time.

The remediation technologies mainly target the source zone of the contamination because it is important to 
remediate the zone, so it does not continuously leach into the plume or an indoor environment. Additionally, 
the majority of the technologies operates in-situ, which means that they are not physical installation on-site. 
The physical remediation technologies have frequently been applied the last 30-40 years, which explain their 
==PAGE:33==

dominance in the overview. Indeed, dig and haul (D&H) remediation in the source zone and pump and treat 
(P&T) remediation in the plume was the most applied technologies in Denmark from 1980’s to 2000’s. 
However, the in-situ remediation technologies have developed the last 10-20 years and are increasingly being 
applied more (EEA - European Environment, 2014). A reason why technologies such as P&T are being applied 
lesser is due to the efficiency and operation time. The P&T lasts for 10-100 years, due to the uncertainties 
regarding rebound of the contamination, which prolongs the operation time. Therefore, remediation 
technologies with greater efficiency during a shorter operation are preferred. 
A survey conducted by the European Environmental Agency in 2014, analysed which remediation 
technologies were the most frequently applied in some of the member states (European Environment Agency, 
2014). Denmark was not analysed; however, the Netherlands was, which have similar soil layers and 
remediation management as Denmark. The analysis on the remediation technologies in the Netherlands 
revealed that out of 100% remedial actions, i) 50% was in-situ physical/chemical, ii) 25% was excavation and 
ex-situ physical/chemical, thermal, or biological treatment, iii) 20% was in-situ biological treatment, and iv) 
5% was excavation and disposal, (European Environment Agency, 2014). The survey thus supports the 
increasing trend of using efficient and less time demanding technologies. 
The remediation of contaminated sites prevents contamination of groundwater, and thus protects human health 
and drinking water resources. The different remediation technologies apply various treatments to remediate 
the soil and groundwater, however, the technologies can potentially have secondary impacts on both the 
environment, economy, and society, which are greater than the primary impacts. To assess the secondary 
impacts, sustainable remediation assessment can be applied to select the most sustainable remediation 
technology based on the outcome from qualitative, semi-quantitative or quantitative methods.

6.2 Synthesis of the plume in the Farum site 
The contamination with TCE is approximately 50 years old, and thus have had time to leach to the groundwater, 
10 mb.g.l. The summed concentration of chlorinated ethenes in the source zone is >50,000 µg/L while the 
summed concentration in the plume is >1,000 µg/L. The source zone has an unsaturated and saturated zone, 
which consists of 35 kg cis-DCE and 9 kg TCE. The mass of the chlorinated ethenes in the plume are 6 kg of 
cis-DCE and 5 kg of TCE (COWI & Region Hovedstaden, 2018). 
Figure 6.4 illustrates a conceptual model for the contaminated site, and how the contamination has mobilised 
in the soil layers and entered the groundwater. The upper soil layer  in the source zone (2-7 m.b.gl)  consists 
of clay till, which means that pools of contamination have likely been created. Considering the age of the 
contamination, the contaminant has likely adsorbed into the fractures of the clay (both vertically and 
horizontally). The contamination has leached to the sand layer and thus reached the groundwater and aquifer 
and mobilised to the plume. Additionally, a photo from the Farum site (Figure 6.5) shows the asphalted area 
with buildings that the plume flows beneath. The highest concentrations of TCE and cis-DCE in the plume are 
measured at the bottom of the groundwater (18-20 m.b.gl) (COWI & Region Hovedstaden, 2018). 
Measurements from the plume have detected higher concentrations of cis-DCE than TCE, which imply 
favourable anaerobic conditions for organohalide respiring microorganisms to degrade TCE to cis-DCE 
through reductive dichlorination. The lower concentration of VC indicates that cis-DCE accumulates, however 
the concentrations of VC still exceed the maximum allowed concentration, and the contaminattion thus pose a 
risk to the groundwater, drinking water and human health.

7 Selection of remediation technologies for the plume in the Farum site 
Based on the characterisation and synthesis of the plume in the Farum site, the most suitable remediation 
technologies are selected. First, the potential remediation technologies are evaluated from the Remediation 
Catalogue (i.e. Afværgekatalog) that was published in Denmark in 2007. The catalogue contains an overview 
of the most common applied in-situ remediation technologies in Denmark (Orbicon, 2007). Second, the 
selected technologies will be presented for both the Capital Region and COWI, to discuss if they are suited for 
the site and representative with respect to nowadays remedial actions. The final decision is made by the 
decision maker. 
7.1 Objective goals 
The objective goals for the selection of remediation technologies it to select: 
1. Remediation technologies that remediate plume 
2. Remediation technologies based on the local geology and hydrogeology of the plume. Hence, 
remediation technologies that cannot operate in meltwater sand are excluded. 
3. Remediation technologies that remediate chlorinated ethenes 
These objective goals will be considered in the evaluation and selection of the remediation technologies. 
 
7.2 Evaluating the remediation technologies 
Table 7.1 is divided according to whether the remediation technology uses a physical, chemical, or biological 
treatments. Parameters such as duration, costs, and level of experience are included, because these parameters 
are important to consider when selecting a technology, since a time consuming, high costs or limited 
experience can make a technology less favourable. Additionally, combination of technologies is also becoming 
a common practice, which the catalogue mentions briefly. For example, the ongoing pilot test in the plume of 
the Farum site, is testing a combination of biological technologies with a permeable reactive barrier (PRB) 
were zero valent iron (ZVI), organic substrate and bacteria is injected (COWI & Region Hovedstaden, 2018). 
A reason for combining technologies is to obtain the most efficient remediation of a contamination, because 
one technology is potentially not enough to obtain the remediation goal. 
To decide which remediation technologies are the most suitable for the plume in the Farum site, parameters 
such as the site characterisation and the objective goals will be used to evaluate the technologies. A brief 
explanation for each technology is covered in Appendix A, as well as a further description of benefits and 
drawbacks of the technology.

7.3 Selection of remediation technologies 
In connection with the selection of the remediation technologies, the contact persons from the Capital Region 
and COWI were involved in the decision process, to give their recommendations on which technologies to 
select. The technologies that were suggested for the Capital Region and COWI were: in-situ enhanced 
reductive dichlorination (ERD) with organic substrate and bacteria, in-situ permeable reactive barrier (PRB) 
with zero valent iron (ZVI) and with or without bacteria and monitored natural attenuation (MNA). These 
suggestions were among others based on the efficiency and operation time. The MNA was also added to 
compare the impacts between a natural degradation and technologies that apply substrate (organic and 
reductants) to the plume. 
Initially, the technologies pump and treat (P&T) and in-situ chemical oxidation (ISCO) were not considered, 
because P&T has a long operation time and ISCO is mainly a source zone technology. However, the contact 
persons recommended to include the technologies because they are often considered as alternatives in 
remediation projects. Therefore, the selected remediation technologies are ERD, PRB with bacteria, ISCO, 
P&T and MNA. The PRB with bacteria is the same technology that COWI is testing in the plume in the Farum 
site, where ZVI and bacteria is injected in injection points to create a permeable barrier, which the groundwater 
flows through. This set-up with a permeable reactive barrier was decided to apply the other in-situ technologies 
ERD and ISCO. Hence, for the ERD technology with organic substrate and bacteria will be injected to create 
permeable reactive barrier, and for the ISCO technology a strong oxidant will be injected to create permeable 
reactive barrier. A short description and evaluation of how the technologies work is covered in the following.  
Natural occurring organohalide respiring bacteria sequentially degrades chlorinated ethenes through metabolic 
anaerobe reductive dichlorination. This process can be enhanced by adding more bacteria and organic substrate 
such as molasses, whey, chitin, lactate, etc. Only the specific bacteria Dehalococcoides can degrade cis-DCE 
to VC and VC to ethene, due to specific vinyl chloride reductase genes. During the fermentation of the organic 
substrate, hydrogen is generated. The hydrogen serves as an electron donor and the chlorinated ethenes serves 
as electron acceptors, which thus gain hydrogen atoms through a redox reaction and gets reduced (pathway 1 
==PAGE:45==
 
Sustainable Remediation Assessment 
 
 
 
36 
 
in Figure 7.1) (Alleman et al., 2010). However, a drawback is that during reductive dichlorination, methane is 
produced during the fermentation of the organic substrate, which can thus leach to the surface and enter houses. 
Additionally, there is a risk that VC will accumulate, which will result in a bigger risk to the human health and 
groundwater.  
A permeable reactive barrier typically injects zero valent iron, which is based on natural abiotic dichlorination, 
where a natural occurring reductant degrades the chlorinated ethenes to ethene (pathway 2 in Figure 7.1). 
During this degradation, VC is not produced which is favourable. However, if VC is already present the abiotic 
dichlorination cannot degrade the carcinogenic compound, which is why the pilot test in the plume in Farum 
is investigating the potential of combing a biotic and abiotic technology. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Monitored natural attenuation is a passive technology where no actual remedial actions are made. The natural 
attenuation occurs due to the biotic and abiotic processes in the soil (Figure 7.1), which can reduce the 
contamination (Alleman et al., 2010). To detect and measure the degradation monitoring wells are installed at 
the site. 
In-situ chemical oxidation applies a strong oxidant, such as persulfate or potassium permanganate. The process 
changes the anaerobic condition into aerobic, due to the chemical oxidation of the chlorinated ethenes 
(Reaction  1-3). The chlorinated ethenes are reduced to carbon dioxide during the redox reaction. The strong 
oxidants are very reactive, which mean it will reduce the microorganism and other natural minerals in the soil 
(iron, manganese, sulphate). Additionally, due to the reactivity there is also risk that the oxidant cannot reduce 
the captured contaminant. 
 
The P&T technology was the first plume technology and was frequently applied in Denmark from the 1990’s 
to the 2000’s. Capture wells are installed in the ground to access the contaminated plume and pump up the 
water. The P&T facility is designed based on how long it takes for one pore volume to pass the treatment zone. 
Hence, knowing the time and the number of pore volumes it requires to pump up the contamination, it is 
possible to design the facility.

8 Multi criteria assessment for the plume in the Farum site 
The multicriteria assessment is selected as one of the methods to assess the remediation technologies that are 
selected for the plume in the Farum site. The reason why a MCA is applied is to involve the relevant 
stakeholders, who will participates in a MCA workshop. The following sections describe the goal and 
objectives for the MCA, the MCA tool that is used, the selected indicators, and the selected stakeholders. The 
results will be presented lastly.  
 
8.1 Goal and objectives 
The objectives for the MCA are based on the requirements for this project, which is to select a sustainable 
remediation technology that is obtained from the results from the MCA and LCA methods. Therefore, the 
objectives for the MCA for the plume in the Farum site are: 
- 
To support the decision making of which remediation technology is the most suitable and sustainable. 
- 
To involve and encourage the relevant stakeholders to participate in the MCA workshop. 
The MCA will support the decision making by comparing the MCA results to the LCA results, and thereby 
assess which remediation technology is the most suitable and sustainable for the Farum site. The decision is 
made by the decision maker. 
 
8.2 MCA tool and indicators 
The MCA tool applied in this MCA, is developed by Søndergaard et al. (2018). The MCA tool is developed 
in Excel and combines results from a MCA workshop with stakeholders and results from a LCA to obtain a 
sustainability score for each of the remediation technologies. Hence, the technology with the best sustainability 
score is the most sustainable for the specific site. In addition, the MCA tool can be applied to compare the 
sustainability between the remediation technologies, but the sustainability score cannot tell if the technology 
itself is sustainable (Søndergaard et al., 2018). 
The selected indicator categories and sustainability indicators (Figure 8.1)  are inspired by the study conducted 
by Søndergaard et al. (2018). These indicators are ranked and scored by the stakeholders during the MCA 
workshop. The indicator categories are ranked on a scale from 1-4 (1 is most prioritised and 4 is least 
prioritised) and afterward the ranks are converted into weights according to the rank order distribution 
(Appendix C, Table A - 8) (Roberts & Goodwin, 2002). The average of the rank order distribution (ROD 
weights) will be applied in the MCA tool. The sustainability indicators for the category’s environment and 
society are also ranked and will be converted into ROD weights, and similarly the average of the ROD weights 
for sustainability indicators will be applied in the MCA tool. Additionally, the stakeholders will give scores 
on a scale from 1-5 to the remediation technologies’ performance in the different sustainability indicators, both 
including the society and economy. The score ‘1’ is given when the remediation technology performs best or 
have no impacts, and ‘5’ is given when the technology’s performance is worst or high impacts. The MCA tool 
normalises the scores on a scale from 0-1, where ‘1’ contributes to the worst performance score of ‘5’. For the 
indicator category time, the time is normalised to 30 years, which mean that if a technology operates in more 
than 30 years, it is normalised to 1. The normalisation from 0-1 is obtained by linear interpolation.  
The sustainability score is based on the linear additive model (Equation 1), which calculates the sustainability 
score, V, for each of the remediation options, x. The sustainability score is calculated by the sum of the

individual indicator category scores, vi, which is multiplied with the categories respective weight, wi,. ‘n’ is the 
total number of indicator categories, and ‘i' is the total number of sustainability indicators. The scores sum to 
1, and the most sustainable technology has the lowest score (Søndergaard et al., 2018). 
 
 
  
There are two sustainability scores: one that consists of the ROD weighted sums of the indicator categories 
and sustainability indicators, which the stakeholders have contributed to obtain, and one where an equal 
weighting of the indicator categories and sustainability indicators is applied. The two different sustainability 
scores will be used to compare the different results for the technologies. 
The indicator categories and sustainability indicators, which the stakeholders will rank and score, are presented 
in Figure 8.1 and further elaborated in Appendix D, Table A - 9 to Table A - 12. 
The environmental indicators emissions to air, ecotoxicity, resources and waste are selected because they both 
cover the local impacts (ecotoxicity and waste) as well as the global impacts (emissions to air and resources). 
An indicator that is excluded from the category is soil quality, because it is assumed that this indicator is 
irrelevant for the Farum site, considering that the site is in industrial area. Additionally, the environmental 
sustainability indicators will be assessed through a life cycle assessment in EASETECH, where the ILCD EC-
JCR (2010) is applied for the non-toxic impacts and the USEtox is applied for the toxic impacts. Selected 
normalised person equivalent (PE) and personal reserves (PR) for resources from the LCA will be applied in 
the MCA tool. 
The economic indicators are costs, technical uncertainties, and technical maturity. The costs are based on 
calculations in the excel tool RemS. However, the costs for the PB-Pro are estimated. The technical 
uncertainties and maturity can both cause extra costs, if the technology has technical difficulties to get in 
contact with the contamination, or if the technology is still under development. Hence, it is assumed that the 
remedial effects are incorporated in this indicator. 
The social indicators inconvenience, health, and work environment are focusing on the impacts that the 
remedial activities have on the site. Inconvenience covers both noise and smells during the remediation and 
operation of the technology. Hence, if a technology is installed and in operation in 20 years, it can be 
inconvenient for the locals. The health and security cover both the health risks that the contamination poses

8.3 Selection of stakeholders 
The goal with the stakeholder involvement in a sustainable remediation assessment is to obtain a sustainable 
and lasting decision, which the stakeholders support. It is essential to have a representative group of 
stakeholders, which consists of the active and passive stakeholders (Reed, 2008), hence, stakeholders who can 
have an impact on the decisions, and stakeholders who will get affected from the decision (Freeman, 1984). 
Additionally, it is important to select the number and relevant stakeholders for the specific case. The most 
relevant stakeholders for the Farum site are: 
- 
1-3 neighbours to the plume, person who lives right next to the treatment zone. These stakeholders are 
therefore passive persons, who can get affected by the decision of which remediation technology is 
selected. For example, they will experience the inconveniences associated with the remediation. It is 
assumed that this stakeholder group will have a low level of technical knowledge about remediation 
technologies and remediation in general, and it is expected that it will require more time to prepare 
these stakeholders. The stakeholders’ answers can be biased due to their concerns regarding the 
inconveniences.  
- 
1-3 regulators who are responsible for the remediation. In general, it is the Danish Regions who are 
responsible for managing contaminated sites in Denmark, and in this case, it is the Capital Region. It 
is assumed that this stakeholder group will have more technical knowledge about remediation and the 
specific site, which can bias their answers in the MCA. 
- 
1-3 local regulators from the municipality in Farum. Even though they are not directly responsible for 
the remediation, they are managing the daily cases within the municipality, and advise and refer the 
citizens to the Capital Region when necessary. Additionally, it is assumed that this stakeholder group 
has a moderate level of knowledge regarding the site. 
- 
1-3 stakeholder from the consultancy company who are performing the pilot test in the plume in the 
Farum site. This stakeholder group will have a high level of knowledge regarding the site, which can 
also bias their answers.  
- 
1-3 stakeholder from a non-governmental organisation (NGO). This stakeholder group has no political 
or financial interest in the contaminated site and can thereby contribute with other perspectives such 
as the environment or local community. It is assumed that this stakeholder group has a low-moderate 
knowledge about remediation.

8.3.1 The MCA workshop 
The stakeholders were contacted 2-3 weeks before the MCA workshop was held, with an email (Appendix E). 
The email elaborated why they were relevant for the project, and that they, as stakeholders, would contribute 
with their perspectives in a multicriteria assessment. All four groups accepted the invitation, and afterwards 
they received an informative document (Supporting Information, SI-1). The purpose with the document was 
to give the stakeholders a background knowledge concerning the plume, the MCA workshop, and which 
remediation technologies they would be ranking and scoring. Additionally, the purpose with the document was 
to make sure that stakeholders with less knowledge (assumable group 1-2), would gain knowledge. However, 
it was also considered that it would not be possible to give the stakeholders in group 1 and 2 the same level of 
technical knowledge and understanding as group 3 and 4. Prior to the MCA workshop it was decided that the 
workshop would be a virtual meeting with each of the groups due to the Covid-19 restriction in Denmark 
during the Autumn of 2020. 
During the virtual meetings it was possible for the decision maker to pay attention to one group at the time and 
be accessible for questions and discussion. A PowerPoint show (SI-2) was prepared for the MCA workshop, 
with the intension to better communicate and interact with the stakeholders during the workshop. Additionally, 
it was important to make the workshop as understandable and simple for the stakeholders, considering that 
they had not participated in a MCA workshop for sustainable remediation before. During the virtual workshop, 
the presentation was visible for the stakeholders, so they could both read and see what they were asked to rank 
or score. 
It is worth mentioning that group 1 and 2 consisted of one participant, whereas group 3 and 4 consisted of three 
and two participants, respectively. During the MCA workshop with group 1 and 2, the decision maker had an 
active dialog and discussion with the participants. The MCA workshop with group 3 and 4 was different, 
because the stakeholders used each other to discuss and share their point of view to achieve an answer that 
they all would agree to. The stakeholders in group 3 and 4, which works together at the Capital Region and 
COWI, would both apply their work related (objective) and personal (subjective) perspectives when they 
answered. The interaction between the stakeholders was indeed beneficial for the MCA workshop. The MCA 
workshop was successfully performed, considering the circumstances. All four stakeholder groups were 
actively participating and contributed with both their professional and personal point of views.

category “society”, which includes the sustainability indicator “human health” was ranked lower than the 
“environment”. It could be assumed that the public health is more important than the environment, however,

9 Life cycle assessment for the plume in the Farum site 
The life cycle assessment (LCA) is applied so assess the five remediation technologies’ secondary impacts on 
the environment and identify which life cycle stage (i.e. extraction of materials, remedial action or disposal) is 
contributing the most to these impacts. Additionally, the results from the LCA will both be applied to estimate 
which remediation technology has the least environmental impacts, and in the MCA excel tool for calculations 
of the environmental and social normalised scores. The LCA is performed with respect to the ISO 14040 
methodology, including: goal and scope definition, inventory analysis, impact assessment, and the 
interpretation (Hauschild et al., 2018). 
  
9.1 Goal and scope 
The intended application of this LCA screening (from now on LCA) is to support the decision making of 
selecting a remediation technology that is the most sustainable application for the plume in the Farum site. It 
is important to stress out that the LCA is not intended to a specific target audience, e.g. a company or 
governmental organisation, but as a supporting tool. Thus, the assessment cannot be applied on its own or 
compared to other studies.  
 
9.1.1 The functional unit for the Farum site 
The focus for the sustainable remediation assessment is to select a remediation technology that is the most 
sustainable for the plume in the Farum site. The five technologies are operating at different timescales (e.g. 
from few day/weeks to hundreds of years), which means that a fixed time scale will not represent all the 
technologies. The functional unit is to reduce the concentration to 10 µg/L of chlorinated ethenes in the target 
treatment zone in the plume. It is assumed that the remediations are effective enough to comply to the Danish 
groundwater quality criteria of 1 µg/L chlorinated ethenes at the point of compliance 100 meters downstream. 
Additionally, the area of the injection zones for the in-situ technologies varies due to the different thicknesses 
of the barriers. The treatment zones are injected 50 meters from the original source zone and the areas are: 700 
m3 for PB-EOS, 400 m3 for the PB-Pro, and 350 m3 for the PB-ISCO. The P&T facility is installed on 
approximately 400 m2, 75 meters downstream from the original source zone and the monitoring wells for all 
the technologies are installed upstream, in the injection zones and downstream to monitor the remediation.  
 
9.1.2 System boundaries 
The system boundaries are illustrated in Figure 9.2, which includes the technologies with a permeable reactive 
barrier (EOS, Pro and ISCO) and monitored natural attenuation. Figure 9.1 illustrates the pump and treat 
system. The three life stages for the technologies (material for injections, installation and maintenance and 
disposal) are illustrated in both figures. Extraction of raw materials and production of materials is connected 
to both the life cycle stages “material for injections” and “installation and maintenance”. Additionally, 
emissions are connected to all three stages. The disposal phase is connected to the soil and filling that is 
excavated during the installation, as well as the recycling of materials once the treatment facility is removed. 
This includes both the potential materials from the monitoring wells and the P&T facility.

9.2 Life cycle inventory  
The data for the LCI is collected from COWI and studies concerning a LCA for remediation technologies. The 
specificity (i.e. primary, secondary or tertiary data) and representativity (i.e. time, geography, technology) is 
presented for each of the remediation technologies.  
Generally for the modelling of the LCI, an attributional modelling is applied, which isolates the technosphere 
and ecosphere for the respective remediation technology from the surrounding spheres (Hauschild et al., 2018). 
In connection with the remediation test of the Farum site, COWI has performed laboratory experiments, which 
reveals that the inhibitory contaminant for degradation is cis-DCE. Hence, cis-DCE is slower degraded 
compared to TCE, and the model parameters and inventory data for the remediation technologies depends on 
the degradation of cis-DCE. All remediation models are only based on a remedial action in the plume, and it 
is assumed that the source zone is remediated or leached into the plume. The in-situ technologies have a 
permeable reactive barrier, but with different substances. The technology using emulsified oil and KB-1 is 
referred to PB-EOS, the technology using Provect and KB-1 is referred to PB-Pro and the last technology 
applying potassium permanganate is referred to PB-ISCO.

9.4 Life cycle interpretation and discussion 
The results from the life cycle impact assessment are presented and discussed in the following sections. The 
results are applied for the sustainability score in the MCA and to the LCA which presents which remediation 
technology has the least environmental impacts. A sensitivity analysis (SA) is conducted for each technology 
to analyse which model parameters are sensitive to variations. The calculations and sensitivity values (SA) are 
presented in Appendix K. The sensible parameters are presented for each of the remediation technologies, and 
the results are provided in percentage. Hence, a negative percentage indicates the sensitivity value has less 
impacts compared to the original value. On the contrary, a positive percentage indicates the sensitivity value 
has increased the impacts on the category. Hence, 0% indicates that the parameter is not sensitive.

A - 32 to Table A - 39, and the results indicate that the P&T technology has the greatest impacts. However, to 
verify the indication, the results are normalised to person equivalence (PE) pr. remediation technology for the 
Farum site.

10 Discussion of sustainable remediation, approaches, and perspectives 
10.1 Sustainable remediation assessment of the Farum site 
The sustainability scores for the five remediation technologies were calculated based on the weighted sum of 
the sustainability indicators in the four indicator categories. The scores were obtained from the MCA tool (SI-
4) by using both equal weights (Figure 10.1a) and the ROD weights from ranking (Figure 10.1b). The 
unweighted scores for the remediation technologies in Figure 10.1a show that the MNA technology obtain the 
worst score at 1, whereas the P&T technology obtains a score at 0.79. However, compared to the weighted 
sums in Figure 10.1b, the MNA technology obtain a score at 0.77, and P&T obtains the highest score at 0.84. 
Hence, the sustainability score for P&T increases because the indicator category environment is weighted the 
most (0.4138), and the P&T environmental scores are normalised to 1, the weighted sum for the environment 
category is 0.42. On the same note, the sustainability score for MNA reduces because the time category is 
weighted the lowest (0.092). The in-situ technologies obtain similar scores, which varies between 0.29-0.37 
for the unweighted sum and 0.31-0.40 in the weighted sum. In both cases, PB-EOS obtain the lowest scores 
and PB-Pro obtain the highest scores for the in-situ technologies. This means that the PB-EOS technology 
obtains the best sustainability score and should thus be selected as the most sustainable remediation

10.2 Choice of remediation technologies assessed 
The results from the MCA and LCA are based on the remediation technologies that were selected for the 
methods. Therefore, the results could have turned out differently if some of the technologies were not selected, 
or if other technologies were. The representativity of the remediation technologies for the site is therefore 
crucial to obtain reliable and sustainable results. One of the technologies that could be questionable is the 
monitored natural attenuation (MNA), where the remedial action is to install monitoring wells and then collect 
samples to measure the degradation. The reason why the technology is not suitable is because of the 
accumulation of cis-DCE, which is more mobile than TCE, and will thus mobilise and contaminate the 
groundwater further downstream, where it potentially can degrade to VC. In addition, the permeable reactive 
barrier with potassium permanganate is questionable since the technology is mainly applied for remediation 
of source zones, due to the uncertainties whether the reactive oxidant will get in contact with all the 
contaminants in a plume. Additionally, a great concern of applying KMnO4 is also that heavy metals are 
injected, due to impurity in the KMnO4, which means that an additional contamination is added. 
An alternative remediation action could be to let the contaminated groundwater flow to a water work and clean 
the water by pumping it through an activated carbon filter (Lemming et al., 2012). However, the suggestion 
conflicts with the Danish groundwater policy (i.e. Miljøbeskyttelsesloven LBK nr. 1218 af 25/11/2019), which 
is to protect the groundwater rather than performing a remedial action (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2019). 
Therefore, currently the water is only being treated with a sand filter to remove iron and oxygenated, which 
removes CO2 or CH4. The mapping of contaminated sites contributes to identifying which sites pose a risk to 
the groundwater, and thereby remediate the site before it becomes a risk. However, the increasing demand on 
groundwater on Zealand induce challenges with finding clean groundwater and puts a pressure on the aquifers 
(Danske Regioner, 2020). Therefore, an activated carbon filter at the water works could be an alternative. Even 
so, if the contaminated plume in the Farum site is treated at a water work, the contamination will be transported 
1.4 km downstream and mobile further into the aquifer and potentially enter streams and lakes.  
 
10.3 Approaches 
10.3.1 Stakeholders and the multicriteria assessment 
The involvement of stakeholders is an essential part of the sustainable remediation assessment because it will 
improve the cooperation between e.g. regulators and stakeholders (Marttunen et al., 2015). Therefore, an early 
and transparent dialog is crucial to establish. Such stakeholder involvement was performed in the sustainable 
remediation assessment for the mega site contamination Groyne 42 in Denmark that was conducted by 
Søndergaard et al. (2018). Here, the stakeholders were involved during the selection of indicators and the MCA 
workshop. Even though it is recommended to involve stakeholders in the early stage of the process (SuRF UK, 
2010; Technical Committee ISO, 2017), the stakeholders for the Farum site were only contacted 2-3 weeks 
==PAGE:85==
 
Sustainable Remediation Assessment 
 
 
 
76 
 
before the MCA workshop. This reduced the possibility of stakeholder involvement because there was not 
enough preparation time to discuss the relevant indicator categories and sustainability indicators with the 
stakeholders, and the indicators were thus selected by the decision maker. During the MCA workshop, the 
stakeholders questioned why the potential indicator category “efficiency” was not considered. A category with 
efficiency would thus cover the remedial efficiency on the contamination, which for some of the stakeholders 
were more important than the economy and environment, because a high efficiency secure protection of the 
drinking water and human health. The argumentation for not selecting the efficiency category, was because it 
was assumed to be a part of the economy indicator “technical uncertainty”, however this was not presented 
clearly for the stakeholders. Therefore, an earlier involvement of the stakeholders, would have been beneficial 
for the MCA workshop, because the stakeholders could have been integrated in the selection of indicators, 
which would have improved their understanding of these, and potentially given another result in the MCA 
workshop. 
Additionally, the stakeholders did not meet before the MCA workshop to share knowledge, concerns, and 
uncertainties. This could have strengthened the technical knowledge level for some of the stakeholder groups, 
and additionally made it possible for the stakeholders with a more objective point of view on the remediation 
actions to get an insight of the concerns from the other stakeholders. In a study conducted by Sparrevik et al. 
(2011), a multicriteria decision analysis was used to involve stakeholders to take part of the decision making 
regarding a remediation of contaminated soil in Bergen. Here, the stakeholders received sufficient knowledge 
concerning the complex remediation alternatives, through lateral learning combined with a multicriteria 
decision analyses, which provided structure, robustness and transparent documentation (Sparrevik et al., 2011). 
Hence, it is essential to provide the stakeholder with sufficient knowledge, from both other stakeholders and 
informative meetings or documents prior a MCA workshop, so they are prepared to take part of a workshop. 
However, a benefit from the individual virtual workshops with the stakeholder groups, was that if there was a 
dominant stakeholder present, the stakeholder would only affect a small group instead of a bigger group. 
Additionally, the setting gave the more reserved stakeholders an opportunity to share their point of views and 
ask questions whenever they were in doubt.  
It is essential to establish a cooperation between decision maker and the stakeholders, were neither of the 
parties are biased by each other. For example, if the stakeholders (e.g. site owner or locals) focus mainly on 
the time frame, they will thus act in favour for an option that removes the contamination as fast as possible. 
Additionally, dominating stakeholder with a strong opinion will undermine more reserved stakeholders. On 
the contrary, decision makers can bias the stakeholders, and have the power to not inform them well enough 
or favour a technology more than another. Hence, to avoid these scenarios it is important to share and inform 
the stakeholders equally with an unbiased opinion.  
For each sustainable remediation assessment, the relevance and number of stakeholder groups will vary. 
Generally, it is recommended to have a representative group of stakeholders: key players, context setters, 
subjects, and crowd. The stakeholder’s interest and power vary and the decision can thus favour one 
stakeholder from another (Reed et al., 2009). The stakeholders that were involved in this project represented 
were mainly key player (the Capital Region) and subjects (COWI, Farum municipality, DN Furesø), but 
represented both local and regional interests. Other relevant stakeholders could be politicians or regulators 
from the Danish Environmental Agency, who are have the power to affect the implementation of sustainable 
remediation in Denmark.

10.3.2 The life cycle assessment  
The life cycle assessment on the remediation technologies PB-EOS, PB-Pro, PB-ISCO, P&T and MNA were 
performed in the LCA tool EASETECH v. 3.3.7. The specificity of the inventory data varied because some of 
the data was collected from COWI and others from studies conducted by Lemming et al. (2010), Lemming et 
al. (2012), and Higgins & Olson (2009). The data from COWI was very specific, because it was based on the 
characterisation and risk assessment for the Farum site. The data from Lemming et al., (2010, 2012), which 
was used in the LCI for the PB-EOS, PB-ISCO and monitoring wells were detailed and contained the 
EcoInvent data for the different processes. The data from Higgins & Olson (2009), which was applied for the 
LCI on the P&T, was however not specific and the representativity was also questionable. The study did not 
specify which EcoInvent data were used or the site characterisations, meaning that the P&T LCIA were more 
uncertain compared to the other technologies. 
There are several assessments and methods within life cycle assessment. For this project, a LCA screening was 
applied, due to the time frame. However, it is possible to perform a life cycle sustainable assessment (LCSA), 
which is the sum of the overall performance of both a life cycle assessment (LCA), life cycle costing (LCC) 
and social life cycle assessment (SLCA). The LCSA thus cover both the impacts on the environment, economy, 
and society, whereas the LCA only covers the impacts on the environment. Therefore, the suitable assessment 
should be applied according to the relevance and size of the remediation project. However, a life cycle 
assessment is a comprehensive method to perform, which requires time and resources. Additionally, the LCA 
only estimates the secondary impacts on the environment, which means that the economy and society is not 
assessed. Due to those parameters, it is recommended by the SuRF UK and the ISO standard 18504:2017 to 
apply the LCA as the third and last tier assessment for sustainable remediation (SuRF UK, 2010; Technical 
Committee ISO, 2017).  
The benefit from the LCA is that it quantifies and objectively estimates which technology have less impact on 
the environment compared to the other technologies. This objectivity is not possible to obtain from the 
qualitative or semi-quantitative methods, where the stakeholder’s subjective opinions can reflect in their ranks 
and scores. Therefore, in cases when stakeholders and decision makers cannot agree to a remedial action, the 
LCA can contribute with quantitative results and point at a remediation action that is most sustainable.  
However, it is important to remind one that the life cycle impact assessment can only assess the life cycle 
inventory data that is being inserted to the model, which mean that even the interpretation of the LCA can be 
biased. It is therefore important perform a transparent and unbiased LCA.  
 
10.3.3 Assessment of EASETECH on a contaminated site 
The LCA tool EASETECH version 3.3.7 was applied to assess the environmental impacts of the remediation 
technologies. EASETECH is typically applied to assess waste from households but have also been applied to 
assess the impacts on wastewater. The programme is simple to approach and the variety of material processes 
make it easy and manageable to model the life cycle stages of a remediation technology. The “boxes” (i.e. 
material processes) makes it easy to visualise and manage the different processes for a remediation technology, 
and the model can both be designed simple or more detailed. Additionally, the material processes are easy to 
manage in the toolbar with “material transfer”, “process exchanges”, “documentation”, etc. to change the 
parameters for the model. Additionally, the toolbar contains the “LCIA” where the characterisation, weighting 
or normalisation is performed with a respective LCIA method. The LCIA can easily be performed for just one 
process or for the whole remediation technology. Additionally, the sensibility analysis of a model is simple to 
establish and incorporate in the model by typing the name of the SA value and then multiplying it with the 
==PAGE:87==

relevant process. A drawback from the modelling of remediation technologies in EASETECH is that it requires 
access to an external LCI database such as EcoInvent because the database in EASETECH mostly contain data 
for waste management. However, the overall impression of EASETECH is that the simplicity and virtualisation 
of the model makes it easy to use the tool and thus manage. EASETECH thus tend to simplify the LCA 
modelling and make it applicable for a broad range of users compared to other more complex LCA tools. 
Therefore, it is recommended to apply EASETECH for modelling of remediation technologies.  
 
10.4 Perspectives of sustainable remediation  
Sustainable remediation assessment has developed the last 10-15 years, and organisations such as Sustainable 
Remediation Forum have established in countries all over the world to guide and consult on sustainable 
remediation in the respective countries (SuRF UK, 2010). However, even though it is highly relevant to 
incorporate sustainable remediation due to the secondary impacts from the remediation technologies, it has not 
gained acceptance in Denmark. 
Sustainable remediation and the common remediation management apply the same introductory phase: 
perform a characterisation and risk assessment of the contaminated site to determine whether a remedial action 
is needed. However, it is during the selection of a remediation technology that the assessments differ. 
Sustainable remediation applies qualitative, semi-quantitative and or quantitative methods to make the decision 
for a remediation technology. Within the qualitative and semi-quantitative methods, stakeholders are involved 
to take part of the decision making to avoid later disagreements. On the contrary, the common remediation 
assessment only involves the stakeholders during the formal hearing process, which is in the end of the 
decision-making process. If the stakeholders disagree with the selected remediation technology, the whole 
remediation project can be postponed, which is why sustainable remediation focusses on involving the 
stakeholders in an earlier stage of the project. However, reasons why stakeholders are not involved earlier in 
the common assessment is due to the efforts it requires to select the relevant stakeholders as well as to involve 
and provide them with the necessary information. Thus, the stakeholder involvement can seem comprehensive 
and challenging, which is why it would be easier to not involve them. However, it is essential that stakeholders 
are not considered as a hampering, but as a supplement to the decision-making and a benefit for the project in 
the long run. On the contrary it is also relevant to question the time management and costs for both assessments; 
will the sustainable remediation assessment require the same amount of time and costs to involve the 
stakeholders sufficiently, compared to a potential postponement of a conventional remediation project due to 
disagreements from stakeholders? In connection to this, it is important to consider the relevance and level of 
involvement of stakeholders, which depends on how many stakeholders will be affected by the remediation, 
and thus depends on the specific site.  
To access the different challenges and improve the implementation of sustainable remediation in Denmark, it 
is necessary to develop a Danish framework for sustainable remediation. Such a framework must contain, 
which methods are recommended with respect to the specific cases. Hence, a big contamination in an area with 
drinking water interests, should be assessed with a higher level of details than a smaller contamination. 
Therefore, it could be beneficial to apply one of two methods depending on the specific case. 
Rambøll A/S have developed a sustainable remediation assessment tool in cooperation with the Danish 
Environmental Protection Agency, which consists of a two-tier approach that depends on the specific case 
(Søndergaard et al., 2020). The assessment has not been published yet, but the report and the sustainable 
remediation tool SURE was provided in connection to this project. Tier 1 is the simple assessment that is 
relevant for smaller remediation projects, and a qualitative or semi-qualitative method is applied during the 
==PAGE:88==
 
Sustainable Remediation Assessment 
 
 
 
79 
 
screening for the different remediation options. Stakeholders are not involved in tier 1 due to the limited time 
frame. On the contrary, a tier 2 approach is recommended for the larger remediation projects, which requires 
more time to perform, and thereby involve stakeholders in the semi-quantitative or quantitative methods. This 
differentiation between the two approaches is understandable and even though stakeholders are not involved 
in the tier 1 approach, the screening of the remediation options are still based on stainable methods. Regarding 
tier 2 and the stakeholder involvement, the methodology does not recommend how to select the relevant 
stakeholder or how many. Hence, a guideline for selecting the relevant stakeholders should be developed to 
support the tier 2 method. If no guidelines are made, the project manager will just assume which stakeholder 
are relevant and potentially leave out important stakeholders. To access the relevant stakeholders, a tool could 
be developed to support the involvement of stakeholders, by connecting the contaminated sites to the relevant 
stakeholders (key players, context, subject and crowd). Hence, a map showing the contaminated sites (in size), 
could provide a list with the affected stakeholders locally (neighbours, local community), regionally (NGO’s, 
municipalities, the Regions, other citizens), and nationally (politicians, NGO’s). Thus, the size of the 
contamination would be the critical parameter.  
The SURE excel tool was accessed to see if it could be applied for the MCA for this project. However, it was 
not possible to assess more than three remediation options, and it was therefore not applied. Even so, the tool 
has potential because it is easy to access and the connection to the 17 sustainable development goals gives the 
user an indication of the options’ performance and impacts on multiple levels. 
Sustainable remediation assessment is applied to compare the relative sustainability between the remediation 
options; however, it does not cover if the actual remediation option is sustainable. This includes the choice 
such as recycled versus virgin materials, local or imported materials, green energy, recycling/ reuse, or disposal 
of the materials after use. These considerations are relevant to incorporate if the purpose is not only to select a 
remediation technology that is relative but actual sustainable and thus has low secondary impacts on the 
environment, economy, and society. The question is if it is possible to make the current remediation 
technologies more sustainable or if new sustainable remediation technologies need to be developed. In 
connection to this, the pilot test at the Farum site is testing a new technology that combines the abiotic and 
biotic degradation of chlorinated ethenes, which thus is more effective and in the long run benefits the costs, 
if the effectivity is sufficient. However, the development and improvement of remediation technologies does 
not include the secondary impacts. Therefore, during the design of the technologies a detailed LCA should be 
applied to assess the environmental impacts and thereby show the hotspots of the remediation and where it is 
possible to make improvements. This requires a lot of data and time to perform, but it will potentially benefit 
the environment, economy, and society. 
The drinking water is becoming a scarce resource and human health is at risk if contaminated sites are not 
managed. However, one can ask themselves if the national security for good health and drinking water 
resources weights higher than the global impacts on environment, economy, and society. To accommodate 
both needs, the application of sustainable remediation is a step towards a global sustainable society.

11 Conclusion 
A sustainable remediation assessment has been conducted to investigate how the process works, how the 
methods are performed, and how applicable the assessment is. 
The project assessed the plume in the Farum site to select the most sustainable remediation technology. The 
plume consists of 11 kg chlorinated ethenes, and is thus a risk to the groundwater, drinking water resources 
and human health. Five remediation technologies were selected based on the characterisation and risk 
assessment of the Farum site; permeable reactive barrier with emulsified oil and KB-1 culture (PRB-EOS), 
permeable reactive barrier with Provect and KB-1 (PB-Pro), permeable reactive barrier with potassium 
permanganate (PB-ISCO), pump and treat facility (P&T), and monitored natural attenuation (MNA). 
The five remediation technologies were assessed in a multicriteria assessment (MCA) and a life cycle 
assessment (LCA). The MCA was conducted with involvement of 4 stakeholder groups who ranked and scored 
the different indicator categories, sustainability indicators, and remediation technologies. The results from the 
stakeholder workshops were applied in a MCA excel tool to obtain the best sustainability score for each 
technology. Additionally, a LCA model was designed in EASETECH to assess which remediation technology 
had the least environmental impacts. These results were applied in the MCA tool.  
The results from the MCA and LCA reveals that most sustainable remediation technology is permeable reactive 
barrier with emulsified oil and KB-1. However, an essential drawback from this technology is the formation 
of VC during reductive dichlorination, which thus increases the risk in drinking water and human health if VC 
is not reduced. Therefore, the developing remediation technology PRB with Provect and KB-1 has potential 
to be applied for the plume in the Farum site, because it reduces the chlorinated ethenes by applying abiotic 
and biotic degradation, which decreases the risk for the formation of VC. 
The selection of remediation technologies is an essential part of both the common practice and in sustainable 
remediation. Hence, the remediation technologies most be suitable for the specific site to obtain the best results. 
However, the approaches differ in the decision-making, which requires stakeholder involvement in sustainable 
remediation. The benefit from involving stakeholder is that the early integration of the stakeholders in the 
project improves the cooperation with the decision makers and the support to the selected remediation 
technology. In the common practice of remediation, the stakeholders are only contacted late in the process 
during the formal hearing of the remediation technology, which can lead to disagreements between the decision 
makers and stakeholders. However, reasons why stakeholder are not involved earlier is due to the effort is 
takes to select the relevant stakeholder and involve them sufficiently enough to be a part of the decision 
making. It is important to address the benefits from sustainable remediation, which is to assess the technologies 
secondary impacts, and thereby select the technology with least impact. The stakeholder involvement is a 
supporting tool and should therefore not be considered as a hampering for implementing sustainable 
remediation. To improve the implementation and application, a Danish framework must be developed for the 
Danish remediation industry, which should provide guidelines for both methods and selection of stakeholder.  
Additionally, improvement or development of sustainable remediation technologies is essential to implement 
in the remediation industry to obtain a complete sustainable remediation of a contaminated site. Hence, both 
costs, effects and secondary impacts of the technology must be assessed during the design of the technology. 
Considering the secondary impacts on the environment, it is relevant to perform a LCA that can reveal the 
hotspots and thus address where the technology can be improved. The LCA could be performed in 
EASETECH, which based on the experience in this project, shows potential to be applied for LCA of 
remediation technologies.

Appendix A 
Physical treatments 
Pump and treat, P&T 
Technical description: the pumping treatment of contaminated groundwater is a well-known technology with 
high experience and low costs. The treatment can last for decades and even hundreds of years, due to the 
limitations connected to the extraction and on-site treatment of the water. However, the technique is most 
efficient when the least amount of water is extracted, and thereby capturing the specific site of the 
contamination in the plume. The hydraulic parameters are used to obtain this state. The costs connected to the 
treatment relies on the amount of water, that is being extracted. Hence, extraction of higher quantities of water 
increase the costs. Additionally, the content of iron and manganese also affects the costs and treatment and 
must be removed by filtration before the treatment of the contaminants. The simplest method to remove 
chlorinated solvents is by filtrating the water through a black carbon filter and thereby achieve the acceptable 
levels of the contaminant. Afterwards, the water is led to a sewer or drain. The treatment can be costly in the 
long-term due to the slow extraction process and treatment, and the costs associated with the drainage 
(Orbicon, 2007). 
 
Chemical treatments 
In situ chemical oxidation, ISCO 
Technical description: chemical oxidation is based on the redox reactions between a strong oxidant and 
substrate, where electrons are exchanged. When an oxidant gain electron the oxidation stage is lowered, which 
is called reduction. Hence, the substate (e.g. TCE) loses electrons (i.e. oxidized), whereas the oxidant gains 
electrons (i.e. reduced). The most common oxidants are hydrogen peroxide, ozone, permanganate, and 
persulfate. Hydrogen peroxide and ozone have short lifetime but fast reagents, compared to permanganate and 
persulfate which has a longer lifetime but more slow reagents. The technology is typically applied on sites, 
where the contamination consists of a mixture of different contaminants, due to the effectiveness of strong 
oxidants, but all four oxidants are able to obtain complete degradation of TCE to ethene (Alleman et al., 2010). 
Regardless of the effectiveness, the oxidants are toxic to both aquatic organisms and hazardous to human 
health. Correct calculations of the amount of oxidant, a strict and controlled work environment is therefore 
essential (Orbicon, 2007). 
 
In situ chemical reduction, ISCR 
Technical description: the application of chemical reduction is only recently been introduced to in situ 
remediation of plumes (Alleman et al., 2010). Even so the technology is an effective method to remedy 
chlorinated solvents, due to the redox reaction between the substrate (e.g. TCE) and the reductant (e.g. iron(0) 
or ferrous iron). The reductant loses electrons (i.e. oxidized), while the substrate gain electrons (i.e. reduced). 
The application of ISCR is limited due to the dependency of the subsurface. This has led to multiple pathways 
of investigations focusing on how to get the iron to the substrate. One development of ISCR is the application 
of permeable reactive barrier (PRB), where Zero Valent Iron (ZVI) is injected in the groundwater plume. If air 
is still present, the redox conditions will change and become anaerobic due to the formation of rust. The 
decrease in the redox potential provides favourable conditions for reductive dichlorination of chlorinated 
==PAGE:96==
 
Sustainable Remediation Assessment 
 
 
 
87 
 
compounds to occur and thereby remove the contaminant (Orbicon, 2007). Additionally, it is possible to 
remediate all products and by-products of the chlorinated solvents. 
 
Biological treatments 
Enhanced reductive dichlorination, ERD 
Technical description: in the natural reductive dichlorination of chlorinated solvents, hydrogen atoms act as 
the electron donor, and the solvents (i.e. chlorinated solvent) as electron acceptors. The degradation is limited 
due to the non-existing presence of electron donors and anaerobic conditions. Reductive dichlorination 
enhance the degradation, by injecting or installing an in-situ permeable reactive barrier with organic substrate 
(e.g. molasses, chitin, compost, mulch, cheese whey, etc), which stimulate organic substrate to grow. Thereby, 
anaerobic conditions are established, and during fermentation of the substrate, hydrogen atoms are released. 
To increase the degradation and avoid accumulation of VC, microorganism such Dehalococcoides are injected 
to degrade VC to ethene (Alleman et al., 2010).  
 
Phytoremediation 
Technical description: several species of plants and trees have proved to be effective when biodegradation 
chlorinated solvents. The plants extract, sequester, transform, degrade, or transpire the contaminants from the 
soil or water and there by phytoremediation is obtained. The technology is highly dependent on the right 
conditions, e.g. hydrology, geology, etc. The technology is not costly, and accepted within the society, however 
the time-period is long (Alleman et al., 2010; Orbicon, 2007). 
 
Natural attenuation, NA 
Technical description: the natural degradation of contaminants is an approach where no remedial actions are 
executed. The presence of microorganism biodegrades the contaminants and thereby reduces the concentration, 
mass, and toxicity. The approach has no costs, but the clean-up takes very long to achieve acceptable levels. 
 
Monitored natural attenuation, MNA 
Technical description: Monitored natural attenuation is a approach, where limited remedial actions are 
performed, however, by monitoring the contamination makes it is possible to detect the degradation of the 
contamination, and thereby measure if the risk towards humans are increasing (i.e. need for more action) or 
decreasing. Hence, even though the remediation is not an “active” approach such as ERD and PRB, it still 
requires a fully characterisation and risk assessment of the site.  Additionally, evidence of natural attenuation 
is required before the monitors are installed. The approach is a long-lasting remediation, with moderate costs 
due to monitoring.  
 
Bioaugmentation 
Technology description: the biological activity of microorganisms that biodegrade chlorinated solvents in soil 
or groundwater can increase by introducing microorganisms that promotes the degradation. In 
bioaugmentation, two technologies are applied: aerobic co-metabolism and anaerobic reductive dichlorination. 
==PAGE:97==
 
Sustainable Remediation Assessment 
 
 
 
88 
 
Aerobic co-metabolism: uses the capability of an organism to grow on a compound to degrade another. With 
respect to the degradation of TCE, bacterial cultures can grow on compounds such as propane or toluene, and 
through aerobic co-metabolism degrades TCE (Alleman et al., 2010). However, issues are connected in the 
usage of aerobic co-metabolism: i) the by-products of TCE can be fatal for the microbial biodegradation, ii) 
inhibition of TCE destruction is likely to happen due to the co-metabolite, and iii) an optimal and effective 
mixing of oxygen (i.e. atmospheres air), organism and co-metabolites are challenging. Despite the issues, the 
advantage of aerobic co-metabolism is the unnecessity to obtain anaerobic conditions, which can be 
challenging to achieve (Alleman et al., 2010). 
Anaerobic reductive dichlorination: natural occurring organisms have proven to have the capability to 
biodegrade chlorinated solvents under anaerobic conditions. However, the natural process has a long time-lag 
and the favourable conditions are often not present. Therefore, to increase the degradation and decrease the 
time, the first step is to establish anaerobic conditions by injecting electron donors, and secondly injecting the 
microorganism Dehalococcoides which have proven to be very biodegradable efficient towards chlorinated 
solvents (Alleman et al., 2010). The anaerobic reductive dichlorination is considered being a low-cost 
technology, however it is time-consuming.

der er beregnet på baggrund af materialer, arbejdskraft, og 
eventuelle drift omkostninger efter installationen af 
oprensningsteknologien.  
Teknologiens modenhed 
Modenheden af teknologien afspejler hvor klar en 
teknologi er til at blive implementeret. Er der tale om en 
ny teknologi, vil det antages at teknologien er mindre 
moden. 
Teknologiens usikkerhed 
Sikkerhed og usikkerhed dækker over sikkerheden for om 
teknologien der anvendes, kan komme i kontakt med 
forureningen. Dette gælder ofte in-situ teknologier, der 
injiceres i forureningen, mobiliserer til forureningen og 
dermed kommer i kontakt med forureningen.
