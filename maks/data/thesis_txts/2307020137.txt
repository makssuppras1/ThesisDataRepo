Abstract
Air foil bearings has since the 1960’s, been an object of research as these
provide high velocity operation without friction, nor lubricant. However the
mathematical models, describing the dynamics of the bearings has not yet
been perfected and must be validated and corrected by comparison to ex-
perimental data. Excising experimental data is primarily obtained from far
smaller test rigs.
This thesis focuses on the experimental characterization of a near symmetri-
cal hollow rotor, supported by two identical 3-pad segmented air foil bearings.
Experiments has been conducted both statically and dynamically, in order to
obtain the stiﬀness of the bearings, the position in the bearings, Frequency
Response Functions, natural frequencies, damping factors and unbalance re-
sponse of the test rig. The obtained results can be used to adjust and modify
the mathematical models, in order to improve accuracy.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Gas bearings has been an objective of research, both experimentally and the-
oretically during the 20th century, as these are advantageous compared to
lubricated bearings. Until the 1960’s, the only gas bearings in object was the
rigid gas bearings. Rigid gas bearings allow high velocity operation with low
friction but suﬀers from numerous disadvantages such as extreme sensitivity
to misalignment and machining tolerances. With the invention of Air Foil
Bearings (AFB), the majority of the disadvantages is eliminated and Agrawal
[2] summed the advantages of the air foil bearings in 1997.
One of the major advantages of the AFBs are that these are much more
compliant to misalignment, as the foils are ﬂexible, compared to a rigid gas
bearing. As the AFBs are not depended on a lubricant, contamination of the
environment is non existing, and these can operate with air and numerous
types of gasses, such as helium, xenon and refrigerants, causing AFBs to be
very environmentally versatile. In addition to varying gasses, the AFBs are
capable to perform at both very high and low temperatures, compared to oil
lubricated bearings, where the viscosity of the oil is highly dependent on the
temperature. Finally, one on the major advantages of the AFBs are the ca-
pability of very high operating velocities. The AFBs has better load carrying
capability at higher velocities, allowing machinery such as turbine rotors to
perform at optimum velocity. As the AFBs is supported by a cushion of air,
the frictional resistance is very low, but this type of bearings also suﬀers from
various disadvantages. At start up and stop, the rotor is in contact with the
top foil, causing the friction and wear to be high. This requires the rotor to
be in continuous use, in order to expand the life time of the bearing. The
general load carrying capacity of the bearing is low compared to other types

of bearings, and the velocity of the rotor must be high in order to initiate a
gas ﬁlm capable of supporting the rotor. Even though the AFBs are more
compliant to misalignment of the bearings compared to rigid gas bearings,
tolerances to rotor and bearings must be very ﬁne in order for the bearing to
operate properly. Finally, the dynamical stability of the rotor is poor, due
to their damping and stiﬀness characteristics.
Especially for cases demanding clean environments, high velocities and
lower load varying capacities, with continuous operation, the AFBs are favourable.
1.2
Motivation and Objective
Since the 1960’s, air foil bearings has been an object of research, with the goal
to develop a mathematical model which can precisely describe and predict
to bearing dynamics. As the current mathematical models, used to describe
the 3-pad segmented bump foil bearings, are not yet fully characterizing the
system, the mathematical modelling continues. In order to perfect the math-
ematical model, it is crucial to posses experimental data to compare to, in
order to make correct adjustments.
The goal of this thesis is to experimentally characterize the rotor-bearing
rig, static and dynamically, in order to obtain various informations to im-
prove the mathematical model. This is to be done by ﬁrst conducting static
experiments in order to characterize the stiﬀness of the bearings as this is
not changing during operation. Furthermore, an extensive series of dynam-
ical tests is to be conducted in order to obtain the position in the bearing,
frequency responses, and unbalance responses of the rotor-bearing rig.

2.1.2
Power Cabinet
Within the power cabinet, an air-cooled KEB F5 110kW Variable Frequency
Drive (VFD), along with a 1,000kHz sinus ﬁlter for the SC20-01 Pole motor,
a series of capacitors and various coils, converts the power supplied to the
cabinet, to the power suitable for the electrical motor.
The combination
of the electrical drive, bearings and power cabinet is designed to have a
operational range of 15,000 to 30,000 rpm. During experiments it has been
observed that the power cabinet tends to overheat. This especially occurs
when the motor is rotating at high velocity, and the ambient temperature is
high. In order to reduce the temperature of the power cabinet and the VFD,
three small computer fans is mounted in the top of the cabinet to extract
the warm air, and one larger computer fan is mounted in the bottom of the
cabinet to establish a supply of cooling air. The fans are toggled on and oﬀby
a manual switch. A computer, connected to the VDF shows the temperature
of the VDF, and it is advised to stop the motor to let the VDF cool down if
the temperature exceeds 65◦Celsius. The VDF cuts power to the motor if
the temperature exceeds 70◦Celsius. On the cabinet, a larger red and green
button enables the user to short circuit the power to the motor, causing
the motor the coast down without induced current interrupting. The green
button must be activated during operation, as the rotor will not function
if not. Attached to the power cabinet is an emergency stop button. This
botton is used to start and stop the motor, and as an emergency stop, if
anything should fail.

Cooling System
During operation the motor generates vast amounts of heat which has to be
removed, in order to maintain the lifespan of both the electrical motor and
the bearings. The cooling system is a water cooled, closed system consisting
of a low pressure Grundfoss pump that maintains the ﬂow, a reservoir, and a
radiator, with a fan that reduces the temperature of the water. The water is
mixed with a coolant, in order to minimize corrosion within the motor. See

2.1.4
Data acquisition
To adequately acquire data from the test rig, a selection of sensors and data
in and output boards are used. To measure the displacement of the rotor in
the bearings during operation, four inductive Bently Nevada proximity sen-
sors are used. These measures displacement in both the x and y direction.
A ﬁfth sensor of the same type is used as a tachometer, to measure the ve-
locity of the rotor. This is mounted in a magnetic bracket, which is mounted
to the motor to ensure that the sensor is within the measuring range at all
times. This is critical, as the shaker excites the motor and can exceed the
measuring range of the tachometer sensor, if this is mounted on the table.
The data from the four proximity sensors measuring the shaft is sampled,
and digitally recorded by a National Instruments NI9229 analogue 4 channel
module with anti aliasing ﬁlter. The data from the tachometer proximity
sensor is sampled and digitally recorded using a NI9233 analogue 4 channel
module.
To investigate the steady state behaviour of the AFB’s, the system is exposed

Labview Programs
3.1
Programs
In order to record and process the signals from the NIdaq, several programs
are created, using National Instruments LabVIEW. LabVIEW is a system-
design based, data ﬂow programming platform that utilizes Graphic User
Interface (GUI). Programming is performed by structuring a block diagram,
where various functions connected by virtual wires determines the data ﬂow.
The program is controlled from a front panel during operation, from which
variables and data logging can be changed.
Several programs are created for the static characterisation, locus curves,
frequency response functions (FRF) and the unbalance response (UR). All
the programs are brieﬂy introduced in this chapter.
3.1.1
Foil Stiﬀness
The Foil stiﬀness VI uses the four proximity sensors to determine the dis-
placement of the rotor, as this is exposed to an external force. The magnitude
of the external force is measured using the HBM force transducer. Both dis-
placements and force is saved in a LabVIEWmeasurement ﬁle (.lvm). In
order to automatise the measurements, the program is designed to log the
data when a 50 N increase in force is detected and stabilized.

4.1
Introduction
As the AFB operates, the stiﬀness of the air ﬁlm changes depending on the
rotor velocity, changing the bearing coeﬃcients. The stiﬀness of the bump
foils and the air foil, on the other hand, does not. This is depending on the
materials, geometry and the interaction between the bearing elements.
The damping in the gas foil is very low and hence the critical speed and
the stability of the rotor setup is highly dependent of the bump and air foil
stiﬀness. The damping generated by friction between the bearing housing,
bump foil and air foil is very complex to model, as the dry friction is not yet
fully examined neither experimentally or theoretically.
4.2
Conduct

ness is examined. Force is applied to the rotor in both the x, y and combined
directions, using a screw jack. The screw jack is attached to the rotor by a
steel wire, which is wrapped around a pulley. The force applied is measured
using a HBM force transducer mounted between the Screw Jack and the pul-
ley. The pulley is used in order to obtain the speciﬁc angles and generate
the least possible friction, which is important, as any friction in the pulley
equals less force applied to the rotor. For future experiments, it will be ben-
eﬁcial to mount the force transducer between the pulley and rotor, as this
will measure the force without any losses to the pulley. This is not possible
on this test rig due to lack of space.

Chapter 5
Dynamic Behaviour
As explained in chapter 4, the position of the bearing center is diﬃcult to
determine.
To provide the essential information needed to determine the
center, it is interesting to examine the radial position of the rotor during
operation. In addition to the radial position in the bearing, the axial position
during operation is also investigated. The rotor is only supported by the two
AFB’s, but these only limits the radial degrees of freedom. As the rotor moves
axially, the load is shifted between the bearings, altering the bearing and
rotor dynamics. During operation, the electrical motor has great inﬂuence
of the position of the rotor in the bearing. This is due to the electrical ﬁeld,
pulling in the permanent magnets on the rotor.
To eliminate the eﬀects
from the motor, the position of the rotor is obtained during a coast down of
the motor. To investigate the inﬂuence from the motor, the position is also
determined at various constant velocities.
5.1
Radial Position or Locus Curve
To obtain the positions of the rotor during coast down is essential, as it is
very diﬃcult to model the electrical motors inﬂuence on the shaft during
operation. Most numerical models assumes that no other external forces or
attributes is present, but those from the rotor and bearings.

Frequency Response
6.1
Introduction
In order to fully determine the characteristic of the rotor-bearing test rig, it
is necessary to identify the dynamic properties of the bearings at the opera-
tional velocities. The FRF is a method to describe the relationship between
the input, and the output at to points on the structure. This shows the
magnitude of the displacement, velocity or acceleration to the magnitude of
the input force at which the system is excited. Once the FRF’s are obtained
for various velocities, an Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA) can be per-
formed from the FRF’s. The EMA method deals with the determination
of both natural frequencies and damping from the experimentally obtained
responses.

6.3
Preparations
The FRFs at various velocities are obtained by accelerating the rotor to a
desired velocity, and exciting the rotor, using a B&K shaker. The shaker is
mounted on the rotor trough a stinger which is attached to a disc mounted on
the rotor, with a stationary part. The stationary part of the disc is mounted
in the disc using two high velocity, precision ball bearings, that ensures best
possible force transfer, along with smooth operation during high velocities.
The force applied is measured using the B&K force transducer, and the data
is logged trough a NI9239 board.
The rotor is excited by linear chirps ranging from 20 to 300 Hz, over 30 sec-
onds. To cancel out the majority of measurement noise, the signals used in
42
==PAGE:51==
Chapter 6. Frequency Response
the FRFs contains 30 blocks of 1 chirp each. The response is recorded using
the 4 Bently Nevada proximity sensors mounted in the motor. The signals
from the proximity sensors and the force transducer are measured with a
frequency of 49 kHz.
Before any measurements are logged, it is desired to isolate the dynamics
of the rotor due to the excitation from the dynamics caused by the an-
gular velocity.
This is done by removing both mechanical, electrical and
operational runout from the signals. The mechanical run-out describes the
geometrical shape of the rotor. As the sensors are inductive sensors measur-
ing on a steel rotor, impurities in the material causes electrical runout to be
present. Finally these sensors are highly sensitive eddy current sensors, which
is mounted directly in a large electrical motor. To subtract these three types
of runout, the runout is measured at the desired velocity, in order to obtain
the best possible run out ﬁle, containing all unwanted eﬀects, associated to
runout. All signals are, as previously mentioned, measured at 49 kHz. A
lower sampling frequency of as little as 600 Hz generates the frequency spec-
trum in which the chirps excites, but this only produces 1.2 data points per
revolution at 28,000 rpm. To obtain a reliable, and the best possible runout
ﬁle a sampling frequency of 49 kHz is chosen, which produces approximately
105 point per revolution and is within the sampling range of the NI boards,
of 50 kHz.
6.4

The aim of obtaining the frequency Response Functions is to perform an ex-
perimental modal analysis, i.e. to obtain the modal parameters from which
the natural frequencies and damping factor can be computed. This approach
assumes linear dynamics of the system, but the rotor supported by AFBs is
expected to show non linear dynamics due to nonlinear properties of the bear-
ing foils and the air ﬁlm. In order to investigate the correlation between the
non linearity of the system, and the excitation amplitude from the shaker, an
experiment with various amplitudes is conducted. This os done for both the
horizontal and vertical direction, as a diﬀerence is expected between these.

In previous experiments, the frequency response functions is obtained with a
sampling rate of 49kHz, chirps of 20 to 300 Hz over 30 seconds, 30 blocks of

is chosen as 30 seconds to ensure proper excitation of all frequencies of the

providing a total of 31 Gb of data.

6.6.1
Introduction
The frequency response of a structure is a quantitative examination of the
input, compared to the response of the structure in the frequency domain.
The excitation amplitudes, which approximately generates linear behaviour
of the system in both directions, is determined in section 6.4 and the suﬃcient
amount of data and blocksize is determined in section 6.5.
As the stiﬀness and damping coeﬃcients is expected to vary depending of
the angular velocity of the rotor, it is interesting to investigate the behaviour
of this.
In order to obtain the coeﬃcients of the rotor-bearing system, the rotor is
excited in both x and y direction, in both the DE and NDE. As determined in
section 6.4, the excitation amplitude of the shaker is 1% in the y direction and
10% in the x direction. A blocksize of 147,000, and an overlap of 50% is used
during post processing. The rotor velocity is kept constant at each velocity
tested, and the runout is subtracted before logging the data. Prior to logging
data, the rotor is accelerated to the desired velocity, where it is allowed to
heat up, ensuring that the correct runout ﬁle is subtracted. Besides the warm
up procedure, the shaker is initiated and the response is observed to check
that the excitation amplitude is correct, prior to data logging.
Each experiment is conducted at night, in order for the ambient temper-
ature to drop, and cause the power cabinet to withstand the temperatures.
To ensure that the operational range is well represented, the experiment is
conducted for of 12,000 to 28,000 rpm with intervals of 1,000 rpm The data
logging of 50 sets of 30 seconds, along with the preparation period yields
an experimental duration with a period of 9 hours for each direction of ex-
citation. This yields a total experimental duration time of 28.3 hours and
generates 223.2 Gb data. The data is post processed in Matlab.

Chapter 7
Unbalance Response
7.1
Introduction
Essential to examining the behaviour of a rotor system is the UR. The UR
shows the eﬀect that a certain unbalance has on the rotor bearing system,
within a certain velocity range. This is an interesting to investigate as un-
balance might occur, either from a damage to the rotor or a loss in precision
when balancing the rotor.
Larsen [7] predicted and experimentally identiﬁed the presence of sub-synchronous,
non linear vibrations of the rotor, within the operational range. These are
only to be present when an unbalance of the rotor is applied. These sub-
synchronous vibrations are to be further examined.
7.2
Method
To obtain the UR of the rotor, the rotor is accelerated to 27,000 rpm and
measurements are conducted during a coast down. It is detected in section
5, that the electrical motor has an inﬂuence on the rotor during operation,
and the coast down method is chosen to eliminate these eﬀects.
During
coast down, the response is measured with 49 kHz and a FFT is computed
every second. The computed FFTs are loaded to Matlab and plotted as
waterfall diagrams. As the measurements are obtained during a coast down,
the runout is neither computed nor subtracted.
Due to the eﬀect of the
electrical drive on the sensors, it is not possible to obtain a the mechanical
run-out of the rotor, as this can only be subtracted during the coast down,
if a higher resolution to the tachometer is available.
To properly examine the eﬀects on the response, caused by unbalance,
21 diﬀerent unbalance masses, of various magnitude has been mounted on

the magnitude of ± 2.5 g × mm, in both ends.

Andres and Kim [3] presented a mathematical model in which the gener-
ation of non-linear eﬀects from the air ﬁlm is neglected under the assumption
that the air ﬁlm is inﬁnitely stiﬀ. The non-linear eﬀects should be produced
primarily by the foils, of which the stiﬀness is obtained during a static exper-
iment, similar to section 4 with a cubic stiﬀness function ﬁtted. The model
predicts sub-harmonic vibrations, but signiﬁcant discrepancies in the URs
between the model and experimental data exists. The theoretical and exper-
imentally obtained sub-synchronous responses obtained by San Andr´es and
Kim, deviates much from those obtained in this experimental thesis, as no
bifurcations are present in their operational range.
Balducchi et al. [4] presents an experimentally obtained unbalance response
of a rotor supported by two identical AFBs. The experimentally obtained
URs show sub-synchronous responses that bifurcates at operational veloc-
ities. Furthermore the spectrum becomes richer with sub-synchronous re-
sponses as the unbalance is increased. The sub-synchronous vibrations how-
ever, does not resemble those obtained in this thesis, since those of Balducchi

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The work comprising this masters thesis is focused on experimental charac-
terisation of a 21 kg rigid rotor, supported by two identical Air Foil Bearings,
to provide data and information required to improve mathematical modelling
of the bearings. Through a static study of the bearings, the stiﬀness coeﬃ-
cients and bearing clearance is determined. The stiﬀness of each bearing is
highly anisotropic, and each direction in both ends shows similar behaviour.
The clearance is found to be 100µm is the Drive End bearing and 103µm in
the NDE bearing.
The rotor position is obtained for a coast down of the rotor, and for constant
velocities. During coast down, the position of the rotor in the bearing re-
veals a diﬀerence between the two bearings, as the bearing in the NDE shows
greater horizontal displacement than the DE bearing. The same behaviour
is observed during constant velocities, which also shows that the electrical
drive adds negative stiﬀness to the rig.
The axial displacement of the rotor is examined for both coast down and
constant velocities, which reveals an axial displacement of up to 50µm from
0 rpm to 28,000 rpm, and an axial displacement of 5µm during constant ve-
locity.
It is shown, through an examination of excitation amplitude dependency,
that the excitation must not exceed 8% of the bearing clearance, in order to
obtain roughly linear behaviour of the rotor-bearing test rig.
Prior to conducting a frequency response, a study of convergence is con-
ducted to identify the amount of information needed to obtain acceptable
FRFs. It is found that data equivalent to 50 chirps of 30 seconds leads to
suﬃciently accurate results.
A frequency response reveals three natural frequencies at ≈65 Hz, 100 Hz
and 150 Hz. All natural frequencies are highly depended of the velocity of
the rotor, as well as the corresponding damping factors. The mode shapes
87
==PAGE:96==

reveals that the natural frequency at approximately 65 Hz is a conical mode,
with larger amplitude at the NDE bearing. The mode shape associated to
the natural frequency of approximately 100 Hz is a conical mode ass well,
but with a note close to the NDE bearing.
During an unbalance response with 21 diﬀerent unbalances, sub-synchronous
vibrations comparative to half speed whirl occurs, and is found to increase
in magnitude and richness as unbalance is increased.
The data obtained during this thesis, to describe the system is measured
over a total period of 50 hours, generating approximately 308.7 Gb data.
8.1
Further Work
During the FRFs computed, several peaks just above and below half velocity,
which is similar to the sub-synchronous vibrations, is identiﬁed during the
unbalance response.
To examine if this is the case, it will be interesting
to conduct an unbalance response of the rotor, with the disc and shaker
mounted. Furthermore, as the test rig shows non-linear behaviour, it will be
interesting to perform high shaker amplitude excitation frequency responses
with linear chirps from 20 to 300 Hz, and down again, to show if any crest
behaviour is detectable. Finally, it is interesting to look into the behaviour of
the rotor-bearing test rig as the velocity is increased, to see the development
of the sub-synchronous vibrations.
