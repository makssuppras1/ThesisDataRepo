Abstract 
As the prevalence of food allergy (FA) appears to be rising, this study aimed to estimate the burden 
of disease (BoD) of FAs in the Nordic countries, in which tree nut allergy (TNA), peanut allergy 
(PA), and cow’s milk allergy (CMA) were identified as the most relevant. An incident-based 
approach was used to estimate disability-adjusted life years (DALYs), including years lived with 
disability (YLD) and years of life lost (YLL). Besides, disability weights (DWs) for FA were 
developed, and a comprehensive literature search provided data of relevant parameters for the DALY 
estimation. Overall, TNA (12776.55 DALY; 95%UI: 6282.81 – 22661.72) accounted for the highest 
burden in the Nordic countries, followed by PA (9332.07 DALY; 95%UI: 6513.84 – 12747.77) and 
CMA (8031.01 DALY; 95%UI: 5291.62 – 11548.43). The DALYs were primarily driven by YLD, 
indicating that FA is affected by quality of life (QoL) rather than premature mortality. Denmark 
presented the highest burden of TNA, Finland the highest burden of PA, and Norway the highest 
burden of CMA. In contrast, CMA showed the lowest DALY per case, indicating a lower loss of 
healthy life years compared to PA and TNA cases. However, across the countries, DALY per case 
was very similar within each FA. For the Nordic countries combined, the total BoD for FA in 2024 
was estimated to 30139.68 DALY (95%CI:20851.86 – 42611.12), while inclusion of worst-case 
scenario resulted in 37049.87 DALY (95%CI: 25849.58 – 51036.24). The burden was higher 
compared to other chronic diseases such as diabetes mellitus type 1, rheumatoid arthritis and atopic 
dermatitis, but lower than asthma. However, each specific FA presented a lower burden than these 
chronic diseases. These findings highlight the need for further research and development of 
management strategies, including raising awareness, providing psychological support for FA patients, 
and improving immunotherapy approaches.

1. Introduction 
Globally, food allergy (FA) poses an increasing health burden, affecting an estimated 220 million 
individuals [1], [2]. Approximately 8% of children and 2% of adults are estimated to have FA, and 
the disease has been recognized as a public health concern by both the World Health Organization 
(WHO) and the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) [3].  
Beyond the physical symptoms, FA has a significant impact on the psychological and social aspect, 
not only for allergy patients but also for their relatives. The constant fear of accidental exposure might 
contribute to decreased quality of life (QoL), anxiety, and social well-being. Moreover, FA 
contributes to an economic burden including aspects such as increased health care expenses and 
educational awareness for both individual and society.  
The term allergy derives from the Greek allos (other) and ergon (activity) [4], referring to an adverse 
overreaction by the immune system to normally harmless substances. These substances, known as 
allergens, provoke an immune reaction often involving specific immunoglobulin E (sIgE) antibodies. 
Within the European Union (EU), 14 allergens are regulated by law and must be clearly declared on 
food labels. These include cereals containing gluten, milk, egg, peanut, tree nuts, soy, fish, 
crustaceans, molluscs, celery, lupin, sesame, mustard, and sulfites [5].  
FAs present with a wide range of symptoms, severities, and disease courses. Misclassification is often 
seen between FA and food intolerance due to overlapping symptoms, treatment strategies, and 
lifestyles adjustments, which complicates the disease burden estimation of FA. To date, no other 
studies have quantified the burden of disease (BoD) of FAs in the Nordic countries, except for one 
study investigating peanut allergy (PA) in Denmark [6]. Considering the increasing prevalence of 
FAs and their wide-ranging impact, it is highly relevant to quantify their associated BoD. Disability 
adjusted life years (DALYs) is an appropriate method for this purpose, supporting health policy 
prioritization and allocation of resources.

2. Problem formulation  
In this project, we aim to answer the research question: What is the burden of disease (BoD) of the 
most relevant food allergies in the Nordic countries, and how can it be estimated by Disability 
Adjusted Life Years (DALYs)? Additionally, we aim to investigate how large the burden is compared 
to other diseases, and how such estimations can be applied in health management. 
 
3. Background 
 
3.1 Burden of Disease  
BoD is a methodology used to quantify the overall impact of diseases and injuries on individuals and 
populations, by including both mortality and morbidity. The largest study within this field is the 
Global Burden of Disease (GBD) study [7]. In 1993, the concept of Disability Adjusted Life Years 
(DALY) was established by the WHO, the World Bank, and C. Murray and A. Lopez. DALYs are 
used to estimate the overall BoD in a population by measuring the number of healthy life years lost 
due to disability or premature death [8], [9], [10].  
Before the introduction of DALYs, BoD was mainly assessed using mortality data alone. However, 
this approach did not cover the entire spectrum of disease burden, especially according to nonfatal 
cases [8], [9], [10]. The GDB study provided a more comprehensive picture by incorporating both 
fatal and nonfatal cases, and enabled comparison of diseases among countries, sexes, age groups, and 
time periods. Moreover, it was a tool to prioritize and allocate resources for healthcare, research, and 
management [11]. Another method to measure BoD is Quality Adjusted Life Years (QALYs), which 
aim to quantify the effect of a clinical intervention against a disease compared to the economic cost. 
QALYs are used to measure the cost-effectiveness of the extra lifetime that could be achieved by an 
intervention [12]. QALYs were not applied in this project, but rather the DALYs were calculated to 
measure the overall population health status.  
 
Disability Adjusted Life Years 
DALY was developed to quantify BoD in specific populations by integrating mortality, morbidity, 
and DW. DALY measures the sum of healthy life years that are lost due to either disability or 
premature death, in which one DALY is equal to one year of healthy life lost in the population due to 
a disease. A negative DALY indicates a health gain for the population. DALY comprises two 
==PAGE:8==
 
Page 3 of 77 
 
components: Years of Life Lost (YLL) and Years Lived with Disability (YLD) which together 
represent DALY, the total BoD (equation 1) [10], [13]. YLL estimates the years of life lost due to 
premature mortality from the disease and is calculated by multiplying number of deaths by the 
Standard Expected Years of Life Lost (SEYLL) in a specific population (equation 2). SEYLL 
represents the difference between the standard life expectancy and the Age of Death (AoD), and it is 
based on the highest observed life expectancy, which in 1994 was seen in the Japanese population. 
The standard life expectancy was set at 82.5 years at birth for females and 80 years at birth for males 
[9]. Additionally, YLD measures the patient’s impairment within the duration of the disease, by 
combining incidence of disease, disease duration, and the corresponding disability weight (DW) 
(equation 3). The DW quantify the severity of health states on a scale from 0 to 1 in which 0 represents 
perfect health and 1 represent death. To estimate duration, important factors include Age of Onset 
(AoO) and possible Age of Resolve (AoR). DALY was visualized in Figure 1.

Disability weight 
There is currently no existing DW for FA. DW is an important component in the calculation of 
DALYs, as they quantify the severity of different health conditions. DWs developed by GBD study 
==PAGE:9==
 
Page 4 of 77 
 
are derived from population surveys using paired comparison questions, where participants are asked 
to judge which of two hypothetical health outcomes is healthier. This approach helped to achieve the 
ranking of diseases based on perceptions.  This GBD study combing data from 2010 and 2013 
included a total of 235 health outcomes [15]. In general, Quality of life (QoL) is a fundamental but 
complex concept to assess in the BoD, as it does not only account for the physical health. It might be 
difficult to define as perceptions of quality of life and well-being varies among individuals. According 
to the World Health Organization (WHO), QoL is defined as “an individual's perception of their 
position in life in the context of the culture and value systems in which they live and in relation to 
their goals, expectations, standards and concerns” [16].  
 
3.2 Food hypersensitivity 
Food hypersensitivity (FHS) describes an adverse reaction towards a food and involves either an 
immune-mediated or non-immune mediated response. FA is caused by an immune-mediated reaction, 
whereas food intolerance is a non-immune reaction, thereby not considered an allergy. In general, FA 
can be divided into three main categories: IgE-mediated FA, non-IgE mediated FA, and mixed 
IgE/non-IgE-mediated FA. A general overview of hypersensitivities can be seen in Figure 2.

3.2.1 IgE-mediated food allergy 
IgE mediated FA is considered the classic type of allergy, characterized by an immediate and 
abnormal immune response occurring within minutes to a few hours after exposure to a specific 
allergen [17]. The reaction includes the production of allergen specific IgE (sIgE) antibodies, that 
bind to mast cells and basophils, causing the release of pro-inflammatory mediators such as histamine 
[18], [19]. This immune activation results in a wide range of symptoms, often affecting several organ 
systems including skin, gastrointestinal (GI) tract, and respiratory system. Common cutaneous 
symptoms include erythema, urticaria, and swelling, while GI symptoms present as abdominal pain, 
diarrhea, vomiting, and nausea. Respiratory symptoms can include shortness of breath and rhino-
conjunctivitis [18]. In non-severe cases of IgE-mediated FA, which are particularly common in adults, 
oral allergy syndrome (OAS) is a frequently observed symptom covering mild symptoms such as 
itching or swelling of mouth, eyes, nose, ears and throat. However, non-severe cases are not always 
associated with OAS and might also include mild symptoms related to the GI system. In severe cases, 
IgE mediated reactions might develop into systemic anaphylaxis, a potentially life-threatening 
condition affecting multiple organs including the cardiovascular system [17].  
Primary- and secondary IgE-mediated food allergy:  
IgE-mediated FA can be further divided into primary- or secondary FA based on the origin of the 
initial sensitization. Primary FA occurs through direct sensitization to the food allergen which mainly 
occurs through the oral route and often develops in early childhood [20]. Importantly, primary FA is 
the subtype most frequently linked to severe reactions, including anaphylaxis. 
In contrast, secondary IgE mediated FA results from cross-reactivity between structural similar 
allergens of pollen and foods, known as pollen food syndrome (PFS). PFS is seen in patients with 
prior existing pollen allergy and due to the cross-reactivity, sensitizations to food allergens occur 
[21]. PFS is more prevalent in adolescents and adults, and mainly a life-long condition. Symptoms of 
PFS are often mild and localized to OAS. The symptoms are often seasonal and escalate in periods 
with high pollen exposure. Even though systemic reactions are rare, studies have reported anaphylaxis 
in up to 1.7% of PFS cases [22].  
 
3.2.2 Non-IgE mediated food allergy 
Non-IgE mediated FA is a T-cell mediated immune reaction, and unlike IgE mediated FA, non-IgE 
mediated FA often occur hours or up to days after consumption of the food allergen. The symptoms 
are diverse and often non-specific, mainly affecting the GI system with symptoms such as vomiting 
==PAGE:11==
 
Page 6 of 77 
 
and diarrhea, as well as the skin, presenting as eczema [19]. The three main types of non-IgE mediated 
FA include food protein-induced enterocolitis syndrome (FPIES), food protein-induced allergic 
proctocolitis (FPIAP), and food protein-induced enteropathies (FPE) [23]. These conditions mainly 
affect infants and young children, often resolving during early childhood. They are characterized by 
subacute or chronic inflammation of the GI tract [24].  
FPIES primarily causes intestinal inflammation and often presents with diarrhea and repetitive 
vomiting within four hours after consuming the suspected food. It is mostly known as an acute 
condition but can develop into a chronic form [25], and in severe cases cause dehydration and 
hypovolemic shock [26]. On the other hand, FPIAP is generally a milder condition and is considered 
the most common non-IgE mediated FA [25]. The FA affects the rectum and colon, causing 
symptoms such as rectal bleeding and mucus in the stool, although the infant may seem healthy [27]. 
In contrast, FPE is a rare and chronic condition affecting the small bowel leading to diarrhea, change 
in the structure of the intestinal mucosa, and malabsorption of fats, carbohydrates, and nutrients [25], 
[26], [28].  
 
3.2.3 Mixed IgE and Non-IgE mediated food allergy 
A subtype of FA is mixed IgE and non-IgE mediated FA, which is caused by the mix of IgE mediated 
and cell mediated immune responses, contributing to a combination of immediate and delayed 
symptoms. This type of allergy is associated with conditions such as atopic dermatitis (AD) and 
eosinophilic gastrointestinal diseases (EGIDs) including eosinophilic esophagitis (EoE). AD is a skin 
related condition characterized by a damaged skin barrier and inflammation [26], [29]. EGIDs is a 
group of chronic conditions in the GI tract, with EoE presenting symptoms such as vomiting, 
regurgitation and dysphagia. Eosinophils are white blood cells that cause inflammation if they 
accumulate in different areas of the GI tract [25]. The diagnosis of mixed IgE and non-IgE mediated 
FA can be difficult due to delayed symptoms, and some might be chronic making it more difficult to 
distinguish between the evidence of allergy. There is a lack of clinical tests, and often the diagnosis 
is based on symptoms that disappear by elimination diet [9]. 
 
3.2.4 Intolerance 
Food intolerance describes a non-immune mediated reaction to food, primarily involving the digestive 
system due to lack of digestive enzymes in the GI system. The response time can be hours to days, 
and mainly include GI symptoms such as abdominal pain, bloating, diarrhea, but can also involve 
==PAGE:12==
 
Page 7 of 77 
 
headache, heartburn and nausea. Symptoms might be similar to non-IgE mediated FA. Food 
intolerance does not cause life-threatening symptoms, and generally small amounts of the culprit food 
can be tolerated without significant symptoms [19].  
Food intolerance is more prevalent than FA, with the most common type being enzymatic intolerances 
including lactose, gluten, and fructose. Lactose intolerance is caused by a deficiency of lactase, an 
enzyme responsible for the digestion of lactose, the sugar disaccharide found in milk. In the same 
manner, gluten intolerance (non-celiac gluten sensitivity) is caused by undigested gluten protein in 
wheat due to lack of specific digestion-enzymes [30]. Besides, fructose intolerance results from a 
deficiency of a protein important in the fructose absorption [19]. Another type is pharmacological 
intolerance to compounds such as food additives, caffeine and histamine [24]. As this project wanted 
to investigate FA, food intolerance was not considered further.  
 
3.2.5 Autoimmune and other food related diseases 
There can be many reasons for adverse reactions to food, but the symptoms are not always caused by 
allergies or intolerances. One of the main diseases that causes adverse reactions to foods is irritable 
bowel syndrome (IBS). IBS is not fully understood, but it is a functional chronic disease, causing 
abdominal pain and other unspecified symptoms due to gut/brain-interactions. It is suspected that it 
might be an intolerance to fermentable oligo-, di-, monosaccharides and polyols (FODMAPs), which 
are all naturally carbohydrates in many foods and vegetables. There are no specific biomarkers, 
however, in order to get the diagnosis, other diseases such as celiac disease, inflammatory bowel, and 
colorectal cancer need to be ruled out [30].  
Another condition is the autoimmune disease ulcerative colitis, which is characterized by 
inflammation in the mucosal layer of the colon. The main symptoms include abdominal pain and 
diarrhea, with severe cases potentially leading to anemia. Ulcerative colitis presents in the rectum and 
colon, distinguishing from Crohn’s disease, that affects the entire GI system and influences multiple 
layers of the colonic wall. The underlying reasons for both diseases are unknown and no biomarkers 
are currently available for diagnosis [31].   
Celiac disease is a better understood autoimmune disease triggered by ingestion of gluten. Celiac is 
a chronic intestinal condition where the immune system attacks the body’s own tissue and damages 
the mucosa in the small intestine. The damage can cause symptoms such as diarrhea, and for long-
term conditions it might cause malabsorption of nutrients and weight loss [32], [33].

3.3 Immunologic mechanisms 
The immune mechanism behind an allergic reaction is important to distinguish between specific types 
of FA. Immune cells, such as dendritic cells, are constantly on alert to recognize and defend against 
foreign substances [34]. When the immune system recognizes a hazardous compound known as an 
antigen, it initiates a defense reaction. In FHS, this reaction is commonly induced by a food protein 
entering the body that is misrecognized as something foreign and dangerous. At this point, the protein 
is known as an allergen and the exposure can occur through ingestion, inhalation, and contact by skin. 
In general, there are four types of hypersensitivity defined by their mechanism, in which type I and 
type IV are relevant for FA. All types of hypersensitivities develop through two phases. The first 
known exposure to a specific allergen is referred to as the sensitization, whereas the following 
exposure to the specific allergen termed the effector phase [35].  
 
3.3.1 Type I hypersensitivity  
Type I hypersensitivity is characterized by an immediate reaction in which IgE recognizes antigens, 
causing degranulation of mast cells, thus secretion of histamine and other pro-inflammatory 
mediators. This type of hypersensitivity is seen in systemic anaphylaxis, asthma, allergic rhinitis, 
atopic eczema, and urticaria [36]. The mechanism of type 1 hypersensitivity can be seen in Figure 3.  
In the primary immune response, an allergen is found by dendritic cells, a type of antigen presenting 
cells (APCs) that recognize and transport the antigen to the lymph nodes. The antigen is presented on 
a major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class II, which binds the antigen-specific T-cell receptor 
(TCR) of naive T-cells. The naive T-cells become activated and differentiate into T-helper 2 (Th2) 
cells, which is driven by the cytokine interleukin-4 (IL-4). The Th2 cells then produce IL-4, IL-5 and 
IL-13 which induce B-cells to undergo class switching and somatic hypermutation, contributing to 
the production of IgE antibodies by plasma cells [37]. Besides, Th2 cells also produce IL-5 and IL-9 
which is responsible for the activation of eosinophils. The IgE antibodies are attached via high affinity 
receptors called FcεRI expressed on the surface of mast cells and basophils [37], [38].  
At the subsequent exposure to a specific allergen, sIgE antibodies have already been produced by the 
B-cells and attached to the surface of mast cells and basophils by the FcεRI receptor. When the 
allergen enters the body, a cross-link between the allergen and the antibodies causes the mast cells or 
basophils to degranulate and release specific pro-inflammatory mediators such as histamine, 
==PAGE:14==
 
Page 9 of 77 
 
prostaglandins, and leukotrienes. These mediators cause blood vessel dilation to increase blood flow 
and contract the smooth muscles which can cause respiratory symptoms [39]. These reactions are part 
of the early phase of the allergic reaction that happens within seconds to minutes [37]. The late phase 
reaction develops 2-6 hours and peaks 6-9 hours after the exposure of the culprit allergen. It involves 
recruiting and activation of more immune cells such as Th2 cells, eosinophils, and basophils. 
Eosinophils are mainly recruited after the basophils and amplifies the reaction by releasing mediators 
such as toxic granule proteins, cytokines, and lipids [34]. Overall, the activation of these cells can 
cause inflammation issues such as oedema, erythema and pain [39].

involves the initial exposure to the specific allergen which is taken up by antigen presenting cells (APCs), which is presented to a naive

3.3.2 Type IV hypersensitivity  
Type IV hypersensitivity is characterized by a delayed reaction in which the immune system responds 
to an antigen multiple hours or days after the exposure. In contrast to antibody-mediated 
hypersensitivity, type IV hypersensitivity is a cell-mediated reaction that involves Th1 cells and 
cytotoxic T-cells (CD8+), as seen in Figure 4. Non-IgE mediated FA is mostly seen as a type IV 
hypersensitivity, and other examples of type IV include contact dermatitis and autoimmune diseases 
==PAGE:15==
 
Page 10 of 77 
 
such as rheumatoid arthritis, diabetes mellitus type 1, and celiac disease [36]. As type IV does not 
involve histamine release, antihistamines are ineffective as treatment. 
During the sensitization phase, APCs recognize the antigen, transporting it to the lymph nodes, where 
the antigen is presented via MHC class II to naive CD4+ T-cells. These T-cells become activated and 
differentiate into Th1 cells. In the effector phase, Th1 cells secrete cytokines such as interferon-
gamma (IFN-γ), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), and IL-2. These cytokines activate 
macrophages and induce proliferation of more T-cells, which contribute a local inflammatory 
response. Moreover, CD8+ cytotoxic T-cells are activated, enabling them to destroy damaged and 
infected cells [29].

hypersensitivity. In the sensitization phase, the initial exposure to the specific allergen is taken up by antigen presenting cells (APCs) 
and presented to a naive T-helper-cell, contributing to the differentiation of T-helper 1 cells (Th1). This results in production of specific 
cytokines such as interferon-gamma (IFN-γ), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), and interleukin 2 (IL-2) In the effector phase, the 
subsequent exposure activates Th1 cells, which through cytokines further activate macrophages and cytotoxic T-cells. Together, this 
promotes inflammation and destruction of damaged and infected cells. The figure was created using BioRender [14].

3.4 Comorbidities, risk factors and cofactors 
FAs are often associated with specific diseases and factors, referred to as comorbidities, risk factors, 
and cofactors. Comorbidities refer to diseases that coexist with FA but are not directly caused by it. 
While they are developed independently, they might share risk factors. Risk factors are characteristics 
or exposures that increase the likelihood of FA which in some cases might develop into comorbidities 
[40]. In contrast, cofactors affect an existing FA, either by triggering a reaction or by worsening the 
severity of the symptoms. Table 1 provides an overview of FA-related conditions within the different 
categories.  
A cohort from the Food Allergy Research and Education (FARE) Patient Registry [41] investigated 
5587 patients with FAs in which they found the most common comorbidities related to FA to be 
atopic dermatitis (48%), asthma (46%), and allergic rhinitis (39%). The study also found that patients 
with several FAs were more prone to have comorbidities compared to patients with a single FA.  
Atopic dermatitis is a skin disease characterized by itchy, dry skin, red patches and scaly skin. The 
skin barrier is damaged, which might cause an increase in penetration of allergens through the skin 
which can provoke eczema. A comorbidity to FA is extrinsic atopic dermatitis, since this type is 
mainly caused by IgE mediated reactions to allergens. However, IgE is not always the trigger and 
some patients have the intrinsic atopic dermatitis which is caused by T-cell reactions [42]. 
Asthma is a disease characterized by inflammation in the airways contributing to symptoms such as 
wheezing, coughing and shortness of breath [43]. Therefore, uncontrolled asthma significantly 
increases the risk of fatal anaphylaxis in patients with FA. Asthma can be classified as allergic asthma 
(extrinsic asthma) and non-allergic asthma (intrinsic asthma). The allergic form is an IgE-mediated 
reaction triggered by allergens such as pollen and house dust mites, whereas the non-allergic form is 
triggered by other factors such as smoke, pollutants, intensive training, stress, and infections related 
to the respiratory system [44].  
Allergic rhinitis (hay fever) is an IgE mediated allergy and a common comorbidity to FA, particularly 
in cases described as PFS. Patients with allergic rhinitis are allergic to allergens such as pollen and 
dust mites, and the symptoms include runny nose, cough, and itchy and irritated eyes and nose [45].  
Risk factors related to FAs include factors such as genetic predisposition, age, and environmental 
exposure. A study from Finland found that children with one parent with FA had about twice the risk 
of developing FAs, whereas children with both parents suffering from FAs had a threefold increased 
risk, compared to children with parents without FA [46]. Age is another risk factor as specific FAs 
==PAGE:17==
 
Page 12 of 77 
 
tend to develop at specific ages. Environmental exposure such as air pollution and more hygienic and 
indoor lifestyle has also been indicated as risk factors [47]. 
In contrast, some of the most FA-related cofactors involve exercise, infection, and non-steroidal anti-
inflammatory drugs (NSAID). Exercise increases intestinal permeability which can increase allergen 
absorption. Moreover, mast cells and basophils included in histamine release are easier activated by 
exercise, which can induce the severity of symptoms. Drugs such as NSAID have been associated 
with food-induced anaphylaxis, due to the similar mechanisms as for exercise. Infections involving 
fever can cause increased blood circulation whereas GI infections might weaken the intestinal layer, 
increasing the availability of the allergen. In addition, alcohol and stress are also seen as co-factors 
as they are suspected to increase the intestinal absorption [48]. Cooking preparation methods have 
also been associated as cofactors in FAs, with dry roasting of nuts linked to an increase in allergen 
potency, whereas boiling and frying were found to reduce the allergenicity [49].

3.5 Anaphylaxis 
Anaphylaxis is the most severe outcome of an allergic reaction, which is seen as an acute and systemic 
reaction that can result in a fatal outcome. Due to the systemic reaction, several organ systems are 
involved such as skin, GI tract, respiratory tract and the cardiovascular system [19]. The reaction 
==PAGE:18==
 
Page 13 of 77 
 
includes symptoms such as difficulty in breathing, swelling, abdominal pain, low blood pressure, and 
even loss of consciousness. Rapid treatment including epinephrine auto-injectors is necessary to avoid 
potentially fatal outcomes [50]. The definition of anaphylaxis is complex and not universal. One of 
the most recognized definitions was conducted in 2004 by National Institute of Allergy and Infectious 
Disease (NIAID) and Food Allergy and Anaphylaxis Network (FAAN). The diagnosis of anaphylaxis 
includes that one of three criteria should be met:  
(1) Acute onset of an illness (minutes to hours) with involvement of skin, mucosal tissue, or both with 
either respiratory involvement or reduced blood pressure and/or associated symptoms of end organ 
dysfunction. 
(2) Two or more of the following that occur rapidly after exposure to a likely allergen for the patient, 
including (i) involvement of skin-mucosal tissue, (ii) respiratory involvement, (iii) reduced blood 
pressure or associated symptoms, or (iv) gastrointestinal symptoms.  
(3) Reduced blood pressure as a result of exposure to a known allergen trigger. 
The criteria were later validated by American Academy of Allergy, Asthma, and Immunology 
(AAAAI), American College of Allergy and Asthma, Immunology (ACAAI) and the World Allergy 
Organization (WAO) [50]. 
 
3.6 Diagnosis of food allergy 
Self-reported FA is common, and misunderstanding between FA, food intolerance and other type of 
FHS is frequently seen [51]. Due to lack of knowledge, individuals might mistakenly assume any 
adverse reaction to food as a FA. Generally, the prevalence of self-diagnosis of FA is higher than 
prevalence clinically confirmed by a professional. Symptoms to specific foods can be caused by other 
hypersensitivities than FA, and due to similar symptoms, accurate diagnosis can be challenging. 
Therefore, it is crucial to conduct structured approaches for correct diagnosis such as clinical history, 
diet diary, elimination diet, skin prick test (SPT), measurement of sIgE antibodies, and oral food 
challenge (OFC). In that way, the number of false positives should be reduced.  
The first step of diagnosis of FA often begins with a clinical history. Here, the aim is to identify 
suspected triggers, understand the symptoms including onset and duration, and consider the age of 
the individual. Moreover, to address severity of the reaction, portion size and preparation method of 
the suspected food is relevant [52]. In addition, identifying risk factors and potentially already 
existing allergies is beneficial to conduct the approach for diagnosis [4]. 
 
==PAGE:19==
 
Page 14 of 77 
 
If symptoms indicate an IgE mediated allergy, the IgE sensitization can be proved by FS tests such 
as sIgE blood test or SPT. EAACI provides guidelines for diagnosis of IgE mediated food allergies, 
which are divided into two parts. First, symptoms might develop within two hours of exposure to the 
food, and symptoms should be reproducible when the food is re-exposed. Secondly, FS to the specific 
food should be confirmed [53]. Even though the appearance of sIgE indicates sensitization, the test 
alone cannot confirm the FA diagnosis unless symptoms also have been reported [40]. 
In general, FS tests have high sensitivity, but lower specificity, which can result in false positives 
cases [23]. The blood test measuring the concentration of sIgE is considered positive when the 
concentration is equal or above 0.35 kU/l [55]. An advantage of the blood test is that it does not cause 
any allergic reaction, which increased safety of the patient. In contrast, SPT includes extracts of foods 
placed on the skin, which are penetrated into the skin by small pricks. The test is positive when a 
wheal measuring at least 3mm in diameter appears on the skin. SPT has the advantage that it is a 
quick, cost-efficient and easy approach to do with the patient [56]. Due to the actual exposure, 
individuals with suspected risk of severe reactions are excluded from SPT.   
Elimination diet can provide valuable information for IgE mediated allergies, however, due to more 
accurate testing methods for this type of FA, elimination diet is not used on its own. In contrast, this 
method is beneficial for non-IgE mediated FA, as no validated tests are currently on the market. Since 
the delayed response makes it more difficult to link the food to the symptoms, the elimination diet 
can be a great tool to assess symptom relief [23]. 
In cases where clinical history, FS tests and elimination diet do not provide any clear diagnosis, OFC 
can be conducted as a definitive diagnostic test, thereby also known as the gold standard test of FA. 
Furthermore, the test can assess eliciting dose and tolerance development. OFC is a supervised test, 
in which the suspected food is consumed by a gradually increased dose [57]. OFC can be performed 
as open, single-blind or double-blind placebo-controlled, in which the double-blinded placebo-
controlled food challenge (DBPCFC) is the most preferred test. This test includes a control food as 
placebo which limits bias of the test [58]. However, a consequence of OFC is the time-consuming, 
costly and resource demanding process. Moreover, OFC can be a stressful and anxious situation for 
the patient, as the test can lead to severe allergic reactions. Therefore, OFC is often excluded and 
instead the diagnosis is based on clinical history together with IgE sensitization.

3.7 Treatment of food allergy 
Currently, there is no cure for FA, and daily management of FA involves strict avoidance from 
exposure to the culprit food. If accidental exposure occurs, treatment for symptomatic relief is 
available including drugs such as antihistamine and corticosteroids [19]. Antihistamine can be used 
to relieve mild IgE mediated symptoms, and it works by blocking histamine receptors, counteracting 
the symptoms caused by the released histamine from mast cells and basophils [59]. Corticosteroids 
are steroids with anti-inflammatory effects against eczema and asthma, but the relief of symptoms 
may take longer time compared to antihistamines [19], [60]. In cases of severe allergic responses such 
as anaphylaxis, epinephrine, also known as adrenaline, is used to treat the reaction. Epinephrine 
enhances blood circulation, dilates the airways, and inhibits the release of histamine and leukotrienes 
from basophils [19]. Moreover, it inhibits contraction of smooth muscles, low blood pressure, and 
dilation of blood vessels [61]. The appropriate dose of epinephrine is easy to inject by using an EpiPen 
autoinjector, or other brands. Due to the fast and easy use of autoinjectors, the risk of severe reactions 
can be reduced, especially if injected within the first 20 minutes after consumption [62]. 
Furthermore, allergen immunotherapy (AIT) has been introduced as a promising proactive approach 
to improve management of individuals with FA. The purpose of AIT is to increase the tolerable 
amount of food by gradually increasing the threshold amount of allergen needed to trigger an allergic 
reaction, thereby reducing the risk of a severe allergic case. The most common route of the 
administration is oral route which can be oral immunotherapy (OIT) or sublingual immunotherapy 
(SLIT) [63]. Besides, subcutaneous immunotherapy (SCIT) is often used in individuals with pollen 
allergy and a well-established and integrated procedure. However, immunotherapy for FA remains 
under investigation and not yet integrated in the Nordic countries as a standard treatment option.

4.1 Selection criteria for food allergies 
This project was limited to assessing the three most relevant FAs in the Nordic countries. To ensure 
an evidence-based selection, three selection criteria. As seen in Table 2, a grading system for each 
criterion was conducted. To provide a comprehensive overview, it was decided to use a meta-analysis 
by Spolidoro et al. (2023) [66] of the “big eight” allergies and thereby exclude the remaining six 
allergens included in the European regulation. The selection criteria were based on prevalence, 
anaphylaxis registry, and severity, and the data should preferably be from the Nordic countries 
including Denmark, Sweden, Norway, Finland, and Iceland.  
The prevalence criterion was based on data from the meta-analysis [66], which provided prevalence 
estimated for each FA and country. The prevalence data was prioritized and graded according to the 
latest data and diagnostic method. OFC was considered as confirmed FA, thus had the highest 
priority. If OFC data was not available, probable FA data were used, defined as the combination of 
clinical history and FS tests such as SPT or sIgE testing. In the absence of these, isolated FS test and 
self-reported FAs were included but given the lowest grade, as such data was considered insufficient. 
The second criterion was based on data from the Anaphylaxis registry, which investigated the 
proportion of specific FAs among registered food induced anaphylaxis cases. The registry covered a 
period from 2007 to 2024 and included 4664 anaphylaxis cases within 11 European countries and 
Brazil [67], [68]. The Anaphylaxis registry reflects both the prevalence and severity of the specific 
FA but may also indicate lifestyle patterns related to the food and risk of accidental exposure. In 
addition, the data was divided by children and adults, identifying age groups of FAs with higher risk.  
The third criteria included the severity of each FA. The symptoms of FA can range from mild, to 
severe and life-threatening anaphylaxis reactions. The rate of anaphylaxis within each specific FA 
was considered beneficial to understand the severity of each FA. It was not possible to obtain country 
specific data, and one study for each food was included.  
To compare the FAs, each FA was graded based on the three criteria, outlined in Table 2. For the 
prevalence criterion, the highest prevalence grade for each FA across the studies were included. For 
the anaphylaxis registry, scores from children and adults were summed for each FA, thereby giving 
this criterion more weight. The severity grading was combined with the other two criteria, resulting 
in a final score for each FA.

4.1.1 Tree nut, peanut, and cow’s milk allergy 
Tree nuts cover a group of nuts and seeds including hazelnuts, almonds, walnuts, cashew, macadamia 
nuts, Brazil nuts, pecan, and pistachio, as defined in the EU [69]. TNA is primarily an IgE mediated 
FA which can present as either a primary or secondary type (PFS) form [68]. The major protein 
families involved in TNA reactions are 2S albumin, vicilin, legumin, and non-specific lipid transfer 
protein (nsLTP) [70]. Proteins such as Bet v 1 and profilin are associated with PFS cases. These 
==PAGE:24==
 
Page 19 of 77 
 
proteins are highly conserved and found in many different plants, causing cross reactions with pollen 
allergens [70]. 
Peanut is part of the legume family, thereby not part of the tree nut group [71]. Similar to TNA, PA 
is also an IgE mediated FA and can be either primary or secondary. 16 peanut allergens have been 
identified as Ara h1 to Ara h17 [72]. Studies indicate Ara h3 as the most abundant allergen in peanut, 
while Ara h1, Ara h2, and Ara h6 also are among the major peanut allergens [73], [74]. In contrast, 
Ara h8 is structurally similar to birch pollen allergen Bet v 1, and it associated with milder symptoms 
[75]. Moreover, co-allergy between PA and TNA are frequently seen, with estimates ranging from 
20-40% [76].  
CMA differs from TNA and PA, as the allergy can be either IgE-mediated or the non-IgE mediated 
type. The IgE mediated type causes immediate and potential severe reactions, whereas the non-IgE 
mediated type mainly cause delayed GI symptoms [77]. Cow’s milk consists of two major protein 
groups including casein (80%) and whey (20%). Caseins includes αS1-, αS2-, β -, and κ-caseins, 
while whey include α-lactalbumin and β -lactalbumin [78].

4.4 Parameters 
A meta-analysis model was conducted to determine the overall effect size of a parameter across 
multiple studies, with each study weighted by the number of participants. The analysis was developed 
using the software program R (version 4.4.1) and included the packages “metafor” and “dplyr”. The 
meta-analysis was conducted for prevalence, severity, and resolution rate. For both AoO and AoR, 
age-weighted means were calculated based on number participants. For mortality rate and AoD, 
simple mean values were performed (see Appendix 6 for mortality rates). 
Different study designs were included when assessing prevalence of TNA, as some studies measured 
specific nut types, whereas used the general term ‘nuts’. A scenario analysis for TNA was conducted 
using two approaches: 1) A mean prevalence of all reported nut types, and 2) a worst-case tree nut 
allergy (WTNA) scenario, estimated by the sum of all different nuts. The worst-case scenario was 
calculated for Denmark, Sweden, and Iceland, based on the assumption that there is no cross-
reactivity between the different nut types.

was self-reported and reported separately: 0.2% for almond, 2.7% for Brazil nut, 6.6% for hazelnut, 
and 0.5% for walnut. The self-reported approach may overestimate the true prevalence. In general, 
the response rate to the questionnaire was 77.1%, increasing the risk of selection bias, and a great 
number of suspected cases were lost to follow up.

Eller et al. 2009 [83] carried out a birth cohort of 562 children, using questionnaires and clinical tests 
such as SPT and sIgE, with follow up at 3, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36, and 72 months. Suspected FHS was 
defined as clinical history and a positive sIgE or SPT, and to confirm the suspected FHS, OFC was 
performed. Confirmed prevalence of PA and CMA was 0.71% and 1.42%, respectively. CMA was 
found to be the most common FA found among infants. A strength of the study is the longitudinal 
design including OFC, whereas a limitation includes loss of follow ups. The prevalence estimate may 
not be representative for the general population. As many FAs develop in early life, the estimate could 
overestimate the prevalence across all groups.  
A cross-sectional study by Mortz et al. (2005) [84] investigated 979 unselected adolescents. 
Participants with clinical symptoms and a positive FS test were offered OFC. Prevalence of confirmed 
PA was found to be 0.5%, compared to FS tests with prevalence estimates of 5.8% (sIgE) and 3.4% 
(SPT), emphasizing the difference between sensitization and confirmed FA. Moreover, the study 
found a correlation between pollen (grass) sensitization and peanut sensitization. Strengths of the 
study include the unselected population and the confirmation by OFC. Besides, the age of the study 
population should be representative for lifelong PA. A limitation is that only 44% of the sensitized 
individuals participated in OFC, potentially underestimating the true prevalence.  
Sweden:  
A cross-sectional study by Rentzos et al. (2019) [85] investigated 1042 participants, aged 17-78 years, 
through self-reported questionnaires, interviews, and sIgE test. Based on clinical symptoms and sIgE 
test, the highest TNA prevalence was 5.3% for hazelnut (95%CI: 4.0-6.7). For other types of nuts, 
prevalence was for almond 0.8% (95%CI: 0.2-1.3%) and for Brazil nut 0.4% (95%CI: 0.0-0.8%). 
Prevalence for PA and CMA was 0.5% (95%CI: 0.1-0.9%) and 0.0%, respectively. 14.6% of the 
participants showed to be sIgE sensitized to birch pollen, emphasizing that birch pollen allergy is 
common in the Swedish population. The study great sample size strengthens the reliability of the 
study even though a limitation was the participation rate for sIgE test (59%) which increases the risk 
of bias.  
Sterner T. et al 2019 [86] conducted a cross-sectional study, assessing 195 children aged 13-15 years. 
Based on self-reported questionnaire and sIgE test, the prevalence for TNA, PA, and CMA was 0.5%, 
1%, and 0% respectively. According to CMA, the prevalence may be zero because of potentially 
CMA cases are resolved at the investigated age. The clinical test is a strength of the study. However, 
the sensitized TNA and PA individuals were only sensitized but did not report any symptoms, which 
==PAGE:31==

could indicate an overestimation of the true allergy prevalence. Furthermore, the small sample size 
decreases the strength of the study.  
A cohort study by Winberg A. et al (2015) [87] including 2612 subjects were invited to participate in 
a questionnaire at age 7-8 and followed up with questionnaire at age 11-12 years old. At the follow 
up, a representative random sample of 695 participants were tested by sIgE test. Participants with 
suspected allergy were further tested by OFC which indicated a confirmed CMA prevalence of 
0.15%. Diagnosis of OFC reflects one of the strengths of the study. However, the included age group 
mainly represents the IgE mediated form, since the non-IgE mediated CMA. Individuals with non-
IgE mediated CMA may have resolved before the time of assessment.   
Östblom et al. [88], [89] analyzed data from the Swedish BAMSE birth cohort of 4089 children, that 
conducted questionnaires as 1-, 2-, and 4-years old. Furthermore, at the age of 4, 2336 children were 
investigated for sIgE sensitization for FA diagnosis. The prevalence of PA and CMA was estimated 
to be 1.5% and 1.1%, respectively. Even though the FA diagnosis was based on symptoms and sIgE 
test, the lack of OFC may contribute to an overestimation of the prevalence. On the other hand, the 
study population was large and unselected, increasing the validity of the results.  
A cross-sectional study by Johansson et al. (2005) [90] investigated sIgE levels of different allergens 
among 1002 blood donors. Prevalence was 3.5% for hazelnut, 2.3% for peanut, and 0.7% for milk. 
The absence of reported symptoms may overestimate the prevalence, thus not indicating true allergy 
patients. Two other cross-sectional studies by Bröms et al. [91] and Strinnholm et al. [92], based on 
self-reported data, examined FHS in 5886 pre-school children and 2585 school children, respectively.  
Bröms reported prevalence of TNA (2.3%), PA (2.4%) and CMA (4.3%), whereas Strinnholm 
estimated prevalences of 3.6%, 3.2%, and 9%, respectively. Even though the studies included a great 
number of participants, self-reporting might give an overestimation of the true FAs.  
Norway:  
Johansson et al. (2005) [90] investigated the sIgE levels of allergens in 500 blood donors. The 
prevalence of hazelnut, peanut, and milk were found to be 0.6, 0.6, and 0% respectively. Since 
symptoms or OFC were not included, this prevalence might be an overestimation of the true allergy. 
A birth cohort by Kvenshagen et al. (2009) [93] investigated 512 children at the age of 2 years. 
Symptoms indicated a suspected FA in 198 children; thus, they were invited for a clinical

examination. The diagnosis of FA was based on either the history of symptoms and SPT/sIgE or OFC. 
The prevalence of probable confirmed PA and CMA was found to be 1% and 5.5% respectively. A 
limitation includes the young age of the study population, which might underestimate the prevalence 
of PA since it could develop later in life. In addition, this CMA prevalence might be overestimated 
when compared to the total population as many children resolve the allergy later.  
Another cohort study conducted by Eggesbø et al. (2001) [94] included 2721 young children, in which 
54 with suspected CMA were further examined by diet SPT, and OFC at age 2.5. The OFC confirmed 
prevalence of CMA was 1.1% (95%CI: 0.8-1.6%). A strength of the study was the use of OFC which 
reduced the risk of false positive results. However, the prevalence estimate may be an underestimate, 
as individuals classified as having probable FA, based on symptoms and SPT, were not confirmed by 
OFC and therefore not included in the final prevalence. A limitation is the loss to follow up.  
Finland:  
A cross-sectional study by Hertzen et al. (2006) [95] examined 365 children aged 7-16 years, and 357 
of their mothers. FS was investigated through questionnaires and SPT. The prevalence of FS to 
hazelnut based on positive SPT was 6.3% for children and 11.3% for mothers. The prevalence of 
peanut sensitization was 8.2% and 10.1%, respectively, while it for milk was 0.3% and 2.8%, 
respectively. A limitation of the study included that the FAs were not confirmed by OFC. The 
diagnosis based on SPT might have caused an overestimation of the true prevalence. Moreover, 
including both children and mothers could affect the prevalence estimated due to genetic 
predisposition.  
Another cross-sectional study by Pyrhönen et al. (2009) [96] found prevalence estimates for TNA 
and CMA based on 3308 young children up to 4 years. FA diagnosis was based on questionnaires 
reported by the parent. The prevalence for TNA and CMA was 0.2% and 6.4%, respectively. A 
strength of the study is the use of an unselected study population increasing the representativeness of 
the results. However, a limitation is the reliance on parental recall and perception of physician 
diagnosis and symptoms. Furthermore, it was assumed that physician diagnosed includes symptoms 
and FS test, however, this was not clearly stated in the study. 
A cross-sectional study by Isolauri et al. (2004) [97] investigated a study population of four age 
groups each with 100 participants. Based on FS test, including sIgE test, the prevalence was 9% 
(95%CI: 4.1-16.4) at age 7, 4.4% (95%CI: 1.2-10.8) at age 27, 1% (95%CI: 0.03-5.5%) at age 47,

and 7.1% (95%CI: 2.9-14%) at age 67. In general, the testing method is a limitation since it is neither 
a confirmed FA nor probable FA. Besides, the variability in the CI is large, indicating uncertainty to 
the prevalence estimates.  
Two self-reported studies of TNA and CMA were conducted by Järvenpää et al. (2014) [98] and 
Kajosaari et al. (1982) [99]. Järvenpää found a ‘nuts’ prevalence of 1.8% (95%CI: 1.3-2.6) among

2, and 2% at age 3. CMA prevalence was 2% at age 1, 5% at age 2, and 2% at age 3. As the results 
were based on parent self-reported questionnaires, the prevalence estimates may be an overestimation 
of the true allergy prevalence.  
Iceland:  
The studies included for Iceland were all based on data from the European project called EuroPrevall. 
EuroPrevall, which included several countries, including Iceland (Reykjavik). Lyons et al. (2020) 
[100] performed a cross-sectional study, assessing FS in 2248 children aged 7-10 years old. Data on

specific OFC results were not reported, thereby excluded here. The prevalence based on symptoms

data was not available, the large sample size supports the validity of the prevalence estimated for 
probable FA.  
Another study by Burney et al. (2014) [101] analysed the prevalence of FS in 2114 adults aged 20-
54 years. Participants completed questionnaires regarding symptoms, and individuals with suspected 
FA along with controls were selected for sIgE samples. Sensitization prevalence was 1.27% for 
hazelnut, 0.07% for walnut, 0.45% for PA, and 1.2% for CMA. The study did not provide any OFC, 
decreasing the reliability of the results. The prevalence of FS might be an overestimation of the true 
allergy prevalence. On the other hand, the large sample size contributed to more representative results.   
Grabenhenrich et al. 2020 [102] investigated a cohort study of 1341 children in Reykjavik aged 6-10 
years. Through parent self-reported physician diagnosis, the prevalence was 1.5% for PA and 4.9%

Conversion factor  
To adjust for the different types of diagnostic methods in the included prevalence studies, a 
conversion factor was applied. Since OFC is considered the gold standard method for diagnosing FA, 
prevalence estimates based on other diagnostic methods were converted into confirmed FA. 
Conversion factors were based on prevalence estimates from a meta-analysis by Spolidoro et al. [2] 
assessing the frequency of FA in Europe. The meta-analysis included studies from 2000 to 2021 and 
examined a total of 110 studies from Europe. The studies used different testing methods, and for self-
reported FAs, the point prevalence was found to be 4.9%. For sIgE FS, the point prevalence was 
16.6%, and for SPT, the point prevalence was 5.7%. For symptoms plus sIgE/SPT, indicating 
probable FA, the prevalence was 2.9%, and for OFC, confirmed FA, the prevalence was 0.8%. 
The conversion factor for each diagnostic method was made by equation 12 and results can be seen 
in Table 5.

4.4.2 Severity  
The severity of FA was categorized into two types; severe form that caused anaphylaxis and non-
severe that covered other symptoms. To ensure confirmed FA, the severity rate was based on studies 
that included OFCs. In total, eight studies were included covering TNA, PA, and CMA.  
Davoren et al. (2005) [104] conducted a retrospective study assessing records of 213 children under 
17 years with nut allergies. Among them, 177 were allergic to peanuts, 27 to cashews, and 9 to a mix 
of tree nuts. Clinical symptoms were divided into anaphylaxis and non-anaphylaxis, with anaphylaxis 
defined as rapidly generalized multisystemic reactions with symptoms related to respiratory and/or 
cardiovascular system. Anaphylaxis was reported in 30.5% of PA patients and 74.1% of cashews 
==PAGE:40==
 
Page 35 of 77 
 
allergic patients, and 66.7% of TNA patients. The large sample size of the PA group increased the 
reliability of the results compared to the smaller cashews- and tree nut group.  
A cross-sectional study by Deschildre et al. (2016) [105] examined 669 children with PA, median 
age 3 years. Participants were recruited based on positive SPT and diagnosed by sIgE or clear reaction 
to peanuts at first allergist visit. 278 patients underwent OFC with 225 being positive on the challenge.  
The category of severe or potentially severe reactions included anaphylactic shock, acute asthma, and 
serious systemic reactions involving two or more organ systems, which occurred in 30% of the 
patients. A strength of the study was the large participation of OFC, however, patients were recruited 
by allergy clinics, thus mild types of PA might be underrepresented potentially overestimating 
severity.  
Another cross-sectional study performed by Nowak-Wegrzyn et al. (2008) [106] examined 100 
children with CMA, mean age of 7.5 years. They assessed the reaction to heated and unheated milk. 
Diagnosis of CMA was based on SPT or sIgE combined with clinical symptoms. All participants 
performed an OFC for heated milk; if negative, an OFC of unheated milk was offered. 23 cases had 
a positive OFC to heated milk, and 41 to unheated milk. Anaphylaxis occurred in 12% of the 
participants. A strength of the study is the use of OFC. On other hand, only participants who tolerated 
heated milk were rechallenged with unheated milk, the study may have excluded those at highest risk 
of anaphylaxis, thereby underestimating the true severity. 
Couch et al. (2017) [107] performed a retrospective study of 109 TNA-patients, with a median age 
of 10.3 years. Participants were recruited based on SPT and sIgE and then tested by OFC. 54 of the 
participants were OFC positive, and anaphylaxis occurred in 28% of them. Notable, 42% of TNA 
patients also were allergic to peanuts, of whom 26% experienced anaphylaxis. It is a strength that 
OFC was used to determine the true allergy. However, the anaphylaxis rate was based on all 109 
patients, including those with negative OFC. This could cause an underestimation of severity. 
Moreover, the anaphylaxis rate of PA might be affected by the patients also being allergic to tree nuts.  
A cross-sectional study by Aquilante et al. (2023) [108] investigated 159 patients with IgE mediated 
CMA, mean age 6 years. 136 patients got positive OFC, and the anaphylaxis rate among them was 
45.6%. The study by performed 266 OFC with whole milk and baked milk, in which 136 OFC were 
positive, and of them, 62 got anaphylaxis. The use of OFC is a strength; however, patients who reacted 
==PAGE:41==
 
Page 36 of 77 
 
to baked milk were not tested further for whole milk which could have excluded anaphylaxis cases. 
Moreover, the study only included IgE mediated CMA which might give an overestimation of 
severity as non-IgE mediated tend to have less severe symptoms. 
A cross-sectional study by Ünsal et al. (2021) [109] examined 613 children with IgE mediated FA 
(median age 2 years) in which 1361 OFC were performed. For tree nuts, 273 OFCs were conducted 
with 44 being positive. From the 44 positive OFC, 21 experiences anaphylaxis (47.7%). For CMA, a 
total of 433 OFCs were performed, with 29 positive OFCs and 10 cases resulted in anaphylaxis 
(34.5%). Strengths of the study include the large sample size and use of the gold standard test, 
increasing the validity and accuracy of the results.   
Gur Certinkaya et al (2021) [110] found anaphylaxis rates for specific nuts including pistachio and 
cashew. The retrospective study involved 112 patients (mean age 4) determined by symptoms and 
sIgE test or SPT. Anaphylaxis rates were studied through 71 OFC of pistachio and 48 OFC of cashew. 
10 participants had positive OFC, in which 3 of them experienced anaphylaxis (30%). For cashew, 
nine subjects had positive OFC with 7 experiencing anaphylaxis (77.8%). While OFC is a strength of 
the study, OFC also contributed to a lack of parental consensus, resulting in a fewer OFCs being 
performed. The low number may affect the accuracy of the results.  
A retrospective study by Santos et al. (2010) [111] investigated 139 children diagnosed with CMA 
through symptoms, SPT, and OFC. The used study population was between 14 months to 18 years, 
and the proportion of IgE mediated and non-IgE mediated was 66 and 72 subjects, respectively. 43 
subjects were classified with lifelong CMA, and the study stated that 3% of the cases developed 
anaphylaxis. A strength of the study includes OFC as the diagnostic method. Furthermore, the study 
included both IgE mediated and non-IgE mediated CMA. This improves the representativeness of 
study population, as other studies might have unintentionally excluded non-IgE mediated CMA. The 
inclusion of non-IgE mediated CMA might have affected the lower rate of anaphylaxis.

4.4.3 Age of onset  
In general, the availability of AoO data was limited across the Nordic countries, thus data of other 
countries within and outside Europe was included. A Danish birth cohort study by Eller et al. (2009) 
[83] investigated the prevalence of FA of 562 children. SPTs and sIgE tests were performed at follow-
ups at 3, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36, and 72 months, and OFC was offered to those with suspected FHS. The 
study found that sensitivity to peanuts was detected at 18 months old. If the study had included more 
frequent follow ups, the sensitization to peanuts might have been more accurate. Besides, it is 
common that peanuts are introduced to the diet later than other foods such as milk, and that it is 
related to PFS which also causes a later onset.   
Sicherer et al. (2001) [113] conducted a cross-sectional study using data from a voluntary registry of 
5149 participants (median age 5 years) to investigate the characteristics of PA and TNA. Data were 
based on questionnaires answered by patients or their parents. Symptoms, first known exposure, and 
first reaction were noted. For peanut, the median age at the first reaction occurred at a median age of 
14 months, whereas it for tree nut 36 months. Among the participants, 68% (peanut) and 74% (tree 
nut) reported that the first reaction occurred at first known exposure. The study population is driven 
by the young population which is beneficial when assessing AoO. However, the self-reported study 
design is a limitation of the study, introducing recall bias and potential overestimation of FA. It might 
be difficult for the participants to recall the exact time of first exposure and reaction.  
Río et al. (2022) [114] performed a prospective cohort study of 260 children, age between 0 and 14 
years (mean age 6.5 years). The children attended an allergy clinic due to suspected IgE mediated PA 
and TNA. The patients were classified allergic if they had history of symptoms and SPT or FS test 
and OFC. The mean age of diagnosis for walnut, hazelnut, cashew, pistachio, almond, and peanut 
was 7, 6.2, 5.4, 6.8, 8.1, and 5.6 years old respectively. Even though the study is prospective, the 
patients enter the allergy clinic with already suspected reactions towards a culprit nut. This might 
cause a later diagnosis than the actual AoO. The first reaction is stated but might be affected by recall 
bias.  
A cross-sectional study by Vereda A. et al. (2011) [115] investigated 115 PA patients diagnosed by 
clinical history and sIgE test or by OFC. The median AoO was found to be 3 years old. It was observed 
that 66% of the participants had pollen allergy, which might have affected the AoO to be higher.  
==PAGE:44==
 
Page 39 of 77 
 
Høst et al. (1990) [116] carried out a prospective birth cohort including unselected 1749 newborns 
that were followed for three years. In total, 39 infants were diagnosed with CMA through elimination 
diet and OFC. A median AoO was estimated to be 4 weeks, ranging from 1-32 weeks. The children 
were rechallenged every 6-12 months, or when suspected symptoms were seen. It was stated that 
none of the infants developed symptoms by the first exposure of cow’s milk, but 19 of the patients 
developed symptoms within the first week. Strengths of the study included the prospective design to 
determine AoO with more accuracy which might reduce recall bias. Moreover, the classification of 
both IgE mediated (16) and non-IgE mediated (23) CMA provided better interpretation of the AoO. 
However, the differences in AoO among the two types were not stated. Furthermore, the low number 
of CMA cases might cause uncertainty of the result. In addition, symptoms in infants can be non-
specific, making it difficult to suspect or determine the exact AoO.  
A retrospective study by Santos et al. (2010) [111] included 139 patients with CMA in which 66 were 
diagnosed with IgE mediated allergy, and 73 with non-IgE mediated allergy.  The study investigated 
predictive factors for lifelong CMA and in relation to that, the mean AoO was estimated to be 3.5 
months with a SD of 2.8 months. AoO was aligned with the age of weaning in most of the participants. 
The retrospective study design increases the risk of recall bias, and the lack of controls over time 
might decrease accuracy of the results. However, since data was based on medical records, the recall 
bias should be limited compared to data based on questionnaires. The study also included a great 
sample size, increasing the reliability of the results, even though the samples were selected from an 
allergy clinic, which might have contributed to an overrepresentation of severe and lifelong cases of 
CMA. Nevertheless, the study managed to classify if the allergy were IgE mediated or non-IgE 
mediated, and the distribution was relatively equal.

4.4.4 Age of resolve and resolution rate 
As seen in Table 8, studies regarding AoR and resolution rate were limited in the Nordic countries, 
thereby country-specific data was not used. Especially, data of TNA and PA was limited, whereas 
multiple studies were available for CMA.  
The only study regarding the resolution of TNA was a prospective cohort study by Fleischer et al. 
(2005) [117], assessing 278 participants (median age 6.9 years) diagnosed with TNA. To avoid 
potential overestimation of resolution, only TNA patients with history of clinical reactions were 
included in the estimation. Among 110 patients, 9 passed OFC, which corresponded to a resolution 
rate of 8.9% (95%CI: 4-16%) with a median age of 10 years. Since the study included sample size 
from an allergy clinic, the resolution rate might not have been representative due to potential bias of 
more severe cases of TNA.  
Peters et al. (2015 and 2019) [118], [119] and Parker et al. (2024) [120] were part of the ‘Australian 
HealthNuts study’ and performed a prospective cohort study of 156 1-year old children with OFC 
confirmed PA. Participants were followed at age 4, 6, and 10, and tested by sIgE, SPT, and OFC. 
Resolution rates of PA were observed to be 22% (95%CI: 14-34%) at age 4, 29% (95%CI: 22-38%) 
by age 6, and 33.9% (95%CI: 25.3-43.3%) by the age of 10. Even though OFC was consistently used 
throughout the study, the study population included very young participants which might not be 
representative regarding disease course, considering that PA might develop later.  
Hasan Arshad et al. (2014) [121] performed a birth cohort of 1456 children. Follow-up questionnaires 
were completed at age 1, 2, 4, 10, and 18 years old. Children that presented symptoms at age 1 and 2 
were offered a SPT, whereas for ages 4, 10, and 18, all participants were tested for PA. PA was 
diagnosed based on a positive SPT and clinical history of a reaction within 2 hours after ingesting the 
peanut. The resolution rate of PA was 17% at the age of 10 years old and 33% at the age of 18 years 
old. The study obtained a low PA prevalence of 0.58% (6 cases) which might have affected the 
assessment of resolution rates.  
Skolnick H. et al. (2001) [122] conducted a cohort study of 233 PA patients aged 4 to 20 years old. 
The patients were diagnosed with a positive SPT and questionnaire of history of symptoms when 
peanuts were ingested. 85 performed an OFC, in which 48 passed the test indicating a resolution rate 
for PA of 21.5%. OFC was used as the most reliable testing method, however only 86 patients 
==PAGE:47==
 
Page 42 of 77 
 
completed the test, and those who did not attend it were more likely to have higher sIgE levels, which 
may have caused an underestimation of the resolution rate.  
A Danish prospective birth cohort by Høst et al. (2002) [123] followed 1749 infants and identified 39 
cases of CMA at age 1, confirmed by OFC. Of these, 21 cases had IgE mediated allergy and 18 had 
non-IgE mediated allergy. Hansen et al. (2020) [124] continued the study and followed the 
participants until the age of 26 years. Resolution rates for both CMA types were 56% at age 1, 77% 
at age 2, 87% at age 3, 92% at age 5, 92% at age 10, 97% at age 15 and age 26. Patients with IgE-
mediated CMA had lower resolution rates of 76% at age 3, 86% at age 5, and 95% at age 10 and 15. 
The study population was representative for the Danish population, and the long and prospective 
study design provided valuable knowledge of resolution rates in many different age groups. A 
limitation includes potential loss to follow-up which can be expected in long-term studies like this.   
A retrospective cohort study by Skripak J. et al. (2007) [125] assessed 807 patients with IgE mediated 
CMA. Resolution rates were 19% at age 4 years, 42% at age 8, 42%, 64% at age 12, and 79% at age 
16. Resolution was defined as passing an OFC, or no symptoms for a year together with sIgE 
concentration below <3 kU/L. As only IgE mediated CMA was included, the resolution rate might 
be underestimated compared to studies also including non-IgE mediated CMA. Moreover, patients 
with levels of sIgE above 3kU/L could potentially tolerate milk and pass the OFC, thereby an 
underestimation of resolution might happen.  
Wood R. et al. (2013) [126] performed a prospective cohort study in 2013 including 293 young 
children from age 3 to 15 months. Based on clinical history and SPT, 244 subjects were diagnosed 
with CMA. 52.6% of the children had outgrown their allergy at a median age of (5.25 years). The last 
follow-up was at a median age of 66 months.  
A similar prospective cohort study conducted by Kaczmarski et al. (2013) [127] examined 291 CMA 
patients aged 2-14 years. Diagnosis was based on SPT, sIgE and OFC, and OFC was conducted at 
follow-ups. The study found a resolution rate of 72.9% at a mean age of 5 years. Limitations included 
loss of follow-up.  
Kubota K. et al. (2023) [128] performed a retrospective cohort study in Japan of 80 patients with 
CMA. Diagnosis of IgE mediated CMA was based on either sIgE samples with clinical history or 
positive OFC. The patients were registered in an allergy clinic before the age of 6, and resolution 
==PAGE:48==
 
Page 43 of 77 
 
rates were estimated to be 31% at age 9, and 58% at age 12. Higher levels of sIgE were linked to 
higher risk of persistent CMA. Generally, resolution rates differed between the types of CMA, as 
non-IgE mediated CMA commonly resolved earlier. Early resolution before the age of 6 might have 
been excluded, which potentially caused an underestimation of the resolution rate.  
A prospective cohort study by Schoemaker A. et al. (2015) [129] assessed 12049 infants, in which 
358 were suspected CMA cases, with 55 cases being confirmed by OFC. After one year, 69% of the 
confirmed CMA cases had resolved. 57% of the IgE mediated CMA had resolved whereas all cases 
of non-IgE mediated CMA had resolved, highlighting the later resolution of IgE mediated CMA. 
Even though a strength of the study includes the large sample size and the use of OFC, a major 
limitation was that only 58% of the confirmed CMA was re-challenged after one year. This could 
cause a potential underestimation of the resolution rate. Moreover, it was stated that the incidence 
rate of CMA was lower in this study compared to others, which together with high unwillingness 
(30%) to participate in OFC, might affect the results of resolution.  
A prospective study by Saarinen et al. (2005) [130] included 6209 infants and 118 of them were 
diagnosed with CMA, in which 64% were IgE mediated. Resolution was confirmed by OFC and rates 
were identified as 44% at mean age 1.6, 51 % at age 2, 81% at age 5, and 89% at mean age 8.6. 
Moreover, a higher risk of persistent CMA was found for the IgE mediated form, since 100% of cases 
of non-IgE mediated CMA had resolved by age 5, compared to 73% of the IgE mediated. The large, 
unselected study population and use of OFC contributes to great strengths of this study. In addition, 
the inclusion of both IgE and non-IgE mediated CMA provides valuable information about the disease 
course of CMA.

4.4.5 Mortality  
Due to limited mortality data within the Nordic countries, expert opinions from Nordic national 
asthma and allergy federations were obtained, as well as mortality data outside the Nordic countries. 
In general, fatal cases of food-induced anaphylaxis were found to be very rare. Fatal cases reported 
in each study were converted into mortality rates per 100,000 as shown in Table 9, with calculations 
provided in Appendix 6. Moreover, AoD was also assessed through the included studies.  
 
According to the Danish National Board of Health (“Sundhedsstyrelsen”), 1-2 individuals die 
annually from anaphylaxis caused by insect stings in Denmark, however, the number of fatal 
outcomes due to food-induced anaphylaxis was not available [131]. 
A study by Foucard et al. (1999) [132] identified 1 fatal case due to TNA and 2 fatal cases due to PA 
between 1993 and 1996 in Sweden. Additionally, Malmheden et al. (2005) [133] investigated a later 
period between 1997-2003, and reported similar 1 fatal case of TNA, 2 fatal cases of PA, but also 1 
fatal case of CMA. Moreover, a report from Swedish Association for Allergology (SFFA) in 2015 
found a decrease in number of deaths caused by FAs among individuals below 23 years. The average 
number of deaths reported was 1.75 per year during 1993-1997, compared to 0.25 per year during 
2004-2011 [134].  
The asthma and allergy federation of Finland stated that the mortality rate from FAs has been zero 
for many years. A Finish article by Mäkelä Mika et al. (2021) stated that 2-3 individuals annually die 
from anaphylaxis, however, those were caused by drugs or wasp stings. However, the article stated a 
rapid increase in food-induced anaphylaxis between 2012 and 2017, particularly peanuts and cashew 
nuts [135].  
 
Outside Nordic countries:  
When investigating outside the Nordic countries, multiple studies outside Europe were identified.  
Macdougall et al. (2002) [136] reported mortality estimates in the United Kingdom between 1990-
2000, identifying two fatal cases of PA anaphylaxis in children and four fatal cases of CMA. Another 
study in United Kingdom by Pumphrey R. et al. identified 9 fatal anaphylaxis cases each caused by 
tree nuts and peanuts, as well as 6 fatal cases caused by cow’s milk, between 1999-2006 [137].  
Kang Liew et al. (2009) noted 3 fatal cases of peanut induced anaphylaxis in Australia between 1997-
2005, with a mean age of 12.16 years [138]. Moreover, a study by Xu Y. M. et al. observed fatal cases 
==PAGE:52==
 
Page 47 of 77 
 
caused by anaphylaxis in Ontario between 1986-2011, reporting 6 deaths caused by TNA, 16 due to 
PA, and 1 due to CMA. The median age for the fatal cases of food allergies was 32 years [139].  
 
Moreover, several studies from the United States were included. Bohlke K. et al. (2004) reported no 
deaths caused by food anaphylaxis between 1991-1997 among 229,422 individuals below 18 years 
[140]. Another study from Florida reported 4 deaths due to peanut-induced anaphylaxis between

4.5 Disability weight 
DWs were adapted from GBD studies [144]; however, these studies have not estimated any DW for 
FAs or for anaphylaxis. The variation of symptoms and severity of FAs complicates the process of 
developing DWs. Therefore, for this project, DWs were developed into a non-severe and severe 
category. These DWs were not food-specific, but were based on PA, TNA, and CMA, as a group. 
The DWs were derived by assessing the severity of symptoms and comparing them using diseases 
with similar symptoms or disease courses. The DWs for FAs should reflect both symptomatic days 
and non-symptomatic days. Moreover, it was assumed that FA patients actively attempt to avoid 
exposure to the specific allergen. 
As seen in Figure 6, multiple DWs and their uncertainty intervals (UI) from GBD study year 2019 
were considered for the two DW categories of FA, and the final DWs were found to be 0.031 (95%UI: 
0.023-0.039) and 0.111 (95%UI: 0.086-0.137) for non-severe and severe FA, respectively. The 
estimated DWs were calculated by the mean of the lowest and highest comparable DW for each 
category. This implies that the DWs of comparable diseases ranging between the highest and lowest 
values were not directly included in the estimated DWs. Instead, they were used to illustrate the 
varying symptoms of FA and explain the reasoning behind final estimated DWs.  
FAs impact QoL in several ways. The psychological burden is often significant for FA patients, 
commonly due to daily anxiety of accidental exposure. FA patients might experience less anxiety in 
controlled environment, but their anxiety may increase in social situations, where they have less 
control. It was assumed that both groups corresponded to the DW of mild anxiety disorder. Mild 
anxiety includes feeling mildly anxious and worried, affecting concentration, but still allowing the 
individual to carry out daily routines [144].  
Moreover, physical symptoms also have an important impact on DW. Non-severe cases of FA might 
include mild asthma, skin reactions and GI complications. When looking at DWs from GBD study 
year 2019, a relevant GI disease to non-severe FA was chosen to be: “mild to moderate 
gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), symptomatic days”. The disease is described as causing 
stomach pain and nausea, but not often affecting daily activities [144]. Common symptoms of GERD 
disease include regurgitation, dysphagia and irritability [145], which are similar symptoms to non-
IgE CMA. Moreover, “controlled asthma” was identified as an appropriate non-severe DW for 
==PAGE:55==
 
Page 50 of 77 
 
respiratory symptoms, whereas “mild atopic dermatitis” was identified as a suitable DW for mild skin 
symptoms [144]. 
Severe cases of FA commonly result in more serious and acute systemic reactions. The different 
organs that commonly are involved in such acute systemic reactions were accounted for by choosing 
“uncontrolled asthma”, representing acute severe respiratory issues, and “moderate atopic dermatitis” 
reflecting a more generalized skin rash [144].  
To further compare DWs, a study by J. Flokstra-de Blok et al. [146] found that patients with food 
allergies stated lower health-related QoL than the general population. More specifically, patients with 
FA were reported with lower QoL than patients with diabetes mellites type 1, but greater QoL than 
patients with rheumatoid arthritis. To account for this observation, uncomplicated diabetes mellites 
type 1 was also used as DW in this project, as it might be a suitable disease for comparison because 
it is a chronic condition, that with medication does not affect much of daily life. Moreover, the disease 
may still cause some worry, especially according to the food intake, which also applies to FA patients. 
Uncomplicated diabetes mellitus type 1 was found to fall between the two estimated DWs for FA.

4.6 Uncertainty propagation 
To propagate the uncertainty associated with the included parameters, the model was developed in 
the software program R (version 4.4.1), and the DALY model conducted Monte Carlo simulations by 
using the package “mc2d”, with 104 iterations (Appendix 5). Overall, 95% UIs were calculated to 
demonstrate the interval of uncertainty in which the true value was located. In general, a PERT 
distribution was chosen as data indicated a minimum, most likely, and maximum, in which this 
project assumed the mean value to be the most likely. Most of the parameters including prevalence, 
severity, resolution rate, mortality rate, and DW fitted into a PERT distribution. 
The meta-analysis provided a mean and 95%CI, which were used as UI in a PERT distribution in the 
incidence model. If the prevalence studies provided several age groups with specific numbers of 
participants, the study was treated as separate studies for each age group. Moreover, the resolution 
rate was calculated by including the highest rate found within each study, in which the meta-analysis 
provided a mean and 95%UI used in a PERT distribution. 
For the AoO and AoR, the data was also weighed by number of participants and only provided a 
mean as a point estimate (Appendix 5). Mortality rates for each FA were estimated based on the 
included studies of fatal cases. Due to very low mortality rates and expert opinions stating that none 
or very few fatal cases annually occur, a PERT distribution was made with 0 as the minimum rate. 
The mean value was calculated by including mortality studies, and the maximum rate was chosen 
based on the study with the highest rate for each FA (Appendix 6). A mean AoD for each FA was 
calculated by including mortality studies with available data of age.

6. Discussion 
6.1 Estimation of DALYs 
This project aimed to estimate the BoD of the most relevant FAs in the Nordic countries by calculating 
DALYs. Moreover, these estimates should enable comparison with other relevant diseases. Overall, 
the DALY results for each FA were mainly affected by the country-specific prevalence. This 
contributed to the observation that countries with a high prevalence of a specific FA also showed 
higher DALY per 100,000. In addition, the DALY per case for each FA showed to be very similar 
across countries, as only prevalence and life expectancy were country specific parameters. Overall, 
the proportion of YLLs was significantly low as they accounted for approximately 1-2% of DALY 
per 100,000. The low proportion was due to very few fatal cases, which in general might reflect a 
high awareness of FA as well as easy and functional emergency treatment.  
==PAGE:66==
 
Page 61 of 77 
 
The highest estimated DALY per 100,000 was found for TNA in Denmark (101.49 DALY, 95%UI: 
14.82 – 251.93) which was very high compared to the other countries. However, the wide UI in 
Denmark suggests a high degree of uncertainty which may explain the larger estimated burden. Only 
two studies were included for TNA in Denmark, and they had very different results, causing a wide 
UI for the incidence, leading to a high value of DALY. Moreover, the two studies only included adults 
which was not representative for the entire population, which might have overestimated the true 
prevalence. The study also noted a potential overrepresentation of participants with FHS, potentially 
overestimating the true prevalence of TNA. However, the use of OFC and sample size increased the 
validity of the study, which is also why the study was included in this project.  
In general, the WTNA scenario might have been overestimated due to the assumption of no cross-
reactivity among the nuts. As the WTNA accounted for the sum of different nut types, some prevalent 
cases might have been counted more than once, which should not be the case. The cross-allergies 
commonly involve the following pairs: walnut and pecan, almond and hazelnut, and cashew and 
pistachios [71].  In reality, the incidence might be in between the two scenarios found by including a 
rate of cross-allergies. Compared to other FAs, TNA prevalence might be more affected by 
uncertainty due to the inclusion of several types of nuts, which contributes to heterogeneity in both 
study design and results.   
The highest BoD for PA was seen in Finland. However, the DALY per 100,000 was reflected by a 
wide 95%UI (42.59 – 118.47), indicating a high degree of uncertainty. As only one prevalence study 
of PA was included from Finland, this study had a high influence on the results. The study design 
included children and their mothers, increasing the risk of potential genetic predisposition, which 
might have overestimated the true prevalence. The participants might have been less representative 
of the general population. 
For CMA, the high BoD in Norway might have been overestimated as two of three included studies 
investigated prevalence in children around the age of 2. Again, the study design of the target 
population might not have been representative compared to the prevalence in the general population. 
In general, the lowest BoD for all FAs were seen in Iceland which was due to the low number of 
incident cases, with DALY per case being more similar to the other countries.  
==PAGE:67==
 
Page 62 of 77 
 
Overall, CMA showed the lowest total burden for the Nordic countries, even though the incidence 
was relatively high compared to other FAs. The disease course of CMA showed to have high rates of 
resolution, which combined with early AoR, resulted in a shorter disease duration. Moreover, the 
severity of CMA was lower, although the UIs overlapped with the other FAs. The greater proportion 
of non-severe cases caused the non-severe DW to have a larger influence, which lowered the burden. 
In contrast, TNA showed the highest severity rate, which together with the higher proportion of severe 
DW resulted in a higher burden. When comparing DALYs per case across FAs, the impact of both 
disease duration and severity, indicated by DW, reflects the differences in BoD among the FAs.  
6.2 Comparison with other diseases 
To better understand the BoD linked to FAs, it is relevant to compare the estimated DALYs with 
other well-known chronic diseases. This can indicate whether the burden of FA is large enough to 
deserve more attention in public health.  
The GDB study from 2021 [147] provided DALY results of diseases including disease DWs used for 
development of FA DWs in this project. It was observed, that the total DALY of the Nordic countries 
attributed to each of the three FAs including CMA (8031.02 DALY), PA (9332.07 DALY), TNA 
(12776.56 DALY), were generally lower than typically chronic diseases such as irritable bowel 
disease (14644.62 DALYs), diabetes mellitus type 1 (25461.30 DALY), rheumatoid arthritis 
(27614.34 DALYs), atopic dermatitis (33849.33 DALY), and asthma (75346.10 DALY). In contrast, 
WTNA scenario (19686.75 DALY) for all the Nordic countries ranked between irritable bowel 
disease and diabetes mellitus type 1. However, when all three FAs were summed together (30139.68 
DALY), the burden reached above chronic diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis and atopic dermatitis. 
This also applies when including the WTNA scenario, as the overall estimation of the three FAs then 
reached 37049.87 DALY. In contrast, asthma caused a substantially higher disease burden than FA. 
Moreover, this project focused on the three most relevant FAs in the Nordic countries. Instead, if the 
BoD was assessed as a whole (for all 14 regulatory FAs in the EU), the overall burden would be 
higher.   
Studies within the Nordic countries investigating the burden of FAs in terms of DALYs are limited. 
A study by Jakobsen et al. (2020) [6] investigated the BoD of PA in Denmark. They found a total 
burden of 906.2 DALY (95% UI: 512.8-1429.6) in 2016, which was much lower than the DALYs 
estimated in this project for PA in Denmark (1729.75 DALYs; 95%UI: 1131.94 – 2468.22). However, 
==PAGE:68==
 
Page 63 of 77 
 
they also provided a scenario with a higher DW, between uncomplicated diabetes mellitus type 1 and 
rheumatoid arthritis, which contributed to total DALYs of 5115. In general, the study differed from 
our study with the assumption of the target population of the incidence, as they assumed the incidence 
to occur in children below 6 years. However, to our knowledge, the number used for the target 
population was highly underestimated, as approximately 60,000 was used as a reference number for 
that specific population. This number might not be representative for that target population of 0-6 
years old and thereby underestimating the incidence number.   
 
6.3 Assumptions, strengths and limitations 
6.3.1 Prevalence and diagnostic method  
The use of questionnaires and self-reported data introduce bias and less credibility regarding 
prevalence and history of symptoms. Moreover, self-reported prevalence and isolated FS tests tend 
to be overestimated compared to studies based on clinical symptoms and testing. Especially for CMA, 
self-reporting data might be more prone to overestimation, mainly because of the confusion with 
lactose intolerance. Therefore, the use of conversion factors for prevalence was a crucial 
establishment and strengthened this project, as it provided more comparable data. However, it could 
be further improved by using specific conversion factors for each FA as sensitivity and specificity 
might vary. A meta-analysis by Riggioni et al. [148] compared sensitivity and specificity of SPT and 
sIgE measurements against OFC and found that test performances varied among food allergens. For 
example, SPT sensitivity was 0.52 for cow’s milk, indicating a low ability to detect positive cases, 
whereas sensitivity for peanut was higher at 0.84. Tree nuts were assessed individually, and for 
hazelnut, SPT had a sensitivity of 0.82 and a specificity of 0.92, indicating high accuracy. In contrast, 
SPT specificity was 0.80 for cow’s milk and 0.86 for peanut. The low sensitivity for cow’s milk 
indicates that many individuals with CMA may receive false-negative results, leading to an 
underestimated CMA prevalence. 
Even though the OFC is considered the gold standard test, and therefore the most reliable test, it also 
introduces some uncertainties. While the method is efficient at reducing the risk of false positives, it 
might underestimate the prevalence due to small sample sizes and low participation rates. Since OFC 
is a demanding, risky, and often an uncomfortable test, individuals with suspected FA may refuse to 
conduct the test. Moreover, those with severe allergy might be more likely to refuse, thus the severity 
==PAGE:69==
 
Page 64 of 77 
 
of FA can be underestimated. Besides, the limited sample size might be related to high costs and the 
time-consuming test. 
Another strength of this project included the meta-analysis that weighted the prevalence by number 
of participants. A large study with a great sample size often provides more valid results, especially if 
the study population is representative of the target population. Furthermore, the use of distributions 
to propagate uncertainty of the different parameters improved the quality and reliability of the results. 
In general, PERT distributions were used for most of the parameters. A triangle distribution was not 
used since the aim was to provide higher weight to the mean value compared to the minimum and 
maximum values, resulting in a more realistic distribution.  
A notable limitation included the use of two assumptions that contradicted each other. As the 
prevalence represented the entire population, it was implicitly assumed that incident cases might 
occur at any age in life. However, the use of a single mean AoO and AoR contributed to another 
assumption stating one lifelong and resolving duration. Therefore, the project did not account for 
incident cases occurring before or after the mean AoO, which is inconsistent with the assumption that 
incidence may occur throughout the entire life. This simplification might have overestimated the 
duration in some cases and thereby overestimated YLD.  
6.3.2 Disability weight  
Since there is no specific DW for each FA, several assumptions were made when estimating the DWs 
for severe and non-severe FA. The DWs were not food specific as it was assumed that the different 
FAs were overall similar in severity and symptoms, which might not be the case. In addition, it was 
assumed that FA patients intentionally try to avoid the culprit food, thereby the rate of accidental 
exposure should be low. The duration in YLD was chosen to be lifelong, in order to fit the everyday 
life of a FA patient. However, if the DWs only represented the symptomatic days, the DWs, especially 
for severe FA, should have been higher. In that case, the duration, used in YLD, should also match 
the duration of the actual symptomatic reaction, thereby being much lower. However, duration is 
difficult to estimate as the duration depends on the behavior of the individual but also on the 
surrounding environment. The decision to apply a relatively low DW is supported by the availability 
of effective medicine against anaphylaxis, which significantly lowers the risk of a fatal outcome.  
In general, it was assumed that the two DW categories had the same DW for anxiety related to 
accidental exposure. However, this may not be the case, as patients with severe FA might experience 
==PAGE:70==
 
Page 65 of 77 
 
higher levels of anxiety due to an increased risk of life-threatening reactions. The reason no 
distinction was made between the two categories of FA was the gap between the DW for mild (0.03) 
and moderate (0.133) anxiety, which was considered to be too broad, and thereby not appropriate.  
Overall, the conduction of DWs of FAs are based on several uncertain parameters, as they are derived 
by comparing symptoms and severity of different diseases, as perception and interpretation of the 
individual diseases might vary. The DWs have a substantial impact on the final DALY estimation, 
making the construction of DW a key component. Further research and better construction of DWs 
would be beneficial to gain more evidence based DWs of FAs.  
 
6.3.3 Severity 
FA was divided into two categories of severity: severe and non-severe. While it is a strength to 
distinction between non-severe and severe reactions, it may still oversimplify the classification of 
severity compared to the complexity of the symptoms. Moreover, especially the non-severe category, 
covering both mild and moderate symptoms, might have contributed to a less precise DW for this 
category. 
Furthermore, the anaphylaxis rate for each FA was estimated by cases of anaphylaxis divided by 
positive OFCs. Limiting the calculation to positive OFC results ensured that individuals with true FA 
were included. In general, the use of OFC increased the accuracy and reliability of the anaphylaxis 
results. However, the terms of qualification to an OFC were not always based on the same approach, 
which contributed to different inclusion or exclusion criteria for the sample population, contributing 
to varying study populations, increasing uncertainty of the results. 
Regarding CMA, not all studies distinguished between IgE and non-IgE mediated FA. Most CMA 
studies used diagnostic tests such as SPT and sIgE, potentially excluding patients with non-IgE 
mediated allergy from OFC. Exclusion of non-IgE patients would underestimate prevalence but 
overestimate severity of CMA. Additionally, a potential exclusion would also affect AoO and AoR, 
as patients with the non-IgE mediated type commonly develop and resolve the allergy earlier 
compared to the IgE-mediated type.  
 
==PAGE:71==
 
Page 66 of 77 
 
6.3.4 Age of onset, age of resolve and resolution rate 
According to AoO and AoR, data from the Nordic countries were limited, thus studies from other 
countries were included. However, the AoO might be affected by risk factors such as lifestyle and 
environment, in which the Nordic countries might differentiate from other countries. Moreover, AoO 
was based on data with either age of first reaction or age of diagnosis, causing uncertainty, as the age 
of diagnosis commonly happens later than the age of first symptoms appearing. Additionally, the 
diagnostic method was not accounted for, and less reliable methods than OFC might produce less 
accurate results. If OFC was not used as a diagnostic method, it might also have contributed to an 
overestimation of the resolution rate, as the individual could be registered as resolved without ever 
actually having the allergy. 
AoO and AoR were investigated at specific ages in the included studies. This was a conservative 
approach in the calculation, as it was assumed that the patient had developed or resolved at the specific 
age and not in between two ages. This conservative approach might have overestimated the AoO and 
AoR, as participants could be registered as developed FA or resolved FA later that reality. This 
uncertainty could have been decreased if the patients were tested more frequently. Moreover, it was 
assumed that if a FA had not occurred by the last investigated age, the person would be defined as 
non-allergic. This might underestimate the AoO as some patients could develop FA later than 
investigated. Similarly, if a patient was not resolved by the last age investigated, it was assumed, they 
had a life-long FA. It could have been beneficial to follow the population for an extended period, as 
this could indicate whether food allergies can develop or resolve later in life.  
 
6.3.5 Mortality 
In general, mortality data for the Nordic countries was limited, and thereby, data outside the Nordic 
countries were included. This might contribute to some uncertainty due to variation in lifestyle and 
health care systems. However, by including more studies, the mortality rates and AoD values were 
considered to be more evidence-based.  
Moreover, it should be noted that fatalities caused by FA might be underreported. Anaphylaxis is the 
main cause of death related to FA, but it is often not registered for each category such as food, drugs, 
and insects. Additionally, the specific food item is rarely specified. Severe asthma attacks might also 
present symptoms similar to anaphylaxis which could lead to the wrong reported cause of death, 
contributing to an underestimation of the fatal anaphylaxis cases.  
==PAGE:72==
 
Page 67 of 77 
 
6.3.6 Choice of criteria 
When choosing the most relevant food allergies for further investigation in this project, the 
combination of prevalence, anaphylaxis registry and severity were thought to provide a solid basis 
and indication of the most relevant FAs to further investigate. However, other parameters such as 
eliciting dose and potency of the food could also have been relevant to investigate. 
Even though the European meta-analysis provided a good overview of prevalence, selection bias in 
the choice of included studies, may have affected the prevalence estimates due to variation in 
diagnostic methods and age of participants, potentially leading to an under- or overestimation. 
Moreover, the prevalence used for the criteria were not converted, as in the DALY calculation, which 
could overestimate the prevalence of studies not using OFC, influencing how they were ranked in the 
choice of criteria.  
 
6.4 DALYs as a management tool  
Estimates of DALYs should be used to evaluate effectiveness of both prospective and retrospective 
management approaches, as they provide a comprehensive measure of the disease burden, 
incorporating both mortality, and morbidity through DWs. DALYs are a useful tool for comparison 
across countries, enabling evaluation of healthcare systems and their effectiveness for management 
strategies. While DALYs can be used to improve existing management approaches, it can also support 
integration of preventive approaches and support resource allocation. Overall, management strategies 
should create a safe environment for individuals living with FAs but also to protect and support 
society. This includes strategies for avoiding the culprit allergens, which are partly obtained by 
regulations and laws of labelling food allergens.  
In this project, the burden of FA showed to be primarily driven by YLD, suggesting that mortality is 
not a major concern, which could be due to the availability of effective medication such as 
epinephrine auto-injectors but also the improved awareness and education in management of FA 
reactions. The high proportion of YLD reflected the reduced QoL from living with FA, particularly 
caused by long term management of daily stress and anxiety of accidental exposure. It is essential to 
develop emergency action plans for each individual with FA, as it might lower the stress associated 
with accidental exposure but also decrease the risk of poorly managed anaphylaxis cases. Increasing 
awareness among the general population, educational institutions, and food industry is also crucial. 
==PAGE:73==
 
Page 68 of 77 
 
Moreover, mental health support might help the individual to decrease anxiety, stress, and social 
isolation, which overall could lower the DW, and thereby reduce the overall burden.  
Currently, there is no cure for FA, but immunotherapy has been indicated as a promising tool to 
improve management of FA, as it might increase the tolerating dose. For pollen allergy, 
immunotherapy is already a well-integrated approach [63]. However, further research of 
immunotherapy for FA is necessary, as safety and efficacy are still under evaluation. The Food and 
Drug Administration (FDA) and European Medicines Agency (EMA) have approved one OIT 
(Palforzia) for PA patients aged between 1 to 17 years, but it is not yet considered a standard treatment 
in Denmark or other Nordic countries [149], [150]. Apart from Palforzia, no other immunotherapy 
has been approved for FAs. If immunotherapy for FAs became a standard part of treatment options, 
it might significantly improve management of FAs. By increasing the proportion of FA patients 
undergoing immunotherapy, the QoL should be increased, and thereby reducing DW, and YLD. 
For long-term management, it could also be relevant to improve monitoring systems by incorporating 
national registers of prevalence and anaphylaxis cases, which could provide more evidence-based 
data and improve data validity. Furthermore, such registers could help identify age groups which are 
at higher risks.  
Even though the mortality rates for FAs are not concerning, the risk of getting anaphylaxis remains. 
According to the acute management, epinephrine auto-injectors inclusion within defibrillators at 
public locations could decrease the fatal risk of anaphylaxis. In Denmark, a volunteer-based app 
called “Hjerteløber” (heart runners) was developed to increase the chance of survival from a cardiac 
arrest [151]. When someone calls the medical emergency service with a suspected cardiac arrest, 
nearby registered individuals are alerted to quickly find and bring a defibrillator to the place of 
incidence. This setup could also be applied for cases of anaphylaxis, which applies beyond just food-
induced anaphylaxis. However, there might be some regulatory challenges, as epinephrine auto-
injectors require prescription, and thereby not permitted to be available at public locations.  
 
6.5 Future studies  
Many aspects of FA remain uncertain, highlighting the need for improved study designs and higher 
data quality. In particular, long-term prospective cohort studies are essential to assess, including the 
natural history of FA, but also its impact on QoL. Consistent and more accurate diagnostic methods 
==PAGE:74==
 
Page 69 of 77 
 
should reduce misclassification of FAs. To improve accuracy and validity of data, it should ideally 
be based on the most reliable diagnostic method which remains the gold standard test (DBPCFC). 
However, OFC are resource intensive, and the challenge might be to ensure high participation rates 
due to refusal. Therefore, persistent focus on safety and education are important key points.  
For future modelling of BoD, it could be interesting to include other parameters than those used in 
this project. This could involve eliciting dose, which could enhance the understanding of the severity 
and develop more refined severity classifications.  
In general, more research regarding the mechanism of FA is necessary. This includes understanding 
why FAs develop, which could be valuable for reducing the incidence of FA in the future. Studies on 
allergen proteins could also improve understanding of specific allergenic proteins and enhance the 
distinction of primary allergy and cross-reactivity. Moreover, handling of comorbidities is essential 
for decreasing the risk of FA reactions, as those diseases can act as comorbidities, risk factors, and 
cofactors. Their interaction might contribute to the BoD and should be assessed in the future. 
Additionally, it is important to monitor new foods on the market, as changes in dietary habits might 
introduce new FAs.  
The economic burden of FA was not estimated in this project, however, it could be interesting to 
investigate both indirect- and direct costs. Together with the BoD, this would provide a more 
comprehensive understanding of FA both on individuals and society.  
 
7. Conclusion 
The aim of this study was to estimate the BoD of the most relevant FAs in the Nordic countries. The 
most relevant FAs were found to be TNA, PA, and CMA, for which a mathematical model was 
applied to calculate DALYs. An incidence model was used to identify essential parameters including 
prevalence, severity, AoO, AoR, resolution rate, mortality rate and AoD. Moreover, DWs for severe 
and non-severe FA were developed to be included in the calculation of DALY. Two scenarios for 
prevalence of TNA were made, in which a worst-case scenario summed all tree nut types.  
In general, the DALY estimates showed that YLD accounted for the majority of the total DALYs, 
indicating that the burden is primarily driven by reduced QoL rather than mortality. Overall, TNA 
(12776.55 DALY; 95%UI: 6282.81 – 22661.72) accounted for the highest burden in the Nordic 
countries, with an even higher burden observed for WTNA (19686.75 DALY; 95%UI: 11310.13 – 
==PAGE:75==
 
Page 70 of 77 
 
30733.55), compared to PA (9332.07 DALY; 95%UI: 6513.84 – 12747.77) and CMA (8031.01 
DALY; 95%UI: 5291.62 – 11548.43). Denmark, in particular, presented a high burden of TNA, while 
Finland showed the highest burden of PA. In contrast, the highest burden of CMA was observed in 
Norway, whereas Iceland consistently showed the lowest burden across all three FAs. DALY per case 
showed the lowest burden for CMA, followed by PA, while TNA presented the highest DALY per 
case, suggesting a greater loss of healthy life years per case. In contrast, DALY per case was similar 
for each FA across the Nordic countries.  
Individually, each specific FA showed a lower burden compared to other chronic diseases such as 
diabetes mellitus type 1, rheumatoid arthritis and atopic dermatitis. However, the total burden from 
the FAs in the Nordic countries was estimated to 30139.68 DALY (95%CI:20851.86 – 42611.12) and 
reached 37049.87 DALY (95%CI: 25849.58 – 51036.24) when the scenario for WTNA was included. 
These burdens are higher than the chronic diseases mentioned, but lower than the burden of asthma. 
Overall, the burden reflects that FA deserves attention regarding political prioritization and public 
health management. DALY is a useful tool to compare diseases and support management strategies 
within and across countries. Currently, there is no cure for FA, and daily management relies on the 
avoidance of the culprit allergen. Reducing stress and anxiety in individuals might be crucial to reduce 
the DW, together with increased awareness of FAs. Immunotherapy has shown promising potential, 
and more research in this area is warranted, as it could potentially contribute to lowering the BoD.
