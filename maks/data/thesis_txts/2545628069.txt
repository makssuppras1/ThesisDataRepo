Abstract
In this thesis, the aim is to analyse the effectiveness of constitutive models for clay ap-
plied to a very high plasticity, highly overconsolidated clay and assess the accuracy in the
prediction of its behaviour in unloading. This type of clay has been found in the tunnel
alignment for the Fehmarn Belt project which emphasises the interest in adequately pre-
dict beforehand the behaviour of the soil for this project due to its swelling properties. The
constitutive model considered is the Modified Cam Clay, widely used in the construction
industry for geotechnical studies. The simplicity of this model has also its disadvantages,
not capturing soil features sometimes present in clays such as de-structuration, anisotropy
or creep.
For performing the analysis, two finite element software are considered, ABAQUS CAE
(or ABAQUS from now on) and PLAXIS 2D - Soil Test module (PLAXIS 2D or PLAXIS
from now on), both able to implement the model mentioned but with different approaches.
Appendix A presents two benchmark tests conducted in both ABAQUS and PLAXIS (be-
sides a user made FE spreadsheet) to prove their accuracy. Data from laboratory CRS
tests conducted on Røsnæs Clay from the Fehmarn alignment is provided by the Tech-
nical University of Denmark. Such data is interpreted under two unloading conditions.
First, a constant K0 condition during unloading, and second a non-constant K0 condition
based of the increase of overconsolidation ratio, OCR during unloading. Consequently,
the main modelling parameters are derived from unloading curves in the e−ln p′ represen-
tation for the simulations input. The results from both group of simulations are presented
and compared to the experimental data in Chapter 4. A sensibility/calibration analysis is
performed in the main parameters controlling the unloading behaviour and conclusions
are presented in Chapter 5.
Results show that the parameters calibration depends on the assumption made for the
variation or not of K0 during unloading although it does not affect the accuracy of the
simulations. High values of overconsolidation ratio in the simulations’ input show better
fitting with the experimental data (at least slopes in the unloading e −ln p′ present more
similarities) although the unloading stress paths derived from laboratory data resulted
difficult to replicate with the MCC model simulations. Alternatively it is observed that the
stress paths from simulations follow the yield locus surface for a long part of the unloading,
especially when this unloading is extended long enough. A change in slope in the e−ln p′
plane is observed in all the simulations independently of the K0 assumption showing
two sections in the unloading graphs, these have been named pre and post bending
slope/curvature. However, the change in curvature is observed sharper in unloading tests
with bigger UIs and unloaded from a higher σ′

1
Introduction
The increasing design life for transportation projects in which bridges or tunnels are in-
volved and therefore the maintenance cost increases in the case of unpredictable settle-
ments/heave, is leading to a more detailed studies and modelling of the aforementioned
long-term deformations during the design process. Besides, 50 years is no longer a long-
term design life. Instead, 100 years is established as the minimum design life for such
projects. In areas with high soil anisotropy and complex soil formation history, the pre-
dictions can turn out wrong or scarce after a few years due to a poor modelling or limited
accuracy.
The relevance of such predictions when the structure to be built is founded in fine grained
soil increases. And not only is this crucial in pure or net loading cases in which the final
settlements will result into terrain subsidence but also in net unloading states. Besides,
under low permeability conditions, typical for fine grained soils, settlements are expected
to develop during a longer term than in coarse grained soils. In addition to that, high
plasticity soils, avid for water and capable of retaining great amounts of water within their
structure, present the major of the challenges.

Geological history
The project area is located in the German Basin. The area was covered by sea water
during the Late Cretaceous, leading to marine conditions and origin of the Tor Formation
equivalent, an homogeneous white or grey chalk. During the Paleocene, at least two
long episodes of erosion during the Danian-Selandian stages are identified, shaping the
basin during the early-middle Paleocene when the sea was more restricted and isolated.
[Sheldon, Gravensen, and Nøhr-Hansen 2012]
Slow marine sedimentation continued during the upper Selandian with a sea-level in-
crease. The deep marine basin characteristic from the late-Paleocene-early-Eocene led

Theory
The background theory supporting the simulations is explained in this chapter. Under
general approach to constitutive models, these can be tagged into three big categories
[Brinkgreve 1994]:
• Elastic and pseudo-elastic models
• Elastic perfectly-plastic models
• Hardening plasticity models
The first one corresponds to models in which elasticity is related with the reversal of strains
once the specimen is unloaded. Models in this category are based on elasticity theory,
although this classification includes the pseudo-elastic soil models in which the reversal
in the strain-energy is not completely satisfied. To clearly differentiate both types of mod-
els within the classification shown here, one could simply say that these models follow
unique (fully elastic) or multi-linear (pseudo-elastic) relations regarding the stress-strain
relationship.
The second big category sets the basis for more advanced plasticity theories. The founda-
tion of these type of models is the split of strains and strain rates into an elastic component
and a plastic component The elastic part is ruled by Hook’s law and the plastic part can
be represented as a strain vector perpendicular to a yield surface, i.e. the derivative of
the yield surface. Thus, a yield surface is defined, but it will not change its shape when
the material strains enter the plastic region. Another limitation of these models is the as-
sumption of pure elastic strains when the specimen is subject to pure compression, which
for soils like clays or silts is not realistic.
For the hardening plasticity models, the advantage lies on the fact that the yield surface,
instead of being fix, it expands with the specimen hardening or shrinks with the specimen
softening. These type of models combine the strain division between elastic and plastic
and add the expansion of the yield to represent new specimen status (account for new
plastic limits or soil ”updated” memory)
The constitutive model applied is shown below:
• Modified CAM-CLAY Model. MCCM
This constitutive model focuses on the elasto-plastic behaviour of soils and although the
name ”clay” appears in it, the model has been successfully evaluated for other types of
soil. It is an useful tool for representing the real behaviour of the analysed problem by
describing the stress-strain relationship of the soil subject to external variations in stress,
strain, temperature, moisture, etc. The model belongs to the Cam-Clay group of models
derived from the critical state concept under the umbrella of the Critical State Soil Me-
chanics (CSSM) theory [Schofield and Wroth 1968], and represent the most extensively
used constitutive soil model in numerical analysis.
Within the cam-clay group of models, is the Modified Cam-Clay Model (MCCM) the one
which represents the baseline for many of the current models developed. Almost any new
constitutive model based on the same principles as the MCC model is basically a tweaked
Modified Cam-Clay Model meant for accounting for extra soil parameters in an effort to
describe the soil behaviour with more accuracy.

Constitutive model
It should be noticed, that the formulation described in this section set the basis for the

2.1.1
Modified Cam-Clay Model (MCCM)
The original Cam-Clay model originated in Cambridge University in the late sixties and
developed until the release of the work done by Schofield and Wroth 1968. The model
was originally addressed to represent the behavior of normally consolidated ”wet” clays
subject to triaxial compression, under the plane strain assumption [Roscoe and Burland
1968]. The yield locus changed its shape from a logarithmic function to an ellipse in the
stress plane thanks to the work done by Burland between 1965 and 1968. Since then,
many researches have used the Cam-Clay model as a basis to develop more advanced
constitutive soil models.
Starting from the basis that any soil is composed by solid grains, liquid and gas, the group
of Cam-Clay models makes the assumption that the voids between soil grains are filled
with water, i.e. the sample is fully saturated. This assumption states the irreversibility of
volume changes (plastic deformation) during loading. As it has been introduced, Cam-
Clay models are based on plasticity theory.

Methodology
Kinslev et al. [Mejlhede Kinslev et al. 2019b and Mejlhede Kinslev et al. 2019a] had
access to a set of CRS and IL tests performed on Røsnæs Clay from Paleogene origin
found in the Fehmarn alignment. The tests replicate soil history conditions by loading the
samples to a vertical stress below the preconsolidation stress followed by unloading. The
samples were later loaded to an estimated stress based on both preliminary designs for
an immersed tunnel segment or a bridge footing. As this thesis concerns, the tests result
of interest for the unloading part. Three random tests are selected, corresponding to the
internal coding shown below to which an additional name is given for future purposes,
thus one can differentiate between unloading tests from low, medium or high stress:

The data retrieved from the Femern A/S data base [A/S 2013] provided to the author
of this thesis already shows both the strains and void ratio during the CRS tests, which
according to Mejlhede Kinslev et al. 2019b represents the soil conditions and soil loading
history to a better detail. The methodology used can be divided into two approaches.
The first approach is focused on the experimental data, and corresponds to the collec-
tion of modelling parameters already presented in the previous chapter (chapter 2). The
tests’ loading path is presented in the e −ln p′ plane as the main representation, which
allows for interpretation of the loading and unloading-reloading parameters (elasticity and
plasticity coefficients). The method for estimating these is both secant and tangential,
and the assessment of which is more suitable is left to the author’s interpretation together
with interpretation of the unloading stress path in the p′ −q plane. In addition, the plas-
ticity parameter is not only estimated from the experimental data but, due to the possible
”loss of preconsolidation memory” of the soil due to destructuration, it is also estimated
from literature review yielding a range of values to simulate between 2.5 and 7 times the
elasticity parameter (which can be accurately measured in the unloading branch). During
this stage, the collection of both secant and tangential elasticity coefficients allows for a
selection of two main κ values for attempting an lower bound fitting for the initial part of
the e −ln p′ experimental unloading curve and a second fitting aiming for the end-of-the-
test results collected from the experimental data. In addition, Fehmern A/S reported high
values of OCR, ranging from 1.5 to 5 [A/S 2013]. Given that the preconsolidation stress
was difficult to interpret from lab results, in this project, three OCR are considered, lead-
ing to a three K0 values. This broad selection of parameters will help to select the most
appropriate combination of all to best fit the experiments.

Abaqus requires the ”presetting” of an axisymmetric model, which initial dimensions have
been presented for each case in table 3.7. This initialization of the model requires the
setting of boundary conditions and initialization of field values.
Boundary conditions
The sample is prevented from displacing in x-direction (radial) in both sides, while dis-
placement in vertical direction (y) is allowed. No friction is imposed in the sides, thus
assuming perfect sliding between the cell and the sample. In reality, if friction appears
it could influence both the consolidation phase and the unloading, preventing the sam-
ple from swelling completely in the latter. The bottom boundary is considered fix in any
case, no x or y displacement and no rotation allowed. The upper boundary of the sample
is free to move, whereas a pore pressure boundary condition is set as u = 0Pa during
the entire simulation. As explained before, the unloading part is controlled by setting an
extra boundary condition applicable only for the third step (unloading) in which the upper
boundary moves vertically a certain value according to the difference between Hunl
ini and
Hf in table 3.7.
Field values
Three field values are set in the initial stage: void ratio, pore pressure and geostatic stress.
The void ratio in the entire sample is set to the corresponding value in table 3.6. The pore
pressure is reset to zero at the initial step, and the geostatic condition is used to set the
result of a K0 consolidation by introducing the stress value at the top and bottom of the
sample (in compression, i.e. negative required here) and the actual value of K0.

progressively until reaching the end of the unloading stage. During this process, a ”Soil”

experimental testing, thus such bending reported in ABAQUS is wanted and therefore the
calibration of both the first and the second parts of the unloading branch have to be fitted
by a sensitive calibration of the parameters controlling both. By looking at the stress path,
the starting point, moves towards the critical state line (M −line) as the OCR increases,
which on the other hand affects the K0 value. Seems reasonable then, to work only with
the lowest κ value derived from the experimental data which fits the beginning of the
unloading curve. Another hint for the parameters calibration that can be drawn here is
that the second part of the curve (after the bending) is should be controlled by λ, or at
least this parameter influences that part together with κ.

The revision and discussion of results for each case of initial unloading stress (low, medium

the OCR.
As it is introduced at the beginning of this thesis, the parameter λ is usually derived from
a compression test in which the sample is compressed up to critical state in which the
development of vertical effective stress and vertical strains reach a quasi-linear relation.
The slope of this linear relation is the mentioned λ. For the given data set, none of the
tests were conducted up to that high stress and therefore the values for λ here used could
be far from real. In order to adjust this, literature offers guidelines to estimate it from κ.
As mentioned before in the text, the relation between these two coefficients usually falls
in the range λ/κ = 2.5 −7. For the OCR, high values seem to shorten the initial part of
the curve and precipitate the bending earlier.

Conclusion
The unloading behaviour of Røsnæs Clay commonly presents a non-linear development
in the e −ln p′ plane. This behaviour has been observed in the results from three CRS
laboratory tests, all of them including an unloading branch showing how the non-linearity
is more marked when the unloading index is larger. Although previous studies [Mejlhede
Kinslev et al. 2019a], showed a double change of slope in the unloading branch for some
unloading indices, the interpretation conducted here showed a marked bending (change
in slope) in the unloading branch short after starting the unloading from a gentle slope
to a steeper section. The interpretation of the laboratory results was done under two
different assumptions. A simpler one considering a constant K0 value during unloading
and a more thorough one which considers the variation of OCR as the sample is unloaded
and consequently, the variation of K0. Both methods showed similar unloading curves in
shape although the actual slopes were different. Calibration of unloading parameters λ
and κ was performed according to these interpretations choosing a tangential method for
the interpretation of the unloading coefficient κ and an estimation for λ. The first estimation
was based on literature review which says λ = 2.5 −7 × κ and later it was found that for
large unloading indices, the slope of the post-bending part of the curve tended to match
the input for λ, so it was adjusted and in consequence the experimental results were
re-analysed.
The data interpretation from the CRS tests under K0 unloading conditions yielded for
all the simulations a tangential value for κ which proved to be accurate enough for the
simulations to fit the experimental data at low unloading index, matching perfectly the
starting linear behaviour of the unloading. On the other hand, only simulations conducted
with high assumed initial overconsolidation values proved to represent closer unloading
branches to the experimental ones. The position of the change of slope is directly related
to the OCR and friction angle as it is related to the proximity of the initial unloading pair
p′ −q to the yield locus. Giving the limitations of the Modified Cam Clay model, the two
ways of bringing closer the yield locus to the initial point of the unloading is to modify ϕ′
and/or the OCR (directly related to p′
c). The effective friction angle has been considered
at ϕ′ = 18◦and could be adjusted within ±2◦, but it would require to re-interpret the
experimental data as K0 would also change. The determination of the preconsolidation
pressure has been an issue due to the allegedly loss of preoconsolidation memory of
the soil. As soon as values like p′
c = 4.5MPa seem to be around a right value for the
unloading from σ′
v0 = 450kPa, it proves to not be suitable for unloading simulations from
σ′
v0 = 3000kPa.
The change in curvature observed in the unloading branch in the e−ln p′ has been identi-
fied with the point in which the stress path reaches the yield locus in unloading in the sim-
ulations, both in ABAQUS CAE and in PLAXIS 2D - Soil Test module. Both FE software
exhibit a linear constant relationship between q and p′ until reaching the yield surface. The
analysis of such stress path led to the observation that the unloading is considered purely
elastic and only dependent on Poisson’s ratio as the theory indicates for an overconsol-
idated soil. The post-interpretation of the experimental data according to this condition,
using Poisson’s ratio in the unloading, led to a perfect matching of both FE simulations
and experimental data in the elastic region. However, the experimental data does not
continue the unloading further enough to reach the yield locus under this interpretation
but continues showing the bending in the e −ln p′ plane. Notice that the full experimen-

==PAGE:98==
tal unloading is considered purely elastic in such interpretation whereas it could happen
that the stress path in the experimental data shows a shorter-than-expected linear elastic
behaviour to suddenly change into a non-linear behaviour or a elasto-plastic behaviour.
Unfortunately the Modified Cam Clay model does not contemplate this kind of behaviour
within the yield surface. This fact suggested that the use of more restrictive constitutive
model, which modifies the shape of the dry side of the yield locus, reducing the simu-
lated peak deviatoric stresses, would anticipate the bending in the stress plane and in
consequence the bending in the unloading plane, matching the experimental data.
Besides, considering that the soil analysed in this thesis is a complex material, with unique
features and geological history, an effective but at the same time simple constitutive model
as the Modified Cam Clay does not capture all these features. One main reported feature
is creep which is not considered in this model. Another improvement could be the use of
a constitutive model which accounts for the soil loss of structure as the Soft Clay 1 with
Structure (SCLAY-1S), which could shed some light about the short pure linear elastic
behaviour observed in the experimental results and its different interpretations.
