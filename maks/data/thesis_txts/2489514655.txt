ABSTRACT 
 
 
Until recently GDP has been used to measure the growth and development of cities and 
assess the quality of life held by the citizens. However, recent studies have suggested that a 
quality of life assessment is more complex than simply measuring the economic growth of a 
region, and so exploration of happiness has developed. 
 
Yet the current measurements and assessments of happiness are purely subjective and 
one-sided, often discarding unhappiness as a countermeasure. Through the use of a mixed 
methods approach, a 60-item indicator-based assessment framework was developed that 
explored 10 pillars of well-being from both subjective and objective perspectives to assess 
quality of life. 
 
These 10 pillars measure poverty, food security, health and well-being, water and 
sanitation, energy, education, employment and economy, equality, city and community, and 
safety and government. These 10 pillars were then applied to both Copenhagen and Melbourne 
in order to identify best practice through the framework in order to assist and inform policy 
makers and critically assess happiness. 
 
By comparing the case studies of both Copenhagen and Melbourne, the framework 
showed that overall residents of Copenhagen were happier and experience a higher quality of 
life than Melbourne citizens with Copenhagen receiving a happiness score of 71/100 and 
Melbourne scoring 60.6/100. However, Melbourne was not far behind in health and well-being, 
employment and economy, city and community, and safety and government, and even managed 
to outperform Copenhagen in areas of poverty and education. 
 
From this practical application of the framework to Copenhagen and Melbourne it was 
determined that best practice cannot be identified, however a city’s strengths and weaknesses 
can be categorised to inform policy makers and assist them in developing strategies to combat 
the challenges and maintaining the strengths.

SIGNIFICANCE OF THE STUDY 
 
 
With dissatisfaction and unhappiness rising and populations rapidly increasing within 
urban areas, citizen quality of life and well-being is becoming a progressively significant 
element of urban development. 
 
This study assesses both objective and subjective measures of happiness and 
unhappiness in order to index human well-being and quality of life within metropolitan cities. 
By better understanding what is required for citizens to maintain a high quality of life, policy 
makers can better develop strategies to maintain the strengths and combat the challenges faced 
within cities.

3 LITERATURE REVIEW 
 
A commitment to happiness within cultures has only arisen in the last 250 to 300 years. 
Until the 18th century, Christianity encouraged a saddened and fearful approach to life as the 
belief at the time was that God would encourage those who ‘allowed no joy or pleasure, but a 
kind of melancholic demeanor and austerity’ (Stearns 2012, para.4). While there are few 
records of personal happiness prior to the 18th century, writings suggest that culturally, people 
felt an obligation to apologise for the happiness they displayed (Schmitter 2010). 
This perception changed during the 18th century when the values of the Enlightenment 
encouraged people to ‘be always cheerful… and not suffer any sullenness’ (Stearns 2012, para. 
5). With an increase in education and access to writing tools, ordinary people were 
documenting enjoying happiness and independence (US 1776).  
While the list of happiness historians is limited, it is often documented that a significant 
shift in happiness occurred within Western cultures with the U.S. Declaration of Independence 
declaring that all men had the right to ‘the pursuit of Happiness’ (US 1776, para. 2). While this 
was not the leading cause of the shift in mentality regarding happiness, it did transpire during 
a time when an intellectual shift towards a variation of worldly matters and a reduced 
commitment to traditional Christian values took place.  
The commitment to happiness did not escalate again until after World War II and then 
again during the 1960s when happiness research began to be conducted across a variety of 
scientific disciplines, including gerontology, social psychology, clinical and medical research, 
and happiness economics (Diener, Oishi & Lucas 2011; Kullenberg & Nelhans 2015). It was 
during this period of scientific research that people began to question the usage and definition 
of happiness and its methods of measurement and application. 
In recent years a similar interest has peaked, however this time it was on the topic of 
unhappiness. This coincides with the rates of depression and anxiety amongst young people 
being at an eighty-year high, while symptoms amongst the adult population are at the highest 
point in 20 years (Klerman & Weissman 1989; Twenge 2015). The Cross-National 
Collaborative Group’s (1992) research shows that people are experiencing depression in 
greater numbers and are showing symptoms at earlier ages with each generation. These higher 
levels of depression and anxiety are reflected by data showing that both men and women report

3.1 What is happiness? 
In order to scientifically measure happiness, a clear definition needs to be developed. 
Despite this necessity, there is literature that suggests that happiness cannot be defined due to 
the variations in its meanings and usage, and a broad diversity in understanding across cultures 
and religions (Haybron 2013). 
In spite of this literature and in order to have a measurable study, happiness within this 
thesis will be defined as a positive emotional or mental state that ranges from serenity to ecstasy, 
and will be used as a context for determining life satisfaction, eudaimonia, subjective well-
being, and well-being (Cambridge University Press 2019; Oxford University Press 2019). To 
clarify the difference between well-being and subjective well-being within this literature 
review: subjective well-being is discussed as being self-measured and related to current 
emotional state, while well-being is measured objectively in regards to health and physical 
states. 
Two separate perspectives often surround happiness research and literature. The first 
concerns the current emotion experienced by an individual on Plutchik’s (1980) wheel of 
emotions related to joy as seen in Figure 1, or an overall state of emotion (Donaldson 2017). 
Plutchik’s (1980) three-dimensional model illustrates the relationships amongst the emotions, 
which assists in simplifying the incredibly complex interactions and emotional changes that 
individuals experience. The three-dimensional wheel of emotion not only depicts the primary 
emotional dimensions, but also explores emotional intensity and the relationships between two 
or more emotions. This thesis will focus heavily on the ‘joy’ dimension, however in order to 
fully grasp the concept of happiness, the polarity, sadness, will need to be discussed and 
analysed (Plutchik 1980). 
The second perspective of happiness is used in the measurement of life satisfaction and quality 
of life, often referred to as life satisfaction theory (Haybron 2013, 2019). This perspective sees

There are usages of happiness that include and combine both perspectives. Subjective 
well-being includes measures of current emotional experience as well as life satisfaction theory 
to create a cognitive and affective evaluation of one’s life (Diener, Oishi & Lucas 2011). 
Bradburn (1969) was one of the leading scholars to create a link between happiness and well-
being, by discovering that positive and negative affects act independently and are more 
complicated than simply being opposite to each other. This resulted in well-being and 
happiness studies that analysed both positive and negative states of emotion, showing that the 
elimination of negative emotions did not result in positive life satisfaction (Wilson 1967; 
Bradburn 1969; Diener 1984). Lyubomirsky (2008) uses subjective well-being to describe 
happiness as ‘the experience of joy, contentment, or positive well-being, combined with a sense 
that one’s life is good, meaningful, and worthwhile’ (p. 32).

Lands (2019) expands on the notion of subjective well-being by proposing that 
happiness also includes measures of eudaimonia – a Greek term that is often translated to mean 
happiness (Kashdan, Biswas-Diener & King 2008). Eudaimonia, happiness that arises from 
good works and focuses on living a life to one’s full potential, is not to be confused with 
hedonism, which focuses on subjective well-being and how one feels about their life (Ryan & 
Deci 2001; Kashdan, Biswas-Diener & King 2008). In contrast to subjective well-being, 
eudaimonic happiness is studied within literature as being objective, comprehensive, and 
holding moral validity due to its reflection of objective social values rather than subjective 
emotions (Annas 2004; Waterman 2007). Happiness research rarely compares the objective 
and subjective approaches unless it is to critique the measurement techniques. However, when 
it comes to measuring and indexing happiness it is necessary to include both objective and 
subjective approaches as objective tradition remains sceptical and recognises that an 
individual’s feelings may or may not reflect their true complex emotional state, while 
subjective self-report is essential in the study of happiness. 
By applying each perspective separately to measure happiness, different results can be 
obtained. Studies demonstrate that the correlation between happiness and income levels have 
shown to have a substantial impact on life satisfaction measures up until a certain threshold but 
exhibit very little impact on current emotional experience measures (Happiness Research 
Institute 2105; Wiking 2018). 
Depending on the context, happiness can hold different meanings, making it difficult 
to measure and quantify. Despite this, there are an increasing number of studies that aim to 
measure happiness in different contexts. 
 
3.2 How is happiness measured? 
Scientists and philosophers have attempted to measure happiness for centuries. In 1780, 
Bentham suggested that the primary goal for humanity was to achieve happiness and so it 
should be measured as a way to determine how well a government was performing (Tokumitsu 
2017).  
Unfortunately, the way happiness has been measured in recent decades is through the 
use of income as a proxy for happiness, well-being, and quality of life. In turn, we determine 
how successful a government is by comparing GDP per capita to measure the progress of

nations. This is a result of income, whether personal or national, being objective whereas 
happiness is considered subjective. As humanity’s understanding of psychology increases, 
working with subjective measures such as anxiety and depression is becoming less difficult. 
As mentioned previously, hedonic and eudaimonic happiness can be classified as 
subjective and objective respectively. Each of these two states require different methodologies 
and techniques for accurate measurement. 
The affective dimension, or hedonism, examines the emotions that individuals 
experience on an everyday basis. Wiking (2018, p. 28) offers a series of questions as an exercise 
to measure the affective dimension of happiness: ‘If you look at yesterday were you depressed, 
sad, anxious, worried? Did you laugh? Did you feel happy? Did you feel loved?’ 
The cognitive dimension, or eudaimonia, requires one to analyse the bigger picture and 
evaluate their lives. ‘How satisfied are you with your life overall? How happy are you in 
general? Think of the best possible life you could lead, and the worst. Where do you feel you 
stand right now?’ (Wiking 2018, p. 28). Within this thesis, the cognitive dimension will be 
studied, however in order to understand how happiness is measured this literature review will 
also examine methods for measuring the affective dimension. 
Several scales have been developed by psychologists in recent years in order to measure 
happiness. Some of the most widely used within literature and clinical testing include the 
Subjective Happiness Scale, the Positive and Negative Affect Schedule, the Satisfaction with 
Life Scale, and the Cantril ladder method. 
The Subjective Happiness Scale was developed in 1999 to measure global subjective 
happiness with four items on a seven-point scale. The scale requires participants to categorise 
themselves as happy or unhappy individuals as well as to identify themselves with descriptions 
of happy and unhappy individuals (Lyubomirsky 2012). The Subjective Happiness Scale 
requests individuals to use either absolute valuation criteria or valuation criteria regarding 
others (Karakasidou et al. 2016). While these criteria allow for standardisation for 
measurement there is very little literature to validate the Subjective Happiness Scale, and any 
research that has been conducted has been relatively small scale with 35 participants or fewer 
(Diener et al. 1985; Schaefer & Greenberg 1997; Foley, Matheis & Schaefer 2002; Mattei & 
Schaefer 2004). Mattei and Schaefer (2004) suggest combining the Subjective Happiness Scale 
with correlating tests such as the Satisfaction with Life Scale and the Positive Affect Scale to 
increase the questionnaire’s validity. While research suggests that the Playfulness Scale for

Adults correlates positively with the Subjective Happiness Scale, the required sample size 
needs to be over 35 to provide concrete evidence of the convergent and discriminant validity 
of the Subjective Happiness Scale (Mattei & Schaefer 2004).  
The Positive and Negative Affect Schedule is a mood scale developed in 1988 to be 
highly consistent and stable alongside high temporal reliability (Watson, Clark & Tellegen 
1988). The 1988 questionnaire consists of 20 items using a five-point Likert scale, however 
Watson and Clark (1994) later developed a longer, 60 item version with additional affect scales 
to increase the scale’s validity. This extended scale not only measures general positive and 
negative affects, but also four basic negative emotions, three basic positive emotions, and four 
more complex states of emotion. The Positive and Negative Affect Schedule uses several 
criteria models to evaluate judgement: item response theory, the Comparative Fit Index, the 
Tucker-Lewis Index, and the Root Mean Square Error of Approximation (Ebesutani & Regan 
2012; Sanmartin et al. 2018). 
The Satisfaction with Life Scale was developed in 1985 as a global cognitive dimension 
assessment of life satisfaction. The Satisfaction with Life Scale uses a seven-point Likert scale 
for individuals to agree or disagree with five statements about their life. Scores on the 
Satisfaction with Life Scale correlate with other measures of subjective well-being and are 
shown to have high internal consistency and high temporal reliability (Diener et al. 1985; 
Corrigan et al. 2013). However, despite this high internal consistency and reliability, the scale 
uses criteria that is set by the own participants resulting in low measurable consistency (Pavot 
& Diener 2009). 
The Cantril ladder method is famously used in the World Happiness Report and asks 
individuals to think of a ladder where their best possible life is ranked as a 10, and the worst 
possible life for them is rated a 0 (Levin & Currie 2014; Helliwell, Layard & Sachs 2019). 
Individuals are then asked to rate their current lives on the 0 to 10 scale. This scale allows 
participants to create their own criteria resulting in low validity due to measurable 
inconsistency. 
The World Happiness Report was first published in 2012 to evaluate global happiness 
in order to identify the countries with the highest levels of happiness. The World Happiness 
Report includes leading experts from economics, psychology, survey analysis, and national 
statistics in order to clarify how measures of well-being can effectively assess the progress of 
nations and their governments. The 2019 report discusses the impacts of mental illness and the

The data used in the World Happiness Report is drawn from the Gallus World Poll 
questionnaire which measures 14 indicators within the core questions: business and economics, 
citizen engagement, communication and technology, diversity, education and families, 
emotions, environment and energy, food and shelter, government and politics, law and order, 
health, religion and ethics, transportation, and work (Gallup 2019). 
However, Wiking (2018) argues that questioning on overall life status leads humans to 
put unnecessary weight on income concerns rather than happiness. The World Happiness 
Report (Helliwell, Layard & Sachs 2019) fails to create an appropriate sample size with only 
an approximation of 1,000 participants are surveyed within each country, however, in order to 
accurately measure at a national scale it is estimated that between 2,000-3,000 participants are 
required (Helliwell, Layard & Sachs 2019). Another failure of the World Happiness Report 
(Helliwell, Layard & Sachs 2019) is the disregard to measure unhappiness within nations, 
despite discussing unhappiness extensively within the report, which could potentially lead to

3.3 Happiness and government

at the time.

majority of studies surrounding inequality focus on the distribution of income and wealth

greater impact on quality of life and life satisfaction (Marmot et al. 1994; Evans, Barer & 
Marmor 1997; Marmot 2005).

3.4 Happiness and sustainability 
The 2030 Agenda and the Sustainable Development Goals can be used to create 
government policies to create a holistic approach to development that includes economic, 
environmental, and social targets to create a world where physical, mental, and social well-

Wahlén 2019). Denmark holds a score of 85.2 (Helliwell, Layard & Sachs 2019), which means 
that the country is 85.2% of the way to the best outcome across of the Sustainable Development

happier nations tend to be more socially proactive, have higher levels of economic equality, 
and policies that effectively promote and protect the natural environment.

and study of links between happiness and environmental sustainability. In 1992, the then U.S.

and consumption. Research has since disproven the relationship between well-being and 
income past a certain threshold, and that decoupling happiness from consumption has the 
potential to increase happiness and create more sustainable societies (The Happiness Research 
Institute 2015; Wiking 2018).

3.5 Cities and happiness 
3.5.1 A happy city 
While Denmark does not hold the title of being the happiest country, its capital city, 
Copenhagen, has been titled as the happiest city in the world (Lerwill 2019). Every year the 
European Commission conducts a large survey to determine how happy citizens are across 
European cities (Martin 2016). This subjective survey uses a five-point Likert scale to ask the 
participants to set their own criteria in order to rank their satisfaction with a wide range of 
systems and facilities that are available to them. 
The survey created by the European Commission (2016) targets over 40,000 people 
across 79 cities. Within this survey, participants are asked about their satisfaction levels 
regarding: infrastructure and facilities, such as public transportation, healthcare facilities, 
sports facilities, cultural facilities, educational facilities, streets and buildings, public spaces, 
and availability of retail shops; employment opportunities; housing; the presence and 
integration of foreigners; safety and trust; city administrative services; environmental 
conditions such as air quality, noise level, cleanliness, green spaces, and climate change action; 
personal circumstances including life in general, place of residence, finances, employment 
situation; personal concerns regarding the participants’ cities; and finally, participants’ overall 
satisfaction with living in their city. 
While this subjective set of criteria results in low replicability, it focuses on an extensive 
range of personal and policy-related items. Due the high tax rate that Copenhagen’s citizens 
pay towards public infrastructure, it is understandable why Copenhagen often holds the top 
rank for the happiest city (Lerwill 2019).  
 
3.5.2 A liveable city 
In 2018, Melbourne was named the most liveable city in the world for the seventh 
consecutive year, with a score of 98.4 (The Economist Intelligence Unit 2019). This score was 
determined from the Global Liveability Index which assesses locations based on living 
conditions for qualitative and quantitative components across five broad categories: stability, 
healthcare, culture and environment, education, and infrastructure (The Economist Intelligence 
Unit 2019). Each component is rated as acceptable, tolerable, uncomfortable, undesirable, or

intolerable bases upon analysists’ judgement and relative performance on a range of external 
data (The Economist Intelligence Unit 2019). 
However, while this index covers a wide range of objectively measured indicators, it 
fails to consider several major influences that are necessary when determining the liveability 
of a city. These oversights are arguably due to the problematic nature of the Melbourne 
government interpreting the report as a universal liveability score when the index was created

3.5.3 A safe city 
With the increase in urbanisation across the globe, one of the major challenges that 
metropolitan areas are facing is the ability to provide safety. The Safe Cities Index (The 
Economist Intelligence Unit 2019) identified four pillars of safety that constitute a safe city: 
personal, infrastructure, health, and digital security. Within each of these pillars, relevant 
indicators are grouped into inputs and outputs. Outputs measure the current safety of the city, 
whereas the inputs are the policies and personnel that enhance safety (The Economist 
Intelligence Unit 2019).  
According to the Safe City Index (The Economist Intelligence Unit 2019) both 
Copenhagen and Melbourne place in the top 10 cities for overall score, ranked 8 and 10 
respectively. Under the personal security pillar Copenhagen ranks 2nd with 93.6 out of 100 due 
to policy related indicators, level of engagement, community participation, and use of data-
based techniques (The Economist Intelligence Unit 2019). Melbourne, on the other, drops in 
ranking to 16 with a score of 86.8.

possibility for improvement. Melbourne ranks 7th place with 93.5 points, while Copenhagen

4 RESEARCH QUESTIONS AND HYPOTHESIS 
 
4.1 Knowledge gap 
By studying the literature several knowledge gaps appear within happiness research. 
When measuring and indexing happiness it is necessary to include both objective and 
subjective approaches rather than using only a single approach as a majority of literature is 
found to do. 
While the World Happiness Report (Helliwell, Layard & Sachs 2019) is the leading 
study on happiness measurements, it has a yearly sample size of approximately 1,000 which 
does not meet the suggested sample size of 2,000 to 3,000 participants at a national level 
(Helliwell, Layard & Sachs 2019). The World Happiness Report also fails to measure 
unhappiness with nations, which has potentially led to a one-sided survey. 
The Sustainable Development Goals provide current data on the national progress and 
has been proven to have an impact on national happiness, however many of the studies 
discussed within the literature review failed to consider sustainability as an impacting variable 
on happiness. 
When happiness is measured it is generally performed at a national scale. If happiness 
were to be measured a subnational or metropolitan level, then it would be possible for local 
governments to adjust their policies to increase well-being and services to enable citizens to 
live happier and better lives. 
Finally, there is a lack of universal assessment frameworks and indicator frameworks 
that consider happiness of residents at a metropolitan level that can be used to assist and inform 
policy makers at determining best practice.

5.1.2 Paradigm 
A paradigm is a shared belief system made up of an epistemological issue which 
concerns what is or what should be regarded as acceptable knowledge within a discipline, and 
an ontological consideration which concerns the nature of social entities (Bryman 2016). This 
belief system influences the types of knowledge researchers seek to obtain and how data is 
interpreted (Morgan 2007, Brierley 2017). 
Kuhn (1962, 1996) was a physicist and philosopher who became responsible for the 
common practice of paradigms as an approach for summarising researchers’ beliefs and 
practices relating to the way they gather and interpret knowledge. Kuhn’s (1962, 1996) belief 
was that scientific research was intertwined with social research as scientific comprehension 
can never be entirely objective and must also account for subjective perspectives. Kuhn (1962, 
1996) states that all objective conclusions are established upon the subjective worldview and 
perspective of the researchers and participants.

5.3.2 Case study design 
This thesis will apply a case study methodology focusing on both Melbourne, Australia 
and Copenhagen, Denmark in order to assess and measure the quality of happiness found within 
each city. 
Case study-based research is becoming an increasingly popular approach amongst 
researchers and urban planners and developers, with several prominent authors contributing to 
the use and advancement of the methodology (Ragin & Becker 1992; Stake 1995; Merriam 
2009; Yin 2009; Denzin & Lincoln 2011; Creswell 2013). 
Case study methodology involves an in-depth investigation of a specific situation and 
location as opposed to an expansive statistical survey (Hyett, Kenny & Dickson-Swift 2014). 
While other methodologies focus on the methods of enquiry, a case study ‘explores a real life 
contemporary bounded system (a case) or multiple bounded systems (cases)... through detailed 
in depth data collection involving multiple sources of information… and reports a case 
description and case themes (Creswell 2013, p. 97). 
In study areas concerning urban development and society, case study methodology is a 
necessary research strategy and arguably the leading methodology used within the 
interdisciplinary field as it enquires into both personal realities and model cases established 
within the subject area (Johansson 2003). 
This thesis will combine case study methodology with comparative study methodology 
in order to measure happiness within two cities through an indicator-based assessment 
framework.

5.3.3 Comparative design 
Within research, case study methodology is often paired with a comparative 
methodology in order to achieve a wider perspective (Bryman 2016). Simply put, this design 
method involves studying two contrasting cases through the use of identical, or near identical, 
methodologies in order to gain a better understanding of a specific social trend (Bryman 2016).  
Each indicator will act as an individual observation and will apply a comparative 
analysis of Melbourne and Copenhagen. Each observation, or indicator, will then be combined 
in order to do an overall comparison of the two cities in order to assess citizen happiness.

When determining quality of life and happiness, the concept of income-based poverty 
can be too simplistic as socio-economic disadvantage is considered multidimensional. Due to 
this Scutella, Wilkins and Kostenko (2009) propose seven dimensions to measure what o\would 
become known as social exclusion: material resources, employment, education and skills, 
health and disability, social, community, and personal safety. 
 
Social exclusion is broken down into three separate categories: marginal social 
exclusion, deep social exclusion, and very deep social exclusion. The social exclusion index is 
ranked from 0 to 7. People who experience scores of 1-2 are considered to experience marginal 
social exclusion, whereas scores of 2-3 are equal to deep social exclusion, and scores of 3 or 
more are considered to experience very deep social exclusion (Brotherhood of St Laurence 
2019). 
It is estimated that approximately 17.7% of Copenhagen residents experience a degree 
of social exclusion due to multiple disadvantages (Eurostat 2016; Christian 2016). While this 
is the highest rate of all Nordic regions, there are many European nations that witness social 
exclusion in over a third of the population (Eurostat 2016; Christian 2016). It should be noted 
that there is currently no data for the depths of social exclusion within Copenhagen. 
 
With the rate of social exclusion in Copenhagen steadily rising, and with the city 
holding the highest percentage of individuals experiencing social exclusion with the Nordic 
regions, the city will receive a score of 7/10. While 17.7% is a relatively low percentage 
comparatively, policies need to be improved if social exclusion rates are to begin decreasing.

Access to clean and safe water is necessary for the prevention of diseases and is the 
foundation for creating good health and resilience within a community. In order to provide an 
entire city with safe and clean water a combination of high-quality water sources, effective 
treatment plants, and an effective and safe distribution network that has minimum water loss is 
necessary (State of Green n.d.). 
Copenhagen, like the rest of Denmark, uses groundwater as its sole source of drinking 
water and as a result minimal treatment is required (State of Green n.d.). This, paired with an 
efficient distribution system, means that contaminants are minimised and so chlorination is not 
required (state of Green n.d.). 
Not only is the drinking water clean in Copenhagen, the city’s harbour is also a popular 
swimming hole for locals (Raidt 2015). This has not always been the case, however the Danish 
government worked on rerouting wastewater, building overflow barriers, and creating 
underground water-storage vessels to keep the harbour as clean as possible (Raidt 2015).  
However, with a changing climate resulting in short bursts of torrential rainfall, basic 
services risk damage with sewage overflow into the harbour occasionally occurring (Raidt 
2015). There is also a risk that an increase in farming within the nation will result in 
Copenhagen’s water reservoirs to become polluted (Raidt 2015).  
While these challenges exist, the Centre for Climate Adaptation (n.d.) and the City of 
Copenhagen (2011) are utilizing urban planning strategies to alter the urban layout in order to 
store higher water volumes and deliver water at an increased rate. It will take several decades 
to fully implement the strategies the city has proposed, but if they are successful the city will

remain protected for many years to come even in the worst-case scenario (Centre for Climate

city hopes to retain its quality of water but with combined efforts between the City of

Access to water has remained a challenge for Australia for several decades due to severe 
droughts and poor rainfall. This means that Melbourne often enforces water restrictions to 
manage water supplies for the future. 
However, these water restrictions do not limit the city’s access to clean water, with the 
city having access to 10 reservoirs that source 80% of their water from protected and 
uninhabited forests (Melbourne Water 2019b). With clearfell logging permitted in these 
catchment areas, there are studies that suggest that regrowth is consuming up to 6% of 
Melbourne’s annual water usage, which only adds to the challenges that the city faces regarding 
water access and usage (Feikema et al. 2006). 
Additionally, due to the location of these reservoirs, Melbourne water needs to travel 
through many kilometres of distributions mains and aqueducts. In order to maintain a high 
quality, the water is filtered and cleaned through the addition of chlorine and fluoride (Yarra 
Valley Water n.d.).

If a society feels as though they can trust their government, local businesses, and the 
media then the community will develop to become more resilient. Trust allows individuals to 
live and work within a community and develop a sense of belonging to a group (Jaffe 2018). 
When a society puts its trust in a leader or leaders, organisations and communities thrive. 
However, when there is an absence of trust, conflict and division can occur (Jaffe 2018).  
Jaffe (2018) describes six building blocks of trust: 
1. Reliability and dependability; 
2. Transparency; 
3. Competency; 
4. Sincerity, authenticity, and congruency; 
5. Fairness; 
6. Openness and vulnerability. 
These building blocks are the foundations that communities determine their levels of trust for 
institutions such as government, media, businesses, and non-government organisations.  
Due to the nature of this indicator, national statistics will be used as there is little to no 
data available at a metropolitan level. 
Danes are considered to be some of the most trusting people in the world as trust is 
considered the core of social interactions and interactions with businesses and government 
organisations (Ministry of Foreign Affairs of Denmark n.d.). Anthropological studies show that 
Denmark benefits from general social trust, which is when individuals trust in those that they 
have never met before (Burrmann, Braun & Mutz 2018).

8.2 Can an indicator-based assessment framework be produced to assist and inform 
metropolitan policy makers? 
 
As can be seen within the data collection and analysis section, an indicator-based 
assessment framework can be produced to assist and inform metropolitan policy makers. The 
framework can be used to assess happiness within cities and identify where the strengths and 
weaknesses lie when concerning citizen well-being. From here, policy makers can determine 
best practice in order to maintain metropolitan strengths and develop strategies to combat the 
challenges that negatively impact citizen well-being. 
 
8.3 Can best practice be identified by building an indicator-based assessment framework? 
 
Through the indicator-based assessment best practice cannot be identified, however the 
framework does isolate strengths and weaknesses in community happiness within metropolitan 
areas. Once these challenges have been acknowledged, best practice can be identified and 
strategies for combating challenges and maintaining strengths can be formulated. 
 
Both Copenhagen and Melbourne have weak performance in pillar 10: Safety and 
Governance. This is mostly due to data not being available in order to accurately measure 
metropolitan happiness. This lack of information generates issues when identifying best 
practice as strengths and weaknesses within a city cannot properly be pinpointed. 
 
In order to best practice to be identified, each indicator within a pillar requires sufficient 
and current data in order to ascertain a metropolitan region’s strengths and weaknesses when 
citizen happiness is considered.

8.5 Further research 
 
This thesis presents several opportunities to produce further research, either as ways to 
extend upon the current framework or as ways to eliminate weaknesses and improve on the 
strengths. 
 
In order to more accurately assess the happiness of citizens one way to expand on the 
data would be to use only primary, self-collected data. This would eliminate several 
inconsistencies within the data collection such as: the need to use national statistics when 
metropolitan data is unavailable; generate data that has not yet been collected or made available 
by organisations; erase inconsistencies due to multiple bodies collecting and analysing data 
with different methods; and create up to date information and erase inconsistencies due to data 
being gathered from different years and different populations. 
 
Expanding on the collection and use of primary data, a greater number of respondents 
to the questionnaire would provide more accurate information. Within this thesis a total of 105 
responses were used, 55 for Melbourne and 50 for Copenhagen. However, in order to produce 
data with a +/- 10% margin of error 100 respondents would be required for each city, a +/- 5% 
margin of error would require 400 respondents, and a +/- 3% margin of error would need 1,100 
respondents. 
 
Within this thesis, each indicator consisted of a slightly different scoring method 
depending on what was being measured. In order to create a more accurate set of scores, a more 
quantifiable and consistent method of scoring could be developed. 
 
Another expansion of this research could be to generate a weighting system for the 
indicators and pillars that could be expanded further by creating multiple weightings based on 
stakeholder profiles. 
 
And finally, a way to apply this thesis to further research would be to apply this 
indicator-based assessment framework to more cities around the world.

9 CONCLUSION 
 
 
Currently, GDP is used to determine the development of a city and the well-being of its 
citizens, however citizen well-being is determined by many other factors outside of economic 
growth and development. 
 
This thesis developed an indicator-based assessment framework to measure and assess 
the happiness of citizens within Copenhagen and Melbourne as a way to assist and inform 
metropolitan policy makers and the strengths and weaknesses a city possesses. 
 
From this framework it was determined that citizens of Copenhagen were overall 
happier than Melbourne residents scoring 71/100, however the city of Melbourne produced 
high levels of satisfaction in terms of education and poverty levels and received and overall 
happiness score of 60.6/100. 
 
While best practice could not be identified from this indicator-based assessment 
framework, this thesis developed an extensive framework that could be used to assist and 
inform policy makers through application to relevant metropolitan cities in order to critically 
assess and quantify happiness and quality of life while also identifying the strengths and 
weaknesses a city possessed.
