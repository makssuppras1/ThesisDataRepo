Abstract
The offshore wind industry has been growing enormously in the last couple of decades
and is, with the increasing demands on renewable energy, set to expand even more.
Boundaries are pushed meaning that the demands for knowledge and analyses for op-
timizing offshore substructures are increased. The fatigue performance of the welded
structures is currently the bottleneck in the design phase.
Tensile residual stresses induced during the welding process has a huge impact on the
fatigue strength and lifetime of a structure. These residual stresses are currently evalu-
ated from a qualitative point of view in the design norms. This generalized treatment
of the residual stress condition is debatable as it may lead to inaccurate assessment of
the actual effects the residual stresses may have. This is in particular in case of high-
strength steels or when compressive residual stresses are present.
In this thesis, an alternative fatigue assessment approach which treats the interaction of
both external loads and welding residual stresses is elaborated. The principle of residual
stress relaxation (shakedown) is presented. Cyclic stabilized residual stresses are then
introduced to quantify the magnitude of residual stresses.
These stabilized residual
stresses are combined with the mean stress from external loads to describe the effective
mean stress locally in a welded component. This effective mean stress approach gives
the opportunity of fatigue strength modification in terms of an improved bonus factor
concept similar to the one available in the current IIW-norm.
Finally, the effective mean stress approach is applied to a practical example, considering
a longitudinal stiffener. Three load cases are evaluated concerning fatigue damage: the
current most commonly used fatigue assessment approach, current fatigue assessment
approach considering available fatigue strength modification, and the alternative fatigue
assessment approach considering the improved bonus factor concept. The purpose is to
show which opportunities the alternative assessment approach provides compared with
the current procedure stated in the design norms.
The investigation carried out in this thesis provides promising results in terms of im-
proved fatigue performance considering the effective mean stress approach. Furthermore,
the improvement shows how the design S-N curves can be modified based on the mean
stress from external loads and quantified residual stresses.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
The Wind Industry
The wind energy industry has been in great development in the past decades and yet set
to develop further in the future. The demand for sustainable energy sources increases to
fulfill the latest climate-agreement within the European Union. This agreement involves
the reduction of emission of greenhouse gasses by 40% before 2030 compared to 1990.
In Europe, several countries have increased investment in both onshore and offshore
wind energy. Figure 1.1 shows the distribution of total installed wind power energy by
country in Europe at the end of 2019 [62].
Figure 1.1: Total installed wind power capacity by country [62].
It is worth noticing that two thirds of the total wind power capacity in Europe are
located in just five countries. However, it should be emphasized that the cumulative
capacity for each country is not a representative value without comparing it to the spe-
cific country’s overall energy demands. For example, the cumulative power capacity in
Germany equal to 61 GW which corresponds to 30% of the EU-countries overall wind
power capacity, does only cover 26% of the Germany’s electricity demand in 2019 [62].
Considering Denmark with a cumulative power capacity equal to 6 GW, half of the
electricity demand (48%) is here covered by wind energy [62].

1 Introduction
In the upcoming years, the Netherlands is set to increase its offshore industry signifi-
cantly. In the second quarter of 2020, the Netherlands got the green light to proceed with
the construction of the world’s largest offshore wind farm. The wind farm will consist of
140 turbines with an equivalent electricity output equal to 1500 MW which corresponds
to the annual electricity consumption of more than two million Dutch households [49].
The fact that the Netherlands is expanding its wind industry is in good agreement with
the short term estimation of the installed capacity in Europe. Figure 1.2 illustrates the
predicted cumulative installed capacity within the next couple of years.
Figure 1.2: Estimated cumulative installed capacity in Europe [63].
It is observed that by the end of 2023 Europe has a cumulative installed capacity
of 277 GW, with 20% being represented by offshore installations. Offshore installations
are advantageous compared to onshore structures with respect to the energy generation.
Offshore structures are in general larger than onshore structures and located at open wa-
ter where the surface roughness is lower and wind speeds are higher, potentially leading
to more energy generation. However, the installation of offshore structures is far more
comprehensive due to more complex conditions leading to more expensive installation
and assembly costs for offshore structures compared to onshore. According to Moné et
al. [43], the installation and assembly of offshore structures represent approximately
20% of the total costs versus 6% for onshore structures.
1.1.1
Offshore Substructures
The increasing demand for renewable energy in the offshore industry requires the in-
creased performance of the offshore wind turbines. Various substructures exist to sup-
port the wind turbine. The choice of the substructure is highly dependent on the site
conditions, especially the water depth and soil conditions. Figure 1.3 illustrates the
different types of support structures for offshore wind turbines.

In the early years of the offshore wind industry, the monopiles are limited to water
depths up to 30 m. These monopiles are usually 50-80 m long with a base diameter
equal to 5-8 m. The installed wind turbine on top has a capacity of 6-8 MW [59]. In
the last decade, even larger monopiles (XL monopiles) have been installed at a water
depth of up to 60 m. These XL monopiles are currently limited to the length of 120 m
and a diameter of about 10 m due to logistics such as manufacturing, transportation,
and installation constraints [25]. The wind turbines mounted on top have a capacity
of up to 10 MW. Figure 1.4b illustrates an XL monopile from the Veja Mate offshore
project in the North Sea. In 2016 this monopile was the world’s largest. The length and
diameter are 82.2 m and 7.8 m respectively and the total weight is 1302.5 tonnes [61].
However, even larger wind turbines are in development meaning larger monopiles are to
be expected in the future as well.
On one side, larger wind turbines lead to the production of more sustainable energy.
However, scaling up the size of current wind turbines brings various challenges along.
Besides the aforementioned logistic challenges with respect to the support structure, the
development must also be considered from an economical as well as a structural point
of view. Larger wind turbines require a larger support structure. Larger structures are
subject to larger external forces in general e.g wave and wind loads along with large
bending moments compared to smaller ones. In order to secure the structural integrity,
more material may be used. More materials increase both the weight and the total
costs of the structure. It is to consider that the cost of transportation and installation
of such structures is partly dependent on the total weight.
Thus, reducing the ma-
terial usage and hereby the weight may lead to optimization from an economical point
of view. However, structural integrity must not be compromised by the economic aspect.
The substructure is one of the most critical parts of the whole wind turbine as it contains
several welded connections that are prone to fatigue failure. Fatigue failure is influenced
by various parameters. However, due to lack of sufficient knowledge about the exact
interactions between these parameters the current fatigue design recommendations are
forced to follow an over-conservative fatigue assessment approach. One of the major
concerns is the residual stresses in the structure that arise from the welding process.
These residual stresses may initially be in the magnitude of tensile yield strength which
is detrimental in terms of fatigue life. Achieving more precise knowledge of the fatigue
behavior may lead to increased fatigue performance of the substructure and hereby re-
duce manufacturing costs of current XL monopiles or justifying the construction of even
larger ones from a structural point of view.

1.3
Scope of Work
The scope of work in this thesis involves various fields of engineering. Initially, a the-
oretical study of literature regarding the fatigue phenomenon, submerged arc welding,
and residual stresses, in general, is performed. This gives a fundamental background
knowledge of the essentials that will be presented later.
Next, the central part of this thesis is the influence of welding induced residual stresses
on fatigue performance is extensively investigated. This includes the concept of me-
chanical residual stress relaxation, which turns out to influence the fatigue performance.
However, this is not accounted for in the current fatigue-norms. This leads to further
review of the current fatigue procedures, regarding how the residual stresses in the fa-
tigue design are evaluated.
An alternative fatigue assessment approach, which quantitatively accounts for the re-
laxed residual stresses is evaluated. Lastly, a practical example considering a longitudi-

the alternative method.
Based on the findings throughout the thesis a discussion is carried out in relation to
the presented theory. Finally, a conclusion summarizing the discussed investigations is
drawn. At the end of the thesis recommendations for future work are proposed.

CHAPTER 2
Fatigue Phenomenon
Fatigue is the phenomenon where metals are prone to failure at loads below the yield
strength of the material. Fatigue failures are seen throughout the construction industry,
especially where dynamic loads are present such as for offshore structures subjected to
wave and wind-induced loads. The fatigue phenomenon has been known for more than
150 years. August Wöhler, a German railway engineer initiated in the 1850s the studies
leading to the discovery of the relation between fatigue damage and the amplitude of
cyclic stresses [42]. In the past couple of decades, research and experimental testing
has been intensified, yet there is still much to discover to fully understand all aspects of
fatigue.
The following chapter will elaborate on the fatigue mechanism in general.
The def-
inition of fatigue is presented, followed by a brief description of fatigue loads. Lastly,
fatigue in relation to welds is presented along with some of the current fatigue assessment
approaches.
2.1
Fatigue Definition
Fatigue failure is a mechanism occurring in components subjected to fluctuating loads.
Permanent structural damage is accumulated at some point(s) due to cyclic loading
leading to the initiation of cracks and later growth of these. In the worst-case complete
failure occurs. To initiate a fatigue crack either tensile or shear stresses must be present
as illustrated in figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Crack initiation modes [37].
Mode 1 illustrates the opening of the crack due to tensile forces, mode 2 and 3 are
the in-plane shear and transverse shear mode respectively. However, mode 1 is the most
common, as the majority of fatigue crack initiations are caused by cyclic tensile stresses
instead of shear [54]. In order to evaluate the fatigue life, the development of a fatigue
==PAGE:26==
8
2 Fatigue Phenomenon
crack until complete rupture must be divided into two phases: crack initiation period
and crack growth period, see figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Different phases of fatigue life [54].
The initiation period starts with a so-called cyclic slip causing plastic deformation
between the atoms in the material. This is an irreversible process occurring near the
surface as it is less constrained compared to inside the material. The cyclic slip occurs at
a microscopic scale involving only a few grains due to stress levels below the yield stress.
Crack nucleation occurs right after the cyclic slip due to the separation of the crystalline
planes. Cracks in the size of one grain can now form. These are called micro-cracks as
they are only visible in a microscope.
The crack growth period occurs in the material at a macroscopic scale. The crack is now
visible to the eye and grows slowly until final failure. Based on fracture mechanics and
experiments crack growth is proportional to the square root of the crack length (assuming
constant stress range and stress concentration) [7]. This leads to issues detecting the
crack, as the size of it grows slowly in the early stages, accelerating until complete failure.
The principle is depicted in figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Typical crack development [57] (modification from [7]) .
It is necessary to distinguish between the two phases in order to understand the
concept of fatigue life of structures. E.g. surface conditions such as notches have a huge
impact on the initiation period, however it has a negligible influence on the crack growth
period [37]. On the other hand, during the crack growth phase material properties plays
a larger role as the crack has grown deeper into the volume of the material.
==PAGE:27==
2.2 Fatigue Load
9
The total fatigue life is therefore the sum of both the crack initiation period and crack
growth period. There is no such definition of a clear transition from the crack initiation
phase to the crack growth phase. However, in practice, it is assumed that the transition
occurs when continuum mechanics becomes applicable. This is for crack length in the
order of 0.1-1mm. For welded connections, the crack initiation phase is relatively short
compared to the crack growth phase, which is deemed to be 70-90% of the fatigue life [33].
Both phases of fatigue life are considered in common practice when dimensioning new
structures or securing already existing structures with respect to fatigue. In this case,
the structural engineer distinguishes between two methods: The first method concerns
the crack initiation period, where the fatigue assessment is based on S-N curves. The
second method deals with the crack growth period and the fatigue assessment is based
on fracture mechanics.

amplitude loading.

S-N Curves - Definition
S-N curves are an illustrative way to represent the fatigue strength of a structural detail.
These fatigue curves are developed based on numerous fatigue tests on identical speci-
mens subjected to cyclic loads until complete failure. The number of cycles until failure
is plotted versus various stress ranges. The result is usually plotted on log-log axes and
may look like depicted in Figure 2.10. It is important to notice how the S-N curve is
divided into three regions.
In general practice, the type of fatigue we as structural engineers mainly are dealing

exceeding the yield strength of the material. This region ranges from 104 −107 cycles de-

the calculation and verification procedures remain within the elastic region. However,
the structure may eventually be subjected to cyclic stresses which repeatedly exceeds
the yield limit. This is classified as low cycle fatigue ranging from zero up to 104 cycles
where the fatigue assessment is based on the plastic fatigue strength rather than elastic,
which of course results in fewer cycles until complete failure.

Welding
Welding is a process that unifies two or more parts.
Metals are the most common
materials to weld, though welding applies to other materials such as thermoplastics and
wood in rare cases [28]. Only the welding of metals will be discussed further on. Joining
the metals requires a high heat input typically generated by an electric arc which will
be the main focus in this thesis. Pressure alone or alongside additional heat can also be
used to weld metal parts. Figure 3.1 illustrates the weld zone of a one-sided butt weld
along with some of the most common welding concepts used in this thesis.
Figure 3.1: Common weld definitions [42].
Arc welding is by far the most widely used welding process. Arc welding includes e.g.
submerged arc welding (SAW), metal inert/active gas (MIG/MAG), and tungsten inert
gas (TIG). Within the offshore industry SAW is the most common welding process due
to its efficiency and automation.
The art of welding requires great understandings within different fields of engineering,
ranging from a microscopic level understanding the metallurgy in the weld to the influ-
ence it has on the structural integrity of a full-scale structure. The following section will
briefly go through the aspects of welding.
3.1
Submerged Arc Welding
Submerged arc welding (Figure 3.2), is to some extent similar to MIG/MAG-welding,
though it is a more high-productive welding method. The principle is that an electrical
circuit is completed by an electrical arc between the base material and one or more
wire electrodes. The electrical circuit generates a large amount of energy in the form of
heat, melting the base material, and consumable wire electrode. The arc is protected
by a layer of flux powder fed to the welding head during the welding process. The main
==PAGE:40==
22
3 Welding
purpose of the flux powder is to cover and shield the molten weld pool from reacting
with e.g. nitrogen and oxygen in the air. Some of the flux powder closest to the arc
melts and merge with the weld pool. On top of the weld is a layer of slag formed, which
is easily removed if the welding parameters are chosen properly. The remaining flux
powder is removed and reused. The flux powder also has the ability to mask the light
from the arc, dampen the noise as well as prevent smoke and spatter [60].
Figure 3.2: Schematic diagram of SAW [8].
As mentioned previously SAW is performed with one or more electrodes. The welding
process 121 refers to single wire welding used for thinner materials. On the other hand
welding process 123 refers to multiple wire welding. This process is beneficial for thicker
materials and larger welding grooves which is found in offshore structures. Process 123
is split into different electrode configurations, where the most relevant in relation to this
thesis is presented.
Twin Wire
: Twin arc welding is the configuration where two electrodes are connected
to the same power source. Two wires are hereby fed simultaneously to the weld pool.
This leads to considerably higher rates of disposition, in the range of 30-40% compared
to single wire setup [60].
Tandem Wire
: Tandem arc welding is the configuration where each of the two
wires is connected to their independent power source and fed simultaneously to the
weld pool. Due to the independent power sources, tandem arc welding allows accurate
customization of the weld as different polarities, currents, and wires can be used. The
leading arc most often operates at high direct current electrode positive (DCEP) and
corresponding lower voltages, which ensures deep penetration. The trailing arc operates
at lower, mostly alternating current, filling, and forming a smooth weld bead [12]. Figure
3.3 illustrates the most common SAW configuration.

3.3
Weld Defects
A high-quality weld has both fatigue and toughness properties superior to those of the
base material [42].
Welding is as any other craft unable to be completed perfectly,
meaning minor imperfections can not be prevented and should, therefore, be mitigated.
Poor welds of low quality may lead to major defects which may be devastating with
respect to structural integrity. Some of the most typical defects associated with welding
are cracks, undercuts, porosity, slag inclusions, lack of fusion, and penetration. Figure
3.14 illustrates the location of where these types of defects commonly occur.
Figure 3.14: Typical welding defects from SAW [42].
3.3.1
Cracks
Cracks are the most critical defect as they are directly related to the fatigue life of the
welded joint as described in Chapter 2. Welding cracks are distinguished between hot
and cold cracks. Hot cracks form at high temperatures during the solidification phase.
Cold cracks may be related to hydrogen embrittlement and occur in the weld or heat
affected zone (HAZ) as the weld reaches room temperature.
Centerline cracks occur due to different causes such as segregation, surface profile, and
bead shape. Segregation induced cracks forms from constituents separate during weld
solidification due to lower melting point. The constituents are the last to solidify why
a crack in the center region is formed. Surface profile induced cracks are caused by
shrinkage stresses in the surface of the weld metal. This phenomenon is often related
to concave weld profiles. Especially during SAW with deep penetration, bead shape
induced cracks tend to occur. Solidification starts from both sides towards the middle
of the weld. When the two sides merged, fusion may not be sufficient which may lead
to a centerline crack.
Transverse cracks are usually initiated in the weld and may grow deep into the base
metal. This type of crack is associated with hydrogen, which may enter the weld pool
==PAGE:48==
30
3 Welding
from different sources such as air humidity, moist flux powder, or grease from the base
material.
3.3.2
Undercutting
Undercuts are a geometrical defect and a consequence of incorrect welding settings. Most
commonly too high travel speed. As illustrated in Figure 3.14 undercutting appears as
a small groove along the weld edge. This leads to insufficient cross sectional geometry
of the weld which gives rise to large stress concentrations.
3.3.3
Porosity
Porosity is mainly caused by either contamination of the weld groove or moist flux
powder. During the welding process, any residual oil, paint, or grease from the base
material will decompose and form various gasses. Gas bubbles may be trapped under
the molten weld pool and hereby remain as a weld defect. If the flux powder is wet,
moisture evaporates during the welding process, and gas bubbles may be trapped in the
weld as well.
3.3.4
Slag Inclusions
Slag inclusions are the result of poor welding techniques. It most often occurs when
slag from a previous weld pass is not removed sufficiently during the welding of multiple
passes.
3.3.5
Lack of Fusion and Penetration
Incomplete penetration is most often found when the weld bead does not penetrate the
entire thickness of the base material or when two opposing weld beads do not interpen-
etrate [13]. Weld defects related to incomplete penetration is most often a consequence
of too low arc current or too high travel speed.
Lack of fusion is caused by various parameters. As mentioned in 3.1.3 a too high voltage
may cause lack of fusion. Focusing the arc in the center of the weld creates a molten
weld pool, but does not necessarily melt the base material. This is seen as a layer of
consumables on top of the side walls without any fusion.
3.3.6
Control of Welds and Acceptance Criteria
As mentioned previously welding is a craft that can not be done completely perfect.
The quality of welds is therefore evaluated based on the degree of imperfections and
definite defects. Within the field of welding engineering, DS/EN ISO 5817 [4] dictates
the acceptance quality criteria in welds. The standard distinguishes between the quality

Based on the quality level some welding imperfections such as porosity and slag inclusions
may be accepted to a certain limit, unlike cracks that are never accepted due to their

Residual Stresses
The following chapter elaborates on the concept of residual stresses starting with a brief
description of the definition in general.
Further on, the residual stresses in relation
to welds are presented followed by the effect it has on fatigue performance.
Lastly,
the mitigation of residual stresses is evaluated based on different post weld treatment

4.1
Definition
Residual stresses are a self-equilibrium stress-state present in a body after the origi-
nal cause of the stress (external load) has been removed. The stresses are hereby also
known as internal stresses. The residual stresses are generated upon self-equilibrium
in the material and may origin from manufacturing and fabrication processes such as
hot rolling, quenching, and welding due to local plastic deformations. The plastic defor-
mations are most typically caused by either mechanical loads, thermal loads, or phase
transformations.
4.1.1
Order Classification
The formation of residual stresses is classified based on the averaged length scale in
which they work [52]. Figure 4.1 illustrates classification of first, second and third order
(σI, σII, σIII) residual stresses.
Figure 4.1: Classification of first, second and third order residual stresses (σI, σII, σIII)
in relation to governing mechanical properties and length scale [17].
==PAGE:52==
34
4 Residual Stresses
First order residual stresses act on a macroscopic level affecting an area in the size of
approximately 1 mm. This type of residual stresses is described by thermo-mechanical
terms i.e. temperatures, stress/strain, and displacements. Both the second and third
order residual stresses are averaged over a microscopic level concerning the metallurgical
aspect. However, second order residual stresses act over an area with an approximate
size of 1 - 0.01 mm between individual grains governed by grain morphology and rate
of grain growth.
Lastly, the third order residual stresses are related to interatomic
mechanisms i.e imperfections and dislocations inside the interior of crystallites averaged
over the size of approximately 10 µm - 1 nm. In structural engineering practice, only
first order residual stresses are of particular relevance. This is corresponding very well
with the fact, that external design stresses applied to components are of the first order
as well. Thus, only residual stresses of the first order will be considered further on.
4.2
Welding Induced Residual Stresses
Residual stresses concerning welds are a major concern. Based on recommendations
and standards such as DNV-GL [11], IIW [23] and Eurocode 3 [14] the residual stresses
after welding is in general considered to be in the magnitude of yield strength. Resid-
ual stresses are caused as mentioned previously by different manufacturing processes.
However, welding induced residual stresses due to thermal expansion and contraction is
mainly considered in this section.
Welding involves a highly localized transient heat output from the welding arc, melt-
ing the surrounding metal and creating a molten weld pool. The weld and adjacent base
material are then exposed to non-uniform temperature distribution causing non-uniform
expansion and contraction of the weld and heat affected zone during heating and cooling.
The thermal expansion causing volumetric changes leads to residual stresses in all three
dimensions: longitudinal, transverse and through-thickness.

heating and cooling [41].

The formation of residual stresses in a weld may be simplified by the three-bar config-
uration illustrated in Figure 4.2. The concept involves three bars fixed at the ends. The
smaller middle bar represents the weld region, and the two larger bars correspond to the
adjacent base material. Figure 4.2a depicts the initial state where all three bars are at
ambient room temperature and no residual stresses are induced yet. Figure 4.2b depicts
the middle bar being heated to a temperature corresponding to the temperature in the
weld during the welding process. These high temperatures cause thermal expansion. The
middle bar is however constrained from expanding due to the colder adjacent bars. This
introduces compression in the middle bar and tension in the outer bars. Furthermore, at
elevated temperatures the material yield strength decreases, which consequently results
in permanent plastic deformations. Figure 4.2c depicts a situation where the middle bar
is not fixed in both ends and hereby able to contract freely. When the room temperature
is reached yet again, the middle bar is then being subject to shrinkage due to the plastic
deformations induced during thermal expansion. However, since all ends are fixed in the
configuration, tension is introduced in the middle bar during cooling while compression
occurs in the two outer bars, see Figure 4.2d.
To fully understand the development of welding residual stresses, it is essential to be
aware of the temperature dependency of the material properties, especially the yield
strength which was briefly mentioned above. When the steel temperature rises from
ambient temperature up to around 500◦C no remarkably drop in yield strength occurs.
However further temperature increase above 500◦C leads to a significant drop in yield
strength.

material behavior and thermal conditions. However, some of the most relevant aspects
of residual stress development during welding has been outlined and forms the base
knowledge for later discussions.

4.2.1
Effects on Fatigue Performance
Tensile residual stresses are in general detrimental with respect to fatigue performance
as they accelerate crack initiation and ultimately fatigue failure. On the other hand,
compressive residual stresses are considered beneficial as these do not contribute to the
crack initiation and growth of these [15]. Tensile residual stresses in the magnitude of
yield strength may be present in welded structures even before it has been subjected to
any external load. Section 2.2 points out the relevance of the stress range ∆σ and mean
stress σm in relation to fatigue. The influence and how to deal with residual stresses in
relation to fatigue performance will be elaborated and discussed in general.
Due to the suggestion from guidelines and codes (DNV, IIW, and EC3), the assumption
of tensile residual stresses up to the magnitude of yield strength may neglect the effect
of mean stress from external loads even though it may be fully compressive load cycles.
Figure 4.6 depicts the resulting stress range due to the superimposing of external cyclic
stresses onto existing residual stresses in the magnitude of tensile yield strength.
Figure 4.6: Effect of existing residual stresses on the stress range ∆σ [40].
This lead to the debate about the exact influence of the residual stresses on fatigue
performance. Some suggest that the assumption of residual stresses up to yield strength
is too conservative and may not represent a real size structure. Others suggest that
the initial residual stress state is relaxed during the first load cycles [19]. This thesis
will discuss some of the uncertainties concerning residual stresses in relation to fatigue
performance of welded structures and hopefully either confirm or reject some of the
current scientific thoughts of this subject. This is done in chapter 6.
4.3
Post Weld Treatment
Post weld treatments (PWT) may cause a shift in the effective stress state experienced
by the structure in the as-welded condition. Numerous PWT techniques exist to miti-
==PAGE:57==
4.3 Post Weld Treatment
39
gate the detrimental effects of residual stress and notch-like features in the vicinity of
the weld. However, PWT is aiming to improve either the residual stress state or the
local geometry e.g. near the weld toe, where local stress concentrations are present.
Geometric improvement is achieved by grinding and in some cases remelting the face
layer of the weld. This provides a smoother transition between the weld bead and the
base material reducing the local stress concentrations. Reducing the stress concentra-
tion leads to the reduced magnitude of the stress range which the weld experience, see
Figure 4.7b. Improving the residual state is achieved from various techniques e.g. peen-
ing, LTT (low transformation temperature) filler material, and thermal stress relieving.
These processes reduce the local stress ratio and hereby the mean stress, see Figure 4.7c.
Peening techniques such as shot-, hammer- or needle peening induces plastic deforma-
tion e.g. near the weld toe, which releases the undesired residual tensile stresses and
furthermore applying beneficial compressive residual stresses [16]. LTT filler material
produces mainly weld metals consisting of martensite during phase transformation con-
trary to conventional filler material which typically leads to either ferritic or bainitic
weld metals depending on the chemical composition of the steel.
The formation of
martensite may negate or even counter the contraction during cooling [31]. Utilizing
LTT filler material may lower the tensile residual stresses or even produce compressive
residual stresses near the weld. Thermal stress relief or residual stress annealing is a post
weld heat treatment (PWHT) and requires controlled heating and cooling of the welded
joint. Thermal stress annealing occurs in the region of the recrystallization temperature
which is approximately half of the melting temperature of steel. Studies by O. Vöhringer
[46] shows complete residual stress relief for various steels exposed to temperatures of
approximately 600◦C.
Figure 4.7: The most common post weld treatments of welded joints [50].
Aside from the beneficial effects from the treatments stated above the different post
weld treatments must be doable in practice. E.g. thermal stress relieving of a full-size
monopile may not be realistic as it requires a large furnace. The amount of energy
required to perform a stress relief of a full-size structure may not be realistic in terms

more problematic when it comes to larger structures. On the other hand, the numerous

Numerical Modeling of Weld Assembly
FE-modeling in engineering practice is a complex coupled problem. Modeling a welding
process is no exception. The majority of the FE-modeling of a welding process is dictated
by the thermal and mechanical analysis. However, disciplines such as metallurgy and
fluid dynamics also affect the modeling. First, the thermal analysis is compared with
an analytical solution, the so-called Rosenthal solution for a simplified case. When the
thermal analysis is validated, the next step is to model the weld geometry and perform
a convergence study to find the optimal element size in the mesh with respect to compu-
tational time and accuracy. When a sufficient FE-model has been established, external
loads may be applied. Thermal loads were applied to replicate the heat input from the

thermal loads applied in the FE-model differ from the heat input recorded during the

6.1
Residual Stress Relaxation
It is briefly described in section 4.2.1 that the assumption of tensile residual stresses up
to the magnitude of yield strength in welded structures suggested by norms and stan-
dards may be too conservative. To clarify this statement it is required to investigate the
influence of residual stresses in a different way than what the recommendations prescribe.
Residual stress relaxation (shakedown) is a known phenomenon in literature [18][44][64].
Generally speaking, the residual stresses in welded components are limited by the yield
strength of the material. The concept is illustrated in figure 6.1c. Residual stress re-
laxation is a mechanical stress relaxation mechanism that occurs when external loads
6.1a superimposed with tensile residual stresses 6.1b exceeds the material yield strength.
This leads to the situation 6.1c, where shakedown has occurred.

6.2
Fatigue Damage Assessment
One of the current fatigue damage assessment methods is based on the mean stress
approach in accordance with IIW [23]. However, this approach may be outdated and too
conservative. Recommendations and design codes are rarely subjected to changes. This
is due to the fact, that it requires the agreement from experts and institutes worldwide
within the respective field of engineering in order to change design standards. Within
the field of offshore structures, experimental work most commonly forms the base for
any changes to the design codes. Extensive amounts of studies from experimental tests
exist evaluating various parameters and their influence on fatigue performance. Fatigue
performance is well-known to be the bottleneck with respect to the structural integrity
of offshore structures, especially the welded joints.
One way to improve fatigue performance is to justify the application of a less conservative
fatigue assessment approaches during the design phase, leading to increased fatigue
strength. This section describes the fatigue assessment according to IIW [23] and fatigue
assessment based on an alternative approach.
6.2.1
Mean Stress Approach
S-N curves in the IIW-recommendations are established under the conservative assump-
tion of high tensile residual stresses represented by a stress ratio R = 0.5. The real
residual stress conditions are typically unknown meaning the fatigue design is mean
stress-independent and ”high” tensile residual stresses are assumed.
However, IIW-
recommendations allows fatigue strength correction in terms of a bonus factor, f(R)
if one can prove ”low” or ”medium” tensile residual stresses in the component. IIW
distinguish between the following three cases: (I) unwelded base material, (II) small-
scale thin-walled structural elements, and (III) complex and thick-walled structural
components [23].

”Unwelded base material and wrought products with neg-

Therefore, the bonus factor f(R) in the current IIW-norm becomes relevant only if

any additional bonus factor in case of a load scenario where the entire stress range is in
compression.
6.2.2
Effective Mean Stress Approach
The mean stress approach described in the previous section is in a recent study by Jonas
Hensel [21] evaluated with respect to some of the conservative assumptions the design
norm states. Furthermore, the material yield strength and the sensitivity to both mean
stress and residual stress are considered in relation to the current IIW norm. This sec-
tion is briefly elaborating the essentials in the study leading to the application of the
effective mean stress approach.
The three cases of residual stress conditions ”low”, ”medium” or ”high” is a qualita-
tive description of the actual residual stress state and may lead to imprecise evaluation
of the residual stress effects. Being able to evaluate the effects of residual stress and
mean stress quantitatively based on their magnitude may lead to more beneficial fa-
tigue designs. Considering the interaction of mean stress and residual stress and their
influence on fatigue strength, they must be treated independently of each other due to
different behavior: Mean stresses may increase locally due to notch-like effects but does
not change in case of local plastic deformation. On the other hand, residual stresses may
be relaxed or increased due to local plasticity. The individual contribution is given in
terms of sensitivity to mean stress m∗and residual stress m respectively.
Taking the influence of both mean stress from external loads and the residual stress
condition into account is achieved by introducing the effective mean stress σm,eff, see
Eq. 6.7 [21].

evaluation of the effective mean stress and effective stress ratio. The subjective assess-
ment of a ”low”, ”medium” or ”high” residual stress state is hereby avoided. Introducing
the enhanced bonus factor gives the opportunity to adjust the S-N curve for a given weld
detail which leads to increased fatigue performance based on a more quantitative basis.

crack growth phase. Limited knowledge of the residual stresses inside a body is available
due to lack of adequate measurement techniques. However, neutron diffraction (ND) is a
viable method for thicker samples. Unfortunately, ND is expensive and cannot provide
investigations on site [51]. In a study by B. Qiang [51] a 40 mm, X-groove butt-welded
plate produced using SAW is investigated and the through-thickness residual stresses
were determined by the use of ND method at a various distance, d from the weld center.
The result is shown in Figure 7.1.

The FE-analysis results in tensile residual stresses in the magnitude of approximately

self-equilibrium, meaning tensile residual stresses must be in balance with equivalent
compressive residual stresses. However, this is not observed in the case of the numerical
results.
It is plausible that the shape of the residual stress field observed in the case of the
numerical results may be a consequence of the thermal load dissipating deeper into the
adjacent base material than desired. This increases the width of the tensile residual
stress field and furthermore shifts the compressive residual stress field further away from
the weld center why it is not visible in Figure 7.4.
With that said, it is not that the FE-model should be completely rejected, though
it might be subject to modifications in the future. Fatigue failure in welded components
such as the modeled butt-welded joint often occurs in the vicinity of the weld, where
the model is most accurate and yield similar results as the experimental measurements.
7.1.3
Fatigue
The results from the fatigue tests carried out by M. Stepien [36] led to 60.49% improve-
ment of fatigue strength at N = 2 · 106 cycles. Previously fatigue tests carried out by M.
Ólafsson [47] of similar 40 mm butt welded joint led to 30.79 and 18.15% improvement of
fatigue strength at N = 2·106 cycles respectively. Welding is a complex process with sev-
eral variable parameters, why it should be emphasized that a direct comparison should
not be made between the two aforementioned studies. Furthermore, it should be pointed
out that one of M. Stepien’s objectives was to investigate improved weld reinforcement
geometry, which may explain why the greater improvement of fatigue resistance was
achieved in his study compared with M. Ólafsson. Common to these studies is that
comparison with similar, but residual stress relieved specimens are highly desired. It is
currently well-known that residual stress annealed specimens exhibits improved fatigue
performance. However, no literature regarding fatigue testing of residual stress annealed
thick butt-welded joints is currently available.

7.2
Alternative Fatigue Assessment Approach
7.2.1
Residual Stress Relaxation
It is known in general, that the presence of residual stresses reduces the fatigue life of a
welded structure. On the other hand, mechanically relaxed or thermal annealed welded
components tend to exhibit increased fatigue performance. In the design phase of a
structure, lack of knowledge about the actual residual stress state is a major concern,
most often leaving the structural engineer without no choice but to assume tensile resid-
ual stresses in the magnitude of yield strength. This assumption is to some extent valid
as it is common to have high tensile residual stresses in the vicinity of the weld, e.g as
seen in Figure 5.7. However, a shakedown of residual stresses is proved to occur for both
butt welds and longitudinal fillet-welded gussets during both initial static loading and
continued cyclic loading. This mechanism is occurring depending on the external loading
on the structure and is hereby a function of time until the residual stresses are stabilized
as described in section 6.1.1. The general assumption of residual stresses in the magni-
tude of yield strength in the entire lifetime of a structure may hereby be too conservative.
Common to these residual stress relaxation methods whether it is mechanical shakedown
or thermal stress relief is that the magnitude of residual stresses is reduced. However,
the exact magnitude of residual stresses prior to the design phase of a structure is most
often unknown despite being subject to residual stress shakedown during the time of
service or post weld treatments during fabrication. This is in conflict with the current
design codes and recommendations which is based on a qualitative assessment of the
residual stress conditions. Thus leading to limited opportunities to account for the ac-
tual residual stress state.
Besides the subjective assessment approach of residual stresses in the design codes in
general, it is worth to point out the description of category I, low residual stress acc.
to IIW: ”Unwelded base material and wrought products with negligible residual stresses
(<0.2·fy) [23]”. This is simply not a reliable assessment approach as even low residual
stresses relative to the yield strength may have a large influence on the fatigue perfor-
mance. This is the case for particularly high-strength steels as shown in Table 6.2.
7.2.2
Quantitative Assessment of Residual Stresses
The lack of knowledge about the actual residual stresses as mentioned previously is one
of the limitations for less conservative and more precise fatigue assessment. Quantifying
the magnitude of residual stresses in a structure is thus a step in the right direction.
This may be achieved by the use of the engineering model Eq. 6.2 which predicts the
stabilized residual stresses in butt-welded and longitudinal fillet-welded gussets. Fully
degraded residual stresses are achieved when the external load reaches the material yield
strength. This corresponds to fully stress relief when a component is reheated to the

recrystallization temperature approximately half of the melting point [46]. However,
thermal annealing of real size structures such as monopiles is currently inappropriately
in practice. This is due to both logistic and economical aspects such as the necessity
of a large furnace which requires a huge amount of energy to reach the recrystallization
temperature in a full-scale structure.
In section 6.1.2 a demonstration of the engineering model was made to investigate to
what extent the model is overestimating the stabilized residual stresses compared with
corresponding experimental data. Two cases were investigated where the stabilized resid-
ual stresses predicted by the model were a factor 1.51 and 13.1 larger than the actual
measured residual stresses respectively. It should be emphasized that a sample size of
two cases is not representative to conclude to what extent the model is overestimating
the stabilized residual stresses. The achieved results are therefore solely to confirm the
model being conservative.

The alternative effective mean stress approach suggested by J. Hensel [21] gives the
opportunity to assess the residual stress conditions in a component more accurately
compared to the current IIW norm procedure. The major difference is the subjective as-
sessment of residual stresses in the IIW norm being replaced with a quantitative method
to account for the actual residual stresses.
Furthermore, the alternative fatigue assessment approach gives the structural designer
the opportunity to link the impact of filler material on residual stresses with the fatigue
design. This becomes relevant when LTT filler material is considered, which according
to literature [30] and [31] significantly reduces the magnitude of initial tensile residual
stresses and in some cases introduced compressive residual stresses. The purpose of LTT
filler material is to delay the formation of martensite. The transformation from austenite
to martensite leads to expansion as martensite has a lower density compared to austen-
ite. The expansion due to phase transformation counteracts the thermal shrinkage in
the HAZ and hereby mitigating the development of tensile welding residual stresses.
The lower temperature that the martensite transformation starts the more impact is
achieved. It might be obvious to raise the question of why LTT filler material is not
applied in all welded joints due to the beneficial mitigation of tensile residual stresses.
It should, therefore, be emphasized that filler materials in general, whether it is LTT or
conventional filler material has different material properties that should not compromise
the quality of the weld. To summarize the discussion about LTT filler material is to
consider the fact, that the alternative effective mean stress approach is able to account
for its influence on fatigue performance. This is advantageous as it creates a link be-
tween all the requirements e.g. WPS, that the welding engineers have for performing the
welds, and relate these to the residual stresses so that the structural engineer for assess-
ing a structure’s fatigue capacity has a detailed calculation model for each welding detail.
From a critical point of view, it is highly desired to find a way to determine the initial
residual stresses reliably and precisely in a real size structure in order for the alternative
fatigue approach to be used ideally in the future. The approach requires the initial
residual stress state as an input parameter and determining these using e.g. HDM is not
a viable option for full-scale structures. Finite element models may be a reliable option
in the future. However, FE-models must be developed adequately in order to account
for all the various aspects related to welded details if the results are to be used for the
design of a structure.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
8.1
Fatigue Assessment
Fatigue assessment of welded structures according to the current design norms are still
subject to improvements. One of the limitations for improvement is the yet not fully
understood topic being welding induced residual stresses. The consequence is fatigue
designs which may be too conservative due to lack of knowledge of the actual residual
stress state. However, it should be emphasized that the material yield strength cannot
be used as a parameter to evaluate the effects of residual stresses in welded components,
even though it is common practice in the current design norms such as the IIW-norm.
As shown in recent studies, even a low fraction of residual stresses relative to the yield
strength may affect the fatigue resistance markedly. The steel grade is therefore not
suitable for assessing the influence of residual stresses on fatigue performance, why the
magnitude of the residual stresses, and the external load stresses should be considered
instead.
8.2
Fatigue Strength Modification
The fatigue strength of welded structures may be subject to various enhancements de-
pending on the considered design norm. The bonus factors, f(R) from IIW is mainly
investigated in this thesis. The use of f(R) larger than 1.0 is limited by the lack of
reliable information regarding the residual stresses in a component. Furthermore, the
IIW-norm distinguishes between three cases (Case I, II and III), though these defini-
tions are very general and not defined very precisely. Lastly, the bonus factor is limited
to 1.6 when effective stresses are resulting in a stress ratio R = −1, meaning no fur-
ther enhancement is achieved even for stress cycles being in fully compression which are
known to be beneficial in terms of fatigue.
The literature proposes an alternative fatigue assessment approach which is based on con-
sideration of effective mean stresses σm,eff and the stress ratio Reff respectively. This
fatigue procedure is based on a quantitative assessment of residual stresses including
the effects of a mechanical shakedown of residual stresses. Furthermore, a bonus factor
similar to the one from the IIW-norm is introduced and extended to include compressive
mean stresses.
8.3
Practical Example
Both the standard fatigue assessment approach and the alternative effective mean stress
approach were used to determine the accumulated fatigue damage in a longitudinal fillet-

welded gusset subjected to three load cases, where two of them are in their extreme end
of the load spectrum.
The improvement in fatigue performance achieved from this investigation gives a clear
indication that the alternative fatigue assessment procedure yields overall larger improve-
ment compared to the standard procedure. No improvement is achieved independent
of the assessment approach when extremely high cyclic tensile stresses are present such
as for LC1. This is currently the most common way to perform fatigue assessment due
to the lack of knowledge regarding the residual stresses in a component. The largest
improvement is achieved using the effective mean stress approach (LC3) leading to 84
and 36% reduction in accumulated fatigue damage in terms of fully compressive and al-
ternating loading respectively. Load case 2 (LC2) under consideration of the IIW-norms
current fatigue strength modification lead to 54 and 31% reduction in accumulated fa-
tigue damage in terms of fully compressive and alternating loading respectively, which
is slightly less improvement compared with LC3.
The alternative fatigue assessment procedure requires reliable information on the magni-
tude of the initial residual stresses in the considered detail as a input parameter. Despite
the improvement in fatigue performance is proved considering the effective mean stress
approach, one major issue remains in order to apply these improvements in the real world:
The issue is, that a practical solution to determine the residual stresses in real size struc-
tures is required. The current residual stress measurement techniques e.g. HDM, XRD
and ND are simply not viable in practice considering full scale structures.

CHAPTER 9
Further Work
The fatigue performance of welded structures and the influence of residual stresses are
still not fully covered yet. Suggestions for future investigations are presented below.
It is highly desired to investigate the fatigue performance of stress-relieved thick butt
welded joints and compare with results from current studies of similar thick butt welds
in as-welded conditions. Furthermore, it is interesting to investigate the fatigue perfor-
mance of thick butt welded joints from a numerical point of view in both as-welded and
stress-relieved conditions.
Expanding the effective mean stress approach to a wider collection of weld details such
as butt welds is also a subject of future work. This involves experimental work in order
to determine the critical effective mean stress of the considered detail. Furthermore, a
similar model to the one presented in this study should be established to adjust the
design S-N curves of the considered weld type accordingly.
Lastly, future studies are strongly encouraged to investigate possible solutions for de-
termining residual stresses in full-scale structures, which are applicable in practice in
terms of time consumption and costs.

Hole Drilling Method
The concept of HDM is that hole drilling and hereby removal of material disturbs the
stress field in the surface of the specimen. Redistribution of the stresses leads to small
deformations around the hole. The corresponding strains are measured by a strain gauge
placed close to the hole, see Figure D.1. Based on the measured strains the magnitude
of existing residual stresses may hereby be calculated.
Figure D.1: Principle of HDM [53].
Before the HDM process can begin it is essential to have a smooth and clean surface
ensuring sufficient link between the specimen and the strain gauge as well as removing
any existing indentations or impurities. Next, the placement of strain gauges happens
followed by the drilling while the strains are recorded through specific software. The
HDM-setup in practice is illustrated in Figure D.2. Lastly, the strains are converted into
corresponding stresses. It should be emphasized that residual stresses achieved from the
hole drilling method are only valid up to 80% of the base material yield strength [53].
