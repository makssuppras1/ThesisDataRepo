Summary
The Danish mutual fund industry is one of the largest in Europe per capita, with 95%
of the assets invested into investment funds being invested into mutual funds. In recent
years mutual funds have received criticism for their performance as they often replicate
the contents of their respective benchmarks and fail to outperform them. If the objective
is to achieve a similar outcome as a market index, why do investors choose mutual funds?
This thesis explores the use of regret tracking models in generating portfolios capable of
tracking index benchmarks. The data used throughout this thesis consists of three dif-
ferent return datasets. A theoretical index return dataset from the Fama-French library
will be used as a benchmark for ETF- and mutual fund return datasets.
The thesis is split into two parts. The first part will focus on the pre-evaluation and
comparison of the datasets through a series of performance measures. These measures
will cover the statistical moments of the datasets and the Sharpe ratio of the datasets.
The second part will cover the implementation of tracking models (regret models). Anal-
ysis of the portfolios’ performance generated with those models will be conducted to test
their tracking ability of a benchmark index. The measures to evaluate the portfolios will
rely on statistical moments and performance measures, with emphasis on ”goodness-of-
fit” measures.
The results show that tracking models can track the majority of the benchmark indexes
with loose ”goodness-of-fit” criteria, and 20% of the benchmark indexes with strict
”goodness-of-fit” criteria. The conclusion is that the portfolios generated are diverse,
and those able to track the benchmark index show little deviation from the benchmark.
The ETFs are better suited for tracking mid- and high return and deviation portfolios
while the mutual funds are better suited for tracking portfolios portraying low return
and deviation.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter will introduce two types of investment funds, exchange transferable funds
(ETFs) and mutual funds, their characteristics, changes in their popularity and man-
agement, and finally, the need for performance analysis of these funds. In the following
chapters Modern Portfolio Theory, virtual-advisories, and tracking models will be dis-
cussed as they are essential to this research. At the end of the following chapter, the
limitations and assumptions made for this project’s purpose are listed. The reader is
asked to keep this information in mind when going through the rest of the thesis.
1.1
Problem description
An investment is an item or an asset that is acquired with the hope of financial gain
throughout the lifespan of said investment. When it comes to people’s savings, people
choose to put money aside for various future expenses and endeavors. These savings
can either lay dormant under the mattress or be put into work by investment. The
possibilities of different investments are numerous, but the most common factors which
stipulate people’s choice is an equilibrium between the financial gains they hope to make,
versus the risk they are willing to take.
Since the late 18th century, people have been investing in investment funds. Investment
funds are financial instruments where individuals invest their money into a fund that
follows an investment policy that suits the investor. Investing in a fund enables the
investor to benefit from working together with other investors as a group. This enables
them to take advantage of the economies of scale, hire professionals to handle their in-
vestment, increase the diversification of the asset portfolio, and reduce the unsystematic
risk, which entails smaller investments.
Danish investors invest heavily in investment funds. In the year 2016, around 2.300
billion DKK were invested in investment funds, which accounts for around 38% of the
consolidated assets owned by Danes, most of which is invested in mutual funds [1].
Per capita, the Danish mutual fund industry is the third-largest in Europe [2]. Mutual
funds account for around 95% of Danish holding in investment funds. Mutual funds are
actively managed funds that pool money to invest by following the fund’s policy. The
remaining 5% is invested in other forms of investment funds, most notable of which are

(ETFs), accounting for 2% in 2016 [1]. ETFs are passively managed funds that aim to

In recent years mutual funds have received criticism for their performance. The criticism
originates from the fact that the mutual funds are often underperforming the benchmarks

That raises a question, Why do investors hoping to achieve an outcome similar to a cer-
tain benchmark index choose to invest in active mutual funds, rather than other types
of investment funds available?
This thesis is the second in a three-part coalition, aiming to conduct a performance
evaluation, and optimization on ETF-, and mutual fund datasets using an index return
dataset consisting of developed market indexes, industry portfolio indexes, and bond

1.1.1
Problem objective
In this thesis, the focus will be put on implementing tracking models based on regret
theory. ETF- and mutual fund datasets will be utilized for creating portfolios capable
of tracking benchmark indexes from an index return dataset (IRD). The evaluation will
be split up into three parts. In the first part, a preliminary analysis of the datasets
will be conducted through an analysis of performance, standardized moments, and risk
measures. In the second part, the mutual funds and the ETFs will be used to track
individual indexes of the IRD, using various tracking methods based on regret models.
The results from the tracking models will be analyzed with regards to performance,
errors, statistical moments, and risk, the results will shed light on how the models
compare, and their drawbacks. This process is explained in the funnel in figure 1.1
below.
In the third and final part, a more thorough analysis will be conducted on
various portfolios from the model which performs the best with regards to ”goodness-
of-fit”. The analysis will be conducted by looking into both portfolio composition and

1.1.2.1
Limitations
The ETF and mutual fund datasets are provided with adjusted rates, meaning after
fees. This skews the results with the fees for ETFs being under 0.7% a year, while the
fees in the mutual fund data range from 1% to 2% a year. It should be noted that this
alleviates the problem of subtracting fees from the returns and therefore, all fees to the
investment funds and capital gains tax are ignored throughout this thesis.
The return series are all weekly compounded simple returns. Therefore, the model can
invest proportionally in the assets it sees fit, ignoring the real cost of each asset share.
The risk-free rate used throughout this thesis is based on returns for 3-month US trea-
sury bills. The majority of the ETFs track indexes in the US while the Danish mutual
funds operate in Denmark, which on average have negative interest rates. This skews
the picture of the performance with regards to Sharpe and Treynor ratio, as they are
calculated based on risk-free assets in the US.

1.3
Investment funds
At the beginning of this paper, it was stated that the first investment funds were intro-
duced in the late 18th century. Since then, investment funds have changed drastically,
although the concept of people pooling their assets together to reach advantages such
as economies of scale and hire professionals to handle their investments still prevails.
For this project’s scope, emphasis will be put on the two significant types of Investment
funds operating in the present-day financial market. These types of funds are mutual
funds and exchange-traded funds.

1.3.1
Mutual funds
Mutual funds are funds that offer investors the ability to diversify their investment, as
well as guidance from professional management at the cost of 1%-2% a year [9]. This is
of importance to investors as it offers greater efficiency in a transaction through profes-
sional management.
Mutual funds are open-end, professionally managed funds that pool together money to
be invested in financial assets, also known as securities. A mutual fund will state the
fund’s objective and attract investors wanting to invest following the fund’s policy [10].
Mutual funds were introduced at the end of the 19th century, although the funds in-
troduced differed significantly from the mutual funds we know today, as they were
close-ended funds with a fixed number of shares. The first open-ended mutual fund with
redeemable shares was introduced in 1924 in the US. The funds gained little popularity,
and due to the great depression and the Wall Street crash of 1929, strict regulations
were put in place, which limited the securities market, with the emphasis being put on
mutual funds. In the 1950s, they began to gain popularity, which has pushed them to
become the most popular type of investment fund in the world, holding 46.7 trillion
USD worth of assets by the end of 2018 [11].

1.3.2
Exchange traded funds
Exchange-traded funds (ETFs) are funds that aim to track certain indexes or baskets of
assets and are continuously traded on liquid markets. The majority of ETFs are struc-
tured like open-ended investment companies and aim to track an index. As mentioned
in section 1.3.1, mutual funds only allow trades at the end of the trading day, while
ETFs allow continuous trading throughout the trading day [12].
ETFs, combine the features of open-end and closed-end mutual funds. Like open-end
funds, they create the opportunity for creation and redemption of the shares in the fund,
while trading the shares on exchanges like in open-ended funds. Due to their low trans-

ETFs were initially developed in 1989 with the hope of selling shares to a fund capable
of tracking the S&P500. That fund was short-lived since lawsuits and the SEC’s tight

noticed by speculators in the US, which led to the development of ETFs in the US. Since

1.4
Virtual Financial Advisories
Wealth management has since its origin revolved around investors bringing their liquid
assets to private banks and financial wealth management institutions for guidance on
how to invest. Traditionally, an investment plan is created by the investor’s financial
institution, which revolves around the return the investor hopes to make and the levels
of risk needed to achieve a said return. This advisory form has usually been for investors
with substantial wealth (over 500.000€) and involves personal meetings with advisors

1.5
Tracking models
Tracking models are a form of portfolio management tool that aims to construct a port-
folio able to track a specific index benchmark. The objective is to minimize the tracking
error which occurs when investing in a few assets instead of investing proportionally in
all the assets which make up the index.
1.5.1
Regret models
One form of tracking models is the tracking model put forth by Dembo and King [17],
which aims to minimize the expected deviation of a random portfolio return from the
random target return while being constrained to reach a said return while doing so.
The model utilizes the concept of investor’s regret, the revenue lost because of the in-
vestor’s inability to predict the future. The model builds on the model put forth by
Markowitz. The model’s concept is to predict the outcome for investors with limited
funds who wish to invest in a few securities to track a target return, instead of having
to invest proportionally in the indexes which make up the target return.
This is a form of asset allocation framework built around the concept of ’downside risk’.
The model is linked to the concept of ’stochastic dominance’, whereby the goal is to
identify a set of few assets from a larger pool of assets, with that in mind to track a
target return.
Tracking models allow an extra degree of freedom, which, for the most significant part,
is the specification of the target return distribution. The investor can use this advantage
to build different scenarios to test the model on, and through stochastic programming
see how the model reacts to different scenarios which might arise in the world.
The concept of regret theory was put forth by Loomes and Sugden in 1982 [18]. The
theory builds on Kahnemans’ and Tverskys’ initial findings, which states that many
observable patterns of choice violate conventionally expected utility axioms. These pat-
terns are neither small scale, nor randomly distributed and, therefore, they must be
stipulated by the choice each investor has.
The theory goes that the investor aims to invest in a way that he may rejoice over
the outcome, and in the same manner, avoid having to regret his investment. They
introduced a model which takes several emotional factors into account, where the most
prominent is the investor’s anticipated regret.
By being able to take feelings into account, the model can give a clearer insight into
what drives investors, beyond what previous conventional economic analysis of choice
was able to explain. In this thesis, the regret model put forth by Dembo and King will
be analyzed, as implemented by Stavros A. Zenios [19].

2.1.1
Portfolio Selection
As explained by Markowitz, the process of selecting a portfolio may be divided down
into two steps.
1. Observation and experience which translates into beliefs about future performances
of available securities
2. The use of aforementioned beliefs to select a portfolio of securities
A general goal of investing is to maximize the discounted expected-, or anticipated re-
turns. The investor must consider contributing factors on the path to reach his goal.
That is to take into account the variance of the portfolio he believes is most viable, as
a high variance is undesirable. To comply with the aforementioned criteria, Markowitz
illustrates relations between beliefs and choice of the portfolio according to both the
expected returns, as well as the variance of the returns.
In a perfect market, it might seem suitable to maximize the discounted return. The mar-
ket is not perfect so the investor must look at other means such as seeking to diversify

the portfolio to get as close to the preferable return while alleviating the risk associated

The investor should diversify his funds among the securities, which give the maximum
expected return. The law of large numbers will ensure that the yield of the portfolio will
be almost the same as the expected yield, with much lower variance and associated risk.
The risk assumes that there is a portfolio which gives both maximum expected return

What this law fails to consider is that in order to maximize return, the investor must

works both ways as with lower variance, the expected return should also decrease. This
puts the investor in an equilibrium where he must choose the level of variance he is
willing to accept to reach a specific goal.
The spectrum of investment schemes and policies varies from ”risk-averse investors” such
as Defined Benefit pension funds (DB), which aim to minimize the variance and with
that solvency- and contribution- risk, to ”risk-seeking investors” such as private investors
who are willing to take on more risk in order to increase their expected return.

CHAPTER 4
Tracking models
In this chapter, the focus will be put on different tracking models, all based on the
concept of regret theory.
The models have the common goal to track an index by
generating a portfolio from another set of assets but differ in the constraints they are
bound by in order to achieve that goal. The datasets used to generate the portfolios
capable of tracking an index are ETF-, and mutual fund return datasets, and a dataset
comprising of both the ETF and the mutual fund data, named the Combined dataset.
The indexes from the IRD will be used as a benchmark. The models that will be explored
are the following, with references to them being stated in bold:
• Model 0: Minimization of the expected downside regret.
• Model 1: Minimization of the expected downside regret, without scalar.
• Model 2: Minimization of the expected downside regret and negative expected
return.
• Model 3: Minimization of the absolute deviation.
All the models are built on an ”ex-post” ideology. Which means that the model has
access to past historical data. This is done throughout the whole training period of

None of the portfolios which aimed to track the indexes of the IRD managed to do so.

certain assets from the training set will continue throughout the testing period. This
results in a model not capable of performing good tracks with the current training and

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
This chapter will cover reflections on the overall process of the research, optimization,
and analysis, which was conducted throughout this thesis. These reflections will be split
into two parts. The first part will cover the reflections of the work that has been per-
formed, while the second part will cover possible future work.
The ETF and mutual fund datasets are return series with adjusted rates, meaning after
fees to the funds. This means that the actual returns of the series are higher than those
depicted in the index returns. The reason why adjusted rates suited this project better
than the actual rates is that adjusted rates give a better insight into what the investor
gains from investing in the fund. The downside is that although a portfolio is capable
of tracking an index perfectly, the return series is adjusted to accommodate the various
fees associated with each fund. These fees vary like mentioned in chapters 1.3.1 and
1.3.2, but the fees associated with ETFs are usually much lower, which may indicate
why the mutual funds usually deliver a slightly worse track than the ETFs.
When the project started, the dataset covered the period from 09.2014-12.2019. With
the economic downswing which took place from February 2020 to April 2020, it was
interesting to include those months to see if the tracking portfolios exhibited similar be-
havior to the indexes they were tracking. The result was that all portfolios, both good
and bad with regards to ”goodness-of-fit” exhibited similar behavior as their benchmark.
The decision to add the periods in 2020 resulted in a Sharpe ratio around or below zero,
52.8% below zero. This is partially due to the decision to use the three-month treasury
bill yearly rate, but also underlines the severity of the economic downfall.
The split of the datasets between a training and testing set was done by having the train
set to cover the first 97 weeks, and the test set to cover 192 weeks. This split was chosen
because both the training and testing period exhibited spikes and drops throughout the
period, with a third of the set going towards the training period and the rest towards
the testing period. This resulted in a small training set for the model, while a broader
training set would have resulted in a more narrow window to test the results from the
tracking models.
The results from Model 0 were disappointing. The constraint that dictated that the port-
folio had to outperform a target return, not related to the actual benchmark, resulted
in the model not delivering a useful track of any sort. This may be the short period the
model was trained on since it chose the highest yielding assets in the training period.

The model did not take into consideration the volatility of the assets, which would have
been smart as many of them dived in the testing period.
Model 1 and Model 2 had similar results. Model 2, in all cases, provided equal or better

this is that the industry indexes follow a market segment based on a good or service,
while the market indexes are comprised of several ever-changing factors which make up
the index.
When backtesting the quality of the models, a ”dummy-index” was included in the
datasets. The index was the gold index from the index return dataset, and when the

from the ETF and mutual fund datasets with regards to tracking error. This underlines

6.1
Future work
Throughout this project, four models were implemented and analyzed to find the model
that produces the best portfolio from the datasets to track benchmark indexes, in terms
of ”goodness-of-fit”. These models yielded results that were then analyzed. The time
that the analysis took resulted in much room for future work on the models. This could
result in both an expansion of the project, and the analysis of the results. The files
utilized throughout this thesis can be found using this link.
In this thesis, the models were trained on a training set, and then their performance
was tested on a test set. An interesting expansion of the model would be to see if the
results would have been better if the training set would have covered a broader part of
the periods. ETFs began to gain popularity after the economic crisis of 2008, and since
then, the number of active ETFs, as well as the amounts invested in the ETFs have been
on the rise. It was not until 2014 when the amount of suitable ETFs was considered
sufficient for this project, so if this project were replicated in 10 years, it would leave
room for experimenting with more extensive training and test sets.
The models implemented throughout this thesis vary. Although similar, they have fac-
tors that set them apart from one another. Models 2 and 3 include scalars which dictate
the weight that is being put on the factors in the objective function. It would be inter-
esting to see if changing these constants, putting more weight into the maximum return
in model 2, or lowering the penalization for overperformance in model 3, would affect
the performance of the models, and then analyze the change.
The models are built around the idea that they are run on training datasets that pro-
duce portfolios that are tested on the testing set. If the model is to be used in tracking
market indexes with an ever-changing composition of assets which make up the index,
like the S&P500, it would be interesting to see the effects of re-balancing the portfolios
periodically, that is always giving the model access to historical data up until the mo-
ment the re-balancing happens.
Model 3 delivered the best results of the models. It was able to generate portfolios capa-
ble of tracking 54.44% of the indexes with regards to loose criteria and 20% with regards
to strict criteria. The ETF- and mutual fund datasets have adjusted rates, so there is
always a discrepancy in the actual returns and the returns in the dataset. This results
in a lag of the results equal to the weighted average fees of the funds, which make up
the tracking portfolio, compounded over the testing period. The results from how much
the tracking would change by considering this would give insight into the true tracking
capabilities of the models.
The number of assets in each of the generated portfolios varies from 3 to 48. The current
models add as many assets to the portfolio as possible to generate the best tracking port-

The models aim to build a portfolio of assets that are suitable for tracking a specific

would be interesting to turn this around and track the ETFs and mutual funds using
the index return dataset. The index return dataset is a collection of general indexes
giving insight into different geographic and industrial market segments, and the dataset
is comprised of weekly returns dating back to 1991, which gives good room for training
the dataset using e.g, machine learning.
As explained in chapter 5.1.4, many of the assets from the ETF, mutual fund, and IRD
dataset are in the vicinity of one another with regards to average return and standard
deviation. It would be interesting to look into clustering methods, similar to those de-
scribed in the thesis written by Auður Anna Jónsdóttir [44], as clustering could eliminate
assets from the portfolios not portraying similar characteristics as the benchmark index
they aim to track. As mentioned earlier in this chapter, this would allow the investor to
choose between the assets he wants to invest in, and reduce the computing power and
time needed for constructing a portfolio.
==PAGE:69==
CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This thesis’s objective was to see to what extent different types of investment funds
could be used to track an index return dataset using regret models and if the investor
should focus on one over the other when deciding on possible investments. This is done
by implementing several tracking models, all based on regret theory, and analyzing the
results from said tracking models.
At the beginning of the thesis, an index return dataset was gathered from Fama-French,
and an ETF return dataset was gathered from Alpha Vantage. Both the datasets were
supplied with extensive meta-data, which provided an opportunity for a deeper dive into
the datasets. In May, a mutual fund return dataset, with information on Danish mutual
fund returns, was gathered from Morningstar. The dataset lacked the meta-data needed
for a more thorough analysis of the assets in the dataset, so samples of the assets were
explored on Morningstar.
The datasets used for the analysis, the ETF and mutual fund return datasets, were
tested against the index return dataset, which was used as a benchmark. The optimiza-
tion took place in the roughly five year period, from 09.2014-04.2020. Initial analysis of
the datasets showed that the ETF dataset exhibited a higher mean yearly return and a
higher standard deviation than the other two. The IRD dataset had lower mean return
and standard deviation than the ETF dataset, while the mutual fund dataset had far
lower mean return and standard deviation than the other two. When the distribution of
the datasets, with regards to average return and the standard deviation, was analyzed,
it showed that the assets in the ETF dataset had the most extensive spread of assets.
The indexes in the index return dataset had a fair spread, while the mutual fund dataset
had the narrowest spread of the three, with most assets in the low average return and
standard deviation region. This gave insight into the characteristics of the datasets for
the optimization that was to take place.
The models tested throughout this thesis had a wide range of results. Model 0 showed
no capability to track the index return dataset using the ETF-, mutual fund-, and a
combined dataset, while models 1-3 showed prominent results. Further analysis of the
models showed that model 3 delivered the best results, the ability to generate a port-
folio capable of tracking the index return dataset, with regards to ”goodness-of-fit”. A
thorough analysis of model 3 showed that it was able to generate portfolios capable
of tracking 98/180 indexes with regards to loose criteria on the ”goodness-of-fit” while
tracking 36/180 indexes with regards to strict criteria of ”goodness-of-fit”. However,
==PAGE:70==
58
7 Conclusion
none of the models was able to generate portfolios capable of tracking the bond data
indexes, as a result of them portraying very low returns and standard deviation. An
analysis of the composition of the portfolios generated, which resulted in a good track,
showed that the model is picking up characteristics from the benchmark index and uti-
lizes assets from the datasets showcasing the same characteristics to create a portfolio
capable of tracking the benchmark index. These portfolios were all comprised of 3 or
more assets which shows that not one, but rather a collection of assets were able to track
the benchmark index.
The results from this analysis show that the regret models are capable of tracking an
index return dataset using datasets comprising ETF-, and mutual fund return series.
The extent of this tracking is roughly 54.44%, with regards to loose criteria, while it is
capable of tracking 20%, adhering to strict criteria. The results also show that the ETF
dataset performs better when tracking indexes with medium to high return and standard
deviation, while the mutual fund dataset performs better when tracking indexes with
low return and standard deviation. Therefore, the results confirm speculations which
arose in the preliminary analysis, that mutual funds are a more suitable investment for
risk-averse investors, while the ETFs are more suitable for more risk-seeking investors.
