Abstract
Mixing of fluids is an important aspect of many industrial applications in the world. To
develop and optimize any mixing application a detailed knowledge about the process
can be trivial. Using computational fluid dynamics (CFD) to predict the mixing, has been
proved to provide detailed knowledge and data about mixing. However using CFD can
end up being a costly procedure measured in required computational resources.
This thesis aims to describe the mixing of the two gasses, air and methane in a cost-
effective manner, where a trade-off between computational resources and accuracy against
experimental results are addressed. The mixing is studied in a pipe flow from two indus-
trial relevant geometries, namely a straight pipe and a pipe with an elbow bend. Two
different meshes approaches are carried out, where one is based on operational settings
and the other on numerical data. The operational meshes is created from a set ratio of
the pipe diameter and the numerical parameter meshes is entirely based on the turbu-
lent scales found in the flow. In total 3 different operational mesh were created, where
the base size was set to respectively D/10, D/20 and D/40. Two different numerical mesh
were created from a set condition to resolve the turbulent integral length scale using either
12 or 6 cells. Both RANS and DES simulation models were studied and for the straight
pipe, the RANS solutions showed better agreement with experimental results. However,
as the robustness was evaluated on the bend geometry, the DES solution with numeri-
cal data mesh showed good consistency with experiments. The RANS solutions showed
poor agreement with experiments.

Introduction
Mixing of fluids is a process widely used in many aspects in modern industry. The prin-
ciples of mixing are applied in various industrial aspects such as, chemical plants, gas
turbines, internal combustion engines, oil refineries etc. Throughout the years, mixing
processes have been investigated both experimentally and numerically in order to gain
more insight into the mixing mechanism and thereby improve any industrial application.
The definition of mixing is outlined from Paul et al. (2004), who defines mixing as a re-
duction in inhomogeneity in order to accomplish a desired process results. The term in-
homogeneity can refer to concentration, phase or temperature. From the mixing process
several secondary effects occurs i.e. mass transfer and reaction and product properties.
For any process design that involves mixing, the mixing process itself can be very critical
to the quality of the outcome. When any mixing problem arises, a good understanding of
the mixing process is crucial in order to solve those problems.
One of the most modern generations of analyzing mixing principles is the use of Compu-
tational Fluid Dynamics (CFD). The major advantages of CFD compared to experimental
analysis is the eliminated need for expensive experimental setups, such as laboratories,
equipment, manual labor etc. With the increasing availability of computer processing
power, another advantage of CFD is the rapid processing time wished by many industries
in the modern world.
1.1
Project Motivation
The motivation for this project is a combined effort between research objectives and indus-
try needs with the main focus of predicting mixing using CFD. From a research perspective
different numerical turbulence models will be investigated in various parametric studies
and their performance evaluated individually.
From an industrial perspective the primary focus is targeted towards cost-beneficial so-
lutions. As touched on earlier the industry calls for reliable solutions within a limited time
budget. The present work seeks to combine the best of both worlds, where several para-
metric studies will be evaluated to find any acceptable solution using minimum computa-
tional power.
1.1.1

From Gotfredsen et al. (2020) several numerical investigations were carried out and well

2.1
Briefly about Turbulent Mixing
The mixing of fluids in a pipe flow has been expressed by H. Schlichting (1949), who
describes the famous pipe flow experiment originally conducted by Reynolds in 1883
(Osborne Reynolds 1883). The experiment detects the laminar to turbulent transition,
by injecting a color dye into a pipe flow at different velocities. It was found that for high
Reynolds numbers a traverse mixing took place, where irregular velocities in radial and
longitudinal direction emerged.
Osborne Reynolds (1883) observed from a snapshot of the flow at high Reynolds number,
that the color trace was transformed into a swirling motion of eddies. It was found that
these eddies entered at a critical velocity and from there increased with the velocity.
From Paul et al. (2004) several mechanisms causing mixing are presented. For the sake
of simplicity these are listed in table 2.1.

One of the most challenging flow phenomena yet to be understood is turbulence and
throughout the years the definition of turbulence has been iteratively updated.
From
Wilcox (2006) turbulence is among other things defined as an irregular condition where
various quantities reveal random variation in time and space coordinates. These quanti-
ties could e.g. be velocity or pressure.

lengths and frequencies.

3.2
Computational Fluid Dynamics
As the whole foundation of this project is based on CFD, the following sections aims to
briefly describe the relevant elements of CFD treated in the present work.
Predicting turbulence quantities using CFD, comes with different options applicable for
any specific flow case. One option is to use a so-called simulation model, which attempts
to direct the turbulence itself and resolve all turbulent scales down to the aforementioned
Kolmogorov length scale. This model is called Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) and
requires extraordinary computational resources and therefore primarily used in research
purposes including simple geometries and low Reynolds number flows. Other simula-
tion models exists including Large Eddy Simulation (LES) and Detached Eddy Simulation
(DES), which will be described later on.
Another more common approach, is the use of Turbulence-energy equation models de-
scribed in Mutlu Sumer (2013), where the turbulence is modelled using the Reynolds
Average Navier-Stokes (RANS) Equations including the closure problem of turbulence.

3.2.3
Simulation Models
Another approach in this thesis is the use of the Detached Eddy Simulation (DES) model
originally proposed by Spalart in 1997 (Philippe R. Spalart 2009). The initial motivation of
DES is high Reynolds numbers or massively separated flows. Briefly described the DES
model is a combination of the RANS and Large Eddy Simulation (LES) model, where a
part of the boundary layer is treated with RANS and regions of massive separation treated
with LES.
LES and DES are often referred to as scale-resolving models, meaning that large turbulent
scales are resolved and small scales are modelled.
Large Eddy Simulation
In a LES model, turbulent scales down to a lower grid limit are resolved in space in the
whole flow domain. Scales smaller than this grid limit is modelled using a sub-grid scale
model. Various subgrid scale models exists including the Smagorinsky and WALE mod-
els. Common for the two subgrid scale models is the aim to model the subgrid scale
viscosity µt from the Boussinesq approximation.
As LES tends to resolve turbulent structures in the whole domain, flow near the wall can
be a critical aspect. Spatial resolving the boundary layer calls for an excessively number
of cells in both normal and parallel direction of the flow.
Detached Eddy Simulation
The DES model aims to solve the expense of resolving the boundary layer discussed
above. Using a pure RANS model has its limitations in predicting large separation regions
and a disadvantage is the lack of unsteady information lost due to mean operation in RANS
(Philippe R. Spalart 2009).
The type of flow presented in this thesis is wall-bounded turbulent flows, where the fea-
tures of DES has been favorized (Han et al. 2018). The boundary layer in such type of
flows can be divided into two regions. An inner layer containing small-scale structures and
an outer later containing large scale structures. Resolving the small-scale structures re-
quires a large amount of computational resources and demonstrates the use of modelling
the inner layer and resolve the outer layer.

Delayed-Detached Eddy Simulation
A disadvantage of the DES model is the switch between RANS and LES causing inap-
propriate behavior of the model.
Briefly described, one problem arises when the grid spacing parallel to the wall is lowered
below the boundary layer thickness causing the DES model to apply LES behavior in the
boundary layer and thus lowering the eddy viscosity. However modelled eddy viscosity
(from RANS) has not been replaced by the resolved eddy viscosity (from the LES model)
which causes the inappropriate behavior. The LES content is typically poor due to lack of
grid resolution in the boundary layer. This problem is typically referred to as Grid Induced
Separation (GIS) (P. R. Spalart et al. 2006).
To overcome this problem a near wall-treatment is applied to the original DES model cre-
ating the updated Delayed-DES model. The idea of the DDES model is to shield the
DES principle and only apply RANS behaviour in the boundary layer. The feature is ap-
plied through a shielding function dependent on the eddy-viscosity and the grid (Siemens
2019).

3.2.5
Temporal Discretization
The major part of simulations conducted in this thesis is done using an implicit unsteady
approach to account for transient effects. The implicit unsteady solver enables discretiza-
tion of the governing equations in time and can be implemented with a 1st or 2nd order
temporal scheme.
A 1st order temporal discretization is usually found to have best convergence rate but less
accurate due to an increase in numerical diffusion caused by the misalignment between
grid and flow direction (Karadimou and Markatos 2018).
For the transient simulations a time step is set prior to every run. Setting a time step is a
trade-off between computational resources and discretization errors. The time step can
be chosen based on the convective Courant number given as
C = ∆tu
∆x
(3.13)
Where u is the local velocity, x the local cell size and ∆t the time step. The Courant
number can be interpreted as the time one particle stays in one particular cell in the flow
domain. Having a Courant number below one means that a particle always moves from
one cell to an adjacent cell during at least one time step.
3.2.6

The spatial discretization is taken care of using a computational grid in the full domain.
The grid is referred to as a mesh and throughout this thesis a volume and surface mesh

this thesis a mesh with polyhedral-shaped cells is used except the boundary layer which
is meshed using a prism layer mesher with prismatic cells.

which is essential for simulation models like LES and DES (Rodi et al. 2005).

Method
This chapter aims to describe the methods used to study the mixing of air and methane
in a pipe flow. The study is entirely based on CFD calculations where different attempts
of numerical adjustments have been done in order to tweak numerical results towards
experimental results.
From the beginning of this thesis no strict simulation project procedure has been outlined.
The desired outcome of this project is a set of well defined operational numerical param-
eters to use when conducting a numerical study of gas mixing. These parameters are
defined based on a continuously exploration of numerical settings, which made a strict
project plan difficult to execute. The following sections are therefore dedicated to the
different adjustments done in the numerical study.
The chapter begins with a description of the fixed settings used throughout the project.
This includes the geometry used in the CFD model and some settings in the spatial dis-
cretization of the geometry. The boundary conditions and physics needed for the numer-
ical model is then described.
Then a simulations strategy is outlined, which describes the investigation of different nu-
merical adjustments. This includes different turbulence models, physics settings, mesh
settings and geometry adjustments all described in the following.
Finally the chapter ends with a description of the numerical post-processing and the pro-
cedure of running simulations at HPC.
4.1
Geometry
The geometry representing the CFD domain is illustrated in fig. 4.1. The geometry con-
sisted of a straight D = 200 mm pipe with a length of 30D. At the inlet a hemisphere was
created to mimic the effect of a vena contracta created by the sharp edged inlet found
in the experiments. At a distance of 10D from the inlet a small pipe was located for the
injection of methane. The pipe had a outer diameter of 6mm and an inner diameter of 4
mm. The methane pipe outlet was concentric with the large pipe. The methane pipe was
created as a solid tube and the inner hollow geometry was created for the last 4 inner pipe
diameters. This length was deemed to be acceptable for representing a correct physical
flow behaviour following Gotfredsen et al. (2020).

further changes in the prism layer mesh were done.
4.3
Boundary Conditions
Boundary conditions were applied to the walls, the air inlet and outlet and the methane
inlet. Due to strong viscous effects close to the wall a no-slip boundary condition was
applied, which created zero velocity at the wall.
From the experiments, ambient air was sucked through the pipe from a fan mounted to
the pipe outlet. This effect was transferred to the numerical model by use of a pressure

outlet boundary condition where a gauge pressure of 0 Pa was set.
The air inlet was applied with a mass flow boundary condition to make sure an evenly and
constant mass flow of air was applied to the inlet surface. The mass flow was determined
based on the volumetric flow rate and density of the surrounding air.
For the methane injection a velocity inlet boundary condition was used. The velocity was
set to match the volumetric flow rate of 500 ml/min described in Gotfredsen et al. (2020).
A turbulent viscosity ratio of 10 and a turbulent intensity of 1% was applied to the inlet,
the outlet and the methane inlet. However these values were deemed less important as
the turbulence was mainly generated from the hemisphere.

4.5
Measurement Plan
The following section describes the overall measurement plan and how the individual
simulation differs from each other.
As stated in section 1.1, the overall goal was to find a trade off between accuracy and
computational cost. A huge effort in this project has been put in the spatial discretization
to find a solution containing a minimum number of cells while maintaining an acceptable
accuracy.
The measurement plan begins with an investigation of the RANS solutions presented in
Gotfredsen et al. (2020). Several different approaches are presented and their individual
influence evaluated. The section continues with the use of DES, where mainly two differ-
ent mesh topology methods are presented. For the DES simulations a mesh sensitivity
study was conducted, where the mesh was either based on numerical data or operational
parameters. Manually creating different areas of mesh refinements was avoided and the
theoretical practice of this has been outlined in section 3.2.7. For practical purposes,
manually created mesh refinement areas, were avoided due to the following two reasons.
One is, that similar work has already been done in the article of Gotfredsen et al. (2020)
and ended up using 69 × 106 cells. A goal was to lower this number significantly without
compromising accuracy.
Another reason is the desired output as a set of operational parameters described in sec-
tion 1.1.3. The operational parameters should be independent of the specific geometry
analyzed and thus a traditional mesh sensitivity study with manually defined area refine-
ments should as far as possible be avoided.
The robustness of both RANS and DES will be evaluated on the ’Bend’ geometry seen in
fig. 2.1.

research perspective, the time step was kept constant and no further sensitivity study of
the time step was done.

4.6
Gathering of Data
Following the experiments of Gotfredsen (2018) data were gathered at distances 15D and
20D from the air inlet. Both vertical and horizontal direction at both distances. Instead
of using a line probe with a fixed resolution, an intersection of 2 planes were used. This
allowed one data point in every cell throughout the radial direction. A XYZ table of relevant
data was created in Star-CCM+ and the location of data was gathered as Centroid[x,y].
This allowed a measurement location at the center of every cell. For the implicit unsteady
simulations, a table was exported for every 10th time step to avoid overflow of files.
4.6.1
Post-Processing of Data
As the majority of the simulations were conducted using an implicit unsteady scheme, any
output data required a thorough process to obtain time averaged parameters. This was
done to make sure any results presented had no inaccuracy caused by an insufficient time
averaging. To eliminate any influence of the initial conditions, at least two flow-through
times were solved before any post-processing were done. Although one may argue to use
more than two flow-through times, this number was accepted due to all DES simulations
were initiated using a steady state RANS followed by an unsteady RANS before switching
to DES. Similarly all unsteady RANS simulations were initiated from a steady state RANS.
The convergence in every simulation was evaluated from a continuous monitor plot of
the velocity and concentration at several points through the pipe. The switch in temporal
scheme and simulation model was done by a macro script to avoid extra queue time at
the HPC. The macro script can be illustrated from the sketch in fig. 4.8. To enable full
control of the time average operations, all post-processing were done in MATLAB from
the exported tables.
The time average was done for a time series corresponding to at least two flow through-
times. The DES simulations created large turbulent fluctuations and to make sure the
simulation time was satisfactory, the RMS error of the mean velocity was studied. Using
eq. (3.18), the RMS error was repeatedly calculated from the mean velocity for an increas-
ing number of time steps. The convergence of the RMS error was used as a guidance to
validate the simulation time.

of large computational resources was necessary. The HPC used in this thesis was the
Nifflheim Linux cluster installed at DTU. The cluster consist of several different node par-
titions with different CPU architecture. Number of CPU cores available are 8, 16, 24 and
40 cores.
To reduce the computational time several nodes were used simultaneously by distributed
memory parallelization. However retaining multiple nodes, would often increase the queen-

Discussion
This chapter aims to discuss the results presented in the previous chapter. The discus-
sion is targeted towards the RANS and DES simulations conducted on the different mesh
types. The study of the bend geometry will also be discussed.
6.1
RANS and DES on the Straight Geometry
The velocity and concentration profiles from the RANS and DES solutions are evaluated
against experimental results in a cost-beneficial manner. Using eq. (3.18) the RMS error
of the difference between the numerical and experimental results can be calculated. To
obtain the difference in the two data sets, the numerical results are linear interpolated to
the positions of the experimental results. The RMS errors are plotted in a bar plot for the
5 different mesh types for the two solutions RANS and DES. The plots are only created
for 15D horizontal direction and are depicted in fig. 6.1. Evaluating the plots in fig. 6.1
the solutions from the RANS model show best agreement against experimental results.
However the results of DES with l0/6 and l0/12 mesh show promising results and by
looking at the methane scalar scenes (appendix A) these two mesh types are the only
ones capable of initiating the characteristic DES behaviour.
Looking at the methane scalar scene for the D/40 mesh, a slightly DES behaviour is
observed. This could indicate that the solution is a mix of both RANS and DES behaviour
and explains the large RMS error seen in fig. 6.1. The solutions of D/10 and D/20 are
believed to only accommodate RANS behaviour, as the methane scalar scenes for the
DES solutions (appendix A) were analogous to the corresponding RANS solutions.
Looking at the velocity profiles from the DES solutions fig. 5.23, all D-mesh approaches
overpredict the velocity at the center of the pipe.
Based on these findings, the operational mesh parameter D needs to be further reduced
to create a mesh, refined enough to accommodate similar DES behavior as seen e.g. the
l0/12.
6.2
RANS and DES on the Bend Geometry
The RANS and DES solutions are evaluated against the experimental results, in the same
manner as presented in section 6.1. The bar plots are illustrated in fig. 6.2. By comparing
fig. 6.1 and fig. 6.2, it is clear that the robustness of the D-mesh approach is lost when
the geometry is changed. As the desired outcome of this thesis are a set of operational
parameters, the use of a D-mesh approach presented here should be avoided, due to a
weak robustness against a change in geometry.
As mentioned before the concentration profiles predicted by the RANS model were un-
usually false compared to the experimental profiles. It was mentioned that this behaviour
could be caused by secondary flow structures existing for too long time in a RANS solution.
This misbehaviour could also be explained by poor modelling of turbulent momentum dif-
fusion, related to the turbulent viscosity. Combining an overprediction of secondary flow
structures by an underpredicted turbulence modelling cause poor concentration profiles
in a bend geometry. A similar behaviour was seen for the D/20-mesh DES solution, due
to the spatial discretization demanding full RANS behaviour.

6.3
Closing Remarks on the Mesh Generation Strategy
The use of a D-mesh approach created reasonable results on the straight pipe with a
RANS solver. However no convergence was observed and when switching to a DES
solver, the accuracy was poor as the mesh size decreased towards D/40. As mentioned
previously, this could be due to the DES solver starting to create real DES behaviour and
not only RANS behaviour as it was believed to happen for the coarse mesh.
The finest mesh D/40 failed to produce entirely DES behaviour similar to the solutions
from l0/12 and l0/6. It is expected that more DES behaviour is seen as the mesh is
further refined e.g. to a D/60 mesh. However this has not been investigated as the mesh
strategy based on the turbulent integral length scale showed promising results. Using
e.g. a D/60 or a D/80 mesh the total number of cells would also rapidly increase towards
similar values as seen for the l0/6 mesh.
One advantage of using a l0 mesh strategy is the intelligent mesh topology, where different
areas are refined based on the flow physics. This is e.g. seen in fig. 5.14, where the areas
around the methane pipe and the hemisphere inlet has been refined. These areas have
been proven important as transient behaviour and flow reattachment zones are seen here.
One disadvantage of the l0 mesh approach is the need of an initial RANS solution which
also requires an initial mesh. However as seen in this thesis, an acceptable accuracy
could be obtained from a coarse initial mesh. Using a coarse initial mesh also ensured a
rapid computational time for the RANS solution.
The l0 mesh uses a manually controlled prism layer mesh in the boundary layer. It was
found that the prism layer mesh was responsible for a large share of the total number
of cells within the domain. The importance of the prism layer mesh was not studied and
remains unclear. A certain interest arises to lower the cost of the prism layer mesh while
still maintaining a sufficient accuracy.
From previous experiments in Gotfredsen et al. (2020), it was found that the value of the
turbulent Schmidt number Sct was strongly related to the mixing. In this thesis, this value
was kept constant, as its influence on the mixing was difficult to anticipate. With limited
time left, no simulations were conducted to study different Sct numbers. The main focus
in this thesis was instead to find a cost-beneficial solution. A proposal is given as future
work stated in chapter 8.

Conclusion
Seen from an industrial perspective the overall goal in this thesis has been predicting
mixing using CFD with the best trade-off between computational cost and accuracy. The
mixing of air and methane in a pipe flow was used as an example for the analysis’.
The study of mixing has previously been investigated both from experiments and numer-
ical studies. The analysis’ were done on three different industrial relevant geometries.
From the experiments, a set of measurement data were available. This included the
methane concentration, axial velocity and velocity rms at different locations and direc-
tions in the pipe. The previous numerical analysis’ attempted to predict the mixing using
the RANS model and DES model. Several numerical settings and their influence of mixing
were presented.
This thesis started of by investigating the RANS solutions presented in the previous work.
A mesh topology analogous to the one used in the previous work was created and two
different turbulence model were studied. The mesh topology used 32.5 × 106 cells. Along
with the turbulence models, the use of gravity, boundary layer discretization and temporal
scheme were studied. From these studies the k −ϵ turbulence model was deemed most
trustworthy along with a second order temporal scheme. Gravity was found to create an
effect and was enabled in all simulations.
The study was continued with the main goal to investigate the DES model and lower
the computational cost while simultaneously study the accuracy. The primary focus was
targeted towards the spatial discretization of the fluid domain. Overall two different mesh
approaches where carried out, where one was based on an operational parameter and
the other on numerical data. The operational parameter mesh was created where the
mesh base size was derived from a fraction of the pipe diameter. The base sizes D/10,
D/20 and D/40, were used. The numerical created mesh was derived from the turbulent
integral length scale, where one turbulent integral length scale was resolved using either
12 or 6 cells.
All mesh approaches were used with both a RANS and DES model to predict the mixing.
The accuracy against experiments of each individual solution was evaluated from the
RMS error for both velocity and methane concentration. In the straight pipe, the RANS
solutions showed best prediction of mixing where the l0/12 and l0/6 mesh had the lowest
RMS error. The D-mesh showed poor convergence and therefore difficult to trust the
results.
Finally the robustness against a change in geometry was studied. With limited time left
only the D/20 and l0/6 mesh were used with a RANS and DES model. The RANS so-
lutions and the D/20 DES solution showed poor consistency with the experiments. Sec-
ondary flow structures and poor turbulence modelling were believed to cause this misbe-
haviour. On the other hand the l0/6 DES solution showed good consistency with exper-
iments and the solution was able to maintain similar RMS errors as for the straight pipe
case.

Future Work
The studies presented in this thesis touched some areas within predicting mixing using
CFD. However there are still more work and studies to conduct. This chapter will briefly
list several points where more work could be done.
From the article Gotfredsen et al. (2020), a geometry including a mixer plate was studied.
This geometry has not been investigated in this thesis and should be examined in the
same manner as the Straight and Bend geometry.
Regarding the different mesh approaches, the D-mesh approach could be investigated
further where the ratio can be lowered to create a finer mesh. By gradually refining the
mesh, the methane scalar scenes can be observed to find the point of fully DES behaviour
similar to e.g. seen in the l0/12 solution.
The use of turbulence model for the DES model should be investigated. In this thesis the
k −ϵ model was used, however it could be interesting to study the performance of the
k −ω model.
An essential thing to study is the turbulent Schmidt number strongly related to the mixing
behaviour. Several different Schmidt numbers could be tested and then evaluate their
individual impact on the mixing. An operational procedure could be outlined, where the
turbulent Schmidt number could be estimated from the flow physics. For inspiration the
reader is referred to Gualtieri et al. (2017).
Finally the boundary layer discretization could be investigated. It was found that the prism
layer mesh was responsible for a large share of the total number of cell. It is believed that
the number of prism layers can be reduced as the mixing primarily occurs in the center of
the pipe.
