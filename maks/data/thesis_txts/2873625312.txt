Abstract
This thesis investigates the impact dynamics in magnetically levitated Flywheel Energy
Storage Systems (FESS), focusing on the physical contact events between a high­speed
rotor and its backup (touchdown) bearings. Unlike prior studies that emphasized control
design and rig development, this work centers on reconstructing and analyzing unmea­
sured contact forces through indirect measurements and signal processing.
A combination of theoretical modeling, experimental testing, and numerical signal analysis
was employed. The FESS test rig at the Technical University of Denmark provided a
controlled environment where the flywheel was tested up to 2250 RPM and subjected
to repeated perturbations. Since direct force sensors were absent, rotor dynamics were
inferred using position and acceleration measurements, which were refined using Fourier­
based signal processing and Butterworth filtering to overcome sensor noise and data
saturation issues.
A mathematical model incorporating linearized active and passive magnetic bearing forces,
as well as contact forces, was developed. Impact cases were categorized, and equilib­
rium equations were solved under various contact conditions. Signal bins corresponding
to repeated impacts were statistically aligned and averaged using correlation techniques,
resulting in a composite signal used to estimate impact forces.
The results indicate that even with limited sensor fidelity, impact forces can be reasonably
estimated and show logical trends with increasing rotor speed. Peak contact forces were
found to be consistent with physical expectations and previous literature, albeit with limi­
tations due to sensor drift and saturation. This work lays the foundation for future stress
estimation and durability modeling in FESS applications.

Introduction
The shift toward electrification, accelerated by the expansion of smart power grids and
the rapid adoption of electric vehicles, has intensified the need for reliable, large­scale
energy storage. While lithium­ion batteries dominate today’s grids and drivetrains, their
chemical nature raises concerns about raw­material supply, ageing, and end­of­life recy­
cling. Mechanical storage offers an attractive complementary path. In a Flywheel Energy
Storage System (FESS), electricity is converted to the kinetic energy of a rapidly spinning
rotor and later recovered on demand. Because energy scales with the square of rota­
tional speed, maximising rotor RPM is crucial; yet conventional rolling bearings introduce
prohibitive friction and wear at high speed.
Magnetic levitation circumvents this limitation. Active magnetic bearings (AMBs) suspend
the rotor without contact, while a passive magnetic thrust bearing (PMB) balances axial
load. Combined with vacuum operation and feedback control, levitation enables rotational
speeds far beyond what lubricated bearings can sustain. The price of this freedom is
complexity. Rotor–bearing stability, backup­bearing touch­down dynamics, and power­
electronic control must all be understood and engineered as an integrated whole.
Santos and co­workers have developed a modular FESS test rig at the Technical Uni­
versity of Denmark to explore these issues. Dagnæs­Hansen delivered the original de­
sign and multiphysics model [1]. Dallabona [2] added a bidirectional motor­generator
set for controlled spin­up and regenerative braking, and Villefrance [3] refined the AMB
controllers to reduce electrical losses and improve stability. Building on this foundation,
the present thesis turns the spotlight onto the short but violent contact episodes that oc­
cur when the magnetically levitated rotor strikes its touchdown bearings. By combining
indirect measurements with signal­processing and dynamic modelling, we aim to recon­
struct the transient forces involved, assess their dependence on rotor speed, and lay the
groundwork for fatigue and life­time predictions of future transport­grade FESS units.

1.1
Research Questions
This work investigates the dynamic interaction between a magnetically levitated flywheel
and its touchdown (backup) bearings during impact events. In contrast to previous work
that primarily focused on system modeling, control development, and test rig construction,
the focus here is on estimating the unmeasured contact forces that arise when the rotor
physically contacts the mechanical supports.
Since the test rig does not include force sensors to measure the force at the contact points
directly, this investigation explores how to reconstruct the underlying impact dynamics
using indirect measurements, such as housing acceleration, rotor position, AMB control
current, and actuator timing.
The central research questions are:
• How can impact events between the rotor and backup bearings be identified and
isolated from sensor data?
• What signal processing methods enable reliable rotor velocity and acceleration es­
timation from potentially noisy position measurements?
• How can contact forces be inferred from measurable quantities using analytical mod­
els or data­driven approximations?
• How does rotor speed influence the magnitude, duration, and frequency content of
impact forces?
Answering these questions contributes to understanding magnetically levitated rotating
systems’ mechanical limits and failure modes, with broader implications for safety, design
robustness, and condition monitoring and maintenance of FESS.
1.2
Original contribution
This work’s primary contribution is the development of a signal processing package ca­
pable of reconstructing sensor data, enabling the extrapolation of the FESS’s dynamic
characteristics. Particular emphasis was placed on position data, which was further pro­
cessed to estimate velocities and accelerations.
Correlation techniques have been used to turn a series of experiments into statistical data,
which can be used in conjunction with a mathematical model of the FESS.
The data processing and mathematical model combined ultimately allowed us to estimate
impact forces between the flywheel and the backup bearing with relatively little sensor
data.

Test Rig
The Department of Mechanical Engineering at DTU has developed a flywheel energy
storage system (FESS) test rig. Originally constructed as part of a research project on
offshore power buffering[1], the rig integrates magnetic bearings, a gimballed housing,
and various sensors for capturing system dynamics under controlled disturbances.
This chapter provides a brief technical overview of the system’s components and instru­
mentation, focusing on the elements relevant to data acquisition and signal processing.
The components can be identified in Figure 2.1 and Figure 2.2.
2.1
Mechanical Structure
Figure 2.1: Picture of the testrig.
1 FESS (Housing), 2 Gimbal, 3 Frame, 4 Pneu­

Foundation and Frame: The rig is mounted on a heavy steel base to provide structural
rigidity. The upper structure is built from aluminum extrusions for modularity and stiffness.
A passive gimbal interface connects the main housing to the frame, allowing controlled
tilt under external perturbations, which are to be described.
Housing: The housing contains the flywheel, two radial active magnetic bearings (AMBs),
a passive magnetic thrust bearing (PMB), backup bearings, and internal sensors. It is
designed for high­speed operation with minimal friction and constitutes the FESS and
flywheel.
Flywheel: The flywheel is a high­inertia rotor supported magnetically in all directions by
the AMBs and the PMB. It is driven by a permanent magnet synchronous motor (PMSM)
via a central shaft and can reach rotational speeds up to 30,000 RPM.
Thrust Bearing: A passive magnetic bearing (PMB) composed of axially aligned perma­
nent magnet rings provides axial support and levitation without active control. The PMB
contributes with negative radial stiffness, pushing the flywheel out of center at the bottom.
Active Magnetic Bearings (AMBs): Two heteropolar, eight­pole AMBs control radial po­
sition using electromagnets and position feedback. These bearings eliminate mechanical
contact and enable precise rotor centering, mitigating the PMB’s negative stiffness.
Backup Bearings: Backup bearings (Touchdown bearings) with radial clearance ( 300
µm) protect the system during power loss or instability events by blocking the flywheel
when it moves uncontrollably towards the AMBs, thereby absorbing the impact from the
flywheel.
Pneumatic Actuator: Mounted between the frame and foundation, the actuator induces
dynamic tilts to simulate external perturbations and generate controlled impacts between
the rotor and backup bearings. Figure 2.1 shows the axes of the movement in the test rig.
The green arrow marks the positive y­direction, and the red arrow marks the positive x­
direction. The actuator tips the frame and thereby the FESS around an axis at the bottom
parallel to the x­axis, yielding a motion that follows a circular path in the same plane as
the y­axis.

2.2
Electronic and Control System
Control Unit: A National Instruments cRIO real­time controller with an FPGA manages
the AMB control loops. Outer PID or LQG loops regulate rotor position, while inner PI
loops control coil current.
Power Electronics: PWM amplifiers drive the AMB electromagnets.
A variable fre­
quency drive (VFD) controls the PMSM, enabling acceleration and regenerative braking
via a resistor bank.
Sensors:
• Position Sensors: Four eddy­current sensors (ECS) measure radial displacement
at the top and bottom of the rotor in both x and y directions.
• Accelerometer: An accelerometer is mounted on the housing at a 45° angle relative
to the tilt direction; it logs the acceleration of the housing in the x and y directions,
which are shown in Figure 2.1.
• PWM Signal Logging: The drive signal to the pneumatic actuator is recorded to
identify the timing and severity of perturbations.
• AMB current logging: The Current drawn by the AMBs is logged to allow calcula­
tion of the magnetic force.
• Control current: The current that the controller feeds to the AMBs is logged.
2.3
Data Collection
Experimental tests were conducted at incrementally increasing rotational speeds, with the
first speed, 0 rpm, acting as a benchmark. The flywheel was accelerated to 500 RPM and
then in steps of 250 RPM up to a maximum of 2250 RPM. At each speed level, the same
set of impact tests was performed by actuating the pneumatic system to tilt the housing
and induce contact events. Because of how the pneumatic actuator is set up, the contact
events would be most severe in the positive y­direction.
The flywheel was accelerated and maintained at each target speed using the PMSM and
VFD system, while the AMBs maintained levitation and position control. The pneumatic
actuator was then triggered via PWM to apply lateral perturbations, resulting in measur­
able impacts between the rotor and the backup bearings. Each perturbation test lasted
90 seconds, resulting in 8 to 9 perturbations, depending on where the rig ends up at the
end of each cycle. The inconsistent number of perturbations was not a problem because,
as will be seen later, the data would have to be trimmed.

3
Mathematical model of FESS
It is important to have a good and well­defined model of the system to investigate the
interaction between the rotor and housing during an impact. The system will be modeled
by sets of linear differential equations.
While the best model will include all the dynamics of the system, some simplifications
must be made. The following assumptions are made.
• Aerodynamics of the rotor is negligible.
• The rotor is a rigid object, and thus has only six DOFs. Three translational and three
rotational.
• The gimbal, which holds the house, is stationary.
• The temperature and its effects on the system are neglected.

Signal Processing Theory
In this thesis, rotor velocity and acceleration are not measured directly; instead, they are
estimated from position signals through differentiation. Since the position data will con­
tain noise, it is necessary to process it thoroughly if the derivatives are to be of sufficient
fidelity to form the base of subsequent calculations of the system dynamics. All of the
processing was done in MATLAB, therefore, many of the considerations regarding pro­
cessing revolved around MATLAB’s capabilities. Two approaches were considered, the
first and simplest being direct differentiation using MATLAB’s native finite difference tool
to approximate the derivative. The second, and more complex, is Fourier­based differen­
tiation, which would involve developing a special script to calculate the derivatives.

4.2
Numerical Differentiation in MATLAB
MATLAB provides several built­in methods for computing numerical derivatives, with the
most commonly used being the diff and gradient functions. The diff function com­
putes finite differences between adjacent elements of a vector, effectively applying a for­
ward difference scheme, while gradient offers a central difference approach that yields
more accurate estimates for internal data points.
• Forward difference:
f′(xi) ≈f(xi+1) −f(xi)
∆x
(4.1)
• Central difference:
f′(xi) ≈f(xi+1) −f(xi−1)
2∆x
(4.2)
Where f is the sensor data and ∆x is the time step, which is equal to the inverse of the
sampling rate.
The central difference scheme generally offers higher accuracy (second­order) compared
to the forward and backward difference schemes (first­order), assuming uniform spacing
and smooth data. However, all methods are sensitive to noise in f(x), as differentiation
tends to amplify high­frequency components.
Impact Dynamics in Flywheel Systems
25
==PAGE:34==
These functions are attractive due to their simplicity and computational efficiency, making
them suitable for real­time or near­real­time applications.
However, numerical differentiation of real­world signals, such as those from the position
sensors in the test rig, is inherently sensitive to noise.
Differentiation amplifies high­
frequency components, including measurement noise, which can result in unstable or
misleading outputs. This issue is especially pronounced when using basic finite differ­
ence methods, such as diff. To mitigate these effects, smoothing techniques are often
required before or during differentiation. One common approach is to use filters or poly­
nomial fits to smooth the signal.
In the context of numerical modeling, the fidelity of the derivative approximation depends
on factors like discretization accuracy, stability, and the nature of the piecewise profiles
assumed.
Concepts like convergence and stability are particularly important in time­
dependent analyses, where improper handling of noisy derivatives can propagate errors
throughout the computational model.
Therefore, while MATLAB’s differentiation tools are powerful, they must be used with care,
especially because we have no idea how the actual signal looks and behaves; in other
words, there is no benchmark to compare against when the baseline is a measured signal.
A common practical approach includes pre­processing via low­pass filtering or smoothing
splines, followed by the application of central or higher­order finite difference methods for
derivative estimation.

Since we are interested in understanding the dynamics of the system, particularly the
velocities and accelerations derived from position signals, it is a natural step to analyze

transforming a time­domain signal into its frequency components, providing valuable in­
sight into which frequencies are present in the system. We expect certain frequencies to
be dominant, such as those associated with the flywheel rotation speed and the natural
frequencies and harmonics of the system.

4.4.1
Butterworth Filter
The Butterworth filter is maximally flat in the passband, meaning the magnitude response
has no ripples in the passband—it is as smooth and flat as possible. Aside from a gradual
roll­off near the cutoff frequency, the passband’s frequency is not attenuated.
The filter order n controls the steepness of the transition between the passband and the
stopband ­ a higher order results in a sharper roll­off. As n increases, the filter becomes
steeper near the cutoff frequency, providing stronger attenuation of frequencies outside
the passband. However, this also increases the filter’s complexity and phase distortion[4].
To implement the filtering, a Butterworth filter is applied directly in the frequency domain:

4.6
Notch Filtering for Specific Frequency Bands
In this case, where known disturbances and harmonics are present, notch filtering was
employed to selectively attenuate narrow bands of unwanted frequencies. The notch
filter is implemented using a raised­cosine shape to ensure a smooth transition at the
band edges and to minimize spectral leakage. For a notch band defined by the lower and
upper angular frequencies ωlo and ωhi, the transfer function transitions smoothly to zero
within the band and restores to unity outside the band.
A known frequency that will always be present unless removed is the rotational velocity
of the rotor. This frequency is straightforward to calculate and use as an example: We

Rather than analyzing the entire signal and handling each impact individually, a statistical
approach was employed to analyze the signal. Two approaches were of interest; the first
was autocorrelation, which was used to determine if the signal had periodicity.

Correlating two different signals was advantageous in the context of this thesis because

was, as previously discussed, divided into bins, and these bins were considered to be
individual experiments conducted under identical circumstances; these were the ensem­
bles.
The ensembles were aligned by determining the time shift τ that corresponded to the
highest correlation between a reference signal x(t) and the rest of the ensemble. When
τ was determined, the ensemble could be aligned, and the mean of the ensemble signal
could be calculated and used as a statistical version of the full signal.
The difference, and thus the uncertainty connected with each ensemble signal, was ex­
pressed through the standard deviation of the mean signal. This statistical method was
applied to all the data signals, which enabled the statistical calculation of the contact
forces.
4.12.1
Considerations for Correlation
It is important to be aware that the choice of reference signal influences the resulting
correlation. Consider an extreme case where an ensemble of signals contains an unex­
pected outlier, and that outlier is used as the reference: the correlation with the rest of
the ensemble will generally be lower compared to using one of the non­outlier signals
as reference. Therefore, it can be advantageous to experiment with multiple candidate
reference signals. Even when the ensemble signals are very similar to each other, the
correlation values will still exhibit some variation depending on the chosen reference, but
in practice, such variation may often be negligible.
Another aspect to be aware of is that when opting to use statistical signals (e.g., ensemble
averages) as the basis for the correlation calculations, all signals involved must be treated
in a statistically consistent manner.
Furthermore, the first signal used for correlation determines the reference alignment for
all subsequent signals. In this work, the correlation was performed on the ensemble of
the top y­position data. Consequently, all other signals were aligned according to the time
shifts derived from that initial correlation.

6
Discussion
6.1
Validity of model
The dynamic model developed in chapter 3 rests on Newton–Euler balance equations
written in the laboratory frame fixed to the housing. Strictly speaking, the only inertial
frame is the laboratory table; the gimbal and the housing undergo small but finite motions
when the impact happens. Treating the housing frame as inertial introduces an error within
the uncertainty already caused by sensor noise and model simplifications. Future tests
done to determine the indentation might require re­deriving the equations in a true inertial
frame, but for the present data set, the approximation is adequate.
The choice to omit the rotating unbalance force Fu is slightly less clear­cut. A previous
study reported an equivalent unbalance amplitude that produces about 15 N at 30000 rpm
for an earlier version of the rig [1]. When scaled to our 2250 rpm limit, this corresponds
to less than 1N. Peak normal contact forces during the first impact reach 350 N, so the
unbalance represents a tiny fraction of the load. Because its direction rotates once per
revolution, Fu can either add to or subtract from the impact force, and, averaging over
many impacts, its net contribution is negligible. Still, it could bias the peak slightly on a
single event. Including a harmonic forcing term would therefore improve fidelity at little
computational cost and should be considered in future iterations.
A more significant discrepancy arises in the predicted angular accelerations ¨ϕ and ¨ψ.
The calculated values resulting from the mathematical model do not match the values
calculated purely from the position sensors. This shows that the model clearly has some
shortcomings, though where exactly the discrepancy stems from is uncertain.
Assumptions
The model rests on some simplifying assumptions that are largely valid for the current
test setup. Aerodynamic drag is neglected because does not spin very fast, and is largely
axis­symmetric. The rotor is treated as a rigid six­DOF body, a choice supported by a first
bending mode well above the impact bandwidth (1.3 kHz). Thermal effects are ignored
because each run is short, and coil heating is negligible and can not be measured in
the setup. Small­angle linearisation stays accurate as the peak tilt never exceeds 0.03°.
Axial motion is set to z = ˙z = ¨z = 0 since there is no sensor detecting axial travel. Finally,
the rotors angular acceleration is held constant at ( ˙Ω= 0). While deceleration during
impact may occur, the optical encoder updates only once every half­turn, producing a
stair­stepped Ω. Thus, taking a derivative gives inflated values. While not strictly valid,
assuming constant Ωavoids adding additional noise and is a necessary compromise with
the present instrumentation. A higher­resolution speed sensor would allow this constraint
to be relaxed.
6.2
Sensor accuracy
When the experiments were conducted, it became clear that especially one of the ECSs
was drifting. The drift caused the sensors to lose calibration, which led to inaccurate mea­
surements. The problem was especially pronounced in the experiments with high RPMs.
The sensors are fastened by threading them into the housing directly, and unfortunately,
the optimal sensor range is not reached when the thread bottoms out, which means that
the sensor is loose when it is correctly calibrated. Then, when the experiments were
running and the rig was vibrating and shaking, the sensors were unscrewed slightly, but
Impact Dynamics in Flywheel Systems
53
==PAGE:62==
enough to disturb the very precise measurement. Since the gap between the flywheel
and the backup bearings was known, the plan was to use the position data to identify
exactly when the two were in contact; however, that was not possible due to the drifting
sensors. Since the foundation of the mathematical model is largely based on the quality
of the position data, the fault could be severe, however the drift was only small enough
to prevent identification of the contact and we find it fair to say that it could not possibly
large enough to cause any meaningful problems to the rest of the calculations.
The accelerometer that was measuring the acceleration of the housing was found to pro­
duce saturated data during the impacts, which is a crucial part of the data. The problem
was especially pronounced at low RPMs in the experiments from 0RPM to 1000RPM. The
reason that the accelerometer was not saturated, or at least severely saturated at high
RPM, could be the gyroscopic effect on the flywheel redirecting its angular momentum,
consequently redistributing more acceleration into the x­direction. This fault complicated
the work for several reasons. As a workaround to the problem with the ECSs, it was pro­
posed to use the acceleration data to detect impacts; however, since it was saturated, it
became impossible. Another purpose of measuring the acceleration of the housing was
to use it to calculate equilibrium equations again, which was impossible as long as the
data was saturated. It would be possible to interpolate some of the data signal to improve
it; however, that would introduce new uncertainties and be a bit out of scope.
6.3
Rotor­house contact
A persistent difficulty throughout the execution of this thesis has been to define when
exactly the rotor and housing are in contact with each other. The positions during impacts
are never quite the same, which could have any number of causes, including sensor
inaccuracies, drift, indentation, and clattering. The method used in this thesis, which was
the most consistent in defining contact, was based on a minimum position criterion and
finding acceleration peaks. This worked very well for the first impacts in each cycle, as
their shape look similar. The following impacts are not similar, though. They either have
what looks like multiple impacts, only one part of the rotor touching, get very close to
contacting, or sudden acceleration peaks while it looks like it’s still in contact.
It is also hard to distinguish between the rotor vibrating from bending, or if it is clattering.
Capturing clattering impacts could be crucial to understanding the forces involved during
these touchdown events. A previous study has found that when a rod hits a surface at an
angle and bounces, the impulse of the second impact can be up to twice as large as the
first and second impulse [7].
6.4
Signal processing
While different ways of filtering the signals were being tested, it was quickly decided that
a Butterworth filter suited us best. It kept the interesting frequencies for the thesis flat in
the pass­band and gave a predictable roll­off tunable by the filter order.
Eventually, while analyzing all the different signals, a higher and higher filter order was
chosen. This came about as we learned what each signal meant and what was important
for it. The filter eventually resembled a sharp frequency cutoff. The filter’s customisability
was very useful in the development, though.
Similarly, the power spectrum was very useful during development to find where most of
the power of each signal was. The most prominent frequencies were all the frequencies
below 200 Hz and the rotor RPM. However, when looking at only the impacts, the power
spectrum became less useful as the frequency bins became wider. This is because when

cycle­to­cycle variations are smoothed away, making it unsuited to tasks where occasional
high peaks matter, such as fatigue or worst­case load assessments. In practice, the av­
erage provides a clean reference, while selected individual recordings remain essential
for capturing variability and extremes.
6.5
Indentation
The sensor drift that was encountered made it impossible to determine the indentation
and the indentation velocity accurately, because there was no way to establish a datum
for the measurement. The problem caused us to avoid working with the Hertzian contact
model and change track by calculating the contact forces utilizing dynamic equilibrium.
This meant that we could not compare our result directly to the work by Aspe and Wagner
[8].
Additionally, the impacts are much longer in duration compared to previous studies [8].
They found several impacts in a 3.5 ms time window, while the impacts in this study last
∼50 ms. This is most likely mainly due to the accelerating housing, forcing the rotor into
contact, and making it ’stick’.

6.6
Forces
Overall, the forces make sense. The shape of the forces is in line with what you would
expect from an impact. The contact force peaks as soon as the impact begins, then drops
when the bottom of the rotor comes into contact, and the force is spread out over both
contact points. The graph exhibits a curved shape that increases and decreases during
the part of the impact where both ends of the flywheel are in contact. Finally, the force
peaks slightly as the top loses contact and all the force gets concentrated at the bottom;
however, the peak is much smaller because the flywheel is in the process of losing contact.
The forces that were calculated in this work peak in the range 350 −400N, Aspe and
Wagner calculate peaks around 120N. Considering that the flywheel is heavier in the
setup that was used in this work it makes sense that the forces should be higher. However,
a direct quantitative comparison is difficult because the previous study excited the rotor
with a magnetic pull rather than the entire rig tilt employed here.
The friction force component is slightly puzzling, however. There is not as much difference
between the different tests done at various RPMs as one would expect. Indeed, even the
test done at 0 RPM has the highest friction force. The cause of this is most likely due
to the sensors not having centered the rotor perfectly in the housing. This will cause the
components of the force to be miscalculated, for example, when the displacement in x
is measured to be 25 µm, but it is closer to 100 µm. With this difference, the calculated
friction force could be doubled from the real value.
A representative frame, shown in Figure 6.1, from an animation of the flywheel movement
has been chosen to demonstrate the geometric measurement error. The animation is
made with data from the experiment at 2000 rpm because it shows more movement in the
x­direction as a result of the high rpm. The backup bearing’s contact surface is concave,
and the position sensor measures the distance in a straight radial line. When the flywheel
strikes the bearing off the central axis, a scenario that occurs more frequently at high
rotational speeds, the contact point lies on the curved surface, and the sensor under­
reports the actual radial displacement. Therefore, our model with acceleration, etc., will
correctly indicate an impact; however, due to the measurement error and concave surface,
it will do so at a different position than in reality.

7
Conclusion
This thesis explores and quantifies the impact dynamics that arise in a magnetically lev­
itated Flywheel Energy Storage System (FESS) when the rotor makes contact with the
mechanical backup bearings. Although secondary to the system’s primary operation, such
events are vital due to the extreme forces and potential damage involved. Understand­
ing them is key to ensuring the long­term durability, safety, and operational robustness
of FESS units, particularly as they are integrated into energy­critical applications such as
transportation and grid buffering.
The core contribution of this work lies in reconstructing unmeasured impact forces using
only indirect sensor data. Through a combination of theoretical modeling, experimen­
tal testing, and advanced signal processing, the thesis successfully demonstrates how
flywheel position data, despite being noisy and at times drifting, can be filtered, differen­
tiated, and statistically analyzed to yield valuable insights into system dynamics.
The flywheel test rig was subjected to controlled perturbations across a range of speeds
up to 2250 RPM. During these tests, the rotor was induced to strike its backup bearings.
Using a custom­developed signal processing pipeline, including Butterworth filtering and
frequency­domain differentiation, accurate acceleration signals were reconstructed. In
conjunction with a linearized mechanical model of the system, these enabled the estima­
tion of contact forces.
The results show that peak contact forces during initial impacts consistently reached sev­
eral hundred newtons, with lower peaks observed in subsequent bounces. Across tests
up to 2250 rpm the first impact in each event reached peak loads of roughly 350–400
N, followed by lower, broader force pulses as the rotor rebounded and load shared be­
tween the two bearing rims. The time sequence extracted from the data, initial strike,
two­point load phase, final release, matches the physical behaviour expected from a rigid
rotor landing on a concave bearing surface.
However, some limitations persist. Sensor drift in the position sensors reduced the pre­
cision of contact detection, while the accelerometer frequently experienced saturation
during strong impacts. These hardware limitations necessitated interpolation and other
approximations, which have affected force estimates. Additionally, the absence of high­
fidelity angular velocity data constrained the model’s ability to depict friction forces ac­
curately. The current model also excludes forces such as rotor unbalance and higher­
frequency events such as multiple impacts in a very short window, i.e., clattering. Future
extensions could incorporate nonlinear Hertzian contact theory and energy dissipation
models to match real­world conditions better.
Despite these challenges, the signal processing framework proved resilient. Cross­correlation
techniques allowed for creating statistical signals, smoothing out noise, and revealing con­
sistent patterns across multiple experiments. While excellent for understanding average
system behavior, this statistical approach obscures rare but potentially damaging outlier
impacts, a trade­off worth considering in future work.
In conclusion, this thesis provides a practical and technically rigorous methodology for
estimating impact forces in FESS without direct force measurements. It demonstrates
that meaningful mechanical insights can be extracted from noisy and partial data with
careful signal processing and a solid understanding of system dynamics.

7.1
Future work
The work presented in this thesis demonstrates the feasibility of modeling and testing
impact dynamics in a magnetically suspended flywheel energy­storage system; however,
a number of open questions remain before an exhaustive description of the system can
be claimed. Future investigations should therefore focus on improving both the quality of
the experimental data and the realism of the theoretical models.
First, the data­acquisition chain needs refinement. Each impact experiment should start
and end with well­defined reference positions, so that the exact location at which the rotor
first contacts the housing can be defined unambiguously. Additionally, the present ac­
celerometer on the housing should be replaced with a sensor that has a higher dynamic
range to avoid saturation during the largest impact events. A higher­resolution measure­
ment of the rotor speed is also desired. This may be achieved by increasing the resolution
of the indicator tape used by the IR sensor, so instead of only updating every half rotation,
it could be every quarter or 1/8 rotation. Perhaps it would be possible to get the angular
velocity from the generator that is spinning the rotor up.
With more reliable data collection and higher­quality data, it could be realistic to estimate
the indentation of the rotor into the housing during impacts. If this were possible, a com­
parison between the forces found in this thesis and the contact force models found in
previous studies [8] [9] could be made. This method can be used to estimate the coeffi­
cients in the model and determine which one best fits the data.
In the same spirit, the influence of the backup bearings could also be taken into account.
The backup bearings may be slightly loose and could become knocked into place when
the rotor impacts them, resulting in a slight discrepancy between the initial impact and
subsequent contacts.
Furthermore, the present model still neglects the periodic unbalance force that acts during
free rotation. The unbalance amplitude could be identified from the steady­state orbit
data collected. When included in the simulations, the unbalance force would improve the
accuracy of the contact force calculation.
Additionally, the system could be tested at higher RPM. All tests conducted in this thesis
are performed at 2250 RPM or less; however, the test rig is capable of handling at least
30000 RPM. Testing at higher RPMs would push the limits of what the models can predict,
while providing more insight into how they would be applied in real­world scenarios.
Finally, contact detection should be improved to more precisely determine when the ro­
tor and housing contact. It should also allow for impacts much shorter in duration than
what was used in this thesis. Doing this would account for the clattering phenomenon.
With a more precise way of detecting contact, a statistical signal would also no longer be
necessary, and the entire signal could be analyzed by itself.
