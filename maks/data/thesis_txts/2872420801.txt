Abstract
To alleviate the worst effects of climate change, a 100% renewable energy supply is needed.
One solution
is electrolysis, the key technology of Power-to-X where e.g., H2O is electrochemically converted into H2 and
O2.
Solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOEC) are the most efficient cells for this technology.
To increase the
production, many SOEC are combined into stacks and sealed to make it gas tight. Recently, a monolithic
stack was produced in a single sintering step. One standard repeating unit (SRU) has a dense thin zirconia-
based electrolyte surrounded by two porous zirconia-based electrode backbones. The cell is surrounded by two
perpendicular sets of gas channels containing supports made of porous ferritic stainless steel and a composite
metallic and ceramic interconnect plate connecting the SRUs in series.
Once it is produced, it cannot be
disassembled without being destroyed. Therefore, X-ray CT is an ideal method to examine the monolith’s
complex 3D structure in a non-destructive way. This is needed to identify defects and analyze the structures,
pores, and interface areas.
Within the micrometer resolution, it has been clarified that the supports are sufficient for pore analysis while
the electrode layers with ∼10 times lower thickness are not. Defects are present within the nonporous layers,
including cracks of the electrolyte with lengths ranging from 2 µm to 105 µm. This results in gas leaking through
the electrolyte and thus lowers the power efficiency. Pore analysis was done on the central support at the scan of
the highest resolution leading to a porosity of 15.2%, tortuosity of 10.9, and connectivity of 91.6%. Even though
the support is less porous than the conventional air electrode, the high connectivity and gas channels in between
benefit the gas flow. The resulting interface area between the support and the interconnect and between the
support and the electrode are sufficient to provide a low contact resistance as expected for monoliths. Thus,
the characterization of the internal 3D structures provided important feedback on the quality of the different
layers of the monolithic design.

1
Introduction
Today’s modern society relies heavily on fossil fuels [9]. Although they serve as a vast and inexpensive source of
heat and electricity on demand, the irresponsible consumption is also considered as one of the causes of climate
change. Cleaner sustainable sources like wind and solar power are more than enough to cover the global energy
demand, but they are intermittent and thus not always available when needed [1]. Alternatively, electricity from
these renewable sources can be used to power electrolysis plants capable of splitting steam (H2O) or carbon
dioxide (CO2) into molecular hydrogen (H2), carbon monoxide (CO) or syngas (H2+CO) in an electrochemical
reaction. This process is defined as power-to-X, where X can be any byproduct like a chemical or a fuel that
can be used to power heavy or long-haul transport [9].
The potent technology for this energy conversion is a solid oxide electrolysis cell (SOEC). When operated in
reverse, it functions as a solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) [1]. One advantage is the unrivaled power conversion
efficiencies because of more favorable thermodynamics and faster kinetics at operating temperatures of 500◦
to 850◦which are necessary to reach sufficient ionic conductivity [9], [10]. There have been other electrolysis
technologies in development, notably the alkaline and polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) which operate
at 60◦C to 160◦C and have been regarded as more mature for the general market than SOEC. However, the
industrial scale-up of SOEC technology has been happening at a rapid pace within the past decade [9].
The components of a solid oxide cell (SOC) are an oxygen electrode, an electrolyte, and a fuel electrode. In
electrolysis mode, electrochemical reduction of e.g., H2O proceeds on the negatively charged fuel electrode,
the cathode. Oxide ions, O2−, are conducted through the electrolyte and onto the positively charged oxygen
electrode, the anode, where they recombine into O2 [9]. The electrolyte is typically made of yttria-stabilized
zirconia (YSZ), a ceramic material and ionic conductor. The fuel electrode is also made of YSZ. but it relies
on catalysts by nickel (Ni), a non-noble metal with a flexible supply. The air electrode consists of lanthanum-
strontium-cobaltite (LSC), a mixed oxide ion and electron conductor [1]. Meanwhile, PEM electrodes consist of
e.g., platinum (Pt), a noble metal [11]. This leads to another advantage of SOC which is the greater availability
of raw materials [9].
The ceramics are still expensive, so the cell layers are only as thick as necessary for
electrochemical function to minimize the cost [10]. However, the electrolyte must be as thin as possible for
sufficient conductivity of O2−and thus higher performance. Consequently, the active cell layers can have a
thickness in the range of few tens of micrometers [2]. To improve the mechanical strength, a support is needed
for e.g., the fuel electrode. It can either be made of a similar ceramic or a metal like porous stainless steel.
Since steel is cheaper than ceramics, metal is often preferred due to the lower raw material costs. Another
advantage is higher thermal stability i.e., higher tolerance of large temperature gradients when heated enabling
faster start-up times. Finally, metal makes the SOC more mechanically strong and flexible [10].
To increase the production of e.g., H2, many SOCs are combined into so-called stacks [9]. They are traditionally
made by stacking the cells on top of each other and sealing them to make it gas tight. The typical structure of a
standard repeat unit (SRU) of a conventional cell is shown in a conventional stack in fig. 1(a). Interconnects are
set between the cells to provide electrical contact and gas separation between the cells as well as gas distribution
across them [1]. Additionally, gas channels are formed within the interconnect to feed gases to the fuel electrode
and lead the electrochemically produced gases out of the air electrode [2]. That way, the cell support, gas
channels, and interconnects constitute the thickest layers i.e., few hundred micrometers [2] which is about 10
times the size of the active layers. This leads to a low volumetric power density making the SOC technology
useful for stationary applications in practice.
In this thesis work, the monolithic configuration of the SOC is introduced. This design has been created for
space applications [12] and since then developed extensively by Capotondo et al [3]. The content of the stack
of this work is three standard repeating units (SRUs). One SRU is illustrated in fig. 1(b). Each unit has
a dense thin zirconia-based electrolyte surrounded by two porous zirconia-based electrode backbones. They
can be infiltrated with electrocatalysts but that is not the case for this work. The three-layer cell is further
surrounded by two perpendicular sets of gas channels containing the trapezoid pillars made of porous ferritic
stainless steel and a composite metallic and ceramic interconnect plate connecting the SRUs in series. The main
difference with respect to the conventional cell is that the gas channels are directly in contact with the cell, so
the mechanical support is integrated in the same layer. This makes the design more compact, thereby reducing
the overall stack height and thus increasing the volumetric density and power efficiency [2].

Additionally, energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDX) can be used to

contact areas between layers. Therefore, X-ray Computed Tomography (CT) is an ideal method to examine

techniques. The material phases include the electrode, the electrolyte, the support, and the interconnect. The
aerial phases include the gas channels, the pores within the support and the electrode, and the defects at the
electrolyte and the interconnect. Finally, the different layers can be analyzed within the possible resolution.

Background
A strong demand for clean sustainable energy has appeared in most countries because of the fear of the green-
house effect and a wish to alleviate the worst effects of climate change [9], [1]. Therefore, the question is whether
there is enough sustainable energy available. The International Energy Agency (IEA)’s estimate of the World’s
Total Primary Energy Supply was 18.3 TWy (terawatt years) in 2016 based on oil equipment [1], [14]. If the
same amount of energy should be produced from photovoltaic cells with 10% efficiency, we need solar irradiation
of 183 TW electrical power on average over the year [1], [14]. The average influx of the energy from the Sun to
the Earth corresponds to 21,000 TW average electrical power i.e., 115 times more than what is needed [1], [14].
Furthermore, the potential of wind power is estimated to be 23 TW and hydropower 1.6 TW [1], [14]. These
potentials are significantly lower, so solar irradiation is considered as a main sustainable energy source for the
future. Despite the competitiveness to fossil fuels, solar and wind power are intermittent and thus not always
available when needed, and the availability of hydropower is not over the year.
To cover the world’s energy demand more constantly, it takes efficient and inexpensive energy-conversion and
storage systems to fully utilize the intermittent solar and wind energy and the varying hydropower [1]. One
example of this utilization is power-to-X where the core technology is electrolysis, and X stands for any chemical
or fuel as a byproduct. It is a conversion technology that can produce fuels like hydrogen (H2) from water (H2O)
or carbon monoxide (CO) carbon dioxide (CO2). From mixtures of H2 and CO (syngas), it is possible to produce
other fuels like synthetic natural gas. The resulting fuels can be used to power heavy or long-haul transport
like planes, ships, and trucks [9]. The transport sector accounts for about 25% of the total energy consumption.
Therefore, this conversion technology helps to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from there [2].
A solid oxide cell (SOC) is thus a potent technology for these purposes. It is argued that the power conversion
efficiency of this utilization of renewable electricity can be about 40% which is higher than the efficiency of the
consumption of fossil fuels which is about 25% nowadays [1]. Research and development are in progress for
the SOEC to reach a more mature state like lower cost, longer lifetime, and improved reliability. Within this
decade, the industrial scale-up of SOEC technology has been happening at a rapid pace [9].
This project is a step in this direction where X-ray CT is used to characterize a SOC in the monolithic design.
Firstly, a background review of the SOC technology is conducted. Secondly, the monolithic design is introduced.
Thirdly, the theory of X-ray CT is covered. Finally, an application of X-ray CT on a SOC is exemplified.

2.1.3
Comparison to another electrolyzer
Yttria and zirconia are abundant materials: solid oxide cells providing 1 TW of power in fuel cell mode would
require one month’s worth of production of Zr2O2 and 21 months’ worth of Y2O3 [9]. To put it into perspective,
24 hours of 1 TW of power generated using the PEM fuel cell system would require 53 months’ worth of global
Pt production [9], [11]. The fuel electrode relies on catalysts by Ni, a non-noble metal with a flexible supply.
Thus, the raw material availability is the inherent advantage of the SOCs.

SOECs operate at elevated temperatures of 500-900◦C to reach sufficient conductivity of O2−ions [9], [10].
This temperature range outmatches the alkaline and polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) electrolyzers which
typically operate at 60-160◦C. Higher temperature means more favorable thermodynamics and faster kinetics.
This is because theoretical thermodynamic efficiencies for both H2O and CO2 increase with increasing temper-
ature [9]. This makes SOEC an effective tool for large scale power-to-X plants when it is devoted to hydrogen
production. Meanwhile, SOFC can be used for X-to-power solutions such as decentralized heat and electricity
production, auxiliary power systems and heavy-duty transport propulsion [3]. The high temperatures also entail
a slower start-up and lower thermal robustness. It is advantageous for more constant operating conditions such
as long-haul trucks, trains, or ships. However, the usage of SOFCs as range extenders in vehicles is at a lower
state than the PEM fuel cells with the lower operating temperatures and a faster system start-up making it
more competitive for automotive applications [2]. Thus, production of SOC’s for the marketing purposes is still
in development.
2.1.4
Configuration of the conventional SOC stack
Instead of using a ceramic-based fuel electrode supported cell, porous metal like stainless steel can be used to
support the thinner electrochemically active layers [10]. The aim of the porous structure is to provide access
for the gas molecules in the electrochemical reactions. That way, the more expensive ceramic electrochemically
active layers are only as thick as necessary for electrochemical function while stainless steel itself has lower cost
and is more mechanically robust. The metal support can also tolerate a more rapid change in temperature
compared to the conventional SOCs. This allows faster start-ups and load regulations [3].
Multiple cells like 30-100 can be combined in series in an SOC stack by metallic interconnects which provide
electrical contact and gas separation between the cells as well as gas distribution across the cells. Stacks are
built into modules and assembled into systems to reach the desired gas production rate [9]. A full stack must
also contain internal three-dimensional gas channels [1]. The aim is to feed fuel gases to the electrodes to enable
electrochemical production in the active cell layer from the fuel electrode to the oxygen electron and feed the
produced gases out. Fig. 4 shows an expanded sketch of a bipolar flat-plate cell stack. This shows that the
gas channels are formed within the interconnect that is made of stainless steel in this design. The flow-field
cylinders are regarded as an ideal design for the gas channel but so far they have not been realized in practical
manufacturing.

2.1.5
Monolithic configuration
In this project, the SOC stack is designed as a monolith. This is an oxide-ceramic structure in which appropriate
electronic and ionic conductors are fabricated in a honeycomb shape according to the first development of the
technology [12]. This type of device can operate in the range of 700-1000◦C [12] which is slightly higher than
the range of 500-900◦C for conventional cells [1]. Because of the small cell size and low resistive losses in the
materials, it can thus obtain a power efficiency of over 50% which is higher than the conventional SOEC’s of
up to 40% [1]. The aim is to make the SOC more convenient for air and space applications [15].
The design that is modified by Capotondo et al [3] is shown in fig. 5 in a cross-sectional SEM image along with
qualitative energy dispersive spectroscopy. This device consists of three standard repeating units (SRU). Each
unit consists of a SOC backbone, composed of a dense thin zirconia-based electrolyte and two porous zirconia-
based electrode backbones to be infiltrated with appropriate electro-catalysts. The cell is further surrounded by
two sets of ferritic steel gas channels structures consisting of supports made of ferritic stainless steel as composite
metal/ceramic interconnect plates connecting in series. The total number of layers co-fired to produce monoliths
within this work is 19 and the monoliths are denoted as ”MNx3” (nomenclature defined for a monolith stack
with 3x SRUs). The reason that the interconnect was composite instead of purely metallic as in the case of
conventional fuel cells was that a complete densification of metal would impose too high stress on the cell.
Furthermore, composite supports in the gas channel layer were not ductile enough to withstand mechanical
stresses [3]. Therefore, the metal-based monolith is defined by also composing metal with the ceramic electrode
backbones. Fig. 5 shows an exemplified image of the monolith with the purely ceramic electrode backbones.

left side shows the EDS mapping of the image indicating the presence of Zr in the cell and the interconnect and

2.2.2
Principle of computed tomography (CT)
Regarding the body of R¨ontgen’s hand, tissue consists of water and hydrocarbons whereas bone contains a lot
of Ca, a heavier element, and correspondingly smaller X-ray transmission [16]. Thus, the internal structure
of parts of the body can be imaged as a radiogram, a two-dimensional shadow picture. A three-dimensional
picture can also be made with a technique called computed tomography (CT). The idea is to record radiograms
from different angles in a full rotation and then use a software tool to reconstruct the 3D object and finally
segment the elements of the material.
X-ray CT is can inspect internal and external dimensions and reveal defects and assembly issues in a non-
destructive way. Therefore, it can be applied to e.g. examine the manufacturing quality of functional materials
[19]. A general experimental setup for materials characterization is based on cone beam CT which is illustrated

Reconstruction and segmentation of a 2D object
To demonstrate the creation of a 3D object through reconstruction and segmentation, an equivalent 2D process
is illustrated. A software program, ’Matlab’, was used to create a 2D phantom object consisting of a large
square with the dimensions of 20×20 value of ’1’ and two smaller squares with dimensions of 5×5 the values ’2’
and ’3’. The result is shown in fig. 10(a). The object is scanned by a parallel beam to obtain a 1D projection
from different rotations from 0◦to 360◦. Fig. 10(b)-(c) shows the exemplified projection from the horizontal
angle and the vertical angle. The projections are collected in a so-called sinogram as shown on fig. 10(d). In
3D, 2D images are recorded instead of lines. In fig. 10(b), the beam goes through the 20 pixels of the large
with the value of 1 and five pixels of the small squares with the values of 2 and 3 at different positions. In fig.
10(c), the beam goes through 20 pixels of the large with the value of 1 again at one position but the two small
squares with the values of 2 and 3 at the same position, so the total value adds up to 25. The mathematical
process for going from an object to a sinogram is called Radon transform R(θ, x′) i.e., a line integral that is a
function of the viewing angle, θ, and the coordinate, x′, perpendicular to the direction along which the integral
is performed [16].
There are several ways to reconstruct the image. One way is backprojection, where the projection intensity is
smeared back into the object space. Fig. 11(a) shows the result of the backprojection. Here, the pixel values of
the squares are much brighter than 1, 2, and 3, and that the brightness is the largest at the center. Meanwhile,
the edges are stretched out and look more circular. Therefore, a so-called ramp filter is used to give the result
shown in fig. 11(b). Here, the intensity of each square is like the object, while the edges are preserved. For the
larger square, the intensity is homogenous. For the smaller squares, it is more smeared out, but the maximum
value is the same. This demonstrates that the filtered backprojection (FBP) is limited by the resolution.
To show how the filtered backprojection is done, a simpler 1D projection is demonstrated i.e., a square object
of 100 pixels on a 200-pixel line detector as shown on fig. 12(a). This projection image is Fourier Transformed
to yield the projection in the frequency domain as shown on fig. 12(b). A ramp filter is created in the Fourier
space as shown on fig. 12(c). This filter weights each spatial frequency such that the high frequencies are kept,
and the low frequencies are reduced. Thereby, the lowest frequencies are at the center of the image and highest
at the edges. The filter is multiplied with the signal in Fourier space and then transformed back to real space
by inverse Fourier transform (IFT) to obtain the result in 12(d). Here, it is seen that the edges are highlighted
while the inside of the square has a more indifferent and homogeneous intensity compared to fig. 12(a).

Another example to consider is a non-local means (NLM) algorithm [21]. This is used to calculate a weighted
average of all the pixels in the image. That is, similar pixel neighborhoods give a larger weight in the average,
while different neighborhoods give a smaller weight. This algorithm does not only compare the grey level of a

technique such that the pixel in each symmetric pair that is closest to the value of the center pixel is selected,
and that the value of the center pixel is computed by the mean value of the four selected neighborhood pixels.
Geometrically, this concept is edge-preserving because it finds the neighboring pixels that most closely resemble
the center pixel before taking the average value of the selected neighbors.

therefore to grow the object back.

spot size of 3-215 µm. Thereby, increasing the current also increases the spot size which in turn increases the
blurring or unsharpness as seen on eq. 2.22. Setting the power to 20 W yielded a spot size of 20 µm according
to the manual of the Nikon scanner. With the geometrical magnification of 11, this gave a blurring of 200 µm.
Furthermore, increasing the exposure time increases the number of counts per projection. 2.83 s was the time
that was set such that the background intensity did not exceed 50000. One could also apply binning. Binning
1 corresponds with using all pixels as one pixel. Binning 2 corresponds to twice the size of one pixel covering a
set of 2 × 2 pixels as one. This option would decrease the resolution but increase the intensity and decrease the
amount of data. In this experiment, binning 1 was used so the physical pixel size was 200 µm.

4.4
Segmentation of the high-resolution scan
One method of segmenting the data is to identify the brightest peaks and then find the less bright peaks. That
is, the electrolyte should be identified firstly due to the largest brightness. Then, the interconnect should be
found. Even though the interconnect is brighter than the support due to ceramic, the presence of metal in
both materials could cause the intensities to overlap. The support and the electrodes have a similar brightness.
Hence, the interface between the support and the electrode as well as the interface between the support and the
interconnect should be determined more manually. As the final steps, the cracks within the interconnect and
at the electrodes as well as the pores within the support and the electrode should be identified.
4.4.1
Segmentation of the electrolyte
As a first step the electrolyte is considered as a starting point since it is a purely ceramic phase and thus the
brightest one as shown in fig. 33(a). This is consistent with the contrast threshold as indicated on the histogram
on fig. 34(a). However, it turns out that the right end of the threshold is not situated at the very end of the
histogram. On Avizo, the histogram could not be seen at the count value beyond 104 compared to the histogram
replicated on Python in fig. 34(a). Consequently, there might be some brightest parts of the selected material
being left out.
On Avizo, a tool called a ‘magic wand’ is used to point out one connected material within the threshold range.
Hence, it was used to point out the electrolyte. The progress behind segmenting the electrolyte is shown in
fig. 34(b)-(d) with an exemplified orthogonal slice on the (x, z)-plane as a point of view. Fig. 34(b) shows
the raw data with an annotation that points out where the electrolyte should be segmented. Fig. 34(c) shows
the segmented electrolyte immediately after it was pointed out by the magic wand. There are some branches
sticking out through the electrode layer and some holes with the size of a pixel within the electrolyte. One of
each example is annotated with a ring. The connectivity of the threshold pixels can be further restricted by
activating a contrast threshold. Setting it to 200 pixels means that the difference larger than this number does
not make it count. This process did not hinder the presence of the branches as seen on fig. 34(c). This indicates
that there is ceramic content present within the electrode even though it is alloyed with metal. Furthermore,
the creation of holes could be a consequence of the brightest parts being left out due to the misconception
of the histogram on Avizo compared to fig. 34(a). To fill the holes, the electrolyte was grown 2 times and
shrunk again. To remove the branches, a so-called ’brush tool’ was used to manually draw interfaces between
the electrolyte and the electrode. Here, the raw data in fig. 34(b) was important to consider when creating the
interface. Then, the segmented electrolyte outside of the interface was subtracted from the remaining material.
The interfaces were drawn across 20 equidistant slices in the (x, z)-plane and interpolated across it to save time.
This leads to the result on the orthogonal slice in fig. 34(d). Here there are still some remaining issues like
lumps across the length and edges that have not been included as annotated. This shows that the electrolyte
must be further modified once the electrode is segmented. The full result of the segmentation progress is shown
as a volume rendering of the electrolyte in fig. 34(e). Here, there is a long crack and a few smaller ones.
Since the segmentation of the electrolyte must be modified in a later stage compared to the electrode and the
delaminations, the cracks will be optimized.

4.5.1
Pores within the support
To analyze the pores within the central support layer of the internal scan, a new rectangular subvolume should
be extracted. The criterion is that phases belonging to the support and the associated pores are the only ones
that remain. This leads to the following result in fig. 43 showing the surface view of support material within

unknown pores in red in (d). Qualitatively, there is a high display of connected pores as shown in fig. 43(b).
This shows that there is a high probability that the gases that are fed into the monolith can reach the SOC
layer, and the newly produced gases can flow out again. Regarding the isolated pores in fig. 43(c), there is a
higher concentration at the bottom. This might be due to the sedimentation of the inks/tapes, which tends to
close the porosity at the bottom.
Figure 43: Surface rendering of all phases of the subvolume of the internal scan, including the support (a), the
connected pores (b), the isolated pores (c), and the unknown pores (d).
Quantitatively, the porosity has been calculated by using eq. 2.23 [24]. The volumes of the four phases have been
extracted by the material statistics. The void volume is found by adding the volumes of the connected pores,
the isolated pores, and the unknown pores. The total volume is found by adding the volume of the support to
the void volume. Thereby, the resulting porosity of the support is 15.2%. This is lower than the typical porosity
value of air electrodes of 25-30% for electrolysis mode [27]. The explanation behind this difference could be
that the electrode layers span the whole plane while the mechanical supports are integrated in the gas channel
such. That way, a larger amount of material with respect to pores could be needed to hold the support more
mechanically stable. On the other hand, this might lead to a higher tortuosity [24] and thereby a more sluggish
gas diffusion.
Concretely, the tortuosity has been calculated by solving eq. 2.25 [24] through a Python package called ’Tau
Factor 2’ [25] that has been developed from the equivalent Matlab package [28]. By default, this calculation
requires a binary volume where the value of the material is set to ’0’ and the value of the pores is set to ’1’.
49
==PAGE:59==
Master Thesis

Elisabeth Mariegaard, s183365
A vertical flux is averaged across the planes parallel to the stimulated boundaries. By default, the direction is
the first index of the loaded image, which is the z-direction in this case. If the percentage error between the
minimum and maximum flux is below a given value (1% as a default value), then convergence has been reached.
Once this is satisfied, an extra 100 iterations are performed to confirm the stability of the system. When Tau
Factor 2 was used to solve eq. 2.25 for the rectangular subvolume of the central support, convergence had not
been reached within the limiting time and computing power. Consequently, the reported value could be affected
by errors. Nevertheless, the final value of the tortuosity resulted to be 10.9. In comparison, the tortuosity of the
air electrode is 5.5 [29]. This might stem from the lower porosity of the support compared to the air electrode.
Another quantity to consider is the phase fraction of the connected, isolated, and unknown pores. By finding
the volumes of each pore with respect to the total pore volume, the resulting phase fractions are 91.6%, 6.7%,
and 1.7%. These quantitative results correspond to the qualitative analysis of fig. 43 where most of the pores
are connected to the outside ends of the support. That is, even though the porosity of the support is lower and
the tortuosity is higher compared to a typical air electrode, the higher connectivity provides a higher probability
of the existing pores to transport the gas particles through the support.

results. The longer dimension with respect to the y-direction could be the cause of a higher phase fraction of

extracted upon a series of (x, y)-planes which have a larger area. This could give more complex pore structure

4.5.2
Pore size distribution of the support
Another Python package, ’PoreSpy’ [23], has been used to calculate the pore-size distribution (PSD). Fig. 45(a)
shows the center of the (y, z)-plane of the rectangular subvolume within tomo-B to highlight an example of
this procedure, including the connected pores in green, isolated pores in teal, and unknown pores in yellow.
However, this package requires a binary volume where the value of all the pores or the selected phase is set to
’True’ and the remainders are set to ’False’. The pore sizes are found by applying a filter called ’porosimetry’
where each voxel in the image shows that radius of the largest sphere that would overlap it. In the case where all
the pores are set to ’True’, fig. 45(b) illustrates how the pore-sizes were found as exemplified on the (y, z)-plane.
Here, it can be seen that the narrowest parts of the larger pores get the smallest radii, while the broadest parts
of the pores get the largest radii.
The overall distribution is also found by using the ’metrics’ sub-module to produce plots based on values in
images that have passed through the filter. That way, the pore sizes are shown as a function of the cumulative
frequency. It is beneficial to distinguish between the connected and isolated pores, so two binary volumes are
used to calculate the PSDs with the results being shown in fig. 45(c). As expected, the overall PSD is larger
for the connected pores than for the isolated pores. Furthermore, the smallest pore size is below ∼2 µm. This
makes sense because the PoreSpy tool cannot find pores that are smaller than the given pixel size of 1.03 µm.
Finally, the cumulative PSDs for the six layers within tomo-A have been calculated with the same technique
with the results being shown in fig.
45(d).
This time, the pixel size is 1.58 µm so that also makes sense.
Furthermore, the overall distribution of connected and isolated pores is similar for each layer. This makes sense
because each support has similar porosity. Regarding the slopes of the PSDs, the steepest ones indicate that
there are many pore sizes within a given interval like 2-8 µm for both tomo-B and tomo-A. Meanwhile there
are fewer pores at larger sizes. Compared to fig. 45(b), this distribution makes sense as there are very few pore
sizes of 14-16 µm compared to the rest. In the case of tomo-A, there are also a few pores with radii of 15-25
µm to be found. During the calculation of the PSD, the voxel size can be scaled to 1.03 µm for tomo-B and
1.58 µm. This could be why the maximum value for tomo-A is set to 25 µm which is around 16 pixels. The
slope of the PSD for the connected pores in tomo-A becomes more constant at ∼15 µm. This could indicate
that there might be no pores that are larger than this value.
4.5.3
Interface areas for the support
To find the interface areas between the central support of tomo-B and the interconnect as well as between the
support and the electrode, a new subvolume is extracted such that these interfaces are accurately included.
Then, only the interfaces between the interconnect and the support and the associated pores at the top as well
as the interfaces between the electrode and the associated holes and the support and the associated pores are
extracted as separate surfaces. Fig. 46(a) shows a surface rendering of the resulting interfaces. Meanwhile, fig.
46(b) shows the bottom view of the interfaces between the support and the interconnect highlighted in red and
remaining interfaces with the usual segmentation color of blue. Similarly, fig. 46(c) shows the top view of the
interfaces between the support and the interconnect highlighted in red and remaining interfaces with the usual
segmentation colors of magenta and purple. It is expected that the distribution of material and pore is random
as the support is a porous material. However, at the bottom right of the interface with the electrode, there
seems to be a larger gap. This could create a larger entrance or exit of the gas particles to the channel.
Quantitatively, the interfaces with respect to the interconnect and the electrode are calculated as the area of the
individual patches of a surface. The result is a spreadsheet containing one row for every patch of the surface,
including the inner region, the outer region, and the resulting surface area. One way to normalize the interface
area between the support and the interconnect and electrodes is to calculate them with respect to the total
interface area of the support and the pores with respect to the interconnect and the electrode including the holes.
As a result, the support interacts with 18.1% of the interconnect and with 21.6% of the electrode. Another
way is to relate them to the rectangular area spanning the interface as annotated in yellow on fig. 46(b)-(c).
From there, the support interacts with 36.8% of the interconnect and with 49.5% of the electrode. Although
the latter way to normalize the interface area is more illustrative, the rectangular area is underestimated. The
highlighted interface areas are extracted over the curved shapes as shown more clearly in 46(b). Hence, the
former way is more accurate as the shape of the remaining interface areas are considered in a similar manner.

shown in fig. 47(d) and including the delamination in fig. 47(e).
The lengths of the cracks and the selected delaminations are measured with ImageJ and shown as annotations
of fig. 47(b)-(e). This both applies to the cracks that fully and partially penetrate through the electrolyte. A
partial penetration is exemplified more clearly in the bottom of fig. 40(c). As a result, the length of the cracks
that are measured on fig. 47(b) and (d) span from 2 µm to 105 µm. The minimum value makes sense as it
is a little above the pixel resolution of 1.03 µm. Thereby, it is confirmed that there are defects present within
the given resolution. Furthermore, the largest crack at the length of 105 µm has a width of 8 µm. Since the
resolution applies to all directions, this crack would not have been found in the overview scan where the pixel
resolution is 18.2 µm.
Regarding the delaminations between the electrolyte and the electrodes, the length and width of the widest
ones are annotated to be 281 µm and 85 µm at the top view and 309 µm and 179 µm at the bottom view.
Concretely, the total volume is extracted from material statistics to be 2.8 · 105 µm3 which covers 5.2% of
the area of the selected electrolyte. Furthermore, it is confirmed that most of the cracks are situated in the
proximity of the delamination. This could imply that there is a connection between the delamination and the
appearance of cracks during the fabrication progress. However, there is one partially penetrating crack with
the length of 16 µm that has not been connected to any of the delaminations. This suggests that cracks can
also be formed by other types of failures that are not related to delaminations that were discovered during
segmentation. Consequently, the presence of cracks allows the exchange of gases between anode and cathode
and thereby present safety issues.

5
Conclusions and Outlook
Thus, the monolithic configuration of the solid oxide cell (SOC) as designed by Capotondo et al [3] has been
analyzed by X-ray CT, a non-destructive method to characterize the internal 3D structure. In the overview
scan, the monolith was scanned in its entirety providing a pixel resolution of 18.2 µm. This was enough to
visualize the supports within the gas channels, interconnects and cell layers in their overall form. Once the
sample was cut to obtain a cylindrical-like cross-section, the pixel resolution was obtained to be 1.58 µm for
the full field of view and 1.03 µm for the internal scan. Thereby, the individual layers of the cell were more
distinguishable. Furthermore, the pores of the support layers were visualized more precisely than the pores
of the electrode layers. Firstly, this is due to the larger thicknesses of the supports of approx. 318-364 µm
compared to the electrodes with thicknesses of approx. 32-34 µm. Secondly, the resolution was not high enough
to analyze the pores within the electrode sufficiently. Therefore, pore analysis was only applied on the support.
After segmentation of the material and aerial phases of the SOC monolith with automatic thresholding and
manual adjustments, it was confirmed that there were defects present within the high-resolution scans. This
includes cracks of the electrolyte with lengths ranging from 2 µm to 105 µm where most of them are situated
within the delaminations between the cell layers that are spread out over the electrolyte as far as 309 µm.
There are also voids within the interconnect, including a larger crack having a length of 52 µm and a width of
3 µm. The electrolyte and the interconnect should not be porous and therefore ensure that the gas flow and
the electrochemical production stayed within its dedicated compartment in the SRU. Since that is not the case
within the microscale, there is a higher probability that the gas leaks from one end of the cell to the other
leading to loss of power efficiency.
Regarding the support within the internal scan, a rectangular subvolume was extracted leading to the resulting
porosity of 15.2%, tortuosity of 10.9, and connectivity of 91.6%. At the full field of view, the same support was
extracted at a larger length and a shorter width leading to a similar porosity of 14.6% but a higher tortuosity
of 17.1 and a lower connectivity of 82.2%. Firstly, the support is less porous than the conventional air electrode
of 25-30% [27] but the gas channels in between benefit the gas flow. Secondly, the lower connectivity is due to
the larger fraction of isolated pores. Thirdly, the tortuosity was extracted at one flux direction where a higher
length or cross-sectional area leads to higher tortuosity. Regarding the pore size distribution, most of these
sizes range from 2 to 8 µm within the given resolution. If the gas particles enter the connected pores, there is
a higher probability that the gas flow and thus the production will be slower.
The interfaces between the support and the interconnect as well as the support and the electrode were segmented
manually to provide as high precision as possible. This led to an interface area with 18.1% of the interconnect
and 21.6% of the electrode with respect to the interaction with the pores within the support. These interface
areas are sufficient to provide a low contact resistance as expected for monoliths.
Regarding the interconnect, segmentation could not be used to distinguish between the metallic and ceramic
phase.
Thereby, there was no 3D particle size distribution nor phase fraction to determine the amount of
ceramic. Mapping with energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDX) as done by Capotondo et al [3] is thus a better
tool because the detector captures the characteristic energies of the individual elements, including metal and
ceramic. However, the analysis is done in 2D. Still, SEM analysis of the monolith could play a larger role for
future investigations supplementary to X-ray CT. From there, different magnifications could be extracted like
the full field of view and a larger focus on each layer. The latter option could enable a more precise EDX
mapping of the composite interconnect than at the full field of view and thus answer the questions that X-ray
CT fails to answer. Meanwhile, SEM cannot be used to analyze the pores as precisely as X-ray CT because the
features are 3D. Thereby, SEM and X-ray CT analysis can complement each other.
Pore analysis within the electrode layers requires a higher resolution than the lab-based CT can provide, so a
synchrotron like the Swiss Light Source (SLS) is recommended. De Angelis et al [8] reconstructed a solid oxide
cell based on ptychography but they applied the same analysis techniques on the pores within the electrodes
compared to the support. Additionally, the data can be used to find the interface areas between the electrode
and the electrolyte as well as the pores of the electrode with the gas channel. Overall, a wider multiscale analysis
can be used to predict the gas flow during the electrochemical production more precisely. These results can
suggest improvements in designing the SOC monolith with regards to reducing or removing the defects.
