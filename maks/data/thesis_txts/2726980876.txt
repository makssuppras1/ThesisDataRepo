Master Thesis
Abstract
Abstract
Wind is clean energy source and technology development is sufficiently mature for humans to harvest
this energy and convert it to electricity in large scale. Especially, offshore wind energy possesses the greatest
potential in wind energy development. The wind resources on the open seas are more abundant, steady
and less turbulent. Nevertheless, the established bottom-fixed wind turbine technology is not adequate for
harvesting wind resources at high seas. Therefore, floating offshore wind technology has become the focus for
next generation wind turbine system development. Currently, multiple floating structures has been studied
extensively, but all of them has their own challenges that need to be tackled. In this research, a Tension-leg
platform(TLP) with a IEA 15 MW RWT mounted atop is being investigated. The investigation will be
focused on dynamic behaviour, loads, ultimate loads and fatigue loads under different environmental condi-
tions. Besides, a comparison of the dynamic behaviour and loads with Umaine semi-submersible model also
constitutes an important part of this research. The Umaine semi-submersible model’s system simulations are
conducted by my thesis partner Qiaoyu Ge in his thesis Load and dynamic investigations of floating
offshore wind turbine system with a semi- submersible floater DTU Wind-M-0678
In the first part of this research, a summary on the state-of-the-art floating offshore wind turbine tech-
nologies, the IEA 15 MW RWT and TLP floater structure (CT-Bos model) are introduced. In the second
part of this research, the dynamic analysis of the TLP model is being performed. First, the components of
HAWC2 is being presented. Secondly, decay tests of the floating structure’s 6 DoFs, i.e. surge, sway, heave,
roll, pitch and yaw, are being conducted to identify their natural frequencies. The knowledge of the natural
frequencies enables the structure resonance inspection for dynamic performance and loads analysis. Sub-
sequently, the control system for the TLP model is being examined. Initially, a review of the DTU Basic
Controller is being introduced. After that the negative pitch damping problem for floating offshore wind
turbine is presented. Finally, a control system parameters tuning is being performed. The tuning process
involves building a linear model of the TLP model based on the recipe by Jian Tian Zhang in her thesis
Control design and validation for floating wind turbines DTU Wind Energy-M-0472. A step
wind test is performed and it shows that the negative damping problem is being resolved. Last but not
least, a dynamic behaviour investigation of the TLP model under 4 steady wind, 4 turbulent wind and a
turbulent wind with irregular wave simulations are presented. Since the negative damping problem is being
addressed, the dynamic behaviour of the TLP model is satisfactory.
In the third part of this research, design load cases (DLCs) tests are conducted.
These DLCs are 1.1,
1.3, 1.6, 2.1, 2.3, 6.1. DLCs 1.1, 1.3 and 1.6 are normal wind turbine operations under different wind tur-
bulent conditions and sea states. DLC 2.1 and 2.3 simulates grid loss, namely control system failure, with
turbulent wind and extreme operating gust respectively. DLC 6.1 simulates 50 year return extreme wind and
wave conditions with turbine shut down. The applied environmental data is the U.S east coast generic data
published by National Renewable Energy Laboratory(NREL). The analysis of the DLCs simulations focuses
on the floating platform motion and forces, loads, ultimate loads, fatigue loads and mooring line tension.
Comparisons of DLC 1.1 with DLC 1.3, DLC 1.6 and DLC 2.1 respectively are presented. This is because
these loads cases are all simulated with turbulent wind model and wind speeds from cut-in to cut-out wind
speed. From the comparisons between DLC 1.1 and DLC 1.3 and DLC 1.1 and DLC 1.6, it is shown that
in general turbulent wind excites the TLP more than severe sea state. However, the surge, heave, pitch,
tower-base for-after moment, yaw-bearing roll and mooring line tension are exceptions. The comparison of
DLC 1.1 and DLC 2.1 indicates that less excited platform motion and loads and smaller extreme, fatigue
loads and mooring line tension with the control system failure. The analysis of DLC 2.3 shows that the
system behaviour changes according to the time that the grid get disconnected. The analysis of DLC 6.1
shows that lift forces are generated with different yaw misalignment angles. The lift forces then affect the
sway and roll motion. The lift forces also influence the mooring line tensions.
The last part of this research is the dynamic behaviour and loads comparison with Umaine semi-submersible
model. From the dynamic behaviour comparison, it is observed that Umaine semi-submersible model has
significantly larger platform 6DoFs motion. This is due to the smaller mooring stiffness rooted from its

1.1
Floating Offshore Wind Energy
More than 80 % of offshore wind resource in Europe are located at regions with water depths of 60 m
or deeper[1]. However, bottom-fixed turbines are not suitable to be deployed at regions where water depth
is deeper than 50 m. Therefore, for maintaining offshore wind energy’s position in global green transition,
it is of the utmost importance to develop floating offshore wind turbine (FOWT) technology. In comparison
with bottom-fixed offshore wind turbines, there are some advantages for FOWT[2]:
• Stronger and steadier wind at high seas.
• Less interference to human maritime activities, such as commercial shipping and fisheries.
• FOWT installation is less affected by seabed terrain.
• FOWT is easier to be removed, relocated and decommissioned, since they can be towed.
The floating substructure is consisted of the floater, the mooring and anchor systems. The mooring lines
are typically steel chains, metallic wires or synthetic ropes[2]. The mooring lines are connected to the seabed
through anchors. The anchors can be categorized into drag anchors, suction bucket or gravity anchors[2].
The types of floater is divided into 4 categories, shown in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Types of floaters for FOWT[1]
Semi-submersibles: Buoyancy-stabilized floaters with catenary mooring lines and drag anchors[2].
Semi-submersibles have shallow draft and thus are suitable for installation in a variety of site conditions[2].
In addition, it is uncomplicated for transportation[2]. Nevertheless, semi-submersibles are generally heavy
and difficult to manufacture[2].
Spar buoys: Ballast-stabilized floater with catenary mooring lines and drag anchors[2]. Its relatively
simple shape renders its manufacture more simple.
However, its deeper draft poses its applicability to
shallower sites and challenges in transportation[2].
Tension-leg platforms(TLPs): Mooring-stabilized floater with taut lines or tendons as mooring system
and suction buckets or gravity anchors as anchor system[2]. TLPs are shallow draft, low weight and more
stiffness on floater motion[2]. However, TLPs have higher demands on mooring systems and cost more on
installation[2].
Barges: Buoyancy-stabilized floaters with catenary mooring lines and drag anchors[2]. Barges have the
shallowest draft, which renders them to be the easiest to install[2]. However, they experience larger floater
platform motion and thus sometimes include a pool for additional damping[2].

System Dynamic Investigations and Controller Tuning
In this chapter, the system dynamic of the IEA 15 MW reference wind turbine mounted on the CT-
Bos floater with 8 tendons is being thoroughly investigated. The tool used for investigations is HAWC2
(Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine Simulation Code 2nd generation) developed by DTU Wind Energy since
2003[5].
2.1
The Construction of HAWC2 Model
2.1.1
About HAWC2
HAWC2 is an aeroelastic code intended for calculating wind turbine response in time domain and is
consisted of models describing external effect (wind, waves), applied loads (aerodynamics, hydrodynamics),
structural dynamics (multi-body system) and connection to the control system [5]. In addition, HAWC2 is
able to simulate floating offshore wind turbine response via coupling with DLLs (Dynamic Link Libraries)
such as ESYSWamit and ESYSMooring to model the floater and mooring lines.
Before going into the details of the HAWC2 structure, it is important to clarify the difference between
the HAWC2 programs, i.e., HAWC2S, HAWCStab2 and HAWC2.
1. HAWC2S: steady-state calculations for turbines’ deflections, aerodynamic forces and other quantities
under a specific mean wind speeds
2. HAWCStab2: steady-state calculations for the same quantities as HAWC2S under specific mean wind
speeds but provide visualisation in wind turbine mode shapes.
3. HAWC2: time-marching simulations of turbine response for time-varying wind inputs e.g., step winds
and turbulent wind.
The above bulletin points came from Jenni Rinker’s LAC course class note[6]. Since all of the design
load cases include turbulent wind modelling, HAWC2 is heavily utilized in this project. HAWC2S is only
used as a reference during the controller tuning process.

2.1.2
HAWC2 htc file composition
Forces, loads ,wind turbine structure, control system and other DLLs are being composed into one single
script .htc file and then calculated in a computer terminal to produce outputs. The outputs contain wind
turbine, floater and mooring lines response that is required to analyze the system performance. Therefore,
having a full comprehension of the .htc file composition ensures the precision of the results acquired from
later simulations. Therefore, in the following sections, each main block is being meticulously explained.
2.1.3
Simulation Block
The first block is the simulation block,which governs the solver[7]. The command name time stop
dictates the simulation length[7]. The solver used is the Sparse Newmark method(command name solver-
type)[7]. Newmark method is a numerical method for solving second order ODEs especially in structural
dynamics. It is renowned for its ability to reach a stable solution. The β and γ used for Newmark method
is 0.27 and 0.51. The simulation continues when convergence is not obtained in a time step(command name
on no convergence)[7]. The time increment is 0.01s(command name deltat)[7].
2.1.4
New_htc_Structure Block
In this block, contains several subblocks, which all named main_body. The first 2 main bodies are
dummy bodies, one named ifb or interface floating body and the other named beam_sealevel. ifb is
a connection point where the tower and the floater is attached. beam_sealevel is a body stretching from
the ifb to the bottom of the floater and is used in decay test to receive force application[8].

Subsequently, a separate file named bodies.inc defined the structure properties of the tower, towertop,
connector between tower top and the shaft, shaft, 3 hubs that connect the RNA(rotor nacelle assembly) and

wave forces. A total of 8 bodies(8 tendons for CTBos) are being set up.

2.2
Decay Tests with 6 DOF of the Floater
In this section, 6 decay tests are being performed to determine the natural frequencies of the platform
structure. These 6 DoF are surge, sway, heave, roll, pitch and yaw. These tests are performed by first
apply large force or moment on the structure for some time to cause displacement of the structure. Subse-
quently, the external force or moment are withdrawn and by analyzing the PSD (power spectrum density)
plots of the oscillations can we determine the natural frequencies. It is worth to be reminded that the exter-
nal force or moment is applied on the body "beam_sealevel", which is the keel of the floating platform[8].
In each decay test, adequate force or moment will be applied and only the testing DoF will result in a clear
response signal.
One important simulation factor is the simulation time, or precisely when to apply the loads and when
to withdraw them. In this research, the simulation time is being divided into 4 phases:
1. Transient motion: First 80 s
2. Gradually adding load: 85-285 s
3. Full load: 286-685 s
4. Free Oscillation: 686 - 1100s
The reason for including a transient phase is that as the floating platform may produce unrepresentative
response when combining applied load and platform motion transience. Besides, the time for the floating
structure to oscillating freely is also critical.
Insufficient oscillating time can result in unrepresentative
response. In this research, the free oscillation time is chose to be 415 s.

2.3
Control System Tuning Explain
The control system is the most important part of the wind turbine operation. It regulates the blade
pitch angle β, rotational speed ω, generator torque Qg, aerodynamic thrust T and generator power P. The
control system enables wind turbines to harvest optimal wind energy before rated wind speed and limit
ω, T and P after rated wind speed to reduce loads on the system. Furthermore, with the wind industry’s
rapid development towards floating wind turbines, it is paramount to improve existing control technologies
to suit the demands of FOWT. In this section, the control system used in this research will be introduced.
Subsequently, the negative damping issue for FOWT will be discussed and so is the method to solve this
problem. Finally, the system performance will be presented.
2.3.1
DTU Basic Controller
DTU basic controller is the control system developed by DTU for wind turbine operation simulations in
HAWC2[9]. This control system is designed for onshore wind turbines and utilizes state-space pole-placement
PI controller.

refers to the description of system DoF’s motion . In this research the interested DoFs for control system

2.3.2
Negative Pitch Damping Problem
Figure 2.10: FOWT motion with respect to incoming wind[12]
Negative pitch damping problem is a control problem typically faced by FOWT. The problem is re-
sulted from the coupling of blade pitch after rated wind speed and the platform motion. From Figure 2.10,
it shows the motion of the floating platform with respect to the incoming wind. When the floater is in
backward motion, the wind speed sensor on the hub will detect smaller wind speed. This smaller wind
speed emerged from the relative velocity at the hub. The same logic applies for floater motion. The wind
speed sensor will detect higher wind speed. However, before rated wind speed, thrust force increases with
higher wind speed, see Figure 2.11. The thrust force increases when the floater is moving forward and de-
creases when the floater is moving backward. Hence, thrust force serves as a damper before rated wind speed.
After rated wind speed, the blade pitch control system is activated.
The control system regulates
the rotor speed and generator power to a fix value. In the mean time, the aerodynamic thrust is being
generally mitigating with higher wind speed, see Figure 2.12. The decreasing thrust reverses the damping
function it served and exacerbates the floater motion. As a result, the floater surge, pitch will be excited
and subsequently, the wind turbine system performance, including aerodynamic thrust, generator power,
rotational speed and blade pitch.

One simple way to address this issue is to detune the control system[11]. By lowering the controller
frequency, typically lower than the platform motion frequency[11], the system excitation issue will be reduced
and achieve a satisfactory system performance. However, detuning the control system will result in generator
over speed[11]. Regardless of the drawbacks of detuning, it will be utilized in the control parameters tuning
in this research. One interesting point to be reminded is that applying constant torque control in Region 3
reduces the pitch activity and also abates the negative pitch damping problem.

3.1
Metocean Conditions
Offshore wind turbines undergo loading from a variety of natural forces, including wind, waves and
current. Thus, when one is conducting load analyses, the metocean data play a significant role on the result-
ing outputs and conclusions.[15] However, high-quality metocean data is hard to find. In this research, the
generic metocean data set that characterized the east coast of the USA is implemented [15] into the HAWC2
DLCs simulations. This data set was created by researchers at the University of Massachusetts, Amherst and
the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) and the data source came from the 23 sites of data-
collection buoys, which belongs to the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Association(NOAA), scattered
throughout the US waters with at least 5 years of data collection[15]. Each sites measured 5 signals that
were deemed crucial for offshore wind energy applications: mean wind speed, significant wave height, wave
peak-spectral period, wind direction and wave direction[15]. Wind speed and direction data were collected
as average over 8 minutes. In addition, most of the anemometers are at 5 m above sea level, with a few at 10
m. Significant wave height was collected as 20 minutes average height of the highest one-third of waves[15].
Peak-spectral period was the wave period with the maximum wave energy over 20 minutes measurement
time. Wave direction was collected as the direction from which the dominant-period waves arrived[15].

3. Constraint: It refers to the forces and moments exerted on a body immersed in a fluid, which arise
due to the interaction between the body and the surrounding fluid.
As a result, the constraint force and moment are chosen to become subject for this research.

4.3.5
Conclusion for the Models Comparison
After comparing DLC 1.1, 1.3, 1.6 and 6.1, it is being summarized that Umaine model, due to its lower
platform stiffness, has larger platform displacements and subsequently larger loads in most cases. In addition,
the tension that one mooring line sustained is also smaller for TLP model. However, it is worth noting that
TLP model has 8 tendons while Umaine model has 3 catenary lines. Therefore, it is expected that Umaine
model’s mooring lines sustained more tension. Last but not least, in the industry, the superiority of platform
model is being considered in a more complicated process. Factors such as installation and maintenance costs
are considered equally crucial as the system loads. However, if financial considerations are omitted, one can
say that TLP model is a better platform since it has less loads and the system is anticipated to be able to
operate longer.

5
Conclusion and recommendation for future studies
In this research, the load and dynamic investigations of TLP floating offshore wind turbine system are
performed. All the investigations can be categorized into three parts. First part is the dynamic performance
analysis of TLP model. A study of the HAWC2 structure is first carried out so that one can be totally sure that
the model is 100 % correct and ready for simulation. Next, the natural frequencies for the floating platform
6 DoFs are identified via decay tests. Subsequently, the control system negative pitch damping problem is
addressed. In order to acquire control system parameters that brings about a stable system operation, a
linear model for TLP model is constructed. Thereafter, the state-space differential equation is established
in combination with the control parameters. Thus, by inspecting the eigenvalues of the differential equa-
tion, the suitable control parameters are discovered. A following system performance test proves that TLP
model’s stable operation. Thereon, 4 steady wind, 4 turbulent wind and a turbulent wind with irregular
waves tests are performed. In the steady wind tests, it is observes that the surge, heave and pitch motions’
magnitude follow the trend of aerodynamic thrust. The same tendency also applies for the turbulent wind
tests. The turbulent wind with irregular wave test reveals that surge, heave and pitch motions are excited
by the wave spectrum peak frequency.
For the DLCs tests, the wind and wave spectrum frequencies differ for cases. Therefore, a inspection for
resonance between wind and wave spectrum, 1P and 3P rotational frequencies and platform 6 DoFs natural
frequencies are conducted. Luckily, no significant resonance issue occurs. For DLC 1.1 analysis, besides the
tendency plots of platform 6 DoFs motion, force and moment, loads and tower clearance, there also include
the ultimate loads, fatigue loads and ultimate tendons’ tension plots. Power spectrum density(PSD) plots
of the time signals of the above channels are incorporated. They reveal that the surge and heave motion are
coupled for TLP model. In addition, the signal of TBFA is excited by pitch motion signal and the signal
of TBSS is excited by roll motion signal. The tendons’ tension plot demonstrates that the tensions of the
wind-facing tendons are higher than that of the tendons on the other side of the platform. In the comparison
of DLC 1.1 vs DLC 1.3 and DLC 1.1 vs DLC 1.6, it is observed that turbulent wind has a bigger effect on
the system performance in contrast to severe sea state. However, the surge, heave, pitch, TBFA, YBRO and
mooring line tension are exceptions. Therefore, if deploying TLP at sites with severe sea state, it is crucial
to monitor its TBFA’s fatigue issue. For DLC 1.1 vs DLC 2.1 comparison, after the grid disconnected, the
platform 6 DoFs show less excitation and it’s probably due to the loss of aerodynamic thrust owing to the
failure of control system. In addition, the thrust-related and torque-related loads stop showing trends that
corresponds to thrust or torque curve. The ultimate loads, fatigue loads and ultimate tendons’ tension are
smaller in contrast to DLC 1.1. For DLC 2.3, grid loss 3s show the largest ultimate TBFA and TBSS loads.
This is probably due to the large thrust drop when the grid got disconnected. However, grid loss 10s has the
largest tendons’ tension. It should also be noted that the tendons that aren’t wind-facing have the largest
tensions. This can be explained by the system experienced negative thrust force after grid disconnection.
For DLC 6.1, 80 yaw misalignment error generates negative lift force and brings about positive sway offset
and negative roll offset. −80 yaw misalignment error generates positive lift force and brings about negative
sway offset and positive roll offset. The PSD plots of the platform 6 DoFs motion reveals that they are all
excited by the wave spectrum peak frequency and that heave, roll and pitch are all excited by surge natural
frequency. This indicates that when the rotor is parked, surge motion becomes the dominant motion of the
platform. The lift forces generate by yaw misalignment errors press one side of the platform and prompt
uneven tensions among the tendons.
The comparison of dynamic behaviour between Umaine and TLP models shows that Umaine model has sig-
nificant lower platform stiffness and thus larger platform motion. This also results in larger loads,ultimate
loads, fatigue loads and ultimate tendons’ tension. The only exception is the TBFA loads for DLC 6.1.
The larger TBFA and TBSS loads for Umaine model are not only because of larger pitch motion, but also
gravity moments generated by said motion. Therefore, TLP model is a platform that has a more satisfactory
performance than Umaine model.

Conclusion and recommendation for future studies
Based on the above listed findings there are some recommendations for further study for TLP model.
• Develop new control system method to not only avoid pitch damping problem, but also avoid the
detuning process. The detuning process will result in generator over speed and larger shaft load.
• Investigation on why TLP model’s TBFA ultimate load is larger than Umaine model’s.
Overall, tension-leg platform is a promising option for FOWT development. This report contributes a
detailed load and dynamic investigations for TLP and performance comparison with Umaine model. It is
hoped that the findings of this research can be a useful reference for future FOWT researches and develop-
ment.
