Abstract
Conversations can be viewed as interlocutors taking turns in talking. The duration of silence between
a talker ending and an adjacent talker starting his / her turn, is called Floor Transfer Offset.
While a gap between two interlocutors corresponds to a positive transfer oﬀset, an overlap of two
talkers’ turns is deﬁned by a negative transfer oﬀset.
Interestingly, talkers are able to neatly align their turns by anticipating turn ends of an interlocutor.
Anticipation is thereby based on interpreting turn-end yielding cues, which are based on mimics,
gestures, lexico-syntactic and prosodic meta-information, given by an interlocutor.
In the present study, danish speech tokens were isolated and extracted from a speech corpus that
included recordings in noise and quiet, and was played back to participants who had the task to
predict the end of a token. As subjects were placed in a booth and listened to prerecorded audio, no
mimics gestures or eye-contact have been available. Thus, listeners could only rely on lexico-syntactic
(danish subjects only) and prosodic cues.
In an acoustic feature analysis, diﬀerences in speech produced in noise and quiet were identiﬁed. Speech
produced in noise exhibited characteristics that reduced saliency of turn-end yielding cues, which could
be conﬁrmed by behavioral data. Subjects prediction performance as measured as response BIAS
(delay between speech oﬀset and button-release event) decreased signiﬁcantly for Lompard-speech
(i.e. speech produced in noise). The experiment further involved listening in noise and with a speech
shaped noise masker (ICRA07), which had mixed eﬀects on the two populations (i.e. danish vs. non-
danish). Whereas danish listeners always exhibited a lower prediction performance when listening in
noise, non-danish listeners were able to perform better, given that the speech was Lombard-speech. It
was concluded that due to the deterioration of the majority of turn-yielding cues in Lombard-speech,
a masking of the latter yields to better turn-end prediction performance.
The experiment further indicated, that turn-end prediction performance on a token is not related to
the transfer oﬀset, that followed the respective token in the original recording, from which the tokens
were drawn from.

Introduction
Conversations can be viewed as interlocutors taking turns in talking. The duration of silence between
a talker ending and an adjacent talker starting his / her turn, is called Floor Transfer Offset.
While a gap between two interlocutors corresponds to a positive transfer oﬀset, an overlap of two
talkers’ turns is deﬁned by a negative transfer oﬀset.
Interestingly, talkers are able to neatly align their turns by anticipating turn ends of an interlocutor. A
proof that talkers indeed predict will be presented in the background section. Anticipation is thereby
based on interpreting turn-end yielding cues, which are based on mimics, gestures, lexico-syntactic
and prosodic meta-information, given by an interlocutor.
In a recent study by Sørensen (2017), ﬂoor transfer oﬀset distributions were quantiﬁed for conversations
held in quiet vs. noise. It was hypothesised that the presence of background noise would make turn-
end anticipation more diﬃcult, which would manifest in a shifted and broader distribution of ﬂoor
transfer oﬀsets. The opposite was observed.
It was then suggested, that while speaking in noise, interlocutors alter their vocal output to presumably
increase intelligibility and thereby, increase saliency of turn-end yielding cues.
1.1
Hypotheses
To follow up on the results found by Sørensen (2017), the present work focuses on whether Lombard
speech is able to increase saliency of cues used to predict turn ends. Two hypotheses were formulated:
1. H01: Listeners will be more accurate in predicting turn ends when listening to Lombard speech
compared to speech produced in quiet.
2. H02: Noise will reduce accuracy of predicting turn ends.
1.2
Experimental Paradigm
To address these hypotheses, an experimental paradigm similar to De Ruiter et al. (2006); Riest et al.
(2015) was followed, presenting isolated speech units whilst measuring subjects’ response delays as
a measure of turn-end anticipation performance. To unravel particular contributions, experimental
conditions involved listening to speech units presented in quiet and in presence of background noise as
well as presentations of normal- and lombard speech units, intermixed. Additionally to native danes
listening to danish speech units, non-danish subjects were recruited also, thus allowing to diﬀerentiate
which cues deteriorate.

Dynamics in Conversations
Conversation is described by the Cambridge Advanced Learner’s Dictionary as talk between two or
more people in which thoughts, feelings, and ideas are expressed, questions are asked and answered,
or news and information is exchanged [2013]. In order to establish conversation, speakers are required
to produce and perceive verbal information. The latin word conversatio is translated as familiar
intercourse, which already hints that a certain dynamic is vital to conversation, i.e. ongoing and
smooth interactions when taking turns in receiving and producing speech.
Conversations have been investigated in several studies, the ﬁrst to conceptualize a turn taking
organization were Sacks et al. (1974), who have used audio recordings of natural conversations to
extract and characterize types of interrelations of conversational sequences. In the paper A simplest
systematics for the organization of turn-taking for conversation, the authors state
that “overwhelmingly, one party talks at a time, although speakers change and size of turns and
ordering varies”. The required ﬁnely coordination is achieved by deploying techniques for allocating
turns, construction of utterances relevant to the a turn status, coordination of turn transfers and the
allocation of speakership.
Sacks et al. (1974) further promote that the organization of conversations must be governed by some
formal, and context1 insensitive, apparatus. In other words, the maxim (i.e. basic set of rules) that
governs conversational dynamics must be commonly known and well established. In fact, Keitel and
Daum (2015) have shown that 3 year old children already have the ability to reliably anticipate a turn
changes. The researchers used recordings of puppets making conversation and used eye-tracking to
detect turn change anticipation.
Sacks et al. (1974) elaborate on a basic set of rules that govern turn allocation and the coordination of
transfer in terms of minimizing gap between and overlap of two speakers. According to the authors,
turn transfers tend to occur at syntactic "possible completion points", which are termed Transition-
Relevance Place (abbreviated: TRP). Whether such a completion point marks a turn transfer is
highly determined by the extend of aligned turn-end yielding cues, see section 2.2.
2.1
Interaction Statistics
It is suitable to investigate verbal interaction by the means of the temporal relation between a turn
and its response, i.e. the duration between consecutive speakers. In literature, these durations are
commonly named Floor Transfer Offset (abbreviated FTO). Whereas positive transfers oﬀ-
sets correspond to gaps, negative oﬀsets correspond to an overlap of two consecutive speakers’ turns.
Figure 2.1 illustrates that an overlap can be further classiﬁed as within- or between-overlap. This
distinction is crucial, as within-overlaps mostly2 accommodate Backchannels, which are produced
by a listener to indicate attentiveness (see section 2.2). A backchannel therefor bears no propositional
content and does not intent to take the ﬂoor from the current speaker. As further mentioned by
Lawkowski (2010), it is controversially discussed whether a backchannel constitutes a turn. For the
present study, a backchannel is not considered a turn.

scale speech production processes with respect to timing.

verbal output. In a comprehensive meta-analysis of literature on word production, Indefrey and Levelt

speech production. A detailed listing of the involved regions and timing can be viewed in table 2.2.
Common in these studies is the use of pictures to measure the time interval between the event of

2.1.3
Adaptations to environmental changes
It can be assumed, that conversation is optimized to be eﬃcient, which entails a fast exchange of
information, that desires no redundancy and no repetitions. On the other hand, turn comprehen-
sion suﬀers from poor intelligibility and thus does desire a certain level of redundancy and adequate
speed, i.e.
rate of speech and turn transitions.
In challenging environmental conditions, humans
adapt automatically to optimize speech intelligibility on both ends, i.e. in production and perception.
As example, Dreher and O’Neill (1957); Summers et al. (1988) found, that speech produced in noise
exhibits spectral and temporal characteristics that are diﬀerent from speech produced in quiet. This
eﬀect was initially discovered in 1909 by Étienne Lombard, and the phenomenon is named after him.
With respect to conversations, it is especially interesting is to investigate changes in interaction statis-
tics e.g. changes in FTO distributions. Sørensen (2017) computed interaction statistics from a task
oriented speech corpus. Subjects were seated in separate, sound treated, and closed up booths foiling
any visual cues. While being recorded, subjects had a task that required collaboration and thus, en-
forced natural verbal interaction. Recordings were obtained in quiet and in noise at a constant noise
level at 70 dBA. For danish subjects speaking their in mother tongue and in noise, Sørensen (2017)
found signiﬁcant drops in transfer oﬀset median by 28 ms, mean by 71.6 ms and standard deviation by
68 ms compared to the quiet condition, suggesting that turn transitions are more rapid and temporar-
ily more distinct in noisy environment. Intuitively, one would expect the opposite; Given a higher
5
==PAGE:10==
cognitive load imposed on the talkers in noise condition and given that the noise used had speech
like spectral and temporal properties and thus imposes time variant masking of variable strength on
diﬀerent phonemes, it was expected that FTO distributions shift to longer durations and increase in
variance, respectively.
Sørensen (2017) deducts, that there may be a “universal pattern in turn taking dynamics that are more
important to maintain than other properties of speech”. In other words, targeting a certain statistic in
ongoing conversational dynamics might a measure of “good conversation”. Thus, conversation could
be viewed as a dance, where the rhythm of turn taking is prioritized over information transfer.
Clearly worth questioning is, what do talkers optimize for if doesn’t necessarily need to be intelli-
gibility? Listed below are changes in conversational dynamics for danish talkers talking danish as
observed by Sørensen (2017). All changes were veriﬁed to be statistically signiﬁcant and quantiﬁed
for conversations in background noise relative to a quiet condition.
In noise;
• Articulation rate increased by 37 %, which according to Levinson and Torreira (2015) remains
still below the speed of comprehension and thus eﬀectively yields to increase of information
conveyed.
• Pause duration increased by 14 %, which was reasoned by higher vocal eﬀort and overall longer
IPU durations.
• Number of pauses per minute decreased by 42 %, thus leaving fewer spaces available for the
interlocutor to take a turn.
• Duration of the overlaps-within increased by 19 %, however showing more one-syllable overlaps
in quiet, whereas there were more two-three syllables in noise. Sørensen (2017) presumes that
“one-syllable answers are backchannels or agreement present-indications, whereas longer overlaps
may be attempts to take the ﬂoor”. One could also argue, that an increase of longer syllables
in noise is a evidence for poor speech intelligibility. As the task provided a limited contextual
frame and since talkers are able to interact, operating at a lower speech intelligibility level might
be compensated for by retrieving missed words using context.
• Duration of the IPUs substantially increased by 39 %. Sørensen (2017) elaborates investigation
on this and ﬁnds that “for longer IPUs preceding an FTO, the distribution of the FTO gets
narrower and the median decreases”, which is also supported by Gravano and Hirschberg (2011),
p. 614. The interlocutor thus has more time to plan and to accurately set up his turn. This is
a key ﬁnding, as it may be a strategy to maintain the overall distribution of FTOs.
2.2
Prediction Cues
Speakers are able to anticipate the end of a turn of an interlocutor. This can be deduced either from
statistical analysis of ﬂoor transfer distributions with respect to speech production latencies, as done
by Sellen (1995); Stivers et al. (2009); Levinson and Torreira (2015), or by conducting experiments
that speciﬁcally ask the subjects to indicate the end of a turn by e.g. pressing a button, as done e.g.
by De Ruiter et al. (2006); Riest et al. (2015). Common understanding is, that interlocutors have
strategies to organize turn-taking by producing and interpreting Cues e.g. for a possible turn transfer
location. According to Sacks et al. (1974) turn transfers can be initiated either by “current speaker
selects the next” or by “self selection” of the next speaker. Thus, speakers need to be able to convey
6
==PAGE:11==
the intention of holding or releasing a ﬂoor. In literature (Beattie and Barnard (1979); ın Gravano
(2009); Gravano and Hirschberg (2011); Levinson and Torreira (2015)), 3 types of cues are commonly
distinguished; Turn-Holding Cues are given if the current speaker wants to prevent turn taking
attempts by the interlocutor. In contrast, Turn-Yielding Cues indicate that the current speaker
is about to release the ﬂoor and thereby prompting the interlocutor to engage. A third, referred to
as Backchannel-Inviting Cue precedes a Backchannel, i.e. short expressions such as uh-hu or
mm-hm that indicate listerns’ attentiveness. Thus, the interlocutor is requested to engage shortly,
but not to take the ﬂoor.
Turn-taking is organized in ongoing speech, thus talkers need to either use nonverbal-channels such as
gestures and facial expressions, or rely on meta-information derived from distinctive lexicosyntactic
or acoustic patterns. The means of communicating turn organization is from now on referred to as
Modality.
2.2.1
Nonverbal Modality
Bosch et al. (2005) have compared counts of overlaps and pauses at speaker change for spontaneous
dialogs taken from telephone and face-to-face conversations.
The authors found that number of
overlaps and pauses were reduced by 41.1% and 18.1%, respectively.
The relative proportion of
overlaps decreased from 52 % for telephone to 44 % in a face-to-face setting, thus clearly revealing the
advantage of having visual cues contributing to turn-end anticipation. A more detailed review and
classiﬁcation of nonverbal “channels” with respect to diﬀerent languages can be read in Stivers et al.
(2009), however will not be considered further in the present work.
2.2.2
Lexico-syntactic Modality
Although objective assessment of lexico-syntactic cues is relatively cumbersome, several studies have
been dedicated to determine the extent to which lexico-syntactic information regulates turn-end an-
ticipation performance.
De Ruiter et al. (2006) extracted turns from a dutch corpus and designed and experiment in which
subjects were asked to indicate the end of a turn by pressing a button. To disentangle relative contri-
butions of diﬀferent cues, the presented turns were modiﬁed to be either natural, have no pitch, no
words, but pitch and no pitch, nor words as well as a reference condition containing only noise
of average turn duration. The researchers compare the BIAS3 distribution and ﬁnd that removing
lexical content has a strong detrimental eﬀect on prediction accuracy. By contrast, removing pitch
information had no inﬂuence on turn end anticipation accuracy at all, thus concluding that, for dutch
listeners, “lexicosyntactic structure is necessary (and possibly suﬃcient) for accurate end-of-turn pro-
jection, while intonational structure, perhaps surprisingly, is neither necessary nor suﬃcient”.
In a later study, Riest et al. (2015) conduct a similiar experiment, however introducing the conditions
Natural Turn, Noise, Advance-Knowledge and Scrambled Word Order. When comparing
the performance in terms of the BIAS accuracy, a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between Scrambled Word
Oder and Noise as well as Scrambled Word Oder and Natural Turn conditions was found,
supporting that although lexical information is a cue itself and supports prediction performance, best
results were achieved with lexical and syntactic information available.
The reseachers further found no signiﬁcant diﬀerence when comparing Natural Turn and Advance-
Knowledge, suggesting that prediction is as good as a priori knowledge, which veriﬁes the statement

2.2.3
Acoustic Modality
Studies exploring turn anticipation in early childhood show, that intonation eﬀectively supports the
perception of conversations and helps to anticipate a speaker’s next turn (compare Keitel and Daum
(2015), Figure 5). In fact, the researchers found that adult performance in turn end anticipation also
increases comparing normal and ﬂattened intonation. In contrast to DeRuiter, ﬁndings from Keitel
and Daum (2015) indicate that adults may be more ﬂexible in terms of which cues to follow. Thus,
“trained talkers” have better means of assessing the most reliable cue. This is supported by Levinson
and Torreira (2015), who suggests that lexicon and syntax, although being a key guide to turn-taking
prediction, are supported by intonation and aspects of prosody and articulation.

hold) is signiﬁcant. Figures taken from ın Gravano (2009).
Duncan (1972) suggests that “individually identiﬁable cues may be combined to form a complex
turn-yielding cue”, which is supported by ﬁndings from ın Gravano (2009) who found linear rela-
tionship between the number of aligned turn-yielding cues and the number of turn taking attempts.
Additive Eﬀects in turn-yielding cues have also been found for non-participating listeners by Hjal-
marsson (2009). ın Gravano (2009); Gravano and Hirschberg (2011) extracted a number of features
in speech taken from the Columbia Games Corpus. Separate analysis of turn holdings and turn
switches revealed, that a falling or rising of intonation, a higher speech rate, a longer IPU duration, a
lower intensity, a lower overall pitch level, a higher frequency of jitter, shimmer and noise-to-harmonics
ratio (NHR) coincide more likely with a turn switch, and thus may be relevant as turn-yielding cues
(compare ﬁgure 2.2).

3
Methodology
This chapter summarizes all methods used for stimuli selection and preprocessing of speech units.
Also, experiment preparation and implementation as well as data analysis methods are discussed.
3.1
Stimuli
The conducted experiment required a minimum of 440 inter-pausal-units (from now on referred to
as IPUs or tokens) to cover the actual experiment and a training session (see table 3.1). As it is
favorable to use the same speech corpus whose statistics motivated parts of the present work, tokens
were extracted from the speech corpus recorded by Sørensen (2017).
A brief recapitulation on how the speech corpus was obtained can be found in section 3.1.1, a descrip-
tion on how unit-extraction was implemented and parametrized is given in section 3.1.2, procedures for
automatic feature extraction using Matlab and Praat, an application for phonetic analysis developed
by Boersma and Weenink (2017), are described in section 3.3.
3.1.1
Speech Corpus
The corpus was recorded by Sørensen (2017) at the Hearing Systems lab facilities at DTU, Denmark.
40 normal-hearing, native-Danish speakers of age 26 (σ2 = 3.7) years were recruited and participated
in pairs of both, same and mixed gender. To elicit natural conversations, pairs had to solve a Dipix-
task that required collaboration. In more detail, the two participants would see drawings showing the
same scenery, which however diﬀered in 10 details (e.g. the number of apples on a display, the color of
a T-shirt, etc). To further support natural conversation, pairs were recruited as friendly pairs. This
had the further advantage of having already established and thus constant conversational dynamics
when solving the task.
Participants were seated in separate booths and conversations were monitored by the experimenter.
Pairs solved tasks in quiet and in presence of the ICRA07, a mixed gender 6-talker speech shaped
background noise. When the noise was present, talkers raised their voice by an average of 9 dB,
resulting in an average SNR of -2 dB.
Comparing the increment of 9 dB in speech level to what has been reported by Summers et al. (1988),
ﬁg. 1, and Dreher and O’Neill (1957), ﬁg. 3, it can be assumed, that speech recorded in this condition
will show the acoustic properties that characterize lombard speech. An investigation of the acoustic
fetures of the stimuli used for the present work will follow in section 3.3.
3.1.2
IPU Extraction
120 recordings with an average duration of ﬁve to seven minutes containing a single speaker on an
individual channel were processed ﬁrst by the means of a Voice Activity Detector (VAD). To do so,
1. compute instantaneous RMS-amplitudes with a temporal resolution of 1 ms,
2. threshold signals at -40 dB FS, yielding two tracks containing binary voice activity marking,
3. low-pass ﬁlter such that gaps of 180 ms or less are bridged by the algorithm.
In other words, two tokens by the same talker, that are separated by a pause of 180 ms or less, will
be merged to one unit.

To compare natural conversation ﬂoor transfers with anticipation of single IPU presentations, the
paradigm required speciﬁcally turn-ends.
• Classiﬁed as a turn were speech tokens that were of duration t ≥930 ms, which is the minimum
IPU duration found by De Ruiter et al. (2006) and thus rather arbitrary.
• To be further classiﬁed as a turn-end, speech tokens had to be followed by a response by an
interlocutor. Thus, a voice activity oﬀset by one speaker had to be followed by voice activity
onset of the interlocutor.
• An interlocutor was not allowed to produce backchannels, however was allowed to start his/her
turn 200 ms before the ﬂoor-holder ended his turn. This criteria was set up to have a distribution
of turn transfers that is as close as possible to the distribution found in natural conversation.
Again, the interlocutors’ disruption had to be a turn, and thus of t ≥930 ms duration in order
to be classiﬁed as response and not as callback.
This automated procedure so far yielded on average 60 IPUs per recording, summing up to about
7200 IPUs. By random selection, circa 3200 IPUs were manually assessed and rejected in case of the
presence of laughter and nonsense verbalizations.

3.1.3
Manipulation
For further processing, 440 tokens were randomly selected to be an equal number of speech produced
in quiet vs. background noise. No other parameters (such as gender, pairs, dipix) were controlled
10
==PAGE:15==
for. However, since tokens were selected randomly, it can be assumed that the selection inherits the
distribution of genter, pairs, dipix, etc., as given in the speech corpus.
The noise condition in the recording of the speech corpus represents an acoustic manipulation itself, as
it signiﬁcantly changes the spectro-temporal properties of the speech tokens presented to the subjects
(see section 4.1 to explore the acoustic features that altered due to the Lombard eﬀect).
All tokens were presented either in quiet or in a -2.4 dB SNR noise condition. The noise was hann-
windowed and played back starting 0.5 s before and 1 s after IPU On/Oﬀset, respectively. Of course,
the same unit was never presented to the same subject twice (i.e. in noise and in quiet). In fact, each
subject was presented a unique subset of tokens to exploit maximum randomization.
Again, it was preferable to have the listening conditions of the turn-end anticipation experiment as
similar as possible to the actual conversation.
Therefore, the noise sequence was taken from the
same time interval where the unit came from.
In other words, the noise that may have allowed
“listening in the dips” during the conversation would allow the same in the prediction experiment.
No quantitative analysis has been done on this, but since the ICRA07 noise can be considered as
time-variant, independent multichannel amplitude modulation, it is assumed, that speech produced
in such noise inherits some of the acoustic properties that are present in the noise. For example, a
short term rise in speech intensity may be misinterpreted as a turn-end yielding cue, but is in fact a
result of the talker keeping an instantaneous SNR (or intelligibility?) constant (this will be important
in the discussion).
A second manipulation in a wider sense is the selection of non-danish participants as test subjects as
it relates perfectly to the experimental condition set up e.g. by Riest et al. (2015), who manipulated
turn ends to be non-intelligible, however leaving prosodic information in tact.
3.2
Experiment
11 native danish speakers and 4 non-danish speakers participated in the experiment. All subjects were
young adults with an average age of 25.5 (σ=2.6) years and reported no hearing diﬃculties. Non-
danish subjects resided in Denmark for less then a year and had not taken danish classes. Stimuli
were always presented at an average sound pressure level of 61 dB rel. 20 µPa.
3.2.1
Measurement Setup
It was desired to have a portable setup, which would allow testing of future subjects in a foreign lan-
guage country. Although high precision digital measurement devices and software exists, the solution
presented here is surprisingly simple and cheap. Figure ?? (MAKE SKETCH) shows a sketch of the
experiment.
An RME UCX usb audio I/O device was conﬁgured to have two parallel output channels and a single
input channel. While one of the outputs was ampliﬁed (Headphone amp) and played back to the
subject via headphones (Sennheiser HD650), the second output was routed to a customized keyboard
(see ﬁgure 3.3) and redirected back to the audio I/O device.

The keyboards plus-key was modiﬁed to act as a switch, bypassing the signal when pressed down
and disconnecting when released. Subjects started an audio presentation by pressing and holding this
plus-key and were asked to release the key at the point in time they anticipated the turn to stop. A
pressed down button connects audio-device output and input. In the event of the subject releasing
the button, connection was interrupted, although the signal presented over headphones was played
back to the end, i.e. participants had no means of prematurely interrupting playback, however could
decide when to start the next speech unit.
A cross-correlation of audio input and output signal revealed a time-variant lag, which was used to
compute the exact time of the button release event in the given stimuli. Subtracting the latter from
the time of IPU-(voice-activity)-oﬀset yields the subjects’ BIAS.
3.3
Data Analysis
Running the experiment, data preprocessing and evaluation was done in Matlab. For the analysis of
acoustic features, a command line version of praat, an analysis software by Boersma and Weenink
(2017) specialized for phonetics, was incorporated in Matlab procedures. The respective PraatScripts
used to obtain acoustic features were adopted from de Jong and Wempe (2009) for speech-rate esti-
mations, Eckey and MacDonald (2015) for fundamental and intensity tracks, Gravano and Hirschberg
(2011) for vocal jitter, shimmer and noise to harmonic ratio (NHR). The scripts can be viewed in
appendix section A.3.
Intensity is related to the output sound pressure, fundamental frequency (and harmonics) describe
the vibration of the vocal folds. Speech-rate, deﬁned as syllables or phonemes per time unit, is an
indicator for speech ﬂuency. Jitter, shimmer and noise-to-harmonics ratio (NHR) correlate with the
perception of voice quality. Jitter is a measure of deviation from periodicity and thus relates to the
variation in fundamental frequencies. Shimmer is a measure of variation in amplitude (Morris and
Harmon (2010)). NHR is computed as the ratio of noise to harmonic components in voiced speech.
Statistical Analyses entailed independent-samples t-test of equal variance, right-tailed t-tests, correla-
tion analyses and repeated-measures ANOVAs, which were performed in Matlab and SPSS statistics
software.

4.2
Analysis of Behavioral Data
In this section, response BIAS data obtained from the experiment is presented. The results do show
contrasts between experimental conditions, however need to be put in perspective considering the
experimental paradigm. The measurement technique did not allow any alternative-forced-choice im-
plementation. Hence, there was no way of pulling subject performance to an optimum (or at least to
know when subjects are sleepwalking and simply reacting to turn ends).
To better align the results, a brief response test was conducted to have some sort of baseline perfor-
mance, that is known to be reaction. Subjects performed the same button press task as introduced in
section 3.2, however with white noise played back. The logic is, that subjects have no cues available
and thus have to simply react to the end of the noise. In this condition, subjects exhibit an average
response BIAS of 202.90 ms (σ = 52.83 ms).

5
Discussion
To address the hypotheses stated in section 1.1, the results of the acoustical analysis of speech tokens
are discussed and linked to turn-end yielding importance. Data from the behavioral experiment was
aligned with these ﬁndings, however only by making some assumptions on prediction behavior.
A measure of eﬀectiveness was deployed to investigate underlying mechanism that would explain
subject behavior and interactions in diﬀerent conditions. At last, a section is dedicated to the abnormal
behavior for longer IPU durations.
5.1
Saliency of turn-end yielding cues in Lombard speech
To a priori investigate, whether Lombard speech is able to increase the saliency of acoustic cues
used for turn-end anticipation, section 5.1.1 is dedicated to a discussion on which acoustic features of
speech are actually relevant, to what extent these are present the speech tokens used for the current
experiment and lastly, how these features are presumably weighted in turn taking decisions. In chapter
5.1.2, results from the behavioral experiment are reviewed.
5.1.1
Stimuli Feature Exploration
In an investigation carried out by Gravano and Hirschberg (2011), speech tokens were categorized in
two groups, depending on if they preceded a turn-switch or turn-hold. They ﬁnd, that pitch slope,
speaking rate, IPU duration, intensity, absolute pitch, jitter, shimmer and NHR are signiﬁcantly dif-
ferent between the groups.
The reciprocal logic is then, that a certain conﬁguration of these features is present when a talker
intends to convey a turn-end. It is controversially discussed to which extent acoustic features -in
contrast to lexico-syntactic information- determine turn-endings. Whereas De Ruiter et al. (2006);
Riest et al. (2015) suggest, that projection is solely based on lexico-syntactic information, Gravano
and Hirschberg (2011); Hjalmarsson (2009) argue that lexico-syntactic information may be suﬃcient,
however prediction more accurate when prosodic information is available.
Assuming, that turn-end prediction does rely on prosodic information, prediction performance should
be better (i.e. more accurate, perhaps earlier), when acoustical features are more emphasized. It was
then hypothesized that Lombard-speech could provide emphasis on these features.
Indeed, the results in section 4.1 show signiﬁcant changes in phonetic features as well as IPU dura-
tion. No eﬀect was observed for overall intensity, which can be explained easily by the normalization
in preprocessing (stimuli were kept on a constant sound pressure level). Although features that are
presumably relevant for turn-switch / turn-hold distinction are signiﬁcantly diﬀerent when comparing
Lombard- to normal speech, this doesn’t mean that turn-end prediction cues are more salient.
Gravano and Hirschberg (2011) found that turn switches are more likely to follow, when a preceding
IPU exhibits an emphasized prosody (i.e. F0 slope), faster speaking-rate, lower intensity and overall
pitch, higher Jitter, shimmer and NHR. Finally, IPUs of longer duration are more likely to be followed
by a turn switch.
Results in ﬁgures 4.1 & 4.2 show a mixed eﬀect in Lombard-speech. Speech tokens obtained from
conversations in noise exhibit a lower speech-rate, jitter, shimmer and NHR. These phonetic properties
are thereby pointing to a turn-holding intention of the talker.
However, intonation and changes in overall IPU duration are in line with the ﬁndings for turn-switches.
Since the eﬀects are mixed, it is diﬃcult to state whether Lombard speech does or does not increase
19
==PAGE:24==
saliency of turn-end yielding cues, although the majority of eﬀects is rather discouraging.
Still, there has been a simpliﬁcation made on the way: In line with the proposal by Duncan et al.
(1979) and the “additive eﬀects” observed by Hjalmarsson (2009), Gravano and Hirschberg (2011)
suggest that cues must be combined to form more “complex turn-yielding signals”. In Gravano and
Hirschberg (2011), p.618, a table lists the total count of turn-switched and holds observed for simul-
taneous occurrences of various turn-yielding cues, i.e. intonation, speaking rate, intensity level, pitch
level, IPU duration, voice quality (jitter, shimmer, NHR combined) and textual completion. A copy
of the table can be found in the appendix, table A.3. By ﬁtting the data to a logistic regression
model, Gravano and Hirschberg (2011) assigned some sort of turn-yielding power to individual cues,
ﬁnding that “textual completion cue ranks ﬁrst in importance, followed by voice quality, speaking
rate, intensity level, pitch level and IPU duration”. No evidence could be found for the importance of
intonation.
Intonation and IPU duration supposedly have none or only minor turn-yielding power, respectively.
Since these cues are the only cues that are present in the Lombard-speech tokens, an improvement of
prediction accuracy would be even less likely.
There is more detail to be considered: Hjalmarsson (2009) found that turn-holding cues are judged
signiﬁcantly faster than IPUs with mostly turn-ending cues present. Thinking about turn-end predic-
tion in terms of signal detection theory, a false alarm seems to be more expensive in conversation
than a miss. In other words, a optimal detector (i.e. talkers) rather have extended gaps (misses) as
disruption of conversational ﬂow (false alarms).
In any way, the two turn-end yielding cues that have been found in the Lombard-speech are supposedly
the least important turn-end indicators, the presence of relatively stronger turn-holding cues and the
fact that interlocutors are more sensitive to the latter may counter longer duration and emphasized
intonation.
5.1.2
Evidence in Behavioral Data
While the stimuli analysis already hints that turn-end yielding cues in Lombard-speech are less likely
to be more salient, results from the behavioral experiment support that listening to Lombard speech
has a negative eﬀect on turn-end prediction performance. Two separate ANOVAs were carried out and
showed signiﬁcant eﬀects of the factor “production” for the danish [F(1, 10) = 7.395, p = .022] and
the non-danish [F(1, 3) = 14.080, p = .033] population. Averaging subjects of each group, the median
response BIAS was larger for Lombard-speech for danish and non-danish listeners, respectively by
14.84 ms and 139.57 ms in quiet and by 18.54 ms and 16.77 ms in noise (inspect ﬁgure 4.4). Thus,
the behavioral suggests that turn-yielding cues are less salient for speech produced in noise.
There is little discrepancy between feature analysis and behavioral data, which may be explained by
the fact that feature analyses as performed so far compares average acoustic properties for Lombard-
and normal speech by using independent samples. Individual investigations of single tokens to assign
one complex turn-end yielding cue and a subsequent correlation with measured response BIAS would
breakdown which complex cues are suﬀering the most when speech is produced in noise.
Correlation analyses between feature magnitude and response BIAS did not show signiﬁcant relations,
except for IPU duration.
A second explanation could be, that subjects are insensitive to acoustic cues, given that lexico-syntactic
information is available. It remains unclear, to which extent talkers use phonetic features for turn-end
20
==PAGE:25==
prediction. De Ruiter et al. (2006) conclude after their cue-manipulation experiments, that “lexi-
cosyntactic structure is necessary (and possibly suﬃcient) for accurate end-of-turn projection, while
intonational structure, perhaps surprisingly, is neither necessary nor suﬃcient” (for dutch listeners).
An eﬀect of prosody was however observed in by Gravano and Hirschberg (2011), Hjalmarsson (2009)
and Keitel and Daum (2015).
The latter states, that “prosodic cues are beneﬁcial not only with
lacking lexico-syntactic cues but also with lacking visual cues”.
Riest et al. (2015) investigated the eﬀect of syntactic and semantic compared to prosodic cues by using
scrambled word order or low-pass ﬁltered speech tokens. By low-pass ﬁltering speech, the authors kept
the intonational cue intact while making the speech unintelligible. However with the disadvantage,
that other phonetic cues are eliminated or at least alleviated. A diﬀerent way to obtain unintelligible
speech has been explored in the present work by recruiting speakers that are unfamiliar with dan-
ish. The logic is that if a listeners have the ability to assess how reliable available cues are and are
indeed ﬂexible in which cues to pursue (Keitel and Daum (2015)), then listeners who cant make use
semantic or syntactic information may put more weight on phonetic cues. Thus, the increment in
prediction performance when comparing Lombard- to normal speech should be larger for non-danish
listeners. Behavioral data shows exactly the opposite. Although the non-danish population was to
small to secure suﬃcient signiﬁcance levels, an independent samples t-test performed on the incre-
ment (Lombard- vs. normal speech) and danish and non-danish as grouping variable showed a trend:
Non-danish listeners seem to suﬀer more from whatever happens in the acoustic domain when speech
is produced in noise.
At this point it can be argued how universal (and thus, how useful) prosodic information is. De Ruiter
et al. (2006), section 10, p. 531 and Beckman et al. (2010), section 2.5, p. 13, hint that prosodic cues
may be language independent to some extent. With respect to the present work, this is an assump-
tion that has been taken into consideration. All in all, the evidence from the behavioral experiment
suggests that turn-end yielding cues deteriorate in Lombard-speech.
5.2
The Eﬀect of Noise on Turn-end Prediction
Turn-end prediction, whether if it is based solely on lexico-syntactic information or indeed incorporates
phonetic features, is eﬀected by noise. Either because speech intelligibility decreases which aggravates
extraction of lexico-syntactic cues, or because phonetic features are less salient in noise. Naturally, the
eﬀectiveness in cue-masking depends on the type of masker as well as the SNR. An objective estima-
tion of the extent to which individual cues are masked by a given instantaneous noise (the ICRA07 is
time-variant) would involve a comprehensive individual analysis including intensity estimation using
partial loudness models, spectro-temporal masking and speech intelligibility estimation using envelope
power spectrum models, which would go beyond the scope of this project. Hence, it is only possible
to quantify the eﬀect of noise heuristically by carefully interpreting the behavioral data.
For danish subjects, prediction performance decreased (i.e. median response BIAS increased) signiﬁ-
cantly [F(1, 10) = 18.472, p = .002] for presentations in noise by 24.72 ms and 21.02 ms, respectively
for Lombard- and normal speech. Turn-end prediction clearly suﬀers from noise, and it does so equally
for both means of production (a t-test on the diﬀerence in means for respective increments failed).
Non-danish subjects seem to beneﬁt from background noise, iﬀthey are listening to Lombard-speech.
In this condition, median response BIAS decreased by -27.97 ms and performance got better. However,
when listening to normal speech, background noise was a huge disadvantage for non-danish listeners

==PAGE:26==
and response BIAS increased by 94.83 ms. As the non-danish population is very small, no eﬀect of
listening-condition nor interaction with production-condition could be statistically validated.
5.2.1
Feature Based Prediction Performance
The observed interaction seems confusing at ﬁrst glance, however may be well explained when inte-
grating previous ﬁndings: Assuming that subjects are able to select (or put stronger weight on) the
most trustworthy cue, danish listeners will probably go with the lexico-syntactic cue. As long as the
SNR allows a suﬃcient level of speech intelligibility, this cue seems to be very robust (see section
5.2.2).
Listeners who can not draw on lexico-syntactic information have to cue on the acoustic properties of
the tokens. Since the noise that was present while listening was identical to the noise that was played
back while production, it may be that non-danish listeners are thereby better interpreting exaggerated
speech. This is unlikely since turn-end yielding cues have shown to deteriorate with Lombard-speech.
Non-danish listeners are also more likely to be vulnerable to masking of acoustic properties that make
up turn-yielding cues, which this group relies on. Evidence has been procured that some acoustic
properties of Lombard speech actually point to a turn-holding intention of the current speaker. In
other words, turn-relevant acoustic cues are partly wrong, when speech is produced in noise. Finally,
the masking of wrong cues would show up as better prediction performance. What is really happening
though is that non-danish listeners are simply less misled.
Clearly this is a new hypothesis that remains to be statistically evaluated.
5.2.2
Lexico-syntactic Robustness
It has been argued, that danish listeners are relying on lexico-syntactic information and therefore
do not exhibit a strong sensitivity to background noise (assuming that the used SNR yielded to a
suﬃcient speech intelligibility level). Still, an eﬀect of background noise was observed for the danish
population, which may be disputable:
Taking a step back to the methodology reveals, that the experimental paradigm doesn’t pull test
subjects to operate on maximal prediction performance.
It is likely, that some percentage of the
data obtained was in fact reacting. Hence, response BIAS data was revised and data entries above
202.6 ms (see section 4.2, reaction time) discarded. Again, an ANOVA was carried out with production
and background as within factors. While the factor production remained signiﬁcant [F(1,10) = 9.67,
p = .011], the listening condition failed [F(1,10) = .672, p = .431] to be signiﬁcant for the danish
population. It should be mentioned, that both factors failed for the non-danish listeners (production:
[F(1,3) = 0.07, p = .937], background: [F(1,3) = 2.776, p = .194]). Still, when isolating data that
can’t be contributed to reaction, danish subjects exhibit no eﬀect of background noise. A comparison
of diﬀerent SNRs would reveal robustness of lexico-syntactic information and whether danish listeners
can be pulled towards using other cues.

5.3

The previous discussion motivated an individual analysis of feature utilization on turn-end prediction
in diﬀerent listening conditions. Therefore, prediction performance was separated to be either driven
by lexico-syntactic information and/or phonetic features. If the two populations are utilizing diﬀerent
cues, the eﬀectiveness of these cues should vary. To be more precise: If danish listeners don’t mind
prosody too much, feature-driven performance should be unaﬀected for this population and the ef-
fectiveness of these features will diﬀer from the non-danish population. The aim was to verify that
non-danish listeners are suﬀering from masking of acoustic cues, whereas danish listeners presumably
remain relying on lexico-syntactic information.

a given feature magnitude (ﬁgure 5.1 shows an example for the analysis of the feature: IPU duration)
and the response BIAS. A more negative correlation coeﬃcient means, that the feature exhibits a
stronger inﬂuence on turn-end prediction. Is there a diﬀerence in means of correlation coeﬃcients
between two conditions for a certain feature, the latter is aﬀected by the condition.

5.4
The Duration Anticipation Paradox
The duration of IPUs has been found to increase when conversations are held in presence of back-
ground noise.
Sørensen (2017) found an increment of 39% for danish talkers talking danish and
contributes this to a compensative behavior. By producing more words within a turn, an interlocutor
would have “more to comprehend”. This may relate to the ﬁndings in the present work, namely that
talkers prefer lexico-syntactic information to convey and predict cues, especially when other cues are
less reliable due to noise. Riest et al. (2015) argue, that lexico-syntactic information is needed for
maximal prediction performance and that longer IPUs allow to “better project the content” of the
turn, and thus the turn-end. The tokens used for the current prediction experiment were taken from
the speech corpus recorded by Sørensen (2017) and thus inherited the same duration distributions:
Feature analysis showed a signiﬁcant change in IPU duration for Lombard-speech tokens (compare
table 4.1).
Gravano and Hirschberg (2011) found, that turn-ﬁnal IPUs are signiﬁcantly longer and concludes,
that IPU duration is essentially a cue for prediction. This can be conﬁrmed by the results in this
study.
Figure 5.1 shows that there are signiﬁcant linear dependencies between IPU duration and
response BIAS for both danish (r =-.26, p<.001) and non-danish (r =-.52, p<.001) populations such
that longer IPUs yield to faster prediction. This observation is in line with ﬁndings from De Ruiter
et al. (2006), who ﬁnd a correlation coeﬃcient of r =-.636, p<.001. Admittedly, the authors contribute
this to a simple stochastic eﬀect, i.e. longer IPUs entail longer periods in which a subject can answer
too early. Subjects who participated in the present turn-taking experiment reported that they were
mislead, regardless of their nationality, which supports the argumentation of De Ruiter et al. (2006).
Remarkably though, IPU duration is a cue that is signiﬁcantly more eﬀective for non-danish listeners,
who can not dwell on content, which ﬁrst of all opposes a “more to comprehend” or “better projection”
theory and secondly opposes De Ruiter et al. (2006). If the correlation between IPU duration and
response BIAS is solely based by the nature of the task, it would not have a signiﬁcantly diﬀerent
eﬀectiveness as the results from the t-test indicated. This in mind, stochastic dispersion can not be
dismissed, but needs to be considered as an superimposing eﬀect. Naturally, the question that follows
is what is to be deﬁned as baseline. Either, non-danish subjects beneﬁt more from longer IPUs or
danish listeners have betters means of preventing false alarms. Respectively either r =-.26 or r = -.52
is the coeﬃcient baseline error due to stochastic dispersion. Unfortunately, De Ruiter et al. (2006) do
not state in which condition the coeﬃcient was obtained.
Approaching this from the other end, false alarms are more likely to occur at embedded Transition
Relevant Places (TRPs), which are constituted by within-token lexico-syntactic and optionally,
intonational completion (De Ruiter et al. (2006); Sacks et al. (1974); Ford and Thompson (1996)).
Consulting table 5.1, property duration: There is no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in eﬀectiveness when com-
paring background or production means. If TRPs are constituted by intonational completion, these
would be masked or deteriorated in noise or Lombard-speech, respectively. Hence, understanding the
speech tokens should be the main reason to mistake a TRP as a turn-end and thus be the reason for
the majority of false alarms. In conclusion, danish listeners do not prevent false alarms, the baseline
dispersion strength is more likely about r =-.26 and non-danish listeners remain to be able to use IPU
duration as a cue to predict turn ends.
Since background noise and production have no decisive eﬀect on the eﬀectiveness of duration as
turn-yielding cue, it remains unclear what non-danish listeners make use of. Perhaps longer durations
enable to get more familiar with the course of the speaker and thus, are more accurate in deﬁning
baseline levels of phonetic features, which in turn will help to spot turn-yielding deviations.

6
Conclusions
It could not be validated, that Lombard-speech produces turn-yielding cues that are more salient. The
analysis of the acoustic features indicated, that speech produced in noise exhibits signiﬁcant changes
in its acoustic properties. However, these changes rather convey a turn-yielding intention of the talker,
which is determined by an reduced speech-rate, jitter, shimmer and Noise to Harmonic Ratio.
Only intonation and IPU duration showed signiﬁcant changes, i.e. larger magnitude and longer dura-
tion, that would enhance the saliency of turn-end yielding cues. It would be advisable to have a more
detailed within-token analysis and allocation of complex turn-yielding cues for each individual IPU.
Nevertheless, results from the behavioral experiment support the ﬁndings so far. Lombard-speech de-
layed the median response BIAS for both populations and in both listening conditions and therefore
impedes prediction, H01 has to be rejected.
No objective evaluation of the impact of background noise on the acoustic properties was carried out.
Behavioral data indicated, that noise reduced turn-end prediction performance for both populations
when listening to normal speech as well for danish talkers when listening to Lombard-speech.
For non-danish talkers, an interaction of production and background condition could be observed,
although statistical evaluation was impossible. Comparing median response BIAS for presentations
of Lombard-speech, noise provided a beneﬁt for non-danish listeners.
Integrating the results from the feature analysis, it was suggested that noise may mask turn-holding
cues such that turn-end prediction performance presumably improves. Nevertheless, the non-danish
population was tiny, and statistical validation diﬃcult.
As a way around, a metric was deﬁned that evaluated the diﬀerence in feature eﬀectiveness for the
three conditions, i.e. population, production, background. Eﬀectiveness was computed as the strength
of linear relationship between acoustic feature magnitude and prediction performance.
Using this metric, it could be shown that non-danish listeners do not beneﬁt from any acoustic features
more compared to danish listeners, except for IPU duration.
In general, danish talkers seem to use lexico-syntactic cues, which are presumably more robust to
changes in production and background, which can be seen from the relative smaller deviations in
diﬀerent conditions compared to the non-danish population. To support this, an analysis on danish
data, excluding any responses that may be contributed to plain reaction, showed no eﬀect of back-
ground noise. Background noise seems to act as a turn-end masker and delays that part of the subjects
behavior, that consists mostly of reacting-responses. A comparison of feature eﬀectiveness in quiet
and noise yielded a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in overall intensity.
Furthermore, no signiﬁcant eﬀects of listening or production condition could be found for standard
deviations in response BIAS distributions, which would have indicated the presence of a time-variant
masker, which has been used as noise. By taking a few assumptions on the experimental paradigm and
corresponding behavior data, H02 has to be rejected. Noise has an eﬀect, but rather on the reaction
to turn-ends, and not on the actual prediction performance.
Overall, the results suggest that non-danish listeners are performing better predicting danish turn-
ends, which is absurd. Given the evidence from Gravano and Hirschberg (2011); Keitel and Daum
(2015); Hjalmarsson (2009) on accumulation of available cues to form a complex turn-end yielding cue,
danish talkers should have a clear advantage and perform better. It is likely that cognitive processes
involved in comprehending speech are the reason for this unexpected behavior (e.g. a more vivid
imagination, interest in what is talked about, etc.).
26
==PAGE:31==
With respect to the maintained dynamics (i.e. transfer oﬀset distributions) as found by Sørensen
(2017) for conversations in noise, the present work has shown that Lombard-speech can not be a
successful strategy for better turn-end prediction. Even worse, response BIAS measures are not cor-
related with ﬂoor transfer oﬀsets. Quick prediction of turn-ends that precede a long ﬂoor transfer
oﬀset are as likely as delayed predictions of turn-ends, that are followed by a short oﬀset. Whatever
mechanism retain conversational dynamics in natural conversation, it has little to do with the ability
to successfully predicting a turn-end in an experimental paradigm such as used for the present work.
