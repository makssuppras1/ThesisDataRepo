Abstract 
The study aims to comprehensively analyse consumers’ perception of red wines using the Napping 
technique, comparing it with Partial Napping and napping coupled with either Flash Profiling (FP) 
or Ultra-Flash Profiling (UFP).  
Participants rated the similarity of five wine samples in each round using the Napping method. 
Results indicated a more random distribution of ratings with no correlation between wine 
consumption and sample ratings. However, wines with rich and intense flavour profiles showed a 
slight tendency to be rated similar. Most participants demonstrated a good understanding of the 
Napping method, but no conclusive evidence linked a more frequent consumption to a better 
understanding. 
Future research should focus on optimising the survey completion rate and have clarified the 
research aim before conducting the experiments to ensure accurate data.

1 Introduction 
In the area of sensory analysis and evaluation as well as consumer research, understanding complex 
sensory perceptions of various products is important to further develop and configure food products. 
To accurately measure and interpret sensory attributes provides insights into different areas including 
product development, quality control, and consumer satisfaction.  
Recently, a growing demand for faster methods of analysing foods is shown. Recent research also 
includes the use of untrained assessors in product research to provide a descriptive analysis of foods. 
The Napping method has gained popularity in recent years due to its ability to provide a rapid analysis 
of foods using an untrained panel, resulting in time and cost savings. Oliver et al. [1]. 
Conventional methods like quantitative descriptive analysis have been used over many years to 
determine sensory attributes and differences between products. However, methods like these 
primarily focus on predetermined attributes or give a narrow comparison of products, thus limiting 
the perception of multiple sensory dimensions between products. The Napping technique is rooted in 
sensory science and also dwells in some parts of psychology, and has become a useful tool to capture 
and analyse complex sensory data, in a structured and efficient way. At the moment, Napping mainly 
differs between two sub-methods; Global Napping and Partial Napping. Global Napping provides a 
holistic perspective by mapping perceived similarities and differences on a sheet of paper among 
different samples with multiple sensory dimensions. Dehlholm et al. [2]. 
Partial Napping provides similar results as Global Napping but focuses on one sensory attribute at a 
time. With this approach, more data points are collected and can give more precise insights into 
product differences and similarities but exclude the overall perception. Dehlholm et al. [2] compared 
Global Napping and Partial Napping and found RV coefficients to be 0.48, p = 0.044 and 0.84, p < 
0.001, respectively.   
In this project, the aim is to explore how consumers perceive wines from the Danish market by using 
the Napping technique. From these results, an exploration is made to reveal if there is a correlation 
between these parameters and the frequent wine consumption of the participants. The descriptions 
provided by the manufacturer both in terms of grape variety and the general flavour profile will taken 
into considered when evaluating the methods.

Specifically, the study aims to investigate the following objectives:  
1. To provide a comprehensive review of the theoretical background and methodology of the 
Napping technique. 
2. To examine whether participants show a tendency towards the wines they determined to be 
similar, considering demographic information or their consumption of wine.  
3. To evaluate the methods used in this study and explore alternative approaches that potentially 
could enhance the collection and representation of data, to ensure a more comprehensive 
analysis.   
4. To investigate whether the participants understood the Napping technique and experimental 
setup and address any potential issues or misunderstandings.   
5. To propose guidelines for future experimental designs as well as consideration for conducting 
similar research, with a focus on improving data collection and general experimental design.  
 
1.1 Significance of the Study 
Understanding consumers’ preferences and perceptions of different sensory attributes is vital for the 
further development of successful products on the market. By understanding how consumers perceive 
and evaluate different sensory attributes, manufacturers can more easily tailor their products and 
increase their acceptance on the market. By implementing Napping as a comprehensive evaluation 
technique, this research provides additional knowledge to the field of sensory science with a dataset 
including more samples and participants than used before.  
Through exploration of the Napping method, the study aims to provide insights into the strengths and 
weaknesses of this technique. The information gathered from executing this research will give a better 
understanding of how to make these types of experiments in the future as well as gain additional 
knowledge of sensory perception.

2 Methodology 
This section presents the research methodology applied to conduct the study, while giving a detailed 
description of the overall design, data collection methods and the restrictions of the methods. A 
discussion of the limitations the method adhere is conducted, whilst including supportive methods 
that could have been coupled to the study.  
 
2.1 Research Design 
The design of the experiments draws inspiration from previously conducted experiments of the 
overall doctoral thesis. The study aims to investigate the sensory profile from over 100 wines and 
determine if the participant rate any combination of random samples as similar or dissimilar. For a 
rapid description and comparison of the wine samples, Napping is proposed as a technique.  
As preparation for the experiment, 10 or 15 different wines are used for each tasting session. In 
advance, the participants have signed up through a sign-up sheet, either from a website or a physical 
poster containing a QR code. For each session, 10 or 15 bottles are used to provide a higher chance 
of a random combination of wine samples for each participant. A higher chance of true randomness 
is preferred to reduce bias. Furthermore, 10 or 15 samples are used due to previous studies that have 
sample sizes around this amount. The number of samples varies in terms of how many sign-ups each 
session is supposed to have. The number of sign-ups is estimated from the sign-up sheet. Before the 
participants arrive, cups are marked with a unique coloured sticker, while the wine bottles selected 
for the respective session are marked with a unique coloured sticker matching the colour given to the 
cups. The wine bottles are placed in covers to prevent the participants being bias toward the wine. A 
setup of the experiment is presented in Figure 1.

The cups are filled with approx. 20 mL of wine. Each participant receives a combination of 5 different 
wines at random, then tastes them in any order preferred, and are eligible to enter up to three times. 
This estimation is made to gather as much data as possible, whilst avoiding the participant to become 
too influenced by the alcohol to make a proper differentiation of the wines. If a participant attempts 
all three rounds, the accumulation of wine consumed equals 2 glasses of wine. To avoid the carryover 
effect from happening, palate cleansers in form of saltine crackers and club soda are available for the 
participants between each sample tasting.  
 
Before the participants start tasting the wine, they are each given a unique identification number 
(unique ID), which will be used to keep track of how each individual has performed the experiment 
as well as conducting a survey to gather demographic information and wine drinking frequency.

2.2 Data Collection 
2.2.1 Napping Results 
As illustrated on figure 2, 5 stickers, or data points are collected on each sheet collected. From the 
data points, the degree of similarity between the wines may vary from person to person. One type of 
participant could place the stickers close together to present a close similarity and another type of 
participant could have the same interpretation but have used more of the sheet of paper, hence some 
stickers may be further apart, but have the same degree of similarity. It is impossible to determine this 
factor in a scientific way. For interpretation of the data, an assumption is made that if stickers are 
more than 2 cm apart, they differ too much from each other to have been compared as similar. 
Therefore, stickers with a distance of 2 cm or shorter are considered to be perceived as similar. 
After collecting all the data from each session, pictures are taken of each sheet. The assumption of 2 
cm distance is applied to photos of the sheets, and the distance between the data points are measured 
on a computer screen with a ruler. There is no indication to whether this approach is scientifically 
appropriate, and might cause some drawback, because of this variable.  
 
2.2.2 Survey Data 
When the participants arrive at the tasting area, they each get assigned a unique ID. With this ID, each 
participant signs in to the survey from SurveyXact using their ID to ensure the right answers 
correspond to the correct person. 
To avoid any form of written consent regarding the European guidelines towards personal information 
from the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), purely demographic data are collected from 
the participants. A full list of questions from the survey is presented in the Appendix on table 5.  
 
2.3 Limitations of the Experiment 
Throughout the experiments a total of 264 answers have been made to the inquiry. Out of those 
answers, 256 participants have a unique identification due to them finishing the survey and going for 
multiple rounds in the same session. The same ID was entered on a new survey, thus duplicates of 
some numbers are present. For the analysis, only participants, who have completed the entire survey 
are taking into account to give an overall interpretation of how the participants have answered the 
questions.  
==PAGE:12==

From the 264 answers, 33 surveys were incomplete, see attached spreadsheet for entire survey data 
and datasets that are excluded. The reason for incomplete survey could have many possible reasons, 
those include participants forgetting to complete the survey or simply not wanting to. The number of 
surveys excluded are few and therefore an assumption is made that they will not have an impact on 
the overall interpretation.  
Besides human errors from completing the survey, there is a minor bias toward intoxication of the 
participants. Whether the participants go for one, two or three rounds there is a slight chance the 
individual participant becomes more intoxicated than another. This is due to the difference in gender, 
age, metabolism or something different. The study assumes, with the given volume handed out to 
each participant that the chance of intoxication, thus making the participant answer in a way they 
normally would not, is very low and therefore not considered.

From the performance of the experiments, some errors occurred. From the first session, March 13

outcome is minimal, as only a small number of participants were involved.

for further investigation when combined with the corresponding survey data.

assumption is based on people that have prior knowledge of drinking wine could have developed a 
heightened sensory awareness, which will indicate more familiarity with wine and facilitating rating 
wines similar to be an easier task. Familiarity by training is one area explored by Liu et Al. [3]. From 
this study, they found that people, who had a bit of training in the method provided more robust and 
reliable results.  
Also including their ability to deduce the instructions given by the researches, a more comprehensive 
illustration of the rating of wines are assumed to be made. The participants deductive skills and prior 
knowledge is not investigated in this study but acknowledges that more frequent wine consumption 
could have influenced the perception of the method. Nevertheless, the analysis did not find sufficient 
evidence to support the theory.  
 
3.3.3 Challenging the Napping Method 
From the evaluation of the Napping method, the technique alone is insufficient in providing enough 
relevant data to provide a comprehensive study of determining similarity in wine. Two suggestions 
are presented to enhance the collection of data as well as optimising the experimental design.   
Following the research of Oliver et al. [1], Dehlholm et al. [2], Liu et al. [3] and Perrin et al. [4], they 
each explore the Napping method coupled with either Flash Profiling (FP) or Ultra-Flash Profiling 
(UFP). With this extension to the method, the participants could subsequently add one to three 
descriptors to each wine. The descriptors are purely subjective, as the participants have not been 
trained. With these descriptors a more comprehensive understanding of how the participants perceives 
the wines are presented and allows for a more in-depth discussion of the sensory profile. For example, 
the participants could imply they have rated the wines similar in terms of taste, odour or some other 
sensory attribute, or something completely different. With this application, the results would have 
been easier to interpret and give a more thorough investigation of how the wines were rated similar. 
In the end, it would be preferred to compare the wines the participants rated similar from the Napping 
method with either FP or UFP, and discuss the descriptors associated with the same wines to 
distinguish if the wines rated similar had a common denominator. This approach could have been 
interesting to further investigate as this combination of methods give a more comprehensive 
understanding of the sensory profile of the wines and give a more in-depth comparison.  
==PAGE:23==
3 Results and Discussion 
Page 19 
 
Besides applying either FP or UFP, the participants could have performed the Napping technique as 
Partial Napping instead. By asserting the experiment as Partial Napping, the participants are forced 
to compare the wines from a predetermined parameter e.g., taste, odour or colour. By only focusing 
on a single attribute, Partial Napping can improve the participants’ ability to differentiate between the 
samples when compared to a more holistic approach, Louw et al [7]. The participants were not 
instructed to compare the wines in a specific manner nor with a specific sensory attribute in mind. It 
can therefore be assumed that the participants have taken a holistic approach when comparing the 
samples, also referred to as Global Napping. From Louw et al. [7], Global Napping and Partial 
Napping are compared in terms of repeatability, reproducibility and effectiveness on varying sample 
sizes. The study showed close to similar results in smaller sample sizes, up to 6 different samples. 
With further investigation, the study showed that Partial Napping appeared more effective in larger 
sample sizes due to a more focussed comparison than a holistic approach. By the Partial Napping 
method, the participants can easier navigate through the comparison as they only need to have one 
sensory attribute in mind for the test. If more sensory attributes need to be investigated, additional 
tasting sessions would be required to cover these.  
To ensure a comprehensive exploration of the sensory profile in wines, the different Napping methods 
should be considered before conducting the experiment. For example, this study could have benefitted 
from performing the experiment as Partial Napping to gain a better understanding of how the 
participant rated the wines as similar by including a specific attribute to distinguish the wines. 
Furthermore, to give the participant completely free will in terms of perception adding FP or UFP is 
also a highly recommended option. With this approach, the participants can create their own 
descriptors for the wines. It would then be the researcher’s responsibility to differentiate between all 
of these from the participants to determine if there is a common denominator from the applied 
descriptors.  
 
3.3.4 Guidelines for Future Experiments 
If this research were to be repeated or a future study wants to explore the same areas, the study will 
suggest some guidelines to prevent possible errors, and possible solutions to enhance the collection 
of data, and critically discuss the application of the type of Napping technique.  
The results gathered from the survey data can be optimised. Of the 263 inquiries, 33 did not finish 
the survey. This is not a substantial number of incomplete surveys, but the collection can still be 
==PAGE:24==
3 Results and Discussion 
Page 20 
 
optimised. For example, to improve the completion rate the researchers could verbally remind the 
participants to complete the survey before leaving the tasting area. Another option is to provide 
written instruction to give each participant and even allocate dedicated time for survey completion. 
The study acknowledges that the participants could easily forget to complete the survey, because they 
have just finished comparing wines, and therefore completing the survey might not be the first thing 
they have in mind. With a higher completion rate, this can in turn lead to a larger dataset, which can 
improve possible statistical analysis and contribute to a deeper understanding of the objectives. On 
the other hand, if more surveys were incomplete, this would reduce the potency of further analysis.   
In addition to survey completion, the study aims to optimise the execution of the Napping method 
used in the experiments. The study acknowledges that some participants had a lack of understanding 
of the method. To address this, the study suggests that a more comprehensive explanation of the 
technique could have been provided. Upon arrival, a brief of how to perform the experiment was 
given to each of the participants, accompanied by an example of how the Napping sheet could look 
in the end as well as emphasising the possibility of rating the wines on a two-dimensional scale. And 
this is where the process could have been optimised.  
Ultimately, the Napping method itself needs to be considered when performing rapid sensory analysis 
of foods. Napping, a technique in sensory evaluation involves the placement and description of food 
samples based on their sensory attributes. Before conducting any desired experiments, it is important 
to understand the intention behind using the Napping technique. 
The study suggests using Global Napping when the sample size is large and the objective is to rapidly 
describe the overall perception of foods. This approach provides a broad overview of the samples’ 
sensory characteristics.  
On the other hand, if the objective is to compare and identify similarities between samples based on 
specific sensory attributes, Partial Napping is recommended. In Partial Napping, predetermined 
modalities, such as smell or taste, is focused on when comparing samples. This focused approach 
simplifies the task of placing and describing the samples. For instance, in a study investigating the 
perception of cheese, assessors can use Partial Napping to differentiate cheese samples based on 
similarity in terms of a chosen modality, such as smell or texture.  
While Partial Napping offers a more in-depth perception of the modalities, the overall perception of 
the samples is not considered. By coupling Napping with either FP or UFP, the overall perception of 
==PAGE:25==
3 Results and Discussion 
Page 21 
 
the samples is considered as well as given descriptors to how the assessors would describe the 
samples, which will give an indication of which modality that is in focus. Using Napping with FP or 
UFP can be beneficial when the objective is to rapidly evaluate multiple samples and understand the 
sensory preferences and perceptions of the assessor.   
To optimise future experiments in this area, following some of these suggestions could enhance data 
collection as well as give an indication to which type of Napping method that could be applying to a 
specific study.

4 Conclusion 
In conclusion, this study aimed to explore the Napping method in wine and investigate whether 
participants tend to select a specific grape variety when rating wines as similar. The results provided 
valuable insight into the Napping experiments and the survey data.  
From analysing the Napping sheets, the study identified few instances of similarity between wines. 
The majority of grape varieties frequently selected by participants were shiraz, merlot, cabernet 
sauvignon, and tempranillo. However, the study did not find a clear correlation between the frequency 
of wine consumption and the similarity in wine rating. This suggests a more random distribution of 
answers rather than tendency. The study also explored the flavour profiles of the wines rated similar. 
A slight tendency is revealed for wines with a rich and intense profile to be rated similar more 
frequently than other flavour profiles.  
 
The evaluation of the Napping method shows that the majority of participants display a complex 
configuration of data point indicating a good understanding of the method. On the other hand, a minor 
group of participants displayed a more simplistic configuration of data point implying a 
misunderstanding of the method. However, the analysis did not find any conclusive evidence 
supporting the assumption that higher consumption of wine would be in correlation with a better 
understanding of the method. A higher consumption of wine is displayed both in the simplistic 
configuration set and the complex configuration set 
 
The Napping method proved to be a useful technique for wine evaluation, but could be improved 
upon by either being coupled with Flash Profiling or Ultra-Flash Profiling, or have been executed as 
Partial Napping to gain more comprehensive results.
