Abstract
For many species in the ocean, spawn are hatched during the spring, and therefore grow
as a cohort together through the summer and autumn. The existence of cannibalism in
the species means that the members of the cohort are playing a game against each other.
Because the size of the individuals of the cohort influences their actions, such a population
can be well understood by modelling how its abundance is distributed on along spectrum
of sizes.
Size spectrum theory describes a size structured population as is grows with time,
but there does not yet exist a model that combines this field with behaviour-optimising
modelling.
This thesis combines size spectrum theory with mean field game theory by constructing
a model for which the growth and mortality terms of an individual in the size spectrum
are functions of the individual- and mean field strategy.
The model is based specifically on the case of a cohort of European eel. All European
eel begin their life cycle as they hatch from eggs in the Sargasso sea in spring. From there
they travel, largely with the currents, to the coast of Europe, from where they undergo
metamorphosis. Since it is advantageous to obtain a high mass before the eel undergoes
metamorphosis, the eel are pressured towards growing as fast as possible, however hunting
for food is associated with a higher risk of predation. Because of cannibalism, both the
amount of food available, and the risk of predation are affected by the behaviour of con-
specifics.
Given a mean field strategy, the population dynamics are modelled with an integro-
differential equation that I solve with the semi-implicit Euler method. From there, I find
the best response strategy using dynamical programming.
When the best response to a cohort that follows a mean field strategy is the strategy
itself, then the population is in Nash equilibrium.
I find this equilibrium by using an
underrelaxed best response algorithm.
I show such Nash equilibria resulting from several systems, and since any population
are attracted towards a Nash equilibrium, the behaviour exhibited in these results are
predictions of the behaviour of eel during their migration.
This thesis presents a model that contributes to the understanding of the effects of
optimised behaviour on the size spectrum, and shows the population dynamics resulting
from optimised behaviour.

1
Introduction
In marine ecosystems, many species spawn in spring, after which they grow in size and,
for many species, reach later life stages by late autumn, for instance by means of meta-
morphosis. All of this while the fish are in competition with each other. Size spectrum
theory is the study of the size composition of the community, which can be used to model
the dynamical changing population.
The behaviour of this cohort of fish is shaped by competing goals, since there is a clear
benefit from being bigger than all other spawn during this period. Therefore, the spawn
can be seen as playing a game against each other.
There does not yet, to my knowledge, exist a model of the game that is played between
individuals in the size spectrum as they grow through the year.
This thesis aims to construct such model.
In Particular, the competition will here
be modelled through the effects of cannibalism; larger spawn can eat smaller spawn, and
therefore there is a constant drive to be larger than other members of the community.
Although such a model can describe the community as a whole, I will in this thesis
tailor the model to a cohort of fish in a single species; the European eel (Anguilla Anguilla).
This species is a suitable choice because all of its eggs are hatched in The Sargasso Sea,
whereafter the spawn move together with the current until they reach Europe (Ginneken
and Maes 2005). Therefore, we can evaluate the performance of an individual eel simply
by their size and their expectancy of survival, at the arrival to the coasts of Europe.
1.1
Model setup
A game is when a number of agents have goals that they simultaneously try to obtain. The
goal of this game is to maximise fitness. Agents are seen as rational actors that will choose
the best option from the actions accessible to them. As will be explained in section 2.2., it
is reasonable to view each individual in an animal population as rational actors playing a
game. This means that the population is ”polymorphic”; each member of the population
cares only about their own success. In this thesis, we will be constructing a ”mean field
game”; a game of a very large group of agents, where each agent is concerned with the
average of all other actors; the mean field.
The strategy, the actions that the fish can take, will here be denoted as a set of ”decision
variables” τ, which is the fraction of time that the fish chooses to ”be active”, in given
situations. I introduce a notion: The ”sphere of interest”, which is simply an unspecified
volume where all eel can be expected to reside. Since the fish are largely carried by water
currents, it is reasonable to model them as being bound together like this. I then assume
that there at the top of this volume, near the surface of the water, is a ”feeding arena”
where far the most of all organisms reside. All the food is in the feeding arena, but also, all
predators reside here (figure 1a.). The decision variable, τ, is then a variable that can be
tuned to whatever is optimal in any given situation. τ theoretically ranges from ”constantly
active”: τ = 1, to ”not active at all”: τ = 0. A similar behaviour is exhibited by adult
European eel, where τ ≈0.5 (Aarestrup et al. 2009). The goal is to reach a suitable mass as

The dynamics of the population as it moves through time, and changes in mass, will
be what drives the game (eq. 8). The rates of increase in mass and -decrease in expected
survival will be affected by the strategy of all given fish. With dynamical programming,
we can find the optimal strategy of one ”invading” fish if there is a preexisting spectrum
of other ”resident” fish with set strategies (eq. 38). A complicating factor is then that an
invader fish with fitness higher than the fitness of the resident population, is expected to
take over the population after enough time (resulting from eq. 23). Replacing the resident
strategy with the invading one will then change the dynamics, which will make a new
strategy optimal. The goal is therefore to find the ”Nash equilibrium”; the strategy for
which there exists no better response.
1.2
Outline of methodology
The goal of this thesis is essentially to first build a model leading to a mean field game
induced by dynamics in the size spectrum. Then secondly to show-, and discuss the results
of, dynamics and strategies in Nash equilibrium. A crude outline of how I will find such
results is as follows:
Most importantly, I construct a model that is ideally both ecologically feasible, and
that fits a number of mathematical criteria, such as the technical constraints required to
ensure existence of a Nash equilibrium.
2
==PAGE:8==
To find the strategy in such an equilibrium, the following steps, that I will elaborate
later, will be taken:
• Find the dynamics of the population given the strategy.
The model will, building from size spectrum theory, dictate how the population
density results from given strategies; f1 : τ 7→N.
• Find the optimal strategy given the dynamics of the population.
Given a specified fitness based on weight at the terminal time, the optimal set of
decision variables can be found using dynamical programming; f2 : N 7→τ.
• The optimal strategy given the dynamics given a strategy f2 (f1(τ)), maps the set of
strategies to itself. For this composite map; h = f2 ◦f1 : τ 7→τ, find a fixed point.
Though it is out of the scope of this thesis to rigorously explore the numerical the-
ory of finding such point, I will show how such point has guarantied existence and
uniqueness, and that such results can be produced, at least for certain parameters
and initial strategies.
The fixed point τ ∗s.t. τ ∗= h(τ ∗) is the Nash equilibrium of the system.

2.1
Size spectrum theory
In ecology, one of the most influential factors on design and behaviour of animals in the sea
is their size. Size is absolutely crucial for determining what trophic layer an animal is in,
since fish tend to only eat fish that are smaller than themselves. Therefore, size determines
what- and which kind of fish that a fish can eat, and also what- and which kind of other
fish that pose a danger. Besides, many biological factors, such as metabolism, tend to scale
in well-defined ways as a function of the size of the animal in question. It thus turns out
that simplifying individual fish to their size is a very practical modelling choice.
A size spectrum is the mapping of abundance of organisms as a function of
their size.
In the mathematical model itself, i will simply let abundance be defined as the number
of individual organisms:
N : T × M 7→R
(1)
where N(t, m) describes the density of the number of fish (eel) at the time t ∈T, as a
function of the size described by m ∈M. Principally, this is a function with dimension,

m”, where # is the number of physical fish. For any fixed t, then N(t, m) is the distri-
bution of fish on the size spectrum. I will often omit the time dependence for brevity and
denote such a function N(m).

2.2
Game Theory
All living organisms are the result of evolution by natural selection (Darwin 1859). There-
fore it is through the lens of evolution that we can most generally model the behaviour of
all living organisms, namely that they are optimising.
2.2.1
Natural selection causes optimisation
Natural selection affects individuals, and not populations. In fact, a more precise state-
ment would be that natural selection affects individual genes, but for the purposes of this
thesis, that distinction is less relevant. It is well understood in ecology, that all individual
organisms that exist, exist as a result of natural selection, and therefore, all active genes in
all currently existing organisms are heavily biased towards self-preservation. That is, that
the gene’s existence is preserved, which necessarily requires the gene to be duplicated onto
other organisms as offspring. The ability for a gene to improve its host organism’s chance
to reproduce, is called its fitness.
In this sense, most active genes in all organisms are optimised with regards to fitness.
The exceptions to that rule, are all the genes that exist by chance, and will always exist
as mutations regularly occur for all organisms. The result of this is that there is variation
14
==PAGE:20==
between individuals in a population. This variation makes it so that natural selection is
always active, and that shifting environments can always be met by newly optimal genes.
In sexually reproducing organisms (and for other reasons, sometimes in asexually re-
producing organisms), there is an additional effect.
Genes have expectation to get duplicated to the whole species. Especially genes with
high fitness will over sufficient time scales be spread to the whole species, since any offspring
will have a mix between two individuals’ genes, who additionally, have higher expected
fitness than average. Therefore, a population will tend towards having genes of the highest
fitness.
2.2.2
Polymorphic Populations
However, there is still an important thing to remember: Natural selection affects individ-
uals, and not populations.
While the whole population will tend towards genes with most fitness, fitness is still
determined from individual chance of reproduction.
This is an important distinction; a population can be modelled as monomorphic and
as polymorphic.
A monomorphic population optimises itself as a population. A polymorphic population
optimises each individual in the population.
This thesis treats the cohort as a polymorphic optimising population.
This means that all individuals will at all times choose their own optimal strategy.
With regards to the model, this means that the fitness of the strategy is defined for the
individual, not the population.

2.3.2
Value function
The method that I will use to solve the dynamical programming problem (37) is to formu-
late and solve the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation(s).
If the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation can be solved, then as a part of the
solution, the optimal strategy is also found (Thygesen 2021 p. 248).
To solve the HJB-equation, we will first need to explore the value function V ∈T ×M 7→
R.
The value function is the expected total reward (here; fitness) given that every future
decision is optimal.
For the system in this thesis, the dynamics are deterministic. With ”expected” fitness, I
therefore mean the known future fitness given optimal behaviour. However, notice that the
formalism of Value-functions and the HJB-equation are equally valid Stochastic systems.
The equivalence in viewing the actor as a population, and individual with expectation
density therefore hold.
The value-function is most easily understood by imagining it as the terminal reward evolv-
ing backwards in time.
A good intuitive reasoning for this is as follows; since the different options of future de-
cisions will tend to expand the space of possible resulting outcomes, then, going backwards
in time will have narrowing effect on the space of possible outcomes.
To make it more concrete: Here, the Value function at the terminal time Tend is the
terminal reward as applied to a uniform distribution on the state space M. We can interpret
V (Tend, m) as the reward for an eel if it is of mass m.
The Value function can then move backwards in time the same as a population would,
but for reversed flux of state. That is to say that terms that lower the population abundance
as a function of the state, µ, also lower the value on that state. Similarly, the terms that
22
==PAGE:28==
change the state of the population g, also change the state of the value (but reversed in
backward time).
The dynamics of the Value function is then also affected by the strategy τ, but since
the momentary value of all available options are known in backward time, we can define
these functions as always being the ones that minimise the loss of value at any moment in
time.
Finding V is primarily important, because the solution that minimises it loss backward
in time is exactly the solution that maximises gain forward in time, i.e. the optimal
strategy. That means that reaching V (0, m) from V (Tend, m) simultaneously gives us all
optimal decision variables for all states from τ ∗(0, m) to τ ∗(Tend, m).
There is an additional benefit to finding V however. The value function is the momen-
tary expected fitness of an the contribution to an actor from any state. So for instance,
from V (t, M0) we can, as a function of t, determine the expected fitness of a newly hatched
egg.
2.3.3
Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation

2.4
Brouwer’s Fixed Point Theorem
For the discrete approximation of solutions, with the right choice of model, existence of a
Nash equilibrium can be proven.
I will in this section, in loose terms, denote the set of all well-behaved population-
functions N : T × M 7→R as N where one might say N ⊆RT×M. Similarly, the set of all
strategies τ : T × M 7→[0, 1] is denoted τ where τ ⊆R[0,1].
I let the population dynamic resulting from a strategy τi (with a fixed given set of
parameters) define the map from the set of strategies τ to the set of populations N, i.e.
the operator f1 : τ 7→N such that
Ni = f1(τi)
(42)
where Ni is the solution of the McKendrick-Von Foerster-equation given that the strategy
is τi.
I let the optimal strategy resulting from a population dynamic Ni define the map
f2 : N 7→τ such that
τi+1 = f2(Ni)
(43)
where f2(Ni) = τ ∗given a population Ni with a mean field strategy τi.
It is then clear that the composite of the two operators, defined h : τ 7→τ such that
h(τi) = (f2 ◦f1)(τi) = τi+1
(44)
is a map from the domain τ : T × M 7→[0, 1] to itself, which is the ”best response”; the
strategy τi+1 that has highest fitness given that τi is the mean field strategy.
26
==PAGE:32==
If τ f is a fixed point of h, meaning that
h(τ f) = τ f
(45)
then this is a necessary and sufficient condition for τ f to be a Nash equilibrium.
The condition is necessary because if h(τ)f ̸= τ f then, as a mean field strategy, then
there would be a response to τ f: τ ∗= f2(f1(τ f)) that is somehow optimal, which is not
τ f itself; the point τ f is then not Nash.
The condition is sufficient because f2(Ni) is by definition an optimal strategy. There
is thus no need to show stability of the point.
I will note that for the system with growth- and mortality rates in (41), there is no
dependence on the mean field strategy, so f2 is a constant function. Therefore τ ∗as shown
in figure 8. is indeed a Nash equilibrium.
2.4.1
Existence of a fixed point
We can know for certain that h has a fixed point if it follows the conditions governing
Brouwer’s fixed point theorem (Conway 1985):
Every continuous function from a convex compact subset of a Euclidean
space to itself has a fixed point.
Meaning that a fixed point of h exists, since it is a map from the space τ to itself, if
• τ is a convex compact subset of a Euclidean space.
• h is a continuous function
The requirement for being convex, compact and subset of a Euclidean space is easily
fulfilled in the discrete case. In the discrete case then the set of strategies is a subset
of Euclidean space: τ ⊆[0, 1]#t·#m ⊂R#t·#m and is a convex compact set (it is a unit
interval for each dimension). I note that I later lift the lower bound of τ(t, m) from 0 to
0 < λ < 1. This does not change τ away from being convex compact.
For the continuous case however, the space τ is not proven convex compact since R[T×M]
is infinite-dimensional. I will not prove existence of a fixed point in the continuous case,
but I discuss why I don’t perceive this as a problem in section 6.4.
The requirement for continuity has the most impact on the model. h is continuous if f1
and f2 are continuous. This means that if continuous τi ∈τ implies continuous Ni ∈N,
and if continuous Ni ∈N implies continuous τi+1 ∈τ, then h is continuous.
f1(τi) is the solution to the McKendrick-Von Foerster equation with a given strategy
τi, so it is continuous if its terms are g and µ are continuous. The optimal strategy f2(Ni)
can be ensured to be continuous if V is a continuous function of t and m (so (40) is not a
good choice), and the minimising τ in −∂V
∂mg + µV is a continuous function of t and m.
Therefore, for a model with continuous g, µ, V and a minimiser of −∂V
∂mg + µV that is
a continuous function of m and t, then the best response map h : τ 7→τ is continuous.

3.4
Cannibalism
We now want to let the individuals in the cohort cannibalise smaller individuals if the size

6
Discussion
This thesis has presented a first attempt at a model that combines size spectrum theory
with fitness-optimising behaviour in the scope of a mean field game.
There are now several questions that should be answered in further research.
6.1
State of the art
To my knowledge, there has not, before this thesis, existed a model of a mean field game
in the size spectrum.
Including fitness optimising behaviour is an emergent paradigm for ecosystem mod-
elling, and since optimisation often implies that a game is played, it is important to un-
derstand how such game influences the behaviours of its actors.
On the size spectrum, there already exists models of evolutionary optimisation with
game-like dynamics in the scope of function-valued adaptive dynamics (Dieckmann, Heino,
and Parvinen 2006); at the time of writing, a promising project in writing, that models
the adaptation of a size spectrum with individuals competing for mating rights.
However, this model uses the different angle of a mean field game to find the optimising
behaviour. There are pros and cons of both methods; one drawback of this method is the
difficulty of computation (section 6.5). However, a big advantage is that the existence of
a Nash equilibrium is known, and that, when found through this method, any equilibrium
is Nash.
I chose to model the game that arises because of the pressure of cannibalism, though
there might have been more relevant games to model (section 6.3).
Regardless; we now have insight in some of the qualitative effects that cannibalism has
on the behaviour and population dynamics of size structured cohorts.
6.1.1
Results
Through this model, I have showed how cannibalism in a species can induce a pressure on
an individual to be as active as possible to get a lead on its con-specifics.
However, another big effect of cannibalism seems to be that, if there is not much pres-
sure to grow, then cannibalism will allow larger fish to be more lazy/careful, since they
can rely on their smaller cousins as a ”feeding chamber”.
The model assumes that the population is polymorphic, meaning they optimise for their
own genes only. Therefore, the existence of cannibalism is quite trivial (explained well by
Diekmann et al. 2002). However, the results showed that given the right circumstances,
cannibalism can in fact benefit the species as a whole. This effect was showcased in this
thesis by a population with such a wide spawn interval, that those individuals that are
spawned later contributes the species only by acting as an easy food resource.
That said, we do not see a big influence from cannibalism on the population dynamics
given probable parameters. This is owed in large part to the fact that fish tend to eat fish
65
==PAGE:71==
that are 400 times smaller, which is quite a big difference. Therefore, a single cohort does
not span the size spectrum wide enough such that large individuals benefit substantially
from smaller individuals.
6.2
Criticisms of the model
It should be noted that for some results, the model starts falling apart, for instance because
it assumes a constant background parameter b. Such an assumption is perhaps alright if
the cohort moves independently of the background. Then the background can reasonably
be seen as a constant since it is renewed often. However, for the case of the European eel
that moves with the sea current, this is somewhat unlikely, especially with the (sometimes
absurdly-) large number of fish that I claim that the cohort contains.
There are other odd assumptions that could perhaps be improved in future models.
Here I highlight a few:
6.2.1
Constant clearance rate
The fact that the clearance rate ρ is constant perhaps seems odd, since bigger fish tends
to swim faster. Perhaps this assumption makes sense if the fish are mostly transported
around randomly by currents. Note also, that the ”sphere of interest” is expanding with
time, and the clearance rate has the dimension V
t where V the sphere of interest.
So
measured with a constant unit of volume, then ρ(t) is in fact increasing. However, it is
still valid to question weather ρ(m) should be constant.
Further insight into the dynamics in the feeding arena is required to answer that ques-
tion.
6.2.2
Shape of value function
It is hard to say weather the definition of the fitness function, V (Tend, m), is a sensible one.
Defining the value function at the terminal time as the fitness at this time and state is
probably the easiest, but not necessarily the best, assumption.
This implies that the fish only undergoes metamorphosis at arrival to Europe. It also
makes the interpretation of F = V (Tend, m)·N(tend, m) quite vague; it is the total fitness
of the cohort, but what does that mean? Since V (Tend, 103mg) is exactly 0.5 then there
is somehow a 50% chance of being a ”successfully matured individual” if one is 103mg at
terminal time.
Then the sharpness parameter z somehow dictates the span of masses for which there
is an increase in being ”successful”.
A vague reasoning for the choice of z = 4, the choice used for all results in this thesis,
(the shape of which is most clearly illustrated figure. 10 (purple) t = 50 weeks.), is given
here:
There is an interval of 4–6 months at which metamorphosis is ”viable” (Ginneken and
Maes 2005) which could be modelled as there being a trade-off at any time t between
undergoing metamorphosis or growing, i.e. there must be a significantly positive slope of
the value function V (m) for the masses m in the time interval of 4-6 months. With the
66
==PAGE:72==
growth rate used in the results, then the eels will roughly span one order of magnitude in
4-6 months, meaning that z = 4 seems like a reasonable choice.
Clearly, the exact shape of the fitness function is a question that can only be answered,
in a precise way, with a deeper understanding of biology than I have, and perhaps empirical
data of success given size of metamorphosis is required.
6.3
Further model suggestions

6.4
Existence of Nash equilibrium
It is regrettably out of the scope of this thesis to fully prove existence or uniqueness for a
Nash equilibrium in the continuous case.
One should not necessarily expect uniqueness of a Nash equilibrium (though I have yet
to encounter a case with multiple equilibria in the full model), since h could certainly have
multiple local fixed points.
However, for all practical purposes, I view the existence of a Nash equilibria as certain.
I have proven that such a fixed point must exist in the discrete case, regardless of the
number of discretisations, by use of Brouwer’s fixed point theorem. I have taken great care
to show that these discretisations are indeed approximations of the continuous case, and
we know that such approximations converge towards the continuous case. Philosophically
67
==PAGE:73==
speaking, realise that the ”real world” is in fact never completely continuous, so it is hard
to see how there would be an issue in the lack of existence in the truly continuous case.
An additional argument that such an equilibrium could be guaranteed, is that (Lasry
and Lions 2007) constructs a model of a game on a state-space with a time component,
solvable with the HJB-equation, and they successfully prove existence and uniqueness of a
Nash equilibrium.
Weather these proofs could equivalently apply to my model could be a topic of further
research.
6.5
Computationally hard problem
As mentioned, it is a limiting factor that finding a fixed point is a computationally hard
problem.
This could no doubt be partially solved by use of more sophisticated numerical proce-
dures.
However, this is also a limit of the methodology of this report; finding best responses
by use of the HJB-equation.
Since there is no guarantee that h converges, then all results are facilitated by long
computational times along with trial and error parameter tweaking of the search algorithm.
For this reason, further research is advised to either include a more sophisticated imple-
mentation of numerical methods, or to instead seek to behaviour through its evolutionary
path using function-valued adaptive dynamics.

7
Conclusion
I have constructed a model that combines size spectrum theory with mean field game
theory, specifically by including cannibalistic behaviour in the size spectrum. By solving
the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation, I have found the optimal strategies for individuals
in the spectrum, with regards to their time spent actively hunting for food.
I present multiple examples of strategies in Nash equilibrium, in which it is shown, that
given the right conditions, the existence of cannibalism changes the mean field strategy. It
is shown that in the case of the European eel, cannibalism imposes a carrying capacity on
the population, while in other cases, the existence of cannibalism can benefit the fitness of
a species.
This thesis introduces a model of a behaviour-optimising size spectrum to the field of
ecology. The existence of such a model will hopefully further the understanding of the
complex mechanisms that govern the population-dynamics in the ocean.
