Abstract
Rate-dependent compaction of chalk is investigated in this thesis by testing samples from Stevns
Klint. To ﬁgure out how the kinematic viscosity of the poreﬂuid in these samples inﬂuences the
compaction, they were saturated with ﬁve diﬀerent ﬂuids: isopar-l oil, hydraulic oil, two mixtures
of water and ethylene glycol that have the same kinematic viscosity as the oils, and at last distilled
water as reference.
The reason for using the kinematic viscosity to resemble the ﬂuids is, because Biots critical fre-
quency states that the strength of chalk correlates to this property of the poreﬂuid, as well as the
permeability and the porosity of the chalk.
The samples were tested by adding axial stress to them inside an oedometer cell. To see which
impact the poreﬂuid had on each sample, a material constant called the b-factor was measured and
thereafter compared. This b-factor is found by testing the sample with two diﬀerent stress rates;
one slow and one fast.
After treating and discussing the results obtained, there was a clear correlation between the strength
of the samples and their poreﬂuid. However, the results from the water and ethylene glycol saturated
samples did not resemble the results from the oil saturated samples, even though the ﬂuids had the
same kinematic viscosity and the chalk was the same. This could be explained by the weakening
eﬀect that water has on chalk. The results were therefore grouped into two categories; one with oil
saturated samples and one with water as a part of the poreﬂuid.

Resum´e
Rate afhængig kompaktion af kalk er undersøgt i denne afhandling ved at teste prøver fra Stevns
Klint. For at ﬁnde ud af, hvordan den kinematiske viskositet af porevæsken i disse prøver påvirker
kompaktionen, så blev de saturerede med fem forskellige væsker: isopar-l olie, hydraulik olie, to
blandinger af vand og ethylenglycol som har den samme kinematiske viskositet som olierne, og til
sidst vand som reference.
Årsagen til at bruge den kinematiske viskositet til at sammenligne væskerne er Biots kritiske
frekvens, der påstår at styrken af kalk korrelerer med denne egenskab af væsken, samt porøsiteten
og permeabiliten for kalken.
Prøverne var testede ved at tilføje aksial spænding til dem inde i en ødometer celle. For at se
hvilken indﬂydelse porevæsken havde på hver prøve, blev en materiale konstant kaldet b-faktoren
målt og derefter sammenlignet. Denne b-faktor er fundet ved at teste prøven med to forskellige
spændings hastigheder; ´en langsom og ´en hurtig.
Når de opnåede resultater var behandlet og diskuteret , var der en klar korrelation mellem styrken af
prøverne og deres porevæske. Dog lignede resultaterne fra de vand og ethylenglycol saturerede prøver
ikke resultaterne fra de olie saturerede prøver, selv om væskerne havde den samme kinematiske
viskositet, og kalken var den samme. Dette kunne forklares med svækkelses eﬀekten som vand har
på kalk. Resultaterne var derfor grupperede i to kategorier; ´en med olie saturerede prøver og ´en
med vand som en del af porevæsken.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
As our standard of living gets higher and higher, the energy demand is increasing proportionally.
Today the main part of this energy demand is provided by petroleum, and even though alternative
sources eventually are covering a continuously larger part of this demand, the petroleum consump-
tion will keep growing. Since there is a ﬁnite quantity of oil and gas, the resources have to be
utilized as good as possible.
Hydrocarbons are a fossil fuel originated from organic materials that have been buried beneath the
ground for many geological times. Beeing applied high pressure and high temperature for millions
of years, these organic materials are transformed into oil.
When producing oil and gas the poreﬂuid is taken out of a rock formation. And by removing the
poreﬂuid, the eﬀective stress on the formation increases which often results in a compaction of
the reservoir (Omdal, 2010). This can have catastrophic impact on the environment above the
reservoir.
It is this compaction of the formation during the production that will be imitated in the laboratory
for this thesis. This will be done by loading oil saturated chalk samples with a fast load rate and a
low load rate. The low load rate imitates the overburden that is applied to a reservoir through
geological times, while the fast load rate imitates an oil production that happens for an extremely
short period of time comparatively.
By doing these tests, a material constant can be found that describes the inﬂuence of loading rate
eﬀects on compaction, called the b-factor (De Waal, 1986). This b-factor will be found to check the
poreﬂuid inﬂuence on the compaction of a medium. Therefore tests will be made on one type of
rock saturated with ﬁve diﬀerent ﬂuids to see what impact the ﬂuids have.
The aim of this project will be to show that the dynamic viscosity-density ratio of the poreﬂuid
has an inﬂuence on the rate-dependent deformation of a saturated rock. This ratio can also be
called the kinematic viscosity. It is Biots’ critical frequency that states that with a higher kinematic
viscosity of the poreﬂuid, the friction between rock and ﬂuid increases and therefore the strength
also increases.
To prove this theory two kinds of oil will be tested, as well as two fake ﬂuids that have the same
kinematic viscosity as the oils. The theory states that the b-factor should be the same for an oil
and a fake ﬂuid with the same kinematic viscosity.
Similar tests have been made before, but for this project the saturated samples were tested in an
oedometer cell, to see how the results turned out when the horizontal deformation of the sample
was eliminated.
The Danish oﬀshore area in the North Sea contains many oil reservoirs, and these reservoirs are
mainly from chalk. Therefore chalk is chosen to be the source of rock for these tests, to make them
as relevant as possible to the Danish oil industry.
Chalk is a highly porous media, so it can contain plenty of hydrocarbons. The best place to see
how a chalk reservoir would look like is at Stevns Klint. Stevns Klint is located in the coast of
Sjælland in Denmark and is about 12 km long and 41 m high.

2.1.4
Water weakening eﬀect
The strength of chalk is highly dependent on which type of pore-ﬂuid it contains. When producing
oil, water is often injected to the well to enhance the production. However it has been observed
that the mechanical strength of water saturated chalk is less than when dry or oil saturated (Risnes
et al. 2005).
Risnes et al. (2005) states, that some of the reasons for the water-weakening are due to capillary-,
chemical- and the solubility of calcite eﬀects. Because of the low solubility of calcite there has not
been given much attention to the chemical eﬀects of the weakening.
Capillary eﬀect makes a water-menisci appear between the grains and pulls them together to a
cohesion. During this process the chalk weakens and the menisci disappears.
Since chalk has narrow pores (Fabricius, 2003) the liquid-solid interaction at pore level has an
inﬂuence in the water weakening of the mechanical strength of chalk. Because of these narrow
pores, the dynamic viscosity of the pore-ﬂuid plays a big role in the deformation of the chalk. While
an oil saturated chalk sample will become stiﬀer than a dry sample, a water saturated sample will
become weaker. This is due to the dynamic viscosity of atmospheric air that is lower than oil but
higher than water (Fabricius, 2010).

loading rate is imitating an oil production.

happens to a formation when hydrocarbons are produced. The eﬀective stress will increase because
of the decreasing pore pressure due to the hydrocarbon production. The eﬀective stress is found as

2.4
Compaction of granular medium
De Waal (1986) mentions the two kinds of mechanisms a granular medium deforms of. The most
common one is a rearrangement of the grains, where the individual position of each grain changes
relatively to each other. Figure 2.6 shows how the grains can rotate or slide over each other.

Figure 2.6: Rearrangements of the grains in a medium when applied stress (De Waal, 1986).
The other kind of deformation for a granular medium is, when each individual grain gets crushed
and, thereby is deformed by itself. This mechanism is more rare since the bulk compressibility
of the medium is much larger than the matrix compressibility. However, when so much stress is
applied to a medium that the grains will not rearrange any more, the grains will start to deform
individually. This is often called hardening.
When producing oil, compaction of the reservoirs often appear with both positive and nega-
tive consequences. The compaction will increase the pressure in the reservoir and, thereby enhance
the oil recovery. However when there is too much deformation in a reservoir, a subsidence can
inﬂuence the land whether it is on- or oﬀshore (Settari, 2002). Two examples of this subsidence
are the project at the Willmington ﬁeld in California and the Ekoﬁsk ﬁeld in the North Sea. In
the latter example the rig had to be jacked up 3.5 meters at the cost of 1 billion dollars (Settari,
2002). Further, there can be problems such as casing-deformation which may have catastrophic
environmental impacts, or a reduction in permeability which can decrease the production (Settari,
2002).

Drilling the samples
Since the oedometer cell is exactly 38 mm in diameter, it was very diﬃcult to ﬁnd a core drill

drill with the diameter of 38.6 gave the samples a diameter of 37.7 mm which was the best result
(Appendix A.1). As shown in Figure 3.1 the core drill is quite long and the lump of chalk is very
big. There is no room for the drill where the lump is highest. So the lump had to be cut in half to
utilize as much as possible of it. A picture of this is shown in Figure 3.2.

Since the test varies between two load rates, a program was used to control the test. This program

To keep horizontal stress on the chalk specimens an oedometer cell was used. An oedometer
cell is basically a hollow steel cylinder that does not have any horizontal displacement. The cell
has an exact inner diameter of 1.5 inches and to apply pressure to the specimen two pistons were
used that ﬁt perfectly to the cell. To completely keep the pore ﬂuid from escaping between the
cell and the pistons a circle of teﬂon was wrapped around the pistons. The pore ﬂuid could not
escape through the pistons, but an outlet was opened for the ﬂuid to ﬂow through. This way pore
pressure was not applied during the tests.

4.8
Improvements
When working in a laboratory, there will always be some improvements that can be made. The
most obvious improvement for these experiments is, that there could have been more samples to
test. It would have been easier to ﬁgure out a pattern of behaviour by testing more than ﬁve
samples with the same poreﬂuid.
Furthermore, each individual sample could preferably have been tested for a longer amount of time.
Throughout this project, the samples were tested for 12 hours each, which meant that one test was
made a day. When the b-factor was measured from the results, it was very important that the slow
and the fast load rates were as clear as possible in the graphs. Especially the slow load rate needed
a lot of time before it was visible in the graphs and therefore the results would probably be easier
to work with, if longer time was spent on each sample.
So instead of using 12 hours for a test, they could have been extended up to approximately 20
hours, by making the slow loading phases longer. Then there would still be time to make one test a
day, and the results would have been easier to treat.

CONCLUSION
5
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to examine the rate-dependent compaction of chalk and its poreﬂuid
inﬂuence, by doing some laboratory tests with an oedometer cell. The tests were performed on
hydraulic oil- and isopar-l oil saturated samples, because isopar-l is a lighter oil than hydraulic oil.
To test if the kinematic viscosity had an impact on the strength of the samples two fake ﬂuids were
made with a mixture of water and ethylene glycol that had the exact same kinematic viscosity as
the oils.
Results from the porosity measurements showed, that the samples that were used for this project had
a very high porosity compared to previous experiments with chalk. This tendency was recognized
when calculations were made for the p’-q plots and the moduli.
Compared to results found by Madland (2002) and Schroeder (1996), the p’- and q values were
generally lower for the saturated samples from this experiment and this indicates some weaker
samples.
The b-factor, which was found from the stress-rate dependency, gave some unexpected results. The
average b-factor for isopar-l oil was a little bit lower than for the hydraulic oil, even though the
kinematic viscosity of hydraulic oil is vastly higher.
Furthermore, since the b-factor is a material factor and, therefore should be constant at all times, it
did not make much sense that the value was not the same when it was measured at three diﬀerent
stress states for the same chalk sample.
Looking at Biots critical frequency, the b-factors for the oils and their respective fake ﬂuids do not
resemble each other at all. This is probably due to the weakening eﬀect water has on chalk and,
therefore some other ﬂuid would be preferred for this experiment.
Generally, all the results had the same tendency. All the experiments could be grouped into two
groups. One with oil saturated samples, and one with a water mixture as the saturation ﬂuid. The
moduli for oil saturated samples were signiﬁcantly higher, as well as the p’-q plots. The b-factors
for these samples were also lower. Further, it seemed as the b-factor was decreasing during an
experiment on an oil saturated sample, while an opposite behaviour was seen for the samples with
a great percentage of water in the poreﬂuid.
The most obvious sign on the water weakening eﬀect is the hardening phase that only can be seen
in the stress-strain plots where water is a part of the poreﬂuid.
When producing oil from a chalk reservoir, and thereby increasing the eﬀective stress on it, problems
can appear. These problems are often solved by injecting water into the reservoir to maintain the
pressure. However, this can have a side eﬀect, since the chalks’ strength is reduced when saturated
with water. Results from these experiments show that the strength of chalk is reduced when
saturated with water, and this can have very a negative impact on the surrounding environment
since the compaction will increase.
