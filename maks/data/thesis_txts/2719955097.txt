Abstract
This work is part of the Light Reconﬁgurable Nano-Fluidics (LIRN) Project at DTU Nanolab, which aims to
control the liquids on open ﬂat surfaces using thin ﬁlms of metal oxides with the photoinduced hydrophilicity (PIH)
phenomenon to make certain areas more favourable to water with exposure to UV light. Here the TiO2, ZnO, HfO2
and V2O5 metal oxide used in an ALD deposited thin ﬁlm is broadly investigated concerning this phenomenon by
exposing them to UV light of 275 nm,300 nm, 365 nm and 405 nm wavelengths.
XPS, REELS and ISS surface characterisation methods narrowed down the possible explanation of the mechanics
behind the photoswitching occur since it has yet to be understood. With these techniques, the chemical composition
and the bandgap could be determined.
The thin ﬁlm photoswitching is modelled theoretically to quantify experimental data with a goniometer for both
the Apparent and the Advancing contact angle that deﬁne the waters behaviour as dependent on oxide thickness,
which were 30 nm for both ZnO and TiO2
Finally, this knowledge is used by using the thin ﬁlms of metal oxide to cover nanopillars to create a hemiwicking
ﬂow between the pillars. This ﬂow was investigated as dependent on the capillary width of a mask developed for
channels on a surface.

Introduction
Controlling liquids are a central part of the many devices used in our society, from energy harvesting to surface
cleaning. One central area is analytical chemistry to investigate the medical industry samples that need certain
liquids to react with each other to reveal information about a particular sample. Applications like this are called
Lab on Chip systems.
These systems can be made in the industry by injection moulding, ink-jet printhead or
lithography processes to make small microﬂuidic plastic circuits that contain reservoirs and capillaries (tunnels) to
replicate laboratory functions to perform an analytical investigation of samples [15, 35]. These parts are one-use
only, as there is no eﬃcient way to clean the tiny narrow capillaries. Therefore this leads to vast production and
waste of plastic.
Figure 1: Picture of a traditional Lab-on-Chip
system, that can replicate entire labarotory anal-
ysis. This picture[1] is an example, but they come
in many sizes and complexities, often designed to
do one speciﬁc analysis.
This thesis investigates an alternative approach to microﬂuidics; here, the intention is to create a surface where
certain areas are much more hydrophilic than others, making the liquid going to those areas much more favourable
for the system. Such areas can be made by covering surfaces with a thin metal-oxide thin ﬁlm which becomes
hydrophilic when exposed to ultraviolet (UV) light. This phenomenon has been demonstrated several times and is
called photo-induced hydrophilicity (PIH).
Controlling this eﬀect by exposing the surface through a mask or a spot from a laser will allow us to control where
the liquid will ﬂow. Reservoirs and open paths with no obstructing walls to prevent cleaning or provide friction to
the ﬂow; this process of controlling the liquid has been shown in ﬁgure 2. This eﬀect is reversible; the PIH eﬀect
can be removed by either leaving the surface in a dark room for weeks or by heating it at low temperatures for a
long time, called annealing. The restoration of the surfaces makes them reconﬁgurable. When all the open areas
have been used to run experiments, they can be cleaned, annealed and exposed in a new pattern.
While the mechanics behind the PIH eﬀect is not well understood, it has been shown in several diﬀerent metal-
oxides such as TiO2, ZnO, and V2O5 [18]. Educated guesses on how and why the switch involves carbon sticking
to the surface, blocking certain surface states which dislodged when exposed to UV light. Others suggest excitation
states in the oxygen vacancies in the oxide [18]. Some of these metal-oxides are already known and studied to use
in catalyse [33] where the eﬀects of the modiﬁed surface site can allow speciﬁc chemical reactions to run on the
surfaces. However, these studies have yet to investigate the eﬀect on wettability.
This master thesis is part of the Light Reconﬁgurable Nano-Fluidics (LIRN-Fluidics) project at DTU Nanolab,
whose goal is to develop this reconﬁgurable surface with the purpose of chemical analysis. The thesis is also a
continuation of two special course projects within the same topic [12, 11] made together with Mathias Adelmark.
His thesis also is part of the LIRN project and made in parallel to this thesis[10] with a focus on creating the
samples and structures.
Furthermore, the LIRN project intends to combine this reconﬁgurable surface with a nanostructured surface con-
sisting of pillars covered with a metal oxide that shows the best properties for photoswitching. In extension to
the large microﬂow that follows the illuminated path, a hemiwicking ﬂow would occur underneath the top of the
pillars that are self-driven by the Laplace pressure. These forces can replace the forces of the closed capillaries of
the traditional system [29].
UV light exposure’s control and precision are the only limits to deﬁning the pattern on the metal oxide. Reasonable
if these systems can be applied in patterns with sizes of 100x100 µm, up to half a million ﬂuid channels will ﬁt onto
a 6” wafer as calculated by professor Taboryski [29]. This number of channels would provide plenty of analysis

TiO2 is the best explored of the PIH metal-oxides, and its eﬃciency in photoswitch is well demonstrated. The

3.1
Hypothesis
This project aims to investigate and understand the PIH eﬀect in TiO2, HfO2, V2O5, and then process to work with
Mathias Adelmark to produce the most optimal pillars for the LIRN projects reconﬁgurable structured surfaces
[10]. Therefore this thesis will work with the following hypothesis:
1. The photoswitching occurrence depends on the metal oxide used for the thin ﬁlm and the wavelength of the
UV exposure.
2. The induced hydrophilicity is dependent on the thickness of the thin ﬁlm and the given dose of UV Light.
3. The original contact angle can be restored through either time in a dark room or annealing it.
4. The photoswitching phenomenon can be shown to depend on a chemical composition change, lattice defects
and electrical eﬀects.
5. Areas with lower contact angles can be used as tracks to funnel the liquid into deﬁned areas.
6. Drops placed on nanostructured pillars will lead to a hemiwicking ﬂow that extends further than the surface
ﬂow on top of the pillars.
7. The hemiwicking ﬂows propagation velocity and propagation distance from the reservoir depend on the path
width deﬁned by the mask and the given dose of UV Light.
The results of this project is split into three parts. The ﬁrst is a surface characterisation of the PIH phenomenon
using the XPS, REELS and ISS techniques introduced in section 6. Then a ﬂat sample characterisation is performed
by measuring the apparent and the advancing contact angle in section 8. The ﬁnal part investigates the surface
and hemiwicking ﬂow on structured samples in section 9. For comparison this should result in completion of the
learning objectives as shown in appendix I.

Theory
As stated in the introduction, the exact mechanism of the PIH eﬀect is unknown.
However, the interactions
between photons and electrons are well described. Since the properties of a material can be explained through
electron conﬁguration and their states, in this project, we assumed that the PIH eﬀect. The interactions of the
photons from the UV light and the electrons of the semiconductor are well-deﬁned[26].
1. Band-to-Band transition: Absorption of the photon results in the transition of an electron from the valence
band to the conduction band.
2. Impurity-to-band Transition: Semiconductors that are doped or have defects as depending on intentions
will have states in the bandgap that can trap electrons, photons can be absorbed to excite an electron from
the valence band to one of those states.
3. Free-Carrier Transitions: The photon gets absorbed, and an electron is excited higher within its current
band; when the electron relaxes, it releases the energy as a phonon (quantiﬁed excited vibrational state).
4. Phonon Transition: Long wavelength photons can create phonons directly in the material.
5. Excitonic Transition: Absorption of the photon resulting in Exciton, creating hole and electrons pair,
bound with a mutual Coulomb interaction.
When an electron is excited away from the valence band, it creates a hole that can move and act as a particle.
Furthermore, an excited state has a lifetime before the electron and hole recombine. Since the PIH eﬀect decay on
its own with time, Impurity-to-band (since defects always are present above 0 K) and band-to-band transition are
suspected to be the essential mechanics for the PIH eﬀect.
The energy transition in the electron state and the wavelength are linked together with equation 1 where λ is the
wavelength of the photon, h are the Planck constant, c0 are light speed in a vacuum, and E is the energy.
λ = hc0
E
(1)
Since the PIH eﬀect works with short wavelength photons, phonon transition seems improbable. TiO2, the most
promising oxide, are known to be very ﬂexible concerning the titanium-to-oxygen ratio. Oxygen vacancies will
travel to the surface to minimize the internal energies; this phenomenon is used for catalysis and could indicate the
impurity-to-band transitions. The bandgap will later be determined with the REELS method in section 6.4.
It has been shown that the oxygen in TiO2 act as hole-trap for both rutile and anatase crystal structures, although
more eﬀective in anatase and can induce potential distributions [36] and this eﬀect could be the reason for the
photoswitching.
The states are often represented by bandgaps as shown in ﬁgure 3 made by Ribeiro et al. [25]. Here an indirect
bandgap are indicated which is not usable in this project since we need conservation of momentum, any excitation
caused by photons must be direct so any valence and conduction band value should only be compared for the same
position in k-space.

4.1
Surface States
Atoms in a material can have diﬀerent positions near the surface, providing diﬀerent positions for molecules that
can react on it as dependent on energy of the atomic conﬁguration.
Since the wettability of a surface depends on the energy it takes to form that surface, these surface sites can also
aﬀect the PIH eﬀect. These sites also allow carbon removal from the surface, which could induce hydrophilicity.
The diﬀerent sites are deﬁned as bridges and folds depending on the atom being on a ”hill” or in a ”valley”.
Their conﬁguration are deﬁned by the number of neighbours. On this scale, planes are not necessarily ﬂat, so
steps between the layer can provide sites aswell. In fact the TiO2 surface are sawtooth and jagged when the (101)
direction form the plane surface [19]. This means that phenomenoms depending on the surface varies with details
of the exact crystal.
It is currently unknown if the UV light alters the surface states, this could be imagined to happen through defects
that would induce changes to surface conﬁguration. This changes could break pre existing bonds allowing the
surface sites that normally would be occupied, free to interact with the liquid. The formations of surface steps are
in the low magnitudes of 10 nJ cm−2 [19].
By being exposed to the atmosphere, many of these sites will be occupied by either hydroxyl groups or carbon
always present in the air. The defects and the chemical composition of the surface are investigated further with the
XPS and the ISS method in section 6.2 and 6.3.

4.2
The Annealing Process
Annealing is heating the sample at a low temperature for an extended period. Annealing will provide more mobility
to the electron, allowing it with increase in energy to have a chance to overcome potential barriers in the band
structures. It also allows defects to move and the crystal structure to rebuild itself.
Annealing will restore the metal oxide to the state before the photo exposure and its original wettability properties.
It was also used as a pre-anneal before any samples were exposed to ensure that all samples were in the same state;
more details on how are explained later in section 7.4.

4.3.1
Apparent, Advancing and Receding Contact angle
The contact angle can be found experimentally by looking at a drop in proﬁle; here, the curvature can be ﬁtted with
a Young-Laplace expression if the drop is symmetrical or a polynomial of it is not. From this, the contact angle can

Pinning states
The contact angle also depends on the pinning state between small structures on the surface. If the liquid penetrates

hydrophilic, so this problem is not expected to occur.

4.3.3
Hemiwicking Flow
The same eﬀect that causes pinning for hydrophilic surfaces can be utilized to pull the liquid. If the ﬂow goes in a
path with pillars, the meniscus has to go up vertical pillars. This will create a negative Laplace pressure that pulls
the liquid forward and thus lower the surface area from the meniscus. Therefore this will create a self-driven ﬂow.
This pressure will be balanced by the surface cost at a certain distance and come to a halt.
All of this can be calculated theoretically and has been done so by Rafael Taboryski and his group, who also presents
the necessary contact angle and ratio of the pillar dimensions found in his work [30]. For Fabrication of these pillars
see Mathias Adelmarks thesis [10]

this to below 35° and back. A signiﬁcant diﬀerence will make it easier to deﬁne detailed paths on a surface.

6
Surface Characterisation
The surface needs to be investigated with several techniques to understand how and why photoswitching occurs in
the metal oxide. The goal was not to reveal the phenomenon’s mechanics but to get a broader understanding.
Typical for all techniques was that they were performed on Thermoﬁsher Scientiﬁc Nexsa G2 Surface Analysis
System. This machine utilizes an aluminium k-alpha source to generate the x-ray (1486.7 eV). It analyzes the
emitted electrons using two concentric hemispheres with adjustable potential, thus sorting for speciﬁc electrons
with speciﬁc energies. All of these are in a vacuum with pressure kept around 10 × 10−8 Pa by two turbomolecular
pumps [2, 9].
6.1
Sputtering
Sputtering uses argon atoms or clusters to bombard the surface and remove elements residing there. The purpose
of sputtering is to remove carbon that unavoidable resides at the surface or remove the surface to obtain a depth
proﬁle of the material by removing surface layers before using other characterisation methods.
This process uses argon ions whose energy is adjustable between 2000 and 8000 eV. The eﬃciency depends on the
roughness of the surface, and the material sputtered. If the energies are not high enough, readsorption occurs. For
high energy the atomic structure can be damaged either by turning into suboxides, becoming amorphous, getting
the sputtered ions implemented as defects and atomic mixing of the elements in the structure. [37]. These factors
ensure that sputtering acts diﬀerently depending on the material and the sputtering parameters.
Any damage from sputtering can be determined using XPS (see section 6.2); this is done by observing the full-width
half measure (FWHM) and the peak energy levels, as several sputtering cycles occur, any signiﬁcant damage can
be noted as changes.
6.1.1
Depth proﬁling with monoatomic Argon
The advantage of doing depth proﬁles is detecting the change in states and chemical composition as dependent on
depth. So if the eﬀect occurs buried beneath the surface, other characterisation techniques can detect it inbetween
sputtering removes a layer.
To remove the oxides, monoatomic Argon is used since they have larger kinetic energy per cross-section area and can
penetrate deeper than clusters; this is also much more aggressive [2]. To know the actual depth of the sputtering,
we need to be able to convert sputtering time to sputtering depth. Since the sputtering rate depends on speciﬁc
materials and tools, the only way to determine it is by sputtering through a well-deﬁned thickness [37].
Nevertheless, before this, it is necessary to ensure that the oxide is stable when exposed to the argon beam. Diﬀerent
energies were attempted on TiO2, and the results of the lowest energy sputtering is shown in ﬁgure 11 where FWHM
and peak value are tracked while sputtered. Since they change shape, it appears that TiO2 degrades into a suboxide.
This mean that depth proﬁling with Argon is not possible in TiO2.
While clusters of Argon could be used, they are much more gentle [2]. Therefore, it would be challenging to estimate
the sputter rate since it will be very low. This could be done by sputtering through an extremely thin, but they
are diﬃcult to deﬁne. Since it could not be compared to TiO2, ZnO has yet to be investigated this way.

6.2
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS)
XPS are a method of acquiring information about the elemental and chemical state in the top 10 nm [16] or around
ten monolayers [2] of a sample surface, electrons probing deeper only contribute to the noise of energies below the
peak. A general introduction to the technique is given in section 6.2.1.
The chemical analysis of this method can provide insight into any changes in the ratio between oxygen and the metal
on the near-surface, and if a change due to induced vacancies occurs when it is exposed to UV light. This technique
is also used to monitor any changes in the naturally occurring Carbon on the surface. While it can measure the
change in Carbon, it cannot tell us if the Carbon are the source behind the photoswitching phenomeno,.
6.2.1
What is XPS
The method works by exposing the sample surface with an x-ray with well-deﬁned energy EP hoton. Electrons will
be dislodged from atoms in the sample and have a kinetic energy EKin characteristic to the orbital they came from,
and can be determined with equation 12 . Varying the allowed energy in the analyser allows for quantiﬁcation of the
amount of that element by integrating a ﬁtted Gaussian, or Lorentzian function [20] on the peak after subtracting
the background. Auger peaks are created by the atom dislodging electrons after reﬁlling an inner shell; these are
characteristic but not useful for quantiﬁcation, so they are generally not used.
While the orbital peaks are characteristic, their energy will be reduced by the potential created by a positive surface
since it lacks electron Epotential. To alleviate the shift in energy, a ﬂood gun is used to provide the surface with
low-energy (< 1 eV) electrons to reﬁll the orbitals , although this cannot prevent charging completely. All peaks
shift the same amount as they see the same potential and can be corrected by calculating the shift from very
well-known orbitals, such as Si 2p (103.4 eV) or C 1s (284.6 eV) [16].
EKin = EP hoton −Eb −EP otential
(12)
The peaks are named for their quantum number level from where the electron originates, and the atom’s environment
deﬁnes its position in energy. Orbitals produce two orbitals (except the s orbitals) with a diﬀerence in energy
matching in the spin-orbit splitting [16].

Furthermore, oxygen is particularly good at reionizing the ions, and the tails tend to scale with the amount of
oxygen in the top monolayer. [17]. Finally, there will be an early exponential noise due to ionized hydrogens
adsorbed to the surface that can cheat the analyzer.

6.4
Reﬂective Electron Energy Spectroscopy (REELS)
The purpose of REELS is to measure a surface’s electronic bandgap. Here an incident beam of electrons with
well-deﬁned energy bounces oﬀthe sample surface. The backscattered electrons are analysed to determine their
energy.
The electron can experience two things at the surface sample:
 Inelastic scattering via the deceleration in a polarisation ﬁeld of weakly bound solid state electrons in the
sample created in response to the incoming projectile. The projectile electron’s energy loss must be to the
sample since the electron used does not have excitation states.
 Elastic scattering with the screened coulomb potential of the nucleus, there is no loss of energy
The inelastic scattering excitations contain information about the bulk and surface excitations of the material, but
utilising this requires a vast knowledge of the pathway of the electron and Monte Carlo simulations, and so was not
performed. [34].
The gap in energy between the lowest inelastic scattered states and the scattered elastic state corresponds to the
electronic bandgap. Found by making a ﬁt that reduces the noise and making a horizontal line in the dip and a
linear ﬁt on the lowest state of the elastically scattered values as shown in ﬁgure 19.

and can excite electrons to a higher state. Right: An example of the data curve with the
inelastic data marked in blue and elastic scattering in green. The relevant data is zoomed

6.4.1
Parameters and ﬁt
The analysing depth of this technique is around 10 nm, and like the XPS method, it shares most of the parameters
since the methodology is similar 2.
Parameters varied on a TiO2 surface in a similar matter as for XPS. Energy steps and pass energy were reduced
in hope of a better resolution in the data since the tail of the gaussian peak expands wide. Reducing the energy
steps did not change the result; it allowed for ﬁtting on more datapoints, making the data more convincing. A 50%
decrease in pass energy reduced the signal to something close to unreadable.
The other parameters did not result in any reason to deviate from the default values.
The ﬁt has several parameters that need to be adjusted, such as the smooth width, maximum distance from the dip,
and minimum line width. These changes were made because several states can be at the lower end of the bandgap,
causing a misﬁt; in this case, ZnO would consistently ﬁt on the wrong states. These parameters were tweaked so it
visually ﬁts the lowest state; this involved reducing the max distance from the dip and the line width to reduce the
interval the software ﬁts on.

7.4
Annealing
In previous projects, it was demonstrated the inﬂuence of pre-annealing [12, 11], which resulted in less carbon
attached, and provided a more stable measurement of the contact angle. The setup optimized in the second project
[12] is the one that is used in this thesis as well. This consists of a hot plate (Schott Instruments SLR 00997974)
with an annealing block designed by me and milled by Søren M. B. Petersen.
The purpose of the annealing block is to add a lot of good heat-conductive material that encapsulated the sample in
small volumes that were easy to keep homogenous. To calibrate, the temperature was measured with a multimeter
(Agilant 34410A 6.5 digit multimeter) and a Platinium PT100 resistance (RS123-5593) placed in a 4-wire setup
to remove the inﬂuence of resistance from the wire. The calibration method and results from the previous project
[12] are given in appendix B.2. The design drawings of the annealing block are given in appendix B.3. The ﬁnal
temperature used was 105 ± 5 °C as it is a bit cooler in the lid, and warmer in the bottom piece. Internally in the
project group, this was referred to as 100 °C.
The annealing parameters used in this project were to preheat the annealing block with the hot plate setting 10 in
10 min before reducing it to setting 4, which will stabilize around the 105 °C. The samples were then annealed for
1 h.
This was a deviation from the earlier project. While it is the same temperature the block stabilized around, in the
previous project it was pre-heated on setting 10 for 14 min and the samples were annealed for 45 min.
The reason for this change in duration was that annealing of 45 min were not enough to remove a normal dose of
UV, as seen later. While pre-annealing does not remove any dose, it was deemed that annealing for longer would
be on the safe side since there was an assumption that the sample could not be annealed too much. The reduction
of pre-anneal time from 14 min to 10 min on setting 10 was admittedly an error from my side. While the exact
consequences of this are unknown, it is assumed the impact is negligible. The reason for this is that after 10 min,
the samples have reached above 80 °C and it would reach the desired temperature within 15 min that the annealing
time was extended with. This error was persistent throughout all the experiments in this thesis.

7.6
Goniometer Measurements
To measure the contact angle of the surface, a goniometer was used, here a camera records a video of a drop of
liquid on a sample with backlight lightning. This creates a high-contrast ﬁlm where each frame is analysed by
the accompanying software, This analyses the baseline, and the two contact angles which average into one. Doing
this is the sessile drop method, these ﬁlms tend to be short before evaporation is dominant and the few seconds of
recording provide amble data to give a good measurement of the apparent contact angle.
The other method used in this project is the inﬂation method where the drop in proﬁle is pierced by a needle, that
while the ﬁlm is recorded, inﬂates the drop with water, this increases the contact angle to its advancing contact
angle where the baseline then has to expand, then it will increase baseline. Afterwards the opposite are happening
the drop is deﬂated until the receding contact angle is reached and the baseline is forced to be reduced. If the drop
inﬂates or deﬂates inhomogeneous, the needle can be moved to keep the drop in focus and/or centre of the drop,
as it did not disturb the measurement in the process.
In this project, this led to two methods, internally in the group referenced as the wet ﬂood exposure (WFE) and
the dry ﬂood exposure (DFE):
7.6.1
Wet Flood Exposure (WFE)
The sessile drop method was used where the UV lamp was installed above the drop, and the time of the measurement
was extended signiﬁcantly. Each frame then presented a measurement of the apparent contact angle at a linear
increase in dose.
To compensate for the evaporation a larger initial drop was used of either 5 µL for shorter
measurements below 10 min or 20 µL for longer measurements up to 30 min.
It is intended to create the paths on the structured samples with a mask, so having a wet surface would not normally
be an option. This means that the measurements do not represent the environment of the photoswitching. Another
issue with this method is that we are measuring the apparent contact angle where we would use the advancing
contact angle, furthermore, the drop provides a lens eﬀect making the dose distribution inhomogeneous.
The reason why this method is still valuable is that it is assumed that the parameters behind photoswitching are
independent of these factors. This means it can be used to identify those metal oxides that switch well. This is
important since this method is much faster to obtain data for diﬀerent dosages than the alternative. as it increases
while the contact angle measurements are taken, this allows for a broader investigation of the parameter space.

7.6.2
Dry Flood Exposure (DFE)
The samples are exposed to the UV LED just before the sample are transported to the Goniometer where the
inﬂation method is used to measure the advancing contact angle. Before measurement, an initial drop was placed
beneath the needle of 3.5 µL. The purpose of this was to skip some of the increase in the contact angle as the
interesting frames are when the baseline increases since this is when we know that we are measuring the advancing
contact angle. For surfaces with high contact angles, the initial drop can also estimate the apparent contact angle.
however, for small contact angles, the meniscus from the needle will distort the measurement too much.
The inﬂation of the drop was done for 60 s at a rate of 0.5 µL s−1 that normally allows the baseline to be extended
from 2 mm to 7 mm and provide around 3-200 datapoints of the advancing contact angle. The deﬂation was kept
at the same rate for 80 s or until the water would run out. The data for the receding contact angle produced very
few data points and were prone to misﬁts. To change this larger drops (and thereby surface area) are needed. You
could detach the deﬂation from the inﬂation, this would provide an opportunity to widen the drop by moving the
needle and widening the baseline and reducing the contact angle before deﬂation. But the time per experiment
would increase drastically.
For this project, the advancing contact angle is an interesting value to obtain hemiwicking ﬂow. While data for the
receding contact angle was taken, it was not used.

7.6.4
The inﬂation data was analyzed
If the surface were homogenous, few data points would not be any problem, but as can be seen in ﬁgure 30,
inhomogeneities in the surface give a variation in the measured advancing contact angle. This means that there is
uncertainty in the data that the software ignores.
So a new method was used to evaluate the advancing contact angle. A python script (shown in appendix H) went
through the ﬁts from the software and selected what data points were suitable to represent the advancing contact
angle from the following criteria.
 Datapoint below a baseline of 3 mm was discarded since they were aﬀected by the pinning state from the
placement of the initial drop.
 Any point where there is more than 0.1 mm diﬀerence in baseline to both the next and the previous data
point is usually misﬁtted and discarded.
 Any point where there is more than 1 deg diﬀerence in contact angle measured to both the next and previous
data point is usually a misﬁt and discarded.
 Data points at the end of the measurement are heavily dominated by inhomogeneities that can send the drop
next to the edge of the sample, and these points are discarded as well.
Any points left, that have a larger contact angle than the previous and the next data point or next to misﬁts, are
then treated as an advancing contact angle measurement. From these points, the average and standard deviation
of the measurement can be determined. Any measurement with less than 3 data points was discarded, and all data
were visually inspected, examples of this are shown in ﬁgure 31.
This means that we need a method of handling multiple data points each with its standard deviation. For this, the
method of Weighted Errors from chapter 7 in Taylor’s book about Error Analysis [31] was used.
This method assumes that the data is representing an unknown Gaussian distribution of the true value.
This
gaussian can then be determined from the measurements taken by weighing the data by their errors, the higher it

and are shown in ﬁgure 34. The pattern was the same with diﬀerent path width dimensions, and each pattern was

planned and each pattern should be able to be illuminated individually and thus it was limited by the spot size of
the LED.
The Pattern consisted of four reservoirs with a 1 mm radius placed in the corner of the samples so they are easy to
hit with en pipette. This radius was increased for the large samples.
In between the four reservoirs are paths that connect them all through the centre of the pattern. These have
diﬀerent width between 100 µm and 900 µm.
The reason the structure has four reservoirs are to be able to run four tests per sample under the microscope. The
second purpose is to place multiple drops and let the ﬂows meet in the middle, as this interaction could down the
road be interesting to investigate as well.
Finally, the mask is made externally by a supplier and made in chrome-covered glass.

Characterisation of ﬂat photoswitching thin-ﬁlm samples
The metal oxide thicknesses and photoswitching behaviour were explored on plain ﬂat surfaces. This separates
the ﬁlm properties from the nanostructures that aﬀect the wettability on their own; furthermore, producing ﬂat
samples rather than structured ones is signiﬁcantly faster. The key parameters of interest here are:
 The wavelength of UV Light, since the behaviour might act diﬀerently. Furthermore, what limit is there
before the phenomenon does not occur?
 The dose, since more exposure, means more photoswitching, how fast and how much can it be reduced?
 The thin ﬁlm thickness inﬂuences the photoswitching. Does it match the expectancy of the absorption theory
in section 5?
 How larger is the diﬀerence between the diﬀerent materials?
 The Reannealing time necessary to recover the wettability of the sample for the dose needed to switch it fully.
 Are the switching repeatable?
The purpose of this investigation is not to map out the parameters. Instead, the focus is to ﬁnd a suitable thickness
and material to produce the structures with and to get an initial impression of the behaviour of the photoswitching
phenomenon. Many of these experiments can be repeated later for structured samples since the very geometry of
the samples changes, and so does the optimal parameters for utilization of the photoswitching phenomenon.

8.4.1
Stop of Photoswitching
As a follow-up to investigate what stops the photoswitching at the ﬁfth exposure in ﬁgure 44, fresh samples from
the same wafers were exposed to the same treatment as the old ones except for the measurement of the contact
angle of the surface.
The reason for this is that the water was suspected to be contaminated with something that could accumulate at
the surface until a barrier between the metal oxide and the water was formed to such a degree that the contact
angle was deﬁned by this and not the metal oxide.
Another suspicion was the handling of the samples involving placement of the drop, and the removal of the drop
afterwards induced scratches from additional handling.
Therefore a set of fresh samples was kept dry throughout the process. In contrast, another set of samples was
dipped in fresh taken water from the dispenser in a beaker that was blown free from particles to ensure that there
was no particle contamination of the water in the lab.
The assessment was that if the wet sample switched well after the same treatment as the old samples, the water
used previously was contaminated. If it did not switch, but the dry ones did, it means the fault is in the exposure
to water. Finally, if nothing would switch, it could be concluded that it was inherited from the photoswitching
phenomenon.
As shown in ﬁgure 45 the initial measurement of the new samples indicated that it would be something in the
phenomenon since they also had a signiﬁcant decrease in the photoswitching in the ﬁfth exposure. To save time,
the decision was taken to skip the extra restoring annealing step to see if the samples would stop photoswitching
entirely after a couple of exposures.
Despite this expectation, the advancing contact angle was lowered, although not the same as the initial photo-
switching. It would appear that over-annealing of a sample is possible. To such a degree, a larger dose is needed
for the sample to switch. This means that the annealing time and exposure dose strikes a balance.
This also means that the prior assumption about setting the annealing to 105 ± 5 °C for 60 min is a too signiﬁcant
simpliﬁcation. This experiment should, in the future, be repeated with various annealing times to map out the
eﬀect of annealing on the photoswitching phenomenon.

Discussion
The project started with a narrow approach focused on characterising a few parameters. However, during the
project, this changed into a broad investigation of the photoswitching phenomenon instead. While it raises a valid
criticism for the lack of data points in the various experiments, it allowed for a better understanding of the inﬂuence
of diﬀerent parameters regarding photoswitching. An example of this could be the importance of annealing in the
recovery process, which challenged the assumption that it was not possible over-anneal the samples.
For the characterisation of the phenomenon, the REELS investigation revealed that having wavelengths with energies
surpassing the material’s bandgap was not essential. Although the results of the experiment diﬀer a bit from values
reported elsewhere, a quick measurement of the resistance of the surface when exposing it to UV signiﬁed no change
in the resistance. If electrons were reaching the conduction band, the resistance would be lowered.
These results indicate that the eﬀect of photoswitching stems from states in the bandgap, which would be defects.
However, the chemical composition in the surface layer and the top 10 nm does not change, which means that is
not an increase in the vacancies as expected.
The ISS data reveals that something signiﬁcant happened to the charging of the surface; higher doses of exposure
led to an increase in signal, and carbon layers on the surface did not cause it. This can only happen if less of the
He+ probing particles are neutralised, and this must mean that the surface charges change.
While the exact eﬀect was not identiﬁed, the photoswitching can be connected to the reduction of the C-C bonds on
the surface; XPS reveals an immediate decrease when exposed. ZnO C-C bonds decreased slower, which connects
to a lower photoswitching. It will, however, take an experiment of a cleaned metal oxide surface that does not
aﬀect the oxide to determine if carbon is the cause of the change in hydrophilicity. This was attempted with oxygen
plasma in a previous project [12], but it aﬀected the oxide.
An argument for the carbon not to be the cause of the photoswitching but a consequence of lowering surface energies
can be made. This is because ZnO’s C-C peak was gone after 12 min of exposure to 365 nm while for dry ﬂood
exposure, a larger dose was needed to entirely switch the oxide advancing contact angles below 80°.
There are also indications that the same mechanics does not govern the ZnO and TiO2 regarding photoswitching
since the dry and wet ﬂood exposure behaved so diﬀerently for both TiO2 and ZnO. Where ZnO switched fastest
when there was water on the surface, TiO2 switched faster without water. Furthermore, there is also a diﬀerent
behaviour of the number of hydroxyl groups after exposure on the two surfaces. However, much of this is more
challenging to be sure of since the carbon obscured much of the data.
To get a better grip on the photoswitching eﬀect, a deeper understanding of the oxides band structure should be
researched; this will limit what defects can be the cause. Furthermore, the density function theorem (DFT) has
previously helped map out the surface states of a material.
HfO2 were known to be challenging to switch from previous project[12], but even a more detailed investigation from
Rucha failed to show any photoswitching. V2O5 were unstable unless annealed where the contact angle instead
became too low before exposure to be practical. Both materials were hence discarded early in the project to avoid
taking time from samples that would be useful in the nanostructure design.
This left TiO2 and ZnO that worked, where TiO2 appeared to be much more eﬀective. When investigating the
thickness dependencies, it was shown that the thicknesses of 25 - 40 nm were the best, as predicted by the theory
that seems to work for this range when ﬁtting the model to the data. However, the model and theory seems to be
too simple to explain the thicker thicknesses. The models also do not take into account the signiﬁcant delay there
was before photoswitching occurred.
For thin ﬁlms below 20 nm, it is suspected that the known high stress induced by the ALD process blocks the
photoswitching since stress often is known to bend bands and can pin defects.
In contrast to the assumption before experimentation, what was discovered was that the dry ﬂood and the wet
ﬂood exposure methods yield two very diﬀerent results in photoswitching; the presence of the drop is signiﬁcant to
the eﬀect.
64
==PAGE:66==
Generally, deviations were a problem that plagued a lot of the results. For example, comparing the doses proved
diﬃcult because a meagre power output from the LED was used due to an undiscovered current limit. The minimum
uncertainties for the lamp were 1 mW which are relatively huge when the power you give is below 20 mW—increasing
the power output by removing the 0.2 A current limit and increasing the output, making the comparison between
doses easier. Most data was chosen to be shown as dependent on exposure time instead of dose simply because
power rates were kept constant; this is no longer the case when future experiments increase the power. All data
should be shown as dependent on dose instead.
Some deviations also stem from the variation in the humidity; this could be lowered by adding a dehumidiﬁer to
the laboratory. The pressure also varied, but this was more diﬃcult to control.
It was also discovered that the achievable advancing contact angle limit depended on a pinning eﬀect that was
dominant below 30°. This led to an increase in uncertainties, which comes from roughnesses. All of the ﬁtted tau
models suggest that the ﬁnal apparent contact angle for TiO2 would be around 12° although almost all measurements
could not get below 22° due to pinning.
To get a better understanding of the roughness and the surface dependency, an experiment could investigate the
surface behaviour of photoswitched samples as dependent on the radial position of the samples on the wafer after
production. While the ALD fabrication process is suitable for creating very homogenous thicknesses, the roughness
of the silicon wafer would be carried over, and those are polished beforehand. One could suspect that it is not
as homogenous as desired as the measurement for the switched samples advancing contact angle seems to vary
signiﬁcantly in their behaviour due to the diﬀerence in pinning eﬀect.
The Recovery experiment revealed that the photoswitching failed after 5th cycle of exposure and reannealing.
Nevertheless, a further experiment shows that annealing is a more dominant player than just restoring the surface
to its original state. This is critical as it was an underlying assumption of the system that it would be; this is
why its annealing parameters were arbitrarily chosen in a range assumed to be enough to limit the variables in the
parameter space. With the results from the recovery experiments, these parameters should be varied to investigate
the eﬀect o photoswitching. Pre-annealing had an eﬀect in previous projects [12, 11], and this might be why.
This also makes the error of not pre-heating the annealing block more signiﬁcant. While it still does not discard
the data since the error was made consistent, comparing it to others’ results is diﬃcult.
With all previously obtained knowledge, Mathias [10] made two versions of the structured surfaces with the nanopil-
lars. While it was made at the last minute of the project, a quick investigation of them was performed.
It was not expected that there would be as good results on the bad aspect ratio pillars. While the 100 µm tracks
proved to be a bit too short to work probably, it was anticipated that there would be a limit, and even expected
for wider tracks since the curvature

Conclusions
This project successfully performed a broad investigation of the photoswitching phenomenon, ﬂat sample charac-
terization and an investigation of the structures samples with hemiwicking ﬂow.
It was shown with XPS that carbon has a vital role in the photoswitching event either as a consequence or as a
source for the phenomenon. While there is no change in vacancy defects in either the top 10 layers or in the surface
layers as shown with ISS, defects states in the bandgap play an essential role since energies of 365 nm light do not
surpass the bandgap of the good switching TiO2.
It was also demonstrated that the photoswitching depends on the metal oxide, where TiO2 was the absolute best,
but ZnO could also work. The photoswitching depended on the thickness, although uncertainties obscured the
wavelength dependencies.
Either way, the induced hydrophilicity could be removed through a dark room and
annealing. Factors like annealing time, substrate and the environment of a drop proved to be more inﬂuenceable
than ﬁrst assumed.
It was impossible to demonstrate the velocity of the hemiwicking ﬂow due to low framerate and quick stabilization.
However, the hemiwicking ﬂow was shown as dependent on diﬀerent pathwidths and the dose, and this can be used
to transport liquid successfully.
Finally, the hemiwicking ﬂow through a path deﬁned by a mask, which was the fundamental concept behind LIRN,
and a good understanding was achieved. The next step would be to optimise them on pillars.
12
Perspectivation
To continue with this project, it is paramount to reduce the uncertainties of the photoswitching behaviour, as
it hinders measurements below 30°. Humidity, pressure and surface roughness vary greatly and impact the mea-
surements. These are diﬃcult to control, but they can lead to a better and more deﬁnitive demonstration of the
phenomenon.
While more data can also help, many uncertainties are inherent in the methodology.
To better understand the photoswitching phenomenon, a way to clean the TiO2 needs to be found that does
not induce hydrophilicity to rule out carbons’ role in the phenomenon. Furthermore, more on am energy-focused
analysis of the structure should be performed for a single metal oxide to indicate what states have energies that
can pin electrons given the energy of 365 nm photons. This could perhaps be done with density functional theory
(DFT).
Suppose there was more time to do experiments before the deadline; annealing temperature would be the focus to
indicate its inﬂuence when applied in large doses. It would be similar to the recovery plot, except the exposure
dose would be kept constant, and the annealing time would be varied. Another experiment would be a redo and
more extensive investigation of the diﬀerent doses and wavelengths, but increasing the power, also to ensure that
the inﬂuence of dose rate increases linearly.

the investigation of pillars was allocated initially. This original project plan are in appendix J.
However, as the project progressed, there was much doubt about the uncertainties and the parameters that could
inﬂuence the results. This led to a focus on broadening and diversifying the investigation. This was chosen since
the parameters chosen not to be investigated added a limit to understanding the planned data. While many of the
experiments could do with more data points, a better grasp of the phenomenon was achieved.

Learning Objective
1. Conduct a comprehensive literature study of the relevant research
2. Map out relevant materials properties for available metal oxides, such as TiO2, ZnO, HfO2, and V2O5 regard-
ing their wetting properties upon illumination, such as metal thickness, contact angles, and UV illumination
doses required to switch their surface wetting properties
3. Employ reﬂection electron energy loss spectroscopy (REELS) to obtain band gaps for the selected metal oxides
4. Employ x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) to verify the expected materials composition of the fabricated
surfaces
5. Employ ellipsometry to obtain the ﬁlm thicknesses of the metal oxide thin ﬁlms for planar surfaces, and relate
those measurements to the UV illumination that triggers super wetting
6. Design and build a suitable setup in the lab that allows semi-automated UV illumination, water application,
and video recording under a microscope of the ﬂow on fabricated surfaces
7. Collaborate with other students and supervisors on performing a functional characterization of the nanofab-
ricated surfaces using the built setup
8. Create relevant documentation of the characterization methods used
9. Ensure safe storage of all data 10. Analyse the obtained data and write a comprehensive report that documents
the work
10. Present the results at an oral defence.
