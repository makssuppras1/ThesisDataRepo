Abstract
The dynamic dictionary is one of the most ubiquitous data structures due to its widespread
usefulness in various areas. It is used in most programs and is also a cornerstone in
many algorithms and data structures, notably dynamic filters. This thesis explores the
theoretical landscape of dynamic dictionaries culminating in an understanding of the re­
cent asymptotically optimal dynamic dictionary that performs queries in O(1) time, and
updates and deletions in O(k) time with high probability. It achieves this while wastes
only O(lg(k+1) n) bits per element, for any 1 ≤k < lg∗n, where lg(k) n denotes the k′th
iterated logarithm. This implies that it wastes only a constant number of bits per element
when k = O(lg∗n). Along the way, several versatile and broadly applicable insights are
developed, which are likely to be useful in related problems.

1
Introduction
This report discusses the classic dynamic dictionary problem, which aims to support up­
dates and queries on a set of elements. Specifically, the focus is on hash maps, which
use randomness to achieve faster operations while using less space. Randomised dy­
namic dictionaries have been researched since they were first introduced in 1953, and
have continued to evolve and improve since, culminating in a theoretically optimal solu­
tion being found in 2021[1][2]. Today, it is one of the most ubiquitous data structures,
not only widely used in practice, but also often a key part of other algorithms and data
structures, notably dynamic filters.
Since chained hashing was introduced in 1953, many developments have happened.
Simple alternatives, such as linear probing and cuckoo hashing, have led to different
guarantees, most of which surpass what deterministic structures can offer[3]. Advances
in analysis have provided greater insight into the behaviours of the algorithms, leading
to better expected behaviour and, in some cases, high­probability guarantees. More re­
cently, multi­level data structures have led to improvements in theoretical performance,
culminating in the achievement of optimal asymptotic space and time[2][4].
This report aims to explore and understand the theoretical landscape and the methods
that have led to the current state of the art. Furthermore, new proofs and methods are
introduced, and edge cases are studied, all to deepen understanding. Many of the things
covered have been demonstrated and proven before; moreover, due to my recent intro­
duction to this vast area of research, others have likely studied much of what I claim to
have figured out. Newer the less, the things I consider my main contributions are:
• Providing an overview of some methods, algorithmic techniques, analytical tech­
niques and more.
• Showing that the upper bound on the information theoretic optimum, of using n lg e
extra bits per element, is to be expected under the usual assumption that the size
of the universe is polynomial in the number of elements.
• Showing how binary tries can be encoded to reach a space­efficient but slow solution
• Showing several uses of small binary tries.
• Giving a stronger bound on the overflow of Backyard Cuckoo hashing.
• Analysing recursion when applied to hashing, and how randomised data structures
are somewhat antithetical to recursion.
• Proposing the ”Backyard­less” succinct dictionary, a (in my opinion) simpler version
of Backyard Cuckoo hashing.

2.1
The problem
The main problem that will be discussed in this report is the dynamic dictionary problem.
Definition 2.1 (Dynamic Dictionary). A dynamic dictionary maintains a changing set St of
key­value pairs (k, v), where keys are unique, and keys and values come from the known
universes of keys and values U and V. The subscript t marks a period in time, aka St is
the set after t operations. The set is updated and queried via the following operations:
• Insert(k, v): Adds or replaces the key­value pair: St+1 = St \ {(k, ·)} ∪{(k, v)}.
• Delete(k): Removes the key (if present): St+1 = St \ {(k, v) | (k, v) ∈St}.
• Query(k): Returns the value v such that (k, v) ∈St, if such a pair exists; otherwise
returns ⊥.
Queries do not modify the dictionary and are not counted as updates.
The dynamic dictionary problem can manifest under various circumstances. Some exam­
ples include maintaining a set of users, a set of products, and a set of articles, along with
the specific information for each user, product, and article. Since programs frequently
work with and store various objects, it becomes clear why dynamic dictionaries are com­
monly used in practice.
There are also many related data structures, two examples are:
Definition 2.2 (Dynamic Multiset Dictionary). A dynamic multiset dictionary maintains a
changing multiset St : k →N, which maps keys to their current multiplicity, where the
keys are from the known universe U. The following operations are supported:
• Insert(k): Increases the multiplicity of k: St+1(k) = St(k) + 1.
• Delete(k): Decreases the multiplicity of k: St+1(k) = max(St(k) −1, 0).
• Query(k): Returns the current multiplicity: St(k).
Definition 2.3 (Dynamic Filter). A dynamic filter maintains an approximate representation
of a changing set St of keys from the known universe U. The filter supports probabilistic
membership queries with a bounded false positive rate ϵ.
• Insert(k): Adds the key k to the filter.
• Delete(k): Removes the key k from the filter.
• Query(k): Returns:

These problems are closely related, meaning that better solutions to one of them often
lead to better solutions to the others[2]. Numerous other data structures and algorithms
use dynamic dictionaries as building blocks. Therefore, better dynamic dictionaries can
have a broad impact.
The primary objective of this thesis is to develop a dynamic dictionary that supports all
operations efficiently with minimal space overhead. The secondary goal is to deepen
understanding of the area by exploring various ideas, as well as the limitations of existing
ones.

Notation

2.1.2
The setting
The RAM model of computation is employed, where it is assumed that memory words are
of size O(lg n) and that bit­wise and arithmetic operations can be performed in constant
time on memory words. It is assumed that one can find the position of the first occurrence
of a pattern in a memory word in constant time (e.g. finding the first 1 bit in a memory
word).
Furthermore, it is assumed that the elements originate from a universe U that is polynomial
in size compared to the number of elements n. It is also assumed that arbitrary pointers
are of size O(lg n). Initially, the more classic approach to space is employed, e.g., counting
memory words instead of bits; however, later on, bits are used.

2.2
Randomized data structures
Typically, when people think of data structures, they commonly consider adding structure
to data to exploit the structure later and achieve faster operations. One can solve the
dynamic dictionary problem, simply with an array and linear search in linear space and
time, or by ordering the elements using for instance red­black trees, in O(n) space and
O(lg n) time[5] or Y­fast tries in O(n) space and O(lg lg n) time[6].
Counterintuitively, using randomness can often lead to faster, simpler, and more space­
efficient solutions. When ordering the elements in a specific way, one typically does not
assume anything about the probability of different inputs. This forces the analysis into
an adversarial viewpoint in which one must ensure good performance even in the worst­
case. In some cases, however, one can use randomness to place the elements, resulting
in performance that is most often great. Randomness can effectively change the focus
from analysing the worst­case behaviour to analysing the expected behaviour and giving
guarantees on the probable cases. This enables us to utilise the vast toolset of probability
theory to analyse how the data structure will likely behave.
For example, if one were to distribute elements into array slots uniformly at random, one
can calculate guarantees showing that it will most likely result in a relatively even distri­
bution. One can then exploit this even distribution by, for instance, breaking the problem
down into smaller subproblems that are easier to solve. The first randomised data struc­
ture (as far as I know) solves the dynamic dictionary problem in O(n) space and O(1)
expected time. This data structure is chained hashing, which was introduced in 1953 by
Hans Peter Luhn in an internal IBM memorandum. Since then, the best and simplest
solutions have been based on randomness.
2.2.1
Hashing
However, if one uses randomness to distribute an element x throughout the data structure,
how does one then find x later? The trick is to have a hash function h that maps x to a
uniform random value. More formally, h maps x from the set U of keys to the set R of
possible random values. Here, U denotes the universe of elements, and R is typically
some location in the data structure. Then, if one wants to figure out the randomness that
was dealt to x, one can compute h(x) again. However, suppose the same h is used in all
instances of the data structure. In that case, this reverts the analysis to the adversarial
perspective, since an adversary could craft inputs that perform poorly under h. The fix is
to have a whole family H of hash functions, such that a random h ∈H maps all elements
x ∈U independently and uniformly at random to a value h(x) ∈R. Then, if one picks this
h ∈H randomly and independently of the data and operations, one can apply probabilistic
analysis to analyse the data structure[7]. More formally:
Definition 2.4 (Hash function). Let X be a set of unique elements from the domain U
such that X ⊆U. Given a co­domain R, then there exists a set H of hash functions,
such that if one uniformly at random picks a h ∈H, then the multi­set {h(x)|x ∈X} is a
multi­set of elements picked uniformly (i.i.d.) with replacement from the set R.
There are some hairs in assuming that the data is picked independently from the hash
function. It is also tricky to practically justify the possibility of quickly selecting and evalu­
ating a uniform hash function. To proceed with dynamic dictionaries, these issues will be
postponed until sections 2.4.1 and 2.9.

2.3
Chained hashing
The chained hashing data structure uses a hash function that maps each element uni­
formly at random to an index in an array of size m = Θ(n), where n is the number of
elements. Each index in the array hosts a linked list that contains all the key­value pairs
(k, v) that hash to the index.
Theorem 2.5. Chained hashing solves the dynamic dictionary problem in O(n) space
(measured in memory words), and O(1) expected time for all operations.
Proof. Chained hashing uses an array of length m = Θ(n), where each index stores a
pointer to a linked list. This pointer uses a constant number of memory words. The total
number of links across all the linked lists is n, one for each element. Each link stores a
pointer to the next link and one key­value pair (k, v), which in total requires a constant
number of memory words. The total space usage is therefore Θ(n) + O(n) = O(n).
As for time, insertions can always be performed in constant time by simply making the
new element the head of the linked list. However, deletions and queries require locating
the specific element in the linked list. In the worst case, where all n elements hash to the
same index, a linear search through the list would take O(n) time. But since m = Θ(n), the
expected number of elements at any index bi is E[|bi|] =

2.4
Good enough?
It might seem as if Chained hashing is already sufficiently good. In many applications,
O(n) space and O(1) expected time are sufficient. But then, why has it continued to be
researched for the last 70 years?
2.4.1
Is O(1) expected time good enough?
There are two cases to consider: a non­adversarial user and an adversarial user. A
non­adversarial user has some task to solve, where the data and operations have no
connection with the randomness used in the data structure. However, the adversarial
user aims to find and exploit weaknesses of the data structure to slow it down.
Non­adversarial user
Constant expected time tells us little about the distribution of time across different ele­
ments. For instance, suppose √n elements hash to the same index while the rest expe­
rience no collisions. Then those √n elements would take O(√n) time for deletions and
insertions, while the rest take constant time. Yet, the average is still O(1) expected time:

This might be bad news if the user tries to solve real­time applications that require all
operations to be fast, since an O(1) expected time might mean that some operations take
Θ(√n) time, which is quite slow.
One might think that the speed will average out due to the faster elements. However,
there’s no guarantee the user will interact randomly with the data structure. The user
might frequently access certain elements. If these instances are slow, the average time
per operation for this particular instance could be significantly worse. Of course, if the
user had one example for each of the h ∈H possible hash functions, then on average
the performance would be pretty good, but this is of little consolation if the user instead
has only one unlucky perpetually running instance.
Therefore, it is preferred if operations take O(1) time, not on average, but almost always.
Specifically, we would want it to take O(1) time with high probability (w.h.p), where w.h.p.

The rarity of these unfortunate events is valuable in itself. It also makes them easier to
overcome. This is because one can typically rehash the data using new randomness in

In some cases, this rehashing can even be done in a de­amortised manner, as will be
discussed further in 2.8.3.
Adversarial user
The user might be an adversary, with the specific aim of slowing down the data structure.
We still assume the adversary does not have direct access to the data structure internals.
Instead, they rely on side­channel information. This is a fair assumption, as anyone with
full access is likely to have more effective attack vectors. The amount of time each opera­
tion of the data structure takes leaks some information about the assumed secret internal
randomness of the data structure. For instance, if one queries all elements and measures
how long each takes, one can infer which elements are slow, and therefore which have
many hash collisions. An adversary with a clock could exploit this information leakage to
select data and operations that intentionally slow down the data structure [8].
The clocked adversary, by picking elements and operations that depend on the internal
randomness, invalidates the assumption that hashed elements are independent of the

the worst case. However, getting O(1) time for all operations proves to be difficult, since
when using randomness, one can always get unlucky. If one instead gets O(1) w.h.p.,
then very rarely information is leaked. Furthermore, by performing de­amortised rehash­
ing (as discussed in 2.8.3), the leaked information would periodically become outdated.
Is w.h.p. good enough?
It’s human nature to want certainty. When we think of computers, we imagine reliable
machines that follow strict sequences to produce perfect results. Therefore, it might seem
weird or even absurd to accept a randomised data structure that sometimes behaves
poorly. However, we cannot control everything, including some things that can have an
impact on the ”running time”:
The world is inherently messy, a coworker could spill coffee, a mouse could gnaw, or
someone’s child might push some buttons in innocent awe. Nature could send a flood, a
quake, a storm, or more. Even the cosmos, if so inclined, could send a solar storm, a
gamma burst, or a cosmic ray any day. Worst of all, at any time for whatever reason,
Windows might update or want to sleep, I weep…
In short, many things might go wrong, and most are beyond our control.

2.4.2
Is O(n) space good enough?
In practice, there’s a big difference between using n space and 5n space, even though
both are O(n). One has to pay for memory hardware, and saving 10%, let alone 80%,
is important. Furthermore, a less compact data structure typically results in more cache
misses, and the data being stored at a lower level in the memory hierarchy. As a result,
the n­space data structure could also be a constant factor faster in practice.
When using the RAM model, it is quite common to report space usage in terms of memory
words. However, due to the size of memory words being O(lg n), one can be more precise
by measuring space usage in bits. If a key k comes from a set of elements U, where U is
typically referred to as the universe of elements, then one would need lg |U| bits to store
k. And if the value v is from the universe of values V, we would likewise need lg |V| bits
to store v.
Adding this up, seemingly leads to the optimal space B for n key value pairs (k, v) from
the universes U and V being:
B(n, U, V) ≤n(lg |U| + lg |V|) bits
However, information theory shows a tighter lower bound of:

2.5
Balls­into­bins
We now examine whether high probability guarantees can be established on the time of
operations in chained hashing, and if we can modify chained hashing to get better results.
Note that the slowest operations are those where one has to look through a long linked
list, and that the elements with the slowest operations are therefore those that have hash
collisions with the most other elements. Thus, one can analyse the behaviour of chained

corresponds precisely to the number of elements hashed to a linked list.

2.6
The power of two choices
But can we do better than using a single uniform hash function in the balls­into­bins game?
Intuitively, one might ask: If, when inserting a ball, the corresponding bin is quite full, why
not try another random bin? Since bins with many balls are rare, would this not result in
the balls likely being more evenly distributed?
Let’s consider the following modification to Chained hashing. Instead of having one hash
function h that maps elements from the universe U to an index such that h : U →m,

x, one searches through all the indices in {h1(x), h2(x), . . . , hd(x)}.

2.7.2
Compact and succinct space
If a data structure used B bits, that would mean that all the space was used to store the
data, and no extra bits can be used to try and make operations faster. Since reasonable
time and B bits has proven difficult, less stringent space complexity groups for dynamic
retrieval data structures have been defined as follows:
• O(B) bits is called compact
• (1 + o(1))B bits is called succinct
Furthermore, when talking about succinct space, one can also state the space as B + rn
where r is the number of wasted bits per element. Even though we found that the optimal
Θ(n) is bounded by n lg e, it is generally considered optimal space as long as r = Θ(1).

only one bit in a memory word, then the rest of the O(lg n) bits will be wasted. All these

lg n −1 bits on the last memory word. lg n bits is small compared to B, and does not
affect any of these bounds much. However, if one tries to speed­up the data structure by
splitting it into sub­structures, this can result in wasting lg n−1 bits per sub­structure. This

The setting

it might also be practically faster and more space efficient (by some constant factor).

2.7.7
Quotienting
The prior ideas for encoding the set of elements save space, but they are somewhat far
removed from typical hashing ideas, and also hard to speed­up since it is difficult to edit
and traverse the tries quickly. Luckily, there is a method called quotienting[18] that saves
a similar amount of bits and is much easier to work with.
Intuition using chained hashing
Let’s pretend that instead of storing the key ki, we could store a hash value h(ki) of length
|ki| = lg |U|. How much space would this require?
Using chained hashing, where the element is stored in the bin bj if the first lg m bits in h(ki)
are equal to j, it would still take O(n lg |U|) bits of space. But notice that all elements that
hash to bin j share the same first lg m = Θ(lg n) bits. This means that storing them for
each of the elements is redundant, since the bin index implicitly stores these first Θ(lg n)
bits, which saves the factor of lg(n) bits needed to approach B.
Furthermore, so far, it has been assumed that the pointers used in the linked lists in
chained hashing are of size O(lg n).

Random permutation of the universe U
One problem with the prior solution is that hash functions lead to the possibility of hash
collisions, where for keys ki ̸= kj but h(ki) = h(kj), the data structure has no way to
distinguish between them, which can lead to errors. Since the dynamic dictionary problem
does not allow errors, one cannot allow the possibility of hash collisions. One way to avoid
collisions is to use a random permutation function π : U →U, instead of a random hash
function. Such a permutation function maps each element to a new random element of
the same size as the prior hash function h did lg |U|; however, since it is a permutation,
it means that there are no collisions! Permutation functions do not lead to totally uniform
and random mappings, since the collision probability is 0, meaning that they are negatively
dependent. However, this negative dependence still allows for tools like Chernoff bounds.
But are permutations π : U →U even doable? How can one ensure that no collisions
occur?

2.8.2
Cuckoo hashing
There are multiple other approaches to hashing, maybe the most intriguing being Cuckoo
hashing[3]. In Cuckoo hashing, one has two arrays T1, T2, each typically of size n. One
further has two hash functions h1, h2, one for each array. When inserting an element x
into the data structure, x is always placed in T1[h1(x)], if the spot was occupied by an
element y, then y gets moved to T2[h2(y)], if that elements was occupied by z, z goes to
T1[h1(z)] and so on. This continues until one finds an empty slot, or until one thinks there
must be a cycle (typically after O(lg n) tries[3]). In the case of a cycle, the entire data
structure can be rebuilt from scratch using new randomness. Alternatively, due to cycles
being so rare, one can store the elements causing the cycle elsewhere [20]. Since there
are at most O(1) w.h.p. elements that would be stored elsewhere, the data structure used
for them has little impact on space or time[20]. Therefore, a simple dynamic array would
suffice.
The only things that take up space are the two arrays. Since both arrays have n indices,
the total space is O(n lg |U|) bits. Furthermore, because x can only be in T1[h1(x)] or
T2[h2(x)], it is clear that queries and deletions can be done in O(1) time. What is less
clear is how long insertions take, or why one can stop after some O(lg n) tries. It turns out
that insertions are O(1) expected time, and O(lg n) w.h.p.[3]. Only a hint for the full proof
is given; for more details, see [3]:
Drawing the edges between T1 and T2 according to which bins share an element forms
a bipartite graph. The size of the connected components in this graph then limits the
insertion time. Therefore, there are two bad cases:

Key differences between Cuckoo and Chained hashing
What makes Cuckoo hashing intriguing? What are the key differences between it and
chained hashing that make it a good candidate for overcoming the barriers of chained
hashing?
Notice that cuckoo hashing, compared to chained hashing, trades the O(1) insertions for
O(1) queries and deletions. As will be discussed in the following subsection, insertions
are relatively easy to handle, since one already has the element. Queries and deletions in
chained hashing are both significantly more difficult to fix, as one must locate the element
within a potentially large sub­structure, which is essentially a new instance of the dynamic
dictionary problem. As will be discussed in 3.2, this new instance of the dynamic dictionary
problem is almost as difficult as the original instance. Therefore, to solve it nicely, one
would need a good solution to the dynamic dictionary problem, and if we had such a
solution, then why would we not use it directly instead of chained hashing?
Another key difference in cuckoo hashing is that the shape of the data structure is more
or less static. There are no growing and shrinking linked lists; there are just some more or
less fixed indices where the data can go. A more or less static structure makes it easier
to share bits among neighbouring memory words, since updates do not lead to a rippling
effect.
Cuckoo hashing is therefore not hampered by the main problems faced when trying to
make chained hashing and binary tries succinct and fast. But we still need to improve the
insertion bound to something better than O(lg n) w.h.p., but can it be done?
2.8.3
De­amortized cuckoo hashing
If companies get tasks that on average necessitates let’s say 10 workers, but every once
in a while more tasks come in, then it is a common practice to have a backlog of tasks,
instead of all of the sudden hiring more workers just for them to have nothing to do under
normal circumstances. Since Cuckoo hashing on average needs O(1) compute for inser­
tions, maybe performing a constant amount of compute per operation, and then having
a backlog for unfinished operations would be good enough? This is exactly what was
shown possible by[21]. More precisely, they show that there exists an L = O(1), whereby

performing L steps for each operation, will lead to the backlog of operations never being
more than O(lg n) w.h.p..
To keep the same ordering as the original cuckoo hashing[3], the backlog is implemented
as a queue, where all updates are put at the back of the queue. However, if an insertion
kicks an element, then this element still belongs to the operation of the insertion, and
therefore skips the queue by being put in front. When a new update arrives, it is placed at
the back of the queue. Then, the L computations available are used by popping updates
from the head one at a time. Since each update takes constant time to add to the queue,
the total time per update is O(L) = O(1). But if there can be O(lg n) updates in the queue,
would it not take O(lg n) or at least ω(1) time to locate the element?

2.9
Regarding some of the assumptions
So far, all probabilistic calculations have made strong assumptions about the hash func­
tions, essentially assuming that the hashed elements can be considered ”fully indepen­
dent and uniformly random. This enables the use of various probabilistic tools, such as
Chernoff bounds. Without these assumptions, many of the calculations might become
intractable or substantially more complex.
2.9.1
Can we access an ideal family of hash functions H?
Fully independent uniform hash functions are neat in theory; however, they are really hard
to achieve in practice if we want them to be fast and space­efficient.
However, requiring that a hash function achieve the same guarantees as an ideal fully
independent uniform hash function is often unnecessary. Instead, as long as the hash
function enables the use of probabilistic tools that give good enough guarantees on the
data structure, then that is enough.

2.9.3
Building a strong house on a weak foundation
One can use more elaborate and theoretically sound hash functions, which might be ben­
eficial despite the typically slower and more space­consuming operations. However, all
these (to the best of my knowledge) assume access to true randomness, which is not as
easy to get as one would hope.
True randomness is fundamentally antithetical to classical deterministic computing. John
Von Neumann even went as far as saying, ”Anyone who attempts to generate random
numbers by deterministic means is, of course, living in a state of sin.”. The best a com­
puter can do on its own is by using pseudo­random number generators (PRNGs), which
rarely offer good theoretical guarantees. Furthermore, most PRNGs do not pass sim­
ple statistical tests, and many can even be predicted given just a few numbers in the
sequence.

As a result, the best hope for obtaining truly random numbers often lies outside the com­
puter itself. However, most ”random” sources (like weather, system clock, or network
latency) are neither truly random nor necessarily private. One of the few things that, ac­
cording to quantum theory, can result in theoretically strong randomness is radioactive
decay or similar. This implies that even obtaining a high­quality seed for a hash function
or PRNG is not straightforward.
Despite these caveats, hash­based structures, such as cuckoo hashing, have demon­
strated excellent empirical performance. Much like Bobby Fischer’s belief that 1.e4 is
‘best by test,’ practitioners often adopt pseudo­random approaches simply because, even
in the absence of rigorous proofs, they consistently work in practice.

3.2
Recursive Hashing: Potential and Limits
This section investigates whether recursive hashing can be used to construct succinct
dynamic dictionaries with O(1) w.h.p. operations. We explore both the potential and the
inherent obstacles of combining recursion, randomness, and high­probability guarantees
in the dynamic dictionary setting.
We have now seen many data structures that utilise the concept of splitting the retrieval
data structure into a primary and a secondary structure, where the first structure aims to
simplify the problem, and the second structure addresses the dynamic retrieval problem

3.2.4
Multi dimensional space of branching factors
The space of potential strategies of recursive hashing is vast. All hash functions could
have the same branching factor, or it could depend on the number of elements in the
current node, or it could depend on the depth of the current node, or possibly some other
factor. The goal would then be to represent as few nodes as possible, with as little space
as possible, while still allowing quick queries and updates w.h.p..
A natural maximal branching factor is O(n3), which is equivalent to the simple BIG struc­
ture covered earlier in section 2.8.1. This wastes space, as the large branching factor
results in a significant number of bins (real, not virtual) being empty. With few modifica­
tions, the natural minimal branching factor of 2 is equivalent to the trie structures from the
prior section 2.7.4. A small branching factor seemingly leads to little wasted space, since
empty branches get pruned as close to the root as possible. In contrast, a large branching
factor easily leads to quick operations due to the shallowness of the resulting tree.
This highlights a connection between many of the ideas covered in this report, suggesting
that they may be combined. One idea is to start by having a branching factor of lg n,
leading to all bins having O(lg n) elements w.h.p, and then either try to have some more

some other element, since it finds an empty slot.

the linked list, where it will find a totally empty slot. Since this slot is totally empty, it will
not delete and reinsert another element z.

3.3.2
The intuitive and less formal explanation
Having bins with capacity O(lg n) gives much more breathing room compared to Backyard

bin can then find the free slot, by using the corresponding Vi. Note that since the total
capacity is greater than n, there will always be some free slots in some bins.

3.4.5
But is this not kind of recursive? No, it is anti­recursive!
As mentioned in section 3.2, recursion and randomisation do not align well; however,
these smaller and smaller bins resemble recursion very much. But in a way, this is almost
the opposite of recursion.
Typically, in recursion, one splits the problem into smaller sub­problems until a trivial base
case is reached. However, here, the largest instance of the problem is the only instance
62
Dynamic dictionaries
==PAGE:71==
that is guaranteed to be at all solvable. However, the solution for a larger case is not as
effective as one for a smaller case, since the cost (the pointer size) is logarithmic in the
problem size. Therefore, we start with the smallest problem, and if it is not possible, we go
bigger and bigger until it becomes feasible, which is essentially the opposite of recursion.
I find this interesting, as I am not aware of any other examples of anything similar. Per­
haps this concept of anti­recursion can be applied in other circumstances where multiple
objectives compete for shared resources? Further exploration could potentially lead to
approximation algorithms for some optimisation problems.

Such a large n goes way past being a galactic algorithm[28], since according to my calcu­
lations, this is approximately equal to the number of Planck cubes in the observable uni­
verse, times the number of Planck seconds since the big bang, to the power of 1.314.816.
Therefore, it is likely that this Θ(n) term needs to be optimised, for this interesting theo­
retical result to lead to interesting practical solutions. If one were to reach n lg e + o(n) or
maybe 4n + o(n), while having reasonable speeds, then it is likely that the results would
be more practical.
3.5.2
Practical improvements
This version, which uses mini tries, is already likely to save some space compared to the
one from [2], simply by being a bit simpler and having fewer moving parts. The versatility
of the mini tries allows them to hold all the extra information. Since we can waste O(lg n)
bits per sub data structure, this is favourable over having more sub data structures like
they do in the state­of­the­art[2].
Another quick improvement is to use the distance from the hashed slot to the stored slot
instead of enumeration as pointers. An illustration of this is shown below:

Where instead of placing x in the left bin, we place it in the neighbourhood (of the same

analysis, since the framing is different for each element.
However, none of these ideas are likely to result in Θ(n) approaching n lg e. It is expected
that for this approach to be optimised much, one would additionally need stronger anal­
ysis, reduce the degree of the polylog bin sizes, avoid forced overflows, reduce the size
of the extra arrays and more. But, it seems tricky, especially since I cannot see a way to
avoid having two pointers per element.

3.5.3
Other approaches?
It remains unclear whether one can approach n lg e without new ideas. Perhaps a stronger
analysis and some tricks could lead to 5n wasted bits or a similar result, but it appears
to be difficult. Pointers might not be the ultimate solution. I suspect that, with a few
clever optimisations, it may be possible to match the state­of­the­art asymptotics using
recursive hashing, while possibly staying much closer to the n lg e wasted bits. Naturally,
other approaches may exist, but I suspect that they require some ideas not touched upon
in this report.
3.5.4
Final comment
In the state of the art [2], they also show how their data structure can be modified to handle
a variable n, by having a hierarchy of bins that in a de­amortised manner are joined and
split. Furthermore, they show how k­independent hash functions suffice to reach their
proofs. Lastly, they also show how one can extend the structure to the cases where |U|·|V|
is much bigger than a polynomial in n, and they get better bounds when it is smaller. All
these extensions should also be applicable when one uses mini­tries. However, I think
that including it all would be detrimental to this report, due to the increase in complexity
and length, and that it would come at the cost of intuition and relative simplicity.

4
Conclusion
This thesis set out to explore the theoretical landscape of dynamic dictionaries, as well as
the approaches that culminated in the current, and proven asymptotically optimal, state­
of­the­art solution.
I believe I have succeeded in creating an approachable view to many of the intricacies,
techniques, and ideas in the dynamic dictionary problem. Furthermore, I believe that
I have successfully conveyed the story of some early ideas, such as chained hashing,
and the work that led to Backyard Cuckoo hashing, the first succinct constant­time w.h.p
dynamic dictionary, and lastly, the state­of­the­art proven optimal solution. Lastly, I have
addressed some of the challenges, both in terms of theoretical assumptions and the short­
comings of asymptotic analysis, and how there is still work to be done for the best theo­
retical result to become practically viable.
