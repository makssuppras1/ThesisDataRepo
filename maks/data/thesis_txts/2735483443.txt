Abstract
Recently, electrochemical carbon dioxide capture has emerged as a promising alternative
to traditional energy­intensive capture methods. An innovative approach in electrochemi­
cal capture involves utilizing redox­active molecules like (anthra)quinones or phenazines.
These molecules undergo activation for CO2 capture through electrochemical reduction,
where their reduced state can trigger CO2 absorption by two processes: pH­swing and
nucleophilicity­swing. A significant drawback of redox mediated capture is the potential
for side reactions with oxygen, which is commonly present in gas mixtures targeted for
carbon capture. In this project an experimental assessment of the oxygen reactivity of
reduced (anthra)quinones and phenazines is performed employing UV­vis spectrometry
measures. A pH dependent study is carried out for Methylene Blue, AQDS, BHPS and
DCDHAQ. The experiments show a consistent trend of increased oxygen reactivity at
higher pH values which could be attributed to energetic reasons. Furthermore, different
organic compounds present diverse reactivity which can be associated to slower or faster
kinetic of the reaction. The study suggests that both thermodynamic aspects and kinetic
consideration should be carried out in the research for oxygen stable redox active CO2
sorbent as compounds with similar redox potentials can present diverse oxygen reactivity
rates.

Introduction
CO2 emissions keep rising year by year calling for new technologies with neutral or neg­
ative emissions to mitigate the damaging effects consequence of climate change. World­
wide carbon dioxide emissions were 37.12 GtCO2 in 2021, which have lifted 0.9 percent
in 2022 to 37.5 GtCO2. 2023 closed with a total of 36.8 GtCO2 emitted, highest value
ever reached, signing an increase of 1.1 percent from 2022. [1, 2] Among the different
green house gases (GHGs), CO2 emission is the major cause to global warming (57.0%),
followed by CH4 (20.0%), CFCs (15.0%), and N2O ( 6.0%). [3] This continuous increasing
trend is well exemplified in figure 1.1a, where data on CO2 concentration in atmosphere
are presented starting from 1980.
Against the danger of climate warming, countries around the world have formulated and
adopted a series of goals and actions to mitigate climate change. The most vigorous is the
Paris agreement (2015) signed by 196 countries which commit to limit the temperature rise
of global average temperature for this century to 2°C compared to pre­industrial values,
set as threshold to avoid un­reversible effects on climate.
As part of the effort to limit the temperature raise, The Net Zero Emissions by 2050 Sce­
nario (NZE), sets the goal for the global energy sector to achieve net zero CO2 emissions
by 2050. However,as IEA reports, of all the 50 components of the energy system consid­
ered critical for clean energy transition only three of them (solar PV, electric vehicles and
lighting) are evaluated as fully on track to achieve 2050’s goal. CO2 capture and storage
(CCS) is among those technologies evaluated as not on track, suggesting that more effort
needs to be spent to reduce the gap.
The CCS shows itself as one of the reliable technologies for decarbonizing existing sys­
tems that operate on fossil fuels and pursuing the transition of plants from traditional to
renewable energy based. Carbon capture aim is to close the carbon cycle by capturing
the CO2 which is being emitted, and then store (carbon Capture and Sequestration, CCS)
or use it for different applications (Carbon Capture and Utilization, CCU). Direct air cap­
ture (DAC) is a CCS procedure which separates CO2 already present in the atmosphere,
reducing the current concentration of the molecule in the air or providing possibility to off­
set emissions from de­localized sources (such as the transport sector). Through carbon

materials, otherwise stored not impacting the atmosphere.
Carbon capture is considered a versatile and potentially cost effective way of cutting emis­
sions because it could help in reducing the impact of difficult to de­carbonize sectors such
the concrete, steel or ammonia industries. As shown in figure 1.1b, although electricity
production is the major CO2 emission source, other sectors such as transportation or man­
ufacturing and construction amount for a big portion of the total global emissions, with an
impact of 13 GtCO2 emitted in 2020.
The optimal pathway would require to empower capture and conversion technologies in
order to transform CO2 in valuable chemicals which could potentially finance the cap­
ture process. While electrochemical conversion processes require further development,
heterogeneous catalytic processes like Fisher­Tropsch are well­established conversion
processes.
This thesis work aims to contribute in the effort of the development of efficient and cost
effective electrochemical carbon capture solutions by investigating the oxidative stabil­
ity of organic compounds potentially employable in capture technologies. Among the
different carbon capture procedures, electrochemical carbon capture is advancing as a
promising and energy efficient capture approach. Among different electrochemical cap­
ture technologies under development or in pilot plant project scale, this project focuses
on the study of CO2 capture electrochemically mediated by redox active compounds. Or­
ganic compounds such as phenazines or (anthra)quinones, are object of study in redox
processes where the reduced form is responsible for the absorption of CO2, while the
oxidized form for the release of the gas. By implementing redox cycles is then possible
to electrochemically create absorption ­ release loops, extracting CO2 from gas mixtures.
One of the main challenges of this procedure is the oxidative effect of oxygen present in
the CO2 gas mixture on the reduced form of the absorbant, which considerably mines the
capture efficiency of these systems. The oxygen effect is to re­oxidize the compound with
consequent loss of capture capacity and efficiency . The importance of the re­oxidation
stability of these compounds is enhanced by the high oxygen concentration in the gas
mixture target of sequestration. For DAC procedures, the atmospheric Oxygen content is
20.94% by volume, while CO2 is roughly the 0.04%.
In this thesis project the oxidative reactivity of four organic molecules is investigated in
different pH regimes by making use of UV­vis spectrometry measures. The main re­
search question this project aims to answer is how fast the studied phenazines or (an­
thra)quinones compounds react with oxygen and which is the pH effect on their reactivity.
Through this report the reader will be first presented to an introduction on the different
state of the art solutions in matter of CO2 capture. At the end of this introductory chapter
attention is given to theoretical principles of electrochemical procedures for CO2 capture,
explaining the concepts of pH­swing and nucleophilicity­swing. These two methods al­
low the use of redox active organic compounds for capture purposes. The introductory
part is then followed by chapter 2 were the experimental methods used for the project
are presented. Proceeding through chapter 3 the results of the carried experiments are
shown and discussed. The difference in the oxygen reactivity of the studied compounds
is presented, highlighting the effects of pH. In the conclusive chapter 4, the main findings
of the work are then summarized.

CO2 Capture Routes
The CO2 capture technologies applied to localized emitting sites are conventionally classi­
fied in four different routes depending mainly at which moment of the combustion process
the decarbonization occur. In addition to those capture technologies there is direct air
capture route (DAC) which aims to remove CO2 already present in the atmosphere, for

Post­combustion capture methods trap the CO2 from flue gases created after combustion

1.1.5
Direct air capture
Direct air capture, due to the reduced concentration (around 400 ppm) of carbon dioxide in
atmosphere is the most expensive capture technology. The limited concentration requires
for higher energy needs and large air flows in the plants, rising costs of the implant and
operational expenses. The peculiar benefit of this technology is to address carbon emis­
sions from decentralized sources such as the transportation sector or other low emission
sources. However, the low concentration of CO2 requires the use of chemical absorbents
with strong affinity to CO2. Hydroxides and amines are usually employed for the scope,
with efforts ongoing to improve their properties.
Due to the particularly high costs which prevents its implementation on large scale, the
future of DAC strongly relies on the development of high­efficiency and low­cost sorbant
materials.[3] According to the 2022 International energy agency (IEA) report, there were
18 small scale and pilot scale direct air capture facilities operating in Canada, Europe
and USA, with one large scale facility under construction in USA which is predicted to be
operating in mid­2020s [10]. Other large facilities are now in planning stage in Iceland
due to the abundant thermal energy available for sorbent regeneration. [11] Those data
helps to understand the state of development of the technology, which however with 0.01
Mt/yr captured in 2022 is well behind the 2030 goal of 85 Mt/yr set by the IEA NetZero
emission by 2050 [10].
An example of a capture technology developed for DAC purposes (but not only) is the
carbon capture through Calcium looping (CaL) . The procedure exploits Calcium oxides
absorbant (CaO) in the two reversible reactions of Carbonation and Calcination [12].
The carbonation allows the sorbent to bind carbon dioxide forming calcium carbonate
(CaCO3). The Calcination then occur through an heating process at temperatures of 850­
950 °C where the CaO is restored, releasing the carbon dioxide. Calcium looping energy
requirements are reported to be (>400 kJthermal/mol) [12]. While, the conventional cycles
based on amine scrubbing presents energy costs of (>100 kJthermal/mol CO2). [13]
DAC is a potential capture application which could addressed by redox mediated electro­
chemical capture studied in this project. Consequently, the energy requirements of this
approach needs to be lower or similar to calcium looping or amine mediated capture in
order for the technology to find space in industry.
All the above mentioned routes present some strength and challenges which makes them
more or less suitable for certain applications. The pre­combustion route creates a mixture
of Hydrogen and CO2 with a higher concentration of CO2, making the separation process
less difficult and more efficient than post­combustion routes.
Post­combustion plants are more preferable since they can easily be retrofitted to existing
plants. however they are less efficient due to the low concentration of the CO2 in the flue
gas, requiring higher energy inputs. In post­combustion routes, the CO2 concentration
varies from 3% to 20% depending from the specific application, well lower than the 40%
that characterize pre­combustion routes. [3].
Oxy­fuel combustion has significant drawbacks due to the need for oxygen separation
procedure and the associated high capital costs. These factors offset its primary advan­
tage, which is the considerably simpler CO2 separation process.
Chemical looping, overcome the needs of a oxygen separation process, however there
are aspects of this technology which require more detailed study as reported in literature
[14].

First efforts in developing electrochemical carbon capture dates back to 1960s, [16] with
various different approaches developed until today’s stage. There are mainly four physio­
chemical processes which enable CO2 capture electrochemically:
• Electrochemical mediated amine regeneration (EMAR)
• Electrochemical capacitive absorption
• Electrochemical generation of nucleophiles (Nucleophilicity­swing process)
• Electrochemical modulation of proton concentration (pH­swing process)
The conventional amine scrubbing capture using MEA requires heating to 110 °C neces­
sary to break the amine­CO2 bond for absorbent regeneration. Consequent drawbacks
of the process, beside high energy expenses, are degradation of amines compounds at
high temperatures or not complete regeneration of the compounds.
In contrast, EMAR employs a similar CO2 binding process as conventional amine­based
CCS, but does desorption using an electrochemical cell. The desorption mechanism uti­
lizes an electrochemically active absorbent blocker that binds CO2 more effectively than
MEA, displacing it in the carbon bond and allowing for sorbent regeneration. One common
amine­blocker combination, is a copper based blocker, which exploits the redox couple
Cu/Cu2+, and ethylenediamine (EDA) as CO2 sorbant. The blocker is activated via oxi­
dation when the CO2 rich amine stream is supplied to the anode, and then is regenerated
via reduction with a consequent release of carbon dioxide. [16]
Electrochemical capacitive systems, initially designed for energy storage, are being repur­
posed for CCS applications. In this framework, CO2 from a gas mixture interacts with the
electrolyte, undergoing dissolution and becoming trapped within the double layer at the
electrode/electrolyte interface due to electrostatic forces. This trapping is made possible
by charged sites or functional groups present in the dissolved CO2 form.
Nucleophiles are molecules with high binding affinity towards CO2, for instance amines are
nucleophiles with excellent affinity to CO2. Of interest for eCC are redox compounds which
nucleophilicity can be tuned electrochemically, by inducing reduction­oxidation cycles and
consequently triggering CO2 capture or release. More in depth description of the process
is provided in section 1.4 on capture mediated by redox active compounds.

1.4
Redox Mediated CO2 Capture
CO2 capture mediated by redox active compounds is emerging as promising eCC ap­
proach. At today stage is an infant process being mainly explored on a laboratory scale.
There are different studies which demonstrated efficient capture employing different com­
pounds with energy consumption comparable to thermal processes, both for DAC and
point emission sources. [16]
Capture through redox active compounds can occur both through nucleophilicity­swing
and pH­swing processes:
• pH swings: Proton­coupled redox processes (PCET) that change the solubility of
CO2 in aqueous solution during reduction­oxidation cycles.
• Nucleophilicity swings: Change in the affinity of the redox active molecule to bind
CO2 at different redox states.
PH­swing relies on the use of molecules that undergo proton­coupled electron transfer
(PCET) in aqueous solution. PCET means that electrochemical reduction/oxidation of
these molecules is associated to proton uptake/release, which trigger changes in the pH
of the solution. Variation of the H+/OH−equilibrium, if large enough, can be the cause of
CO2 absorption at high pH and released at low pH as shown with the carbonate equilib­
rium.[18, 7].
A PCET redox behaviour is presented in equation 1.9 with regards to an ideal redox pair
Q/Q2−characterized by a double electron exchange. The reduction process involves the
couple Q/Q2−which spontaneously translates in Q/QH2, realizing electron uptake from
the solution in the reduced form.

During reduction, simultaneously to a pH increase, the molecule can undergo a natural

The Nucleophilicity swing process relies on the electrophilic nature of the carbon atom of
the CO2 molecule. The CO2 molecule is a relatively stable molecule where the 4 valence
electrons of the carbon are employed to create two double bonds with both the oxygen,
fulfilling the octet rule for all the atoms in the structure. The electrophilicity nature of the
molecule derives from the electronegativity difference between carbon and oxygen, being
oxygen more electronegative than carbon. The effect of this unbalance is a displacement
of the shared electrons towards the oxygen, making the carbon of the CO2 molecule a
slightly positive specie which can be stabilized by interacting with a nucleophilic specie,
which by definition presents electron donating properties.
Electrochemical reduction of redox­active compounds can therefore generate CO2 ab­
sorption by direct bond of the reduced nucleophiles with electrophilic CO2 , and by pH­
swing whenever the reduction is proton coupled. [22]

To perform a UV­vis experimental measure, a calibration of the instrument is needed in or­

physical interest is the concentration of the reduced/oxidized molecules in the solution
rather then their absorbance at different wavelengths. By making use of the Lamber­Beer
law is possible to relate the concentration of the molecule in the solution with the intensity
of their absorbance spectrum. The Lamber Beer law defines a linear correlation between

(3.2)

calibration line throughout the report which is an experimental curve that needs to be

Reduction Step
The experiments start by connecting the catholyte bottle to the Nitrogen inlet and pumping
Nitrogen at 50 LPM, in order to create an oxygen free environment. The flow cell used is
a two electrode system. To perform the electrical connections the negative cable of the
potentiostat (red cable) is connected to the anode, while the positive cable (blue cable)
is connected to the cathode. The reference output of the potentiostat (white cable) it is
short­circuited with the red cable and connected to the counter electrode (anode), making
it both the reference for the potentiostat from which measure and the negative electrode
of the cell. This layout of the electrical connections allows to work with positive currents
and potentials during the reduction of the compounds.
To perform the reduction a galvanostatic charge + a constant potential step are applied.
It allows to set an initial positive current at which start the reduction. As consequence,
due to the increase in concentration of the reduced state and according to the Nernst
equation, there is an increase in the potential of the cell which is measured meantime
the constant current is applied. The current is kept steady until the potential reaches a
maximum value set by the user, from which moment on, the reduction is driven by the
potential, which is kept fixed, and the current is measured. The potential is then kept
steady until the current decreases to a minimum value, when all the species that could
be reduced at that potential are consumed. At that state, the reduction step is finished,
and if the parameters were set properly, the compound is completely reduced. (Close to
100% State of charge (SOC)). The characteristic plots for current, potential and capacity
resulting from these double electrical step are shown in figure 3.4. It can be seen as they
are in accordance with the described process, when the current is varying the potential is
fixed and viceversa.
Furthermore, a key condition necessary for a complete reduction of the catholyte is to
keep the catholyte as limiting electron side of the cell. The concentration of the redox
active anode compounds (Iron ions) is kept in between 100­1000 times higher than the
one of organic compounds in the anolyte with comparable electrolyte amounts on the two
sides of the cell, providing a sufficient electron reservoir for several complete reduction
processes. In therms of catholyte/anolyte composition, attention was also given in us­
ing the same buffer solution for both anode and cathode electrolytes, in order to avoid
H+/OH−unbalancing on the sides of the membrane and possible unwanted crossover.

Bubbling Step
Once completed the reduction of the compound, the gas inlet in the catholyte bottle is

curves of concentration vs time are then used to calculate the re­oxidation rate derived

3.2
Experiments development
The majority of the project was spent working on the potentiostat and the UV­vis spec­
trometer trying to produce good experimental results. Some of the most time consuming
mistakes and experimental aspects are mentioned in this paragraph as part of the learning
curve needed to produce better results and considerable portion of the project.
A first experimental aspect important to mention are the parameters of the UV­vis spec­
trometer. In order to obtain good UV­vis spectra there are three parameters of the detector
which needs to be optimized:
• Integration Time: Specifies how long the detector monitors the incoming photons.
As reported on the spectrometer manual it is set such that the highest amount of
light that is predicted reaches 85% of the spectrometer capability.
• Scans to Average: It specifies the number of spectral acquisitions employed to
calculate the spectrum. It reduces the signal to noise ratio.
• Boxcar Width: This parameter determines the amount of pixels over which the
spectrometer averages the light signal.
For every solution analyzed these parameters were adjusted accordingly to needs. The
integration time for all the results presented was kept below 20ms, as it is the threshold
value over which the detector saturated above 80% of its capacity. In some experiments
it was further reduced to 10 or 5 ms in order to acquire faster spectra necessary to detect
fast re­oxidation of very oxygen reactive compounds.
The Scans to average was adjusted according to the time resolution needed for succes­
sive spectrum, being set to 1 for very oxygen reactive compounds. The Scan to average
26

==PAGE:38==
is the parameter that smoother the spectrum. Its value was kept between 5 and 30. It was
optimized in order to set it high enough to not see unwanted spikes along the spectrum,
but low enough to not lose resolution of the absorbance peaks. Increasing it too much
leads to shift in absorbance peaks positions and loss of peak intensity, due to averages
occurring between absorbance values at near wavelengths. To check whether the pa­
rameters were properly adjusted the experimental spectrum were compared to spectra
found in literature when available.
A mention and partial explanation is now given on the major experimental mistakes oc­
curred during the project. A step which at firs seemed trivial that however presented to
be very important and source of mistakes is the calibration of the UV­vis spectrometer,
which procedure is described in paragraph 3.1.1.
A first mistake in the calibration regarded the saturation of the spectrometer detector.
More in detail, the spectrum need to be acquired below the saturation limit of the detec­
tor. Unfortunately, even tough attention was given in acquiring spectrum (of the whole
solution, solvent + molecule) below the saturation limit, the reference spectrum (taken on
the solution with only the solvent, with no dissolved organic molecule) was saturating the
spectrometer in an extend wavelength region.
The error occurred because the reference compound (only solvent) being more trans­
parent than the solution (molecule + solvent), led more light through the detector. This
surplus amount of light resulted in saturation of the detector. The correct procedure is to
set the integration time which saturates the detector to a value not higher than 85% of its
capacity with respect the reference compound (only solvent).
A further interesting mistake to mention, regards again the spectrometer calibration pro­
cess. This mistake is a reminder that reference and black spectrum need to be acquired
in the exact same conditions at which spectrometer measures will be performed. Different
conduct leads to distortion of the spectrum.
In the specific case the reference spectrum and the successive experimental spectrum on
the molecules were acquired by making use of two different sample holders. As presented
in the description of the set­up, experimental spectra were acquired by making use of
the white cell presented in figure 3.2d which places the spectrometer in the flow loop of
the catholyte. Conversely the reference spectrum, like the spectra measured to obtain
calibration lines were taken through cuvettes placed in an apposite cuvet holder which
comes with the spectrometer, without using the set­up and its white cell.
This erroneous process introduced a partial distortion of the spectrum, with an off­set er­
ror and different heights of the peaks. The peaks were however being measured at their
right position.
The reason why these cuvet measures were erroneously used, it was mainly to get more
precise calibration lines. In acquiring calibration lines, spectra are measured by inserting
in the set up catholytes at known concentration of the molecule under study. However, do­
ing it in a cuvet would guarantee higher precision in concentration than doing it through the
experimental set up, because in the latter case the catholyte would have to flow through
the tubes of the set up and into the electrochemical cell, getting partially diluted by the
reminiscent liquid already present in the device.

reduction process of the compounds. While reducing the compounds, there is very often
the effect of reducing other ’impurities’ (or water) beside the organic molecule. This effect
was especially observed at higher pH, where the reduction potential of the molecule is

The reason for the presence of these spikes is due to a non stable flow of the catholyte in
the tubes of the set­up, with a consequent unstable flow in the UV­vis spectrometer cell.
The result of this unsteadiness is a shaking in between subsequent measured spectra,
with different spectrum being translated upwards depending whether the liquid completely
fills the spectrometer cell at the instant of the measure or not. The effect is to see ab­
sorbance spectrum shifted up of a random value depending on the state of the flow in
that moment. This translation is homogeneous at all the wavelengths of the spectrum,
creating no distortion of absorbance at different wavelengths. Being it an homogeneous
shift, is then sufficient to define a baseline in the wavelength region where the spectrum
is known to be zero, and fix that value to zero. It allows to translate the spectrum back
down whenever it is shifted up. Unfortunately when the spectrum is shifted up too much,
leading to absorbance values which are higher than the saturation limit of the spectrum,
which is at around 2, the baseline subtraction present its limit because the spectrum gets

A possible explanation could come from the section of the tubes changing along the

The diameter of the tubes employed across the UV­vis cell are

Discussion
In the attempt to understand the oxidative reactivity of compounds, help comes from ther­
modynamic theory. The key to have a stable compound to oxygen is a reduction potential
of the compound more positive than the oxygen reduction couple. The difference of Gibbs
free energy between reactants and products defines whether a reaction is spontaneous
or not. In order for a reaction to be un­favoured ∆G > 0, where G is the Gibbs free
energy. In the case of an oxidation reaction as the ones studied, the organic reduced
compound will oxidize by reducing oxygen. ∆G is defined as ∆G = −nF∆Ecell, with n
being the number of electrons exchanged in the redox reaction, F the farday constant,
and the potential of the cell defined as Ecell = Ecath −Ean. As consequence Ecell < 0 is
the condition for a non spontaneous reaction.
Following the presented equations a re­oxidation of the reduced compound with oxygen
is then energetically non spontaneous if EMolecule > EO2, where E indicates the reduction
potentials of both the redox active CO2 sorbant molecule and of Oxygen.
Is then possible to conclude that in order to have a stable reduced compound to oxygen is
necessary a molecule with a reduction potential which is higher than the oxygen reduction
potential.
Figure 3.41 summarizes all the oxidative rate obtained from the experimental studies car­
ried out at different pHs. The histogram presents a trend of increased oxidative reactivity
with pH. This trend can be associated to the more negative reduction potential of the or­
ganic redox couple at higher pHs. This decreased potential, has the effect of making the
re­oxidation with bubbled oxygen even more energetically favoured, effect which could
potentially explain the enhanced reactivity to oxygen.
Figure 3.41: Histogram of oxidative rate study relative to AQDS, DCDHAQ and BHPS
All the organic molecules studied undergo a PCET redox reaction at least in acidic envi­
ronment. The involvement of H+ ions in the reaction leads to a reaction potential which is
dependent from pH as shown in the Pourbaix diagram of AQDS in figure 3.25. The slope
of the Pourbaix diagram is direct consequence of the Nernst equation, which relates po­
tential of reaction to concentration of reactant and products species. The H+ dependent
relation of the potential due to Nernst equation leads to: [33]

where E′ is the standard reduction potential, n is the number of electrons involved in
the reaction and m is the number of H+ ions involved. An increase in pH hinders the
involvement of H+ ions in the reaction until it is not proton coupled anymore, as it happens
for AQDS at pH>11 (figure 3.25). The pH value which defines the transition of a proton
couple reaction or not is the pKa value of the reduced form of the organic molecule. For
pH<pKa there is proton coupling, at pH>pKa no protons are involved anymore. The pKa
value then defines a capacity limit for pH­swing mediated capture.
In addition to thermodynamic reasoning, there are kinetic aspects of the oxidation re­
actions which leads to different compounds presenting different rates. This is what is
evinced by comparing rates of different compounds at same pH. The plot in figure 3.42
aims to present this result. In the plot, reduction potential of the molecules are presented
at pH 14, plotted against the calculated rates at the same or similar pH. Ideally both the
rates and the reduction potentials should be presented at the same pH value. However,
reduction potential of the studied compounds were only found in literature at pH14, value
of pH where the rate was measured only for DCDHAQ.
The plot presents no correlation between potential and rate. It seems that is not true
that compounds with more negative reduction potential present higher oxidation rates.
There are kinetic aspects, likely related to the activation energy, which make compounds
more or less reactive with oxygen. Apparently, although a reaction is favoured for en­
ergetic reasons, it can be very slow kinetically. The green region in figure 3.42 aims to
indicate a suitable region both thermodynamically and in kinetics. The goal is to have a
molecule with higher (or similar) reduction potential than oxygen and a slow kinetic for the
re­oxidation reaction with oxygen.
Further experimental measures could be carried out in order to investigate this appar­
ent non correlation between reduction potentials and oxidation rates. Cyclic voltammetry
measures could be carried out at different pHs to produce Pourbaix diagrams for all the
compounds studied and relate them with the observed oxidation rates.
In addition to the oxygen reactivity of the compound, a further effect of a more negative
reduction potential is an enhanced nucleophilicity of the reduced molecule. As reported
by Bui et. al, the binding capabilities to CO2 of quinones molecules are proportional to
the negativity of the reduction potential. In the study different electron donating group are
substituted as side chain in different (anthra)quinones, with the goal of increasing the nu­
cleophilicity of the molecule. The effect is that groups which enhanced the nucleophilicity
of the organic molecule increased both the reactivity with CO2 and made the reduction
potential more negative. These trend lines where however found to be different for var­
ious (anthra)quinones species, where various molecules have different CO2 affinities at
different redox potentials. [35]
There is a trade­off between the reduction potential, which affects the reactivity to oxygen,
and the binding affinity to CO2. A more cathodic redox reaction (more negative reduction
potential) corresponds to a stronger nucleophilicity of the reduced specie. However, at
least based on the knowledge acquired in this study, is possible to find an (anthra)quinone
molecule which ’maximize’ this trade­off by presenting good CO2 affinity at an enhanced
reduction potential with slow oxygen re­oxidation kinetic.

fine which could be the molecule, or the side chain combination that could enhance the

Regarding the capture mediated by pH­swing the endeavour is different. In this capture
approach, the pH difference between the oxidized and reduced compounds is a key pa­
rameter which defines the capture capacity of the electrolyte. With high ∆pH resulting in
enhanced capture capacities as long the reaction remains proton coupled.
The pH studies performed presented a consistent trend of increased oxidative reactivity
with pH which likely comes form the enhanced cathodic nature of the molecule. As pre­
sented by Pourbaix diagram on AQDS and by equation 3.9, enhanced pH translates in
more negative reduction potentials and likely consequent increased oxidative reactivity
justified by thermodynamic reasons. PH­swing processes are then characterized by a
new trade­off with higher pH resulting in enhanced capacity but increased oxygen reac­
tivity.
A further aspect of great relevance in pH­swing procedures is the need to keep the maxi­
mum pH, reached when the compound is completely reduced, below the pKa value of the
redox molecule involved. As resulted from the experiments in non buffered KCl solution
for AQDS and BHPS where pH measures were carried out during redox cycles, it seems
that at pHs higher than the pKa value of the reduced molecule, the redox reaction stop
being a PCET by not involving H+ ions anymore.
The effect is that a consequent oxidation do not provide withdraw of H+ ions to reduce
the alkalinity of the solution, breaking the pH­swing loop. All the successive reduction and
oxidation cycles will occur at the same alkaline pH, interrupting the capture mediated by
carbonate and bi­carbonate equilibrium in water.
Since nucleophilicity mediated capture and pH­swing mediated capture are two different
effects which however act simultaneously, choices in the electrolyte composition can be
performed in order to favour one or the other especially in therms of capture capacity. For
nucleophilicity mediated capture, the capacity is directly affected by the solubility of the
redox active molecule, higher is the concentration of CO2 binding molecules in the solution
and higher will the amount of CO2 capture/released. Differently, in capture mediated by
pH­swing processes, the capacity of the capture system is limited by the pKa value of the
compound.
In order to simultaneously exploits nucleophilicity and pH­swing processes, the following
target properties could be defined for a redox mediated CO2 capture catholyte:
• Stable molecules
• Reversible Redox reactions
• High reduction potential
• High solubility
• High pKa values
• Slow kinetic to re­oxidation with oxygen

Conclusions
In this project, I explored the reactivity of reduced organic redox­active compounds with
oxygen, for potential applications in carbon capture and storage. In electrochemical CO2
capture mediated by redox active compounds, oxygen reactivity is one of the major chal­
lenge which hinders the efficiency of the system, motivating the experimental study of the
project. The project followed an highly experimental focus with the goal of presenting pH
dependence of reactivity of various reduced phenazines or (anthra)quinones with oxygen.
The majority of the project was spent acquiring expertise on the UV­vis spectrometer and,
more broadly, in setting up and managing the experimental study. Consequently, only the
last few weeks were available to generate the results outlined in this report. Major mis­
takes that led to producing results so late regards mainly calibration problems and issues
in reducing the compounds. However, addressing these challenges formed a substantial
part of the learning curve necessary to produce more reliable results.
Oxidation rate measures were performed at various pH values for the following organic
compounds: Methylene Blue, AQDS, BHPS, DCDHAQ. The in­situ UV­vis measures car­
ried out during reduction­oxidation cycles, allowed to calculate in most cases rates of the
re­oxidation reactions, providing insights into the kinetic of interaction between Oxygen
and the organic molecules.
Methylene blue was used as initial compound to test the set up. It showed stability to
oxygen at pH 2, 4 and 6.5, by not fully reducing under bubbling of oxygen at 1.5 LPM.
An enhanced reactivity resulted by increasing the pH: with pH2 leading to a final 50%
discharge state of the system, pH4 to 70% and pH6.5 to 80%.
For the other studied compounds the oxidation rates measured are reported in figure 3.41.
For the various organic solutions analyzed some deviations from plain linear behaviour
were observed.
Whenever the re­oxidation reaction differed from a linear behaviour, a reaction rate anal­
ysis was carried out in order to define, or try to define, the order of reaction with respect
the oxidized form of the compound. Unfortunately not always a resulting reaction order
was found, suggesting the occurrence of more complex reaction dynamics.
The kinetic experimental studies are then compared to thermodynamic observations lead­
ing to the main conceptual results of this project. The re­oxidation of a reduced specie
in contact which oxygen occur only if it stabilizes the system, leading to a reduced Gibbs
free energy. That principle leads to conclude that the unwanted re­oxidation happen if
the reduction potential of the organic redox active compound is more negative than the
oxygen reduction reaction.
This thermodynamic reasoning presents good accordance with the kinetic experimental
results, where an increase of reactivity was consistently observed at higher pHs. This
behaviours can be attributed to higher pHs translating directly in more negative reduction
potential due to the proton coupled nature of the studied reduction reactions, as presented
in equation 3.9.
A further relevant outcome is the kinetic effect on the reactivity of the compounds. As
reported in figure 3.42, there is no correlation between oxidation rates and reduction po­
tential in between different compounds. This suggests that in principle investigation could
be carried out on various (anthra)quinones and phenazines, looking for compounds which

could present a good oxidation stability by keeping reduction potentials more negative
than the oxygen couple to enhance nucleophilicity.
A trade­off is reported between the binding affinity of redox molecules to CO2 and the
reduction potential. Higher affinity is associated to lower potential, which can enhance
the oxygen reactivity. This effect has to be monitored for nucleophilicity­swing mediated
capture.
A further trade­off is presented for pH­swing mediated capture, resulting from the mea­
sured oxygen reactivity being higher at higher pHs. The capacity of a pH­swing capture
system is defined by the pH difference in between the oxidized state of the catholyte and
the reduced one. In maximizing the capacity, attention has to be given in the enhanced
oxygen reactivity at higher pHs. In addition to this trade off, in order to keep the redox
reaction proton coupled, the pH of the solution needs to be kept below the pKa values
of the reduced molecule. At higher pHs the pH­swing loop is broken, with the solution
keeping enhanced alkalinity even in the oxidized form.
The research project concludes with a list of target properties for a redox active organic
compounds for applications in eCC, where both pH­swing and nucleophilicity­swing cap­
ture wants to be maximized:
• Stable molecules
• Reversible Redox reactions
• High reduction potential
• High solubility
• High pKa values
• Slow kinetic to re­oxidation with oxygen
