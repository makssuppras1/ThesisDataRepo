Abstract
The human gut microbiome has been associated with adverse clinical outcomes in patients
undergoing allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT). The presence of
speciﬁc species within the patient microbiota has been linked to either beneﬁcial or detri-
mental eﬀects on the survival of patients and development of graft-versus-host disease.
In this study, I isolated Lactobacillus spp. and Enterococcus spp. from fecal samples
collected over time from children who had undergone HSCT. These isolates were whole
genome sequenced and characterized. Isolates were obtained from seven patients with
moderate to severe GvHD, where three of the patients died from the primary malignancy.
Generally, the isolates obtained from deceased and surviving patients, respectively, dif-
fered at species-level.
Furthermore, sequence typing showed diﬀerence in strain-level
between patients and even between samples. In silico prediction of putative resistance
genes and virulences was performed on Enterococcus isolates, indicating a general ten-
dency of E. faecalis harboring a higher number of resistance and virulence genes than that
of E. faecium. An overlay assay showed inhibitory activities between isolates obtained
from the same patient. Especially one E. faecium isolate seemed to exhibit inhibitory
eﬀects against other enterococci.
This study has demonstrated the diﬀerences in members of the microbiota at strain-level
and that members of the same genus can harbor diﬀerent functional genes. Furthermore,
it is suggested that microbe-microbe interactions can contribute to the modulation of
the human gut microbiota in HSCT patients. These ﬁndings emphasize the need for
exploration of the gut microbiota on strain-level in context of HSCT and other diseases.

1
Introduction
1.1
Context of the project
The human gut is inhabited by millions of microbes and is essential for the human health
and well-being. However, dysbiosis in the microbial community has been associated with
a range of diseases including inﬂammatory bowel disease, diabetes and cancer. Determi-
nation of diﬀerence between a healthy and unhealthy microbiome may improve diagnosis
and outcome prediction of diseases [1]. This study is following a PhD project performed by
Anna C¨acilia Masche, PhD student at Research Group for Genomic Epidemiology at the
Technical University of Denmark. She and co-workers investigated associations between
the intestinal microbiome and immune reconstitution following allogeneic hematopoietic
stem cell transplantation (HSCT). The cohort consisted of 30 children at the age of 1.1-
16.5 years, who had undergone HSCT. Each patient provided one or more fecal samples
over time, ranging from 33 days before to 45 days after graft infusion. The gut micro-
biota was characterized by 16S rRNA gene proﬁling of each sample and four diﬀerent
bacterial community patterns were detected, designated community state type (CST).
Each of these CSTs was characterized by the following dominating taxa. CST1: Lac-
tobacillaceae, CST2: Lachnospiraceae, Erysipelotrichaceae and Ruminococcaceae, CST3:
Enterobacteriaceae, Streptococcaceae and Staphylococcaceae, CST4: Enterococcaceae. The
study found correlations between high abundances of Lactobacillaceae and slow immune
cell reconstitution and high mortality in patients with severe graft-versus-host disease
(GvHD). Furthermore, results from the study suggests that the presence of Lactobcillus
spp. before onset of GvHD might play a role in reducing GvHD and mortality (Anna et
al., unpublished [2]). Increased knowledge about these lactobacilli might help understand
their role in the clinical outcome of HSCT.
1.2
Purpose and Aims
It is hypothesized that Lactobacillus spp. present in the gut microbiota before onset of
GvHD have a beneﬁcial eﬀect as to the clinical outcome. The purpose of this study is to
conﬁrm or disconﬁrm this hypothesis through isolation and characterization of lactobacilli
from HSCT patients with GvHD. Furthermore, it will be investigated whether there is a
diﬀerence in the Lactobacillus spp. between patients who survived and patients who died
following HSCT.
This project aims to:
• Isolate Lactobacillus spp. from fecal samples from diﬀerent time points of patients
undergoing HSCT
• Extract DNA from the Lactobacillus isolates and generate whole genome sequencing
data for these
• Predict their antibiotic susceptibility proﬁles
1
==PAGE:7==
• Identify the taxonomic identity and phylogenetic relationship between the Lacto-
bacillus isolates
• Examine their interactions with other species from the gut microbiota samples
• Interpret the results in comparison with clinical observations and microbiome data
Due to very few Lactobacillus isolated from the fecal samples in this study, the scope was
expanded to include investigation of Enterococcus spp. from the HSCT patients. This
was included because previous studies have found Enterococcus spp. to be correlated
with adverse clinical outcomes in this type of patients [3, 4]. Thus, Enterococcus isolates
were characterized and analyzed in the same manner as the Lactobacillus spp., described
above.
1.3
Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation
Leukemia is a malignant disease, which originates in the bone marrow, leading to ab-
normal development of the stem cells. This is mainly the red blood cells and immune
cells such as white blood cells. Leukemia cells can spread quickly to the rest of the body
through the blood stream, making it a very severe disease, which is challenging to cure
[5]. One very promising treatment of leukemia is hematopoietic stem cell transplantation
(HSCT). This treatment seeks to replace the patient’s damaged stem cells with healthy
stem cells from the bone marrow, peripheral blood or umbilical cord blood. They can
be derived from the patient (autologous) or from a donor (allogeneic). The allogeneic
hematopoietic stem cells can be derived from a related donor or an unrelated donor [6].
The amount of HSCTs performed has been increasing within the last decades. A report
from 2014 by Passweg et al. revealed that the annual number of HSCTs performed has
increased since 1990 and reached almost 41.000 in 2014. Especially the amount of HSCTs
from unrelated donors has increased signiﬁcantly, since the knowledge and success rate
using unrelated donors has improved over the years [6, 7].
Prior to HSCT, a preparative regimen, also called conditioning regimen, is administered
to the patient. This regimen can be chemotherapy or total body irradiation, or a combina-
tion of the two [8]. The goal of the regimen is to; (1) eliminate the recipient’s immune cells
to avoid rejection of the donor cells, (2) avoid development of graft-versus-host disease,
and (3) to eradicate malignant cancer cells. Furthermore, antibiotics are administered to
the patient to prevent microbial infections during the conditioning regimen, where the
immune system is suppressed [9].
1.3.1
Complications and Side Eﬀects
HSCT can cause several complications and side eﬀects. Among these are graft-versus-
host disease (GvHD), graft rejection, malignancy relapse and bacterial infections. These
complications can be very severe and in worst case lead to death.
Graft-versus-host Disease:
One of the major complications in relation with HSCT
is GvHD, which is a disease where the donor T-cells are reacting against the recipient’s
2
==PAGE:8==
healthy cells [10]. This is the case in 35%-50% of the patients post allogeneic HSCT [11].
Acute GvHD (aGvHD) is normally developed within 100 days after HSCT. The disease
can manifest in the skin, liver and gut, which can lead to symptoms such as skin rash,
blisters, abdominal pain, diarrhea and hepatitis. The severity of each of the three aﬀected
areas is evaluated and assigned a stage between 0 and 4, where 4 is most severe. Based
on the extent to which each of the three individual organs is involved, the severity of
aGvHD can be described with an overall grade between I and IV, where IV represents
the most severe case [12]. An overview of this is shown in table 1.

One of the important factors in developing GvHD is the human leukocyte antigen (HLA).
The genes encoding HLA are located within the gene complex coding for Major histo-
compatability complex (MHC) [11]. Diﬀerences in these genes between donor and patient
could induce an immune reaction in the stem cell recipient and contribute to the devel-
opment of GvHD because HLA is a possible target for the donor immune cells. Because
many alleles for these genes exist, it is challenging to ﬁnd a suitable donor. The optimal
donor would be an HLA-identical sibling [13]. Furthermore, the microbiome of the host
is thought to play a role in the risk of developing GvHD. This will be covered in detail
later.
Graft rejection and relapse:
Graft rejection is to this date a concern regarding
malignancy relapse and mortality after HSCT. As part of the adaptive immune response,
MHC molecules are loaded onto the surface of all nucleated cells and are presenting
antigens to T-cells of the immune system, allowing the immune system to distinguish
between the host cells and foreign cells [11].
After HSCT, MHC incompatibility can
happen because the conditioning regimen is not able to eradicate all of the recipient’s
immune cells. This means that an immune reaction can occur towards the donor immune
cells, which will lead to eradication of these cells because the few remaining host immune
cells is not compatible with their MCH molecules [14].
Bacterial infections:
Bacterial infections, or bacteremia, is another common compli-
cation in connection with HSCT. The occurrence in HSCT patients can have several
explanations. The immunosuppressives administered to the patient prior to HSCT, as a
part of the preparative regimen, weakens the immune system and increases the risk of
bacterial infection. Furthermore, chemotherapy has a degenerative eﬀect on the mucus
3
==PAGE:9==
layer in the intestines, which weakens the protective barrier between the epithelial cells
and the bacterial community in the intestinal lumen. Also, a change in the composi-
tion of the intestinal microbial community is often a consequence of HSCT, leading to
domination of speciﬁc bacteria, which can again can lead to infection of the dominating
species [15].
1.4
Genomics-based Microbiome Analysis
The human body is colonized by millions of microorganisms, collectively called the mi-
crobiota. These organisms have been shown to be part of a symbiotic relationship and to
have inﬂuence on the health of the host [1]. The total gene pool of these microorganisms
is called the microbiome and a lot of eﬀort have been put into examining the identity of
the organisms and their genes in order to obtain knowledge about their role in the human
health [16].
Several methods have been developed in order to study the genes of the bacterial spp.
within the microbiota. These methods are constantly improving and some of them have
made it possible to examine bacteria, which can normally not be cultivated.
1.4.1
16S ribosomal RNA Gene Sequence Analysis
The 16S rRNA gene is coding for the 16S ribosomal RNA subunit. The gene is an essen-
tial gene in bacteria and includes both highly conserved and variable regions, which makes
it a good candidate for distinction between species and evolutionary analysis. However,
phylogenetic assignment based on analysis of the 16S rRNA gene alone does not permit
precise prediction of functional abilities, as bacteria can undergo horizontal gene trans-
fer. Thus, genes which are usually not found within the species in question can be found
within their genome [17].
The 16S rRNA gene was the ﬁrst gene to be successfully used to characterize the evolution-
ary relationship between members of the prokaryotic domain [18]. Originally, cultivation
techniques were used to sequence the 16S rRNA gene, also from mixed microbial popu-
lations. However, next generation sequencing (NGS) has provided a cheaper, faster and
less labour intensive way of characterizing bacteria within a microbial community [19].
16S rRNA gene sequencing is depending on PCR ampliﬁcation and design of primers.
These primers can induce a bias toward certain bacterial species, leading to the pop-
ulation skewness. This is tried to be circumvented by designing primers that bind to
highly conserved regions of the 16S rRNA gene, creating so-called universal primers [19,
20].
While 16S rRNA gene sequencing provides a way for identifying, whole genome
sequencing (WGS) can be used to explore the remaining genetic content of the bacteria
[19].
1.4.2
Whole Genome Sequencing
Whole Genome Sequencing can be performed as a part of metagenomic analysis, where
genes from a whole community of microbes are sequenced, or it can be done using cul-
4
==PAGE:10==
turing and sequencing of a single isolate. WGS presents a way of determining microbial
identity more precisely. Furthermore, determination of the total gene content can be per-
formed as well as predictions of metabolic pathways [19]. Various types of analyses can
be performed on WGS data. To mention a few, these include subtyping through multi-
locus sequence typing (MLST) and detection of single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs)
between the isolates to investigate their relation [21]. Furthermore, it is also possible to
predict virulence factor determinants, antibiotic resistance genes, or other speciﬁc genes
[21, 22].
1.5
The Intestinal Microbiota
The human microbiota comprises all the living microorganisms that colonize the human
body. These microorganisms, which co-exist with the host, can have a great impact on
overall health and a lot of focus has been dedicated to this area of research in recent
years. Especially the gut microbiota has drawn much attention. Over 1000 diﬀerent
bacterial species have been found within the human gut. Each individual possesses a
unique composition of bacteria within their intestines and these are thought to play a
critical role regarding overall health [23].
1.5.1
Composition and Function of the Microbiota in Healthy Individuals
Composition of healthy microbiota:
One of the original hypotheses was, that the
healthy human microbiota consisted of a set of ’core’ microbial taxa, and that the pres-
ence or absence of speciﬁc microbes could be associated with dysbiosis. However, studies
have shown that both the microbial taxa and the abundance of the shared taxa between
healthy individuals can vary greatly. This lead to a change of the ﬁrst hypothesis. An
alternative hypothesis is, that a set of functional ’core’ genes is comprised within the
microbiome of each healthy individual. Thus, the healthy individual share a range of
microbial functions, without necessarily sharing composition of the microbial commu-
nity. However, the two phyla, Bacteriodetes and Firmicutes has been detected in general
within the healthy gut, even though the ratio is very individual [1]. Furthermore, the
presence of speciﬁc bacterial species such as Lactobacillus spp. and Blautia spp. have
been associated with a healthy gut [3, 15].
Several studies have been performed to map the microbial content of the gut of children
and assess the impact of environmental factors on the microbiota [24–26]. In the ﬁrst years
of life, the microbiota is very dynamic. Abundances of Enterobacteriaceae, Biﬁdobacte-
riaceae and Clostridiaceae has been found to decrease between the age of two months
and 18 months, whereas abundance of members Lachnospiraceae and Ruminococcaceae
increased during this time period [26]. The composition of the microbiome is aﬀected by
factors such as mode of delivery, diet, antibiotic administration and surroundings. As an
example, Ceasarean section is correlated with an altered microbiome [26] and increased
presence of opportunistic pathogens [25]. Also, Lactobacillus is in general found to be
present within the microbiota of children [27], especially prominent in breast-fed children
[25]. After a certain age the microbial community is stabilizing into a more adult-like
composition [25]. However, antibiotic treatment is associated with delayed development
5
==PAGE:11==
of the microbiota of healthy children [28].
Function of healthy microbiota:
The gut microbiota plays an important role in the
host’s dietary metabolism, immune system and protection of the gut. Some bacteria play
an important nutritional role as they have shown to be a part of metabolism of lipids,
proteins, carbohydrates and some vitamins, providing nutritions, which are normally not
available to humans [29]. Furthermore, some bacteria have a protective role within the
gut. These can convert potentially harmful metabolic by-products or components of food
into innocuous elements. An example is oxalate, which is produced during fermentation
of carbohydrates in the gut. Several Lactobacillus spp. have been shown to be able to
degrade this by-product, which could otherwise contribute to the formation of kidney
stones [30].
Commensal bacteria also function as a defense line in the gut.
Interactions between
the microbes are contributing to prevent colonization of pathogenic bacteria and main-
taining homeostasis. This can be through competition for nutrients, alteration of the
environment, and production of antimicrobial compounds [31]. Some bacteria such as
Lactobacillus produce acids, which lower the pH of the environment, suppress growth,
and kill some pathogens [32]. Short chain fatty acids (SCFAs) and bacteriocins, produced
by some bacteria, can directly inhibit growth and virulence of some pathogens. Further-
more, SCFAs can decrease pH locally and inhibit bacteria with a higher pH optimum
[31].
Several studies have shown that gut commensals play a crucial role in maintaining home-
ostasis and avoiding bacterial invasion of the intestinal wall by interacting with the host
immune system [33–35]. Especially the diversity of the microbial community is thought
to contribute to maintaining an equilibrium between the microbiota and the host, which
prevents infection through the intestinal barrier [15].
The Intestinal epithelial cells (IECs) lining the gut and dendritic cells (DCs) of the innate
immune system are bearing pattern recognition receptors (PRRs), which sense commen-
sals through microbial-associated molecular patterns, such as lipopolysaccharides (LPS)
and ﬂagellin [36]. Paneth cells, which are a lineage of epithelial cells, are located within
the intestinal crypts. They produce antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) against commensals,
primarily to retain homoestasis in the gut.
They sense commensals directly through
PPRs and in response they produce and secrete AMPs (ﬁgure 1)[34]. Some of the known
AMPs are α-defensins and β-defensins, which induce cell rupture by destroying the cell
membrane of targeted bacteria [37]. Furthermore, SCFAs produced by commensal bac-
teria are used as energy for IECs and they have shown to be a part of diﬀerentiation,
recruitment, and activation of immune cells, such as regulatory T-cells (Treg cells) (ﬁgure
1) [38]. Treg cells plays a role in modulation of the immune system and help maintaining
tolerance to self-antigens [39]. Due to this interplay between the microbial community
and the host immune system, dysbiosis in the gut microbiota has been associated with a
range of diseases including GvHD [3, 4].

1.5.2
Composition and Function of the Microbiota in patients undergoing
HSCT
Composition of microbiota in HSCT patients:
Several studies have been dedi-
cated to the investigation of intestinal microbial composition of adult HSCT patients.
HSCT has been associated with decreased commensal diversity [3, 4, 15] and an increase
in Enterococcus spp. after HSCT [4, 15]. These eﬀects were enhanced in the patients
who developed GvHD [3, 4]. This has also been conﬁrmed in children. In a longitudinal
study examining the gut microbiota of children undergoing HSCT showed decrease in
microbial diversity during HSCT but showed recovery of the microbiota approximately
two months after HSCT [40]. Loss of diversity has been associated with increased mor-
tality and might be enhanced by use of antibiotics, intestinal inﬂammation, and loss of
appetite [41]. Taur et al. [15] found no connection between sex, age, conditioning regi-
men intensity, or stem cell source and the dominating species within the gut of adults.
However, the dominating species of bacteria seem to be partly determined by the type of
prophylactic antibiotics used during treatment. Administration of metronidazole was as-
sociated with increased risk of domination by Enterococcus, whereas ﬂuoroquinolone was
associated with increased proteobacterial domination [15]. The presence of certain com-
mensal bacteria such as the spp. Blautia and Lactobacillus has primarily been detected
in patients with decreased GvHD and lower mortality [3, 42].
7
==PAGE:13==
Function of microbiota in HSCT patients:
The loss of bacterial diversity in the
gut of patients undergoing HSCT is critical for a range of host protective functions. Re-
garding microbe-microbe interactions, a loss diversity may reduce resistance of pathogen
colonization, allowing pathogens to expand within the gut and increasing the risk of
bacterial infection [15]. Furthermore, several factors have shown to aﬀect the interplay
between the gut microbes and the host immune system during HSCT. As mentioned, the
composition of the microbiota changes in many cases toward a lower diversity and a dom-
ination of one bacterial genus. The decreased diversity is thought to disrupt the natural
equilibrium between the microbiota and the immune cells in the gut, thus increasing the
risk of GvHD and bacteremia with the dominating species [4, 15]. The mucosal layer in
the intestinal lumen is often impaired and the number of paneth cells is reduced due to
the preparative regimen prior to HSCT. This adds to the risk of bacterial penetration
of the intestinal epithelial layer as the production of AMP is impaired as a consequence
[38].
The direct interplay between gut bacteria, the immune cells and receptors is also af-
fected during GvHD. The loss of bacterial diversity entails less activation of the PRRs by
the bacterial MAMPs, which leads to decreased induction of the antimicrobial responses
elicited through these receptors [34]. Vaishnava et al. showed that production of the
AMPs, RegIIIγ and RegIIIβ was dose dependent on lipopolysaccharide (LPS), which is
a ligand for on of the PRRs, toll-like receptor 4. In some cases the loss of some bacte-
rial species can lead to the loss of important microbial metabolites such as SCFAs. The
development of GvHD has been correlated with a decrease in these, in pediatric HSCT
patients [40]. The importance of SCFAs has been reviewed by Mathewson et al. [43].
They tested the impact of the SCFA on IECs, butyrate and found that this metabolite
decreased apoptosis of IECs and mitigated GvHD severity, suggesting a worsening of
GvHD with a loss of butyrate producing bacteria [43]. Clostridiales are SCFA producing
bacteria and the presence of these within the microbiota has been associated with reduced
mortality from GvHD [42]. As mentioned, SCFAs can directly regulate the amount of
Treg cells in the colon, thus a loss of these can reduce tolerance toward self-antigens and
induce inﬂammatory and autoimmune diseases [39].
1.5.3
Lactobacillus in HSCT Patients
The members of the bacterial genus Lactobacillus are lactic acid bacteria (LAB), belong-
ing to the Lactobacillaceae family, which convert carbohydrates into lactic acid through
fermentation. Lactobacilli are Gram-positive bacteria, facultative anaerobe, which are
normally found within the healthy human gut community [44]. Lactobacillus spp. are
generally known to be beneﬁcial to human health, and are widely used as probiotics,
which according to FAO (Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations) and
WHO (World Health Organization) are deﬁned as "Live microorganisms, which when
administered in adequate amounts, confer a health beneﬁt on the host" [45]. The mode
of action of the probiotic bacteria can include; Exclusion of pathogens by their ability to
adhere to the gut wall, production of AMPs, inhibition of pathogen adhesion, enhance-
ment of the epithelial barrier, and modulation of the immune system [46]. The use of
Lactobacillus spp. as probiotics are based on their ability to exert many of these abili-
ties. Studies have shown that some lactobacilli adhere strongly to colon cells compared
to other dairy strains [47] and that some can prime the development of Treg cells and

1.5.4
Enterococcus in HSCT Patients
Bacteria of the genus Enterococcus belong to the family Enterococcaceae and are LABs.
They are facultative anaerobic organisms, which are found in the intestine of humans
and animals [44]. Some Enterococcus have probiotic properties and can be beneﬁcial
regarding human health. However, at the same time they are important opportunistic
pathogens able to cause severe infections [52]. Several virulence genes have been iden-
tiﬁed within the genome of Enterococcus faecalis and Enterococcus faecium [53]. This
includes so called pathogenic islands, which is a genetic element composed of several
open reading frames with genes coding for various virulence factors. Genes within these
pathogenic islands have been found to be transferred between bacteria [54]. These can
encode virulence factor involved in colonization, invasion and resistance toward protec-
tive host mechanisms. The spread of both virulence genes and antibiotic resistance genes
between bacteria, through horizontal gene transfer, propose a challenging problem, espe-
cially in hospital settings and enterococci have emerged as one of the leading causes of
nosocomial infections [55]. E. faecalis is one of the species often found in clinical settings
and have a high content of mobile genetic elements within its genome. The eﬀectiveness
of Enterococcus species to transfer genetic material to one another through horizontal
gene transfer has contributed to the spread of antibiotic resistance and virulence traits
[54].
As mentioned previously, HSCT and GvHD are often associated with expansion of entero-
cocci in the gut microbiota. Bacteremia with vancomycin resistant Enterococcus (VRE)
has been correlated with the domination of Enterococcus [15]. Furthermore, some intesti-
nal Enterococcus spp. have been shown to produce a toxic compound, metalloprotease,
which contributes to impairment of the protective epithelial barrier [56].
1.6
Objectives
The composition of the gut microbiota in pediatric patients with GvHD has been estab-
lished by PhD student, Anna C¨acilia Masche. It has been proposed that lactobacilli have
a beneﬁcial eﬀect as to the outcome of HSCT and that they have a compensatory eﬀect
when expanding after onset of GvHD. Furthermore, lactobacilli present before HSCT are
hypothesized to have a protective eﬀect (Anna et al., unpublished [2]). Enterococcus spp.
are of great interest, as some studies have reported an eﬀect of enterococci on the outcome
of HSCT and and it has been proposed that lactobacilli might have a neutralizing eﬀect
on enterococci [3]. As mentioned, Enterococcus was included in the following study, in
addition to Lactobacillus due to a low number of Lactobacillus isolates obtained and the
interesting properties of this genus explained above.
The Lactobacillus and Enterococcus spp. within the gut of these patients still remain to
be investigated on strain level. Whole genome sequencing of these will make it possible
to investigate the genetic content of the diﬀerent strains. The end goal is to determine

underlying mechanisms, within the present Lactobacillus and Enterococcus strains, which
may inﬂuence development of GvHD and mortality.
In this context, it is investigated whether there is a diﬀerence in the species composition

who did not survive. Furthermore, isolation of the bacteria makes it possible to examine

2.3.2
Whole Genome Sequencing
A subset of 82 isolates identiﬁed by 16S rDNA sanger sequencing was selected for whole
genome sequencing (WGS) (Supplementary_material excel ﬁle, Table S1). Members of
the genera Lactobacillus, Enterococcus, and a few Pediococcus were included. However,
within one sample, only one representative of each accession number, assigned by SILVA
ACT, was submitted for WGS.
DNA Extraction:
The isolates were streaked directly from the freeze stock (-80°C)
onto MRS plates and incubated for 48h at 37°C in microaerophilic conditions. After incu-
bation, the bacteria were harvested (3/4 of a 10µl loop) and suspended in 200µl Phosphate
Buﬀered Saline (PBS). 30µl lysozyme (10mg/ml) was added to the suspension followed
by incubation at 37°C on shake (550rpm) for 20minutes. 10µl of 10% Sodium Dodecyl
Sulfate (SDS) was added followed by gently mixing and addition of 15µl Proteinase K
(20mg/ml). The suspension was incubated at 37°C for 20 minutes.
The DNA was isolated and precipitated using the Easy-DNATM Kit from Invitrogen
(product number: 45-0424). The DNA was quantiﬁed using Invitrogen QubitTM dsDNA
Broad Range Assay Kit (ref: Q32853) and Invitrogen QubitTM dsDNA High Sensitivity
Assay Kit (ref: Q32854) from Thermo Fisher Scientiﬁc. The samples were diluted to a
ﬁnal concentration between 0,18ng/µl and 0,28ng/µl.
Library preparation was perfomed Jacob Dyring Jensen from the Genetic Epidemiol-
ogy group from DTU Food. The genetic content of each isolate was sequenced using
NextSeqTM 500 sequencer.

a quality control pipeline, which includes quality control of the raw reads using FastQC

these by searching databases such as UniProt, RefSeq and HMM proﬁle databases with
BLAST+ [69] and HMMER [70]. The predicted 16S rRNA genes from all the isolates
were extracted from the PROKKA output and used for a second identiﬁcation of the

As opposed to the 16S rRNA gene sequences from sanger sequencing (size:

(CGE) (https://cge.cbs.dtu.dk/services/MLST/, accessed 24/05-2018). MLST is com-

schemes for diﬀerent organisms. Unique combinations of alleleic variants are assigned a
sequence type, presented as a random integer. This provides for deeper identiﬁcation and
allows for more detailed comparison of the isolates [71].

2.5
Statistical Analysis
The associations between survival of the HSCT patients and potential explanatory vari-
ables were assessed statistically in univariate and analysis. For this, Fisher’s exact test
was used due to small sample size. The included risk factors were presence or absence
of bacterial spp. isolated from the gut of the patients, as well as presence or absence of
virulence genes and antibiotic resistance genes.

3.1
Identiﬁcation of Isolates from HSCT Patients
It was hypothesized that Lactobacillus spp. have an eﬀect on the outcome of HSCT in
leukemia patients. In order to test this hypothesis, targeted isolation of Lactobacillus was
performed using MRS agar. The 16S rRNA gene sequencing revealed a low number of
Lactobacillus isolates but a high number of Enterococcus species. Therefore, Enterococcus
spp. from patients with moderate to severe GvHD were also included in the analysis.
The ﬁrst step was to identify the bacterial isolates from the HSCT patients
3.1.1
Genus-level Identiﬁcation of Isolates from HSCT Patients
In order to obtain lactobacilli and enterococci from HSCT patients to later identify and
characterize them, bacteria were isolated from fecal matter provided in between a time
span from before HSCT to maximum ﬁve weeks after HSCT. Each fecal sample (41 in
total) from a total of 13 patients with no to mild GvHD (n=5) or moderate to severe
GvHD (n=8) was plated onto the Lactobacillus selective media MRS and Rogosa media.
16S rRNA gene sanger sequencing of a selection of isolates allowed identiﬁcation on
genus level using SILVA ACT (Supplementary_material excel ﬁle, Table S5). In total,
Lactobacillus and/or Enterococcus isolates were obtained from 15 samples, which were
all derived from patients suﬀering from moderate to severe GvHD (surviving: P16, P19,
P22, and P29, deceased: P24, P26, and P30). A total of 18 Lactobacillus isolates were
isolated from three of these samples, and 53 isolates of enterococci were isolated from 14
samples. Lactobacillus was found in P22 and P24, while Enterococcus was obtained from
all seven patients (ﬁgure 4).

3.2
Phylogenetic Analysis of Isolates from HSCT Patients
The phylogenetic relationship between the isolates from HSCT patients was examined us-
ing the 16S rRNA gene sequences extracted from the PROKKA gene annotation output.
This was done to get a general overview of their relation and to conﬁrm the identiﬁca-
tion performed earlier. A phylogenetic tree was computed for the Lactobacillus and the
Enterococcus isolates, respectively and SILVA ACT was used for this purpose. The 16S
rRNA gene sequences from the type strain for each species and from one representative
of the best match within the SILVA rRNA database (nearest neighbor) was included as
reference genes (Supplementary_material excel ﬁle, Table S3).
Subsequently, the phylogenetic relationship of the isolates within each species was sep-
arately examined.
Isolates were compared to a reference, and single nucleotide poly-
morphisms (SNPs) were called between the genomes (Supplementary_material excel ﬁle,
Table S9-S11).
SNP-trees were computed using CSI phylogeny from CGE. This was
done in order to obtain a deeper perspective into the genome diﬀerences and the re-
lationship between the isolates within each species. Furthermore, associations between
the presence of speciﬁc isolate identities, patients, time point and sample CST were
assessed. Where available, reference genomes, type strain genomes, and a pathogenic
strain and/or a probiotic strain were included in the phylogenetic tree with a purpose
of comparison. The reference strain used to compute the trees was based on the best
match obtained from KmerFinder (Supplementary_material excel ﬁle, Table S4). All in-
cluded genomes, which were not isolated during this study were downloaded from NCBI
(https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov).

3.3
Genetic Analysis of Bacterial Isolates from Intestines of HSCT
Patients
In order to obtain knowledge about the pathogenecity and antibiotic resistance of the iso-
lates, the genomes were screened for resistance genes, virulence genes and plasmids. This
was done using the tools ResFinder, VirulenceFinder and PlasmidFinder, respectively,
from CGE. No plasmids were detected in any of the genomes.
3.3.1
Predicted Antibiotic Resistance of Isolates
The genomes of both Lactobacillus and Enterococcus isolates were screened for resis-
tance genes using ResFinder from CGE. Detailed output are available in Supplemen-
tary_material excel ﬁle, Table S12. No resistance genes were detected within the genomes
of the Lactobacillus isolates, except for the lnuA, which was only found in L. casei. How-
ever, only a part of this gene was present (ﬁgure 12). Also, no resistance genes were
detected in E. dispar, E. raﬃnosus or E. sp. HMSC064A12.
A general pattern was that isolates of same sequence type share resistance genes (ﬁgure
12). Four putative resistance genes were detected in E. faecalis ST69 (str, ermB, tetM,
and lsaA). All of these were a perfect match except lsaA, which was detected with an
ID% of 99,5. The E. faecalis isolates with fewest resistance genes present were ST290
and ST373, which were only obtained from deceased patients. These carried the putative
resistance gene lsaA with an identity of 99% and 99,7%, respectively. E. faecalis ST102,
from a surviving patient carried the genes tetM and lsaA with identities at 99,1% and
98,1% respectively.
The majority of the E. faecium isolates contained one putative resistance gene, namely
msrC. However, only two of these (ST39) from surviving patient 29 had a 100% match.

4
Discussion
During disease and treatment, such as with leukemia patients receiving allogeneic HSCT,
the human microbiota and the host immune system are aﬀected [38]. Furthermore, the
change in composition of the microbial community seems aﬀect the host immune system
and impact on the clinical outcome. This interaction is not fully understood yet, but
might be crucial as to predicting the outcome and provide the best possible treatment to
individual patients. To the the best of my knowledge, no studies have yet isolated lac-
tobacilli and enterococci from HSCT patients and performed WGS to characterize them
on strain level. In this study I have isolated, identiﬁed and characterized Lactobacillus
spp. and Enterococcus spp. from this type of patients. Furthermore, I have investigated
microbe-microbe interactions between members of these genera.
4.1
Proposed eﬀects of Lactobacillus spp. in the gut of HSCT
patients
The primary intention with this study was to isolate Lactobacillus spp. from the gut of
HSCT patients. The reason for this was that a preceding study found Lactobacillaceae
to be associated with low immune cell reconstitution and high mortality, when members
of these were found after onset of GvHD. In contrast, it was observed that patients with
high Lactobacillaceae abundance before onset of GvHD survived, which pointed towards
a protective eﬀect (Anna et al., unpublished [2]). Furthermore, it has been proposed that
members of the genus Lactobacillus have a protective eﬀect against GvHD in the gut of
mice and humans [3]. In this context, it is interesting to investigate the identities and
genomic diﬀerences between Lactobacillus spp. from surviving and deceased patients,
respectively. Unfortunately, no Lactobacillus isolates from late time points of deceased
patients were obtained and thus no eﬀects of these could be investigated.
I observed a higher number of Lactobacillus isolates from surviving HSCT patients com-
pared to deceased patients. In addition, the present species diﬀered between surviving
and deceased patients. I should be mentioned that these observations were based on
isolates obtained from two patients only. Lactobacilli are generally associated with ben-
eﬁcial eﬀects and have shown promising results in treatment of various diseases such as
inﬂammatory bowel disease, diabetes, and cardiovascular diseases [90]. L. sakei was one
of the Lactobacillus spp. obtained from surviving HSCT patients. L. sakei is a food-borne
bacterium, which have been suggested to originate from diet rather than being a human
commensal because they did not persist in the gut of mice with existing microbiota [91].
The decrease in number of isolates of L. sakei from day of transplantation to week +4
in P22 may be explained by the patient obtaining this spp. from diet. However, L. sakei
might still exhibit beneﬁcial traits such as pathogen inhibition. This is suggested by
the inhibitory abilities against Enterococcus spp. present before HSCT in the surviving
patient 22 (ﬁgure 14).
Two other Lactobacillus spp. were obtained in this study, L. brevis and L. casei. Inter-
estingly, L. brevis was observed in a surviving patient (P22), while L. casei was observed
39
==PAGE:45==
in a deceased patient (P24).
L. brevis has been shown to decrease oral mucositis in
HSCT patients [92], while L. casei showed anti-inﬂammatory properties in a Shigella
infected human epithelial cell line [93]. Furthermore, other studies have shown promising
results regarding the protective eﬀect of Lactobacillus spp., such as L. johnsonii and L.
rhamnosus GG in mice with experimental GvHD [3, 50]. However, the probiotic strain
Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG used in human trials did not have beneﬁcial eﬀects on the
microbiota diversity [51]. This can indicate that some Lactobacillus spp. are beneﬁcial
for human health, while others do not have any eﬀect, and that L. brevis could have
beneﬁcial eﬀects in patients from this study as opposed to L. casei. Another explanation
of the eﬀect of these Lactobacillus spp. could be the diﬀerence in the number of Lac-
tobacillus isolates obtained from the P22 and P24. Even though the isolation does not
provide an exact quantitative measurement of the abundance of each species, it can still
be representative of such. Only one isolate of L. casei was obtained from P24 compared
to ten isolates of L. brevis obtained from P22 in total. This could indicate that the
possible protective eﬀect of these bacteria increases with increased abundance.
L. brevis from patient 22 did not show clear inhibitory activities against Enterococcus
isolates derived from the same patient. One potential explanation is that the possible
beneﬁcial eﬀect from this strain is exerted through microbe-host interactions rather than
microbe-microbe interactions. To my knowledge, this has not been investigated in the
context of HSCT. However, such immuno-modulatory and anti-inﬂammatory abilities
of this species has been reported in experimental periodontal inﬂammation [94]. Thus,
it is possible that similar eﬀects are happening in HSCT patients. Furthermore, some
Lactobacillus spp. including L. brevis have shown to produce SCFAs such as acetate
and formate under diﬀerent environmental conditions [95, 96]. Furthermore, acetate pro-
duced by Biﬁdobacteria have been associated with anti-inﬂammatory and anti-apoptotic
response in mice infected with E. coli [97]. It is possible that the conditions within the
gut of HSCT patients is favorable for the production of some of these SCFAs by some
lactobacilli and maybe even L. brevis or other Lactobacillus spp. present in the gut of sur-
viving HSCT patients. Taken together, one might speculate that L. brevis could induce
protective abilities in the gut of HSCT patients through production of SCFAs.
In this study, Phylogenetic analysis showed one Lactobacillus brevis isolate clustering
away from the other L. brevis isolates in the SNP-tree (ﬁgure 8). All L. brevis isolates
were obtained from the same patient (P22). The high number of SNP diﬀerences (∼
11,000) between the one isolate and the rest of them could indicate that two diﬀerent
strains of L. brevis are present in the gut of this patient. Furthermore, the genetic vari-
ation could entail a diﬀerence in functional properties, highlighting the need for further
examination of functional properties of Lactobacillus spp. In general, Lactobacillus iso-
lates were obtained from few samples in this study, thus expanded research is needed to
obtain conclusive results regarding the eﬀect of Lactobacillus spp in HSCT patients.

==PAGE:46==
4.2
Proposed eﬀects of Enterococcus spp. in the gut of HSCT
patients
Isolated Enterococcus spp. were included in this study because they have previously been
found important in the context of HCST outcome. This includes association between
enterococcal expansion and GvHD in HSCT patients [3, 4] and increased risk of infection
with vancomycin resistant Enterococcus following this expansion [15]. Furthermore, no
cultivation-based investigation of this genus has yet been performed in the context of
HSCT patients, to my knowledge. In the present study, I observed an increased number
of E. faecium and a decreased number of E. faecalis in surviving HSCT patients over
time. Simultaneously, a higher number of E. faecalis was observed in deceased opposed
to surviving patients. Two independent studies investigating clinical Enterococcus iso-
lates from Malaysian and Bulgarian hospitals, respectively, found patterns of E. faecalis
harboring a higher number of virulence determinants than E. faecium [98, 99]. In Bul-
garian isolates, a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the incidence of the metalloproteinase coding
gene gelE, aggregation substance agg and hyaluronidase hyl genes was found in E. fae-
calis compared to E. faecium. In contrast, E. faecium showed higher incidences of the
adhesion gene efaA [99]. Also, relatively higher incidences of gelE was found in E. faecalis
compared to E. faecium in Malaysian hospitals, though the diﬀerence was not signiﬁcant
[98]. In the current study, gelE, agg, and hyl genes were harbored only by E. faecalis (ﬁg-
ure 13). This points to an indication that E. faecalis is generally more virulent than E.
faecium. Even though the current study found no eﬀect of Enterococcus spp. on survival
in HSCT patients with severe GvHD, the above could indicate detrimental eﬀect of high
abundance of E. faecalis in HSCT patients. However, conﬁrmation of this data requires
a larger study isolating and functional characterizing E. faecalis isolates in the context
of HSCT.
In addition to the higher number of E. faecium in surviving patients as opposed to
deceased patients, I observed an increase in number of E. faecium over time in two
surviving patients (P22 and P29). In another study analyzing the gut microbiome of
patients undergoing stem cell transplantation, they found a general increase in number of
E. faecium in patients with GvHD as compared to patients without GvHD [4]. Given the
challenges with isolations (explained later) it is possible that E. faecium is present at late
time points in deceased patients from the current study, even though I was not able to
isolate any members of this species from the fecal samples. On the other hand, a relatively
high number of Staphylococcus was isolated from samples collected at late time points in
deceased patients 26 and 30 (Supplementary_material excel ﬁle, Table S1). Thus, viable
cells were indeed present in the samples and there is a possibility that E. faecium was
either not present at all or in too few numbers to be detected. It should be mentioned
that no Enterococcus isolates were obtained from patients with no or mild GvHD, which
means that comparison of Enterococcus spp. present in patients with diﬀerent grades of
GvHD was not possible. The lack of isolates limits the analysis of bacterial spp. present
over time in these patients and further investigations would be necessary to provide more
information about the role of E. faecium in HSCT patients.
In the current study, sequence typing of the present Enterococcus spp. was performed. I
observed a diﬀerence in the sequence types present in patients and even in samples, with
a few exceptions. E. faecalis ST69 was present in both alive and deceased patients and
41
==PAGE:47==
might be a commensal strain. Furthermore, an interesting observation was that E. faecalis
ST290 was observed independently in two deceased patients and not in any surviving
patients (ﬁgure 5). These isolates were sharing virulence and resistance determinants
and three of the virulence genes (100% match: camE, cad and cCF10) are involved in
plasmid conjugation. This indicates an ability to acquire resistance genes and virulence
genes through horizontal transfer, which is in congruence with earlier ﬁndings that E.
faecalis is eﬃcient in this aspect [54, 100]. Since isolation from some samples did not
yield any Enterococcus spp., this interpretation is based on relatively few isolates from
each patient and additional isolation from HSCT patients is needed before any conclusive
remarks can be stated.
Enterococcus spp. acquiring antibiotic resistance are increasing and pose a problem as to
nosocomial infections [55]. As previously mentioned, resistance genes can be eﬀectively
transferred between bacteria through horizontal gene transfer [54]. The patients within
this study were treated with antibiotics as prophylactic treatments, posing a risk of
members of the gut microbial community conferring resistance towards the antibiotics
used. Overall, the resistance pattern of the isolates obtained from HSCT patients did not
indicate that this was the case. As an example, E. faecalis carried the resistance gene
msrC, which confers resistance to macrolide antibiotics. None of the patients included in
the cohort from the current study received these types of antibiotics prior to or during
HSCT (Supplementary_material, excel ﬁle, Table S12).
However, trimethoprim was
administered to all patients, from which isolates were obtained, and three isolates from
patient 29 were found to carry the resistance gene for this antibiotic (dfrK). Thus, it is
possible that these speciﬁc isolates conferred resistance due to the use of trimethoprim.
Interactions between members of the microbial gut community are essential for retention
of a healthy gut environment and to obtain colonization resistance against pathogens
[31]. I assessed the interactions between Lactobacillus and Enterococcus isolates from a
surviving HSCT patient (P22) to investigate possible modulatory eﬀect of the isolates
on one another. Inhibitory activity was observed from E. faecium (sample: week +4)
against all enterococci obtained from before HSCT (E. faecalis, E. faecium, E. raﬃnosus
and E. HMSC064A12) (ﬁgure 14). This inhibitory activity could explain why E. faecium
was isolated from samples after HSCT (week +4) in patient 22 as opposed to other
Enterococcus spp.
(ﬁgure 5).
These results, combined with low number of virulence
and resistance genes carried by E. faecium as compared to E. faecalis, suggest that E.
faecium provides a protective eﬀect to the patient by inhibiting the growth of E. faecalis.
To the best of my knowledge it is the ﬁrst time cultivation based interactions between
members of the gut microbiota, more precisely enterococcal isolates, from HSCT patients
has been examined. Probiotic eﬀects of an E. faecium strain (NCIMB 10415) have been
reported to enhance the epithelial barrier integrity in human epithelial cell lines [101].
Furthermore, the same study indicated that this E. faecium strain was able to reduce
the harmful eﬀects of a pathogenic Escherichia coli on the barrier function of epithelial
cells. This further endorses the possibility that the E. faecium from week +4 in the
current study might be able to reduce the damaging eﬀects of other potential pathogenic
microbes in the gut of HSCT patients by inhibiting their growth.
The two E. faecium, obtained from two diﬀerent time points (before HSCT and at week
+4), included in the overlay assay were not exhibiting the same inhibitory activity (ﬁg-
ure 14). Furthermore, they were not of the same sequence type and were located within
42
==PAGE:48==
diﬀerent clusters in the SNP-tree used in the phylogenetic analysis of E. faecium isolates
(ﬁgure11). This demonstrates a possible diﬀerence in the genomic content, which might
entail diﬀerent functional properties between the strains, as previously mentioned in re-
gard with L. brevis from HSCT patients. Furthermore, it demonstrates the importance of
looking into strain level aﬃliations in microbiome research to get a deeper understanding
of the role of gut microbes in health and disease. Regarding the study conducted here,
further analysis of the isolates from patient 22 could be performed in order to deter-
mine the mechanism of the inhibitory eﬀect. However, an important aspect of microbial
community to keep in mind is that the bacterial composition and interplay between the
members of the human microbiota is complex and includes members of many genera be-
sides Lactobacillus and Enterococcus. Thus, the examination of additional species from
the isolation can provide us with valuable insights into microbial dynamics within the
guts of HSCT patients, but we have to consider the community as a whole.
4.3
Limitations of the study
The focus of this study was supposed to be on lactobacilli and their eﬀect on the outcome
of patients undergoing HSCT. However, only few isolates were obtained. The diﬃculty
of isolating lactobacilli could be caused by several factors. One of the explanations could
be the direct freezing of the fecal samples. Storage conditions can aﬀect the viability of
bacterial cells [102]. Thus, some of the lactobacilli from the fecal samples might have died
due to direct freezing of the samples. Also, the samples have been thawed and frozen
at least once prior to this study, which can also aﬀect the viability of the bacterial cells,
especially during the ﬁrst two freeze/thaw circles [103].
Furthermore, the process of choosing the colonies during the isolation was based on the
morphology of the colonies, which was hard to distinguish from one another. It is possible
that more Lactobacillus colonies were present but that they were not chosen for the
further analysis. Despite the diﬃculty of isolating lactobacilli, a relatively large amount
of other bacterial genera, such as Staphylococcus and Enterococcus was isolated on MRS
plates.
The viability of Enterococcus strains has also shown to decrease signiﬁcantly
during freezing [104], which could indicate that Lactobacillus spp. were generally less
abundant in the concerned samples as compared to e.g.
Enterococcus spp.
Another
explanation could be that the conditions within the samples from HSCT patients favor
the survival of Enterococcus spp. rather than Lactobacillus spp.
After WGS, correct assembly of reads into contigs can be challenging, e.g. due to repeats
in the DNA fragments. This means that some parts of the genome may not be present in
the assembled contigs [105]. When predicting resistance genes, virulence genes and plas-
mids for the isolates, a full gene sequence is preferred. The tools used in this study are
able to identify truncated genes, which may be present at the end of a contig. However,
a partial gene is not enough to predict a phenotype, and even with a gene present with
a 100% match, the gene might not be expressed. Thus, it will not yield any phenotype.
In vitro studies would have to be performed to obtain phenotypic characterization. Fur-
thermore, the resistance gene database might not be suﬃcient to predict the resistance
pattern of a certain isolate. Reasons for this can be that some resistance genes might not
have been discovered yet and that mutations within intrinsic genes can lead to antibiotic
resistance. Regarding the plasmids, these are detected based on a database containing
43
==PAGE:49==
origins of replications of a range of plasmids [77]. The fact that no plasmids were detected
within the isolates in this study, does not necessarily mean that they are not present.
These DNA sequences may have been discarded during quality control of the sequences
from WGS due to bad quality.
Regarding the overlay assay performed in this study, the diﬀerences in cell concentrations
between the test strain (spots on agar plate) and target strain (inoculated in top-agar)
might impact on the susceptibility of the target strain towards a potential antimicrobial
compound produced by the test stain. Furthermore, I often obtained an inhibitory eﬀect
from the negative control (same strain used as both test strain and target strain). The
negative control was used as a baseline. Thus, the clearing zones should be compared
to the negative control and test strains showing clearing zones larger than this baseline
are considered to have inhibitory activity [106].
This assay is used as a quantitative
measurement of microbe-microbe inhibition only. Furthermore, the experiment was only
performed once during the course of this study.
In order to obtain more conclusive
evidence of inhibitory activity of the isolates from this experiment, it would have to be
repeated.
5
Future Perspectives
During this study, isolates were successfully achieved from patients with moderate to
severe GvHD. Isolation from patients with no or mild GvHD was attempted but un-
successful.
Isolation from these patients would be needed in order to investigate the
diﬀerences in the bacterial microbiota on strain level between them and patients with
moderate to severe GvHD.
For further elucidation of the possible role of speciﬁc Lactobacillus and Enterococcus
strains in the survival of HSCT patients, it would be interesting to look more into the
metabolic pathways and bioactive compound production of these isolates. This could be
done through the KEGG database (Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes), which
uses genomes for pathway mapping, including prediction of cellular processes, functions
of the organism, and disease related genes [107] .
Furthermore, in vitro experiments
can provide additional information about the antibiotic susceptibility and the virulence
factors produced by the isolates.
Antibiotic susceptibility could be assessed through
minimal inhibitory concentration tests, whereas the expression of virulence associated
proteins could be examined through northern blotting (detecting RNA) or western blot-
ting (detecting peptides), or reporter genes for enzyme activity. Furthermore, virulence
properties could be assessed through virulence assays investigating bioﬁlm formation and
toxic eﬀects against IECs. I did perform one in vitro assay in this study, which was
assessing the inhibitory properties of isolates within one patient. Due to time limita-
tions, this assay was only performed once and would need to be repeated to draw any
ﬁnal conclusions. Furthermore, the assay was a qualitative assessment of the inhibition
abilites of the isolates. However, I saw an inhibition of E. faecalis from E. faecium in
the surviving patient 22 and it would be very interesting to investigate this inhibition
further, for example by . Interaction assays between other isolates would be interestesting
to conduct as well. Speciﬁcally it could be interesting to look into E. faecalis ST290, as
44
==PAGE:50==
this sequence type was only found in deceased patients. Interactions between these and
some of the Lactobacillus isolates could give an indication whether this Enterococcus is
especially inhibitory against lactobacilli.
This project has shown the importance of strain level investigations of the human micro-
biota. The diﬀerence in strain aﬃliation between patients along with their diﬀerences in
virulence genes, resistance genes and inhibitory activity against other microbes tells us
that identiﬁcation at genus level or even species level is not suﬃcient for getting the full
picture of the interplay between host and the microbial community. This emphasizes the
need for identiﬁcation and characterization of bacteria from HSCT patients.

Conclusion
Lactobacillus and Enterococcus isolates were isolated from fecal samples of HSCT patients
with moderate to severe GvHD and whole genome sequenced in order to elucidate strain
diﬀerences and mechanisms possibly involved in the disease outcome of these patients.
Diﬀerent Lactobacillus spp. were obtained from deceased patients compared to surviving
patients. Furthermore, Enterococcus spp. also diﬀered between deceased and surviving
patients, even at strain level. Mainly L. sakei, L. brevis, and E. faecium were obtained
from surviving patients, whereas L. casei and E. faecalis were obtained from deceased
patients, indicating a possible beneﬁcial eﬀect of some species and a detrimental eﬀect
of others on the outcome of HSCT. Furthermore, putative resistance and virulence de-
terminants for the isolates were predicted through in silico analysis and showed that E.
faecalis in general carried a higher number of these compared to E. faecium.
Interactions between isolates obtained from a single surviving patient showed inhibitory
eﬀects between some of the isolates. Notably, one E. faecium strain showed inhibitory
activity towards other enterococci, including an E. faecalis isolate and strain of E. fae-
cium obtained from an earlier sample.
Limitations of the study, for instance small sample size, diﬃculty of isolating lactobacilli
and lack of time necessitates further investigation of the microbe-host and microbe-
microbe interactions between members of the gut microbiota in HSCT patients.
In
conclusion, the current study shows that bacteria found within the gut of HSCT pa-
tients diﬀers between patients and even within samples obtained from the same patient.
This highlights the need to resolve strain level aﬃliations in microbiome research because
of the examination at genus level or even species level does not provide a suﬃciently
detailed insight into the eﬀects of the microbiome in HSCT patients.
