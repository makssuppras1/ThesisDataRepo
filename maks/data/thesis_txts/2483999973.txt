Abstract
The study documented in this report investigates the modal analysis of a physical
structure, mathematically described by a Linear Time Periodic system of equations.
Since system parameters are time dependent, modal analysis cannot be performed
using the standard techniques developed for Linear Time Invariant systems. As a re-
sult, special techniques must be developed for modal analysis of this class of systems.
It this study a rotating structure consisting of a rotor with four пђВexible, radially
clamped blades is studied theoretically and experimentally. Experimental modal
analysis (EMA) based on system input (force) and output (displacement) is applied
in order to identify modal parameters of the system. Additionally, the structure is
analysed based on output only. This approach is normally referred to as Operational
Modal Analysis (OMA), and typically used when artiпђБcial excitation of the analysed
structure is impractical, expensive, or impossible.
The theoretical foundation for the applied methods of analysis is reviewed, before
the data acquisition process is described, and the results of the analyses are pre-
sented and analysed.
The analysis has shown good agreement between theretical predictions and experi-
mental results, especially regarding natural frequencies and mode shapes.

Introduction
The experimental analysis of mechanical structures traditionally involved measure-
ments of frequency response functions to a multitude of excitation conditions. Data
attained from these measurements are used to estimate the dynamic behaviour of
the analysed structure in terms of natural frequencies, damping properties, and vi-
brational shapes, and constitutes the procedure we know as Experimental Modal
Analysis.
Performing this analysis usually involves purpose built laboratory test facilities, and
the process can be expensive and ineпђГcient. In the labratory some boundary condi-
tions may be hard to reproduce which can inпђВuence the results negatively, providing
inaccurate estimates of physical properties. Additionally, it may be a challenge to
apply the relevant excitation in a test rig compared to operating conditions.
For larger structures, these factors can make experimental modal analysis very im-
practical, this is especially true in civil engineering, where bridges or highrise build-
ings are the structures of interest. As a result, output only analysis, or Operational
Modal Analysis was developed and dates back to the 1930s. SigniпђБcant advances
in the applied testing techniques were made in the early 1990s and the techniques
have been developed ever since.
While traditional EMA requires measureable excitation of the analysed structure,
OMA analyses the response to ambient excitation experienced by the structure in
operational conditions. Some assumptions are needed in order to perform this anal-
ysis, mainly it is assumed that the input does not contain speciпђБc information and
is therefore approximately white noise.
In recent years the use of OMA techniques has been adopted for analysis of struc-
tures commonly analysed with EMA, as OMA testing can be implemented relatively
easily compared to the traditional approach. However, mechanical systems often in-
volve rotating bodies and internal harmonic forces, which poses a challenge for the
assumptions behind OMA identiпђБcation techniques. The application of OMA tech-
niques is practical for many applications in the study of machine elements, and in
some cases in-situ testing can be performed with the additional possibility of struc-
tural health monitoring.
The class of time-variant systems as the one investigated in this study introduce
internal harmonic forces depending on e.g. the speed of rotation. Physical structures
exhibiting this behaviour include for example wind turbines, in which case OMA
testing is especially desireable due to the sheer size of the structure.
Structure and Aim of the Study
This study aims to investigate the application of experimental and operational modal
analysis theory for analysis of Linear Time Invariant (LTI) systems to a Linear Time

gular velocity by Fourier transforming the blade signals. The signals are lowpass
FFT пђБltered to isolate oscillations below 3 Hz as the maximum angular velocity
investigated is around 2.5 Hz.

Theoretical Foundation
The following sections describe the theoretical foundation for the analyses conducted
in this study. This includes mathematical modelling, general statistics concepts,
and applied techniques for both experimental and operational modal analysis. The
mathematical model describing the rotor-blade system developed by Christensen [2]
is used as a basis for the theoretical analysis in this study. During his work, Chris-
tensen estimated vibrational mode shapes of the rotating blades of the structure
by пђБnite element modelling, and while his results are used for this study, the пђБnite
element modelling is not covered here. The reader is referred to the PhD thesis by
Christensen[2] for further details on this subject.
For experimental modal analysis (EMA), well established theory and techniques
widely described in the litterature are applied. The mathematical foundation cov-
ered by Bucher & Ewins [19] and Irretier [17] is combined with the techniques and
methodology described by Santos [15], Inman [14], and Ewins [18] to estimate nat-
ural frequencies, damping ratios, and vibrational mode shapes.
The basis for the operational modal analysis is supplied by the theory and techniques
described by Brincker & Ventura [5].
Their OMA framework [12] is used as a
template for the software developed during this study, which is an adapted version
of the software supplied by Bricker & Ventura adapted to run Python 3.7. The
operational modal analysis conducted here focuses on time domain identiпђБcation of
natural frequencies, damping ratios and vibrational mode shapes.
4.1
Mathematical Modelling
The mathematical modelling of rotor-blade systems similar to the one investigated
here has previously been studied and published by several authors.
In their re-
spective theses, Saracho [6] and Christensen [2] developed multibody dynamic mod-
els for theoretical analysis of rotor-blade systems. The rotation introduces time-
varying parameters to the model, which means that standard solution methods are
no longer valid. To overcome this, the system matrices are Fourier expanded to
form a time-invariant problem, following the methodology by Xu & Gasch [13]. The
time-invariant problem is then analysed using the standard techniques as presented
e.g. by Zadeh & Desoer [28].
System Description
The rotor-blade system investigated here, exhibits vibration coupling between rotor
and blades. The lateral motion of the rotor is coupled to пђВexible motion of the
blades. The system is modelled as a rotating rigid disk with four пђВexible blades
attached. The blades are equally spaced on the disk and radially clamped. Each
blade is equipped with an additional mass at the tip end, to further emphasize
the inertia of the blades thus increasing the vibration coupling between rotor and
blades. Figure 4.1 shows the mechanical model of the rotor-blade system. The rigid
disk is mounted to a shaft which is assumed to be completely rigid. Shaft and disk

IdentiпђБcation Algorithms
Multiple OMA techniques for identiпђБcation of modal parameters exist divided into
three main categories depending on the method of data processing; time domain
techniques, frequency domain techniques, and joint time- and frequency domain
techniques. For this study, three time domain techniques, as described by Brincker
& Ventura [5], are implemented for modal parameter identiпђБcation during the in-
vestigation of the rotor-blade system. The three algorithms are:
(i) Auto Regression/Poly Reference (AR).
(ii) Ibrahim Time Domain (ITD).
(iii) Eigensystem Realization Algorithm IdentiпђБcation (ERA).
The Ibrahim Time Domain identiпђБcation technique was developed пђБrst, and intro-
duced by Ibrahim and Milkulcik [23]. Shortly after, the poly reference identiпђБcations
technique, developed by Vold et al. [24], was introduced. Juang and Pappa [25] later
presented the eigensystem realization algorithm. All three algorithms assume that a
free response function can be obtained from each measured time series. These three
techniques are all based on the assumption, that all information about the physi-
cal system, is contained by the correlation function matrix of the measured data.
This assumption holds if the measured system output is approximately Gaussian
distributed, which according to the central limit theorem is a valid assumption for
large sample sizes. By пђБtting a mathematical model to the correlation function of
the measured system output, modal parameters can be estimated.

Oversizing the Modal Model
Being able to distinguish physical modes from noise modes in the output from the
identiпђБcation algorithms gains relevance especially when additional noise modes are
introduced into the modal model. When applying OMA identiпђБcation algorithms,
the number of returned modes can be adjusted to allow for noise modes, which
can aid the accuracy of the model пђБt to experimental data. It is common in OMA
to introduce noise modes by oversizing the model.
For this study, the physical
system is modelled with six degrees of freedom, and OMA identiпђБcation will be
carried out for three model sizes; 6, 12, and 18 modes. For the AR identiпђБcation
algorithm, overzising is accomplished by altering the number of AR matrices, Ana,
used for modelling the correlation functions. With the ITD algorithm, the number
of block rows in the data matrix H determines the number of modes returned.
Lastly, the ERA model size is determined by the number of sigular values included
in the realisation of the system matrix. The number of modes estimated by the
OMA algorithms will be referred to as the model order or identiпђБcation order i.e.
model/identiпђБcation order n returns n estimated modes.

5.5
Limitations and Challenges
There are certain limitations to the applied methodology. When performing ex-
perimental modal analysis, input is normally applied at multiple locations on the
physical system. This means that multiple rows of the FRF matrix can be esti-
mated, and a better indication of the measurement quality can be established along
with the extraction of experimental mode shapes as described by Ewins [18], Bucher
& Ewins [19] and Irretier [17]. Measuring system response to multiple input loca-
tions and thereby estimating multiple columns of the FRF matrix, provides more
data for identiпђБcation of modal parameters from measured frequency responses and
also enables the estimation of left and right eigenvectors of the system matrix. The
current iteration of the experimental facilities used for this study does not support
measurement of input at more than one location, namely the horizontal input to the
rotor-hub, Fhx. This is not ideal, and the accuracy of the results from the analysis
remains uncertain. As a result of this current limitation, mode shapes are qualita-
tively estimated from the magnutide and phase of the estimated frequency response

Remarks on Test Results
Some remarks have to be made in relation to the experimental results presented in
this section.
In relation to the multibody dynamics based model presented in section 4.1, the
physical parameters for the test rig are not up to date. Some adjustments were
made to the model for this study, in order to match the analysis results for the sta-
tionary system. However, actual physical quantities have not been measured, and
as a result of this the model is presumably not as accurate as it could be.
Regarding the пђБndings from experimental modal analysis, the applied methodology
is not entirely accurate. The shifted excitation spectrum P(ѕЙвИТ(v+u+h)вД¶) accord-
ing to equation 4.12 is not properly taken into account as the frequency response
estimates obtained from measured data are based on the time-invariant formulation.

For time-variant systems, the shifted excitation spectrum should ideally be taken
into account. Concerning extraction of vibrational mode shapes, the methodology
described by Irretier [17] and Bucher & Ewins [19] including multiple response mea-
surements could be used to estimate left and right eigenvectors for the asymmetric
system matrix by regression, unlike the qualitative estimates obtained here. The
physically symmetric nature of the rotor-blade layout introduces double modes with
nearly identical natural frequencies, which makes general extraction of modal in-
formation diпђГcult. This is very clear for the stationary analysis of the rotor-blade
system, where multiple modes coincide in the frequency domain.
Operational modal analysis techniques have been utilised to estimate the modal pa-
rameters of the rotor-blade system. The assumptions linked to OMA identiпђБcation
as listed in section 4.3 are somewhat challenged by the rotor-blade structure. Each
of the listed assumptions are brieпђВy reviewed here.
Gaussian distributed input forces
Scalar multiples of the rotational speed are part of the theoretical frequency re-
sponse in equation 4.12 describing the system behavior in the frequency domain.
This implies that the system will experience excitation at those rotation related fre-
quencies, and the assumption of inputs with Gaussian probability density functions
is therefore not adhered to.
Linear time-invariance and stationary response
In section 4.1 the equations of motion for the rotor-blade system were presented and
classiпђБed as time-variant or time-periodic for constant angular velocities, ЋЩвД¶= 0.
This is in clear conпђВict with the assumption of time-invariance. A time-variant sys-
tem cannot produce a stationary response, if the system parameters are varying, so
is the response. Although it is true that the stationary response assumption is an
important premise for development of OMA theory, most of the theory may work
quite well under weakly non-stationary conditions [5].
Response ergodicity
The assumption about ergodicity is closely related to that of stationarity, since sta-
tionarity for a signal is deпђБned by time-invariant probability density function of the
underlying stochastic process. It has been established that the rotor-blade system
is time variant, hence system response is not stationary. However, this does not
necessarily guarantee the system to be ergodic or not and response ergodicity is not
straight forward to evaluate.
Gaussian distributed response
Following the central limit theorem, for a stationary data set, the responce is ex-
pected to be approximately Gaussian for large sample sizes [5]. However, due to
the time-variance of the rotor-blade system, this may not be the case. The response
distribution is investigated by plotting the Probability Density Function (PDF) es-
timate for the case uniform loading, for lowest and highest rotational speeds. Prob-
ability density function estimates are shown in пђБgure 6.57.

CONCLUSIONS AND FINAL REMARKS
7
Conclusions and Final Remarks
The modal analysis of a time-variant rotor-blade system has been treated in this
report. Two main parts make up this study; theoretical and experimental modal
analysis. The theoretical foundation for a multibody dymanics based model of the
considered structure has been presented, along with some theoretical aspects of the
accompanying experimental analysis. Theoretically obtained results have been com-
pared in detail to the experimental пђБndings.
For the comparison of analysis tools, a comprehensive experimental campaign has
been carried out on test facilities at the Technical University of Denmark.
The
response of the rotor-blade system has been investigated for two main load con-
ditions; foundation excitation only, and foundation excitation combined with indi-
vidual blade excitation. Moreover, blade excitation following two distributions has
been applied and response has been investigated, namely Gaussian and uniformly
distributed excitation.
Experimental modal analysis was utilised to analyse the modal parameters of the
rotor-blade system, and dispite some limitations, decent results were obtained in
comparison to theoretical predictions.
Output only analysis techniques commonly referred to as operational modal analysis
have also been applied to recorded data in order to analyse the structure. Three time
domain algorithms have been implemented and used in the data processing. The
outcomes from the Ibrahim Time Domain algorithm are presented and compared
to theoretical predictions as well as EMA пђБndings. Some inconsistency in OMA
identiпђБcation has been observed in the results obtained here, especially in frequency
bands where natural frequencies of multiple modes intersect. It is possible that a
more sophisticated algorithm for post processing of OMA output could remedy this
issue to some extend.
Overall good agreement between the applied methodologies has been observed even
if some main assumptions regarding EMA and OMA theory are violated by the
time-varying nature of the structure. The agreement in results is mainly attributed
to suпђГcient spacing between most of the natural frequencies of interest and rela-
tively low angular velocities during testing.
Consequently, while theoretically questionable, the output-only identiпђБcation tech-
niques developed for linear time-invariant systems can produce good results when
applied to a structure modelled by a time-periodic model.

FUTURE ASPECTS
8
Future Aspects
There are multiple interesting possibilities for expansion and improvement regarding
the subjects treated in this study. Some are listed below with brief descriptions.
1. Re-design of driving mechanism for the rotor on the test rig. Some challenges
were met during the experimental work concerning the driving mechanism for
the rotor. Angular velocities would пђВuctuate slightly as there is not regulator
for the DC motor power supply. Measurement of rotor angular position is also
high on the list of improvements.
2. Implementation of blade excitation force measurement in the rotating frame of
reference. Experimental modal analysis could be further improved if measure-
ments of blade excitation forces were included in the analysis. Hall sensors
could be mounted in order to measure magnetic excitation of rotor-blades.
Similarly, vertical hub/foundation excitation could be added relatively easily.
3. Updating the mathematical model to include rotor tilting modes, and measur-
ing the physical properties of the current iteration of the test rig in order to
improve the model predictions.
4. A comprehensive numerical simulation study to investigate the inпђВuence of
present harmonic forces when applying OMA identiпђБcation techniques.
5. Implementation of more advanced techniques for dealing with time-variant
system identiпђБcation, e.g. Multiblade Coordinate Transformation (Bir [29])
or Harmonic Power Spectrum (Allen et al. [7]), and subsequent investigation
of identiпђБcation results. Additionally, implementation of frequency domain
identiпђБcation algorithms as described in by Allen [30].
