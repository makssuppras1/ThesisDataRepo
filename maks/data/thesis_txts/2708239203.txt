Abstract
This thesis proposes the use of Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy) to exВӯ
tract the dynamical model of a soft robot from measurement data. In addition, a smoothed
superВӯtwisting sliding mode controller (SSTSMC) is proposed for closedВӯloop task space
control to facilitate trajectory tracking.
The ability of biological systems like elephant trunks and octopus tentacles to move in variВӯ
able environments and interact with delicate objects has inspired the design of soft robots.
Soft robots are made from soft and flexible materials and are named accordingly. The sucВӯ
cessful application of soft robots depends on developing dynamic controllers. However,
the nonlinear dynamics introduced by the soft materials have made soft robot modelling
and control an active research topic. This thesis reviews methods used to model and
control soft robots, particularly continuum robots.
The soft robot arm used in this thesis is a cableВӯactuated continuum robot. The method
called Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics is applied to extract the nonlinear dyВӯ
namics of the system from measurement data. A smoothed superВӯtwisting sliding mode
controller is implemented based on the system model. The inverse of a part of the sysВӯ
tem model is found using a method to invert a nonlinear equation analytically. The inverse
mapping is used to map the control signal to a robot input.
The dynamical model is validated by comparing the robot response and model response
to the same input series. The model represents the robot sufficiently well to be used to
design a modelВӯbased controller. The controller that is developed for the system is able to
facilitate trajectory tracking in the robot workspace. Three different trajectories are used
to demonstrate the accuracy of the robot trajectory tracking after the control is applied.

Introduction
Conventional rigid robot arms are made from materials like metals, ceramics and hard
plastics. It prevents them from deforming under heavy loads and helps facilitate fast,
repeatable and accurate movements [1]. Those qualities are well suited for industrial
use, but not in environments that are unpredictable or if safe humanВӯrobot interaction is
needed [2]. The use of robots is expanding to more fields than before. For example to
biomedical applications, like surgical robots and devices to aid with the rehabilitation of
patients [3]. These tasks require robots that can adapt to changes in their surroundings,
handle delicate objects and provide safe humanВӯrobot interactions.
The way animals and biological systems, like snakes, elephant trunks, or octopus arms
for example, interact with their surroundings has inspired the design of soft robots. Those
animals can move around obstacles with ease or grab and interact with delicate objects
without damaging them [4]. Soft robots are made from soft, flexible and stretchable maВӯ
terials. The soft and lightweight materials make them automatically safe to use around
humans. The materials are similar to biological tissue like tendons, ligaments, and musВӯ
cles [5]. Soft robots are ideal for use where low speed and low force are needed, and low
accuracy can be tolerated [6].
Despite the benefits that come from the soft and flexible materials, they also introduce nonВӯ
linear deformations and dynamics to the robot system that are hard to model accurately
using analytical methods [4]. There is no existing framework for the design, analysis, and
control of soft robots like there is for rigid robots and control of soft robots is an active
research topic within the robotics community [7]. The development of dynamic controllers
for soft robots is important if soft robots are to achieve the level of efficiency and agility in
movements of the biological systems that inspired their design [8].
The field of soft robots covers a number of different robot structures and actuators. The
one thing they all have in common is that they are made from soft materials. The robot
used in this thesis is a soft robot with a continuum structure resembling an elephant trunk,
and is actuated by cables. Most continuum robots have a flexible, continuously curving,
central backbone that can bend and often extend and contract. In addition, the backbone
is compliant, meaning that the shape change in response to any external forces along the
robot arm. The natural state of a continuum robot is typically a straight line, and then the
robotвҖҷs actuators bend it into different shapes [9].
The objective of this thesis is to model the nonlinear dynamics of a soft robot and design
a closedВӯloop task space controller to facilitate accurate trajectory tracking.
1.1
Scope of Thesis
The following goals define the scope of the thesis:
вҖў Perform a literature study focusing on modelВӯbased and modelВӯfree control of soft

вҖў Obtain and validate a sufficiently accurate model for the soft robot that can be used

вҖў Design a closedВӯloop control strategy to facilitate accurate robot positioning.

2
Literature Review
This literature review is focused on methods used to model and control soft robots, particВӯ
ularly continuum robots similar to the one used in this thesis. It also introduces a method
to discover dynamical models from system data.
Modelling and control of soft robots can take place in different coordinate spaces: the
actuator space, configuration space, or task space. Actuator space has variables deВӯ
scribing the actuator states, for example, length of cables. Configuration space describes
the shape of the robot arm in variables that represent the backbone length, curvature, and
orientation of the arm. Task space is the coordinate space surrounding the robot and is
typically used to represent the robotвҖҷs endВӯeffector position in Cartesian coordinates [10].
2.1
Soft Robot Control
Soft robot controllers can be classified by the modelling approach used. ModelВӯbased
controllers are derived from analytical models of the system. ModelВӯfree controllers use
machine learning or empirical methods to control the system. Finally, hybrid controllers
use a combination of modelВӯbased and modelВӯfree approaches [7].
2.1.1
ModelВӯBased Control
Kinematic Models
Kinematic, or static, models have been commonly used to model soft robots. They provide
a static mapping from actuator states to the position of the robot endВӯeffector in task space.
However, they require some steadyВӯstate assumptions and approximations, and the most
common one is Constant Curvature (CC) approximation. The CC approximation ignores
any effects of gravitational forces and payload weights that affect the shape of the robot.
According to [11], it is easier to develop controllers based on kinematic models of conВӯ
tinuum robots than dynamic models. However, the downside of kinematic controllers is
that they lack accuracy and efficiency and ignore the natural dynamics of soft robots due
to their steadyВӯstate assumptions. As a result, kinematic controllers are more limited in
reachability, efficiency and speed than controllers based on dynamic models.
Dynamic Models
According to [7], one of the most challenging fields in the control of soft robots is the
development of controllers that consider the full dynamics of the system.
A secondВӯorder dynamical model was derived in [10] using EulerВӯLagrange formalism.
The model was used for simulations, and the inverse dynamics were calculated and used
for modelВӯbased openВӯloop controller design for path planning. However, this method
required heavy computations that were too slow to be used as a part of a closedВӯloop
controller of a real robot.
A dynamic secondВӯorder model in configuration space includes Coriolis, centrifugal and
damping terms. A model reduction was suggested in [11] where the inertia term was
ignored, considering high damping values and low inertial properties of soft robots. The
approximation was shown to be a powerful method to develop closedВӯloop task space
dynamic controllers for soft robots.
In summary, static controllers, based on kinematic models, are easy to develop but sacВӯ
rifice accuracy and efficiency and ignore the natural dynamics of the robots. Controllers
based on secondВӯorder dynamical models are computationally heavy and are therefore

not well suited for closedВӯloop task space control. A firstВӯorder dynamical model can be
found with model reduction, but ignoring some dynamics in the system inevitably results
in some loss of accuracy.
2.1.2
ModelВӯFree Control
Modelling soft robots using physics and mathematical methods has proven difficult due to
their nonlinearities, hysteresis, high degrees of freedom, among others. Instead, various
machine learning methods have been used to model, calibrate, and control soft robots
[12].
A neural networks based approach was used in [13] for forward and inverse kinematic
modelling of a continuum robot. The inverse kinematic model provided a mapping beВӯ
tween the endВӯeffector position and the corresponding input voltages. The models could
be used for kinematic control.
A machine learning based approach was used to develop dynamic models for a soft robot
manipulator in [8]. The forward dynamics model of a soft robot manipulator was learned.
Trajectory optimisation was performed on the learned dynamic model, and an openВӯloop
predictive task space controller was developed. The controller was tested experimentally
on a pneumatically actuated soft robot. This method derived a mapping directly from
the input variables in actuator space to task space, skipping the intermediate mapping to
configuration space that most analytical modelling methods used.
A dataВӯdriven method was used in [14] to discover a forward and inverse dynamic model
of a soft robot. The dataВӯdriven method captured nonlinearities that analytical methods
ignore, like friction and backlash. An openВӯloop controller was implemented in simulation
and on an experimental setup.
A benefit of modelВӯfree control is that the mappings can be made directly from actuation
space to task space. It eliminates the need for complex models and reduces the numВӯ
ber of variables and operating spaces needed. Furthermore, modelВӯfree methods are
independent of the different manipulator shapes and actuation methods that continuum
manipulators can have [7]. They have, however, some practical limitations due to training
time and the need for extensive training datasets. Moreover, analysing the stability of
modelВӯfree controllers is not always trivial or even possible [7].
2.1.3
ModelВӯBased and ModelВӯFree Hybrid Control
A hybrid of modelВӯbased and modelВӯfree modelling methods was used in [15]. The task
of finding the inverse kinematics of a robot was split into two parts. From task space
to configuration space and from configuration space to actuation space. A modelВӯbased
CC approximation was used to get the mapping from task space to configuration space.
Then a modelВӯfree method using a neural network was used to estimate the mapping from
actuator space to configuration space.
A feedВӯforward control based on a hybrid of an analytical model and a learned error model
was introduced in [16]. The addition of a learned error model improved the modelling accuВӯ
racy significantly. This hybrid model also showed advantages compared to an exclusively
modelВӯfree approach where the entire model is learned.
2.2
Dynamical System Models Discovered From Data
A method called Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy) has been used sucВӯ
cessfully to extract nonlinear dynamical models of complex systems from measurement
data. SINDy uses sparse regression to avoid overfitting and needs less training data than

the models attained using neural networks (blackВӯbox models) and can be used to extrapВӯ

2.3
Summary
Purely modelВӯbased methods that have been developed for soft robots have not been able
to capture all the dynamics of the robots. There are still some properties of the kinematics
and dynamics that are difficult to model. For example, the soft materialsвҖҷ nonlinearities,
friction, and hysteresis. In addition, the more precise the models are, the heavier the
calculations get.
ModelВӯfree methods do not ignore any dynamics but are prone to overfitting and need
large amounts of good quality training data and training time [16]. Moreover, it is not
always possible to analyse the convergence of modelВӯfree controllers.
A dataВӯdriven method, SINDy has been used successfully to extract nonlinear dynamical
models of complex systems. However, no literature was found during the literature review
where this method was used to model soft continuum robots. Based on this reasoning, it
was decided to use the SINDy method in this thesis work to implement nonlinear modelВӯ
based control without ignoring any nonlinear dynamics. The goal is to use the model as
a base for closedВӯloop control in task space.

Methodology
This chapter introduces the methods used to model the soft robot system and develop
nonlinear position control.
3.1
Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy)
3.1.1
Motivation For Using SINDy
Soft continuum robots have complex nonlinear dynamics, and it has proven challengВӯ
ing to derive their dynamical models through analytical methods. The dynamical models
developed in related works were computationally heavy, limiting closedВӯloop control in
task space. ModelВӯfree methods require large amounts of data and training time, and the
models are not easily interpretable.
The possibility of combining the strengths of modelВӯbased and modelВӯfree methods inВӯ
spired the use of a method called SINDy. SINDy utilizes the fact that nonlinear dynamical
system equations are generally a combination of only a few nonlinear terms. It takes meaВӯ
surement data from a system and uses sparse regression to extract nonlinear equations
describing the data. Deriving the dynamical model of a system using SINDy can give a
model that describes the data well, while the sparsity methods help avoid overfitting to the
data. Models found using SINDy are more interpretable than models found using neural
networks and can be used for modelВӯbased control.

3.2
Sliding Mode Control
The nonlinear system model of the soft robot derived using SINDy captured the majority
of the system dynamics, but there were still some bounded modelling errors and disturВӯ
bances. It was decided to implement a Sliding Mode Controller (SMC) for the system
since those are known to be robust to such errors. The following introduction to SMC is
based on Chapter 7 Sliding Control from [20].
SMC is a control method that drives the system states towards a specific manifold in the

Chattering Attenuation
The theory behind SMC relies on highВӯfrequency switching of the control signal ksgn(s).
HighВӯfrequency switching works well for some systems, like purely electronic systems.
However, high frequency switching in the input signals of a mechanical system can have
an undesirable outcome called chattering. Chattering is unwanted highВӯfrequency oscilВӯ
lations that appear in the control signal or even the systemвҖҷs output. It will damage the
equipment and cause unwanted oscillations in the output. Therefore some methods for
chattering attenuation were explored.
Saturation
One known method to reduce chattering is to approximate the discontinuous switching
term with a continuous saturation function. A thin boundary layer ОҰ defines a neighbourВӯ
hood around the sliding manifold. The switching control is made less aggressive inside
the boundary, which smoothes the chattering effect that can happen when the system
is close to S(t). Instead of driving the system to the sliding manifold, it is driven to the
boundary layer [20]. However, using a boundary layer can reduce tracking precision since
the convergence to the sliding manifold is not guaranteed to happen in finite time.

6
Nonlinear Position Control
The nonlinear model derived in Chapter 5 captures the majority of the system dynamics,
but there are still some bounded modelling errors and disturbances present. The aim of
this chapter is to design a nonlinear position control method that is robust to modelling
errors and disturbances.
The control objective is position control of the robot to make it follow a trajectory in task
space. The controller needs to minimize the tracking error e(t) = ПҮ(t) вҲ’ПҮd(t) between
the measured robot position ПҮ(t) and the desired trajectory ПҮd(t).
6.1
Control Architecture
Figure 6.1: The control architecture
The control architecture consists of two major parts. The first part is a controller that takes
ПҮ and ПҮd and produces a control signal v вҲҲR3. The control signal represents the desired
value for h(ПҮ, u) in Equation 5.4. The control problem is represented by the following
equation
ЛҷПҮ = f(ПҮ) + v
(6.1)
To simplify the control design, it was designed as three decoupled control loops, one for
each state x, y, and z. This design decision approximates the system as a decoupled
MIMO system. Any effects of coupling in the system are regarded as a bounded disturВӯ
bance.
The second part of the control architecture is a mapping from v to u that determines
which u values should be sent to the robot to make h(ПҮ, u) вҶ’v. Ideally, a simple inverse
mapping u = hвҲ’1(v) would be derived analytically to solve this problem. However, since
h is a complex nonlinear multivariate function it is not trivial to invert. Instead, an update
law is used to asymptotically invert the nonlinear equation and create an estimate, ЛҶu, of
the input values that make h(ПҮ, ЛҶu) вҶ’v.
6.2
Control Design
A nonlinear modelВӯbased controller was implemented for the system. Sliding control was
chosen out of the other known nonlinear control methods because of its known robustВӯ
ness to model uncertainties like unmodeled dynamics and unknown disturbances. Sliding
control methods are based on the nominal model of a system and also the bounds of the
uncertainties [20].
The first step in the sliding control design is to define a timeВӯvarying manifold S(t), called
the sliding manifold
S(t) = {ПҮ вҲҲR3|s(ПҮ, t) = 0}
(6.2)

s(ПҮ, t) = ПҮ вҲ’ПҮd
(6.3)
Next step is to calculate the expression for the equivalent control, which is the part of

nominal system.

Discussion
According to the literature review, it is challenging to achieve closedВӯloop control in task
space based on a dynamic model of the system. The modelВӯbased methods in the literaВӯ
ture review required some approximations and ignored parts of the nonlinear dynamics in
the system. The controllers based on the most precise models needed to be openВӯloop
due to heavy calculations caused by the complexity of the models.
The objective of this thesis was to model the nonlinear dynamics of a soft robot and design
a closedВӯloop task space controller to facilitate accurate trajectory tracking. The results
from Chapter 7 indicated that the model achieved using SINDy represented the system
dynamics well enough to be used for modelВӯbased control. A closedВӯloop control in task
space was achieved using SSTSMC. Results showed that it helped the robot endВӯeffector
accurately track trajectories in the workspace.
The problem caused by h not being invertible through analytical methods was solved by
casting the problem as parameter estimation. Results indicated that the input estimator
was able to invert the complex nonlinear function h, and help map the control signals v to
robot inputs u. The projection part of the input estimator kept the input values within the
allowed range of values. The value of h(ПҮ, ЛҶu) converged to v unless the ЛҶu values were
approaching the boundaries of the allowed input values. Then the projection worked to
constrain the values within the boundary. That resulted in the h values diverging from the
v values at some points.
The SSTSMC was chosen for its finiteВӯtime convergence and robustness to bounded
disturbances. It was not possible to use the large control gains to guarantee a finiteВӯtime
convergence on the robot system. The high gains worked in simulations but did not suit
the robot system in the experimentation. However, the results showed that despite the
smaller gains, the robot could follow the three test case trajectories. The MAE and RMSE
values were just around 1mm for each axis. The big control gains were found through a
conservative method. The lower gains worked well for the test cases, which had slowВӯ
changing trajectories.
The control frequency is a limiting factor in the control system. SlidingВӯmode control is,
in theory, a method that has infinitely fast switching terms. Therefore it benefits from the
fastest frequency possible. A faster control frequency would allow for increased control
gains, making the tracking error decrease faster and the system robust to more significant
modelling errors or disturbances. In addition, it would allow the system to track fasterВӯ
changing trajectories.
The similarities between the input signals to the robot from the SSTSMC and the PI conВӯ
troller were unexpected. The assumption beforehand was that the SSTSMC input would
have significantly more chattering effects than the PI control and much lower tracking erВӯ
rors. Higher control gains for the SSTSMC and higher estimator gains would most likely
increase this difference.

Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to model the nonlinear dynamics of a soft robot and deВӯ
sign a closedВӯloop task space controller based on the model. The control objective was
position control, enabling the robot endВӯeffector to track different trajectories in the robotвҖҷs
workspace.
The literature review focused on modelВӯbased and modelВӯfree control of soft robots, parВӯ
ticularly those with continuum structures. The literature suggested that control based on
analytically derived models required approximations, ignoring parts of the nonlinear dyВӯ
namics of the system. Purely modelВӯfree methods were prone to overfitting and required
vast amounts of data and training time. A method SINDy was introduced, which had been
used to extract nonlinear dynamical models from other systems using only measurement
data. The SINDy method had, as far as could be found, not been used before to model
the dynamics of a soft continuum robot.
The robot was given a series of randomized inputs to generate measurement data that
spanned the whole workspace. The SINDy method was used to extract nonlinear dynamВӯ
ical equations from the measurement data. The method combined the qualities of modelВӯ
based and modelВӯfree approaches. It provided a dynamical model of the system without
any approximations or deliberately leaving any nonlinear dynamics out of the equations.
It also provided a mapping directly from the actuation space to task space without using
configuration space variables. The sparse regression performed helped avoid overfitting
and needed less data than typical learningВӯbased methods that use neural networks. The
model found had some bounded modelling errors but represented the system sufficiently
well to be used for modelВӯbased control.
It was decided to implement a nonlinear closedВӯloop control strategy. The control archiВӯ
tecture consisted of a controller that produced a control signal and a mapping that mapped
the resulting signal to robot inputs. The controller needed to be robust to modelling errors
and disturbances, so it was decided to use a sliding control method. However, those methВӯ
ods can be prone to chattering, which would cause strain the robot mechanics. Therefore,
the search was made for known methods of chattering reduction, resulting in the choice of
SSTSMC as the control method to be implemented on the robot. The mapping of control
signals to suitable robot inputs was done by inverting a part of the nonlinear dynamical
system. The equation was not analytically invertible, so an estimator was used to invert
the equation asymptotically, casting the problem as a parameter estimation problem.
The closedВӯloop system was evaluated through simulations and experiments on the soft
robot system. Results from simulations showed that the chosen control method SSTSMC
reduced chattering significantly compared to the other control methods considered.
Results from experimentations on the robot showed that the input estimator was able to
map control signals to robot inputs for most points in the workspace. However, some
cases where the mappings deviated from the correct values were observed from plots.
The results from applying the control on the robot indicated that the thesis objectives were
met. The robot was able to track a circular, triangular, and infinity shaped trajectory in the
robot workspace with a mean absolute error and root mean squared error of around 1mm
in each coordinate direction of the task space.

9.1
Future Work
The control frequency was around 40Hz and was a limiting factor in the control system. It
led to a size limit on the control gains and estimator gains. A possible task for future work
would be to optimize the control program to make each iteration faster and increase the
control frequency. That would allow for larger control gains and tracking of faster changing
trajectories.
Another interesting idea for future work could be to add a neural network based comВӯ
pensation control as a feedВӯforward term to the existing closedВӯloop system. A neural
network could be trained to learn the tracking error dynamics of the existing closedВӯloop
system. The neural network would provide a feedforward control term to compensate for
the trajectory tracking errors, while the feedback loop would ensure the stability of the
system.
