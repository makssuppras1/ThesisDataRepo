Preface 
The master thesis is submitted in support of candidature for the degree of Master of Science in 
Engineering. Respectively on the master study program Sustainable Energy at the Technical 
University of Denmark (DTU). This project is undertook by Department of Civil Engineering and 
accomplished in cooperation with District Heating Company of Albertslund (DHCOA). 
 
The thesis is carried out as a proposition to future supply systems to the ongoing development 
project on transition to Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) in the municipality of 
Albertslund. It consists of two case studies investigating energy savings through possible 
refurbishments in single-family houses and the possibility to comply with LTDH in Albertslund 
West. The proposition is substantiated with background study, methods, results, discussion, 
conclusion and recommendations for further work.

Abstract  
Anthropogenic climate change and rapid depletion of fossil fuels have led to reinvigorated 
interest in the transformation of the energy systems. The Danish Government has issued energy 
policy milestones in order to achieve the subsidiary goal of implementing fossil free heating 
system by 2035.  
 
The thesis presents case studies of two single-family houses built in 1967 and 1971 in 
Albertslund municipality. The investigation of the space heating system, i.e. radiator 
performance of Flintager house and air handling unit in Kongsager house was conducted in co-
operation with District Heating Company of Albertslund. The aim of the studies was to identify 
which type of refurbishment measures were required to reduce the actual energy consumption 
in these houses in order to be connected to low-temperature district heating. Mainly, it was 
done through field research and IDA-ICE a numerical simulation program chosen for the energy 
optimization.  
 
With the known energy demand the houses were modeled in IDA-ICE and simulated to expose 
the contributions of the individual refurbishment, i.e. light and extensive, to the energy savings. 
According to the simulations an energy reduction of approximately 41% and 57% were 
possible achievements through extensive refurbishments respectively for Flintager and 
Kongsager houses. The reduction requires replacement of windows, re-insulation of walls and 
roof according to BR10 and BR15. In Flintager house hydraulic radiator formulation was used 
to obtain the required logarithmic mean temperature difference (LMTD) which was then 
graphed to identify the required radiator performance to fulfill the desired thermal comfort. 
Concurrently, the supply and return temperature were optimized in compliance with LMTD 
and the result was assessed with possibility to comply with LTDH. Additionally, user behavior 
impact on energy savings was accounted for in Kongsager house due to incongruence between 
the design and actual energy consumption.  
 
The results demonstrate it is possible to connect Flintager house to LTDH after the extensive 
refurbishment. However, the heat load duration curve exposed a higher heat load than expected 
of the living room. Kongsager house indicated that only after changing the heat coil in the air 
handling unit it will be profitable when connecting to LTDH. Bearing the user behavior of the 
inhabitant of Kongsager house in mind, i.e. ventilation through open windows for 15 minutes 
each day while the AHU is running, the energy consumption increased with approximately 72%. 
The observed incongruence of energy demand in Kongsager house was possibly an outcome of 
the user behavior. 
 
   
Keywords: Transition to low temperature district heating, energy optimization, refurbishment 
suggestions, user behavior influence

Resumé  
Menneskeskabte klimaændringer og det hastige udtømning af fossile brændsler har ført til 
styrket interesse i omstillingen til grøn energi. Regeringen har således et mål om 100% 
vedvarende energi i Danmark i 2050. Sektormål for el- og varmeforsyningen er planlagt til 
2035, hvor det tilstræbes at have 𝐶𝑂2 neutralt varmesystem i Danmark i 2035. 
Dette speciale præsenterer casestudier af to parcelhuse, bygget i henholdsvis 1967 og 1971 i 
Albertslund kommune. Analysen af husenes varmesystem dvs. radiatorernes ydeevne i 
Flintager hus og Hess Varme anlæg (varmeblæser) i Kongsager hus, blev udført i samarbejde 
med Albertslund Fjernvarmeværk. Formålet med analysen var at identificere, de nødvendige 
renoveringstiltag for at nedbringe det aktuelle energiforbrug for husene, dermed muligheden 
for at kobles til lavtemperatur fjernvarme. Energioptimeringen bygger på flere 
sammenhængende analyser, bl.a. besøgsundersøgelser og undersøgelser udført primært 
gennem et numerisk simulerings program IDA-ICE. 
Energibesparelsesbidragene ved henholdsvis lette eller omfattende renoveringer af de to huse 
blev afdækket vha. modellering og simulering med kendt energiforbrug i IDA-ICE. Ifølge 
simuleringerne har husene henholdsvis mulighed for at opnå en energi reducering på ca. 41% 
(Flintager huset) og 57% (Kongsager huset) ved omfattende renovering. Reduktionen kræver 
udskiftning af vinduer, efterisolering af vægge og loft i henhold til BR10 og BR15. I Flintager 
huset med centralvarmesystem blev en hydraulisk radiator formulering anvendt til at finde den 
ønskede logaritmiske gennemsnits temperaturforskel (LMTD). Ved hjælp af grafisk afbildning 
fandtes den korrekte radiator ydeevne til at opfylde den ønskede termiske indendørs komfort. 
Samtidig blev indløbs- og returløbstemperaturen optimeret i overensstemmelse med LMTD og 
sammenholdt med muligheden for at koble til lav temperature fjernvarme. Afslutningsvis, blev 
brugeradfærdens indvirkning på energibesparelser analyseret i Kongsager huset på grund af en 
høj afvigelse i energiforbruget mellem det designede og det faktiske energiforbrug. 
Resultaterne demonstrerer, at det er muligt at tilslutte Flintager huset til LTDH efter 
omfattende renovering. Imidlertid viser kurven over varmeforbruget en væsentlig højere værdi 
i stuen end forventet. Kongsager husets resultater for varmeanlægget indikerede, at tilslutning 
til lavtemperature fjernvarme kun er rentabel, såfremt varmefladen i varmeblæseren blev 
udskiftet. Imidlertid, kan fundet ikke helt forklare det høje energiforbrug i Kongsager huset. Ved 
analyse af brugeradfærden fandtes, at udluftning af huset gennem åbne vinduer i 15minutter 
hver dag, mens varmeanlægget kører, forårsagede et øget energiforbrug på ca. 72%. Således 
har brugeradfærden muligvis en vigtig indflydelse på energioptimeringen.

1 Background and motivation 
In recent years the focus of anthropogenic climate change and the rapid depletion of fossil fuels 
have led to a renewed interest in the transformation of the energy systems. The Climate 
Commission's report of September 2010 documented how the energy system in Denmark can 
be switched to renewable energy sources (RES) by 2050. This is to be done without significant 
additional costs on society [25]. The Danish Government has therefor set a long-term objective 
of being fossil-free by 2050. The Danish Energy Agency has issued several energy policy 
milestones which were published in 2008. One of the significant milestones is RES covering 
electricity and heat supply to buildings in 2035 [39].  In Denmark 64% of the heat demand is 
supplied by district heating (DH) which is the greatest energy supplier in the heating sector [18]. To 
become a fossil-free building sector it is necessary to reduce the energy consumption by 
carrying out energy renovations, increase energy efficiency and convert heat supply into RES 
[39]. 
Already today, DH systems have an important role in collecting heat from various sources - 
including combined heat and power (CHP) and plants as well as industrial sites - and in 
distributing it to end users. In the future, this role may be strengthened with more heat 
suppliers. That will include new elements such as industrial surplus heat, heat pumps based on 
sewage water, groundwater, seawater, geothermal energy, solar heating or even privately-
owned heat pumps and other resources [14]. In order to achieve the 2035 energy policy 
milestone the transition from 3rd generation to 4th generation DH of the heating sector is of great 
importance. Fourth generation DH, also known as LTDH, is able to further enhance the reduction 
of fossil usage in the Danish DH market [16]. 
Traditional 3rd generation DH systems operate with a supply temperature between 80℃< 𝑇𝑠𝑢𝑝𝑝𝑙𝑦<
100℃. Applying LTDH, that is 4th generation DH, with a supply temperature of 55℃ and a return 
temperature of 25℃, it will give us an opportunity to exploit the low-temperature renewable energy 
heat sources, i.e. geothermal heat, solar heat, etc. With lower operational temperatures in the DH net, 
the heat losses from the distribution pipes will decrease. These changes will increase the efficiency 
of DH systems. On the other hand, when reducing the DH supply temperature it might cause 
discomfort for the occupants [16].  
Existing buildings which are supplied with 3rd generation DH are a large share of the building 
stock in Denmark [18]. The question is whether such buildings can cope with LTDH and if not, 
what renovation measures needs to be carried out or how should the DH network be operated? 
With regards to further development of DH, the LTDH project in Lystrup should be mentioned 
here [8][9]. Although LTDH ensures the most cost-effective exploitation of energy, the 
conversion from high to low temperature DH will require continuous reductions in the 
temperature levels in DH systems. That is including better cooling of the DH water at end-users 
and reduced line losses in the DH networks. Yet, LTDH is still the optimal concept for integrating 
100% RES of heat at the moment [25]. 
November 2015, Albertslund municipality published their vision and strategy plan of the 
suburb. One of the main goals is to have 𝐶𝑂2 neutral electricity and heat supply by 2025. This 
==PAGE:10==
Page 10 of 60 
 
will lead them to be one of the leading sustainable suburbs in Denmark. The vision is believed 
to be achieved through energy savings and utilization of RES [1]. By implementing LTDH in 
existing and as well as in new buildings in Albertslund it is possible to reduce the energy 
consumption. However, along with the conversion it is important to ensure an acceptable 
thermal comfort temperature at the end-user throughout the year [16]. 
A research [4] by Marek Brand and Svend Svendsen shows that a small refurbishment measure 
in a typical single-family house from 1970s allows reduction in supply temperature. For 
instance by replacing the windows a maximum reduction of the DH supply temperature from 
78℃ to 67℃ was gained and 98 % of the year the temperature was below 60 degrees. The 
research provides a good approach to investigate area 13 in Albertslund, which is an area with 
typical single-family houses, built in the period 1965-1972. With such approach it might be 
possible to recommend small renovation proposals to allow the transition of the DH system 
from HTDH to LTDH. 
Finally, it is appropriate to mention that to become independent of fossil fuels on time, the 
Danish Government support a lot of research activity via Energy Technology Development and 
Demonstration Program (EUDP). It is possible to see the complete list of the projects on their 
website [15]. Furthermore, at the strategic Research Centre for Zero Energy Buildings and 
Bolius several on-going energy renovation projects are also to be found [38][19]. However, with 
short timeline to 2035 urgent actions are needed to actualize objectives of fossil-fuel free 
Denmark [39].

2 HTDH vs. LTDH  
 Heat supply in Denmark 
64% of the heating in private Danish houses is provided by DH in Denmark [18]. The heating is 
not only space heating (SH) but also hot water tapping. The Danish Energy Agency states that 
the production of DH in Denmark amounted to 134 Petajoules (PJ) in 2013. 72.8% of all DH was 
produced in cogeneration with electricity (CHP), thus saving around 30% of fuel compared with 
separate generation of heat and power[10].  
 
 Renewable Energy Sources (RES) 
RES are naturally replenished and mostly used 
RES to the DH systems are straw, wood chips, 
wood pellets, and combustible part of business 
and household waste which may no longer be 
deposited. Biomass as fuel is transitional, as the 
resource simply is not big enough for the future. 
Solar is becoming increasingly common in DH 
systems throughout the country. Concurrently, 
biogas has been predicted as a great resource in 
the future along with geothermal energy. 
However, geothermal energy heat from the 
earth’s interior, runs in a closed circuit and has 
to be brought up from underground to be 
exploited in DH.  Electricity production from e.g. 
wind turbines can be converted into DH. It is 
done with electric boilers or via heat pumps 
which are able to use the heat of for example 
sewage. Without DH the surplus heat from 
industry and waste would be lost. [25]. 
The deputy director of the industry association Danish DH, Kim Behnke states that 𝐶𝑂2 
reduction with 40% by 2020, an energy milestone policy set by The Danish Energy Agency, 
seems to be reached before time. The statement is based on the fact that the conversion to 
biomass at decentralized plants and the growth of solar DH system are going faster than 
expected [3].

 Introducing LTDH 
LTDH is a realistic goal and will provide energy 
savings and reduce dependence on fossil-fuels 
[10]. According to the national codes and 
principles the hydronic heating system in 
buildings, designed with specific supply and 
return temperature, varies from country to 
country [32]. Figure 2 is a simplification of the 
evolution of DH through time.  
 
Denmark is ahead in implementing the 4th 
generation DH systems.  Over time, Danish DH 
system has achieved better efficient conditions, 
for instance through building renovations, better 
pipe insulations, better control equipment and 
lower supply temperature[32].  
 
Mostly DH systems are built as indirect systems, which means, when using a heat exchanger 
the internal hydronic heating system is hydraulically separated from the DH system, see Figure 
3. Suppose, the hot water tank is replaced with a heat exchanger, it would benefit continuous 
flow of hot water (when needed), require small spacing and will be able to secure a lower return 
temperature. However, in some cases the DH system is directly connected to the house, see 
Figure 4. As a result of the required supply water is directly delivered without any heat 
exchanger or tanks. The concept is often known as used in rather flat cities with low static 
pressures of the DH system [32].

LTDH system should be able to operate 50 −55℃ in supply temperature and 25 −30℃ in 
return temperatures and yet meet the thermal indoor comfort of the consumer. Theoretically, 
these features make it possible to implement LTDH both in existing buildings as well as new 
built law energy buildings. According to existing literature in the field i.e. [7], [28] some of the 
main advantages of LTDH are listed below: 
 Ensuring efficiency of energy supply and better energy performance 
 DH supply losses can be reduced by reducing network temperature 
 Increased utilization efficiency of Low temperature renewable energy resources 
 More use of solar thermal collectors and geothermal plants 
 Increases the possibility for flue gas condensation 
 Reduces pipeline thermal stress  
The outcome of implementing LTDH in Danish DH systems is higher energy efficiency and 
better use of RES. It means not only attaining a political subsidiary goal issued by the Danish 
Energy Agency, but also Denmark becoming more sustainable in the matter of DH supply 
systems [10]. 
 
 Applying LTDH  
By reducing heat loss in traditional DH system it enables supply to areas with low heating 
demand density. The process for applying LTDH requires knowing the heat demand at 
consumer nodes with respect to simultaneity factors and by choosing the network pipes in a 
hydraulic optimization. Both in economically and environmentally LTDH makes more sense for 
the whole system. LTDH concept is commonly applied in four different ways by (see also Figure 
5) [13]:  
1. Connecting a new development area to an existing DH system 
2. Establishing a small-scale DH system in a new development area 
3. Connecting an existing area, e.g. replacing gas boilers with DH 
4. Renovation of existing DH systems, e.g. as general strategy to reduce supply 
temperature.

existing buildings the time horizon would be longer compared with newer buildings. Further,

3 Albertslund District Heating 
 Transition to LTDH 
A number of Danish heating companies have established projects that facilitate the transition 
from HTDH to LTDH. The main objective of the case studies was to reduce the supply and return 
temperature in the DH system. Although each building type and network system is different 
and require customized solution. These trials are done in order to develop plans for widespread 
reduction in temperature over time. The outcome of the projects shows significant reduction of 
the temperature of the traditional DH network [10].  
Albertslund is a suburban town in Eastern Denmark, with approximately 28.000 inhabitants. 
Over 60% of the 23 square kilometers is covered by forest and green area. In view of 
Albertslund municipality vision and strategy plan, 2,000 town houses are to be completely 
renovated and converted to LTDH in cooperation with DHCOA. It is an investment yielding the 
opportunity of becoming one of the leading sustainable suburbs in Denmark [1].  
DHCOA presents an overview of Albertslund municipality (see Figure 6) by outlining 23 areas 
based on industries, townhouses, apartments and single-family houses. The overview has the 
purpose to highlight areas of energy reduction either already implemented to LTDH or 
scheduled for in 2025.

 The district Heating Company of Albertslund 
The oil based heating plant in Albertslund was established in 1963 and connected to an 
incinerator in 1967. The timing was perfect due to the oil crisis in the 1970’s. In 1985 it was 
operated with a coal mill plant. It was beneficial regarding to the price differences in oil and 
coal. In 1982, Albertslund and nine other municipalities in Vestegn signed a cooperation 
agreement with Vestegnens Kraftvarmeselskab I/S (VEKS). In 1987 Avedøre power plant, 
which is run by VEKS, for the first time delivered heat through a heat exchanger to Albertslund 
municipality [2]. VEKS was established in 1984 and today it covers the West part of Greater 
Copenhagen. By utilizing surplus from CHP, industries and waste incineration, VEKS is able to 
reduce 𝐶𝑂2-, sulfur-, nitrogen oxides-emissions and produce heat economically [36]. VEKS 
covers the heat supply to approximately 27.000 consumers in Albertslund with netto heat sale 
of 240.000 MWh. According to data from DHCOA the year 2015 had 2461 degree days and 3112 
normal days. VEKS supplies DHCOA with 90℃ meanwhile the return temperature from DHCOA 
is averagely 55℃ [Data from DHCOA].  
Today the Avedøre heat plant consists of two blocks, Avedøre 1 and Avedøre 2 which produce 
825 MW in total. Avedøre 1 from 1990 uses coal as primary fuel and can be assisted with oil as 
well. Avedøre 2 from 2001 is a multi-fuel plant utilizing natural gas, biomass, straw, wood chips 
and wood pellets [37]. Despite the fact that Albertslund municipality settled with Avedøre, 
Albertslund heating plant still remains important. This is because the Albertslund municipality 
has an obligation to secure heat supply in times of high heat demand. Thus Albertslund heating 
plant functions not only as peak load central but also remains as general reserve. Although, the 
peak load was measured to be just 100hours in 2015, which underline that there is still a need 
to be met [2].

and fee arrangement. For the consumer it means getting a bonus when returning a lower 
temperature and fee when using higher than designed. It is expected to regulate inconvenient 
consumer behavior, thus also improving the environmental friendly approach.

The building energy simulation software IDA-ICE 4.7 was used to make the numerical 
simulations to determine the energy consumption before and after the refurbishments. IDA-
ICE is known as an Indoor Climate Energy analysis program. The program helps to determine 
the energy consumption in each room of the building or in total for the whole building. IDA-ICE 
makes it possible to simulate the building with different levels of refurbishments and heating 
systems. The user can design and apply changes to the building to optimize the energy 
consumption and indoor climate [17].  
In the case studies the houses were designed in its original construction from 1967 and 1971 
i.e. base model and compared to the calculated design consumption. This secured the precision 
level of the houses modeled in IDA-ICE. Following the processes the refurbishments were 
implemented to the model to see the energy savings. As the final simulation the supply 
temperature was designed to supply 50 −55℃ to verify the possibility to connect the houses 
to LTDH - without reducing the thermal comfort of the consumer.  
The maximum heating output for SH and the limit flow rate of DH water were determined and 
applied to the system. Both values are important to secure correct dimensioning of the SH 
system and DH network.  The nominal design heat load for the houses are calculated according 
to Danish design conditions using indoor temperature of 20℃ and outdoor temperature of 
−12℃ and without any internal gains. The Design Reference Year (DRY) weather file for 
Denmark was used in simulations for scenarios with the base-, light- and extensive 
refurbishments models.

5   Conclusion and further work recommendation 
The case studies verify that both Flintager house and Kongsager house has the possibility to be 
connected to LTDH if the houses undergoes extensive refurbishments. The energy consumption 
reduces approximately 41% and 57% with an extensive refurbishment respectively for 
Flintager and Kongsager house. Since the user of the Kongsager house is not interested in 
changing the heating system, the heating coil is advisable to be changed so it meets the 
requirements to be connected to LTDH.  
In future, it might be recommendable to change space heating system in older houses, however 
it will not contribute to the energy savings. The change will only reduce the supply temperature 
from DH to the houses. Refurbishments such as replacing windows, re-insulating the walls and 
roofs are needed to achieve energy savings in houses built in 60s and 70s. 
In case studies appreciable energy savings are seen when the windows were replaced with 3 
pane energy efficient glazing. Normally windows have been calculated to have a lifetime of 
30years. Since the houses in area 13 are older than 30 years, assuming the windows having not 
being changed, the first proposal of refurbishment to be implemented will be to replace the 
windows.  
For houses with radiator heating system in the area, the hydraulic radiator formulation with 
LMTD optimization is advisable. To have reliable output by this method, the demand is that the 
heating system are properly controlled. The AHU from 70s are designed in an non- profitable 
way when connecting to LTDH due to the heat loss. As the long as the consumer demands to 
proceed with the old AHU it requires changing the heating coil to save the heat loss in order to 
be profitable.  
The first step in connecting to LTDH is collecting energy report on home condition and classify 
them. Beside the energy refurbishments it is also important to analyze the user behaviors of 
the end-user. The higher energy consumption should not only to be based on the condition of 
the house, but must accounted to user behavior as well. In that matter personal interviews or 
surveys might be a proper investigation tool. Finally DHCOA could apply motivation tariff to 
motivate the consumers to apply refurbishments to their houses.

10 Evaluation of the project process 
 
Project process: 
• 
District Heating was chosen as the thesis topic with Svend Svendsen and Hongwei Li 
• 
Cooperation with District heating company of Albertslund was signed - Energy savings 
• 
Several energy saving methods was discussed at DHCOA. Service pipe, bypass, floor 
heating, radiator performance, energy refurbishments, network optimization, reducing 
supply return temperature, building typology, user behavior, area optimization, 
renovating old apartment buildings and  single-family houses all due to LTDH transition 
in Albertslund municipality. 
• 
The scope was reduced to energy optimization (IDA-ICE) for single-family houses in 
Area 13, (763 houses) and network optimization (TERMIS) in are 20 and 22. 
• 
Because of the lack of information and constraint in the network file which was designed 
by COWI A/S, the network optimization for specific areas was not possible. Since only 
two houses was willing to participate in the investigation (time limit) a typology was 
either not possible.  
• 
The scope of the thesis was redefined to optimize energy consumption and analyzing 
the radiator performance, for the selected two single-family houses respectively with 
high and low energy demand in area 13. The investigation should give DHCOA an insight 
of potential energy refurbishments for similar houses in the area.  Energy savings that 
will lead to the possibility to be connected to LTDH.  
Even though the project about the transition from HTDH to LTDH were very interesting to work 
with, the time limit and lack of detail information was a challenge. The scope of the thesis was 
revised several time during the period that made the research and content difficult to frame.  
Working with the thesis gave me the ability to independently work and reflect on my own 
learning. Field work, research, digital modelling, dealing with hutch amount of data etc. has 
given me an understanding and ability to work with such projects. The time limit and the 
consequences that occurs when data collecting needs more time for specific investigation has 
also been a learning. Overall the project has given me understanding of the challenges that I can 
meet and the method to work with when processing transition projects.
