Abstract 
 
 
Atmospheric CO2 levels continue to increase at an alarmin rate causing an increase 
in global warming and thus provoking extreme changes in earth’s climate, which is why new 
measures for the CO2 reduction are needed. Microbial formate dehydrogenases (FDH)  
(EC 1.17.1.9) catalyze the reversible oxidation of formate to carbon dioxide (CO2). FDH’s 
can be used to reduce the carbon dioxide to formate and use the formate as a carbon substrate 
for production of high in demand chemicals. The aim of this project was to obtain a highly 
efficient FDH for the CO2 reduction using the CIG auxotroph glycine strain of E. coli as an 
in vivo screening system. We selected FDHs of interest and transformed them into the CIG 
E. coli strain to use it as a biosensor for CO2 reduction under aerobic conditions. Out of all 
the enzymes the formate dehydrogenase of Rhodobacter sphareoides is showing promising 
results at being able to catalyze the CO2 reduction in vivo but further experiments are needed 
for confirmation.

I. 
Introduction 
 
The CO2 gas emissions have been a topic of discussion in the last 20 years due to the 
alarming increasing numbers each year. Even though the pandemic brought an economic 
slowdown in 2020 it was registered 412.5 ppm setting a new record high (Lindsey, 2020). 
As a greenhouse gas one of the consequences of its high amounts is the global warming and 
it is estimated since the pre-industrial period that humanity has increased Earth’s temperature 
by 1 degree Celsius. The rising temperatures have caused ice loss in the poles, mountains and 
glaciers, rising sea levels and ocean’s temperature, droughts, and wildfires (NASA, 2021). 
The main strategies for the CO2 reduction in the atmosphere involve mainly mitigation of the 
gas emissions but is not enough. An attractive option would be using the CO2 as a carbon 
source for production of chemicals, this way the CO2 is mitigated, and fossil-fuel processes 
could be replaced (Clazadíaz-Ramírez & Meyer, 2022).  
 
1.1 Formate dehydrogenases 
 
Formate dehydrogenases (FDH) (EC 1.17.1.9) catalyze the reversible oxidation of 
formate to carbon dioxide (CO2) using NADH (or NADPH) as an electron donor (Eq.1) 
(Calzadíaz, 2020). FDHs are divided into two classes: metal-dependent and metal-
independent, based on the requirement of metal ions for their activity. Metal-dependent 
FDHs require metals such as molybdenum, tungsten, selenium, iron as cofactors in addition 
to NAD+ as a coenzyme such as Clostridium thermoaceticum and Clostridium pasteurianum 
(Ljungdahl, 1980). As where metal independent only require NAD+ as a coenzyme. The 
advantage metal-dependent FDHs have over the metal-independent, is that they favor 
catalyzing the reverse CO2 reduction reaction more by accepting a broader spectrum of 
electron donors, nonetheless the metal-dependent FDHs appear to be more sensitive to 
oxygen than the metal-independent FDHs (Calzadíaz & Meyer, 2022).

1.2 Potential FDHs for CO2 reduction 
There are some FDHs of main interest for experimentation with the CIG E. coli strain. 
For example, some metal dependent FDHs like Rhodobacter capsulatus and Cupriavidus 
necator have had protein engineering to improve its catalytic properties as well as chemical 
and thermal stability and is also known to be a facultative aerobe (Calzadíaz & Meyer, 2022). 
In the case of the FDH of R. capsulatus it has been proven to catalyze the reduction of CO2 
to formate with a kcat of 89 min-1 in the presence of oxygen (Hartmann, 2013). Recombinant 
formate dehydrogenase form Clostridium carboxidivorans strain P7 has shown a lower 
binding ability for formate which combined with its preference towards NADH makes it a 
better catalyst for formate production through CO2 reduction (Alissandratos et al., 2013). 
FDH from Thiobacillus sp. KNK65MA has superior CO2 reducing properties compared to 
Candida boidinii, it showed a 21.2- fold higher turnover number shown in Table 1 along with 
other FDHs and their kinetic parameters (Choe et al., 2014).  
 
Clostridium pasteuranium formate dehydrogenase is a great reduction catalyst and 
shows catalytic efficiency towards formate oxidation but requires anaerobic expression 
(Alissandratos et al., 2013). One engineered FDH variant from the Chaetomium 
thermophilum named variant A1 where the positions G93H/I94Y were randomized showed 
a 5.4-fold increase in the catalytic ability for bicarbonate reduction compared to the wild type 
and when using dissolved CO2, the A1 variant showed a 2.7-fold increase in catalytic 
efficiency (Çakar et al, 2020). Ancylobacter aquaticus has a NAD-dependent FDH activity 
and this enzyme was specific to formate (Nanba, Takaoka & Hasegawa, 2003).  From 
Methylosinus trichosporium two different forms of FDHs have been purified each with 
different properties but both are NAD- dependent FDHs (Jollie & Lipscomb, 1991). 
Rhodobacter sphaeroides FDH performs best in microaerophilic environments but can 
tolerate high oxygen stress (Mackenzie et al., 2007).

1.3 Protein Engineering 
 
Protein engineering is an important tool for the development of the enzymes with 
improved characteristics, they can aid in the evolution of different properties such as activity, 
stability, expression, inhibition, and selectivity (Wójcik et al, 2015). In the last years new 
advances in mutagenesis have been made with the help of computational design tools, the 
advances include directed evolution and computational methods. Directed evolution is a 
powerful strategy that has been used to engineer any property of an enzyme, including protein 
activity, selectivity, binding affinity, substrate scope and stability. This strategy consists of 
iterative rounds of gene mutagenesis and expression with a high-throughput screening or 
selection method. The best variant obtained is used as a template and the process is repeated 
until the desired improvement has been reached. The most often used methods are random 
mutagenesis via error-prone polymerase chain reaction (ep-PCR) (Leung et al., 1989), which 
introduces mutations in a whole gene or gene fragment at different rates; DNA shuffling 
(Meyer et al., 2014), a method for in vitro recombination of homologous genes; staggered 
extension process (StEP) (Zhao et al., 1998), method based on PCR using highly abbreviated 
annealing and extension phases to generate staggered DNA fragments and promote crossover 
events along the full length of the template sequences (Rozak & Bryan, 2005). 
 
1.4 In vivo screening platforms 
 
 
Finding the best approach to generate a mutant library is the first step to the desired 
characteristics but selecting a screening or selection systems is one of the most important and 
challenging steps of library analysis. In screening systems each variant has to be evaluated, 
while the selection systems eliminate nonfunctional variants (Xiao et al., 2014). Unlike the 
in vitro protein evolution, for many years the in vivo protein engineering evolution did not 
improve due to the lack of tools to generate a bigger variety of mutations and characterizing 
the mutants in vivo. But with computational advances as well as in the screening methods, 
they have enabled the advance in in vivo protein engineering (Kim et al., 2019). For example, 
an in vivo platform made by doing an assay in the periplasm of E. coli linking aggregation to 
an antibiotic resistance act as a sensor for the innate aggregation of antibody fragments (Ebo 
et al., 2020). Using a NADPH auxotrophic strain of E. coli that serves as a biosensor for the 
==PAGE:9==
capability of the enzymes to regenerate NADPH in vivo (Lindner et al., 2018). The latter has 
proved to be a better in vivo screening method than the conventional screening methods to 
identify highly efficient FDH variants to generate NAD(P)H (Calzadíaz-Ramírez et al., 
2020).  
 
1.5 C1-glycine auxotroph E. coli strain 
 
Since organisms cannot naturally grow on formate it was important to have an 
engineered organism that could successfully grow in these conditions. Using E. coli 
auxotrophic to the C1-building blocks and Glycine (CIG E. coli strain), is possible the 
assimilation of formate into the cellular components with glycine provided in the medium 
(Yishai et al., 2017). Nonetheless, the serine pathway, the ribulose monophosphate pathway, 
and the dihydroxyacetone pathways, are restricted to specialized groups of microorganisms 
and cannot be easily established in biotechnological hosts due to their metabolic complexity, 
making it difficult to design and implement tailor-made pathways that could potentially 
transform any microbe into an efficient C1-metabolizer. The C1G strain of E. coli we tested 
had one insertion in the genome of FTL (10-formyl-tretrahydrofolate-ligase) enzyme and 
three different deletions inserted with the Kanamycin (KAN) and Chloramphenicol (CAP) 
cassette: DglyA, DgcV, and DpflAB, as shown in Figure 1, to block the natural pathways for 
glycine and the one carbon units to force the strain to grow only when glycine and formate 
are in the media and later inserting the FDHs into the strain to catalyze the conversion of CO2 
to formate.

2.1 Media 
 
LB agar medium from Sigma Aldrich (Agar, 15 g/L; NaCl, 10 g/L; Tryptone, 10 g/L; 
Yeast Extract, 5 g/L) was used for growth during cloning together with antibiotics including 
streptomycin (100 µg/mL) chloramphenicol (34 mg/mL), 30mM formate and 3mM glycine. 
LB agar broth from VWR Chemicals (NaCl, 5 g/L; Tryptone, 10 g/L; Yeast Extract, 5 g/L) 
was used for growth and selection of colonies from the agar plates, containing streptomycin 
(100 µg/ml), 30mM Formate and 3mM Glycine. SOB media for the DH5a competent cells, 
the composition is 0.5%(w/v) yeast extract, 2% (w/v) tryptone, 10mM NaCl, 2.5 mM KCl 
and 20mM MgSO4. For the selection experiments M9 minimal media was used along with 
trace elements, 25mM formate, 3mM glycine, 10mM glucose. The components are: 50 mM 
Na2HPO4, 20 mM KH2PO4, 1 mM NaCl, 20 mM NH4Cl, 2 mM MgSO4, 100 μM CaCl2,  
134 μM EDTA, 31 μM FeCl3·6H2O, 6.2 μM ZnCl2, 0.76 μM CuCl2·2H2O, 0.42 μM 
CoCl2·2H2O, 1.62 μM H3BO3 and 0.081 μM MnCl2·4H2O.

2.2.1 Synthetic genes 
 
The primers were synthesized by IDT (Integrated DNA Technologies) and the 
synthetic genes of the enzymes inserted into the pSEVA4413 vector were synthesized by 
GenScript. For the 9 genes already inserted into the pSEVA4413 vector they were 
resuspended in MiliQ water according to their nanomoles, the number of nanomoles is

by 10 and the result is the volume needed to add of MiliQ water in μl. The primers then have 
a concentration of 100 μM but for the working solution it needs to be 10 μM, so a dilution 
1:10 is made by adding in a new Eppendorf 10 μl of the original stock and 90 μl of MiliQ 
water.

2.2.3 Electrophoresis protocol 
 
For the electrophoresis gel it was used Agarose from Invitrogen; the Gene Ruler 1kb 
DNA Ladder and loading buffer are from Thermo Scientific. The TAE (Tris-Acetate-EDTA) 
buffer 50X is from Thermo Scientific, the 1X buffer dilution composition is 40mM Tris, 
20mM acetate and 1mM EDTA. The Ethidium bromide 1% is from Bie & Berntsen. 
 
For making 1% Agarose gel 10 g of agarose were dissolved in 1L of TAE buffer 1X 
and heated up in the microwave oven for several minutes until it was boiling. Poured the 
agarose in the electrophoresis gel tray and added 3µl of Ethidium Bromide and waited for it 
to solidify. Once it was solid put the gel into the electrophoresis chamber and covered the gel 
with TAE buffer 1X which was made by mixing 20 ml of TAE 50X buffer and 980ml of 
MiliQ water. After the gel was covered, we added 7 µl of the 1kb DNA ladder in the first 
hole, the ladder was mixed with the loading dye beforehand by mixing 50 µl of DNA ladder, 
50 µl of loading dye and 200 µl of MiliQ water. Then the samples were loaded in the other 
holes in the gel. Closed the chamber and connected it to the voltage source and used 110 
Volts and waited for the samples to advance.  
 
2.2.4 Transformation of FHDs wild type into E. coli DH5a, BL21DE3 and C1G E. 
coli strain 
2.2.4.1 E. coli DH5a competent cells 
 
The E. coli DH5a competent cells were made by using a Mix and Go! kit from Zymo 
Research. First an overnight culture of 1000µl of DH5a in LB was made to inoculate 25ml 
of SOB media until the OD600nm es between 0.4-0.6. After this the cells were transferred to 
ice for 10 min and centrifuge in 4ºC for another 10 minutes. The supernatant was removed 
and resuspended the cells in 2.5ml of ice-cold Wash Buffer. Centrifuged again for 10 min 
4ºC. Removed the supernatant and resuspended cells in 2.5ml ice-cold 1X Competent Buffer. 
Added aliquot of 0.1ml of the cell suspension into sterile microcentrifuge tubes.

2.2.4.2 Propagation of FDHs plasmids into E. coli DH5a 
 
The vector used was pSEVA4413 with streptomycin resistance, the reason of using 
this plasmid is because of its constitutive promoter PEM7 (Benedetti, Lorenzo & Nikel, 
2016). In Figure 2 is the image of the plasmid map with one of the FDHs enzymes (CnFDH) 
were all the subunits can be located, gamma, beta and alpha the unit of interest because is 
where the active site with the metal dependent unit is located.  The plasmids containing the 
genes of the different enzymes were transformed into the E. coli DH5a competent cells by 
adding 1µl of the DNA plasmid to 50µl of the DH5a competent cells, mixing it gently. 
Incubated the cells on ice for 10 min and added 4 volumes of SOC medium. Incubated at 
37ºC for 1 hour. Spread 100µl on LB Agar plates with streptomycin. Incubated at 37ºC 
overnight. The day after when the plate had grown another plate containing streptomycin was 
used to select the colonies and at the same time transferring them to culture tubes with LB 
and streptomycin incubating at 37ºC overnight. After the colonies have grown on the tubes 
the plasmids were extracted using the miniprep kit GeneJET Plasmid from Thermo Scientific 
according to the supplier’s instructions. The DNA was eluted in MiliQ water instead of the 
elution buffer to avoid unnecessary salts and other components.

2.2.4.3 Expression in E. coli BL21DE3 
 
For the transformation of E. coli BL21DE3 with the plasmids coding for the FDHs 
the BL21DE3 competent cells were needed so the protocol followed was the same as with 
the DH5a competent cells using the Mix and Go! kit from Zymo Research. The 
transformation was done by adding 5µl of DNA plasmid to a tube of the BL21DE3 competent 
cells and mixed gently. Incubated on ice for 10 minutes and added 4 volumes of SOC 
medium. Incubated at 37ºC for 1 hour. Inoculated directly onto tubes with 4ml of  
LB + Streptomycin (100 µg/ml). Incubated the culture tubes at 37ºC overnight. The next day 
the culture tubes that grew were used to keep in a glycerol stock by duplicate of each enzyme. 
For the expression protocol the medium used was LB with 25mM Formate, 4mM Glycine, 
Streptomycin (100 µg/ml). For preculture 4ml of medium were inoculated with 50µl of the 
glycerol stock and incubated at 37ºC and 180 rpm overnight. For expression 2 L Erlenmeyer 
shake flasks containing 250 ml of expression medium were inoculated with 1 ml of 
preculture. Cultures were incubated 24 hours at 30ºC and 180 rpm. The cultures were grown 
to OD600 = 0.4-0.8. To harvest the cells the centrifuge had to be precooled with a temperature 
of 4ºC, 5300 G and 15 min time. Cells were frozen at -80 degrees. 
 
2.2.4.4 Transformation of the FDHs plasmids into the CIG E. coli strain 
 
The transformation of the FDHs plasmids into the CIG E. coli strain was done using 
another method: electroporation, due to the advantages the method brings like having a 
higher efficiency of absorption of the DNA plasmid which is the main objective with the 
engineered strain of interest. Before beginning the process and overnight preculture of 4ml 
of CIG E. coli was made, containing LB, 30mM Formate, 3mM Glycine and 
Chloramphenicol (CAP, 34 mg/ml). The day after, the centrifuge was set up to 4 degrees 
beforehand. On an icetray put the Glycerol 10% along with the cuvettes for the 
electroporation from Bio-Rad laboratories with 0.1 cm gap. Labeled 9 sterile Eppendorf of 
1.5 ml and put them on the ice. From the preculture of CIG E. coli and using another sterile 
Eppendorf the culture was centrifuged at 4 degrees and 11,000 rpm for 30 seconds, removed 
the supernatant and repeated the previous steps until there was no more culture to use. 
==PAGE:16==
Washed the cells with 1ml of glycerol 10% 3 times at 4 degrees, 11,000 rpm for 30 seconds. 
After the washes the pellet was resuspended with 1200µl of cold glycerol 10%. On the 9 
labeled Eppendorf it was added 5µl of minipreps from the DH5a transformation DNA and 
100µl of the resuspended CIG E. coli cells. The cells had some mixing with the pipette and 
all the volume was transferred to the cuvettes.  
 
The electroporator used was from Bio-Rad and it was set up on Ec1 and set the cuvette 
on the electroporator and clicked the button form the pulse. After the pulse it was added 
quickly 800µl of LB, 30mM Formate, 3mM Glycine and no antibiotics. Incubated for 1 hour 
at 37ºC. Plated 150µL of the culture in LB plates with 30mM Formate, 3mM Glycine, 
Streptomycin (100 µg/ml) by duplicate. Incubated the plates overnight at 37ºC. The next day 
it was selected the best and isolated colonies from the plate and transferred in a new numbered 
plate with the same components and culture tubes by duplicate, the culture tubes containing 
LB, 30mM Formate, 3mM Glycine and Streptomycin (100 µg/ml). Once the culture tubes 
were grown one of the culture duplicates was used to make minipreps from the kit GeneJET 
Plasmid from Thermo Scientific to extract the plasmid, according to the supplier’s 
instructions and the other one to keep a glycerol stock of the transformation. The minipreps 
were eluted using MiliQ water 25µl.  
 
2.3 Growth experiments of the C1G E. coli strain under the expression of the FDHs

The M9 minimal media, as its name implies, has the minimal components for a 
medium to allow the selection of the nutrients and have control over what the strain is

Streptomycin, in this test we don’t add formate because the carbon source will be from the

constructions.

2.5 Verification of the FDHs expression on CIG E. coli strain cultures 
 
Some cultures of the CIG E. coli strain carrying the enzymes: RcFDH, RsFDH and 
CnFDH, apparently reduce CO2 in vivo. Therefore, we have decided to verify the expression 
of these enzymes through western-blot and an enzymatic assay.  
 
2.5.1 Sample preparation for SDS-PAGE  
 
First to prepare the samples for the SDS-PAGE we made cultures in LB with 25mM 
Formate, 3mM Glycine, 50µg/ml Streptomycin, 1mM Molybdate and cultures with M9, 
25mM Formate, 3mM Glycine, 10mM Glucose, 50µg/ml Streptomycin, 1mM Molybdate. 
We inoculated the culture tubes with 150µl and separated 100µl in an Eppendorf of the 
cultures that grew in the CO2 test with M9, 0.5mM Formate, 3mM Glycine, 10mM Glucose 
and 50µg/ml Streptomycin and supplied with 20% CO2. Incubate at 30ºC and shaking. After 
24 hours we used the cultures along with the separated sample to make the SDS- PAGE, 
which is going to be used later for the western blot to verify the presence of the enzymes 
according to their molecular weight.  
 
2.5.2 SDS-PAGE 
 
The SDS-PAGE is a technique to separate proteins based on the molecular weight. It 
uses sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) that has a strong denaturing effect and binds to the protein 
backbone and along with a reducing agent it cleaves the disulfide bonds, so proteins unfold 
into linear chains with negative charge proportional to the polypeptide chain, so it eliminates 
the influence of structure and charge (MBL, 2017). Then the proteins are separated by 
electrophoresis in the polyacrylamide gel, if the protein is smaller, it will migrate faster 
through the gel as shown in Figure 3. As for the reaction preparation first, the samples were 
denatured by heat treatment at 90ºC for 5 min. Then in an Eppendorf we had to mix 15µl of 
sample volume, 5µl of Laemmli buffer 4x, and 2µl of DTT 500mM. Once the samples were 
prepared, they were loaded in a 4-20% Mini-PROTEAN TGX Precast with 10 µl of the 
DUAL COLOR marker in a tank with approx. 1.3L of TGS 1X buffer from BIO-RAD. The 
==PAGE:21==
gel was run at 200V, 0.05A, 10W until the migration of the sample reaches the bottom 
approximately 30 min. The gel was extracted from the cassette and washed in deionized water 
3 times.  
 
 
Figure 3. Image of migration of high molecular weight and low molecular weight proteins in an SDS-PAGE 
after the disulfide bonds have been cleaved leaving a polypeptide chain. (MBL, 2017). 
 
 
2.5.3 Western Blot  
 
The principle of the Western blot is that the target proteins are transferred to a 
hydrophobic membrane after the SDS-PAGE and detected using antibodies. After the  
SDS-PAGE is done a membrane is added on top of the gel to which the proteins from the 
SDS-PAGE are going to be transferred. Then a primary antibody is added to the membrane, 
washed, and reacted with a secondary antibody labeled with an enzyme as shown in Figure 
4. The detection of bands occurs because of the enzyme activity that is used to detect the 
target protein and visualized by a chemiluminescent or chromogenic method (MBL, 2017).  
 
The protocol followed was to first to cut the PVDF (polyvinylidene difluoride) 
membrane from Amersham Hybond to fit the gel size. Soak the SDS-PAGE in electroblotting 
buffer (1X Tris-Glycine buffer) for 5 min. Wet the membrane in 96% ethanol for a few 
seconds and then transfer to the electroblotting buffer. Dip the sponges and filter paper in a 
separate container with the electroblotting buffer. Assemble the system with sponge, filter 
==PAGE:22==
paper, membrane, gel, filter paper, sponge, there should not be bubbles between the gel and 
the membrane. Because the current will go from the black side (negative side) to the red side 
(positive side) the assembly should be black side, gel, membrane, and the red side so that the 
sample can transfer to the membrane. Insert the cassette in the blotting apparatus and fill with 
blotting buffer. Run at 10V at room temperature for 10 hours. After the transfer is complete, 
remove the blotting membrane and rinse with ddH2O, if done correctly the marker should be 
visible in the membrane.  
 
For the antibody treatment we incubated the membrane in 1X TBS+ 2% milk + 0.1% 
Tween20 for 1 hour and changed the buffer every 20 min. Then incubate in 1X TBS+ 2% 
milk + 0.1% Tween20 primary antibody: Monoclonal Anti-polyHistidine-Peroxidase 
antibody (1:10000 dilution in volume of 30 ml) for 1 hour. Wash 4 times each of 15 min with 
1X TBS+ 2% milk + 0.1% Tween20. Then wash again 3 times each of 5 min with 1X TBS+ 
0.1% Tween20. For the detection of the bands the AEC staining kit from Sigma Aldrich was 
used, and the solution was prepared according to the supplier’s instructions: 4 ml of deionized 
water, add 2 drops of acetate buffer, 1 drop of 3-amino-9-ethylcarbazole (AEC) chromogen 
and 1 drop of hydrogen peroxide. In a container incubate the membrane with the AEC 
prepared solution and the bands should show up in 5 to 10 minutes.

2.5.4 Enzymatic assay: detection of the reduction of NAD+ to NADH 
 
For this assay we wanted to see if the FDHs were expressing in the C1G E. coli 
cultures and thus being able to reduce the NAD+ to NADH in the formate oxidation reaction. 
We compared two different conditions: cultures in LB with 25mM Formate, 3mM Glycine, 
50µg/ml Streptomycin, 1mM Molybdate; cultures with M9, 25mM Formate, 3mM Glycine, 
10mM Glucose, 50µg/ml Streptomycin, 1mM Molybdate. We inoculated the culture tubes 
with 150µl of the cultures that grew in the CO2 test with M9, 0.5mM Formate, 3mM Glycine, 
10mM Glucose and 50µg/ml Streptomycin and supplied with 20% CO2. Incubate at 30ºC 
and shaking.  
 
After 24 hours the cultures had a high biomass and to have better results in the assay, 
we decided to use ultrafiltration falcons from Sartorius to concentrate the proteins. First the 
cultures had to be centrifuged to have the supernatant, then the supernatant, 6ml, was inserted 
into the ultrafiltration falcons and centrifuged for 30 minutes at 44,000 g and 4ºC. The 
concentrated protein was then moved into an Eppendorf to begin the assay where is going to 
be done by duplicates and using two different buffers, 1M potassium phosphate buffer 
(K2HPO4) with pH 7.0 and 1M Tris-HCl with pH of 9.0. In a microplate reader it was added 
6µl of Formate 6mM, 4µl of NAD+, 10µl of buffer either phosphate or Tris-HCl, 5µl Enzyme, 
10µl of Molybdate 100mM, and 65µl of MiliQ water to have a total volume of 100µl. As for 
controls they were also done by duplicates, we have Control 1 that consists of 6µl of Formate 
6mM, 10µl of buffer either phosphate or Tris-HCl, 10µl of Molybdate, and 74µl MiliQ water, 
this control does not have the NAD+ and the enzyme. Control 2 consists of 6µl of Formate 
6mM, 10µl of buffer either phosphate or Tris-HCl, and 84µl MiliQ water, this control does 
not have the NAD+, the enzyme and the molybdate. Control 3 consists of 6µl of Formate 
6mM, 4µl of NAD+, 10µl of buffer either phosphate or Tris-HCl, and 80µl MiliQ water, this 
control does not have the enzyme and the molybdate. The assay was performed on an Epoch 
2 microplate spectrophotometer from Agilent BioTek at 30ºC for 15 min. The reaction 
procedure was monitored by following the reduction of NAD+ et 340nm.

IV. 
Discussion 
 
 
The main goal of this project was to obtain engineered formate dehydrogenase (FDH) 
mutants with higher catalytic activity for the conversion of CO2 to formate. To reach the 
objective we made a series of experiments using the engineered strain of E. coli CIG as an in 
vivo screening system to rely solely on the CO2 as a C1 compound as well as a selection of 
FDHs of interest and using random mutagenesis to create libraries of the mutants.  
 
First, to ensure a good cloning application the strain of E. coli DH5a was used. The 
DH5a strain has a recA1 mutation that disables the activity of recombinases and inactivates 
homologous recombination, it also has the endA1 mutation that inactivates an intracellular 
endonuclease which prevents it from degrading the plasmid (Samanthi, 2021). These cells 
were made to maximize the efficiency of transformation by increasing the insert stability and 
improve the quality of plasmid DNA.  
 
 
Once a successful cloning was ensured, for the expression of the protein the E. coli 
BL21DE3 was used. This strain is deficient in the LON protease which degrades foreign 
proteins, additionally, it is missing a gene that codes for the outer membrane protease 
(OmpT) whose function is to degrade extracellular proteins (Samanthi, 2021). All this lack 
of proteins triggers the successful production of foreign protein in the BL21DE3 cells, 
making it perfect for the expression of our proteins. 
 
 
The vector used was pSEVA4413 with streptomycin resistance, the reason of using 
this plasmid is because of its constitutive promoter PEM7 (Benedetti, Lorenzo & Nikel, 
2016). Promoters are classified from low to high strength and PEM7 is a strong constitutive 
promoter. Strong constitutive promoters have the advantage that are simple and are 
independent of extra requirements but have a disadvantage in their expression strength. They 
can produce significantly less protein compared to a strong inducible promoter in the same 
conditions (Fitz, Wanka & Seiboth, 2018). Nonetheless, inducible promoters induced by 
isopropyl-β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) have associated toxic properties such as 
increasing the toxicity of haloalkane substrate that can be mitigated with the amount of 
==PAGE:39==
concentration of IPTG (Dvorak et al., 2015). For our purposes it would be worth to do a 
construction of a promoter library to see if changing the strength and type of promoter can 
fine tune the expression of the proteins (De Mey et al., 2007). 
 
 
For the in vivo screening we used an engineered strain of E. coli that is named CI-
Glycine auxotroph strain that was obtained from the Max Planck Institute of Molecular Plant 
Physiology. The CIG E. coli strain is auxotroph to the C1-building blocks and glycine, 
meaning that the only way to obtain the one carbon units is from the CO2 or the formate, 
making it the ideal screening system to test if our FDHs can make the conversion of CO2 to 
formate. The C1G strain of E. coli we tested had one insertion in the genome of FTL (10-
formyl-tretrahydrofolate-ligase) enzyme and three different deletions inserted using the 
Kanamycin (KAN) and Chloramphenicol (CAP) cassette: DglyA, DgcV, and DpflAB, to block 
the natural pathways for glycine and the one carbon units to force the strain to grow only 
when glycine and formate are in the media and later inserting the FDHs into the strain to 
catalyze the conversion of CO2 to formate. This means that if the bacteria start growing in 
those conditions, then the FDHs are able to catalyze the conversion of CO2. 
 
 
For the growth experiments we tested 3 different conditions, the positive, where the 
media had formate, glucose, glycine, streptomycin, so it was expected for the cells to grow 
under these conditions because the formate is the one carbon unit (C1) necessary for the 
metabolic way to work as well as having glycine for the strain’s auxotrophy to it.  The results 
are shown in Table 10, with it we can confirm that all the enzymes grew without problems 
except for the AnaFDH. The negative, composed of glucose and streptomycin to ensure that 
the cells will not grow unless glycine, formate, or CO2, are provided in the media so our 
results match with the fact that it did not grow. Finally, the CO2 test only had glucose, glycine, 
and streptomycin because it was put in a CO2 incubator with 10% of carbon dioxide meeting 
all the necessary requirements for the cells to grow if the FDHs are able to catalyze the 
conversion, but it did not go according to our expectations because there was no growth in 
most of them and it seemed that after some days they start dying. Only the Rhodobacter 
sphareoides tubes seemed to be little growth two times but we were not able to recover the 
strain. For the moment we have not managed to make the FDHs grow in vivo using the CIG 
==PAGE:40==
E. coli strain with CO2 as a C1 compound. Expressing FDHs in vivo is very different from in 
vitro where you can control the exact amount of every substrate meaning a controlled 
environment, whereas in vivo you cannot and also are involved more complex metabolisms 
of the cell. This does not mean the FDHs are not capable of reducing the CO2 into formate 
in vivo, but rather we have not found the right conditions for them to express correctly or the 
wild type FDHs do not have good enough kinetics to support the growth in vivo.  
 
 
 Since most of the FDHs were not growing when there was CO2 instead of formate, 
we decided to test different concentrations of formate to see if it could supply the enzymes 
in the early stages. At the same time another experiment was executed were we tried different 
concentrations of Sodium Molybdate to see the toxicity of different quantities. The results of 
the growth experiment supplied with different quantities of formate (1, 5, 10mM) were that 
there was a high production of biomass and no significant difference between them. This 
growth can be attributed mainly to the formate instead of it being just a supplement to the 
CO2 because no matter the quantity of formate there was no difference in growth like a 
gradient that was expected. Later with the experiment of growth with 1 and 0.5mM formate 
in the plate reader it looks like 0.5 formate can support the growth in the early stages. It could 
be a possibility that the formate was just helping in the beginning and later rely on the carbon 
dioxide but there is no way to tell at this point. To know exactly when this changed happened 
and option could be to do carbon label (13C) to see how much the cell relies on either formate 
or CO2 (Gleizer et al., 2019).  
 
With the experiment of the toxicity of molybdate it showed that a concentration above 
750µM affects the growth but below that the concentrations are not toxic and safe to use 
(Figure 7). In the past molybdenum substances were thought to cause genotoxic effects 
because of the molybdate ion [MoO4]2− but after review of the results many of them were 
inconsistent and later with new studies done in vitro using sodium molybdate dihydrate prove 
that they do not show evidence of reverse mutations in different strains of Salmonella 
typhimurium, as well as, no mutagenic or clastogenic effects with lymphoma cells coming to 
the conclusion that it does not cause genotoxic effects (Burzlaff et al., 2017). Whereas 
potassium molybdate (K2MoO4) is reported to be weakly genotoxic and ammonium 
==PAGE:41==
heptamolybdate ((NH4)6Mo7O24) was reported as genotoxic in strains of Bacillus subtilis 
(Nishioka, 1975).  
 
Now with all the data obtain from the formate growth experiment and the toxicity of 
molybdate we decided to try a different approach by doing a preculture in LB media with 
25mM formate, 3mM glycine, streptomycin. The next day inoculate in M9 media with 25mM 
formate, 10mM glucose, 3mM glycine, 50µg/ml Streptomycin let it grow until it reaches the 
exponential phase based on the curves in Figure 8. Finally wash the cells and inoculate in 
M9 media with 0 and 0.5 formate, 10mM glucose, 3mM glycine, 50µg/ml Streptomycin and 
0.5mM molybdate but this time with CO2 at 20%. The results were optimistic because after 
two weeks the tubes with the enzyme of Rhodobacter sphareoides showed a clear growth as 
well as some little growth the ones with the enzyme of Rhodobacter capsulatus. To confirm 
our results, we set up the experiment again to see if we get the same results. It could be that 
the 0.5mM of formate is indeed helping the strain grow a little before relying solely on the 
CO2 for the growth as well as having the 0.5mM of molybdate instead of 1mM like in 
previous experiments because according to the Figure 7 above a concentration of 750µM 
affects the growth of the strain.  
 
 
To obtain the library of FDHs we decided to do random mutagenesis, a method used 
in directed evolution for library construction where random mutations are created in the target 
gene to have genetic diversity (Tee & Wong, 2013), usually it is done by doing Error-prone 
PCR and that is the strategy followed. In this type of PCR random mutants are inserted into 
the desired piece of DNA because the polymerase makes mistakes in the base pairing during 
the DNA synthesis and results in the introduction of errors (Ward & de Oliveira, 2015). That 
is why is important to use a polymerase that does not have the proof-reading ability as to not 
correct the error it already made, generally the Taq polymerase is used because it qualifies 
into the characteristics. In the results obtained by using the error prone technique it was 
difficult to obtain the ideal conditions for the bonding to happen, it was trial and error of 
different temperatures as well as primers, for the metallic FDHs the primers coded for the 
subunit alpha and for the non-metallic FDHs the primers coded for the gen. As it can be seen 
==PAGE:42==
in Figure 9 and 10, we finally found the optimal conditions for the Error prone PCR of the 
metal and non-metal FDHs.  
 
 
After the insertion of the mutations a technique called MEGAWHOP was made. 
MEGAWHOP allows the DNA fragment to be cloned into the template vector using whole-
plasmid PCR (Miyazaki, 2011). After the synthesis DpnI is added to digest the template 
plasmid. The mixture is then introduced into competent E. coli cells to yield the plasmid with 
the replaced fragments and thus constructing the library of mutants. As you can see in Figures 
11 and 12, we still have not managed to successfully insert the DNA fragment of the Error- 
prone PCR of both types of FDHs. In the case of the metal dependent FDHs the total size of 
the plasmid is approximately 10kb which is probably why the amplification is not taking 
place. As for the metal independent FDHs the total size is about 6 kb but is still nor working. 
An option could be trying the same trial and error technique with the Error-prone but now 
for the MEGAWHOP. Or changing completely the technique and doing restriction digest. 
 
For verification of the expression of the FDHs in the western blot we did not get the 
results we expected because it showed no positive bands at all. There could be several reasons 
it did not work, one was that we used the culture as it was meaning that mostly it was just 
going to be extracellular proteins and it could be that ours was mainly intracellular, we did 
not perform sonication to try and break more the cell. The antibody solution for the western 
blot was used already more than 7 times where the optimal recommended use is just 2 to 3 
times, we could not make a new solution because there was no stock left of the antibody. We 
also wanted to repeat the method but there was also no Trizma base left to make the TBS and 
the other combinations of buffers.  
 
In the enzymatic assay we got strange behaviors in the absorbance. In comparison 
with the controls especially with the control that had NAD+, it seems like the reaction already 
took place according to the values showed in Figures 14 and 15 for the different enzymes. 
While the controls begin at low levels of absorbance, with the enzymes there is a great 
increment and remains constant. In Figure 15D, although not the best, the RcFDH in Tris/HCl 
buffer shows more of a curve of increment and then remains constant which is what was 
==PAGE:43==
expected to happen for all the other FDHs.  There could be several reasons why the assay did 
not go according to the expectations, first, when concentrating the protein in the case of the 
LB samples it showed some yellow coloration as opposed to the samples of the M9 media, 
so the coloration could have been an interference when doing the assay. Also, since the assay 
was made by duplicates and using two different buffers there was 24 samples in total so when 
finishing to introduce all the samples in the microplate reader it could be that the reaction 
already took place before the reading. 
 
 
V. 
Conclusion 
 
 
In conclusion, the in vivo system conditions of the CIG E. coli strain need to be 
improved. The RsFDH is showing promising results in the CO2 tests to be able to catalyze 
the reduction of the carbon dioxide so further investigation and experimentation is needed. 
The random mutagenesis protocol is working but the technique of amplification of the whole 
plasmid needs to be improved or changed. In the enzymatic assay especially with RcFDH in 
Tris buffer it looks like the NAD+ reduction reaction is taking place, so a repetition of the 
assay is needed to corroborate the result.  
 
 
VI. 
Outlook 
 
 
As stated before, making a library of promoters could be a good option to see which 
strength type will allow the better expression of the protein or also trying different vector 
systems. With the collaboration between Garry and Marc, finding new tunnels that allow the 
entrance of CO2 to the enzyme, as well as selecting specific potential residues could be more 
promising than doing the random mutagenesis. For the verification of the expression of the 
FDHs in the western blot it could be a good option to use sonication to have a better result in 
the lysis of the cell and try to use new media in each step to avoid problems. For the enzymatic 
assay it would be better to do washes with the buffer after the concentration of the protein to 
avoid any other type of residue or coloration left, as well as trying to just make and read 3 
==PAGE:44==
samples at a time because the reaction takes place in a matter of seconds and when doing 
several samples in a plate reader it could be that when you finish preparing the samples the 
reaction already took place so we are not going to get an accurate representation of the 
behavior.
