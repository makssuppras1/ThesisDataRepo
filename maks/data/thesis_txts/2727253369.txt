Abstract
This thesis formulates and optimizes a stochastic version of the Integrated Timetable
and Vehicle Scheduling Problem (IT-VSP). The IT-VSP is a complex operation research
problem that public transportation planners need to solve for getting the transportation
network’s timetable and the vehicles’ schedules. It involves setting the departure time of
the different trips from the served stops as well as deciding how each vehicle should be used
under numerous operational constraints. To capture the inherent uncertainty of travel times
in urban environments, a stochastic programming approach is adopted. The advantages of
the stochastic programming framework are investigated through two main contributions.
First, a state-of-the-art Mixed Integer Programming solver is employed to solve a small-
scale instance of the problem. The results demonstrate that the stochastic programming
solution outperforms the classic approach, yielding an average cost reduction of 16.4% com-
pared to 13.6%. Moreover, the average transfer waiting time is reduced by 5.3%, and the
overall reliability of the transportation system is significantly enhanced.
Second, a Large Neighborhood Search heuristic algorithm is developed to tackle a real-
world instance provided by Movia, a Greater Copenhagen’s public transportation agency.
The assessment of final solutions reveals that the stochastic model achieves a 6.5% reduction
in the total variable cost, surpassing the 3.8% improvement obtained by the classic determin-
istic approach. Additionally, notable improvements in delays and transportation reliability
are observed.
The comparison between the stochastic programming and deterministic frameworks
demonstrates that the added complexity of the stochastic approach is justified by its ben-
efits. The stochastic programming approach not only leads to expected reductions in total
costs but also enhances the overall reliability of the public transportation system.
By addressing the IT-VSP using stochastic programming, this thesis offers valuable
insights and practical solutions that can assist transportation planners in optimizing timeta-
bles and vehicle schedules, particularly in urban environments characterized by uncertain
travel times.

Introduction
"At rejse er at leve" - "To travel is to live". H.C. Andersen’s timeless proclamation
rings truer than ever in the contemporary world, where the vitality of transportation has
soared to unprecedented levels. While this mobility enables cities to connect and thrive,
its persistent pollution casts a shadow over our environmental well-being. Indeed, accord-
ing to C40 (2019), the transportation sector accounts for one-third of the greenhouse gas
(GHG) emissions in major cities, while a study conducted by the European Parliament.
(2019), indicates that it was responsible for approximately 25% of carbon dioxide emissions
in the European Union in 2019. Achieving a significant reduction in these emissions poses a
daunting challenge to the industry, as highlighted by Masson-Delmotte and al. (2018) who
emphasize the need for a "rapid and far-reaching" transformation. According to the OECD
(2021), one of the primary avenues to curb transportation emissions is by "Increasing the
share of cleaner forms of transport by encouraging a shift from private vehicles to public
transport, biking or walking". This shift not only promises to reduce GHG emissions but
also to alleviate congestion and noises paving the way for more sustainable and livable cities.
Making public transport a prominent component of daily mobility emerges as a pivotal
objective to pursue. Embracing this goal requires to seek the seamless travel experience: "a
smooth, efficient, safe, secure and enjoyable travel experience from a traveller’s point of ori-
gin to a destination [...]." OECD (2020). According to Chakrabarti (2017), this ambition is
fundamental to fostering the shift from private vehicles to public transportation services. As
an example, the Singaporean government launched the Land Transport Master Plan 2040
to "build a convenient, well-connected, inclusive and fast land transport system that will
meet the needs and aspirations of Singaporeans over the next two decades." Land Transport
Authority Singapore. (2019).
According to the data collected by Moovit (2022), the 1.5 billion people commuting
every day by using transit are spending on average over an hour on public transports. More-
over, about a third of this travel time is due to the waiting time at the different stops.
However, by considering that the passenger knows the arrival time at his departure stop,
the waiting time can be reduced by the user if the planned timetable is realized. Thus, it
is more important to decrease the waiting time during a transfer where the customer has
no influence on the transport arrival. As an example, Petersen et al. (2013) declared that
the average waiting time for a transfer connection in the Greater Copenhagen area is ap-
proximately 9.75 minutes. This number corresponds to nearly half of the total waiting time
obtained by the data from Moovit (2022). Reducing the waiting time during transfers will
increase passengers’ satisfaction. Indeed, Susilo and Cats (2014) showed the transfer’s neg-
ative influence on the user’s experience by conducting a multivariate statistical analysis of
different factors impacting the customer’s well-being. Other behavioural studies such as Hine
and Scott (2000) and Beirão and Sarsfield Cabral (2007) demonstrated that the passenger’s
aversion to transfers is due to transfers’ inconvenience, risks of missing the connections, and
considerable waiting times. Hence, creating reliable transfers with short waiting times could

1.1
Transportation planning
Improving the passengers’ experience by reducing the transfer waiting time helps moving
towards a seamless travel experience. A lot of waiting times can be avoided if the trans-
fers are considered while planning public transportation services. Alongside the ascending
innovations in data collection and connectivity, more and more complex models are devel-
oped for integrating transfer-related concerns in transit network planning. Historically, six
consecutive problems are solved sequentially according to figure 1.
Figure 1: The six steps of transportation planning according to Lusby et al. (2011)
At a strategic level, the network design and line planning problems define the routes
to operate and with which level of service. At the tactical stage, the timetabling, vehi-
cle scheduling, and crew scheduling problems are allocating the resources (trips, vehicles,
workforce...) to minimize the operating costs and maximize the users’ satisfaction. More
precisely, the timetabling problem sets the departure time of the different vehicles from their
terminals and all the served stops. Meanwhile, the vehicle scheduling problem sets which
vehicle is assigned to which trip. Finally at the operational level, the real time control en-
sures quick and efficient answers to unexpected events and disruptions.
Transfers can be improved with the line planning problem. Indeed, if the lines have lower
headways, the transfer time will mechanically decrease. However reducing the headway re-
quires to operate more trips and very often this means using more buses. This represents
a very high capital cost that transportation planners are often not willing to take. Alter-
natively, an efficient timetable can improve transfers by introducing synchronization. This
comes with no additional investments and simply some changes in the timetable. It is a

low-hanging fruit for improving the transfers in the network.
The vehicle scheduling problem and the timetabling problem (TP) are linked by many
aspects. As the TP ignores the operational constraints such as the number of available ve-
hicles and the distances between the terminal, it is not guaranteed that a timetable can give
a feasible vehicle schedule. Therefore, solving the two problems is a complex task, and it
often requires several iterations. At the tactical stage, numbers of studies showed the pos-
itive impact of integrating the timetabling and vehicle scheduling problems but not many
introduced uncertainty in the decision making process. This is why this approach will be
further investigated in this thesis.
1.2
Uncertainty
In an urban environment, road transportation times are by nature stochastic. Many
unpredictable elements can influence the travel time between two points. Among others,
one can cite the traffic, weather, construction work, other road users’ behavior. Due to this
uncertainty, it is impossible to know in advance the exact arrival time of a vehicle at a stop.
Therefore, a timetable can never be respected. Actually, transportation planners try to find
timetables that works the majority of the time. The approaches to this problem can be
divided in two families.
First, the planner can consider an average value for the travel time between two stops
and build a timetable from there. This is called a deterministic approach as the stochasticity
of the travel time is not taken into account anymore. This approach is used traditionally
as it is the simplest and the easiest to implement. Many optimization techniques have been
developed to solve it. However, it suffers from a theoretical issue: it ignores the stochasticity
in the timetable and thus the realised timetable can be very far from the planned one.
Second, the planner can fully embrace the stochasticity and consider it in the decision
making process. This family of methods is referred as the stochastic approaches. Those are
much harder to model and to implement but promise to deliver timetables adapted to the
specificities of the city and its uncertainties. From the confrontation of the two described
families of approach, the following research question emerges.
1.3
Research question
Can a stochastic approach of the IT-VSP overcome the classic deterministic approach
in terms of cost efficiency, reliability and transfers performances?

1.4
Outline
In this thesis, we try to answer the above mentioned research question. The timetabling
and vehicle scheduling problems are considered as an operation research problem. The previ-
ous approaches to the modelling and the resolution of such problems are reviewed in section
2. In section 3, we formulate under mathematical terms the Integrated Timetabling and
Vehicle Scheduling Problem (IT-VSP) as a Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) problem. As
real instances of the problem can’t be solved with classic solvers, we develop in part 4 a
heuristic algorithm for the IT-VSP. Then, section 5 describes the experimental design used
to answer the research question. The idea is to benchmark a stochastic and a deterministic
timetable solved using a state-of-the art MIP solver and the heuristic developed in part 4.
The comparison between the stochastic and the deterministic timetable using a MIP solver
is done in section 6 on a small scale instance of the IT-VSP. Then, our heuristic is used to
compare the two frameworks in part 7 based on a real-world case. Our reflections, discus-
sions and recommendations for future work are presented in 8. Finally, our findings and
conclusions are summarized in section 9.

Literature review
Models for the Timetabling
Liu et al. (2021) reviews all the different models addressing the timetable synchroniza-
tion problem. At first, some heuristic rule-based approaches were designed. Most of them
consist of graphical human-computer interactive decision-making processes Keudel (1988),
Vuchic et al. (1981). While being easy to implement, those algorithms cannot guarantee an
optimal solution.
Another major family of approach to the timetabling problem is analytical modelling.
According to Liu et al. (2021), those methods define a cost function, usually composed of
two main components: operating and passenger costs. Then the procedure consists in trying
to minimize it using numerical optimization methods. In their groundwork, Wirasinghe and
Hurdle (1977) developed a continuous and smooth benefit function. Next, the model was
optimized by considering its partial derivatives. Despite being easy to solve without opti-
mization solvers, analytical modelling relies on continuum approximations which are made
at the expense of the realistic aspects of the model. (Sivakumaran et al. (2012))
The third main family approach for the timetabling problem is mathematical program-
ming. In this category, the variables take discrete and exact values thus tackling the ap-
proximation issues faced by the analytical models.
The first study to use mathematical
programming for the timetabling problem was Klemt and Stemme (1988). In this work,
an IP model is created for the synchronization of timetables. The model aims at minimiz-
ing the transfer waiting time and uses a nearest neighbour constructive heuristic to solve
it. Following this groundwork, several studies developed different heuristics to address this
mathematical formulation: Domschke (1989) with the regret method, Voß (1992) with a
Tabu search, and Nachtigall and Voget (1996) with a genetic algorithm.
Ceder and Tal (2001) is the first study to address the timetable synchronization problem
at a network level and modelling it as an mixed-interger program. It defines a transfer as
the simultaneous arrival of vehicles on a network node and tries to maximize the number
of transfers. The model is solved using a specifically designed heuristic. Inspired by these
new foundations, several studies focused on reformulating Ceder and Tal (2001)’s formu-
lation to better fit some use cases. Some newly developed heuristics were among others
genetic algorithms Shrivastava and Dhingra (2002), and Tabu search Jansen et al. (2002).
In his literature review, Liu et al. (2021) assesses the different methods and approaches to
solve the timetable synchronization problem. Ibarra-Rojas and Rios-Solis (2012) brought
a major contribution by redefining the notion of transfers as the arrival of vehicles within
a specific time window. This definition is now widely adopted in the literature. In addi-
tion, Ibarra-Rojas and Rios-Solis (2012) demonstrates the NP-hardness of the timetabling
problem. While most of the studies focused on maximizing the number of transfers in the
network, Wu et al. (2016b) introduced some novelty by including the transfer’s demand in
the objective function. Using a different approach Takamatsu and Taguchi (2020) models the

Models for the IT-VSP problem
Traditionally, the different planning stages identified by Liu et al. (2021) were done
sequentially. Guihaire and Hao (2010) simultaneously tackled the timetabling and vehicle
scheduling problem (VSP). Formulating a nonlinear cost function, the model was solved on a
real transportation network using a local search heuristic. It showed that the solution of the
integrated timetable and vehicle scheduling problem (IT-VSP) can outperform the solution
of the two problems solved sequentially, both in terms of quality of service and in terms of
resource utilization. Petersen et al. (2013) wrote an IP formulation of the IT-VSP problem
and solved it using a neighbourhood search heuristic. Ibarra-Rojas et al. (2014) proposed
another approach with a bi-objective IP model aiming at finding the Pareto-optimal couples
of passenger transfer benefit (linked to the timetabling problem) and operating costs (linked
to the VSP). The set of Pareto optimal couples was found using an ϵ-constraint method.
Liu et al. (2017) also formulated the IT-VSP problem by extending Ceder and Tal (2001)’s
model. A meta-heuristic was then developed for this model. Fonseca et al. (2018) designed
another version of the IT-VSP allowing flexible dwell times and developed a matheuristic.
Stochastic approach
According to Liu et al. (2021), most of the literature takes deterministic travel times as
an assumption. However, Lee et al. (2022) characterizes travel times as inherently stochastic
as they can be influenced by many exogenous elements such as accidents and congestion. Wu
et al. (2015) is the first study to include stochastic travel times in a timetabling problem. It
considers the arrival time of a vehicle as a random variable following a shifted truncated ex-
ponential distribution (according to Bookbinder and Desilets (1992)). A stochastic IP model
is designed. A genetic algorithm is used to solve it and to find the optimal value of the trans-
fer waiting time expectancy. The method is then tested on a small-scale network showing
good results in the reduction of transfer failures. Other approaches to tackle uncertainties
were made such as Wu et al. (2016a) with robust optimization or Yu et al. (2022) with an
augmented ϵ-constraint method. To the best of our knowledge, only Wei and Sun (2017)
includes stochastic travel times in the IT-VSP. A bi-level programming model is developed.
The lower-model (timetabling problem) is used to generate feasible solutions which are then
given as inputs to the upper-model (VSP). The density function of the travel time is then
determined as a normal distribution using the Estimation of Distribution Algorithm.

This part aims at formulating a mathematical model for the Integrated Timetable and
Vehicle Scheduling Problem (IT-VSP). First, the guiding principles of the model are stated
in part 3.1. Second, the modelling assumptions are documented in part 3.2. Then, we define
the notations used for translating the problem in mathematical terms in part 3.3. Next, we
detail the modelling process of the timetabling problem and the vehicle scheduling problems
in part 3.4 and 3.5 respectively. The full model is made available in part 3.6. Finally we
reflect on the obtained model in part 3.7.
3.1
Guiding principles
As stated in the literature review, including uncertainty in the travel times for the VSP
and Timetabling problems can be done through different ways. In our case, the large amount
and the quality of travel times data enables the use of stochastic programming. Therefore a
classical two-stage stochastic programming model as described in Louveaux and Birge (2001)
will be used. This framework makes the difference between the first stage decisions and the
second-stage decisions. The first stage decisions are taken before knowing the values of the
random parameters (in our case the travel times) while the second stage decisions are taken
after the realization of the random parameters. In our case, the first stage decisions can be
listed as follow:
• The timetable of the network, (i.e. the departure time of each trip of each line from
each stop).
• The affectations of the vehicles to the different trips.
Those decisions are made before the beginning of the planning period without the knowledge
of the travel times. At the contrary, the second stage decisions are made "during" the day.
They are listed bellow:
• The actual departure and arrival time of the buses at or from each stop.
• The actual dwell time (def. 9.6) of the buses at each stop.
• The target line’s trip (def. 9.11) the transferring passenger from one feeder line (def
9.10) are taking.
• The transfer times of the passengers are facing.

ables. Let ω be the representation of the random variables. Finally let v ∈V (u, ω) be the
representation the second stage decisions variables. One can note that the solution space
of the second stage decisions depends on the first stage decisions and the realization of the

3.2
Assumptions
3.2.1
Independence of the travel times
Accounting for the correlation between the different travel time can be very complicated.
Then, generating scenarios accounting for interdependence of the travel times would be
almost impossible. To overcome this issue, the travel times are assumed to be independent
from each other. This assumption is relatively reasonable if the planning period is chosen
carefully, (for example the off-peak period). Indeed, in the later case the possible traffic
congestion are more likely to be very local rather than the manifestation of a city-wide
phenomena.
3.2.2
Passengers behaviour
• We assume that the passengers enter the network with a journey plan and they keep
following it, whatever happens.
• While transferring, passengers are always taking the first available trip of their target
line.
• All the passengers take the same time to transfer from one line to another at a given
stop.
• Passengers’ demand is fixed. The possible variations in the passengers’ experience on
certain transfers do not influence the travel demand.

• We intend to model an urban bus network.
In this context with relatively small
headways, the passengers do not necessarily align with the timetable. We therefore
assume that their time of arrival follows an uniform distribution between the departure
of a trip and the departure of the next one.
3.2.3
Physical characteristics
• The vehicles are assumed to have infinite capacity, all the passengers willing to onboard
at a stop are actually getting onboard. Since our focus will be on off-peak hours, this
assumption is not unrealistic.
• All the bus stops have infinite space capacity.

3.7
Analysis
3.7.1
Interlining
Interlining is the possibility for the vehicles to operate on different lines, Gkiotsalitis
et al. (2019) showed that it can bring a lot of flexibility and reduce the passenger waiting
time on The Hague’s bus network. However, interlining is sometimes not allowed because of
regulatory issues. Our model is able to handle such cases by setting an infinite (in practise
very high) deadhead cost on the deadhead trips joining two trips from different lines.
The choice of adding interlining is problem dependent but has several impacts.
To
illustrate this, let us consider a macro-line L (def. 9.5) which represents two directed lines
l1 and l2 (def. 9.4). Typically l1 and l2 have similar routes but in the opposite directions.
Without interlining, a vehicle attributed to L can only operate on line l1 and l2. Therefore
when a vehicle completes a trip from l1, it faces three options:
• Returning to its depot of origin.
• Performing a trip in l1.
• Performing a trip in l2.
In practise, the starting terminal of l2 and the ending terminal of l1 are in the same place.
Therefore, the cheapest option (both in terms of time and cost) will always be to do a trip
for line l2. In that context, the VSP has almost no impact in the cost optimization. Indeed,
its values are almost set in advance. However, because the timetabling and the VSP parts
of the problems are linked by constraint 25, it is still relevant to consider it and ensure the
feasibility of the solution.
The model formulated in part 3.6 was expressed in general terms, but we also intend to
solve the problem using a heuristic. Such a task requires a higher degree of adaptation to the
problem than solving on a MIP solver. Therefore the choice of allowing interlining should
be made prior to the development so that the heuristic is correctly adapted to the problem.
Since our main case does not allow interlining, we decide to continue without considering
this option.

4
Heuristic for the IT-VSP
This section intends to describe and explain Heu-IT-VST a heuristic method for the
IT-VSP based on the Local Neighborhood Search (LNS) framework. We will first give an
outline of the algorithm in 4.1. The theoretical framework for the LNS is introduced in 4.2.
Then, we provide in-depth explanations of Heu-IT-VSP in part 4.3. Finally we conclude and
comment on the method in 4.4
4.1
Outline
Heu-IT-VSP is a heuristic method based on two successive LNS. The first LNS (LNS_Trip)
focuses on shifting the departure time of the trips while the second (LNS_Stop) changes the
dwell time of the different trips at the transfer stops. The two LNS relies on similar mech-
anisms. They use a greedy-randomized method to destroy the current solution and two
different repair methods to build a new (and hopefully better solution). Among the two
repair methods, one is purely random while the other works in a greedy fashion. The general
blueprint of the heuristic is showed in figure 2.
4.2
Large Neighborhood Search
In the context of Operation Research, Laguna and Martí (2013) defines a heuristic as
"a collection of rules or steps that guide one to a solution that may or may not be the best
(optimal) solution". By using a custom algorithm one can achieve to get a "good" solution
in less time than a MIP solver. According to Laguna and Martí (2013), a metaheuristic is
"a high level problem independent algorithmic framework that provides a set of guidelines
or strategies to develop heuristics". Among meta-heuristics, one of the most fundamental is
the local search (details in the appendix 9). It gave birth to a family of local-search based
metaheuristics. One of them is the Large Neighborhood search that we use in our specific
case.
Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) was first introduced by Shaw (1998). This meta-
heuristic belongs to the local search framework and is based on the idea of destroying and
repairing a solution to get new neighboring solutions. According to Pisinger and Ropke
(2019), LNS algorithms are characterized by the implicit definition of the neighborhood.
Indeed, in a classic local search, the neighborhood function is explicitly defined whereas the
LNS methods only define destroy and repair methods from which a neighbor is generated.
Following the notation defined in appendix 9, the LNS general algorithm as formulated
by Pisinger and Ropke (2019) is showed in algorithm 1 with d and r being the destroy and
repair methods respectively. The cost function of the problem is represented by c and the
"accept" function is a general acceptance criteria. More details are given below.
A LNS’s destroy and repair methods rely on some more basic heuristics (greedy or ran-
dom for example) and embeds some degree of stochasticity. Usually, at least one of the

4.3
Algorithm design
4.3.1
Framework
The previous research on heuristic for the TP and the VSP has found a lot of success
when using meta-heuristics from the local-search framework. Indeed, as showed by Liu et al.
(2021), many approached the problems by Iterated Local Search (ILS), Simulated Annealing
(SA), Tabu Search (TS) or Large Neighborhood Search (LNS). Building-up on this work, we
also choose to work within the macro-framework of local search and choose a more precise
framework within it. This choice is motivated by the following reasons.
• The extensive literature on the topic.
• The success encountered by the researchers who tried to use it on the timetabling
problem.
• The simplicity and flexibility of those heuristics.
• The high level of customization opportunities offered by those methods.
Inside the local search framework, many meta-heuristic have been defined and the choice
of a specific method has to be made. Our problem is characterized by a very large number of
variables and constraints. Therefore checking the feasibility of a solution is computationally
expensive and should always be avoided by constantly maintaining a feasible solution. A
classic local search framework would define a deterministic neighborhood explicitly. Such a
neighborhood might be very complex and hard to define.
At the opposite, one can very easily formulate fast heuristics that can keep the solution
feasible.
For example we could simply find the best feasible dwell time for one trip of
one line at one stop. This gives easily a large family of neighborhood functions and lays
the foundations for a Large Neighborhood Search. In addition, this framework can easily
be combined with others (Simulated Annealing or Tabu Search for example) thus giving
opportunities for further research or improvement. For these reasons, we choose to develop
a heuristic based on the Large Neighborhood Search meta-heuristic.
4.3.2
Principles and structure
Interlining and VSP
As stated in 3.7.1, interlining is not considered. The VSP becomes almost a parameter
of the timetabling problem. Therefore, we chose to focus on improving the timetables and
Page 29 of 111
==PAGE:31==
4
Heuristic for the IT-VSP
not to modify the vehicle scheduling problem. Starting with an initial solution of the IT-
VSP, our algorithm will be restricted to improving the timetable while keeping it feasible
according to the VSP. By doing this, we simplify the problem and reduce the number of
variables and as a result the complexity of the problem.
Shifting a trip and changing dwell time
Most of the research done on bus timetabling is restricted to shifting the departure time
of a trip from its starting terminal. We call this action "shifting a trip". It can be done
forward (increase the departure time) or backward (decrease the departure time). In both
cases it should be done within the limits of what the VSP allows. Following Fonseca et al.
(2018)’s groundwork, our model allows to shift the departure time of a trip from a regular
stop. This action can be seen as adding or removing dwell time at the stop and therefore we
call it "changing dwell time". One can notice that shifting a trip is essentially changing the
dwell time at the starting terminal stop.
Shifting trips has been proven to be an efficient method (Ceder and Tal (2001)) as we
affect all the transfers in the trip by only one shift. Therefore, one could quickly improve
a solution by shifting the trips. However it is still a rough method that does not go into
the details. At the opposite, changing the dwell time can be seen as less impactful on the
line’s timetable because one change only affects this stop and the ones downstream. But,
it allows to go deeper and to refine the solution. In the end, we can see this as two layers.
One at the trip level, one at the stop level. We choose to take this perspective and develop
two families of neighbourhood functions. The functions belonging to the trip-based family
will generate neighbours based on trip-shifting moves. Similarly the stop-based family will
contain neighbourhood functions based on changing dwell times at the stops.
4.3.3
Handling the stochastic framework
As we intend to solve a problem formulated in a stochastic framework, this aspect should
be handled carefully by our algorithm. As explained in 3.1 and 3.4, we primarily operate
on the first stage decision variables i.e. the planned departure times from the stops. Once
those are known, because of the problem’s structure, the second stage variables (actual de-
parture or arrival time, transfers...) can actually be inferred. Therefore every time a change
is made on the first stage variables, a simple program will update the second-stage variables
accordingly for all the scenarios.
Behind this theoretical simplicity, there is a complex practical challenge. Indeed, let us
consider a first stage decision variable x for which a change in its value has an effect on Nx
second stage variables in a deterministic framework. Then in the stochastic framework with
a set of scenario Ω, the same change in x requires Nx|Ω| changes. Therefore the number
of assignations increases very fast with the number of scenarios. Obviously, the same thing
goes with the cost evaluation. This becomes a problem for the repair methods as those func-
tions need to change the values and calculate the variation in the cost incurred by the change.
Page 30 of 111
==PAGE:32==
4
Heuristic for the IT-VSP
To address this issue, we can generate a subset of scenarios ΩN ⊂Ω. The repair function
will only update the second stage variables linked to the scenarios in ΩN. It will also calcu-
late the cost variation incurred by the change for all the scenarios of ΩN. The average cost
variation over those scenarios is then used to accept or not the repaired solution. For those
reasons, the size of ΩN should be chosen carefully. Indeed, if it is too small, we might accept
a bad solution performing well on the subset and refuse a good solution doing poorly on the
subset. If it is too large then we loose the point for which it was introduced: reducing the
number of calculations to do. Additionally, the subset should change at every iteration and
be based on random selections. If not, we would basically run our stochastic optimization
on the set ΩN rather than Ω. This is why we introduce NbScenario indicating how many
scenarios should be sampled from Ωto create the temporary ΩN. The value of NbScenario
has to be set beforehand but as we want to have an instance-independent algorithm, we
choose to control its value with the parameter ScenarioRatio. It indicates the share of
scenarios to sample for creating the temporary ΩN.
This method allows to save time in a simple way but it comes with some drawbacks. We
do not update the second stage variables outside of ΩN. Actually this is not so important,
indeed those variables could either:
• Be naturally updated during a following iteration when ΩN will change.
• Be updated at the very end of the LNS when making sure that the solution is feasible.
In addition, because the value of those variable does not change immediately, the cost of the
scenarios do not change as well. Again, since the acceptance criteria is to have a negative
average cost variation over ΩN, this is not a problem if ΩN is big enough (as explained
above). The final value of the scenarios costs will then be calculated at the very end of the
LNS.

Comments
The two destroy methods are built on the same structure and share the same strengths.
First of all, it is a greedy randomized approach which is recognized as an efficient type of
destroy methods for an LNS. Indeed, it guides the search while still allowing to escape local
minimums by having a randomized selection. Second, the non-revisit parameters make sure
that the elements which have the highest cost for structural reasons are not systematically
chosen. In addition, it adds a pseudo-tabu layer. Indeed, during a Tabu Search the previous
items selected (trips or trip-stops) would be stored and the heuristic would forbid choosing
them again for a certain number of iterations. In our case, when a trip is chosen, its weight
becomes smaller and therefore it is less likely to be chosen for the next iterations. This
pseudo-tabu layer allows to efficiently diversify the search.
Our destroy methods are also good at controlling a key parameter of an LNS: the degree
of destruction. As described in 4.2, it has to be chosen carefully in order to guarantee enough
diversity in the neighbourhood while keeping the gains obtained through the previous iter-
ations. In our destroy methods, the degree of destruction is controlled by the parameters
TripRatio and StopRatio. They control the percentage of trips or trips-stops to destroy
in the neighbourhood which, in our case, is the degree of destruction. Therefore, a lot of

attention has to be put in the tuning of those parameters.
Despite these points, these methods are not flawless. First it requires to keep track
of the scores and the probability distributions. The probabilities update shoud be done at
every iterations but it represents an important cost in terms of time . To address this issue,
we introduce f, the frequency of score updates. This parameter sets the frequency (in terms

4.3.5
Repair methods
According to the design principles (4.3.1), we want to constantly maintain feasible solu-
tions. Therefore, the repair methods must guarantee the feasibility of the repaired timetables.
This is addressed in part 4.3.5. Once the method can guarantee the feasibility of its output,
we need to define how it chooses between the different feasible options. Following the prin-
ciples of a LNS, we choose to use simple heuristics to repair the timetable. The first way
to repair is a greedy insertion while the second is a purely random method. Because those
simple heuristics work the same way whether the item destroyed is a trip or a trip-stop,
we will detail them in a general framework. In this settings, the repair methods will be
operating on an "item".
Feasible repairs
In order to simplify the solution space, we choose to work with discrete departure times.
The considered departure times are spaced by a constant step (noted step) which is a pa-
rameter of the repair methods.
The selection of the feasible departure times is done as
described in algorithm 4. The departure time from a stop is determined by two things: the
arrival time and the dwell time. The arrival time is fixed by the previous departures and
travel times. Therefore, the only degree of freedom lies in the dwell time and the algorithm
will then find the feasible dwell time. From there we can calculate the departure time.
The sub-function FeasibleTime gives a boolean indicating whether inserting the de-
parture time provided is feasible or not. It is based on the model’s constraint (3.6). First
of all, the algorithm tries to add dwell time to the initial solution (line 8 to 13). Constraint
19 forces to have a maximum dwell time, it gives the stopping criteria (line 13) as we know
that adding more dwell time will not be feasible. The algorithm tries to remove dwell time.
It stops when no more dwell time can be removed.

Regardless of the repair method, the cost of the repaired timetable has to be calculated.

practise, we successively consider the downsteam stops and assess the variation in cost due
to a the change in the arrival and departure time. As explained in 4.3.3, the cost is only
assessed on a random subset of NbScenario.

Choosing the repair method
When considering to repair an item (trip or a trip-stop), two choices are possible. Either
a random or a greedy repair. Both come with advantages and shortcomings. The random
is expected to be faster but delivering low-quality timetables. This should result in a low
acceptance rate and therefore few improvements in the total solutions. The greedy method
should provide a higher quality in the repaired timetables. However this comes with a higher
computational time. As a result, more time will be used for each iteration and a smaller
part of the solution space will be explored. Therefore, there is a trade off to find between the
quality of the repaired timetables and the time needed to find this neighbour. The choice
of a greedy or repair method will be based on probabilities. We introduce the parameters
pg and pr the probabilities of choosing the greedy method and random method respectively.

Note that pg = 1 −pr. Those probabilities are fixed at the beginning of the LNS and are
not meant to change during the heuristic.
4.3.6
Acceptance criteria
As explained in 4.2, the acceptance criteria has an important role in an LNS. In this
work we aim at developing a heuristic that can help us answering the research question (9).
Therefore, we choose to take the simplest acceptance criteria: the repaired solution will be
accepted only if it improves the average value of the objective function 26 over the selected
subset of scenarios (i.e. the expected total cost).

4.4
Comments and conclusion
The basic design principles for this heuristic is to provide an effective way to find feasible
solutions for the real-world instances of the IT-VSP. This way, we will be able to answer the
research question. We then detailed Heu-IT-VSP a heuristic method for the IT-VSP. The
algorithm is based on the LNS meta-heuristic framework which relies on a greedy-randomized
destroy method and two different repair methods. The repair methods are based on two
simple heuristics (random and greedy). As mentioned before, this heuristic is rather simple
and we believe that it can be the groundwork for further improvements or research. First,
looking at the destroy and repair methods, one could introduce more methods or develop
an adaptative layer for the probabilities of selections turning the LNS into an Adaptative
Neighborhood Search (ALNS). Then one could try using different acceptance criteria like
the one from the Simulated Annealing metaheuristic.
It would also be possible to have
several iterations of Heu-IT-VSP in order to leverage the interactions between LNS_Trip and
LNS_Stop. One could also look into integrating the VSP further by allowing some changes
in its structure. Finally, since our algorithm has several parameters, one could look into
implementing some automatic parameter setting (either in an adaptative or static way).

Experimental design
This section aims at explaining how the research question 9 will be answered. Sub-
section 5.1 defines how the timetables will be assessed and how the stochastic framework will
be taken into account. Sub-section 5.2 describes the evaluation criteria used for the timetable
assessment. Then sub-section 5.3 specifies the experiment protocol to be undertaken in order
to answer the research question. In particular, it details how the heuristic’s parameters will
be tuned.
5.1
Handling uncertainty
As we work with a stochastic framework, the optimization will be done on a set of
training scenario Ωt. The goal behind building stochastic timetabling is to generate a more
reliable and resilient timetable. It means that we aim at having the timetables performing
well in general. Therefore every over-fitting phenomena should be avoided as we don’t want
a timetable to perform well only on a few scenarios. As a consequence all the assessments
are done on a set of evaluation (or testing) scenarios Ωe chosen such as Ωt ∩Ωe = ∅. This
way, we ensure that the comparison between two timetables is not biased by a potential
over-fitting effect that can happen when optimizing the timetable over a set of scenarios.

we want to focus on the total cost. Therefore, the first metric will be the Expected Total

When comparing different timetables in terms of pure performances, the ETC suffers
from a bias.
Indeed, let us consider a timetable X1 performing extremely well on one
specific scenario and has average performances in the other scenarios. Another timetable
X2 is generated and performs generally well on all the scenarios. Because of the aggregation
effect, it might happen that X1 has a lower ETC than X2. However, in that case X2 would
probably be more desirable. This is why we propose to cover this bias by introducing a new

5.2.3

Generally speaking, stochastic programming is used to build more resilient solutions
and in this case, more reliable timetables and cost structures are desired. The first idea
would be to measure the variance in the total cost. However, if a timetable has a cost with
a high variance due to a fat left tail in the cost distribution, it will not be perceived nega-
tively by the different stakeholders. Therefore, assessing the reliability of a timetable is not
a trivial task since common metrics such as the variance or the standard deviation are not
appropriate here. As a consequence, we choose to measure the right part of the total cost
distribution tail since it is the one having negative consequences. The 90% quantile is chosen
to be the metric in cost reliability.

5.3
Experiment protocol
Underlying the research question 9, two main aspects can be identified. The first one
is to know if a stochastic programming approach can theoretically outperform the classic
deterministic framework. The second aspect is more practical and would be to understand
if a stochastic approach can in practice outperform the deterministic approach.
5.3.1
Theoretical performances
In order to establish the theoretical superiority of a timetable optimized with a stochastic
approach, we propose the following approach.
• Generate a small-scale instance, a set of training scenarios Ωt and a set of evaluation
scenarios Ωe.
• Calculate ω the average scenario. It consists of the average of the travel times across
all the scenarios in Ωt.
• Run the IP-VSP model on the set of scenarios Ωt with an MIP solver to obtain STO
the timetable optimized using a stochastic programming approach.
• Run the IT-VSP model for the scenario ω with an MIP solver to obtain DET the
timetable optimized using a deterministic approach.

==PAGE:45==

• Assess STO and DET performances on the set of scenarios Ωe.
5.3.2
In practise performances
After assessing the potential theoretical gains brought by a stochastic programming
approach, it is crucial to understand if those gains can be transposed to practical cases.
Given the NP-hardness of the problem established by Ibarra-Rojas and Rios-Solis (2012)
and the usual size of the real-world instances, solving a real-case on a MIP solver is not a
viable option. Therefore, a heuristic is developed in 4.3.1 to solve a real-world case. This
algorithm uses a set of parameters which have to be tuned with a method detailed in 5.3.3.
Once this is done, the same method as in 5.3.1 is used to conclude. In addition, both DET and
STO could be compared to the current timetable in use within the transportation network
and to other timetables generated using other heuristics.
5.3.3
Parameter tuning
Parameter tuning refers to the task of finding the appropriate parameters of an algorithm
to optimize its performance. According to Huang et al. (2019), it consists of a complex task
and is traditionally approached by hand using trial and errors methods. The method used
is detailed in figure 4. It consists of a two layers approach with first a pre-processing and
then a Generate-Evaluate method.
Our heuristic is based on a meta-heuristic framework but is highly adapted to the
problem. It can be qualified of problem specific. We aim at solving any instance of the
IT-VSP and therefore it shouldn’t be instance specific. For this reason we will tune the
parameters on a set of instances and choose the ones that give the best performances over
this set. By doing this, we make the parameters problem-specific and not instance-specific.
In order to describe the parameter tuning method, we define the following notations:
• Let I be a set of instances used for the parameter tuning.
• Let P be the set of all the possible parameter values.
• Let p ∈P be a vector representing the parameters values.
Additionally, our heuristic includes a lot of stochasticity (in the destroy and random
repair methods). As a matter of fact, the outcomes of Heu-IT-VSP are the realisation of a
random variable. Most of the time we would like to know the expectancy of those random
variables. To address this problem we use a Monte-Carlo approach to calculate the average
performances of a set of parameters on an instance. Therefore, we will run N times our
heuristic on the same instance with the same parameters values.
The Heu-IT-VSP is made out of two different LNS. In order to break down the tuning
problem into smaller bites we choose to tune the two LNS separately.
This is done for
practical reasons and the following paragraphs will detail the tuning method for one LNS.
As the two LNS are very similar, the detailed method can be applied on both.

travel time usually highly correlates with the distance between the stops. The same proba-
bility is then affected to each scenarios. This probability is used in the model (26) to weight
the different scenarios. Here all the scenarios are given the same weight.

eral framework (parts 3.4.2, 3.5.1), the overall cost is separated into 6 different components
: the boarding cost, the onboard cost, the transfer cost, the delay penalty, the dwell cost
and the deadhead trip cost. The shares of the different costs are represented in the figure 8.

We can see that the most important cost is the boarding cost, which is linked to the
headway and the number of passenger waiting for the bus to come. According to Transport-
ministeriet et al. (2022), the value of time assigned to this component is cWT = 219 DKK/h,
corresponding to approximately 0.06 DKK/s. The same value of time is allocated to the
transfer cost as transferring passengers are also waiting for the next bus to come. Every
transferring passenger is also a boarding passenger of the feeder line it onboards initialy.
This is why the boarding cost will represents a higher share than the transfer cost. From
the simulation of passengers’ demand conducted in part 6.1.4, the proportion of transfer
passengers reaches approximately 39% of the total boarding passengers. This leads to a
smaller representation of the transfer cost compared to the boarding cost even though it is
not negligible.
Another big influence on the cost is the delay penalty. Indeed, according to Trans-
portministeriet et al. (2022), its value of time is set to cDT = 329 DKK/h which is around
0.09 DKK/s. The weight is higher than the boarding and transferring weight, which is con-
sistent with the passenger’s experience. Indeed, the passenger is experiencing an additional
unexpected waiting time due to arrival time delays at the stop which is perceived more neg-
atively.
However, the delay penalty is also expected to be smaller than the boarding cost. In-
deed, in our model, we assume that the arrival of passengers at the stop follow a uniform
distribution and thus, the passengers are waiting on average half of the headway (3.4.1).
The headway at each stop in this case is approximately 12.5 minutes whereas the average

delay at each stop for the initial timetable is only 3.2 minutes. As a result, the boarding cost
represents a higher share in the total cost than the delay penalty even though the weights
assigned are in favor of the delays.
The last big component is the dwell cost. According to our definition in 3.5.1, it repre-
sents the additional operating cost for adding dwell time at a stops. The weight assigned to
this component is 0.1 DKK/s. It includes the salary of the driver, the fuel used as well and
other operational costs due to the extra dwell time.
The two other costs have less impact on the total cost of a timetable. The onboard
cost represents the travel time experienced by the passengers. The value given by Trans-
portministeriet et al. (2022) is cTT = 0.03 DKK/s . This time is perceived less negatively
by the passengers compared to the other waiting or delay time. Finally, the deadhead trip
cost accounts for only 3% of the total timetable cost. In the section 6.2.1, we will see that
this cost doesn’t change very much between the different optimised timetables, and as it is
only a small part of the overall cost, it will be set aside.

with only one scenario the planned and actual departure time are necessarily equal and
therefore no delay can be found.
The stochastic timetables seem to share the same behaviour. Indeed, it is found that
each timetable has a considerable increase in dwell cost and onboard cost while reducing
the delay penalty and the boarding cost. The transfer cost slightly raised but it remains
very low compared to the other types of costs. The decrease of nearly 30% of the overall
timetable costs is still satisfying for the stochastic models.

only depends on the number of passengers boarding and the headway at the stop consid-

boarding costs implies smaller headways in the timetables. An analysis of the headways is
conducted in part 6.2.2 and explains further the reasons for this decrease. Another similarity
is the reduction in the delay penalty for the stochastic timetables while the deterministic

of time. Indeed, the value of time in the case of a delay is three times higher than the value
of time for travelling (associated with the onboard cost).

the dwell cost are directly linked there is no surprise in finding the same trend in both costs.

the stochastic timetables, the worse travel times were taken. As a result, and following the
analysis on interlining 3.7.1, more buses were able to perform another trip on the same line
in the deterministic optimisation compared to the stochastic one. As we can see in table 11,

6.3
Number of scenarios and solution quality
We performed an analysis to evaluate the influence of the number of scenarios in the
training set Ωt. Thus, to obtain comparable results, the initial timetable and the maximum
run time are kept constant. The number of scenarios tested were {4,7,10,15,20,50}.
The following steps were applied to choose the scenarios :
• 10 scenarios were generated randomly, and the first one is set to be the reference.
• For the 9 remaining scenarios, the average and the standard deviation of the difference
compared to the reference are calculated
• The 3 most disparate scenarios are chosen with the reference scenario to make the set
of 4 scenarios
• Following the same procedure 3 more scenarios are added to make the set of 7 scenarios
• For the other sets, we added random scenarios, as the more scenario you have, the less
likely it is to get the same scenarios.
It is important to note that the set of 4 scenarios is included in the other sets ; we
added scenarios to fill the other sets. This prevents from having different results due to the
randomness. Hence, the additional scenarios are considered as added information for the
optimisation process.

scenarios. From this point of view, the stochastic timetables outperforms the deterministic
and the initial timetable. Indeed, the total cost is reduced by more than 15% compared to
only 13.5% for the deterministic timetable.
To face the unpredictability of the travel times, stochastic timetables added massive
amount of dwell times at nearly every stops. This leads to augmented onboard and dwell
cost, but it reduced consequently the transfer cost and the delay penalty, two components
with higher weights than the onboard cost and dwell cost. The analysis of the different
outperformance index of transfers and delays also shows that the stochastic timetables are
more reliable for passengers than the deterministic timetable.
Overall, this part gives a clear answer to the part of the research question we intended
to answer. Using a stochastic programming model to solve the IT-VSP with an MIP solver
improves the solution in all the defined success criteria. Nonetheless, the difference between
the deterministic and the stochastic timetables isn’t very high. As stochastic optimisation
requires more time and more data collection and thus, comes with a higher cost. This is
why, the deterministic approach does not become irrelevant from a cost perspective. How-
ever, from the passengers’ standpoint, the stochastic timetables are very interesting since
the transfers and the delays are considerably improved. In practise it results in an enhanced
passenger experience. Our model does not account for the demand’s elasticity (c.f. 3.2.2),
but in the reality the demand should react positively to a better passenger experience. If the
infrastructure allows it, it should lead to an increased usage of public transports and pave
the way for more sustainable and pleasant cities.

with only six different trips passing through five transfer stops.
Thus, on a real world

time will also increase.
Moreover, in this model, the departure time are considered as
continuous variables.
Although convenient from a mathematical perspective, continuous
values are impossible to handle in practice. Indeed, it is already unthinkable to ask bus
drivers to leave the stop at an exact to the second time. In practice the transportation
planner would give departure times at the scale of the minute. Then, there is a need to
confirm the obtained results in a more practical environment.

7.1
The Greater Copenhagen area network
The Greater Copenhagen area is equipped with a network of suburban buses and trains.
Faster and with fewer stops than the regular buses or metros, this network aims at providing
connections between Copenhagen and its suburbs as well as between the different suburban
areas.
This case is the result of a collaboration with Movia, a Danish public transport agency
coverring the Greater Copenhagen area. It is a public transport agency owned by local
authorities such as regions and municipalities. Movia is responsible to plan and manage
bus and some rail lines in Zealand and particularly in the Copenhagen urban area. The
company doesn’t operate the lines, but subcontracts them to bus operators such as Keolis
or Arriva. With over 450 different lines in the country, Movia is one of the largest Danish
public transportation agency. According to Movia (2023), around 180 million passengers are
using the agency’s transportation services each year.
For our thesis, the analysis is restricted to 8 different bus macro-lines operated by
Movia. The different names of these macro-lines are {150S; 200S; 250S; 300S; 350S; 400S;
500S; 600S} and the set is labeled S-Buses. In our study, we also want to optimise the
transfers with trains. They are managed by DSB, the Danish national train operator. DSB
operates regional or long distance trains as well as a commuter rail system in the Greater
Copenhagen area. This urban-suburban rail network contains different lines gathered in the
set called the S-Trains. The transfers between S-Buses and S-Trains are important for
Movia because the trains are massively used in Copenhagen. Indeed, according to the DSB
Annual Report (2022), S-Trains were used for more than 98 millions of journeys in 2022.
The other reason is the complementarity between S-Buses and S-Trains. In the visuali-
sation of the network found in 18, it can be seen that the S-Trains, in dashed lines, are
heading in all the directions, whereas the S-Buses are connecting the train stops together,
especially for the 500S and the 600S lines. As a result, optimising these transfers will al-
low passengers to experience faster journeys, leading to an enhance customer satisfaction.
However, as the S-trains’ timetables are not under Movia’s scope, those will be treated as
parameters and none of them can be modified.

Another advantage of reducing the time window between 9am to 15pm is the elimina-

The next step is to set a transfer threshold. Indeed, in the Transfers file, a lot of values
are small for the transferring passengers from one line to another. Due to the conversion
equation 40, if the daily amount of transferring passengers is low, then in our time window
it will be even lower. By setting a transfer threshold, those transfers will not be taken into
account, which will reduce the number of stops considered in the optimisation. Thus, the
impact of the transfer threshold on the number of stops should be assessed. Figure 19 shows
the evolution of the number of transfer stops against the transfer threshold. As observed in
the Transfers file, the majority of stops have a small amounts of transferring passengers.
The transfer threshold chosen is 10 passengers per day, which corresponds to a minimum
of 2.5 transferring passengers during our time window. This choice enables to reduce the
amount of stops considered in our problem to 52. But, we obviously keep starting and ending
terminals for trip completion purpose. As a result, the number of stops left after this process
is 54 because two terminals are not considered as transfer stops in our case.

7.5.1
A problem-specific heuristic
A key characteristic of a good heuristic algorithm is to be able to deliver similar re-
sults on a wide range of instances from the same problem. It is said that the algorithm is
instance-independent if it delivers similar results whatever the instance is. This way, we avoid
over-customization to specific cases. This subsection will focus on assessing the instance-
independence of Heu-IT-VSP. But first, let us define two levels of instance independence.
Definition 7.1 (Total instance-independence). Capacity of an optimization algorithm to
deliver similar performances no matter the instance given.
Definition 7.2 (Partial instance-independence). Capacity of an optimization algorithm to
deliver similar performances across instances of the same size.
Of course, a totally instance-independent algorithm is also a partially instance-independent
algorithm but the equivalence does not hold.

methods, each instance is optimized several times. Then we calculate the average total cost

Discussions and future work
8
Discussions and future work
8.1
About the model
The mathematical model expressed in section 3 relies on several assumptions. One key
postulate is that the travel times are mutually independent. This simplifies an already com-
plex system and allows to account for very local events such as local traffic, other road user’s
behavior or accidents. While justified, this approach can’t capture the macro-phenomenon
happening at the scale of the city. For instance the peak hours, holiday seasons or extreme
weather events can’t be considered while having independent travel time. This is why, one
could look into introducing themed scenarios. For example one scenario could represent the
travel times under a very heavy rain or snow.
Another assumption is that passengers’ demand is fixed. While this assumption is not
unrealistic in the short run, it might not be in the long run. Indeed, by optimizing the
timetable the passenger’s experience is improved and the citizens might be more likely to
use the public transports. Therefore, it could be interesting to include some elasticity in the
demand. Alternatively, one could look into introducing stochasticity in the demand as we
did for the travel times.
As explained in part 3.7, real-world instances have a tremendous amount of variables.
Several options could be considered to reduce this number. One could be to define a set of
possible transfers: for each transfer request, one would calculate the trips from the target
line for which it makes sense to allow a transfer. Indeed, if the target trip arrives at the stop
at 12h30, it makes no physical sense to consider a transfer to the trip from the target line
that left at 9h50.
The results from 7 showed that the boarding cost represents a crushing majority of the
total cost. The boarding cost is linked to the actual headway and the number of passenger
boarding. The headway cannot change much since more buses can’t be added. And in our
case, due to the lack of precision in the data, the number of boarding passenger at a stop is
the same througout the day. As a result, the boarding cost appears as a fixed cost. However,
with more detailed data, one could see the headway being adapted to fit the demand. There-
fore it would be very interesting to consider an instance in which the passengers demand is
not constant throughout the day.
8.2
About the heuristic
In its structure, the heuristic simplifies the problem by considering the VSP as a pa-
rameter. Although this choice is justified, it could be an idea to modify the algorithm to
account for it. The consequences of such a choice will be twofold. First, the problem will
be made much larger and therefore more complex to solve. Second, more flexibility is given
and the optimized timetable can potentially find better solutions.
Page 99 of 111
==PAGE:101==
8
Discussions and future work
Our heuristic algorithm is a Large Neighborhood Search. It contains two destroy meth-
ods and 4 repair procedures. An immediate idea to improve our heuristic would be to add
an adaptative layer to turn the algorithm into a Adaptative Large Neighborhood Search.
This layer generally consists in giving a score to each destroy and repair method. Then,
each destroy or repair method is given a probability of selection based on its score. At each
iteration, the algorithm samples a method based on the calculated probabilities. Ideally,
the probabilities are calculated after each iteration. ANLS showed good results on various
operation research problems and it could therefore be a good idea to implement it.
The parameter tuning was performed on a set of instances from the Movia case. Despite
being different (in size and content), all of them originates from the same data. Therefore,
they share some characteristics such as the operational settings (costs, constraints...). One
could try to tune the algorithm using the same method but on a different set of instances
originating from different sources.
When it comes to the practical implementation of the algorithm, we ran on one major
hurdle. By nature, a LNS requires to destroy and repair a solution. However if the new solu-
tion is not accepted by the acceptance criteria, a old version of the solution should still exist
in memory. By using Julia as the programming language chosen for the implementation, this
requires to create a deep copy of the solution. This means that several times per iteration,
a new object in memory is created. Unfortunately, the data structure chosen is extremely
slow for such purposes. As a consequence, our algorithm spends a tremendous amount of
time deep copying the solutions. This explains the relatively low amount of iterations of the
heuristic in part 4. We believe this can be the biggest source of improvement for the heuristic
and it could be done by simplifying the data structure to its maximum and optimizing it for
the deep copy function in Julia.
8.3
About the results
In both the small-scale instance and the Movia case, the best timetable was the stochas-
tic timetable using the deterministic timetable as the initial solution. This result makes
perfect sense as this timetable called STO_DET was optimized for a longer time than any
other but it shows that there are some benefits of using a two-layers optimization. From
this postulate, one could assess into how to divide efficiently the available time between the
deterministic and the stochastic optimization.

9
Conclusion
In this thesis, we model and optimize a stochastic version of IT-VSP. Then, we bench-
mark two approaches: a deterministic and a stochastic one.
To do so, we compare the
performances of each method when the problem is solved by a MIP solver based on a small-
instance. Then we collate in the same way using a heuristic on a real-world instance provided
by Movia, a Copenhagen’s public transportation agency.
The IT-VSP is formulated as an MIP problem that captures the stochasticity of the
travel times leveraging stochastic programming. A heuristic algorithm was developed under
the framework of the Large Neighborhood Search meta-heuristic framework. Two random-
ized destroy methods were created as well as two greedy and random repair methods. The
parameters were tuned using a pre-processing and a brute-force approach. The analysis of
the heuristic performances showcases the appropriate behaviour of the algorithm. Indeed,
it demonstrates strong evidence of partial instance independence as well as an acceptable
trajectory in the objective value evolution throughout the iterations.
Solving the model on a small-scale instance using a MIP solver showed that the deter-
ministic timetable (referred as DET) outperforms the stochastic timetables (referred as STO)
when the set of training scenarios is used for the assessment. This is mainly due to two
reasons. First, the deterministic approach is less complex and the solver can reach better
solutions. Second, DET fits entirely the given scenario thus leaving no place for flexibility
and adaptation. This is why the assessment is performed on a different set of scenarios. On
this set it is found that the stochastic framework outperforms the deterministic in the three
chosen criteria. First, in the expected total cost, DET improves the total cost by 13.5% while
STO can bring it down by 16.4%. Second, it is showed that STO reduced costs than DET 90%
of the time. Finally, STO also performs better when it comes to the reliability with 90%
quantile for the total cost decreased by 17.2% against 14.3% for DET. Additionnaly, a deep
analysis demonstrates how the stochastic helps catering uncertainty and thus reducing the
delays and the transfer times.
Solving the model on the Movia case showed the benefits of using stochastic program-
ming in a real case.
A similar overfitting behaviour for DET is witnessed validating the
observation made on the small scale instance. Due to the lack of precision in the data and
the operational set-up of the case, a crushing part of the cost is actually a fixed cost. It is
then removed from the total cost in the following results. In terms of expected total cost,
STO can reduce the cost by 6.5% whereas DET only provides a 3.8% improvement. Regarding
the outperformance index, STO results in lower costs than DET for all the scenarios. Finally,

Local Search
The local search (LS) is one of the most fundamental meta-heuristic. The idea is to
iteratively improve a feasible solution by exploring the other solutions considered as "close"
to the current solution. To go further in details let us first define some notations.
• Let π be an instance of an optimization problem. In particular we note c the cost
function of the instance.
• Let x be a feasible solution for the π
• Let N : π, x 7→N(π, x) be a function associating a set of solutions of π neighboring x.
N(π, x) is then defined as the neighborhood of x.
• Let Step : N(π, x) 7→xN be a function selecting an solution from the neighborhood
N(π, x). Typically, it takes the best or neighbor according to a given criteria (cost for
example).
• Let accept : x1, x2 7→b be a Boolean function indicating if solution x1 is chosen over
solution x2. It is called the acceptance criteria.
Algorithm 9 Local Search (minimization problem)
1: Input: π: instance, x: feasible solution
2: Output: xB: best found solution
3: xB ←x
4: repeat
5:
xN ←Step(N(π, x))
6:
if accept(xN, x) then
7:
x ←xN
8:
end if
9:
if c(xN) < c(xB) then
10:
xB ←xN
11:

12: until termination criteria met
With the above mentioned notation, we define the general algorithm of a local search
for a minimization problem in 9. The acceptance criteria has a central role in the algorithm,
indeed, it "directs" or "guide" the search through the iteration. In the primary version of the
local search, the greedy acceptance criteria is used. It means that the neighbouring candi-
date xN is accepted only if it outperforms the current best solution xB (i.e. c(xN) < c(xB)).
In that case the algorithm is also called the hill-climber (even if in our case of a minimization
problem it should be the hill-descender). Despite its simplicity and efficiency, the hill-climber
has one major issue: it is might happen that the neighbour selected does not improve the

solution. In that case, the local search ends because it reaches a local optimum (in our case
minimum) and cannot escape it. To tackle this challenge, Kirkpatrick et al. (1983) defined
Simulated Annealing (SA) methods by changing to an acceptance criteria which can accept
non-improving solutions with a certain probability. Many other methods were proposed to
escape the local minimum among other one can cite Iterated Local Search (ILS), Tabu Search
(TS) or Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) which will be further developed in 4.2.
The termination criteria in line 12 is expressed in general terms but, in practise it always
consists of a certain amount of computation time elapsed. In the case of the hill-climber,
the algorithm also stops when it reaches a local optimium.
One critical element of a local search is the neighborhood function. Indeed, it defines
the neighborhoods to explore and if the generated neighborhoods are too small (not enough
neighbors), then there is a risk of not finding any acceptable neighbor (especially in the case of
the hill climber). But, generating and exploring a large neighborhood can be computationally
expensive and then limit the number of iterations that can potentiallly be done. Therefore, a
trade-off has to be found and a lot of efforts are put into finding fast neighborhood functions
or finding synergies with other part of the algorithm (such as the cost evaluation in line 9
for example).

Glossary
Definition 9.1 (Journey pattern). A journey pattern is a route or more precisely a sequence
of stops that vehicles serve.
Definition 9.2 (Trip). In the context of transportation planning, we define a trip as a single
instance of a vehicle’s travel between a starting terminal and an ending terminal. It has the
following characteristics.
• A journey pattern describing the stops to serve.
• A starting terminal which is the vehicle’s first stop.
• A ending terminal which is the vehicle’s last stop.
• A planned departure from all the stops in the sequence.
Definition 9.3 (Trip-stop). A trip-stop is the service of a stop by a trip.
Definition 9.4 (Directed line). A directed line refers to the set of trips that have the following
characteristics:
• Same operating conditions (cost, headway...).
• Geographically similar journey patterns.
For practical reasons, we will use the word line to refer to a directed line.
Definition 9.5 (Macro-line). A macro-line refers to a set of directed lines for which their
trips share the same set of stops.
Definition 9.6 (Dwell time). The dwell time is the time that a vehicle spend idling at a
stop. The necessary time to serve a stop (also called service time) is not considered as a
dwell time.
Definition 9.7 (Travel time). The travel time is the time used by a vehicle to go from one
stop to another.
Definition 9.8 (Deadhead trip). A deadhead trip refers to a vehicle travel from a depot or
a terminal to a depot or terminal. During a deadhead trip a vehicle is not in service and no
passengers are onboard.
Definition 9.9 (Turnaround time). After finishing a trip, some time is required before being
able to move to the next trip’s starting terminal. This time is defined at the turnaround time.
Among other things, it includes the time to clean and reconfigure the vehicle, the driver’s
break, the time to physically turn around the vehicle.
Definition 9.10 (Feeder line). In the context of a transfer, the feeder line is the directed
line from which the passengers transfer.

Definition 9.11 (Target line). In the context of a transfer, the target line is the directed
line to which the passengers transfer.
Definition 9.12 (Transfer request). A transfer request refers to the passengers’ will to
transfer from a feeder line’s trip to a target line at a specific stop.
Definition 9.13 (Transfer impact). The transfer impact is the product of the transfer time
and the number of passengers using the transfer.
