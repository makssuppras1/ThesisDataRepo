Abstract
Successful deployment of autonomous agents is contingent on the process of reliable
pose estimation from onboard sensors.
In the absence of a global positioning system,
visual-inertial odometry has proven to endow robots with outstanding real-time localiza-
tion capabilities, as long as the observed scene contains enough static elements that can
be consistently reobserved. In maritime environments, however, the dynamic sea surface
is the only constant source of visual information. With no approach currently available to
handle such a situation, this thesis expands the boundaries of VIO by allowing dynamic
structures to be included in the estimation process. For point features located on the
water plane, the assumption of rectilinear motion is adopted, in accordance with evidence
obtained from the real world. Using the state-of-the-art MSCKF localization framework,
suitable replacements are proposed for its triangulation and measurement update build-
ing blocks. While the presented method of robust trajectory reconstruction is shown to
give good results, pose correction based on the distance between an image point and its
reprojected trajectory is not achieved under general circumstances. Modifications made
to a high-quality implementation of the original filter are seen to cause divergence during
tests conducted with a UAV in a simulated dynamic environment. In a separate vision-
only experiment, the nonlinear optimization of the suggested residual is seen to perform
well, as long as trajectories fulfill the conditions of non-parallelism and low uncertainty.
Further theoretical and practical studies of this novel VIO system are required to give
a definitive answer regarding its feasibility. Nonetheless, the contributions made are rel-
evant in the context of progressing towards eliminating the static world assumption in
visual localization.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Artificial autonomous agents, commonly referred to as robots, are nowadays om-
nipresent in society and industry, owing to the constant technological progress being made
towards improving computation power. From the manipulators working on assembly lines,
to the self-driving vehicles facilitating transportation, the list of use cases for intelligent
systems goes on. With the constantly increasing complexity of the scenarios in which
these systems are expected to operate, the research field of robotics has become richer
and more relevant than ever.
In the recent decade, drones, formally known as UAVs
(Unmanned Aerial Vehicles), in particular have seen a dramatic increase in popularity
within the branch of mobile robotics, mainly because they are 6-DoF (degrees of freedom)
devices, perfectly suited for deployment in 3D (three-dimensional) environments. When
equipped with suitable sensors, the versatility of a drone is unmatched by any other type
of autonomous vehicle [1].
Visual Localization
Arguably the most fundamental problem in the world of robotic perception is localiza-
tion [2]. The rationale is obvious: for any tasks to be carried out autonomously, the agent
must be aware of its position relative to its surroundings. It is quite diﬀicult to imagine
how an entity could navigate between any two points A and B without knowing, at all
times during the motion, how much and in which direction it has moved with respect to
its initial location, A. Unsurprisingly, the matter has been extensively studied in the lit-
erature, and thus, a very wide array of techniques, employing different sensor setups have
been developed. In aerial robotics, for instance, where clear weight constraints must be
met, visual localization approaches emerged as the strategy of choice, since modern cam-
eras are easily portable as compared to laser scanners [3]. Of course, there is no general
method, camera-based or not, that can be simply applied in all possible environments,
with most approaches being limited in their scope of applicability [4]. Obviously, cameras
cannot be used in very poor lighting conditions, and in areas where GNSS (Global Nav-
igation Satellite System) input is not available, receivers of this type of information are
rendered useless [5].
Visual odometry (abbreviated as VO) is a term coined by Nister in [6], and is defined as
the estimation of the ego-motion of a platform carrying one or multiple cameras, only from
visual data. Since its first successful real-time implementation, it has become the de facto
localization method for most autonomous robot systems, with exceptional importance
in GNSS-denied environments.
In reality, visual odometry is a particular case of the
more general computer vision problem of structure from motion (SfM), in which both the
camera poses and the 3D map of an observed scene must be recovered (offline) from a
set of potentially unordered images. In VO, however, the visual stream contains frames
ordered in time, and the main goal is online pose estimation [7].

Introduction
An important distinction is also made between visual odometry and visual Simulta-
neous Localization and Mapping (V-SLAM, or simply SLAM). In the former, the focus
is on doing only incremental pose estimation, i.e.: the current frame is most often being
compared only to the previous image. SLAM contains the additional component of loop
closure, or long-term data association, leveraging a place recognition module to ensure
previously visited locations are detected. The implications of these differences can be vi-
sualized in Fig. 1.1. With odometry, only local consistency is achieved for the map and the
trajectory, since points B and C are not identified as being near each other. Nonetheless,
using SLAM, a globally consistent result is obtained, and the correct topology and motion
are fully recovered [4, 7, 8].

Visual-Inertial Fusion
Regardless of the estimation scheme, camera-reliant localization might degrade due to
high-speed movement, high dynamic range (HDR) or motion blur [9]. One solution iden-
tified to cope with such unwanted circumstances is to combine the visual information with
high-frequency proprioceptive inertial measurements, obtained from an Inertial Measure-
ment Unit (IMU). In the literature, this type of fusion paradigm is denoted Visual-Inertial
Odometry (VIO) [3, 9] or Visual-Inertial Navigation (VIN) [8, 10], and it is shown to
give great results in the absence of global positioning readings, because the two sensors
complement each other, leading to an overall increase in accuracy and robustness [11, 5].
Dynamic Scenes
Perhaps the main shortcoming of all visual localization algorithms stems from what
is referred to as the ”static world assumption”, according to which any displacement per-
ceived from time-adjacent images is considered to be attributed solely to the motion of the
camera-carrying agent [8]. Such a strong assumption holds true only in specific situations,
e.g.: indoor environments devoid of people, but it is quite unlikely to be encountered in
the very challenging real-world scenarios most mobile robots are facing. In the scientific
community, it is well-known that all classic methods are susceptible to non-negligible per-
formance decay, or even utter failure, in dynamic scenes [12, 13]. A very simple example
aimed to illustrate how pose estimation is affected when applying vision-based algorithms
on observations of dynamic objects is given in Fig. 1.2. Essentially, what happens is that
the motion of a detected moving point is incorrectly interpreted as motion of the camera.
In this specific case, however, since the agent is actually not moving, the addition of an
IMU generally suﬀices to prevent such a faulty estimation [14].
In partially-static cases, the motion ambiguity can be circumvented by detecting the
moving objects and eliminating them from the estimation process [12].
Progress has
been also made towards the joint estimation of the agent’s ego-motion and the dynamic
components of the 3D scene, but reliable algorithms are not currently mature [15]. Thus, it
can be confidently stated that the problem of localization in non-static worlds remains an
active area of research in the VO/SLAM literature, with many aspects yet to be explored.

Mohamed Bin Zayed International Robotics Challenge
All the work presented in this manuscript has been conducted as part of a larger
project in which the Technical University of Denmark is involved, namely the biennial
MBZIRC (Mohamed Bin Zayed International Robotics Challenge) competition. For the
2023 edition, the organizers put forward the ”Maritime Grand Challenge”, a set of tasks
participating teams are expected to complete within a predefined amount of time, using
a fleet of collaborating autonomous aerial and surface vehicles. This collaboration effort
should result in the successful retrieval of items located onboard a subset of a given larger

Introduction
Moreover, depending on the nature of the robotic platform used, additional limitations
are set vis-à-vis the available range of sensors. In the case of UAVs, only combinations
of the following devices are allowed in the competition: camera, IMU, magnetometer, 3D
Lidar (Light Detection and Ranging), and RF (Radio Frequency) range sensor [16]. With
none of the vehicles in the fleet having knowledge about its own position, it makes sense to
reason about how only one agent might be able to independently infer such information.
Accordingly, for a particular drone, a camera-IMU tandem is taken into consideration in
this project.
1.2
Goal and Delimitations
The main goal of the thesis is formulated as the identification of a solution for the
localization of a UAV flying across the sea, and equipped only with a camera and an IMU.
Devising an estimation scheme able to cope with the possibility of having no fixed reference
in sight (no shore or stationary vessels) is attempted. To tackle the problem at hand within
the given time constraints, a particular sub-scenario must be selected and analyzed. First,
the separation between the static and dynamic parts of the image is assumed. In other
words, it is known a priori which sections of each camera frame contain features moving in
a chosen inertial system of reference. Second, light conditions are presumed appropriate
for applying visual algorithms. Furthermore, a maritime environment resembling the one
in the top-right of Fig. 1.3 is considered, i.e.: the weather conditions are mild, with no
large waves or strong winds. Finally, only the pose estimation aspect of the localization
is examined, with no objective being set for creating a map of the surroundings.
Given the extensive body of research dedicated to visual-inertial odometry, the starting
point of the investigations conducted in this work is set to be one of the well-known
existing techniques. Hence, the research question that determines the aim of the thesis is:
Can established VIO localization methods be adapted to an inherently dynamic maritime
scenario?
1.3
Thesis Outline
This section gives a brief explanation of how the present text has been compartmental-
ized. First, Chapter 2 offers a review of the developments in the visual and visual-inertial
localization literature. Then, Chapter 3 establishes the theoretical background necessary
for understanding and building a VIO pipeline. This is followed by Chapter 4, which goes
into the details of a particular VIO algorithm known to work in static environments. In
Chapter 5, an extension of the algorithm previously introduced is proposed to tackle the
localization problem in a maritime environment. Results for the conducted localization
experiments are shown is Chapter 6. Finally, Chapter 7 concludes the thesis and discusses
the challenges to be addressed in further work.

Chapter 2
Related Work
Throughout this chapter, a general overview of the existing visual and visual-inertial
localization schemes is provided, building on top of the background already introduced.
A high-level description of relevant and state-of-the-art pipelines is made, and one special
section is dedicated to the methods of pose estimation in dynamic environments.
2.1
Visual Odometry
When looking at purely visual approaches, arguably the main separation is drawn
according to the way in which visual information is processed.
Specifically, there are
feature-based (or indirect) methods that compute intermediate representations from senor
readings, and direct methods, that skip this step [13, 18].
Indirect Methods
A typical odometry pipeline is one having four components: feature detection, feature
matching (or tracking), motion estimation, and local optimization, as depicted in Fig. 2.1.
The first two blocks rely on the ability to extract and pair salient keypoints between suc-
cessive frames, employing image analysis algorithms. Some points that can be consistently
identified are corners or blobs [19]. In the former category, popular detectors are Har-
ris [20], Shi-Tomasi [21], FAST (Features from Accelerated Segment Test) [22] and ORB
(Oriented FAST and Rotated BRIEF) [23], while in the latter SIFT (Scale Invariant Fea-
ture Transform) [24] and SURF (Speeded Up Robust Features) [25] are worth mentioning.
Matching is then done appropriately by comparing feature descriptors, or by tracking the
point with correlation-based search techniques, such as the Kanade-Lucas-Tomasi (KLT)
tracker [21], or dense optical flow [4].

As a last step, refinement is performed over the last m frames to decrease the drift in the

as the nonlinear optimization problem of reprojection error minimization. Usually, the
procedure is utilized to not only improve the pose estimates (in that case it is actually
called motion-only BA), but also the locations of the 3D features in the map, also known as
landmarks [7]. Global bundle adjustment over all the past frames is also possible, though
it incurs significantly more computation [4]. The optimization problem is usually solved
via the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm [19].
Nister is the first to implement a standard VO pipeline in [6]. His work inspires many
other algorithms, including SLAM methods. As a first example, PTAM (Parallel Tracking
and Mapping) [29] is based on FAST features with patch descriptors, and introduces
mapping and bundle adjustment on spatially-distributed keyframes. In [30], Mur-Artal
et. al. developed ORB-SLAM, one of the most popular monocular localization systems,
which is also keyframe-based, but unlike PTAM, uses the ORB descriptor, implements
loop closure through bag-of-words place recognition, and is able to function in real-time,
even in large environments. The work is then extended to stereo cameras, through ORB-
SLAM2 [31].
Apart from points, image line features can also be extracted, especially in human-built
environments [32], although they might be more easily occluded and consequently more
diﬀicult to match [19]. The most prominent detector-descriptor pair appears to be the
one formed by LSD (Line Segment Detector) and LBD (Line Band Descriptor) [33, 34].
Compared to the point feature scenario, different formulations of the camera model and
reprojection error are necessary, and also special algorithms for line matching (or tracking)
and triangulation. Most of the existing pipelines described in the literature use both points
and lines, but an entire Ph.D. thesis on the topic of localization with lines only can be
found at [32].
Direct Methods
Most of these feature-based (or indirect) approaches are effective even in the presence
of significant view-point changes or light-varying conditions, but are only applicable if
there is enough texture in the acquired images. This issue has inspired the development
of direct methods, in which the cost to be minimized is the photometric error, i.e.: sum
of pixel intensity differences [35]. Notwithstanding the highly nonlinear and non-convex
nature of this metric [13], the tactic has been implemented successfully in several instances.
Depending on the proportion of the pixels included in the error computation, three classes
of methods (see Fig. 2.2) can be distinguished: dense (the entire visual information in the
image is exploited), semi-dense (only high-intensity gradient pixels are used) and sparse
(consider only specific pixels in areas of interest) [35, 36].
DTAM (Dense Tracking and Mapping) [38] is one influential example of direct monoc-
ular VO methods, and is able to construct dense maps in real-time leveraging a GPU
(Graphics Processing Unit). An important semi-dense solution is the one suggested in
[39], as it allows for both pose tracking and large-scale map creation on platforms having
only a CPU (Central Processing Unit) available. Termed LSD-SLAM (Large Scale Direct
SLAM), the method makes use of keyframe-based pose-graph optimization and loop clo-
sure. Two of the authors of LSD-SLAM also developed DSO (Direct Sparse Odometry)
[18], an algorithm that uses sparse corner-based image alignment, and photometric depth

Localization in Marine Environments
Since the application for the localization solution to be developed in this work is tied
to the sea, it is worth mentioning briefly some of the existing solutions tailored to marine
environments. The main thing to note here is that no work on visual odometry seems to
have been carried out in this case for flying robots, with most of the literature focused on
AUVs (Autonomous Underwater Vehicles). An EKF-based VIO system including pressure
measurements is devised in [40], whereas Caraffa evaluates in [35] how well deep-learning-
based monocular VO can work in natural sub-aquatic mediums.
2.2
Visual-Inertial Odometry
It was mentioned in Chapter 1 that the addition of an IMU improves the accuracy
and robustness of standard visual localization. In the monocular case, the presence of the
inertial information also resolves the scale ambiguity [7]. The highly desirable properties
of VIN prompted a deluge of attention to be given to this fusion subclass.
A first classification of the methods concerns precisely how the exteroceptive and pro-
prioceptive sensory data is fused.
Accordingly, there are loosely-coupled and tightly-
coupled techniques [11], for which graphical representations have been included in Fig. 2.3.
In loosely-coupled VIO, the visual and inertial components function independently at dif-
ferent rates and output their own estimates of the motion, which are then fused, most of
the time with a statistical filter, such as EKF (Extended Kalman Filter) or UKF (Un-
scented Kalman Filter) [11]. While this allows for computational eﬀiciency, direct use
of established VO pipelines, and system modularity, i.e.: one sensing modality could be
readily exchanged with another, loss of information occurs. For this reason, such fusion
structures are almost never pursued [41, 42].
Tightly-coupled VIO is the area on which most of the research has focused, since the
results obtained were found to be superior. A single estimator is utilized to directly fuse
the raw measurements from both sensors and generate a unified pose estimate, taking into
account the correlations between all the involved states. Two branches have emerged in
this direction: filter-based approaches, and optimization-based methods [43].
Filter-based Methods
In this class of pose estimation, IMU measurements are employed to propagate the
state (position and orientation of the sensor) forward in time, and keypoints extracted
from the visual feed are geneally utilized in the correction phase, through a minimization

The photometric error between multi-level image patches is utilized to define the update
rule for the filter, and point landmarks, parameterized in a robocentric fashion, are kept
in the state.
Optimization-based Methods
Compared to the filter formulation introduced in the preceding paragraph, the methods
based on optimization (also known as batch methods) define a nonlinear cost function in
terms of both the IMU and camera measurements, and iteratively minimize it at each
iteration [3, 43]. The repeated error reduction limits the detrimental effects of linearization,
which are ineluctable with filtering solutions, but this higher accuracy comes at the expense
of higher computation demands [43].
One of the most prominent implementations of such paradigms is OKVIS (Open
Keyframe-based Visual-Inertial SLAM) [43], which achieves real-time state estimation by
only performing the optimization over a fixed-sized window of keyframes. This pipeline
maintains environmental landmarks in the state, and was designed to operate on stereo
camera setups. An example of a direct optimization-based VIO pipeline is the approach
from [42], developed by the authors of LSD-SLAM. It is also keyframe-based, and only
suitable for stereo cameras, but the error minimized contains photometric information and
inertial energy terms. The semi-dense reconstruction results achievable with this method
are impressive indeed.
It should be stated that, as it happens in the purely visual case, line features are
sometimes also included in the VIO localization process, usually along with points. Ex-
ample pipelines are [48], which employs the MSCKF, and [49], where the authors opt for
nonlinear optimization.

2.3
Localization in Dynamic Environments
It is understood that the static world assumption must hold for all the methods re-
viewed in the preceding sections to function as intended. Nonetheless, visual localization
systems developed with the goal of maintaining accurate estimation in environments with
moving objects do exist.
One trend is the robustification of standard VO/SLAM against dynamic objects. Al-
cantarilla et. al. use thresholding of the motion priors computed from dense scene flow
[50], the 3D extension of optical flow, in a stereo VO setup to segment out moving objects
from the pose estimation [51]. Another available option is DynaSLAM [52], built on top of
ORB-SLAM2, which detects moving objects with a combination of multiple view geome-
try and deep learning, and in-paints the previously occluded image section with intensities
from past frames.
Another path pursued by some authors is the joint estimation of ego-motion and
static and dynamic structures in the scene.
In [53], VO is extended to Multi-motion
Visual Odometry (MVO), a method able to recover the motion of the individual rigid
objects observed in the images, additionally to that of the agent carrying a stereo or depth
camera. A variation of MVO is also proposed by Zhang in his Ph.D. thesis [13], in the
form of VDO-SLAM (visual object-aware dynamic SLAM), which is a pipeline for depth
cameras, showing slightly superior results when compared to the original formulation.
The main issue with these approaches is that they all require partially static parts of
the scene for ego-motion computation. In this thesis, what is sought is not a solution to
the additional task of object tracking per se, but rather an accurate camera localization
using the visual information dynamic objects supply. Because estimation under these cir-
cumstances does not seem to be a well-studied problem, certainly not in a marine setting,
there is clear motive for investigations being made towards its elucidation. In this endeav-
our, the MSCKF VIO algorithm from [44] is selected as the basis of the developments to
be made. The arguments for the choice of this particular method are the maturity of its
underlying Kalman filter formulation, and its capability to work very well in real-time,
even on resource-constrained systems [5].

3.1
Quaternions
Quaternions are 4-dimensional mathematical entities with one real part and three
imaginary components, introduced as an extension to standard complex numbers. More
specifically, a member q of the quaternion space H is defined as a linear combination:
q = qw + qxi + qyj + qzk
(3.1)
where qw, qx, qy, qz ∈R, and i, j, k are hyperimaginary numbers associated with
the three basic quaternions.
If qw = 0, q is referred to as a pure quaternion, which
belongs to the space Hp. Analogous to the manner in which a complex number of unit
length describes a planar rotation, unit quaternions have the ability to eﬀiciently encode
rotations in 3D, while also avoiding gimbal lock, hence their popularity in engineering,
and robotics specifically. In the technical reports [54] and [55], the authors present and
prove many other mathematical properties of quaternions in exhaustive detail.
Based
on these sources and [56], a succinct overview is given here for the reader’s convenience.
Importantly, there are 12 possible choices for determining a quaternion, with arguably the
two most popular being the original Hamiltionian formulation, and the more recent JPL
convention, proposed by NASA’s Jet Propulsion Laboratory. The latter is preferred in
[55], and it is adopted from this time forward in the present thesis as well, in accordance
with well-known algorithms for visual-inertial navigation such as [3, 10, 44]. Thus, most
of the statements below hold true only within the JPL framework.

Lie Group Theory
The authors of [57] define a Lie group (M, ◦) as a smooth manifold M, i.e.: a topo-
logical space which is locally identical to the Euclidean space, where all member elements
fulfil the axioms of a group, in relation to the binary composition law ◦. Such special
mathematical structures are ubiquitous in robotics, with some examples being the pairs
(Rn, +), (SO(3), ·) and (S3, ⊗).

includes a three-axis accelerometer, a three-axis gyroscope, and, most of the times, also
a three-axis magnetometer or electronic compass, all on a single chip. Thus, one can be

stemming from angular velocity and the other three from proper linear acceleration [59].

Vision Algorithms

Image Features
Corner features have enjoyed great popularity and success in VO implementations,
such as [3, 10, 48], due to the eﬀiciency they provide during the extraction process [19]. A
particular detector engineered for the exact purpose of accelerating keypoint identification
in SLAM applications is the FAST detector, developed by Rosten et. al. [22]. A summary
of the inner workings of their method is provided in the following paragraphs.
An algorithm, based on intensity thresholding in a circular pattern, is at the core of
the extraction. Fig. 3.5 shows an example of how this ring is defined. Around each pixel
zp, a contiguous arc of NF = 16 pixels is built, and the intensity of the center pixel is
compared to those of the surrounding ones. If at least nF = 12 out of the NF pixels are
brighter or darker than zp by a predefined amount tF .

For this combination of NF and nF , the authors propose a high-speed test that involves
only the comparison of the center with pixels 1, 5, 9 and 13. Only if zp passes this initial
verification step, the stronger condition described above is checked. Although incredibly
fast, this 2-step approach has its limitations, as it was observed that there are usually many
features extracted within close vicinity to one another. Moreover, the authors concede that
quality results are not obtained for nF < 12, and the computation time is dictated by the
order in which the pixels on the circumference are selected for intensity comparison.
As it is commonly done in the context of other feature detectors [24], non-maximal sup-
pression is enforced to eliminate spurious keypoints, thus resolving the first shortcoming.
For the sets Sbright and Sdark of pixels darker and brighter than zp, respectively, the sums
of absolute intensity differences from the ring’s center are computed. The ”cornerness”
score function utilized is defined as the maximum of these two sums.
The other two imperfections of the algorithm can be addressed using machine learning,
by pre-training a decision tree classifier on a set of images. For each true corner identified,
all intensities of pixels in the surrounding ring are stored in a vector and entries are
removed recursively so that an entropy measure of information gain is minimized. The
decision tree model thus created can then be used for extra-fast detection in new images.

3.6
Error-state Kalman Filter
The Kalman filter is probably the most well-known type of linear state estimator (ob-
server), formulated so as to minimizes the mean-square error of the estimated parameters.
For systems linear in nature, it has been proven to be optimal in the presence of white
noise [71]. Although this is not the case anymore in the nonlinear world, the suboptimal
extended Kalman filter, based on a first-order Taylor series approximation of the system,
has been heavily used in practice [63]. The reader is directed towards [72] for a detailed
treatment of Kalman filters, and the mathematical arguments and derivations entailed.
In discrete time, the representation of a dynamic system consists of the process (or
dynamic) model xk+1 = f(xk, uk, nk) and the measurement model zk = h(xk)+ηk, where
f and h are vector-valued functions of the state xk to be estimated, uk is the system input
and zk is the output. The vectors nk and ηk are the random process and measurement
noise vectors, respectively, assumed to be independent, white Gaussian with zero-mean.
To these, the covariances Qk and Rk are assigned. In EKF, a moment parametrization
in terms of mean and covariance is utilized for xk, and the estimation of these values is
carried out recursively, in a prediction-update succession [73].
According to [73], one particular variation of Kalman filtering that has found success
in the field of robotics is the error-state Kalman Filter (ErKF, or ESKF, in [54]). This is
also the type of estimator utilized in most filter-based visual-inertial odometry pipelines
[3, 44, 10]. The distinction lies in the fact that the true state is written as a combination
of a nominal (mean) state and an error-state:
xk = ˆxk ⊞δxk
(3.39)
where the ⊞operator acts as a generalized addition, whose exact representation is
dictated by the nature of the state variables. As an example, for a quaternion, the defini-
tion is Eq. 3.17 must be used, whereas ordinary addition is the choice for any part of the
state defined in the Euclidean space. During propagation, the nominal state receives only
the large signals, i.e.: the mean of the process function, while the error-state, for which
the mean stays zero even after prediction, accumulates the noise and is used to represent
uncertainty, i.e.: deviation from the nominal state. Therefore, in ErKF, the covariance
maintained and updated is that of the error-state [54].
As discussed by Solà [54], there are clear reasons why such a representation in desirable
for state estimation. First of all, if orientation is involved, a minimal parametrization can
be utilized, reducing the risk of covariance singularities. Also, the error-state is small, and

Adaptation to Dynamic Scenes
A novel extension to the standard MSCKF fusion discussed in Chapter 4 is proposed
in this chapter. The addition made leverages the very specific nature of maritime environ-
ments, in which global 3D points can be assumed to move in a common plane, and along
rectilinear trajectories.
5.1
Assumptions and Overview
Performing visual-inertial odometry in dynamic environments through any optimiza-
tion method reliant on static world points is not a viable option, for two reasons: (1)
The 3D locations of the detected features cannot be obtained through point triangulation,
no matter the number of views (see Fig. 3.7); (2) Unless these positions are known with
complete certainty, it is not clear how they can be included in an optimization algorithm
for camera pose estimation. Even within a SLAM framework, adding all the 3D locations
of all features to the state vector very quickly becomes computationally unmanageable.
In truth, intuitively, it does not even make sense to build such a gigantic state vector to
begin with, since most of the tracked point landmarks will not be reobserved.
Dynamic Point Trajectories
The former challenge is essentially a question of 2D-to-3D scene reconstruction ability:
having image observations of a set of points belonging to objects known to be moving, is it
possible to reconstruct the historical 3D coordinates of these points? Avidan and Shashua
tackle this very question in [78], and explain that the problem is not solvable for general
point motions. Notwithstanding, they show that, under the assumption of trajectories
that obey certain parametric forms, solutions do indeed exist, and they coin ”trajectory
triangulation” as a term describing such scenarios. The authors introduce linear algo-
rithms for the estimation of rectilinear and conic-section trajectories from sequential point
observations in a calibrated monocular camera, which is also moving, but its extrinsics
are known.
In the context of localization off-shore, it was previously shown in Fig. 3.6 that the sea
surface offers enough texture to allow feature extraction and tracking in aerial images taken
from a moving UAV. As part of the same data acquisition session, a different sequence of
images was captured while the drone was hovering. Applying FAST corner detection and
KLT tracking, as before, resulted in the output illustrated in Fig. 5.1. Since the camera is
stationary, the observed feature tracks appear only as a result of the water itself moving
due to currents.
What is particularly interesting to realize is that every track defines a quasi-rectilinear
history of image point positions. For a camera that obeys the pinhole model (Section 3.4),
it is known that the back-projection of an image line is a plane [32]. Since the sea surface
in Fig. 5.1 is itself planar, its intersection with the back-projection will be a line too.
Thus, it can be inferred that any observed keypoint zp moves along a rectilinear path in

Impact of Uncertainty
What is not treated at all in [78] is the aspect of triangulation robustness to noise
in the camera poses and the point observations. In this project, the algorithm described
above was implemented, and some experiments were conducted on synthetic camera and
dynamic point trajectories, in the presence of manually added Gaussian random noise.
The results will be shown shortly, but the main highlight of the tests was that the linear
algorithm fails to produce a valid Plücker line in realistic, non-ideal conditions, meaning
that the smallest singular vector of AL does not satisfy Eq. 5.2.
Very often in computer vision estimation pursuits, uncertainty renders standard linear
algorithms insuﬀicient when it comes to the fulfillment of hard mathematical constraints.
For instance, as discussed in Section 3.5 and [26], this is the case in Caravaggio epipolar
geometry, whenever the 8-point algorithm is used to recover the fundamental matrix, which
is known to have rank 2. An additional correction step is taken to find the closest singular
matrix to the linearly estimated one, in the sense of Frobenius norm, which is again done
with SVD. In a similar vein, for the case of Plücker lines, Bartoli and Sturm [80] propose
a Plücker correction step, which outputs the closest 3D line to a given 6-vector.
A 2-step SVD trajectory triangulation is therefore a potential solution for improving
the algorithm [78]. With an initial WLf found in this manner, nonlinear optimization
can be utilized for refinement, and it is again [80] that introduces some methods for this
purpose, ensuring Eq. 5.2 remains true. However, given the objective of this project to
deploy a VIO system in a maritime environment, there is additional available information
concerning the placement of the 3D lines in space. In particular, all the lines are coplanar,
specifically in a plane defined by z = 0, in a reference frame having the origin at sea level
and the z-axis perpendicular to the sea plane. Therefore, it is possible to increase accuracy
by imposing these additional constraints during the estimation process.

5.4
Results for Synthetic Triangulation
As stated before, a series of experiments was carried out for manually-generated camera
poses and rectilinear point trajectories, by making use of the Python packages NumPy [82]
and SciPy [83]. During triangulation, zero-mean random Gaussian noise was included in
the camera poses, the feature measurements and the z-coordinates of the plane in which
the trajectories reside. The standard deviations utilized were chosen to be comparable to
those used for testing an algorithm for the triangulation of 3D lines from static line features,
employed in a state-of-the art VIO pipeline [48]. To be specific, the perturbations applied
had the following characteristics: σθ = 0.02 rad (≈1.15 deg) for the camera orientation,
σp = 0.1 m for the translation, σI = 2 pixels for the point measurements, and σz = 0.25 m
for the height WzL. For quantification of the results, the Euclidean norm between the CP
representations (see Eq. 5.6) of the true and estimated trajectories was utilized, similar
to what was done in [48]. Furthermore, the image reprojection error, in the form of the
square root of the definition in Eq. 5.15, was monitored as well. Finally, regarding the
intrinsics of the simulated camera, a resolution of 1200 × 800 was considered, together
with the focal lengths fu = fv = 700 pixels.
Unconstrained Triangulation
Initially, two scenarios were considered to illustrate the effect of noise. For a tracked
dynamic point moving 0.5 m between each of N = 6 frames in which it is observed, and a
sinusoidal-like camera trajectory, linear triangulation was applied both with and without
the added uncertainties. Only the position of the camera is changed, while its orientation is
maintained constant with respect to the scene. The setup and results are given in Fig. 5.5.
Both trajectory estimations were implemented using the unconstrained algorithm in [78],
without consideration of the constraints discussed previously. It should be mentioned that
in the noisy case, the Plücker correction proposed in [80] was employed to get a valid line
in 3D.
Even without any error metric being given, it is visually obvious from these plots that
noise has a non-negligible detrimental impact on the quality of the triangulation. A CP
error of 1.825 m and a reprojection error of 87.75 pixels were computed. Both of these
errors are of course zero in the absence of perturbations.

Conclusion
What the foregoing analysis illustrates is that the alteration of the standard trian-
gulation approach in [78] gave rise to a more robust algorithm, tailored to the specifics
of a dynamic environment in which points move in a known plane and along rectilinear
paths. As it was explained throughout this chapter, modeling a maritime environment in
this manner is reasonable, given the empirical evidence represented by the data acquired
off-shore, in a real setting. It is undeniable that the results obtained here strongly suggest
that trajectories can be estimated from noisy camera extrinsics and feature observations.
Drawing a parallel to the classic case of static point triangulation, it appears possible to
use this method to estimate line trajectories, which can be then utilized as landmarks
within a MSCKF framework.

Experiments and Results
This chapter puts to the test the proposed modifications of the standard MSCKF-
based localization algorithm. In an effort to asses the feasibility and quality of the devised
theoretical solution, experiments are shown for both the static and dynamic worlds.
6.1
Codebase
By now, it has probably become quite apparent that a visual-inertial odometry pipeline
is a very elaborate algorithm, where multiple computation components, each with its own
individual complexity, must coalesce and produce a well-functioning and reliable system.
A failure of any of these building blocks can have catastrophic effects on the performance
of the entire localization procedure. That is why constructing a complete pipeline without
an existing basis is rife with risks, especially of a programming nature. Fortunately, given
the vast amount of research done in the field, many open-source implementations [3, 43,
10, 47] have been released, with the purpose of being exploited and driving the progress
in visual-based pose estimation further.
One such research platform, which implements the multi-state constraint Kalman filter
presented in [44], is OpenVINS [10], a C++ project developed by the Robot Perception and
Navigation Group (RPNG) at the University of Delaware. Apart from containing multiple
extra features compared to the original algorithm, such as the addition of zero-velocity
update, support for ArUco (Augmented Reality University of Córdoba) markers, and the
possibility of performing online calibration of camera intrinsics, camera-IMU extrinsics
and camera-IMU time offset, the codebase has its own documentation page [84], which is
extremely convenient. Interestingly, this is actually a SLAM system, since the MSCKF
was extended to optionally store in the state and update a predefined maximum number
of landmarks, in addition to the standard history of poses employed in VIO. Furthermore,
the authors have ensured integration with ROS (Robot Operating System), by allowing
datasets to be loaded in rosbag format, and they have written the program in a modular
fashion, without any assumptions on the setup utilized (e.g.: number of cameras). The
versatility of this code, together with the fact that it is an actively-maintained project,
were considered to be very strong incentives for its utilization in this thesis.
To be clear, not all of the aforementioned features were necessary for testing the
approach the present work proposes, but the presence of all the extra components is very
beneficial, especially in the context of potential future development.
Regarding the exact libraries utilized, OpenCV [85] is employed to handle the image
processing in the VIO front-end, with eﬀicient functions for FAST feature extraction, KLT
tracking, and RANSAC being readily available. Aspects concerning linear algebra are
dealt with through Eigen [86], because it is high-level and provides optimized algorithms
for matrix operations, such as multiplication, inversion, factorization and decomposition.
Another freely-available package used was NLopt [87], which contains nonlinear solvers
necessary for trajectory refinement during triangulation.

Trajectory Alignment
In VIO, a trajectory, be it the ground truth or the estimation result, is represented
as a set of K discrete IMU position and orientation states X = {xk}, k = 1, . . . K. The
main diﬀiculty stems from the fact that the true history of poses Xgt, and the output
from the odometry system ˆX, have different numbers of samples and are expressed in
different reference frames. The coordinate system discrepancy is a direct consequence of
the observability properties of VIO, made in Section 4.4. In [88], this issue is introduced
as an ambiguity in the solution of the nonlinear least-squares optimization problem that
describes visual-inertial odometry. It is shown that, for an IMU-monocular camera tan-
dem, the solution is invariant to a 4-Dof yaw-only rigid body transformation, where the
yaw is defined as the angle around the gravity axis.
It is noted that that the preceding comment is true only for point-based localization.
As shown in [79, 76] there could be 5 unobservable directions for line-based approaches,
if all the lines were parallel, with the extra degree of ambiguity attributed to the motion
of the agent along the direction of the line landmarks.
Trajectory alignment boils down therefore to the estimation of the rigid body trans-
formation between Xgt and ˆX. Umeyama’s method [89] is essentially the standard course
of action to achieve this, and its author proves that the sought parameters are recovered
by solving a linear least-squares optimization via SVD. Before applying this procedure, a

6.3
Simulation Setup and Data Acquisition
The evaluation of the filter-based VIO algorithm proposed in this thesis was done in
the Maritime Grand Challenge Simulator [90], the oﬀicial simulation environment of the
MBZIRC, developed by Open Robotics. The simulator runs in Ignition Fortress, under
the ROS2 Galactic distribution, and provides a range of sensors and robotic platforms,
which can be deployed in a synthetic coast-like environment.
A suitable VIO-capable agent, in the form of a quadrotor, with onboard IMU and
camera (pointing directly at the water plane), was spawned off-shore, at an initial height
of about 10 m with respect to the sea level. The UAV was given as an input a helix-like
trajectory in the form of velocity way-points, with its true pose, the IMU raw readings,
and the camera feed ROS messages being recorded and stored in a rosbag during the
flight. Importantly, the camera intrinsic parameters, the IMU noise characteristics and
the transformation between the camera and IMU frames are all known. In fact, these, to-
gether with other simulation-specific parameters (e.g.: weather conditions) can be suitably
changed by altering the associated SDF files in the simulation repository.

the simulated maritime environment for what has been defined as the objective of this
project.

With regards to the unsuccessful MSCKF-based estimation experiment from Sec-

the dynamic environment utilized there was not suitable for localization in the suggested
fashion, even with a nonlinear optimization method. Therefore, due the parallelism in the
estimated trajectories, the Kalman filter cannot be expected to fully correct the pose drift.
The main diﬀiculty with this is that, in real maritime settings, such as those shown in
Fig. 3.6 and Fig. 5.1, the trajectories of the tracked points seem to exhibit a high degree of
parallelism, matching the intuition and experience in relation to the movement of floating
objects at sea due to currents.
Furthermore, trajectory triangulation in the presence of uncertainty was shown in
Section 5.4 to produce an average CP error of 0.2 m, implying that noise with much higher
standard deviation than σL = 0.001 m is necessary when modeling this perturbation. Once
again, since nonlinear optimization was unable to provide accurate estimations under
increased noise levels, the filter cannot be expected to cope with this scenario either.

7.1
Conclusion
The present thesis has focused on one of the core research problems in the very vast
field of robotic perception: localization. From the very beginning, the extremely ambitious
goal of visual-inertial pose estimation for a UAV operating in a maritime setting, far from
any fiducial land landmarks, has been defined. Due to its inherently dynamic nature, the
sea is not a friendly environment for traditional visual-based methods, which rely heavily
on the assumption of a predominantly static world.
As the literature review made in Chapter 2 proved, the state-of-the-art VO/VIO ap-
proaches, although highly complex, are currently incapable of utilizing any dynamic in-
formation in the surroundings to infer ego-motion. This work intended to expand the ap-
plicability of established and well-understood algorithms to the specifics of marine scenes,
by making new assumptions regarding the global motion of the visual features that can be
extracted and visually tracked in real aerial photographs acquired off-shore. In Chapter 5,
relevant empirical evidence was brought forward to indicate that corner-like pixels corre-
sponding to points on the surface of a calm sea move along coplanar rectilinear trajectories
in 3D. This is a key observation that allows for the reconstruction of the motion profiles
of the dynamic points from a moving camera, and one robust solution for achieving this
for lines lying in a known plane was delineated and tested in the aforementioned chapter
as well. The achievement was quite relevant, because, as the theoretical overview pre-
sented in Chapter 3 goes to show, standard triangulation is one of the VIO steps that is
guaranteed to fail when moving objects are present.
In the context of the powerful and eﬀicient MSCKF estimation framework, which was
described in detail in Chapter 4, the conjecture that rectilinear trajectories of dynamic
points can be interpreted as fixed anchors in the world was made. Such a speculation
was backed by the existence of localization sub-paradigms that employ either fixed points,
fixed lines, or both types of geometric primitives, as visual aids for correcting the inertial
measurement predictions. Accordingly, a novel reprojection error residual and a modified
Kalman update rule were formulated to accommodate the available visual information
consisting of point-reprojected trajectory pairs.
At the time of conducting localization experiments, in Chapter 6, for evaluating the
performance of the proposed algorithmic alterations, data was acquired in a highly-realistic
robotics simulator, in both static and dynamic world scenarios. For the same appearance
of the visual scene, agent trajectory, and sensor parameters, the MSCKF adapted to dy-
namic environments produced significantly inferior results when rectilinear feature motion
was enabled, as compared to the original filter applied in the static setting. This assertion

is substantiated by the quantification of the estimation quality via errors unanimously
approved in VIO benchmarking. In the same chapter, the divergence of the suggested
solution was investigated further, and, given that very high-quality research code was
utilized in the implementation, the rationale behind the failure of the system was easily
deduced to be caused by issues in the new measurement model. Therefore, a separate
estimation test was conducted, in a similar simulation setup to the one used for triangu-
lation assessment, by casting the localization problem as a task of nonlinear optimization
of point-to-line reprojection errors.
The aforementioned additional experiment shed some light on the potential diﬀiculties
that coplanar rectilinear trajectories entail when they are employed as the only landmarks
in the world. In particular, an inverse correlation between the localization performance
and the degree of parallelism among the line directions was noticed. In the absence of very
restrictive trajectory slope constraints, significant pose correction was achieved through
the minimization of the proposed objective function. However, the optimization seemed
to degrade in quasi-parallel cases. A further decline in localization ability was found to
transpire when the uncertainty in the line landmark positions was increased.
Focusing now the research question posed in Chapter 1, it can be concluded that, with
certain assumptions in place, there is potential to the adjustment of well-known VIO al-
gorithms to a scenario in which dynamic points are known to move on rectilinear paths.
Considering the overall study conducted as part of this thesis, nonlinear optimization
methods can be utilized to reduce the error between feature observations and their associ-
ated reprojected trajectories. Although the method that was studied in detail here was the
MSCKF, and divergence was observed during preliminary experiments on visual-inertial
data, a study of the system observability properties, together with additional tests in more
diverse environments, might show the theoretical and practical suitability of this method
in the given context.
In conclusion, throughout this thesis, significant strides on a previously uncharted
research trail have been made, and some partial extensions of visual localization have
been proposed to achieve estimation in a very challenging dynamic environment. Although
the advanced mathematical postulates have not been completely proven, and the results
obtained cannot be qualified as revolutionary nor spectacular, the progress made can serve
as an inspiration for future research, even if the specific use case might differ.
7.2
Future Work
Some recommendations for the improvement of the solution presented in this report
have already been pointed out in the conclusion and throughout the text. Probably the
matter that should be given the most attention is the one concerning the observability
of the proposed VIO system, which will offer clarifications regarding which states can be
recovered, and in what conditions.
Based on the outcome of this analysis, simulation
experiments should be conducted, and the recommendation is to utilize synthetic data
(in a programming environment such as Python), to allow for modifications to be made
quickly, and avoid the practical problems that the use of large C++ codebases entail.
Also, the tests should not be restricted solely to the MSCKF as an estimation method,
but alternative, more powerful optimization-based approaches should be investigated too.
There seems to also be a merit to the detailed study of visual localization methods that
employ static lines, given that the reprojection error suggested in this thesis is falls at
the intersection between the residual used in point-based VIO, and the one utilized in
line-based estimation schemes.
Another aspect that would be important to study is the issue of dynamic object seg-
mentation in images acquired at sea. Although it was mentioned in the introduction that
==PAGE:75==
7.2. Future Work
63
this step is excluded from the thesis, it actually represents a very relevant component
of the pipeline in a real-world scenario. When the agent is close to the shore, standard
visual-inertial odometry should be applied on the section of the image classified as land
area. However, as soon as there is nothing but plain sea in the visual field of the camera,
a method that leverages the dynamic information must be utilized. Thus, the sea/shore
separation could lead to an improved performance in cases when the visual information
is mixed, and prevent the use of the wrong estimation paradigm when the underlying as-
sumptions about the world are violated. Additionally, a floating object detection module
could be added as well, since such structures may provide additional fixed anchors that
could aid the estimation off-shore.
In relation to the triangulation sub-block of the VIO pipeline, the trajectory recon-
struction method proposed here revolves around the assumption that all dynamic points
lie exactly in the same plane. Even though this assumption greatly simplified the problem,
it may be detrimental in some cases. For example, seaborn vehicles also tend to travel
along rectilinear paths, and the estimation of these must utilize the points extracted from
the vessel’s surface, which do not, of course, lie on the sea surface. Further work might
very well look into alternative ways to recover such trajectories without enforcing the
coplanarity constraint.
Finally, the creation of a standard dataset for UAV localization in a maritime environ-
ment, comparable to, for example, the EuRoc MAV dataset, may greatly benefit future
research. As discussed, only sequences of underwater video and IMU raw readings for
AUVs seem to be available, with nothing similar having been released, at the time of writ-
ing, for drones operating above the surface. Carefully planning and carrying out this data
acquisition once, and publishing it, of course, would prevent this logistically complicated
process to be repeated by each researcher interested in the problem of localization at sea.
