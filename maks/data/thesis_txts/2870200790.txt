Product platforms as enables for reuse: standardization practices for 
circular economy implementation in construction industry 
 
==PAGE:2==
2 
 
Abstract  
In terms of resources, the construction industry is one of the most demanding sectors, defined 
by high raw material supply and significant waste production globally. By using standardized 
modular product platforms aiming to promote material reuse, this thesis explores how circular 
economy principles can potentially be applied to the construction industry. It investigates the 
barriers of material recovery, including the lack of standardization of parts and processes, 
irreversible building interfaces, and inadequate collaboration across stakeholders. Furthermore, 
this study explores how product platforms can enhance recovery of materials through Design for 
Disassembly (DfD), reverse logistics capabilities, and sharing of information across the value 
chain. Using an explanatory case study strategy, this thesis collects qualitative data through semi-
structured interviews from the construction industry stakeholders and from an extensive 
literature review. An abductive approach is used to integrate empirical findings to existing 
theories on DfD, reverse logistics and platform thinking. The findings show that product platforms 
can reduce the dependency on virgin resources and minimize environmental effects by improving 
the recovery of valuable materials and streamlining the assembly and disassembly processes in 
construction. This thesis provides a strong framework for implementing circular economy 
principles, especially with product platforms that are capable for triggering innovation, resource 
efficiency, and economic growth within the construction industry.

1.1. 
Background of the Topic  
 
Among global industries, the construction sector is arguably one of the most resource-intensive 
industries as it uses a large spectrum of raw materials whilst also associated with large amounts 
of waste generation. The volume of construction and demolition waste is estimated to be 
between 30%-40%, with immense economic and environmental consequences (Liu et al., 2019). 
In the ‘take, make, dispose’ model, the baseline resource-consuming model, there is no attempt 
to economize waste, permitting extremely poor management resources. Historically, the 
construction and materials industry has failed to address the so-called ‘end-of-life’ issues of 
materials and structures. Since waste is rarely seen as a valid, usable material during design and 
construction, it allows for linear systems of resource exploitation instead of value creation. 
Hence, a considerable amount of greenhouse gas emissions, material, and economic resources 
have their economic value buried alongside landfills, rather than be utilized. To tackle these 
issues, a transformative model called the Circular Economy (CE) has been developed (Liu et al., 
2019). Unlike the linear system, a CE focuses on the recovery of resources through lengthening 
the lifecycle of materials and waste management by reusing, refurbishing, and recycling 
resources as shown in Figure 1.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
 
Figure 1: Circular Economy in the Construction Industry 
(https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666790823001209) 
The adoption of circular strategies can minimize the negative effects on the environment, save 
resources, and improve the overall economic efficiency of the construction industry. This 
transformation revolves around product platforms, standard components, and systems that can 
be modified and reused. The use of product platforms has been noted to increase the levels of 
efficiency, innovation, and scalability in almost all industries, including manufacturing. Regarding 
construction, product platforms present a CE approach through modular and standard systems 
capable of reusing and recycling building components. These further include the standard 
features of recovering disassembled components at the end-of-life stage of a structure. 
Moreover, product platforms can enable greater flexibility in the design and construction of 
components that will be repurposed in multiple projects, eliminating the need for new materials. 
Sustainable goals, including the United Nations SDGs (Liu et al., 2019), are paramount for the 
successful use of product platforms in circular economies, in particular the focus is on SDG 11, 
Sustainable Cities and Communities, and SDG 12, Responsible Consumption and Production, by 
adopting and implementing policies that promote construction waste mitigation practices.

1.3. 
Problem Formula@on  
To align with these objectives, it would be meaningless to attempt the highest level of 
deconstruction of a building or structure. The interfaces of buildings are singularly irreversible, 
meaning the components of these structures cannot be modularized or reused. In contemporary 
construction, the replacement, augmentation, or removal of structural parts are nonexistent 
which forces reuse of materials as they expire, i.e., construction practices that inhibit the goals 
of a CE in the field (Osei-Tutu et al., 2024). This is exacerbated by gaps present in the 
communication between the different stakeholders in the construction value chain. Information 
of great value regarding the reuse of materials, lifecycle features of materials, or the optimal 
methods of disassembly, is too critical to be ignored, but architects, contractors, and demolishers 
do not have access to it until those structures are in use or demolition. It not only impedes the 
shift toward circular practices, but demolishers are also unwilling to adopt sustainable demolition 
methods (Charef et al., 2021). Consequently, there is an overproduction of construction and 
demolition waste that has remote chances of being reused, thus increasing the shortage of 
resources and environmental damage. 
 
 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
Adopting practices, such as energy efficiency, has been smoother in the construction industry, 
whereas sustainable CE integration has been less researched. As stated by Leising et al., (2018), 
circularity enables waste minimization, material lifespan maximum, and focuses on reuse, 
refurbishment, and recycling. The potential of circularity scope to disrupt how construction 
engineers work has not been properly embedded within existing construction practices. 
Resulting in increasing emphasis on energy-efficient measures has fostered designs that are not 
systemically oriented towards recovering and reusing materials, further deepening the gap in the 
industry’s sustainability effort. Design for Disassembly (DfD) offers a potential solution to this 
problem by enabling the construction of buildings that facilitate the easy retrieval and reuse of 
various components. The DfD approach houses the ability to greatly decrease the volume of 
construction on-site waste and building deconstruction waste generated at the end of a building's 
life cycle. This in turn allows for the provision of adaptable designs that facilitate the replacement 
and recovery of certain elements without compromising the stability of the structure. With 
modularity in the design process, DfD moves buildings close to material bank systems, i.e., places 
of depositories of reusable materials instead of disposables.  
 
It is often observed that the DfD strategy proves difficult to adopt due to various limitations, 
irrespective of obvious benefits. Nevertheless, construction practices, supply chains, and 
business structures usually do not comply with the business model of DfD. Moreover, there is a 
lack of coherent guidelines and tools that facilitate industry’s accommodating to disassembly 
friendly designs. These challenges arise from lacking financial, understanding, and regulatory 
capital. Stakeholders are often unwilling to bear the initial costs associated with DfD despite the 
long-term savings and ecological advantages that appeal to all groups. There is a need for a shift 
in how buildings are being designed, constructed, and deconstructed which can tip the scale (Liu 
et al., 2019). When there is an integration of product platforms, enhanced information sharing 
and more modular and standardized systems, there will be a shift toward a circular, sustainable 
economy in construction which reduces waste through the building of dynamic living systems 
that adapt and evolve over time.

1.3.1. Reversibility of Interfaces 
The lack of reversibility in the construction components presents one of the most significant 
challenges for contemporary hybrid structural systems. This is due to sections assembled and 
integrated in such a way that disassembling them becomes complicated. These functions 
between different materials tend to create conditions where components become 
unreplaceable, or irreversibly modified in ways that could have been reused are destroyed during 
demolition or renovation, and during the process a lot of waste is created (Osei-Tutu  et al., 2024). 
As mentioned, waste results from lack of reversibility, in this case lack of potential reuse and 
refurbishment which otherwise makes it easier to adopt a CE in construction. For instance, if 
components cannot be accurately disassembled, the resources within them remain "locked," 
limiting their recovery potential. For example, many structural components, such as columns and 
beams, cladding, and even thermal materials, are often becoming contaminated by other 
materials during the demolition process and, therefore, excluded from any extensive processing 
for reuse. Yet, these limitations do not only affect resources but also increase the cost of 
replacing uncontrolled materials with raw materials that undergo additional environmentally 
degrading processes to become usable components.  
 
Many building interfaces, in addition to being easily constructed, are also difficult to reverse and 
alter, which hinders modification. However, they achieve flexibility throughout the lifespan of a 
structure. Buildings are complex structures that evolve in terms of their functionalities and 
working standards. Even so, there are limitations with designing these systems as they require a 
great deal of effort in deconstructing and reconstructing the structure, not only time consuming 
but resource intensive too. To develop a solution regarding the problem of interface reversibility, 
standardization and modular approaches should be amended to focus on disassembly and 
modification (Liu et al., 2019). New technologies, such as snap-fit joints, mechanical fasteners, or 
even reusable technologies can make deconstruction more efficient and less damaging as their 
implementation would enable smoother separation of parts and components. Moreover, other 
technologies such as Building Information Model (BIM) are assisting in better planning and 
tracking of structure components and deconstruction interfaces to ease the process of materials 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
separation and reusability (Osei-Tutu et al., 2024). Such interfaces can effectively reduce waste 
generated and enhance the overall value of building systems by prolonging the lifespan of their 
components. Consequently, it helps buildings attain the status of material stock, where elements 
can be recovered and reused with minimal effort and waste.  
 
1.3.2. Lack of Flexibility in Design  
Inadequate modularity and flexibility in the building design constitute a major hindrance to 
improvement of sustainable practice in the construction sector. Most of the buildings have rigid 
layouts and fixed interfaces incorporating potential barriers to adaptation, maintenance, and re-
utilization, especially in the operational life of buildings. Most traditional designs focus on 
functionality and aesthetics in terms of short-term solutions without considering their flexibility 
and sustainability in the long run. The inability to anticipate a building's future needs diminishes 
its capability to adapt to changing demands and reinforces a wasteful "take-make-dispose" 
model. Liu et al., (2019), explains that much potential is offered by modularization and built-in 
flexibility in layouts and interfaces for transforming construction practices. Components can be 
easily prefabricated, assembled, and disassembled, which makes the processes of loading and 
unloading much more effective and reduces the wastage of material. 
However, a critical impediment is a lack of proper information sharing and knowledge about the 
properties of building elements that can be recycled. Much of the information on materials, 
lifecycle characteristics, and deconstruction methods is, in some respects, fragmented and, in 
other respects, is not accessible to appropriate stakeholders, who cannot take any proper 
decisions in the design and construction phases and demolition without data standardization. For 
architects and structural engineers, identification of compatibility-compatible components for 
reuse is hard; demolishers are uninformed about the ways of recovering and modifying the 
materials they extract for reuse. According to Liu & et.al, 2019 this knowledge gap continues to 
encourage inefficiency, standing in the way of the industry realizing full potential in reuse and 
refurbishment. It is, therefore, important to first address these challenges to initiate a step 
towards data-driven design and building practices.

1.3.3. Lack of Standardiza*on in Components and Processes 
The propensity of standardized components and processes is challenging in building 
industrialization. It is crucial to standardize building components and achieve the ability to 
replace, adapt, and reuse them between different projects. Without standardization, the 
construction

more 
customized, 
increased 
assembly/disassembling difficulties, and complicated waste management. Insufficient uniformity 
inflates material waste and undermines prospects for recovering and reusing materials, 
reinforcing reliance on a linear economic model characteristic of the industry (Liu & et.al, 2019). 
Building components are standardized to simplify construction processes by such modular design 
that works consistently on several projects. If the approach focuses on designing standardized 
components with consistent dimensions, materials, and connection methods, this will lead to 
components being built to assist integration into new structures or reintroduced to the supply 
chain as part of secondary markets. Such standardization reduces the level of redesign or 
retrofitting needed and thus decreases time and cost.  
 
Standardization mostly optimizes processes and contributes to the reintegration of materials into 
secondary markets. For example, uniform modules, such as sewage pipes, bricks, sidewalks, steel 
rebar, windows and doors, and card stock, can be refurbished or repaired and reintroduced into 
the construction supply chain without requiring virgin materials. It helps build a more sustainable 
construction model by stretching the life cycle of materials and lowering the total environmental 
impact on construction activity. Standardization is, therefore, best achieved through 
collaboration across the construction value chain. Industry-wide agreements on component 
dimension, material, and connection methods can provide a basis for standardizing processes. 
Further, BIM could serve as an added tool for standardization by creating a common platform for 
building component documentation and management (Liu et al., 2019). In addition, regulatory 
frameworks and certifications can facilitate a move toward standardized designs which 
encourage consistency and circularity of construction. The standardization of the construction 
industry through development allows it to become more effective and sustainable in the context 
of construction. Standardized components and processes will simplify construction and

deconstruction, which lays a foundation for CE with the reuse, repurposing, and reintegration of 
materials to avoid waste and reduce resource depletion.  
 
 
1.4. 
 Role of Product PlaDorms  
Product platforms can be understood as a standardized, modular design framework that strongly 
enables the solving of many major construction industry challenges. Design and development is 
made much more accessible since the various components are readily reusable, redistributed, or 
replaced. A product platform standardizes materials, dimensions, and connection methods, 
therefore decreasing the level of complexity and reducing the likelihood of errors during 
construction and disassembly accordingly. According to Liu et al., (2019), this approach 
significantly simplifies the assembly process while keeping wastes and valuable resources with 
more efficient recovery and reutilization of building components. Among the most significant 
advantages of product platforms is the flexibility granted at the design stage. Since these 
platforms bring modularity and adjustability into building designs, variations over time can be 
allowed without having to undergo an intensive redesigning or demolition process. 
Apart from this, the key features of interface reversibility and the ability to carry out separation 
and re-assembly with minimal manpower of building modules are critical characteristics for 
circular design strategies. It goes hand-in-hand with design principles for disassembly, 
adaptability, and reuse, thereby providing an actual route toward sustainability in the 
construction industry. The possibility to separate components easily without damaging them 
through interface reversibility means that more value can be recovered, refurbished, or reused 
from the valuable material resources, thus reducing discards, and saving resources. Interface 
reversibility is only possible with the use of standardized components and modular designs 
supported by product platforms. The assembled modules comprise standard components, which 
make construction easier but also aid easier deconstruction, maintenance, repair, and upgrading 
of building modules (Liu et al., 2019). At the same time, this simplifies refurbishment and 
improves the compatibility of components between projects, thereby bringing about the 
possibility of reusing materials associated with the outcome of different projects. In such a 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
framework, buildings are conceived as dynamic systems in which lines of evolution can meet 
changing needs. 
Additionally, product platforms go beyond this, as they bridge the knowledge gap by 
standardizing components and documenting and sharing data about components across projects 
and other stakeholders. It permits integration into secondary markets easier, which in turn makes 
the waste recovery system more efficient in terms of existing waste recovery infrastructure. This 
thesis addresses the persistent inefficiency of present construction practices, where modularity, 
reversibility, standardization and sharing of appropriate information impede the possibility of 
reuse and refurbishment of buildings. This research explores how these strategies can warrant 
the construction industry to transition toward a more circular model that minimizes waste and 
maximizes the lifecycle value of buildings (lifecycle value of buildings) through a detailed 
examination of product platforms and design for the disassembly and reuse of construction 
components (Liu et al., 2019). This study directs the research focus on standardization and 
modularization to enhance future construction practices capable of accommodating sustainable, 
adaptative and resource-efficient building designs. 
 
1.5. 
Case Descrip@on  
 
This thesis investigates the possibility of reusing construction elements to implement CE 
strategies through product platforms. The case examined in this research attempts to integrate 
product platforms into construction processes to solve major issues such as non-standardization 
practices, inflexible design elements, and irreversible deconstruction loss. Furthermore, the 
research examines the use of CE principles in construction by considering how product platforms 
allow modular design, and flexible adaptation to change conditions. The case will investigate 
actual construction practices that use resource efficient and environmentally friendly modular 
standardization.  
 
Further comparison in the automotive and electronics industry demonstrated that product 
platforms may be successfully implemented (Liu et al., 2019). It is designed as a case description 
to show how challenging and rewarding moving away from the classic site-based construction 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
schemes towards more cyclic, modular approaches can be. Examining building components 
designed for disassembly and reuse forms a central part of the case study. Generally, the existing 
buildings in conventional construction are built with strategic permanent interfaces and a lack of 
modularity. Hence, they cannot be easily disassembled into components for maintenance or 
recycling. This thesis will apply a research case of how standardized product platforms can 
overcome these problems through reversible interfaces that make components easy to 
assemble, disassemble, and reconfigure. These platforms are evaluated for their capacity to 
support DfD principles, flexibility in layout, and efficiency in material reuse. 
 
Besides addressing technical design components, the case study will examine how collaboration 
and information sharing across stakeholders (Liu et al., 2019), contribute to decision-making. 
Transparency and communication across the construction value chain are major obstacles that 
hinder the implementation of circular practices. This case will discuss how the product platforms 
may become the enabling common ground among the stakeholders like architects, engineers, 
manufacturers, and demolishers in putting the stakeholders together without any obstacles. 
According to Liu et al., (2019), efficient material tracking, component standardization, and supply 
chain monitoring are the keys towards unleashing the potential for circularity, and this 
collaboration enables it. Furthermore, it looks upon the economic and environmental impact 
caused by the acceptance of product platforms. One key point will be the energy balance for 
transitioning to modular and reusable design. This will assess how these platforms can influence 
construction costs, operational efficiency, and waste management at the end of life. 
It will discuss how an increase in material lifecycles and a reduction in construction waste 
contents concurrently achieve reductions in environmental impacts such as resource depletion 
and greenhouse gas emissions (Liu et al., 2019). The case study will assess some chosen examples 
and best practices that help draw how much product platforms can accelerate CE principles in 
construction. This will be done by defining the factors that contribute to the successful 
deployment of these platforms and a roadmap to overcome the barriers to adoption.

2. Literature review  
2.1. Circular Economy  
 
 
Figure 2: Circular Economy and Green Chemistry 
 
 
 
Source: https://www.mdpi.com/1996-1073/16/4/1752 
 
Various challenges emanated from the traditional linear economic model, thus triggering a rise 
that gave birth to what is known as the CE concept. Zhao et al., (2011), argued that based on the 
'take-make-dispose of' approach, this model exploits finite natural resources, generating waste 
and degrading the environment. In comparison, the CE desires to lessen resource input and waste 
and build a regenerative one that helps prolong the lives of materials and products. 
Consequently, this approach saves resources and leads to lower emissions and energy leakage 
via long-lasting design, maintenance, repair, rechargeable, replacement, reuse, remanufacturing, 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
and recycling practices as seen in Figure 2. The adoption potential of the CE is increasingly 
recognized by governments worldwide, which are launching various measures to support its 
advancement across different sectors. The building sector is one of the most relevant areas for 
this transition to a CE as it is among the major consumers of raw materials. Construction, 
renovation, and demolition generate huge volumes of waste within the sector. This further 
results in massive landfill waste, leading to environmental degradation and the loss of valuable 
resources. A set of policies and initiatives still encourages the building industry toward circularity. 
It refers to incentives to recycle materials, laying down regulations regarding sustainable building 
practices, seeding research, and innovation on circular construction technologies. 
 
Adopting circular construction practices requires rethinking how we learn to design and build. 
Considering this perspective, the concept of circular construction suggests avoiding the 
conception of buildings with fixed and permanent structures, but on modular and flexible bases. 
Easy disassembly, fixing, or reuse of components is built into these designs such that materials 
stay in circulation for as long as possible. This, again, requires a fundamental shift in the selection 
of materials, the way of constructing buildings, and managing waste (Murray et al., 2016). 
Probably, one of the major challenges for the implementation of a CE within construction is the 
issue of cooperation among stakeholders. Architects, engineers, contractors, manufacturers, and 
policymakers need to collaborate in order to create a common vision and pathway for unlocking 
circularity. For example, with the help of designers, priorities can be drafted using resilient, 
recyclable, nontoxic materials, while manufacturers can introduce more standardized parts 
within the market that can fit easily within different projects. Policymakers can establish the 
corresponding regulations and incentives toward adopting reclaimed materials and punish those 
with too much wastefulness. The CE within the construction sector also embraces information 
sharing and transparency. This can be done at the core of building information modeling. Using 
BIM, stakeholders can trace how materials move in their life cycle, hence having valuable 
information concerning their materials, including composition, origin, and its potential for reuse. 
It will, therefore, enable decision-makers to act upon knowledge and favor efficient recovery and 
reutilization of materials near the end of a building's life. This thesis is concerned with what 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
solutions are practical in integrating reclaimed materials into the construction industry in line 
with the CE. This paper explains how such circular construction may solve ecological difficulties 
and waste and natural resource preservation while offering economic viability simultaneously. 
This research furthers this broader sustainability objective of the built environment through an 
in-depth analysis of circular practices and those applied within the construction sector. 
 
Furthermore, the CE is built on the aim of designing a system that can be regenerative by design 
with the used resources for as long as possible. Product and material use is optimized, recycling, 
and repair are maximized, generating quite minimal waste and an environmental footprint 
considerably reduced in size. That means keeping the value in the system for a time as long as 
possible as well as extending the life of the resources. However, building a restorative system 
where the process of regeneration is equally crucial, this includes a self-sustaining process of 
recovering and renewing materials and resources to provide balance throughout the economy 
(Liu et al., 2019). One of the main objectives that the CE aims to introduce into the world of 
economics is to decouple economic growth from the consumption of resources and 
environmental degradation promised and brought about by traditional linear models of 
production and consumption. Whereas the 'take-make-dispose' approach is characterized by the 
relentless growth of waste and pollution, a CE works to essentially design waste and pollution 
out of the system from the start. It aims to gradually bring industries and society along a less 
unsustainable integration of production and consumption patterns. It focuses on product and 
business system design that brings about resource efficiency and waste reduction. Besides 
reducing daylight to finite resources, the approach provides new opportunities to recycle, 
remanufacture, and promote sustainability in innovation (Liu et al., 2019). A CE operates by 
promoting resilience in resource scarcity, facilitating environmental sustainability, and endorsing 
industry collaboration to share resources and knowledge. It reduces environmental degradation, 
and resource use optimization brings economic activity to a more sustainable and prosperous 
future in concert with ecological health.

Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen’s 1971 book The Entropy Law and the Economic Process is the point 
of origin of its roots. The basis of this work is Georgescu-Roegen’s argument that, like any ﬁnite 
system, the economy cannot conTnue to grow indeﬁnitely, and there are associated principles of 
thermodynamics. Georgescu Roegen (1971) urged that the concept of entropy, or the inexorable 
dissipaTon of energy and materials, be the basis for economic thought. Since most materials are 
subject to decomposiTon (e.g., due to deterioraTon, accidents, or ineﬃciencies) and are 
subjected to some level of degradaTon when created, used, and even recycled, entropy is 
unavoidable. This inherent limitaTon exists since recycling and reusing processes are valuable but 
never complete or perfect. Therefore, rather than eliminaTng entropy, the CE is trying to extend 
the resource lifecycle boundaries to slow the negaTve eﬀects of entropy. The CE maximizes the 
useful life of materials and minimizes waste, thus extending the Tme materials remain within the 
economic system by prioriTzing reuse, repair, and recycling. The presented approach brings to 
light the tension between resource eﬃciency and thermodynamic principles of material 
degradaTon, signaling that sustainability is achieved through insulaTon against, rather than 
deﬁance of entropy.

2.2.1. Circular Building Components  
 
Circular building components are key elements for the construction industry’s transition towards 
sustainability and efficient resource utilization. Two methodologies that support such a circular 
integration into building design and material application are Material Flow Analysis (MFA) and 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). MFA monitors the flow of materials from construction through 
usage and the ultimate end of life so that stakeholders can determine where and why materials 
are moving to the landfill. This analysis can help us understand what volumes and types of 
materials are reclaimed, reused, or recycled, reducing consumption of resources (Akhimien et 
al., 2021). Just as LCA lays out a comprehensive framework for evaluating the environmental 
impact of building components from extracting raw materials through production, use, and 
disposal, it provides a similarly comprehensive framework for the same buildings after 
construction. MFA and LCA go hand-in-hand in providing designers, manufacturers, and 
policymakers with the information needed to make sustainable choices, reduce their carbon 
==PAGE:28==

footprint, or even extend the life of building components. This should bring practices of MFA and 
LCA in line with innovative design approaches like modular construction and DfD, integrating 
circular building components. Practices like these guarantee that the building components are as 
durable as they are adaptable and recoverable, thus minimizing waste and resources used to the 
fullest extent (Akhimien  et al., 2021). MFA data, for example, helps move forward designing with 
a minimum amount of material; LCA can assure that when used, the employment of the 
components guarantees the least environmental impact possible over the entire life cycle. 
Standardized modular components simplify deconstruction, sorting, and reintroduction into 
secondary markets. Thus, supporting the aims of a CE, merging MFA and LCA makes possible a 
well-defined strategy for circularity in construction through transparency, resource allocation, 
and environmental management. With these, the construction sector can shift from a linear to a 
CE model which fosters innovation and meets the need for sustainability.

2.3.1 Reverse Logis*cs Supply Chains  
The reverse logistics flow of products, services, and information originating at the point of origin 
and moving towards the point of consumption is termed as Reverse Logistics System Chain 
(RLSC), a part of Supply Chain Management (SCM). This is manufacturing, recycling, returns and 
refurbishing of products. Reverse logistics aims to reduce waste and increase resource or goods 
value when the product's life cycle has ended (Wijewickrama  et al., 2021). Reverse logistics takes 
multiple supply chain players, consumers, merchants, producers, and service providers, to work 
together and coordinate in order to be implemented properly. With the increasing significance 
of businesses trying to cut their environmental footprint and improve their sustainability, reverse 
logistics is on the rise. An integrated strategy, including forward and reverse logistics, is needed 
to manage reverse logistics efficiently.

3.1. Global Sustainability and Waste Challenges in Construc@on 
3.1.1. Industrial Ecology and Material Flow in Construc*on 
Construction is generally understood using an industrial ecological framework of materials, 
energy, and waste flow-based ecology. It is about a process of interdependence where the 
byproducts of one process can be a resource for another while increasing resource use efficiency 
and less environmental impact. Construction is applied through industrial ecology to build 
buildings as nodes of a larger urban ecosystem, known as urban metabolism. Urban metabolism 
studies investigate inputs, outputs, and storage of materials in urban systems. They can explain 
how materials move through and among buildings, construction sites, and waste facilities (van 
Stijn et al., 2021). Here, buildings are understood as components in a closed-loop system that 
centralizes the management of resources throughout a building's life cycle. This approach is in 
line with the optimization of the built form with regard to material use and contributes to the 
development of pathways that stimulate reuse and recycling. It thus represents mainstream 
integration into the urban infrastructure of a CE. 
 
3.1.2.  Material Flow Analysis (MFA) in Building Construc*on 
MFA is an important tool for industrial ecology to measure resource transfer through systems. 
Systematic accounting of material stock, input flows, and resource losses of a building project 
makes it possible for the different stakeholders to keep track of all the uses of materials and 
unroll material inefficiencies (Thushari et al., 2020). MFA constitutes a very fundamental element 
in BCDW management, defining where materials could be recaptured or rerouted for reuse in 
such a way as to reduce waste and preserve resources (Eberhardt  et al., 2019). In the context of 
construction, the application of MFA through data-driven modeling can provide valuable insights 
into resource utilization and identify opportunities for waste reduction. This approach ultimately 
contributes to the advancement of sustainable building practices. MFA traces the flow of 
materials from the source to utilization and from utilization to its end-of-life, informing strategies 
for material recovery, refurbishment, reutilization, and use. This approach closely aligns with the 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
principles of product platforms and modularity, which rely on efficient flows and reuses of 
standard components. 
3.1.3. Product PlaQorms as Enablers for Sustainable Material Flows 
In manufacturing, product platforms were originally developed to encourage reuse and reduce 
waste, and we realize that they can also play a role in construction. A product platform is a set of 
standard components and subsystems that can be combined in different ways to form various 
building designs. It helps components become more modular and standardized, making them 
easier to disassemble and reuse across different projects. In construction, product platforms 
provide structured management of material flows. Product platforms allow firms to apply the 
same interchangeable parts to several system designs with effective resource utilization and 
reduction of waste, while enabling component reutilization and not destruction. Modularity, in 
this case, is also combined with DfD, targeting a building that could be taken apart without 
destroying the components, allowing for the reuse, and recycling process (Malabi Eberhardt et 
al., 2024).  
3.1.4.  Integra*on of Product PlaQorms with Material Flow Systems 
Integrate product platforms with MFA and reverse logistics for a complete material flow 
management in construction. The product platform will be able to provide interchangeable and 
standardized parts to increase the efficiency of those flows. Standardization of customization, 
simplification of assembly and disassembly, and promotion of reuses are further supported. 
Product platforms integrate interchangeable parts, providing a basis for modularity toward 
circular construction practices (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2024). MFA will complement this by 
elaborating an account of material used in all phases of the construction cycle. MFA allows any 
stakeholder to quantify material inputs, identify their sources, and assess their possible reuse. 
For example, MFA may analyze what material is recoverable at the end of a project, what 
efficiencies are to be enhanced onward, and where material loops can be closed. Such a data-
driven approach allows optimization of resource allocation and illustrates paths which product 
platforms can help to reuse. MFA has thus made a great contribution to answering this research 
question, since it quantifies and visualizes those very unique barriers that product platforms 
mediate, like misused textiles or being prone to waste. 
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
 
MFA findings are operationalized through reverse logistics. It ensures that there is recovery of 
the materials withdrawn and their access again into the supply chain. Such is the case when 
reverse logistics, in conjunction with product platforms, can reuse standardized parts for 
reassembly in new projects. It provides a circular material flow and reduces BCDWs while pushing 
materials through the supply chain. Malabi Eberhardt et al. (2020) present how construction 
businesses can systematically overcome barriers to material reuse by aligning product platforms 
with MFA and reverse logistics. The integrated approach, therefore, can enable the adoption of 
CE principles with materials inventoried, recovered, and used again in a loop such as this. Using 
these complementary strategies, we can answer how product platforms enable material reuse 
and propose a scalable platform for embedding circular practices into the construction industry. 
 
3.1.5.  Barriers to Eﬃcient BCDW Management and Reuse 
The following characteristics are some barriers to the efficient BCDW management and reuse 
despite product platforms and MFA possessing strong capabilities by facilitating material flow:  
1. Irreversibility of Interfaces: most of the contemporary construcTon permits the 
establishment of dissociable bonds between secTons which make it nearly impossible to 
disassemble and reuse the components.  
2. Uniqueness and ModiﬁcaTon: Specialized components in various construcTon projects 
greatly limit reusability.  
3. Financial and Legal Barriers: it is a signiﬁcant barrier to use recycled components when 
low-cost new materials set the price range for renewable parts. In some cases, these parts 
are not able to pass the legal guidelines.  
4. Obstacles of LogisTcs and Supply Chains: The acquisiTon and processing of constructs 
labeled as BCDW, requiring reverse logisTcs increases the cost and other general expenses 
that come with it (Malobi Eberhardt et al., 2020).

3.1.6.  Towards a Circular Material Flow System in Construc*on 
A final collection of work concludes with the integration of product platform, MFA, and industrial 
ecology theory into one framework, which transforms construction from flux-based into a 
circular ecosystem. It provides for the realization of the CE in construction since one allows for 
the module, standardized components while tracking material flow, and the other enforces 
reverse logistics effectively. This shall aid in the movement of building materials within the urban 
ecosystem without generating any wastes and shall also preserve the resources from cradle to 
grave in the life cycle of a building. The model forms the basis for further research and practice 
in bringing together integration of construction practices with larger aspirations for sustainability

including what difficulties the interviewees are confronting, what they perceive to be product 
platforms, and what material reuse implies in practice. The interviews also bring in context

overcoming design limitations, economic constraints, and logistical challenges as major barriers

4.2. Theore@cal Framework  
The thesis provides a comprehensive theoretical framework based on Design-of-Disassembly, 
Reverse Logistics and Platform Thinking. Such concepts are important to any understanding of 
how construction practices can make a greater shift into material reuse, especially in terms of 
necessary technical and systemic mitigation (Saunders et al., 2012).  The framework underlines 
the main points of the DfD principle, which are respectively the areas that are designed in a way 
that enables a building to be easily disassembled and then assembled again are important. The 
approach ensures that such materials would be recovered with high efficiency at the end of their 
life cycle and that the wastes are at minimum levels because of the maximum reuse. Going 
further, the concept is completed by reverse logistics, emphasizing the operational part of 
material recovery into the supply chains, including setting up pathways for their collection from 
the construction site, transportation, processing, and new projects in an efficient way. Another 
layer of innovation uses platform thinking to promote modular standardized designs. That 
approach makes it more scalable and flexible by allowing component reuse between different 
projects while adjusting just a few. These concepts provide a structured approach to 
understanding how the construction industry can overcome the barriers to design, limitation, 
and logistical and stand and radiation shortages. It provides a framework for the research study 
and conducts a systematic investigation into the barriers that have been identified and the 
potential solutions that the CE concept provides. 
 
4.2.1.  Approach to Theory Development – Abduc*ve  
As an abductive theory development, this research is based on a very dynamic relationship 
between empirical findings and theoretical insights. This research iteratively moves between 
abstract ideas and practical applications in the construction industry through sequences of data 
collection, literature review, and analysis (Yin, 2009). It is an avenue to extend existing theories, 
namely Design-for-Disassemble and platform thinking, regarding real-life issues and challenges.

4.2.2. Research Design – Exploratory Design  
The exploratory research contribution investigates the role that product platforms and guiding

exploratory design seeks to identify new and innovative ways to bridge the gap between the 
practice and stakeholder engagement for accelerating circularity and sustainability in the

1. Barriers and Challenges to Material Reuse  
The examined data shows several obstacles that limit the reuse of materials in the construction 
sector. The foremost of these is the absence of standardization or, rather, its presence in terms 
of materials, dimensions, composition, interfaces, and many other factors. This absence inhibits 
the disassembly and reuse of constructed components as non-standard parts are costly to 
realign. Another claimed non-technical form is the irreversibility of interfaces. Structural 
components of buildings are set in such a manner that almost eliminates the chance of 
segregation. Due to the complexity of nondestructive disassembly, the economic value of such 
component’s plummets. Moreover, the absence of modularity increases the difficulty and 
therefore limits the economic efficiency of reusing single modular components.  
 
Furthermore, an important cost related to market issues is the supposed lower price of second-
hand materials. It can be argued that whilst second-hand materials are available, they attract a 
cost premium from further processing, quality control, and logistics gluts. The absence of 
attractive rewards grows together with the strong industry new-material oriented mentality for 
reuse-based systems. In addition, the market is not geared up for anything other than 
plasterboard and timber parts, suggesting that regulatory change or some other form of incentive 
is necessary to get the materials. Another major obstacle is the fragmented value chain in the 
construction industry. Architects, engineers, and contractors are ineffective with one another, 
resulting in lost opportunities for incorporating material reuse during the early stages of the 
design process. Information-sharing is often poorly done which further limits the choices 
available regarding the materials that can be reused.

2. Logistical Barriers to Material Reuse 
There are several service barriers that revolve around the supply chain and affect the efficient 
reutilization construction materials. Sorting and storing reusable materials are difficult, because 
in the absence of basic infrastructure, there is no effective segregation or quality maintenance. 
Many industries point out that there is insufficient space to build for stored salvaged 
concentrates which leads to ineffectiveness and suffrage of the concentrates.  
Another logistics issue is the expense and complexity of the transportation. The transportation 
of reusable materials to and from deconstruction storage facilities or new building sites is 
expensive, and even more so when materials need special attention and care. Furthermore, the 
incomplete state of available materials and the non-systematic character of deconstruction work 
add even more unsatisfactory criteria for transportation schemes and scheduling. 
An underlying constraint with the existing logistics structures is that there are no centralized 
reverse logistics systems. Companies, however, that they have no reliable ways to track, collect 
and redistribute reclaimed materials. The absence of a composed logistics network is leading to 
materials flow remaining inconsistent and unsystematic, making the scaling of reuse projects 
unsustainable. 
2. Use of Product PlaSorms for Reuse Purposes 
From the data collected, product platforms exist to enable material reuse. Some companies seem 
to grasp the concept of product platforms, yet they do not implement them. The standard design 
of platforms across projects is not prevalent which stops system shifts towards modular, 
reusable, and widespread systems. The adoption of modular designs is low across the industry 
which reveals a clear gap in design-for-reuse implementation. The few pilots implemented by 
companies using modularization assist in disassembly and reassembly, however widespread 
implementation is severely prevented by conventional methods of construction design and legal 
restrictions. Companies tend to be more developed in implementing reuse measures when 
fostering relationships with material suppliers and design companies. However, in most 
situations, companies tend to remain self-contained, which prevents the learning and sharing of 
reusable material systems.

3. Reverse LogisTcs and Material Flow Analysis (MFA) 
One of the main barriers for reuse strategies has to do with the lack of existing frameworks for 
reverse logistics. The data suggest that no one of the companies surveyed possesses any degree 
of reverse logistics processes which makes the systematic retrieval and reintegration of 
materials into projects impossible. The absence of a centralized infrastructure implies that 
material collection, sorting, and redistribution processes are not only unstructured but also 
highly inefficient. External partnerships also provide a measure of remedy to the absence of 
internal reverse logistics capacity. Numerous companies depend on partnerships with 
specialized material recovery and redistribution companies. Although these collaborations are 
necessary for some degree of reuse, they do not eliminate the requirement for comprehensive 
in-house logistics systems that are tailored to the specific requirements of the companies. 
 
The shortfall of application of MFA by all surveyed companies is, perhaps, one of the most 
shocking results. MFA is one of the foremost techniques to monitor materials used, waste 
produced, and materials that can potentially be reused. In the absence of consistent as well as 
systematic integration of MFA, a company is not able to account for its material flows and this 
makes it difficult to develop optimized material reuse strategies and measure the circular 
economy's impact. Moreover, two companies referred to the use of BIM as a possible aid in more 
effective tracking and management of reusable materials. BIM allows for better documentation 
and representation of material data, which may improve the efficacy of reuse planning and 
reverse logistics processes.

6.1.2. 
Design Approaches and Frameworks for Reuse  
 
In order to overcome the barriers to material reuse, design approaches and frameworks are 
necessary to be applied. One of the most promising approaches is the Design for Disassembly 
(DfD) for developing buildings and materials that could be easily disassembled, considering 
their environmentally friendly disposal. By offering effective recoveries and reutilization of 
materials, DfD furthers principles of circularity and resource optimization. This is, in fact, the 
mindset which shifts from designing for stability towards designing for adaptable outcomes in 
order to keep the materials in circulation as long as possible. It would be very easy to 
implement DfD on product platforms to reach essential gains concerning construction practices’ 
sustainability. One further important framework within the platform thinking is modularity and 
scalability. It standardized and operated components for project diversity and efficiency. This 
will reduce not only waste but also make the construction process easy in which “recycled” 
materials can fit without much effort.  
 
Platform thinking enables the development of circular and resource efficient systems. On the 
contrary, the development of reverse logistics systems will be required, which can collect, sort, 
and recover resources effectively to support the reuse of materials. These systems will return 
claimed materials into the value chain with ease, thus closing loops and ensuring a minimal 
generation of waste. By developing effective reverse logistics processes, there will be a need for 
stakeholder collaboration, investment in infrastructure, and the adoption of technologies in 
developing optimized recovery pathways.

6.2.1. 
Barriers to CE Adop*on 
The history literature highlights a major barrier to adopting CE. In fragmented industries, where 
there is no standardization in product components, processes, and systems. The fragmentation, 
therefore, renders it challenging for various stakeholders, including manufacturers, suppliers and 
consumers, to coordinate well on material reuse and recycling. Reusing materials is slow without 
a common framework, which has yielded inefficiencies and added costs (Murray et al., 2016). In 
addition, industry-specific regulations and practices can further exacerbate this fragmentation. 
Suppose different industry standards inside the automotive industry or electronics 
manufacturing limit the return of materials for recycling from this end-of-life product because 
components are not designed with ease of disassembly or reusing.  
The second is the economic barrier, which also prevents the adoption of CE. What often acts as 
a deterrent to implementing circular models is the cost of establishing reverse logistics systems 
or product redesign for remanufacturing, both of which require initial investment. Companies 
may be reluctant to adopt circular systems without certainty about the investment return, 
particularly if the upfront investments are unwelcome. Additionally, the circular mindset is 
difficult to sell to stakeholders as it clashes with the core and deeply embedded traditional linear 
economic model mostly focused on efficient production and disposal of goods. The lack of 
production in circles and principals is another big challenge. Understanding how to design 
products for circularity and implement reverse logistics systems is inadequate or requires 
expertise in many businesses (Murray et al., 2016). Furthermore, the complexity of circular 
business models frequently necessitates organizations to reconsider the entire construct of their 
value chain, beginning with product design, transitioning through use, maintenance, repairs, 
product refurbishment and product reuse before reaching the end of the so-called product 
lifecycle, i.e. disposal. Thus, organizations sometimes face challenges with adopting CE when the 
advantages are readily apparent, all without access to relevant information or training.

6.2.2. 
Stakeholder Collabora*on 
The literature repeatedly argues that stakeholder collaboration is a key issue in overcoming the 
barriers to CE adoption. Building successful collaboration across the value chain between 
manufacturers, suppliers, consumers, regulators, and reprocessing companies is key to 
streamlining circular processes. However, goals are misaligned, stakeholders mistrust one 
another, and collaboration is often difficult (Murray et al., 2016). Manufacturers may prioritize 
short-term profits and operational efficiency, while customers may not be aware of or interested 
in recycling. These divergent interests can result in gradual efforts and lost opportunities for 
reusing materials (Murray et al., 2016). With the arrival of a digital platform, product life-cycle 
data is collected, and the various stakeholders are connected to each other through the platform, 
which helps to ease collaboration. From overcoming these barriers to collaboration, the role of 
product platforms increases in terms of communication, transparency, and sharing knowledge. 
 
6.2.3. 
Design for Circularity (DfC) 
Design for Circularity, DfC, is one of the main theoretical frameworks for adopting CE. According 
to the basic principles of DfC, a product should be designed with a holistic view of its whole life 
cycle in order to reduce resources used and waste produced. According to the literature, 
products for circularity should be modular, easy to be disassembled, and manufactured from 
materials that can be easily reused or recycled. Adoption of DfC principles at the product 
development stage will increase the viability of circular models. Moreover, product platforms are 
in line with the principles of DfC. According to Munday et al., 2016, product platform is a 
standardized set of components that is utilized across multiple models. Hence, more than one 
product model shares common parts from the product platform, leading to product design 
simplification. Moreover, product platforms enable the reuse of components at the end of the 
life of a product, and with that it is a practical resolving solution for the economic and 
informational barriers laid above. By standardizing components, manufacturers can simplify 
production, reduce waste, and ensure the design of components for recovery and reuse. Product 
design is not the only role of product platforms in CE as this platform makes it possible to scale 
and increase efficiency in circular practices. Companies can start with a design for modularity, 
making it easier to design a reverse logistics system once a product has been designed for 
==PAGE:56==

disassembly and modularity. Additionally, standardised components improve the marketability 
of remanufactured parts because their reuse across different products is more desirable to 
remanufacturers. 
 
6.2.4. 
Reverse Logis*cs and Technological Enablers 
CE relies on reverse logistics to reverse its material flows once a product reaches the end of its 
relevance. Reverse logistics is a concept that integrates recovery pathways involving materials 
returning to the supply chain after use. Reverse logistics systems are efficient and allow for the 
collection, sorting, and redistribution of the materials to be repurposed into new products or 
remanufactured goods. The efficiency of reverse logistics systems is greatly enhanced with the 
help of technological enablers. Stakeholder traceability, tracking, and coordination can be 
improved by adopting digital platforms, IoT (Internet of Things) sensors and data analytics 
(Hassan et al., 2022). These can also signify technology for transparency and responsibility in 
reverse logistics. Technological innovations allow companies to improve material flows, reduce 
costs and increase the circular models' overall effectiveness. For example, IoT sensors in a 
product can report its condition, informing you when a product is past its useful life for recycling 
or reusing. That is why blockchain can also help those who need to know about the product, from 
the earliest stages of design to its end-of-life recovery, to receive honest access to accurate data. 
 
6.2.5.

disassembly during the end-of-life vehicle process. Similarly, companies in the wider 
manufacturing sector have followed, where there has been the use of standardized components 
that are easily recyclable or reusable for new products (Hassan et al., 2022). It is illustrated in the 
electronic sector that a company has introduced a closed-loop system where old electronic 
products are collected, refurbished, and sold again, reducing raw material need. These few

7. Conclusion 
 
This study has investigated how product platforms may support construction industry companies 
in their material reuse for CE in view of standardization challenges. The thesis’ objective is to 
explore the possibility of product platforms overcoming the barriers that might prevent the 
diffusion of those CE enablers such as DfD, reverse logistics, and stakeholder collaboration due 
to irreversible interfaces, missing standardization, and fragmented value chains. Product 
platforms offer standardized, modular, and easily separable components designed for reuse and 
hence decrease the barriers to material reuse in the construction industry. Standardizing the 
interfaces within the industry allows the adoption of components which are interchangeable with 
each other, thus enabling the recovery and reuse of materials from existing buildings for new 
projects. Additionally, product platforms have helped address the critical challenge of interface 
irreversibility in the construction sector by enhancing compatibility between various components 
and systems. Material circulation is a feature in the contributions made toward product 
platforms, where resources can be reused in these product platforms. An extendable and flexible 
building construction base allows CE practices. By paying attention to modularity and reusability 
versus initial cost of creation, construction companies can not only create products that are easy 
to assemble but also easy to disassemble and recycle to meet key sustainability goals. Product 
platforms can further be designed by integrating reverse logistics systems, which allow better 
material recovery and reuse in the last phase of a building's life. 
The possibility to separate components easily without damaging them through interface 
reversibility means that more value can be recovered, refurbished, or reused from the valuable 
material resources, thus reducing discards, and saving resources. Interface reversibility is only 
possible with the use of standardized components and modular designs supported by product 
platforms. The assembled modules include standard components, which make construction 
easier but also facilitate easier deconstruction, maintenance, repair, and upgrading of building 
modules. At the same time, this simplifies refurbishment and improves the compatibility of 
components between projects, thereby bringing about the possibility of reusing materials 
==PAGE:60==
60 
 
associated with the outcome of different projects. In such a framework, buildings are conceived 
as dynamic systems in which lines of evolution can meet changing needs. 
According to the series of research, non-standardization, irreversible design interfaces, and 
inefficient or fragmented information flow have been reported as fundamental obstacles to 
practical material reuse within the construction industry. The value chain fragmentation in 
construction has obviously reinforced the above-mentioned factors. For instance, stakeholders, 
architects, engineers, contractors, and suppliers tend to be incompatible in system and process 
issues. They have identified how product platforms can reduce such problems by establishing 
common material and component reuse frameworks across the range of projects. It also 
indicated how DfD principles could be a practice within a product platform to offer the potential 
to enable the construction industry, considering CE challenges, to recover and reuse building 
materials by reducing waste and prolonging the life of materials. Also included is the reverse 
logistics and the extent to which the product platforms have enhanced this domain in the areas 
of gathering materials for reuse, sorting, and then redistributive efforts. Such logistics are 
simpler, as taking the decision factors away means recovering materials even more effectively 
today. The research further indicates the importance of platform thinking within the construction 
industry through developing flexible and modular systems that may easily be used over several 
projects. Some of the important lessons the construction industry might learn from other 
industries, such as automotive and manufacturing, relate to the idea that product platforms 
guarantee reuse and modularity. The research also underlines that strong collaboration among 
the stakeholders is crucial for the successful implementation of product platforms in 
construction. Among the strategies to deal with fragmentation and align interests of all parties 
in the construction value chain, information sharing and digital platforms for data exchange are 
included. 
This research has demonstrated how product platforms, with the implementation, can 
contribute to solving some of the problems scalability and irreversibility pose to design 
interfaces, while providing flexible solutions that could be used for different types of buildings 
and contexts. Adoption of product platforms like reduced material waste and lower production 
costs, has been shown to provide economic and environmental benefits. The research also found

innovations in the form of digital twins and the integration of BIM, which can improve not only 
the development and use of product platforms but also material flow management, decision-
making processes, and increase modularity and adaptability in construction. In summary, product 
platforms leverage a powerful enabler of CE practices in construction. By standardizing material 
use, preventing fragmentation, and eliminating inefficiencies in material reuse, product 
platforms provide a practical and viable means to overcome the obstacles of creating more 
sustainable and resource-efficient infrastructure. To achieve a more circular and resilient 
construction ecosystem, DfD principles will be integrated along with better reverse logistics 
systems and collaboration with stakeholders. Moving forward, developmental support of 
product platforms, digital tools, and innovation may drive the construction industry towards a 
primary desired goal, sustainability. Transitioning to circular practices can significantly reduce the 
environmental impact, conserve resources, and enhance the economic effectiveness of the 
construction industry. Product platforms, standardized components, and systems with the ability 
to adapt and reuse are important to this transformation. The standardization of the construction 
industry through development allows it to become more effective and sustainable when it comes 
to building. Standardized components and processes will simplify construction and 
deconstruction, which provides a foundation for a CE with the reuse, repurposing, and 
reintegration of materials in order to avoid waste and reduce resource mitigation.
