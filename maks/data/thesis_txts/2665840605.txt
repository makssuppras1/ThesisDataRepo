Abstract
This study presents an analysis of how the angular wave spreading affect the pre-
dicted Morison forces of first-order waves over a plane seabed. A multidirectional
sea state, the corresponding particle kinematics, and Morison forces are computed
from a directional JONSWAP spectrum. To further asses the limitation of the first-
order approximations, the results are compared with results from a fully nonlinear
wave model. To address the uncertainty of wave load predictions, a Morison in-
spired model including subharmonic and stochastic contributions are developed.
The model is used to quantify how much stochastic fluctuations from inhomoge-
neous vortex shedding impact the Morison force predictions. The study shows
that the inclusion of a directional function in the wave energy spectrum can affect
the Morison force predictions with up to 30 pct. To our knowledge, this marks
the first estimations of how the angular wave spreading affect the structural loads.
In addition, the study finds that accurate force predictions in flow regimes with
large Keulegan-Carpenter numbers or a unidirectional wave propagation require
nonlinear wave modelling. Finally, the study reveals that for Keulegan-Carpenter
numbers above seven, the Morison model seems to underestimate the inline forces
with around 20 pct.

Introduction
In the recent years, there is a greater and greater political, scientific and economic
interest in the field of offshore engineering. Offshore wind turbines are currently
one of the most promising sources of sustainable energy. Researchers and profes-
sionals are constantly pushing the boundaries and developing new technologies
that make offshore structures more cost-efficient. For offshore wind turbines, this
constant development is necessary if we want the price of offshore wind energy
to be compatible with traditional energy sources like oil and gas. The design of
cost-efficient offshore structures, whether it is wind turbines, oil rigs or cables
at the seabed, requires a qualified understanding of the metocean condition on
site. As researchers and professionals are developing the offshore structures in in-
creasingly harsh environments, detailed knowledge of the surrounding waves and
particle kinematics is required to make accurate predictions of the structural loads.
Ocean waves are irregular and stochastic by nature, since they usually are com-
posed of different frequencies, phases and directions. Calculating the forces on
an offshore structure is therefore not a trivial task and is often associated with a
relatively high degree of uncertainty. Professionals and researchers use different
approaches in the industry, in which some of them relies on linear wave theory.
Often they assume that wave fields are unidirectional, with all the frequencies
propagating in the same direction. This assumption, however, is rarely satisfied.
Even though offshore structures are often exposed to multi directional wave fields,
the literature on structural loads from multi directional sea states is rather limited.
To my knowledge, it has not been analysed how the magnitude of directional wave
spreading influence structural loads. Based on first-order wave theory, the present
study will analyse how the wave spreading influence the inline Morison forces.
The analysis will cover three different sea states with three different degrees of
wave spreading.
The force calculations obtained from the first-order wave model will be compared
to the results from a fully nonlinear wave model recently developed by Klahn et
al., 2020. From the comparison between the two models, I assess the degree to
which the nonlinear effects of the wave field affect the predicted structural loads.
Another uncertainty factor influencing the design of offshore structures is the force
transverse to the inline force. The transverse force (often referred to as the lift
Irregular Wave Kinematics and Stochastic Processes for Wave Load Predictions
1
==PAGE:9==
force) arises due to vortex shedding at the wake of the cylinder. Even though the
lift force has been studied extensively through the past decades, estimating its time
course remain a difficult task. The process of vortex shedding is turbulent and dif-
ficult to predict. In addition to this, the formation of vortices are highly dependent
on the cylinder roughness, which is constantly changing in marine environments
due to marine growth. The Morison equation provide a good estimates of the mov-
ing average of the forces. However, subharmonic contributions from the lift force
are not included. Nor are stochastic variation from incoming turbulence or ran-
domness in the vortex shedding process included. As a result, the Morison force
model might underestimate the forces from short term variations significantly.
The thesis is divided in two parts. The first part examines the effect of wave
spreading on structural loads. The second part examines the effect of subharmonic
contributions from the lift force and stochastic variations in the forces. The pur-
pose of the study is to contribute to a better understanding of the structural loads
on offshore foundations. Reducing the uncertainty in force predictions will give
a better design basis. Ultimately this will reduce the uncertainty of financial in-
vestments in the offshore sector and pave the road to better economical decision
making.

First-Order Random Wave
Model in Finite Depth
First-order wave theory, also referred to as linear wave theory, Airy waves or first-
order Stokes’ waves, is a linear description of small amplitude surface gravity
waves. Strictly speaking, first-order wave theory is only applicable for ideal fluids
with gravitation as the only external force. The theory can be used to describe the
individual regular waves, which together forms a random sea also known as an
irregular sea state.
In this chapter, a 3D irregular first-order wave model will be established. The
model calculates the surface elevation, η as a function of time and space based
on which the wave kinematics in x, y and z direction can be obtained. From the
particle kinematics, the Morison force acting on a cylindrical submerged structure
is calculated. The waves in the model are propagating in a finite water depth (h)
over a plane horizontal seabed. The wave length is denoted λ, and the wave height
is denoted H. A principal sketch of the model is illustrated in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Principle sketch of numerical model.
One of the main requirements behind first-order waves is that the wave amplitude
(H/2) is small relative to the water depth and wave length. This requirement is
also known as the small-amplitude approximation (Holthuijsen, 2010). One of the
reason that we need to consider only small amplitude waves is due to the small,
but yet important, boundary layer near the seabed. The boundary layer can have
a significant effect on the wave kinematics. The layer thickness grows with the
4

distance that the fluid particle near the seabed travels. If H/h becomes too large,
then the viscous and turbulent effects of the boundary layer cannot be neglected
(Svendsen & Jonsson, 1976). Depending on the bottom roughness, Svendsen and
Jonsson, 1976 have shown, that Hλ/h2 < 20 or 30 is an appropriate upper limit.
Within this limit, the flow conserves vorticity according to the Kelvin Theorem,
and potential flow theory can be applied to solve the problem.

Spectra are widely used in many different engineering applications. It was first
discovered by Newton, who decomposed sunlight into a range of colors with a
prism (Goda, 2010). In this way it became evident, that white light is composed

In the physical world, an infinite number of frequencies will be present in an
oceanic wave field. This makes the spectral function continuous and smooth. Time
is also a continuous function in the physical world. However, we need to discritize

1.4
Directional spectra
Despite having different frequencies, waves in the ocean also tend to propagate in
different directions. This gives rise to the random and at first sight unorganised
motion of water at open sea. To capture the superimposed directional components
of the waves, the directional spectrum is introduced by including a directional
function D(θ|f) in the spectral density function. The new directional wave spec-
tral density function (S(f, θ)) is written on a general form as:
S(f, θ) = S(f)D(θ|f)
(1.17)
The directional function quantifies the directional distribution of wave energy.
Thus D(θ|f) represents the magnitude of directional spreading of energy (Goda,
2010). Since the directional spectrum describes the wave energy as a function
of propagation angle and frequency, we can now extend Eq. 1.13 to include the
directional function, i.e.:

The wave induced forces on an offshore structure are governed by the particle
kinematic. With the velocity potential expression given in Eq. (1.8) it is straight

stretching (Wheeler, 1970).

and the inertia force can be represented by an inertia coefficient, CM.

1.9
A Statistical Remark
As seen until now, the sea surface can be described as a stochastic process, with
an infinite number of possible realisations (Holthuijsen, 2010). A times series of
the surface elevation at a given point, is therefore just one out of many possible
realisations. The representation of the linear random waves given in preceding
sections, must satisfy the two well-known conditions of stationarity and ergodicity
and be Gaussian distributed. The independent variable in the stochastic process
can either be time or spatial coordinates (Goda, 2010). The statistical properties
of the sea will not depend on which independent variable is used. This statistical
characteristic will be relevant at later stages, where the results from a time domain
model will be compared with the results from a spatial domain model.
26
Irregular Wave Kinematics and Stochastic Processes for Wave Load Predictions
==PAGE:34==
The condition of stationarity means, that all statistical properties should be inde-
pendent of time. The ergodicity condition means, that the time average statistics
for a given sample are equal to those of the ensemble average (Goda, 2010). If
these two conditions are satisfied, a wave record (η) can be described by a number
of smaller ensembles of η. The number of observations obtained from the Monte-
Carlo simulations described in the above sections can therefore be increased, by
running the simulations multiple times with different seeds. For a fully linear sea
state, the standard deviation of the surface elevation is ση = 4Hm0. The skew-
ness (S) and kurtosis (K) of the surface elevation in the linear case is 0 and 3
respectively.

3
Linear vs. Nonlinear Sea States
In the previous sections, the surface elevation was assumed to be normally dis-
tributed. Field observations has shown that this is not always the case. Surface
gravity waves are more complex than the simple first-order wave theory suggest.
While ocean waves are generally thought of as only weakly nonlinear, the nonlin-
ear contributions is proven to have an impact on the surface elevation and particle
kinematics (Klahn et al., 2020, Goda, 2010 etc.). Nonlinear numerical simula-
tions of larger wave fields is expensive in terms of computational effort. To make
the nonlinear wave problem easier to solve, the simulations has historically been
based on weakly nonlinear models like the nonlinear Schrödinger equation or a
low order truncation of the High Order Spectral method. Klahn et al., 2020, raise
concerns about these models long term accuracy and robustness. Furthermore,
only the surface kinematics are directly obtained in the weakly nonlinear wave
models. Klahn et al., 2020 has developed a method for simulating larger nonlin-
ear wave fields and the corresponding particle kinematics for the entire body of
water. In this chapter, the methodology behind the model of Klahn et al., 2020 is
briefly reviewed and thereafter used to analyse the nonlinear wave effects on the
structural loads.
As touch upon previously, the skewness and kurtosis are two statistical parameters,
that gives a good indication of the distortion between the observed distribution and
the normal distribution. We follow Klahn et al., 2020 notation, and write these as
S = ⟨η3⟩/⟨η2⟩3/2 and K = ⟨η4⟩/⟨η2⟩2 respectively. The angle brackets ⟨·⟩denotes
spatial averages. We recall that fully linear sea state will have the values S = 0
and K = 3. This limit state is reached as the wave steepness approaches zero.
Records of ocean waves often shows a positive skewness. This means that the
mode of the surface elevation distribution is lower than the mean. The skewness
is found to be proportional to the wave steepness. Huang and Long, 1980 have
suggested the relation S ≃8π(η2)1/2/λ where the bar indicates temporal aver-
aging. The kurtosis is a measure of the sharpness of the mode of the statistical
surface elevation distribution. for K > 3 the distribution has a sharper peak (and
longer tails) than the normal distribution and vice versa.
In literature, waves are often referred to as skewed if the wave crest heights are
greater than the wave trough depths. A positive skewness of the PDF suggests
Irregular Wave Kinematics and Stochastic Processes for Wave Load Predictions
41
==PAGE:49==
that the individual waves in the record are skewed. Despite asymmetry around the
still water level, Waves can also be asymmetric in the propagation direction. The
asymmetry in the propagation direction is often just referred as asymmetry. The
asymmetry is a measure of the surface gradient of the wave front relative to the
surface gradient of the wave tail. Generally, when waves approach the surf zone,
the wave front will become steeper, which will have an impact of the particle
kinematics. To get the full picture of the structural wave induces loads, nonlin-
ear effects like wave steepening, skewness and asymmetry should be taken into
consideration.
The nonlinear wave model by Klahn et al., 2020 will be used in this study for two
purposes: 1) Validating the results of the linear model by lowering the wave steep-
ness to an extend where the nonlinear contribution is negligible. 2) Analyse how
much the wave steepness (i.e. nonlinearity of the wave field) affect the particle
kinematics and thereby the wave induced structural loads for different direction-
alities.

3.4
Discussion
In this part of the thesis, we have developed a directional wave model using first-
order wave theory. We found that when the directional wave spreading decreases
the Morison loads on a vertical cylinder will increase up to around 30 pct. The
linear wave model utilise a stochastic process to model ocean waves. We have
seen that the surface elevation is normally distributed and the envelope follows a
Rayleigh distribution. The advantages of linear wave theory is easy to locate: It is
the lowest order wave approximation, which makes the theory simple and highly
computational efficient. With the Fourier method, the surface elevation of larger
wave fields can be computed within a couple of minutes on modern computers.
Many studies have examined the probability function of ocean waves (some of
the more recent studies includes Onorato et al., 2009 and Toffoli et al., 2010). It
52
Irregular Wave Kinematics and Stochastic Processes for Wave Load Predictions
==PAGE:60==
is by now a well-established fact that in real life settings we observe waves with
higher crest and lower troughs than those predicted by linear wave theory. This
finding will evidently have an impact on the particle kinematics and ultimately the
structural loads patterns. As such, concerns have been raised about the accuracy
of linear wave theory.
In an attempt to quantify the nonlinear effects on force predictions, we compare
the structural loads from the linear model with the structural loads obtained from
a fully nonlinear wave model developed by Klahn et al., 2020. We found that the
nonlinearity primarily have an impact of the particle drag rather than the particle
inertia. Consequently, the magnitude of the nonlinear effects depend on the D/H
ratio: For slender cylinders with large Keulegan-Carpenter numbers, the nonlin-
earity plays an important role when assessing the structural loads. Furthermore,
the nonlinear contributions seem to depend on the angular spreading of wave en-
ergy. For larger cylinders in sea states with a high degree of directional spreading,
the nonlinearity seems to have only limited effect. However, If the waves tend to
come from only one direction then the nonlinear effects seem to be rather impor-
tant.
When designing a numerical study, one of the challenges is to balance computa-
tional efficiency towards numerical accuracy. In the present study of linear waves,
we left out the convective acceleration term when we calculated the particle iner-
tia. This was done in order to keep the computational time down. Because of
the utilised Wheeler stretching, the velocity profiles could not be computed in a
Fourier space. If we included the convective acceleration, it would imply doing a
simple summation for each of the three terms that the convective acceleration con-
sist of. This procedure would increase the overall computational time manyfold.
In hindsight, it might have been beneficial to dedicate more resources in terms
of computational time to the simulation the linear wave model. The sensitivity
analysis has shown that leaving out the convective acceleration creates one of the
largest uncertainty factors in the linear model’s results.
In engineering practice, it is often desired to find a solution that provide accu-
rate but yet conservative estimates. Leaving out the angular spread of energy in
the sea state will definitely be a conservative approach, but it will also be an un-
necessary inaccurate approach. Usually the metocean conditions for an offshore
development site is well described. The description could either be in terms of
measurement data from the site or detailed numerical hindcast models. In most
cases, it would be beneficial to find a suitable directional function and include
information deriving from this function when predicting structural wave loads.

The hydrodynamic around cylinders and forces on them in oscillatory flows have
been intensively researched for many decades. Among other contributors, Keule-
gan and Carpenter, 1958 study show the forces from a standing wave on a vertical
cylinder. They found that the force coefficients (CD and CM) is a function of
the KC number. Sarpkaya, 1976 did an experimental study of the forces on a
cylinder in oscillatory flow in a u-tube. He found that besides the KC number
the force coefficients depend on a parameter (β = KC/Re), which is the ratio
between the Keulegan-Carpenter and the Reynolds number. Longoria et al., 1991
study showed the forces on a cylinder for both harmonic and irregular flows with a
given β value. Based on experimental data, they calculated the force coefficients
as a function of the observed KC numbers. Sumer and Fredsoe, 2006 summarise
some of the most important findings in their general introductory textbook on flow
around circular structures.
It is a well known phenomenon that when the KC number becomes sufficiently
high vortices will start to form at the wake of the cylinder. The vortices are formed
in accordance with the vorticity in the shear layer between the outer wall of the
cylinder and the free stream. For even larger KC numbers, the vortices will de-
tach from the cylinder and vortex shedding will make the flow unstable. The vor-
tex shedding will affect the cylinder by imposing a force transverse to the inline
force. This force will be referred to as the lift force denoted FL in the following
sections. The Morison equation, which we have used to calculate the inline forces
in the previous chapters, are inaccurate once vortices starts to affect the flow. The
equation provides a good prediction of the smooth moving average over a half
flow cycle, but fails to capture the oscillating peaks from the vortex induced sub-
harmonic and stochastic dynamics.
In Part I, we made a rough estimation of the drag and inertia force coefficients. We
assumed that the coefficients was constant in time. We also assumed that the coef-
ficients was constant throughout the water column. In large simulations where we
are interested in reducing the degrees of freedoms as much as possible, this prag-
matic approach can be justified. In reality, however, the hydrodynamic processes
are more complicated. The flow regime defined by the KC and Re numbers will
Irregular Wave Kinematics and Stochastic Processes for Wave Load Predictions
55
==PAGE:63==
depend on the characteristic of the individual wave passing the structure. Further-
more, the flow regime will change as a function of depth due to depth variations
in the particle kinematics.
1.1
Flow Regimes
Williamson, 1985 has studied the nature and patterns of vortex shedding for dif-
ferent sinusoidal flow regimes. His study found that the flow reversal causes the
vortex formed in the one flow half cycle to sweep across the cylinder in the fol-
lowing flow half cycle. This event affects both the magnitude and the frequency
of the forces on a cylinder. As a guideline Williamson, 1985 divided the flow into
five regimes:
1. 0 < KC < 7 (Pairing of attached vortices): In this flow regime, two
rather symmetrical vortices will form. The vortices will stay attached to the
cylinder. In the range of 4 < KC < 7 the vortices become asymmetric in
terms of strength and size. This asymmetry will result in a lift force with the
same frequency as the oscillating flow. The lift force has a low amplitude
and hence we will disregard it in the following analysis.
2. 7 < KC < 15 (Transverse street/Single pair): A pair of vortices are shed
in each flow half cycle. This will give rise to a lift force of two times the
frequency of the inline force. If we introduce the variable NL as the nor-
malised frequency relative to the inline Morison force, we will have NL = 2
in this flow regime.
3. 15 < KC < 24 (Double pair): Two pairs of vortices are shed in each flow
half cycle. In this flow regime, the lift force will have a frequency of three
times the oscillating frequency of the flow (NL = 3).
4. 24 < KC < 32 (Three pairs): Three pairs of vortices are shed in each flow
half cycle. In this flow regime, the lift force will have a frequency of four
times the oscillating frequency of the flow (NL = 4).
5. 32 < KC < 40 (Four pairs): Four pairs of vortices are shed in each flow
half cycle. In this flow regime, the lift force will have a frequency of five
times the oscillating frequency of the flow, (NL = 5).
The shape of the time series of the lift force varies across the different flow regimes.
For 7 < KC < 15, the lift force is more or less sinusoidal. This is not the case
for the flow regimes with higher KC numbers, where the shape of the lift force
is deviating more measured up against a sinusoidal curve. Vortex shedding is a 3-
56

==PAGE:64==
dimensional process. The vortex shedding is correlated in cells through the water
domain. We will therefore not see one large vortex spanning from the free surface
to the seabed, but rather a system of vortices shedding with phase shifts along the
cylinder. Since vortices are turbulent structures, they will tend to mix momentum
between layers through the water column and thereby affect each other. Turbulent
flows, like vortices at the wake of cylinder, is characterised by random fluctuations
in velocity and pressure. These fluctuations will give a stochastic contribution to
both the inline and transverse force.
1.2

The MSSC-model (Morison with Stochastic and Subharmonic Contributions) in
this chapter is formulated based on engineering reasoning. Afterwards, the model
has been calibrated and validated by comparing our results with a small sample

using a more quantitative approach of a so-called goodness-of-fit parameter (δ).
The data from Petersen et al., 2020 stems from a study on harmonic sinusoidal
flow. Consequently, we also use a harmonic sinusoidal wave as baseline. Once
our model produced satisfactory results for this idealised wave, we used the results
to compute the forces from an irregular wave train.

surface elevation time series is identified by a zero down crossing analysis. The
water body within each wave is then discretized into a matrix of m×n nodes. The

1.4
Structural Load Sensitivity to Marine Growth
Another uncertainty factor that influence wave load predictions on offshore struc-
tures is the occurrence of marine growth. Marine growth is random and unpre-
dictable. Some areas on the structure or certain places of a development site can
be more exposed to marine growth than others. Furthermore, The biologic compo-
sition of marine growth varies across different sites. Consequently the thickness
and roughness of the cylinder varies with the type of marine growth. Despite all
the uncertainty marine growth yield, one thing is certain: Marine growth will in-
crease the cylinder diameter, which influence the KC number and ultimately the
force coefficients. In most cases, the marine growth will also roughen the cylinder
which most likely will increase the CD coefficient.
The research field of how marine growth effect the hydrodynamic of cylinders is
still relatively unexplored. It is not well known whether the presence of marine
growth will increase or dampen the vortex shedding and flow instability. This
might also depend on the specific type of marine growth: If the marine growth is
permeable, one could imagine that this will dampen the vortex formation process.
On the other hand, if the marine growth is impermeable, the formation of vortices
might occur at lower KC numbers as a result of a increased roughness size. We
will in the present section try to estimate how much marine growth effect the loads
on a cylinder with a diameter of 1 meter. The analysis will be carried out by
increasing the cylinder diameter with a random amount in every one of the 20
water layers. An increase in the diameter size represents the presence of marine
growth. The force coefficient will be adjusted according to the amount of marine
growth at a given cross section. At cross sections with marine growth, we will
assume that it covers the entire surface of the structure.

Discussion
The performance of the Morison model depends on many parameters such as the
KC and Re numbers, cylinder roughness and potential secondary structures that
72
Irregular Wave Kinematics and Stochastic Processes for Wave Load Predictions
==PAGE:80==
influence the flow. Furthermore, it is essential to make accurate estimations of
the force coefficients CD and CM in order to make accurate Morison force predic-
tions. Estimating the force coefficients is a difficult task in offshore environments
where stochastic processes such as marine growth, currents, wave irregularity and
turbulence interference have to be accounted for.
We have seen that the KC numbers are strongly dependent on both water depth
and the individual wave characteristics. Making accurate force predictions will
therefore yield many degrees of freedom, which demands many resources to solve.
For larger structures, where the risk of vortex shedding is low, it might be sufficient
to simplify the force predictions with the methodology used in Part I. However, for
jackets and other slender designs stochastic vortex shedding should be considered.
In the MSSC-model we have divided the flow into four regimes and one sub-
regime. In each of the regimes, we defined two variables (RA and RNL) used
to model the stochastic variations. By Increasing the number of flow regimes
even more, we could probably have matched the goodness-of-fit (δ) parameter by
Justesen, 1989 even better. Currently, we do not capture increase in δ observed
in the region of 5 < KC < 7. Neither do we capture the decrease in δ seen
between 12 < KC < 15. Another way of refining the MSSC-model is to use a
more detailed model for the lift force. In the current model we assume that the
lift force follows a sinusoidal flow. Measurements have shown that this is a good
approximation in the region of 7 < KC < 15. For larger KC numbers, the shape
of the lift force will start to deviate from the harmonic oscillation. One could
also incorporate a more sophisticated lift force model, like the one introduced in
Bearman et al., 1984 etc.
Finally, we gave a rough estimate on how much the presence of marine growth
will impact the predicted forces. This is a difficult task, since the biological com-
position of marine growth is diverse by nature. Some marine growth is hard and
impermeable and some is soft and permeable. Furthermore, the literature on how
marine growth impact the force coefficients is rater limited. The literature that
exist on how the force coefficients is effected by the cylinder roughness (Justesen,
1989 etc.), is mostly small scale laboratory experiments. There might be a scaling
problem, once these test results are applied on larger structures. Based on our very
simplified model, we estimated that marine growth can impact the force prediction
with a factor of O(2).

Conclusion
The overall scope of the study is to produce a model that can reduce the uncer-
tainty in force predictions on cylindrical offshore structures. Waves in offshore
environments are usually composed of different frequencies propagating in differ-
ent directions. Even though this is the conditions that offshore developers often
faces, little is known about how the angular spread of energy affects the structural
force predictions. Part I is dedicated to examine how an increase in the angular
spreading of energy affects the Morison forces on a cylindrical structure. The re-
sults from a linear wave model is compared to the results of a fully nonlinear wave
model. This is done to further quantify how big contributions the nonlinear effects
have on the force predictions.
By an inverse Fourier transformation, we have computed the sea surface from a
predefined directional JONSWAP spectrum. Afterwards, we have calculated the
depth integrated particle kinematics in order to calculate the inline Morison force
acting on a vertical cylinder. Then we have simulated three different sea states
with three different wave spreading distribution parameters (s = 0, s = 1 and uni-
directional). The analysis is based on approximately 62000 waves corresponding
to a time duration of around 8 days. In general the loads increase as the magnitude
of wave spreading decreases. In the unidirectional sea state, the loads were up to
30 pct. higher than the loads from the directional sea state with s = 0. The loads
from the sea state with spreading parameter of s = 1 are roughly 10 pct. higher
than loads from the sea state with s = 0.
In order to achieve high loads in the unidirectional case, the phases of the in-
dividual frequencies need to match. However, when we have directional wave
spreading, both the phases and directions need to match to get high forces. An ad-
ditional degree of freedom will thereby reduce the risk of high loads. We can also
describe this process from an energy perspective: The total energy of the sea state
is given by the directional JONSWAP spectrum and is thereby identical regardless
of the degree of wave spreading. Narrowing the directional wave spreading will
narrow the directional spread of energy in the sea as well. The resultant energy
vector from the x and y energy vectors will increase as spreading of energy de-
creases. Since the resultant energy is proportional to the inline force, narrowing
the directional distribution will increase the inline force as well.

We found that in the case with a cylinder diameter of 6 meter the nonlinearity
had large impact on the unidirectional sea. The sea states with directional wave
spreading seemed uninfluenced by the nonlinear contributions. We also found that
the nonlinearity mainly affects the drag term of the Morison equation. By lowering
the cylinder diameter to 1 meter the forces from all three sea states were impacted
by the nonlinear effects.
Another uncertainty that influence the design of offshore structures is the stochas-
tic processes of vortex shedding. in flow regimes with large Keulegan-Carpenter
numbers (typically above 7), the vortex shedding will impose a force on the struc-
ture transverse to the inline force. Furthermore, experiments and CFD simulations
has revealed that vortex shedding gives a stochastic contribution to both the inline
and transverse force. The Morison model fails to capture these stochastic effects.
Hence, Part II is dedicated to building an engineering model that captures the trans-
verse force and the stochastic force fluctuations that is often seen in measurement
data. The aim in Part II is to assess how big an impact the stochastic contributions
have on the overall force predictions for linear irregular waves.
With a few relatively simple steps, we have developed a MSSC-model (Mori-
son with the inclusion of Subharmonic and Stochastic Contributions). Our model
seems to reproduce the shape of the inline forces acting on cylinder under the in-
fluence of waves. We validated the shape by comparing it with measurements and
CFD simulations found in the literature. Our model thereby solves the stochastic
fluctuations and subharmonic contributions, which remain unsolved in the simple
Morison model. Our model relies on the assumption that the stochastic fluctua-
tions can be described as a summation of a number of random sinusoidal functions.
Furthermore, we allow the KC numbers in the model to vary over time and depth,
which gives a more accurate force coefficient estimation. The MSSC-model is
further validated by a goodness-of-fit analysis between our model and the simple
Morison model. The result of the goodness-of-fit analysis fits nicely with the
literature of other goodness-of-fit parameters.
If we consider the peaks of the inline forces with a 10 pct. exceedance probability
or lower then the MSSC-model gives approximately 20 pct. higher force predic-
tions than the Morison model. If we consider the peaks of the total forces with a
10 pct. exceedance probability or lower, the MSSC-model gives around 25 pct.
higher force predictions than the Morison model. The time scale of the fluctuating
peaks that exceeds the Morison force is shorter than the time scale of the Morison
force. According to the results, the Morison model will neither produce accurate

nor conservative results for flow regimes with KC > 7. This should especially be
taken into consideration when designing jacket foundations. The individual mem-

In future studies, it would be interesting to examine how other distributional func-
tions (like the exponential distribution by Sand and Mynett, 1987 etc.) affects the
force predictions. It would also be interesting to include the term of convective
acceleration when calculating the particle kinematics to see if this affect the re-
sults from the linear wave model. Furthermore, research could be done on how
a stochastic expansion of the Morison model could be developed. The MSSC-
model proposed in this study is without a doubt simplistic and based on a small
data sample. However, despite the MSSC-model’s simplicity it demonstrates the
potential advantages of a more sophisticated Morison model. Finally, it would be
beneficial for offshore development to gain a deeper understanding of how marine
growth effects the force coefficients and vortex shedding. Marine growth remains
one of the large uncertainty factors when designing offshore structures.
