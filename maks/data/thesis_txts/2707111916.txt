Abstract
This project seeks to model and prevent high temperature events in the post combustion
chamber of Energos waste to energy plants. This is desired because it has the potential to
reduce fouling on the walls of the post combustion chamber(PCC). In order to model to
the temperature dynamics in the chamber several data driven approaches were explored.
Both offline and online system identification techniques were tested. The offline system
identification efforts fell short due to the slowly changing dynamics and disturbances of
a waste to energy process. The online system identification utilized the recursive least
squares algorithm and provided a more reliable result. Then a simple control strategy
where the nominal controller is interrupted once the temperature either exceed, or is
predicted to exceed, a preset limit, a corrective action is performed. This corrective
action was shown to reduce the post combustion chamber temperature. After testing
the solutions on the plant the operational staff confirmed that the fouling was lower than
previous operational periods, and the temperature was also successfully kept within its
limits the majority of the time. A preliminary conclusion is therefore that the final
solution mitigates the issue, though longer term tests needs to be performed to verify
this.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Waste to energy plants provide an important role in society. They reduce the need for
landfill while providing power either in the form of electricity for residential or commer-
cial use, steam for industrial purposes or district heating for consumers. However, a
WtE1 process comes with control challenges. Since the waste used as fuel in the process
changes over time, the fuel itself can seen as a disturbance. Furthermore, there are
transport delays between a change in actuation and the resulting change in measured
outputs. This means that classical linear control methods rarely are suﬀicient.
This project is based on a Norwegian waste gasification plant of the Energos type.
A waste gasification plant is more eﬀicient at recovering energy from the waste than an
incineration plant. Incineration processes rely on simply using the waste as fuel for com-
bustion with large amounts of air turning it into carbon dioxide and heat. Gasification
processes work on a different principle. The gasification process works by turning the
waste into synthesis gas, commonly referred to as syngas. The gasification happens in
the primary chamber on top of the waste grate. The gasification is a substoichiometric
partial oxidation process, meaning that the environment must be controlled to have in-
suﬀicient oxygen for a complete combustion. The syngas then flows into the PCC2 in
order to combust there.[Cou21]
This project is done in collaboration with Dublix Technology. Dublix has delivered an
upgraded control strategy to the plant. This upgraded control strategy is implemented
in Dublix’s in-house software called FuzEvent. FuzEvent reads process values from the
DCS3 of the plant via an OPC4 connection, and sends calculated values back to the
DCS. This means that a control strategy of a plant can be upgraded regardless of spare
computing capacity in the DCS.
The plant consists of two identical lines, that are controlled independently. Line 2 has
been experiencing some issues where running at nominal power production periodically
resulted in excessive temperatures in the PCC. These high temperatures can cause the
fly ash to form fouling on the walls of the PCC. Fouling reduces the eﬀiciency of the plant,
thus requiring higher temperatures to maintain nominal power. This causes a vicious
cycle where once the fouling have formed, the prevention of further fouling becomes
more diﬀicult.[Rin01]

use that model as a basis of predicting and preventing high temperature events. This
will be done under the assumption that the plant is generally being controlled well, so

this assumption will have to be tested. The effect of other air related actuations will
also be explored.

1.2
Challenges
An operational period between maintenance stops for the plant considered in this project
is in the range of 7-12 weeks. Where 12 weeks is very good and 7 weeks is a symptom
of poor performance. The last couple of operational periods before the start of this
project was around 8 weeks.
A contributor to this shorter operational period could
be excessive PCC temperatures, since substantial fouling was observed in the PCC.
Furthermore, PCC temperature violations become more and more frequent during the
operational period. If these assumptions are true, then allowing violations in the PCC
temperature early on in the operational period, would cause a self-reinforcing loop of
temperature violations, causing more temperature violations until continued operation
becomes infeasible. Documentation for this issue can be found in Section 2.3
1.3
Limitations
This project seeks to test the solutions developed on the plant. Therefore the solution will
have to be implementable in FuzEvent. FuzEvent uses a proprietary scripting language
called FUEL6. As the name implies the language was developed for the purpose of using
it with fuzzy logic control. This means that little to no emphasis has been placed on
linear algebra. This means that any matrix vector product must be explicitly calculated.
Furthermore, any machine learning or deep learning solutions are completely infeasible,
but could be considered if another tool is employed.

The reader of this thesis is expected to have a basic understanding of linear control theory,
linear algebra and digital signal processing. No experience with WtE or gasification is

CHAPTER 2
Analysis
2.1
Introduction
This chapter discusses relevant concepts required to solve the issue. The different types
of air injection in the plant will be listed, and their influence on the PCC temperature
will be analyzed. Then the air inputs that should be used to solve the issue is decided.
Furthermore, the progression of the PCC temperature is discussed.
2.2
Lower temperature limit
EU directive 2010/75/EU article 50.2 [EU10] states:
”Waste incineration plants shall be designed, equipped, built and operated in such a way
that the gas resulting from the incineration of waste is raised, after the last injection
of combustion air, in a controlled and homogeneous fashion and even under the most
unfavourable conditions, to a temperature of at least 850°C for at least two seconds”.
Which essentially means that going below 850◦C in the PCC is not allowed.
In
practice this is solved by a gas fired burner turning on if the limit is reached. Therefore,
no specific low temperature handling is desired.
2.3
Temperature progression
During the operation of the plant the temperatures in the PCC trend higher, as the time
since last cleaning stop increases. Because the combustion of syngas happens in the PCC,
the temperature there is higher than in the primary chamber the vast majority of the
time. There is evidence that when the temperature exceed 1100◦C buildup of sticky
residue, called fouling, happens rapidly.[Rin01] In order to minimize the time above
1100◦C the normal operating range should be below 1070◦C.
This particular value
was chosen by the plant management, however it still makes good sense to shoot for
1070◦C and below to allow for disturbances and for hot spots in unmeasured areas to
be compliant.

Secondary air
The secondary air actuation input is responsible for controlling the oxygen level. This
input is connected to the end of the grate as well as at the end of the primary cham-

increases in carbon monoxide pollution which is not desired. This means that no matter
which influence on the PCC temperature is found, it is unlikely that this input will play
a major role in controlling the PCC temperature.

This section will look into offline system identification. Which means that model pa-

types of modelling will be examined in this section, namely:
• State space model
• Process model
The process model is a sum of linear transfer functions plus dead-times.
Which

3.3.1
State space model
Since it has been seen that there seems to be a transport delay between the actuation
and the measurement, it makes sense to include a transport delay in the state space
model. The Matlab function n4sid that can be used for system identification onto state
space models, only supports a known delay. Since the delay is not known, it will have
to be estimated.
In order to identify the delay between an actuation signal and a measured variable,
some statistics is done. This is done to find the delay between a change in the primary
air injection and the PCC temperatures.
Firstly, the fluctuations in both signals are isolated by taking the difference between
a moving average and the latest value. Secondly, the co-variance is calculated. The
co-variance is calculated on shifted versions of the data in order to find the sample delay
where the co-variance between the two signals is the strongest. This does not yield a
dynamic model of the system, but it does provide information on how much delay there
is between the variables. Whether the delay is caused by system dynamics or simple
transport delays is not considered here.
When the delay between the signals that optimizes the co-variances have been ob-
tained, system identification techniques can be applied. The thought behind doing it
this way is to obtain the ”delay free” dynamics, and consider the delay separately. This
means that system identification techniques that cannot handle delays can be used be-
cause the delay has been removed prior to the system identification.
This approach has its limitations.
Since the delay and the system dynamics are
estimated independently, it is hard to consistently differentiate between slow dynamics
and a true transport delay. This issue could possibly be partially resolved by estimating
the delay and the dynamics simultaneously. The delay found on the day considered
consists of 72 10 second samples.

3.4
Recursive least squares algorithm
The recursive least squares algorithm can be used to find gains associated with a number
of regressors that minimize the square error on a desired signal. The RLS2 algorithm
can be used on both linear and non-linear applications. RLS is in its essence a general
purpose adaptive filter.
There are two major types of the RLS algorithm, namely:
• Growing window RLS
• RLS with forgetting factor
The growing window RLS is applicable for situations where the system is known
to be time invariant, but only noisy measurements are available. The growing window
RLS will recursively consider a greater amount of data, and will eventually settle on
the correct parameters. However, if the system is bi-modal, the growing window RLS
will settle somewhere in between the two modes and provide inaccurate results. If the
system is time varying it is also not ideal, where the parameter estimate will follow the
system initially, but once the window grows large enough the contribution of new data
will be little to none.
The issue with the time varying system can in many cases be solved by using the
RLS algorithm with a forgetting factor. The forgetting factor is a tune-able parameter
that puts an exponentially declining weight on old data while fully considering incoming
data. The forgetting factor will in this report be denoted λ where:

CHAPTER 4
Control strategy
4.1
Introduction
The control strategy considered in this project is the one controlling the primary air.
This chapter will describe a special case interrupting the nominal control. Once this
special case is triggered, the control action performed by the nominal controller can be
modified in its magnitude, or completely blocked, depending on the situation.
4.2
Nominal controller
The nominal controller controlling the primary air injection, is a proprietary controller
technology to Dublix Technology called a FECA1. A deeper explanation of the inner
workings of a FECA is beyond the scope of this project. However, the working principle
will be discussed briefly.
A FECA tries to adjust the actuation of a dynamic system with large transport
delays or slow response times, similar to how a human would do it. By making an
adjustment/control-action and then waiting for the system to respond. Once certain
conditions are met reverse actions are made in opposite direction of the initial action.
Furthermore, a deadband around the setpoint is defined where no control-actions will
be applied, in order not to mess with what is working, or trying to correct insignificant
errors or noise. FECAs operate on an index between -1 and 1. This index is typically
calculated from a process value scaled around the desired operating point. Other things
relevant to the control may be incorporated in the index. In the primary air control
4 FECAs are employed. Two FECAs handle negative indices and two FECAs handle
positive indices.

This chapter examines the performance of the solution developed in this project on the

5.2
Analysis
The developed RLS prediction algorithm was implemented in the FuzEvent scripting
language FUEL. The implementation can be found in Appendix B.1. All the operations
in the matrix multiplication are written explicitly, since FUEL was not developed with
the intention of being used with linear algebra. The implementation was made with
very few divisions. This was done because divisions are generally slower to compute
than multiplications. This should in theory result in lower computing time. Further-
more, calculations that are performed multiple times are calculated and stored in local
variables, to avoid redundant calculations.

and the temperature in the PCC was not the limiting factor for operating the plant.

CHAPTER 6
Future work
This project was limited to algorithms that could be implemented through periodically
executed algebraic operations. Therefore, more advanced techniques such as machine
learning could not be employed. Once tools enabling more advanced techniques are
available these should be investigated and subsequently considered. This could in the-
ory be done using a Python engine for the computations since a plethora of advanced
libraries, such as Tensorflow, are available. Furthermore, Python is a fairly fast language
considering it is an interpreted language.
The relevant data should be collected for longer periods of time to validate the long
term performance.
This could also be used to adjust the solution to have a closer
to optimal performance.
Furthermore, the OPC server providing the data has been
configured with a deadband. This means that the values provided only update if change
is greater than the deadband compared to the previous sent value. This way of managing
signals makes sense to reduce network congestion, however this might have some negative
influence on system identification efforts. Therefore, the significance of this effect should
ideally be explored.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
During this project several algorithms and modelling methods were explored to predict
the short term evolution of the temperature in the PCC. The RLS algorithm was chosen
due to its reliability and adaptability. Having a constantly adjusting model proved to be
essential in getting usable results. Using the RLS algorithm enabled the system to make
predictions when warnings were present in the measurements related the temperature.
The amount of false predictions made by the system were found to be negligible.
The PCC temperature was shown to be controllable through control-actions on the
primary air injection. Periodic decreases in the primary air injection as well temperature
based gain scheduling effectively removed severe violations in the PCC temperature.
This was seen during some of the colder months. Historically, it has been harder to
control the PCC temperature during the colder months.
During the end of December 2021 there was a stop on Line 2 unrelated to the scope
of this project. During that stop the PCC was inspected for fouling by the operational
staff, and it was found that the fouling had been significantly reduced.
In conclusion, the objectives of this project have been met to a satisfactory level.

APPENDIX D
Dead-Time Compensation
Actuation on gasification plants don’t have measurable effect immediately. This causes
that classic Linear Control strategies, such as PID’s & Statefeedback, fall short. There-
fore Dead-Time Compensation(DTC) should be considered. There are two overall ways
of doing DTC; Implicit DTC, and Explicit DTC. These should ideally enable the design
of a control strategy with less regard to the transport delay, and more regard to the
dynamics of the system.
D.1
Implicit DTC
Consider a state space description of a discrete-time system in the following form.
x(k + 1) = Fx(k) + Gu(k −d)
(D.1)
y(k) = Cx(k)
(D.2)
where x is the state vector, F and G are the discrete time system dynamic matrix and
G is the discrete time actuation matrix. And d is the Dead-Time in samples between
the actuation and the effect on the state of the system.
An implicit DTC strategy works by expanding the order of the system by d states
responsible for delaying the actuation inputs until the appropriate time. This method of
DTC works well in systems where d is small, but increases the computational complexity
in system with higher values of d. This means that implicit DTC is not desirable for
online use in systems with high dead-times. [San+12]
D.2
Explicit DTC
Explicit DTC works to estimate the system state at x(k+d). This is made possible by
the fact the actuation only has an effect after d samples an therefore the trajectory of
the state space is uniquely defined in the absence of disturbances. This means that
theoretically the system state can be predicted d samples ahead by using the following
expression.
