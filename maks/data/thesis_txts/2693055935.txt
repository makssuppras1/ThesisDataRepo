Abstract
Geothermal energy is a renewable energy source that can serve as reliable baseยญload
in future power systems. However, low thermal efficiency and high upยญfront costs have
caused slow development of geothermal energy compared to other renewable energy
technologies. Due to the high upยญfront costs, a better performing power system can be
justified to recover the high investment cost of the heat source from increased power
sale revenues. The partial evaporating organic Rankine cycle (PEORC) is a modification
of the traditional subยญcritical organic Rankine cycle (SCORC), where the working fluid is
partially evaporated and expanded in the twoยญphase region. The PEORC system has
shown promising thermodynamic performance in previous studies, although at a higher
cost due to increased heat exchanger surface area. Technoยญeconomic studies comparing
PEORC against SCORC are generally missing in the literature.
The primary aim of this thesis is thus to identify how the PEORC system compares to
the SCORC from a technoยญeconomic viewpoint in lowยญtemperature geothermal applicaยญ
tions. To achieve this, a Matlab model has been developed where each componentโs perยญ
formance and costs are estimated, as well as considering relevant parameters such as
reinjection temperature, the cost of the heat source, and twoยญphase expansion efficiency
penalty. The performance of the system is evaluated based on relevant technoยญeconomic
indicators such as net produced power, specific investment cost (SIC), levelized cost of
electricity (LCOE), and net present value (NPV). Four cases have been studied based
on the temperature of the heat source (100 ยฐC and 150 ยฐC) and the minimum reinjection
temperature (70 ยฐC and 30 ยฐC).
The thesis results have demonstrated a higher net power output of 62% and 42% for
PEORC compared to SCORC for a heat source temperature of 100ยฐC and 150ยฐC reยญ
spectively. The PEORC requires a higher investment cost, which leads to a higher SIC
of around 20% for PEORC compared to SCORC. A higher minimum reinjection temperaยญ
ture of the geofluid have a more negative effect on the power output of PEORC compared
to SCORC, which results in a net power gain of only 25% over SCORC for a reinjection
temperature of 70ยฐC. Introducing a penalty on the turbine efficiency results in a lower net
power output and a higher SIC, and thus the feasibility of the PEORC system is highly deยญ
pending on the efficiency of the twoยญphase turbine. PEORC results in a lower LCOE than
SCORC and a higher NPV, meaning the PEORC is technoยญeconomically more beneficial
than the SCORC. The optimal system configuration which yields the highest NPV and the
lowest LCOE leads to a power system design close to the highest attainable net power
output, which makes PEORC the better performing system compared to SCORC. A water
condenser leads to a higher investment cost, although the higher net power output results
in a lower LCOE and higher NPV for the water condenser compared to an air condenser,
making the water condenser the preferable choice. A sensitivity analysis concluded that
the interest rate is a very influential parameter, together with the electricity price, which
greatly influences the economic feasibility of the system.
The thesis shows promising results for the PEORC system for lowยญtemperature geotherยญ
mal applications and can increase the feasibility of geothermal power due to the increased
power output. PEORC is in the context of this thesis better performing technoยญeconomically
than the SCORC and can be considered as an interesting alternative to SCORC. Even
though the results are promising, the PEORC strongly depends on the availability of a
highly efficient twoยญphase turbine, and there is uncertainty connected to the heat source
cost.

2.1
Geothermal energy
Geothermal energy is energy stored within the earth [8] and is available anywhere in the
world. The thermal energy within the earth is believed to have two contributions. Firstly,
the crust of the earth consists of various rock types which contain radioactive isotopes,
and the radiogenic heat released by radioactive elements in the ground is believed to be a
source of the natural heat that reaches the earthโs surface [8, 9]. Secondly, heat within the
earth is stored from the earthโs formation and the subsequent gravitational differentiation
between the earthโs layers [9]. The heat stored within the earth and the consequent heat
flow towards the crust is the source of the geothermal gradient. In stable regions, the
normal geothermal gradient is approximately 30โฆC/km [9, 8]. Geothermal active areas
are regions where the geothermal gradient is higher than normal and can be as high as
500โฆC/km in tectonic active areas [9].
Figure 2.1: Cutaway view of the earths layers, from U.S. Geological Survey [10].
Geothermal resources can broadly be categorized into two types, namely hydrothermal
and unconventional resources [11]. In a hydrothermal geothermal system, the resource
has liquid water naturally flowing within the rock formation. Water flowing in and around
the rock formation is heated by the relatively hotter rocks and can be extracted either
as hot water or steam, or a mixture [11]. Liquidยญdominated resources are the most comยญ
mon, but vaporยญdominated resources are important since they often have higher energy
potential and dry steam which can be used directly in a steam turbine [9, 12]. Accordยญ
ing to R. DiPippo [8] there are five essential features necessary to make a hydrothermal
geothermal resource commercially viable:
โข A large heat source: A large enough heat source is important for the geothermal
project to be worth exploring.

โข A permeable reservoir: With low permeability, the fluid will be unable to flow, and
thus the stored thermal energy in the rocks will not be transferred sufficiently.
โข A supply of water: Without a supply of water, there is no heat transfer fluid and
thus the thermal energy will stay in the rock formation.
โข An overlying layer of impervious rock: Without this, the geofluid might escape

โข A reliable recharge mechanism: If there is no recharge of water to the reservoir,

maintain the amount of fluid available and maintains the pressure in the reservoir. The

resources are the predominant type of geothermal reservoir that is used in commercial
geothermal plants, although in the future EGS is likely to enter the commercial market, as
the vast majority of geothermal potential is accessible only through EGS [14].

A dry steam plant utilizes steam from the reservoir directly into the turbine [26]. Dry steam
plants were the first type of geothermal power plant in Larderello in 1904, and are still
used today. The largest operating geothermal power plant in the world, the Geysers in
California, use dry steam to produce electricity [26]. Dry steam plants often have a high
capacity, although, dry steam reservoirs are rare, and dry steam plants are not frequently
built. In flash steam plants highยญtemperature twoยญphase mixture geofluid above 180 ยฐC
is pumped into a tank on the surface held at a reduced pressure, which causes some
of the fluid to rapidly vaporize [26]. This increases the steam fraction which increases
the amount of steam available. The liquid part is separated from the steam and can be
reinjected into the reservoir or flashed again in a second tank to extract more of the energy
in the geofluid [26], which is called a double flash plant. A third flashing stage can also
be used, a triple flash plant, but the efficiency gain is relatively small, making triple flash
power plants rare, and are generally only applied if the cost of the heat source is very

large potential worldwide [27, 28]. In lowยญtemperature geothermal settings using a system

soยญcalled binary systems, which will be studied in this thesis. Binary geothermal plants
have been widely implemented for the last 30 years to add the possibility to produce
power from lower temperature geothermal resources and are likely to continue to grow
as a common technology in the geothermal field [9]. Binary plants are more costly, but
they promote better resource management by always reinjecting the geofluid [9]. The

either, which results in no emissions other than potential leakages of the working fluid [26],
making binary plants among the most benign of all power plants regarding environmental
impact.

The organic Rankine cycle (ORC) is today considered a mature technology for power
production from low to intermediate temperature heat sources, such as in geothermal
binary plants where a secondary fluid is used as the working fluid [21, 22, 27, 29, 30,

31, 32, 33, 34]. The ORC is essentially a Rankine cycle, where an organic working fluid
other than water is used as the working fluid. Water is a good choice of working fluid
for Rankine cycles operating with high temperatures such as coal or nuclear plants [22].
However, when the heat source is limited by temperature or thermal power, using working
fluids with a lower boiling temperature than water becomes more attractive [21, 22, 32, 35,

water, such as simpler plant design and lower operating pressures allowing for cheaper

The idea of using another working fluid than water is not new. Sadi Carnot already sugยญ

In 1826 T. Howard experimented with the use of alcohol as a working fluid in a power cycle.
This idea led to several naphtha engines being built but confined to niche markets as they
were involved in accidents that limited the development of the ORC [37]. Luigi DโAmelio
was the first to employ an organic fluid in a real turbine system in the 1930s, heated by
solar energy [22]. DโAmelio also developed what is believed to have been the first binary
geothermal plant in the early 1940s. In the late 1950s, the National Physical Laboratory
of Israel (INPL) developed clear selection criteria of motive fluids as part of a program to
harness solar energy. Based on the work of DโAmelio and the INPL the technology was
developed and was applied to various geothermal and solar applications. However, not
before the midยญ1960 to 1970s the development of the commercial ORC gained momenยญ
tum [36]. Since the 1960s and 1970s several ORC plants have been applied worldwide,
especially by the two largest ORC companies ORMAT and Turboden [37]. Despite the
introduction of the ORC systems on the market and the technology being wellยญproven, it
is still a niche in the power market.

The Rankine cycle is a thermodynamic cycle departing from the ideal Carnot cycle and is
widely used in power systems [14, 38, 39]. The Carnot cycle consists of two isothermal
and two adiabatic processes and provides an upper efficiency limit to the Rankine cycle,
where the maximum theoretical efficiency is the Carnot efficiency in eq. (2.1). Figure 2.9
shows the temperatureยญentropy diagram (ts diagram) of the working fluid, in this case a
wet fluid. The ideal Rankine cycle, 1ยญ2sยญ3ยญ4ยญ1 in fig. 2.9, is the case when the working fluid
passes through the components without irreversibilities and no friction losses [38]. The
working fluid will flow through the condenser and evaporator without pressure losses, and
the processes through the pump and the turbine will be isentropic. These idealizations
do not apply to realยญlife situations but are often used when describing the Rankine cycle.
Process 1ยญ2ยญ3ยญ4ยญ1 represents the cycle with nonยญisentropic expansion which is the real
case in real applications. The ideal cycle processes can be described as following [38]:
โข Process 1ยญ2s: Isentropic expansion of the working fluid from saturated vapor at
turbine inlet to the condensing pressure.
โข Process 2sยญ3: Heat transfer from the working fluid at constant pressure and temยญ
perature.
โข Process 3ยญ4: Isentropic compression in the pump from saturated liquid to the comยญ
pressed liquid region.
โข Process 4ยญ1: Heat transfer to the working fluid at constant pressure.
In the design of ORC systems, the choice of working fluid greatly influences the cycle
performance and is one of the most important degrees of freedom in the design [22].
The working fluid selection criteria might vary depending on the prioritizing of the plant
operator. The fluid with the best thermodynamic performance is not necessarily the one
that gives the best economic result. Some fluids are well proven commercially but have
a high global warming potential (GWP) such as HFCยญ134a, other fluids might have low
GWP but are toxic and/or flammable. Thus the criteria for selecting a proper working fluid
might be complex and vary depending on local regulations. The selection of the working

fluid depends greatly on the temperature of the heat source and the cycle configuration.

Firstly, the working fluid and its suitability is linked with the shape of its saturation curve in
the ts diagram. The working fluid can be classified as wet, dry, or isentropic. As seen in
fig. 2.10, the shape of the saturation curve is different depending on the type of fluid. The
slope of the saturation vapour line of the fluid is more positive when the number of atoms
in the molecule is higher [40]. This mainly has implications for the expansion process. If
the fluid is wet, the (isentropic) expansion will end up within the twoยญphase region which
has implications as turbine blades are damaged by droplets during expansion. Thus wet
fluids, e.g. water, are often superheated to avoid droplets in the turbine. Isentropic fluids
have almost a vertical saturation vapour line, while dry fluids have a negative slope of the
saturation vapour line meaning the expansion ends up in the dry region. The design of
the power cycle is depending on the choice of the fluid, and the resulting shape of the
saturation curve, as different configurations are required (e.g. superheater for wet fluids,
desuperheater for dry fluids). Isentropic and dry fluids are generally suggested for ORC
systems to avoid turbine damage.
The density and specific enthalpy of vaporization of the fluid has an impact on how much
heat the fluid can absorb from the heat source in the evaporator, which reduces the mass
flow required and the size of components [41, 42]. The condensation of the working fluid
should be above the ambient temperature, and the freezing temperature of the working
fluid must be below the minimum operating temperature [41]. Furthermore, the working
fluid should be chemical stable at the temperature range, nonยญcorrosive, and compatible
with the lubricating oil and cycle materials. Other considerations of the working fluid are
the safety and health aspects. The fluid should ideally have a low global warming potential
(GWP) and zero ozone depletion potential (ODP), as well as having low flammability and
toxicity [29, 30, 42, 43, 44, 45, 46, 47, 48].
From the discussed working fluid characteristics, it is not possible to choose an optimal
working fluid without knowing the plant site conditions and the heat source. Furthermore,
different parameters are considered for the optimal fluid such as thermal efficiency, exergy
efficiency, system efficiency, and cost performance [42]. Thus, changing the objective,
different fluids might be the optimal one. Therefore multiple fluids should always be conยญ
sidered in the design of ORC systems, although previously it has been common to simply

The trilateral cycle is promising, with a good temperature match with the heat source and

The low thermal efficiency and the large heat exchanger area required in the trilateral sysยญ

fluid, the required working fluid mass flow rate is reduced for the same heat transfer rate
with the heat source, thus reducing the pump power consumption. The heat transfer coยญ

to the trilateral cycle. Furthermore, the expander design benefits from the lower increase
in volumetric flow rate in the expansion (compared to trilateral), increasing the efficiency
and reducing the number of stages and the costs. By partial evaporation of the working
fluid, higher thermal efficiency is obtained, as well as reduced heat exchanger area, by
the expense of a decreased heat input [34, 49]. Therefore, the partial evaporating Rankยญ
ine cycle (PEORC) is a mix between a regular SCORC and a trilateral cycle, where the

Previous work on PEORC
Zhou et al. [50] proposed a PEORC with a zeotropic mixture working fluid of R245fa and
R227ea, and found it to be able to generate about 24.7% more power than SCORC with
pure R227ea as working fluid. However, they expected the costs to increase compared
to SCORC due to the increased heat exchanger costs. Another study by Surendran and
Seshadri [51] also showed that PEORC with using a mixture yields a higher power output
than PEORC with pure fluids. PEORC with a mixture of R245fa and cyclopen performed
up to 65% more power output than a SCORC with pure R245fa. Although, the PEORC
with mixtures only presented a 3ยญ5% higher power output than PEORC with pure R245fa.
The benefit of PEORC relative to SCORC was seen higher for low temperatures, and
decreasing as the temperature gets higher. The heat exchanger area per unit net power
was found to be the highest for the optimized PEORC with mixture working fluid, which
means the heat exchanger economy is not very good. Furthermore, the turbine economics
looks better for pure fluid PEORC. Thus the small increase in power output with mixture
does not facilitate the increased costs, while pure fluid PEORC shows excellent system
performance.
Lecompte et al. [34] found that PEORC always outperforms TCORC and SCORC for a
waste heat recovery system maximizing net power with working fluid R245fa, with the
PEORC outperforming the SCORC by 18.8% at a heat source temperature of 140 ยฐC.
They also found that when the heat source temperature is increased, the benefits of PEยญ

heat source temperature of 300 ยฐC. Another study by Lecompte et al. [33] found that the
PEORC have a 73% higher second law efficiency than SCORC for a waste heat recovยญ

for lower temperature. The study also showed that the optimal fluids for PEORC have
low GWP, which is not the case for SCORC. The optimal cycle type in terms of net power
output was always the PEORC. Another study by Lecompte et al. [52] investigated the offยญ
design operation of PEORC and SCORC systems in quasiยญsteady state simulations and
found that the PEORC produce an increase of yearly generated net electricity of 6.8%
more than SCORC. Regression models were formulated in another study by Lecompte et

law efficiency for low temperatures. Another Lecompte study [49] showed that the PEยญ

2.3
Summary and basis for new research
All of the topics introduced and discussed in the previous sections represent the informaยญ
tion available in the literature. Geothermal energy is an option as baseload power, thus
potentially replacing coal which today is one of the major polluting power technologies.
Lowยญtemperature geothermal resources have the largest global potential, although the
efficiency of such plants is rather low due to the Carnot limitations. The development of
geothermal energy depends strongly on the individual reservoir, and the cost of accessing
the geofluid can be significant and vary from one field to another. The high upfront costs,
combined with the risks and uncertainty in the exploration and development phases have
halted geothermal development in recent years, with only a few countries adding signifiยญ
cant capacity, in strong contrast to other renewables such as wind and solar. The cost of
the heat source can be reduced by new methods and technology, but will be a large part
of the project cost regardless due to the nature of the origin of the heat source. Therefore,
another way to improve the economic aspects of geothermal power in lowยญtemperature
resources is to improve the performance of the power unit.
There is clear evidence of the potential of increasing the net power output with the trilateral
cycle and the PEORC from lowยญtemperature sources from the literature. The PEORC sysยญ
tem has showed increased net power output compared to the regular SCORC, and also
other cycle architectures such as the TCORC. The literature also suggests that the costs
are increased with the trilateral cycle and the PEORC, although there is a lack of studies
taking into account technoยญeconomic performance of the PEORC [28, 42], including conยญ
sideration on the cost of the heat source as well as the uncertainty included in twoยญphase
turbines. This thesis aims to fill the gap by performing a technoยญeconomic analysis of the
PEORC system, and thus identifying in which conditions it can compete with the SCORC
system. The PEORC is well suited to low to medium temperature heat sources such as
geothermal applications. The more expensive the heat source is, the more attractive it
generally is to increase the power output, and thus the increased initial investment from
the PEORC can be more easily paid off by higher revenue from increased power generยญ
ation [9].

Recuperator
A recuperator is a heat exchanger which increases the thermal efficiency, by utilizing the
energy at the turbine discharge to preheat the working fluid at the pump outlet state. This
allows for a high power output to be maintained for a decreased heat input [6, 42]. If the
working fluid is of the dry type, then the fluid exiting the turbine is always superheated
for SCORC, and sometimes for PEORC for high turbine inlet quality, which increases the
load on the condenser. Thus, from a purely thermodynamic point of view a recuperator
is beneficial. The recuperator is a shell and tube type heat exchanger, like the PHE, but

3.3.4
Condenser
The condenser is a very important part of the power block due to the relatively low efยญ
ficiency meaning a large part of the heat added to the cycle has to be removed in the
condenser [28]. The condensing temperature and pressure are important parameters in
the cycle design which affect the performance. The condensing pressure should be above
the atmospheric pressure to reduce the risk of gas infiltration which in turn decreases the
performance [42]. The lower the condensing temperature, the higher the gross power
output is because of a larger turbine pressure and enthalpy drops which is beneficial for
the cycle efficiency [22]. However, it also increases the power consumption of the ORC
pump and the condenser fans [55]. Furthermore, the logarithmic temperature difference
in the condenser is reduced which leads to a bigger heat exchange surface area and a
larger turbine with an increased number of stages due to higher volume flow rates, which
impose a relevant increased cost of the power block [22, 55].

3.4.4

The design of the condenser in geothermal ORC systems is important for the thermodyยญ
namic performance of the cycle. It also affects the cost in different ways. Geothermal
plants often have proportionately larger and more costly condenser systems, and higher
parasitic loads which increase the cost of multiple components [9]. Furthermore, the coolยญ

very low heat transfer coefficient, which leads to bigger condensers than if water is used
as the cooling source. However, air is a more realistic option as cooling medium because
of the scarcity of water close to most geothermal plants [9, 22]. Thus, in geothermal power
plants, the condenser often consists of a significant share of the total plant cost [22]. On
the other side, the use of an air condenser makes the power plant blend more easily into
the landscape due to no visual impact, which can be beneficial due to many geothermal
power plants being located in national parks and conservationยญworthy areas [22].
The cost of the air condenser is reported differently in different papers, with some papers
reporting higher specific investment cost of the air condenser compared to water conยญ

3.5
Heat source investment cost
The biggest challenge in geothermal power development is the high cost of the heat
source, and its uncertainty. The cost of drilling is the biggest and usually most uncertain
cost during the geothermal project, as drilling of several kilometers into unknown territory
is required to access the reservoir. It is common that drilling cost is more than 50% of
the total project costs [19]. In a study by Lukawski et al. [69] a database of around 150
geothermal wells drilled between 1972 and 2013 was assembled, and the average well
drilling cost was established. From their estimates, a 4 km well with a production temยญ
perature of 130 ยฐC assuming a 30 ยฐC/km geothermal gradient will cost around 11 million
USD for drilling and completion, without considering exploration, reservoir stimulation,
and surface plant costs. This cost is paid before power revenues can be received, and
thus highlights the high investment costs before any power can be sold.
Figure 3.9 shows costยญdepth data from actual and predicted geothermal wells plotted on

are actual costs while empty markers represent predicted costs. There is a clear trend of
increasing costs with depth, although there is great variation in costs for wells at similar
depths. This highlights the variation from well to well, and no model or estimation of
the costs can deliver accurate cost predictions without existential information about the
reservoir, and still, the uncertainties are high. There is also generally lacking available
data on the cost of drilling because of the confidential nature of drilling costs [69].

3.5.1
Well production decline
Geothermal energy can be considered a renewable energy source due to the abundant
energy stored within the earth. However, a single geothermal resource can be depleted
if not operated sustainably, and thus the renewability and sustainability of geothermal enยญ
ergy is depending on proper reservoir management and sizing of the power plant [75,
76]. If the power plant is sized to the maximum power output, the reservoir is likely to
be depleted sooner. In the past, it was common to design the power plant with the highยญ
est production rate in order to reduce the payback time for the huge initial investment
of geothermal projects. This could lead to a premature stop of productivity due to flow
rate decrease or temperature drop. Thus, modern geothermal plant design requires careยญ
ful considerations concerning a power production that does not empty the reservoir too
soon. It is also crucial that the geofluid is reinjected back into the reservoir to maintain the
geofluid in circulation and to maintain the pressure in the reservoir [75], and is common
practice for binary geothermal plants today. The sustainable operation and the decline
in temperature and/or mass flow rate of the reservoir depends on many parameters and
factors, both natural and technological. Permeability, hydraulic linking between producยญ
tion and reinjection areas, siting of the wells, natural recharge, and scaling are some of
the parameters influencing the lifetime of the reservoir [75].
Geothermal fields do degrade, and thus assuming constant heat flow from the reservoir
to the power plant over the lifetime is not accurate [76]. This thesis does not aim to
describe or identify parameters or detailed information about the reservoir, and thus a very
simplified method to estimate the production decline of the resource has been included.

Pinch point temperature difference influence
The minimum pinch point temperature is constrained to 10 ยฐC for both the condenser and
the PHE. From a purely thermodynamic point of view, reducing the pinch point temperaยญ

increasing the power output [55]. Thus if the optimization goal is net power, the pinch
point temperature will always be the lowest allowable. However, if the optimization goal
is SIC, there is a tradeยญoff between the increased costs of the heat exchanger due to its
increased size, and the increased net power [22]. Thus, the effect of the reduction in
pinch point temperature is only studied for SIC as optimization goal. In figs. 4.3 to 4.4 the
pinch point temperature constraint has been reduced to 1 ยฐC in the condenser and PHE
respectively. The plots show the change in SIC with a minimum pinch point temperature
of 1 ยฐC compared to the case with the original 10 ยฐC pinch point temperature, both with
the turbine inlet quality constrained to 0.2 and 0.1 with SIC as optimization goal.

4.2.1
Total system SIC
When the optimization goal is SIC, it is the cost of the power system that is minimized and
not the total SIC of the heat source and power block combined, as the heat source cost
is constant and only based on the depth of the well. Thus, when SIC is the optimization
goal the algorithm finds the lowest specific cost without considering the heat source. The
SIC of the power block has proven to be higher for PEORC than the SCORC. However
the specific cost of the power block and the heat source together shows a different result
as seen in fig. 4.9, which shows the total SIC for cyclopen. Because of the higher net
power output from the PEORC and the fact that the heat source is equally expensive
for SCORC and PEORC, the total specific cost of the heat source and the power block
together is less for PEORC without a turbine penalty than for SCORC. Thus, a higher
specific investment on the power block with an increased net power output results in a
higher initial investment but a lower total SIC. Furthermore, as was seen previously, the
power block SIC is higher for the cases that are optimized for net power. However, due
to the fact that the system optimized for SIC only optimizes the cost of the power block,
the total system SIC is lower when optimized for net power than for SIC for the full system
consisting of the power block and the heat source together. Thus, optimizing for SIC
will result in a more economical standยญalone power plant without a heat source cost, while
when considering the heat source cost a more expensive system with higher power output
gives the better economic solution.

Sensitivity analysis
Because of the uncertainty connected especially to the NPV and economic parameters
and the cost of the heat source, a sensitivity analysis is performed. Van Erdeweghe et
al. [59] found that the same geothermal project can be profitable or lossยญmaking only
depending on the economic situation, and thus the interest rate, the numbers of hours the
plant is running during the year, and the cost of the heat source is investigated further in
the sensitivity analysis to study these parametersโ influence on the system performance.
All the results in the sensitivity analysis are optimized for NPV with cyclopen as the working
fluid.
4.3.1
Interest rate
The interest rate can vary between projects and can greatly affect the economic perforยญ
mance [59]. The interest rate is often higher in countries or regions with less economic
stability as the loan risk is higher, and reduces the profitability of a project. The interest
rate can be considered as the cost of lending money, and thus a higher interest rate leads
to a more expensive loan [82]. Thus, a low interest rate promotes development and inยญ
vestments, and in some cases a lower interest rate can be applied if the investment will
be beneficial to society.
Figures 4.17 to 4.19 shows the NPV and PBT for the cycles with an interest rates ranging
from 2% ยญ 10%. The vertical region of the curves in the PBT figure represents PBT higher
than the project lifetime (35 years). The interest rate has a big influence on the NPV,
especially for higher electricity selling prices. The higher the interest rate is, the flatter the
graph will look. For E7 the case with an interest rate of 2% has a NPV more than four times
higher than the 10% interest rate case for SCORC, and for PEORC the difference is even
bigger. The trend is similar for PEORC and SCORC with more profitable projects with
lower interest rates. The PBT is generally lower the lower the interest rate is. For all the
different interest rate scenarios, similarly to what was experienced in the optimization of
the NPV in the previous section, the cycles are close to the highest obtainable net power
output, with a trend of a slightly higher net power output for increasing electricity price.
Thus, the change in the interest rate does not change the power plant design much, but
it affects the feasibility of the project.

4.3.3
Heat source cost
Since one of the most uncertain parts of a geothermal project, and in this thesis, is the
cost of the heat source, a sensitivity analysis is performed on the cost of the heat source.
The cost is in the range of 0%ยญ200% with respect to the heat source capital cost derived
from eqs. (3.70) to (3.74). 0% heat source cost is very unlikely, but can represent a case
where an existing highยญtemperature power plant exists and the wastewater is used as the
heat source. Figures 4.23 to 4.25 shows the NPV and PBT for the cycles with varying
heat source costs. The heat source cost has a higher influence on the NPV than the
availability, but lesser than the interest rate. The lower the cost of the heat source the
higher is the NPV. Even though a higher heat source cost would more likely result in a
power system with a higher net power output, as seen from the previous sections, the
optimal NPV system is very close to the optimal net power output. Thus, a cheaper heat
source does not change the plant design, since the optimal design is a very high power
output regardless of the heat source cost.
More interesting and useful for investors is the strongly reduced PBT. For a heat source
cost of zero, the system becomes feasible for the lowest electricity price for SCORC and
PEORC with fe = 0. For higher electricity selling prices the PBT does not change much.
Thus, for low electricity selling price, lowering the heat source cost significantly reduces
the PBT, although the NPV is not changed by much. This is in line with the high risk
involved in geothermal power, as the PBT is very much affected and is desired to be
as low as possible for investors. Even though a geothermal project might result in a
higher NPV than another renewable energy project, if the PBT is too long the investment
becomes less attractive and might be discarded.

Conclusions
In this thesis the performance of a partial evaporating organic Rankine cycle for lowยญ
temperature geothermal applications compared to the traditional subยญcritical organic Rankยญ
ine cycle has been studied. Most geothermal plants today operate with heat source temยญ
peratures above 180 ยฐC, although the potential of lowยญtemperature resources is huge on
a global scale. Lowยญtemperature geothermal plants hold a low efficiency due to thermodyยญ
namic constraints, and a high investment cost. The partial evaporating organic Rankine
cycle is a thermodynamic cycle where the working fluid is partially evaporated and exยญ
panded within the twoยญphase region. This cycle architecture allows for a better temperaยญ
ture matching with the heat source and a potential higher net power output at the expense
of an increased heat exchanger area. Higher power revenue from a higher power outยญ
put has the potential to better recover the high investment cost of the geothermal heat
source, and thus increasing the feasibility of lowยญtemperature geothermal power. Cyยญ
clopen, toluene, pentane, and R152a are the working fluids considered for four cases
based on the heat source temperature (100ยฐC and 150ยฐC) and the reinjection temperaยญ
ture (30ยฐC and 70ยฐC). A penalty on the turbine efficiency is included for PEORC in order
to account for the possible detrimental effect of twoยญphase flow on the turbine.
The results from the thesis work have shown that PEORC outperforms SCORC with 62%
and 42% for a heat source inlet temperature of 100ยฐC and 150ยฐC respectively in terms of
net power output when there is no penalty on the turbine efficiency. PEORC showed a
higher investment cost than SCORC which resulted in about 20% higher specific investยญ
ment cost of the power block for the PEORC without turbine penalty. Higher reinjection
temperature has a limiting effect on the PEORC, unlike SCORC, with only an increased
net power output of 25% for PEORC compared to SCORC with a reinjection temperature
of 70ยฐC for both 100ยฐC and 150ยฐC heat source temperature. Introducing a penalty on
the turbine efficiency reduces the net power output of PEORC, which also increases the
SIC, and reduces the feasibility of PEORC. When the turbine penalty gets too high, PEยญ
ORC does not have a higher net power output and is therefore not feasible compared to
SCORC. Reducing the pinch point temperature in the PHE and the condenser can reduce
the SIC further for both SCORC and PEORC. The turbine design is more expensive for
PEORC compared to SCORC due to a higher working fluid mass flow rate, which leads
to a higher volume ratio and a turbine with an increased number of stages compared
to SCORC. A lower heat source temperature results in a lower turbine inlet quality for
PEORC and a higher degree of superheating for SCORC. For higher heat source temยญ
peratures the turbine inlet quality of PEORC is higher and the cycle is approaching a
SCORC.
PEORC with no turbine penalty always results in the lowest LCOE with 0.0758 โฌ/kWh as
the lowest for a heat source temperature of 150ยฐC and 0.3067 โฌ/kWh as the lowest for
a heat source temperature of 100ยฐC, while PEORC with turbine penalty always gives a
higher LCOE than SCORC. The cases with a heat source temperature of 100ยฐC give quite
poor results in terms of NPV, and a high electricity price or monetary support is required to
make it feasible, for both SCORC and PEORC. For PEORC without a turbine penalty as
well as with a turbine penalty of 0.1 with a heat source temperature of 150ยฐC, the NPV is
always higher than for SCORC, and for a heat source temperature of 100ยฐC PEORC gives
higher NPV for SCORC for high electricity selling prices. Thus PEORC has the potential
to outperform SCORC in lowยญtemperature geothermal applications for both 100ยฐC and

How can the partial evaporating organic Rankine cycle become a feasible option for

both SCORC and PEORC the optimal cycle in terms of a high NPV and a low LCOE

compared to SCORC results in a higher power output which adds more value during
the project lifetime than SCORC, thus resulting in a lower LCOE and higher NPV. In
the context of this thesis a PEORC system is thus better performing than SCORC
for the investigated cases.
โข In which conditions does the PEORC architecture outperform the traditional
SCORC architecture?: The benefit of PEORC compared to SCORC is higher for
lower heat source temperature. The turbine inlet quality approaches closer to zero
and a more expensive but higher power output system for a lower heat source temยญ

with a higher volume ratio which results in a twoยญstage turbine that is more expenยญ

net power output, leading to a higher power revenue and higher NPV for PEORC
compared to SCORC for higher electricity prices. For very low electricity prices, a
cheaper power system is preferable and SCORC shows a less negative NPV comยญ

from a technical (minimum reinjection temperature) and economic (total cost
of extraction of the heat source) point of view?: Higher reinjection temperature
has a more negative effect on the feasibility of PEORC than for SCORC, thus PEยญ

Recommendation for further work
Further work departing from this thesis that could be interesting to investigate could be
the following:
โข This thesis uses a very simplified heat source model, thus the complexity related
to the heat source in terms of production capacity, decline and total cost is highly
uncertain. A more precise and detailed heat source model which takes into acยญ
count important characteristics of the heat source such as permeability and geofluid
chemistry, and more detailed and accurate cost correlations, will make it possible to
easier adapt to different cases, making the model more versatile. The interplay beยญ
tween the power plant and the heat source regarding extraction rate and the lifetime
and capacity of the reservoir could also increase the usefulness of the results.
โข A specific case study with less uncertain economic parameters such as interest rate
and electricity price will result in less uncertain results. Thus a specific location
with more insight into economic parameters as well as the costs and availability of
materials and resources would make the study results more solid.
โข This thesis assumes a successful twoยญphase turbine with the same cost correlations
as for a regular turbine. A more detailed turbine model which accounts for a more
detailed description and analysis of the twoยญphase turbine with efficiency and cost
correlations would give a more precise comparison of the actual feasibility of PEORC
compared to SCORC.
