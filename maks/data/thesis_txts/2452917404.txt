Abstract
The motivations of this thesis are the general understanding of the geological struc-
ture of the Nuussuaq peninsula and the use of the passive exploration technique called
the magnetotelluric method. At the surface of the Earth are measured the daily vari-
ations of the electric and magnetic ﬁelds. From these measurements, through the
resolution of an inverse problem, is inferred the Earth’s crust electrical conductivity
distribution beneath the stations of measurements. The inversion scheme used is the
Occam’s method, which seeks for the smoothest model that ﬁts the data, and the
inversion is based on assumptions on the dimensionality of the problem. A dimen-
sional analysis is ﬁrst performed and two diﬀerent assumptions are ﬁnally tested: 1D
and 2D electrical conductivity distribution. The objective of the 1D inversion is to
get a ﬁrst feeling about the electrical conductivity structure. The results show that
the electrical conductivity seems to increase with depth. The objective of the 2D
inversion is to build a model of the electrical conductivity distribution. To do so, an
area suiting the requirements of the assumption is selected according to the geology,
and the 2D inversion is run. The ﬁnal results, the proﬁles of estimated electrical
resistivity with depth along the lines of stations, provide diﬀerent information. First,
general trends, as the increase of electrical conductivity with depth or the speciﬁc dis-
tribution around a geological fault are observed. Second, local conductive anomalies
are spotted and assessed according to neighboring geological features.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The magnetotelluric (MT) method is based on the fact that the geomagnetic ﬁeld
shows daily natural variations. They are used as power source for electromagnetic
induction in the Earth. As a consequence, the MT method is a passive exploration
technique because it does not need any injection of current into the ground as source
of power for conduction [1]. Tikhonov (1950) [2] (reprinted 1986) [3] and Cagniard
(1953) [4] discovered than complex ratios, named impedances, describing the penetra-
tion of electromagnetic ﬁelds within the Earth can be computed from simultaneous
measurements of the natural electric and magnetic ﬁelds variations [1]. As the pene-
tration depths of electromagnetic waves depend only on the electromagnetic sounding
frequency and on the Earth’s electrical conductivity structure, if the ﬁrst is known,
the second can be determined.
It exists several causes of natural variations of the geomagnetic ﬁeld, which can
depend for instance on the location (high/low latitudes) or on the moment (day/night
or high/low solar activity). In this master thesis, the focus is done on the Nuussuaq
peninsula in western Greenland, which lies above 70◦N of latitude. As a consequence,
only the sources of natural variations in the high latitudes will be exposed. For exam-
ple, at high latitude, the interaction between the solar wind and the magnetosphere
generates a large-scale magnetospheric electric ﬁeld. This electric ﬁeld, by convection,
gives a motion to the ionospheric plasma to generates ionospheric currents. This ends
into ionospheric currents ﬂowing along the auroral oval so that convection electrojets
are formed. During period of high solar activity, a disturbing phenomenon called
substorm is generated by the magnetosphere. The transfer of energy from the solar
wind to the magnetosphere is enhanced. This causes a quick intensiﬁcation of the
ionospheric electrojets until they reach their maximum, creating ﬂuctuation in the
ionospheric electromagnetic ﬁeld. Finally the magnetosphere slowly returns back to
its quiet-time state [5]. Another cause of natural variation of the geomagnetic ﬁeld
at high latitudes is the meteorological activity such as lighting discharges which can
encompass a broad range of electromagnetic frequencies [1].
The objective of the MT method is to determine the electrical conductivity struc-
ture of the Earth.
The principle is that extending the MT sounding frequencies,
electromagnetic responses from diﬀerent depth can be obtained. An electromagnetic
ﬁeld with low frequency is penetrating deeper than an electromagnetic ﬁeld with high
frequency. Electromagnetic ﬁelds naturally induced in the Earth exploitable in MT
usually have a frequency within the range of 10−4 to 105 Hz. This leads to a depth

range from a few meters to hundreds of kilometers that can be investigated. The
fundamental idea behind the MT method is that an electromagnetic response of an
electromagnetic ﬁeld penetrating to a maximum depth d is insensitive to what hap-
pen deeper. From the electromagnetic response can be computed what is called the
apparent resistivity which is equivalent to the average resistivity of a uniform half-

This master thesis is a partnership between the Denmark Technical University
(DTU) and the Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS). The objective
is to create a 2D model of the Earth’s electrical conductivity from magnetotelluric
data taken on the Nuussuaq peninsula, western Greenland. GEUS is a research insti-
tution within the Danish ministry of climate, energy and utilities. The main activities
of the institute are mapping, compilation and storage of data, research, monitoring
and consultancy within water, energy, minerals and climate and environment [6]. The
motivation of this project is the general understanding of the geological structure of
the Nuussuaq peninsula. Very speciﬁc geological features as horizontal layered struc-
ture, volcanoes, faults, are present in this area, and the results of this project may
give additional information to their investigations.
After presenting the fundamental principle of the MT method and its properties
in 1D and 2D electrical conductivity distributions, a focus is ﬁrst done on the dimen-
sionality analysis of the data. While the dimension of the data have been investigated,
suitable parameters and choices of inversions can be selected and applied. The in-
version scheme used is the Occam’s method and is then explained with its 1D and
2D characteristics. The assessment of the data and a geological background of the
area are needed to get a feeling about what results to expect. The results of the
dimensionality analysis, the 1D inversion and the 2D inversion are then exposed and
analyzed. Finally, through an objective discussion, the results and their consistency
are assessed.

CHAPTER 2
The Magneto-Telluric
(MT) method
As mentioned previously, the MT method measures the earth’s natural electric and
magnetic ﬁelds’ variations over a wide range of frequencies at the surface of the
Earth. To do so, stations for magnetotelluric measurements, which consist of several
electrodes and magnetometers, are typically set-up in lines. By processing and com-
bining the voltages measured, it is possible to build a model of the earth electrical
conductivity following the lines. The horizontal components of the electric and mag-
netic ﬁelds are linked through a transfer function which can be expressed as a tensor
and which is inferred from the processing of the data. The entire principle from the
fundamental assumptions needed for this method and the built of the impedance ten-
sor to the computation of the apparent resistivities and impedance phases through the
hypothesis of speciﬁc electrical conductivity distributions is exposed in this chapter.
The main part of the theory explained and discussed in this chapter has been read in
the book Practical Magnetotellurics by Fiona Simpson and Karsten Bahr, chapter 2
[7]; and in the course support Lectures Notes in Geomagnetism by Chris Finlay and
Nils Olsen [8].

2.1
Assumptions
Theoretically, the MT method is based on several fundamental assumptions [7] :
1. The electromagnetic ﬁeld follows Maxwell’s general equations (2.1).
2. The Earth only dissipates or absorbs the electromagnetic energy, but does not
generate any.
3. The electromagnetic waves are uniform, plan-polarized and near-vertical inci-
dent. This is called the plan-wave assumption, and has one fundamental con-
sequence: it implies time-invariance of exciting source.
In other words, the
impedance tensor measured at one site for one speciﬁc frequency is always the
same whatever is the time of record. This assumption is true at mid-latitude
because the electromagnetic ﬂuctuations are stable. However at high-latitudes,
the electromagnetic sources, as for instance the auroral electrojets, show more
disturbances. In this master thesis, even if the latitudes of the sites of measure-
ments are high, this assumption is still considered.
4. Except at the borders between two layers of constant conductivity, no accumu-
lation of free charges are expected within the layers.
5. Charges are conserved and the Earth acts as an ohmic conductor : j = σE (j
is the total electric current density in A.m−1, σ the electrical conductivity in
S.m−1 and E the electric ﬁeld in V.m−1).
6. The MT method is used under the quasi-stationary approximation which is ap-
plicable when the process is "suﬃciently slowly changing" [8]. In geomagnetism,
this assumption is usually valid for period bigger than 1/8 of second [8]. In this
master thesis, even if a part of the measurements are done with periods smaller,
the quasi-stationary approximation is still assumed because the large majority
of the measurements are made with high-periods/low-frequencies.
Under it,
time-varying displacement currents can be neglected and the electromagnetic
induction is only a diﬀusion phenomenon.
7. The variation in permittivity and permeability of the Earth are neglected com-
pared with the variation of the electrical conductivity.
These assumptions are adding a non-negligible part of uncertainty to the results,
even more because the conditions of the plane-waves assumption (3.) and the quasi-
stationary approximation (6.) are not fully full-ﬁlled. However, being aware of these
limits allows a more critical analysis of the ﬁnal results.

2.4
Set-up for magnetotelluric measurements
2.4.1
Magnetic field sensors
There are two types of magnetic sensors that are used in two diﬀerent situations. The
ﬁrst type corresponds to induction coils. The principle is that a time variable magnetic
ﬁeld ∂B/∂t generates a voltage in the coil which is measured and used to estimate the
magnetic ﬁeld B. This sensor is more sensible to rapidly varying magnetic ﬁeld and
as a consequence suits best the short-period/low-frequency electromagnetic waves.
The output voltage is dependent on the geometry of the coil: their measurements
are more precise when the coils have a large number of loops, in other words when
the loops are big. However, in MT where the measurements have to be done on the
ﬁeld sometime under diﬃcult conditions, the transportability of the sensor has to
be taken into account. Moreover, an induction coils’ sensor is only able to measure
the variations of the magnetic ﬁeld in one direction. Three induction coils’ sensors
are then needed to measure the 3 components of the time-varying magnetic ﬁeld.
The second type of magnetic sensors corresponds to ﬂuxgate magnetometers. They
==PAGE:28==
14
2 The Magneto-Telluric (MT) method
consists of three ring-core sensors. A ring-core sensor is made of two bars of high
susceptibility ferromagnetic metals. These two bars are placed close to each other
and wrapped by a single coil in reversed direction. The principle is that an external
magnetic ﬁeld variation, through its induced magnetic ﬁeld from the ferromagnetic
bars, creates a measurable electric current in the wire.
These sensors rely on a
hysteresis principle, which is a long process and as a consequence these sensors are
reliable for long period magnetic ﬁeld variations with high amplitudes. In MT, it
can be interesting to use both types of magnetic sensors to cover the largest range of
period possible, but this implies complication in transportation and management.
2.4.2
Electric field sensors
The objective of the electric ﬁeld sensor is to measure the diﬀerence of electric po-
tential between two electrodes. Two electrodes are connected by a shielded cable to
form a dipole. They are buried in the ground at a known distance which is usually
between 10 and 100 meters. The orientation of the dipole deﬁnes the direction of the
electric ﬁeld measured. In MT two pairs of electrodes are installed in two orthogonal
and horizontal directions.
2.4.3
Stations
The full installation is usually including three magnetic ﬁeld and two electric ﬁeld
sensors (four electrodes in total). An usual disposition is to set one pair of electrodes
in the N-S direction and the second in the W-E direction. The magnetic sensors mea-
suring the magnetic ﬁeld in the horizontal plan are aligned with the electric dipoles
while the third one points toward the center of the Earth. All of these sensors are
together connected to an Acquisition Unit where the data acquisition and processing
is done. A representation of a typical MT station is available on Figure (2.6).

3.3.6

The MTPT can be used as a separator of dimension taking advantage of the properties
discussed previously. First, both in 1D and 2D, the skew angle β is equal to zero.

to zero, a 1D or 2D electrical conductivity distribution is characterized by a very
small β. An acceptance interval of 3◦is assumed [15]. In practice, it is observed that
1D data generally gives β smaller that 2D data. β larger than 3◦indicate 3D eﬀect.
Second, the shape of the ellipse can be studied: if it looks very circular, it would
mean that the structure is 1D, while an elliptic shape would mean that it is 2D or
more complicated. Merging both properties, the MTPT can be used as a separator
of dimension between 1D, 2D and 3D. The entire principle is summarized in Figure

3.4.2
Induction arrow
Theory
Induction arrow (IA), or tipper, has been introduced by two scientists independently
at the same time in 1962: Wiese [16] and Parkinson [17]. They both determined
the IA in time domain, which is not in use anymore, however the way to represent
it is still the same [18]. The purpose of it is to locate the observed induction eﬀect
due to lateral boundaries between zones of diﬀerent electrical conductivity. Indeed,
a conductance discontinuity in the horizontal direction provokes the generation of a
vertical magnetic ﬁeld components Bz. Bz can be deﬁned as a linear combination of
the horizontal magnetic ﬁeld components Bx and By (3.33) by a transfer function T
which has two components Tx and Ty (3.32):
T =

(3.33)

nents Tx and Ty are complex numbers. The idea consists in considering the real part
of these two complex numbers and displaying them on the map. Parkinson [17] and
Wiese [16] were using opposite conventions:

Inversion - Theory
In order to explain the concept of an inverse problem, three quantities have to be
introduced. The ﬁrst is collected on the ﬁeld and is the result of a physical phe-
nomenon: the observation data d. In this thesis, the data corresponds either to the
C-response (1D inversion), or to the real and imaginary parts of the impedance tensor
(2D inversion). The second is the source of the data and is not measurable directly:
the model m. In this thesis the model is the electrical conductivity (or resistivity)
distribution of the subsurface. Finally the third one is the forward function F deﬁning
the forward problem (4.1).
d = F(m)
(4.1)
The inverse problem involves the estimation of the model m from the observed
data d [19]. A trivial case concerns linear forward problems where the function F can
be discretized as a simple matrix. The model m can then be expressed as function of
this matrix and the data d. However in reality the forward problem is often nonlinear.
In this master thesis, it is linearized and solved iteratively. The inversion scheme used
is called the Occam’s method and aims for the smoothest model possible. First, the
theory behind the Occam’s method will be exposed. And in a second time, the focus
will be done both on the 1D and 2D inversions.

4.3.3
Finite-element code
Wannamaker et.al (1986) [23] introduced a method to perform the forward computa-
tion required by the inversion scheme with better accuracy. The main idea is that
two diﬀerent grids/meshes are used for the inversion and for the forward computation.
The forward mesh consists into a sub-set of the inversion mesh. During the inversion,
at each iteration is computed a new model and each cell of the inversion mesh gets
a distinct model value. The model value of an inversion cell is then aﬀected to all
the forward cells included in this inversion cell. Finally the forward computation is
done on the forward mesh. This principle allow a better accuracy in the forward
computation, leading to a more consistent ﬁt of the data. The dimensions of the
two meshes have to be set consistently according to the location of the stations, and
to the penetration depth of the electromagnetic waves used for these measurements.
To be able to compare the diﬀerent results between each other, the same grids will
be used for all the 2D inversions made. The 2D inversion is applied to four lines of
data from the second set (L08S, L10N, L20Nand L32N) (see section 5.2). The
characteristics of the two meshes, in z and x directions, are listed below:
z-direction
• The number of forward cells by inversion cell in the z-direction decreases with
depth. The 6 ﬁrst inversion layers contains each 5 forward layers, the next 5
inversion layers contain each 4 forward layers, and etc. Finally the z structure
is ﬁnished by one bigger inversion layer containing 5 forward layers, to model an
inﬁnite dimensional layer at the bottom of the structure, and avoid side eﬀects
from below.
• The main parameter used to build the z-direction of the grid is the skin depth.
All data taken into account, the skin depth’s range limits are:
fmax = 15000 Hz & ρ ≈102 Ω.m =⇒δmin ≈40 m
fmin = 5.49.10−4 Hz & ρ ≈100 Ω.m =⇒δmax ≈21339 m
The thickness of the top forward cell should be smaller than the minimum
skin depth. Here it has been chosen to be 40% of the minimum skin depth :
0.40 ∗δmin ≈16 m. All the forward layer contained into a single inversion layer
have the same thickness. As a consequence the thickness of the ﬁrst inversion
layer is 5 ∗16 = 80 m.

• By principle, the sensitivity of the MT method decreases with depth. As a
consequence, the cell sizes in the z-direction should grow gradually with depth,
both for the forward and the inverse meshes. The thickness of the inversion
layer is growing by a factor from 1.2 to 1.6 with depth. The depth reached
before the last layer is 38323 m, almost twice the maximum skin depth. The
total depth reached including the last layer is 312815 m, around ﬁfteen times
the maximum skin depth.
• In total the z structure contains 21 inversion layers and 75 forward layers.
x-direction
• At a given depth, the cells in the x-direction have constant width all along the
proﬁle for both the forward and the inversion meshes.
• All lines taken into account, the minimum distance between two consecutive
stations is 71 m. It should be at least one inversion cell between two consecutive
stations. Hence, the width of the inversion cells at the surface has been set to
hxi = 60 m.
• For the ﬁrst layer, it is assumed than one inversion cell contains three forward
cells. The width of the forward cell is then hxf = 20 m. The forward mesh
does not change with depth in the x-direction.
• The same x-structure is assumed for the 6 ﬁrst inversion layers. For the 5 next
inversion layers, neighbored inversion cells are merged together two by two.
Same mechanism for the 4 next inversion layers, and etc.

to avoid side eﬀects from the sides. The ﬁrst layer has a width of 40 m, the

5 The data
The data provided are stored in .edi format which is speciﬁc to MT data. In a
ﬁrst time, the manual "MT/EMAP Data Interchange Standard" [29] has been used
to understand and convert them to .txt format. An example of converted table is
available in Appendix F. The data consist into auto and cross power spectral density
(psd) estimates between Ex, Ey, Hx, Hy and Hz, for each frequency and each station
(H denotes the magnetic ﬁeld strength B/µ0). A psd is the distribution of power along
the frequency axis. A cross-power spectral density (cpsd) denotes the power shared
and the phase shift between two signals by frequency. An auto-power spectral density
(apsd) is a (cpsd) where both signals are in fact the same signal. These quantities
are used to avoid biasing eﬀects due to noise from sources external to the Earth
[30]. These sources can be due to the civilization (electric power lines, car traﬃc),
to the meteorology (strong wind, lightning discharge) or to the sensor itself (internal
electric circuit). On one hand, inconsistent local noise will not be correlated between
the estimates of two diﬀerent quantities, and will be invisible in the cpsd. On the
other hand, since any quantity is coherent with itself, it will be enhanced in the apsd
[30]. Bahr et.al. (2005) [30] explain that by multiplying the two equations from (2.14)
with the complex conjugates of the electric and magnetic spectra, formulas allowing
the computation of the impedance tensor from cpsd and apsd can be reached (5.1):

CHAPTER 6
Geological
background
Until 145 million years ago (Mya), Greenland was attached to North America. During
the Cretaceous period (145-66 Mya), it separated and expanded. The gap between
Greenland and Canada increased, and sedimentary areas developed at continental
margins.
The Nuussuaq Basin was covered by a thick layer of sediments, which
developed until the end of the Cretaceous period. The important amount of fossils
found in the sediments shows the former existence of a forest and a wild fauna life at
this period. The western boundary of the sediment layer is not precisely determined
yet, but should be close to the coast of Canada. On the east, the sediment layer
becomes thinner and thinner and the basement shallower and shallower.
Around
75 Mya, the area became unstable and several phenomenon happened that changed
its face: a rapid uplift, a segmentation by faulting and the erosion of deep canyons.
The biggest faults are aligned parallel to each other in the SSW-NNE direction. The
Paleogene period (66 to 23 Mya) is characterized by a strong volcanic activity. A
plateau basalt, which is a rock formed by rapid cooling of lava at the surface, was
created. It was 4 to 10 km thick and overlays the sediments layer. It is called the
Vaigat formation. The eruption center is thought to be located in the middle of the
basin, and the lava spread in all directions.
The basalt plateau was thinner and
thinner toward the east and nowadays, the basaltic rocks are mainly located on the
mountains. In the case of no mineralization from metal en graphite, the basalt and
the basement rocks have higher resistivity that the sediments. This means that in the
case of the Nuussuaq peninsula, the resistivity would be high in shallow subsurface,
reducing through the sediment layer and rising again when the basement is reached.
However in this region it is known that intrusions including graphite (which is very
conductive) coming from larger depth cut through the sediments and basaltic rocks.
Some mineralized intrusions do not reach the surface, and may be causing a systematic
increase of conductivity in depth. A map of the current geological situation of the
Nuussuaq peninsula is displayed on the Figure (6.1). Nowadays, the basement in this
area lies between 5 and 10 km of depth. [33]

Conclusion
The 1D inversion is extremely simplistic, ﬁrst because the earth electrical conductivity
distribution is obviously more complex than 1D, but also because multiple simpliﬁca-
tions have been done (Zxx and Zyy avoided and Berdichevsky average applied). As a

consequence the results are not used to infer anything concrete about the real electri-
cal conductivity distribution below the stations. However it gives a positive feeling
about the Occam’s method. The inversion was successful, the data are ﬁtted by the
estimated model, and the proﬁle of the estimated model with depth is consistent. The
inversion scheme seems to solve well the data.

CHAPTER 10
Discussion
According to the dimensional analysis of the data, the structure beneath the stations
of measurement is mainly simple 1D. This result is in accordance with the geological
background of the area which predicts a layered 1D structure composed of a top
layer of basaltic rocks and a layer of sediments just beneath. These two layers cover
the basement which can be seen around 5 to 10 km of depth depending on the
location. Of course, the real structure of the area, which was particularly geologically
dynamic in the past, is more complex than only layered 1D. But it is still consistent
to perform a 1D inversion to get a ﬁrst feeling about the Earth’s crust electrical
conductivity structure of the area. The results of the 1D inversion show that the
electrical resistivity is globally decreasing with depth. This was expected according
to the data, the apparent resistivities being also clearly decreasing with depth. And
as mentioned in the chapter about the geological background of the area, mineralized
intrusions not reaching the surface or subsurface could be the reason of the increase
of conductivity with depth. The results of the 1D inversion also show layers of high
resistivity surrounded by layers of high conductivity. By principle, the MT method
does not solve well the high resistivity features, because it is based on the conduction
of electromagnetic waves in conductive matter. However, a test done on a resistive
layer shows that it might be real, which is a ﬁrst clue that the structure of the area
is not that simple.
Having a closer look at the results of the dimensional analysis, it is observed several
zones of 2D and 3D dimensions. This suggests that a 1D assumption is deﬁnitely not
enough to model the reality. A 2D structure is then assumed. To do so, the orientation
of the 2D model, the strike direction, has to be investigated. Using the computation
of strike angles and the study of induction arrows, the strike direction is found to be
oriented SSW-NNE. This result is consistent with the geological background of the
area, which indicates that the geological faults around there are oriented in this same
direction. As a consequence, the 2D inversion should be done on lines of stations as
perpendicular as possible to this direction, meaning lines oriented in the NWW-SEE
direction. The Aaﬀarsuaq valley, oriented W-E, is then a good location to perform the
2D inversion, and it is crossed perpendicularly by the massive Qunnilik fault, which
may creates interesting features to study. The 2D inversion is done on a 19 km long
proﬁle, extending from West to East of the Qunnilik fault, along the Aaﬀarsuaq valley.
The ﬁrst obvious elements observed on the results are two enormous anomalies: one
resistive on the western side of the proﬁle, and one conductive on the eastern side. The

basement, and how they are tilted by the faults. On the West side of the Qunnilik fault

The sediments, inﬁltrated by water, could have a conductivity a lot higher than the

basement on the eastern side of the fault, and this could explain the observation.
Indeed the western side of the fault is close to the coast and furthermore to the sea
water. Water in the ground usually increases the conductivity of the rocks. The
reason is that free ions (from e.g. diﬀerent type of salts) are dissolved in the water
and cause the increase of conductivity. As a consequence, the subsurface gets more
conductive by the inﬁltration of water and that might have happened there.
Finally, a lot of small vertical features are observed in the shallow area all along
the proﬁle. One of them was tested and assessed to be possibly real. In fact, the
electrical conductivity distribution in the shallow area are often very complex, and for
several reasons. The ﬁrst possible reason is that some sections of the proﬁle may still
be frozen (permafrost), while other may be melted. The liquid water, as explained
previously, increases the conductivity, but not the ice, and that might create vertical
variations of conductivity close to the surface. Another possible reason is that there
are unconsolidated sediments at the surface before the hard bedrocks starts. The grain
size of these unconsolidated sediments and the amount of water able to inﬁltrate into
them could have an impact on their electrical conductivity.

CHAPTER 11
Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to model the Earth’s electrical conductivity distribu-
tion beneath the Nuussuaq peninsula from magnetotelluric data. To do so, the mag-
netotelluric method were investigated and understood: how from natural variations
of the electromagnetic ﬁeld, a model of the Earth’s electrical conductivity distribu-
tion can be modeled? Theoretically, the electric and magnetic ﬁelds can be linked
by a transfer function called the impedance tensor. The main idea is to simplify this
impedance tensor reducing the dimensionality of the problem. In low dimensions, the
components of the impedance tensor can be used to model the electrical conductivity
structure of the ground. However, to keep the resolution of the problem consistent,
the simpliﬁcation should be in accordance with the geology of the area. So, a dimen-
sional analysis of the data is performed. In this thesis, two diﬀerent methods were
used. The ﬁrst is the Bahr ﬂowchart and is fully based on properties of the impedance
tensor. Several indices computed from the components of the impedance tensor allow
a separation of the data into several classes of dimension. The data could then be
grouped into four diﬀerent classes of dimension: simple 1D, simple 2D, regional 1D
and complicated 2D/3D. The second method is the study of the magnetotelluric phase
tensor. This tensor represents the relationship between linearly polarized components
of the horizontal electric and magnetic ﬁelds. Its properties allow the separation of
the data into three diﬀerent classes of dimension: 1D, 2D and 3D. The two methods
were proved to be very consistent with each other because they gave very similar
results. The result of the analysis was that the area is very complex: it has a thick
layer of mostly 1D structure in the middle, covered by a chaotic layer close to the
surface, and covering a 2D/3D structure below. Several zones were already showing
what could be local conductive or resistive anomalies.
To start with, a 1D inversion was performed on individual stations. The assump-
tion of 1D electrical conductivity distribution is very simplistic. As a consequence
the objective of this ﬁrst inversion was only to get an additional feeling about the
structure of the area, and also to test the inversion scheme, named Occam’s method.
The Occam’s method seeks for the smoothest model ﬁtting the data: a simple solu-
tion is preferable to one which is unnecessarily complicated. The results were mainly
showing an increasing conductivity with depth, which was expected according to the
geological a-priori knowledge of the area.
In a second time, a 2D electrical conductivity was assumed. The objective was to
model the Earth’s crust electrical conductivity following a line of stations of measure-
==PAGE:110==
96
11 Conclusion
ments. A total of 187 stations, arranged in line, has been used. The same inversion
scheme, the Occam’s method, was used. To do so, the data had to be managed, and
oriented along the strike direction. This strike direction was investigated using the
induction arrows. Then, the full proﬁle, several kilometers long, was ﬁnally modeled.
The results were still showing a resistivity mainly increasing with depth, as from the
1D inversion. Two very huge anomalies, one resistive and the other conductive, were
spotted. Using geological knowledge of the area, the veracity of these two anomalies
were assessed. On one hand, the resistive anomaly is thought to be unreal because
by principle the MT method does not solve well resistive features, and this anomaly
is locate on a huge geological fault, where a 2D assumption might be too simplistic.
On the other hand, the conductive anomaly is thought to be real because of the pres-
ence of a very special volcano around it. The anomaly could correspond to a part of
a magma chamber of this volcano, which magma is mineralized with graphite, and
furthermore very conductive. Finally, the principles of how inﬁltrated water could
increase the conductivity of sediments, how melted ice could creates vertical conduc-
tivity area in the subsurface and how intrusions of magma not reaching the subsurface
could make the resistivity proﬁle appear more conductive were discussed.
The studied area is not very well explored for several reasons: the Aaﬀarsuaq
valley is inland, and nowadays surveys are mainly focus on coast lines; and the min-
eralized basaltic rocks on the top of mountains create strong anomaly which make it
diﬃcult to monitor magnetic surveys. However, a possible future work would be to
look at magnetic data and compare them with the results found to assess or refute
the hypothesis done. For instance, the huge conductive anomaly which is assessed
to be possibly a part of the magma chamber of the Ilugissoq volcano, with its pos-
sible surrounding intrusions, would be visible on magnetic data, if they are really
there. Moreover, from the second set of data, only the data from 183 stations of
measurements have been used, but it contains more than 1000 stations all around
the peninsula and the Dikso island. A common study of all these stations could help
to map a more global Earth’s crust electrical conductivity model of the area, and
furthermore help to its better understanding.
