Abstract 
The project is undertaking a back-analysis on the monitored data of a newly built metro 
station.  The influence of deep excavations in heavily urbanized areas felt by the surrounding 
buildings and/or other structures. Hence, a high degree of conservatism is usually adopted in 
the design phase. This thesis has as purpose to analyse the data measured by the monitoring 
equipment, and based on it, compute new numerical models which simulate the ‘real’ soil 
behaviour.  
By analysing the monitoring equipment, one can gain better insight into the site specific 
issues and maybe reach an optimised design. Mainly, the soil stratigraphy is investigated 
firstly, in order to establish the difference in soil conditions. Further, the monitored 
measurements of the horizontal inclinometer are analysed in order to understand the influence 
of the construction process on the retaining structure. The soil behaviour is measured by the 
levelling points installed around the excavation, both on the ground and on buildings. 
Contour maps are developed in order to have an overview of the excavation process influence 
on the surrounding soil and buildings. Relevant zones are further investigated by analysing 
the levelling points measurements.   
The soil stratigraphy is established and based on available advanced laboratory tests and in-
situ tests, the soil parameters for each unit are computed. For verification, two other methods 
are used in the determination of the soil characteristics: the SoilTest function in Plaxis 2D, 
which manually calibrates the parameters previously derived through empirical methods and 
the Parameters Optimisation function in Plaxis 2D, for the optimisation of the calibrated 
results. The manual calibration in SoilTest is found to simulate the best the results of the 
available tests, on which the derivation of the soil parameters is based.  
A numerical model is computed further by using commercial software Plaxis 2D. The results 
are mostly focusing on the wall displacement behaviour and surface settlements conditions. 
The retaining wall behaviour based on the established soil parameters shows larger 
displacements compared to the monitored data measurements from the horizontal 
inclinometer. Hence, a sensitivity analysis is performed, aiming to get better insight into the 
issue. Comparisons of a numerical model containing investigated soil parameters, a 
numerical model containing characteristic measured design soil parameters as well as model 
simulating the parameters used in the ‘beam on Winkler foundation’ analysis in the design 
phase, is also computed.

1. Introduction 
In this first chapter of the thesis the motivation is presented, as well as the project description 
with the simplifications assumed and the outline of the thesis.  
1.1. Motivation 
In heavily urbanized areas, requirements for transportation infrastructure lead to construction 
of deep excavations. Their proximity to existing buildings and/or adjacent infrastructure can 
cause settlement induced damage in the neighbouring structures and due to this, a high degree 
of conservatism is adopted in the designs. Commonly, deep excavation projects are equipped 
with numerous monitoring systems, as a warning procedure for any issues that might appear 
during or after completion of construction. The gathered monitoring data can be considered a 
knowledge database to be accessed for future projects. Furthermore, a back analysis on a 
specific project may lead to a better understanding of the site specific soil behaviour and soil 
structure interaction, thus achieving optimized design solutions.     
Through the construction of a new metro line in Copenhagen, called Cityringen (The City 
Circle Line), the opportunity of such a study arose. Cityringen is very well equipped in 
monitoring devices, and it offers an extensive database in gathered monitored data. This 
project is focusing on one of the stations in Cityringen, Nuuks Plads (NUP), from a total of 
seventeen.     
1.2. Objectives 
The project is intended to gain better insight into the site specific issues caused during deep 
excavation by means of back analysis of the monitored data. The objective is to reach an 
optimized design by analysing the soil properties, the surface settlements, the wall deflections 
from monitored data and make comparisons to the initial design and to a numerical model. In 
this case, optimization means to find a less conservative design, being also a safe solution. An 
evaluation of current numerical approaches for design of deep excavation and comparison 
with monitored results is also an aim of the investigation. This kind of analysis can have a 
significant impact on future projects, since it has the potential to introduce improved designs 
related to choice of elements, soil parameters, soil constitutive models, etc. for the 
investigated area, and ultimately lead to a more cost-effective solution. 
1.3. Methodology 
The first objective is to get an understanding of the soil conditions through analysis and 
interpretation of the monitored data, at Nuuks Plads station. The design itself is to be well 
analysed in order to understand possible improvements. Further, with all the design 
calculations and assumptions analysed, a thorough investigation of the monitored data is 
==PAGE:11==
Margareta Cristina Vranau

undertaken. This includes investigation of soil characteristics, surface settlements, wall 
deflections, water levels, strut forces and others. A numerical model which uses the finite-
element method is evaluated with the purpose of establishing a connection between ongoing 
numerical approaches and ‘real’ measured data from monitored equipment. Finally, 
improvements for the numerical models and assumptions regarding soil parameters and 
constitutive soil models are to be suggested. This will help identify any improvements that 
could be further used in future projects.  
1.4. Thesis outline  
The thesis is divided into the following 6 principal chapters: 
1) Introduction 
2) Case study  
This chapter presents the design data used for Nuuks Plads and summarises the design reports 
of this work. The reports concern to the geological conditions (Geotechnical Deign Report 
and Geotechnical Interpretative Report Level III), temporary and permanent works (reports 
regarding assumptions, calculations and verifications of the works), building assessment 
(reports on the surrounding existing buildings and assessment done for the building 
considered in ‘dangerous’ zones), monitored data (reports on used equipment and details on 
measurements for Cityringen and Nuuks Plads in particular). 
3) Analysis of monitoring data 
This part refers to the investigations done on the monitored data for Nuuks Plads. Mainly it is 
focused on the boreholes data (for analysing the soil conditions), the inclinometers data (for 
investigation of the retaining wall movements) and on contour plots (which show an 
overview of the settlements) monitored using levelling points installed both on the ground 
and on the buildings. Likewise, data on water levels, the 3D prisms, strain gauges, and others 
will be discussed here. 
4) Numerical modelling 
In the numerical modelling chapter, a series of investigations on the soil parameters are done 
and based on them the numerical model is developed. The soil parameters are first derived 
from available laboratory and in situ tests, then manually calibrated using the Plaxis 2D 
SoilTest function, and finally optimised by using the Parameters Optimisation function. 
Based on these investigations, the soil parameters to be used for the numerical model are 
chosen. The modelling is done using Plaxis 2D software. Finally, the results are analysed and 
compared with the design results and the monitored data results.  
5) Sensitivity analysis 
The sensitivity analysis is based on the investigations done on the soil parameters and the 
numerical model in the previous chapter. As discussed, the thorough soil conditions analysis

2. Case Study 
The case study presents an overview on Nuuks Plads station characteristics. Considering the 
entire Copenhagen Metro system (Figure 1), Nuuks Plads is located on the new M3 line, 
Cityringen (The City Circle Line). Figure 1 shows the NUP’s plan view, where one can 
identify three parts forming the station: the box (1), the main entrance (2) and the emergency 
exit (3). This project will mostly focus around the station box, due to its deep excavation. The 
other parts do not pose significant risks due to the limited excavation depth.  
Nuuks Plads’s box is an underground station and its main characteristics are presented below. 
Notice that all levels are expressed with respect to the Danish datum, ie DVR90: 
 for the main box continuous embedded retaining wall constructed by means of secant 
piles, overlapped for depths of 27 m (work level +7.1 m, toe level -19.9 m)  from 
which 3.34m are embedded , and for main entrance and emergency exit maximum 
length is 13.36 m (toe level -6.5m) 
 secant piles have a 1000 mm diameter at 785 mm horizontal spacing along both 
longitudinal sides and 800mm horizontal spacing along both transversal sides and 
perimeter of main entrance and emergency exit 
 width of 16.8 m and a length of 61.5 m between lining walls  
 maximum depth at 22.5 m from GL (down to -16.56 m) 
 ground level at approx. +7.6m 
 main stair case to concourse level +0.87 is located in the northern end of the station 
 tunnel constructed using TBM on both sides of the station 
The excavation is carried out by means of a bottom-up construction method with two 
permanent slabs (top and bottom) and three levels of temporary steel struts designed to 
prevent any unacceptable settlement in the surrounding buildings during construction. 
Nuuks Plads is constructed in a heavily urbanized environment, meaning that identification of 
proximate structures which could be at risk due to the deep excavation is of great importance.  
Figure 3 shows that there are two buildings relevantly close to the station, one on the east, on 
the longitudinal side of the box and one on the south side of the box, on transversal. The other 
structures surrounding the stations are roads, with city traffic, on Jagtvej and Rantzausgade.

The investigations on soil concentrated on the soil stratigraphy from different boreholes on 
the site and around it, in order to observe changes in the soil type and thickness of layers. 
This information is needed so the influence of certain soils and their thicknesses on the deep 
excavation’s behaviour can be investigated. The information on boreholes is available from 
Kronos, which is the software used as database, where all the measured data is stored and can 
be easily accessed. 
The design reports give a good overview on the soil type and soil stratigraphy found at Nuuks 
Plads station. However, a first question regarding the soil conditions arose from the fact that 
in these reports, the comparisons were made for a limited number of boreholes (see Figure 4). 
Hence, it might be that a detailed analysis of all the boreholes will bring into attention new 
issues in layering, consequently, in soil parameters. Figure 5 is an overview of all the 
boreholes contained in NUP. The black marker is showing the boreholes for which the 
information is given in reports and the blue marker represents the boreholes for which 
information is extracted from Kronos. It is important to mention that for boreholes P13.100, 
P13.101, P13.102, P13.104, more detailed data is found, compared to the other boreholes. 
This might be due to the fact that these three boreholes are considered the most representative 
and their data is accounted for most of the soils tests. For the boreholes type ‘C’ no 
information was found in Kronos and one can only base on the ‘C’ boreholes data presented 
in the design reports. However, the ‘C’ boreholes are mostly spread on the outside of the 
excavation pit, and other boreholes of type ‘P’ are found in their vicinity, which are 
considered reliable enough for the investigation. Another specification refers to the number 
coding, which represents the type of usage of a specific borehole, as follows: 
 Boreholes ending in numbers related to 300 represent the boreholes used for 
monitoring the water levels 
 Boreholes ending in numbers related to 400 represent the boreholes used for water 
pumping 
 Boreholes ending in numbers related to 500 represent the boreholes used for water re-
infiltration  
The first conclusion from reviewing the soil stratigraphy was that, for this case, there seem to 
appear four main types of soil: fill unit, clay till/sand till unit, meltwater sand/meltwater 
gravel unit and Copenhagen limestone unit. The identification of the individual layers has 
been based on the geological description in combination with the classification tests and the 
results of the geophysical tests carried out in situ.

The fill is consists of a sandy clay, clayey sand and sandy gravel, but also peat mull, clay 
mull and sand mull. An overview is showing relatively similar thickness throughout the

Upper clay till and sand till units are located right below the fill layer and in the design they 
are considered as one unit. The upper till layer a cohesive soil of very sandy to very silty clay 
till. As sand till is also found in certain boreholes and the clay till is silty and sandy in places, 
this unit is considered in design a transitional soil between a typical cohesive and a typical 
cohesionless soil. Nevertheless, investigation on the permeability can determine whether it 
has a cohesive or cohesionless behaviour. An oedometer test is performed on a ML/MS

while tests in purely silty/granular soils gave a range of permeability of 10-5-10-6 m/s, [17]. 
Hence, it can be concluded that the ML/MS layer has a cohesive behaviour. Nevertheless, a 
more detailed investigation on the spread of ML and MS can be seen in Figure 7. The showed

middle Copenhagen limestone is found (MCL) at about -21.1 m. Due to the significant 
disturbance during the drilling process, a geomechanics description of fractures rock cores is 
normally not representative. The degree of induration of the intact rock ranges from 
unlithified (H1) to very strongly indurated (H5).  
By examining the boreholes inside and outside the excavation pit, the conclusion is drawn 
that the fill and limestone layers have a quite equal distribution amongst different boreholes 
(meaning that the layers start and finish at about the same depth thus having comparable 
thickness in all boreholes). However, the clay till, sand till, meltwater sand and meltwater 
gravel units need a further investigation in order to establish their spreading through NUP

2.4. Building assessment 
According to the building assessment carried out for Nuuks Plads station, the surrounding 
structures do not present high risks.  
In Cityringen there are three stages for analysing building assessment as follows: 
 Stage 1: predicted settlement from excavations is lower than 10 mm and predicted 
ground slope is lower than 1/500. Buildings are not subjected to further assessment 
 Stage 2: predicted settlement from excavations is above 10 mm and predicted ground 
slope is above 1/500 
 Stage 3: Not necessary for Building Assessment of NUP 
However, for Nuuks Plads case only the first two stages are necessary, since the limit values 
are not reached. This implies a good and stable behaviour of the building in the excavation’s 
proximity.

3. Analysis of monitoring data 
In the monitoring data analysis, the focus was mostly on the wall deflections and surface 
settlements. The information is taken from Kronos and it is analysed in this chapter.  
The data on wall deflections is given by the inclinometers measurements. The inclinometers 
are placed on a wall or in the very proximity of a wall and record all deflections happening 
during the construction phase.  
The surface settlements were first analysed in terms of contour plots, such that one can get an 
overview of the settlements or heave experienced around the excavation pit. The zones with 
large settlements are further investigated through the measured data of the levelling points. 
3.1. Inclinometers analysis 
For the analysis of the horizontal displacements, the measurements taken by the 
inclinometers installed in NUP’s retaining structure are considered. According to reports, 
eight inclinometers are installed in NUP retaining structure, three on each longitudinal side 
and one on each transversal side. An overview of the inclinometers position is found in 
Figure 27.  
It is stated that three of the eight inclinometers are installed without the inclinometer sleeve, 
thus, they will be drilled just behind the retaining wall, 50 cm from the corresponding piles. 
During the analysing process it was found that these three inclinometers, located on the West 
side of the station, are not giving reliable measurements (see dotted line), due to poor 
installation. In addition, they were decommissioned after a short period (about two months). 
Hence, for this project, one can only rely on 5 inclinometers, 3 on the East side: NUP-IN21, 
NUP-IN 22, NUP-IN 23 and 2 on the transversal directions: NUP-IN 24 and NUP-IN 28.  
The first approach for analysing the data is to consider one inclinometer measurement every 
month from Kronos, during the entire period. However, after a short analysis was established 
that these measurements do not give accurate results, since the data is given as an arbitrary 
value within that specific month, thus, not reliable. A more detailed data extraction is made 
by considering two measurements for every day (one measurement every 12 hours), during 
the entire construction process.

3.2. Surface settlements analysis 
Soil settlements are generally the results of a change of effective stress, whether these may be 
due to loading a change of total stress and/or due to a change in porewater pressure. It is 
important to regard all aspects when investigating vertical displacements, especially in the 
case of a deep excavation in a highly urbanized area. For this, equipment is installed to 
monitor the ground movements and for NUP station, these are measured using levelling 
points. They are installed on the surfaces (roads, pavements and ground) around the 
excavation pit, in order to determine the shape of settlement trough. The project will be 
referring to two types of levelling points: on the ground, levelling pins denoted by LP, and on 
the buildings, placed at the base of the building, called levelling bolts and denoted by LB. 
Additional levelling points are also installed in extended section, beyond the expected 
influence zone, in order to verify the extent of the zone influenced by the excavation works. 
Figure 26 in the previous chapter shows the influence zones around the excavation. An 
overview of all containing levelling points around NUP is found in Figure 38. 
 
Figure 38 Overview of existing levelling points around NUP 
As noticed, the levelling pins on the pavements along Jatvej, Rantzausgade and Borups Allé 
are installed beyond the predicted 2 mm zone. In some areas, like Jagtvej and Rantzausgade, 
it is difficult to have levelling points along the site fence, and surface prisms are installed 
instead.

which builds contour maps based on the data given by the levelling points. This is useful

4. Numerical modelling 
For a deep excavation, the monitoring data measurements are considered the most reliable 
results one can have for an analysis of its behaviour. However, in any project the monitoring 
results come as a phenomenon happens, meaning that in most of the cases it is already too 
late to take measures. This is the main reason why a back-analysis is recommended. One can 
learn how the soil behaves in certain situations and certain areas, such that a following project 
can be safer and more economical.  
There are numerous numerical models used in the simulation of a structure or a project. It is 
of high importance to understand the chosen numerical model, its assumptions and 
limitations, in order to have reliable results. A comparison with the monitoring data will aid 
in understanding the reliability of the numerical model. For this, one has to establish very 
well the input data in the model, to simulate as close as possible the on-site characteristics. 
This consists in providing very good soil parameters properties, settling the structural aspects 
of the construction, the correct loadings, and so on. 
In the design, Paratie software is used as a numerical model. For this project, Plaxis 2D is 
adopted, due to its good reliability and quite increase usage. One of the deciding factors of a 
good numerical model concerns the soil parameters, due to the fact that geomaterials have a 
complex behaviour and are challenging to simulate. There are numerous characteristics to be 
considered when working with soil, starting with parameters characteristics, to soil models, 
thickness of layers and others. With this regard, a special attention is given to the 
determination of the soil parameters. They are determined by using three methods: derivation 
from available laboratory and on-site soil tests, manual calibration of the derived parameters 
through Plaxis 2D SoilTest function and optimisation of the calibrated paramteres through 
Plaxis 2D Parameter Optimization function. Their results are to be evaluated and a decision 
on the specific values to be used in the model is made.  
As a reminder, the conclusion after boreholes investigation in the previous chapter is that 
NUP has a consistent stratigraphy, thus, any cross-section contains the same layers (with 
small differences in thickness): fill unit, ML/MS unit, DS/DG unit and limestone unit. Based 
on test availability, the focus in determining the soil parameters is on the the clay till unit and 
the meltwater sand unit.  
After establishing the soil parameters, the structural properties of the excavation’s elements 
are regarded (wall stiffness, strut and slab characteristics, etc.), the external loads found and 
the Plaxis 2D model is computed.  
4.1. Soil parameters derivation from triaxial test 
As mentioned above, it is of great importance to establish the correct and closest to reality 
characteristics of a numerical model, in order to get reliable and comparable results. The most 
reliable data to establish the soil parameters comes from tests performed on site or in

laboratory on a specific soil unit. This project has a variety of advanced tests, as well as in 
situ tests, to be considered as base for soil parameters derivation. 
The goal is to establish soil parameters to simulate as close as possible results of the available 
soil tests, and based on the results, to form soil models to be used in the numerical model.  
4.1.1. Fill unit 
Advanced tests are not performed for the fill unit and derivations come mostly from in situ

4.1.5. Soil constitutive model 
Investigating the design’s soil models, it seems that the Mohr-Coulomb model is adopted for 
all the soil layers. The Mohr-Coulomb is a perfectly elastoplastic model, considered a good 
first-order model, reliable enough to give trustful first insight into a problem. The fact that it 
uses a constant average stiffness, can be both an advantage, from the simplicity point of view 
(there is no need to establish more parameters), but also a shortcoming (usually, the average 
stiffness is too simple when accurate results are needed). Typically, in a detailed 
investigation, more accurate soil models are recommended, such as the Hardening Soil model 
or HS-small strain model. From the soil parameters investigation, it is concluded that the use 
of a Hardening Soil model is possible for the ML and DS units. The Hardening Soil is an 
advanced hyperbolic soil model formulated in the framework of hardening plasticity [15]. 
The stress-strain relationship is approximated with the hyperbolic function by Duncan-Chang 
(a non-linear curve), leading to a more realistic reproduction of the soil deformations. The 
main difference with the Mohr-Coulomb model is the use of three different input stiffness 
(triaxial loading stiffness, E50, triaxial unloading stiffness, Eur, and oedometer loading 
stiffness, Eoed). Apart from this, it also takes into account soil dilatancy and the fact that the 
yield surface can expand due to plastic straining.   
In [13], Obrzud (2010) is presenting a table containing recommendations for the model 
choice for soil type and types of analysis (Table 17). It also shows that the best options for 
sand and clays are the HS model and HS-small strain model.

4.2.1. Manual calibration 
The Plaxis 2D SoilTest function can be used to compare laboratory test results with soil tests 
simulated by the software for any soil saved as input in a project.. It has incorporated five 
types of tests: triaxial test, oedometer test, CRS test, DSS test, and a general test. Even 
though the calibration for this case is based on triaxial test results, the general test is more 
suitable to use, due to the possibility of defining the specific stress and strain conditions.   
It is important to mention the fact that Plaxis 2D has a few limitations when it comes to the 
stiffness parameters, as follows:

Nevertheless, these limitations impose a relationship between the parameters; thus, if one 
changes, usually others need to change as well, and this is narrowing the degrees of freedom 
in selecting stiffness parameters.  .   
Due to test availability, the focus in this part is on the ML and DS samples. It was previously 
established that the fill unit and the limestone unit are to be considered with their design 
parameters. Having two available triaxial tests for both ML and DS units, there are two cases 
considered for each soil unit in the calibration, all by using the Hardening Soil model.  
The ‘General’ panel in the SoilTest function allows the selection of type of test, drained or 
undrained. The value of the vertical preconsolidation stress is manually introduced. Likewise, 
the mobilized relative shear strength is available, but only for HS model and HS-small strain 
model, to set the initial shear hardening contour. Next, the phases are to be established. For 
this project, five phases are considered: initial, loading, unloading, reloading 1 and reloading 
2. In a usual project, the reloading phase can have only one input. However, during the 
investigation, it was noticed that in the deviatoric stress-axial strain plot, the reloading curve 
has a strange, sudden behaviour. Knowing that the reloading curve simulates the shearing 
curve, one can understand that by considering only one phase with different values for σ1 and 
σ3, the simulation is not complete. In laboratory, the water around the sample is first brought 
to an equal pressure (σ1 = σ3) and then σ1 increases, because it needs to reach consolidation 
before the shearing starts. Hence, simulating first reloading phase with σ1 = σ3, and a second 
one with σ3 = 0 and σ1 = value - σ3, an appropriate shear curves is obtained. 
An important remark here is the fact that the program uses the increments of stresses, starting 
to Phase 1, after Initial stresses are established. By clicking the ‘Run’ button the simulation is 
started and the panel on the lower part of the tab is presenting the results under graphical

form. More information on the SoilTest function tabs and options are presented in Appendix 
D.2 – SoilTest function tabs and options. 
The manual calibration is based on a ‘trial and error’ approach. The derived soil properties 
from the laboratory tests are introduced in the Property panel and changed such that the 
stiffness and strength plots suit best the laboratory results.  
The stress increments used as input require a sign when implemented in the SoilTest 
function. The sign relates to the considered phase, as follows: 
 A positive stress increment implies tension or unloading 
 A zero stress increment implies a period with constant stress 
 A negative stress increment implies compression or loading

4.4. Plaxis 2D modelling 
The numerical modelling is done by using Plaxis 2D program. This program operates by 
using the finite-element method, thus, it allows the modelling of complicated nonlinear soil 
behaviour and various interface conditions, with different soil properties and geometries. 
Plaxis 2D has the ability to deal with excess pore-pressure phenomenon, to give a good 
insight into the soil-structure interaction (interfaces can be used to simulate the shearing zone 
==PAGE:87==
Margareta Cristina Vranau

in contact with the pile), and also it allows work with different advanced constitutive soil 
models, for the simulation of non-linear behaviour.  
4.4.1. Cross-section 
The first step, in establishing the numerical model, is to choose a relevant cross-section for 
the excavation. As discussed, a transversal cross-section is relevant for analysis, because it 
shows the differences between the conditions on the East side (left) compared the conditions 
on the West (right). Due to the consistent stratigraphy, any transversal cross-section is 
considered to give similar results, thus, the choice is based on trying to find the best fit, for 
establishing relevant comparisons. The red line in Figure 66 shows the chosen cross-section. 
It passes through inclinometer 21, meaning that relevant wall deflections comparisons can be 
made, and includes also building UK2105 in the analysis. Regarding the surface settlements, 
the section passes through the most dangerous zone, as showed on the contour maps; 
meaning that a numerical model carried out in this area, could bring a better insight into the 
understanding of the settlements. The choice is also based on the design verification sections. 
In Figure 3, in the Case Study analysis, one can see that Verification section n.1 is 
corresponding to the chosen cross-section. This is important for giving reliable comparisons 
with the design results.

4.5. Comparisons  
In order to better understand the numerical model’s results, a series of comparisons are 
carried out. The conclusions of all the investigations done so far are summed and a 
comparison between the design results, monitored data results and numerical model results is 
presented. The interest is mostly on the deflections and the surface settlements.  
Here, the ‘design results’ are referring to the results given by Paratie Plus, the software used 
for numerical analysis. Thus, an investigation is done on the outcomes from Paratie Plus and 
they are gathered and interpreted in this chapter. The modelling of wall-soil interaction in 
Paratie Plus is based on ‘beam on elastic foundation” theory (Winkler approach), while Plaxis 
2D is a software based on finite element method.  
Comparisons between these models and the monitored data are giving very good information 
on the reliability of the available investigated parameters.

6. Conclusions 
The aim of this thesis was to back-analyse one of the newly built metro stations in the recent 
developed Cityringen (The City Circle Line), in Copenhagen. The investigation is based on 
the measurements given by the installed monitoring equipment. Both the excavation and its 
surrounding structures are heavily monitored during the entire construction process and even 
a while after it has finished. Hence, the availability of information from the provided database 
is large. The purpose of the installed equipment is to oversee the effect of the excavation on 
the surrounding structures and soil. The borehole analysis is the first to consider, for 
establishing the stratigraphy fluctuations. By investigating all the available borehole 
information, it is established that the stratigraphy of Nuuks Plads station and its surroundings 
does not present large discrepancies. There are mainly four types of soil and rocks found at 
Nuuks Plads: fill unit, clay till/sand till unit, meltwater sand/meltwater gravel unit, and upper 
and middle Copenhagen limestone. The differences in the type of soil appear mostly 
underneath the fill unit, where a differentiation between clay till and sand till can be made, 
for certain zones of the excavation. All units found beneath the clay till/sand till are 
consistent. Large changes in the thickness of the investigated units do not occur and are 
considered negligible.  
Investigations on the excavation retaining wall are performed by using the horizontal 
inclinometers measurements. Results from five out of a total of eight inclinometers are 
available, three on one of the longitudinal retaining wall and one on each transversal retaining 
wall. Comparisons of the inclinometers measurements from the longitudinal wall presented 
similar behaviour. As expected, the tendency of the retaining wall is to move towards the 
excavation, having displacement values ranging from 0mm to 18 mm (for the final stages of 
the excavation process). To provide the exact dates of the construction stage represented a 
challenge. The initially assumed periods were further investigated by the help of the on-site 
available photos, leading to divisions and new construction stages.  
The effect of the deep excavation was analysed through measurements of levelling points 
(which include levelling pints and levelling bolts) and 3D prisms. An initial analysis by 
means of contour plots was done, thus establishing faulted measurements, and negligible 
zones. Around the station, especially about the surrounding buildings, positive values of 
settlements were showed, indicating a heave of a maximum +1 mm. Nevertheless, it was 
established through measurements sensitivity of 1 mm and the reliability of the contour plots, 
that this result should not be outcome is negligible. By using the contour plots, on overview 
of the surface settlements determined by each construction stage was presented. Further, 
zones of maximum settlements were investigated by analysing the levelling point 
measurements. The maximum settlement values were found around -9 mm, for the final 
stages, in the most extreme cases. However, none of the investigated levelling points 
presented measurements to exceed the alert or alarm limit.   
==PAGE:137==
Margareta Cristina Vranau 
 
128 
 
The aim of investigating the monitored data was to establish relevant parameters in order to 
establish numerical models which can present an optimised design. Two-dimensional finite 
element models are setup using commercial software Plaxis. The soil parameters are 
thoroughly investigated by a number of methods. Based on advanced laboratory tests as well 
as in-situ tests, the soil parameters are firstly derived by empirical equations. For verification, 
Plaxis SoilTest function is used and a manual calibration is performed in an attempt to 
simulate the laboratory test results. A further investigation of the soil parameters related to 
the Parameter Optimisation function, available in Plaxis. The purpose is the optimisation of 
the calibrated soil parameters, by means of Plaxis software. However, the Parameter 
Optimisation function is considerably new and detailed information on the algorithms used 
for optimisation is not available. The resulting parameters are considered not fully reliable 
due to small values of m (around 0.1) which characterise all the investigations. Because a full 
understanding of the function and the comprehension of its results cannot be done, the 
resulted soil parameters are not used in the numerical model. The conclusion of the analysis 
was that the manual calibration done through SoilTest function is the most reliable, thus its 
parameters are used in the numerical model. To be noted that the established soil parameters 
are based on the available laboratory and in-situ tests, meaning that more tests give a better 
reliability. For these investigations, the fill unit and the limestone unit were detained and their 
parameters remained with the design values. Two samples were available for both clay till 
unit and meltwater sand unit.  
The structural characteristics of the excavations were mostly based on design considerations. 
The numerical model results were compared to the monitored data measurements. First, the 
wall deflections were analysed, and they showed an inconsistency with the monitored data in 
the upper part of the excavation, relating to the soil layers. Larger deflection values appeared 
in the numerical model results; however, this was foreseen, since a conservative approach is 
expected from the numerical model. However, the shape of the wall displacement showed 
quite large, continuously increasing displacements in the upper part of the excavation, which 
did not correspond to the behaviour observed in the inclinometer measurements. The surface 
settlements were also investigated and compared. For this case, comparable results are 
obtained, showing that the settlements are having a good evaluation in the numerical model.  
It is decided to further inspect the wall displacement behaviour, by undertaking a sensitivity 
analysis on various factors. As discussed, the irregularity in the wall displacement given by 
Plaxis results is related to the clay till layer, whose parameters were computed from a triaxial 
undrained test. Thus, a further investigation on the layer is done, by analysing a drained 
triaxial test, taken from the investigations on Frederiksberg station. The results are considered 
appropriate since the same was taken for the same type of clay till, and Frederiksberg station 
is close to Nuuks Plads. The laboratory test showed increased strength and stiffness 
parameters. To be noticed that the levels from which the soil for the sample is extracted are 
different for these case. At NUP, both samples are extracted from the layer above +4 m level 
(DVR90), while the sample from Frederiksberg was found at a level of below -4 m level 
(DVR90). In the design phase, a distinction is made between these two levels, and the upper 
==PAGE:138==
Back analysis of monitoring data of deep excavations 
 
129 
 
layer is considered a transitional one from the fill unit, consequently having lower strength 
and stiffness. The sensitivity analysis perceives this distinction, and shows a better behaviour 
for the analysis of the clay till from the Frederiksberg station.  
While investigating the calibrated soil parameters, it was of interest to make a comparison 
with the characteristic measured design soil parameters, such that one can understand the 
differences between the initially assumed parameters and the analysed ones. In the design, a 
“beam on elastic foundation” model was computed in order to establish the excavation 
behaviour. Thus, this part regards both the characteristic measured design soil parameters 
presented as final ones in the design reports by using a Mohr-Coulomb soil constitutive 
model and the soil parameters used for the BWF model, for which a Hardening Soil model 
was computed. The sensitivity analysis for this case shows that the behaviour of the 
numerical model containing characteristic measured design soil parameters simulates the best 
the wall displacement behaviour of the monitored data. The BWF model is not inaccurate; 
however, due to lowest values of stiffness, the wall displacement values are increased.  
While analysing the wall displacement shape, it was noticed that one of the differences 
between the monitored data results and all the other investigation related to the movement of 
the wall head. The same behaviour is observed while investigating the difference between the 
three inclinometers on the longitudinal side of the excavation. The cross-section for the 
numerical model was chosen to pass through IN21, such that all measurements from the 
inclinometer can be related to the numerical model results. However, an investigation in this 
direction showed that the inclinometer in the middle, IN22, is simulating better the movement 
of the wall head for the investigated models. The assumption is that the behaviour is related 
to the inclinometer proximity to a strut, which resembles the numerical model characteristics.  
Due to test availability, all the discussions so far were related to the influence of the clay till 
unit. However, the borehole investigation showed that the area where the cross-section is 
considered contains a sand till unit. In the design it is specified that due to the frictional 
behaviour of the sand till, if considered in investigations, it can be characterised by the 
meltwater sand parameters. Thus, a sensitivity analysis regarding the replacement of the clay 
till unit with the sand till unit having the meltwater sand parameters is undertaken. The results 
showed that the assumptions are correct. The values and shape of the wall displacement are 
very close to the monitored data results. This leads to the conclusion that the soil type 
assumed for numerical model investigations has a very large influence on the results. Future 
investigations on the topic can be related to developing new cross-section, though boreholes 
containing clay till and observe the differences. The general boreholes contain a 
generalization of the soil type, however, a few of them are very detailed analysed (P13.100, 
P13.101, P13.102, P13.104) and show an interposing of the clay till and clay sand. A 
numerical analysis containing the detailed borehole data could be of interest in understanding 
the consequences of generalisation of a soil layer.
