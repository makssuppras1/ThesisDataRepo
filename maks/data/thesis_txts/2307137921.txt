Abstract
Speech perception relies on both vision and audition. This is mani-
fested behaviorally in enhanced speech perception for congruent au-
diovisual speech compared to visual (Sumby & Pollack 1957), and in
the McGurk illusion where an auditory speech percept is modiﬁed by
incongruent visual speech. Within the study of audiovisual speech per-
ception, several quantitative models have been proposed to describe
the computational process of audiovisual integration in the brain. Al-
though some of the dominant models view this process as universal
and automatic, recent studies suggest that audiovisual integration is
subject to inter-subject variability (Schwartz 2010) and can be mod-
ulated by the phonetic context (Nahorna et al. 2012). The current
study addresses the question of how these factors can be incorporated
into quantitative models by comparing the maximum-likelihood esti-
mation model, postulating universal and automatic integration, to the
weighted model allowing for individual weighting of the auditory and
visual inputs. The models are compared both for a ”standard” behav-
ioral dataset and for a pilot experiment devised to probe the eﬀects of
phonetic context on sensory weighting. Although model comparison
results are inconclusive, several valuable insights for future modeling
work are proposed.
1
Introduction
For the greater part of our evolution, before the advent of the radio and tele-
phones, humans have primarily been speaking to each other in face-to-face
situations. Seeing the face of a speaker, and in particular the movements
of the visible articulators (lips, jaw and tongue) provides a source of infor-
mation about the speech signal that aids us in understanding its content,
particularly when the auditory signal alone is faint or ambiguous. There
is a wide body of evidence suggesting that this information is indeed used,
and that auditory and visual sensory signals are integrated in the brain to
enhance speech perception – for instance, seeing the face of the speaker has
been shown to improve speech comprehension in noise (Sumby & Pollack
1954).
A striking demonstration of audiovisual integration in speech perception
is the McGurk illusion, in which an auditory speech recording is dubbed with
an incongruent visual speech video, leading to an illusory percept (McGurk
& MacDonald 1976). In certain cases, the resulting integrated percept is
a fusion, seemingly a compromise between the two incongruent inputs (eg.
auditory / ba/ and visual /ga/ often perceived as /da/). In other cases, the

==PAGE:3==
integrated percept is a combination, including elements from both inputs
(eg. /ga/ often perceived as /bga/ when dubbed with visual /ba/). Finally,
in visual capture illusions, perceivers hear the phoneme articulated in the
visual speech video and not the auditory speech recording.
The problem of quantitatively measuring audiovisual integration may
seem trivial at a ﬁrst glance – one could simply expose test subjects to
repeated presentations of McGurk stimuli and count the proportion of il-
lusion responses. However, individual diﬀerences in the propensity for the
McGurk illusion need not be caused by diﬀerences in integration, but could
stem from individual variability in unisensory processing of the auditory and
visual components of the stimulus (Schwartz 2010). If a person, for instance,
has weak auditory speech perception but good lipreading, one would expect
a stronger visual inﬂuence on audiovisual speech perception than for a sub-
ject who has excellent auditory speech perception and the same lipreading
abilities and integration mechanism. Thus, a key challenge for computa-
tional models of audiovisual integration is to disentangle the relationship
between unisensory perceptual processing and the mechanism of perceptual
fusion.
The quantitative study of audiovisual speech perception has long been
dominated by the fuzzy-logical model of perception (FLMP), which deﬁnes
the probability of a certain response to an audiovisual stimulus Pav, as
the normalized product of the response probabilities for its auditory and
visual components, i. e. Pav = PaPv/(PaPv + (1 −Pa)(1 −Pv)) Massaro
(1987). The assumption of the FLMP is thus that a separate categorization
of the auditory and visual components is ﬁrst carried out (resulting in the
discrete probabilities Pa and Pv), and that these unisensory decisions are
subsequently combined by the multiplicative rule. If one assumes that the
auditory and visual components are indeed categorized separately prior to
fusion and that the unisensory estimates are independent from one another,
the multiplicative rule is statistically optimal.
According to the FLMP, audiovisual integration is thus a universal and
automatic process, only depending on a subject’s (discrete) response prob-
abilities for the auditory and visual components of an audiovisual speech
stimulus. Although the FLMP has been shown to provide good ﬁts for ex-
perimental data in a number of studies, critics have argued that these good
ﬁts are primarily an eﬀect of excessive ﬂexibility of the model (Schwartz
2006), rather than its ability to describe the computational mechanism of
audiovisual integration per se. Moreover, a growing body of empirical stud-
ies challenges the assumption of the FLMP that audiovisual integration of
occurs uniformly and automatically.

1.1
Integration is subject to inter- and intraindividual dif-
ferences
The uniformity of the perceptual fusion process was ﬁrst contested by stud-
ies arguing that it is subject-dependent. This argument was ﬁrst brought
forward by studies suggesting that there exist culturally driven diﬀerences
in the strength of the McGurk eﬀect (Sekiyama & Tokhura 1991), with
Japanese subjects displaying a weaker illusion compared to English.
Al-
though these results are highly contested due to methodological problems
associated comparing diﬀerent spoken languages, a more recent study by
Schwartz (2010) focusing on inter-subject variability between speakers of
the same language suggests that the fusion mechanism is to a large degree
subject-dependent. In this study, a standard implementation of the FLMP
is compared to a weighted version, where a free weight parameter determines
the relative importance attributed to the auditory and visual components
in the fusion process.
Modeling results indicate a signiﬁcantly better performance of the weighted
FLMP compared to the standard implementation and a clear divide between
“audio” (higher weight to the auditory component), “visual” (high weight
to the visual component) and “intermediary” (approximately equal weight-
ing of the components) subjects when comparing the resulting auditory and
visual weights of subjects Schwartz (2010).
The existence of “audio”, “visual” and “intermediate” perceivers does
not, however, necessarily imply that perceptual fusion is not a universal and
statistically optimal mechanism. One could imagine that, in addition to the
response probabilities for the auditory and visual stimulus components, the
brain uses information about the precision of each unimodal estimate to de-
termine the weighting of the auditory and visual components. This approach
was formalized by Ernst & Banks (2002) in their study of visuo-haptic inte-
gration of distance estimates, and called the maximum likelihood estimation
(MLE) model. In the MLE model, each unisensory internal representation is
a continuous random variable following a Gaussian probability distribution,
and the distribution of the integrated percept is the normalized product of
the unisensory distributions. Again, this multiplicative rule for integration
is statistically optimal under the assumption of independence between the
auditory and visual probability distributions.
The MLE model thus mainly diﬀers from the FLMP in the internal rep-
resenation of the stimuli (continuous vs. categorical) – not in the assumption
of independence of sensory estimates or uniformity and optimality of per-

Andersen (2010) introduced response boundaries assigning each response

percept is preserved in this categorical implementation.
Modeling results suggest that while MLE does not ﬁt the data as well
as FLMP, it is less prone to overﬁtting to the data and might thus have
higher generalizability (Andersen 2010). Still, both models regard the fu-
sion process as automatic, ruling out the possibility that weighting of the
auditory and visual components may vary within a subject. This view has
been contested by studies showing that the fusion process can be modulated
by external factors.
The discovery that audiovisual integration is modulated by attention
showed that in addition to possibly being subject-dependent, the fusion
process cannot be argued to be fully automatic. In the “face-leaf” study
by Tiippana et al. (2004), a visual distractor in the form of a transparent
leaf moving on the face of the speaker superimposed on a McGurk stimulus
led to subjects reporting a lower proportion of fusion percepts compared
to the distractor-free condition. Later experiments have indicated that this
modulation, resulting in a decreased weighting of the visual modality in
the perceptual fusion, is not necessarily an eﬀect of simply diverting the
visual attention of the subject away from the visual speech cues but can be
achieved by increasing attentional load by superposing an additional task
involving the processing of tactile, visual or auditory stimuli (Alsius et al.
2005, 2007). Thus, in addition to the evidence for subject-speciﬁc weighting
of the auditory and visual modalities, these studies suggest that weights can
change dynamically within the same subject depending on the attentional
load imposed during the audiovisual task.
Finally, the strength of the fusion process is suggested to depend on
contextual factors inﬂuencing the perceived probability that the auditory
and visual information come from the same source, and that this estimation
of coherence determines the strength of perceptual fusion.

Ernst (2006) notes that although multimodal integration generally en-
hances perception, complete fusion at the perceptual level comes with the
cost of the brain potentially losing access to the individual auditory and
visual input signal. In the case of the signals being in agreement with one
another, this is unproblemantic. If, on the other hand, a conﬂict between
the auditory and visual signals is detected, the mapping between the sig-
nals cannot adapt in response to this conﬂict, since the individual signals
cannot be recovered. However, empirical data shows that the degree of per-
ceptual fusion varies with the perceived discrepancy of unimodal signals in
both visuo-haptic paradigms (Ernst 2006) and for the audiovisual sound-
ﬂash illusion (Shams et al. 2005), suggesting that the brain computes some
measure of the coupling between the signals which determines the degree of
integration, prior to the perceptual fusion.
In a similar paradigm, Nahorna et al. (2012) tested whether the strength
of audiovisual fusion depends on the audiovisual coherence of the context.
They let McGurk stimuli be preceded by a phonetic “context” stimulus,
consisting either of a video of a speaker uttering 5-20 syllables (coherent
context), or of the same audio track but coupled with a video of a person
uttering completely unrelated and out-of-sync sentences (incoherent con-
text).
Without varying the target stimulus (a visual /ga/ dubbed with
auditory /ba/) they achieved a reduction of fusion responses by half for tar-
gets following an incoherent context stimulus compared to those presented
in a coherent context. The authors suggest that this striking eﬀect is caused
by an “unbinding” of the auditory and visual streams due to the perceived
low coherence between auditory and visual signals in the incoherent con-
text stimulus. Conversely, a coherent context stimulus is suggested to cause
a “rebinding” of the auditory and visual sensory streams. In a follow-up
study, (Nahorna et al. 2015) showed that this unbinding eﬀect can be trig-
gered by an incoherent context stimulus as short as one syllable, and that
the reduction in fusion responses persists, although not as dramatic, if the
audio and video components of incoherent context stimulus only diﬀer in
their phonetic content but not in their timing.
In light of the evidence of the variability of audiovisual integration, both
between diﬀerent contexts of presentation and between individuals, there is
a noticeable lack of insight into the implications for models of audiovisual
speech perception. One may ask, ﬁrstly, if the inter-individual variability
of audiovisual integration can be explained by the MLE model proposed by
Andersen (2010), or if the weighting of auditory and visual estimates are
inﬂuenced by other factors. In Bayesian terms, while the MLE estimates
is statistically optimal with respect to a uniform prior (i.e. no top-down
5

prior knowledge shapes the perceptual process), it is likely that priors are
non-uniform (Ernst & B¨ulthoﬀ2004). This question could be addressed by
comparing the MLE model ﬁt to a weighted model in which the sensory
weights wa and wv in equation (1) do not depend on the sensory reliabilities
but are instead free parameters (cf. Andersen, 2015).
A second question is whether the current models successfully capture
the intra-subject variance in integration suggested by the unbinding studies.
Although model comparisons have hitherto favored simple models (Ander-
sen 2010), the complexity of the data used for model comparison has been
low, using a constant intensity of the auditory and visual stimuli and not
accounting for attentional or contextual factors. This approach generally
favors simple models (such as MLE) over more complex models such as the
weighted model (Andersen 2010). Using a more complex set of data which
better reﬂects the variability of the conditions under which audiovisual inte-
gration takes place could shed more light on which models oﬀer more insight
into the mechanism of integration.
The current study addresses these two questions by comparing two mod-
els with a continuous internal representation, MLE and the weighted model,
on two diﬀerent sets of data. First, both models are ﬁtted to a “standard”
dataset similar to previously used paradigms to investigate whether inter-
individual diﬀerences in responses to audiovisual stimuli can be attributed
to the relative reliabilities of the auditory and visual signals when the stim-
uli. Secondly, we present a new experimental paradigm manipulating both
the sound intensity of the stimuli and the context of presentation. Manip-
ulation of the sound intensity allows for investigation of whether the MLE
assumption holds for varying levels of stimulus reliabilities or if it is speciﬁc
to the intensities used in standard experiments. Moreover, manipulation of
the phonetic context allows for a comparison of the MLE, which does not
take phonetic context into account, to a weighted model allowing sensory
weights to vary with context. We will demonstrate the new insights such
experimental paradigms could bring to the quantitative study of audiovisual
speech perception by showing modeling results of two pilot subjects.

are presented in detail in section 2.
Thereafter, the standard and pilot
experiments are presented in section 3 and 4, respectively, followed by a
concluding discussion in section 5.

2
Models
Given a stimulus S and two sensory signals A and V , the internal represen-
tation of S provided by the sensory inputs can be described by the likelihood
functions p(A|S) and p(B|S). We use likelihood functions and not determin-
istic variables for the internal representations since the perceptual process is
aﬀected by noise, which causes the internal representation value to ﬂuctuate
with repeated presentations of the same stimulus. Each likelihood function
has a mean, µa and µv respectively; and a precision parameter.
2.1
Cyclical internal representation
In the context of this study, the axis on which the likelihood functions p(A|S)
and p(B|S) are deﬁned is the unit circle, following Andersen & Winther
(2013). This representation is grounded in the physical properties of the stop
consonants used in both of the experiments (the unvoiced stop consonants
P, T and K for the standard experiment and the unvoiced stop consonants
B, G and D for the pilot experiment). Experimentally, the cyclical nature
of the continuum between the stop consonants has been veriﬁed by varying
the frequencies of the second and third formants of the closing phase of the
consonants, each formant representing one dimension. Restricting the 2D
variations to a circle was shown to produce sounds perceived as the three
stop consonants (Summerﬁeld 1979). A practical implication of the cyclical
internal representation for modeling is that although the models are one-
dimensional, each estimate of a sensory signal falls on a point on a circle
rather than on a straight axis.

2.2
Early MLE
The early maximum likelihood model of audiovisual integration of speech
is based on the assumption that the perceiver integrates auditory and vi-
sual speech cues in a statistically optimal fashion with a ﬂat Bayesian prior
(i.e., without any prior bias toward either modality) (Ernst & Banks 2002).
Moreover, according to the early MLE model, integration takes place before
any categorical judgment of the phonetic content of the speech takes place,
so that each unisensory internal representation has a continuous represen-
tation. These two continuous representations, which are assumed to belong
to independent probability distributions, are then combined into an audio-
visual internal representation with a probability distribution equal to the
normalized product of the unisensory distributions.

3
The Standard Experiment
The standard experiment was conducted within the framework of an earlier
study (see Andersen & Winther 2013). The subjects in the experiment were
10 native speakers of Finnish with normal hearing and normal or corrected-
to-normal vision according to self report. They were naive with regards to
the purpose of the experiment.
3.1
Stimuli and procedure
The stimuli were made up of video recordings of a native Finnish speaker ar-
ticulating the Finnish non-words /eke/, /epe/ and /ete/. The set of stimuli
contained 3 auditory-only stimuli (the audio track of each video recording),
3 visual stimuli (a silent version of each video) and all the 9 possible combi-
nations of the auditory and visual stimuli. In the auditory-only condition,
a still face of the talker with their mouth closed was shown instead of a
video. Incongruent audiovisual stimuli were created by dubbing the audio
track onto the video, aligning the bursts of the stop consonants in time with
the original audio track. Each stimulus was presented 20 times to the ob-
server in randomized order. The sound level was approximately 50 dB(A)
in background noise of about 30 dB(A) from computer ventilation.
The observers responded by typing any set of one or two consonants a
standard computer keyboard.
Before further analysis, the data was pre-
processed by re-labeling double consonants (eg. “PP”) as single, classifying
the voiced stop consonants G, B and D as their unvoiced counterparts (K, P
and T, respectively), and disregarding the order of consonant pairs (letting
“KP” and “PK” belong to the same class). After this pre-processing, over
99% of responses fell within the categories K, PK, P, PT, T and TK. The
remaining responses were excluded from further analysis.
3.2
Modeling
The MLE model outlined in section 2.2 was implemented for the standard
experiment by assigning the free parameters µa,i, µv,i for i = 1, 2, 3, denoting
the mean for the probability distribution of the internal representation of
each auditory and visual stimulus component, σ2

ﬁxed response boundaries (ρ6 = 2π being held ﬁxed). This implementation
of the MLE model thus has 13 free parameters for each subject.
In addition to the 13 parameters used in the MLE, the weighted model

3.3
Results and preliminary discussion
The mean RMSE was found to be 0.0142 for the weighted model and 0.0153
for MLE. Both models thus ﬁtted the data well, compared to the expectation
value of 0.13 for the RMSE based on the multinomial distribution Andersen
& Winther (2013). Although the weighted model yields a slightly lower mean
RMSE than MLE, it cannot be concluded that it is signiﬁcantly better than
the MLE since the inclusion of en extra model parameter makes the weighted
model more ﬂexible. On a subject level the two models also yield very similar
ﬁts. Ranking subjects by their RMSE results in identical lists for MLE and
the weighted model – thus, it seems that there is no particular group of the
subjects for which the models give substantially diﬀerent predictions.
Thus, for the standard dataset, we do not ﬁnd substantial support for the
claim that the mechanism of weighting the auditory and visual components
is not directly proportional to the reliabilities of the unisensory estimates.
On the other hand, limitations in the experimental design prevents us from
concluding that the MLE provides a good description of the mechanism of
perceptual fusion. Firstly, in light of the evidence for intra-subject variability
of audiovisual integration one may ask whether weighting varies during the
course of the experiment, as a function of the coherence of the audiovisual
context. That is, could the weighting vary as a function of the preceding
trial being congruent or incongruent? Moreover, even if the MLE weighting
may be relatively successful in describing the integration mechanism for this
particular level of auditory and visual reliability, this does not necessarily
imply that the relationship wa = ra/(ra + rv) holds for diﬀerent choices
of ra. The standard dataset cannot answer these concerns – thus, a new
experimental paradigm is needed in order to test the assumptions of MLE
and weighted model against each other.

4
The Pilot Experiment
In response to the limitations of the standard experimental paradigms out-
lined in the previous section, we propose a novel experimental design al-
lowing for a more thorough comparison of the MLE and weighted models.
Building on the result by Nahorna et al. (2015) that unbinding and rebind-
ing of the auditory and visual streams can be caused by a single-syllable
context stimulus, we devise an experiment in which each stimulus presen-
tation is viewed as a context stimulus for the directly following stimulus.
Thus, responses to a certain stimulus are analyzed separately according to
whether the directly preceding stimulus (hereafter referred to as the con-
text) was congruent or incongruent. This allows for a comparison of the
MLE (assuming that integration is automatic) and an implementation of
the weighted model each context condition is assigned a separate set of sen-
sory weights (i.e. weights may vary as a function of the phonetic context).
In the light of the unbinding eﬀects seen in earlier studies (Nahorna et al.
2012, 2015), we hypothesize that this design will favor the weighted model
over MLE.
Additionally, the proposed experiment manipulates the relative reliabil-
ity of the sensory signals by presenting auditory and audiovisual stimuli
with three diﬀerent sound intensities.
This allows for comparison of the
MLE and weighted models on more than one level of sensory precision. We
hypothesize that if weighting cannot be described as the normalized sensory
reliabilities for all sound intensities, the weighted model would outperform
the MLE.
In order to demonstrate this concept, a pilot experiment with two sub-
jects will be presented in this section. Behavioral responses will be reported
along with modeling results of the MLE and two diﬀerent implementations
of the weighted model.
4.1
Stimuli and procedure
The stimuli consisted of video recordings of a native Danish speaker uttering
the Danish non-words /aba/, /aga/ and /ada/. They were presented in an
auditory-only condition, displaying a still face of the speaker with her mouth
closed; a visual condition with a silent version of the videos, and audiovisual
stimuli with congruent and incongruent components. All three congruent
combinations of the auditory and visual stimulus components were included,
along with three out of the six possible incongruent audiovisual stimuli (au-
ditory /aba/ with visual /aga/, auditory /aga/ with visual /aba/ and au-

three kinds of audiovisual illusions described in the Introduction: auditory
/aga/ and visual /ada/ leading to the fusion percept /ada/, auditory /aba/
and visual /aga/ leading to the combination percept /abga/, and auditory
/aga/ with visual /ada/ leading to the visual capture illusion /ada/. The
reason for only using three incongruent stimuli was that, in order to balance
the sequence of the stimulus delivery so that each stimulus was preceded
by an incongruent trial and a congruent trial an equal amount of times, an
equal number of congruent and incongruent trials were needed.
The stimuli were presented on a MacBook™pro laptop computer and a
pair of Genelec™6010A active speakers. The sound output for the three in-
tensity levels was approximately 56 dB(A), 48 dB(A) and a nearly inaudible
intensity which could not be reliably measured due to background computer
and ventilation noise. Each audiovisual stimulus was presented under (2
contexts)·(3 sound intensities)= 6 diﬀerent conditions, and there were 20
repetitions of each stimulus under each condition.
The experiment session consisted of a visual-only block of 20 repetitions
of each visual-only stimulus followed by an auditory-only block where each
(stimulus, sound intensity) combination was presented 20 times, both with
randomized order of delivery. Thereafter followed the audiovisual block with
20 repetitions of each (stimulus, context, sound intensity) combination.
In order to ensure that the audiovisual stimuli were presented in a con-
gruent and incongruent context an equal amount of times, the order of trial
delivery was restricted to alternate between showing sequences of congruent
and incongruent stimuli. These sequences consisted of one, two or three
stimuli from the same congruency class, and the order of delivering the se-
quences was randomized. An illustration of a possible trial delivery sequence
ordered according to this principle is found in Figure 1.

should report what they saw. Although the order of stimulus delivery was
not fully randomized in the audiovisual block, the subjects did not report
that they perceived it as deterministic.
4.2
Modeling
The MLE model was implemented according to the description in section 2.2,
with a mean µa,i, µv,i, i = 1, 2, 3 for each auditory and visual stimulus, the
visual sensory variance σ2
v and an auditory variance parameter for each
sound intensity, σ2
a,j for j = 1, 2, 3. Moreover, the three non-ﬁxed response
boundaries ρi, i = 1, 2, 3 were parameters in the model. In sum, this MLE
implementation has 13 free parameters.
In addition to sharing the 13 parameters used in the MLE model, the two
implementations of the weighted model each have a set of sensory weights.
In the 3 weights implementation, a weight is assigned for each sound inten-
sity level. In the 6 weights implementation, a weight is assigned for each
(sound intensity, context) combination – thus, the 3 weights implementation
is very similar to the MLE but allows for weights to deviate from the MLE
assumption, whereas the 6 weights model additionally allow weighting to
vary according to the phonetic context.

Subject 2: High weight to the visual modality
Subject 2 has a much higher classiﬁcation accuracy on the lipreading task
than Subject 1, and is in that sense more “typical” – subjects with normal
vision and no impairments in speech comprehension typically do not have
great diﬃculties with this type of task (Andersen & Starrfelt 2015). More-

illusions (fusion or combination) or a visual capture – thus, virtually no
responses corresponded to the audio track of the incongruent videos. This
subject would thus make a good candidate for Schwartz’s (2010) “visual
subjects” group.

Discussion
In the current study, we have investigated whether the inter- and intra-
subject variability of audiovisual integration in speech perception can be de-
scribed quantitatively as a variability in the weighting of auditory and visual
sensory information. Two quantitative models of audiovisual speech percep-
tion – the maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) model and the weighted
model – have been applied to two diﬀerent behavioral experiments, a “stan-
dard” experiment where the conditions of presentation are assumed to be
constant and a pilot study where the sound intensity and phonetic context
are manipulated.
Both for the standard experiment and the pilot study, model errors as
indicated by RMSE were very similar between models. In the case of the
standard experiment, the RMSE measures were very low, indicating that
both models ﬁtted the data very well. For the pilot study, on the other
hand, error rates were higher. This is not surprising, given a similar model
complexity and the comparatively high number of response probabilities to
model (48 stimuli· 4 response categories = 192, compared to the standard
dataset of 15 stimuli · 6 response categories = 90).
In the pilot study, the errors produced by the models were both quantita-
tively and qualitatively similar, although a tendency toward overestimating
the precision of the visual modality in the weighted implementations should
be noted. In essence, the similarity is not surprising since the models are
very similar to one another – they are both based on a continuous inter-
nal representation on a cyclical continuum, and audiovisual responses are
modeled as a weighted combination of unimodal sensory estimates. In all
model implementations, means and precision parameters of the unisensory
estimates are associated with the responses to audio-only and visual-only
stimuli. The failure of the models to account for the integration eﬀects in
Subject 1 of the pilot experiment suggests that this approach may need to be
reconsidered. The low performance of Subject 1 in the pure lipreading task
would imply that the beneﬁts of integrating this estimate with the auditory
sensory signal is low, contrary to the substantial observed eﬀects. Thus, in
line with Bejjanki et al. (2011), we note that the visual-only task seems to be
a poor indicator of the visual sensory precision in the audiovisual condition.
This observed dissociation between visual and audiovisual speech may sug-
gest that conscious lipreading is processed separately from the pathway that
interacts with auditory sensory processing, a proposition previously made
by Andersen & Starrfelt (2015). Thus, alternative approaches to quantifying
the visual sensory precision should be explored in future modeling studies.
22
==PAGE:24==
Manipulating the phonetic context for audiovisual stimuli by using the
preceding trial as a context stimulus had marginal eﬀects on responses in
the pilot study. This should not be regarded as an argument against within-
subject variability of audiovisual integration; rather, it may indicate that a
short stimulus presentation followed by a response collection when the sub-
ject has to type their response on a keyboard may not suﬃce as a phonetic
context for the subsequent stimulus. This conﬁrms the result by Nahorna
et al. (2015), who did not ﬁnd a signiﬁcant eﬀect of the previous stimulus on
audiovisual integration when a behavioral response was collected between
context and target stimulus presentations. The authors suggested that the
act of phonetic judgment prompted by the response collection may “reset”
the degree of audiovisual binding to a default state. Thus, before a more
comprehensive study is devised to test the eﬀects of phonetic context on
sensory weighting, one may want to consider changing the experimental de-
sign.
The comparison of the goodness-of-ﬁt of the MLE and weighted models
as indicated by the mean RMSE rendered inconclusive results. Given that
the weighted model has more free parameters than the MLE, and that the
mean error rate decreases with increasing model complexity, small diﬀer-
ences in favor of the weighted model might simply be an eﬀect of increased
model complexity. In the standard experiment, the weighted model had a
slightly lower error measure than MLE, but if the error measures are cor-
rected for the number of free parameters this diﬀerence is annihilated. In
the pilot study the preferred model diﬀered between subjects, with the 6
weights model being favored for Subject 1 and MLE favored for Subject 2.
Moreover, although error rates were similar between models, the sensory
weights (summarized in tables 1 and 2) diﬀered considerably between mod-
els in some cases. This suggests that there exist multiple local minima in the
error function which yield similar error values. Therefore, a more relevant
indicator of goodness-of-ﬁt than the RMSE at one of these individual local
minima would be the behavior of the models in the whole space of viable
parameter values. Thus, using a more sophisticated model comparison cri-
terion such as the Bayes factor (Schwartz 2006) could provide more insight
into which model better describes the data.
In conclusion, although the modeling results of the current study are
inconclusive, they provide several valuable insights to the ﬁeld of quantita-
tive modeling of audiovisual speech perception. Firstly, given the observed
dissociation between visual and audiovisual speech perception, the possibil-
ity of ﬁnding new measures of visual sensory precision should be explored.
Secondly, with regards to the lack of eﬀect of phonetic context on the over-

all behavioral results, future modeling experiments should exploit the more
reliable context stimuli found in earlier studies, rather than using the pre-
ceding trial as a context stimulus. Thirdly, given the very similar root-mean
squared error of the respective models, alternative approaches to model com-
parison, such as for instance the Bayesian model selection criterion, should
be employed.
