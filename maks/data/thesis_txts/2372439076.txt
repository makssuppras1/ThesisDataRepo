Abstract
Water temperatures have been shown to be a good indicator of microbiological water quality
in drinking water distribution systems (DWDS). The World Health Organization recommends
that the temperature of drinking water does not exceed 25 ◦C at the tap, while the Danish
recommendation is that the water temperature should not be higher than 12 ◦C at the tap.
Temperatures are thoroughly monitored at the water works, but seldom monitored in the
DWDS. A tool is therefore needed for assessing temperatures throughout the network in order
to better identify areas of concern with regards to complying with regulations and maintaining
a good water quality. This thesis tries to set up such a model, based on an analysis of three
years of high-frequency ﬂow, pressure and temperature data from a danish utility network.
Three years of data collected on a minutely basis from the Nordvand utility area north of
Copenhagen is processed and analyzed with focus on ﬁnding spatial and temporal patterns
in temperatures. Temperature and ﬂow data is compared to see if any correlations exist. A
hydraulic model of the Nordvand utility area in Aquis is converted to EPANET. Complications
with adapting object controls following the model conversion meant, that the Nordvand model
had to be simpliﬁed to only cover water distribution to the district metered areas (DMA).
Flow measurements were implemented into the model, to get the hydraulics resemble reality
as much as possible. Using EPANET MSX a module was added to the hydraulic model, en-
abling modelling of temperatures throughout the network.
The analysis of the raw meter data showed clear temporal patterns in network temperatures,
showing both seasonal and daily variations. Water is cooled in the network in winter and
heated in summer. The changes in temperature show some correlation with the distance from
the source, but is in fact mostly dependant on residence time in the network. No signiﬁcant
seasonal changes in ﬂow are found, dismissing a seasonal correlation between ﬂow and tem-
perature. The measured ﬂow in the logging stations however shown a clear resemblance with
temperatures, where the largest change in temperatures are seen when ﬂow is low.
The EPANET MSX temperature model, showed an ability to estimate correct temperature
levels in most logging stations, based on global parameters for soil temperature, water work
eﬄuent temperatures and thermal conductivities of soil and pipes. The model is also able
to describe developments in temperature following changes in water sources. The model has
a limited ability to describe daily variations in temperature, as it tends to underestimate them.
The model created has the potential for being used for assessing which parts of a DWDS
are most likely to experience high temperatures as well as when.
It could furthermore be
used for estimating temperatures following changes in sources, relevant for detecting anoma-
lous events not necessarily resulting in ﬂow changes. To reach the full potential of a network
temperature model, being able to model temperatures from the water work all the way to the
consumer, the hydraulic model setup should be expanded and input parameters allowed to be
deﬁned locally and with temporal variations.

Chapter 1 - Introduction
1
Introduction
The World Health Organization recommends that the temperature of drinking water does not
exceed 25 ◦C at the tap (World Health Organization, 2017), while in Denmark, the recommen-
dation is that the water temperature should not be higher than 12 ◦C at the tap (Miljø- og
Fødevareministeriet, 2014). While temperatures are carefully monitored at the water works,
they are only measured few places in the main pipes of some drinking water distribution sys-
tems (DWDS). At the water works temperatures are usually on-line measurements providing
the central monitoring system with a live indicator of the current state of the water, but are
seldom used for later analysis. The same is seen where temperatures are measured in the
DWDS.
A major focus within modeling of DWDS is on pressure and ﬂow for use in detection of
leakages, as a drop in pressure without corresponding increase in consumption indicates a
burst or fraction of the distribution pipes (Puust et al., 2010; Loureiro et al., 2016a). This
method is diﬃcult to use in low pressure pipes, in which case temperature monitoring might
be an alternative.
The present literature is mainly focused on thermodynamics and heat transfer from atmo-
sphere to soil and pipes, while little attention has been on the temporal and spatial variation
of temperature in the DWDS and its eﬀect on the water quality. No articles have been pub-
lished on Danish studies of temperature modelling, although a master thesis was done in 2015
(Hubeck-Graudal, 2015) within the subject, trying to assess the potential for delivering energy
from the DWDS to the district heating system using heat pumps. A similar study has been
carried out by De Pasquale et al. (2017), trying to model temperature in the DWDS in order
to assess whether it is economically and environmentally proﬁtable to recover heat from the
drinking water to the district heating system. The temporal variation in water temperatures
over the day, week and seasons has not yet been analyzed and compared to the well described
variations in ﬂow and consumption (Marriott, 2007; Shang et al., 2008b; Vertommen et al.,
2012).
Only few studies have tried to model temperature in the DWDS. Blokker and Pieterse-Quirijns
(2013) set up a model to predict temperatures of the water in a real distribution network, tak-
ing conditions such as soil type, pavement type and weather data into consideration. They
found that the downstream drinking water temperature very much depends on the soil tem-
perature at the pipe depth, due to a longer residence time than heating time. Moerman et al.
(2014) elaborated on this model by including the ﬁnal step in the distribution system; the
domestic water supply, enabling modelling of the water temperature all the way to the tap.
It was found that the water temperature at the tap depends more on the residence time than
on the ambient temperature and that the water temperature changes signiﬁcantly within the
domestic water supply. Agudelo-Vera et al. (2014) also elaborated on the model developed by
Blokker and Pieterse-Quirijns (2013) by evaluating the possibility to use weather forecast data
to predict soil and water temperature. Predicting and validating the temperatures could serve
as an “early warning” system for indicating when water temperatures in the DWDS might
exceed the limits.

1.1 - Aim of thesis
Many studies have been conducted and published within water quality monitoring and control.
Prest et al. (2016b) studied the temporal dynamics in water quality for a full-scale DWDS.
They found that large seasonal variations in water quality occurs in the DWDS with bacterial
growth being very dependent on water temperature. Liu et al. (2016) studied the growth of
bioﬁlms in the DWDS, their eﬀect on the water quality and the possibilities to monitor and
control it. Amongst others it was found that pipe materials, ﬂow rates, temperature and pH
aﬀect all stages of the bioﬁlm growth. Blokker et al. (2008) studied the inﬂuence of demands
on water quality modelling and the relation between water quality models and hydraulic net-
work models. They found that a hydraulics based network water quality model validated with
ﬁeld measurements needs to be developed. Thus an evident need for models able to describe
temperature developments in the distribution systems exists.
1.1
Aim of thesis
The aim of this thesis is to analyze high frequency meter data, with focus on temperature,
to see if any temporal and spatial patterns and tendencies exist and furthermore to see how
variations in ﬂow aﬀect the temperature in the network. This should provide a better under-
standing of the network temperature processes enabling the development of a model able to
estimate temperature variations in a DWDS. The Nordvand DWDS is used as case study as
they have a long record of high frequency temperature measurements in several locations in
their network, consisting of minutely data recorded over three years, from 2014 to 2016. An
existing Aquis model of the system is used as basis for developing a temperature model in
EPANET.
First the temporal variations in measured water temperatures are compared to the varia-
tions in drinking water consumption (and thus ﬂow velocity and retention time), to see if there
is a temporal relation between temperature and consumption. It also assesses which ambient
parameters inﬂuence the water temperature.
Secondly the already existing model of the Nordvand distribution area north of Copenhagen
is converted to EPANET, where the hydraulics are veriﬁed by comparing them to the origi-
nal Aquis model as well as to actual measurements from the system. When a hydraulically
satisfying model is set up in EPANET, the EPANET-MSX extension package (through MAT-
LAB) is used to facilitate modelling of temperature in the network. Temperatures are modelled
depending on eﬄuent water temperatures at the water works, soil conditions and pipe material.
The temperature model is optimized and validated using temperature measurements from
the Nordvand area. The model results are compared to the network measurements and the ﬁt
of the model is evaluated. The inﬂuence of the input parameters is analyzed and an assessment
of the parameters is made in order to be able to optimize the model performance.

Background
2.1
Demand and consumption
A hydraulic model simulates the behaviour of water in a network based on physical parameters
such as elevations and pipe dimensions, but is also very much dependant on water production
and consumption volumes. Especially the consumption volumes determine how much water
ﬂows in the system at a given point in time, as the production is often buﬀered by a storage
facility - usually a reservoir at the water work or a water tower (Winther et al., 2005). The
consumption volumes and thus the network ﬂow varies in time and space depending on the
diﬀerent types of consumers connected to the distribution system and their demand.
2.1.1
Importance of demand patterns
Knowledge on the daily water demand of a distribution system is essential to the utility com-
panies (Alves et al., 2014). In order to ensure suﬃcient production of drinking water and to
avoid periods of shortage in supply, they need to be able to estimate the daily water demand
(Winther et al., 2005). Usually the water demand does not vary much from year to year,
but does vary on a seasonal, weekly and daily basis (Loureiro et al., 2016b). A diﬀerence
between weekdays and weekends is also seen (Kirstein, 2016a). Furthermore water demands
are generally higher during summer, as more water is used for gardening and other outdoor
activities and since people tend to use more water for personal hygiene e.g.
baths, when
temperatures are higher (Blokker and Schaap, 2015). Especially for residential consumers the
demand is higher in summer. Some institutions and oﬃces have lower demands due to vacation.
In order to dimension a distribution system properly more speciﬁc information on demands
is needed (Sanz et al., 2016). The production volume at the water works is usually constant
over the day while the consumer demand is not. Therefore storage facilities are needed to
store water in periods of low demand and to supply water in periods of high demand. This
storage capacity is usually provided as clean water tanks or basins at the water works or as
water towers placed more locally in the distribution area (Winther et al., 2005). To optimize
the water distribution with regards to pressure and ﬂow, information on diurnal patterns in
demand is needed (Vertommen et al., 2012). This is e.g. used when designing water storage
facilities to secure suﬃcient water for distribution at all times.
The daily and weekly variations in demands are applied in the model using demand pat-
terns. The demand patterns are hourly factors multiplied to the base demand of a consumer.
The base demand represents the mean demand of the consumer and thus the hourly factors
indicate how large a fraction of the base demand is requested by the consumer at a given time.
2.1.2
Demand pattern variation
The demand patterns vary depending on the type of consumers it represents and thus diﬀerent
demand patterns are created for diﬀerent types of consumers. For most consumers the demand
is signiﬁcantly higher during the day than at night, but depending on the consumer type, the

2.1.3
Water balance
A water balance of the system can be used to assess the amount of water lost to leaks and
unmetered consumption or to indicate that measurements might be oﬀor missing. The water
balance is the diﬀerence between network in- and outﬂow. An excess of water in the system
(inﬂow > outﬂow) can be caused by several things such as leaks, pipe bursts, unmetered
consumption and of course measurement errors. If leaks occur, the loss would be expected
to be highest at night, when pressures are highest due to low consumption.
Likewise loss
due to pipe bursts is also expected to peak during the night. The burst itself might be seen
as a sudden change in the water balance. If unmetered consumption dominates, the loss is
expected to be highest during the day where consumption peaks. Measurement errors are
harder to detect, as they might be proportional to the consumption and thus be diﬃcult to
diﬀer from unmetered consumption (Sanz et al., 2016). If the water balance shows a deﬁcit
of water it is most likely due to measurement errors in either in- or outﬂow as an unmetered
inﬂow or intrusion is unlikely due to the pipes being pressurized.
2.2
Water temperature
Water temperature is an important determinant for water quality as temperature aﬀects both
microbial, chemical and physical processes in the distribution network (Agudelo-Vera et al.,
2014). Temperature is an important factor in bacterial growth and formation of bio-ﬁlms (van
Summeren et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2016; Prest et al., 2016a; Liu et al., 2013). Temperature
also aﬀects chemical processes in the network e.g. absorption of chemicals and the decay of
disinfectants such as chlorine (Powell et al., 2000).
2.2.1
Factors aﬀecting water temperature
The water temperature depends on processes in several steps of the water distribution, from
the extraction of groundwater, through the water work and DWDS, to the point-of-use e.g.
6
==PAGE:21==
Chapter 2 - Background
at the tap of the consumer. In this report the circumstances aﬀecting water temperatures
at the water work e.g groundwater temperatures and treatment processes are not considered.
The eﬄuent temperature at the water work is simply used as an input. Neither is changes in
temperature in the domestic distribution system and the factors controlling them considered.
With regards to the ﬁnal temperature at the consumer, this part of the system was shown by
Moerman et al. (2014) to be important though. In this report focus is on the factors aﬀecting
water temperature in the DWDS. These factors are divided into two categories; ambient and
hydrological factors.
2.2.1.1
Ambient factors
The ambient factors aﬀecting the water temperature in the DWDS includes soil temperature,
temperature of the eﬄuent water from water works and pipe material.
The soil temperature itself depends on numerous factors. A close correlation has been shown
between air and soil temperatures including seasonal and to some extend daily variations
(De Pasquale et al., 2017; Agudelo-Vera et al., 2014).
The temperature of the soil is also
inﬂuenced by other weather conditions such as radiation and precipitation, and also by envi-
ronmental conditions such as the thermal conductivity and heat capacity of the soil (Blokker
and Pieterse-Quirijns, 2013). Several models have been created to estimate the soil tempera-
ture depending on the surface (air) temperature with the potential of being included in DWDS
temperature models (Mihalakakou, 2002; De Pasquale et al., 2017).
Eﬄuent temperatures from water works have been shown to have a seasonal variation ranging
from around 10 ◦C in winter to 15 ◦C in summer (Blokker et al., 2013). As processes at the
water work is not included in this study, the sources of these variations will not be adressed
further.
The pipe material also has an inﬂuence on the water temperature due to its importance in
the heat exchange between soil and water (van Summeren et al., 2015). The thermal con-
ductivity of the pipe deﬁnes how heat is transferred between water and the surrounding soil.
The thermal conductivity varies depending on the material. Common pipe materials are PVC,
concrete and cast iron, which have thermal conductivities of 0.42, 1 and 55 W/(m · K) respec-
tively (De Pasquale et al., 2017). Thermal conductivities for soils can be found in Blokker
and Pieterse-Quirijns (2013). The speciﬁc inﬂuence of the thermal conductivity is described
in section 2.2.2.

2.3
Water quality
In the European Union in general and especially in Denmark, strict regulations and directives
are established to ensure the drinking water quality.
2.3.1
Water quality parameters
Water quality is measured on several parameters, which can be categorized into three main
groups (The Council of The European Union, 1998):
• Chemical parameters i.e content of toxins.
• Microbiological parameters i.e. the content of microbes e.g. coliforms.
• Indicator parameters i.e. taste, smell, color, pH etc.
Chemical parameters
The chemical parameters speciﬁes maximum limit values in mg/L or µg/L for a number of
chemicals, heavy metals and other toxins. Pesticides are among the chemicals in this parame-
ter group.
Microbiological parameters
The microbiological parameters consists of cell counts for the most common coliform bacteria
such as Escherichia Coli and Enterococci, where maximum limit values are speciﬁed in num-
bers/volume. The standardized test covers plate count (22 ◦C), presence of Coliform bacteria
(37 ◦C) and presence of E. Coli (Miljøstyrelsen, 2005).
Indicator parameters
The indicator parameters includes a broad spectrum of compounds and physical parameters.
Maximum concentrations are speciﬁed for nutrients, metals and disinfectants e.g. ammonium,
manganese and chlorine along with maximum and minimum values for speciﬁc parameters
such as radioactivity, oxidisability and conductivity. Subjectively assessed parameters such as
odour, smell and taste are also mentioned, however no speciﬁc guidelines are given.

2.3.2
Eﬀect of water temperature
Microbial growth
As brieﬂy mentioned in section 2.2 the microbial growth and the occurrence of bio-ﬁlms in the
DWDS partly depends on the water temperature and as water quality and microbial growth
in the DWDS is related, so is water quality and temperature. Even though microbial growth
might not always result in a poorer water quality, it is usually assumed to be the case (World
Health Organization, 2017). Likewise it is expected that the microbial growth increases with
increasing temperatures (Liu et al., 2016; Lehtola et al., 2004). Therefore it is also expected
that the water quality deteriorates as temperatures rise due to poorer microbiological state of
the water. It is recommended by the WHO that drinking water temperatures do not exceed
25 ◦C at the point-of-use, while in Denmark the the ministry of Environment and Food recom-
mends that it does not exceed 12 ◦C at point-of-use (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2014). The
lower danish recommendations are mainly due to disinfectants such as chlorine not commonly
being used in the danish DWDS.
Disinfectants and other chemicals
Besides directly stimulating the growth of microbes, increasing temperatures can also indirectly
aﬀect the microbial growth by increasing the decay rates of disinfectants in the DWDS (if dis-
infectants are used). In general the disinfectants are more active and thus eﬀective against
organisms when temperatures are higher, but this also means that the deterioration rate in-
creases (Powell et al., 2000). Common disinfectants such as chlorine and mono-chloramine
are well know to be required in larger amounts as temperatures increases due to the more
rapid depletion in order to avoid reappearance of microorganisms in the DWDS (Sadiq and
Rodriguez, 2004; Richardson, 2003). The by-products may also pose a risk to human health,
which could likewise be considered a deterioration of the water quality (Powell et al., 2000).
Increased temperatures can also aﬀect the degradation of other chemicals, which might aﬀect
water quality with regards to chemical parameters and by-products.

4
Software
4.1
Aquis
4.1.1
Background
Aquis is a hydraulic modelling tool developed and distributed by Schneider Electric (Schneider
Electric, 2015). Aquis is able to simulate ﬂow velocity and pressure using real time data for the
monitoring and analysis of a given situation. The software can be used to show and analyze
previously recorded pressure and ﬂow data, but also to simulate and estimate the behaviour
of the distribution network in future anomalous situations e.g. if a ﬁre hydrant is turned on
or a pipe is closed.
Aquis is used in water distribution companies to monitor and manage distribution systems.
The SCADA operation system enables the operator to estimate the behavior of the distribution
network and anticipate the impact of planned and unplanned events. As Aquis can take real
time data into consideration the software is able to consider the ever changing condition of the
network.
4.1.2
Use of Aquis
Aquis is used in this project for running the hydraulic model of the Nordvand distribution area
build by Niras A/S. The model is used to get initial estimations of the size and consumption
of the network, for assessing water ages and sources as well as for getting a general overview of
the system. The model also contains information on pipe materials and source controls used
when analyzing the network.
No actual modelling for this project wass performed in Aquis, as the model wass converted to
EPANET in order to enable temperature modelling. Furthermore the Aquis model is heavy to
run due to the many analysis possibilities resulting in long computation times and hence only
some preliminary basic hydraulic modelling was done in Aquis.
4.2
EPANET
4.2.1
Background
The EPANET software enables modelling of water distribution piping systems. It is developed
and maintained by the US environmental protection agency (EPA) (Rossman, 2000). EPANET
is a public domain software and can therefore be freely copied and distributed.
EPANET
performs extended period simulations of the water movement and quality behavior within
pressurized pipe networks. The EPANET networks consists of pipes, nodes (junctions), pumps,
valves, storage tanks and reservoirs. EPANET tracks:
• The ﬂow of water in each pipe.
• The pressure at each node.
21
==PAGE:36==
4.3 - EPANET-MSX
• The height of the water in each tank.
• The type of chemical concentration throughout the network during a simulation period.
• Water age.
• Water source.
• Tracing of compounds.
4.2.2
Use of EPANET
The main reason for using EPANET is the MSX package enabling modelling of temperature.
This is further described in section 4.3 below. EPANET however have additional advantages.
As the analysis possibilities in EPANET are more limited compared to Aquis, the computa-
tion time is signiﬁcantly lower. Furthermore EPANET ﬁles are also easily accessible through
Microsoft Notepad, MATLAB and R. This makes it possible to alter the input ﬁles e.g. the
applied patterns, without opening the EPANET software.
4.3
EPANET-MSX
The Multi Species eXtension (MSX) package for EPANET is, like EPANET, developed and
maintained by the US environmental protection agency (EPA) and like EPANET it is open
source. The extension package was initially created to enable modelling of complex reaction
schemes between multiple chemical and biological species in both the bulk ﬂow and at the pipe
wall in EPANET (Shang et al., 2008a).
The EPANET-MSX requires an input ﬁle specifying the mathematical expressions that gov-
ern the reaction dynamics of the system being studied, allowing users the ﬂexibility to model
a wide range of reactions and processes, both chemical and physical (Eliades and Kyriakou,
2014). Thus it is also possible to model water temperature based on thermodynamic expres-
sions describing the heat exchange between the drinking water and the ambient medium (soil)
through the pipe wall. The governing equations are described in section 2.2.2 while the input
ﬁle setup is described in section 5.3.
The software is run through MATLAB, where two input ﬁles are required.
One .inp-ﬁle
containing the EPANET model and one .msx-ﬁle describing the MSX modelling inputs. Con-
stants, variables and starting conditions are deﬁned in the .msx-ﬁle.
Furthermore eﬄuent
concentrations (or in this case temperatures) can be deﬁned in speciﬁc nodes and reservoirs
(sources) and rate expressions can be applied to pipes and tanks. The simulation and results
can be called through simple MATLAB commands as shown in Eliades and Kyriakou (2014),
and thus they are also easily analyzed and plotted in MATLAB.

5.1
Processing of raw meter data
In this section the processing of the logged data from the Nordvand network is described.
5.1.1
The raw meter data from Nordvand
The data used in this study is high frequency measurements of ﬂow, pressure and temperature
logged in the Nordvand distribution network. It is common to monitor ﬂow, pressure and
temperature in DWDSs, but while ﬂow and pressure are very commonly logged and used for
later analysis and modelling, temperature is not (Kirstein, 2016b). This is also seen in the
Nordvand system where ﬂow and pressure is measured in more than 45 places while tempera-
ture is only measured in eight. This is not because it is diﬃcult to measure temperature, but
simply because it is not used for analysis of the network in the same extent as ﬂow and pressure.
The raw meter data, which can be seen plotted in appendix B.1, contains some errors and
irregularities. The errors are mainly zero measurements, meaning time-slots where the tem-
perature is measured as being 0. Furthermore some negative temperatures measurements exist.
Zero and negative values have been removed from the temperature data sets. The zero and
negative measurements are considered erroneous and removed since it is assumed that such
values will not occur in the DWDS as this would mean that the water is freezing.
Irregularities in the data are mainly seen as shorter periods of time where the temperature
increases signiﬁcantly. This is especially seen at Vesterbyvej PS (ﬁgure B.8 in appendix B.1).
The irregularities have not been removed from the data set, as they might not be measuring
errors but caused by disturbances in the network e.g. maintenance work causing changes to
the ﬂow. Irregularities as well as zero and negative measurements are also seen in the ﬂow
data (see appendix B). However, with regards to ﬂow these are expected, as e.g. closing of a
pipe might cause the ﬂow to change signiﬁcantly. Therefore no pre-processing of the ﬂow data
is conducted.

temperature measurements from the danish meteorological institute (DMI) as well as water
work eﬄuent temperatures from the Geus Jupiter database (Danmarks Meteorologiske Insti-
tut, 2016; Geus, 2017a). The DMI data on soil is monthly averages of the years 2001-2010, the

Annual variations

Daily and monthly variations

analysis described above.

5.2.2
Converting the Nordvand model from Aquis to EPANET
As brieﬂy described in the software chapter (4) there is a diﬀerence in complexity of the two
modelling programs.
Aquis oﬀers a lot of analysis possibilities including sectioning of the
network, more advanced control options for both pumps, valves and reservoirs and numer-
ous possibilities for creating customized features such as theme maps, GIS layers, simulation
scenarios, speciﬁc analysis areas, maintenance plans etc. Many of the features applied in the
Aquis model will simply be lost in the conversion process as they do not exist in EPANET.
The diﬀerent possibilities of control options in Aquis are one of the major challenges of con-
verting the Aquis model to EPANET, especially with regards to pump controls. Three pumps
are included in the Nordvand model: At the inﬂow to Jægersborg WT, at the outﬂow of
Jægersborg WT and at Vesterbyvej PS controlling the ﬂow between the Gentofte and Glad-
saxe sections. The three pumps have diﬀerent pumping strategies implemented. The pump
upstream Jægersborg WT is controlled by a pump curve, specifying a ﬂow/head relationship
i.e. the more head is required, the less water is pumped. EPANET also has this control feature
and thus no problems occur in the conversion between the two models. The pump downstream
Jægersborg WT however, is controlled depending on the pressure in the downstream node.
The pump is set to maintain a speciﬁc pressure in the node at all times, meaning that at some
points the pumps has to pump water from the node towards the water tower. Such a reverse
pump control does not exist in EPANET and thus the control of this pump fails in EPANET
after the conversion. The pump control in the third pump at Vesterbyvej is also controlled
depending on the pressure in a node. In this case the node is located further downstream in
the system. As such a control type does not exist in EPANET either, this control also fails
in the conversion. The missing pump control conversions pops up as an error message during
the conversion, notifying the modeller of this problem. To solve the pump control problems,
it might be possible to apply some kind of rule-based control in EPANET, but it has not been
done successfully in this project. The pumps can be controlled depending on the pressure in
a downstream node, but it seems that a two way pump capable of producing a reverse ﬂow,
is not a possibility. Several solutions such as two pumps in two parallel pipes, one going each
way and rule based controlled valves, have been implemented without successfully solving this
problem.
Another major conversion issue has been the control of the eﬄuent ﬂow from the water works.
In Aquis the water works eﬄuent ﬂow can be controlled either by a deﬁned head or by a deﬁned
ﬂow. In the Nordvand model all water works are controlled by a constant ﬂow. This is not a
possibility in EPANET, where the water works (reservoirs) can only be controlled by head. A

pattern can optionally be applied to the head to allow predeﬁned variations as it is done for
the consumer demands, but variations in ﬂow are inevitable. As described in the EPANET
Users manual (Rossman, 2000) this can be solved by replacing the reservoirs with nodes with
negative demands. This allows the deﬁnition of a constant ﬂow along with a variation in head.
Replacing all reservoirs with nodes in the EPANET model will however most likely results in
some complications. If this i done, it requires that the inﬂow equals the outﬂow exactly, as wa-
ter otherwise will accumulate or the system will be drained, due to no ﬂexibility in the system
(besides the small capacity that the water tower provides). If the water balance is not 0, then
the system head will reach unrealistic values and in the end fail. Aquis is more ﬂexible with
regards to this problem as consumptions are automatically scaled depending on the amount of
water in the system (Schneider Electric, 2015). Therefore the EPANET network has to have
at least one reservoir to buﬀer the system or alternatively an exceptionally large tank has to
be implemented. Both solutions can prevent the system in failing, but will inevitably alter the
system hydraulics to some degree.

5.2.3
Simplifying the Nordvand model
The two major problems with pump control and water work production mentioned in the
above, resulted in large complications in getting the hydraulics of the EPANET network to
match the ones in Aquis and thus also to match the logged ﬂow and pressure measurements.
A satisfying solution was never reached and therefore several attempts where made to simplify
the system in order to get a correct hydraulic model. As all temperature measurements are
from the Gentofte section, the ﬁrst simpliﬁcation of the system was to remove the Gladsaxe
section and insert either the ﬂow simulated in Aquis or the measured ﬂow at Vesterbyvej PS in
at the connecting pipe. The ﬁrst option was tested, but complications with the water balance
and pumping controls where still sources of hydraulic errors. The second option was therefore
never tested.
Instead the model was further simpliﬁed by removing everything except the
piping between the measuring locations (inﬂow to DMAs) and the water works. This allowed
the hydraulics of the system to be deﬁned in each outgoing node by inserting the measured
ﬂows in these nodes. This was done by applying demand patterns to the DMA inﬂow nodes
and the water work nodes, using a R-script created by Jonas Kirstein (see appendix C).
A simpliﬁed system with in- and outﬂows deﬁned solely as demands in nodes, turned out
to be a problem. An EPANET model has to have an open node, in which the ﬂow can freely
vary depending on system demands. This is usually provided by a reservoir. In order for a
system without a reservoir to function, the in- and outﬂow would have to match at all times.
The water tower can provide a little buﬀer for this, but not nearly enough in this case. There-
fore having a known ﬂow implemented in all in- and outﬂow connections using only nodes, the
system would either need at reservoir to be implemented somewhere in the system or a water
27
==PAGE:42==
5.2 - Hydraulic model
tower large enough to buﬀer the system.
To evaluate the actual diﬀerences in the system in- and outﬂow, a water balance was made. It
could also be used to check that no sources or consumers were missing in the system. The ﬂow
at the DMAs where summed as the outﬂow, not including the few consumers in the system as
their total base demand was less than 0.01 LPS. The eﬄuent ﬂow of the two waterworks were
summed as the system inﬂow. Calculations were made both excluding and including the ﬂow
measurements at the Hjortekjær WT in order check that it did not aﬀect the water balance
and to see the eﬀect of the water tower as system buﬀer.
Despite water balance revealing only a small diﬀerence between in - and outﬂow, when con-
sidering all of the three years included in the study, the water balance also showed that the
net ﬂow in and out of the system varied a lot over shorter time periods. This meant that the
buﬀer capacity of the water tower was not nearly large enough. Three solutions where tested
to solve this problem:
• Sjælsø as reservoir
The ﬁrst option considered was to leave Sjælsø as a reservoir, hoping that the strictly
deﬁned ﬂow in all other in- and outﬂow points would result in the modelled ﬂows at
Sjælsø matching the measured ones. Not on a minutely base, but over the course of
the day, not altering the hydraulics too much. This was however not the case, as it
resulted in negative ﬂows at Sjælsø, meaning that water left the system. This aﬀected
the hydraulics to a degree that could not be accepted.
• Dyssegård Nord as reservoir
The second option considered was to place a reservoir at the Dyssegård Nord node,
located far from most temperature measurements (except the Jægersborg WT inﬂow).
By doing so hopefully only the one branch of the system ending at Dyssegård Nord would
be aﬀected, not changing the ﬂow in the other branch signiﬁcantly and thus not aﬀecting
the temperature modelling signiﬁcantly either. As with the previous tested solution with
a reservoir at Sjælsø, the reservoir at Dyssegård Nord also experienced negative ﬂows,
which likewise aﬀected the hydraulics in both branches. The hydraulics in the branch
ending at Hellerup Syd did not change signiﬁcantly, but the water composition did, which
would deﬁnitely aﬀect the temperature modelling.
• Enlarged water tower
The last option considered to solve the problem, was to increase the size of the water
tower allowing it to buﬀer the system and not have it crashing when simulating. The
size depended on the time of simulation, as it should only have to be large enough to
buﬀer the variations in system in- and outﬂow in the period of simulation. This option
increased the residence time in the water tower, but as the temperature model does not
include temperature changes in the water tower, this is not expected to have a signiﬁcant
eﬀect on the temperature modelling results. This solution was assessed as the best and
therefore implemented.
The system water balance showed a slightly larger consumption than production. In order
to try to balance the system a R-script was developed by Jonas Kirstein enabling an overall
percentual reduction of the outﬂow to all DMAs by applying a multiplication factor to the DMA
demands (see appendix C). The R-script was used to create a simpliﬁed Nordvand model with
a water balance close to zero for each of the ﬁve selected simulation periods (see section 5.4).

5.3
Temperature modelling in EPANET-MSX
Running EPANET MSX in MATLAB is simple and fast, as it is running the hydraulic model in
EPANET. Running a one month simulation of the hydraulic model in EPANET takes less than
ﬁve seconds while running a one month temperature simulation in MSX for the same hydraulic
system takes approximately one minute. Assuming a correct .inp-ﬁle of the EPANET model
and .msx-ﬁle describing the governing processes and conditions controlling the system water
temperatures, the extension package can be run with three MATLAB lines:
1. yyy = epanet(’xxx.inp’) loading the EPANET ﬁle xxx and naming it yyy.
2. yyy.loadMSXFile(’zzz.msx’) loading the MSX ﬁle zzz and applying it to the EPANET
model yyy. When doing so, the program check the input for errors and shows error codes
if errors occur.
3. rrr = yyy.getMSXComputedQualityNode computes the Quality (in this case tempera-
ture) for the yyy EPANET network based on the information and rates deﬁned in the
MSX ﬁle zzz and stores it in MATLAB as rrr. rrr then contains water quality (tem-
perature) data timeseries for every node in the model (if requested under [REPORT] in
the .msx input ﬁle - see appendix D).
As the running of MSX is simple in MATLAB, a lot of the conditions have to be deﬁned
in the .msx input ﬁle. Simulation time step, solver, simulation specie, inﬂuent water quality
(temperature), starting conditions, reporting type and of course simulation rates are amongst
others deﬁned in the .msx-ﬁle. This can either be edited directly in the input ﬁle using e.g.
Microsoft Windows Notepad or through MATLAB using the functions described by Eliades
and Kyriakou (2014).

5.3.2
Water tower
The water tower is included in the hydraulic model, where it serves as an important storage
buﬀer for the system, evening out the diﬀerences in system in- and outﬂows. In order to not
have the system crash when modelling a month, due to a shortage or excess of water in the
system, the water tower is enlarged in these systems. In a standard EPANET setup the sys-
tem would have a reservoir, where inﬂow would be controlled by water demand and network
pressures. In this system, where all in- and outﬂows are implemented as nodes with a deﬁned
ﬂow, the enlarged water tower is used to buﬀer the system, making pressures stay within the
expected range and the hydraulics not change more than necessary.
EPANET MSX allows a rate expression to be deﬁned for the water tower as it is for the
pipes. This means that temperature in theory could also be modelled in the water tower.
Modelling temperatures in an enlarged water tower would not be realistic for the system and
therefore not improve the model. Therefore this is not attempted. Only mixing of tempera-
tures is considered in the model. In order for this to work properly, the "Model mixing" setting
of the tank should be set to mixed. In the Aquis model it is set to FIFO (ﬁrst-in-ﬁrst-out) but
using this setting in EPANET, for some unknown reason, ruins the ability of MSX to calculate
mixing temperatures in the tank.

5.4
Simulation and validation periods
Simulation periods of one month were selected as the soil temperatures are monthly averages.
And as the eﬄuent temperatures from the water works were also more than a month a part
in most periods, one month simulation periods seemed appropriate. This also allows for easier
comparison with the analysis of weekly variations on a monthly basis described in section 5.1.3.
5.4.1
Simulation
Four periods where initially selected for simulation.
1. January 2015: 15-01-01 to 15-01-31.
2. February 2015: 15-02-01 to 15-02-28.
3. July 2016: 16-07-01 to 16-07-31.
4. September 2016: 16-09-01 to 16-09-30.
These periods where selected for several reasons:
• They cover both the coldest and the hottest months.
• They represent diﬀerent ﬂow scenarios, as January and September have normal ﬂows,
while the ﬂows in February and July are slightly aﬀected by the winter break and the
summer holiday. Looking at the plots of the raw ﬂow data in appendix B.1 a decrease
in ﬂow in mid February is seen, most likely caused by the winter break while the entire
July has a lower ﬂow, most likely due to the summer holiday. This is seen in all three
years of measurement in almost all locations.
• Despite representing diﬀerent ﬂow scenarios, the ﬂow is rather stable during all three
periods. No sudden and anomalous variations in ﬂow occur.
• They cover periods of stable ﬂow from the sources (water works), meaning that the
composition of water does not seem to change signiﬁcantly during any of the periods.

5.4.2
Validation
In order to validate the model and its ability to model the aﬀect of change in ﬂow on tempera-
ture, a less stable period was selected to be modelled, going from mid July to mid August 2015
(16-07-10 to 16-08-09). In the ﬁrst weeks of this period only Sjælsø is supplying the network
with water as Ermelunden is shut down, but in the beginning of August Ermelunden is put
back in production. During the period of only one water work, the system water composition
is obviously completely changed. Furthermore the DMA demands are slightly decreased, while
going back to normal when Ermelunden is operational again. This means that the hydraulics
change signiﬁcantly during this one month period. Both with regards to ﬂow and water com-
position. Hence this period should be able to test whether the model is able to cope with the
changes.
The model hydraulics are strictly deﬁned by the speciﬁcation of ﬂow in all major consumers
(DMA inﬂows) through the measurements incorporated in the model. Furthermore the tem-
perature modelling is limited by the constant input parameters (soil and water work eﬄuent
temperatures). This limits the need for model validation as there are no actual parameters to
validate, as the model performance can simple be assessed by evaluating the ﬁt to the measured
data. Hence no further validation was done.
5.5
Link between temperature and water quality
Having set up a simpliﬁed model of the Gentofte part of the Nordvand utility area, water
temperatures could be estimated throughout the network. Assuming that temperatures aﬀect
microbiological water quality, the model should be able to point out areas of concern with
regards to water quality. In order to see if any link existed, water quality measurements from
the GEUS Jupiter database (Geus, 2017b) was compared to the model results.
5.5.1
Water quality measurements from GEUS
The newest microbiological water quality samples in the GEUS Jupiter database from the
Gentofte WDN where taken in 2012. Although being from 2012, the samples where compared
to the modelling results from 2016 to see if any link between modelled temperatures and water
quality were present. As most water quality samples where taken in the late spring (end May)
and summer (late June), they were compared to the measurements from the July 2016. Six
water quality measurements where selected from the Jupiter database, as they were sampled
in locations rather close to DMA inﬂow points modelled in the MSX temperature model.
As the water quality data from the Jupiter database is scarce, it is only included as an ex-
ample on how to compare modelled and measured results. It can not be used to conﬁrm or
dismiss any assumptions on relations between temperatures and microbiological water quality,
but simply give an idea of the water quality in the distribution network.

7.1
Temporal and spatial variations in temperature
The results in section 6.1 clearly show that temporal and spatial variations in the temperature
measurements from the Nordvand DWDS. Temperatures change both on a seasonal and daily
basis and diﬀer depending on where in the network, they are measured.
The causes and
dynamics of these changes will be discussed in the following section.
7.1.1
Seasonal variations in temperature
Seasonal variations in network temperatures where clearly shown in ﬁgures 6.1 and 6.2. Small
seasonal variations are seen in the water work eﬄuent temperatures while the largest variations
are seen in the parts of the system furthest from the water work. Several factors have inﬂuence
on how large the seasonal variations in network temperatures are. The two main factors af-
fecting the seasonal variation in drinking water temperature seems to be the soil temperature
and the eﬄuent temperature from the water work. Both parameters that are included in the
model, although as constants.
The eﬄuent temperature at the water work is important as it sets the starting point for
the further change of the water temperature. The soil temperature measurements from the
GEUS Jupiter database used in the model shows an yearly variation of up to 3.4 ◦C at Sjælsø
WW and 2.4 ◦C at Ermelunden WW, with temperatures lowest in winter and highest in sum-
mer. The small variations in eﬄuent temperatures might be aﬀected by small seasonal changes
in the temperature of the extracted groundwater, but as these are normally not more than
1 ◦C (De Pasquale et al., 2017), the main temperature change is expected to happen during
the treatment processes at the water work. Even though the treatment takes places inside
buildings, the air temperature outside the buildings will have an eﬀect on the indoor temper-
ature and thus also on the drinking water temperature. The groundwater temperatures being
relatively stable and the inﬂuence on temperature at the water work being little, the eﬄuent
temperature is not a parameter that can be altered much in the temperature modelling however.
The soil temperature seems to be strongly aﬀected by air temperature as it shows a clear
seasonal variation between 3.5 and 15.3 ◦C. The soil temperatures are averages of the years
2001-2010 though and therefore no direct comparison with soil temperatures should be made
from the plots.
Nevertheless, as the soil temperature is measured at 1 m depth, it would
be much more likely to be aﬀected by surface temperatures than the soil (and water) in the
groundwater wells usually several tens of meters down. As the soil temperature is the ambient
temperature of the drinking water pipe, the soil temperature has a large eﬀect on drinking
water temperatures. The diﬀerence between soil and water temperature sets the heat trans-
fer rate and thus the rate of water temperature change. If soil temperature is easily aﬀected
by air temperatures, hourly and daily variations could be expected. Daily variations in soil
temperatures would reduce the daily variations in water temperatures, as the soil would be
warmer during the day i.e. in the periods where water is expected to be colder due to high ﬂow.
53
==PAGE:68==
7.1 - Temporal and spatial variations in temperature
Another factor that could have inﬂuence on seasonal variations in water temperature is the soil
moisture level. A very moist soil has a higher thermal conductivity than a very dry soil and is
thus able to transfer heat more quickly (Abu-Hamdeh, 2003). This would make the inﬂuence of
both soil and air temperatures on the water temperature larger, as the heat exchange between
them would be quicker.
7.1.2
Spatial variations in temperature
In general network temperatures seems to increase the further from the source the water gets.
This suggest a spatial variation of network temperatures. Figure 6.1 showed a small tempera-
ture change from Ermelunden to Søndersø Syd (∼2,400 m) and a larger one from Ermelunden
WW to Hellerup Syd (∼5,000 m). As Hellerup Syd is more than twice as far from Ermelunden
WW as Søndersø Syd, this indicates an increase in temperature change the further you get
from the source. When considering Søndersø Nord, which is only ∼230 m from Ermelunden
WW but shows the highest temperatures (in summer), this assumption is questionable. The
reason that temperature measurements at Søndersø Nord are higher than at Søndersø Syd and
Hellerup Syd is most likely that some of the water at Søndersø Nord originates at Sjælsø WW,
which is more than 10 km away. This reestablishes the spatial relationship between tempera-
ture change and distance from source. Distance from source should however not be considered
a direct indicator for temperature change, but instead an indicator of expected retention time,
although retention time and temperature change are not proportional either.
Jægersborg Nord measures higher temperatures than Vesterbyvej PS in summer, even though
Vesterbyvej PS is further from the water works (ﬁgure 6.2). Like for Søndersø Nord this is
most likely due to Jægersborg Nord receiving most water from Jægersborg WT, while most
water at Vesterbyvej PS comes directly from Sjælsø WW. This would indicate that in summer
water in the water tower is heated, before exiting.
The residence time is not necessarily highest in the farthest parts of the system. The ﬂow
velocity in the pipes vary greatly from 0.02 m/s up to 0.5 m/s. This means that the residence
time in a short pipe might be high if the ﬂow velocity is low. This is most likely also a factor
at Søndersø Nord and to some extend also between Søndersø Syd and Hellerup Syd, supported
by the water ages in table 6.1. Yhe temperature changes in Søndersø Nord are not as large
as in summer. In winter the change in Søndersø Nord is actually lower than in Hellerup Syd.
This is most likely not due to shorter retention times, as the ﬂow in Søndersø Nord does not
seem to change, but instead caused by a change in water composition, indicating more water
coming from Ermelunden WW. In this way temperature can also be used as an indicator of
water sources.
In summer Søndersø Nord furthermore has the largest daily variations in temperature, while
in winter they are way smaller. This could also very well be due to changing water sources,
as the water from Sjælsø WW will have signiﬁcantly higher temperatures than the water from
Ermelunden WW. Another reason could be that the water that Søndersø Nord receives in high
ﬂow periods has been stored in the Hjortekjær WT where the water temperature has changed.
Hence Søndersø Nord would be expected to receive colder waters from Ermelunden WW during
night and warmer water from Sjælsø WW/Hjortekjær WT during day. The inﬂuence of the
WT on temperatures and the fact that a temperature change in the WT is not modelled, will
be discussed later on in this chapter.

7.1.3
Correlation between ﬂow and temperature
A discussion of the correlation between ﬂow and temperature basically comes back to the
correlation between retention time of the water in the system and the temperature change.
The relative ﬂow can be used as an indicator of the velocity and thus the retention time. By
relative ﬂow, the magnitude of the ﬂow compared to the average is implied. Although ﬂow and
ﬂow velocity are not proportional, as the relationship depends on the pipe diameter, change
in ﬂow is a good indicator of how the velocity is changing. Hence if the relative ﬂow is low,
the velocity is also expected to be low. This is mainly an local indicator though. In a system
with many consumers where consumption patterns are not necessarily the same, the velocity
can be low in one part of the system without the velocity being it in other parts.
This also relates to the daily variations in temperature compared to the overall temperature
level. In Søndersø Nord large temperature variations are seen in summer, without the overall
temperature change being high compared to e.g. Hellerup Syd. This can be due to diﬀerent
ﬂow velocities combined with diﬀerent thermal conductivities of the pipes. If a pipe stretch
with two consumers have a high ﬂow, high velocity and low thermal conductivity in the ﬁrst
part upstream consumer one and a low ﬂow, low velocity and high thermal conductivity in the
second part downstream consumer one, the temperature change will be small in the ﬁrst part
while being high in the second part. And so will the daily variations. During daytime when
the relative ﬂow is high, water will ﬂow through both pipes quickly, not being aﬀected much by
ambient temperatures. In night when the relative ﬂow is low, the water will be aﬀected little
by ambient temperatures in the ﬁrst part, but a lot in the second part. Furthermore changes
in the relative ﬂow in each part of the pipe will not aﬀect the temperatures in the other part
of the pipe. The example could very well resemble what happens at Søndersø Nord.
Nevertheless the ﬂow can be used to assess approximately when peaks in temperature should
be expected. As the analysis shows, the peaks in temperatures are occurring in periods of low
ﬂow, while at high ﬂows the water temperature change from the source is smaller.
It should also be considered whether a opposite relation can be assumed: Temperature as
an indicator of ﬂow. Considering how simple is it to measure temperature it might be useful.
From the correlation described above, temperature might not always be able to describe ﬂow,
but it might be a good indicator of retention time. Furthermore it might also be used for
assessing the water source. If measured temperatures much higher or lower than expected it
will most likely be a sign that the water originates from other sources than expected or ﬂow
along other routes in the network than expected.
7.1.4
Reliability of measurements
No focus has yet been put on the reliability of the measurements. It has been established
that the temperature measurements contain errors of zero and negative measurements. But
some of the measurements are also of questionable quality. The temperature measurements
from Gentofte 3 (ﬁgure B.1) are the best example, as the temperatures stretches from -8 ◦C to
+50 ◦C. Daily variations vary more than 30 ◦C. As temperatures in this range and which such
high variations are unrealistic, these measurements must be considered erroneous. The fact
that temperatures in this unrealistic range has been logged for more than three years indicates
how much the temperature data is used. This could question the other temperature data,
however as these are within realistic ranges and are more or less consistent with what would

7.1.5
Importance of residence time
To sum up this discussion section it is clear that the retention time of the water in the network
is central in the estimation of the temperature change throughout the network. While eﬄuent
water temperatures and soil conditions determine the size of the temperature change, the
residence time is essential for predicting when temperatures are expected to peak where in the
system. This means that the ambient conditions are the determining factors for the spatial
changes in temperature, while the retention time is the determining factor for the temporal
variations.
7.2
Hydraulic model performance
As the hydraulic model is the basis for the temperature model, the validity of the modelled
temperatures to some extend comes down to the quality and precision of the hydraulic model.
Small deviations in the ﬂow of speciﬁc pipes will not aﬀect the overall temperature modelling
signiﬁcantly.
It seems as if the sources of water and the mixture of water is of far more
importance. Especially if the sources are far apart as is the case in the Nordvand Model. In a
hydraulically constrained model with strictly deﬁned in and outﬂows as the simpliﬁed Nordvand
model used in this project, the ﬂows and ﬂow velocities would be expected to be reliable.
Therefore the main reason for hydraulic errors will probably center around the enlarged water
tower. While not changing the ﬂow signiﬁcantly it still might disturb the modelling of water
streams, deciding where the water from the diﬀerent sources goes. If this is the case is will
deﬁnitely interfere with the temperature modelled results. Therefore improving the hydraulic
model, by allowing more ﬂexibility and a correctly dimensioned water tower, the temperature
model will most likely also improve.
7.3
Temperature modelling
7.3.1
Temperature modelling equations
Only one governing model has been considered, while three diﬀerent methods of assessing the
overall heat transfer coeﬃcient has been considered. The Undisturbed soil model, the shape
factor model and Krarti-Kreider’s model. All the models where tested on the single pipe sys-
tem, while only the shape factor model was used when modelling the simpliﬁed Nordvand
system.
The undisturbed soil model does not take the surrounding soil into consideration, but just
assumes a constant soil temperature and thus a constant ambient pipe temperature. Even
though being used by Blokker and Pieterse-Quirijns (2013) and Blokker et al. (2013) the
model has not been deemed suitable as the importance of including the soil resistance has
been emphasized by several other studies (De Pasquale et al., 2017; Hubeck-Graudal, 2015;
Bøhm, 2000). Therefore the undisturbed soil model was never considered as an option for
modelling the Nordvand system. It was included in this study for comparison with the shape
factor model, which resembles the undisturbed soil model, but does include the thermal resis-
tance of the surrounding soil.

==PAGE:71==

The heat transfer coeﬃcients for water and pipe are described in exactly the same way in
the shape factor model as in the undisturbed soil model. The diﬀerence is that the heat trans-
fer coeﬃcient for soil is also included in the overall heat transfer coeﬃcient used in the general
temperature change rate expression, equation 2.5. It is included by considering the thermal
conductivity of the soil multiplied with a shape factor depending on the pipe thickness of
the surrounding soil layer aﬀected by the thermal changes (calculated from the pipe burying
depth). The resemblance to the undisturbed soil model can be shown if the pipe burying depth
is set to 0 or if the the thermal conductivity of the soil, λpipe →∞. The shape factor model
resembles the models used by Hubeck-Graudal (2015); De Pasquale et al. (2017) and Lauritsen
(2009). [Tjek op på sidste reference!]
The Krarti-Kreider’s model likewise considers the thermal conductivity of the soil layer also
depending on the thickness of the surrounding soil layer. It does not feature the convection of
the ﬂowing water though.
The results from applying the models in a single pipe system, ﬁgure 6.11, shows that the three
models estimate very diﬀerent temperatures. There is a clear resemblance between them, con-
sidering the daily variations, displaying the dependency on residence time of the overall rate
expression. The variations in overall temperature change levels as well as the amplitude of
the daily variations depend on the model selected for assessment of the overall heat transfer
coeﬃcient. As the undisturbed soil model does not include the thermal conductivity but is
otherwise similar to the shape factor model, the diﬀerence in these two models shows the eﬀect
of including the ambient soil layer in the model. A large eﬀect is seen as the overall tempera-
ture change for the undisturbed soil model is approximately three times higher than the shape
factor. The distinct eﬀect of including the thermal disturbance of the pipe on the surrounding
soil in the model, clearly shows why the undisturbed soil model was not further considered.
Krarti-Kreider models temperature changes slightly higher than the shape factor, mainly due
to not including the convection of the water. As simulations of the simpliﬁed Nordvand model
later showed that the models generally overestimates temperature changes, the shape factor
model was chosen for modelling of the Nordvand system as it includes most factors and esti-
mate temperatures closest to the measurements.
7.3.2
Inﬂuence of model parameters
As established from the single pipe model, some parameters have the potential of changing
the model outcomes more than others. The soil and eﬄuent water work temperatures have
a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the overall temperature level, by deﬁning the system maximum and
minimum levels. The diﬀerence between them also has an inﬂuence on the change of tem-
perature in the network as it makes up the factor multiplied with the overall heat transfer
coeﬃcient. A large temperature diﬀerence will thus result in larger temperature change as well
as larger daily variations in temperature. The two temperature parameters are however rather
settled. The eﬄuent water work temperatures does not vary more than a few degrees Celsius
and the levels are well established from measurements. Although the soil temperature varies
more, the seasonal levels are also well established from measurements. Some daily variations
could however be expected to occur in soil temperature, which could be implemented to im-
prove the model accuracy.

7.3 - Temperature modelling
The thermal conductivities has a signiﬁcant role in determining the eﬀect of the ambient
temperature on the water temperature. The thermal conductivity of water has not been con-
sidered as a variable parameter as the value is relatively ﬁxed. The thermal conductivity of
the pipe can vary a lot as it depends on the pipe material. The thermal conductivity for the
soil can vary depending on e.g. how coarse, how compressed and how moist it is. The main
focus was on pipe material as it has known variations in the system, but not in time while
the soil conditions are unknown and will vary in time. Modelling was conducted using three
diﬀerent pipe materials, PVC, concrete and cast iron, as they make up most of the network -
see table 3.2 (assuming that PVC and PE as well as Concrete and Bonna has similar thermal
properties). As it is not possible to ﬁt the model in all measurement locations using only one
common value for the entire system, knowing that the pipe material changes throughout the
network, an extensive attempt to ﬁt the model to the measurements was not done. Likewise
the model was not extensively ﬁtted adjusting the thermal conductivity of the soil either, as
the soil conditions changes in time and space, making it impossible to ﬁt the model using a uni-
versal value. Nevertheless three thermal conductivities for the pipes (materials) was simulated
and two for soil, enabling an overall assessment of the inﬂuence of the thermal conductivities
on the model ﬁt.
7.3.3
Model setup considerations
Focus in the modelling has in general not been on obtaining the perfect model ﬁt, but instead
on understanding which conditions aﬀect the model results and how. The number of variables
included in the model as constants, makes it impossible for the model to precisely estimate
temperatures in the network. This would require the deﬁnition of spatial variations in e.g. soil
temperature and pipe material as well as temporal variations in e.g. soil conditions. Attention
has also been on how well the model describes temporal changes in temperature and what can
be done to improve this.

shown good indications on being able to model daily variations in temperature. Even though
the amplitude is underestimated, it can currently still be used for assessment of when peaks
are expected to occur.
7.3.4
Model validation
The results of the modelled validation period are very similar to the other two summer months
modelled. The model overestimates the temperatures in all locations except Søndersø Nord
where it is underestimated, exactly as in the other two summer periods. It is not able to
describe the large daily variations in temperature either. The interesting part of the validation
period is however the change in sources of the water taking place in start August.
In all
locations except Jægersborg WT the measured temperature decreases from July to August, in
the course of a couple of days, as the water work productions change. Especially in Hellerup
Syd and Jægersborg Nord this is very evident. Meanwhile the ﬂow only increases very little.
This shows that the increase in temperature is not caused by a change in ﬂow, but instead by
a change in the water sources. The modelled temperatures shows the exact same development

7.3.5
Model improvements
The model is good at describing steady state conditions. The only variable in the current model
is ﬂow, and it is evidently not enough to describe all aspect of the temperature variations in
a DWDS. Hence some improvements could be implemented in the model.
The suggested
improvements mainly focus on improving the ability of the model to include local conditions
as well as to incorporate temporal variations of parameters.
• Spatial variations in soil temperature
To include spatial variations in temperature would ﬁrst of all require that soil temperature
was actually know locally. In the current model a national average is used. Implementing
local soil conditions would however enable the model to better describe both the overall
temperature level and the daily variations in temperature.
• Spatial variations in pipe material
Opposite to soil temperature, knowledge already exist on local pipe materials. It is thus
the technical implementation that is the hurdle.
Implementing pipe speciﬁc thermal
conductivities would most likely improve the ability to estimate temperatures spatially.
• Spatial variations in pipe burying depth
Implementing local pipe burying depths would be expected to further improve the accu-
racy of the temperature level estimations. However this is a very local parameter, which
it is probably not possible to gather suﬃcient information on. The eﬀect of implementing
it, would, based on the results in ﬁgure G.3, probably not be as large as most of the other
improvement suggestions. Therefore this should not be prioritized. At least not in large
complex systems.
• Modelling of temperatures in water tower
Little is known on the eﬀect on temperature of the water stored in the water tower. If
temperature was measured at the in-/outﬂow of the water tower this would give some
good indications. Water temperatures are very likely to change during storage. The heat
exchange calculations of a water tower should not be too diﬀerent from those of a pipe,
if assuming a cylindrical shape. And as a rate expression input for tanks already exist in
EPANET MSX, this could deﬁnitely be implemented, if more knowledge existed on the
ambient conditions.
• Temporal variations in soil conditions
An ability to include temporal variations in soil temperature and in soil thermal con-
ductivity (e.g. due to changes in soil moisture level), would make the model better at
estimating the daily variations in water temperature.

7.4
Applications of the DWDS temperature model
For the current model to be able to estimate correct water temperature levels, it needs good
input estimates of soil and water work eﬄuent temperatures as well as of thermal conductivities
of soil and pipe. As the model uses global parameters for the thermal conductivities of soil and
pipes, these can not be assessed based solely on theory. If the model should perform optimally
these two parameters need to be calibrated based on measurements. If this is not done the
model might not be able to assess water temperature levels correctly.
7.4.1
Model for assessing water quality
Although the current model is not able to estimate precise temperatures in the network, it is
still able to estimate in which part of the system the highest temperatures would be expected
and when. Hence the model could be used for assessing areas vulnerable to poor water quality.
The model could also be used assess in which parts of the system the recommendations for
temperatures to not exceeding 12 ◦C would actually be met. As temperature is shown as a
good indicator for where and where mixing of sources occur, it would also be able to support
trace testings and in detection of anomalous events, where the ﬂow is not changed signiﬁcantly.
Within water quality assessment the model would be limited to indicating in which branches
of a system the highest temperatures would be expected. As the current model is tested and
tuned for modelling temperature in major pipes, it is unknown whether it would also be able to
assess temperatures in smaller branches of the DWDS. Without implementing locally deﬁned
parameters this is unlikely. Enabling modelling of temperature within the DMAs would also
require an expansion of the hydraulic model. Improvements of the model could increase the
possibilities for using the model within water quality assessment. The best case scenario would
be that the model would be able to model temperature all the way to the start of the domestic
pipe system at the consumer i.e. covering the entire utility network.
7.4.2
Other applications for a DWDS temperature model
As described by De Pasquale et al. (2017) and Hubeck-Graudal (2015) implementation of heat
pumps retrieving energy from the drinking water for use in the district heating network is
an obvious use for a DWDS temperature model. In order to assess whether the heat pumps
are economically viable, it is necessary to know how much energy can be retrieved from the
drinking water and how this would change the water temperatures at the consumer. Lowering
temperatures at the end tap would mean that the consumer would have to use more energy in
heating the water, which is also a big part of assessing the beneﬁt of the heat pumps.
7.5
Modelled temperature and water quality
A comparison between the measured microbiological water quality in table 6.2 and the modelled
water temperatures in ﬁgure 6.20 show no immediate correlation. Although the data is not
covering the same years, both water quality measurements and water temperatures are of the
summer period and should therefore be comparable. In general the water quality measurements
are very low, indicating that the microbiological water quality in the WDN is very good. As the
diﬀerence in water quality is little between locations, it is hard to diﬀerentiate the locations
from each other, making it diﬃcult to compare to diﬀerences in temperature. To properly

Chapter 8 - Conclusion
8
Conclusion
Raw high frequency meter data from the Nordvand utility company has been processed and
analyzed for patterns in the spatial and temporal variations in network temperatures. Flow
and temperature data has been compared to see if correlations exists and to understand which
are the governing processes for temperature development in the network. A simpliﬁed model
has been build in EPANET, capable of recreating the hydraulic conditions in a part of the
network, by including measured system ﬂows. Temperatures have been modelled in this part
of the network using EPANET MSX and measurements and model results have been compared
to evaluate the ability of the model to estimate temperatures in the drinking water distribution
system (DWDS).
The metered data show clear temporal and spatial variations in water temperature. Water
temperatures in the DWDS have both seasonal and daily variations. The seasonal variations
are caused by changes in soil temperatures and eﬄuent temperatures from water works fol-
lowing the general development in atmospheric temperature. The daily variations in water
temperatures are a product of changing network residence times. Variations in ﬂow has shown
resemblance with residence time, making it a good indicator of variations in drinking water
temperature.
Even though being simpliﬁed, the temperature model has shown a promising ability in estimat-
ing network water temperatures. The model uses measurements of network in- and outﬂows
for deﬁning the system hydraulics. It is able to estimate water temperatures in all nodes based
on thermal conductivities of soil and pipe, soil temperature and water work eﬄuent temper-
ature, thus taking both the water, pipe and soil into account. If reliable approximations of
these parameters are provided for the model input, the model has shown good abilities to esti-
mate overall monthly temperature levels as well as being able to model the tendencies in daily
temperature variations. The model has also shown a capability of estimating the aﬀect on
temperature of a changing water composition (in systems with multiple sources). The model
can therefore be used for indicating in which parts of a system the highest temperatures should
be expected, as it is well able to estimate the relative diﬀerences in temperature throughout
the network. Depending on the changes in conditions throughout the network, the model is
also able to estimate the actual temperature in the network nodes. Local diﬀerences in ther-
mal conductivities and soil temperature can however interfere with this. The model can, in
its current state, furthermore be used for assessing the relative eﬀect on temperature of major
changes in hydraulics e.g. the closure of a water work.
In order for the model to spatially predict temperatures more precisely, an ability to de-
ﬁne parameters locally is needed.
In the current setup thermal conductivities are deﬁned
globally. As pipe materials are already known, the potential is present for deﬁning thermal
conductivities for each pipe material. As soil temperatures have a large eﬀect on the modelled
water temperatures, a possibility to deﬁne soil temperature in both time and space would
also improve the model. Spatially dependant soil temperatures would improve the ability of
estimating local temperature better, while temporally deﬁned soil temperatures would enable
a better estimation of the daily water temperature level. In contrast to the current model,
63
==PAGE:78==
where the constant soil temperature results in a constant temperature level throughout the
entire simulation period.
A shortcoming of the model is its ability to model the amplitude of the daily temperature
variations, as the measured daily variations are 2-5 times larger than modelled. In the cur-
rent model, the amplitude can not be altered without also changing the overall temperature
level. This suggests that a modiﬁcation of the governing model equations might be needed.
Introducing a buﬀer capacity of the surrounding soil layer by including the soil heat capacity
is a possibility. This is however likely to decrease daily variations rather than increase them.
Modelling of temperature change in water towers should also be included, assuming a model
with a realistically dimensioned water towers. Inclusion of temperature change in water towers
would most likely increase daily variations in temperature.
All in all the model shows promising results and can most certainly ﬁnd useful applications
in the current state e.g. within water quality assessment. However an improved model, able
to estimate network temperature conditions more precisely, based on local parameters, would
deﬁnitely have an even larger potential of use.

Chapter 9 - Reﬂections and Perspectivation
9
Reﬂections and Perspectivation
The temperature model built in this project can only be seen as a basis for further develop-
ment. Although the model is able to estimate temperatures on a level that might be useful
within some applications, the model is simple both with regards to hydraulics and to its ability
to estimate network temperatures. The temperature model could deﬁnitely be improved by
implementing the suggestion in section 7.3.5. This includes enabling implementation of local
parameters as well as an ability to deﬁne temporal variations in parameters. Modelling of tem-
perature in water towers, would deﬁnitely also improve the ability to model daily variations in
temperature in the network.
As the current hydraulic model is very simple, it would be evident to expand the model
used to cover larger areas and more complex systems.
As the simpliﬁed Nordvand model
only cover the main transport pipes, future work on the hydraulic model should also focus on
getting to cover smaller branches of the network closer to the consumers. The solutions to re-
solve the complications regarding the water balance when including ﬂow measurements in the
model should also be improved. Enabling the implementation of live ﬂow measurements in the
model, would take the model to an even higher level, allowing live modelling of of temperatures.
The input parameters could be optimized in the current model, as no thorough parameter
assessment has been carried out ﬁtting the model to the measurement data. An analysis of
model parameters could be done by setting up a Monte-Carlo simulation testing the model
parameters. This would enable better estimation of parameters as well as giving a better un-
derstanding of which model parameters are important.
An eﬀort should also be put into locally assessing the MSX input temperatures on e.g. a
daily basis. A better basis for estimation of parameters would be possible if better data on
water work eﬄuent temperatures where acquired as well as data on soil temperatures.
To properly assess the correlation between water quality and temperatures, better data on
water quality is needed. Future work could therefore also include doing water quality sam-
pling throughout the network over a longer period of time.
This would provide data the
suﬃcient data for comparing water quality and water temperatures. This would also provide
a basis for evaluating the potential of using temperature models for indication of water quality.
Finally great potential still lies in the raw meter data from Nordvand WDN. As this the-
sis has mainly analyzed the temperature data, a lot of information is waiting to be discovered
in the ﬂow and pressure data. The ﬂow data can be used for doing water balances for each
DMA assessing water use and loss or used for optimizing hydraulic models of the network. The
pressure data can be used for optimizing pressures in the system in order to minimize water
loss e.g. during night where pressures are usually high.

script very much.
The script loads the simpliﬁed Nordvand EPANET model and assigns
demand patterns to the ﬂow of all nodes representing inﬂuent points to DMAs within a deﬁned

consumption equally for the selected DMAs in the network. An option oﬀered to adjust the
