Abstract
With the growing prevalence of high-resolution and high-quality digital imagery,
efficient image compression has become increasingly essential. Traditional compression
methods like JPEG apply uniform compression across the image, often resulting in
perceptual degradation in visually significant regions. This thesis investigates enhance-
ments to JPEG XL, a modern image coding format, by leveraging auxiliary information
such as distortion heatmaps and region-of-interest (ROI) masks to optimize lossy and
progressive image encoding.
The work introduces two primary methods of optimization: one based on percep-
tual distortion heatmaps generated by ColorVideoVDP (CVVDP), and another using
ROI masks derived from segmentation models combining GroundingDINO and Seg-
ment Anything Model (SAM). These auxiliary inputs guide block-level adjustments
in quantization, allowing finer control over bit allocation to improve perceived image
quality, especially in visually critical areas.
Additionally, the work explores optimizing JPEG XL’s progressive loading feature
by reordering encoding groups to prioritize ROI regions, ensuring faster visual clarity
in salient areas during image loading.
The work includes detailed modifications to the JPEG XL source code to support
these enhancements and evaluate performance using metrics like PSNR, SSIM, CVVDP,
FVVDP, and Bjøntegaard Delta-Rate.
Experimental results demonstrate that incorporating semantic and perceptual data
into the JPEG XL workflow significantly improves visual fidelity while maintaining
or reducing overall bitrate. This work contributes a perceptually aware, bit-efficient
approach to modern image compression.

Introduction
As the image resolution and quality increase, nowadays, more and more large image
files are uploaded to the Internet. In the past, an image might have been just a few tens of
KB in size, but now image files of several tens of MB are becoming increasingly common.
This caused the increased demand for efficient image compression technique, the classic
JPEG [1] image compression method applies uniform image compression to all regions of
an image, which limits its ability to achieve optimal coding efficiency. JPEG XL [2], as
a modern image coding standard, offers advanced features such as adaptive quantization,
variable DCT sizes, and progressive coding that provide new opportunities for optimization.
This work focuses on foreground-prioritized image coding, the term "foreground" here
does not simply refer to the foreground of an image; it can also refer to the region that shows
the most severe distortion after compression.
The primary method of this work is to optimize the performance of JPEG XL lossy
encoding by leveraging auxiliary information in the form of distortion heatmaps and region-
of-interest (ROI) masks. Our goal is to allocate more bits to regions with severe distortion
or in the ROI to obtain better perceptual image quality.
Specifically, we transform the distortion heatmap from ColorVideoVDP (CVVDP) [3],
a perceptual metric that estimates local visual differences, into a block-level mask to guide
fine-grained quantization level adjustments. We also manage to generate ROI masks from a
segmentation model that combines GroundingDINO [4] and the Segment Anything Model
(SAM) [5], and convert it into a block-level mask of the same format.
In this work, by studying the source code of JPEG XL, we gained a more detailed
understanding of the features introduced in related JPEG XL papers. At the same time,
we modified the source code of JPEG XL so that the encoder can refer to the auxiliary
information we provide during the encoding process. We also introduced new parameters to
JPEG XL for a more fine-grained adjustment of the quantization level.
This work also explores the possibility of optimizing the JPEG XL progressive coding
by prioritizing the order of ROI groups in the output bitstream. By doing that, the ROI
will become clear earlier during the progressive loading process, hence increasing the image
quality during progressive loading.
Through these methods, we aim to demonstrate that existing JPEG XL encoding can
be made more perceptually aware and bit-efficient by integrating external semantic or per-
ceptual knowledge into the compression workflow.

Literature review
Many previous works have employed spatial non-uniform image encoding. For example,
some methods utilize a foreground segmentation model to distinguish between the foreground
and background, then assign different quantization levels to them, achieving lower compres-
sion rates for the foreground at the same bpp. As a result, the foreground retains a higher
image quality.
The paper [6] introduces two bit allocation strategies, Maximum Bit Transfer (MBT)
and Joint Bit Assignment (JBA) for encoding the foreground and background separately. It
used a face segmentation algorithm to separate the foreground and background of an image.
The paper achieves different bit allocation for the foreground and background areas by
adjusting the quantization parameters of the foreground and background areas. When using
the MBT strategy, the background area will always be encoded with the coarsest quantization
level, so when the bpp for the whole image is fixed, it will provide more bits for foreground
area usage, hence enabling a finer quality of foreground area. When comparing the default
image coding and the image coding with MBT, the average PSNR of the foreground has
increased from 31.91 dB to 35.58 dB.
The paper [7] intended to give priority to the foreground in sign-language video, so that
the motion of hands and arms will have better quality under the same frame rate. It defined
the region-of-interest (ROI) in two ways, statically and dynamically. The static ROI is a
static area shaped to include the sign space. The dynamic ROI is produced by a skin seg-
mentation method of videos ("Adaptive training of skin color distributions for segmentation
of video sequences." ). The paper applied different quantizer value Q and motion threshold T
for foreground and background, in this way it achieved a better foreground quality compared
to the uniform algorithm.
Other approaches classify image blocks and allocate higher sampling rates to visually
more salient blocks. Similarly, this technique uses spatial non-uniform image encoding to
enhance the quality of salient regions in the image.
The paper [8] proposed an adaptive block-based compressed (ABCS) technique for pro-
gressive image coding. An image is divided into two layers: the base layer and the enhance-
ment layer. At the base layer, the image is sampled with a low and fixed measurement rate,
this layer will build the outline of the image. Then the blocks in the enhancement layer
will be classified into three types: SMOOTH, TEXTURE, and OTHER. The classification
is done by using the statistics of pixels of each block. Then, different rates are allocated to
different types of blocks, TEXTURE-type blocks are assigned a high rate. The results show
that ABCS method achieved a better quality in TEXTURE-type blocks.
The paper [9] proposed a region-adaptive DCT block partitioning method. This method
will first divide the input images into 32x32 sized blocks, then evaluate the texture homo-
geneity metrics of each DCT block. If a block is a homogeneously textured region (HTR),
which means a region with a similar texture regularity, the block will not be divided any
further. In other words, the method will tend to assign larger DCT blocks to the region with
Page 2 of 61
==PAGE:7==
similar texture regularity. In the experimental results, despite the traditional metrics such as
PSNR and SSIM, this paper uses many metrics that are closer to human visual perception,
such as Butteraugli and VIF. The paper also performed a human subjective evaluation with
15 participants. The result shows that the proposed method achieves a better performance
than default JPEG XL at the same bpp, and it is more resistant to blocking and blurring ar-
tifacts. In conclusion, this paper proposes a region-adaptive DCT block partitioning method
to improve JPEG XL coding; the idea is to assign larger DCT blocks to the region with
similar texture regularity. The result shows that the proposed method achieves a better per-
formance than default JPEG XL at the same bpp. This shows that optimizing the existing
JPEG XL workflow is feasible.
These previous works have demonstrated that by adjusting the compression levels of
the foreground and background, we can achieve a clearer foreground at the same bpp. After
reviewing the relevant literature, we consider adjusting the quantization levels of the ROI
and background to be a general approach in ROI-prioritized encoding. In our work, we also
adopt this method.
Our another approach is to prioritize the encoding of the foreground data in the data
stream without changing the compression levels of the foreground and background, then
progressively loading the image. With this approach, we can maintain the quality of the
background area once the image is fully loaded, while ensuring that the foreground gradu-
ally becomes clearer during the loading process. This eliminates the need to set different
compression rates for the foreground and background. Additionally, when the desired bpp
changes, we only need to adjust the amount of loaded data rather than re-encoding the
image. Since the foreground region is encoded first, the decoded image will always have a
clearer foreground compared to the default encoding method under the same bpp.

3.1
Introduction of lossy image coding
Lossy image coding is strongly connected to data compression. In our work, we use
JPEG XL as the image encoding tool. Its lossy image compression pipeline follows the same
general structure as JPEG, as Fig. 1 shown, although there are differences in implementation
details. For example, JPEG XL employs variable-sized DCT, while JPEG uses fixed-sized
DCT; JPEG XL uses Asymmetric Numeral Systems (ANS) [10] for entropy coding, whereas
JPEG relies on Huffman coding or Arithmetic coding. Overall, as a modern successor to
JPEG, JPEG XL introduces various innovations for improved compression efficiency and
image quality, but it still adheres to the traditional image coding framework, which consists
of DCT, quantization, and entropy coding.
This framework has stood the test of time.
Therefore, in this chapter, we will introduce the process of JPEG image coding to provide a
concise overview of lossy image encoding, which also serves as a general outline of the JPEG
XL lossy image coding.

3.1
Introduction of lossy image coding
3.1.1
DCT
Just like we can convert a time domain signal into frequency components representation
by Fourier transform, we can represent the image data as the sum of lots of cosine waves.
In this way, we can get rid of some of the higher frequency signals, and the general gist of
the image will still be there. In JPEG, we split each image into 8x8 pixel blocks, and each
of those pixel groups is separately encoded with its own discrete cosine transform. Each 8x8
block can be represented by the weighted sum of 8x8=64 cosine waves (basis images), we
can achieve compression by setting some of the DCT coefficients to zero, normally the high
frequency components.
Each image of the basis matrices is an 8x8 image, as Fig. 2 shown. It can be seen as the
product of two cosine waves. The frequency increased from top to bottom and from left to
right. The weighted sum of these basis images can make any 8x8 image. The basis matrix
corresponding to the DC coefficient is a constant matrix (Top left).
Figure 2: The basis matrices for the DCT [12].
In JPEG-XL, the block size or the DCT size will not be fixed, it can be larger like 32x32
or smaller like 4x4. This is determined by the texture of local regions in the image, which
will be discussed in more detail in later sections.

3.1.2
Quantization
Quantization enhances image compression by retaining only the level of detail neces-
sary to preserve the desired visual quality. In other words, it discards some high-frequency
components of the image data that have little impact on perceived visual quality.
The amount of compression of each DCT coefficient is controlled by the corresponding
elements in the quantization table. The elements in the quantization table representing the
high frequency are bigger, meaning more compression on a higher frequency. The human
eye is more sensitive to luminance signals than to colour difference signals, so we used two
different quantization tables for the luminance channel and chrominance channel.
Instead of fixed quantization tables, JPEG XL adopts adaptive quantization. Adaptive
quantization uses per-block quantization step sizes that also guide the restoration filter,
which will be discussed in more detail in later sections.
3.1.3
Zigzag scan
After the DCT and the quantization, most of the coefficients are concentrated in the
upper left corner of the block, which is also called the low-frequency component region. By
arranging the elements in zigzag pattern, as Fig. 3 shown, the zeros will be more likely to
be grouped together and reduce the size of the compressed data.
Figure 3: 8x8 zigzag scanning pattern [12].

In the field of information theory, entropy coding refers to any lossless data compression

We will briefly introduce Huffman coding in JPEG here, it is a commonly used entropy
coding in JPEG encoding. Huffman coding deals with DC and AC in a different way.
The DC coefficients are Differential pulse-code modulation (DPCM) coded. Because the
basis matrix for the DC coefficient is a constant matrix. This means that the DC coefficient
is simply a multiple of the average value of the 8x8 block. The average pixel value in one
8x8 block is usually very similar to the average in the block next to it, so the DC coefficients
of adjacent blocks are typically quite similar too.
For the AC part, we use Run-Length Coding, which maps a sequence of numbers into
symbols. The JPEG chose Run-Length Encoding of Zeros to efficiently handle blocks with
little high-frequency content.
Baseline sequential entropy coding involves two main steps: first, turning the quantified
DCT coefficients into a sequence of symbols, and then, giving these symbols variable-length
codes.
Each non-zero AC coefficient is represented by a pair of symbols as Fig. 4 shown.

starts. Such choices are the business of the applications which use JPEG; the JPEG proposal
specifies no required Huffman tables [11].

Huffman coding, since it is a stream-based encoding, just like Arithmetic coding. We will
discuss about that in later sections.

3.2
JPEG XL
JPEG-XL is a next-generation image compression architecture for efficient compression
of high-quality images. The JPEG [1] standard, released more than 30 years ago, is still
the most widely used lossy image compression standard. In the attempt to replace JPEG
with more efficient image coding, people find it difficult to migrate existing JPEG images
to another format.
JPEG-XL enables lossless transform from JPEG files to JPEG XL
files. The JPEG XL file can be restored into the exact same JPEG file, ensuring backward
compatibility with existing JPEG-based applications. This compatibility makes JPEG XL
a promising candidate to replace JPEG and become the new dominant image coding.
JPEG-XL can achieve significantly smaller size at equivalent subjective quality, an ex-
periment showed that it can reach a 50% size reduction over JPEG. In the experiment, 36
healthy adult participants were asked to indicate the lowest quality that they would find
acceptable to compress pictures with no perceptible loss of quality. The result showed lower
BPP value was consistently selected for JPEG XL compared to legacy JPEG, as Fig. 5
shown. The mean of selected BPP of JPEG XL was 48% lower than standard JPEG [2].
Figure 5: The result of experiment [2].
JPEG-XL uses parallelization decoding and encoding, which can achieve a faster coding
speed. It also supplies features such as progressive coding, lossless coding. JPEG-XL is
suitable for high-quality images with wide gamut, high resolution or bit depth [2].

3.2.1
Data structure for input image
If the input image is in JPEG format, JPEG XL encoder will not decode the input
JPEG image and store it into pixels by default, it will lossless transcode JPEG data. If it
is lossless JPEG mode, the JPEG image data will be directly stored in jpeg_bytes. If the
input image is not in JPEG format, it will be decoded first and stored in a structure called
PackedPixelFile. Also note that if the input image is in JPEG format, but lossless_jpeg
is not set to False, the input JPEG image will also be decoded and stored in PackedPixelFile
.
PackedPixelFile is the data structure for storing the data of decoded input image
[13].
It can include multiple frames PackedFrame.
Although we typically think of that
one image is equal to one frame, but JPEG XL supports multi-frame images (for example,
animations, or images with multiple layers), so the term "frame" is used internally to handle
each individual piece of image content.
In our project, we will use static images as input so we only have one PackedFrame in
PackedPixelFile. All the color channels in an image are stored in one PackedImage inside
PackedFrame.
The pixels of the input image are stored in the private variable pixels_ inside the
PackedImage.
std::unique_ptr<void, decltype(free)*> pixels_;
pixels_ is a smart pointer to an unknown type (void), the class of deleter is decltype(
free), which means the runner will use free to release the memory the smart pointer points
to when it leaves the scope.
Function const_pixels is used to retrieve the value of a pixel from pixels_.
const uint8_t* const_pixels(size_t y, size_t x, size_t c) const {
return (reinterpret_cast<const uint8_t*>(pixels_.get()) + y * stride +
x * pixel_stride_ + c * bytes_per_channel_);
}
pixels_.get() will return a raw pointer, which points to the beginning of this memory
location.
Since the type it points to is void, a reinterpret_cast is used to explicitly
inform the compiler that the memory will be accessed as uint8_t (1 byte). Hence it is not
converting the pointer address into uint8_t here.
The pixels of an image after decoding are stored in pixels, continuous in heap memory.
Since uint8_t is 1 byte, so reinterpret_cast<const uint8_t*>(pixels_.get())+ 1 will
move the pointer by 1 byte, and reinterpret_cast<const uint32_t*>(pixels_.get())+
1 will move the pointer by 4 bytes.

3.2.2
Variable DCT
JPEG uses a fixed-sized DCT with size 8x8 for all the areas in one image. In JPEG XL,
the unit for DCT is still an 8x8 block, but the encoder will also choose to use variable-sized
DCTs based on the local features of the image, for example, it will use 16x16 DCT or 32x32
DCT for homogeneous areas, and will use small DCTs of size 2x2 or 4x4 for heterogeneous
areas.
Note that the output of DCT will still be stored in 8x8 blocks, for 4x4 DCT, the
transform is applied on each of the 4x4 areas in the 8x8 block, then the encoder will mix the
coefficients so that DC ends up in the top-left 2x2 corner, a 2x2 DCT is applied on the DC
coefficients so that we only have one DC coefficient for this 8x8 block [2].
For larger DCTs, the problem is that we only have one DC coefficient, the encoder will
split this coefficient into multiple sub-DC coefficients for each block by some manipulation.
To ensure that the DCT coefficients are still stored in 8x8 blocks.
According to JPEG XL source code [13], each 8x8 block will be marked by a structure
AcStrategy.
AcStrategy will specify the actual block transform strategy for this block,
such as 8x8, 16x16 or 32x32 and so on, also it will flag whether this block is the first block
in its superblock.
3.2.3
Adaptive quantization
JPEG uses a fixed quantization table for each color channel, and the size of quantization
table is fixed as 8x8. JPEG XL uses variable-sized quantization tables for each variable-
sized DCT, and we can adjust the quantization level for each var-DCT or even each block.
Adaptive quantization uses per-block quantization step sizes, which also guide the restoration
filter [14].
The advantage of this approach is that it allows dynamic adjustment of the quantization
level based on specific requirements. If a higher compression ratio is desired, the quantization
level can be increased in regions with more detail to more aggressively reduce high-frequency
components. Conversely, if higher image quality is the priority, more bits can be allocated

==PAGE:17==
3.2
JPEG XL
to heterogeneous regions to better preserve image quality.
According to JPEG XL source code [13], the quantization is done on the var-DCT level
via the information in AcStrategy. Normally, the blocks inside the same block transform
strategy (superblock) will have the same quantization level scaling.
But it is also possible to adjust the quantization level of each block, since the quantiza-
tion level is stored in an internal matrix ImageF* quant_field for each block, not for each
var-DCT superblock.
3.2.4
Entropy coding
The JPEG format adapts Huffman coding or Arithmetic coding as entropy coder. JPEG
XL uses Asymmetric Numeral Systems (ANS) [10] as entropy coder. ANS was introduced
by Jarek Duda in 2014, it can achieve the compression rate of arithmetic coding and the
speed of Huffman coding. The advantage of ANS is that it addresses a common drawback
of stream-based coding: the decoding process tends to be very slow.
JPEG XL uses rANS as entropy coding, which is similar to Arithmetic coding, it is
a stream-based encoding, it does not provide a codeword for each symbol. The encoder
will generate tokens(symbols) for each DCT coefficient, then use all the tokens to generate
global AC entropy information. Instead of fixed codeword tables, the encoder will use the
information to streamy encode each token by state transitions and (occasionally) flushed
bits.
Tokens are intermediate states between DCT coefficients and codewords, they are called
symbols in JPEG. The Huffman coding in JPEG will transform the DCT coefficients into
symbols like (RUNLENGTH, SIZE), the probability distribution of these symbols is then used
to generate the Huffman table, where each symbol can be converted into a codeword.
The tokens in JPEG XL are represented as (ctx, u_coeff). ctx is calculated from the
current number of remaining non-zero coefficients in block, the position of the coefficient in
the zigzag scan order, and whether the previous coefficient is zero and so on. It represents
the context of the coefficient. u_coeff is the value of the coefficient converted to an unsigned
integer and retains the sign information.

The unit for entropy coding is a 256x256 group, which means we can easily trace how

We can easily track how many bits are used for encoding one group, but it is difficult

inside the same ANS stream.
In ANS process, the output corresponding to each token is uncertain and depends
entirely on its previous state. That is, it is also inaccurate to track how many bits the
output stream increases after the encoding of a token, because ANS will flush some bits
after the state meets certain conditions. When the output stream increases by 16 bits after
encoding a token, these 16 bits may not be entirely the information of this token, but are
related to the previous tokens and are likely related to the coefficients outside this 8x8 block.

has two primary parameters: num_coefficients and shift.
num_coefficients must be greater than 1, and at most 8. It is the size of left-top
square of an 8x8 block. For example, num_coefficients=2 means this pass only includes
each 2x2 left-top square of each 8x8 block.

shift value represents the number of bits by which each coefficient is right-shifted when
interpreted as a binary number. So if a coefficient value is 5, and shift=2, after shifting it
will become 1.

4.1
Optimization of lossy JPEG XL encoding by CVVDP heatmap
We plan to optimize the lossy JPEG XL encoding by CVVDP heatmap. Using the
information in CVVDP heatmap, our method enables the JPEG XL encoder to allocate
more bits to regions with severe distortion, while assigning fewer bits to regions with only
minor distortion.
4.1.1
CVVDP heatmap
ColorVideoVDP (CVVDP) [3] is a perceptual image or video quality metric designed to
predict the perceived difference between a reference image and a distorted image. It is built
on the human visual system (HVS) and a psychophysical model to simulate how humans
perceive color and structural distortions. It outputs a Just-Objectionable-Difference (JOD)
score to quantify the overall perceptual difference, where a higher score indicates higher
image quality.
In addition to the scalar JOD score, CVVDP provides a detailed heatmap to visualize
the perceptual difference across different regions of an image.
For example, in supra−
threshold mode, the areas with large differences will be rendered in yellow color, and the
areas with small differences will be rendered in blue color, as Fig. 11a shown. The heatmap
is composed of per-pixel distortion intensity, which is used to help interpret the single-value
JOD score.
When we enable the output of heatmap, we can choose from one of these three modes:
supra−threshold, threshold and raw.
According to the source code of CVVDP, if we choose heatmap=raw, the heatmap will
be initialized as a single channel two-dimensional matrix, and its size is consistent with the
original image resolution, so the output heatmap image will be black and white. If we choose
other modes, the heatmap will be initialized as a three channels two-dimensional matrix, so
the output heatmap will be a color image.
If we choose heatmap=raw, the content of heatmap will be diff_map. diff_map records
the relative intensity of visual differences at each pixel, with 0 indicating no difference and
1 representing the strongest observed difference. It is not directly the JOD score but serves
as the basis for generating both the visual heatmap and the overall JOD score.
If we choose other modes, diff_map will be rendered into different color heatmaps
according to different color mapping strategies, helping humans to understand the quality
differences across various regions of the image.
diff_map values from 0 to 1 are mapped to a JOD range of 0 to 10. A value of 0.1 (1
JOD unit) means the distortion is barely perceptible, in other words, it reach the perception
threshold, so if we choose threshold mode, the area with distortion within 1 JOD unit

==PAGE:25==

will be rendered from blue to red, where blue means small distortion and red means large
distortion, however, the area with distortion larger than 1 JOD will be all red.
If we choose supra−threshold mode, the area with distortion within 3 JOD units will
be rendered from light blue to yellow, where light blue means small distortion and yellow
means large distortion. The area with distortion larger than 3 JOD units will be all yellow.
A value of 0.3 (3 JOD units) in diff_map means a clearly noticeable difference.
Hence, threshold mode emphasizes subtle differences, while supra−threshold mode
is designed to highlight larger, more objectionable differences. The comparison of these two

4.1.3
Adjust the quantization level for each block
As stated in Section 3.2.3, the quantization level for each block is stored in an internal
matrix ImageF* quant_field. ImageF means the value inside the quant_field is float, the
size of this matrix is the number of 8x8 blocks.
The float type quantization levels quant_field will be transformed into integer quanti-

step.

From the JPEG XL source code above we can find that quant_field is multiplied
by inv_global_scale_ to transform into raw_quant_field, the inv_global_scale_ is the
inverse global scale. Hence, the larger the value of quant_field, the smaller the resulting
raw_quant_field, meaning the actual quantization level is lower, and vice versa.
In var-DCT mode, a large block (e.g., 16x16) may consist of multiple smaller 8x8 sub-
blocks. Initially, each sub-block might have its own quantization level. However, to reduce
blocking artifacts or bitrate overhead caused by differences between neighboring blocks, it is
often necessary to unify the quantization levels across these sub-blocks.
The AdjustQuantField function in enc_adaptive_quantization.cc is implemented
for this purpose. The strategy here is to take either the maximum quantization level among
all sub-blocks within the large block, or a weighted combination with the average, and then
apply this unified value back to all the sub-blocks.
When high visual quality is required, using the maximum quantization level within the
block helps prevent some sub-blocks from being quantized too coarsely, resulting in better
perceived quality.

4.3
Optimization of progressive JPEG XL encoding by ROI mask
The above two methods optimize JPEG XL by non-uniformly adjusting the quantization
level of 8x8 blocks based on auxiliary information. Apart from these, we also want to explore
whether the ROI mask can be used to influence the order of groups in progressive coding,
so that ROI regions are loaded earlier during the progressive decoding process. This aims to
improve the overall perceived image quality during the progressive coding process.
We used the same method as stated in Section 4.2 to generate the ROI mask.
Since the group size for progressive coding is 256x256, we need to transform the ROI
mask into a binary file where each byte represents whether this group is inside ROI. Hence,
we calculate the percentage of pixels inside ROI for each 256x256 group in the mask. We
also set a threshold of 50%, which means if half of the pixels in one group are inside ROI,
we consider this group as a ROI group. This is better than purely considering the center of
the group, because a block typically covers a relatively large area, at the edges of the ROI,
it is possible that the block’s center does not fall within the ROI, even though most of the
block actually lies inside the ROI.
The ROI mask here has the same size as the number of groups in an image, and each
byte in it represents whether the group is in ROI. We intended to use this file as input to
JPEG XL to change the group order of progressive coding.
We used the same modification of JPEG XL source code as in Section 4.1, since we want
to reuse the process of loading a binary mask and storing it in the encoder parameters. But
we did not pass the JPEG XL parameters quant_inc_ratio and quant_dec_ratio so that
the ROI and background will not have different quantization levels, since these parameters
have default values of 1.
According to the JPEG XL source code[13], the JPEG XL encoder will choose to use
streaming mode based on preset encoding speed level and compression level.
When the
streaming mode is enabled, the encoder minimizes memory usage at the cost of compression
density, also greatly increasing the encoding speed. But the images produced with streaming
mode might not be progressively decodeable. Since encoding speed is not our concern, we
choose to manually disable that to ensure that the output .jxl file can be correctly decoded.
The code responsible for ordering groups in progressive coding is located in the PermuteGroups
function within the enc_frame.cc file. This function reorders the AC group encoding order
by preset ordering type. It takes group_codes as input, which are the bitstreams for each
group after entropy coding, in their original order.
If we choose the center_first mode in progressive coding, each group is ordered based
on the value returned by get_distance_from_center(group_id), where groups closer to
the specified center group are placed earlier in the order. To prioritize ROI regions, we
modify get_distance_from_center to include whether the current group is the ROI group
as the primary sorting criterion. By doing so, ROI groups will always be ordered ahead of
non-ROI groups in the progressive coding order.

5.2.1
CVVDP
ColorVideoVDP(CVVDP) [3] predicts the perceptual difference between images, it
is built on psychophysical models.
The prediction is represented by Just-Objectionable-
Difference (JOD) score, this value is regressed from the pooled visual differences values
across the bands and channels. Where 10 represents the highest quality, indicating that
the test and reference image are identical. The drop of 1 in JOD score means that 75% of
observers will notice such a loss of quality. The overall structure of CVVDP is shown in Fig.
15.
Figure 15: CVVDP Overview [3].
The JOD score provided by CVVDP is based on its display mode, which defines the
perceived similarity between the reference image and the test image under specific display
conditions. A display mode specifies parameters such as display resolution, maximum lumi-
nance, contrast, and ambient lighting. Therefore, it is important to select an appropriate
display mode that matches the resolution and viewing conditions of the test images. For
example, since our test images have a resolution of 5888x3840, using the standard_fhd dis-
play mode would be inappropriate, since it has a limited resolution of 1920x1080. So we
use the standard_4k display mode instead, which represents an SDR monitor with higher
resolution. It is also the default display mode in CVVDP.
CVVDP lacks higher-level models of saliency, therefore, its score will lack the impact of
salient areas in the image on the perceptual image quality assessment.

5.2.2
FVVDP
FovVideoVDP(FVVDP) [16] is a visual quality metric that predicts the perceptual
difference between pairs of images and videos. Its key idea is to take into account the visual
characteristics of the human eye, especially the significant difference in the ability of the
fovea of the human eye to distinguish details. When viewing videos or images, the human
eye tends to focus on the center of the picture or the region of interest (ROI), which affects
the subjective perception of distortion.
FVVDP gives different weights to the degree of
distortion at different spatial positions by introducing the mechanism of foveation and visual
attention distribution, thereby more accurately simulating the sensitivity of the human eye
to distortion in different areas when watching videos or images.
Conventional video visual difference models often perform distortion calculations with
the same weight at each pixel position, without additional consideration of the non-uniformity
of the human eye in spatial resolution and attention distribution. FVVDP uses insights from
physiological and psychological vision studies about where the human eye focuses and how
resolution decreases as distance increases in the periphery of the field of view to weight
and modulate distortion sensitivity to better match human subjective perception under real
viewing conditions.
By including the distinction between the foveal area and peripheral vision, FVVDP can
make more precise judgments about distortions in salient (noticeable) regions. This aligns
more closely with the actual subjective perception of humans. However, ColorVideoVDP
mainly measures color and brightness distortions in a uniform way across the whole image. It
does not model visual attention as FVVDP does, so it cannot evaluate noticeable distortions
in salient regions as accurately.
Therefore, ColorVideoVDP does not distinguish the areas that viewers are most likely
to focus on, while FVVDP adjusts its measurements according to human visual attention.
So when we have a clear ROI mask and we want to pay more attention to the image quality
of ROI, FVVDP can give us a more appropriate overall image quality assessment result.

5.2.3
Bjøntegaard Delta Rate
The Bjøntegaard Delta-Rate (BD) [17] metric is a commonly used method to compare
the performance of different video or image compression methods. It was introduced by Gisle
Bjøntegaard to help evaluate video coding standards by providing an average performance
difference across a range of quality or bitrate levels.
When we have two rate-distortion curves produced by two different methods, we would
like to compare the performance difference in numerical form.
Initially we have several
bitrate-quality pairs for each method, usually by running them with different quantization
levels, then we need to perform interpolation to generate the rate-distortion curve for each
method. We fit each curve with piece-wise cubic interpolation, but we do it on the logarithm
of the bitrate so that the high-bitrate region does not dominate the result. Now we can
compare the performance of different methods by comparing the quality of the two methods
at a fixed bitrate, or comparing the bitrate of the two methods at a fixed quality.
Figure 17: Computation of BD-PSNR [18].
Next we want to compare the performance difference between the two methods over a
certain interval, rather than at a single point. Inside that common interval we integrate each
curve to obtain the area under it, as Fig. 17 shown. The BD-Rate is then the difference
between the two areas divided by the width of the interval, converted back from the log
scale and expressed as a percentage. In the comparison between Method B and Method A, a
negative BD Rate means Encoder A provides better compression efficiency since it achieved
a lower bitrate for the same quality. When we integrate over bitrate instead of quality, the
same procedure gives BD-PSNR, which is reported in decibels. A positive BD-PSNR means
Method A can achieve a better image quality at the same bitrate.
The BD metric averages over a range, it is more stable than a single-point comparison

fact that the ROI typically exhibits more complex structures, while the background tends
to be more homogeneous.
By allocating more bits to the ROI, we are able to better reconstruct structurally rich
areas of the image, which in turn leads to a slight improvement in SSIM.

5.5
Optimization of progressive JPEG XL encoding by ROI mask
We used the test image in Section 5.1. The reference image is cropped such that its
dimensions (height and width) are divisible by 256 to avoid the issue of padding.
We generated a ROI mask ROI_mask_256.bin via the method stated in Section 4.3, this
file indicates whether each group lies inside the ROI.
We used this mask as the input to JPEG XL, enabling progressive coding. We set
the quality level to 90 to ensure the output image is of high quality. Progressive coding is
more suitable for high-quality images, as it allows gradual refinement of image details. For
low-quality images, it is generally more efficient to load and decode the entire image at once.
We generated two files for comparison. image_progressive_ROI.jxl is generated as
described above, the ROI regions are given higher priority and placed earlier in the bitstream.
We also generate image_progressive.jxl to serve as a representation of the default pro-
gressive mode, we set the quality to 90 as well and set group_order=1, so that the center
of the image will be placed in the bitstream first, then grows concentric squares around the
center.
Since we have disabled streaming mode, the output file is progressively decodable. To
compare the performance of our method and the default method under different data loading
percentages or BPP, we truncate the .jxl file to simulate the process of progressive image
loading. The BPP will be the size of .jxl file divided by the size of the image. Although
the .jxl file also includes some header data, but their size is relatively small compared to
the image data, so we can just ignore them.
To truncate the .jxl file. We use head command of Linux to get the first N bytes of
data of a .jxl file, and then store the data into a new .jxl file.
The truncated .jxl file can be decoded by JPEG XL decoding tool djxl, if the param-
eter allow_partial_files is set.
To verify the impact of the ROI mask on group ordering, we extracted the first 550 KB
of data from the files generated by both methods. This simulates a scenario in progressive
image loading where only a portion of the bitstream is received. The truncated data was
then decoded, and distortion heatmaps between decoded images and the reference image
were generated by CVVDP.

According to Fig. 38, we observe that the default method performs better during the
loading of the first pass, while our method shows superior performance during the second
pass. This is a result of the uneven spatial distribution of image details, as well as the
non-uniform distribution of information across the two passes. Most of the high-frequency
content in the image is concentrated within the ROI, whereas the background, aside from
the palette area, is largely homogeneous.
The first pass stores the information of each AC coefficient shifted left by 2 bits, which
can be considered the low-frequency component of the AC coefficients. The second pass
stores the remaining two bits, which correspond to finer high-frequency details. Since the
background contains little high-frequency information, most of its information is stored in
the first pass. On the other hand, the ROI holds a significant amount of high-frequency
information, much of which is stored in the second pass. Therefore, during the loading of
the second pass, prioritizing the ROI leads to better reconstruction performance.

6
Conclusion
This work presents a set of techniques to enhance JPEG XL lossy image compression
by incorporating auxiliary information, including perceptual distortion maps from CVVDP
and semantic ROI masks. By modifying the encoder to accept binary masks as input, we
enable spatially adaptive quantization that allocates bits according to distortion severity or
region importance.
When using CVVDP heatmap as the auxiliary information, at very low quality setting
(quality = 10) this method shifts the PSNR-bpp curve upward; Bjøntegaard Delta-Rate
metric shows an average PSNR increase of 0.165 dB or, equivalently, a 5.97% bitrate saving at
equal quality. At medium quality (quality = 30) the same method produces negligible benefit.
In conclusion, experiments show that our method can achieve marginal improvements in
visual quality, particularly at relatively low-quality images.
We further introduce a local rate-distortion refinement step, which selectively preserves
only those quantization changes that contribute positively to global rate-distortion perfor-
mance. Since we are unable to obtain the rate of individual 8x8 blocks due to the nature of
rANS encoding, we can only perform coarse estimation at the level of 256x256 groups. As a
result, our method did not achieve very good outcomes.
When using ROI mask as the auxiliary information, with a moderate adjustment the
encoder preserves high-frequency detail in the foreground while gently increasing compression
in the background. Across qualities from 10 to 90 the resulting PSNR-bpp, CVVDP-bpp
and FVVDP-bpp curves lie on or above the default ones, and at the highest point the same
perceptual quality is obtained with roughly 0.10 bpp less data. Increasing the foreground
boost to a ratio of 2.0 offers no further improvement, indicating diminishing returns once
the foreground has been given sufficient precision.
We also extend our method to progressive JPEG XL encoding by modifying the group
permutation logic to prioritize ROI groups. With the first 40% of the data the proposed
order already meets its peak CVVDP and FVVDP scores, whereas the default center-first
order needs an additional 0.75 bpp to reach the same level. Corresponding heatmaps show
that the foreground is clear during the progressive loading.
Although the absolute improvements are modest, the results confirm the feasibility of
perceptually and semantically informed JPEG XL encoding. These methods are compatible
with the standard bitstream and require only encoder-side modifications, making them a
practical step toward more intelligent image compression.
The results also expose clear limitations. First, all the experiments rely on a single por-
trait photograph; broader evaluation is required to confirm generality across diverse content.
Second, the ROI masks are binary, so the classification between ROI blocks and non-ROI
blocks is abrupt and may invite visible edges in some scenes. Third, the lack of detailed
rate-distortion on block-level hinders our rate-distortion refinement method, an analytical
approximation of the number of bits for tokens may help.
Page 57 of 61
==PAGE:62==
Future research should therefore address three directions. First, we can use a larger
dataset, including landscapes, low-light images and graphics, it would reveal how strongly
the observed gains depend on content. Second, we can adopt multi-level or continuous ROI
masks to provide smoother bit allocation without manual thresholds. Third, we can use
quantization optimization guided by neural models that predict perceptual loss, while still
preserving the structure of the JPEG XL coding. Together these steps would move the
JPEG XL coding system closer to a fully content-adaptive, perception-optimized image-
coding framework.
