Summary
In a modern society it’s expensive and time consuming to maintain the ever
growing road network, research shows that damaged roads has negative effects,
it can cause both accidents and increase the cost of repair needed on a drivers
car. The goal of the thesis is to see how the use of machine learning on car
sensor data, especially car acceleration perpendicular to the road, can asses road
damages enabling the damaged roads to be maintained better and faster. At this
time most research in this area focuses on classifying potholes and cracks, this
thesis uses the approach of regression, with the target being a combined damage
index (DI) to asses all kinds of road damages and the severity of those damages.
The thesis tries two approaches of solving the problem, first a Random Forest
Regressor is used on features extracted from the acceleration data, this approach
reaches a R2 value of 0.327 on synthetic data and a R2 value of 0.349 on real
data from cars. The second approach is using convolutional neural networks on
scalograms calculated from the acceleration data, this approach reaches a R2
value of 0.308 on synthetic data, and a R2 value of 0.109 on real data from cars.
The performances isn’t as high as one would think, but since there is a mismatch
between training data and target values, as the training data is collected from
cars driving on two wheel paths, compared to the target value which covers the
entire lane of the road and not just the wheel path of a personal car. With this
in mind obtaining a R2 value of 0.349 might be a higher performance than it
first indicates.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The road network in modern society has become a necessity, and is ever growing,
resulting in a time consuming and expensive task. Damaged road contributes
to a lot of negative effects, both on the driver and the government that is
maintaining the roads. For the driver it can be dangerous to drive on faulty road,
according to data from USA, it is estimated that 1/3 of traffic accidents is caused
by poor road conditions[2]. It is also more expensive for the driver to drive on
faulty roads, according to an US research group it results in an additional 599
US dollars annually[3] to repair the vehicle. From the government/municipality
side it is time consuming and expensive to detect the road anomalies such as
potholes, cracks and other pavement anomalies.
Currently in Denmark, the
Danish Road Directorate detect these anomalies either by a public app that
citizens can send them documentation of faults, or by using ARAN (Automatic
Road Analyzer), which utilizes laser- and 3D surface scanning. Such profilers
as ARAN are expensive to own and therefore makes data collection rare on the
large modern road network.
Since this is and has been an issue for some time, countries across the world
and researchers began studies how to solve it better and cheaper.
This can
be seen in [4] as they show that since 2007, the number of papers surrounding
road damages and smartphone, is averaging an annual growth of 11%. This
shows the interest of solving the issue at hand is gaining more traction, and is
in need of a good solution. The majority of solutions proposed are the use of

Machine Learning methods, on the data from accelerometers data and gyroscope
data captured from a smartphone. Newer publication shows that the solutions
proposed are trying out the use of Deep Learning networks, so the prepossessing
step of doing feature extraction reduced, which is also shown in [4].
This thesis is in collaboration with the Live Road Assessment (LiRA) project[1],
the LiRA project is using data from car sensors from regular road cars to de-
velop models to asses the road condition on the danish road network. The data
collected from LiRA is acceleration data perpendicular to the road, the ground
truth data comes from a state-of-the-art Laser Cracking Measurement System.
The LiRA project consists of different partners, such as the Danish Road Direc-
torate, DTU Compute department, GreenMobility and SWECO, and together
they aim to develop a new method of road assessment. The LiRA project has
different goals targeting the International Roughness Index (IRI), energy con-
sumption and DI. This thesis will focus on the latter, which is the DI, and how
the DI measures the amount of potholes and cracks in the pavement.
1.1
Research questions
The thesis will focus on and answer the following research questions
• How well can the use of a machine learning method solve the regression
problem, which is to determine the severity of road damage?
• Can the use of a deep learning model improve the performance on accel-
eration data?
• How well is the information in the data learned and does the results reflect
it?
• Which steps are necessary to further analyze to implement the use of a
machine learning method in the real world setting?
1.2
Related works
The literature review will look into how other researchers have solved the issue
of detected road damage, and what is currently being researched in the area of
road damage. Further the topic of time series data will be examined, to discover
what is the state-of-the-art methods and how other researchers with time series
data in other areas have solved their problems.

1.2.1
Related works on road damage
The early works on road damage detection is by making a filter. Here the data
is collected from taxi’s with an accelerometer attached, the ground truth is a
passenger hand labeling each event. Eriksson et al. [5] uses a basic algorithm
which classify based on z-peaks, xz-ratio and speed vs z ratio, and is trained
by doing a grid search. This method achieved a 90% success rate of detected
severe road anomalies.
Later work started using machine learning approaches, such as Support Vector
Machine (SVM) [6][7][8] as it is more flexible compared to threshold algorithms.
Seraj et al. [6] trained a SVM to classify the road anomalies on accelerometer
data from smartphones. They extracted features from time domain, frequency
domain and wavelet decomposition. The ground truth is from a passenger using
a microphone to label the data, the SVM obtained an accuracy ≈90% of severe
anomalies. Other machine learning approaches which have been used in multiple
papers is Decision Trees (DT) [8][7], K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) [7] and Neural
Networks (NN) [8].
Basavaraju et al. [8] used multiple Machine Learning methods, such as SVM,
DT and NN. The paper classifies into multiple classes, cracks, potholes and
smooth road.
The data used was collected from smartphones, with features
extracted from time domain, frequency domain and wavelet domain, and the
ground truth is labelled from a second smartphone filming the road. The paper
tests different number of folds for cross validation and different implementations
of the methods. The best for SVM is 90% accuracy for only z-axis, with low
avgerage. precision and recall for cracks (20%-40%), avgerage. precision and
recall for pothole around 65%-75% and avg. precision and recall for smooth
around 94%-98%. The best for DT is 88% accuracy with all axis data, with low
avg. precision and recall for cracks around 42%, avg. precision and recall for
pothole around 67% and avg. precision and recall for smooth around 94%. The
best for feedforward NN is 91% with all axis data, with low avg. precision and
recall for cracks around 49%-52%, avg. precision and recall for pothole around
75%-78% and avg. precision and recall for smooth around 96%-97%.
Martinelli et al. [7] also test different Machine Learning methods, such as SVM,
KNN and DT. The paper classifies into, long-time distress (cracks), no distress
(smooth road) and short-time distress (potholes). The data is collected from
smartphones as well, and the features extracted is from a STFT and the features
is coefficient of variation and entropy, where the ground truth is labelled man-
ually. The best performing DT has an accuracy of 87.9%, the best performing
KNN achieves an accuracy of 88.9% and the best SVM achieves an accuracy
of 91.9%. Looking at the different classes, the SVM achieves 97% accuracy for

Yu et al. [4] researches the topic of road damages and the use of smartphones,
they do a literature search and find that since 2007, the avg. annual growth rate
of papers written in this topic is 11%. The topic of road damage is becoming
more popular as the review shows, and it’s relevant in countries and big cities
across the world e.g. Boston [5], Texas [8], Italy [7], Albania and Netherlands
[6]. A area the topic lack is a common used data set, such that it’s easier to
compare different methods from different sources. A common data set could
also address the issue of having expert labelled ground truth for validating the
methods, as the sources do the labeling in a questionable way, e.g. [5] does a
spot check on the 48 highest-confidence defections and drives out to check if
they are correct, so no way of knowing the false negatives. A common data
set with expert labelling would also make it better to classify the severity, since
[6][7] uses classes with severity, but uses manually labelled data as ground truth,
which would inflict some biases.
Here the papers and the data they have used, is not comparable, as each have
made their own or generated their own data. Even the label process for their
data have been done with out some consistency between papers, so it most
likely bias their data and performance, which makes their performances seem
alot more optimistic than they probably are.
1.2.2
Related works on time series
As we are dealing with time series data, looking at what is being developed for
the general time series case is important. Multiple algorithms, such as ROCKET
[9], InceptionTime [10] and HIVE-COTE 2.0 [11] are getting state-of-the-art
performance on the UCR achieve, which is a achieve of 100+ datasets for time
series classification. Most state-of-the-art methods for time series is classification
problems, but going from classification to regression isn’t complicated, so looking
into classification literature is important.
Middlehurst et al. [11] uses an ensemble method (HIVE-COTE 2.0), consisting
of 4 modules for voting. First one is a Shapelet Transform Classifier, second is
the Arsenal, which is an adaption of ROCKET, third is the Temporal Dictionary
Ensemble and last one is the Diverse Representation Canonical Interval Forest.
Each module is trained independently and used as a vote that is weighted for
each module based on the quality. HIVE-COTE 2.0 outperforms the other state-
of-the-art algorithms, at the cost of run time, as running all the modules for the
ensemble is a magnitude greater than the others.
==PAGE:13==
1.3 Proposed method
5
Fawaz et al. [10] also uses an ensemble method (InceptionTime), that consists
of 5 of the same neural network, that is created by cascading multiple Inception
modules, all 5 networks is exactly the same but is trained with different random
initialized weights. InceptionTime is on par with the previous implementation
of HIVE-COTE, and has a run time orders of magnitude lower.
Dempster et al. [9] uses a method called Random Convolutional Kernel Trans-
form (ROCKET), the method is initializing 10.000 random kernels (default) and
then doing a logistic regression to classify. This makes ROCKET the clearly
fastest method and is on par with InceptionTime, in only a fraction of the time,
and even works fast on large time series.
1.2.3
Related works on similar signal related tasks
Looking into how other time series problems have been solved, I’ve looked into
heart rate data and the electrical activity in a brain, since these kind of signal
data, shares similarities to acceleration signal data. Looking at how other areas
have solved their time series problems, we can look at how Reiss et al. [12]
have solved the issue of predicting the heart rate from photoplethysmography
(PPG) signal. The model used is a CNN with 8 convolution-maxpool layers
into a flatten layer and a fully-connected layer, on the input from a spectral
analysis. They found that on larger datasets the convolutional neural network
(CNN) outperformed the older methods.
Singh et al.
[13] solves the same
problem as [12], they utilized the GoogleNet which is a deep CNN consisting
of inception layers, and the input was of a 5 second scalogram, which resulted
in them achieving high accuracy. Hasan et al. [14] classifies sleep states based
on electroencephalography (EEG) data, the model here is a Residual Network
(ResNet) with input data from a power spectral density, the model achieves
accuracy up to 88%.
1.3
Proposed method
Reviewing the literature review, it is clear that good results are obtained from
machine learning methods such as SVM’s, KNN’s and Random Forest methods
[15], and some early trials with Neural Networks show promising results. The
literature review shows that the data used in training and testing, differs from
article to article, and the ground truth lacks consistency, so comparisons is
more less impossible. This issue we target with the use of the Danish Road
Directorate expertise and vehicles. The literature review also shows that they

Data collection

10 meters prior.
Another vehicle used by DRD is the p79, which is a profilometer. The pro-
filometer is equipped with 25 lasers spread across the whole lane. These lasers
measure the distance from the car to the road and texture depth of the road.
This vehicle have driven the same routes as ARAN9000 and the GM cars, and
it collect data same as ARAN9000 and have a data point for each 10 meters
driven, expect the lasers that was placed at the wheel paths of the vehicle, laser
5 and laser 21, was sampled at a constant rate every 5 cm.

fraction is zero or close to, which is when the predicted values is equal to the
actual values, the R2 value would be 1, so this is the upper bound of the score.
If the R2 value is 0, it means the numerator is equal to the denominator, which
means the model isn’t better than just predicted the mean every time.

increases, which will make it more likely to overfit and the number of learn-
able parameters will increase the computational resources needed to train the
network. [16] proposes a structure that go deep, while still maintained relative
low learnable parameters. which is the GoogleNet, and more importantly the

mainly to reduce the dimensions of the feature maps, before doing the larger

layer then is concatenated into a single feature map, ready for the next layer in

For training the neural networks, we divide the data into 3 sets, training data,
validation data and test data, the training data is what the neural networks is
used to train, the validation set is used to figure out when to stop the neural
network training as a indication of when the networks goes from learning the
problem to overfitting the problem. At the end we use the test data set to, see
the performance of the network on unseen data, the splitting is done in the same
way as in figure 4.6.
For neural networks, the network on its own is one big hyper parameter as which
layers and how many to use, activation functions, dropout and so on, will affect
ones performance. Therefor as per the literature review we use the structure of
GoogleNet for one of the networks to test, for GoogleNet as it is a really deep
neural network, we test it at different depth and start at shallow level, we use
one of its auxiliary layers as last layer. The reason for this is we wont know if
this model will be to complex for the problem at hand, and if that is the case
we will overfit the data instead of learn it.
For a neural network to learn on a computer, a optimiser are used, here the
gradient descent method is used and improved, either in computational speed
or adding extra terms to make smarter decisions. One optimiser is the stochastic
gradient descent (SGD), SGD uses a random small part of the observations to
calculate the gradients, instead of using all of the observations. The reason is
purely computational, as it can decrease training time, but a caveat is the road
to a global minima becomes noisy.

Chapter 6
Discussion
6.1
Choice of model
We selected the first model as the Random Forest, as the literature review
showed that most previous work was done with machine learning models and
they showed good results on classification task. For this project the goal was
regression, so choosing the Random Forest model, seemed a strong candidate
as going from classification to regression here is simple. Evaluating the Ran-
dom Forest Regressor on the synthetic data, we saw a test performance of
R2 = 0.186 ± 0.114, which seemed low, especially since the standard devia-
tion is relative high compared to the R2. Comparing the test performance with
the out-of-bag performance, which was, R2 = 0.294 ± 0.03 we get a much bet-
ter score, which indicates that there could be some discrepancies in test splits.
Testing the Random Forest Regressor on the GM data, only CPH1, we got a
test score of R2 = 0.230±0.085 and a out-of-bag score of R2 = 0.278±0.017, we
see that the performance on the GM data is within the synthetic performance
intervals. The GM data performance might even be slightly better, but it could
be since the GM data is only from route CPH1, where we saw some bad R2
scores on the synthetic data, when the test data was from route CPH6.
We then used CNN’s to see if it could improve performances, as some liter-
ature showed, that utilizing CNN’s in signal data could increase performance
==PAGE:61==
6.2 Dataset evaluation
53
[13]. We therefor tested the architecture of GoogleNet [16], it reached the best
performance with a R2 value of 0.276 on the synthetic data, but the training
curves indicated that it was over regularized and still the training curve was
way higher than validation. Therefore a shorter version of GoogleNet was used,
which behaved a bit better, resulting in a R2 value of 0.308 on the synthetic
data. Still this version showed that the validation set stopped learning quite
early on, so a simpler CNN was tested. This architecture didn’t perform better,
ended with a R2 value of 0.25, therefore choosing the short version of GoogleNet,
and deploying on the GM data from CPH1, gave a performance with a R2 value
of 0.109.
From these tests, the Random Forest Regressor seemed superior, as the training
was more stable, which likely was from the use of cross validation, which was
easy to do here, since training time for a Random Forest Regressor is way faster
than training a CNN. There is no clear indication that Random Forest Regressor
is straight up better, but then again there is no indication that the use of CNN is
giving better performance, only that the training and evaluating new segments
would take longer. Since all these tests had a big difference between the test
performance and validation performance, we wanted to test out the behaviour
of the available data.
6.2
Dataset evaluation
During testing we saw some performance differences, when we tested of different
splits from the different routes. Starting with the Random Forest Regressor on
the synthetic data, we saw that when testing on the split 5 from CPH6, the test
performance was just bit bet than random guessing, with a R2 value of 0.031,
but if testing on split 3 from CPH1, the R2 value is 0.327. This indicates some
difference in the splits, showing that some splits might be to different from the
rest of the data, and some splits just have easier segments for the models to learn.
Since we are working on roads, and as talked about earlier in the thesis, that
the reason we split it in this fashion, was that the lanes going different direction
shares some correlation, as when they are maintained, they usually upgrade the
pavement on both sides. More so when two segments are neighbouring each
other, we except the same kind of correlation can happen. If we look at the out-
of-bag score from the Random Forest tests, we can compare these scores to if
we had done a leave-one-out cross validation. Lets say the data available would
contain the whole road network of Copenhagen, we would expect this kind of
performance, since every new entry could compare to some road already driven
and trained on. The performance differences was also confirmed from tests with
the short version of GoogleNet, where different splits from both routes was used

6.3
Deployment of a model to assess the road
damages
If we had to choose and deploy a model based on the work done here, choosing
the Random Forest Regressor is the best choice, as it has shown more robust
performances on test data and it has the added value of having the out-of-bag
estimate. Lets say we use the best test performance, which was R2 = 0.349
on the GM data, having this performance on a deployed model in this scenario
could be worked around, as we have the power of the GreenMobility fleet of
cars. Lets say 10 cars drove the same stretch of road during a day, and 6 out
of 10 determined that some where there was some road damage. Having the
fact that multiple cars indicated that the chance of road damage was present,
would increase the probability of actual road damage. Instead lets say only 1 car
would indicate some road damage, but the other 9 cars would indicate low road
damage, then this would be more probable answer. This means that even with
a lower performing model, having the strength of deploying multiple cars, can
strengthen the lower performing model into giving a strong probable answer.

6.4
Future work
As of any machine learning method, having more and better data is never a bad
thing. Which I would say is a strong case here, especially looking at the GM
data, especially since CPH6 was deemed unusable, leaving only 1 route to train
and test on. If the dataset would be increased, another thing I would argue that
could increase the performance and the model development the most, would be,
to have the ARAN9000 only record the data of the two wheel paths of a car.
This was looked into by LiRA, but it wasn’t accomplished for some reason. If
this could be done, it wouldn’t only increase the performance, but would make
model development better and more trustworthy. Since right now when ever a
R2 value was reported, it is hard to say if it comes from poor parameter choosing,
poor model development or just from the difference between acceleration data
(two wheel paths) and the ground truth (ARAN900 data across the whole lane).
It is hard to address what could be improved on the model development, since
the doubt of the dataset is there. Therefore looking into more domain specific
areas could be beneficial, since in this work, when doing Random Forest we
segmented into 50 meters segments, this could be tested and examined if this
was optimal, or what happens when increasing/decreasing the length. For the
CNN’s we segmented based on number of samples, which would make the length
each segment a variable (in meters), so testing this segmentation method on the
Random Forest, to see whether or not that would have an effect, as this would
make the two methods more comparable.

Conclusion
This thesis aimed at developing a model that could assess the severity of dam-
aged road pavement, this was done in collaboration with LiRA [1]. As we were
working with signal data, which is acceleration data perpendicular to the road,
we planned to develop two models that could learn the behaviour to asses dam-
aged roads, as we couldn’t be confident based on the literature review, that a
specific model would work. After reviewing the literature, it was clear that most
who solved the problem at hand, did it as a classification problem, and not a
regression problem that this project would like to do. The literature review also
showed the lack of a common dataset used between papers, so comparisons is
deemed impossible, therefor developing two models was chosen, as hopefully one
or both would work.
Before during model development started, we created a synthetic dataset to
do training and tests on, as the GM data from LiRA had been aligned and
one of the routes was deemed unusable. Therefore we generated synthetic data
by using the quarter car model, over both routes and we got a good dataset.
Then a Random Forest Regressor was trained for the synthetic dataset, we used
nested cross validation with gridsearch to both evaluate the best parameters
and evaluating the behaviour on unseen data. This resulted in a performance
of R2 = 0.186 ± 0.114 with a best performance of R2 = 0.327, then we trained
the model on the GM data to see how it performed on real acceleration data,
which resulted in a performance of R2 = 0.230±0.085, with a best performance
of R2 = 0.349. After the training it was discovered that the choice of which
==PAGE:65==
57
part of the data used for test set, was really impactful, which we can see with
the high standard deviations.
Then we trained different CNN architectures, to test if a more complex model
could better capture the data. Based on the literature review, that showed other
signal data problems had success with using GoogleNet, this was chosen as the
architecture. The best performing CNN was a shorter version of GoogleNet,
as using the fully depth of GoogleNet overfitted the problem. The best test
performance of the short GoogleNet was a R2 = 0.308 on the synthetic test and
R2 = 0.109 on the GM data. We then tested the effect of choosing different test
sets, and again the effect was greater than tuning some hyper parameters.
At the current state, deploying one of these models doesn’t guarantee any re-
sults as we have seen the performance depends heavily on, if some unused road
is chosen as test data. The out-of-bag estimate from Random Forest, does in-
dicate that if new acceleration segments added from road used during training,
it could produce some stable results, but for this to be viable the whole road
network of Copenhagen have to be included in the training data.
