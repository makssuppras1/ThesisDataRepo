Abstract (English) 
Algae are a promising source for biopolymers due to their abundance. However, in most 
applications, single components are extracted from the algae through energy- and solvent-intensive 
steps, leaving the remainder as waste or a low-value product. By separating biomass from the algae 
Ulva fenestrata into only two phases, high energy and solvent consumption can be avoided while 
utilising the whole biomass. Several different chemical and physical treatments were investigated 
that aimed at the separation of the cytoplasm from the polysaccharide-containing cell wall. In the 
extraction process, the main tissue of the algae was first separated from the holdfasts, then exposed 
to sheer forces, and finally exposed to sonication waves at a temperature of 45 °C while submerged 
in a solvent solution. Through microscopy, absorbance, and fluorescence measurements, it was 
found that the extraction was favoured by a solution of 10 vol% ethanol with the addition of 1 vol% 
of the amphoteric surfactant cocamidopropyl betaine. The recovered, treated algae combined with 
chitosan were used to form films through solvent casting. And an attempt was made to form 
crosslinks between the polysaccharides within the recovered algae as well as between the cell wall 
polysaccharides and chitosan with citric acid. The products were subjected to thermogravimetric 
analysis and used to form films in a Rapid Köthen instrument, which was used for tensile testing. The 
addition of citric acid led to decreased tensile strength and thermal stability in the samples. The 
findings reported here may serve as a foundation for future research into making isolated algae cell 
walls a viable source of biomaterials.

1. Introduction 
Due to the increasing environmental concerns about the use of fossil-based materials, a need for 
alternative, sustainable material sources has arisen. As an alternative to petroleum-based materials, 
biomass as a sustainable material resource is of increasing interest. In addition to avoiding the use of 
fossil-based materials, the advantage of biomass is that, through the uptake of carbon dioxide during 
the plant’s growth, carbon is fixated, reducing the total amount of greenhouse gases in the 
atmosphere. However, using terrestrial plant products as a material source raises environmental and 
ethical concerns. For instance, the growing utilization of landmass and the subsequent deforestation 
result in a decrease in biodiversity. Furthermore, food crops are commonly used for material 
development, which is questionable from an ethical point of view as food security is still not a given 
globally. 
One alternative that has been investigated in recent years is the use of algae biomass. Algae have 
the benefit of growing quickly and requiring little or no land mass. Several approaches to utilizing the 
algae biomass have been explored, such as the extraction of one single component and the 
sequential extraction of multiple components. However, when extracting single components, a 
significant portion of the biomass ends up as waste, and the sequential extraction involves 
numerous time-consuming steps and is both energy and solvent-intensive. 
This research aims to reduce the number of steps and the amount of solvent needed by separating 
the biomass into only two phases. The first phase, mainly sourced from the cytosol, contains lipids, 
proteins, and minerals, while the second phase contains the polysaccharide components Ulvan and 
cellulose which are the main components of the extracellular matrix. The first fraction may then find 
further applications as food or feedstock, as it is high in protein. The polysaccharides in the second 
fraction can be used for renewable and biodegradable materials. 
During the extraction, the goal is to maintain as much of the natural cell wall structure as possible. 
The aim is to utilize the native properties of the cell walls, but still remove most or all of the other 
components in the cell. There are several different methods that will be explored to rupture the cell 
wall for subsequent separation into the two phases. Broadly, these methods may be divided into 
mechanical and chemical methods. Additionally, combining different methods may prove more 
efficient. This approach will also be explored. 
Once the cell walls have been isolated, they will be used to make bio-based materials. It has been 
shown that the cell walls of Ulva fenestrata (U. fenestrata) have desirable properties such as 
transparency and flexibility. The goal is to utilize these properties and modify the extracted material 
so that it may be used as a viable alternative to petroleum-based materials. For this purpose, the 
possibility of crosslinking the hydroxyl groups with carboxylic acids containing compounds such as 
citric acid and the use of biopolymers such as chitin as binders will be investigated.

2. Literature study 
2.1 Ulva fenestrata anatomy and biochemical composition 
Algae can be broadly divided into micro and macro algae. Microalgae are unicellular organisms while 
macroalgae are multicellular. Macroalgae have been categorized into three groups, the brown, red 
and green algae. They show considerable differences not only in color but in chemical composition 
and structure. 
In this project the use of biomass from the green algae species U. fenestrata (previously named Ulva 
lactuca) is explored. U. fenestrata belongs to the Chlorophyta division of green macroalgae. It is 
commonly referred to as sea lettuce and is known for its rapid growth. As it is tolerant to a wide 
range of environmental conditions it can be found worldwide.1,2 The algae grow in thin blades 
anchored by a holdfast they vary in length between 5 and 50 cm and are two cells thick (Figure 1).3 
 
Figure 1: Illustration of U. fenestrata 
On a cellular level the components of the algae can be divided into the ones found within the cell 
and those which are part of the cell wall. The green colour of the algae is due to the high amounts of 
the chlorophyll-a within the cell, additional pigments found in the green algae include chlorophyll-b 
and beta-carotene. Other components found in the cell include lipids which are found among other 
places in the cell membrane, proteins and minerals, salt as well as starches.4 The cell wall of the 
algae can be considered a complex fiber composite with a framework of fibrillary polysaccharides 
embedded in a matrix of charged and neutral polysaccharides and different proteins. 5 The cell wall 
polysaccharides make up 38 - 54 % of the weight of the dry algae and the main polysaccharide 
components are cellulose, ulvan and one type each of the hemicelluloses xyloglucan and 
glucuronan.6 Pectin was found as one component in the cell wall of an Ulva species (sp. (singular)) as 
well, though not much literature is available. 1 The cell wall polysaccharides are estimated to make 
up 38 - 54 % of the dry weight of the algae in Ulva species (spp. (plural)).7 
It was proposed that in Ulva spp. the semi crystalline cellulose makes up a fibrillary component that 
is embedder in a matrix of mainly ulvan with minor content of proteins, β-1,4-D-xyloglucan and β-
1,4-D-glucuronan (Figure 2 A).7 However it has been found that the composition of the cell walls 
depends on their position in the blade. While ulvan is distributed throughout the cell wall it was 
found that it exists in higher amounts in the median cell wall while glucuronan was found mostly in 
the lateral and external cell walls (Figure 2 B). 8

2.1.1 
Cellulose 
Cellulose is the most common polysaccharide on earth. In terrestrial plants it is the main structure-
bearing component of the cell wall. It can also be found in the cell walls of some algae and bacteria 
species. In U. fenestrata it has been found to make up 19 % of the total dry weight. 9 Chemically it is 
a homopolysaccaride made up of D-anhydroglucopyranose repeating units (often referred to as 
simply glucose units), which are connected through β-(1,4) glycosidic linkages. It is a linear polymer 
in which the hydroxyl groups form hydrogen bonds both within the polymer chain as well as with 
neighbouring polymer chains (Figure 3). The resulting cellulose sheets tend to form attractive van 
der Waals forces towards each other, which lead to their self-assembly into semi-crystalline 
structures. This contributes to the high tensile strength and stiffness of cellulose. 
Cellulose has been found to have four different polymorphs that differ in cellulose chain orientation 
and hydrogen bonding structure between the chains. The allomorph cellulose I is the most common 
in nature, while the other three types (cellulose II, III, and IV) can be synthesised. Cellulose I can 
itself be divided into two different types, α and β, which belong to different crystal systems. 
Cellulose Iα is the sub-allomorph commonly found in algae, and Cellulose Iβ is the predominant 
crystal form of cellulose found in terrestrial plants. 10 The different morphologies of the cellulose 
semi-crystals express themselves through variations in their physical properties such as density, 
mechanical strength and thermal stability.

2.1.2 
Ulvan 
Ulvan is the second major component in the extracellular matrix of Ulva spp. Ulvan is a water-
soluble sulphated heteropolysaccharides and makes up around 30 wt % of the cell wall of U. 
fenestrata. 7 It has also been found in the cell walls of other species in the Ulva and Enteromorpha 
genera. Ulvan is mostly comprised of D-xylose, L-rhamnose, D-glucose, and D-glucuronic acid. 11 
However, the chemical structure and sugar composition can vary between the species and depend 
on the growth conditions.7 Additionally, it has been discovered, that arabinose is also a part of the 
ulvan found in U. fenestrata beside the more common components. 12 Ulvan, found in U. fenestrata, 
has a polymolecular nature; the molecules are chemically similar, branching polymers with a wide 
range of molecular weights and charge densities. Often, the ulvan contains impurities and has 
various molecular weight distributions with weight ranging from 1.5 × 102 to 2 × 103 kDa depending 
on the source. Apart from the most common monosaccharide components in ulvan, it was found 
that the polymer is not entirely random, and a number of different repeating structures have been 
identified.7 Various disaccharide units are commonly found in ulvan. The most prevalent unit 
consists of glucuronic acid and rhamnose arranged in a (β-D-GlcA (1 → 4) α-L-Rha 3S → 1) structure 
(Figure 4 A). Another common structure involves iduronic acid and rhamnose arranged in an (α-L-
IdoA (1 → 4) α-L-Rha 3S → 1) configuration (Figure 4 B). 13 Additionally, a number of other sugar 
sequences typical in ulvan have been identified.7 As part of the cell wall, the ulvan is believed to be 
responsible for controlling the osmotic pressure and ensuring the protection of the cell. In the cell 
wall ulvan is often found complexed with protein moieties, which is a characteristic observed 
commonly with cell wall polysaccharides.

2.1.3 
Hemicellulose 
Hemicelluloses are a minor form of polysaccharide found in the cell walls of Ulva spp. They consist of 
diverse sugar units with various linkages connecting them. Unlike cellulose, hemicelluloses are 
typically shorter and more branched, with a lower average molecular mass. These polysaccharides 
are built from a number of different monosaccharides, including xylose, glucose, mannose, 
galactose, and arabinose. Some hemicelluloses also incorporate non-saccharide units and uronic 
acids like glucuronic acid and galacturonic acid into their polymer structures; these are sugar acids 
where the C-6 carbon of the monosaccharide is part of a carboxylic acid group. These uronic acids 
are either interspersed along the hemicellulose chains or present as side chains, giving the 
polysaccharide distinct chemical characteristics. The inclusion of uronic acids makes hemicelluloses 
polyelectrolytes, which facilitate interactions with charged molecules. Hemicelluloses play diverse 
roles within the plant cell wall, including providing structural support, facilitating cell-cell adhesion, 
and modulating cell wall porosity. Hemicelluloses exhibit considerable variation in composition and 
structure among different plant species and tissues.

2.1.4 
Pectin 
Pectin is another polysaccharide that was found in the cell walls of an Ulva spp. 1 It is a complex 
polysaccharide primarily composed of chains of galacturonic acid, a sugar acid derived from glucose. 
In terrestrial plants, pectins are a major component of the primary cell wall, where they contribute 
to cell adhesion. Pectins are known to be present in all primary plant cell walls, though in differing 
quantities. 15 Generally, pectins are easily dissolved and degraded by alkalis. The chemical structure 
and composition can vary between species and in different growth stages. While the whole structure 
is not understood, it is presumed that pectins have a heterogeneous block copolymer structure. 
Galacturonan and rhamnogalacturonan were found to form the block-copolymer backbone of 
pectins. The presence of rhamnose interspersed within the rhamnogalacturonan blocks introduces 
structural kinks into the otherwise strait polymer chains. Commonly, side chains of sugar units such 
as galactose and arabinose are attached to the C-4 position on the rhamnose units. Due to the 
presence of sidechains, the parts of pectin with a rhamnogalacturonan backbone are referred to as 
hairy regions, while the unbranched galacturonan parts are referred to as smooth regions (Figure 6 
A). In the smooth region, the regular structure and presence of the carboxylic acid group lead to the 
binding of Ca2+ ions. These Ca2+ ions bind to two carboxylic acid groups of two different chains and 
form a crosslink between them (Figure 6 B). 16

2.2 Biorefinery processes for macroalgae 
As mentioned in Section 2.1, green algae are rich in compounds that show promising properties for 
applications such as functional food, pharmaceuticals, nutraceutical applications, cosmetics, and 
plant protection. Furthermore, the polysaccharides can be processed for biofuel production. 
Different biorefinery processes have been explored that enable the isolation of these functional 
compounds from macroalgae and their subsequent valorisation. 
The so-far most common strategy used in the industry is the isolation of a single component from 
the algae, while the other components are discarded. Among the most extracted compounds are 
alginate, agar, and carrageenan. Another strategy recently explored in the literature is the sequential 
extraction of multiple valuable components from the biomass. 
There are a number of research papers investigating the sequential extraction of the components of 
different Ulva spp. pigments, antioxidants, and proteins, in addition to mineral-rich liquid extracts, 
lipid, ulvan, and cellulose, have been extracted in this sequential manner from different Ulva 
ssp.18,19,20 These are high-value extractives that have a variety of applications. The drawback of these 
methods are the numerous steps involved and the high quantities of solvents and chemicals 
required as well as the high energy consumption. 
Another alternative approach is that the whole algae are used to produce biofuel. Here, the number 
of steps is minimised, but the final product has not as much value as the products of the sequential 
extraction. It is clear that algae biomass is a versatile resource, and depending on the specific needs, 
different approaches may be followed for its processing and valorisation. It is, however, important 
that a balance be struck between the number of steps used in the bio refinery process, the amount 
of energy, chemicals, and solvents needed, and the value of the final bio refinery products.  
By separating the algae biomass into only two usable phases, the energy consumption and solvent 
amount are reduced while still allowing the isolation and further processing of high-value products. 
Bar-Shai et al. reported the successful decellularization of an Ulva sp., which resulted in a cellulose 
scaffold suitable for biomedical applications such as tissue engineering.21 They reported the use of a 
boiling acetone bath followed by treatment with acetate and sodium chlorite, which was 
intentionally used to remove all polysaccharides aside from cellulose. Such decellularized structures 
have recently drawn attention for their use as cell scaffolds for tissue engineering. However so far, 
no attempts have been made to maintain all polysaccharides in the cell wall while maintaining the 
cell structure and to use these extracted cell wall structures as a resource for bio-based materials. 
There are a number of treatments, both physical and chemical, that are available and can aid in cell 
disruption and consequent isolation of cell wall structures (Figure 7 A). These methods or a 
combination of them may prove suitable for isolating the cell walls while maintaining the native 
structure. Chemical methods can aid in the solubilization of the cell components and the weakening 
of the cell wall, which can be further aided by increased temperatures. It has been shown that the 
use of surfactants can be beneficial for the solubilization of lipids, DNA, and proteins.22,23 Due to their 
amphiphilic nature surfactants, added in concentrations above the critical micelle concentration 
(CMC), form micelles in solution that allow the dissolution of hydrophobic compounds in aqueous 
solution (Figure 7 B).

Amphoteric surfactants are especially suitable for the solubilization of proteins because, due to their

purely anionic or cationic surfactants tend to. Cocamidopropyl betaine (CAPB) stands out among

resources, namely coconut oil, as opposed to the majority of surfactants, which are petroleum-

The different physical methods have different pathways for breaking the cell walls and promoting 
the release of the cell components. Ultrasonic waves have been known to induce bubble formation 
within cells that lead to a chain reaction like cell rupture once they burst. Shear forces aid in cell 
disruption by applying mechanical stress that breaks or punctures the cell walls, which may be 
promoted if the cell walls have been previously softened through a solvent treatment. 
2.3 Processing of biomass for materials 
Once the first step of the bio refinery process has been established the next step is the material 
formulation. As the pure products of bio refinery are seldom directly usable as materials often 
different kinds of treatments and the addition of other compounds are used to modulate the 
material properties. 
2.3.1 
Cellulose and ulvan materials 
Though not much research is available on materials designed with the plant cell walls of algae in 
their native form, there has been considerable effort to explore the properties and viability of 
materials with isolated cellulose from terrestrial sources as well as some research on materials 
based on isolated ulvan. 
==PAGE:16==
8 
 
While Ulvan is the main component in U. fenestrata cell walls, it is otherwise not a common 
polysaccharide. However, due to the presence of unusual sugar units and its intriguing features, 
Ulvan has been of interest in a variety of research fields. Among others, ulvan has been used to 
create membranes, particles, hydrogels, porous 3D structures, and nanofibers. 24 These different 
materials have shown potential for use in the food, chemical, pharmaceutical, and biomedical fields, 
as well as in agriculture for plant protection. 7, 25, 26  
Cellulose, as a more common biopolymer, has found a much wider application field. Cellulose is a 
major component of wood and is, in that form, used in construction, furnishing, and transportation. 
Extracted cellulose fibers are used in the pulp and paper industries. The isolated cellulose from 
different sources including algae can also be dissolved and regenerated into fibres. Alternatively, 
cellulose if processed into nanocellulose, which have been of increasing interest in recent years in 
application fields ranging from barrier films and pH sensors to components of Pickering 
emulsions.27,28  
Furthermore, cellulose is being used to form hydrogels, which can be used in a variety of fields, such 
as for hygiene products, in agriculture for cosmetics, and in biomedical applications.29 
The combination of cellulose and ulvan has also been studied, specifically in the development of 
edible films.24 However, in these studies, ulvan and cellulose were separately extracted from the cell 
wall and then recombined with additional binders to form the films. 
2.3.2 
Chemical and physical crosslinking 
When the mechanical and chemical properties of bio refinery products are not satisfactory, they 
may be improved through physical or chemical crosslinking. In physical crosslinking, intermolecular 
bonds between the polymer chains or with a crosslinking agent are formed. These intermolecular 
bonds can be dative and ionic bonds or weaker hydrogen and van der Waals bonds. Physical 
crosslinks tend to not be thermally or mechanically stable but can often be reformed through the 
application of heat. Chemical crosslinking, on the other hand, involves the chemical modification of 
the polymer chains to form covalent bonds between them. Depending on the crosslinking density, 
they can make polymers more elastic, enhance their strength and toughness, and transform them 
into thermoset polymers. By preventing the disentanglement of the individual polymer chains, 
chemical crosslinking generally decreases the creep compliance of materials. 
In principle, the reactions of functional groups on a polymer chain behave similarly to organic 
reactions with low-molecular-weight compounds. However, there are some key considerations that 
apply to reactions in polymer systems. One important difference is the accessibility of the functional 
group. Depending on the polymer and its supramolecular structure, the reactive groups may not be 
accessible for reactions. In semi-crystalline polymers, even relatively small molecules such as water 
cannot penetrate due to the strong intermolecular forces. Furthermore, the inter- and 
intramolecular forces may change during the course of the reaction, which may also lead to phase 
changes and an increased miscibility of previously immiscible phases. Lastly, one often encountered 
problem is the production of side products during the reactions and the difficulty associated with 
removing these from the polymer system. Especially the removal of side products of crosslinking 
reactions that result in thermoset polymers proves challenging as the product cannot be dissolved 
without compromising the crosslinking structure.

2.3.2.1 Crosslinking cellulose 
Cellulose has per repeat unit three hydroxyl groups that are theoretically available for chemical 
modification and crosslinking. The reactivity of the hydroxyl groups differs due to steric hindrance 
and tendencies for hydrogen bonding. As cellulose is semi-crystalline, it has both amorphous and 
crystalline regions. In the amorphous regions, the hydroxyl groups are generally available for 
reactions; however, in the crystalline regions of cellulose, these are involved in a strong hydrogen 
bond network that makes them inaccessible. There are two approaches that may be taken in the 
process of chemically modified semi-crystalline cellulose. Either only the groups accessible on the 
surface of crystalline regions and in amorphous regions can be modified, or the cellulose is activated 
in a first step, making the hydroxyl groups in the crystalline region more accessible for a following 
step. In the former method, the intermolecular hydrogen bonds between the cellulose chains will be 
preserved, and the general structure will be left intact. In such heterogeneous reactions, there is 
only partial accessibility to the hydroxyl groups located on the surface of crystalline regions. The 
hydroxyl located on C-3 is known to rarely react as it is involved in intermolecular hydrogen bonding. 
While the most reactive hydroxyl group is located on C-6, the hydroxyl group on C-2 also reacts once 
a certain percentage of modification of C-6 has been reached. 30 However, as the exact surface area 
of the crystalline regions is not always known, the results of such modifications have been 
somewhat unpredictable and hard to reproduce. The latter method is therefore often preferred, 
though it is more chemically intensive. To activate cellulose, most commonly, aqueous alkali 
solutions are used that are able to break some of the inter- and intra-molecular bonds, which leads 
to swelling and, in high alkali concentrations, the dissolution of the cellulose. Subsequently, the 
functional groups within the cellulose are accessible for chemical modification; however, the native 
cellulose supramolecular structure is disrupted. 31 
There has been considerable research into the crosslinking reactions of cellulose and other 
carbohydrates for a number of different types of materials. Regenerated celluloses tend to have the 
problem of fibrillation, which is a process where microfibres form on the surface of the fibres when 
exposed to sheer forces in wet conditions. This is caused by weak cohesion between the cellulose 
sheets. Chemical crosslinking of the hydroxyl group in cellulose has been explored to reduce such 
fibrillation. Crosslinked cellulose has also been used to form hydrogels, as the crosslinking process 
reduces the solubility and strengthens hydrogels. 29 
The most commonly used cellulose crosslinkers are amino-based, such as triazines, amines, and 
urea. Specifically, 1,3,5-triacryloylhexahydro-1,3,5-triazine and dimethyloldihydroxyethyleneurea 
have been popular in the past. 32 However, these types of crosslinkers tend to be toxic or have 
residues of toxic chemicals such as formaldehyde in their commercially available products, which is 
why alternatives have been explored in recent years. One promising alternative that has been 
studied is the use of tri [carboxylic acids] such as citric acid (CA) and poly [carboxylic acids] like 
1,2,3,4-butanetetracarboxylic acid (BTCA) as crosslinking agents. For the crosslinking with these 
compounds, they need to first be brought into a cyclic anhydrous form, which is then followed by 
esterification with the hydroxyl groups of the cellulose, which results in crosslinked cellulose. For the 
reactions with both CA and BTCA, the catalyst sodium hypophosphite has proven suitable, as it 
promotes both the ring-closing reaction to form the cyclic anhydrite as well as the esterification to 
form the crosslinks. 33 A mixture of sodium dihydrogen phosphate (H2NaO4P) and sodium hydrogen 
carbonate (NaHCO3) (1:1 w/w) has also been used in the past in cellulose crosslinking reactions with 
CA. 34

2.3.2.2 Crosslinked ulvan 
Ulvan, unlike cellulose, does not have one regular repeating sequence and therefore does not tend 
to form crystalline regions. Its monosaccharide unit composition may vary depending on species and 
growth conditions; therefore, chemical modifications such as crosslinking may produce differing 
results depending on the specific ulvan source. Ulvan has, like cellulose, hydroxyl groups in most of 
the repeat units, which may participate in similar reactions as those in cellulose. Furthermore, the 
sulphate groups and carboxylic acid groups present in the ulvan structure could offer potential for 
crosslinking. 
As ulvan is a less common polysaccharide than cellulose, there is less literature on crosslinked ulvan 
materials. However, given its promising properties for biomedical applications, there has been some 
exploration in this area. 1,4-butanediol diglycidyl ether has been used for the crosslinking of ulvan to 
make membranes for drug delivery applications. 35 Divinylsulfone was used in an alkaline aqueous 
condition to crosslink ulvan to form a hydrogel. 36 Several papers also discuss the option of cross-
linking derivatized ulvan. Ulvan methacrylate and glycidyl methacrylated Ulvan were crosslinked in a 
photochemical reaction. 37 Ulvan-tyramine and ulvan acrylate have similarly been used as 
intermediates for the crosslinking of ulvan. 38  
Furthermore, the sulphate and carboxylate groups in ulvan have the potential for ionic crosslinking 
with positively charged groups such as amines. Films of chitosan and ulvan were made utilizing this 
interaction between the amino groups of chitosan and the negatively charged groups of ulvan. 39 
2.3.2.3 Biopolymers as binders 
In addition to the chemical modification and chemical crosslinking of a polymer to change and 
improve its properties, it may also be mixed with other components. Such mixtures are 
differentiated into blends and composites. In blends, the two or more components form one 
homogeneous material, while composites are heterogeneous materials with different components 
forming phases with distinct properties. Generally, the role of the matrix element of a composite is 
to hold the reinforcing component(s) in place while the reinforcements provide mechanical strength. 
While in polymer blends, the miscibility of the polymer components is vital, in composites, miscibility 
is not required. However, to obtain good mechanical properties, the adhesion between the binder 
and the reinforcing components must be strong. Strong adhesion is promoted by intermolecular 
adhesive forces such as hydrogen bonds between the components. Reversibly, the adhesion 
between polar and non-polar components will inevitably be weak. 40  
There are a number of different biopolymers that have been used as binders, specifically as binders 
for cellulose materials. Most often, they were used in combination with nanocellulose or in the 
paper and pulp industry with wood fibers. For the rather uncommon polysaccharide ulvan, less 
research is available exploring its utility in composite materials in combination with biopolymer 
binders. 
As described before, cellulose has many applications and is being used in a number of different 
forms. As such, cellulose itself has been used as a binder, and all-cellulose composites have been 
investigated, where cellulose takes on the roles of both the matrix polymer and the reinforcing 
component. Such one-component composites promise high interfacial bonding at the matrix-
reinforcement interface due to their identical chemical structure and the possibility of hydrogen 
bonding. 40 
Chitosan is another polysaccharide with a similar chemical structure to cellulose, the main difference 
being the replacement of the hydroxyl group on the C-2 position in cellulose with an acetamide 
==PAGE:19==
11 
 
group in chitin or an amine in the chitin form (Figure 9). Chitosan is obtained from chitin, which is a 
primary component of crustacean exoskeletons, though some natural fungal sources of chitin exist 
as well. 41 It exhibits good film-forming capabilities, biocompatibility, nontoxicity, and antimicrobial 
activity; however, its mechanical strength is generally low. 42 Chitosan is dissolvable in water in the 
presence of acids such as acetic acid, which aids dissolution by protonating the amino groups. 43 The 
possibility of cellulose-chitin composites has been explored, and aerogels, hydrogels, foams, films, 
membranes, fibres, and nanofibers have been developed. In these materials the mechanical strength 
of the cellulose and its water stability are complemented by the film-forming and antimicrobial 
properties of the chitosan. Furthermore, materials of ulvan and chitosan have been developed in 
which the ionic crosslinking of the amino groups of chitosan and the negatively charged sulphate and 
carboxylate groups in ulvan has been observed. 39

Other biopolymers that have been used as binders are starches, carrageenan, pullulan, pectin, 
hydrophilic plant-based gums, and mucilage. Furthermore, the combination of biopolymers and 
glycerol has shown promising results, as the latter acts as a modulator for the ductility of the 
material.

3. Experimental plan 
3.1 Cell wall isolation 
The first part of the research is the isolation of cell walls from U. fenestrata. For this purpose, both 
chemical and mechanical treatments are combined.  
1. In the first step, the biomass will be heated in a solvent mixture of different solvents and 
concentrations (5, 10 25, 50 vol%) will be tested. Furthermore, the impact of using surfactants in 
addition to the solvents will be evaluated. 
2. Following this treatment, the samples will be ultrasonicated, which should induce bubble 
formation within the cell, which, when combusted, can lead to the rupture of cell walls.  
3. As a final step, the samples will be exposed to shear forces by an ultra Turrax which should 
ensure that all cells are ruptured, which is essential to the removal of the components from 
within the cell.  
Alternatively, to this three-step procedure, the efficiency of a sonication bath for the extraction will 
be evaluated. The products from the cell wall extraction will be analysed with microscopy to 
evaluate particle size and the morphology of the cell wall structure. The extraction liquid will be 
analysed to determine the extent of the extraction and compare the different solvent systems. 
3.2 Material formulation 
The material design will use the isolated cell walls from the first part of the research. Both physical 
and chemical crosslinking methods will be evaluated for making materials from the call wall 
fragments. The chemical crosslinking of the extracted cell walls with different amounts of CA will be 
tested. A combination of sodium dihydrogen phosphate (H2NaO4P) and sodium hydrogen carbonate 
(NaHCO3) will be used as a catalyst in this reaction (Figure 10).

evaluated. Specifically, chitosan which forms ionic crosslinks with ulvan and hydrogen bonds with 
cellulose will be investigated (Figure 11). The applicability and impact of the modifications on the 
mechanical and thermal properties will be analysed.

4. Methods 
4.1 Materials 
U. fenestrata algae biomass grown and collected in an offshore farming cite on the west coast of 
Sweden in Tjörn was stored frozen. Acetone and ethanol were obtained from VWR Chemicals. CAPB 
was supplied by Evonik (40 vol% concentration in water). Chitosan was obtained from Fluka 
biochemicals (medium molecular weight, relative molecular mass = 400,000 g mol−1) with a degree 
of deacetylation of 84.7% 44,45. Carbotrace 680 was purchased from Ebba Biotech AB, a solution of 
0.1 % (w/v) in MilliQ was prepared and used. Coomassie blue was obtained in the form of Pierce™ 
Bradford Protein Assay Reagent from thermo scientific. Citric acid, Sodium dihydrogen phosphate, 
sodium hydrogen carbonate and 0,65 µm PVDF membranes with a diameter of 47 mm were 
obtained from Merck. 
4.2 Cell wall extraction 
4.2.1 
Three-step extraction 
As the first step, the algae was thawed and washed by being submerged in distilled water. Then the 
algae biomass was sorted by removing any dirt and the holdfast regions, which are tougher and 
thicker. 
For each sample, 0.1 g of U. fenestrata biomass was submerged in the solvent solution and heated 
for 360 min at 45 °C. The different solvent concentrations used were 5, 10, 25, or 50 vol% of either 
acetone or ethanol in MilliQ water. Samples with only MilliQ were produced as well. The same 
experiment was also carried out with 1 vol% CAPB added to the different solvent solutions. Samples 
are denoted by referring to the solvent Ethanol (EtOH) or acetone (Ac), the concentration of that 
solvent in MilliQ water (v/v) unless it is pure, followed by a note on the addition of CAPB (with (w) or 
without (wo)). For example, the sample named EtOH 25% w, refers to a sample prepared in a 
solution of 25% ethanol, 75% MilliQ (v/v), and 1 vol% CAPB with respect to the solvent mixture. 
During the heating, at regular intervals (after 10, 30, 60, 90, 120, 180, 240, 300, and 360 minutes), 
samples in technical duplicate (2x 100 µL) were taken from the extraction liquid and added to 
individual wells in a 96-well plate, which was covered with aluminium foil until analysis. After 6 
hours, small samples of biomass were taken and stored submerged in 5 mL of MilliQ water. These 
were stored in a fridge for later analysis. 
The remainder of the samples were ultrasonicated with a sonics Vibra-Cell™ instrument with a probe 
diameter of 3 mm at 20 kHz for 1 minute in repeating cycles of 9 seconds of sonication at 40 % 
amplitude and a 1 second break. After sonication, the samples were left to rest for 30–60 minutes 
covered with aluminium foil, after which the liquid fraction was removed and replaced with MilliQ 
water, which was filled up to a total volume of 50 mL. This step was repeated three times. The first 
supernatant that was removed was stored, light-protected, in a fridge for future analysis. Small 
samples of the fragmented biomass were collected and stored in a fridge for later analysis. The 
samples in which the algae fractured into small pieces during the first two treatments were then 
treated with an ultra Turrax (IKA T 25 digital ultra Turrax) for 20 min at 18 000 rpm. Samples from 
this final product were stored in the fridge for later analysis. 
This process was repeated for all samples on a slightly bigger scale, using double the amount of all 
the components while keeping the same sampling volume. The well plates used in this analysis were 
made of polypropylene instead of the previously used polystyrene plates.

4.3 Material Design 
4.3.1 
Chemical crosslinking 
To crosslink the polysaccharides, 6.5 g of treated algae (92.42 wt% moisture content (measured with 
VWR MBG instrument)) were added to different concentrations of CA (1, 5, 10, 30 wt% with respect 
to the wet-treated algae). Sodium hydrogen carbonate and sodium dihydrogenphosphate were used 
as catalysts for the crosslinking reaction and added at 50 wt% with respect to the CA content. All 
samples were prepared with a total volume of 50 mL. The mixture was placed in an oil bath at 40 °C 
for 24 hours while being continuously stirred at 350 rpm. The flasks were then refluxed for 75 
minutes at 150 °C under stirring at 350 rpm. Afterwards, the mixture was transferred to falcon tubes 
and centrifuged for 10 min at 8000 rpm. The supernatant was removed, and the tube was refilled 
with MilliQ water. This process was repeated three times to remove residual CA and catalyst from 
the product. After the supernatant was removed once again, the moisture content was measured. 
Films were produced with a diameter of 4 cm and a weight of ca. 0.08 g. For the film formation, the 
wet material was added to 50 mL of water. In a vacuum filtration setup, a wet cake was formed on 
PVDF membranes. The wet cake was covered with a second membrane and dried in a Rapid Köthen 
instrument (Model RK3A-KWT, PTI laboratory equipment) at 93 °C and around -0,9 bar for 15 
minutes. The weight of the produced films was recorded (see Appendix). The films are named by the 
treatment of the algae, meaning that 5% CA describes the films produced with 5 wt% CA. No T refers 
to the sample prepared without going through the temperature cycle with no CA added. No CA has 
had the same temperature cycle, but neither catalyst nor CA were added. 
4.3.2 
Physical crosslinking 
A 1 wt% solution of chitosan in a 0.2 M solution of CA (in MilliQ) was prepared by stirring for 4 hours 
at 50 °C. The treated algae biomass (94.51 wt% moisture content) was mixed with chitosan solution 
to obtain mixtures with solid content weight ratios of chitosan to algae of 25/75, 50/50, and 75/25. 
Water was added to obtain an equal volume in each of the mixtures. Films were produced through 
solvent casting, for which 10 or 20 mL of the mixtures were added to petri dishes to obtain films 
with 0,1 and 0,2 g, respectively. The Petri dishes were loosely covered with aluminium foil to prevent

dust from settling in the films. The petri dishes were left undisturbed at room temperature for four 
days to let the water evaporate.

4.4 Analysis and data treatment 
4.4.1 
Light microscopy 
The algae tissue samples that were collected in the different experiments were analysed with light 
microscopy. Additionally, untreated U. fenestrata and the algae after the meat grinder treatment, as 
well as the holdfasts, were analysed for comparison. The microscopy images of the different samples 
were recorded with a Leica DM IL LED instrument equipped with a Leica DMC2900 camera. The 
==PAGE:27==
19 
 
software LAS X was used. Images were recorded with a 20x magnification, and some were recorded 
with a phase contrast filter. 
4.4.2 
Plate reader data 
The extraction liquid was analysed with a Tecan Infinite M200Pro plate reader used with the 
software i-control 1.12. The analysis of the kinetic samples was done within two and a half hours 
after the first samples in the plate were taken, meaning that three plates were used (plate 1 with 
samples after 10, 30, and 60 minutes, plate 2 containing samples after 90, 120, and 180 minutes, 
and plate 3 with samples after 240, 300, and 360 minutes). All samples were analysed with technical 
duplicates. 
From the plate reader, different kinds of data were obtained. At all-time intervals, the absorbance 
associated with chlorophyll at the wavelengths 647 nm and 664 nm and the absorption at the 
wavelength 595 nm after the addition of 100 µL Coomassie blue were recorded. Additionally, 
fluorescence spectra with four different excitation wavelengths were recorded (excitation at 405 nm 
with emission measured from 440 to 800 nm, excitation at 488 nm with emission measured from 
520 to 800 nm, excitation at 561 nm with emission measured from 590 to 800 nm, and excitation at 
639 nm with emission measured from 668 to 800 nm). Absorption spectra for the samples after six 
hours for the wavelengths from 340 to 850 nm were recorded. To the samples taken after 360 
minutes, 1 µL Carbotrace 680 solution was added, and the emission from 590–800 nm with an 
excitation wavelength of 561 nm was measured. 
To obtain the absorbance of the chlorophyll in the samples and to eliminate the influence of the 
different solvent systems and the well plate, blanks were measured for the different solvent 
systems. The blanks, the same as the samples, were analysed with at least one technical duplicate. 
Before the values were averaged, they were checked for outliers. For this purpose, the percentage 
difference between the two values was calculated. If the percentage difference was more than 50%, 
the data point that was significantly higher or lower than measurements taken in the time interval 
before and after was removed. The averages of the blanks were subtracted from the values of the 
samples. The same was done with blank samples with the addition of Coomassie blue. For the 
fluorescent measurement with Carbotrace 680, from the obtained data, the fluorescence obtained 
before the addition of the Carbotrace 680 was subtracted. Blank samples with Carbotrace 680 were 
recorded as well to ensure the validity of the method. 
4.4.3 
Ultraviolet-visible analysis 
Ultraviolet-visible (UV-vis) spectra were recorded on a Shimadzu UV-2550 spectrometer using the 
software UVProbe. The final extraction liquid of the half-upscaled and pre-treatment experiments, 
as well as the samples collected in the full-upscaled procedure, were analysed before and after they 
were centrifuged to remove solids. The analysis was done in quartz-glass cuvettes. Spectra were 
recorded in a range from 340 to 850 nm. 
4.4.4 
Thermogravimetric analysis 
For the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), between 5 and 10 mg of freeze-dried samples were 
analysed in a Mettler Toledo STARe TGA/DSC instrument. The decomposition of the sample was 
recorded in the temperature range of 30 to 800 °C. During the temperature ramp, the temperature 
was increased by 10 °C per minute while the sample was kept in a nitrogen atmosphere (50 mL/min). 
Once the final temperature of 800 °C was reached, the atmosphere was switched to oxygen, and the 
temperature was kept constant for 15 minutes. The obtained data was normalised to displace the 
weight change as a percentage of the original weight of the sample. The derivatives of the obtained 
data were plotted.

4.4.5 
Furrier transform-Infrared analysis  
For the furrier-transform infrared (FTIR) analysis, freeze-dried samples were analysed with a Perkin 
Elmer spectrum 100 FTIR spectrometer. The transmission was recorded in percent in a range from 
600 to 4000 cm -1. The obtained data was background noise- and base-line-corrected. 
4.4.6 
Particle size distribution  
The particle size distribution was investigated with two different methods. On the one hand, the 
weight of different particle size fractions was recorded. For this purpose, the product was sieved 
with a mesh with a pore size of 500 µm. The particles from the filtrate when the treated algae was 
first recovered were isolated by centrifugation. Finally, the weights and moisture contents were 
recorded to calculate the comparative weight of each of the fractions. 
The other method used to analyse the particle size distribution was to record 50 microscopy images 
(20x magnification) and use the software ImageJ to circle the individual particles. The resulting data 
gave the particle size distribution in terms of area (µm2). As this is a rather uncommon way of 
displaying particle size, the area-equivalent diameter (DA) was calculated. The calculated diameter 
was then used to form a histogram that shows the number of particles in the different size fractions. 
4.4.7 
Tensile test 
For the tensile test, the produced films were cut into 5 mm-wide strips. The tensile test was 
conducted on an Instron 5944 with a 500 N load cell. The gauge length was set to 25 mm, and the 
thickness of each of the test strips was measured at three locations. For the calculations of the 
tensile strength and modulus, the average of these three values was used. The strain at break in 
percentage to the original length was also calculated. The strain rate was set to 10%/min. Two marks 
were placed on the specimen before measurement to follow the strain through digital image 
correlation. The software Bluehill 3 was used to control the instrument.

5.1 The algae starting material

5.2 Cell wall extraction 
The goal of the cell wall extraction was to decellularize the algae biomass to obtain a scaffold of only 
the extracellular matrices. In addition to the development of a suitable method for cell wall 
extraction, the method of evaluating the progression of the extraction was refined during this 
process as well. The morphology of the product was analysed through light microscopy, and 
extraction kinetics were studied through the analysis of the extraction liquid. The absorbance and 
fluorescence associated with chlorophyll were used as indicators for the extraction kinetics. 
Furthermore, the absorbance associated with the complexes formed by protein and the dye 
Coomassie blue was measured over time. The optotracer Carbotrace 680 was added to analyse the 
glucan content in the final extraction liquid. 
5.2.1 
The three-step process 
Different parameters were tested in the series of reactions, and the results give some indication of 
which could be most applicable for producing the cell wall scaffold. Different solvents, namely 
acetone, ethanol, and MilliQ, were tested in different concentrations. Furthermore, CAPB was added 
to observe the impact of a surfactant on the extraction efficiency. 
5.2.1.1 Microscopy 
In the three-step process, tissue samples were taken after each of the three steps (oil bath, 
ultrasonication, and ultra Turrax), which were analysed with a light microscope to evaluate the 
tissue fragmentation and cell rupture caused by the treatments. From the microscopy images, it 
appeared that after heating in an oil bath for six hours, the cells did not visibly rupture. In most 
samples, the tissue seemed to be in similar condition to the untreated algae tissue. After the 
ultrasonication, however, in some samples, bubbles filled with liquid were visible without 
magnification, and microscopy images appeared brighter in some areas in the same lighting 
condition (Figure 14). 
The observation of lighter areas under the microscope could indicate that in some parts of the algae 
tissue, the double layer of cells split into single layers, and therefore more light could penetrate the 
tissue under the light microscope. The bubble formation within the algae tissue is another indicator 
of the loss of cohesion between the two cell layers. It has been proposed that the median cell wall 
between the two layers contains higher amounts of ulvan, which is more soluble than some of the 
other cell wall components, such as cellulose. 8 This could therefore indicate that during this solvent 
treatment or the subsequent ultrasonication step, the adhesion between the median cell walls is 
weakened to allow the splitting of the median cell wall.

Some of the samples also fragmented into small pieces after the ultrasonication. As seen in Figure 15 
A, the two samples with 10 vol% and 25 vol% acetone seemed to be more disintegrated than the 
other two with 5 and 50 vol%. Only the first two were further treated with the ultra Turrax. An 
interesting observation was that higher solvent concentrations did not always lead to more 
fragmentation. In most sample series, the 10 % solvent concentration induced fragmentation 
sufficient for an ultra Turrax treatment. Except in one sample series with 10 % ethanol and the 
addition of CAPB, it is to be noted that even in this sample series, the 10 % sample did show more 
disintegration than the others (Figure 15 B). For the samples with the addition of surfactant, it may 
be reasoned that the surfactant behaviour is highly dependent on solvent concentrations. 
Specifically, the CMC is dependent on the solvent. The tendency of surfactants to form micelles is 
driven by the hydrophobic effect, namely the hydrophobic tails trying to avoid contact with aqueous 
solutions. When the solvent is less hydrophilic due to higher amounts of non-polar solvents, this 
hydrophobic effect decreases. Meaning the surfactants are not as driven to form micelles, and 
therefore the CMC increases. This also means that in more non-polar media, the surfactant is less 
likely to associate with the hydrophobic components in the cell, such as the hydrophobic parts of 
proteins, and form micelles around these components. Therefore, the solubility of hydrophobic 
components is decreased. However, this is only true to a certain extent, as when the solvent is 
mostly non-polar, the hydrophobic components will be dissolvable without surfactant interaction. 
But in this case, it appears that in the samples with 10 and 25 vol% solvent, the surfactant is driven 
to solubilize nonpolar components of the cell, while the present acetone can work towards cell 
rupture by increasing the permeability of the cell membrane and potentially denaturing proteins.

Chlorophyll 
Chlorophyll is used as an indicator for the cell wall rupture and consequent leakage of cytoplasm. 
Chlorophyll is naturally part of the chloroplast of the algae cells. As a chromophore and fluorescent 
compound, it is relatively easy to detect by spectroscopy and may therefore provide information on 
the kinetics of the extraction procedure. Absorbances at 647 nm and 664 nm were measured for all 
samples in the given time intervals in a plate reader. The wavelengths 664 nm and 647 nm were 
chosen as they are the peak of the absorbance curves in the blue range of chlorophyll a and b, 
respectively, and can be used for chlorophyll quantification. 48 The absorbance values at the two 
wavelengths generally showed similar trends and intensities (Figure 17). However, the exception is

in the acetone samples with surfactant added, where the intensities of the absorbance measured at 
664 nm are about one third higher than those at absorbance measured at 647 nm (Figure 17 C, D). 
This result was also present in chemical duplicates. In samples produced without surfactant and in 
samples with ethanol or MilliQ, such behaviour cannot be observed. These results may indicate that 
the surfactant interacts differently with chlorophyll a and b in acetone.

the solution. The fact that this phenomenon cannot be observed in the samples with ethanol and 
MilliQ may be due to the fact that acetone is less polar and therefore influences the surfactant 
differently.

When observing the development of absorbance over time in many samples, no clear increasing 
trend can be observed (Figure 17 A, B). Furthermore, outliers can be observed in samples that do 
show a somewhat curved progression (Figure 17 C, D). And in many of the samples, the absorbance 
decreases between earlier and later samples. Assuming that the samples are representative of the 
experiment at each given time, the reasons for a decrease in absorbance could be either a random 
error, degradation of the chlorophyll, or another factor that arises over time and influences the 
absorption. If this can be attributed to a random error, an increase in the sample size could 
potentially increase the accuracy of the measurement. Secondly, the degradation of the samples 
before and during the measurement needs to be considered. Chlorophyll can degrade in a 
photooxidation reaction, which is why the reaction vessels and the plate with the samples were 
covered in aluminium foil during the experiment. However, this will not have prevented all 
degradation, and therefore later samples (latest after 6 hours) may have shown lower values due to 
the fact that they were exposed to high temperatures and some light during the experiment (45 °C) 
for a longer time than the chlorophyll present in the extraction liquid samples taken earlier from the 
experimentation flask. Another possible explanation could be that as the cell wall extraction 
progresses, more fragmentation occurs and the fragmented pieces float in the extraction liquid. 
These and other components that are only solubilized or suspended in the extraction liquid towards 
the end of the procedure may influence the absorbance measurements. Because only low volumes 
were taken from the flasks for the kinetic study, they could not be filtered or centrifuged while at 
the same time ensuring no changes in the volumes of the sample. Taking a higher volume for each 
sample would have further skewed the measurement over time as the solvent volume remaining in 
the extraction flask would be substantially reduced. But due to the lack of filtering, it is possible that 
the tissue fragments left in the extraction liquid interfered with the absorbance measurement as 
their different refractive indices compared to the solvent led to light scattering, which then showed 
in the data as an increased absorbance. Both the possibility of chlorophyll degradation and 
fragmentation interference were tested in later experiments. (See sections 5.2.2.1 and 5.2.3, 
respectively.) 
When comparing the absorbance values, it appears, in general, that the addition of CAPB leads to 
higher absorbance values, which indicates that the extraction is promoted through the presence of 
surfactant. As previously explained, surfactants can help in solubilizing hydrophobic compounds in 
aqueous solutions. Due to the long hydrophobic chain, chlorophyll dissolution will be increased by

5.2.1.3 Proteins 
Proteins are part of the cell cytosol as well as the cell wall, and their presence in the extraction liquid 
can give further indications on the progression of the extraction process.  
For the protein determination, Coomassie blue was added to the samples in the well plate, and the 
absorbance at 595 nm was measured. The dye Coomassie blue forms complexes with the amino acid 
residues of arginine, histidine, tyrosine, and lysine. The complexation causes a shift in the 
absorbance maximum from 465 nm (reddish-brown) to 595 nm (blue). In the complexation, 
Coomassie blue changes from its unbound cationic form to a more stable anionic-bound form. This 
characteristic of the Coomassie blue dye generally allows the relatively simple analysis of the protein 
content through absorbance measurements at 595 nm in samples. 
The plots of absorbance show irregularities and no clear trends over time (Figure 19), similar to 
some of the chlorophyll absorbance measurements (Figure 17 A, B). The experiment was repeated, 
and polypropylene well plates were used instead of polystyrene plates as cationic peptides can bind 
polystyrene.50 However, the results also showed irregularities without having any of the same 
tendencies over time as the first experiment. Generally, the Coomassie essay is used for solutions 
with acetone or ethanol concentrations of up to 10 %, which might partially explain the results with 
the higher solvent concentrations.51 However, the measured values are similar and irregular for the 
samples with lower solvent concentrations. The irregular results were found in all samples, 
independent of solvent, solvent concentration, and use of surfactants. The samples without 
surfactant may show more development over time, but these results are also not reproducible. The 
reason for these results may be related to the difficulties of absorbance measurements for this 
application.

There are some problems that appeared when using the plate reader in this experiment that might 
be responsible for variations in the results. A general problem with the plate reader method is that 
the path length of the sample is dependent on the fill height in the well, as the light absorption is 
measured through the surface of the sample. Aside from the possibility of slight variations in the 
volume due to pipetting, the surface tension also needs consideration. The addition of surfactant 
greatly impacts surface tension and, therefore, the path length. An additional problem is the air 
bubbles that form in the wells during the pipetting and further skew the path length. Furthermore, 
there is the problem of fragmented pieces of algae tissue in the analysis of the extraction liquid. 
Lastly, the well plate was always covered with a plastic film to prevent evaporation of the solvents; 
however, sometimes it could be observed that the solvents condensed from inside on the foil. As the 
measurement was through this foil, this would also have an impact on the absorbance 
measurement. The more commonly used UV-vis instrument avoids many of these potential 
problems. The fill height is irrelevant because the measurement is parallel to the surface of the 
liquid sample, and therefore the path length is not influenced. Furthermore, the problem of 
condensation is avoided as well. However, as UV-vis measurement generally requires a higher 
sample volume, it is unsuitable for the kinetic analysis of low-scale extraction and can only be used 
here for the absorption of the samples at the very end of the solvent extraction procedure. 
Specifically, for the chlorophyll determination, however, there is the alternative of measuring the 
florescence instead of the absorbance. For the protein analysis, there is the option to analyse the 
extraction liquid after the completion of the extraction. As there is a higher volume available, it is 
feasible to either filter or centrifuge to remove floating cell wall fractions. This, however, only 
eliminates one of the problems. Figure 20 shows the absorption correlated to the protein-dye 
complexes in the different samples in the final extraction liquid. The absorption appears higher in 
some of the samples after centrifugation (Figure 20 B) than before centrifugation (Figure 20 A), 
which might indicate that the cell wall fragments and particles were successfully removed and 
therefore less light scattering interferes with the absorbance measurement. However, other samples 
seem to have reduced absorption, which indicates that another interfering factor might skew the 
measurement. Another problem that needs to be considered is that centrifugation may remove 
some of the proteins from the extraction liquid and therefore invalidate the measurement. An 
evaluation of the protein content in the pellet after centrifugation may be conducted in the future to 
eliminate this as a possible interfering factor.

5.2.1.4 Carbohydrates 
The optotracer Carbotrace 680 leads to fluorescence around 680 nm in the presence of some 
saccharides. Specifically, it is known to stain glucans. Since there are some starches present within 
the cells of U. fenestrata, the Carbotrace 680 may also give an indication if this is present in the 
extraction liquid during the extraction from the cell wall. Though so far it is unclear if the Carbotrace 
680 would also interact with ulvan, it should be able to detect if cellulose from the cell walls is 
solubilized. 
Emission spectra were recorded for samples to which Carbotrace 680 had been added (Figure 21 A). 
After the subtraction of the values corresponding to the chlorophyll emission, none of the samples 
without the addition of CAPB showed a clear increase in emission around 680 nm. In the samples 
with surfactant, an emission was detected. However, a similar emission was also detected in blank 
samples (only solvent with surfactant and Carbotrace 680) (Figure 21 B). While the peaks of the 
samples with acetone and ethanol may indicate the presence of some glucans, the signal is too noisy 
to be conclusive. Therefore, for the samples with surfactant, this method of detection for glucans is 
unsuitable. What could alternatively be attempted to gain knowledge of the sugar composition is an 
HPLC sugar analysis of the extracted cell material compared to untreated U. fenestrata. This could 
then also give further information on the presence of ulvan in the extracted cell walls.

5.2.2 
Sonication bath 
The experiments with the tree-step extraction procedure showed that these treatments were 
insufficient for the isolation of the cell wall structure. Therefore, an alternative method in which the 
chemical and physical forces are working simultaneously towards the cell rupture was explored. For 
this purpose, a similar series of experiments was conducted in a sonication bath. As in the three-step 
approach, samples with lower concentrations showed more fragmentation and more clear cells, as 
well as higher chlorophyll absorption. It was decided to do the samples in the sonication bath with 
solvent solutions of 10 vol% ethanol, 10 vol% acetone, and MilliQ. Again, samples with and without 
added surfactant were produced. 
In the tested systems, the simultaneous approach generally yielded more empty cells and promoted 
tissue fragmentation more reliably than the sequential approach. Generally, samples prepared with 
water in the sonication bath showed more fragmentation and empty cells than the similar sample 
prepared in an oil bath with a sequential 1-minute ultrasonication. A sample produced in MilliQ 
yielded a completely emptied cell-wall structure; however, when this test was repeated, this result 
was not observed (Figure 22). This showed that despite the removal of the holdfasts, there was 
considerable variation in the biomass, causing differing results.

5.2.4 
The final method for extraction 
The final extraction used a solution of 10 vol% ethanol with 1 vol% CAPB. In this upscaled 
experiment, 10 g of algae were added to 1.5 L of solvent solution. The algae were pretreated with 
the meat grinder and remained in the sonication bath for six hours. To further investigate the 
response of the holdfast regions we exposed to the same treatment, the experiment was also 
repeated with only the holdfasts. 
Due to the high solvent volume of the extraction, it was possible to take higher volumes of the 
samples over time, therefore allowing the analysis with an UV-vis instrument. Furthermore, the 
higher sampling volume allowed the removal of floating particles in the extraction through 
centrifugation. This allowed the evaluation of the extraction kinetics through absorption as originally 
intended (Figure 30 A). 
When both fluorescence and absorbance are plotted over time at their respective maxima, a very 
similar development can be observed (Figure 30 B). This establishes a convergent validity to 
measurement techniques, indicating the absence of systematic error at least in this solvent system. 
This may further show that fluorescence can be used as a reliable alternative to UV-vis 
measurements for chlorophyll determination and does not require centrifugation before analysis to 
obtain reliable results. Interestingly, the absorbance increases over time in the non-centrifuged 
samples, even when the fluorescence and absorbance of the centrifuged samples plateau after 
about three hours. This could indicate that while the chlorophyll content in the extraction liquid 
stagnates, the number of particles in the extraction liquid increases. It might therefore be that in the 
final three hours, the already-emptied cells fracture into smaller particles.

sense. In the context of the particle size distribution, this might mean that the bigger particles had 
more cells that were not ruptured than the smaller particles. Therefore, the different compositions 
of the particles will lead to deviations. 
Both analysis types give different kinds of information. However, the analysis by weight seems to 
give results that more intuitively represent the sample. The representation of the size distribution by 
number of particles per size fraction gives more differentiated results and the possibility to set 
specific bin sizes, but the results may be misleading.

5.3 The material formulation 
To make a material from the treated algae, two different approaches were chosen. For one, there is 
the option of using the algae as the reinforcer or filler in combination with another biopolymer with 
good film-forming abilities, and secondly, the treated algae should at this point contain mostly 
polysaccharides. These should have some available hydroxide groups, which can therefore be 
crosslinked by the reaction with CA. 
5.3.1 
Chitosan films 
The idea to use chitosan as a binder for the treated algae stems from the possibility of ionic 
crosslinks forming between the chitosan and ulvan within the algae, as well as hydrogen bonds 
forming with the other polysaccharides. For this purpose, it was attempted to solvent-cast films with 
differing compositions of treated algae and chitosan (Figure 36). However, this attempt failed in 
most cases, as no full films formed. The exceptions were a film of only treated algae, a film with 
75wt % algae, both with a weight of a weight of 0.2 g, and the pure chitosan films, both with a 
weight of 0.1 and 0.2 g. It appears that the addition of higher percentages of algae interferes with 
the film-forming abilities of chitosan. It might be simply that, due to the bigger particles of the algae, 
the films would need to be thicker. Interestingly, the sample with only algae did form a film, though 
it was very porous, and tensile testing could not detect any stress in response to the applied strain. 
The fact that a film formed in the only algae sample but not when chitosan was added may be due to 
the high surface tension of the chitosan solution in the polystyrene petri dishes. Specifically, once 
the majority of the water has evaporated, the chitosan solution appears to have accumulated

5.3.2 
Crosslinking with citric acid 
For the samples that were intended to be crosslinked with CA, films were formed after the thermal 
sequence for the crosslinking reaction. First, a wet cake was formed through vacuum filtration, 
which was followed by a dying step in a Rapid Köthen instrument that used both heat and pressure 
to remove moisture from the film. The product is a much denser film than that obtained through 
solvent casting. A number of films were formed from the different samples, including the samples 
with different amounts of CA as well as films made from only the treated algae and a sample that 
had the same thermal procedure as the samples with CA but without any CA added. Additionally, 
films were also formed from the treated algae that had been separated into two size fractions 
through sieving for the determination of particle sizes. With the exception of the last two films 
mentioned, all were intended to have a weight of 0.08 g, though as the algae was not dried before it 
was used to form the wet cake, the weight of the needed wet processed algae needed to be 
determined through moisture content. The results of the moisture content proved unreliable; 
therefore, the produced films had weights ranging between 0.05 and 0.1 g, which also resulted in 
variations in thickness (see Appendix).

Strips of the different films were subjected to tensile tests to determine the modulus and tensile 
strength of the different films and to see if significant variations due to the treatment could be 
observed (Figure 37) (stress vs. strain curves see in Appendix). 
The averaged results of the tensile test show that the treatment with CA did not increase tensile 
strength, and higher amounts of CA (10% and 30%) show significantly lower modulus compared to 
films prepared with the sample that underwent the same thermal treatment without CA. The strain 
at break was is also lower in the samples with CA (see Appendix). It would be expected that 
successful crosslinking would result in increased tensile strength and modulus. Further, it can be 
observed that there is significant variation between the duplicates of the samples. This may be due 
to the inhomogeneity of the films caused by the large particle size of the treated algae. Furthermore, 
the weight of the films and, therefore, the thickness may impact the results, despite the fact that the 
film thickness was measured for each of the test strips and used to calculate the modulus and tensile 
strength. This behaviour can be observed in the samples in which the particles were separated by 
particle size and the fractions were used to form the films. The films made from particles smaller 
than 500 µm showed the highest average tensile strength and modulus among all the samples, while 
the films formed from particles bigger than 500 µm showed the lowest tensile strength and modulus 
in the film, with around the same thickness as the others. The film with the sample particle size but 
double the weight shows a much higher tensile strength and modulus. This may be due to the much 
higher contact area between the fragments in thicker samples. Generally, modulus and tensile 
strength are material properties independent of the amount of material used. However, here it 
appears that due to the inhomogeneity of the films, there is a dependence on the thickness of the 
films.

6. Conclusion 
During this master thesis research, different solvent systems and treatments were explored to find 
an effective way to isolate cell walls from U. fenestrata. It was shown that amongst the tested 
treatments, the best extraction could be achieved through a combination of a 10 vol% ethanol 
solution in combination with 1 vol% CAPB and a combination of different mechanical forces, namely 
shear forces from a meat grinder, sonic waves, and heat from a sonication bath. Furthermore, 
different approaches to determining the progression and success of the extraction were evaluated. 
It was found that fluorescence measurements are a more robust alternative to absorption 
measurements in the determination of chlorophyll content in the extraction liquid. Moreover, 
several factors that could potentially lead to inaccurate results from the absorbance measurements 
were identified. As well as a solution by which the absorbance of chlorophyll can be reliably 
measured in UV-vis instruments. The kinetics of the extraction were studied, and it was found that 
the chlorophyll content in the samples plateaus after three to four hours of treatment, but 
fragmentation of the algae may continue beyond that time. The particle size distribution of the 
treated algae was analysed through two different types of analysis. Additionally, the structure and 
response of the holdfasts of the algae to the treatment were explored. In the second part, it was 
attempted to solvent-cast films of chitosan and the treated algae, which was mostly unsuccessful. 
Furthermore, CA was added to the treated algae with the intention of inducing crosslinks between 
the hydroxyl groups of the polysaccharides. TGA indicates that this attempt did not yield the desired 
results as the thermal stability decreased, which may be due to acid hydrolysis or hydrogen bond 
dissociation. The algae after the different treatment stages were used to form films in a Rapid 
Köthen, which was successful. The films were further analysed by tensile testing, showing that the 
addition of CA did not improve mechanical properties. However, it was found that films formed from 
smaller particles had greatly increased strength and modulus compared to films formed from bigger 
particles. Finally, a method to form films of algae with chitosan and CA crosslinking was attempted, 
which may warrant more research in the future. 
Furthermore, in the future more time could be invested into further improving the extraction 
method and find the reason why some of the cells appear to be resistant to the treatment. 
Additionally, it may be interesting to investigate the composition of the extracted cell walls through 
HPLC sugar analysis to identify which of the major cell wall polysaccharides remain after the 
treatment. Moreover, calibration curves may be made for chlorophyll in the different solvent 
systems to allow the direct comparison of chlorophyll content in the different samples. The 
composition the holdfast region of U. fenestrata may also warrant more research. The second phase 
recovered from the extraction process, namely the extraction liquid, may be further investigated to 
discover possible applications, such as food or feedstock resources, and to determine if the proteins 
did retain their native structure during the extraction process. 
For the material formulation, it may be interesting to attempt the crosslinking again with a different 
catalyst, and to analyse the success: One way may be to do a titration. Furthermore, the 
combination of chitosan crosslinked with CA and cell walls may be further explored in the future as 
well.
