Abstract
Coastal proﬁle evolution is modelled using a new tool, created by DHI. The tool
is a non-hydrostatic wave-ﬂow model, named MIKE 3 Wave Model FM. The new
model is eﬀective compared to full CFD models, when it comes to computational
time.
However, this eﬀectiveness does come with certain simplifying assumptions.
The models general performance is investigated as three analyses. Firstly, the models
sensitivity to mesh conﬁgurations are investigated. Secondly, the models ability to
simulate the free surface, particle velocities and turbulent quantities accurately, is
investigated by comparison with physical experiments. And lastly, the cross-shore
proﬁle evolution determined by the morphological model is investigated similarly to
the hydrodynamics, by comparison with physical experiments.
The model was seen being very stable to alterations of the mesh conﬁguration, when
modelling the free surface and particle velocities. When turbulent quantities, such
as the eddy viscosity, were considered, the results were seen converging to certain
values for reﬁnement of the mesh. This means that if turbulence, sediment transport
and morphology are to be modelled accurately, a certain reﬁnement of the mesh is
necessary.
Generally, the free surface was seen corresponding well to test results.
The mean
surface elevation and wave heights across the domain was ﬁtting experimental results
and phenomenons such as reﬂection of the waves on the sloping beach was captured
by the model. However, when considering the mean surface elevation close to the
shore, it was observed that the set-up was overestimated by the model, by a factor of
up to 10. The magnitude of particle velocities were captured well by the model, but
some inconsistencies were observed, especially outside the surf zone. Here, the model
was seen producing velocity proﬁles resembling undertow, even though the physical
experiments showed no proof of this undertow current.
Turbulent quantities were
also determined and compared with test results. Turbulent kinetic energy proﬁles was
seen ﬁtting measured values very well and in general the production of turbulence was
seen having no to little variation from the experiment across the domain. However,
when considering turbulent shear stresses, several diﬀerences between measurements
and model results was seen. The most important being a general overestimation of
the magnitude of the results, which was most likely due to an underestimation of
the dissipation of turbulent kinetic energy. Another signiﬁcant diﬀerence was found
before breaking, which showed a positive shear stress, when the experiment suggested
a negative shear stress.
This was related back to the incorrect undertow velocity
proﬁles. Which yielded positive velocity gradients and not negative as the experiment
showed.
To investigate the eﬀect of the inconsistencies found in the hydrodynamic analysis,
simulations with morphological evolution was performed. The model was seen being
able to handle coastal proﬁle evolution to some extend. The model was capable of
creating a breaker bar from an initially straight sloped bed. In this morphological
analysis, the breaker bar location was modelled quite on-target, however the height
and length of the bar was seen being overestimated for both investigated cases. Fur-
thermore, physical experiments showed a deep and narrow trough immediately after
the bar crest, and this was not captured by the model, which only showed superﬁ-
cial erosion of the bed at the trough location. This is believed to occur because a
single-value-function is used to model the surface elevation, which makes the break-
ing process diﬃcult to capture completely, and this is why there was seen a lack of
sediment transport locally immediately after the breaker bar.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Methods for numerical hydraulic modelling are under constant development. DHI has
created a new tool for modelling waves and currents, called MIKE 3 Wave Model FM.
This model is a non-hydrostatic wave-ﬂow model and is created to simulate the trans-
formation and development of propagating surface waves.
This is done to accurately
investigate surf and swash zone dynamics. The model uses Incompressible Navier-Stokes
equations to govern the waves and ﬂow, while the turbulence quantities are governed by
the k−ϵ-turbulence model. A more thorough introduction to the governing equations and
the turbulence model is presented in chapter 2.
Like most other non-hydrostatic wave models, this model describes wave breaking with
the free surface described as single value function of the horizontal plane. Such that the
free surface is described by a height function consisting of the still water depth (d) and
the surface elevation (η), e.g. h = d + η. Using this formulation eliminates two diﬃcult
aspects of modelling wave breaking. The ﬁrst being the mixing of air and water at the
surface. By using the this single value formulation, there is no mixing of air and water in
the model. The second aspect is that a wave cannot overturn or generally be described
by more than one point on the water surface. Eliminating these aspects from the model
causes less accuracy, but makes the model very eﬀective in terms of computational time
compared to other full CFD models.
1.2
Motivation
Having a fast and eﬀective computational model can prove to be very valuable. Many
choices concerning the model can alter the time it takes to run and more importantly
the accuracy of the results.
The motivation for this thesis is to ensure that this new
non-hydrostatic wave model in fact can produce accurate results. Furthermore, overes-
timations, underestimations and other diﬀerences from test measurements are expected.
It is desirable to perform testing so that these errors are documented and discussed.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.3
Research questions
As stated the purpose of this project is to investigate and validate the models performance
and to document any positive or negative eﬀects that may exist in the model. To study
this, a series of research questions are posed to clarify what exactly is investigated and how.
1. How sensitive is the model to diﬀerent mesh conﬁgurations?
How sensitive is the model to the mesh conﬁguration and what resolution is required
to produce accurate results? This question will be answered to some degree by a mesh
analysis. Before attempting to reproduce test results and measurements, it is desirable
and recommended that some knowledge of the mesh conﬁguration is obtained. Initially,
a total of 12 diﬀerent mesh conﬁgurations will be created. These consists of cases where
the mesh close to the bed is logarithmically reﬁned, where the mesh at the surface is
equidistantly reﬁned and one where both the mesh at the bed and surface is reﬁned. Fur-
thermore, each of these three conﬁgurations is created with 10, 20, 40 and 80 cells in the
vertical direction, while the cell size in the horizontal direction is kept constant at 0.2 m.
This value has proven suﬃcient in earlier model runs conducted by DHI and reﬁnes the
wavelength suﬃciently.
To answer the posed question, three speciﬁc parameters are investigated. Firstly, the
wave height as a function of the horizontal cross-shore distance is plotted for all cases.
Then velocity proﬁles before, at and after breaking are plotted and lastly, eddy viscosity
proﬁles for the same three locations. The results in this preliminary analysis is used to
determine a tendency of convergence and to determine a mesh conﬁguration that can be
used for the following analyses and also to comment more generally on how signiﬁcant
diﬀerences is seen from diﬀerent mesh conﬁgurations.
2. How well does the model capture the free surface, particle velocities and turbulent
quantities?
To answer this question the model is set up to run against a physical experiment done by
(Van Der A et al., 2017). Certain requirements to this hydrodynamic analysis is proposed.
Firstly, it was desirable to use an experiment performed with a ﬁxed bed - this ensured
that no inconsistencies could arise due to diﬀerence in the morphology between the model
and the physical experiment. Secondly, it was necessary to have detailed measurements
of both the surface elevation and the particle velocities. These demands was met by the
article by (Van Der A et al., 2017). The results from the test also contains turbulent
parameters such as the turbulent kinetic energy, turbulent shear stress and more. There-
fore this analysis is a rare opportunity to investigate the models performance on a ﬁxed
bed with respect to surface elevation, wave heights, velocities and the relevant parameters
determined by the implemented turbulence model.
This analysis determines and describes what errors and inconsistencies may arise in re-
lation with the hydrodynamic quantities in free surface modelling using the MIKE 3
Wave Model FM. It should be noted that the sediment transport model used in this
non-hydrostatic wave model is identical do the sand transport module for any other three
dimensional MIKE models, thus it is the input quantities such as ﬂow velocities and turbu-
lent quantities that actually govern the sediment transport. Therefore, the hydrodynamic
analysis is very relevant with respect to investigating the ﬁnal cross-shore proﬁle evolution.

3. How well does the model capture sediment transport and how well does it perform

To answer this question the model is set up to run against another physical experiment

2.2
The turbulence model
The turbulence in the hydrostatic as well as the non-hydrostatic model is modelled using
an eddy viscosity concept. In general the eddy viscosity can be assumed to be divided
into a vertical and horizontal component - in some cases, when using a hydrostatic model,
determining the vertical and horizontal component using diﬀerent formulations can prove
to be necessary. But in the case of free surface modelling using the non hydrostatic MIKE
3 Wave FM model, both the horizontal and vertical eddy viscosity are considered equal and
both are determined by the k −ϵ-formulation. The turbulence model is seen implemented
in the momentum equations (2.3), (2.4) and (2.5), by the eddy viscosity νT .

Vertical distribution of σ-layers
In the following, the importance of meshing will be investigated. More speciﬁcally, the
number and distribution of σ-layers, is of interest. This analysis is done to validate the
stability of the model with respect to the meshing of the domain. The analysis is performed
on a domain, based on (Baldock et al., 2011). This test case is chosen since it has been
modelled previously by DHI employees and because (Jacobsen et al., 2014) has succesfully
validated their OpenFOAM morphological model against this case.

Domain

decreases as we move closer the the shoreline which is located at x = 67 m. This gives

3.3
Discussion
To investigate the performance of the model for diﬀerent mesh conﬁgurations, a total of
12 models were run, all with diﬀerent mesh conﬁgurations. The analysis distinguished
between the number of layers in the vertical direction, and whether the bottom, the sur-
face or both were reﬁned compared to the remaining domain.
Firstly, the root mean square wave heights as a function of the horizontal distance was de-
termined for all 12 cases, and later these results were compared to check for convergence.
It was noted that the oscillations of the wave height in the horizontal direction, caused
by reﬂection, became smaller in magnitude for a higher number of layers, other than this
very little variation was seen between the diﬀerent cases. From this it is arguable that the
mesh conﬁguration has very little eﬀect on the wave height and the surface elevation.
Next, the horizontal velocity proﬁles were created for three diﬀerent locations along the
domain. One outside the surf zone, e.g. at x = 10 m. One approximately at breaking,
x = 59 m. And one in the surf zone at x = 64 m. From this presentation of results, the
main conclusion is that the mesh needs to be reﬁned logarithmically at the bed. This is
the case because of signiﬁcant diﬀerences in the velocity proﬁles seen close to the bed, for
the case with a coarse mesh at the bed. Furthermore, small diﬀerences are seen close to
the surface for the case of no further reﬁnement of the mesh at the surface. Therefore it
is reasonable to choose a mesh conﬁguration that reﬁnes both the bed and surface, since
the largest velocity gradients are seen here. With respect to diﬀerences due to a diﬀerent
number of layers, there are no results suggesting a minimum amount of layers since no
real convergence is yet to be seen.
Lastly, the eddy viscosities describing the production/dissipation of turbulence, was presen-
ted as vertical proﬁles at the same three locations described above. The obvious observa-
tion from these results, were the fact that a clear convergence was seen for all mesh con-
ﬁgurations and all three investigated locations. It was noted that the diﬀerence between
results computed with 40 and 80 layers were minimal for almost all results - while the
diﬀerence between 10, 20 and 40 layers was seen being signiﬁcantly larger. From this,
it can be concluded that the modelling of the turbulence parameters needs a much ﬁner
resolution, compared to other quantities, in order to produce accurate and stable results.
To ensure a good balance between computational time and accurate results, it is decided
that future simulations in this project should be run with 40 cells in the vertical direction.

Chapter 4
Hydrodynamic analysis
In the present chapter, a thorough analysis of the hydrodynamics of the non-hydrostatic
MIKE 3 Wave Model FM is presented. The analysis is carried out as a comparison with
test results obtained by the authors of (Van Der A et al., 2017). The authors, has carried
out tests in a large-scale wave ﬂume located at the Polytechnic University of Catalunya
in Barcelona, Spain. More precisely, these tests are carried out investigating a ﬁxed cross-
shore proﬁle with a developed breaker bar, under regular wave action. The model setup
is described in detail in the following section.
4.1
Model setup
4.1.1
Domain
The wave ﬂume test setup has a total length of 80 m. A wave generator is placed at x = 0
m. The depth is constant with a value of d = 2.65 m until x = 34 m, where the ﬁxed
sloping bed begins. Note that the bed proﬁle was created in a preceding experiment (Van
Der Zanden et al., 2016). Here the bed was mobile, consisting of grains with d50 = 0.25
mm. The initial slope of this mobile bed was 1:10. Regular waves was then applied until
the breaking location had reached equilibrium and the breaker bar had formed its geo-
metry. 10 cm of sand was then removed all over the bed, and 20 cm of concrete was cast
in order to create the ﬁxed bed. The roughness of the concrete is estimated to be between
1mm-2mm therefore, the grain size in the model input is set equal to d50 = 0.75 mm.
The run-up seen in the model was slightly greater than what was seen in the physical
test, this caused the model to crash and therefore the model domain length was extended
with 10 m. Furthermore, the length of the wave generation zone was set equal to 20 m,
thus the total length of the model domain is equal to 110 m, and not 80 m, as seen in the
physical test setup. To be clear, x = 20 m in the model, corresponds to x = 0 m in the
physical test. The cross-shore proﬁle can be seen in ﬁgure 4.1.

strong undertow proﬁle, which is not commonly seen before the wave has broken. In the
case of morphology modelling, this could deﬁnitely lead to an overestimation of sediment
transport, an thus an inaccurate depiction of the breaker bar.

approximately x = 61 m, while the wave in the simulation has already past the vertical
proﬁle located at x = 63 m.
4.2.3
Turbulent kinetic energy (TKE)
To investigate the models performance with respect to determining turbulence quantit-
ies using the k −ϵ-turbulence model, the turbulent kinetic energy is outputted from the
model, to compare to the existing measurements from the physical test. Figure 4.10 shows
the vertical proﬁles of time-averaged turbulent kinetic energy (k) at the 12 locations dis-
tributed across the breaker bar. In general, the simulated model results are seen ﬁtting
the measured data quite nicely, both the order of magnitude and the behaviour of the
proﬁles correspond very well together. In the bar-trough the model is seen slightly overes-
timating the k, while it is under-estimated just after breaking, at x = 55.5 m and x = 56 m.
As expected, the results show a high amount of TKE at just as the wave is breaking
and immediately after, e.g. at x = 55 m to x = 57 m. The TKE is seen dissipating faster
in reality, than in the model, hence the underestimation of TKE after the breaker bar.

Discussion
When considering results from the ﬁrst part of the morphological analysis, e.g. the ﬁrst
of the two simulated cases, two signiﬁcant diﬀerences between experiment results and
modelled results was observed.
The ﬁrst, being that the geometry of the breaker bar was oﬀtarget. Essentially the
bar size was overestimated both in terms of height and length and the bar had migrated
further oﬀ-shore in the simulation, than what was measured in the experiments. The
inaccurate position of the breaker bar can be partly explained by including observations
made in the hydrodynamics analysis. Here, it was determined that the model created
strong undertow velocity proﬁles, even outside the surf zone, e.g. on the oﬀ-shore side of
the bar crest, even though no such proﬁle was measured in the physical experiment. This
undertow velocity proﬁle can induce a higher rate of sediment transport in an outward
direction and thus moving additional sediment outward and essentially repositioning the
breaker bar. This can also be veriﬁed by the fact positive turbulent shear stresses were
seen on the outward side of the bar crest, even though negative stresses were measured
at this location. The overestimation of the bar size, can also be addressed by considering
the turbulent shear stresses from the hydrodynamic analysis. Here it was determined that
the model overestimated the magnitude of the stresses. Furthermore it was seen that the
model also determined high stresses above the bar trough, which means that additional
sediment would be transferred to the bar crest - creating a larger breaker bar as a result.
The second noticeable diﬀerence between simulation and reality, was the length and
depth of the trough shoreward of the breaker bar crest. In the physical experiment, a very
distinguished trough was measured, where it was seen being quite deep and narrow. The
model did not capture these characteristics of the trough entirely. The simulation results
show a very low and long trough. It is believed that this relates back to the wave breaking
mechanism and the turbulence model. As mentioned in former chapters, the model is set
up with a single value function for the surface elevation and this causes diﬃculties when
modelling wave breaking, especially for waves with an overturning breaking mechanism
and cases where the mixing within the water column is high. It is believed that the break-
ing process suspends a lot more sediment locally just after breaking in the experiment
than what is simulated by the model.
When considering results from the second part of the morphological analysis, e.g. the
second of the two simulated cases, several signiﬁcant diﬀerent between experiment results
and modelled results was observed.
Similarly to the previous test case, the breaker bar is seen being signiﬁcantly longer
and taller than what is measured in the experiment.
Because of the similarity of the
problem, it is also believed the same reasons causes this behaviour. The primary reason
being that more sediment is seen being transported outward due to the larger oﬀshore
ﬂux seen in the time averaged velocity proﬁles shown in the hydrodynamic analysis, in
chapter 4.
The next issue in relation with the second test case, was that the model showed erosion
of the shoreline, where the physical experiment showed accretion. In relation with the hy-
drodynamic analysis, it has been discussed that for some wave types, the model does not
dissipate enough energy for from the wave and this could aﬀect the erosion of the bed
close to the shoreline. If the wave is forced the break at the bar due to reduction of depth,
a combination of reﬂection and shoaling can actually cause the wave to increase in height
and break a second time inside the surf zone, this is true for the simulations and the
energy of the second wave break can contribute to increased erosion at the shoreline.

The non-hydrostatic wave-ﬂow model MIKE 3 Wave Model FM proved to be very eﬀect-

this project and many related to the posed research questions, which were introduced in
chapter 1. To answer the posed research questions, several analyses and investigations

6.1
Research questions
1. How sensitive is the model to diﬀerent mesh conﬁgurations?
A simulation of regular wave action on a sloped bed was run 12 diﬀerent mesh conﬁg-
urations. For each simulation, wave heights, velocity proﬁles and eddy viscosity proﬁles
were determined at three diﬀerent locations - before, at and after wave breaking. The
purpose of the analysis was to determine any convergence of the results, which would lead
to guidelines of how ﬁne the mesh needed to be, in order to produce accurate results.
As a secondary analysis, it was also investigated whether or not the area close to the
bed and/or surface should be reﬁned further, with respect to the remaining domain. In
total four models with 10, 20, 40 and 80 cells in the vertical direction, were run for three
diﬀerent ways of distributing the mesh.
From the root mean square wave heights as a function of the horizontal distance, no
real convergence was observed. It was concluded that ’oscillations’ were present due to
wave reﬂection and the magnitude of these oscillations was seen reducing as the mesh was
reﬁned. But the model can be concluded to be very stable with respect to meshing, when
it comes to modelling the free surface. The development of wave height as a function of
horizontal distance was also concluded to be in accordance of the physical expectation,
which means that the wave heights were seen increasing up until the breaking point, where
a rapid decrease was seen.
Furthermore velocity proﬁles were outputted and analysed at three locations. For these
results, some deviation/convergence was seen towards the bed and surface. However, the
diﬀerences were not seen in the comparison of number of cells in the vertical direction,

of the maximum turbulent shear stress was seen further on-shore in the model than in the
experiment. It was concluded that this was also a symptom of a too low dissipation of
TKE and possibly also too low dissipation of the energy of the wave, as seen on ensemble
averaged surface elevation plots.
3. How well does the model capture sediment transport and how well does it perform
when including morphology updating?
To investigate the model performance with respect to modelling morphology and cross-
shore proﬁle evolution, simulations are run as replicas of the experiment setup by (Baldock
et al., 2011). In the physical test, many wave types were applied to the live bed, and in
the analysis performed in this report, the two regular wave cases were modelled.
When observing the comparison between results for the experiment and results from the
simulation, it was concluded that the model did have some capabilities when it comes to
modelling the evolution of cross-shore proﬁles. For both cases the model was seen creating
a breaker bar and following the test results, to some extend. However, there was observed
several diﬀerences between simulation and experiment.
The ﬁrst case simulated regular wave action that was expected to erode the bed close
to the shoreline. Here the model bar was seen being shifted 1-2 m in the oﬀ-shore dir-
ection and that the magnitude of the bar was overestimated both in height and length.
Furthermore, the physical experiment showed a quite steep and deep trough immediately
after the crest of the breaker bar. This trough was not captured by the model, which

showed some erosion after the breaker bar, but not an actual trough. This observation
was valid for both simulations.
It was concluded that the trough was modelled inac-
curately mostly due to the fact that model uses a single value function for the surface
elevation, which makes it impossible to model the plunging jet of water, which is what
causes this high and very local sediment transport.
The second simulation experienced some of the same problems as the ﬁrst, e.g. overestim-
ated bar geometry and inconsistencies with the trough behind the bar crest. Furthermore,
this second case was designed to induce accretion of sand close to the shoreline, and by
considering physical test results this was accomplished. However, the model was not able
to capture this phenomenon, since it actually eroded the coastline by approximately 0.5-
1.0 m, where 1.0-2.0 m of sand was actually seen deposited in the experiment. This is
consistent with the notion that the model could not dissipate energy from the wave fast
enough, thus modelling a too high sediment transport close to the coast.
6.2
Suggestions for future work
Further work is necessary to fully understand the model behaviour. In the present section
suggestions for future work are proposed. Some of these suggestions will be aimed at
further investigating speciﬁc observations done in this report, while some will be aimed
at generally investigating the model performance better.
6.2.1
Analysing the overestimated set-up
It was concluded that the model did overestimate the set-up measured by (Van Der A et
al., 2017). One of very applicable features of this non-hydrostatic model is to correctly
model the propagation of dispersive waves, including wave heights and set-up at the
coastline. Therefore it is recommended to apply the model to other cases were a mean
surface elevation has been measured, and to investigate whether or not the phenomenon
of overestimation is seen generally or for only some cases.
6.2.2
Extensive morphology research
The morphological analysis presented in this report, was performed on a limited amount of
test cases. To get a full picture, it is recommended to perform simulations of diﬀerent wave
action, and diﬀerent types of geometry. This report has focused greatly on investigating
the hydrodynamic results on a ﬁxed bed, and utilizing these observations to conclude
on inconsistencies with respect to the cross-shore proﬁle evolution. It is recommended
that an analysis is performed on a live bed, with measurements of surface elevation,
particle velocity and the proﬁle evolution. This way, it is possible to utilize results of
a hydrodynamic analysis to comment on the morphological development with a higher
certainty.
6.2.3
Complex geometry
Often, an area of interest can have complex geometry, and it would be very interesting to
see the model simulating domains with piers, groynes or other structures that can inﬂuence
the cross-shore proﬁle evolution. Structures will also interact with the dispersion of the
incoming waves, so performing a study where more complex geometry / structures are
implemented can be valuable, but also diﬃcult.
