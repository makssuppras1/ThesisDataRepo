Abstract
The off-grid solar (OGS) sector in Kenya has experienced rapid growth in the past decade, as it has been found
to be a solution to dramatically increase electricity access rates by being able to provide affordable energy
in rural, underserved communities. However, the amount of e-waste hereof has been growing at a similar
pace, and with the absence of any formal e-waste management, it has given rise to growing environmental
concerns. With solar devices containing various hazardous materials, such as lead and cadmium, improper
disposal or informal recycling may cause serious adverse effects to human health and the environment.
A research project at the UNEP DTU Partnership, titled ’Governing solar electronic waste recycling in Kenya’,
aims to investigate the end-of-life management of OGS products in Kenya, with a focus towards assessing the
scale of the problematic consequences hereof, and identifying ways in which current e-waste management
practices can become more sustainable. As part of this project, the purpose of this thesis was to develop a
framework, that is able to quantitatively assess e-waste management options from several criteria that con-
cerns both the economic, environmental and social sustainability. The objectives of the study were then 1) to
create a national overview of the OGS sector, and e-waste in general, in regard to the composition, typology,
present and projected volumes as well as current e-waste management infrastructure and related legislation
2) to describe the background governing e-waste management, and the potential barriers and drivers that
exist for such, both generally and specifically for Kenya 3) to construct a framework consisting of multiple
criteria that are able to qualitatively assess the economic, environmental and social sustainability of e-waste
management options - with consideration for the often limited data available 4) to test the assessment per-
formance of the framework from a set of fabricated e-waste management scenarios, and compare them in
terms of their assessment results.
The framework was modelled using Excel, and the assessment based on 12 different criteria divided equally
into the economic, environmental and social dimension. Each criteria was weighed equally towards the overall
assessment on the basis of creating an objective assessment. From the test, that consisted of the assessment
of four fabricated scenarios, the framework yielded higher overall scores (and thus indicating better per-
formance) for those scenarios which included more comprehensive e-waste management solutions. Both be-
cause these were assessed to provide larger environmentally beneficial impacts, and to be more economically
profitable as the value of the recovered materials was higher. They were thus was able to quickly compensate
for the higher initial investment cost. Prior to the assessment, there was an immediate expectation as to how
each scenario would perform both economically, environmentally and socially, based on knowledge through
experience and education. The assessment for each dimensions aligned with what could have been expected
for each scenario. For an example the inclusion or exclusion of battery recycling, which have very little eco-
nomic value, but contain many hazardous materials, was shown to affect the Economic and Environmental
Dimension oppositely to each other. Conclusively, the results were regarded as very plausible. However, more
tests would be necessary to confidently assess the performance of the framework. Thus it can be concluded
that a framework that is able to assess e-waste management options was constructed. It should however be
noted that this is with simplifications in regard to many aspects with the advantage of being easily realizable.
Naturally, the simplifications made may come at the expense of accuracy. As such, the framework may serve
as an appropriate tool to quickly filter through e-waste management options, in order to determine which
particular ones should be considered for further evaluation.

Sammenfatning
Salget af såkaldte ’off-grid’, eller blot små solceller, er eksploderet i Kenya i det seneste årti. Dette er tilfældet,
da det er fundet som løsning til at forøge den andel af befolkningen som har adgang til elektricitet, ved at
kunne tilbyde ’billig’ elektricitet i de undertjente samfund i landdistrikterne. Dog er mængden af elektronikaf-
fald heraf vokset i samme tempo, og med ingen formel elektronikaffaldsinfrastruktur, giver det anledning til
voksende miljømæssige bekymringer. Da solceller indeholder forskellige giftige materialer, såsom bly og cad-
mium, kan uhensigtsmæssig bortskaffelse få alvorlige konsekvenser for menneskers sundhed, samt miljøet.
Et forskningsprojekt ved UNEP DTU Partnership, ved navn ’Governing solar electronic waste recycling in Kenya’,
har til formål at undersøge hvordan solceller i Kenya bliver håndteret i deres "efterliv", med særligt fokus på at
vurdere omfanget af de problematiske konsekvenser heraf, samt identificere måder, hvorpå den nuværende
praksis for elektronikaffaldshåndtering kan blive mere bæredygtig. Som en del af dette projekt var formålet
med dette speciale, at udvikle et såkaldt ’framework’, eller model, som er i stand til kvantitativt at vurdere
mulige elektronikaffaldsinfrastrukture. Dette skulle gøres ud fra flere kriterier, der vedrører både økonomiske,
miljømæssige og sociale bæredygtighed, i overensstemmelse med den opfattelse af bæredygtig udvikling som
er defineret i Brundtland-rapporten fra 1987. Formålet med specialet var da 1) at skabe et nationalt overb-
lik over solcelle sektoren, og elektronikaffald generelt, i hensyn til dens komposition, kategorier, nuværende
og forventede mængder, samt nuværende elektronikaffaldsinfrastruktur og relateret lovgivning 2) at beskrive
baggrunden for elektronikaffaldshåndtering, og de potentielle barrierer og drivkræfter, som eksisterer, både
generelt og specifikt for Kenya 3) at konstruere et framework som består af adskillige kriterier, der er i stand til
kvalitativt at vurdere den økonomiske, miljømæssige og sociale bæredygtighed af elektronikaffaldsinfrastruk-
ture - hvor der samtidigt tages hensyn til de ofte begrænsede mængder data som er tilgængelig 4) at teste
vurderingsevnerne af frameworket ud fra et fremstillet sæt af scenarier som indbærer forskelige måder at
håndtere elektronikaffald, og derefter sammenligne dem med hensyn til vurderingerne givet af frameworket.
Frameworket blev modelleret i Excel, og vurderingen baseret på 12 forskellige kriterier, som var fordelt ligeligt
i den økonomiske, miljømæssige og sociale dimension. Alle kriterier blev tildelt samme vægt i beregnin-
gen for den samlede vurdering for at skabe en objektiv vurdering. Fra vurderingen af de fire fremstillede
scenarier, blev det observeret af frameworket gav en højere score (og dermed en indikation af bedre præsta-
tion) til de scenarier som indebar mere omfattende løsninger til elektronikaffaldshåndtering. Dette var både
fordi at disse blev vurderet til at give en større gavnlig miljømæssig effekt, og til at være mere økonomisk
rentable, da den samlede værdi af de genvundne materialer var højere. De var således i stand til hurtigt
at kompensere for den større investering som de kræver. Forud for vurderingerne af scenarierne, var der
en umiddelbar forventning om, hvordan hvert scenarie ville præstere både økonomisk, miljømæssigt og so-
cialt, baseret på viden gennem erfaring og uddannelse. Vurderingerne for hvert scenarie stemte udemærket
overens med det som kunne være forventet. For eksempel viste inklusionen eller eksklusionen af genan-
vendelsen af batterier, hvilke har en meget lille økonomisk værdi, men stort indhold af giftige materialer, at
påvirke den økonomiske og miljømæssige dimensions i modsætning til hinanden. Resultaterne fra vurderin-
gen blev betragtet som værende meget plausible. Det ville dog være nødvendigt at teste med flere scenarier
for at kunne fastslå frameworket’s vurderingsevner med sikkerhed. Det kan således konkluderes, at der blev
konstrueret et framework, som er i stand til at vurdere mulige alternative elektronikaffaldsinfrastrukture. Det
bør dog bemærkes, at dette er med simplifikationer i hensyn til mange aspekter, derimod med den fordel

1
Introduction
The off-grid solar (OGS) sector has been rapidly growing in Kenya in the past decade, and has been one of
the most exciting trends in renewable energy generation. The ability of OGS-products to generate affordable
electricity in rural areas, where there is no connection to the utility grid, has made it possible to dramatically
increase the electricity access rate, and break the cycle of energy poverty that has held back socio-economic
progress for millions of people - all the while reducing CO2 emissions (World Bank Group, 2020).
However, it does not come without repercussions. The ever growing number of OGS products in Kenya eventu-
ally becomes electronic waste (e-waste), and thus the e-waste being generated and disposed of is increasing
at a similar pace. In 2022, the e-waste generated from OGS products was estimated to be 5,000 tonnes, from
which only 1 percent is estimated to be recycled (ACE TAF, 2021c). From the contents of various hazardous
materials, such as lead, cadmium, mercury and sulphuric acid in OGS products and their constituent com-
ponents (including batteries), their accumulation from the lack of proper waste management, may result in
serious adverse effects to the environment and health of the very people benefiting from them (Latunussa
et al., 2016; GIZ, 2018). The imperative to act on this problem is growing increasingly urgent, both for the OGS
sector and for other stakeholders, including charitable donors and governments.
There is thus a need to implement suiting e-waste management, which includes the collection and recycling
of OGS products. This would not only help mitigate the adverse environmental and human health effects, but
would also be essential in the transition to a more circular economy by recovering valuable materials and pre-
venting the production of new materials (CDC Group, 2021). But, given that Kenya is a 3rd World Country, there
exist very little relating information and accurate data, which makes it difficult to decide which e-waste man-
agement that should be implemented from comprehensive studies such as economic feasibility studies (EFS)
and life-cycle assessments (LCA). This thesis attempts to solve this issue, by providing a simplified framework
that is able to assess both the economic, environmental and social sustainability of an e-waste management
option, from which a decision can be supported with the ability to compare options.

1.2 The Scope and Objective of the Thesis
2
1.1
The Research Project
The results of this thesis will contribute to a larger, ongoing research project at the UNEP DTU Partnership,
titled ’Governing solar electronic waste recycling in Kenya’, led by project coordinator Ulrich Elmer Hansen
(Ulrich E. Hansen, 2021). The project aims to investigate end-of-life (EOL) management of OGS products in
Kenya, with a focus towards assessing the scale of the problematic consequences hereof, and identifying ways
in which current e-waste management practices can become more sustainable (Lasse Hemmingsen, 2021). This
will include the identification of appropriate private and public schemes for the collection and recycling of
e-waste from OGS products in a manner that create economic value for local communities.
The project will proceed through the execution of five interconnected work packages (WPs) focusing on analysing:
(WP1) the role of leading solar system suppliers in governing and managing e-waste from their devices; (WP2)
the disposal practices of end-users and the processing of solar e-waste in local recycling economies; and
(WP3) scenarios for future regulation and planning of solar e-waste in Kenya (Ulrich E. Hansen, 2021).
The results of this thesis will relate to the latter (WP3). In WP3, there is a plan for the further development
of the multi-objective method by Boldrin et al. (2020), in order to create a quantitative assessment, which
considers effects regarding economic, social and environmental aspects of an e-waste management - which
this thesis attempts. This will be used to help identify which waste management options that are considered
appropriate and feasible (Hansen et al., 2020).
1.2
The Scope and Objective of the Thesis
The aim of this thesis was to support the decision-making in the above mentioned research project, by creating
a framework that could be used to assess the economic, environmental and social sustainability of an e-
waste management option, specifically one for OGS-panels. The assessment was to be done from multiple
criteria, that would provide the user with an easy to understand, quantitative and unbiased assessment. And
although the framework has the potential to be broadly applicable, it was made with particular consideration
to circumstances in Kenya and the limited data available here.
In order to achieve this, the objectives of the thesis were:
• To create a national overview of the OGS sector, and e-waste in general, in regard to the composition,
typology, present and projected volumes as well as current e-waste management infrastructure and
related legislation.
• To describe the background governing e-waste management, and the potential barriers and drivers that
exist for such, both generally and specifically for Kenya.
• To construct a framework consisting of multiple criteria that are able to qualitatively assess the eco-
nomic, environmental and social sustainability of e-waste management options - with consideration
for the often limited data available.
• To test the assessment performance of the framework from a set of fabricated e-waste management
scenarios, and compare them in terms of their assessment results.
Although this thesis is focused towards OGS products, it is, as will be described throughout, apparent that
a solution would have to include other types of e-waste, in order to be economically feasible and environ-
mentally impactful. As such, the scope has been widened, so that aspects surrounding e-waste in general are
described conjointly with those for OGS products.

2.1.4
Typology of e-waste
In order to define the typology of e-waste, it is necessary first define electrical and electronic equipment
(EEE). EEE is defined as “any household or business item with circuitry or electrical components with power or
battery supply” (Step Initiative, 2014). EEE becomes Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE), or e-
waste, after the equipment and their parts “have been discarded by the owner as waste without the intention
of reuse” (Step Initiative, 2014).
As EEE comprises many different products, their material composition also differs, as well as their related
methods of recycling and disposal (Forti et al., 2020). From the WEEE directive (2012/19/EU), which have been
adopted by European Members States, EEE are grouped into six categories based on their waste management
attributes (Forti et al., 2020). These six categories of e-waste are shown and described in Figure 8 below.
Figure 5: E-waste categories as defined by the EU’s WEEE directive.
As mentioned, the categorization above is in compliance with the WEEE directive adopted by European mem-
ber states (European Paliament 2003), and also the internationally recognised framework for e-waste statistics
(Forti et al., 2020). As to whether OGS products and their constituent components is definable as e-waste is
a technical and legal discussion due to the dissonant legislation that exist in the world (NORCAP, 2022). In
the EU, OGS products are regarded as e-waste and perceived as hazardous waste, where contrarily in the USA
they consider it to be neither (Magalini et al., 2016).

2.1.5
E-waste management infrastructure
In Kenya, as well as in numerous other African countries, the collection and recycling of OGS and other types
of e-waste happens predominantly by smaller informal e-waste collectors and recycling shops, or the solar
companies themselves (Global LEAP Awards, 2019). There is no public infrastructure that supports the collec-
tion of OGS, or e-waste in general. Instead, small scale collectors travel from door-to-door of both residential
and commercial areas to collect recyclable materials (Magalini et al., 2017). Also, there exist networks of small-
scale collectors who scavenge municipal general solid waste landfills for potentially valuable e-waste, such as
those containing copper, steel, aluminium, plastic etc. They then dismantle and extract materials of interest,
which are are then resold locally or exported. Informal business models typically require a small fee for items,
or acquire products for free from the scavenging of landfills. An informal business like described, operates in
the open air, and thereby avoids the need to rent a premise or pay for a business license (Global LEAP Awards,
2019). Although the business of informal recycling may retain its relevance, it is likely to change with the
adoption and enforcement of the new e-waste regulations, which will also regulate the process surrounding
the obtainment of license for collection points and treatment plants, the procedures of which are described
further in Section 2.1.6.
There are a very limited number of formal e-waste dismantling operations in Kenya, such as the Waste Elec-
trical and Electronic Equipment Centre (WEEE Centre) in Nairobi. The WEEE Centre serve multiple solar clients,
but is due to privacy agreements and Memorandums of Understanding, unable to divulge into much detail
(Global LEAP Awards, 2019). In general, there is a concern for security when managing e-waste, as these may
involve the task of destroying sensitive data on hard-drives (Global LEAP Awards, 2019). For OGS, there are
also concerns for data, as well as consumer protection, intellectual property, and product design (Global LEAP
Awards, 2019). Issues has also been posed about the WEEE Center’s operating standards and transparency, as
it is not always explicit where companies and collectors move e-waste products.
The WEEE centre has a capacity of 200 metric tonnes of e-waste per month (Kimani Chege, 2021). However,
as a result of inadequate awareness among the public, the non-existing collection, as well as lacking laws
and regulations regarding recycling electronics, they are only processing 35-50 tonnes per month (Kimani
Chege, 2021). All together, less than 10 percent of e-waste generated in Kenya is properly managed, leaving

2.1.6
Relevant strategies, policies and regulations
The primary legislation that guides e-waste management in Kenya is the Environmental Management and
Coordination Act 1999 (Revised in 2015), together with the Waste Management Regulations (2006). The laws
within these prohibits the handling, transportation and disposal of waste without a valid license issued by
the National Management Authority (NEMA) (Macharia, 2021). As of 2021, only six recyclers have a license to
handle e-waste in Kenya (Macharia, 2021). This is meager, considering the large number of EEE consumers in
Kenya. Generally, even e-waste recyclers with a license fail to bid to purchase e-waste, as they are able to
acquire it free of charge from diverse sources instead (Macharia, 2021). These laws fails to provide further
guidance in these instances where the recyclers fail to bid to purchase e-waste. This bureaucratic process
is slow and as a result, huge stock of obsolete computers and other e-waste builds up in public institutions
(Macharia, 2021).
The Ministry of Kenya has finalized the development of the Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) Regula-
tions, and now undergoes final drafting at the Attorney General’s office (Macharia, 2021). Among others, the
EPR regulations identify e-waste as one of the products subject to EPR. According to the regulation, compa-
nies that sell, import or manufacture a wide range of consumer products would be obligated to implement “a
mechanism of controlling banned or dangerous substances in products” (Boucher, 2021). This would include
the hazardous materials found in solar panels and e-waste in general, as well as plastic, rubber, aluminum
and glass. The regulation would require companies to either independently create an EPR compliance scheme,
or to join an established collective EPR Compliance Scheme – a Producer Responsibility Organisation (PRO).
A PRO assumes the legal obligation to implement and execute EPR on behalf of its members, which is the
covering of minimum targets for reuse, recycling or recovery, as well as logistics for collection and product
design guidelines (Boucher, 2021). The regulations are described to target “all products and packaging in all
phases of their life cycle”. EPR regulations await enactment in the near future (Macharia, 2021).
In 2019, a new Energy Act was passed, replacing the previous Act of 2006. The new Act aligns with the 2010
Constitution and specifies the roles and functions of both national and county governments in the energy
sector. It places a lot of focus on renewable energy resource development in the country.
On February 2021, the Cabinet approved the Sustainable Waste Management Policy and Bill (2021) proposed
by the Ministry of Environment. Both documents propose a transition from linear to circular economy with
increased recovery of value from waste, employment of Kenyans, reduction in demand for virgin materials,
saving energy, reduce green house gas emissions and carbon footprint. The Sustainable Waste Management
Bill awaits the Attorney General’s Office transmission to Parliament for enactment.
In 2020, the Ministry of Finance re-introduced a 14 percent Value Added Tax (VAT) on solar equipment including
batteries with the new Finance Act 2020, and further increased it to 16 percent in 2021. Prior to this, OGS
products were exempt from VAT. It is estimated that the new VAT rate could generate around USD 13 million
per year – and import duties another USD 16 million – for the Kenyan government (ACE TAF, 2021b). However, it
is also estimated that the additional cost passed on to consumers will result in a price rise of 10 to 24 percent
for OGS systems, making them unaffordable for many households (ACE TAF, 2021b). Considering this impact,
the VAT change could result in as many as 470,000 fewer households accessing SAS products by 2025 (ACE TAF,
2021b). The decrease in sales volumes also comes at an estimated cost of 2,500 jobs in the solar industry,
and a slowdown economic business worth USD 46 million in tax revenues for the government (ACE TAF, 2021b).
It will also reduce available lighting for study hours, increase the use of environmentally damaging energy
alternatives such as wood-fuels and kerosene.
All the while, the government is implementing Kenya Vision 2030, a development plan, which aim is to help
transform Kenya into a "newly industrializing, middle-income country providing a high quality of life to all

2.1.7
E-waste management value chain
The value chain of OGS products and e-waste in general is different from other waste stream from the va-
riety of actors involved, particularly when considering the downstream actors that come into play once the
e-waste has been dismantled. The first stakeholders involved in the e-waste collection and recycling chain
comes from the e-waste generation itself. These include individuals at the household level, commercial in-
dustries and governmental organisations such as schools. The way of disposal is heavily dependent on the
disposal behavior and awareness of the consumers as will be described in Section 2.3.1.3. When the waste
is collected it mainly involves actors such as retailers, e-waste collectors and aggregators. As explained pre-
viously in Section 2.1.5, this collection is dominated by informal collectors, such as small scale door-to-door
collectors and landfill scavengers. The collected e-waste is then dismantled or treated in secondhand mar-
kets, by dismantlers, scrap dealers or recyclers. The downstream partners are then disposing or recovering
materials from the remains of the different fractions and are critical for overall proper e-waste management
ACE TAF (2019).
E-waste is collected and treated depending on the treatment process required. Some fractions may only
require manual disassembly in order to be able to remove hazardous components and recover valuable com-
ponents, while others may require complicated chemical, thermal and/or mechanical processes. For OGS
products, specific processes are required, as will be described in Section 2.2.3. Valuable recovered materials
are sold on national or internal commodities markets, or channelled through downstream partners for further
treatment ACE TAF (2019).

2.2
Solar panel recycling
2.2.1
Types and technologies
In order to achieve the most optimal waste treatment for the distinct solar products, the type and material
composition of the solar panels needs to be taken into consideration. The different technology types typically
differ in terms of materials used in their manufacturing and can contain varying levels of hazardous substances
that must be considered during handling and processing (International Renewable Energy Agency, 2016). In
general, all solar panels are PV panels, that are able to convert sunlight into electricity using semiconducting
materials. In the broader definition, there mainly exist two technology categories of PV panels - crystalline
silicon (c-Si) and thin-film based panels.
Currently, c-Si based panels dominate the global market, as well as the market in Kenya, in terms of annual
production, with around 95 percent market share, as can be seen in the Figure 9. Within c-Si based panels are
multi-crystalline (multi-Si) and mono-crystalline (mono-Si) silicon panels, which accounts for 15 percent and
80 percent of the total market share, respectively.
Thin film technologies makes up about 5 percent of the PV market (PSE Projects GmbH, 2021). The currently
dominant technologies within thin-film are cadmium telluride (CdTe), copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS)
and amorphous silicon (a-Si) with, approximately, 70 percent, 25 percent and 5 percent of the total thin-film
market share, respectively (PSE Projects GmbH, 2021).
The high market share of mono-Si panels is likely explained by it being the most efficient at rates above 20
percent, and therefore able to produce more electricity per area than its competitors (PSE Projects GmbH,
2021). However, mono-Si is also the most expensive, giving market space for less efficient, but in turn more
affordable options that is multi-Si and thin-film based panels.
Figure 9: PV Production by Technology (Percentage of Global Annual Production) (PSE Projects GmbH, 2021).
Other technologies than those already mentioned will not be discussed in terms of material composition and
treatment processes, as their market share is less than 1 percent, and therefore negligible in terms of their
influence.

2.2.2
Material composition
Overall, the key components of a silicon based solar panel is the solar PV cells, encapsulant material, metal
and glass. The components can be separated into fractions as shown in Figure 10.
Figure 10: Construction of crystalline silicon based solar panel (PSE Projects GmbH, 2021).
Surrounding the solar panel itself is the aluminium frame, which plays a essential role in protecting the edge
of the laminate section housing the cells and providing a solid structure to mount the solar panel in position.
On the top is transparent glass, which is installed to protect the PV cells from the weather and impact from
hail or airborne debris. In order to withstand the elements, the glass is typically high strength tempered glass
which is 3.0 to 4.0mm thick (Svarc, 2020). The encapsulant material - EVA (short for ‘ethylene vinyl acetate’),
is a specially designed polymer highly transparent (plastic) layer used to encapsulate the cells, and plays and
important part in the long term performance and durability by preventing moisture and dirt ingress. The rear
most layer, the insulating back sheet, helps to create a final moisture barrier and is made from polymers or
plastics, that offer different levels of protection, thermal stability and long term UV-resistance.
By weight, a typical crystalline silicone based solar panel module consists of 76 percent glass (panel surface),
10 percent polymer (encapsulant and backsheet foil), 8 percent aluminum (mainly the frame), 5 percent sili-
con (solar cells), 1 percent copper (interconnectors) and less than 0.1 percent silver (contact lines) and other
metals (mostly tin and lead) (Sander, 2007). Similarly, thin-film based panels, specifically CIGS panels, are
composed of 89 percent glass, 6 percent aluminium and 4 percent polymer and 1 percent other rare metals,
including 10 percent copper, 28 percent indium, 10 percent gallium and 52 percent selenium (Sander, 2007).

(International Renewable Energy Agency, 2016).

2.2 Solar panel recycling

2.2.3
Technologies and processes
It is important to note that this thesis does not attempt to present an exhaustive examination of all published
methods for the recycling of all types of solar panels. Rather, the intention is to provide an examination of
the common methods currently in use for recycling crystalline silicon and thin-film based panels, in order to
outline the potential inputs necessary for recycling the most abundant types of solar panels.
2.2.3.1
Crystalline silicon panels
As illustrated in Figure 12, the recycling of crystalline silicon panels is achieved firstly by manually separating
aluminium and glass parts. Nearly all (95 percent) of the glass can be recycled and reused (International
Renewable Energy Agency, 2016). Likewise, all the aluminium (100 percent) can be remolded into new frames
and thus reused.
The remaining material is thermally processed at 500°C in order to weaken the bonds between cell modules,
and evaporate the encapsulating plastic. This enables the physical separation of cell modules, as well as
leaving silicon cells exposed ready for further treatment. Due to the calorific value of plastic, it may also be
reused as a heat source in further thermal processing.
Afterwards, the modules are psychically separated, whereof 80 percent is immediately able to be reused,
while the remaining 20 percent needs further treatment. This treatment consists of chemicals etching of the
silicon particles, so-called wafers, using acid. These wafers are then melted to be used again, resulting in a
85 percent recycling rate of the silicon material contents.
2.2.3.2
Thin-film panels
Full-scale recycling of thin-film solar panels is still in its infancy and is likely to advance with larger waste
volumes and treatment experience (International Renewable Energy Agency, 2016). Currently, the recycling of
thin-film panels are achieved through a combination of mechanical and chemicals processes.
A leading method of this process follows the steps outlined in Figure 12, which is able to recover 90 percent
of the glass content and approximately 95 percent of the semiconducting material by weight (International
Renewable Energy Agency, 2016).
Firstly, the panels are shredded. Then shattered in a hammer mill into particles no larger than 4-5mm. This
insures that the lamination bond, which hold together the inner materials breaks, and thereby is able to be
removed. Simultaneously, the dust is collected using an aspiration system with a high-efficiency particulate
air (HEPA) filter for protection against the possible release of nanoparticles into the atmosphere.
Unlike crystalline silicon-based PV panels, the remaining substance consists of both solid and liquid material.
In order to separate the solid and liquid material, a rotating screw is used, which essentially keeps the solid
parts rotating inside a pipe while the liquid drips into a container.
The semi-conductive layer in the solid material is etched using a mixture of sulphuric acid and hydrogen
peroxide. The glass contents and fragments of EVA are separated on a vibrating screen. Finally, the glass is
rinsed with water to remove residues and is dried on a belt filter unit.
For the liquid, it undergoes a precipitation process and/or ion exchangers, from which metals are extracted.
These metals, including cadmium and tellurium, may be further purified by third parties for reuse in the solar
industry.

Legislation
The almost non-existing laws surrounding e-waste management pose a permanent challenge for the recycling

supported in order to be able to develop and enforce regulation.

2.3.1.2
Health effects
The rudimentary processing of the e-waste in informal recycling sites through manual removal, open burning
and open acid digestion leads to widespread contamination, even affecting children who lives, works, and
plays in these sites (Forti et al., 2020). Both adults and children are exposed by inhaling toxic fumes and
particulate matter from open burning practices, and through skin contact with corrosive agents and chemicals,
as well as ingesting contaminated water and food (Forti et al., 2020). Specially for children, they can also
be exposed through hazardous chemicals passed on by the mother during pregnancy and/or breastfeeding
(Forti et al., 2020). Additionally, they may have increased risk of exposure from playing outside and frequently
putting their hands, objects, and soil in their mouths. Furthermore, because of their physiology, fetuses,
infants, children, and adolescents are particularly vulnerable to damage from exposure to toxicants in e-
waste (Landrigan and Goldman, 2011). Several reports have also shown elevated levels of chromium, arsenic,
cobalt, nickel, silver , tin, mercury, lanthanum, and cerium in blood samples of those residents living close to
e-waste recycling site in comparison to control areas. And especially those working at an e-waste recycling
center, show significantly higher concentrations of toxic metals in the blood (Lebbie et al., 2021).
The number of studies concerning the adverse health effects associated with e-waste are ever growing. These
studies continue to bring attention to the danger that exposure to e-waste may oppose to human health.
For informal e-waste recycling, as that found most often in Kenya, research has found increasing amounts of
adverse health effects. These effects include DNA damage (Alabi OA et al., 2012.), cancer (Davis JM et al., 2019),
adverse learning outcomes (Soetrisno et al., 2020), hearing loss (Xu L et al., 2020), adverse cardiovascular ef-
fects (Cong X et al., 2018), adverse birth outcomes (Zhang Y et al., 2018), adverse respiratory effects (Amoabeng
Nti AA et al., 2020), altered neurodevelopment (Huo X et al., 2019b), adverse effects on the immune system
(Huo X et al., 2019b) and skin diseases (Decharat S et al. 2019; Seith et al., 2019).

2.3.2
Economical
2.3.2.1
Circular Economy
Throughout the value chain of OGS products, from the mining of valuable materials to manufacturing, dis-
tribution, sale, consumption, and disposal, significant amounts of e-waste and environmental impacts are
created. Utilizing a more circular approach e-waste and for electronics by recycling, could offer significant
health, environmental, and economic benefits by reducing the need for mining valuable materials, extending
the lifetime of electronic products, and mitigating material demand in a secure and sustainable way (NORCAP,
2022).
The circular economy is “a system in which all materials and components are kept at their highest value
at all times, and waste is designed out of the system” (PACE and E-waste Coalition, 2019). This differs from
the more common linear economy of today, in which materials are used to manufacture products which are
then disposed of at their end-of-life. In Figure 19, the approach of circular economy can be seen, where it is
applied throughout the value chain, including design, manufacturing, distribution, consumption, collection,
recycling, and the extraction of raw materials. As an example, OGS products could be designed to be more
durable, reparable and easier to dissemble. This would then result in products staying in use for longer
periods from the added durability, or ability to be easily repaired, from which e-waste volumes can then be
reduced. For products at their EOL, take-back schemes shall ensure collection, transportation and potential
storage. Effective recycling should be established to ensure that valuable materials can be properly recovered
to be used in the production of new products. Conclusively, the loop can be closed, by using only materials
==PAGE:38==
2.3 Drivers and barriers to e-waste management
23
extracted from e-waste for the production of new products.
There exist several business models for circularity, for example products as a service, sharing of assets, life-
time extension, and recycling to reduce waste (PACE and E-waste Coalition, 2019). However, rural communities,
in which OGS products are often most heavily present, should not be a proving ground for circular economy.
Instead, the principles should be adopted to ensure a more sustainable approach to the selection, repair,
repurposing and recycling of e-waste while providing opportunities for livelihoods programmes for humani-
tarian organisations (NORCAP, 2022).

2.3.2.3
Financing models
Most commonly for OGS markets there exist industry self-regulation, whereby OGS producers pay EOL ex-
penses at a voluntary basis GOGLA (2019c). In cases where e-waste have negative value, there is thus very
little incentive for its treatment. It could thus be proposed that government regulation is essential to in-
crease the rate of treatment, and allocate the cost hereof among producers. Such could, for example, be
either of the following, which utilizes different financing models and mechanisms:
Waste-holder financing:
The individual person disposing of the e-waste is responsible for the costs.
Consumer financing:
The consumer contributes directly to an e-waste fund (that would support
treatment) upon purchase of a new OGS product.
Producer financing:
The producer of OGS products is responsible for the costs. This could be from a
payment upon placing the product on the market (e.g. an Eco-levy as in Ghana),
or upon the e-waste being treated (EPR).
Hybrid model:
The population finances access through taxes and producers finance the
remaining actions needed.
The most common form of regulation from the above is producer financing, specifically the EPR model GOGLA
(2019c). This is seen in both industrialised and developing economies, and as described in section Section
2.1.6, EPR regulations are also in the final drafting stage in Kenya, and await enactment in the near future.

2.3.2.4
Economies of Scale
From microeconomics, economies of scale is the cost advantages that businesses, such as a recycling business,
may obtain due to their scale of operation. These advantages are brought about by operational efficiencies
and synergies as a result of an increase in the scale of operations (Corporate Finance Institute, 2022). From
the book ’Corporate Finance Institute (2022)’, it is estimated that the manufacturing costs can be reduced by
70 to 90 percent every time a business doubles its output. For the process of recycling e-waste, this could for
instance be from the presumption that the mass of recycled e-waste could be increased, while not requiring
a proportional increase in labor cost, as large parts of the recycling process would consists of automated
processes. Also, with recycling equipment, the efficiency may increase with size, both in terms of capital costs,
but also from reduced resource consumption with the example of electric motors being more efficient with
size. Efficiencies could thus occur from both from managerial and technological aspects. From managerial
aspects, the average cost could be lowered with better management structure and the hire of better skilled or
more experienced workers. From the technological aspect, the process may be improved from more manual
and labor extensive processes to more efficient automated equipment. Furthermore, the logistic costs related
to the transportation of the e-waste may be reduced from carrying full truckloads instead of less than full
truckloads, or from infrastructural efficiencies from using combined and more efficient transport networks.
Aside from internal economies of scale as those mentioned until now, external economies of scale may also
exist. External economies of scale occur outside of the individual business, but within the industry. This
could for example include tax breaks or subsidies from the government should the business attain a valuable
impact from its size (Corporate Finance Institute, 2022). Thus, the overall costs associated to any e-waste
management option is expected to be reduced with increased capacities.
However, economies of scale may also reach the point of diseconomies of scale. This is when a business
becomes so large that the point of maximum efficiency has been exceeded. With this principle, rather than
experiencing continued decreasing costs and increasing output, a business sees an increase in costs when
output is increased (Investopedia, 2022). For a recycling business, this may occur with the collection of e-
waste, where obtaining the last amounts of e-waste has diminishing returns from being significantly harder to
obtain - compared to smaller more readily available amounts. Furthermore, internal communication is likely
to become more cumbersome with more employees requiring more channels of communication as a result,
thus increasing the time and costs of communication. Ultimately, even miscommunication may occur, where
e-waste may become treated incorrectly and efforts may be duplicated between employees.

2.3.3.2
Soil and air pollution
As mentioned in Section 2.2.2, typical solar panels may contain several hazardous materials such as indium,
gallium, selenium, cadmium, tellurium and lead. While the disposal of solar panels is rather harmless as long
as the toxins remains protected by the tempered glass and EVA film, as illustrated in Figure 10. However, it may
be reasonable to assume that during the end-of-life management, the protective layers will become damaged
if not so already. According to studies, acidic environmental conditions then have the potential to cause the
leaching of these hazardous metals. Negative consequences for soil and water quality are thus apparent, if
solar panels are not properly treated (Sander, 2007).
Specifically, the leaching of lead, which is primarily associated with c-Si based panels, will occur if the panels
are exposed to low levels of pH, such as nitric acid or rain. This will incite leaching between 13 to 90 percent of
the mass of lead found in a typical c-Si panel (Sander, 2007). With approximately 12.67 g of lead contained in
the average c-Si panel (which weigh 22 kg), this will result in a potential lead leaching into environment of 1.64
g to 11.4 g per panel, or 75.0 g to 518 g per tonne of panel disposed. Lead has a high potential for accumulation
in the environment, and also in humans (Sander, 2007). Ecosystems in close proximity of lead sources has
also shown adverse effects, such as loss in biodiversity, decrease in growth and reproduction in both plants
and animals (Sander, 2007).
Similarly for cadmium leaching, which is primarily linked to thin-film based panels, the exposure to low levels

2.3.3.3
Climate Change
A study conducted by Golsteijn and Martinez (2017) in the Netherlands, estimated the environmental ben-
efits that could be achieved from appropriate collection and recycling from the avoided emissions of CO2-
equivalents on the basis the avoided production of new materials. This for example being the avoided CO2-
equivalents that would occur from recycling copper estimated on the basis of the environmental impacts of
new copper production. In order to also account for the potential loss of resources when recycling any mate-
rial, the study utilized a material-specific correction for losses. The life cycle modelling was done in SimaPro.
Life cycle inventory data were collected from the ecoinvent 3.1 database. This included, for example, data
about raw materials supplies and emissions related to the production of the different materials. For the cal-
culation of the carbon footprint the IPCC 2013 method was used. Information about the material type per
e-waste category was collected with the WF-RepTool database application.
Six e-waste categories were evaluated, including; large household appliances (LHHA), small household ap-
pliances (SHHA), cooling and freezing appliances (C&F), televisions (CRT), flat panel displays (FPD), and light
bulbs (LAMP), which included materials such as aluminium, copper, iron, zinc, glass (used for production of
glass and concrete) and plastics (ABS, Polystyrene and Polyethylene). The study concluded that the recycling
of 110,000 tonnes of e-waste resulted in 155,000 tonnes of CO2-equivalents being avoided. This would mean
that for each tonne of e-waste recycled, 1.41 tonnes CO2-equivalents are avoided. Although not including OGS
products, it is thus apparent that proper recycling of e-waste in general could constitute significant environ-
mental benefits from the avoided production of new materials.

In order to assess how well an initiative (i.e a e-waste management solution) would perform in a given situ-

must both be economically efficient, socially equitable and environmentally sustainable (World Commission
on Environment and Development, 1987). The framework therefore includes criteria concerning all three di-
mensions; Economic, Environmental and Social, in which they have been divided.

in the following page. On the basis of objectivity, all criteria have been assigned an equal weight of one.
However, it is expected that the importance of each criteria heavily depends on the interest of each indi-
vidual user, from which the framework model allows the weight to be adjusted easily (See Section 5.2.1).

3.2.3
Ecotoxicity (TOX)
An important environmental and health aspect to consider of any initiative, is the identification of potential
toxicological hazards and the exposures that may occur, as part of a risk assessment. As mentioned in Section
2.3.1.2, this is especially relevant as e-waste landfills and e-waste recycling sites in African countries had sig-
nificant elevated concentrations of hazardous metals relative to Dutch and Chinese standards. Futhermore,
several reports have also shown elevated levels of chromium, arsenic, cobalt, nickel, silver , tin, mercury,
lanthanum, and cerium in blood samples of those residents living close to e-waste recycling site in compar-
ison to control areas. And especially those working at an e-waste recycling center, show significantly higher
concentrations of toxic metals in the blood. Thus, it is important that a potential initiative includes working
conditions and recycling processes that do not pose a risk to human health, and that of the environment
through proper waste management throughout, i.e. from collection to disposal.
The TOX score is then based on a linear relation between the number of possible toxic chemicals that may
be able to leach into soil, water, or come in contact with human skin. The risk is to be evaluated, based on
whether harmful amounts of exposure to hazardous materials can happen to either humans or the environ-
ment anywhere in the recycling process, or from the lack hereof. This is to be considered for all 10 chemicals
mentioned below.

Through regulation, or the leasing of OGS products instead of upfront purchase,
products are forcibly collected from consumers at their EOL.
Voluntary take-back
Collection is solely dependent on consumers willingness to relinquish OGS
products at their EOL.
Incentivized take-back
Incentives such as cash, discount vouchers or free products are given in exchange
for the return of EOL OGS products.

to their potential affect on the price of OGS products. Initiatives that are financed in such a way that it will
increase the price of OGS products, will score lower, and reversely, initiatives that will lower the price of OGS

5
Discussion
Because of the atypical structure of this report, many aspects have been discussed in an ongoing fashion
throughout the thesis. This has been done with the purpose of providing the reader with a thorough under-
standing of all the necessary steps made during the construction of the framework, the data input fabricated
to represent the four scenarios, and the resulting assessments results of the framework. However, this section
seeks to provide a summary of all of the different topics discussed, along with any additional interpretations,
evaluations, reflections, and recommendations.
5.1
Interpretation of the Results
From the results of the framework test, it was observed that the framework generally gave higher scores to
those scenarios which included more comprehensive e-waste management solutions, i.e. Scenario 1 and 2.
These recycled vastly larger amounts of e-waste and scored 56 and 53 in comparison to 36 and 34 for Scenario
3 and 4, which were focused only towards recycling e-waste from OGS products. From observing the individual
criteria scores and dimensions score, this was seen mainly explained from the frameworks assessment of eco-
nomic and environmental aspects. The more comprehensive e-waste management solutions, that is Scenario
1 and 2, were both assessed to yield larger environmentally beneficial impacts compared to Scenario 3 and 4.
This was especially apparent from criteria such as GHG and TOX, which would indicate that more greenhouse
gas emissions were avoided as a result from the avoided production of new materials, and that less hazardous
materials were released into the environment. This is to be expected just from the difference in mass recycled,
but also from the recycling of several e-waste categories, which would mean more hazardous materials were
managed.
Furthermore, the economic profitability for Scenario 1 was shown to be excellent with a score of 93. This
was explained by the cumulative value of the material recovered together with the large mass recycled, which
meant that it was able to more than compensate for the higher initial investment cost. Small changes were also
observed to be able to have large effects on the assessment. This was seen with the inclusion or exclusion of
battery recycling. Batteries had very little economic value and were expensive to recycle, but contained many
hazardous materials, which was thus shown to affect the Economic and Environmental Dimension oppositely
to each other. This was the case for Scenario 2, where the inclusion of battery recycling meant that the PF
was reduced from 93 to 0, but the TOX improved to 100 from 85. As such, the framework was able to also
illustrate how the recycling of certain e-waste fractions may be able to change the performance of the waste-
management across different dimensions in accordance to what could be expected from general knowledge
through experience and education.

Framework setup in Excel
The framework has been modelled in Excel, from which the results shown in Section 4 have been extracted

5.2.2
Availability of the required data
When constructing the different criteria it was continuously assessed how much data that may be available
in any given situation, and how this may be used to describe the performance of an initiative. From this
process, some criteria had to be simplified, while others were more comprehensive. However, from assessing
the four fabricated scenarios it became apparent that the data necessary for some criteria was difficult to
obtain, even with the simplifications made. This was especially the case with specific construction costs in
correlation to both the recycling capabilities and location, and also quantitative data concerning the resource
consumption in different recycling processes. Thus, as explained in Section 4.1, assumptions had to be made
to be able to input the data required for the framework. In order to be able to provide the most accurate
assumptions, several studies, reports and scientific articles had to be reviewed and evaluated to determine
which data would provide the most accurate representation for each scenario. This was discovered to be
a quite time consuming process. However, despite given the assumptions, the framework still managed to
create assessments of each scenario, which were in-line with immediate expectations. Furthermore, it may
be expected that the actual users of the framework would include industry actors, for which it is possible to
obtain more accurate data, and thus improve the certainty of the overall assessment.
5.2.3
Limitations and Uncertainties
In order for the framework to be able to assess on complicated contents, such as that of an e-waste stream,
simplifications had to be adapted. This included the assumption that e-waste streams can be divided into
10 different categories, and that these categories are homogeneous in their composition. However, in real
life, it is likely that different e-waste categories are mixed together, such that pure waste streams of each
category does not exist. If the e-waste, contrary to expectations, is or can be separated in accordance to
the categories, they are likely not homogeneous in their composition regardless, as there in each category
exist different manufacturers, creating the same products differently. For the best result, the actual e-waste
stream(s) would have to be analyzed in order to determine average composition(s), and potentially also the
possibilities of segregation if necessary. This would limit the uncertainties hereof, and provide a more precise
assessment overall.
In the case a criteria would score below 0 or above 100, they were given a default score of 0 or 100, respectively.
This could be problematic in cases of extremes, where the score would be much higher than 100 or much
lower than 0, and thus potentially understating the actual assessment. An example of this can be found for
the assessment of profitability. Here the use of the payback period in PF only scores on the basis of how
quickly an initiative is able to payback the initial investment on a scale of 10 years. Should an initiative not
be able to recover the investment within 10 years a score of 0 is given. And thus, initiatives which make very
little profit, such that they for example first will recover the investment after 11 years, will be scored equal to
those potentially making heavy losses, as they are similarly not able to recover the investment within 10 years.
Should two initiatives be compared which both score 0 for the PF, it may be desired to further investigate the
average net cash flows, as these may be magnitudes apart, which the scores does not reflect.

5.3 Reflections and Recommendations
60
5.3
Reflections and Recommendations
As described in Section 5.2.2, some criteria were simplified with the consideration that the framework should
be able to function in Kenya, where data availability was assumed to be very limited. Naturally, the simplifi-
cations made may come at the expense of the accuracy of the assessment. Should the framework be used in
situations where more data may be available, an improvement could be for the framework to be flexible to
accommodate extra data, while still functioning at simpler levels of data. This would help keep inaccuracies
at a minimum.
Furthermore, the simplicity of the calculation of the payback period unfortunately comes at the expense of
not accounting for the time value of money, the effects of inflation, or the complexity of initiatives that may
have unequal cash flow over time. The time value of money is the concept that a sum of money is worth
more now than the same sum will be at a future date due to its earnings potential in the interim. Should the
aforementioned aspects be accounted for, it would in turn also require more comprehensive data in regard to
the cash flows, which was expected to be very limited by default. Depending on the available data, this could
then be altered.
Another aspect not considered is the value and variable lifespan of an initiative. Should an initiative for
example be the construction of a recycling facility, this would be assumed to have a lifespan of 10 years and
zero value at its end-of-life. However, it could be that a facility has a different lifespan and salvage value at its
end-of-life, which is thus not accounted for. Should the lifespan and value of the initiative differ vastly from
the default assumption, it is recommended changing the assumptions to include this.
Upon further reflection, as observed in Section 2.1.3, there also exist a time lag between the time which elec-
tronic devices are put on the market and their appearance in e-waste streams. This issue is complicated, as
the time lag is not just a simple reflection of the average product lifespans. Instead, users may receive a
replacement PV panel or keep a product at the household for an extended period of time before disposing it.
Similarly, products may not enter waste streams as wholes, but in parts with different weights and a different
material composition than at the point of sale. In order to support viable e-waste businesses, such data gaps
should be clarified and filled.
Lastly, if more time had been available, it would have been beneficial to expand the framework to include a
post-analysis with the probability of rank reversibility (such as that performed by the tool pyTOPS). This is
considering the framework would be used to compare several initiatives for the purpose of choosing one over
the other. From calculating the probability of rank reversibility, as well as the mean and standard deviation
of the relative closeness concerning each initiative, the robustness of the assessment can be determined to
support more reliable decision-making between different initiatives.
==PAGE:76==
6
Conclusion
This thesis was written with the purpose of constructing a framework for assessing e-waste management op-
tions for OGS products. However, it quickly became apparent from the very small fractions that OGS products
constitute, that any waste management solution would need to include the management of e-waste in gen-
eral, both in order to be economically feasible, but also to provide any meaningful environmental impact. In
consideration of this, the applicability of the framework was expanded to be able to assess waste management
options for all types of e-waste, including OGS products. The framework was modelled using Excel, and the
assessment based on 12 different criteria divided equally into three different dimensions, which included the
Economic, Environmental and Social Dimension, in concordance to the concept of sustainable development
as defined in the 1987 Brundtland Report. Each criteria was weighed equally towards the overall assessment
on the basis of creating an objective assessment. But as an addition, from the expectation that different users
may be more concerned with specific criteria, the framework allows the weight to be adjusted easily. These
criteria included an assessment of profitability, recycling rate, greenhouse gas emissions, ecotoxicity and local
employment to name a few.
The framework was tested from the assessment of four differently fabricated scenarios. These included both
partial and more comprehensive e-waste management options for the e-waste generated in Kenya. More
specifically, two of the scenarios were focused only towards the recycling of OGS products, while the other two
included the recycling of several e-waste categories. Although all criteria were constructed with consideration
of what limited data may be available in any given situation, accurate data for some criteria was still difficult
to obtain. Thus, assumptions had to be made in order to provide the required data for the framework to run
the test. However, it is expected that potential users of the framework may include industry actors, for which
it may be possible to obtain more accurate data.
The results of the test showed higher overall scores (and thus indicating better performance) for those sce-
narios that included more comprehensive e-waste management solutions. Both because these were assessed
to provide larger environmentally beneficial impacts, and to be more economically profitable as the value of
the recovered materials was higher. They were thus was able to quickly compensate for the higher initial in-
vestment costs. Prior to the assessment, there was an immediate expectation as to how each scenario would
perform both economically, environmentally and socially, based on knowledge through experience and edu-
cation. The assessment for each dimensions aligned with what could have been expected for each scenario.
For an example the inclusion or exclusion of battery recycling, which have very little economic value, but con-
tain many hazardous materials, was shown to affect the Economic and Environmental Dimension oppositely to
each other. Conclusively, the results were regarded as very plausible. However, more tests would be necessary
61

to confidently assess the performance of the framework.
Thus it can be concluded that a framework that is able to assess e-waste management options was con-
structed. It should however be noted that this is with simplifications in regard to many aspects with the
advantage of being easily realizable. Naturally, the simplifications made may come at the expense of accu-
racy. As such, the framework may serve as an appropriate tool to quickly filter through e-waste management
options, in order to determine which particular ones should be considered for further evaluation.

Assumptions for Scenarios
A.1
General assumptions
Below are all of the assumptions that follows all four scenarios.
Locality (Nairobi, Kenya)
All of the facilities are to be built in Nairobi - the capital city of Kenya. This is made on the assumption
that this will provide the best possible conditions for the operations, that is in regard to already established
infrastructure for resources such as water and energy, as well as access to the road network for efficient
collection and transport of e-waste in large parts of the country (World Food Programme, 2020).
Energy mix (92.3 percent renewables energy share)
According to the 2021 Economic Survey from the Kenya National Bureau of Statistics (KNBS), the total energy
generated in Kenya was 11,466 GWh. In this review period, 92.3 percent of electricity generated was from
renewable sources (Kenya National Bureau of Statistics, 2020). The main sources were geothermal, hydro
and wind power accounting for 43.6, 36.5 and 11.5 percent of electricity, respectively, while solar power only
accounted for 0.763 percent (Kenya National Bureau of Statistics, 2020). The only non-renewable source were
from thermal oil, accounting for 7.69 percent of the electricity generation (Kenya National Bureau of Statistics,
2020).
Job creation (1 job per tonne of e-waste recycled per month)
An article from the Nation Correspondent concerns the establishment of an e-waste recycling plant in Machakos,
Kenya. In the article it is mentioned that a recycling plant will mark a milestone in the management of e-waste
products in Kenya by creating "green" jobs - implying jobs associated with the recycling of e-waste. In addi-
tion, is an included interview with Chief executive officer of East African Compliant Recycling, Robert Truscott,
who says that with rising consumption of electronic products, there are opportunities for job creation in e-
waste management, and predicts " one job created per tonne of e-waste collected and treated every month"
(Truscott, 2020). Based on this, the estimated amount of jobs created is assumed to be proportionate with
the amount of e-waste recycled at 1 job per tonne per month, or 0.0833 per tonne per year.
Total e-waste generation (56,650 tonnes per year)
As shown in Figure 4 in Section 2.1.3, the amount of e-waste generated in Kenya was 55,000 tonnes in 2017,
excluding PV panels and OGS-products (Magalini et al., 2017). Including the e-waste generated from OGS-
products in 2017, the total amount of e-waste generated would be 56,650 tonnes in 2017. This is thus assumed
to be the total amount of e-waste generated.
E-waste generation from OGS-products (1,650 tonnes per year)
In Figure 3 in Section 2.1.3, the e-waste generation from OGS-products from 2015-2022 is shown. From here, the
amount from 2017 is selected in order to be proportionally accurate with the assumed total e-waste generated,
which is also for 2017. Therefore, 1,650 tonnes of e-waste from OGS-products is assumed generated each year.

Population (55.8 million)
From Worldometer (2022), the population size of Kenya is estimated at approximately 55.8 million, which is
the basis for the assumption of population size.
Distance traveled per tonne e-waste collected (8.44 km)
In order to recycle any e-waste, it must first be collected. This is assumed to be achieved by truck. In a study
of the in-use activity, fuel use, and emissions of heavy-duty diesel roll-off refuse trucks in regard to hauling
municipal solid waste in the United States, it was found that a truck on average travels 1,392 km to collect
165km municipal solid waste. This would then equal the need to travel 8.44 km per tonne collected (Sandhu
et al., 2015). From this, it is assumed that the same distance traveled would be needed in order to collect one
tonne of e-waste in Kenya.
Employment rate (2.98 percent)
In June 2021, the employment rate in Kenya was 2.98 percent from a modeled ILO estimate (The World Bank,
2021b).
Clean water access (50 percent)
According to the United Nations, it is estimated that just 50 percent of Nairobi’s residents had direct access
to piped water in 2019 (NCWSC, 2019). There is currently about a 25 percent shortfall in supply to households,
according to the city’s water provider, NCWSC. There are also significant issues over the quality of the piped
water, with only 76 percent of drinking water samples complying with standards for bacteriological quality,
particularly in the city’s informal settlements (NCWSC, 2019). However, projects are in progress to expand local
dam capacity and construct a new water transfer tunnel, which is expected to increase the water capacity in
the near future (NCWSC, 2019). For the scenarios a clean water access rate of 50 percent is thus assumed.

A.1.1
Scenario 1 specific assumptions
Below are all of the assumptions that follows for Scenario 1.
E-waste mass recycled (23,651 tonnes per year)
From the total 55,650 tonnes of e-waste generated per year, 23,651 tonnes of e-waste is assumed to be recycled.
This is based on the assumption that the e-waste recycling rate is able to match that which is the average of
Europe, which is 42.5 percent (Statista, 2020).
Traffic Level (Heavy)
With 23,651 tonnes of e-waste being transported into the facility, and presumably also out again as recovered
material, it is assumed that heavy traffic will occur in proximity of the facility as a result.
Investment cost (7 million EUR)
In 2019 a recycling hub was built in Dubai, featuring state-of-the-art Swiss technology that surpasses strict EU
Standards for e-waste recycling (Zawya, 2019). This facility is able to recycle all types of end-of-life electric
and electronic equipment, with a yearly capacity of 100,000 tonnes (Zawya, 2019). The total investment was
120 million AED, or approximately 28 million EUR.
For Kenya, there is only a need for having half the capacity to adequately recycle all e-waste generated. The
investment needed is thus likewise assumed to be able to be halved. Additionally, considering there is neither
a need for a facility which surpasses EU standards, but only fulfills them the price is again halved, bringing the
assumed investment needed for at facility which can handle all e-waste fractions in Kenya down to 7 million
EUR.
Treatable e-waste categories (10 out of 10)
As stated above, the facility in Dubai is able to recycle all types of end-of-life electric and electronic equip-
ment and therewith all 10 e-waste categories. With the construction of a similar facility in Kenya, the same
capabilities are assumed.
Air pollution (Negligible)
The facility built in Dubai used industrial air filtration, allowing recycling with "zero air and liquid emissions"
(Zawya, 2019). Assuming the same is possible for a similar facility in Kenya, the air pollution occurring from
the facility is negligible.
Noise Level (Moderate)
Recycling such large amounts e-waste is likely to bear high levels of noise with it as a consequence, but on the
assumption that most of the processes are contained in buildings the noise level is considered be moderate.
Start-up time (2 years)
Using the proxy of the recycling hub in Dubai once more, the facility began construction in March 2017 and
finished same month in 2019, and was thus took two years to construct (Zawya, 2019). Assuming the same
construction time applies to a facility in Kenya, the start-up time is determined as two years.

E-waste mass recycled (21,286 tonnes per year)

waste is assumed to be recycled in comparison to Scenario 1. This is based on the same assumption that the
e-waste recycling rate is 42.5 percent, but with the addition that batteries constitute 10 percent of the total
mass of e-waste generated, which is not recycled.
