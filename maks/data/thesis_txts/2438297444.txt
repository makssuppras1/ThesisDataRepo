Abstract
Droplet impact on surfaces has been the subject of numerous experimental, theoretical
and numerical studies in the past decades. Various experiments have been conducted
as well as numerical methods have been developed for predicting and understanding
the behaviour of a droplet after an impact, however, there are areas where still the
knowledge is limited. One of these areas is the droplet impact on porous media, and
particularly in metallic meshes.
The present study focuses on the numerical reproduction of experimental runs of
droplet impacting on metallic meshes. Within the framework of this dissertation,
further validation of an enhanced Volume-of-Fluid (VOF) method is also performed for
droplets impact on solid surfaces, with high We numbers. The numerical studies are
conducted in an open-source toolbox OpenFOAM. The transport phenomenon in an
unsteady two-phase Ô¨āow behavior is studied, while the Ô¨āow is incompressible, and the
Ô¨āuids immiscible.
After validating the results, numerical simulations of droplet impacts on metallic
meshes are performed. Both in the validation of the model as well as in the numerical
investigation of droplet impact on metallic meshes, the simulation results are veriÔ¨Āed
with experimental data from literature as well as with in-house experimental data.
Last but not least, parametric analysis is conducted to interpret the eÔ¨Äects of isolated
properties of droplets on metallic meshes (e.g. isolated eÔ¨Äect of surface tension, while
keeping all the other properties the same).
In general, the present thesis explores and illustrates the usability and applicability of
CFD simulations in providing further insight for complex hydrodynamic phenomena
such as droplet impacts on metallic meshes.

1 Introduction
Droplet impact onto dry surfaces is a widely studied phenomenon which has extensively
investigated in the last century, since it is involved in many natural and industrial
applications. To name a few, impinging of a droplet during spray cooling and how this
could contribute to increase the rate of heat transfer [45], high-speed diesel engines
which have smaller combustors and hence fuel droplets during injection events may
impact the chamber walls [87]. Droplet impact phenomena are also of great interest for
Ink-jet printing technology that can deposit very small volume of liquid with relatively
low cost, high accuracy and high speed. Droplet impact phenomena are also studied
with an ultimate goal to give future insight in new technologies for printed electronic
devices such as plastic organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs), sensors, memory devices
and others [41]. Spray painting quality [5](i.e in cars) is directly related and highly
depends in many droplet impact characteristics, such as droplet size, velocity and
Ô¨āuid properties (surface tension, viscosity, density) as well as the spraying angle of
the droplet cloud. Wrong application of spray painting may cause signiÔ¨Ācant defects
such as wrinkling, bubble entrapment, running and sagging. To be able to understand
and hence avoid such issues, further investigations of droplet impact phenomena are
needed.
For understanding the behaviour of droplets, the underpinned droplet impact dynamics
must be extensively investigated . The droplet dynamics depends on a number of
parameters that are related to the droplet itself, the solid surface and the local gas layer
near the wall [3], [21]. The droplet is characterized by its diameter D0, velocity U0 as
well as the impact angle, liquid density ŌĀ, liquid viscosity ¬Ķ, and the surface tension
coeÔ¨Écient ŌÉ. The ambient gas pressure and hence density was also found to play an
important role in determining the impact outcomes. In this study we will examine only
iso-thermal, ambient conditions of water droplets as well as "virtual" Ô¨āuid droplets.
1
==PAGE:33==
Chapter 1. Introduction
The type of the solid surface is also considered a very important parameter of droplet im-
pact behavior that signiÔ¨Ācantly aÔ¨Äect the overall phenomenon. The surface condition is
characterized by its wettability (i.e. hydrophilic/hydrophobic, super-hydrophilic/super-
hydrophobic), material properties, dry/wet state (i.e. with/without liquid Ô¨Ālm), rough-
ness Ra, and temperature T. The importance of surface on droplet impact (i.e. anti-icing)
was studied by [50] where it was found that super-hydrophobic surfaces can prevent
ice creation under super-cold conditions.
While droplet impact on plain, solid surfaces has been widely investigated so far, only
few experiments in porous surfaces and particularly in metallic meshes are available in
the literature today.
The main aim of the present thesis is the validation of a Volume Of Fluid (VOF) based
interface capturing model, developed by Georgoulas et al. [33] using the open-source
CFD software OpenFOAM ¬ģ, for the numerical simulation of droplet impact phenomena
on metallic meshes. Initially, a validation of 2D as well as 3D simulations of droplet
impacting on solid surfaces is conducted comparing the numerical model predictions
with experimental measurements available in the literature. Then, 3D simulations of
droplets impacting on metallic porous layers is carried reproducing the experiments
conducted by Boscariol et al. [14].
The numerical predictions and the corresponding experimental measurements are
compared and discussed in each case. Finally, utilising the same computational set-
up further runs are conducted, utilising "virtual" Ô¨āuids that have diÔ¨Äerent viscosity
or surface tension than water in order to investigate and isolate the eÔ¨Äect of such
fundamental controlling parameters in the droplet impact characteristics.
1.1
Overview
Droplet dynamics constitutes an important phenomenon in diÔ¨Äerent disciplines of
nature and also technological applications. A physical common example is interactions
between droplets and machine surfaces and more speciÔ¨Ācally interactions between rain-
drops with aircraft surfaces under rainfall [99]. Even if droplet impact on solid surfaces
has been investigated for many decades now, the research that has been conducted
on droplet impact on porous surfaces is considered relatively new. Experiments have
been conducted on porous surfaces in the past both isothermal and non-isothermal
conditions [17], [100]. Boscariol et al. [13], [14] conducted experiments on metallic
(stainless steel) porous layers under isothermal conditions.
2
==PAGE:34==
1.2. Aim
In addition to the experiments, in general, also numerical studies has been accomplished
using commercial software [76]. However, there are not any numerical simulations
available on literature that have been performed in the past using an open-source

Numerical methods of tracking and capturing the inter-
faces
The numerical methods for tracking and capturing the interfaces can be categorized in

26

3.1. Two-phase Ô¨āow and numerical models
Each of these methods are used for speciÔ¨Āc occasions. Initially, the Interface tracking
methods can respresent the interface between two immiscible Ô¨āuids explicitly. This
can be done either adapting the Eulerian Ô¨āuid mesh in a way to resemble the interface,
usually known as Moving-Mesh method [74], or introducing an immersed boundary
representing the interface, i.e. Front-Tracking method [73]. The Moving-Mesh methods
can accurately reproduce the jump condition due to the corresponding Ô¨āuid properties
jump across the interface and also impose the force acting along the interface, since
they include the surface tension parameter. Conversely, the Front-Tracking methods
do not require to alter the Eulerian Ô¨āuid mesh, therefore this method is limited to
topological changes in multiphase-Ô¨āuid, such as merging or breaking of droplets [19].
Interface capturing methods rely on an implicit interface representation, which means the
explicit position and shape of the interface is not known [23]. There are many numerical
models for interface capturing that are used today. Some of them are presented and
analyzed by [59].
The most well-known and broadly used Interface capturing methods are the Volume-of-
Ô¨āuid (VOF) and the Level-Set (LS) methods, introduced by [37] and [69], respectively.
In more detail, the VOF and LS methods are the mot suitable for studying complex
gas/liquid interface topologies, by modeling both Ô¨āuid phases as a single mixture.
In VOF methods, the Ô¨āuids on each side of the interface are marked by an indicator
function. In more detail, the volume fraction őĪ is tracked for this purpose. It takes
values between zero and one in order to distinguish the two diÔ¨Äerent phases 3.2. The
volume fraction is advected based on the underlying Ô¨āow Ô¨Āeld. The VOF method is
accurate in the sense of volume preserving, and break-up of droplet and/or bubbles can
be captured, but very Ô¨Āne meshes are needed to diminish numerical artifacts. A usual
drawback of the VOF method is that the reconstruction of interface process makes it
diÔ¨Écult to keep the topology of the interface. [18].
The Level-set (LS) method is another quite commonly used method today. This method
uses a distance function from the interface. The implicit interface representation is
given by the zero set of the scalar Ô¨Āeld ŌÜ. When ŌÜ = 0 means that is where the interface
lies. The LS method can provide accurate results when the interface is advected parallel
to one of the coordinate axes. It has simple mathematical formulation and it is easy to
solve. However, an important drawback of this method is that volume is not always
preserved while advecting the interface [23].
In this thesis the interface capturing method that will be used is the VOF method.
In more detail a previously developed and tested enhances version of the VOF-based
solver of OpenFOAM is going to be utilised for the overall numerical simulations. The

Ô¨Ārst, the solution of momentum equation gives the Ô¨Ārst approximation of the velocities,
having Ô¨Ārst results of volumetric Ô¨āuxes. From the predicted velocities Ô¨Ārst estimations of
the pressure are obtained, solving the pressure equation. The next step is the correction
of the Ô¨āuxes and velocities which are based on the obtained pressure value. More
analytical description of the algorithm can be found here [22], [43]
The maximum value of courant number Comax was set to Comax = 0.2, so for every
new calculation step, the time is adjusted in that way so it will meet the indicated
Comax value.

4.1
Computational domain and boundary conditions
Most of the experimental runs that are chosen to be reproduced numerically, are
selected tso that the droplet spreading and recoiling, can be assumed to be axisymmetric.
Therefore, the validation of the model will be mainly conducted by running two-
dimensional axisymmetric simulations. The computational domain therefore consists
of a 5¬į wedge geometry, with a width of 5 mm and a height of 8 mm. The dimensions of
the domain have been chosen so that both the width and the height to be much bigger
than the maximum spreading and maximum height of the droplet in the spreading and
recoiling phase respectively. Furthermore, the outlet at the top ans the side wall are
positioned safely away from the point of interest in order to ensure that the outcome of
the drop is not aÔ¨Äected by the location of these boundaries. Trial simulations have been
performed in order to ensure this. The computational mesh is structured and consists
of 1.6 million hexahedral cells (1000√ó1600√ó1), with width and height clustering in
the direction of the bottom left corner of the computational domain. The selection of
the total number of cells has been done through a mesh independency study, which
will presented in section 4.3. The smallest grid cell is about 5 ¬Ķm. This size is found to
be suÔ¨Écient Ô¨Āne for studies of droplet impact [33]. Figure 4.1, depicts the numerical
grid and the boundary conditions used for the validation simulations.
Regarding the boundary conditions, at the solid walls a no-slip velocity boundary
condition has been used with Ô¨Āxed Ô¨āux pressure for the pressure values, as well as a
contact angle condition according to the selected in each case treatment for the volume
fraction. The use of the contact angle boundary conditions are used for correcting
the surface normal vector, and therefore adjust the curvature of the interface near the
wall, in relation to the considered surface wettability characteristics. At the outlet, a
Ô¨Āxed pressure value (atmospheric) boundary condition and a zero-gradient boundary
condition for the volume fraction, were prescribed. For the outlet an inlet-outlet type
is prescribed, that constitutes a combined type of boundary condition for velocity
values. In more detail this applies a zero-gradient when the Ô¨āuid mixture exits the
computational domain and a Ô¨Āxed value condition to the tangential velocity component,
when that Ô¨āuid enters the domain.

5.3
Mesh generation
Mesh generation (or Grid generation) is considered as one of the most critical aspects of
solving problems through CFD. Grid generation is deÔ¨Āned as a discrete number of cells
or elements where the domain/model is sub-divided. The Ô¨āow variables (e.g. velocity,
pressure, temperature) and other variables are solved at the centres of these discrete
cells. The process of breaking-up a physical domain into smaller cells/elements is
known as grid generation (or mesh generation). Having an appropriate grid generation
is essential for obtaining accurate solution, and increases the overall eÔ¨Éciency of the
simulation as well as the required computational time.
There are diÔ¨Äerent types of cell/grid shapes e.g. triangles, quadrilateral, tetrahedra,
hexahedra etc. Moreover there are diÔ¨Äerent types of grids, such as cartesian, structured,
75
==PAGE:107==
Chapter 5. Droplet Impact on Metallic Meshes
unstructured and hybrid meshes. For more information about mesh generation the
reader is referred to [? ] and [9].
Implementation of numerical methods on structured meshes using Cartesian system is
possible only for simple rectangular or axi-symmetric geometries. The structured mesh,
uses elements that are aligned in a speciÔ¨Āc manner and/or they follow a structured
pattern. By contrast, when the elements are arranged in a random pattern, the mesh
is called unstructured. Last but not least, a hybrid mesh is formed by parts of small
structured sub-domains combined in an overall unstructured pattern.
The structured mesh, grid generation produces the highest quality meshes from the
point of view of solver accuracy, however it requires more computational time due to
high total number of cells. Conversely, fully unstructured meshes are fast to generate
and solve the solution faster, however do not allow the solvers to perform their highest
quality solutions.
5.3.1
snappyHexMesh
In the validation chapter, for generating the mesh the blockMeshDict Ô¨Āle and blockMesh
utility was enough, since the geometry of the domain was very simple. However, for
complex geometries the mesh generation is considered the most diÔ¨Écult part of running
a case, and some researches have shown that many users may spent up to 50% of the
project time in generating the mesh [75].
Considering that the structure of the metallic mesh is very complicated, the generation
of the grid cannot be conducted by only using blockMeshDict dictionary Ô¨Āle and the
blockMesh utility. Therefore, a diÔ¨Äerent way of the creation of the grid should be
applied.
Most of the commercial CFD software oÔ¨Äer their own mesh generators [9]. In Open-
FOAM it is possible to generate the mesh in a commercial CFD software (e.g. ANSYS)
and then by using a speciÔ¨Āc utility, the mesh can be converted from ASCII format to
foam format and subsequently the simulation can be run in OpenFOAM. However,
OpenFOAM oÔ¨Äers mesh generators itself. Apart from the mesh generation with the
blockMesh utility, a very well-known mesh generator is the snappyHexMesh utility,
which is supplied with OpenFOAM. The snappyHexMesh (sHM) utility can create
high quality hex-dominant meshes based on arbitrary geometry, and can be used for

5.3. Mesh generation
5.3.2
snappyHexMesh in Metallic Meshes
Mesh resolution and quality are considered key characteristics for the success of any
numerical simulations. Parameters such as the accuracy of the results, the convergence
of the numerical solution and the computational requirements are directly dependent on
the mesh. Having the right mesh resolution, can minimize the numerical inaccuracies.
However, the computational resources and computational time should also be taken
into account, when generating a mesh (or grid).
Grid generation of the metallic meshes, has been the most challenging thing in this
thesis, and about 30% of the work time was spent for generating the grid of the metallic
mesh by using the snappyHexMesh utility.
For the grid generation of the three cases of the metallic mesh, both blockMesh
and snappyHexMesh utilities were used. The blockMesh utility ise used for the
generation of the boundary box of the Ô¨āow domain, and next for the grid generation
around the metallic mesh the snappyHexMesh utility is used.
The mesh is generated from a dictionary Ô¨Āle named snappyHexMeshDict which is
located in the system directory, while the stl format Ô¨Āle exported from the CAD should
be placed in the directory constant/triSurface. In the snappyHexMeshDict, some of
the parameters that the user should set, are the number of reÔ¨Ānement levels, whether
the reÔ¨Ānement will be inside or outside the volume region, as well as the maximum
amount of cells that the Ô¨Ānal grid will consist of. Further details regarding the adopted
mesh generator can be found in OpenFOAM Documentation [68] as well as by available
training presentations by [27].
As previously mentioned a hybrid mesh consists of various regions of structured cells
combined with unstructured regions. However, in the present cases, due to the big
background domain, the number of structured cells is much higher, and the unstructured
cells are applied only close to curves of the wires of the metallic mesh.

mesh stl Ô¨Āle. Many combinations of reÔ¨Ānement levels were tried, however in all of the

5.11
Summary
In this chapter, experimental data of droplet impact on metallic meshes have been
reproduced numerically. The results of three diÔ¨Äerent cases (A, B, őď) with diÔ¨Äerent pore
and wire diameters, have been studied. Overall, the results of numerical simulations
showed good agreement with the experimental runs, however for more precise results
such as capturing exact disintegrated droplet diameters after the liquid has crossed the
metallic mesh, more reÔ¨Ānement levels and number of cells are required.
Among the three cases, only in case A there was not disintegration of the droplet
after partially penetration of the drop below the metallic mesh. This is because of the
small pore and wire diameter of the metallic mesh as well as the relatively high surface
tension of water. This is also conÔ¨Ārmed by the corresponding experimental output.
In comparison of the numerical results of cases őď and őď 2 it is evident that when one
less reÔ¨Ānement level is used, both cases showed good agreement qualitatively, and
quantitatively in the advancing phase of the droplet. However, there is a delay in the
volume of water crossing the metallic mesh for case with less reÔ¨Ānement level (case őď
2). In both cases, about 50% of the water has crossed the metallic mesh within 1.60 ms,
the relatively low viscosity of the water is considered a very important element for the
fast penetration of the water.
The simulations were not able to capture the small in diameter droplets that appeared
in case B and case őď, after the disintegration of the water. The reason for that is the
grid resolution, which in our case is dependent on the levels of reÔ¨Ānement. Two more
levels of reÔ¨Ānement is estimated to be enough for capturing such details.
Last but not least, it has been shown that the droplet initial position before the impact
can aÔ¨Äect the results in respect of volume of the liquid that remains above the mesh
over time, as well as can contribute to faster or slower penetration of the droplet. Also
in this case more reÔ¨Ānement levels are required for clearer conclusions.
In general it can be concluded that the proposed numerical simulations despite not
being mesh independent can adequately capture the global characteristics and trends of
the considered experimental cases. Therefore, they can be safely used as a Ô¨Ārst step to
further explain the experimental observations or further extend the experimental runs
by performing "virtual" experiments that are diÔ¨Écult or impossible to be performed in
the laboratory.
Finally, the implementation of a dynamic mesh reÔ¨Ānement technique that automatically
reÔ¨Ānes and unreÔ¨Ānes the mesh following the interface motion, can signiÔ¨Ācantly increase

7 Summary and Conclusions
Drop impact on porous media, has attracted immense number of attention in the last
years both in academia and industry due to the wire range of applications. The objective
of this thesis was to perform numerical simulations, using open-source toolboxes,
of droplet impacting on metallic meshes. The simulation results were veriÔ¨Āed with
experimental data from literature as well as with in-house experimental data.
In the Ô¨Ārst step, in order to be able to perform numerical simulations, validation of the
numerical model was conducted. The key insights of the validation of the model are
described as follows:
‚ÄĘ The numerical model showed good agreement with the experimental runs, in
relative high We number (We = 90), when performing 2D axis-symmetric simu-
lations .
‚ÄĘ When reproducing numerically the same experimental case, in 3D simulation
using one-fourth of the domain, the model is capturing better the results, com-
pared to 2D simulation, and most importantly is able to show good results even
if in the experiment the drop has lost its symmetry.
‚ÄĘ The slight deformation of the droplet due to drag does not eÔ¨Äect signiÔ¨Ācantly the
behaviour of the droplet during the impact. For very high We number values, the
3D simulation was not able to perform good results compared to the experimental.
The reason for that is that the mesh was not Ô¨Āne enough for capturing the receding
phase of the droplet, causing signiÔ¨Ācant diÔ¨Äerence between the two cases. For
better results, the further reÔ¨Ānement of the grid is suggested, by at least two
more levels (minimum cells 6.75 ¬Ķm), however this would give a grid of more
than 200 million cells in total.

‚ÄĘ In general, the implementation of a dynamic mesh reÔ¨Ānement strategy in the
code is suggested for capturing high We number impacts with 3D features, with
a relatively low computational cost.
In the second step, after validating the numerical model, the numerical performance
of droplet impact in metallic meshes was performed. The key insights are described
below:
‚ÄĘ Among the three cases investigated, case A is the only case where not disin-

‚ÄĘ Reducing the reÔ¨Ānement by one level, on case őď 2, compared to case őď, reduced

it aÔ¨Äected the results, causing a small delay in the water crossing from the metallic

‚ÄĘ For being able to validate better the numerical results of case A and B, continuing

‚ÄĘ More reÔ¨Ānement levels are required for being able to capture details such as the
small in diameter droplets after the disintegration of the droplet when the later

‚ÄĘ In case ‚Ą¶it is evident that droplet‚Äôs initial position before impacting the mesh

‚ÄĘ Summarizing, it can be concluded that the proposed numerical simulations despite
not being mesh independent can adequately capture the global characteristics
and trends of the considered experimental cases. Therefore, it can be used as a
Ô¨Ārst step to further numerical runs, by conducting "virtual" experiments.

‚ÄĘ When increasing the viscosity by ten times compared to the base case, signiÔ¨Ācant

slower penetration of the water, with delayed disintegration.

resolution is used.

identify, quantify, the eÔ¨Äects of these important controlling parameters.
