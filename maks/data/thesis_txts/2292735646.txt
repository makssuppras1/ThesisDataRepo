Abstract
Precast concrete walls are often jointed by overlapping U-bars in the horizontal plane.
These connections have a low limit of capacity that makes them unsuitable for structures
with high shear forces - like high rise buildings. In this thesis a recently suggested con-
nection, where the U-bars are places in the vertical plane is examined. The joint is ﬁlled
with mortar.
This thesis deals with testing and rigid-plastic modelling of the load carrying capacity
of this recently suggested solution. In addition the overlapping U-bars are tested and
modelled when loaded purely in tension to describe how the shear capacity of the U-bar
conﬁguration depends on the tension capacity.
Part I deals with the U-bar conﬁguration loaded in tension. A rigid-plastic model is
developed to describe the capacity from the tests. The model is calibrated to the test
data using the eﬀectiveness factor. Initially an eﬀectiveness factor developed for beam
shear problems is used, and this factor is modiﬁed based on a suggestion from [Jørgensen,
2014]. The factor suggested by [Jørgensen, 2014] works very well with the results from
the tests and this formulation is used later on.
Part II deals with the U-bar conﬁguration loaded in shear combined with indented keys.
This part deals with full scale tests of the precast wall joint tested in pure shear. A
rigid-plastic upper bound solution is also developed for this problem. The test results
and the model is compared and the model is also calibrated using the eﬀectiveness factor.
Both factors used in Part I are examined in this part, and it turns out that the modiﬁed
formulation, suggested by [Jørgensen, 2014], ﬁts very well.

Resumé
Præfabrikerede beton-vægelementer samles ofte med overlappende U-bøjler, der ligger
i det horisontale plan. Disse samlinger har en lav kapacitet, der gør dem uegnede til
konstruktioner med store forskydningskræfter - f.eks. højhus-byggerier. I dette speciale
undersøges et nyt forslag til en samling, hvor U-bøjlerne er i vertikalt plan. Fugen fyldes
med mørtel.
Dette speciale omhandler test and plastic modellering af kapaciteten af denne nye løsning.
Derudover er de overlappede U-bøjler testet og modelleret i træk for at beskrive, hvordan
forskydningskapaciteten af U-bøjle konﬁgurationen afhænger af trækstyrken.
Del I omhandler U-bøjle konﬁgurationen belastet i træk. En plastisk model udarbejdes
til at beskrive kapaciteten bestemt ved forsøg. Modellen kalibreres til forsøgsresultaterne
ved hjælp af eﬀektivitetsfaktoren. Til at starte med benyttes en faktor, der er udviklet
til forskydningsproblemer i bjælker and denne faktor ændres baseret på et forslag fra
[Jørgensen, 2014]. Faktoren forslået af [Jørgensen, 2014] passer rigtig godt sammen med
forsøgsresultaterne og denne formel benyttes senere her.
Del II omhandler U-bøjle konﬁgurationen belastet i træk kombineret med fortanding.
Denne del omhandler fuldskala forsøg af en præfabrikeret væk samling udsat for ren
forskydning. En plastisk øvre værdi model udvikles også for dette problem. Forsøgsre-
sultaterne og modellen sammenlignes, og modellen kalibreres ligeledes ved hjælp af eﬀek-
tivitetsfaktoren. Begge faktorer fra Del I undersøges i denne del og det viser sig, at den
modiﬁcerede formel, fra [Jørgensen, 2014], passer meget ﬂot på forsøgsresultaterne.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In precast concrete element buildings, on site cast joints between elements are often the
weakest link. For this reason it is often the strength of the joint that is governing for
the load bearing capacity and thereby the design of the entire structural system. Current
best practice for construction of loop connections as shear joints is challenged by the
installation process, which is to be performed in a too narrow zone. This occasionally
leads to inadequate structural continuity across the narrow construction zone. Further-
more, the load bearing capacity of the joint is limited by an asymmetry of the horizontal
reinforcement loop design. The execution of these joints on site is time consuming and
the reinforcement layout often prevents optimal and fast installation of the prefabricated
concrete elements.
In this project, a recently suggested design concept for shear joints is further investigated.
The function of these joints is to ensure structural continuity in the structure, primarily
by transfer of shear, and to ensure a ductile behaviour of the joint. The overall aim is to
establish a conceptual joint conﬁguration that is 1) construction friendly, 2) highly ductile
and 3) at the same time can be used when large shear forces have to be transferred, e.g.
in high rise structures or other buildings where the geometrical conﬁguration requires
high strength and ductility. The new joint design may replace the conventional in situ
cast approach, which currently is preferred due to short comings in the conventional joint
design. The basic idea is to design the keyed joint as a symmetric indented joint, where
the reinforcement is laid out as overlapping loops in the vertical plane of the joint. This
will, as opposed to current practice, increase the construction friendliness because vertical
lowering of the elements into the ﬁnal position is possible for every single element. The
aim of this project is to examine the strength and ductility of the joint design through a
series of experiments, and on that basis develop the necessary theoretical basis for design
calculations.
The cornerstone of this particular project is the investigations of the inﬂuence of the
indented key area on the structural behaviour. The load bearing capacity is recorded
in terms of a load deformation relation, with respect to deformations in characteristic
directions. Digital Image Correlation is being used to map local deformations, rotations
and relative movements over the entire area of the joint, as well as to identify the type of
failure, e.g. local failures or multi-body failure mechanisms. Furthermore direct tension
2
==PAGE:14==
tests is carried to study the strength of a single loop joint in a direction perpendicular to
the shear loading direction. The correlation between the responses in the smaller scale
tension tests, and the larger shear tests is studied with focus on the inﬂuence of the sym-
metric reinforcement layout in the vertical plane of the joint and the inﬂuence of the cross
sectional area of the indented keys.
A model to calculate the shear strength of the new joint design is established based on
the extremal principles of plasticity. This includes an upper bound solution.

Chapter 2
Theory
In this chapter the basic theories used in this thesis is presented.
Normally disks are treated as plane stress problem. When introducing joints however the
weakest link is often that joint. In joints lateral out of plane strains will more or less be
prevented. It is therefore a good approximation to treat these keyed shear joints as plane
strain problems [Nielsen and Hoang, 2011].
Both the concrete and the reinforcement are treated as perfectly rigid-plastic components.
For the reinforcement it is assumed that it carry axial forces only - meaning that dowel
action is neglected. The joints manufactured in the test series have a smooth surface.
Throughout the thesis it is therefore assumed that they are perfectly smooth and that
the cohesion of the joint is c′ = 0. The smoothness of the surface is not ensured using
any means other than the surface is not roughened. No grease or other sort of lubricants
are used.

2.1.1
Extremum principles
The idealisation illustrated in Figure 2.1-1 implies that we are unable to determine the
stress state in a body when the stress is below yielding.
In the theory of plasticity there are three principles of extremum; the lower bound theorem,
the upper bound theorem, and the uniqueness theorem. These will brieﬂy be lined out.
The lower bound theorem
The lower bound solution must satisfy the equilibrium and statical boundary conditions
for the actual load [Nielsen and Hoang, 2011].
The stress distribution obtained from
this theorem is denoted a safe and statically admissible stress distribution. The solution
obtained for this theorem is therefore always below or equal to the actual failure load.
The upper bound theorem
This theorem is based on a geometrically possible failure mechanism. This implies that
the correct failure mechanism has to be identiﬁed in order to obtain the correct solution.
This solution is therefore always above or equal to the failure load. This can easily be
imagined - if the chosen solution for a very simple structure is very complicated, it can
be imagined that a more simple solution exists, which require less energy.
The uniqueness theorem
The uniqueness theorem says that if the solution obtained from the lower bound solution
and the upper bound solution are identical they must be equal to the actual failure load.
The load is then uniquely determined - hence the name.

2.5
Ductility
Ductility is a mechanical property closely tied with plasticity. A material is considered
ductile if it can deform plastically without fracture [duc, 2015].
The shear joint is considered fully ductile, if the load can be increased until failure has
spread to such a large area of the joint, that a ﬁnal failure occurs for other reasons [Hansen
et al., 1974].
A conventional wall shear joint is considered ductile if it is designed so that the U-bar
reinforcement yield at failure [Jørgensen, 2014].
An example of a ductile joint is when the load can be increased, after the ﬁrst peak failure,
to a value higher than peak.
Steel which is considered ductile has a plastic working curve as seen in Figure 2.5-1.

Chapter 5
Analytical plastic model
In this chapter an analytical model that can explain the behaviour seen in the results
shown in Chapter 4 is presented.
Initially a simple model that ignores the tension strength of concrete is presented. This
model highly underestimates the capacity of the joint at low reinforcement degrees. It
should be noted that the tests performed in this thesis does not lie in the area with
low reinforcement degree.
But in the eﬀort to give the full theoretical picture of the
subject matter, an additional model is suggested. In this additional model the transverse
reinforcement is ignored, but the tension strength of the concrete is utilised.
5.1
Upper bound solution
The U-bar reinforcement contributes to the failure capacity of the shear joint investigated
in Part II. The dowel action of the U-bars is neglected and only the tension capacity is
taken into account. In order to asses the tension capacity, plastic upper bound models
are proposed for the U-bar connection as an individual object.
5.1.1

to look like Figure 5.1-1. According to [Jørgensen, 2014] the true failure mechanism is a

for design-oriented strength prediction. That is what have been done here.

three diﬀerent pieces that move diﬀerently; I, II, and III.

Chapter 7
Introduction to Part II
This part of the thesis concerns wall joint loaded by in-plane shear. In the previous part a
joint consisting of overlapping U-bars was investigated. In this part the thesis that same
overlapping U-bar conﬁguration is used in a joint loaded in shear.
Conventional joints in precast wall elements is indented keys combined with U-bars in the
horizontal plane. Due to installation issues these U-bars are bent upwards(or downwards)
when installed, afterwards bent into place at the construction site. A conventional joint
is illustrated in Figure 7.0-1.
Figure 7.0-1: Conventional U-bar conﬁguration in indented key shear joint.
The design of the joints tested in this thesis has U-bars in the vertical plane instead of
the horizontal plane. This has the advantage that when the elements are lowered in the
vertical direction there is room for the U-bars without bending them. This means that the
size of the U-bars can potentially be increased and that the installation can potentially
be easier.
The vertical overlapping U-bars are ”locked” with a lacer bar in the horizontal plane and
a locking bar as in conventional joints. The joint is ﬁlled with mortar.
A conventional joint is designed so that the U-bars are yielding before obtaining concrete
failure. This ensures a ductile behaviour, due to the ductile behaviour of steel. This is
41

sought for the new design examined in this thesis. As the behaviour of the overlapping
U-bars itself have been examined in Part I it is possible to design the U-bars to yield from
the expressions in Part I and using that conﬁguration in Part II.
Digital Image Correlation, DIC, technique is used to analyse diﬀerent thing that are visible
at the surface of the joint. The software Aramis is used for the analysis.

Summary of main ﬁndings
The inﬂuence of two diﬀerent parameters have been analysed in this chapter - the lacer
bar diameter and the key length.
Based on the available data it cannot be concluded that there is a clear correlation be-
tween the lacer bar diameter and the shear capacity. In Part I there was a very clear
57
==PAGE:74==
relationship between the lacer bar diameter and the tension capacity. For large reinforce-
ment degrees this relationship was constant, as shown in Figure 5.1-5. It might be the case
that the failure load is independent of the lacer bar diameter for the chosen geometries of
the specimens - i.e. yielding of the U-bars is actually occurring.
For the length of the keys there seem to be a correlation between this parameter and
the failure load. Meaning that an increase in the key length results in an increase in the
failure load. In short;
• The tests performed gives not clear indication of the correlation between the shear
capacity and the lacer bar diameter.
• The mortar core in the overlapping U-bars have an increased strength compared to
the tension tests as the U-bars have yielded.
• The locking bar creates some sort of conﬁnement in the joint.
• The failure load increases with increasing key area.

Plastic models for keyed shear joints
In this chapter an upper bound solution is proposed for the shear capacity. This is later

9.1
Tensile capacity of U-bar connection
The tensile capacity of the U-bar connection have been examined in details in Part I.
In practice it is actually the U-bar connection examined in Part I that have been used
in the shear test performed in Part II. In Part I it was shown that the tensile capacity
of the overlapping U-bars depend on the reinforcement ratio - i.e. the diameter of the
lacer bar. The dimensions of the specimens tested in tension is equal to those tested in
shear - i.e. same thickness, lacer bar diameter and U-bar diameter and layout. The results
from Part I might therefore be implemented directly in the shear capacity developed here.
The results from Part I show that the connection failure mode was a mortar core failure.
However the shear tests show yielding of the U-bars. To develop a plastic model the
capacity of the overlapping U-bars is simply denoted Pu and the value of this is later
examined when comparing the test results with the analytical model.

10.4
Discussion
The results presented in Figure 10.3-2 are quite neat. These results are based on the
assumption that the U-bars are yielding. The model and experimental data from Part I
suggests that the U-bars are not yielding for these conﬁgurations. However, it is argued
that the results from Part I cannot be used directly in Part II, where the U-bars are part
of a larger structure. The experimental results from Part II suggests that the U-bars are
yielding.
In Part I it is assumed that a fairly simple failure mechanism represents the failure mode.
It is also argued that the true mechanism is more complicated and three dimensional.
When implementing the U-bar conﬁguration in the shear joint, it becomes part of a larger
structure. This suggests that the true failure mechanism becomes even more complicated.
With increasing complexity the strength of the mortar core increases, while the yield
capacity of the U-bars is constant.
During testing in Part I it was observed that ”excess” mortar peels oﬀof the mortar core
quite early during testing. This is not possible for the specimens tested in shear, as the
neighbouring mortar and locking bar creates conﬁnement.
It is believed that these factors results in an even more complex true failure mechanism.
This results in that yielding of the U-bars become more critical than mortar core failure.
68
==PAGE:85==
Due to these circumstances it is believed, that it is a good assumption, to assume yielding
of the U-bars. In order to asses the restriction of this assumption, further tests has got
to be performed where the properties of the U-bars is varied in the shear tests.
10.5
Practical applications
In order to apply the solution proposed it has to be ensured that the assumptions made
are correct. An important assumption is that the U-bars are yielding. To ensure that
the U-bars are actually yielding it is proposed that the U-bars are designed using the
approach in Part I. As seen in Part II the U-bars that were supposed to have mortar core
failure, have actually failed by yielding. So if the overlapping U-bars are designed to yield
using Eq. (5.1.24) > Eq. (5.1.15) from Part I it must be conservative to assume that they
actually yield under shear.
It is thought to be a great advantage to design the joint to fail by yielding in the U-bars,
as the yielding in the U-bars is more ductile than mortar core failure.
Once the overlapping U-bars have been designed to yield, the shear capacity of the joint
can be determined using (9.3.1) when using ν determined from (10.1.2).

Chapter 11
Recommendations for future work
In Part I it was determined that a modiﬁed eﬀectiveness factor matches the test data quite
neat. All of the tested specimens ended up having mortar core failure. It is recommended
that more tests should be performed in order to determine when the failure changes to
yielding of the U-bars. As explained earlier, it is recommended that the U-bars should be
designed to yield. It is therefore important to verify the transition experimentally.
As shear stresses often are dominating in precast wall joints this is the most critical load
case. However, it could be interesting to investigate load combinations, where the shear is
combined with either normal force or moments, to see how this aﬀects the capacity. The
combination of forces is most likely going to increase or decrease the capacity depending
on the load direction.
In Part II it was concluded that the overlapping U-bars were yielding, as oppose to the
tension tests in Part I. It could be interesting to do strain measurements in the U-bars
throughout the test to see when the strains occur. It is very evident when removing the
joint mortar that the U-bars have yielded, but it is not easy to determine when. These
strain measurements, will however, be diﬃcult to obtain. As seen from Figure 8.3-3 the
form of the U-bars are quite diﬀerent. In addition; if strain gauges are used, this might
manipulate with the material geometry when applying these.
The construction business is thought to be rather conservative. Both contractors and
designers are often thought to be resistant to adapt new solutions, that on the face of
it appears more complicated and time consuming. When the technical aspects of this
shear joint solution is further investigated, it could be very interesting to do a business
case based on this solution, as opposed to conventional shear joints or cast in situ solutions.

Chapter 12
Conclusion to Part I and Part II
The purpose of this thesis was to test and evaluate a newly suggested design concept for
shear joints for pre-fabricated wall elements. The purpose was to examine the inﬂuence
of the indented key area on the structural behaviour. In addition the correlation between
the tension capacity and the shear capacity of the overlapping U-bar design should be
examined.
Part I covered the tension capacity of the U-bar conﬁguration. On the basis of the tests
conducted and the rigid-plastic model developed a suggestion for the eﬀectiveness factor
was veriﬁed. The main ﬁndings from the experimental work were
• For all of the tests specimens concrete core failure was critical.
• The strength of the joint increases with increasing lacer bar diameter and thereby
reinforcement degree.
• The concrete cover does not contribute to the capacity of the joint.
The experimental work was expressed using a rigid-plastic model. The eﬀectiveness factor,
ν, was investigated. A suggestion (12.0.1) could be veriﬁed on the basis of nine tests with
the lacer bar diameter as the varying parameter.

Chapter 1
Experimental work
This section contains information on the production, and the experimental set-up for all
the tested elements and materials.
1.1
Production of wall elements
The production of the elements and joints are performed for three diﬀerent projects si-
multaneously. In total 80 diﬀerent elements are cast creating 40 wall joints. Of these 40
wall joints 10 are covered in this report.
1.1.1
Preparation for production
The joints consists of lacer bars and locking bars some of which have strain gauges mounted
on them ﬁlled with mortar. Prior to production of the joints Jesper Sørensen prepared
the lacer bars and dowels with strain gauges. This was done at DTU in Lyngby.
1.1.2
Production at EXPAN
The tested wall elements were produced at EXPAN A/S, a part of CRH Concrete, located
in Søndersø on Funen. The wall elements were cast on December 17th 2014 in moulds
like the one shown in Figure 1-1. Together with all the wall elements eight cylinders were
cast to later test the compression strength of the elements.
All of the elements that were cast, were positioned side by side in order to create a smooth
and consistent production process. After the wall elements were cast they were leaved to
dry in the production hall at EXPAN until the joints are cast. The cylinders were brought
to DTU on January 8th and stored in similar conditions to the wall elements itself - i.e.
initially indoor and then outdoor.
The joints were prepared on January 7th and 8th with positioning of lacer and locking
bars both with and without strain gauges.

The compression tests of the concrete cylinders were performed in two diﬀerent machines.
The tension tests of the steel members were all performed on the same machine with

All the shear tests of the wall elements were performed in a specially build test set-up

The tension tests of the joints were also performed in a specially build setup. The data
from these tests are documented using a data logger and ARAMIS.
1.2.1

The compression tests were performed on two diﬀerent machines; Toni Technik and In-
stron 8508. The Toni was used in the beginning where the only output was the compres-
sion force. The tests were switched to the Instron machine in order to obtain a working
curve for the cylinders. The Toni was then used again, this time monitoring and data
logging both the displacement and force.

1.2.2
Tension test of U-bar reinforcement
Figure 2-2: Test set-up of a U-bar tested in
tension without extensometer.
The U-bars were K8 steel. The U-bars are
tested in tension in order to determine the
yield and maximum stress. The machine
used to test all the steel members is the
same. This was done to ensure consistency
in the results.
The U-bars were tested either with ex-
tensometer or strain gauge or both. The
extensometer were mounted somewhat in
the middle, removed after the maximum
stress was reached but before the specimen
breaks. The strain gauges were mounted
by sanding down a small area of the bar
to obtain a smooth surface.
The advan-
tage of the strain gauge should be that
it could measure further than the exten-
someter since it is a one time device. The
goal was to achieve the strain at which the
specimen breaks. As it turns out due to
the large deformation and strain, the strain
gauge quickly became non functional.
It
was therefore determined that the exten-
someter was preferred.
The specimens that were tested, were the legs of an actual U-bars cut in to smaller pieces.
1.2.3
Tension test of locking bar
The locking bars were K12 SAS steel. The test set-up for the locking bar was the same
as for the U-bar. The locking bars were delivered in very long pieces and cut into the
correct length of the shear tests.
1.2.4
Tension test of lacer bar
The lacer bars were double headed T-bars with a diameter of 12, 14 and 16 mm. The lacer
bars had a total length of 195 mm. Due the clamps in the test machine, shown in Figure
2-2, the head was cut oﬀ. Due the short length the specimens could not be fully clamped.
For the Ø16 specimens the tests were unsuccessful. During the ﬁrst test it was quickly
determined that the specimen was slipping in the set-up. The test was then aborted. It
was tried to sand down the areas where the clamps were gripping but the specimen still
slipped. Finally it was tried to machine down the specimen to 10 mm. Those were tested
with success. It was tried to drill down the same type of lacer bar to diﬀerent diameters.
These test all gave very diﬀerent results. On the basis of these inconsistent results the
manufacturer was consulted. Peikko supplied working curves and other results from their
tests and these data were used further on.
