Abstract  
The process of Vitamin D3 synthesis in microalgae, occurs through the transformation of 7-
dehydrocholesterol to vitamin D3 under the influence of UVB radiation. Recent studies have shown 
that increased dose of UVB exposure leads to an increase in vitamin D3 production in N. oceanica. 
This study focuses on N. oceanica, a promising candidate for biofortification, to investigate how UVB 
exposure influences vitamin D3 synthesis under various stress and health conditions. Through a series 
of controlled experiments, the relationship between UVB exposure duration and vitamin D3 synthesis 
was systematically examined. Results from the experiments demonstrated that increased UVB 
exposure directly correlates with higher levels of vitamin D3 synthesis, with maximum production 
observed at the highest exposure duration tested, which was 259.7 ± 6.0 (n=2) after 120 min (UVB 
dose of 19.6 vitD3 kJ/m2). Samples exposed for UVB for 15 min (UVB dose of 2.4 vitD3 kJ/m2) and 
30 min (UVB dose of 4.9 vitD3 kJ/m2) exhibited vitamin D3 contents of 135.7±2.7 ng/gDM and 
204.7±5.0 ng/gDM, respectively, with a p-value < 0.05, indicating statistically significant, all groups 
were statistically significant. The control group was not exposed to UVB light, therefore the biomass 
level of vitamin D3 was at the level of quantification. 
 
Furthermore, the study revealed that the physiological state of the N. oceanica cultures significantly 
impacts vitamin D3 production. Cultures maintained in optimal conditions showed higher vitamin D3 
content compared to those under stress. Vitamin D3 content in stressed culture was measured  28.8 ±
0.6 ng/gDM (n=2), 43.0 ± 1.4 ng/gDM (n=2), 55.2 ng/gDM (n=2) after 30 min (UVB dose of 4.9 
vitD3 kJ/m2), 60 min (UVB dose of 9.8 vitD3 kJ/m2) and 90 min (UVB dose of 14.7 vitD3 kJ/m2) of 
UVB exposure, respectively. Although there is a significant difference in vitamin D3 production 
among the groups, with the longest exposed group showing the highest content, it is considerably 
lower compared to vitamin D3 content in the healthy N. oceanica culture. Observations showed that 
stressed cultures exhibited cell clumping and, in some cases, changes in color. 
 
The study also highlighted the complexity of contributions from healthy versus stressed cells within 
the cultures to the overall production of vitamin D3. While it remains unclear whether stressed cells 
can contribute effectively to vitamin D3 synthesis, the findings suggest that maintaining cellular 
health is crucial for enhancing production outcome. Additionally, there was a potential upper limit to 
effective UVB exposure and vitamin D3 production, prolonged exposure might not yield significantly 
more Vitamin D3 after a certain point. Overall, the research establishes a foundational understanding 
of how UVB radiation can be utilized to enhance vitamin D3 production in microalgae cultures, 
emphasizing the need for precise control over culture conditions to harness the full potential of N. 
oceanica as a viable source of vitamin D3.

1 Introduction 
 
Microalgae have received significant attention in recent years for their potential as cell factories to 
produce various bioactive compounds. Among these, oils are particularly significant, serving crucial 
roles in industries ranging from food and nutrition to biofuels and oleochemicals. N. oceanica, a 
highly oleaginous microalga, is distinguished by its robust oil production capabilities, notably 
including the valuable omega-3 fatty acid, eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA). This makes it an invaluable 
resource for the biodiesel, nutraceutical, and aquaculture industries (Xu, 2022). In recent research 
conducted by Ljubic et al., (2020, 2021) it was found that exposing N. oceanica to artificial UVB 
light significantly boosted its production of vitamin D3, with the increase in vitamin D3 levels being 
directly proportional to the intensity of UVB exposure. However, the study also uncovered several 
adverse effects associated with UVB radiation, such as reduced growth rates and decrease in lipid 
content. 
 
Vitamin D exists in two primary forms: vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) and vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol). 
Vitamin D3 is mainly found in animal-derived foods like fish, eggs, and meat while vitamin D2 can 
be found in wild mushrooms and UVB-exposed fungi and yeast (Bresson et al., 2016). Following 
consumption, vitamin D undergoes conversion to 25-hydroxyvitamin D in the liver, and 
subsequently, it is hydroxylated to its active form, 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D, in the kidneys. This 
activated form is crucial for regulating calcium levels in the body, supporting bone formation, and 
performing other vital physiological functions (Acar & Özkan, 2021). Besides dietary intake, vitamin 
D3 can also be synthesized in the skin when exposed to sunlight, UVB light stimulates the 
transformation of a precursor called 7-dehydrocholesterol into vitamin D3 (Holick, 2023). The 
recommended daily vitamin D3 intake in Europe and the US is 10 to 20 μg/day (Ljubic et al., 2020). 
The actual daily intake typically is around 3–7 μg/day, which is far below the recommendations 
(Švarc et al., 2022).  
 
To address vitamin D deficiencies, supplements of vitamin D2 and D3 are available. Vitamin D3 is 
mainly synthesized by transforming lanolin, a substance obtained from sheep's wool (Ljubic et al., 
2021). Alternatively, wild lichens are a commercially available vegan source of vitamin D3. The 
documented content of vitamin D3 in lichens (Cladina arbuscula) is from 0.67 to 2.04 μg/g dry matter 
(T. Wang et al., 2001). However, wild lichens are not considered a sustainable source of vitamin D3 
==PAGE:9==
2 
 
due to their extremely slow growth rate, where most of the species increase in size by less than 1 mm 
per year (Ljubic et al., 2021). In contrast, microalgae offer a promising solution. These rapidly 
growing microorganisms can be cultivated on a large scale and are rich not only in vitamin D3 but 
also in valuable bioactive compounds like protein, omega-3 fatty acids, and β-carotene (Ljubic et al., 
2021).  
 
There is a growing need to produce vitamin D3 for dietary supplements. The primary source of 
vitamin D3 is derived from animal-based products, and vegan alternatives are currently limited in 
availability. Therefore, the main goal of this study is to establish an additional source of vegan vitamin 
D3 by investigating the potential of UV-irradiated N. oceanica. 
 
 
2 Aims of the project  
 
Vitamin D3, also known as cholecalciferol, is typically sourced from sunlight exposure and certain 
animal-based foods, it is less commonly found in vegan-friendly forms. Microalgae, such as N. 
oceanica, have emerged as a promising plant-based source that could bridge this gap. By harnessing 
their photosynthetic capabilities, it may be possible to induce microalgae to produce vitamin D3, thus 
providing an alternative for dietary supplements, especially for those with limited sunlight exposure 
or dietary restrictions. 
 
The project, therefore, sets forth the following aims to explore and potentially harness this capacity 
of N. oceanica: 
 
• Test whether a short, intense UVB exposure just before harvest can be used to induce the 
production of vitamin D3 in the microalgae, N. oceanica.  
• Optimize the exposure setup to result in a vitamin D3 concentration of 10-15 µg/g dry matter 
in the exposed biomass.   
• Investigate the effect of the UVB treatment on other cellular components such as fatty acids 
in N. oceanica.

3 Theory 
3.1 Vitamin D 
Vitamin D, a fat-soluble vitamin, essential for bone health and calcium metabolism, has been 
extensively studied worldwide. Its relevance extends to immune modulation, cellular growth, and 
cardiovascular function (Dominguez et al., 2021; Holick, 2006). Despite its significance, vitamin D 
deficiency is a public health issue of global proportion, affecting populations across various 
geographic and socioeconomic backgrounds (Dominguez et al., 2021). Severe vitamin D deficiency 
is especially common in the Middle East, China, Mongolia, and India (van Schoor et al., 2023; van 
Schoor & Lips, 2017). In India, Tunisia, Pakistan, and Afghanistan the level of 25(OH)D in serum is 
<30 nmol/L in more than 20% of the population. Alone in India it has been estimated that 490 million 
individuals are vitamin D deficient (Amrein et al., 2020). The recommended serum 25(OH)D level 
to avoid deficiency is generally considered to be above 30 nmol/L, as indicated by the US Institute 
of Medicine. A level below 25 nmol/L is traditionally used in Europe as a cutoff to define vitamin D 
deficiency in the context of metabolic bone disease (Cashman et al., 2016). The percentage of vitamin 
D deficiency, with serum levels of 25(OH)D <30 nmol/L, stands at 5.9% in the United States, 7.4% 
in Canada, and 13% across Europe (Amrein et al., 2020). These figures reflect a significant portion 
of the population in each region with levels of vitamin D considered insufficient for optimal health. 
It is important to note that such deficiencies can vary by age and ethnicity, with some groups being 
more at risk than others (Amrein et al., 2020). 
 
Elmer McCollum and Marguerite Davis’ groundbreaking work in 1913 on fat-soluble vitamins laid 
the groundwork for the discovery of vitamin D. Known for addressing rickets, a common condition 
in industrial England dubbed "English Disease", vitamin D's significance was spotlighted when 
Huldschinski's work showed ultraviolet light exposure could prevent and cure the disease. This 
finding hinted at a link between sunlight and the production of vitamin D, leading to the innovative 
solution of irradiating foods with UVB light to combat deficiencies (DeLuca, 2014; Holick, 2023). 
Rickets, osteoporosis, and osteomalacia are conditions primarily caused by deficiencies in vitamin D, 
calcium, or phosphate. Rickets, often observed in children, leads to bone deformity and growth 
disturbances due to insufficient mineralization during growth. Osteoporosis is characterized by 
reduced bone mass and increased fracture risk, often associated with aging and lower estrogen levels 
in post-menopausal women. Osteomalacia, prevalent in adults, is marked by softened bones, typically 
==PAGE:11==
4 
 
due to a prolonged deficit in vitamin D, resulting in decreased bone mineralization (Audran & Kumar, 
1985; Holick, 2023). Without vitamin D, dietary calcium uptake is limited to 10-15% and phosphorus 
to about 60%. Activation of vitamin D receptors by 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D enhances intestinal 
absorption of calcium to 30-40% and phosphorus to nearly 80% (Holick, 2007). Vitamin D deficiency 
is linked to multiple health problems beyond bone disorders, including cancer, diabetes, arthritis, and 
Parkinson’s disease. Understanding how vitamin D functions in these diseases is key to developing 
new therapeutic approaches (Jeon & Shin, 2018; Spiro & Buttriss, 2014). 
 
There are two main form of vitamin D, vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol) and vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol), 
where both forms are obtainable through diet and supplementation (Pludowski et al., 2018; 
Ramasamy, 2020). Vitamin D3 can also be synthesized in human skin upon UVB radiation exposure 
with wavelength of 290-320 nm (Ramasamy, 2020). Sunlight exposure is the predominant source of 
vitamin D for the majority of the global population, driving the synthesis of 90–95% of the body's 
vitamin D3. Unfortunately, the essential radiation required for this synthesis is consistently present 
year-round only in locations situated below a latitude of 35o (Acar & Özkan, 2021; Jäpelt & Jakobsen, 
2013). When human skin is exposed to sunlight, the naturally present provitamin known as 7-
dehydrocholesterol (7-DHC) forms into pre-vitamin D3. Pre-vitamin D3 is thermodynamically 
unstable which then spontaneously isomerizes to vitamin D3 due to body temperature (Acar & Özkan, 
2021; Holick et al., 2007). Both forms of vitamin D binds to vitamin D-binding protein (DBP) in the 
bloodstream and is transported to the liver (Dominguez et al., 2021). In the liver, vitamin D undergoes 
a hydroxylation step converted by the enzyme 25-hydroxylase into 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D], 
known as calcidiol, which is the primary circulating form used to assess vitamin D levels, due to 
longer mean half-life of about 13-15 days, largely because of its robust binding to DBP (Dominguez 
et al., 2021; Jones et al., 2014). The second hydroxylation occurs in the kidneys by the enzyme 1α-
hydroxylase, which converts 25(OH)D to the biologically active form of vitamin D, 1,25-
dihydroxyvitamin D [1,25(OH)2D], known as calcitriol. The half-life of 1,25(OH)2D in the 
bloodstream is measured in hours (Makris et al., 2021; Simmerman & Jones, 1998). Calcitriol 
interacts with the vitamin D receptor (VDR) across various tissues, influencing gene expression and 
facilitating calcium and phosphate absorption in the intestines and calcium mobilization in bones 
(Bresson et al., 2016; Saponaro et al., 2020; Spyksma et al., 2024). A schematic overview of the steps 
involved in vitamin D metabolism is provided in Figure 1.

In the dietary landscape, vitamin D is sourced predominantly from animal-based products, with 
vitamin D3 being mainly present in fatty fish, meat, egg yolk and cod liver oil. Fortified food items 
also play a role in contribution of vitamin D3 (Jakobsen et al., 2019; Wilson et al., 2017). Notably, 
fatty fish stand out for having the highest natural content of vitamin D3; for instance, salmon from the 
Baltic Sea have an average of 18.5 ± 4.6 μg/100 g, while those from the North Sea contain around 
9.4 ± 1.9 μg/100 g, indicating that regional fishing grounds can influence vitamin D3 levels (Jakobsen 
et al., 2019). In contrast, vitamin D2 is sourced primarily from plant-based products, like mushrooms 
and UVB exposed fungi and yeast, and from plants that is contaminated with fungi (Jäpelt & 
Jakobsen, 2013; Ljubic et al., 2021). It is evident that dietary sources rich in vitamin D are relatively 
limited. Moreover, the contribution of such vitamin D-rich foods to daily dietary intake is typically 
minimal, as they do not frequently constitute a regular component of the dominant dietary patterns 
(Dominguez et al., 2021).  
 
Therefore, especially in regions where there is limited UVB exposure, it is important to think about 
the need for alternative strategies to increase vitamin D levels. For vitamin D to form in the skin, it 
depends on the intensity of UVB radiation, which depends on the season and latitude (Christakos et 
al., 2016). For instance, in cities like Boston located (42.2 oN) and Copenhagen (55 oN) the skin does 
==PAGE:13==
6 
 
not synthesize vitamin D from November to February and from October to March, respectively, due 
to insufficient sunlight, whereas in San Juan (18 oN) and Crete Greece (35 °N) there is adequate 
sunlight for vitamin D production in human skin throughout the entire year (Christakos et al., 2016; 
Ljubic et al., 2020). Obtaining sufficient sunlight is necessary for the synthesis of vitamin D3 in the 
skin; excessive exposure, however, is discouraged due to the increased risk of developing skin cancer 
(Pilz et al., 2018). Apart from season and geographic latitude, several other factors can influence the 
skin's ability to synthesize vitamin D from sunlight. Complete cloud cover can reduce UVB radiation 
by up to 99% (Bresson et al., 2016; Engelsen, 2010). Additionally, factors like the amount of time 
spent outside, sunscreen use, skin color, advancing age, significant pollution, and clothing coverage 
can all diminish the skin's capacity for vitamin D production (Brouwer‐Brolsma et al., 2017; 
Engelsen, 2010; Webb & Engelsen, 2006). The skin's ability in synthesizing vitamin D wanes as 
people age, alongside a general decline in calcium absorption and the kidneys' ability to produce the 
active vitamin D. An increased body mass index has also been associated with lower levels of vitamin 
D in the bloodstream, with obesity linked to reduced serum 25(OH)D and 1α,25(OH)2D levels 
(Maclaughlin & Holick, 1985; Makris et al., 2021; Meehan & Penckofer, 2014). 
 
Therefore, vitamin D supplements and fortifying foods with vitamin D stands out as the most practical 
method to enhance vitamin D consumption and levels across the overall population (Pilz et al., 2018). 
When fortifying food there is two ways of doing it; the direct way, by adding vitamin D to foods and 
the indirect way, known as biofortification or bio-addition. Biofortification involves naturally 
boosting the vitamin D content in foods, such as by enhancing the feed of aquaculture and livestock 
to increase vitamin D in farmed fish and meat respectively (Pilz et al., 2018). Another biofortification 
technique is exposing fungi, like mushrooms or yeast used in bread-making, to UVB light, which 
converts their inherent ergosterol to vitamin D2 (Ljubic et al., 2021; Pilz et al., 2018). In addition, the 
high levels of vitamin D3 found in fish are from their dietary consumption, beginning with 
phytoplankton, rather than from internal nonphotochemical processes (Rao & Raghuramulu, 1996). 
At the foundation of the aquatic ecosystem, phytoplankton are rich in provitamins D, which can 
convert to active forms of vitamin D upon exposure to UVB radiation. Zooplankton, feeding on these 
phytoplankton, may in turn accumulate significant levels of vitamin D. This cycle continues up the 
food chain: small fish dine on plankton and larger fish consume the smaller ones (Rao & 
Raghuramulu, 1996; Takeuchi et al., 1991). Prior to 2020, there was a limited amount of research 
dedicated to investigating vitamin D3 in UVB exposed microalgae. This changed when Ljubic et al. 
==PAGE:14==
7 
 
published several studies examining the presence of vitamin D3 in UVB exposed microalgae, yielding 
positive and noteworthy results that justify further exploration and investment (Ljubic et al., 2020, 
2021). 
 
3.1.1 Vitamin D3 production in microalgae  
In the study by Ljubic et al. (2020), the potential of microalgae to produce vitamin D3 upon UVB 
exposure was investigated. The research focused on four species from different classes: 
Nannochloropsis oceanica (Eustigmatophyta), Nannochloropsis limnetica (Eustigmatophyta), 
Chlorella minutissima (Chlorophyta) and Rhodomonas salina (Rhodophyta). The experiments were 
set to up to cultivate and then expose these microalgae to different doses of UVB radiation (3, 6, 18 
and 24 h per day for 7 days). 
 
The findings revealed that among the species tested, N. oceanica uniquely demonstrated the ability 
to produce high level of vitamin D3 after UVB exposure, with an increased yield corresponding to 
higher UVB doses, while the other species did not produce vitamin D3. The result indicates a strong 
dose-dependent relationship between UVB radiation and the synthesis of vitamin D3 in this particular 
microalga. 
 
N. oceanica, when exposed to UVB radiation, could potentially offer a novel, vegan and natural 
sustainable source of vitamin D3, which could be utilized directly or in animal feeds. This is 
particularly significant because vitamin D3 is predominantly found in animal products, and this 
research opens up ways for producing vitamin D in a more environmentally friendly and ethical 
manner. The paper suggests that further research is needed to explore this possibility, including the 
evaluation of bioaccessibility and bioavailability of vitamin D3 from this microalga. 
 
 
3.2 Microalgae  
Algae play a crucial role in aquatic ecosystems, forming the basis of food chains and contributing to 
more than 50 % of the world's oxygen production (Levine, 2018). The term algae are used as a general 
label for a diverse group of simplistic, photosynthetic organisms that contain chlorophyll a, and 
typically produce oxygen through photosynthesis. These organisms are distinct from more evolved 
and advanced land-based plants, such as mosses, ferns, conifers, trees, and flowering plant. The algae 
classification spans from extensive multicellular seaweeds, which can reach tens of meters in length, 
==PAGE:15==
8 
 
to microscopic unicellular forms like the green alga, with sizes ranging from 0.5 to 1.3 μm 
(Borowitzka, 2018a). To get an overview the algae can be classified into two groups based on size, 
which is macroalgae and microalgae. Macroalgae or seaweeds, are large, visible, and multicellular 
marine algae rooted in seafloor environments (Ahmad et al., 2022; Suganya et al., 2016). While 
microalgae are a diverse group of microscopic organisms prevalent in both freshwater and marine 
ecosystems. These organisms vary in size from a few to several hundred micrometers, depending on 
the species. They are descripted as microscopic eukaryotes with unicellular structures, capable of 
existing individually or forming collectives such as chains or groups, including the photosynthetically 
active prokaryotes known as cyanobacteria. Although cyanobacteria belong to the bacterial domain, 
their functional and structural similarities to microalgae often justify their inclusion in the same 
category for their size, morphological features, and photosynthetic abilities (Borowitzka, 2018a; 
Suganya et al., 2016).  
 
Microalgae are an ancient group of organisms, with an evolutionary history stretching back about 
2500 million years, making them significantly older than seaweeds, which are 1600–1900 million 
years old (Levine, 2018). Throughout their long history microalgae have adapted to live in many 
different environments, which include floating or moving through the water in oceans, seas, lakes, 
and ponds as well as living on the bottom of these water bodies, in the sand, mud, or soil (Levine, 
2018). This ability to adapt to various places shows how important microalgae have been to life on 
Earth for a very long time. Taxonomically, they are placed into two superkingdoms, which is Bacteria 
and Eukaryota. Within the Eukaryota, microalgae are further classified into "supergroups", including 
Archaeplastida (Glaucophyta, Rhodophyceae, Chloroplastida), Chromalveolata (Cryptophyceae, 
Haptophyta, Stramenopiles, Alveolata), Rhizaria (Chlorachniophyta), and Excavata (Euglenozoa) 
(Adl et al., 2005; Ruggiero et al., 2015). It is estimated that over 50.000 microalgae species exist 
(Morales et al., 2021), with about 41.000 strains identified across 38 classes (Sexton & Lomas, 2018). 
However, only around 30.000 have been thoroughly studied and analyzed, highlighting a rich field 
for future scientific exploration (Morales et al., 2021). Species like Nannochloropsis belongs to the 
class Eustigmatophyceae in the phylum Ochrophyta which belongs to supergroup Chromalveolata 
(Stramenopiles) (Borowitzka, 2018a; Suda & Miyashita, 2019). They contain a yellow-green 
chloroplast with the main pigments being chlorophyll a, and the violaxanthin and vaucheriaxanthin 
(Suda & Miyashita, 2019). Currently there are five recognized species of Nannochloropsis: N. 
australis, N. granulata, N. limnetica, N. oceanica, and N. oculate (Fawley et al., 2015).

3.2.1 Historical use of microalgae, nutritional content, and health benefits 
Human utilization of microalgae is documented as far back as 2000 years, with ancient Chinese 
civilizations employing Nostoc species during periods of famine (Spolaore et al., 2006). These 
microalgae are recognized for its rich protein and pigment content and are regarded as a nutritious 
dietary source (Rizwan et al., 2018). In the Inca Empire, Nostoc was consumed for its bone and tooth 
fortifying properties. Post-colonization, its use declined, remaining with the Andean peoples. Known 
as cushuro, this alga was hand-gathered in the Andean wetlands, sun-dried, and stored. It still features 
in countryside Chilean and Peruvian cuisine, especially during festivals, in dishes like spicy fish stews 
and hearty milk-based soups with algae (Jacob-Lopes & Zepka, 2023). Additionally, Nostoc 
sphaeroides, a particular species, has a long-standing history in traditional Chinese medicine for 
treating illnesses such as diarrhea, hepatitis, and hypertension and is still valued as a key component 
in various medical formulations (Rizwan et al., 2018). Algae can also be toxic, the earliest reported 
harm caused by toxic algae was in 1793 in British Columbia, where Captain George Vancouver died 
after eating shellfish contaminated with dinoflagellate toxins. Local indigenous knowledge had 
recognized the danger associated with bioluminescent waters during algal blooms (Borowitzka, 
2018b). George Francis first documented toxic algae's effects in scientific literature in 1878, 
following livestock deaths in Australia due to contaminated water. Microalgae have also been known 
to affect water quality, causing unpleasant tastes and odors, a problem first noted in Boston's water 
in 1875 (Borowitzka, 2018b).  
 
The majority of microalgae species cannot be harvested in sufficient quantities from their natural 
habitats, instead, they require advanced cultivation and harvesting techniques to produce the 
necessary amounts for various applications (Borowitzka, 2018b). It was during the 1950s, amid 
concerns about the rising global population and a potential protein shortage, that algal biomass 
emerged as a promising new source of protein (Spolaore et al., 2006). This led to earnest consideration 
of large-scale microalgal cultivation for food, which coincided with studies into Chlorella's 
antibacterial characteristics through "chlorellin". Initial studies primarily in the United States, 
Germany, Japan, and England, and later in Israel, Sweden, and Italy, laid the groundwork for

3.2.2 Bioactive compounds in microalgae  
Bioactive compounds are naturally occurring chemicals which can influence health positively. 
Microalgae are a source of several environmentally sustainable bioactive compounds, including 
carotenoids, proteins, vitamins, lipids, fatty acids, and sterols, which are crucial for providing 
necessary nutrients to the human body and are beneficial for the health (D. V. Singh et al., 2021).

3.2.2.1 Lipids 
Lipids are biomolecules that are insoluble in water and fulfil several crucial functions in living 
organisms, including energy storage, acting as signalling molecules, and contributing to the structural 
framework of cell membranes (Morais et al., 2021). Microalgae are important sources of lipids and 
fatty acids. Species capable of accumulating significant amounts of lipids are classified as oleaginous. 
Within this category, numerous species can accumulate more than 20% of their dry cell weight in 
lipids under standard growth conditions, and in certain strains, lipid content can exceed 70% under 
specific culture conditions (Ahamefule et al., 2023). Increasing lipid accumulation by adjusting 
culture conditions, such as changes in irradiance, temperature, and nutrient availability, which 
presents a significant benefit for lipid production in microalgae (Bellou et al., 2014). However, while 
stressing microalgae cells can lead to increased lipid content, it typically results in lower overall cell 
productivity rates (Vasudevan & Briggs, 2008). 
 
Microalgal lipids can be grouped into two major categories which is polar and nonpolar (or neutral) 
lipids (Ahamefule et al., 2023). Polar lipid’s function both as essential parts of cellular membranes 
and as key intermediates in cell signalling pathways. Phospholipids (PL) and glycolipids (GL) are the 
primary polar lipids found in microalgae (Mimouni et al., 2018). Neutral or nonpolar lipids, which 
lack charged groups, cannot significantly integrate with the layered membranes, and are primarily 
used by organisms for energy storage. In microalgae, these include triacylglycerol (TAG), sterols and 
free fatty acids (FFAs) (Mimouni et al., 2018; Morais et al., 2021). 
 
Attentionally, in microalgae, common GLs such as monogalactosyldiacylglycerol (MGDG), 
digalactosyldiacylglycerol (DGDG), and sulfoquinovosyl diacylglycerol (SQDG) are predominantly 
found in chloroplast thylakoid membranes. MGDG and DGDG, making up 40%-50% and up to 20% 
of polar acyl lipids respectively, are rich in polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) (Mimouni et al., 
2018). These include long-chain polyunsaturated fatty acids (LCPUFAs) like 16- and 18-carbon 
omega-3 fatty acids and very long-chain forms such as eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA), which have 
more than three double bonds (Ahamefule et al., 2023). Storage lipids are primarily found in the form 
of TAGs and have a high saturated fatty acid (SAFA) content (Morais et al., 2021). TAGs are 
primarily produced by microalgae during periods of light exposure and stored within lipid bodies. 
These stores are then used to create polar lipids when light is not available, helping the cells adapt to 
and survive in challenging environmental conditions (Ahamefule et al., 2023). The SAFAs and 
==PAGE:21==

MUFAs include C14:0 (myristic acid, MA), C16:0 (palmitic acid, PA), C16:1 (palmitoleic acid, 
POA), C18:0 (stearic acid, SA), and C18:1 (oleic acid, OA).

Arachidonic acid (AA), γ-linolenic acid (GLA), eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA), and docosahexaenoic 
acid (DHA) are all recognized as LCPUFAs, characterized by the presence of multiple double bonds 
within their carbon chains. The location of the first double bond, counted from the methyl end of the 
molecules, determines their classification into the omega-3 or omega-6 (Janssen & Kiliaan, 2014; 
Mimouni et al., 2018). These LCPUFAs are crucial for maintaining the integrity of human tissue and 
have been associated with a variety of health benefits. DHA, in particular, is a major structural 
component of the brain and eye and plays an essential role in the development of these organs in 
infants. Additional health benefits linked to LCPUFAs include support for skin health, reduction of 
cardiac disorder symptoms, and potential relief from conditions such as arthritis, hypertension, heart 
disease, asthma, and migraines (Janssen & Kiliaan, 2014; Mimouni et al., 2018).  
 
The PUFAs are essential to human health since the body cannot synthesize them on its own, they 
must be obtained through dietary sources (Morais et al., 2021). Currently, one of the most common 
and popular sources of PUFA is fatty fish, such as salmon, mackerel (Ahamefule et al., 2023). 
Microalgae are noted for their high levels of PUFAs and are increasingly viewed as a viable 
alternative to fish oil for delivering these essential nutrients (Morais et al., 2021). Within the 
aquaculture sector, there is growing evidence that microalgae could serve as substitutes for traditional 
inputs like fish oil and fishmeal in the diets of various species, including Atlantic salmon, carps, and 
European sea bass (Ahamefule et al., 2023). Notably, certain strains of microalgae, such as

3.2.3 Nannochloropsis oceanica (N. oceanica) 
The microalgae N. oceanica is one of the five recognized species within the Nannochloropsis genus, 
which includes N. australis, N. granulata, N. limnetica, N. oceanica, and N. oculate. Belonging to 
the class Eustigmatophyceae, all species of Nannochloropsis are marine, except for N. limnetica, 
which inhabits freshwater environments (Borowitzka, 2018a). Nannochloropsis species are small, 
typically 2 to 5 μm in size, unicellular, spherical, or oval cells, and they are known for their fast 
growth rate (Fawley et al., 2015; Lupette, 2023). These species contain pigment profile that is 
particularly rich in chlorophyll a and various carotenoids, which play crucial roles in photosynthesis. 
Additionally, these species have become very popular for their high lipid content, which makes them 
an attractive option for sustainable biofuel production, and for their health-promoting long-chain 
polyunsaturated fatty acid content, especially EPA and DHA. (Fawley et al., 2015; Lupette, 2023).  
 
Numerous research studies have investigated the potential of Nannochloropsis as a feed source for 
various aquaculture species such as Pacific white shrimp, Atlantic salmon, kuruma shrimp, and 
European sea bass (Ye et al., 2024). Adissin et al., (2020) successfully replaced fish oil with algal 
powder in diets, which not only enhanced the growth of shrimp but also changed their fatty acid 
composition, significantly increasing their polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) content. 
==PAGE:23==
16 
 
Additionally, Nannochloropsis has been tested in human food applications; for instance, Lafarga et 
al., (2019) incorporated 2.0% Nannochloropsis into functional breads, assessing their 
physicochemical, organoleptic, and nutritional properties. These enriched breads demonstrated a 
substantially higher antioxidant capacity due to an increased level of bioaccessible polyphenols. 
Furthermore, inactivated cells of N. oceanica have been shown to be effective adsorbents for the 
removal of azo dyes from textile wastewater (Ye et al., 2024). 
 
N. oceanica has been sourced from diverse global locations, including the coasts of China, Japan, 
Taiwan, and western Norway (Fawley et al., 2015). The most favorable temperature range for N. 
oceanica growth spans from 25 to 29°C, while growth completely ceases at temperature above 31°C 
and below 9°C (Ye et al., 2024). Studies has shown that N. oceanica can accumulate lipids up to 60% 
of its dry weight, including valuable omega-3 LC-PUFA EPA, and has long been used as feeds in the 
aquacultural industry (Xu, 2022). Additionally, N. oceanica is an excellent protein source, accounting 
for 35−44% of its dry cell weight, and is considered for use as a single-cell protein in novel food and 
feed products (Xu, 2022). Under conditions of nutrient scarcity, N. oceanica primarily accumulates 
carbon as lipids instead of carbohydrates, resulting in an increase in lipid content from 10−28% to 
29−69%, while carbohydrate levels remain fairly constant, ranging from 6−29% to 7−28%. In 
contrast, the protein content decreases from 7−60% to 5−30%. This significant rise in lipid content is 
largely attributed to the buildup of TAG, which constitutes up to 90% of the total lipids. Although N. 
oceanica is capable of accumulating 3−42% EPA, the content of EPA typically declines as TAG 
accumulation increases, due to EPA's association with membrane lipids (Xu, 2022). Under light 
exposure at an intensity of 500 μmol/m2/s, N. oceanica showed a 45% decrease in total protein and a 
38% reduction in sugar levels, while the total lipid content significant increased by 44% and there 
were more than twofold increases in β-carotene, zeaxanthin, astaxanthin, and canthaxanthin (Ye et 
al., 2024). Astaxanthin stands out as one of the most commercial interesting compounds of 
microalgae. In the aquaculture industry, it is added to feeds to intensify the coloration of muscle tissue 
in salmon, trout, and shrimp (Esteves et al., 2021). The high lipid and LC-PUFA content promote 
strong interests in developing N. oceanica as a model system for industrial applications in biodiesel, 
nutraceutical, and aquaculture industries (Xu, 2022). 
 
Recent research by Ljubic et al., (2020, 2021) has highlighted the capability of N. oceanica to produce 
vitamin D3 when exposed to UVB radiation. This is particularly significant as it provides a natural, 
==PAGE:24==
17 
 
vegan source of vitamin D3, crucial for bone health and immune function. The ability to enhance 
vitamin D3 production through controlled UVB exposure makes N. oceanica an excellent candidate 
for developing functional foods that deliver health benefits beyond basic nutrition. Furthermore, 
Gatrell et al., (2015) conducted a study on the utility of oil derived from N. oceanica biomass as a 
dietary supplement for chickens, aiming to add value to a byproduct of biofuel production by 
increasing the commercial viability of microalgal biomass. Incorporating this microalga into the 
chicken feed did not affect the total fat content or levels of polyunsaturated fatty acids but did result 
in higher levels of total omega-3 fatty acids, which improved the omega-6/omega-3 fatty acid ratio 
and increased the content of EPA and DHA in the plasma, liver, breast, and thigh meat. Adding just 
2% of this microalga to the feed proved sufficient to improve the nutritional quality of the chicken 
meat.  
 
3.2.4 Cultivation of microalgae and the influence of environmental factors 
When cultivation microalgae the first step is to decide on the species of interest, as this helps to guide 
certain factors during production, and it defines the sustainability and economic viability of 
cultivation system. Microalgae can thrive under various metabolic settings, including autotrophic, 
heterotrophic, and mixotrophic conditions. In autotrophic cultivation, microalgae use light as their 
primary energy source for metabolic processes. Where in heterotrophic cultivation, they rely on the 
use of organic carbon sources for metabolism. And mixotrophic cultivation is a combination of the 
autotrophic and heterotrophic processes. Several species, such as Arthrospira platensis, Chlorella 
vulgaris, and Haematococcus pluvialis, are capable of growing under both heterotrophic and 
mixotrophic conditions (Khan et al., 2018; Rizwan et al., 2018). 
 
The next step is to determine the operation mode, where the growth of microalgae in batch or 
continuous system is quite common. The choice of cultivation system depends upon a number of 
factors including the nature of microalgae species, nutrients availability, mode of cultivation 
(autotrophic/heterotrophic), and more importantly the final use of biomass. During cultivation it is 
important to monitor the growth since the health of the microorganism is reflected by this. 
Microalgae growth is measured in terms of doubling time and biomass productivity (Rizwan et al., 
2018). Biomass productivity is somewhat proportional to culture density, typically measured as 
optical density (OD). Monitoring growth is therefore possible by measuring OD. The specific 
absorbance wavelength can vary due to differences in the coloration of various microalgae species, 
==PAGE:25==
18 
 
as the pigments present in the cells absorb light differently across the spectrum. Most of the 
microalgal absorbance range is between 600 and 700 nm. Additionally, growth rates can also be 
determined through total cell counts (Rizwan et al., 2018). 
 
The growth of microalgae cultures is characterized by five phases as illustrated in Figure 6: (1) the 
lag or induction phase, where cell metabolism adapts physiologically to new nutrients or 
environmental conditions, often with minimal increase in cell density, (2) the exponential phase, 
characterized by rapid growth that follows an exponential function, (3) the declining growth phase, 
where the rate of cell division slows due to physical or chemical limitations factors such as nutrients, 
light, or pH, (4) the stationary phase, where growth rate and limiting factors are balanced, leading to 
stabilized cell density, and (5) the death phase, during which unfavourable conditions like nutrient 
depletion, pH disturbance or contamination leads to a decrease in cell density (Pires, 2015). To 
shorten the adaptation period in the lag phase, the cultures can be inoculated with algae that are 
already in their exponential growth stage.   
 
 
Figure 6: Growth phases of microalgal cultures: (1) lag or induction phase, (2) exponential phase, (3) declining 
growth phase, (4) stationary phase, and (5) death phase, Adapted from (Pires, 2015). 
 
To achieving high microalgal production rates, it is essential maintaining microalgal cultures in the 
exponential growth phase. Continuous addition of fresh medium to the cultures allows for ongoing 
exponential growth, ensuring a continuous cultivation process (Pires, 2015). Microalgae are cost-
effective and sustainable for producing valuable bioactive compounds, using inexpensive resources 
like light, CO2, and water. This makes them a promising option for low-cost, high-value production

19 
 
(Khanra et al., 2018). The success of microalgae cultivation depends on the species and is influenced 
by factors such as nutrient availability, temperature, pH, and salinity, as well as mixing, harvest 
frequency, and dilution rate (Khan et al., 2018). If some of these conditions are suboptimal, it can 
impact the growth and composition of microalgae. It causes stressful situation which can influence 
valuable metabolite accumulation in microalgae. It has been noted that salinity stress and nutrient 
starvation are commonly employed to enhance the production of specific valuable metabolites for 
commercial purposes, particularly lipids used in biofuels and animal feeds (Gauthier et al., 2020). 
 
3.2.4.1 Nutrient media: composition and importance 
For the cultivation and growth of microalgae, a wide range of macronutrients and micronutrients, 
along with other environmental factors, are essential. Macronutrients includes nitrogen (N), 
phosphorus (P), potassium (K), magnesium (Mg), calcium (Ca), sulfur (S), and carbon (C), where 
micronutrients are such as iron (Fe), manganese (Mn), copper (Cu), zinc (Zn), molybdenum (Mo), 
boron (B), and cobalt (Co). Some microalgae species also requires salt and vitamins in the culture 
medium in order to grow (Juneja et al., 2013; Khan et al., 2018). Nitrogen and phosphorus are 
important macronutrients for the growth and metabolic functions of algae cells. Nitrogen is 
fundamental for creating proteins and nucleic acids. Phosphorus, meanwhile, is fundamental as it is 
part of ATP, the molecule that carries energy within cells. It is also a component of the structural 
framework of DNA and RNA and is necessary for the formation of phospholipids (Juneja et al., 2013). 
 
Carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen are necessary nutrients for the growth of algae. Hydrogen and oxygen 
are typically abundant in algal culture media, ensuring their ready availability for cellular growth and 
metabolic functions. Carbon, however, must actively be supplied as it is crucial for photosynthesis, 
which supports algal growth and reproduction. If the rate of carbon fixation decreases, algal growth 
also slows down. For photosynthesis, algae need an inorganic carbon source, which can be in the 
form of CO2, carbonate, or bicarbonate for autotrophic growth, or as acetate or glucose for 
heterotrophic growth (Juneja et al., 2013). Wastewater contains the nutrients necessary for microalgae 
cultivation, making it an effective medium for using organic effluents from food and agricultural 
industries to support and nourish microalgae growth (Khan et al., 2018).

3.2.4.2 Regulating pH, temperature, and salinity for optimal growth 
The optimal growth of microalgae is significantly influenced by environmental factors such as pH, 
temperature, and salinity. Each microalgae species requires specific pH levels, typically between 6 
and 8.76, which can vary with the growth medium used (Khan et al., 2018). Temperature directly 
affects fundamental biochemical processes like photosynthesis, with each species having its optimal 
range for growth (Khan et al., 2018). Additionally, salinity impacts osmotic balance, crucial for 
nutrient and water uptake (Hellebust, 1985). Effectively managing these factors is key to optimizing 
microalgae cultivation for high productivity. 
 
Temperature is a crucial factor affecting algal growth rate, cell size, biochemical composition, and 
nutrient requirements of algae. Algae can grow within a broad temperature range from 15 °C to 40 
°C, dependent on the specific algae strain, geographic region, and seasonal factors (Juneja et al., 
2013). Typically, the optimal temperature for most algae lies between 20 and 30 °C (Khan et al., 
2018). Cultivating microalgae outside their optimal temperature range results in significant biomass 
reduction. Within the optimal temperature window, algal growth increases exponentially, but any 
deviation (either higher or lower) can significantly slow or even cease algal growth and metabolic 
functions. Additionally, manipulating temperatures can act as a stressor, eading algae like Chlorella 
vulgaris to produce more valuable compounds, such as higher lipid levels at 25 °C compared to 30 
°C (Khan et al., 2018). 
 
Another very important factor in algal cultivation is pH since it affects the solubility and availability 
of CO2 and essential nutrients, significantly influencing algal metabolism (P. Singh et al., 2015). As 
algae absorb inorganic carbon, the pH of their cultures can increase substantially. While algal growth 
is typically highest around a neutral pH, the optimal pH is actually the initial pH condition to which 
the algae are best adapted. At higher pH levels, the availability of carbon from CO2 decreases, 
subsequently hindering algal growth (Juneja et al., 2013). 
 
Salinity, primarily relating to the concentration of sodium chloride unless noted otherwise, 
significantly impacts the biochemical composition of algal cells. When algae are exposed to salinity 
levels either below or above their natural (or adapted) environmental conditions, it can lead to changes 
in their growth rate and biochemical structure (Juneja et al., 2013). For instance, an increase in salinity 
has been noted to raise the lipid content in algae (Gauthier et al., 2020). Moreover, heightened salinity 
==PAGE:28==
21 
 
acts as a stressor in microalgae, triggering alterations in growth rates and leading to cell death 
(Zuppini et al., 2010). Optimal growth and lipid content in N. oceanica have been shown to occur 
when cultured with a salinity of 25‰ (Pan et al., 2016). 
 
3.2.4.3 Light 
Visible light (VL), part of the electromagnetic spectrum, covers wavelengths from 400 to 700 nm, 
shown in Figure 7. This range, known as the visible spectrum, interacts with the retina to produce the 
sensation of sight. Sources of visible light include natural sunlight or artificial lighting (S. K. Wang 
et al., 2014). Light plays a critical role in microalgae cultivation, primarily by providing the energy 
necessary for photosynthesis, the process by which microalgae convert light energy into chemical 
energy, using sunlight and water to fix CO2 to produce biomass and release oxygen as a byproduct. 
The intensity, duration, and quality (wavelength) of light can significantly influence the rate of 
photosynthesis, and consequently, the growth, biomass productivity, and biochemical composition of 
microalgae (Wan Mahari et al., 2024). Only the visible portion of the sunlight spectrum is 
photosynthetically active in plants and algae. 
 
 
Figure 7: The range of visible light in electromagnetic spectrum. 
 
Photosynthesis in microalgae takes place within the thylakoids, in the photosynthetic membranes 
found inside chloroplasts. This process begins with the absorption of light by pigments located on 
light-harvesting antenna complexes (Wan Mahari et al., 2024). These pigments, each absorbing 
specific wavelengths of light, are characterized by distinct colors: chlorophylls are green, carotenoids 
range from yellow to orange, and phycobilins appear red and blue. Each pigment plays a unique role 
in photosynthesis, with chlorophylls being essential for oxygen-producing photosynthesis, while 
==PAGE:29==
22 
 
carotenoids and phycobilins serve as important accessory pigments. Microalgal species vary in their 
response to light wavelengths due to differences in these pigments. The 600–700 nm range is typically 
most effective for the growth of many algal species (S. K. Wang et al., 2014). Additionally, under 
intense light conditions, microalgae produce protective pigments, such as those in the xanthophyll 
cycle found in green algae, which help dissipate excessive light energy as heat (Torzillo et al., 2021). 
 
The pigments in microalgal cells capture photons to produce adenosine triphosphate (ATP) and the 
electron carrier, nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH). These molecules are critical 
outputs of the light reactions, the initial phase of photosynthesis. Following this phase, the dark 
reactions occur, utilizing ATP and NADPH to fix CO2 into sugars through the Calvin cycle, essential 
for cell growth and energy storage (Wan Mahari et al., 2024).  
 
The absorption of light energy by microalgae is determined by the photon flux density that reaches 
the culture surface. Only a portion of this photon flux is absorbed by the cells, and the amount of 
absorption is influenced by several factors such as the cell density, cells optical properties, the length 
of the optical path in the reactor, and the rate of culture mixing, when other environmental conditions 
like temperature, nutrients, and pH are optimal (Richmond, 2013). If these conditions are not optimal, 
factors such as suboptimal temperature, pH, and nutrient levels can significantly impair light 
utilization efficiency. Unabsorbed photons are mostly converted to heat. High photon flux densities 
in cultures with dense cell populations can lead to maximal photon absorption, increasing cell density 
until all available light is used for photosynthesis. Beyond this point, growth may stabilize or decline 
unless limited by low light per cell or other growth-inhibiting factors (Richmond, 2013). Effective 
mixing is essential in algae cultivation to prevent sedimentation and to circulate algae between light-
exposed and dark areas of the pond or reactor. Without active mixing, surface algae might over-
absorb light and become photoinhibited, while those in deeper layers could experience light 
deficiency. Additionally, mixing helps keep the cell concentration in the medium uniform (Yousuf, 
2019). 
 
3.2.4.4 UVB light: enhancing vitamin D3 production in microalgae 
UVB light, is a component of the ultraviolet spectrum, which is grouped with visible and infrared 
radiation under the broader term of optical radiation. Ultraviolet radiation (UVR) covers wavelengths 
from 100 to 400 nm, as shown in Figure 8, and accounts for approximately 5% of the sunlight that 
==PAGE:30==
23 
 
reaches Earth's surface (Diffey, 2002). This segment of the spectrum is further differentiated into 
three categories, which is UVA (320 – 400 nm), UVB (290 – 320 nm), and UVC (200 – 290 nm). 
The characteristics and intensity of UV radiation at the Earth's surface are influenced by several 
environmental factors, such as the sun’s elevation, atmospheric ozone levels, and the presence of 
clouds, which absorb and scatter this radiation. UVC is entirely absorbed by the ozone layer in the 
Earth's atmosphere, preventing it from reaching the surface, while both UVB and UVA penetrate the 
atmosphere to some extent. UVB rays can be blocked by materials like glass and plastics (de la Guía-
Galipienso et al., 2021). UVB light is more effective in causing biological damage compared to UVA, 
making it a critical focus in studies on the impact of UV light on microalgae and plants. Research 
involving spinach thylakoids, whole cyanobacterial cells, and whole leaves has demonstrated that 
photodamage is primarily influenced by the total dosage of light rather than the intensity of the light 
at which it is delivered. This insight underscores the importance of considering both the quantity and 
quality of light in studies related to photobiological effects (Sicora et al., 2003).  
 
 
Figure 8: Illustration of the electromagnetic spectrum, highlighting UVC (200-290 nm), UVB (290-320 nm), 
and UVA (320-400 nm). 
UVB radiation effects a wide range of physiological processes in cyanobacteria and microalgae, 
including growth, survival, pigmentation, orientation, development, metabolism, photosynthesis, and 
the fixation and assimilation of nitrogen. The impacts of UVB can be attributed to its direct effects 
on cellular components such as membrane proteins, photosystem II, DNA, enzymes, pigments, and 
growth regulators. Additionally, UVB contributes to indirect effects through the generation of 
reactive oxygen species like superoxide (O2−), hydroxyl radicals (OH−), and hydrogen peroxide 
(H2O2) (Xue et al., 2005). In research conducted by Ljubic et al., (2020), it was found that UVB 
radiation effectively stimulates the production of vitamin D3 in microalgae, particularly demonstrated 
==PAGE:31==
24 
 
in N. oceanica, where increased UVB exposure directly correlated with higher levels of vitamin D3 
synthesis. Similarly, Ljubic et al., (2021) found that UVB light also enhanced the production of 
antioxidants such as α-tocopherol and β-carotene in Dunaliella salina, further underlining the 
potential of UVB to boost essential bioactive compounds in microalgae. However, in the finding by 
Kumar et al., (2018) showed that while UVB promotes beneficial compound production, it 
simultaneously reduces growth rates and changes in lipid profiles, particularly reductions in PUFAs, 
potentially compromising microalgal health, nutritional quality, and productivity. This underscores 
the need for optimized UVB exposure to maximize vitamin D3 production while minimizing adverse 
growth effects in microalgae cultivation.

4.2 Microalgae strain and general cultivation

Nutribloom Plus was filtered through a 0.2 µm syringe filter and added after autoclaving. For solid 
medium and preculture cultivation the ASW was supplemented with 2 mL/L of Nutribloom Plus, and

Cultivation on Solid Medium: To maintain N. oceanica, a few colonies were transferred from an 
agar plate to new agar plates using a sterile 10 µL incubation loop. A control plate was included each 
time from the same agar plate cultivation batch to ensure contamination-free conditions. The plates 
were then placed in a growth cabinet (Aralab, Portugal) set at a controlled temperature of 23°C and a 
light intensity of 150 µmol/m2/s using white LED lights. The same growth cabinet was consistently 
used throughout the cultivation experiment, and thetemperature was maintained at 23°C throughout. 
 
Cultivation of Preculture: To cultivate N. oceanica for preculture in liquid medium, a few colonies

required to be a minimum of 0.2 and preferably not exceed 4.0 to maintain an optimal environment 
for cell growth. The preculture was cultivated until it reached an OD750 of 2-3. Afterward, the contents 
of all cell culture bottles were mixed to measure a combined OD750, and the preculture was then 
upscaled.

the rate was increased to 220 mL/min. The CO2 flow was monitored using a handheld Flow Tracker 
2000 (0-500 mL/M Gas Chromatography Flow Meter, Agilent). In the 1 L bottles, the tube reached 
the bottom allowing for stirring. When scaling up in 5 L bottles, the bottle was placed on a magnetic 
stirrer with a magnet inside, as the tube did not extend to the bottom of the bottle, and bubbling with 
CO2-enriched air alone did not provide sufficient stirring for the larger volume.

4.3 UVB-lamp and UVB-lightning treatment 
UVB-lamp: The UVB lamp used for the experiment featured two identical 120 cm UVB fluorescent 
light tubes (UVB-313 40W, Q-Lab Corporation, Hungary). These tubes were placed in a fixture and 
covered by clear plexiglass. The lamp’s irradiance has been determined to be 1.82 W/m² at a 40 cm 
distance, measured at the lamp’s midpoint (see Appendix A for the UVB lamp’s wavelength radiation 
measurement graph). Further details on the characterization process are available in the study by 
Švarc et al. 2022. Due to differences in the effectiveness of different wavelengths in converting 7-
DHC into vitamin D3, a CIE factor was used to accurately determine the vitamin D3 irradiance. This 
resulted in the sum of 0.854 W/m2 within the 275 nm to 320 nm wavelength range. Then, 0.854 W/m2 
is multiplied by the exposure time (s) of the sample to UVB light, determining the amount of vitamin 
D3 producing radiation received by the sample at 40 cm distance. The process of determining the CIE 
factors is detailed in the article Bouillon et al. 2006, These factors were measured while using 
plexiglass, as it absorbs some of the radiation. 
 
Sample Preparation: four identical 500 mL beakers were used for samples preparation. In two of 
these, 300 mL of N. oceanica culture, was measured out. A magnet was placed in each beaker before 
positioning them under the UVB lamp. The timer was started, and the location of each sample on the

magnetic stirrer plate was noted. The completed samples were then transferred to 500 mL plastic 
centrifuge bottles and stored in the refrigerator until biomass harvesting, either on the same day or

UVB light treatment: The goal in our experiment was to position the UVB lamp as close to the 
sample as possible, since the closeness of the UVB correlates with increased radiation exposure to

adjusted irradiance was then multiplied by the exposure time in seconds to calculate the amount of 
vitamin D3 synthesized due to the energy absorbed by the sample at the distance of 10 cm  (Table 1

4.4 Biomass harvesting 
For biomass harvesting, an in-house protocol was used. Once the bottle weights were adjusted, the 
culture was centrifuged at 7000 G for 15 minutes at 5°C. Following centrifugation, and the 
supernatant was discarded. The pellet was then vortexed until fully dissolved in 150 mL of 0.5 M 
ammonium formate (0.5 times the volume of the centrifuged culture), to wash the salt off the pellet. 
Afterwards, the solution was centrifuged again at 7000 G for 15 minutes at 5 °C. The supernatant was 
discarded, and the pellet was fully dissolved in 10 mL of DI water and transferred to 50 mL centrifuge 
tubes. Next, the samples were placed in a –80 °C freezer overnight, followed by lyophilization for 48 
hours at 0.5 mbar and room temperature using a Scanvac Coolsafe –55 freeze dryer (Labogene, DK).

4.6 Vitamin D analysis  
The analysis method for vitamin D3 is well-documented and described in more detail in the 
publications by (Gill et al., 2019; Ložnjak Švarc et al., 2021). 
 
Chemicals and reference materials: Internal standard 13C-labeled vitamin D3, 4-phenyl-1,2,4-
triazoline-3,5-dione (PTAD) were from Sigma-Alrich (Steinheim, Germany), sodium ascorbate, 
ethanol 96 %, and potassium hydroxide were from Merck (Darmstadt, Germany). Anhydrous 
acetonitrile was from VWR Chemicals (Leuven, Belgium), Ultra-pure Milli-Q were prepared at DTU 
Food using Milli-Q Advantage A10 water deionizing system (18.2 MΩ, Millipore, Bradford, USA).  
 
The Vitamin D3 analysis method spans over two days. In 50 mL centrifuge tube, 0.04-0.1 g of 
microalgae was weighed with an accuracy of four decimal places. To the same tube, 0.2 g of sodium 
ascorbate, 100 µL of 13C-labelled vitamin D3 internal standard (80 ng/mL), 9 mL of ethanol, and 3 
mL of 60% KOH was added. Atmospheric air was replaced with nitrogen (standard quality), and the 
samples was saponified for 18 hours at 60 °C with rotation (in a VWR DRY-Line, Reax 2 Heidolph). 
For extraction, 13 mL of Milli-Q water and 10 mL of 20% ethyl acetate in n-heptane was used. The 
organic phase was transferred to a clean 50 mL centrifuge tube, the extraction was repeated twice 
more, each time with 10 mL of 20% ethyl acetate in n-heptane. The combined organic phases were 
washed with 20 mL of Milli-Q water by inverting the tube 10 times. The organic phase was transferred 
to new 50 mL centrifuge tubes and evaporated under vacuum centrifuge for 1 hr. and 30 min. The 
samples were redissolved in 1 mL of acetonitrile with 0.1% formic acid and transferred to 4 mL 
brown vials. A hybrid solid-phase extraction is then performed, initially conditioning the hybrid SPE 
column, adding 1 mL of acetonitrile with 0.1% formic acid (allowing it to drip without vacuum), then 
transferring 325 µL of the extract onto the activated SPE column (the extract elutes without vacuum). 
Then, elute with 0.5 mL of acetonitrile with 0.1% formic acid without vacuum and dry the columns 
with vacuum.  
 
==PAGE:40==
33 
 
The final stage of sample preparation was derivatization. The combined eluates were evaporated 
under nitrogen at 35 °C. Then, 250 µL of 0.75 mg/mL 4-phenyl-1,2,4-triazoline-3,5-dione (PTAD) 
in acetonitrile was added to the dried samples, and they were left to stand for 5 minutes in the dark. 
This derivatization step enhances the sensitivity and selectivity of the method, allowing for the 
accurate measurement of low levels of Vitamin D3. Afterwards, 75 µL of Milli-Q water was added to 
end the derivatization reaction. The samples were transferred to 1.5 mL centrifuge tubes and 
centrifuged at 10,000 g for 10 minutes. The supernatant was transferred to HPLC vials with inserts. 
The prepared samples was quantified using LC-MS/MS, which consisted of an Agilent 1200 series 
HPLC coupled to an Agilent 6470 series triple quadrupole mass spectrometer (Santa Clara, CA), with 
separation performed used an Ascentis Express C18 column (2.1 mm x 10 cm, 2.7 μm) and a guard 
column, Ascentis Express C18 (2.1 mm x 5 mm, 2.7 μm) (Supelco Analytical, Bellefonte, PA). 
Mobile phase A consisted of 5% methanol in MQ-water and 2.5 mM ammonium formate, and mobile 
phase B consisted of methanol and 2.5mM ammonium formate. 
 
4.7 Fatty acid analysis (GC-FID) 
The method protocol used to analyze the fatty acid content in microalgae is based on the article by 
Safafar et al. titled “Two-Step Direct Transesterification as a Rapid Method for the Analysis of Fatty 
Acids in Microalgae Biomas” (Safafar et al., 2019). 
 
Chemicals and Reference Materials: The composition of the used internal standard C23:0 FAME 
was purchased from Sigma (St. Louise, USA) and Fluka (Deisenhofen, Germany). Heptane, 
methanol, toluene, BHT (butylated hydroxytoluene), and boron trifluoride (BF3) were purchased from 
Sigma. HPLC grade water was prepared at DTU Food using Milli-Q Advantage A10 water deionizing 
system from Millipore Corporation (Billerica, MA, USA).  
 
The analysis was conducted in duplicate. In glass tubes with screw caps, 40-50 mg of microalgae was 
weighed, to the same tubes, 1 mL of 1.0 M NaOH in methanol, 1 mL of toluene, and 100 μL of the 
internal standard solution (2% (w/v) C23:0 in n-heptane) were added. The samples were then treated 
with ultrasound for 10 minutes then boiled in a water bath at 100 °C for 2 minutes. After cooling in 
cold water, 2 mL of boron trifluoride (BF3) was added, and the samples were boiled again in water 
bath at 100 °C for 2 minutes followed by cooling with cold water. Then, 2 mL of saturated salt 
solution and 1 mL of n-heptane containing 0.01% BHT were added. The tubes were mixed by 
==PAGE:41==
34 
 
inverting them a few times, after which approximately 1 mL of the upper phase (the heptane phase) 
was transferred to brown GC vials. The samples were analysed using a gas chromatograph (HP-7890 
A, Agilent Technologies, CA, USA). The fatty acid methyl esters (FAMEs) were separated applying 
a GC column DB WAX 127-7012 (10 m x 100 µm x 0.1 µm) from Agilent Technologies (CA, USA). 
The injection volume was 0.2 µL in split mode 1:50, with the temperature program set as follows: 
start at 160 °C, then increase to 200 °C (10.6 °C/min), hold at 200 °C for 0.3 min, rise to 220 °C (10.6 
°C/min), hold at 220 °C for 1 min, then to 240 °C (10.6 °C/min), and finally hold at 240 °C for 3.8 
min.

6.1 Experiment 1: vitamin D3 synthesis in N. onceanica with pre-harvest UVB 
exposure  
During this experiment 2x1 L N. ocenaica culture was cultivated where the goal was to cultivate both 
bottles to an OD750 of 3.0, unfortunately, growth in bottle B ceased (see Figure 13). As a result, bottle 
A was cultivated to an OD750 of approximately 6 and then diluted to an OD750 of 3.0 for the UVB light 
treatment. Bottle A emitted a fresh, clean scent reminiscent of seaweed and algae. 
==PAGE:43==
36 
 
 
Figure 13: Comparison of N. oceanica culture bottle A (left) and B (right). While culture bottle A demonstrates 
normal growth, bottle B exhibits stunted growth, left image, and has stopped growth for some days, image to 
the right. 
 
Usually, the changes in coloration of microalgal cultures from green to yellow-green and eventually 
to milky white can often be due to several factors that are primarily linked to environmental stress 
that impacting cellular health. The discoloration often results from certain form of nutrient 
deficiencies, such as a lack of nitrogen, sulfur, or phosphorus, can cause microalgae to undergo 
chlorosis, a process where the cells become paler due to a reduction in chlorophyll content. Different 
nutrient limitations can lead to different responses in terms of pigment reduction and cellular growth 
(Collier’ & Grossman’, 1992). While light is fundamental for microalgal photosynthesis, excessive 
exposure can negatively affect microalgae, causing the algae to turn yellow as it generates reactive 
oxygen species (ROS) that harm cellular structures (Grudzinski et al., 2016). Increased levels of ROS 
in microalgae can even lead to cell death (Gauthier et al., 2020). Temperature changes can also 
influence the microalgae’s color by affecting their metabolism, including nutrient uptake and pigment 
synthesis (Sakamoto & Bryant, 1997). The white and milky appearance can indicate cell lysis, where 
the microalgae cells burst and release their contents, causing the culture to become cloudy. if bacteria 
or other microorganisms contaminate the culture, they can outcompete the algae for nutrients, leading 
to microalgae cell death and a white or cloudy appearance as the bacterial population grows (Wells 
et al., 1996). In this experiment bottles A and B were processed identically, therefore the precise 
reason for the cessation of growth in bottle B remains unclear.  
 
==PAGE:44==
37 
 
As shown in Figure 14, vitamin D3 can be produced by exposing N. oceanica culture to a short and 
intense UVB treatment just before harvesting. The control group was not exposed to UVB light, 
therefore the biomass level of vitamin D3 was at the level of quantification. Samples exposed for 
UVB for 15 minutes and 30 minutes exhibited vitamin D3 contents of 135.7±2.7 ng/gDM and 
204.7±5.0 ng/gDM, respectively, with a p-value < 0.05 indicating statistically significant, all groups 
were statistically significant. The results also showed that higher doses of UVB lead to greater vitamin 
D3 production in N. oceanica culture. Compared to the vitamin D3 content in Growing diluted cultures 
by Ljubic et al., 2021, the vitamin D3 content in our experiment is a little lower. But when considering 
the amount of UVB energy, our 30-minute samples received only 4.9 kJ/m2, which is substantially 
lower than the 15 kJ/m2/day over 3 days, as indicated in previous studies. Despite this lower exposure, 
a high level of vitamin D3 production was still achieved in our experiments.

group received no UVB exposure. The x-axis indicates the exposure time along with the corresponding UVB 
energy received (in kJ/m²) within the parentheses. Error bars represent standard deviation(n=2). Letters above

To investigate vitamin D3 production in healthy microalgal cultures, Ljubic et al., 2021, examined 
the production of vitamin D3 in N. oceanica cultures at three different stages by exposing them to 
UVB light (15 kJ/m2/day for 3 days): 1) Growing diluted cultures (during growth), 2) Harvested 
biomass (wet paste), and 3) Freeze-dried biomass (dry biomass). The results showed that vitamin D3 
==PAGE:45==
38 
 
production in the growing diluted cultures (285 ± 5 ng/g DM) was significantly higher compared to 
the harvested biomass (13 ± 0.4 ng/g DM) and freeze-dried biomass (2 ± 0.2 ng/g DM). One reason 
for this was mentioned to be that the production of 7-dehydrocholesterol increases under UVB 
exposure of the growing diluted cultures. In another study by Ljubic et al., 2020, it was found that 
exposing N. oceanica cultures to UVB light over several days during growth also negatively impacts 
growth rate, especially at higher doses of UVB. This demonstrated that UVB exposure can slow 
growth, particularly when the duration and intensity of exposure are increased. Specifically, the study 
showed that higher UVB doses led to significantly lower biomass accumulation compared to lower 
doses and control treatments. Our experiment is a combination of this two, by exposing N. oceanica 
cultures to UVB light just before harvesting, this allows the culture to grow without affecting the 
biomass accumulation. Furthermore, when the culture is exposed to UVB light, it undergoes a short 
and intense treatment, as shown in Table 1. This treatment does not extend over several days. 
 
The process of Vitamin D3 synthesis in microalgae, specifically N. oceanica, involves the conversion 
of 7-dehydrocholesterol to Vitamin D3 under the influence of UVB radiation. This conversion is 
similar to the synthesis of Vitamin D3 in human skin when exposed to sunlight. It is the presence of 
cholesterol in microalgae indicates their capacity for Vitamin D3 synthesis since cholesterol is a 
precursor to 7-dehydrocholesterol and, subsequently, Vitamin D3. The ability to synthesize 7-
dehydrocholesterol and consequently vitamin D3 varies among different microalgal species, largely 
due to genetic and metabolic differences (Ljubic et al., 2020, 2021). Furthermore, UVB exposure not 
only stimulates Vitamin D3 synthesis but can also influence other bioactive compounds in the 
microalgae. For instance, UVB exposure increased the accumulation of α-tocopherol and β-carotene 
in some microalgal species. Factors like temperature, light, and growth stage can impact the synthesis 
of sterols including 7-dehydrocholesterol. These environmental stresses can lead to variations in the 
sterol composition of the microalgae, thus affecting their capability to produce vitamin D3 (Ljubic et 
al., 2020, 2021).

cultivated into 4x1 L blue-cap bottles, where the goal was to grow each bottle to an OD750 of 6.0.

favorable growth conditions, where cells can produce significant amounts of valuable compounds 
like vitamin D3 and EPA under non-stressful conditions (Bongiovani et al., 2014; B. Wang & Jia, 
2020). The aggregation or clumping of cells is often a response to environmental stressors such as 
changes in salinity, nutrient availability, or light intensity. These clumps can serve as a protective 
mechanism, potentially reducing the surface area exposed to harmful conditions. Such responses are

part of the alga's adaptation to ensure survival under adverse conditions (Bible et al., 2015; B. Wang 
& Jia, 2020). Despite no changes in the growth medium, light intensity, temperature, or other 
environmental conditions, the cells exhibited signs of stress. This suggests the need for further 
investigation into other factors that could be inhibiting cell viability. 
 
As known, UVB radiation triggers the production of vitamin D3 which is demonstrated in Figure 16.

OD750 3.0 (green) and OD750 6.0 (blue), across varying durations and intensities of UVB radiation 
exposure. Both curves show an increase in Vitamin D3 content as the duration and intensity of UVB 
exposure increase. The culture with a lower density OD750 3.0 consistently demonstrates a higher 
Vitamin D3 content compared to the culture with a higher density OD750 6.0. This observation 
suggests that UVB radiation penetrates more effectively in less dense cultures, enabling a more 
efficient synthesis of Vitamin D3. The enhanced penetration in the lower density cultures likely allows 
the UVB light to contact more cells throughout the medium. In contrast, in the denser OD 6.0 culture, 
the UVB light is rapidly absorbed and possibly blocked at the surface, limiting its reach deeper into 
the culture. Despite having a higher cell concentration at the top, the denser culture does not promote 
as effective contact between the UVB light and the cells below the surface, ultimately affecting the 
overall Vitamin D3 production. For maximizing Vitamin D3 output, maintaining cultures at lower 
optical densities might be beneficial, especially under controlled UVB exposure. This could influence 
protocols for cultivating N. oceanica when Vitamin D3 enrichment is a goal.

6.3 Experiment 3: vitamin D3 production in stressed N. onceanica culture 
In this experiment 4 L N. oceanica culture in a 5 L bluecap bottle was cultivated. Initially, the culture 
thrived, increasing OD750 from about 0.1 to 5.3 over the first week. However, growth slowed, reaching 
only 5.6 by day 10, and decreasing to 5.4 by day 11. In an attempt to address this issue, the light 
intensity was increased from 300 to 400 µmol/m²/s on day 11, hypothesizing the dense cell growth 
might have blocked light penetration, especially given the larger volume compared to Experiments 1 
and 2. Despite this, OD750 dropped to 3.9 by day 12, therefore the cultivation was halted. The culture 
was transferred to four 1 L centrifuge bottles and frozen over the weekend to prevent any further drop 
in OD750 and cell death. Unlike the fresh sea aroma of previous experiments, this batch had a slightly 
sour and off smell, though its color remained a consistent dark green. Upon thawing on Monday at 
room temperature, the combined OD750 was measured at 3.0. Despite these challenges, UVB 
treatment and vitamin D3 analysis continued to determine the impact of these stressed cells 
suboptimal conditions on the culture’s vitamin D3 production. Figure 17, illustrate the condition of 
the stressed culture.

7 Conclusion  
The findings of this project provide valuable insights into the relationship between UVB exposure 
time, optical density, and Vitamin D3 production in N. oceanica cultures. The results showed that 
short, high dose UVB exposure effectively produce vitamin D3 without adversely affecting the 
essential fatty acid contents of eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and Palmitoleic acid (POA). There is a 
clear correlation between the duration of UVB exposure and the production of vitamin D3 which is 
shown in all 3 experiments. Increased exposure generally lead to higher synthesis of vitamin D3. 
Furthermore, cultures in optimal health produced significantly higher amounts of vitamin D3, culture 
of OD750 3.0 produced 204.7 ng vitD3/g DM vitamin D3 after 30 min UVB exposure, compared to 
those in suboptimal conditions, where culture of OD750 3.0 only produced 28.8 ng vitD3/g DM vitamin 
D3 after 30 min UVB exposure. The highest amount of vitamin D3 measured was for cultures with 
OD750 of 3.0, which reached 259.7 ng/gDM after 120 minutes of UVB exposure. Stressed or 
suboptimal culture conditions showed physical observations such as color changes, cell clumping, 
and off odor. Additionally, cultures with a lower density (OD750 of 3.0 versus 6.0) demonstrated 
higher vitamin D3 production for same exposure durations, possibly due to better light penetration. 
The study also identifies a saturation point in vitamin D3 production, beyond a certain level of UVB 
exposure, no further benefits are derived, indicating the existence of an upper limit to the efficacy of 
UVB-induced vitamin D3 synthesis in N. oceanica. This threshold underscores the importance of 
calibrated UVB exposure in maximizing vitamin D3 production while avoiding unnecessary 
prolongation that offers no additional value.

8 Future perspectives 
 
Future studies should aim to optimize UVB light exposure protocols to maximize vitamin D3 
production, this includes identifying the optimal duration and intensity of UVB exposure that 
effectively balances vitamin D3 synthesis with minimal adverse effects on algal growth and lipid 
profiles. Additionally, integrating UVB-treated N. oceanica into human food systems as a natural 
source of vitamin D3, particularly for vegan diets, could significantly impact health. This will require 
an assessment of the bioavailability and bioaccessibility of the synthesized vitamin D3 when 
incorporated into various food. Translating these laboratory findings to commercial-scale operations 
is a crucial next step. Conducting pilot-scale studies to assess the feasibility of continuous, large-scale 
production of vitamin D3-enriched N. oceanica can pave the way for its application in nutraceuticals 
and functional foods, potentially developing dietary supplementation and health promotion strategies.
