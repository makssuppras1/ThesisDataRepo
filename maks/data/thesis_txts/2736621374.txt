Abstract
Leading edge erosion is a contemporary problem affecting the working efficiency of turbines. It de-
creases energy yield, and with increasing energy demands, reliable and efficient wind turbines are es-
sential. The issue is combated using leading edge protection (LEP) systems that shield the blade against
erosion. These systems range from paint coatings to shells and tapes. This thesis investigates the latter
solution, specifically, a thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU). The material is rubber-like and exhibits
hyperelastic properties.
The thesis aims to investigate the fracture responses using the material’s energy potential in a specimen
with a pre-defined crack fixed between clamped grips subject to tensile loading. A plane stress condition
is emphasised due to the thinness of the coating systems. The testing configuration is modelled using the
commercial finite software, Abaqus. Furthermore, an analytical model is also created. The geometry
of the experimental fracture mechanics test specimen was designed by varying the crack length and
specimen width to analyse the J-integral which is valid for the nonlinear elastic stress-strain law. The
study of stresses ahead of the crack tip and the visualisation of contour plots are employed for exploring
the stress and strain fields throughout the material in the proper mesh configuration tested by mesh
sensitivity study.
The results demonstrate that exploiting the built-in Python interpreter in Abaqus allows for rapid
model testing when manipulating the geometry ratio between crack length, specimen width, and height.
For a hyperelastic material, it is found that a significant crack to specimen dimension ratio is required
for the model to yield results equal to the corresponding analytical solutions. It is shown that a specimen
width to height ratio of 40, and a crack to height ratio of 5 are required to reach a strain of 30 % to align
with the analytical approach. It is found that the finite element model becomes unstable when using
a third-order Ogden formulation upon reaching the 30 % strain. Moreover, the stress field reveals
a distinct linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) singularity in the linear elastic case and blunting
and nonlinear effects in the hyperelastic case. In sum, the model is validated with the corresponding
analytical solutions to capture fracture behaviours in both linear elastic and hyperelastic cases.
Keywords: Finite element analysis, Fracture mechanics, Rubber-like materials, Leading edge protec-
tion, J-integral, Linear elastic material, Hyperelastic material

Introduction
With increasing awareness of environmental conservation and energy independence, wind energy plays
a crucial role and takes over one-third of the total renewable energy yield in the EU. The energy targets
proposed by the European Commission aim to achieve over 300 GW of annual accumulative wind
energy capacity in the EU by 2030 and to enhance reliance on internal energy sources. [1]
However, several challenges exist in wind turbine development, such as managing steady aerodynamic
forces and ensuring component reliability and longevity. One such component is the protective system
attached to the leading edge of modern wind turbine blades. These systems range from paint coatings
to shells and tapes. The primary purpose of these coating systems is to protect the blade against erosion
from harsh rain, dust, hail, and other abrasive particles. Surface erosion on the leading edge significantly
affects the energy performance and longevity of wind turbine blades [2]. An example of these surfaces
becoming more severely damaged as time pasts, ranging from 1 to 10 plus years of service, is depicted in
figure 1.1. A reliable leading edge protection (LEP) system that can sustain the duration of a turbine’s
service life, approximately 25 years, is therefore, a vital component.
Figure 1.1: Leading edge after different years in service [3]
The current state of the art in rain erosion testing is a whirling arm test machine. It is ”basically” a
horizontal rotor with three arms, including a shower head with numerous needle-shaped nozzles as
exhibited in figure 1.2a. Figure 1.2b shows the schematic of a LEP on a testing rotor blade. The
test method is designed to create a velocity vs. number of impact curves analogous to SN curves in
”standard” fatigue analysis. However, this method reveals nothing about the stress history or fracture
properties during testing.
This thesis takes a step back and examines the fracture mechanic properties using a two-dimensional
(2D) specimen to evaluate the reliability of protective coatings based on the experimental findings by
Elmukashfi [4]. A 2D specimen with clamped grips and a pre-defined crack in estimating strain energy
density for the fracture mechanics is presented in the literature [4][5][6][7]. Rubber-like material
coatings are analysed as they are widely utilised as LEPs with an emphasis on a plane stress condition
as the thinness of coating systems. This thesis highlights designing a simulation model with analytical
validation to evaluate the fracture behaviours under different material conditions and varied geometries.

1.1
Statement of work
This thesis investigates how a thermoplastic polyurethane also known as TPU behaves under loading.
The purpose of the work is to design geometries of the specimen for experiments and enhance the
understanding of the material’s resistance to fracture energy. Two conditions are addressed in this the-
sis: the linear elastic condition exhibits a linear relation between stress and strain, and the hyperelastic
properties allow for a nonlinear relation between stress and strain.
Linear elastic case:
1. Build a 2D specimen with clamped grips and a pre-defined crack in linear elastic material using
Abaqus
-The aim is to get familiar with Abaqus and build in Python scripting syntax.
2. Model linear elastic fracture mechanics
-The aim is to get familiar with the concept of stress intensity factor and the J-integral, using
analytical and finite element methods.
3. Measure the mesh convergence for stresses
-The aim is to determine the appropriate mesh size and number of elements to strike a balance
between the correct results and the computation efficiency.
4. Understand the effect of specimen geometry on J-integral
-The aim is to demonstrate the optimal specimen design for comparing to a specific analytical
solution
5. Evaluate the stresses as a function of position ahead of the crack tip
-The aim is to explore stresses near the crack tip and the K-dominated zone in linear elastic
fracture mechanics (LEFM)
6. Analyse the displacement, stresses and strains based contour plots
-The aim is to visualise the stresses and strains throughout the specimen
Hyperelastic case:
1. Describe the concept of hyperelasticity
-The aim is to shed light on how a material model is created from an experimental dog bone
tension test.
2. Implement and model hyperelastic fracture mechanics
-The aim is to implement the material model in the existing finite-element framework and create
an equivalent analytical solution.

Fundamental theory background
This chapter elaborates on the fundamental theory of the fracture mechanics and hyperelastic material
models. The purpose is to provide essential background knowledge relevant to the study.
2.1
Fracture mechanics
Fracture mechanics studies how materials fail under applied stress. For the linear elastic case, Griffith’s
theory evaluates how much energy is required for crack propagation and the stress intensity factor
quantifies the local stress near a crack tip for evaluating fracture behaviours. For nonlinear behaviours,
the energy-based parameter, J-integral are introduced since it is valid for nonlinear stress-strain relations
in an elastic region. These concepts provide an understanding of fracture mechanics and help assess
failure prediction and structural design.
2.1.1
Energy balance theory
Griffith’s theory states that crack propagation in a linear elastic material occurs when the strain energy
stored in the material caused by displacement becomes larger or equal to the energy required for creating
new free surfaces [8]. The energy released, resulting from crack propagation is called strain energy
release rate (G). Rice generalised this to nonlinear stress-strain laws using the J-integral as illustrated
in figure 2.1 [9]. The difference of the stored elastic energy (dU) from length ”a” to length ”a+da” to
strike a balance for the energy required for cracks to create the free surfaces at the displacement (u0).
Figure 2.1: Rice’s energy balance theory [10]
2.1.2
The tearing energy with a modified model theory
The tearing energy, the concept of fracture evaluation as strain energy release rate, was proposed by
Rivlin and Thomas [6]. They developed a 2D specimen model with an infinite strip and a semi-infinite
crack based on Griffith’s theory [5], to simplify the conditions for calculating strain energy density as
shown in figure 2.2. This model allows for evaluating the strain energy density without investigating
the overall structure, as long as the total specimen width and the defined crack are sufficiently wide
to create a stress free zone behind the crack tip and a uniform stress field far ahead of the crack tip,
which is elaborated on later. Furthermore, the model focuses on the energy changes in the purely

large deformation without considering plasticity.
Figure 2.2 displays the 2D specimen with grips under quasi-static loading with four regions based on
varied deformation states: Region A located behind the crack tip is considered load-free; Region B
is situated a decent distance away from the crack tip as well as from the edge. The stress state is
relatively uniform and is dominated by biaxial stresses in the x1 and x2 directions. Shear stresses are
neglected because region B is far away from the localised effect of the crack tip; Region C is positioned
between regions A and B, showing a transition deformation state during crack propagation; Region D
is impacted by the traction-free edge.
When the crack length grows from ”a” to ”a+da”, regions C and D experience a minimal change in
the deformation state when the crack propagates, whereas region A increases and region B decreases
with the same amount [6]. In sum, as long as regions A and B are sufficient, the change of the potential
energy (dU) can be simplified and described by the elastic stored energy per referential volume (W)

2.1.3
Stress intensity factor
The stress intensity factor (K) describes the stress field (σij) near the crack tip, considering linear elastic
fracture mechanics (LEFM) proposed by Irwin [11]. The LEFM gives infinite stresses at the crack tip
and the stress is directly proportional to the strain, which is investigated in the linear elastic case. Figure
2.3 illustrates that different loading cases can be classified into three types of cracking modes: Mode 1
is an opening mode where the crack surfaces are directly separated apart; Mode 2 is an in-plane shear
mode where the crack surfaces slide parallel to each other in the in-plane direction; Mode 3 is an out-
of-plane shear mode where slide parallel to each other in the out-of-plane direction. In this thesis, the
mode 1 loading is of interest since the crack tip experiences tensile stress perpendicular to the crack
propagation direction.

2.2
Hyperelastic material models
The stress-strain relation of hyperelastic material models is defined by quantifying the strain energy
density as a function of the deformation gradient tensor, denoted as W(F). This function can be
expressed using different hyperelastic mathematical models, each showing a different fit to the actual
material behaviours. In this thesis, an incompressible and isotropic hyperelastic material is explored
based on hyperelastic materials models.

The Cauchy-Green deformation tensor (F) is introduced as it takes both linear and nonlinear defor-
mations into account and maps infinitesimal elements in the undeformed configuration to the corre-

Analytical method
This chapter develops analytical solutions for fracture energy evaluation under specific conditions based
on fundamental theory. The observed clamped infinite strip with a semi-infinite crack is illustrated in
figure 3.1. Due to the nature of the path-independency, the energy release rate near the crack tip can
be assessed using the contour that simplifies the calculation developed by Rice [7]. The way to simplify
the calculation is assuming the specimen width (W0) and crack length (a) are adequately defined to
establish a stress-free zone behind the crack tip and a uniform stress zone far away from the crack tip
as well as the specimen end. These allow for simplifying the evaluation of the strain energy density, as
the J-integral adapts to the assumptions.

Numerical method
This chapter explains the methods for conducting and analysing fracture mechanics and mechanical
behaviours of the clamped strips with a pre-defined crack specimen as depicted in figure 3.1. The
linear elastic and hyperelastic material properties are applied to the finite element (FE) model with the
software, Abaqus.
A designed model of the system is illustrated in figure 4.1. The setup is modelled by exploiting symme-
try conditions, meaning the lower half of the specimen is replaced by a set of fixed boundary conditions,
visualised in figure 4.1. The symmetry plane is fixed from the crack tip to the specimen end located at
0 ≤x1 ≤||W0 −a||. A constant displacement, denoted as u0

the region −a ≤x1 ≤||W0 −a||.
The finite element modelling approach is a numerical technique that discretises a continuous struc-
ture into a series of finite elements, hence the name. It interprets its complex physical behaviours by
integrating mathematical functions over each finite element.

and validate the fundamental knowledge analytically and numerically under relatively uncomplicated
material conditions. With the validated model, one can utilise it for further simulation incorporating
more complicated conditions, such as nonlinear behaviours, large deformation, etc.
For the model setup, a swept mesh is assigned around the crack tip. It ensures that the near infinite stress
of the singular K-field is found when r approaches the crack tip with the small applied displacement to

The J-integral results come close to the analytical result for a relatively coarser mesh in this case as there
is no excessive blunting effect. Thus, σ22 behaviours are focused on the mesh convergence study. With
the inversing the number of elements technique, the discrepancy between the σ22 value gained from
the observed number of elements and the extrapolation point envisioned in an extremely fine mesh
configuration can be evaluated. The results suggest using 1676 elements since it strikes the balance
between gaining accuracy and maintaining reasonable computation efficiency.
In the geometry study, the specimen height H0 is fixed in this thesis to avoid varying every parameter
and reduce the complexity of the analysis as the specimen height directly affects the J-integral value as
shown in equations 3.4 and 3.10. The baseline for the a/W0 ratio ranges from 0.2 to 0.8 to match the
analytical result, J-integral: 6.5934 MPa*mm. However, a wider specimen width generates a broader
a/W0 ratio range of convergence. A specimen width that should be ten times H0 is suggested to give
accuracy while saving computation time and experimental force. When the a/H0 ratio is smaller, dif-
ferent specimen width curves overlap with each other. This provides the insight that short crack length
regarding the specimen height significantly impacts accuracy even if the specimen is wide. The crack
length is suggested to be at least two times H0.
The stress singularity results show a clear K-dominant zone and meet the expectation based on Irwin’s
theory. It is intriguing to point out that the mesh converges slower inside the defined circle due to the
high stress gradient near the crack tip. It indicates that finer mesh is needed for capturing the stress state
near the crack tip. Thanks to the mirror model and homogeneous material throughout the specimen,
the stress and strain values extracted at the nodes are valuable to evaluate.
One of the treasures of using numerical software is to provide valuable insights and help understand
that the J-integral obtained numerically meets the analytical result when sufficient stress-free zone be-
hind the crack tip and a region adequately distant from the localised stress concentration zone and the
specimen’s end. Moreover, the contour plots also help distinguish the different deformation zones as
defined in figure 2.2 and help compare it to the analytical solution.
Finally, introducing the issues of using a blunted crack in LEFM facilitates the understanding of the ge-
ometry effect without considering nonlinear behaviours. The study reveals that an integration contour
should reach the plateau of the crack to fully integrate all strain energy density contributions and yield
accuracy. Furthermore, due to the nature of the blunted geometry, stresses distribute uniformly near
the crack tip compared to the ideally sharp crack. Thus, the stress singularity is no longer valuable to
analyse. These findings point out the necessity for caution about the blunting crack issues and facilitate
the discussion in the next chapter, the hyperelastic case.

Discussion
The reflection on the overall fracture behaviours in both linear and hyperelastic cases is revealed.
First of all, in the mesh convergence study, mesh sensitivity is found to be more significant when
analysing the hyperelastic material due to the complexity of its mechanical properties. With the effect
of blunting and nonlinearly, the size of the defined circle in the hyperelastic case should be determined
more cautiously to mitigate numerical error compared with the linear elastic case.
The length of a predefined crack plays a key role in affecting the J-integral value and the specimen
width must be sufficiently wide so that the J-integral value obtained numerically is identical to the
analytical result. To further explore the impact of crack length, curves with a fixed W0/H0 = 20
ratio in figure 5.2, where the material behaves linearly under strain 0.1%; and in figure 6.6, where the
material includes nonlinear behaviours at larger strains are investigated. It is intriguing to point out
that for the curve at strain 1% in figure 6.6, the range of the a/W0 ratio does not deviate significantly
from the linear elastic case. It suggests the dimension is still valid for hyperelastic material testing as
long as it is under a small strain. Besides, figures 5.2 and 6.10 demonstrate that when specimen width
is short, the effect of a short crack length is more serious. On the other hand, if the specimen width is
longer, the impact of a long crack length should be more cautious. Thus, a W0/H0 ratio of 40 and a
a/H0 ratio of 5 are required to align with the analytical approach under strain 30 %.
In both linear elastic and hyperelastic cases, the stresses as a function of the distance ahead of the crack
tip demonstrate that different mesh levels show varying stress responses and converge slowly near the
crack tip as revealed in figures 5.4, 6.15, 6.16, and 6.17. It is distinct to observe the blunting effect
at higher strain levels as shown in figures 6.16 and 6.17. The highest value of the stress component,
σ22, strongly deviates roughly 1 mm from the crack tip. This aligns with the results of McMeeking
[20]. Furthermore, even if the applied displacement is low in the linear elastic case, it is still possible to
capture slight blunting behaviour when the mesh approaches very close to the crack tip, as exhibited
in figure 5.4.

Future works
The rubber-like material, thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU), highlighted in this report, has been
widely used in many applications such as coating systems for leading edge protection. This report
mainly focuses on designing a numerical model to investigate fracture behaviours under both linear
and nonlinear elastic conditions and validating the model with analytical solutions. However, it is
challenging to maintain perfect elastic conditions in real-world cases. Thus, integrating more compli-
cated parameters, such as rate-dependent, temperature-dependent viscoelasticity is suggested for future
works.
Furthermore, inelastic behaviours, such as plasticity can result in significant dissipation of strain energy
during deformation as proposed in Elmukashfi’s work [4]. Figure 8.1 illustrates inelastic behaviours
should be considered when aiming to evaluate strain energy density in realistic cases; therefore, using
the modified method is advised. To understand the knowledge of damage mechanism, especially delving
into a micro-scale near the crack tip where the stresses and strains tend to be concentrated and easily
leads to fracture. Incorporating void nucleation and defect growth is suggested in McMeeking’s paper
for enhancing the model [20].

Conclusions
A finite element model was developed in this thesis by using Abaqus to design a fracture mechanics test
specimen for rubber-like materials for leading edge protection. A testing specimen with a pre-defined
crack under a tensile loading setup was evaluated. The analytical solutions were included for validating
the corresponding mechanics obtained numerically to ensure the model was set properly. Strain energy
density, J-integral, was presented as a standard to analyse the energy changes in the system under linear
elastic and hyperelastic conditions.
Mesh sensitivity test ensured proper mesh configuration and struck the balance between maintaining
accuracy and reasonable computation force. To decide the number of elements to apply, the inversing
number of elements technique was employed to evaluate the chosen number of elements with an ex-
trapolation point. More elements were determined in the hyperelastic case with 1980 elements in the
defined circle compared to the linear elastic case with 1676 elements in the entire model. Moreover,
the blunting and nonlinear effects were focused in the hyperelastic case. Therefore, the mesh became
more sensitive than in the linear elastic case.
The impact of variations in geometries on J-integral values was investigated by altering the ratio of
a/W0, a/H0, and W0/H0 with the constant specimen height to fix the analytical J-integral value. In
the linear elastic case, W0/H0 =20 ratio had already had broad alignment with the analytical result, J =
6.5934[MPa*mm]. However, in the hyperelastic case, a wider specimen width was required to meet the
accuracy, especially for conducting the higher strain. Thus, a W0/H0 ratio of 40, and a a/H0 ratio of 5
are suggested to meet the analytical result under strain 30 %. Furthermore, the length of a predefined
crack plays an important role in accuracy, especially when the specimen width was sufficiently wide.
Therefore, it is vital to define the critical range for crack length.
The LEFM singularity can be found by analysing the stresses ahead of the crack tip in the ”ideal” sharp
notch case as stress is asymptotic to r approaches 0. However, when blunting and nonlinear behaviours
became severe, the stress singularity vanished. Furthermore, the comparison of σ22 values analytically
and numerically gave the picture of the zone in such region B in figure 2.2. In the hyperelastic case, as
the strain increases, the smaller range for meeting the analytical result.
Contour plots were demonstrated to visualise stress and strain fields throughout the specimen. This
facilitated an evaluation of the sufficient stress-free zone behind the crack tip, stress concentration near
the crack tip, stress distribution uniformly away from the crack tip and specimen end, and the material
contraction at the specimen end. Besides, the effect of applied boundary conditions on the grip which
plain strain in x11 direction was also observed.
The thesis focused on the blunting effect for fracture energy investigation and demonstrated the crack
blunts from the predefined sharp crack under large deformation in figure 6.2. The equation 7.1 ex-
plained that to gain the applied J-integral Jtot more accurately, one should consider Jc in the cohesive
zone along with the blunting effect Jbl.
Overall, the FE model was validated with the corresponding analytical solutions to capture mechanical
behaviours and energy states in both linear elastic and hyperelastic cases. To create a more reliable
model for simulating material responses close to real life, it is necessary to incorporate additional in-
fluential factors to describe more detailed fracture mechanics.

Proof of frame variance or invariance with
Cauchy-Green deformation tensors
To prove the variance of the frame, first define the Right(C) and Left(B) Cauchy-Green deformation
tensor [16].
dx = FdX
dx·dx = (FdX)·(FdX)
|dx|2 = dX(F T F)X = XCdX
|dx|2 = dX(FF T )X = XBdX
(A.1)
C = F T F
B = FF T
(A.2)
To transform F into a different frame, Q stands for Q(t), a time-dependent orthogonal tensor, which
is employed to indicate the change in frame. The term ”orthogonal tensor” implies that QT Q = 1.
Right Cauchy-Green deformation(C):
F ∗= QF
C = F T F
C∗= (F ∗)T F ∗= F T QT QF
C∗= C
(A.3)
The result shows that the right Cauchy-Green deformation(C) is frame-variant due to C∗.
Left Cauchy-Green tensor is frame-invariant proven as:
B = FF T
B∗= F ∗F ∗T = (QF)(QF)T = QFF T QT
B∗= Q(t)BQ(t)T
(A.4)
