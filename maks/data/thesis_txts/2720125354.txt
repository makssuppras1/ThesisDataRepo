Abstract
Cybercrime is still a common crime worldwide, and comes with potentially high costs.
Within Denmark, Authoritites publish statistics, indicating an increase in security incidents
from previous years. Additionally, new legislation from the European Union, NIS2 Direc­
tive, sets forth new requirements for certain medium sized companies. AddPro Denmark,
a Managed Service Provider wants to tackle these issues, by introducing a Security Oper­
ations Center. Previous Security Offerings under a ”Security as a Service” scheme does
not sufficiently cover the needs of the customers, nor the requirements of the NIS2 Di­
rective. We use a combination of cyber security knowledge, business intelligence and
entrepreneurial tools to propose 1 core offering, with 5 offerings extending the core. We
describe which technologies are used, deduce onboard processes, create report capabil­
ities and estimate costs. The academic findings from this thesis shows that the proposed
offerings map well into the NIS2 Directive. It also finds a cost estimate for a 25­employee
sized company, if the company were to acquire the offerings themselves. The estimate
may be compared against a risk analysis, in order to determine if the cost of risk out­
weighs the cost of the offerings, while the NIS2 Directive mapping validates the usage in
companies which are in scope of the Directive.

Introduction
Cybercrime is seen as a frightingly common crime worldwide ­ according to Check Point
Research in 2021 it is reported that ”Globally, 1 out of every 61 organizations impacted
by ransomware each week”. In Europe alone, the average weekly number of attacks per
organization went up 65 pct. from 2020 (400 attacks) to 2021 (660 attacks). [1]
Such crimes are not without a cost to the targeted organizations. PurpleSec, a cyber
security company with establishment in Washington, DC, U.S, notes several numbers to
support this claim [2], including:
• That the global annual cost of cybercrime is estimated to be 6 trillion USD, and rising
to 10.5 trillion USD by 2025
• That enterprises saw an annual cyber security increase in cost, by 22.7 pct. in 2021
• The average cost of data breach to a small business, varies from 120.000 USD to
1.24 million USD
Organizations in Denmark are no exception. The Danish Business Authority (Erhvervsstyrelsen)
made an analysis in September 2021, concluding 10pct. of SMBs had an incident in 2019.
The analysis also concludes 40pct. of SMBs have an insignificant level of cyber security
compared to their risk profile. [3] (SMB is in Denmark defined as min. 10 employees, max
250 employees [4]).
This indication is also backed up by Statistics Denmark (Danmarks Statistik), where the
increase in yearly incidents had risen greatly: In 2020, the percentage of organizations
who had a security incident was 9 pct. for organizations with 10­49 employees. That
increased to 23 pct. in 2022. For organizations with 50­99 employees, it went from 13 to
34 pct. [5] Considering that there were 213.168 companies under the term SMB (SMV)
in 2017 [6], one could imagine that even with 23 pct., almost 1 in 4 of these organizations
will have a yearly incident.
The cause of these concerning numbers could be due to a number of factors ­ for instance,
Statistics Denmark reports a lack of it­specialists. [7] This could mean that there is a lack
of cyber security specialists, with competencies to implement safeguards in an organi­
zation. As for other it­positions, lack of competencies in administration of it­systems and
development could lead to mismanagement, lack of safeguard strategies during program­
ming can respectively lead to vulnerabilities, some more critical than others. The Danish
Business Authority analysis also indicates that employees lack knowledge to identify cer­
tain attacks such as phishing. [3]
Such concerns is also a driving force behind new legislation within the European Union,
the Network and Information Systems 2 Directive (NIS2). [8] [9] The directive expands
upon the NIS directive by targeting additional business sectors, and set forth several cyber
security obligations, including:
1. Risk and Incident management
2. Business Continuity systems (e.g. backup)
3. Human Resource security and cybersecurity training

While NIS2 will mostly target medium and large organizations, there may be scenarios
where smaller ones are in scope of the directive. Failure to uphold these obligations may

1.1
Motivation and challenges
The issues with increasing cybercrime, lacking cyber security measures, staffing and new
legislation will most certainly put a significant pressure on SMBs in Denmark. AddPro
Denmark, a Managed Service Provider (MSP) business, took this as an initial spark of
motivation and opportunity to try and resolve such issues. Having AddPro Denmark tar­
geting SMBs and customer sectors being in scope of the directive made it feasible to
realize working on new services. It is services which aids in providing organizations with
specialized cyber security consultants, knowledge and technology. Facilitating all these
resources into a single unit, would require a new department: The Security Operations
Center (SOC).
A realization of a SOC does not come without potential challenges ­ there is still the need
of human resources to design, plan, implement and operate such a center. Throughout
working on the thesis, alongside the statements above, some of the challenges identified
are:
1. How should the Security Operations Center be organized?
2. Which services should be offered to the customers such that they are affordable and
in scope of the NIS2 directive?
3. What technology/solutions are needed to support the operations center?
4. Can we make partnership with other companies in certain offerings, to put focus
towards certain offerings and strengthen the quality of these?
5. Can we estimate a total cost in time and money, from a 25­employee sized company
perspective?
The thesis will make an effort to take these challenges into account.
1.1.1
Security Incident and Event Management
AddPro Denmark has a great focus on a Security Incident and Event Management (SIEM)
technology acquisition. It is seen as a general pillar when structuring a Security Opera­
tions Center, as it covers aspects such as incident response, auditing and compliance.
The challenge is how to manage such a solution for multiple organizations, without ex­
hausting the consultants or incurring significant administrative overhead?
Certain ”need to have” requirements must be met, with respect to these challenges. It
must be possible to get a single overview of all customers with a single pane of glass
(SPOG) approach. Customer data must also be segmented, in order to respect current
laws and regulations (e.g. GDPR). Because of few human resources to begin with, it is
also needed to have a solution that requires low administrative overhead. It should also
be a solution that integrates well with organization assets. To support reporting features
in the future, API integration is a must.
”Nice to have” requirements are more loose, that is we can possibly omit having these
features. Some considerations here are Extended Detection and Response (XDR), fixed

Scope and Structure
It is an complex challenge to prepare a SOC and have it up and running within 5 months.
The thesis will scope the work, just as described in the thesis title ­ to planning, designing
and implementing the SOC. What it means, is that we are focused on system architecture,
processes for onboarding, managing several customers and integrating these with exist­
ing systems to reduce the administration overhead. We shall try to estimate the time and
costs associated with acquisitions and certain processes, in order to determine a com­
plete cost, if an organization were to pursue the work themselves. We will not delve further
into incident response handling, risk management, SLAs or offboarding procedures, but
do recognize their importance, as these are actionable next steps to cover after the thesis
has concluded the groundwork.
We structure the thesis as several chapters, each containing one or more sections. Chap­
ter 2 establishes a foundation of information for who AddPro Denmark is as a company,
and the identified concerns with cyber security. An organizational and technical infras­

Operations Center entails is explained in 2.2.

base are given in section 3.2. A brief introduction to the Autotask PSA system i found

As it is expected that a SIEM­solution will be a major part of the SOC, chapter 5 is ded­

Background
Initially founded under the name 4dimensions in 2009, AddPro Denmark A/S (also referred
to as ”AddPro Denmark”) currently employs over 60 people. The company was acquired
by AddPro A/B (Swedish Company) and investment company Axcel in 2021. The invest­
ment gave financial support in order to acquire companies in Jutland and Zealand, to
provide coverage of all of Denmark.
Not long after, AddPro was acquired by itm8, which is a corporate company consisting of
many IT­service brands. Other brands include ITRelations and Improsec. While there is
a corporate direction that AddPro must follow, the company still continues with its current
management and infrastructure, and also has much independence in performing different
IT­services, including cyber security services.
In the grand scheme of things, AddPro is an IT service provider (also known as a Man­
aged Service Provider, abbreviated MSP), with a vision to become the leading provider
of IT as a Service (ITaaS) in Denmark. Current IT offerings include:
1. Hosted services
2. On­site and remote support
3. Hardware sales
4. Consultancy
5. Cyber security support
6. Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) support/development in various solutions
With the recent acquisitions, there will soon be more than 130 employees in 3 main offices
and a number of smaller hubs all over Denmark. Our services is being provided to over
300 SMBs – a number which is steadily growing. There are also customer relations with
a few larger organization, with more to follow.
We note that for the remainder of the thesis, we only refer to AddPro in Denmark, un­
less explicitly specified otherwise.

2.2
Describing a Security Operations Center (SOC)
In order to perform any work on a ”Security Operations Center” (SOC), some initial intro­
duction is required, both to what the term entails, as well as the fundamentals. A SOC has
historically had several terms, which includes ”Cybersecurity Operations Center” (CSOC)
or ”Network Operations and Security Center” (NOSC). The latter may also cover, more
broadly, the monitoring of the network infrastructure, to ensure uptime and availability.
As Matthew Shutock and Dr. Glenn Dietrich [10], as well as Stef Schinagl and Ronald
Paans [11] describes in their papers, there is a common understanding that a SOC is
built around a ”People, Processes and Technology (PPT)” mindset, following the holistic
definition provided by SANS Institute, Crowley and Pescatore. They see the SOC as ”A
combination of people, processes and technology protecting the information systems of
an organization through: proactive design and configuration, ongoing monitoring of sys­
tem state, detection of unintended actions or undesirable state, and minimizing damage
from unwanted effects” [12].
A major role of the SOC is mainly focused towards the technical infrastructure, that is,
monitoring servers, applications, networks, endpoint devices. For a SOC, the monitor­
ing may be done by ”performing continuous monitoring, vulnerability scans, compliance
scans, log data collection etc.” [11]. Such logs can be inspected manually, or analyzed/­
correlated in a Security Incident and Event Management (SIEM) solution. Reacting on
incidents is equally important, as steps are afterwards needed to remediate incidents in
a swift manner, such that any damage is reduced to a minimum.
The staff of a SOC may consist of Security Analysts in a tiered approach, going from level
1­3, where level 1 looks at initial alerts, and level 3 staff goes beyond alerts to perform
active threat hunting. There may also be other dedicated staff, specialized in Penetration
Testing or Digital Forensics. The SOC may be managed by a SOC Manager.
2.2.1

While the primary activity is the monitoring and response of any incidents that may occur,
there can also be other activities (operations) within a SOC ­ and this can vary much from
one MSP to another, or from one organization to another. Looking at the SANS Insti­
tute Survey provides more insight into this. [12] We see many notable activities beyond
monitoring and incident response, such as:
1. Data protection and monitoring
2. Security administration
3. Security road map and planning
4. SOC architecture and engineering
5. Threat research

2.2.2
Challenges
The people element is, perhaps, the most important part of the PPT triad. Technology
will never work to the full potential, if no one is using it. The same goes for processes,
where in most instances, people have to be involved. As the SANS Institute Survey [12]
reports on ”Lack of skilled staff” being a major issue, this causes concerns. While the
survey percentage was reduced from 2018 to 2019, it is still a high percentage, short of

3.1
AddPro Denmark Business Model
A good and proper business model, is a key component to business success, and one
that ensures customers will onboard the SOC. We look towards the identified models by
St. Gallen in the Business Model Navigator [13], in order to get a closer look into what
models matches the AddPro profile.
As AddPro Denmark is a Managed Service Provider, it comes naturally that the preferred
business model is that of a ”Solution Provider” combined with a ”Subscription” based
model. The former describes a company where there is a great coverage of products
within a certain domain (in this case, IT) and the latter describes a company which charges
a regular fee on a monthly, quarterly or annual basis. Our SOC should try and follow this is
as closely as possible, as additional model approaches can make selling/service manage­
ment more complex for the sales department, or misalign expectations with the customer.
For SOC activities like security event and incident response, vulnerability management
and security training, it is reasonable to put such services into a monthly fee, but it also
depends on how the pricing is when acquiring the technology for a SOC. For a small orga­
nization, the less expensive, monthly up­front cost also makes payments more digestible.
An additional margin can be put on the price, such that there is room for negotiations
when a customer experiences growth.
There may be some exceptions. For the penetration testing, we believe it is of interest
to go with an affiliation model. The idea of this model, is that it ”lies in supporting others
to successfully sell products and directly benefit from successful transactions”. And this
approach is justifiable, and as outlined in subsection 2.2.1 such testing cannot be per­
formed by the same company that manages the cyber security of the customer ­ it could
potentially lead to bias and trust issues. Usually such a service also only occurs yearly,
and that would not always fit well with a monthly payment service. As such a service is
also expensive, it may not be suited for all customers.

3. The ”New” MFA authentication setup (integrated into the Azure AD experience and

4. The Conditional Access feature

Neither of the above prevent MFA fatigue attacks. Fatigue attacks occur because of the
user approving multi­factor authentication prompts sent by an attacker several times, who
knows the credentials of the user. This type of threat may occur if the user logging in them­
selves may be timed with a prompt from an attacker, and they do not read the contents
of the prompt.
Azure Active Directory has additional built­in features to mitigate this with ”number match­
ing” [35]. Number matching requires the user to enter the shown number during login, and
enter into the MFA device. It prevents attackers to perform fatigue attacks since the user
does not know the number shown to the attacker, unless the attacker can somehow no­
tify and social engineer a user into typing the number on the right MFA notification. The
Number Matching feature will fortunately be rolled out to all tenants in Q1 2023, and this
feature is therefore only necessary to manually configure for a short while.
We want to onboard customers with features that will be supported in the future. There­
fore, legacy MFA should be disabled, and a discussion is needed to confirm whether con­
ditional access is necessary or security defaults is needed (conditional access is needed
in instances where service accounts with no MFA must be used, or if MFA bypass is re­
quested on certain trusted workplaces/sites). We still recommend self­service password
reset option to reduce the burden of customers calling to request password resets.

4.2.2
Hosted Servers at AddPro Denmark
All AddPro Denmark hosted servers are contained within the Nutanix AHV cluster. The
cluster has several backup policies in place to suit different backup strategies. Thus,
there is no additional work needed to setup backup, other than selecting the right policy
(a server can only join one policy at a time). All policies mimic a 3­2­1 backup approach,
with 3 backups in total, 2 at different mediums (clusters contain 3 hosts that replicate
virtual machines, including backup data) and one offsite. The most common policy is
given below, to elaborate on this further.
Common policy
In the common policy, we establish the following retention scheme for snapshots (Nutanix
uses the term ”Recovery Points”, but it is effectively snapshots, which have some benefits
compared to full backups, including a reduced backup size):
1. 1 snapshot per hour for 12 hours within the daily work
2. 1 snapshot per day for 30 days based on the last snapshot of the day
3. 1 nightly replication of the 1 snapshot per day to the offsite backup
This policy means we can achieve a Recovery Point Objective (RPO) of 1 hour. The
Recovery Time Objective (RTO) is more difficult to estimate ­ based on the customer
server size, it can take a significant amount of time. If we assume a server size of 100GB
(800gbit), with recovery transfer over a dedicated 1Gbps line and uncompressed backup,
there is an expected recovery of 13 to 14 minutes in RTO per server.

Acquiring a SIEM solution
Before delving into incident response, a SIEM solution must be acquired. Being a core
feature of incident response, we shall dedicate a complete chapter to the acquisition.
Outside of being a core feature, it also
1. Is customer­wise associated as a fundamental part of a Security Operations Center
beyond incident response
2. Provides capabilities of collecting audit logs for the customer
3. Centralizes all logs into a single solution, needed to gain the greater overview of
customer assets
4. May provide additional capabilities like vulnerability management, reporting or hon­
eypot traps, which may be beneficial in other offerings
Before putting down requirements, we describe what a SIEM is.
5.1
What is a SIEM?
As mentioned in the introduction, SIEM stands for ”Security Incident and Event Manage­
ment”. The main idea of a SIEM is
1. Ingest logs from many different systems/applications, called log sources
2. Understand the log contents such that they are normalized (standardized) within the
SIEM solution
3. Ingest security information with respect to user and device behavior, usually given
as rules that matches patterns in logs
4. Correlate security information, rules, against logs to determine if there are any po­
tential threats, giving rise to alerts and incidents. Alerts and incidents can be trig­
gered with different severities, with the severity determined by the rule
The correlation is done almost in real­time, and investigators can pursue alerts that are
usually shown in a visual dashboard.
Other features may also be included in a SIEM solution to enhance capabilities:
1. Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR): Ability to monitor endpoint devices (typ­
ically an endpoint application is installed), and correlate with SIEM solution data.
Devices can typically respond automatically by isolating themselves if a potential
threat is found, and trigger alerts within the SIEM solution
2. Extended Detection and Response (XDR): Extends the EDR feature to cross cor­
relate between many endpoints, networks, servers etc. This means a SIEM solution
can make complex decisions by correlating data from several devices at once
3. Security Orchestration and Automated Response (SOAR): A term to describe
the development and automated running of staged security procedures, termed run­
books, to perform actions throughout different applications with little intervention
from an investigator

XDR and SOAR can both be seen as independent systems, outright replacing the SIEM
system. They can also be seen as extensions to the SIEM solution. In this thesis, we
shall go with the latter.

To get a better understanding of incident response, we look towards the Carnegie Mellon

2. Buy equipment and build any necessary network infrastructure to support the team
3. Develop the initial set of CSIRT policies and procedures to support your services
4. Define the specifications for and build your incident­tracking system

We are mainly concerned with the last 4 bullet points, as the initial staff will be consisting

In terms of the equipment, that is what has been primarily outlined in the initial chapters.

6.1
Policies and procedures
6.1.1
Incident taxonomy
We want to classify incidents based on various factors. Instead of reinventing the wheel,
we look into already existing taxonomies. For our policy, we opted to apply the ENISA Cy­
bersecurity Incident Taxonomy. [86] While the scope of the taxonomy is explained to be
broad (large­scale incidents requiring European Union Cooperation), their scope is also
defined as ”Incidents affecting the security of network and information systems, in any
sector of society”, so it still covers many aspects such as the underlying cause (nature)
as well as the impact on different aspects (such as outlook, impact and sector impact) no
matter the company size.
To briefly describe the taxonomy, it is composed of two parts: The incident nature, and
incident impact. The former explains root cause and severity, while the latter explains
sector impact, impact scale (although on a national level) and outlook. For most of the
Planning, Designing and Implementing a Security Operations Center for a Managed Service Provider

==PAGE:62==
companies, a national level impact scale does not make much sense, and preferably it is
better to see it as how great the impact is on individual customers in terms of economy,
reputation etc.
Considering that AddPro is a MSP, the sector impact will be on Digital Services and Digital
Infrastructure, but having other options does allow for some more special case scenarios
­ which is good to have, if AddPro is to provide services to larger customers.
To define potential threats, we utilize a compliment to be above, the ENISA Threat Taxon­

MITRE ATT&CK Framework
InsightIDR builds upon the ATT&CK Framework [88], and this gives a common under­

We opt to use this framework as well, when describing the tools, techniques and stages
performed in incidents.
6.2
Incident­tracking system development
6.2.1
Implementing Single pane of glass capabilities
One of the issues with InsightIDR, was the missing ”Single pane of glass” (SPOG) feature.
A SPOG makes it significantly easier to get a single overview of all tenants, with respect
to any incidents that may be created on each tenant. As mentioned during the SIEM
chapter and InsightIDR, there were mentions of a solution in development. Until then,
development has to be made such that an overview is achieved.
Autotask setup
As outlined in section 3.7, the service desk solution used by AddPro Denmark, is Auto­
task PSA. We therefore want to have an integration, where each system can complement
each other, such that an overview of all tenants is established, and reduce the amount of
time needed for a consultant to traverse between solutions.
In Autotask, we create a ”SOC Incidents” queue for all incidents. We create ticket tags
based on the MITRE ATT&CK Framework, more specifically the mitigations and tac­
tic/techniques matrix. Only certain naming lengths are possible for tags, we create them
such that the tactic ID is the prefix, the technique ID is the suffix, and in­between is a colon
character for separation.
For the ticket, the ENISA nature and impact fields are created as User­defined Fields
(UDFs) within Autotask, which are then assigned to the particular SOC incident ticket.
Lastly, there is a dashboard feature in Autotask. Whenever a ticket is created that matches
a SOC incident and Rapid7 InsightIDR tenants, we will get notified via the dashboard.
Dashboards also provides a quick overview of closed tickets, the amount of time spent
per employee on SOC Incidents and more. It is possible to project dashboards onto a
large monitor, viewable for all consultants in AddPro.
Integrations
Both Autotask PSA and InsightIDR provides APIs in order to integrate with their respective
solutions. Additionally, both provide Webhook capabilities such that certain events can

Solution development
We want to develop a solution that can:
1. Import InsightIDR investigations of high and critical priority into Autotask PSA as a
ticket. A ticket should include the priority, include which company it is, and a link to
the investigation in InsightIDR
2. Close investigations in InsightIDR based on closing the linked ticket in Autotask PSA
We opt to go with PowerShell as our programming language of choice. PowerShell works
well under the solution proposal, as it is integrated with the Microsoft Windows and Azure
environment (given that it comes pre­installed), and is built with task automation in mind.
Additionally, it is well known by all consultants in AddPro Denmark, so if another consul­
tant is to perform any maintenance, then less work in understanding another programming
language has to be done. To target a wide range of systems with PowerShell, version 5.1
is selected.
The solution should preferably create tickets and update investigations within a few min­
utes. This is because in cases of very critical investigations, a few minutes may be crucial
in responding to an incident. Two approaches where considered to tackle this situation:
1. A PowerShell script that runs every 5 minutes polls data from all InsightIDR tenants
and the Autotask PSA tenant, and checks if any updates to these are applicable
2. A PowerShell script that awaits a webhook response, whenever incident events
occur
We went with the latter option, for a few reasons. One is that we do not need to do po­
tentially wasted polling every few minutes. Polling would also require setup of specific
conditions to not poll all tickets, e.g. check for currently modified Autotask tickets only
in the SOC incident ticket queue. With webhooks, a PowerShell script for that particular
event is triggered whenever the system does specific updates, that is, when a ticket is
closed, when an investigation is opened etc.
AddPro Denmark has their own Windows Servers, capable to running specific tasks/jobs.
Additionally, Microsoft Azure subscriptions are available as well, with a free amount of
credits available (estimated to be a few thousand dollars per year).
It is possible to setup a webhook listener on an on­premise server (with HTTP services
like Apache, Internet Information Services), but this would also require managing certain
aspects, such as keeping the webhook service up­to­date to mitigate any potential vul­
nerabilities and bugs, but it would also require managing an SSL certificate. Based on
these considerations, discussions led to using Microsoft Azure and their SaaS platform.
Their SaaS solution, Azure Automation Account, provides capabilities to run scripts and
trigger them based on webhooks. The pricing is also affordable, not only because of the
free credit, but also because 500 minutes of automation running is given for free, and the
additional per minute price is low.
Another benefit of the Automation Account, is the capability to off­load running scripts
to a Hybrid Worker, which can be an on­premise Windows or Linux Server. As long as
the server has the necessary modules required by the scripts, it can run just as if it was

load balancing. Hybrid Workers also process webhooks faster, compared to letting Azure
do all the work.
The last benefit, is that the Automation Account can queue webhook events, such that
the Hybrid Worker isn’t overloaded with responses. This also has the implicit benefit, that
if the on­premise servers were to be offline, the calls will be in a waiting state until one or

There will be access to many tenants, and it is therefore crucial to look into a setup for

We once again look into Microsoft Azure. Here, an Azure Keyvault can be created, which

based on per 10.000 successful secret calls against the vault, for less than 1DKK.
To establish a connection to the vault, we make an application registration on the Ad­

and can be assigned to Azure resources. Thus, we assign this service account read­

To authenticate as the application, we add a self­signed certificate as the authentication
method. This certificate can be installed on the on­premise application servers acting as
Hybrid Workers for the Automation account. Certificate private key access can also be
controlled on the server to ensure that only the system and a local service account can
read it. The certificate can easily be revoked if abuse is detected.
After authentication, API credentials can be fetched only when needed and discarded
again after use, thus we avoid hard coding any credentials into the solution.

In PowerShell, the method of segmenting scripts/solutions is with modules. A module

1. We have a root module called ”AddProDK.SOC”
2. Each module will be added within this namespace, e.g. ”AddProDK.SOC.Rapid7”

individual module. The latter increases the speed of the script, as less modules has to be
loaded. It also means we will only load modules that are of relevance.

InsightIDR webhook challenges
Throughout development, it was discovered that InsightIDR webhook calls produce an
error, when calling the respective Azure Automation Account webhook. The issue is that
the Automation Account responds with an HTTP response status code of 202, but Insight­
IDR only recognizes code 200. Code 202 is a successful response, but indicates that the
request has not been processed yet (it can be considered asynchronous). This makes
sense, as the Automation Account queues the process, and wants to wrap up connections
to InsightIDR. The Automation Account does not support changing this response to code
200. Consequence of this issue, is that InsightIDR will retry sending alerts, creating a lot
of repeated calls.
A bug report has been created to resolve this issue. Until then, we need to mitigate the
issue. This can be done with an Azure Logic App. The purpose of this app is to provide
a webhook just as with the Automation Account, but the logic app allows us to send the
status code 200 back to InsightIDR, and then continue to relay the event over to the au­
tomation account webhook. The Azure Logic App can be programmed to consider both
status codes 200 and 202 a success.

Data sources
The data ingestion sources identified, also based on the customer IT analysis, are as

2. Azure AD (events related to identity management)
3. Microsoft 365 (events not found in Azure AD, e.g. file access, mail access)

5. Fortigate Firewall

When collecting logs, we always ensure to detect the correct timezone, so all times are
UTC normalized within InsightIDR.
Active Directory Domain Controllers
Domain controllers have several components that must be addressed, including security
logs, DNS, DHCP and LDAP. [90] [91] [92] [93]
For security, DNS and DHCP, there are different ways to get their respective logs. Rapid7
offers Windows Instrumentation Management (WIM), folder watching and NXLog integra­

administrative rights in order to work right. During testing, it also had connectivity issues
when using the non­domain joined collector. NXLog requires a simple install and config
file to run, and can get logs from security, DNS and DHCP logs. It also supports en­
cryption in transit to the collector. NXLog must be installed and configured on all domain
controllers.
To retrieve the correct security logs, the same guide provided by Rapid7 for Active Di­
rectory outlines the Domain Controller policy settings that must be enabled, in order to
log correctly. [90] In our approach, we contruct a dedicated GPO for this logging, as it
also allows to easily backup and restore the GPO on other customer tenants, if needed.
For LDAP, a non­administrative service account is setup to allow the collector to read
directory data of the domain. The account is specified within the InsightIDR tenant.
Azure AD

in Azure. This means we must create a new Azure subscription, deploy the event log,
setup Azure AD to send logs to the Event Hub, and lastly provide Event Hub credentials
to InsightIDR.

6.3.3
Honeypots and files
InsightIDR includes deception technology to deceive intruders to access specially crafted
services and files which trigger high severity alerts. [96]
Honeypots are a form of deception technology, which appears as an ordinary, vulner­
able asset within a network. As a common user would never know that a honeypot exists,
it is in many instances an actual intruder performing actions on it. In short, honeypots
provide a high ”signal­to­noise” ratio.
A honeypot does not necessarily have to be a service. It can also be tied to specific files
or actions on real assets. An example would be a network file shared named ”password
62
Planning, Designing and Implementing a Security Operations Center for a Managed Service Provider
==PAGE:71==
store”, which contain word documents with fake passwords. Complexity (interaction) can
vary a lot, from completely mimicking a real production environment, to running very few
services and give early warnings, or even be accessible files which trigger an alert. [97]
A virtual appliance is available, which registers to the InsightIDR tenant, and is activated
with an activation key. Thus as part of the onboarding, we deploy the honeypot on AddPro
hosted infrastructure. Any activity on the honeypot will trigger alerts of high severity.
Honey Files
Another deception technology supported by InsightIDR, is the Honey Files option. This
allows us to place spoofed files, acting as legimimate files, on a customer file server. They
can be named in many ways, but usually something like ”passwords” or ”credential” is an
option. The file type can be anything, and Word, Excel files work well since it is common
file types in the customer workplace.
If the customer uses SharePoint, we can instead create custom alerts which is triggered
when a spoofed file is read.

Penetration Testing
According to the National Institute of Science and Technology (NIST) in ”An Introduction
to Information Security”, referring SP800­53, Penetration Testing is ”A test methodology
in which assessors, typically working under specific constraints, attempt to circumvent or
defeat the security features of a system.” [98]
Thus, it is the activity of performing tests against a customer it­infrastructure and ser­
vices, in order to breach and gain elevated access to services. This testing is either con­
ducted manually, or via automation tools. The former is more expensive since it requires
a specialized person capable of performing such services, while the latter is significantly
cheaper, but may reduce the value of the test due to potential lack of coverage. We see
automation tools as a good candidate for conducting on­going testing of externally ex­
posed services, but to perform an in­depth analysis on internal systems, a human may be
better suited for the job.
We see two reasons for offering penetration testing. One is that it allows for validation of
the customer setup done by AddPro Denmark, the other is to check if there is any signifi­
cant misconfiguration of systems.
In this chapter, we look into the partnership establishment and test process setup, such
that manual penetration testing can be performed for the customer. We are not concerned
with automated tools, as we believe offerings such as Vulnerability Management can offer
some of this automation, in order to identity and aid in remediation of vulnerabilities both
internally and externally.
7.1
Partnership establishment
The partnership establishment is necessary, in order to perform an unbiased penetration
test. AddPro Denmark cannot do these by themselves, as knowing the customer setup
and potential vulnerabilities will lead to significant bias. The test has to be done by some­
one who knows nothing of the environment they are put in. AddPro Denmark opted to go
with ImproSec because they:
1. Are a part of the itm8 group, which AddPro Denmark is also a part of and there is a
joint effort to promote/use brand companies for external services
2. Have reputable accrediations, memberships and awards [99] [100]
3. Do not wish to have Microsoft certifications, as they believe this will skew their rep­
utation and give potential bias
4. Are unbiased because they know nothing of the AddPro Denmark customers, cus­
tomer infrastructure or customer services
5. They contribute to open source knowledge and tools, by providing this freely via
GitHub [101]

”Internal Penetration Test (Assume Breach)”. [102] The breach assumption would be if an

Security Awareness
Security awareness is a threat to many companies, as employees may be persuaded into
performing malicious actions with social engineering. They might also fall into phishing
and spear phishing attacks, clicking on links in emails, leading to installation of malware
or providing credentials. Examples of successful attacks include Ubiquiti Networks who
in 2015 suffered a loss of around 45 million USD due to CEO fraud [103] or a Toyota
subsidiary who lost around 37 million USD in 2019. [104]
Because of these concerns, and the upcoming NIS2 directive, we want to offer security
awareness training. Several challenges must be solved in order to provide the offering,
including what solution to use, the onboarding and ongoing process. We also want to do
it in a way that is as unmanaged as possible, to reduce the amount of maintenance.

shortcut similar to other Microsoft365 Applications. It therefore utilizes the Security
Core offering capabilities that are mandatory for the customer before they can get
security awareness offerings
4. A phishing alert button can be pushed to users via Microsoft 365, which will allow
them to report phishing mails (both real and simulated). It will apply to Exchange
both via Web, Phone and PC application
5. KnowBe4 fares well in the Gartner Peer Insights October 2021, being a ”Customers

Vulnerability Management
The Security Core does ensure patch management of certain software and operating sys­
tem, but does otherwise not provide any insight into any potential vulnerabilities of a wide
suite of software. This means that certain critical vulnerabilities may not be discovered or
patched within a reasonable timeframe. The very purpose of this offering, is to resolve this
scenario. Our expectations is to provide a monthly review on the state of vulnerabilities
found, and offer to resolve the vulnerabilities within a reasonable timeframe.
9.1
Technology selection
Two general options were considered, when offering Vulnerability Management. One be­
ing Defender for Endpoint Vulnerability Management, included in the Microsoft 365 Busi­
ness Premium package, the other being another solution from Rapid7, InsightVM.
Through Microsoft Defender, there are also APIs to faciliate better software and vulnera­
bility management of endpoint devices, which could be collected into a single overview.
[110] Due to the reason that Defender for Servers cost extra at a non­competitive price
(estimated 5 eur per month, or 60 eur per month depending on the plan) and that it does
not cover other devices, we opt not to use this service.
To maintain better integration with other offerings, we went with Rapid7 InsightVM. The
pricing is based on a per assessed device scheme, which means that it follows closely the
count of assets for InsightIDR. Other devices will not count, as long as they are not being
assessed. It also has two benefits when combined with InsightIDR. One is InsightVM can
feed data into InsightIDR, and therefore produce alerts based on the vulnerability severity.
Second is that the InsightIDR agent can perform vulnerability assessments [111], leading
to a significant computation reduction from an InsightVM self­hosted scan engine as it
only needs to initiate the assessment on each workstation. It also means that the scan
engine does not need to be close to the workstations that must be scanned ­ an internet
connection will suffice. The price for each device, no matter the type, is estimated to be
around 10 euros per year. Lastly, InsightVM also provides a RESTful API allowing us to
pull out data for customized reports. [112]
9.1.1
Architecture
InsightVM works similarly to InsightIDR by following a hybrid apporach ­ there is a pri­
mary on­premise security master console with built­in scan engine, which connects to the
Insight platform. Secondary scan engines without a console can be deployed and con­
nected to the Insight platform as needed. This may be necessary when a customer has
several networks that are not reachable from the master console.
The engine can perform two types of scan techniques, ”authenticated” or ”unauthenti­
cated”. The former will provide significantly more vulnerability detail, as the engine can
remotely interact with the scanned device with given credentials. The latter will scan
based on a ”no credential” scheme, and will therefore check as much as it can without
having credentials.
Vulnerabilities found provide many different parameters to aid in estimating the next steps
and remediation. [113] Vulnerabilities are defined together with a given severity (based
Planning, Designing and Implementing a Security Operations Center for a Managed Service Provider
85
==PAGE:94==
on CVSSv2), the vulnerability CVSSv3 and CSVVv2 score. A risk score is also deduced
from the vulnerability score severity and site impact ­ the higher the score, the more risk.
InsightVM sums up the risk scores of all vulnerabilities on a device, which can be used
to prioritize devices with the most risk. It is possible to view if there are exploits available
for a given vulnerability. More detailed remediation steps are also given for a vulnerability.

Cyber Security Maturity
Purpose of this chapter, is to outline the cyber security baseline/maturity services offered
in the new security wheel, and how these can be improved. We shall look at the Fit/Gap
analysis, as well as finding a framework for cyber security maturity which may be beneficial
to a broad range of customers.
10.1
The Fit/Gap Analysis
The Fit/Gap analysis is typically used by management to decide on which areas to invest
in within the near future. It can target both customers using the AddPro Denmark Security
offering, or customers without. It is mainly applicable to customers not currently invested
in the AddPro Denmark offerings, but can also be a starting point to recognize potentials
in the offerings, such that security issues can be resolved.
The definition of such an analysis can be seen as that it ”measures actual against ex­
pected results to identify suboptimal or missing strategies, processes, technologies, or
skills.” [120] The analysis can be concrete or conceptual, i.e. if the analysed environment
is a real­world scenario or a hypothetical one with several assumptions. It can also be
scoped to the whole organization or certain operations within the organization, e.g. a
team or department.
10.1.1
Current offering
AddPro Denmark offers a Fit/Gap analysis, in order to provide customer management
with actionable next steps with respect to information technology security.
The offering can be summarized with the following:
1. Depending on the company size, the analysis work is sold as a fixed 10 or 20 hours
of consultancy work
2. The scope is the whole organization, and on the current real­world state of IT within
the organization. Thus, there are only very few assumptions made
3. There is a generic template where findings and details are written in
4. The areas investigated are Network, PC, Server, Backup, Printer, Physical Access,
Mail, Company Data, (Azure) Active Directory Domain and User Rights
5. Each area investigated has their own conclusion, outlines the Gaps (GAP) and sug­
gests changes to fill out the gaps (FIT). Suggestions can be hardware/software ac­
quisition, security hardening or configuration settings. A summary of devices, users,
servers etc. are also provided to let the customer get an inventory of all their digital
assets
6. A final conclusion is provided where recommended changes are prioritized as a
”Must fix”, ”Should fix” or ”Consider if it should be fixed”
The benefit of this analysis is that it covers many aspects of the typical AddPro Denmark
customer, and there is also the option to scope out certain parts, if the customer does not
have on­premise or cloud services. Recommendations can be based on a cyber security
maturity framework, which strengthens the trust in recommendations given, as well as
providing a good transition into the cyber security maturity framework.

10.2
Finding and Implementing a Maturity Framework
There are several frameworks to choose from. We investigate the following 4 to target
larger frameworks, as well as smaller ones to find out which one will accommodate a
customer best:
1. International Organization for Standardization (ISO) ­ ISO27001 [121]
2. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) ­ Cyber Security Framework

3. United Kingdom National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) ­ Cyber Essentials Scheme
[123]
4. Center for Internet Security (CIS) ­ CIS Framework v8 [124]
10.2.1
ISO 27000
ISO27000 is a series of standards related to information security by the International Or­
ganization for Standardization (ISO) ­ the standards are especially concerned with fol­
lowing and managing an Information System Management System (ISMS), and outlines
several areas of information technology where security is applicable. There is a focus
to ensure management onboarding, risk management and take action in terms of imple­
mentations with respect to the risk assessments. ISO27000 is the general introduction to
the standard, and subsequent document provides standards such as the requirements,
implementation controls, guidelines for specific business sectors.
Certification is possible by certified auditors, and is achieved by complying with the ISO27001
standard which sets the requirements. Certification lasts for 3 years at maximum, and
must be renewed afterwards.
10.2.2
NIST Cyber Security Framework
National Institute of Standards and Technology, under the United States Department of
Commerce, provides the Cyber Security Framework, applicable to any organization, irre­
spective of their sector. It consists of a Core, Tier and Profile component. [125]

CIS Framework v8
Center for Internet Security (CIS) provides a cyber security framework that is built upon 18
sets (controls) covering different areas and aspects, including inventory and account man­

(implementations) adhering to that particular control. The safeguards give concrete ex­
amples from products to to help in the implementation. Because of the 18 controls, and
153 safeguards, CIS provides three groupings called Implementation Groups (IGs) such
that organizations can focus on a smaller implementation, and then expand. Hence, IG1

Other frameworks usually go with an approach of performing a risk assessment, and then
implementing certain countermeasures to mitigate or reduce risk. CIS Controls does it
almost the other way around ­ that is, it sets up safeguards that should be implemented
and documented, and if they are to be avoided, then you assess the risk and consider if
safeguard implementation avoidance is a good idea. In short, it does not rely on any risk
assessment beforehand.

11.1.2
ETL
ETL stands for Extract, Transform, Load. There is also another closely related concept
called Extract, Load, Transform (ELT). The former is an older data processing method
where one extracts data, transforms it in various ways (e.g. data scrubbing, filtering, data
type changes) until it is lastly put in a loading stage (ready for consumption by other sys­
tems). The latter, ELT, is a more newer approach, were data is extracted and loaded into
a Data Warehouse (with no to little manipulation done), which is then ready for consump­
tion by different solutions performing the transforming themselves. ELT is more new, and
used widely within the cloud­based data warehouses as it better suits the need to ingest
a large amount of data quickly (e.g. Data Lakes).
The company Rivery provides a good coverage of the differences between them [136],
with some differences being data privacy, regulatory compliance or data ingestion size
related. For our use case, considering the need to only perform nightly imports, with a
not too large data size, and working with the data on a less frequent basis, we go with the
traditional ETL approach. The database is discussed more in detail within its own section.
11.1.3
DWH
This is a custom reporting database designed to suit the reporting needs of AddPro Den­
mark. It differs from the ETL by containing an abstracted model, as well as providing his­
tory (changes over time). The former is a design decision made, such that the database
does not directly depend on acquired solutions, while also providing better support for so­
lution replacement. An example would be if AddPro Denmark were to replace KnowBe4
(KMSAT) with another awareness solution. While we in this chapter focus on the SOC,
the database is also meant to support other areas of AddPro Denmark, e.g. Sales, ERP
and Infrastructure solutions.
As mentioned for the PowerShell parts, the DWH database will have the capabilities to
read from the ETL database ”load” stage tables, import said tables, merge data into the
abstracted model, and ensuring history data is recorded.

11.3
The ETL Database
As briefly mentioned ­ the purpose of the ETL database is to store the extracted data, per­
form some transformations when necessary, and store a ready­to­import dataset which
can be loaded/imported into the data warehouse.
Some initial decisions are made. SQL Server provides an option to make a database
Partially Contained. [139] This option makes the database more authoritative in terms of
validating credentials (Non­contained databases let the instance perform this part, and
usually instance logins are mapped to users within the database.). This containment type
also ensures better portability and less database instance cohesion (decoupling). In terms
of recovery mode it will be ”simple” (meaning no transactions logs), which should suffice
considering it has less importance compared to the DWH database.
The authentication hand­off to the database has both positives and negatives. Some
administrators like to handle this themselves as users are then potentially able to add
new users themselves. For this purpose, it is not much of an issue.
11.3.1
Permissions and schemas
Securing the databases are important, to restrict authorization data and stored procedure
execution. We do this to ensure a ”principle of least privilege” approach. Permissions can,
within the database, be set on a database user or role (group). Each object (e.g. tables,
views, stored procedures) that can be secured by permissions are called ”securables”.
To avoid a high amount of granularity, we perform permissions on schema securables.
Schemas allow for segmenting and structuring objects into their own categories. The de­
rived schemas for the ETL database will be mentioned soon. Microsoft provides a list of
permissions which can be set, and what they do. [140] We create a ”powershell_role” role
and the ”powershell_user” user which is a member of said role. Because of the contained
database feature, we can either make ”powershell_user” a contained, SQL Instance in­
dependent user, or we can create a SQL Instance login, which is then mapped to the user
within the ETL database. The role permissions will be explained later.
As for the schemas, the ETL database shall consist of the following ones:
1. txtract: All raw data from the PowerShell scripts are put into this schema.
2. transform: Extracted data is transformed in several ways (i.e. deduplicated, sani­
tized, data type converted, filtered, UTC timezone normalized)
3. load: The transformed data is put into this schema, ready to finally be imported into
a data warehouse or other potential solution.
4. setup: A special table with rules which may be needed for transforming and loading
data (e.g. for filtering)
Extract schema
For the extract schema, we assign the PowerShell role rights to alter this schema, as well
as execute procedures. To better separate each system in this schema, each table will
Planning, Designing and Implementing a Security Operations Center for a Managed Service Provider
107
==PAGE:116==
have a given prefix (e.g. KnowBe4 has kb4, Rapid7 InsightVM has r7ivm), which we use
to separate and keep track of where the data is coming from.
Transform schema
In the transform schema, each system also has their own prefix. Depending on each
system, there can be one or more stages of data processing. To create processing logic
for each stage, stored procedures are used, which allows for programming logic into a
database, or call functions (which allows for logic on a column level). In order to ensure
easy identification stored procedures are named with the prefix letter ”p”, a system pre­
fix, a 3­digit number and a short description of the actions it is doing (the p stands for
”procedure” to distinguish it from tables). The first procedure may be a cleanup proce­
dure named ”p_r7ivm_005_cleanup”, to reset the transformation process state. The next
procedure could be named ”p_r7ivm_010_data_type”, which references the initial extract
tables of InsightVM, and corrects the data types.
The output is a table called ”r7ivm_010_data_type”. This table may then be an input
to the next transform stage, which could be ”r7ivm_020_deduplicate”. Naturally, every
time data is to be transformed, all the transform schema tables of the particular system
are truncated or deleted beforehand. To support flexible configuration, the setup schema
and tables within may allow for rules in terms of filtering out certain types of data, or mak­
ing settings for procedures.
The PowerShell role can only execute stored procedures herein, as it does not need
to read the data within the transformation stage. Even though procedures may select or
work with data not accessible by the caller (the PowerShell user), ownership chaining en­
sures that this is allowed. [141]
Load schema
Lastly, the final data will be stored in the load schema, ready to import into the DWH
Database. The PowerShell role can perform read operations (SELECT) on these tables,
as well as execute the stored procedures herein. The only function the stored procedure
has, is to take the data from the last transform state of a given system, and put it one­to­
one into a load schema table.

11.4.1
etlimport schema
The data from etl database load schema is put one­to­one into here. To allow the power­
shell user this action, the powershell role has permissions to alter the schema, as well as
execute procedures within. This schema is not related to any of the other schemas.
Another option instead of etlimport tables, would be to create synonyms, and reference
the ETL database load schema tables this way. This would remove the etlimport schema
entirely from the DWH database. However, this approach would make the DWH database

11.4.2
masterdata schema
The masterdata schema contains data which is manually provided by AddPro Denmark.
This includes general customer data, such as company name, business identification

viously used, including the type of system. The last data is a mapping table, which maps
the masterdata customer to the applicable sourcesystems of that particular customer.
This way, we also allow to map to two or more instances of the same sourcesystem to
one masterdata customer, if that case should be applicable.
11.4.3
soc schema
We use the soc schema as a way to divide data relevant to each department. Within
the soc schema, we have the abstractions for our sourcesystems, which we abstract as
”awareness”, ”ir (incident response)” and ”vm (vulnerability management)”. Concretiz­
ized, that would be KnowBe4, InsightIDR and InsightVM respectively. For all abstraction,
there is a customer table, which contains the identifications of the customer within the
respective sourceystem. For awareness, we have training, phishing and user tables. For
vulnerability management, we have assets and vulnerabilities. For incident response, we
have cases (i.e. investigations, incidents). Initially since we only have one sourcesys­
tem under each abstraction, the table columns will be similar to that of the sourcesystem
columns.
Attribute tables
Over time, we do expect abstractions to be an issue when it comes sourcesystem replace­
ment or addition. What if there are two sourcesystems for a given abstraction, and one
sourcesystem provides data not found in the other sourcesystem? Or what if we outright
replace the sourcesystem with another? To tackle these issues, we add an ”attribute”
table for certain tables, including the asset, case, training and phishing tables.
To give an example for awareness and the phishing table. The attribute table contains a
column for a foreign key pointing to the matching row in the phishing table, as well as a
key and value column. Whenever we have columns from an awareness sourcesystem not
applicable within the columns in the abstracted model, we can transpose these columns
into rows, and place the data into the attribute table. If we then ever need to retrieve
that data back, we can revert the transposition. Within SQL, there are different ways to
perform this transposition, including a UNION ALL, aggregate and CASE version. It can
also be done with built­in functions for pivoting and un­pivoting. [142]

11.4.5
Creating historic data
When data is imported into the data warehouse, we need to ensure that there is history.
Reasoning for this, is that we want to keep track of changes over time, which is most
beneficial to reporting scenarios. For instance, we want to track an asset in terms of vul­
nerabilities, and check over time that these are remediated.
One approach to tracking changes, is creating a separate history table, creating triggers
on the current table, which will make an entry within the history table, containing the
changes. The issue with this approach, is that it requires more manual work to setup, and
one might forget to add any new form of changes. Another newer approach, supported
from Microsoft SQL Server 2016 and onwards, is the use of Temporal Tables. [143]. Since
we use SQL Server 2019 and newer, this feature is applicable to use.

row entries. This is the case, even if columns on both target and source contain identical
data. The consequence of this, is that the temporal table will detect an UPDATE and thus
adding redundant history to it.

An important area of the thesis, was the upcoming NIS2 directive. As of december 2022,

2. Entity should be at least ”medium­sized” or exceed ceilings of ”medium­sized” enti­

Paragraph 2 explains other entities that are covered as well, regardless of their size. For
the most part, we shall be concerned with only the first paragraph.

finding a better definition. The European Commission does fortunately provide such a
table. [146] From this we can deduce that a medium sized organization is determined by
three factors:
1. Employee count between 50 and 249
2. Annual turnover less than 50 million EUR

12.2.1
The remaining cost
The abovementioned costs may provide a fair estimate for onboarding and some main­
tenance. However, there are also other significant costs associated with operating the
cyber security offerings, some of which include:
1. Employee onboarding/offboarding. Every time an employee starts or stops, a cost
of hours is needed
2. Incident handling. Whenever an alert is triggered, there is a cost in hours in order
to investigate and handle potential incidents derived from the alerts.
3. Vulnerability Management. There is a cost in hours in order to remediate any found
vulnerabilities that are not automatically covered in regular patch management.
4. Staffing. We are missing the ”People” element of the PPT triad. That is, an organi­
zation must also have the human resources capable of running the operations. In
most instances, it is a qualified person with cyber security competencies.
For our chapters, we opted not to include these estimates, as we wanted to focus on
the processes that were described in each chapter instead. There is also the issue that
numbers can vary significantly on a customer by customer basis. We shall try and discuss
them below, in order to calculate a new estimate with the considerations.
Minimum seat/asset license count
In these estimates, we take for granted that an organization can go out a purchase the
exact amount of licenses in order to cover the whole organization. This is not always
possible, and can therefore impact the costs.
For Security Awareness and KMSAT, we mentioned the minimum seat count of 25. Af­
terwards, seat increments and decrements must be done on with 5 seats at a time. For
Rapid7 InsightIDR and InsightVM, there is an estimated minimum of 500 assets.
We will not go further into this issue, and still count with the assumption that customers
can buy the exact license amount necessary. This does, however, indicate the need of
a Managed Service Provider partnership, because of bulk licence purchase capabilities,
which can cover a significant amount of customer companies under a 500 minimum asset
count.

12.3
The importance of risk management
Risk management has not been broadly covered in this thesis. We have mostly discussed
a proposed offering model, shown that we can implement it, and estimate a cost associ­
ated with the implementation. Even more so, it is not explicitly mentioned in our selected
cyber security maturity framework CIS Controls. Some frameworks also exist for manag­
ing riks, most notable may be the ISO31000 standard [150] or NIST Risk Management
Framework (RMF) [151].
That being said, the assessment is from a NIS2 directive perspective an important (essen­
tially mandatory) aspect to cover. Reviewing the previously mentioned Article 21, and then
paragraph 1, explains that member states should ensure that scoped entities ”take appro­
priate and proportionate technical, operational and organisational measures to manage
the risks posed to the security of network and information systems which those entities
use for their operations or for the provision of their services, and to prevent or minimise
the impact of incidents on recipients of their services and on other services.”. What this
effectively means, is that any measures taken must be argued for from a risk assessment
perspective. Outlined in paragraph 2, the different hazards should take into account many
of the different measures explained under our other discussion section, regarding NIS2
Directive Compliance 12.1.
Beyond the above, a risk assessment is also a great way to identify risks that may or
may not fall under other types of legislation, e.g. fire and environment hazards. The im­
portant output of a risk assessment is and the cost associated with said incidents, and how
often they may occur. If an organization can deduce a risk assessment which shows an
annual potential cost equal to or higher than the total costs given in figure 12.8, then the
offerings that AddPro Denmark provides may be a good consideration. Going back to the
introduction and the PurpleSec provided numbers [2], an average cost of a data breach is
at minimum 120.000USD (840.000DKK with a USD to DKK conversion rate of 7). If this
would occur on an annual basis, our offerings could then be a potentially good investment.
In short, we do recognize the great importance of a risk assessment, especially con­
sidering the above. We opted not to do any detailed description of risk assessments in
the thesis, as the risk varies significantly from organization to organization.

12.4.1
CIS Controls Improvement
Currently, none of the employees at AddPro Denmark are certified in the CIS Controls im­

Another feature that was discovered for the Vulnerability Management solution InsightVM,
is that it also allows to perform CIS benchmarks [153]. This means if the customer has
both Vulnerability Management and interest in Security Maturity analysis, we can poten­
tially combine the two in order to ensure better compliance. It may even be of interest to
combine the offerings into a single offering in the future.

12.4.2
Security Awareness Webhook API
Sometime after development of the Security Awareness polling module, it was discov­
ered that in november 2022, the webhook feature of KnowBe4 KMSAT was released. It

12.4.3
PowerShell module signing
Considering our heavy reliance on PowerShell modules, scripts, it would be a good se­
curity measure to attain a code signing certificate, to sign these (It is also called Authenti­
Code in some instances). Code signing makes them tamper resistant, as well as verifying
that they do come from AddPro Denmark. The signature is added as a comment block at
the bottom of the scripts, which is then validated upon script execution. This can either
be done either
1. With a self­signed code sign certificate under a self­signed certificate authority, and
adding the authority to the trust store of servers running the scripts
2. Attaining a code sign certificate from a globally recognized authority
When signing, we shall ensure that a digitally signed timestamp is included, which en­
sures the script code signature does not expire, when the certificate expires.
Microsoft provides documentation on how to perform the signing, which can all be done
with PowerShell commands [155].
12.4.4
Data polling and Data extraction
Currently for Rapid7 InsightVM and KnowBe4, we both have data polling for data going
into Autotask, and Data extraction for reporting. It may be of interest to merge these two
solutions together, since both solutions do almost the same. In order to support both
systems, an additional call parameter can be introduced, indicating the target destination
(Autotask or ETL Database). The benefits of such a merge means code size reduction
and code maintenance reduction.
Additionally, there are possible improvements when invoking the Invoke­RestMethod in
PowerShell. Because PowerShell 7.0 an onwards support running pipelines in parallel
[156] it may be possible to create a parallel instance for each customer, instead of fetch­
ing customer API data one­by­one (in sequence). In the beginning with a few customers it
may not be as significant, unless the datasat of each customer is significantly large. If we
suppose a case where fetching system API data for a single customer takes 2 minutes,
and we have 5 customers, this would take 10 minutes. Performing this in parallel would
reduce the total fetching time to 2 minutes. In order to support it, it must be considered if
a transition from PowerShell 5.1 to 7.0 can be performed.

relate data between several systems for the customer. This is extremely beneficial for
both AddPro Denmark and the customer, in that a great overview can be provided from a
single data source.

The current state of the Data Warehouse is that manual work must still be done to take
the data, and put into a report. The usual scenario is to utilize Microsoft Excel, connect
to the SQL Server to extract tables, views and then send the Excel file to the customer.
Initially this will be fine for a few customers, but scalability wise it is an issue.
To tackle this issue, we initially investigated the use of Microsoft PowerBI, which is a
solution for creating graphs, tables and more, which can be put into dashboards and re­
ports. While the per user license is 10 USD or 20 USD depending on the tier, none of
these supports exporting PDF files over the PowerBI API. In order to do so, a per capacity
license, costing 5000EUR per month is required. [157] This cost is significantly high, and
PowerBI was therefore not investigated further.
Thus, the current investigations are now pointing towards Microsoft Power Automate,
which is available for free. It enables to perform different automated actions, and can be
run ad­hoc or on a schedule. It integrates with many Microsoft 365 solutions, including
SharePoint (where we store files). It was found that there are capabilities in Microsoft
Word, which allows to create templates. The templates have identifiers for places where
data must be inserted. Power Automate can handle these identifiers, and therefore insert
data from a data source into the given identifier.
Additionally, Power Automate can take Word files and not only produce PDF­files from
these, but also send attached files over e­mail (Microsoft Exchange Online).

Automation

administrative overhead, so we instead can focus on the critical parts, i.e. investigations,

removes a lot of the overhead with producing tickets within Autotask, as well as manually
having to look over each customer tenant, in order to keep track of incidents, vulnerabili­
ties and more.
In addition to the above, by creating the reporting system, we are able on a monthly
basis to keep customers up­to­date on their current state within our offerings, with little
manual work involved. This also frees time needed to work on other SOC operations, like
the ones described above.
There may still be possibilities in automating parts of operations, including incident han­
dling. Specific incidents could be handled under a Security Orchestration, Automation
and Response. Given that our current focus was on the onboarding and managing of
several customer companies, this has not been given much thought.
Onboarding procedures may also be more automated. Currently, some aspects are laid
out in a more formal manner (recipes), and the next step here would be to look even more
into automating these recipes.
As we also briefly discussed for reporting, some additional measures can be taken in
order to automate the report creation process.

Conclusion
The following report has been based on the 5 month long work of a Masters Thesis, with
the goal being to plan, design and implement a Security Operations Center for AddPro
Denmark, a Managed Service Provider.
An introduction was given, highlighting the global state of cybercrime, and narrowing it
down to the state within Denmark and Small to Medium Businesses. The results showed
a yearly rise in incidents and author thoughts were given, pointing out reasons for the
increase. Additionally, upcoming legislation within the European Union, the NIS2 Direc­
tive, was also introduced. The findings both validated and initiated the thesis work, as
well as the startup a new department within AddPro Denmark ­ the Security Operations
Center. Challenges with the new department were identified, including technology ac­
quisition, customer onboard processes, Security Incident management cost estimates. A
scope was provided, and thesis contributions were set forth.
An introduction to AddPro Denmark was given, and theory regarding a Security Oper­
ations Center was described, including what activities are seen within. A model, Central­
ized SOC with local liaisons, was selected as starting point for structuring the SOC. In
order to place the SOC within Addpro Denmark, organizational structure information was
set forth such that placement options could be described. Internally used solutions were
also described.
The business model were outlined and explained, as well as investigating the typical cus­
tomer setup. The results found for instance, that a majority of companies are within the
SMB range, and there is a primary use of Microsoft products, including the use of Mi­
crosoft cloud service products, e.g. Microsoft 365.
The current value offerings of AddPro Denmark were analyzed, and a new offering was
given with Core Cyber Security, Pentesting, Security Awareness, Vulnerability Manage­
ment, Maturity Analysis and Incident Response in mind. Documentation and version con­
trol of these could be done in Azure DevOps. To establish a better context in the upcoming
chapters, the Professional Service Automation system, Autotask PSA, system was briefly
described.
For the Security Core offering, five technologies were further laid out in terms of onboard­
ing and the reasoning behind these. The technologies in question were Microsoft 365
Business Premium and Defender for Endpoint, Multi­Factor authentication in Azure AD,
Datto SaaS backup, Datto Remote Management and Monitoring and Fortigate Firewall
setup. A cost estimate was deduced based on a 25 employee sized organization, based
on a customer wishing to setup the offering by themselves.
A separate chapter for given for selecting a SIEM solution, and several criteria/require­
ments for a Managed Service Provider. Different vendors and their solutions were in­
vestigated based on these criteria. The results we summed up, and showed that Rapid7
InsightIDR would suit best for a SIEM solution, having a hybrid cloud architecture and
reasonable pricing. It was found to be missing a single pane of glass (SPOG) feature.

Enterprise Asset Inventory
Establish and maintain an accurate, detailed, and up-to-date 
inventory of all enterprise assets with the potential to store or 
process data, to include: end-user devices (including portable 
and mobile), network devices, non-computing/IoT devices, and

infrastructure physically, virtually, remotely, and those within 
cloud environments. Additionally, it includes assets that are 
regularly connected to the enterprise’s network infrastructure, 
even if they are not under control of the enterprise. Review and 
update the inventory of all enterprise assets bi-annually, or more 
frequently.
