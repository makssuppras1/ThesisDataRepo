Abstract 
 
The energy transition scenarios require a wide range of critical minerals for the deployment of 
renewable energy technologies. The spatial concentration of these minerals’ extraction and processing 
and the demand increase pose supply risks. The extraction and processing operations represent a major 
share of these technologies’ life-cycle socio-environmental impacts. With the Green Deal, the European 
Union announced a set of regulations to achieve a socially fair energy transition and reach its climate goals. 
The supply chains of minerals for the energy transition in the European Union tend to be located outside 
of Europe. The dynamics of all these supply chains regarding the supply and socio-environmental risks for 
the energy transition specifically are unknown. Here we show these risks threaten the success of the 
energy transition in the European Union. More than 65% of the footprint weight of these minerals for the 
energy transition is located outside Europe. Europe’s share in this footprint is expected to decrease as 
climate goals ambition increases. The requirements in lithium, nickel, and cobalt for the energy transition 
in 2040 overtake the actual total European Union footprint for these minerals. The greenhouse gas 
emissions of mining sectors for the energy transition are spread worldwide but hotspots are identified 
such as Australia and China. Our results demonstrate the need for detailed analyses of the European Union 
supply chains on minerals for the energy transition. These analyses must not only focus on minerals with 
a high supply risk but also on identified hotspots for socio-environmental impacts. The coupling of supply 
risk with outsourced socio-environmental impacts is needed to inform efficient policymaking.

1. Introduction  
It has now become accepted with the sixth assessment report from the Intergovernmental Panel 
on Climate Change (IPCC) that climate has experienced unprecedented changes in the last decades and 
that human activity is responsible for these changes (Masson-Delmotte et al., n.d.). This dramatic situation 
calls for new policies to limit global warming to below 2°C above pre-industrial levels as per the Paris 
agreement. With the covid-19 pandemic, we are now at a cornerstone in the redefinition of our politics to 
meet our climate goals (Kuzemko et al., 2020). The International Energy Agency (IEA) together with the 
International Monetary Fund designed a Sustainable Recovery Plan to recover from the economic crisis 
caused by the covid-19 pandemic by building cleaner and more secure energy systems (IEA, 2020a). 
The roadmaps developed by the IEA and the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) to 
meet the world climate objectives are highly relying on the electrification of end uses and a large 
deployment of renewable energies (IEA, 2021a; IRENA, 2021). The IEA identified a set of technologies, 
referred to as “clean energy technologies” which include low-carbon power generation (solar PV, wind, 
other renewables and nuclear), electricity networks, electric vehicles and battery storage, and hydrogen 
(electrolyzers and fuel cells). These technologies require a wide range of minerals and are often more 
mineral intensive than their traditional counterparts (IEA, 2021b). This IEA report claims an electric vehicle 
needs six times the mineral inputs of a conventional car. This higher mineral intensity threatens the 
deployment of electric vehicles for the energy transition. (Ballinger et al., 2019; Hawkins et al., 2013; IEA, 
2021b). Awareness and concerns are rising over the future access to the minerals and metals needed for 
such a transition (Gielen & Papa, 2021; Watari et al., 2019). According to the Organization for Economic 
Co-operation and Development (OECD) predictions, this shift toward a more mineral-intensive energy 
system is anticipated to occur in a global economy of declining material intensity but increasing primary 
materials use (OECD, 2019). 
The minerals needed for the energy system transformation are in great majority extracted, 
transformed, and processed in a limited number of countries. In 2019, Australia, Chile, and China were 
together responsible for more than 80% of global lithium extraction. The same geographical concentration 
was observed for cobalt and rare earth elements (REEs) extraction and processing (IEA, 2021b). The lack 
of diversity in these minerals’ supply chains is expected to exert increasing pressure on the downwards 
value chains in the coming years (Eurometaux, 2019). The IEA and the IRENA released reports highlighting 
the criticality of lithium, cobalt, nickel, copper, REEs, and 27 other minerals in terms of supply security and 
sustainability (Gielen, 2021; IEA, 2021b). The extraction and processing of these minerals also pose 
ecological and social threats. While primary mining and metal production represented 10% of global 
greenhouse gas emissions in 2018, these emissions are expected to increase in the coming years driven by 
higher demand and a lower ore grade (Azadi et al., 2020). Environmental impacts of mineral production 
are not limited to global warming potential but also relate to resource and water use or acidification and 
eutrophication potential as others (Balaram, 2019). Studies have shown their magnitude varies with the 
place and methods of extraction. (Jiang et al., 2020; Marx et al., 2018). The impacts at the mineral 
production stages largely contribute to the end-product life-cycle environmental impacts as highlighted 
by the examples of REEs in permanent magnets (Marx et al., 2018) and lithium in batteries (Jiang et al., 
2020). Social threats are also high for these materials production, with forced labor and child labor risks. 
They lead to complex social issues as illustrated by the artisanal small-scale mining (ASM) in the cobalt 
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
supply chain in Congo (Baumann-Pauly, 2020; Faber et al., 2017).  Recycling can bring benefits both in 
supply security and in socio-environmental impacts. However, according to a World Bank study, recycling 
to the highest extent possible (a 100% end-of-life recycling rate) in 2050 would not prevent new 
investments in primary supply (Nguyen, 2020). Regarding batteries, for example, the IEA estimates that 
recycling could reduce by 10% the primary supply requirements of combined cobalt, nickel, copper, and 
lithium in 2040 (IEA, 2021b). This increase in raw materials extraction is expected to drive an increased 
environmental and social cost. This issue is even more problematic for critical materials such as lithium 
and REEs for which very few alternative supply chains exist. These environmental and social impacts 
threaten the efficiency of current policies to transition towards a new energy system with a lower carbon 
intensity. 
To address this issue and explore the socio-environmental impacts of the energy transition, 
research has been conducted highlighting the impacts of ore extraction and minerals. The case of lithium 
extraction is well known but so far research has been limited to production-oriented impacts of the 
extraction and processing stages be it in Portugal or Chile (Chaves et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2019; Liu & 
Agusdinata, 2020). According to the published scenarios, the transition of energy systems is going to 
extend to all world regions in the coming decades (IEA, 2021a; IRENA, 2021). The global extension of these 
minerals’ consumption combined with the high international degree of their supply chains and the 
geographical concentration of reserves undermine the efficiency of production-based approaches to 
address the socio-environmental impacts of their extraction and production. The design of energy and 
environmental policies should therefore rely on consumption-based approaches to capture the impacts of 
the energy system transition throughout the value chain (Tukker et al., 2018). 
Multi-Regional Input-Output (MRIO) models provide an appropriate framework for consumption-
based approaches. These models capture the global economy with input-output tables that allow linking 
supply chains to the final demand, no matter their degree of internationalization and decentralization 
(Lenzen et al., 2012; Tukker & Dietzenbacher, 2013). Their environmental extension captures the 
environmental impacts of the global economy (Stadler et al., 2018) and it allows to calculate the socio-
environmental impacts of global trade by tracing the global impacts of local consumption (Hubacek et al., 
2016; Wiedmann & Lenzen, 2018). Environmentally extended MRIO models (EEMRIO) are used to calculate 
the footprints of households’ (Ivanova et al., 2016), nations’ or world regions’ consumption on a wide 
range of stressors among which carbon, land, water, and materials (Tukker et al., 2016).  The latter stressor 
is of particular interest for this study, but current material footprints lack enough disaggregation to 
efficiently inform policies (Giljum et al., 2016).  
To address these limitations, this study combines available environmentally extended satellites 
with detailed data on the mineral production and mineral requirements for the energy transition. By 
combining these data sources, this study presents a detailed mineral footprint and studies the part of this 
footprint specifically directed to the energy transition. Associating current mineral requirements for the 
energy transition and the projected requirements for 2030 and 2040, the study gives a picture of how this 
footprint is expected to change given the current international value chains. Finally, the socio-
environmental impacts of mining sectors worldwide associated with these different footprints are 
calculated. The study takes the European Union (EU) as a study case. To transition its energy system, the 
EU announced the European Green Deal defining a set of actions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 
55% below 1990 levels by 2030 and to reach climate neutrality by 2050 (European Commission, 2019). 
==PAGE:12==
3 
 
According to the EU, one objective is to achieve a socially fair transition of the energy system besides the 
climate goals. However, minerals extraction and processing supply chains tend to be increasingly located 
out of the EU (Eurometaux, 2019; Marx et al., 2018). In 2020 the European Union released its last report 
on Critical Raw Materials to analyze the supply risk and economic importance of more than 80 minerals 
(European Commission, 2020b) in a general effort to secure a sustainable supply of raw materials and to 
increase the EU’s resilience to these supply chains (European Commission, 2020a). The environmental and 
social impacts of these supply chains must be studied to ensure the energy transition will achieve all the 
objectives announced by the Green Deal(European Commission, 2019). 
The method to calculate footprint with MRIO models is presented in section 2. Section 3 presents 
the overall method of the study with a detailed presentation of the approach used to combine the input-
output databases with the mineral production data and mineral requirements for the energy transition. 
Results are presented and discussed in section 4. It is first presented how the EU’s material footprint is 
changing when going from an ore extraction level to a mineral level. Then, the part of the footprint driven 
by the energy transition is presented with an estimation of what this part could become in 2030 and 2040. 
Finally, the changes in the energy transition footprint are linked with the associated socio-environmental 
impacts.

2. MRIO models for mineral footprints 
2.1. General Introduction to MRIO models 
 
Multi-Regional Input-Output models are a global extension of so-called Input-Output (IO) models. W. 
Leontief first studied these models during the 1930s when he developed a quantitative economic model 
illustrating the relations between economic sectors in the United States (W. Leontief, 1936). W. Leontief 
has also been a pioneer in using the IO approach of the economy to quantify the environmental impacts 
of consumption (W. Leontief, 1970). These IO models later led to the computational general equilibrium 
models, widely used in economic science, by adding a dimension of responses to perturbations when IO 
models only capture a static view of the global economy.  
An IO model in its simplest form is a system of linear equations representing the distribution of 
industries’ products throughout the economy. This system of equations is built from observed economic 
data. It represents the flows from each industry considered as producers to other industries including itself 
considered as consumers. These flows illustrate the interdependencies between the industries, and they 
together constitute the transactions table of IO models. The columns represent the goods consumed by 
each industry (inputs) while the rows represent the goods produced by each industry (outputs). Apart from 
the inter-industries transaction table, two tables compose the IO models: the final demand table and the 
value-added table. The final demand table represents the consumption of goods on final markets such as 
consumption by households. The value-added table gathers the non-industrial inputs such as labour and 
capital. The flows are usually expressed in monetary values. The IO models can be extended to more 
sectors to better describe the whole economy of a single country. They can also be extended to several 
countries; in which case they are called MRIO models. MRIO models capture flows in the same way as for 
a single country economy as illustrated in figure 1. The inputs are either domestic inputs or imported from 
other countries. Similarly, outputs can be directed to other sectors of the same country, exported to 
sectors of other countries, directed to the domestic final demand, or even exported to satisfy the final 
demand of other countries. The diagonal of the MRIO transaction matrix is composed of the domestic IO 
tables. In environmentally-extended models, the environmental externalities are included in the model as 
value-added inputs. In the same way any industry would require the input of a certain labour quantity to 
produce goods, in an environmentally-extended MRIO, any industry requires a certain input of greenhouse 
gas emissions or water consumption to produce goods.

2.3. Material footprints with MRIO models 
Because the MRIO models capture the interdependencies between the economic sectors, they 
allow linking the final demand to the upwards supply chain no matter their complexity and degree of 
internationalization (Lutter et al., 2016). As introduced in section 2.2, it allows calculating footprints for 
any environmental stressor included in the input-output satellites. An example of a stressor is the raw 
material extraction that led to dedicated nation’s footprint calculations (O et al., 2015). The majority of 
the material footprints focused on nations or regions (Giljum et al., 2016), and only a few are targeting a 
specific sector such as electric vehicles (Sen et al., 2019). The energy transition would benefit from the 
calculation of dedicated material footprints to better inform policy-making. The scenarios from the IEA 
were integrated with Exiobase to estimate future environmental footprints but the material footprint is 
still kept at an aggregated ore level (Wiebe et al., 2018). Actual material stressors in MRIO models only 
allow for calculating footprints on all aggregated materials extraction or a couple of different ores. 
The footprints realized with MRIO models are based on monetary flows which are assumed 
proportional to physical flows. The latter assumption is not always verified especially when different 
products or industries are aggregated in a single industrial sector. Hybrid models coupling monetary flows 
with physical flows are developed to increase the footprints' accuracy (Bruckner et al., 2015; Giljum et al., 
2019; Ye et al., 2022). The level of sectoral (number of industries per country) and spatial (number of 
countries or regions) disaggregation is an important factor in the precision of footprint calculations with 
IO models since their structure assumes a homogenous product output per aggregated sector. It means 
products with different environmental impacts are aggregated in a single category with a single 
environmental impact value. Increasing the sectoral and spatial disaggregation has been a major field of 
research for IO databases (Lenzen et al., 2013; Stadler et al., 2018). The input-output approach assumes 
all economic transactions, and ultimately the material use, are driven by final demand (Lutter et al., 2016). 
Furthermore, this view of the economy is only static with no reaction of the global economy to changes in 
the demand nor reaction of the final demand to changes in the global economic structure (Lutter et al., 
2016). Finally, the MRIO databases present data deviations from one to another depending on their 
sectoral and spatial disaggregation and the method used to integrate the economic data in a balanced 
input-output table. These data deviations are particularly impacting the final footprint results when it 
comes to primary sectors such as the mining sectors (Giljum et al., 2019).  
This study targets several gaps previously mentioned. The MRIO models are combined with 
detailed mineral production data to calculate footprints at the mineral production level instead of the ore 
extraction. The new disaggregation level on minerals and countries brought by this study allows to 
specifically capture the critical minerals for the energy transition. The study also integrates mineral 
requirements data from the IEA. It allows for calculating footprints of the energy transition uses only 
despite the sectoral aggregation of input-output tables. Finally, the calculations are run with two different 
input-output databases and three different years of study to balance data deviations due to year-specific 
events or economic data integration in the models. Eora and Exiobase were respectively selected for their 
high sectoral and spatial disaggregation and better coverage of environmental indicators such as raw 
materials extraction (Giljum et al., 2019). However, this study is still subject to limitations inherent to MRIO 
models such as the static view of the economy. The projections made following scenarios of the IEA are 
projections assuming all other things equal. It especially assumes the international trade structure in 2030 
or 2040 would be the same as for the years used to run the MRIO models (2017-2019).

The overall methodology is summarized in Figure 2 to Figure 4. The calculations at each step are 
briefly explained in this section and detailed in the following sections. 
The first step of the study presented in Figure 2 consists of calculating the footprint of the 
European Union's consumption both in terms of ore extraction and in terms of mineral production. The 
satellites of the input-output databases used in this study (Eora and Exiobase) capture the ore extraction. 
The mineral production data obtained from World Mining Data (WMD) and the United States Geological 
Survey (USGS) was combined with the existing satellites to allow the calculation of a mineral footprint 
(Reichl & Schatz, 2021; U.S. Geological Survey, 2021). Because the MRIO models capture static views of 
the economy (see section 2.3), the footprint accuracy is increased by calculating it on three consecutive 
years to avoid capturing year-specific distortions of the global economy. For the same reasons of accuracy, 
the years of study are chosen to be 2017, 2018, and 2019 to get the most recent possible picture without 
the effects of covid on international trade. 
The second step consists of estimating the part of the two previously calculated footprints directed 
to clean energy technologies. The current mineral requirements for the clean energy technologies in the 
EU were derived from the global mineral requirements for the clean energy technologies published by the 
IEA (IEA, 2021b). The results obtained from the mineral footprint calculated in step 1 were compared with 
these mineral requirements for the EU to obtain the part of the footprint driven by the energy transition. 
The IEA report also gives the mineral requirements for clean energy technologies in the years 2030 and 
2040 following two scenarios for the energy systems transformation: the Stated Policies Scenario (STEPS) 
and the Sustainable Development Scenario (SDS) (IEA, 2020b). Therefore, the study also calculates the part 
of today’s EU footprint represented by the clean energy technologies in 2030 and 2040 following these 
scenarios. 
Finally, the socio-environmental impacts driven by the ore extraction for the clean energy 
technologies in the EU are calculated. The environmentally-extended input-output databases used in the 
study, allow us for calculating environmental impacts from the financial outputs of industrial sectors. The 
financial output of mining sectors worldwide corresponding to the ore extraction for the production of 
minerals used in clean energy technologies in the EU is first estimated. The calculation of social impacts is 
performed by integrating data from the social hotspots database (C. B. Norris et al., 2013) into the MRIO 
models. 
 
3.2. Minerals scoped in the study 
The IEA identified 35 minerals as critical for the energy transition in its report published in May 
2021 (IEA, 2021b). The critical criteria given by the IEA is based on their essential character for what they 
define as clean energy technologies: low-carbon power generation (solar PV, wind, other renewables, and 
nuclear), electricity networks, electric vehicles and battery storage, and hydrogen (electrolyzers and fuel 
cells). Among those 35 minerals, 21 are considered critical by the European Commission (European 
Commission, 2020b). The European Commission defines a material as critical based on two different 
parameters: its economic importance and the risk of potential disruption in its supply. Among the 35 
minerals identified as critical by the IEA, arsenic is considered of low economic importance and the 
supplying risk of 13 others (such as copper, lead, and nickel) is considered not significant. The study focuses 
on 34 of these 35 minerals. Arsenic was not considered due to a combined difficult integration to the 
models and low importance in the footprint calculation.

A mineral is defined by (United States Geological Survey, n.d.) as “a naturally occurring inorganic 
element or compound having an orderly internal structure and characteristic chemical composition, crystal 
form, and physical properties”. A mineral can be metallic or non-metallic. Metallic minerals are minerals 
containing one or more metals that are “characterized by high electrical and thermal conductivity as well 
as by malleability, ductility, and high reflectivity of light” (Britannica, n.d.-a). Minerals are usually not found 
alone but aggregated in what are called ores, from which they can be mined and processed. According to 
Britannica (Britannica, n.d.-b), the word ore has for long been restricted to metallic mineral deposits, but 
the term is now expanded to include non-metallic minerals. The latter meaning will be used in this study.  
3.3. The EU mineral footprint (Step 1) 
The first step consists in calculating the footprint of the total European Union demand in terms of 
mineral production for the 34 scoped minerals. This footprint is calculated from the ore extraction 
footprint. Satellites of both MRIO models used in this study already capture the ore extraction following 
the stressors presented in Table 2. The two models also allow capturing the final demand of the European 
Union as the 27 countries of the European Union are included individually. The ore footprint is directly 
calculated with the method presented in section 2.2. The MRIO models indirectly capture the mineral 
production through the ore mining and processing industrial sectors, but the footprint calculation required 
the mineral production weight which is not available. Therefore, the MRIO databases were combined with 
two external data sources for mineral production. The data from WMD, a yearly publication from the 
Austrian Federal Ministry of Agriculture, Regions, and Tourism listing the production of 65 mineral 
commodities from 168 countries (Reichl & Schatz, 2021) was cross-checked with the commodities data 
published by the United States Geological Survey (USGS) when available (U.S. Geological Survey, 2021). 
The mineral production data were gathered for each of the 34 minerals for every country worldwide and 
the 3 years considered in the study (2017, 2018, and 2019). The dataset finally obtained is summarized in 
Appendix A: Minerals global production figures 2017 - 2019. 
Each mineral is associated with an existing ore category to be combined with the ore footprint 
calculated with the MRIO databases. This association is summarized in Appendix B: Association of minerals 
to corresponding ore categories in MRIO satellites for both models. Several ore categories were 
disaggregated on Exiobase to increase the precision of the study. This was not performed for Eora because 
of differences in the sectorial disaggregation between countries making it more difficult. The categories 
“Other non-ferrous metallic ores”, “Iron ores, “Gravel and sand”, and “Other chemical and fertilizers 
==PAGE:20==
11 
 
minerals” were each disaggregated among ores specific to the minerals studied and a category gathering 
the remaining ore weight. The method for this disaggregation is detailed in Appendix C: Disaggregation of 
ore categories in Exiobase satellites. 
The ore footprint calculated in step 1 gives the total extracted ore weight per ore and per 
extracting country finally consumed in the European Union. This footprint can also be expressed as the 
part of the ore extraction per country and per ore type which is directed to the EU consumption. The 
mineral footprint is obtained by multiplying the mineral production weight per producing country 
(obtained from WMD and USGS sources) by the ratio of the EU consumption of the corresponding ore for 
this country (obtained from the ore footprint). However, as illustrated in Figure 2, a country can extract 
ore without producing minerals afterward or inversely. The first situation where a country extracts the ore 
and another country, also extracting this ore, processes it to produce the minerals, is indifferently captured 
by the method applied here. It means the ore ratio of the producing country (which does not necessarily 
match the ratio of the extracting country) is used to determine the part of the mineral production finally 
consumed in the EU. Referring to the illustration in Figure 2, it means the ratio of the ore extracted per 
country A directed to the EU will be applied to the total mineral production of country A disregarding 
whether the ore used for mineral production is coming from country A itself or country B. In the second 
situation, illustrated by country C in Figure 2, there is no ore ratio to apply since the producing country is 
not himself an extracting country. Two ratios can be applied, either the ratio of the producing country 
(country C) of another ore or the ratio of the same ore from another extracting country (or even the 
average ratio for this ore worldwide). As illustrated in Figure 5, the ratios are mainly depending on the 
location rather than on the ore type. Therefore, the first solution was adopted and the ore ratio of the 
same country for another ore is taken. Figure 5 also shows that discrepancies brought by the 
approximation made in the first situation are limited if the two countries are located in the same region 
or at least both located in or outside Europe. The different ratios estimated from the ore footprint are 
applied to the mineral production data to obtain the mineral footprint. The final result is the mineral 
quantity produced per mineral type and country for final consumption in the EU. 
The application of a ratio calculated per ore assumes the part of a mineral production directed to 
the EU demand is equal to the part of its ore extraction directed to the same demand. Implicitly it considers 
two different minerals produced from the same ore in the same country or region also share the same 
ratio for the part directed to the EU consumption. Copper ores are for example both used for copper 
production and selenium production as a by-product. Copper is used in a wide range of end-uses in the 
electrical, construction, and transport sectors. On the other hand, selenium is mainly used in electronics 
and photovoltaic cells. Because selenium end-uses are less diverse and more specific to developed 
economies, one can expect the part of selenium production directed to the EU consumption is higher than 
the one for copper metal. However, the method used in this study does not capture this difference in 
ratios between minerals produced from the same ore.

3.4. The EU mineral footprint for clean energy technologies (Step 2) 
As presented in section 2.2, the MRIO models can capture the footprint of any final demand 
vector. Therefore, one could use these models to only calculate the footprint of the European Union 
demand from industry sectors corresponding to the clean energy technologies. However, due to the 
model’s limitations mentioned in section 2.3, this method is not reliable due to the uncertainties and 
discrepancies of input-output tables. Instead, the footprint of the EU demand for clean energy 
technologies is estimated from the footprint of the total EU demand combined with mineral requirements 
data and energy systems transition scenarios published by the IEA (IEA, 2020b, 2021b).  
The IEA published the mineral requirements for clean energy technologies in 2020, 2030, and 
2040. The requirements for 2030 and 2040 are based on two energy transition scenarios developed by the 
IEA: Stated Policies Scenario (STEPS) and Sustainable Development Scenario (SDS) (IEA, 2021b). The STEPS 
reflects the current policies both in place and announced by the governments. This scenario would lead to 
a 2.6°C temperature rise in 2100 compared to pre-industrial levels (IEA, 2020b). The SDS considers 
advanced economies will reach net zero emissions by 2050, China will reach it by 2060, and all other 
countries by 2070. It leads to a temperature rise of 1.6°C in 2100 (IEA, 2020b). The mineral requirements 
for clean energy technologies in the EU are estimated from the mineral requirements worldwide, and the 
ratio between the primary energy demand from renewable sources in the EU and worldwide. The details 
are presented in Appendix E: Mineral Requirements for Clean Energy Technologies.  
The mineral footprint calculated in step 1 gives the total mineral consumption in the European 
Union for all end-uses while the mineral requirements give the consumption of the same minerals in the 
EU for clean energy technologies only. This consumption for clean energy technologies is expressed in 
mineral weight from 5 different perspectives: in 2020, 2030 for the two scenarios, and 2040 for the two 
scenarios again. Divided by the total EU footprint per mineral, it gives the ratio of the mineral requirements 
for clean energy technologies from each perspective with the total current EU consumption. The mineral 
footprint of the EU consumption for clean energy technologies from each perspective is calculated by 
multiplying these ratios with the previously calculated mineral production per country worldwide and per 
mineral for final consumption in the EU. The results obtained are the mineral production weight per 
country and per mineral type for clean energy technologies in the EU under all perspectives. 
3.5. Socio-environmental impacts assessment (Step 3) 
The satellites of EEMRIO models capture environmental impacts for each sector per unit of 
financial output. This step aims to use these satellites to calculate the socio-environmental impacts of the 
mining activities for the previously calculated footprints. To use these satellites, the financial output of the 
mining sectors driven by a final consumption in the EU for clean energy technologies must be estimated. 
It was achieved by first estimating the ore footprint of clean energy technologies in the EU from the 
mineral footprint calculated at the previous stage. Then, the financial output was calculated by combining 
this ore footprint and the total ore extraction attributed to each mining sector.

determined for the ores created in Exiobase at step 1 by using the ratio of the associated mineral. For 
other ores in Exiobase and all ores in Eora, intermediate calculations are needed since these ores are used 
to produce several minerals.

4.1. European Union footprint 
4.1.1. European Union ore footprint 
The European Union footprint was calculated both in Eora and Exiobase with the existing satellites. 
The lists of ores and minerals included in the calculations are summarized in Table 1 and Table 2. The EU 
has a total ore footprint of 4.04Gt (4.02-4.06Gt) of which the non-metallic ores represent 84% and the 
metallic ores represent 16% as represented in Figure 7. Figure 8 shows that 62% of the total EU footprint 
weight is located in Europe representing more than 2.5Gt. The main region from which comes the EU 
footprint after Europe is Asia-Pacific representing 22% of the footprint weight. Except for Africa which 
accounts for 5.3% of the EU footprint weight, the 3 other regions (North America, South America, and the 
Middle East) individually account for less than 5% of the total weight. The picture is changing when the 
focus is brought on the metallic ores only. In that case, the EU footprint located in Europe decreases to 
39% of the total weight. Asia-Pacific is still the leading region outside the EU totalizing 27% of the EU 
footprint weight in metallic ores. South America and North America now account for more than 5% with a 
total of almost 20% of the metallic ores footprint weight for South America. The detailed results in weight 
and share of total footprint are summarized in Appendix J: Footprints results. The results show a different 
picture when the focus is brought on all ores or specifically on metallic ores. A majority of the EU footprint 
is located in Europe for non-metallic ores but only a minority for metallic ores. The results when all ores 
are considered relate well with the results obtained by (Tukker et al., 2016). They consider a material 
footprint of Europe for metallic and non-metallic ores but also biomass and energy materials which were 
not included in this study. According to Tukker et al., Europe’s contribution to the global material footprint 
in terms of production represents 69% of its contribution in terms of consumption. Their number falls in 
the upper range of our results for Europe’s contribution (51 to 72%) shown in Figure 8. It is partly because 
their result also includes Europe’s contribution to the rest of the world. The localization of the footprint 
outside of Europe shows major changes depending on the scope. Some regions are estimated to represent 
a bigger share in the EU’s metallic ores footprint weight such as South and North America whereas other 
regions are estimated to represent a bigger share in the EU’s non-metallic ores footprint weight such as 
the Middle East. The total EU’s footprint gross weight is 4 to 5 times bigger for the non-metallic ores 
compared to the metallic ores. Therefore, the spatial repartition of the EU’s footprint weight on all ores is 
much influenced by the non-metallic ores. It enhances the importance to segregate these categories when 
studying the EU’s material footprint to design policies.

illustrated in Figure 11, the top four minerals (chromium, copper, manganese, and zinc) account for more 
than 65% of the EU mineral footprint weight calculated over 34 minerals. Calculating the EU ore and 
mineral footprint on different levels highlights the importance of the scope considered in the results 
obtained. Europe’s contribution to the EU ore footprint is estimated between 51 and 73% while this 
estimation decreases to between 38% and 41% for the metallic ores footprint only. The picture is also 
different when the footprint is calculated on the minerals scoped in this study. Between 33% and 50% of 
the EU footprint for these minerals is estimated to originate from Europe. These differences emphasize 
the importance for policy-making to reduce the focus as much as possible on the minerals or ores to avoid 
such distortions. This focus reduction is limited by current input-output databases data quality. Figure 11 
also suggests that IO models capture better global pictures or aggregated mineral categories rather than 
individual results. Results show a clear top three or top four minerals no matter the database used for the 
calculations however the order inside these top four and their share of the total footprint weight changes 
with the database. Another solution to the inequal individual contributions to the aggregated category 
results would be to correct the weight of the minerals with other indicators such as supply risk or socio-
environmental risks to have a final picture better representative of these issues.

4.2.1. The spatial repartition of the EU mineral footprint for clean energy technologies 
The requirements in the 34 minerals scoped in the study for clean energy technologies in the EU 
in 2020 are estimated from the IEA data to add up to 1.1Mtons. This study provides the spatial repartition

40% and 60% for the mineral footprint weight on all end-uses and the EU total ore footprint weight. This 
is mainly explained by an increased share of South America, from a contribution of 12% in the mineral 
footprint weight for all end-uses to a contribution of 29% for the clean energy technology uses only. The 
main observation on the different perspectives is that the scenario considered bears much more influence 
than the time step on the spatial repartition. The more ambitious the climate goals will be, the more 
international is the footprint expected to become. Furthermore, the evolution of the EU footprint for 
minerals for the energy transition is expected to already occur in 2030. As observed in Figure 12, each 
region shows an increased contribution to the footprint in terms of weight both from a time and from a 
scenario perspective with a higher footprint for the SDS scenario compared to the STEPS scenario. In terms 
of share of the total footprint, however, Figure 13 shows some regions see an increase while others see a 
decrease in their share. Europe’s share in the footprint weight decreased from 33% in 2020 to respectively 
32% and 29% for STEPS and SDS scenarios in 2040. South America also sees its share in the EU’s footprint 
weight for clean energy technologies decrease from a value of 29% to a value of respectively 26% and 23% 
in 2040 for the STEPS and SDS scenarios. The region benefiting from the decrease of the two main 
contributors is Asia-Pacific with an increase of its share from 17% in 2020 to 22% and 27% respectively for 
the STEPS and SDS scenarios. The increase in Asia-Pacific share in the footprint is its major share in the 
graphite and nickel footprint weight for the EU which is expected to see a major increase in its 
consumption under the SDS scenario as illustrated in Figure 14. In 2040, under the SDS scenario, Europe, 
Asia-Pacific, and South America would respectively account for 29%, 27%, and 23% of the EU’s mineral 
footprint weight in critical minerals for clean energy technologies totalizing close to 80% of the total 
footprint weight. The EU footprint in critical minerals for the energy transition is not concentrated in a 
single region but widespread around the world with Europe contributing to less than 40% of the footprint 
weight today. This share could decrease to less than 30% in 2040.

4.2.2. The share of clean energy technologies uses in the EU mineral footprint 
Clean energy technologies are one consumption among others for those 34 minerals. The 
influence of the increase in the clean energy technologies consumption on the total EU mineral 
consumption is different from one mineral to another depending on the share of the clean energy 
technologies consumption among all different end-uses. As illustrated in Figure 16, there are some big 
differences in the consumption increase of minerals for clean energy technologies uses only and the 
increase of the overall EU consumption. Magnesium and niobium for example will see their consumption 
for clean energy technologies increase considerably (mainly caused by concentrated solar power) with a 
consumption multiplied by more than 10. However, the effect on the EU's overall consumption is very 
limited with consumption for clean energy technologies in 2040 following the scenario SDS representing 
less than 0.1% of the current EU footprint for these minerals. The minerals for which the energy transition 
requirements are expected to get past the current EU demand are gallium, vanadium, lithium, graphite, 
cobalt, nickel, neodymium, and other REEs. This is also illustrated in Figure 17. These minerals 
consumption for clean energy technologies is expected to be greater than the current EU total 
consumption in at least one perspective. Gallium, lithium, and graphite are the frontrunners with 
consumption in 2040 for clean energy technologies expected to be more than twice the current total EU 
footprint for these minerals under both scenarios and both models. These three minerals all show a 
geographic origin of the EU consumption in 2020 very limited with Australia being responsible for 46% of 
the provenance of lithium and China respectively responsible for 61% and 89% of the provenance of 
graphite and gallium. These minerals highly contribute to the delocalization of the EU mineral footprint 
outside Europe and their supply for the EU consumption will put current supply chains under pressure. 
The country's repartition of lithium and gallium is shown in Figure 18. These results are consistent with 
the results obtained by (Deetman et al., 2018) Their study announces the demand for copper, neodymium, 
and tantalum for electronic appliances, cars, and renewable energy will increase by a factor of 2 to 3.2 by 
2050 while the demand for lithium and cobalt is expected to increase by a factor of 10 to more than 20. In 
comparison, our study based on the IEA scenarios expects an increase in cobalt consumption for clean 
energy technologies by more than 10 and an increase in lithium consumption by 25 by 2040. The material 
bottlenecks identified here are based on the EU supply and they do not reflect supply risk at a global level. 
(Valero et al., 2018) studied these bottlenecks in the global future development of green technologies.

reasonable bounds given the global reserves. On the other hand, cobalt, lithium, nickel, and gallium must

4.3. Socio-environmental impacts of the mining activities 
4.3.1. Direct and indirect environmental impacts of mining activities 
As illustrated with previously calculated footprints, the energy transition in the EU requires mining 
activities worldwide. Mining sectors are responsible for socio-environmental impacts during their activities 
but also in the upwards supply chain of these activities. The environmental impacts are studied through 
the greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, and the water consumption and withdrawal. The GHG emissions are 
compared with domestic EU countries' GHG emissions obtained from the European Environment Agency.  
The direct greenhouse gas emissions of the mining sectors worldwide to extract resources finally 
consumed in the European Union represented between 600ktons and 1700ktons of CO2 equivalent in 
2020 according to Figure 19. The increase in mineral requirements for clean energy technologies following 
the SDS scenario would drive these emissions above the domestic emissions of Malta (the EU country with 
the lowest GHG emissions in 2019) in 2040. If the total GHG emissions of the mining sectors are considered, 
these emissions become much larger and are estimated to already be equivalent in 2020 to the domestic 
emissions of Malta. In 2040, the total GHG emissions could reach the level of the domestic GHG emissions 
of Latvia (4th country in terms of the lowest domestic GHG emissions in the EU) or even Slovenia (7th). The 
results presented in Figure 19 highlight the importance of taking into consideration the indirect GHG 
emissions of mining sectors. The direct water blue consumption of mining activities worldwide for the 
energy transition in the EU is estimated between 0.6 and 1.6 million cubic meters in 2020 according to 
Figure 20. This consumption is expected to reach between 3.2 and 3.8 million cubic megameters in 2040 
with the SDS scenario. This water footprint is multiplied by 15 to 20 when the indirect water consumption 
is also considered. In 2040, under the SDS scenario, the total blue water consumption is thought to reach 
around 70 million cubic meters. The increase in freshwater consumption when considering the indirect 
impacts is even greater than for the GHG emissions. As illustrated by the GHG emissions, mining activities 
for the energy transition in the EU are responsible for significant environmental impacts. These impacts 
are both direct (directly linked to the mining activities) and indirect (linked to the upwards supply chains 
of these activities). The part of the indirect impacts is substantial and therefore cannot be neglected when 
assessing the environmental performance of the energy transition in the EU.

Conclusion  
 
In this study were successively calculated and compared footprints of the European Union in terms 
of ore extraction and mineral production. The mineral footprint was calculated over 34 minerals 
categorized as critical minerals for clean energy technologies by the International Energy Agency. This 
mineral footprint was calculated for all end-uses and clean energy technologies only.  
 
The results in this study show the importance of the scope used for the footprint. Indeed, so far 
material footprints focused on ore extraction. However, the study shows the footprint greatly differs 
between the different scopes. The EU ore extraction footprint is estimated to be originating at 60% from 
Europe. Asia-Pacific is the first region outside Europe representing around 20% of the ore extraction 
footprint. When the scope is brought to the metallic ores only the picture is already different and Europe’s 
contribution to the EU ore extraction footprint decreases to 40%. Europe’s contribution to the mineral 
footprint of the EU for the 34 minerals considered also falls to 40%. For the mineral footprint, Asia-Pacific 
is now the third region after Africa which accounts for almost 30% of the mineral production for the EU. 
This conclusion does not only apply to the material scope of the footprint but also the consumption scope. 
If the clean energy technologies use in the EU only are considered, Europe contributes only to a third of 
the mineral production while South America becomes the second region sharing almost 30%. Following 
the scenarios published by the IEA, this internationalization of the mineral production for clean energy 
technologies in the EU is thought to increase. The study estimates that around 70% of the mineral 
production for the energy transition in the EU will originate from outside Europe in 2040. At this time, Asia 
and South America are respectively expected to represent 27% and 23% of the mineral production 
footprint for clean energy technologies in the EU. This conclusion is part of a spatial divergence between 
consuming and producing countries in the global dynamics of critical metals (Watari et al., 2020). Among 
the four perspectives studied (two time perspectives for two different scenarios), the scenario bears more 
influence than the time perspective. The SDS scenario which is the most ambitious of the two keeping 
global warming well below 2°C requires more minerals but also shows a higher degree of 
internationalization. This scenario requires multiplying the mineral requirements for clean energy 
technologies in the EU by 2.4 before 2040 and 2.1 before 2030. In comparison, the STEPS scenario which 
illustrates the policies in place and under development only requires multiplication of these requirements 
by 1.6 in 2040. The increase in mineral requirements for the energy transition is an issue that must be 
dealt with now to secure the required supply for 2030. This supply is expected to be more international as 
the European climate goals will become more ambitious and the deployment of clean energy technologies 
greater. Finally, it also shows the importance of the technologies that will be chosen to reach the climate 
goals. Different technologies are under development for solar power for example and the technology 
massively deployed will greatly impact the mineral requirements and ultimately the footprint. 
 
The supply risk is very different for each mineral. The requirements of 26 of the 34 minerals 
considered are thought to increase in 2040 compared to current levels. Some of these minerals experience 
a low increase in their requirements for clean energy technologies whereas others experience a massive 
increase such as iridium whose consumption could be multiplicated by 1000 by 2040. However, this 
increase in the requirements for clean energy technologies does not induce the same stress on the EU 
supply of these minerals given the magnitude of other end-uses. Seven minerals are identified with a major 
supply risk as their requirements in 2040 under the SDS scenario are greater than the total footprint of the 
EU demand today. These minerals are gallium, vanadium, lithium, graphite, cobalt, nickel, and REEs. Some 
of these minerals such as gallium, lithium, and graphite are moreover lacking diversity in their supply 
==PAGE:44==
35 
 
chains for the EU consumption today. These supply chains can hardly be diversified, and it increases the 
importance of actions to limit the socio-environmental impacts of these existing supply chains. Cobalt, 
gallium, lithium, and nickel are more generally identified to become bottlenecks globally in the 
development of green technologies and must require particular attention (Valero et al., 2018; Watari et 
al., 2018). The socio-environmental impacts of mining sectors on the energy transition in the EU are 
significant. The total GHG emissions of mining sectors worldwide are expected to reach the same level as 
current individual domestic emissions of 25% of the EU countries by 2040. These emissions are greatly 
international with Asia-Pacific being the first region with 33% of the GHG emissions from mining sectors 
for clean energy technologies in the EU in 2020. Europe is only second with 28% of these emissions. 
However, several countries emerge as hotspots for these emissions such as Australia and China. The same 
happens for freshwater use, showing the environmental impacts are located worldwide but the major part 
is concentrated in a limited number of countries (Top 3 countries and top 8 countries respectively 
represent 53% and 73% of the total GHG emissions of mining sectors estimated for 2040 under the SDS 
scenario).  
 
Recommendations 
Recommendations for the science community 
The present study comprises several limitations, some are inherent to MRIO models, and their 
precision, and others are due to the precision of the integration of the mineral requirements for clean 
energy technologies.  
The first main limitation of the study due to MRIO models is that it only captures a static view of 
international trade. Therefore, the perspectives studied in 2030 and 2040 under different scenarios show 
a picture of what would happen all other things being equal. It means the demand for clean energy 
transition minerals will change in the EU but not worldwide, all other demands will remain equal, and the 
structure of the international trade won’t react to these changes in the demand. It also implies minerals 
supply won’t change and the EU demand for minerals will always be fulfilled. The second limitation is the 
homogenous behaviour of all products inside the same industry. All minerals inside the same ore category 
and all countries inside the same region for Exiobase will share the same trade characteristics. This is 
particularly limiting when identifying hotspots of greenhouse gas emissions or supply risk both in terms of 
ore categories, minerals, or countries. It calls for a better sectoral and spatial disaggregation of the MRIO 
models that is not easily achievable without losing precision. Studies using hybrid models combining MRIO 
models with physical flux could capture the supply chains with higher precision and draw more detailed 
conclusions. Finally, some limitations are due to the method used to combine mineral production data and 
mineral requirements data with the MRIO models. MRIO models can already capture the processing and 
transformation of ores into minerals through disaggregated industries. Another method could be to 
modify the input-output satellites to capture the mineral production as a stressor associated with these 
industries. This would increase the precision of the mineral footprint by capturing countries exporting or 
importing ores before the processing stages. All the same, if the mineral requirements were detailed per 
originating countries or regions, it would allow their integration with more precision. 
This study identified several hotspots. It identified the requirements for the energy transition in 
the EU in 2040 for gallium, vanadium, lithium, graphite, nickel, cobalt and REEs could become higher than 
==PAGE:45==
36 
 
the current EU footprint for these minerals over all end-uses. It also identified countries such as Australia 
and China concentrating a large part of the environmental impacts and sectors such as mining of copper 
ores and nickel ores driving the increase in these impacts in the coming decades. These hotspots identified 
require more detailed analysis such as a detailed life cycle analysis to confirm the observations of this 
study and further quantify their importance.  
 
Recommendations for policymakers 
The set of recommendations made here is formulated for the EU policymakers since it has been 
the case study. However, it applies to a certain extent to other countries and world regions operating their 
energy transition. 
The present study identified various hotspots both in terms of minerals and in terms of countries 
concentrating the EU supply in minerals for the energy transition or socio-environmental impacts 
associated. The main bottleneck minerals identified are nickel, lithium, gallium, and cobalt. These minerals 
require detailed studies on the current EU supply chains and the supply possibilities to cope with the 
increasing demand led by the energy transition. More generally, these minerals and other minerals that 
are also associated with a high supply risk or a high socio-environmental risk such as copper and rare earth 
elements appeal for studies using consumption-based approaches and capturing three different risks: the 
supply risk, the environmental risk, and the social risk. The design of the EU policies for the energy 
transition must deal with these three risks to ensure a successful Green Deal. 
More generally, the EU must diversify its supply chains of critical minerals for the energy transition. 
This diversity is needed on two different levels which are the ore extraction and the ore processing and 
refining to produce minerals. The first level is highly dependent on the geographical location of exploitable 
ores and leaves little possibility of diversification for most minerals. However, the possibility of diversifying 
the places of ore processing and developing the activities inside the EU must be investigated. In a 
combined effort to diversify the supply chains and to decrease the socio-environmental impacts caused 
worldwide, the mining activities in the EU should be developed. These impacts must become a key 
criterion during the selection of supply chains for these minerals and they will be more easily monitored 
and improved if located in the EU. The EU also gets other levers to develop the value chains in the EU 
through recycling and to a broader extent circular economy (Månberger & Stenqvist, 2018; Watari et al., 
2018, 2020). The development of recycling in the EU will require R&D to develop new recycling 
technologies but also the design of policies to ensure product manufacturing allows for high recycling rates 
(IEA, 2021b).  
Finally, the combination of supply and socio-environmental risks will be lowered with a decrease 
in consumption. One lever is to increase the metal intensity. Another is to diversify the sub-technologies. 
In the case of solar PV particularly, various sub-technologies exist requiring different minerals. The more 
diversified these sub-technologies will be the lower will be the risks be on the corresponding minerals 
(Månberger & Stenqvist, 2018). Finally, the decrease in the demand can come from a decrease in energy 
consumption and a decrease in the consumption of critical minerals for the energy transition from other 
end-uses. The supply and socio-environmental risks carried by the critical minerals for the energy 
transition can be lowered with more sobriety in our society, especially in energy consumption.

Appendix I: Method for the social impact assessment 
 
The method for the social impact assessment used in this study was developed by Etienne Berthet. 
The qualitative risks evaluation of the SHDB for the sector “Mineral nec” that concerns all the mining 
activities were extracted. The social risk theme selected is “Forced Labor” as it is a significant risk in mining 
and ore extraction activities. Then to evaluate the relative probability of an adverse situation occurring, 
those qualitative evaluations are combined with the number of people working worldwide working today 
in the mining sector due to the current energy demand in Europe and the number of people working in 
the mining sector with the projected energy demand in Europe based on the different perspectives. This 
calculation is based on the output of our I-O model. This is based on the following assumption: if x% of the 
mining production of one country is used to supply the European Energy demand, then we assume that 
x% of the mining workforce of this country is directly employed by the energy European demand.   
The final percentage of each Exiobase and EORA sector used to supply the European Energy 
demand is directly accessible from our I-O calculation. Thus, we only need to quantify the total 
employment for each of our I-O sectors. Some employment data are already enclosed into Exiobase and 
EORA however their qualities are low. Thus, we have implemented new data as described in the next part.   
 
Employment data  
 
Employment data is taken either from the International Labour Organization (ILO), a United 
Nations agency whose mandate is to advance social justice and promote decent work by setting 
international labour standards: Employment by sex and economic activity from ILO. The main ILO source 
is the model: ILO modeled estimates – ILOEST. ILOEST includes both nationally reported observations and 
imputed data for countries with missing data. The imputations are produced through a series of 
econometric models maintained by ILO. Using a robust model able to estimate labour market indicators 
for countries with missing data is a great help to obtain balanced panel data with consistent country 
coverage that can be easily computed in an EEIO building set. For this database, employed persons are all 
persons of working age who, during a specified brief period, were in the following categories:   
- 
Paid employment (whether at work or with a job but not at work) 
- 
Self-employment (whether at work or with an enterprise but not at work).   
To see the full description of the model see (International Labour Organization, 2022). To summarize, the 
ILOEST database is built as follows:   
- 
The data obtention and selection process: Approximately half of the data is directly taken 
from the ILOSTAT database  
- 
Source: Data is derived from a labor force or household survey, a population 
census, or official government estimates. Labour force surveys are the most 
comprehensive source for internationally comparable labour force data. National 
labour force surveys are typically very similar across countries, and the data derived 
from these surveys are generally much more comparable than data obtained from 
other sources.   
- 
Age coverage and geographical coverage  
- 
Missing value estimation procedure (which is approximately for the other half of the 
ILOEST database)  
- 
First, to ensure within-bound estimates of labour force participation rates, a 
logistic transformation is applied to the input data file.   
- 
Second, a simple interpolation technique is used to expand the baseline data in 
countries that report labour force participation rates in some years.   
==PAGE:59==
50 
 
- 
Next, the problem of non-response bias (systematic differences between 
countries that report data in some or all years and countries that do not report 
data in any year) is addressed and a solution is developed to correct this bias.   
- 
Finally, the weighted least squares estimation model, which produces the actual 
country-level LFPR estimates, is explained in detail.

As the salaries in the added value of Exiobase are detailed in low qualified, medium qualified, and high

imputation has been only performed for Exiobase due to time limitations.
