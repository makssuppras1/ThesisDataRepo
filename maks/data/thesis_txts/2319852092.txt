ABSTRACT 
Energy and freshwater resources are strictly interconnected: the former is used to 
extract, clean and transport freshwater needed, the latter to help produce the energy 
required. Their policy goals often conflict and interact in the so called “Water-Energy 
Nexus”. Thermoelectric power industry, which is responsible for about 80% of global 
energy generation, is also one of the main water users, representing 40 % of 
withdrawals in the US and is therefore an important element of the Nexus. In the last 
years, several thermal power plants in Europe faced production restriction because of 
insufficient water availability for their cooling purposes. This shows the importance of 
taking into account these water requirements when planning future energy systems or 
establishing water management policies.  
The objective of this thesis is to find a proper method to further improve the modelling 
of water-energy nexus in order to analyze the effects of thermal cooling constraints on 
both thermal and overall energy production, as well as on the entire water-power 
system. A second objective was to be able to use this method in a real-time water-
power management perspective. This study is based on Ph.D. study done by Pereira-
Cardenal et al. (2013): “A framework for joint management of regional water-energy 
systems” and reuses the same case Study: The Iberian Peninsula. In order to achieve the 
goals, a deterministic, single objective, resource allocation problem is implemented. The 
single objective consisted in minimizing the net costs of the system and is optimized 
through a linear programming solver. In order to be usable in a real-time water-power 
management perspective, a stochastic dimension is added to the deterministic model 
through Model Predictive Control. 
Cooling water requirements have no significant impact on the water system of the 
Iberian Peninsula. The value of cooling water is insignificant for most of the rivers and 
does not impact the water management. Only nuclear power plants with large water 
withdrawals have an impact on the water value in the same order as hydropower plants. 
The impacts are more significant on the power system at the scale of individual power 
plants, which face production restriction because of water scarcity.  
In the MPC approach, the quality and the length of the forecast are the main parameters 
optimizing the additional costs to the optimal solution. With more specific data, the 
model could be used as a real-time water management tool, as a frame for local 
reservoir management optimization models. 
 
Keywords: Water-Energy Nexus, Iberian Peninsula, Thermal Cooling Constraints, 
Deterministic, Hydro-economic, Linear Programing, Model Predictive Control.

1 Introduction and objectives 
The water and energy systems are strongly interdependent: while water is used for 
energy production through hydropower, extracting and refining combustibles and 
cooling purposes of thermal power plants, energy is used to extract, treat and supply 
water as well as recollect and treat wastewater. Therefore policies in terms of water and 
energy management should take in account this link in order to avoid conflicts 
(Hoffman, 2010).  
An example may be the promotion of bio-fuels in by the European Union energy policy 
(Hardy et al., 2010): bio-fuels are the most water demanding energy production (King et 
al., 2008) and their expansion will increase the pressure on the water resource. Thus, 
they will compete for example with hydropower production, cooling water requirements 
and agricultural water demand. In order to face decreased water availability, the 
agriculture sector will use more power intensive irrigation technologies and therefore 
increase the power demand while the hydropower availability will be decreased at the 
same time. This shows how the water and power system are interconnected and have to 
be considered simultaneously, in the so called “Water-Energy Nexus” to assess the 
global impact of a policy (Scanlon et al., 2013). Furthermore growing population and 
economy will increase pressure on the limited resource and climate change, for 
numerous places, is supposed to decrease and shift water availability (IPCC, 2007) while 
it will increase and shift the power demand (International Energy Agency, 2008). This 
calls for a stronger assessment of the Nexus in water or power related policies or 
strategies. 
Water for thermo-electric generation, which already represents 38% of water 
withdrawals in the US (USGS, 2010), is one of the nexus related issues which has to be 
considered now when defining energetic strategies or building new thermal power 
plants that have a lifetime of around 30-60 years (Vliet et al., 2016). 
Therefore joint power-water models including interactions in the spatial and temporal 
scale, as developed in Pereira-Cardenal et al., (2013), seem to be promising tools. The 
main challenges of these models are to find the appropriate temporal and spatial scale 
and the relevant connections in the Nexus to be representative. In fact, while water 
management can be done at the watershed scale, power systems span over an entire 
nation or even several countries and power requirements have to be met on a second by 
second basis while water requirements are more flexible through storage (Pereira-
Cardenal et al., 2013). Another challenge for these models is to be able to be used in a 
real-time water-power management perspective. This requires them to be 
representative enough at the local scale while taking in account the relevant parameters 
at the global scale.

These considerations led to the Thesis objective formulation, based on the Ph.D. study 
done by Pereira-Cardenal et al. (2013): “A framework for joint management of regional 
water-energy systems” where the goals were to develop a method to model spatial and 
temporal interaction between water and energy, improve the representation of 
hydropower benefits in hydro-economic models, evaluate the influence of spatial 
aggregation on the analysis and assess some impacts of the Climate change on the 
power-water system in the Iberian peninsula. The conceptual model and most of the 
input data used in this thesis has been adopted from Pereira-Cardenal (2013) with focus 
on implementing cooling constraints. This study aims to: 
- Formulate the water-energy nexus of the Iberian Peninsula as a multi-stage 
deterministic optimization problem to be solved with Linear Programming. 
- Develop for the nexus formulation a methodology to include the cooling water 
requirements of thermal power plants.  
- Assess the impact of thermal cooling water requirements on the hydro-economic 
optimization of the water- power management. 
- Use the LP formulation in a Model Predictive Control environment to upgrade the 
deterministic model to a real-time water-power management tool. 
- Analyse the impact of the stochastic runoff on the model performance. 
The work is structured as follows: Chapter 2 briefly presents the context, hydro-
economic modelling, cooling water requirements of thermal power plants and real-time 
water-power management. Chapter 0 shows the main features of the water and power 
systems of the Iberian Peninsula and details the adopted conceptual model. In chapter 4 
the joint water-power optimization modelling and the Model Predictive Control 
approach are presented. Main results of the study are presented and discussed in 
chapter 5 and conclusion is done in chapter 6. References can be found in chapter 7 and 
supplementary materials completing the study are in chapter 8.

2 Context 
2.1 
Hydro-economic optimization 
High temporal and spatial variability of the natural water resources are often causing 
increased local water scarcity. Due to population and economic growth; water scarcity 
has increased in even traditionally water-abundant countries (OECD, 2015). Combined 
to this, climate change is supposed to worsen the picture for most countries (IPCC, 
2007). 
As water is a vital resource for multiple sectors, managers have to decide how the 
scarcity should be managed and the water allocated, in order to maximize the societal 
benefit from the scarce resource. While access to clean drinking water and sanitation is 
recognized as a human right by the UN, the fourth Dublin principle (UN, 1992) states 
that water should be managed as an “economic good”. In practice water is usually 
managed according to the historical, institutional, political, legal and social context, 
which may be slow to adapt to variations in demand and availability. Thus the European 
Water framework directive requires member states to use economics principles and 
tools allowing to define water management policies (EU Commission, 2000).  
Hydro-economic models enable to integrate economic principles in the decision making, 
as they couple economic concepts to a hydrological representation, so each decision is 
characterized by its marginal economic benefit or cost and the optimal management is 
reached by maximizing (/minimizing) the whole economic benefit(/cost). According to 
OECD (2015) water has three values: an ecological one, a socio-cultural one and an 
economic one derived from productive uses, which shows that water is not a purely 
“economic good” and sets the challenge for hydro-economic models to internalize all 
the externalities. The economic value derived from productive uses is the easiest to 
define by using standard approaches for economic production from a resource. The 
ecological value of water can also be taken into account, but assigning a monetary value 
to ecological services can be a hard task and raise many questions (Loucks et al., 2005). 
However another approach is to do multi-objective optimization, where different 
objectives, such as economic benefit and ecological quality can be optimized in different 
units. There are also different attempts to introduce an “equity” dimension of the model 
(Zhineng et al., 2016, Tilmant et al., 2009). 
As stated in Harou et al., (2009), which provides an overview of hydro-economic models, 
using economic tools doesn’t mean advocating water markets or the privatization of 
water. Independently from the actual water allocation policy, hydro-economic modelling 
is frequently used to assess the economic impact of alternative scenarios or strategies 
(Pereira-Cardenal et al. 2013, Davidsen et al. 2014, Kahil et al. 2016) and is therefore 
highly suitable for the present study.

2.2 
Cooling water for thermal power plants 
In thermal power plants, large amounts of water are often required to absorb the loss 
heat from the steam/gas turbines or nuclear reactions. Water for cooling in energy 
production is an important part of global withdrawals but only a small part of 
consumptions, for example, as stated previously in the U.S it represents 38 % of total 
withdrawals but it is only 2% of consumptions (USGS, 2010). Therefore, as models often 
take into account only consumption, the cooling water demand is not represented or 
not connected to the energy model. Furthermore, water availability is not the only 
parameter that influences the cooling process; the water temperature is also a critical 
parameter: as thermal power plants are obliged to respect ecological standards, the 
heat discharged to the stream has to be reduced if the natural temperature is already 
high.  
Therefore in period of droughts, high water temperature combined with water scarcity 
can force thermal power plants to reduce their activity as it happened in France in 2009 
where one third of the nuclear power plants had to be temporary turned off during the 
summer (Rübbelke and Vögele, 2010). This impacts the energy production and therefore 
the economic balance. As this phenomenon is supposed to increase with climate change 
(IPCC 2007), it seems important to assess the impact of cooling water requirements on 
the water management.  
Several studies deal with this issue, Koch and Vögele, (2009) proposed a method to 
model shortages in thermal production linked to water temperature constraints, in Koch 
and Vögele, (2013) the method was applied to a hydrological model to assess the impact 
of climate change on a nuclear power plant in Germany forecasting an increase of water 
demand linked to a water temperature increase. The study also shows the importance of 
the time scale, a daily time scale will show more shortages than a monthly one because 
of peak temperatures. Using the same method, Rübbelke and Vögele, (2010) studied 
climate change impacts in Europe on the power system, considering power transfers 
between the nations and showed that reduced production in the water scarce scenario, 
particularly in France, will impact power systems of all neighbour countries, in particular 
Italy. Vliet et al., (2016) modelled at a world scale the impact of climate change on 
thermal and hydropower production and found that worldwide the usable capacity of 
thermal power plants will be reduced by 5% in 2020, even if this could be mitigated by a 
change in cooling technology resulting in increased costs.  
In the present study, a similar approach is used to represent the cooling water 
requirements of thermal power plants. However the study is not limited to the impact 
on the power system but also evaluates how the water management can adapt in order 
to limit the impacts on the joint water-power system.

2.3 
Model predictive control and real time water-power 
management 
Water management at the reservoir scale takes in account several objectives such as 
flood control, hydropower generation, agricultural demand, domestic and industrial 
water supply, recreational uses, navigation (Wu and Chen, 2012). These competing 
objectives are represented by different stakeholder. Most reservoirs are operated on 
the basis of rule curves considering the interests of the different stakeholders, supposed 
to fix a frame to the water management with associated release rules (Wu and Chen, 
2012). The research of past decades has developed many optimization models to 
improve day to day water management, optimizing the benefits of the different users in 
the rule curve frame and/or refining the rule curves with hydrological forecasts. (Ngo 
2006, Madsen et al., 2009, Olofintoye et al., 2016) 
These models use real-time information and forecasts to optimize operations on the 
basis of short-term and long-term objectives (Madsen et al., 2009). It was shown that 
they are able to significantly improve the benefits from the water. As discussed before, 
the water and power systems are strongly interconnected and are not limited to the 
boundaries of the basins. Therefore decision and conditions in other basins may 
influence the optimal operation decisions in the considered reservoir. For this, 
nationwide water-power model such as developed by Pereira Cardenal, (2013) would be 
able to provide useful information for this local reservoir models.  
However the method used in Peireira Cardenal, (2013) does not permit a disaggregated 
representation of the system, computational resources limit the SDDP (stochastic dual 
dynamic programming) method employed in the study to a few reservoirs (the Iberian 
Peninsula was modelled by 7 aggregated reservoirs). Therefore the model results are 
difficult to export at the reservoir scale. For this reason, the approach considered in the 
present study is based on a disaggregated representation of the water and power 
systems considering watersheds and reservoirs individually. An adapted and convenient 
optimization method for this representation is Linear Programing. However it is a 
deterministic method and does not take into account stochasticity in the hydrological 
conditions, which is problematic in a real-time water-power management perspective 
where future conditions are unknown.  
Model Predictive Control is a methodology able to introduce stochasticity in a 
deterministic model. It considers several scenarios and evaluates them in the 
deterministic model, the final output is a balance of the different deterministic 
solutions, introducing thus a stochastic dimension to the model. This approach was 
considered to be relevant for the present objectives and was therefore adopted in the 
study.

3 Case study: the Iberian Peninsula 
According to Pereira-Cardenal et al. (2013) the Iberian Peninsula is an interesting case 
for the study of the water-energy nexus and the integration of thermal cooling 
requirements for the following reasons: 
-The hydrological system is only shared among Spain and Portugal without significant 
communication with other countries. 
-The power market is shared between Spain and Portugal with few exchanges with other 
countries.  
-The water and power resource are interdependent because of relatively high 
hydropower production and thermal power cooling requirements.  
-There are known cases of shortage in the thermal power production because of 
insufficient cooling water availability and the situation is supposed to worsen with 
climate change. 
The following sections present the global picture of the water and power system in the 
Iberian Peninsula and the conceptual model adopted here. Some of the data are only 
available for Spain, if so, only Spanish data is cited and is assumed to be representative 
for the entire Peninsula which spreads over 580 000 km2 covering Spain and Portugal 
main lands and hosts around 53 million people.  
3.1 
Overview of the Water-Power System of the Iberian 
Peninsula 
  Water resource and uses 
In Spain the annual freshwater withdrawals are around 38 km3/year (Carrillo et al., 2009) 
which represents 34% of the sustainable resource (Spanish National Institute of 
Statistics, 2008). The agricultural sector is the main water user, accounting for 61% of 
total withdrawals, followed by electricity production with 17% (MMA 2007), this last one 
grew by 30% from 2001 to 2006 and is therefore an emerging demand (Carrillo et al., 
2009).  
However only 60% of the total withdrawn water is consumed and 95% of the cooling 
water is returned to the streams (Spanish National Institute of Statistics, 2008), making 
the electricity production sector only accountable for 3% of water consumption (Carrillo 
et al., 2009) while agricultural sector remains the most important one with 75% of 
overall consumption (Figure 1).

As seen on Figure 2, agriculture is spread overall Spain with particular concentration in 
Andalusia, which alone represents 23% of Spanish agricultural water demand. The 
agriculture water demand is provided by 80% of surface water, 19% of Groundwater and 
1% of other sources such as sea water desalinization (Spanish National Institute of 
Statistics, 2008). 
In an opposite trend, rainfall in the Peninsula follows a southeast to northwest gradient 
(De-Castro et al., 2005; Gonzàlez-Hidalgo et al., 2011) as shown on Figure 2. The rainfall 
varies from an annual mean of 300 mm/yr in the southeast to 1500 mm/yr in the 
northwest.

This distribution of rainfall and agriculture demand shows the regional aspect of water 
scarcity, while the Guadalquivir basin in the southeast has an irrigation demand of 8700 
hm3/year (Wriedt et al., 2009) with a mean annual streamflow1 of 3780 hm3/year 
(Lorenzo-Lacruz et al., 2013), the Mino-Sil basin in the northwest has an irrigation 
demand of 240 hm3/year with a mean streamflow of 10570 hm3/year.  
 Water allocation and rules 
In Spain and Portugal water allocation priority is given to domestic supply, followed by 
Agriculture and then industry; energy production being the less prioritized use, which 
contrasts to France for example, where nuclear power plants cooling is the first priority. 
(OECD, 2015) 
About thermal cooling water discharge, the Spanish regulation Reglamento de Dominio 
Público Hidráulico (RDPH) states that if no ecologic requirements are specified, the 
maximum increase in the downstream mixed river temperature is 3 °C, up to a 
maximum of 28 °C according to European freshwater fish directive (Carrillo et al., 2009), 
while the discharged water to lakes and reservoirs should not exceed 30 °C.  
  Hydropower and storage 
There are 208 hydropower plants in Spain and 38 in Portugal with a capacity over 10MW 
accounting for a total capacity of more than 20 GW (Pereira-Cardenal et al., 2013), while 
small Hydropower plants with a capacity under 10MW reach 2 GW (Europe energy 
portal, 2008). Hydropower plants are also the main way of storing water and regulating 
the flows, the constructed storage capacity is 56 500 hm3 for the entire Peninsula, 
regulating around 40% of the flow in Spain (Berga-Casafont, 2003). 
  Electricity market: production and price 
Thermal power is the main electricity production source in the peninsula and accounts 
for 47% of total electricity production with 68 main thermal power plants including 5 
active nuclear power plants. The so called “Special regime”, accounting for 37%, is a mix 
of energy sources from which 60% are renewable (mainly wind and solar), these 
producers have special agreement and the energy is always cleared at a special price 
(Pereira-Cardenal et al., 2013). Hydropower is the second highest renewable energy 
source after wind energy and represents on average 14% of the global production. 
Finally, as stated before, International transfers, mainly importation from France 
(Rübbelke and Vögele, 2010), represent only 2% of electricity supply.

Between 2008 and 2015, the average market price of electricity was 48 €/MWh, the 
hourly price variating from 13 cent/MWh to 93 €/MWh. The power price is correlated to 
the produced quantity to some extent. As seen on  Figure 4, a higher demand leads to 
higher price, but the price is also strongly dependent on the energy source. As seen on 
the graph a peak in special regime and therefore “cheap” energy leads to a decrease in 
price. 
On a monthly scale, production and price are less correlated, external factors such as oil

4 Materials and Methods 
This chapter presents the adopted joint model of the water and power systems and the 
model predictive control approach implemented. 
4.1 
Joint Water-Power optimization model 
The adopted modelling approach in this study works as follows: The Water System is 
represented by the water resource that can be allocated in a network model approach 
to the Water Demand, Thermal Power and Hydropower, this last one being also able to 
store water and make it available for the resource again. The Water System is connected 
to the Power System through the Thermal and Hydropower that contribute together 
with the unconstrained power to the power production which has to meet the power 
demand. Costs are associated to power and water flows, and the optimal resource 
allocation is reached through the optimization of the global cost. The Water system is 
based on a network representation, while the fluxes (water allocation, power 
production) and economic optimization are ruled by linear programming.

4.1.1  Time and time step 
As stated before, in order to represent cooling water shortages, Koch and Vögele, (2013) 
show that a small time step (such as daily) is more accurate than a large one (monthly) 
as it is able to represent extreme conditions. On the other side in order to model longer 
time series and therefore different climatic episodes, the time step should not be too 
small such that the model doesn’t become computationally too expensive. Furthermore, 
some of the data is difficult to get on a daily basis, such as irrigation requirements. For 
these reasons, a weekly time step is supposed to be adequate for the problem 
representation, small enough to represent warm and dry episodes but large enough to 
run the model through several years and have representative data and modelling 
approach.  
4.1.2 Power system 
The power system is modelled by a single power demand that has to be met at any time 
step interval by the different power producers: hydropower, thermal power plants and 
unconstrained thermal power production representing the producers that are not 
individually modelled. Each producer has a unit energy production cost. 
To represent the power demand, a linear regression is done between air temperature 
and power demand in Pereira-Cardenal et al. (2013) using the cooling and heating 
degree-day approach from Valor et al. (2001). Air temperature data is from Haylock et 
al., (2008) and electricity demand from OMIE (2016). Based on this model a weekly 
power demand time series from 1961 to 1990 is generated from temperature data. 
As stated in the case study presentation, special regime has to be cleared all the time 
due to special agreements, therefore this last one is simulated for the considered time 
frame in Pereira-Cardenal et al. (2013) and subtracted from the total power demand to 
get the “net power demand” that has to be satisfied by thermal production and 
hydropower.  
This fix weekly demand neglects the potential elasticity in the demand depending on the 
supply prices, however the elasticity is assumed to be small within a weekly interval.  
4.1.3  Network representation of the water system 
The water system is represented as a network, the concept is a simplified one of Cheng 
et al., (2009) where sources and sinks are connected by flow paths based on a node 
representation.    
 Sources and sinks 
The structural nodes of the system are the 123 watersheds defined by the hydropower 
plants and basin outlets. There are five types of elements considered in the hydrologic 
==PAGE:31==
19 
 
system: hydropower plants, inflows, outlets, water demand and thermal power plants. 
They are nodes connected to their watershed and are either a sink or a source:  
- Hydropower plants are at the same time a sink and a source as they store and release 
water and are therefore modelled by two nodes as in Cheng et al., (2009). One sink node 
able to store water and one source node able to allocate the stored water. 
- Inflows represent the runoff from each watershed, they are directly added into the 
hydropower plant reservoir source node, and are therefore not represented in the 
network, this in order to reduce the number of elements without losing any detail. 
- Basin outlets work as fictive hydropower plants to delimit the watershed situated 
downstream the last hydropower plant in the basin and still in the modelled area. The 
sink node represents the water flowing to the sea or out of the model area, while the 
source node is only for the watershed inflow. 
- Water demands are sinks, representing the complete water demand of their watershed 
(the agricultural demand here), except thermal cooling water. 
- Thermal power plants are sinks, representing the thermal cooling water use of the 
power plant(s) of the watershed. 
To illustrate the conceptual model, a simple case is considered with two watersheds, 
with one hydropower plant delimiting the upstream watershed while the basin outlet 
delimits the downstream watershed. One thermal power plant is present in the 
downstream watershed and there is one water demand per watershed. Figure 9 shows 
the conceptual model and the flow path representation.  
 Flow paths  
All the possible connections between sources and sinks are computed and called “flow 
paths”; more detail is available in supplementary materials 8.5. The method requires 
that there is only one possible flow path between a source and a sink and does 
therefore not allow loops in the system. 
Water can flow from sources to sinks according to the available flow paths. As in Cheng 
et al., (2009) hydropower plants reservoirs store through their sink node at time step t 
and make the water available for their source node at time step t+1 independently from 
the flow paths. Outlets are modelled as fictive hydropower plants but do no connect the 
“storage” from time t to the source at time t+1 as the “storage” is an outflow of the 
system.  
In the adopted approach a hydropower plant source node is able to allocate water to its 
own watershed water demand which is actually upstream of the reservoir. This is to 
model local storage capacities and avoid “fictive curtailments” in the more upstream 
watersheds. 
==PAGE:32==

The model also assumes that in a time step, the water is able to flow from any source to 
any sink that are connected by a flow path.

In the model, as Coal and Gas thermal power plants have a very close marginal cost (58 
and 57 €/MWh respectively), they are assumed to be equal in order to avoid creating an 
artificial difference between them. In fact, if no cooling water constraints are active the 
model would produce only with gas plants. This would not be representative as actual 
costs from one power plant to another vary significantly and some Coal power plants 
may be more competitive than Gas ones. The Hydropower production marginal value is 
very low (3 €/MWh) compared to the thermal power one, therefore it is set to zero to 
simplify the model which is a reasonable assumption as the important parameter is the 
difference between the costs and not the cost itself.

4.1.6 Synthesis and Optimization 
The modelled area is divided into 123 watersheds defined by 116 hydropower plants 
and 7 basin outlets. With each watershed a weekly water demand is associated 
corresponding to the agricultural water demand. There are 15 aggregated thermal 
power units represented subject to cooling water constraints and contributing to the 
power supply together with the hydropower production and the unconstrained thermal 
supply curve divided into 10 generation units representing the non-modelled thermal 
power plants; this power supply has to meet the peninsula weekly power demand. 
The synthetic map in Figure 11 represents the elements of the Water System and the 
decision variables, while the input data and parameters are summarized on Table 3. 
 
The optimal water-power management, which consists in water allocations, power 
production and storage decisions represented by the decision variables, is found by 
minimizing the sum of the power production and agricultural curtailment costs for the 
entire Peninsula and time frame considered through Linear Programming.

4.2 
Model predictive control 
The previous modelling approach assumes full knowledge of the hydrologic conditions 
over the entire simulated period, also called a “perfect forecast” or “deterministic 
optimization”. It is therefore over-performing the management of the water as it is able 
to anticipate droughts and high inflows. For example, more water can be stored before 
high temperatures and low inflows in order to compensate a reduction in the thermal 
production by hydropower and make more water available for cooling purposes during 
droughts. Thus, the impact of the drought may be underestimated. The previous 
modelling approach is still able to indicate “trends” or compare scenarios on the water-
power system, evaluating for example the impact of a shift in technology of the power 
plants, an increased power demand or the impact of climate change. But in order to 
better assess the impact of cooling constraints uncertainty in the water resource must 
be taken into account. Therefore a stochastic dimension through Model Predictive 
Control is implemented in order to improve the model to an actual water-power 
management tool and assess the impact of stochasticity on the importance of thermal 
cooling water constraints. 
4.2.1 Conceptual model 
Model Predictive Control (MPC) uses the base deterministic optimization model 
established previously with the same data input. Instead of running once the 
optimization for the entire considered management period, MPC runs the model for a 
large amount of different synthetic inflow scenarios and saves the optimal storage policy 
for each inflow scenario (Figure 12). From this a weekly average optimal storage policy is 
computed by averaging the storage policy through the different inflow scenarios and the 
different years.  
In the simulation run at a time step t the present conditions of the system are known. 
The optimization is run for the forecast horizon in which a hydrologic forecast is 
available, with the end storage fixed to the previously computed average optimal 
storage policy for the last time step of the forecast horizon. From this the optimal 
allocation for the present time step can be found, at the next time step the procedure is

repeated. The forecast used for the planning horizon is out of the scope of this study, 
two different approaches are considered:  
 
- In MPC with perfect forecast the hydrologic conditions are assumed to be known for 
the entire forecast horizon. 
- In MPC with imperfect forecast several forecasts are available in the forecast horizon. 
Here the same synthetic inflow generation model is used as a forecast model and 
generates synthetic inflows starting with the present inflow. The resulting (sub)-optimal 
allocation for the time step is the average of the optimal (deterministic) allocations for 
the different inflow scenarios (/forecasts) for the first time step.  
The main parameters of the MPC model are the number of inflow scenarios (nis) and the 
length of the forecast horizon (ntsp). Besides the quality of the forecasts, in principle a 
larger amount of inflow scenarios and a longer forecast horizon should provide a policy 
closer to the optimal one. But computational time may become a limiting factor, in 
particular when the model has to be used in a real-time perspective.

4.2.2 Generating synthetic inflow scenarios 
In order to find the average optimal allocation and storage policy, the deterministic 
optimization needs to be run for a high number of inflow scenarios. To generate inflow 
scenarios, a simple and widely applied method has been developed by Thomas-Fiering 
(Loucks et al., 2005).

For each considered watershed the Thomas-Fiering method can be used in order to 
generate synthetic inflows, by using statistics from the historical dataset. The method 
preserves the non-stationary character of mean, variance and co-variance of seasonal 
inflows and is expressed by the following formula (Loucks et al., 2005):

optimization the same model is used. In this case, the present Inflow is set as the first 
“synthetic” inflow and the method is applied to the rest of the forecast horizon.

more averaged global inflows as droughts and floods would appear at the watershed 
level and compensate each other over the basin area. Therefore a correlation is

5 Results and Discussion 
This chapter presents the results obtained for a 20 years optimization simulation 
(simulated hydrological conditions of 1966 to 1985) of the deterministic model, different 
scenarios and different settings of the MPC approach. The average values are from the 
years 1967 to 1984, the first and last simulation years are excluded to avoid start and 
end effects. 
5.1 
Model Validation:  
Water Balance 
 
The water balance shows values comparable to the Aquastat data, remembering that 
the model area covers only 70% of the Peninsula. Cooling water consumption is only half 
of the observed one, which can be explained by the fact that only half of the thermal 
power plants are modelled with water requirements (even if among the other half, 
numerous power plants use seawater which does not count in freshwater consumption) 
and that the reference data also takes into account all uses for electricity production 
(such as cleaning water). Furthermore several Gas power plants that did not exist in 
2003 (reference data for observed cooling requirements) are taken into account in the 
model and are generating less cooling water requirements than Coal power plants.  
 Share of electricity production 
The model has slightly higher coal and thermal production and lower hydropower and 
nuclear production than the averaged observed one (Figure 14). However these values 
vary over the years and it can therefore be considered as a quite good fit. Hydropower 
production has been calibrated in Pereira-Cardenal et al. (2013) so that the modelled 
hydropower plants represent the peninsula production.

At the basin level observed and simulated withdrawals show significant differences 
(Table 5). The total water withdrawal simulated seems to be lower than the observed 
one for the year 2003 but this result is highly dependent on the assumed temperature 
increase in the cooling water. This value is supposed to vary between 5 and 15 °C (and 
10°C is taken as an average) which would correspond to withdrawals from 5840 to 1950 
hm3/year. However this does not impact the model, since cooling constraint is 
considered globally for the entire river water and a maximal legal temperature increase 
of 3°C. 
 
Observed and simulated withdrawals at the level of thermal power plants explain the 
differences observed at the basin level: the Almaraz power plant in the Tajo has much 
higher withdrawals and the Asco power plant in the Ebro has much lower withdrawals. 
There are several reasons: (i) the observed value is for one specific year (2003) and may 
not be representative for the average value; (ii) In the observed value the Acceca and 
Asco thermal plant are supposed to have once-through cooling systems while updated 
data suggest that the power plant have cooling towers; (iii) average efficiencies, 
marginal costs and water consumption factors are used and may not be representative 
at the scale of a single power plant. 
It is therefore difficult to assess the validity of the model. It can be seen that general 
trends match the observed ones even if specific numbers do not necessary. This is not 
really surprising as numerous average data is employed for the power system, the model 
can only be representative to the extent of the representativeness of the input data. The 
results discussed below are therefore more relevant as general trends or relative 
impacts.

5.4 
Sensitivity Analysis 
To test the robustness of the deterministic model, a sensitivity analysis is carried out. 
The main varied parameters are: total inflow, air temperature and resulting water 
temperature, total power demand, total agricultural demand, marginal production cost 
and thermal efficiency of constrained thermal power plants. The impact on the model is 
expressed in terms of total costs and % increase of cost per % increase of parameter. 
This may not reflect exactly how the optimal decisions vary with the parameters as costs 
can increase while allocation decisions remain the same or at the contrary costs may 
stay stable while allocation decisions change. However it is a relevant single indicator. 
Most of the parameters show a linear impact on the model, except temperature 
increase that presents a polynomial shape (Figure 23). Power demand increase has the 
most impact on the total costs because the highest costs are associated to power 
production but this does not mean that the model is sensitive to this parameter. 
 
These parameters are indeed expected to vary in the future because of technological 
development, socio-economic evolution, and climate change. Therefore this sensitivity 
analysis can also be seen as a scenario analysis and some parameters have been 
discussed more in detail in the results as scenarios (section 5.3). 
Figure 23: Sensitivity analysis of main parameters. S is expressed in (%/%) as the % variation in cost per % increase of 
parameter, for the temperature scenario S is calculated between the reference and the 15% temperature increase
scenario. const TPP refers to constrained thermal power production.

As one of the goals of the study is to assess the impact of cooling constraints, the 
sensitivity analysis is also represented in terms of average production with active cooling 
constraints (Figure 24) to assess the validity of the previous conclusions. 
The impact of the parameter variation on the average production with active cooling 
constraints shows less linear trends as for the total costs. Especially the marginal cost of 
constrained thermal power plant that has an oscillating effect. This comes from the 
stepwise approach of the unconstrained thermal producers and the unique marginal 
cost of Coal and Gas power plants. This instability could be overcome with a more 
detailed supply curve of the thermal power plants.  
Temperature is the most sensitive parameter. The air temperature data is available with 
a good spatial and temporal resolution, however the air-water temperature model is 
calibrated with only one watershed leading to approximations in the maximum river 
temperatures. This analysis shows the relevance of the parameter and calls therefore for 
a well calibrated air-water temperature model.

5.6 
Limitations and further research 
Considering the limited time frame of the project several assumptions have been made 
to simplify the model which has led to limitations in the presented results. Further 
research and possible upgrades are suggested here to overcome the limitations of the 
model and to better represent the water-energy nexus. 
 Power System 
In the adopted modelling approach the power system is found to be very sensitive to the 
water system. This is for two main reasons: the individually modelled (or constrained) 
coal and gas thermal power plants are all represented with the same single production 
cost and no other factor as cooling water requirements is taken into account. Therefore, 
if no active cooling constraints apply, the optimal production will be based on the water 
consumption of the thermal plants and the water value in the considered watersheds. In 
reality this has a very small impact as cooling water consumptions and the associated 
costs are very low compared to production costs but in the model no other factor 
influences the production. Therefore it leads to unrealistic production of thermal power 
plants.  
The uniform price of constrained gas and coal thermal production in the model is also 
resulting in a constant market price of electricity that has an important impact on the 
hydropower production. The two factors influencing hydropower production are the 
value of water from other uses and the electricity market price (that determines the 
added value of hydropower). As the electricity market price is constant in time the 
temporal hydropower production will only depend on the temporal water value. 
Therefore, as storage capacities are high, a relative small change in the temporal 
distribution of the water value can lead to an important shift in the temporal 
distribution of the hydropower production (as seen in section 5.3). 
A way to overcome these limitations would be to have more detailed supply curve of the 
constrained coal and gas thermal power plants (the same supply curve shape as for the 
unconstrained thermal power production could be adopted for individual constrained 
thermal power plants). This would lead to more even and realistic production 
distribution among them and would not significantly increase the dimension of the 
problem. Otherwise the yearly production could be calibrated with data and the cooling 
constraints could be evaluated as a penalty in case of insufficient cooling water for the 
planned production. In both cases it would introduce a varying electricity market price 
and impact the temporal hydropower production. 
Also the cooling water requirements of the thermal power plants are partially coming 
from average data such as the share of waste heat discharged in the cooling tower or 
the thermal efficiency of the power plant. Specific data would make the model more 
==PAGE:67==
55 
 
representative for local water management issues. Water consumption for carbon 
capture system should also be added if future scenarios are analysed.   
If a strong interest is given to the power system, other factors as the water system 
should also be taken into account, such as the price of gas, coal, fuel or CO2 emissions 
that may have a bigger impact on the production as the cooling constraints. In the 
adopted approach power demand is considered at the peninsula level, assuming that 
power transfers are not limiting. This limitation could be overcome by representing the 
power system with a flow path model, similarly to the water system, with basin power 
demands, limited capacity of transition lines and eventually transition costs.  
 Water System 
The freshwater resource in the model is only represented by surface water. 
Groundwater is indirectly taken into account as it is added to the surface water resource 
but no storage capacity is represented. As 20% of Spanish agricultural water 
consumption is coming from groundwater resources, this could be an important feature 
to model. Groundwater could be represented at the watershed level by an additional 
storage capacity that would be recharged by infiltration linked to the precipitation. The 
groundwater resource could then be limited to the sustainable resource or over 
extraction could also be modelled. 
The only represented water demand in the model is the agricultural demand. Industrial 
and domestic demand could easily be added to better represent the entire water 
demand. The crop water demand is distributed in time according to the observed 
irrigation requirements distribution of the Ebro basin and there is only one agricultural 
marginal benefit per basin. Different demand curves in the basins could be defined with 
different marginal benefits for upstream and downstream users or several agricultural 
users with different water demands and marginal benefits. 
The water temperature of the rivers is modelled only depending on the air-temperature. 
Therefore the heat discharge of the thermal power plants is not taken into account as it 
does not increase the temperature in the river. This assumes that resulting from heat 
exchange with the air, the water temperature reaches its equilibrium temperature 
before the next thermal power plant. In the model nearby thermal power plants were 
aggregated to compensate for this assumption. However in basins with multiple power 
plants this assumption may overestimate the thermal capacity of the river and a 
dynamic river water temperature could be implemented to better represent the impact 
on the river. Beside of this the air-river temperature model is calibrated with only one 
catchment and same parameters are used for the entire peninsula, as temperature is a 
sensitive parameter for cooling constraints different parameters for the different basin 
and sub-basins should be used. 
==PAGE:68==
56 
 
The adopted flow-path model, where all explicit links between sources and sinks are 
considered, leads to a high amount of decision variables. The advantage of the adopted 
method is that the source of the water is trackable and different suppliers can be 
considered. However this potential is not used and may not be relevant for the study. 
Therefore a more classical nodal network approach, where decision variables would be 
the reservoir release/storage, agricultural and cooling water consumption at each 
watershed (leading to 3-4 decision variables per watershed) would decrease the 
computational size of the problem. In the present model around 85 % of the decision 
variables are water allocations (1600 per time step), this could be reduced to around 
300-400 variables and therefore the size of the whole problem by more than 50%.  
 Real time management 
In the real-time management approach implemented by MPC the only considered 
stochastic parameter is the run-off. In reality future power demand, renewable 
electricity production and agricultural demand are also unknown parameters. It would 
fast become computationally too expensive to have individual forecasts on each 
parameter and implement them all in the MPC approach. However most of the 
unknown can be linked to the climatic factors: run-off and agricultural demand depend 
on precipitation, power demand on the temperature and renewable energy on the 
sunshine and wind. Therefore a feasible approach would be to have a climatic forecast 
model able to generate one or several forecasts from which all the sus-cited parameter 
could be determined.  
By now the MPC approach with imperfect forecasts assumes that the best solution is the 
average of the optimal solutions from the different inflow scenarios. However this does 
not take into account the risks in terms of costs associated to the different scenarios and 
the average solution is probably not the cost-optimal solution. The solution in real time 
management could be optimized by taking into account the different costs linked to the 
different scenarios and weight the allocation decisions by the associated costs.  
It could be seen that the quality of the forecast is essential in the MPC approach. The 
adopted Thomas-fiering method to generate synthetic inflows is basic and probably not 
the most adapted to a weekly time step. Beside of this, inter-annual correlation in the 
inflows and cyclicity can be observed in the Iberian Peninsula that is not taken into 
account in the Thomas-fiering method.  
If the model is supposed to be use in a real-time management context, it has to be 
representative for the basin or watershed scale level where the actual management of 
water is performed. For this most of the input data needs to be more disaggregated and 
specific as suggested in the water and power system improvements.

6 Conclusion 
The goal of the study was to model the water-energy Nexus of the Iberian Peninsula, 
focusing on the integration of cooling constraints and assess their impact on the water 
and power management. Beside of this, the purpose was to upgrade the deterministic 
model to a real-time water management tool. To achieve the goal, the water-energy 
nexus of the Iberian Peninsula has been formulated as a deterministic optimization 
problem including water allocations and storage, hydropower production and thermal 
power production. A disaggregated approach of the water system has been adopted 
considering 123 watersheds defined by 116 hydropower plants and 7 basin outlets to 
which a weekly agricultural demand is associated. The power system is represented by a 
weekly power demand that has to be met by hydropower production, 15 aggregated 
thermal power production units subject to cooling water requirements and the 
unconstrained thermal power production represented by 10 generation units. The 
optimization problem was solved by Linear Programing with a single objective function 
minimizing the power production and agricultural curtailments costs at the Peninsula 
level for the considered management period. 
Because of the lack of specific data for thermal power plants it is difficult to validate the 
methodology to include cooling water requirements at the power plant level but it could 
be done at the peninsula level. It was found that cooling water constraints have a very 
small impact on the yearly average water management scheme increasing the total costs 
by 2.6% mainly because of two nuclear power plants equipped with once-through 
cooling systems that faced reduced production (Santa Maria de Garona in the Ebro and 
Almaraz in the Tajo). The added value of non-consumptive cooling uses to the water is 
only significant for the two sus-mentioned power plants. In these basins it was found to 
be in the same order as the added value by a medium-large hydropower plant (0.03- 
0.08 €/m3). Only the consumptive water uses of thermal power plants have a relevant 
impact and could therefore be added as an industrial demand. Agricultural demand is 
the main decision factor for water allocation policies. The impact on the power system 
and on the temporal water management scheme was found to be more significant but is 
difficult to validate because of the assumptions made in the model. 
It is difficult to predict if in the future these constraints will become more binding as 
many parameters are supposed to vary: (i) the power demand should increase and 
therefore increase the constraints on the augmented production, but (ii) this could be 
compensated by other sources of energy (such as renewable energy) which are 
supposed to increase and should decrease the constraints on the thermal power plants; 
(iii) with climate change water temperature will increase and water availability decrease 
and shift in time, leading to more binding cooling constraints, more agricultural demand, 
less hydropower production and therefore more thermal production, but (iv) changes in 
==PAGE:70==
58 
 
technology such as increased efficiency or expansion of cooling towers or “dry cooling” 
may compensate for this changes, finally (v) energy policies, such as increased CO2-
emission prices may switch the thermal production to more efficient gas thermal power 
plants and therefore less constraints, but may also lead to more implementations of 
carbon capture systems and therefore more water consumptions of thermal power 
plants (Byers et al., 2014). Depending on the scenario it is likely that the cooling 
constraints will become more binding. The model showed the individual impact of these 
parameters, for example a temperature increase of 15% would lead to an increase from 
12% to 25% of production with active cooling constraints.  
The estimated “price” of air-cooling systems for thermal power plants is a penalty of 3 to 
11 % in the energy output of power plants (Byers et al., 2014). This will therefore limit 
the maximum cost of cooling requirements in the future. However it will only be the 
case if the price and impact of cooling constraints are taken into account properly in the 
scenario analysis for energy supply and water management strategies. Furthermore 
because of the long life-time of power plants the technological adaptation may be slow 
(Vliet et al., 2016) and therefore cooling water constraints for large thermal power 
plants may be critical in the next decades and could therefore be taken into account in 
the local water management or by the planning of new energy infrastructures.   
The upgrade to a real-time water management tool of the model is performed by 
implementing a Model Predictive control approach. The real-time simulation induces 
additional costs in comparison to the optimal solution, linked to the unknown future 
hydrologic conditions. The two main parameters impacting these additional costs are 
the quality of the forecast and the length of the forecast. Increasing the length of the 
forecast up to one year is improving significantly the solution. Over one year, 
improvement potential is reduced but still significant. The quality of the forecast is one 
of the main challenges of the approach. The adopted forecast model based on the 
Thomas-fiering method shows poor performances as it results in a sub-optimal solution 
with an additional cost of 500 million euros per year compared to a solution with a 
perfect forecast available. Half of these costs were found to appear during droughts 
representing only 5% of the management period. The number of considered forecasts is 
only slightly improving the performances. In the present case considering up to 50 
different forecasts improved the performances. 
Despite the assumptions made, the model showed that it is an adapted tool to take into 
account cooling water requirements of thermal power plants and that the cooling water 
requirements of large (/nuclear) power plants are a relevant but not crucial aspect of the 
water-energy nexus at the local scale. The model is very flexible and more features can 
easily be added to better represent the water-energy nexus. More specific data should 
be employed to be representative of the local scale. With the MPC approach the model 
could also be used as a real-time water-power management tool if combined to more 
local and specific models for the day to day or hourly decision making.

8.2 
Unconstrained thermal supply curve 
The unconstrained thermal supply curve is representing all the thermal power plants 
that are not directly represented in the model. It is called “unconstrained” as it is 
assumed to be not depending on cooling constraints in opposition to the explicitly 
modelled ones that are called “constrained”. The approach is based on Pereira-Cardenal 
et al. (2013) and the supply curve is modelled by power production steps and associated 
production prices.  
As stated before, production costs are confidential data and not likely to be found. A 
way to estimate the price of producers is to take the market price for a given amount of 
produced energy, as the market price represents the marginal cost of the “last supplier”. 
For this daily energy production and associated average market price from OMIE (2016) 
were converted into weekly data.  
Total thermal production is obtained by subtracting Special regime and hydropower 
from the total production using daily electricity production share per category data 
(OMIE, 2016). From this thermal production, part is coming from the constrained 
thermal power plants and should therefore also be subtracted. This part was estimated 
by computing the production share of the modelled thermal power plants from the 
global thermal electricity production from the years 2006 to 2008 (Material 2). So 50% 
of the thermal power production is estimated to come from the modelled thermal 
power plants.

8.3 
Power plant aggregation 
From the 32 modelled thermal power plants, some are actually only different units 
situated at the same site, using different technologies or not, sometimes owned by 
different companies. As the water temperature is not modelled on a dynamic way, as 
stated in X.X, consider that each power plants has access to the full thermal capacity of 
the water would overestimate this last one as it actually has to be shared. Therefore 
thermal power plants located in the same watershed and located on the main river are 
aggregated to a single generation unit which’s characteristics (efficiency and share of 
waste heat in evaporated water) are the averaged one of the single thermal power 
plants proportionally to their production capacity. TPPs with different energy production 
costs, such as nuclear power plants and coal power plants, should not be aggregated as 
the averaged cost would change the optimization strategy and not be representative for 
the actual costs, however no such case was found. This led to 15 electricity production 
units presented in Material 4.

8.4 
Thermal Power Plants cooling requirements 
Thermal power is any process transforming heat energy into electric power. The heat 
source can be fossil (coal, fuel and gas combustion or nuclear), geothermal, biomass or 
solar; and the most common way to transform it is by heating water to steam and lead it 
through steam turbines which drive an electric generator. Thermal power is the primary 
source of electricity in the world and accounts for 81 % of worldwide electricity 
production with around 16 million GWh generated in 2010. (Vliet et al., 2016).  
Material 5 presents the conceptual scheme of a typical coal thermal power plant: the 
coal combustion is heating the boiler 9 which evaporates water into steam, the steam 
goes through the steam turbines 6 ,7 and 4 while it’s pressure and temperature 
decreases, it is finally converted back to water in the condenser 5 and goes for a next 
loop from the boiler to the turbines. Steam turbines drive electric generators 3 
producing electricity that is injected in the grid through the transformer 2. In Combined 
Cycle Gas Turbines power plants (CCGT) the process is similar but before running a 
steam turbine through the combustion of the gas, the gas is directly use to run gas 
turbines that generally represent 2/3 of the global power capacity. 
The condensing step 5 requires to cool down the steam and its remaining heat, the most 
common cooling medium is water, but can also be “dry cooling” by using other mediums

(such as air). If water is used, it absorbs energy by two different ways: through its 
calorific capacity by increasing its temperature or by changing phase and being 
evaporated. This leads in two main types of “wet cooling” technologies: once-through 
cooling and cooling towers 1 (with or without recirculation) showed in Material 6. 
The once through technology mainly withdraws and warms up water and directly 
returns it to the source, generally the river or the sea, which leads to high withdrawals 
and small consumption, however part of the water is still evaporated afterwards in the 
river because of increased temperature, this amount of water is estimated not to be 
negligible (NETL, 2011).  
Cooling towers evaporate all (in closed circuit systems with recirculation) or part of the 
water in order to reduce water withdrawals as the amount energy absorbed per unit of 
water is much higher but lead to increased consumption (Koch and Vögele, 2009).  
Dry systems, cad not using water as a cooling medium lead to very small water 
consumption (Byers et al., 2014) and are therefore not relevant to this study. Only few 
power plants in Spain are equipped with this technology, but it is an increasing trend as 
water constraint become stronger and technology develops.  
Other processes are consuming water, such as cleaning, losses of water in the steam 
cycle or flue gas desulfurization in coal facilities (Macknick et al., 2012) but their water 
requirement are very small compared to cooling water, except for carbon capture 
systems that have consumptions in the same order of magnitude but are not very 
extended now (Byers et al., 2014).

 Construction of the matrices 
The matrices are constructed time step per time step, basin per basin. As the river basins 
are hydrologically independent, the constraints are independently considering the 
different basins, except for the power equilibrium constraint that makes the link 
between the different basins. Therefore the matrix is build basin per basin, considering 
all constraints except the Power market equilibrium, which is then added at the last line 
of the matrix connecting the different basins as in Material 9.
