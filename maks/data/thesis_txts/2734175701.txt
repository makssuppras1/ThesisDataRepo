Abstract
TICRA, a world leading provider of antenna design software, is pursuing advancements
in phased array synthesis methods. Phased arrays use phase control to manipulate the emit-
ted far-field by setting the phase of each antenna in an array. This allows for flexibility and
precision in long-distance information transmission. Current methods for phase control are
slow iterative algorithms or neural networks limited to grid arrays, where the antennas are
placed in a grid structure. However, grid arrays are not always feasible due to physical con-
straints, and the efficiency of irregular arrays’, where each antenna can be freely placed, is
under-explored. Our twofold objectives are (i) to develop methods enabling phase control in
irregular arrays and (ii) to investigate the benefits of irregular arrays compared to grid arrays
for phase control. For (i), we establish a framework for training models general to a wide
range of antenna arrays and far-field requirements. In this framework, we introduce the One-
shot Solution, a model that predict the antenna phases given an antenna array and far-field
requirements. Furthermore, we introduce the Initial Excitation Solution, a Deep Deterministic
Policy Gradient (DDPG) method that improves the phase control of iterative algorithms, by
predicting effective initial guesses. For phase control, we introduce a GNN, a Transformer,
and our proposed Distribution-Based Transformer (DBT). For (ii), we introduce the Position
Generation Solution, a method for optimizing antenna positions, tailored to a distribution of
far-field requirements and informed by a phase control method. To facilitate antenna array de-
sign and research, we have developed a user interface for real-time analysis of trained models.
Our methods are compared to TICRA’s state-of-the-art solver (SOCS) and CNN solution. We
show that the One-shot Solution significantly outperforms TICRA’s CNN, despite being more
general in antenna arrays and far-field requirements. In addition, we demonstrate that the
Initial Excitation Solution enhances speed and performance of its iterative solver, outperform-
ing SOCS. Finally, the Position Generation Solution generates significantly superior solutions
compared to randomly sampled irregular arrays and grid arrays. Our findings suggest that the
One-shot Solution enables real-time phase control for irregular arrays if the far-field require-
ments are simple. For more complex far-field requirements, iterative algorithms excel and can
be enhanced with the Initial Excitation Solution. The Position Generation Solution suggests
that irregular arrays that are superior to grid arrays can be synthesised.

Preface
This project has been developed as a research-oriented project in close collaboration with the
leading antenna software company TICRA. The primary focus of this project has centered around
exploring the potential advantages and limitations in utilizing neural network solutions for phase
control in irregular antenna arrays.
Given the innovative and broad nature of the project’s scope, we’ve taken a research-oriented
approach, resulting in a diverse and non-linear exploration of ideas and solutions. Initially, the
project’s scope was outlined through four specific objectives:
• Implement the encoder-decoder architecture and set up an appropriate training-validation
scheme.
• Adapt and implement GNNs for the encoder architecture, as well as relevant alternative
models and baselines to be compared in terms of predictive performance and computational
efficiency.
• Assess how the choices of models can solve the control problem for a fixed, non-uniform array
configuration.
• Investigate to what extent these models can be used to generalize across antenna array
configurations. The aim is to move beyond regular arrays by investigating GNNs for non-
uniform arrays and irregular arrays.
Upon addressing these four research questions, our findings led us to the conclusion that single-
pass neural network solutions were insufficient for solving more complex control problems. Simple
iterative algorithms, however, proved effective albeit time-consuming.
Consequently, we expanded the project’s scope to include solvers during network training, surpass-
ing the initial implementation of the encoder-decoder architecture. This decision was deemed a
necessary next step to ensure the research’s direct relevance.
The positive results of our methods sparked interest from TICRA, leading to a more ambitious
scope. In particular, it was of interest whether we could build on top of our solver-based methods
to also optimize for the position of the antennas.
This became a part of the project’s scope which replaced the objectives of training models for fixed
and non-uniform antenna arrays. These objectives were deemed less relevant in comparison, since
training models on these arrays are subsets of our more general methods.
In light of these considerations, we revised our learning objectives to:
1. Create and evaluate a model that takes as input an irregular antenna array and far-field
requirements, and outputs excitations for the antennas that fulfills the far-field requirements.
2. Create and evaluate a model that takes as input an irregular antenna array and far-field
requirements, and outputs good starting guesses to a solver which solves for best excitations.
3. Create and evaluate an optimization method that outputs antenna positions facilitating im-
proved phase control for a distribution of far-field requirements.
The initial among our updated learning objectives encompasses all the previous goals, excluding
the third, which has been discarded, and the aspect related to non-uniform grids in the fourth
objective. Two new, more ambitious objectives have been introduced instead.
We extend our thanks to our tutor, Tue Herlau, and to Lasse Hjuler Christiansen, Niels Skovgaard
Jensen, and Line Katrine Harder Clemmensen for their invaluable guidance and advice.

Introduction
This project is done in collaboration with the leading antenna software company TICRA. TICRA
provides a sophisticated software package known as TICRA tools, equipped with a state-of-the-art
second-order cone solver designed for phase control for antenna arrays (SOCS).
Antennas are devices to receive and/or transmit electromagnetic waves [1]. It is often desired to
be able to control in which direction and to what strength the signal is transmitted [2]. In this
project, we look at how we can control the electric field that an assembly of antennas emit by
manipulating the phases of the antennas. This is called phase control for antenna arrays [1, 3].
Antenna arrays are used on satellites such that the emitted signal can be constructed to satisfy
different goals [1]. For example, one goal may involve transmitting a signal to another mobile
satellite with the aim of creating a peak in a specific direction (this is called beam steering [2]).
Achieving this particular task is relatively straightforward as we just need all the antennas to make
constructive interference in the given direction. However, when more requirements are added to
the desired far-field as goals, the problem becomes non-trivial [4]. In many cases, there is a desire
to integrate the peak with specific null directions. This may be attributed to the need to either
prevent signal transmission in those directions or optimize the satellite’s signal reception from
those specific orientations [5].

The current approaches for phase control of antenna arrays involves arranging antennas in a grid
formation [6], as in the case of the UI illustration. This typically includes specifying a certain
number of antennas in both the x and y dimensions, along with designated spacing between anten-
nas for each dimension. Here, array setups can vary between satellites, with different spacing and
antennas in each direction, but all spacing between two consecutive antennas along a dimension
is identical [1]. This allows for CNN-based model architectures to be trained and used for phase
control by predicting excitations [7, 8].
Our research regarding phase control consists of exploring the capabilities of deep neural networks
expanding beyond the limitations imposed by a grid-based configuration to allow for the placement
of antennas in arbitrary positions within a 2D space, forming irregular arrays. This holds signifi-
cance for two main reasons. Firstly, setting antennas in a perfect grid is not always feasible due
to practical constraints or limitations imposed by satellite design [9]. Secondly, the limited prior
research on irregular antenna arrays makes it worthwhile to investigate the capabilities of neural
network solutions when utilizing array structures that deviate from the conventional grid pattern
[10].
When going beyond grid arrays, CNN-based model architectures fall short [7]. To overcome this
challenge, we introduce three different models: a Graph Neural Networks (GNN) [11], a Trans-
former [12], and a novel proposed Distribution-Based Transformer (DBT). The DBT has the ad-
vantage that it is invariant to translation of the antenna array, as it only relies on the relative
positions of the antennas.
Although our methods generalize straightforwardly to 3D antenna array structures, practical con-
siderations often make it impractical to position antennas directly in front of each other in most
scenarios. Therefore, we set the parameter pz to zero for the antennas, acknowledging this practical
constraint [1].

State-of-the-art for phase control involves using analytical methods, genetic algorithm, global opti-
mization algorithms, or neural networks [13]. We make the distinction between single-pass models,
such as neural networks, and iterative algorithms, which we call solvers. The choice between the
two often depends on the use case. Solvers, being iterative, take more time, but may yield su-
perior performance [14]. Therefore, for real-time inference, a single-pass model is often preferred.
Especially in non-ideal situations where the objective function is expensive to run, iterative solver
methods become infeasible [13].
The phase control problem poses an inverse problem - we aim to map far-field requirements to
excitations, while conversely, we can analytically map excitations to the far-field. Similarly, we
can think about this as an encoder-decoder structure. Going from antenna array and far-field
requirements to excitations is the encoding, and then from antenna array and excitations to far-
field is the decoding [13]. This is visualised in Figure 4:

Far-field requirements (the goals, g) comes in the form of direction where we want a peak, gP
or a null, gN. The loss is evaluated by measuring how close the resulting far-field is to the
desired values in the direction of the goals.
The inverse nature of the problem is advantageous, it provides direct access to gradients which
allows the use of gradient-based iterative algorithms (solvers) like gradient descent. This feature
also grants our neural network models access to the gradients of the excitations, which can be used
in training.
Finally, we introduce a method to optimize the positions of the antennas in an array, facilitating
efficient phase control tailored to a distribution of far-field requirements. Other approaches that
does this are limited to only enabling and disabling antennas [15, 16] and controlling spacings
between structured arrays such as grid and circular arrays [17]. In contrast, our method yields
solutions that enables arbitrarily placed antennas. In addition, TICRAs’ well-established antenna
design synthesis tool (TICRA tools) does not support any positions optimization scheme.
Our collaboration with TICRA led us to collectively formulate three research questions, forming
the basis for our work.
1. Create and evaluate a model that takes as input an irregular antenna array and far-field
requirements, and outputs excitations for the antennas that fulfills the far-field requirements.
(One-shot Solution)

2. Create and evaluate a model that takes as input an irregular antenna array and far-field

To the best of our knowledge, no prior research for adopting neural networks to irregular arrays
exists. Likewise, to the best of our knowledge, no prior research exists for generating antenna
positions not constrained to some systematic pattern.
1.1
Related Works
This project challenges the fixed-grid assumption for antennas in neural network solutions. We
extend to arbitrary 2D array structures using GNN and various transformer-based architectures.
This flexibility accommodates varying antenna numbers, placements, and far-field requirements.
Our experimentation involves training more versatile foundational models for a broad spectrum
of antenna configurations and far-field requirements. Additionally, we propose an optimization
method to generate tailored antenna positions for specific far-field classes, contributing to advancing
the possibilities in antenna array synthesis.
Other approaches for phase control of antenna arrays consist of evolution based based algorithms
such as Generic Algorithms (GA) [18] and Differential Evolution Algorithms (DEA) [19]. However,
evolution based solutions suffer the disadvantages of slow convergence and low computational
efficiency, especially for large array synthesis [13].
Some approaches which attempts to use solvers to generalize to irregular arrays are [20, 21]. In
these papers the potential benefits of irregular arrays are described, and different solvers are tested
on arrays.
Our methods makes idealistic assumptions about the underlying physics of the system. For ex-
ample, we assume no coupling effect between the antennas.
For methods which adresses this
assumption see [22].
Other approaches which attempts to do one-shot phase control with machine learning models on
grid arrays are [13], [23] and [24].
Our Excitation Initiator Solution frames the control problem as a contextual bandit problem. This
allows us to draw inspiration from Reinforcement Learning, and we end up using a Deep Deter-
ministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) method. This same re-framing was similarly done successfully
on images [25]. Other state-of-the-art approaches than DDPG which can also work on continuous
action-spaces are CATS and CATS_OFF [26].
For generating positions of antennas most other approaches consists of formalizing an optimization
problem or a constrained optimization problem. Then, solver algorithms such as a genetic algorithm
[15, 16] or a evolutionary algorithm in the form of swarm optimization [27, 28] or differential
evolution [17] are used. We are also dealing with a constrained optimization problem, as antennas
are not allowed to get too close to each other. However, we model this by incorporating it in the
loss function.

TICRA Tools
TICRA provides a sophisticated software package known as TICRA tools, equipped with a state-
of-the-art second-order cone solver designed for phase control for antenna arrays (SOCS). TICRA
has not published a paper about this solver yet, but an interested reader can see [29, 30] for a basis
about second-order cone solvers. The solver uses a minmax loss minimization scheme: the reader
can read about minmax solvers here [31].

Furthermore, TICRA has developed a CNN-based architecture that exhibits flexibility in the num-

versatile compared to our models in two key aspects. Firstly, it is limited to grid arrays, and
secondly, it lacks the ability to generalize across different goal scenarios, necessitating the training
of a specific model for each case.
Despite these limitations, for the purpose of establishing a concrete basis for comparison, we

2.3
Strategic Choices
In order to accommodate the requirements for our neural network models for the excitation gener-
ation problem some design choices have to be made regarding the training framework and model
architecture. In this section, we aim to give the reader insights in our implementation choices while
attempting to answer the "why" of the choices.
This list summarizes some key requirements we want our models and pipeline to be able to handle.
• Models have to be general in the amount of inputs. (Must have. We want to be general in
the amount of antennas and goals)
• Models have to be general in the amount of outputs. (Must have. We need to output an
excitation for each antenna, but can also eg. be a value for each goal as we shall see in
section 3.3.)
• Models should be modular in a way such that we can transfer learn from a problem type to
another. (Nice to have. Our models are deep and takes a long time (up to a week) to train,
so it’s an essential way to save computation time).
2.3.1
Training Overview
We refer to all kind of input to our models as tokens. Here is a list of the tokens used in this
project:
• Antennas (p). These are by default 3-dimensional, described by their position with px, py, pz,
where pz is zero for all antennas in this project. Additionally, if the antenna excitation, α,
is provided (which is the case for the Q-net described in section 3.3), this value, represented
by its phase arg(α), is appended to the token, transforming it into a 4-dimensional entity.
• Goals (g) Each goal is characterized by 5 values, serving to define aspects such as direction,
weight, and whether it represents a peak or null. The detailed breakdown is provided in
section 2.4.2.
• Other informative tokens (t1, t2...tk). These are all other tokens which inform the models
about it’s task. An example of such an token is the iteration token describing how many
iterations the solver will run after the model outputs it’s excitation for the solver. Details in
section 3.3.
To meet the first two requirements—ensuring generality in both the number of inputs and out-
puts—we employ a mask-based training pipeline.
During the batch data sampling process, each index may have a varying number of tokens. To
address this, our implementation involves initially sampling a maximum number of tokens for all
input indices. Subsequently, masks are sampled for each token type, indicating which tokens are
alive and which are not. Concrete descriptions of how these masks are generated are found in
section 2.4, and concrete descriptions of how they are utilized internally in the models is described
in section 3.4.
In the calculation of the loss the mask tensor is used to eliminate all values corresponding to
masked-out tokens. Furthermore, the model itself keeps track of the mask tensor, ensuring that
13
==PAGE:17==
the information flow remains independent of these values. This careful handling guarantees that
the model can effectively handle batch tensors with diverse token counts, training correctly on each
instance as if only the tokens designated as alive according to the mask are present.

2.3.2.1
Embedder
The models initiate by embedding its token-types separately. For example, antennas employ a
small network called the antenna-embedder, while goals utilize a similarly designed goal-embedder
network. In essence, we maintain distinct embedder networks for all token-types intended for the
model.
Embedders transform tokens into vectors of size state_size. We call the collection of all the
embedded tokens a state, s. The state is what is fed to the main block of the model.
An embedder follows a similar implementation to the following:
class Embedder(nn.Module):
def __init__(self, state_size: int):
super().__init__()
self.net = nn.Sequential(
nn.LazyLinear(4*state_size),
nn.ReLU(),
nn.Linear(4*state_size, state_size),
)
def forward(self, x):
return self.net(x)

Consider a scenario where a model is being trained on a dataset containing 1 to 20 antennas
uniformly drawn, with a batch size set to 4096, a single goal and no other informative tokens.
In this case, the input to the antenna-embedder network takes the shape 4096 × 20 × 3. Here,
the first dimension represents the batch, the second dimension denotes the maximum number of
antennas in the batch (very likely 20 for 4096 examples), and the last dimension describes the x,
y, and z-coordinates of each antenna. Similarly, the input to the goal-embedder is then of shape
4096×1×5. The mask of the antennas is of shape 4096×20, and the mask of the goals is of shape
4096 × 1.
Following the embedder processing, the embedded antennas and goals are concatinated into a
state of shape 4096 × 21 × state_size, where the default value for state_size is set to 64.
2.3.2.2
Main
The main block takes the state as input and produces another tensor with the same shape. As
such, there is an output vector of size state_size from the main block for each token provided to
the model.
We have implemented three models for the main block:
• a Transformer, see section 3.4.2

2.3.2.3
Selection
The purpose of the selection phase is simply to choose the relevant tokens from the output of the
main block and disregard the rest. The main block outputs a vector for all tokens, so, depending
on the task at hand, we extract the indices corresponding to the relevant vectors. For instance,
when predicting excitations for antennas, the relevant output indices align with the antenna token
indices. Similarly, for the Q-net in the case of the Initial Excitation Solution, we need a value for
each goal, and so the relevant output indices align with the goal token indices
2.3.2.4
Head
The head processes the returned vectors from the selection phase by running them through a small
network closely resembling the encoder network. The specific design may slightly vary based on
the type and dimensionality of the desired output, such as complex or real numbers.
Here’s an example of the head used to generate excitations from the output of the main block:

def __init__(self, state_size, output_size):
super().__init__()
self.net = nn.Sequential(
nn.LazyLinear(4*state_size),
nn.ReLU(),
nn.Linear(4*state_size, output_size),
)
def forward(self, x):
return self.net(x)
class StateToExcitations(nn.Module):
def __init__(self, state_size: int) -> None:
super().__init__()
self.transform = FeedForwardStandard(state_size, 1)
def forward(self, states: Tensor, antenna_masks: Tensor) -> Tensor:
x = self.transform(states).to(torch.float32)
x = torch.exp(1j * x * torch.pi)
x = x.squeeze(-1)
return x * antenna_masks

In our implementation, despite what was introduced in section 2.2, goals are technically denoted

Methods
In this section, we go through our approach for each of the three research questions developed in
collaboration with TICRA. From section 3.1 up until and including section 3.4 tackles the two first
questions relating to the excitation generation problem, and its only in section 3.5 we write about
the position generation problem.
In section 3.1, we discuss creating a model that takes an irregular antenna configuration and
far-field requirements as input, generating excitations that fulfill the specified far-field criteria
(One-shot Solution). Moving to section 3.3, we present our method for crafting a model that, with
similar input, produces effective initial guesses for a solver aimed at determining optimal excitations
(Initial Excitation Solution). Lastly, in section 3.5, we explain the optimization method that finds
good antenna positions for a distribution of far-field requirements (Position Generation Solution).
In addition, in section section 3.2 we explore the solvers involved in the project, and in section 3.4,
we provide a detailed description of the three main model architectures used in this project.
3.1
One-shot Solution
The One-shot Solution takes in a list of antennas, a list of goals and their respectively masks.
Given these two token-types as input, it outputs an excitation for each antenna. The properties
the model should have are outlined below:
• General in the amount of antennas and goals (inputs) to the model. (Must have)
• Output of same dimension as amount of antenna. (Must have)
• Able to deal with irregular antenna arrays. (Must have)
• Invariant to permuting the list of antennas or the list of goals (Nice to have)
• Invariant to translation transformation of the array (Nice to have)
The first three requirements are defined in the scope of the project.
In section section 2.3 a
description of how our framework allows us to fulfill these conditions is given.
The last two requirements are properties that would make sense for the model to have, since the
solution would not change by permutation or translation of the array. All our models are invariant
to permutation, and our Distribution-Based Transformer is also translation invariant. Descriptions
of the models are given in section 3.4.
The excitations generated by the model serve as inputs to the decoder which measures the dB
value for each goal (Equation 13), and the logloss is used to calculate the error compared to the
desired values (Equation 24). We opt to use the logloss, because it emphasizes sharpness around
nulls—an essential characteristic for accurate predictions. Additionally, the log version of the field
is commonly employed in antenna-related domains, and we chose to align with this established
practice.

class StateToExcitations(nn.Module):
def __init__(self, state_size: int) -> None:
super().__init__()
self.transform = FeedForwardStandard(state_size, 1)
def forward(self, states: Tensor, antenna_masks: Tensor) -> Tensor:
x = self.transform(states).to(torch.float32)
x = torch.exp(1j * x * torch.pi)
x = x.squeeze(-1)
return x * antenna_masks
class FeedForwardStandard(nn.Module):
def __init__(self, state_size, output_size):
super().__init__()
self.net = nn.Sequential(
nn.LazyLinear(4*state_size),
nn.ReLU(),
nn.Linear(4*state_size, output_size),
)
def forward(self, x):
return self.net(x)

3.2.2
Combining One-shot Solution with a solver
When seeking to integrate a network with a solver for improved speed and performance, the first
approach that comes to mind is to employ the network’s output as the initial excitations for the
solver. In this setup, the solver essentially serves as a post-processing tool following the one-shot
method.
After experimenting with this approach it became evident that more effective approaches may
exist.
Specifically, the approach in the next section attempt to integrate solvers into the training process
of the network itself.
3.3
Initial Excitations Solution
This approach aims to enhance solvers by generating good initial excitations for them, thereby de-
creasing the time required for the solver to generate effective solutions, and possibly outperforming
solvers in the time-limit as well.
3.3.1
Including the solver in training
The problem with simply applying solvers as a post-processing tool is that there is a misalignment
with what the model does and what would help the solver.
The model attempts to solve the
problem by itself, which is not necessarily the same as generating excitations which are easy and
fast for a solver to continue improving.
Thus, we want to include the solver in the training, so the model becomes well-aligned with it’s
post-processing tool.
5I is the value describing how many iterations the solver should run for each index in the batch (see section 2.4.4).
In most cases this is the same value for all indexes, which means the indicator function, 1(j ≤Ii), simply always is
a 1 - making no differences for each batch index.
30
==PAGE:34==
To generate these good initial excitations for a solver, we can use the same model pipeline as in
the One-shot Solution, where we give goal-tokens and antenna-tokens as input, and outputs an
excitation for each antenna-token. However, while in the previous section, we gave these excitations
to the analytical decoder to obtain gradients, our current objective is different. Instead of using the
excitations directly for controlling the far-field, we aim to assess their effectiveness as initial guesses
for a solver. The obstacle here is that running the solver leads to a loss of access to gradients,
preventing us from employing a straightforward backward pass to train our generative model.
Instead, therefore, we train our own network to act as a decoder that incorporates the effect of
the solver. This network predicts the value in dB for each goal given excitations, positions and
an amount of solver iterations. For instance, when trained with 100 solver iterations, our decoder
network takes excitations, antenna positions and goals as input and predicts the corresponding dB
values for each goal after 100 solver iterations. Then, from this trained decoder that captures the
effect of the solver, we can backpropagate the gradients.
For reasons which becomes clear in the next section, we refer to this decoder network as the Q-net.

This approach of training a function to get access to gradients is a special case of a method from
Reinforcement Learning called Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) [35] [36], namely in
the contextual bandit case where the environment terminates after a single action. The next section
frames this problem in a Reinforcement Learning setting and explains how we can use tricks that
are used in DDPG to create a model which generates good starting guesses.

3.3.5
Q-net filtering
As described, the DDPG algorithm trains a policy model and a Q-net. After the training, the
policy is then used as a generative model for generating the good starting guesses for the solver.
However, there’s an alternative approach to consider. Instead of using the policy, we can draw
random excitations and utilize the Q-net as a filter to distinguish between good and bad excitations
for initial guesses. This is because a forward pass through the Q-net is significantly quicker than
running the solver to assess whether an excitation serves as a good starting point.
This approach, dubbed Q-net filtering, involves randomly sampling a bunch of excitations, and
using the Q-net to predict their reward. If we intent to train a Q-net for this purpose, it makes
little sense to also train a policy. After all, we’re aiming to randomly sample excitations and let the
Q-net filter them out, so it makes sense to focus on training the Q-net exclusively and with random
excitations. To facilitate this, our DDPG algorithm includes a Q-net only mode, dedicated solely
to training the Q-net.
When testing the Q-net filtering, we have 2 parameters, Nsamples and topK. The former, Nsamples,
determines the quantity of random excitations we draw and subsequently present to the Q-net.
Meanwhile, the latter, topK, dictates the number of top values, as per the Q-net’s evaluation, that
we retain and pass on to the solver.

3.4.3

The DBT is build on the idea that we want a model that is invariant to translating the antenna-
array. In other words, we don’t want to input the global position of the antennas to the model in
any way. Instead, we only want to input the relative positions of the antennas to the model.

embedder for antennas that discards all positional information it gets from them. This is done by
replacing the (x, y, z)-coordinates with a randomly sampled number. We add the random number
to allow the model to have an initial way of separating the antenna-tokens from each other.

product between key and query, we also want to let antennas pay attention based on their relative
positions, considering direction and distance from each other.

3.4.3.1
Weighted Normal Distribution Attention
The query, qr, empowers antennas to specify a direction and distance for attention.
For this
attention, rather than employing the usual scaled dot-product attention, we introduce a novel
mechanism which we coin Weighted Normal Distribution Attention. In this approach, qr is
used to parameterize a normal distribution for each of the 2 features. The resulting attention score
corresponds to the probability density function (pdf) of the normal distribution measured in the
key of the queried edge scaled by the weight.
We opted for this attention mechanism based on our understanding of the problem. For instance,
if an antenna is seeking attention 5 wavelengths away, it’s likely interested in anything close to
that distance. Similarly, if it’s looking for attention in a specific direction, anything close to that
direction is probably relevant.
Contrastingly, using the scaled dot-product attention would result in a linear score based on Eu-
clidean distance, where antennas farther away yield higher values. This linear approach lacks the
specificity we need. By introducing the Weighted Normal Distribution Attention, we enable
antennas not just to express which features they are interested in but to specify the value of those
features through the parameterized normal distribution.

3.4.4
Graph Neural Network - GNN
The Graph Neural Network (GNN) employed utilizes the graph transformer operator [38] and
closely resembles our transformer model, with only a few distinctions. Primarily, the models differ
in that we define before-hand which nodes (tokens) are allowed to do attention with each other by
making edges between those nodes. From a theoretical standpoint, this is essentially the same as
using a transformer and changing its attention mask, As, as the mask would in this case simply
represent which nodes are connected by an edge.
In our implementation we use this concept to restrict antenna-to-antenna attention in terms of
proximity.
We keep the attention to and from goals and other informative tokens the same.
Restricting the proximity of the antennas’ attention allows the model to avoid the quadratic com-
putational running time in the amount of antennas that the two other models suffer from. We
choose arbitrarily that if the distance between two antennas is less than 2λ, we make an edge
between them.
We define the attention matrix of the GNN as E, and in first order logic, we have that we want
edges between token i and token j if

(53)
where E(i, j) denotes there being an edge between the token i and j. As(i, j) see Equation 45.
p(·) means the token is an antenna.

have to be closer than 2λ to be connected. This is visualized in Figure 23.

3.5.1
The Optimization Algorithm
Our approach is a gradient descent based method. We start from some initial antenna array, p,
and sample a goal-matrix g ∼G. Then, we generate excitations α ∼M(p, g), and with those
excitations for the antenna we take a gradient step in the loss Llog(p, α, g) with respect to the
positions (∇p).
This iterative process continues until convergence. The alternation between solving for excitations
and taking a gradient step converges akin to stochastic gradient descent, ensuring progress toward
a local minimum.
To expedite the algorithm, we adopt a batch sampling strategy, wherein a large batch of goal-
matrices (in our case 218) is sampled for every position. This allows us to determine excitations for
all sampled goals and take a position step that improves the mean performance across the sampled
goals. This approach significantly reduces the variance of the outer expectation and accelerates
convergence.
For our results, we choose the initial array to simply be a grid in order to get a better understanding
of how much our method can improve this array.

Our random antenna-sampler, Prandom, does not inherently model the physical constraint that
prohibits antennas from being placed too close to each other. This makes it hard to do a fair
comparison between the generated arrays of our Position Generation Solution and the random
sampler in the result section, as some of the sampled antennas will significantly violate the soft
constraint we set.

4.5
Comparing to State of the Art
In this section, we’re comparing our methods—the One-shot Solution and the Initial Excitation
Solution—to the TICRA second-order cone solver (SOCS) and TICRA CNN solution, which are
considered state-of-the-art. In order to compare to these we have to work on planar arrays since
their methods aren’t designed for irregular arrays. We’re exploring various scenarios involving a
range of antennas, starting from a minimum of 9 (3 times 3) up to a maximum of 144 (12 times
12). Given the scale of some of these antenna arrays, we exclusively rely on our GNN for running
the comparisons.
The comparison is done over three scenarios of increasing difficulty:
• The first scenario has 1 peak and 2 nulls
• The second scenario has 2 peaks and 3 nulls
• The third scenario has 4 peaks and 4 nulls
For the first scenario we look at antenna array sized 8 to 12 antennas in each dimension. For
the other two scenarios we have 3 sub-scenario settings of antenna array sizing.
We have the
smaller sized one of 3 to 5 antennas in each dimension, the medium size of 4 to 8 antennas in each
dimension, and the large ones of 8 to 12 antennas in each dimension.
For each of these benchmarks 1000 cases are sampled and used to calculate the statistics.
In addition, TICRA choose some more specific ranges for their goals that aren’t able to be specif-
ically modelled with our goal-sampler without training on more general cases. In particular, they
have a range for nulls that is split into two. For each scenario, we will list the goal-description.
70
==PAGE:74==
For our methods, we decided to train a foundation GNN model for the One-shot Solution as well
as a foundation policy GNN trained with 50 solver iterations. These two models are broad enough
to be compared on all three scenarios on all antenna array sizes given. This should demonstrate
the generality of our solutions. The foundation models are trained for 5 days.
Finally, to show how such a foundation model can be used for finetuning, we finetune the One-shot
Solution foundation model for a day on the specific case and include it as a comparison.
When bench-marking the results, we use the following names to denote the different methods and
models:
SOCS (TICRA) is the second-order cone solver of TICRA run until the algorithm meets stopping
criteria. CNN (TICRA) is the CNN solution of TICRA trained on the specific goal and antenna
configuration of the scenario it is displayed in. GNN (our) is the One-shot Solution foundation
model, trained on goals from 0 to 4 peaks and nulls and antennas from 1 to 12 in each dimension.
GNN finetuned (our) is the GNN foundation after it has been trained for a single day more on
the specific settings of this scenario. Log solver (our) is our log solver starting from a random
excitation. Policy + Log solver (our) is our log solver that starts from our foundation Initial
Excitation Solution policy trained with 50 iterations.
For each two pairs of these 6 methods a paired Z-test is done and the p-values are given in
Appendix H. For testing of significance between the performance in peaks and nulls we use a
Benjamini–Hochberg correction and a false discovery rate of 0.05. We will list all the comparisons
which are not significant, as these are much rarer than the contrary. These will be mentioned in
their corresponding tables showing the performance in peaks and nulls. There is a total of 210
tests: the 6 methods compared in the 7 described scenarios, compared for both mean peak and
mean null values, which gives 6·5
2 · 7 · 2 = 210 tests.

Discussion
Our findings reveal a significant difference in the predictive power of neural networks when tasked
with predicting excitations in irregular arrays as opposed to structured ones, such as grids or linear
arrays—as portrayed in Figure 27. This discrepancy suggests that structured arrays, like grids,
allows the model to utilize simple rules for effective field control. In contrast, irregular arrays
cannot as easily be controlled by simple rules. This raises the possibility that a scalable solution,
dependable for real-time inference, may necessitate the integration of a solver rather than relying
solely on a neural network approach.
Our findings suggest the potential to train foundational models that are versatile in handling vary-
ing quantities of antennas and goals. This versatility holds true for both our One-shot Solution and
Initial Excitation Solution, outperforming TICRA’s methods. This approach carries significant im-
plications for antenna designers, enabling real-time analysis across diverse scenarios. Additionally,
having access to a pre-trained foundational model facilitates much faster training of specialized
models, as it allows for the initiation of new model weights from the established foundation, as
was demonstrated in section 4.5, where we compared our foundation GNN to a GNN finetuned for
one day starting from the foundation GNN.
The One-shot Solution and Initial Excitation Solution complement each other. Solvers have evident
performance advantages on more complex tasks, however when using either a well-structured array
like a grid or an irregular array with few goals, our One-shot Solution is fast and reliable. In fact,
it can be specialized to outperform solver-based solutions in these settings, as seen in Figure 52.
Moreover, our Initial Excitation Solution offers the advantage of not requiring access to the analyt-
ical decoder, making it a more general approach. The method does not rely on the encoder-decoder
structure of the problem. This means we, for example, would be able to train a Q-net in a setting
where we consider antenna coupling if we had a data-set of positions, excitations and measured
far-field.
In section 4.1.2 we showed that our DBT was an improvement from using a Transformer for the
excitation problem. This could have been due to the DBT being translation invariant, meaning
the model doesn’t have to learn this aspect by itself. It could also be related to the antennas being
able to specify which direction and distance they want attention from.
Our DDPG approach provided us with the capability for Q-net filtering as a straightforward way
to find good initial excitations for the solver. This strategy proved to be a big improvement from
using only a solver, as seen in Figure 46. The Q-nets are able to accurately predict the performance
of the solver after initialization as shown in Figure 39. The reason for this network being so strong
in predicting the effect of the solver on the excitations may be attributed to the solver ’smoothing
out’ the far-field goal-space. As such, the network does not need to be able to tell if eg. a null is
precisely on the goal, as long as it is close enough to the null the solver will get it.
The harder part proved to be training a policy network. We saw in section 4.4.2 that the policy
is on par with a Q-net with 5 samples. While this is enough to significantly improve from giving
random excitations to a solver, it may be possible to stabilize the training of the policy more. The
gradients are noisy as they come from the Q-net, which may lead to some bias in the policy.
Regarding the Q-nets (and thus the policy), we showed in section 4.3.1 that the fewer iterations
the solver has the higher the correlation of the Q-net. This is probably because it is harder to
predict further into the future. This puts some limitations for the method to be used if the user
wants many iterations. However, as we showed in Figure 52, Figure 55 and Figure 58, using a
Q-net trained on just 50 iterations still helps even after as much as 25000 iterations.
We introduced the weights of the goals in the training process to enable antenna designer flexiblity
over the far-field. While the One-shot Solution showed it was capable of shifting which goal it
focuses on depending on the weight, we also saw it did so in a blunted way compared to eg. our
solvers as shown in Figure 36. This might be because the One-shot Solution uses the weights as a
88
==PAGE:92==
focus since it does not perform as well with many goals. Nevertheless, in contrast, TICRA’s CNN
approach incorporated the goal weights exclusively into the loss function, offering no direct control
over weights for a single model.
Consequently, their approach necessitates training a separate
model for each desired goal-weight setting.
For the Position Generation Solution we saw in Figure 62 that our approach enables generation
of arrays which are significantly better than what is achieved by random sampling arrays. In the
same plot and Figure 65 we showed that by using our solver as the excitation generator irregular
arrays has better solutions than grid arrays on average. This was both the case for 500 and 5000
solver iterations, and the same finding is seen when changing to a goal-sampler of only peaks as
shown in Figure 67. This is a strong argument that irregular arrays allow for better performance
than grids.
The evolution of the antennas for our experiments seems to differ significantly depending on which
goal-sampler is used. See for example Figure 68 (5 peaks) compared to Figure 63 (0-4 peaks 0-4
nulls). This indicates different antenna arrays are preferred for different tasks. While our method
may be prone to find locally optimal antenna array solutions rather than globally optimal solutions,
the method allows us to get an idea of what kind of arrays are good both depending on the model
used to generate excitation and the task of the array.
An example of such a local solution that still enables us to learn and understand the underlying
best positions is in Figure 71, where we removed the penalty function when solving for positions
on 5 peaks. Here, we saw that our Position Generation Solution put the antennas on top of each
other, effectively creating new single antennas but with increased amplitude. From a theoretical
standpoint, we know the best solution for 5 peaks, assuming no physical constrains, would be to
put all the antennas on top of each other, enabling a perfect peak in all directions.
5.1
Future Work
Our research utilized relatively small networks and limited training time, in contrast to the larger-
scale models with multiple billion parameters currently available. Consequently, we propose an
exploration into the scalability of the One-shot Solution on irregular arrays by employing a deeper
model with extended training duration. Additionally, we suggest a potential revamp of the data-
generation process where we let the model learn simpler cases first and gradually progress to more
challenging ones, following a curriculum learning approach.
We chose to embed all tokens simultaneously, allowing the antenna-tokens to engage in self-
attention with goal-tokens and other informative tokens from the first layer in the main block.
Here, we present some alternative approaches that we believe are worth exploring:
• First have some main block layers only with the antennas, then later introduce the goals.
This would allow us to cache the antenna layers speeding up the computation when working
with different goals on the same grid (during use of model on finalized designs).
• First have some main block layers only with the goals, then later introduce the antennas.
This would allow us to cache the goal layers speeding up the computation when working with
the same goals on different grids (during antenna design).
• Encode goals and antennas separately, and then later let them communicate with each other.
This could be used in both previously mentioned cases, but also efficient in the UI since we
only have to recalculate the part that changed.
Another architectural design specifically relating to the DDPG algorithm is to use a dual-head
network for the Q-net and policy, such that these two nets shared some of the same layers in the
main block. This could save memory and stabilize the training of the policy by anchoring the
network to the Q-net, which has been shown before to be more stable.
89
==PAGE:93==
We saw that the DBT and GNN were on par in performance. This suggests the potential for merg-
ing these two approaches, using GNN proximity based attention in combination with the Weighted
Normal Distribution Attention of the DBT to make the model invariant to translation.
It would be relevant to investigate whether our Initial Excitation Solution works for other solvers
and methods. Especially testing the method using the already established state-of-the-art SOCS
solver would provide direct insight in the relevance of the method.
A big reason our project went the way it did was because of the discovered effectiveness of solvers.
In our DDPG approach we showed that neural networks could be used as assistance to solvers. As
such, we could expand on this idea by including other solver aspects than just the start excitations
in the action-space of the policy. For example, we could let the policy predict case-specific hyper-
parameters for the solvers, such as learning-rate, weighting of goals, and amount of iterations
run.
For our Position Generation Solution we started the optimization from a grid array. This could
lead to local minimums. Instead, we could sample many random arrays as starting arrays and
hopefully find a more global minimum. Additionally, analyzing multiple solved arrays can help
identify general patterns for creating high-quality antenna arrays.
Our Position Generation Solution has been tested for our solver with 500 and 5000 iterations.
Here, different types of methods to generate excitations than solvers could be tested, for example
our One-Shot Solution. This would test the relevance and generality of our approach.
Our Position Generation Solution is limited to offline solving positions for one scenario at a time. In
order to facilitate real-time predictions of positions, we could use our DDPG approach to generate
positions. This would entail the goal-sampler being part of the state, and the positions the action
of the policy. Predicting antenna positions in real-time would allow an antenna designer to quickly
investigate different designs based on different goals. We have started investigation of this approach
as described in Appendix A.6.
5.2
Limitations
When it comes to the comparison we made to TICRA there is no convention for nulls which makes
comparison harder. Particularly, SOCS sometimes solves nulls to values way below our thresh-hold
of -20 dB.
We didn’t have access to SOCS, preventing us from testing it in conjunction with our Initial Exci-
tation Solution and Position Generation Solution. Instead, we relied on a limited set of benchmarks
from SOCS, forcing our comparison to focus on scenarios involving grid arrays. Our goal-sampler
is more general and can’t replicate these goal-settings without sampling from a superset of goals.
Similarly, TICRA’s CNN solution is designed for grid arrays, restricting our ability to compare it
with our methods on irregular arrays, which is our main scope of the project.
In extension of this, the research done on irregular arrays is shallow, and therefore it is hard to find
other approaches to compare our methods with, which is why we decided to show a comparison to
the baseline of random excitations for some of our results.
Our main limitations is access to compute. We had access to around 10 GPUs, which meant that
we only could run a limited number of experiments. This was one of the key factors that led us to
limit the scope of our project in various ways. Without this constraint, we could have investigated
fixed and non-uniform arrays, branching out into DDPG position generation methods, and tested
many more hyper-parameters. In addition, our runs are limited to one GPU each, which puts
memory-constrains on how big our models, inputs, and batch sizes can be. See Appendix B for
more details on compute resources.

6
Conclusion
In this project, we established a mask-based training pipeline employing an encoder-decoder struc-
ture, allowing us to train models for phase control with the ability to generalize across various
irregular antenna arrays, considering varying antenna quantities and goals.
Within this pipeline, we introduced the One-shot Solution—a model that, given an irregular an-
tenna configuration and far-field requirements, outputs excitations fulfilling the specified criteria.
Evaluation of this solution across three different model architectures (GNN, Transformer, DBT)
on irregular antenna arrays revealed comparable performance between the two best models namely
the GNN and DBT, with GNN exhibiting superior scalability in terms of antenna quantity (linear
scaling instead of quadratic). Our models demonstrated the capability to prioritize various goals
based on weights, showcased the ability to generalize, and even performed well on more antennas
than they were trained on.
The One-shot Solution GNN is shown to outperform TICRA’s CNN solution for both mean value
of peaks and nulls, despite being trained in a much more general setting. Only in the setting
where the goals are few and fixed to vary in just one dimension does the CNN, trained on this
specific case, show better performance. However, by finetuning the GNN to this specific setting,
it surpassed the CNN and even the solvers. In more complicated cases with numerous goals, our
results revealed that neural network-only solutions do not scale well for irregular antenna arrays. In
contrast, solvers demonstrated the ability to scale to these complexities, albeit at a slower inference
speed.
We extended the training pipeline to allow for the Initial Excitation Solution. Here, we used DDPG
to create a model that takes as input an irregular antenna configuration and far-field requirements,
and outputs good starting guesses to a solver which solves for best excitations. This method was
shown to improve the performance of our solver, and could be used both to do Q-net filtering and
train a policy. We showed that by using a policy to generate the initial excitations for the solver,
we got improved solutions that also converge faster. Here, a comparison to SOCS and our solver
without a policy showed a significant improvement in mean dB value of peaks. Additionally, we
demonstrated that by training the DDPG networks on solvers with varying numbers of iterations,
we could empower the model to grasp the trade-off between solution quality and speed.
This
enabled the model to identify the optimal initial excitations for a given number of solver iterations.
Finally, we proposed the Position Generation Solution, a novel optimization method capable of
producing antenna positions facilitating efficient phase control for a distribution of far-field require-
ments and informed by a phase control model. The method was shown to generate significantly
better arrays than randomly sampled irregular and grid arrays as measured by average error in dB
for peaks and nulls. The method was evaluated using our solver and demonstrated that it tailors
its solutions to different solver settings and distributions of far-field requirements.
A UI has been developed as part of the project. The UI enables interactive configuring of antenna
arrays and far-field requirements, as well as real-time inspection and analysis of antenna arrays and
models. The UI serves as a valuable tool for gaining in-depth insights into phase control models.
Our work sets the foundation for the advancement of phase control for irregular arrays.
The
research suggests that irregular arrays hold significant advantages to grid arrays, and can be
synthesised for improved phase control. Having a single foundation neural network with multiple
purposes opens up the door for antenna designers to explore a much wider range of ideas in real-
time. The research suggests the potential for enhancing the performance of current methods, such
as solvers, through the use of neural networks.

Memory Tokens
An other approach we tried was to give our models memory tokens. Our model design allows for
arbitrary tokens which then gets encoded end fed to the main models. Memory tokens would have

A.6
DDPG for Position Generation
We implemented the DDPG algorithm for generating positions, here the antenna positions, p, are
the action of the policy rather than the excitations. The G as well as the model M is then the
state.
Then we introduce what we call a goal sampler sampler which is another abstraction layer, such
that it samples goalsamplers which the model is then trained to predict good positions for.
The idea is that if we can have a model that can predict good positions for different goal distribu-
tions, then it becomes really easy for designers to get an idea of what a good antenna array looks
like given the types of goals they need to do, eg. do they need 5 peaks or is it more 1 peak and 1
null, the solution will vary between those cases.
We decided to narrow the scope of the report by excluding this, mainly because there were many
choices regarding the tokenization of the goal-sampler and position generator model which we
didn’t feel like we solved completely. In addition this is an abstraction level beyond the DDPG for
the excitation generation, and our approach entails sampling many goals for each sampled case,
meaning the batch size we can use is limited and the training time necessary high. Nevertheless,
the idea was to tokenize the sampled goalsamplers as an other informative token and feed this
to the policy network as part of the state.
