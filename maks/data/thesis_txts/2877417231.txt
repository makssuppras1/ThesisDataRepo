Abstract
PhaseВӯtype (PH) distributions provide a flexible and mathematically tractable framework for modelling
of continuousВӯtime Markov chains (CTMCs) and are widely used in applications involving lifetimes and
waiting times. In many practical situations, however, systems generate multiple correlated cumulative
quantities rather than a single time to absorption. Multivariate phaseВӯtype distributions with rewards
(MPHвҲ—) extend the classical PH framework by introducing stateВӯdependent reward structures that
produce multivariate accumulated outcomes.
The primary objective of this thesis is to investigate likelihoodВӯbased parameter estimation for MPHвҲ—
distributions, with particular focus on applying ExpectationВӯConditional Maximization (ECM) methods,
which are extensions of the ExpectationВӯMaximization (EM) algorithm. After presenting the theoretical
foundations of CTMCs and the PH family, the thesis derives the completeВӯdata likelihood for the
univariate PH distribution and reviews the corresponding EM algorithm. The analysis is then extended
to the MPHвҲ—setting, for which a general closedВӯform expression for the observedВӯdata likelihood is
not available.
To address this challenge, the thesis examines five different MPHвҲ—model structures. These include
the five cases: (i, ii) a bivariate and multivariate case with a known CTMC structure with an invertВӯ
ible reward matrix, (iii) a bivariate structure with an unknown path structure for the CTMC with an
invertible reward matrix, (iv) a binary structure an almost deterministically known CTMC path with a
nonВӯinvertible reward matrix, and (v) with an CTMC structure there produce a unknown path with a
nonВӯinvertible reward matrix. For these models, explicit likelihood expressions are derived under reВӯ
strictive assumptions. Based on these results, ECM algorithms are derived and implemented for the
proposed MPHвҲ—structures with computational numerical assumptions, demonstrating that likelihoodВӯ
based estimation is feasible in carefully structured settings, and that the ECM performance rely a lot
on the amount of knowledge known about the process.
The results clarify the structural conditions under which EMВӯ and ECMВӯbased estimation methods can
be successfully applied to MPHвҲ—models. Overall, the thesis contributes to a clearer understanding of
when likelihoodВӯbased parameter estimation is feasible for multivariate phaseВӯtype distributions with
accumulated rewards.

Motivation and Outline of the Thesis
Complex systems in, e.g., engineering, finance, healthcare, and environmental modelling often evolve
over time in ways that are inherently stochastic. In such systems, events occur randomly, their timing is
uncertain, and different components may influence one another as the system progresses. Modelling
these dynamics, un order to utilize them for interference, requires probabilistic frameworks capable
of capturing both temporal behavior and structural dependencies. ContinuousВӯtime Markov chains
(CTMCs) provide one such framework: they describe systems that jumps between states over time
according to memoryless stochastic mechanisms.
PhaseВӯtype (PH) distributions arise naturally in this context. A PH distribution represents the time
until absorption in a CTMC whose transient states model intermediate phases of a process. Because
PH distributions can approximate any positiveВӯvalued distribution arbitrarily well and are closed under
mixtures, convolutions, and order statistics, they have become widely used for modelling lifetimes,
delays, and event durations.
In many applications, however, a single time to absorption is not sufficient to describe the system
of interest. Real systems often generate multiple correlated quantities as the underlying process
evolves. This motivates multivariate extensions of PH models. The first such extension, the multiВӯ
variate phaseВӯtype (MPH) distribution, characterizes several hitting times of a common CTMC. While
MPH captures dependence among events, it remains limited in settings where interest lies not in first
hitting times but in accumulated quantities such as cost, workload, energy, or damage.
To address this, the class of MPHвҲ—distributions was introduced. In an MPHвҲ—model, the CTMC is
augmented with a reward structure that accumulates vectorВӯvalued outputs as time progresses. Each
state contributes to each component of the observed reward vector at a specified rate, and the final
observed data consist of the accumulated rewards up to absorption for specified attributes. MPHвҲ—
distributions therefore offer a flexible and interpretable multivariate representation that extends the
modelling capabilities of PH distributions to a broad class of realВӯworld systems where cumulative
quantities are of primary interest.
Despite their modelling potential, parameter estimation for MPHвҲ—distributions remains challenging.
Unlike the PH case, where likelihoods have a general closed form and efficient estimation methods
(e.g., ExpectationвҖ“Maximization (EM) algorithms) are well established, the likelihood of a general
MPHвҲ—model cannot currently be expressed analytically. This complicates the development of general
estimation techniques for the MPHвҲ—class. There is, therefore, a need for practical estimation methods
that exploit the structural properties of MPHвҲ—models.
The purpose of this thesis is to contribute to this need. The thesis provides a systematic exposition
of the PH family, with particular attention to likelihood formulations and the role of sufficient statistics.
Building on this foundation, the EM algorithm for PH distributions is analyzed and implemented, folВӯ
lowed by an extension to the MPHвҲ—case with conditional maximization via the ExpectationвҖ“Conditional

The Family of PhaseВӯType Distributions
The family of phaseВӯtype distributions is a class of distributions for which the behaviour is driven by
an underlying Markov process. The simplest member of this family is the standard univariate phaseВӯ
type (PH) distribution, which models the total time until absorption in a system that moves through a
sequence of transient states on the nonnegative real line. The process begins in a transient state,
jumps randomly among the transient states, and eventually enters an absorbing state, where the
process remains permanently.
This chapter begins with an introduction to Markov processes. This is followed by a presentation of
the univariate PH distribution, then a brief introduction to its multivariate extension, the multivariate PH
(MPH) distribution. After this, the MPHвҲ—class, which is the primary focus of this thesis, is introduced.
Although the estimation of MPHвҲ—models is the primary focus of this thesis, the derivation of the
broader PH family is included to provide a structural and conceptual understanding. Unless otherwise
stated, the following theory follows [1] and [2].

2.2
PH Distributions
A PH distribution is defined as the distribution of the absorption time of a CTMC with m transient states
and a single absorbing state a. Working with a single absorbing state does not reduce the generality of
PH distributions [6, p. 9], since any PH distribution constructed from a CTMC with multiple absorbing
states can be represented by an equivalent CTMC with a single absorbing state that yields the same
absorptionВӯtime distribution.
PH distributions are highly flexible because they are closed under finite mixtures, finite convolutions,
and coherent system constructions. In practice, this means that mixtures of PH distributions, sums of
PHВӯdistributed random variables, and system lifetimes described by PH distributions all remain within
the PH family. As a result, PH distributions can represent a wide variety of lifetime and waitingВӯtime
behaviors arising from multiple interacting subprocesses or stages [7, p. 267].

as the expected completeВӯdata logВӯlikelihood. This approach avoids the direct evaluation of the often
intractable integrals appearing in the observedВӯdata likelihood, as discussed in Chapter 3.

in the set of feasible observations, which can cause the algorithm to settle in suboptimal regions
of the penalized likelihood surface. In this setting, more reliable estimation may require multiple
initializations, stronger regularization, or alternative optimization strategies to reduce sensitivity to
local optima and feasibility boundaries.

Discussion
This thesis has investigated likelihoodВӯbased inference for MPHвҲ—models using ECMВӯtype procedures, with
particular focus on how model structure affects estimation accuracy, numerical stability, and computational
cost. The examples considered throughout the thesis demonstrate that the behavior of the estimation procedure
depends strongly on how much is known about the latent CTMC dynamics and how the reward structure maps
observed data Y to latent quantities.
A central theme across the examples is how the reward mapping links Y to the latent sojourn times Z. When
the mapping allows Z to be recovered directly through an invertible R, the latent structure becomes more
explicit in the likelihood evaluation and the algorithm can often move quickly toward feasible parameter values.
However, the results also show that invertibility alone is not a guarantee of stable inference. In Example 3,
where the CTMC path is unobserved and R is updated jointly with the rate parameters, the reconstruction
step Z = Y RвҲ’1 makes the objective highly sensitive to intermediate values of R. Even if R is invertible by
construction, it can still be close to illВӯconditioned during the iterations, so small parameter changes may flip
reconstructed sojourn times from feasible to infeasible. This creates abrupt changes through penalties or subset
effects and leads to an irregular likelihood surface with multiple local optima. The Monte Carlo experiments
therefore show sensitivity to initialization and convergence to different likelihood levels across replications.
In contrast, Example 4 illustrates that nonВӯinvertible reward structures can yield highly stable estimation when
latent variables are handled through conditional expectations rather than direct reconstruction. In that setting,
the EВӯstep integrates out the latent components smoothly, which produces a smoother objective and more
consistent convergence behavior across runs. This comparison highlights that what matters in practice is not
only whether R is invertible, but more generally whether the latent structure is treated in a way that avoids
abrupt feasibility boundaries and discontinuities in the objective function.
The results in Example 5 sharpen this point. Example 5 is also an unknownВӯpath setting with backjumps, but
it replaces direct reconstruction and penalization with an explicit representation over latent visit counts and
path indicators, combined with adaptive truncation and stable numerical integration in the EВӯstep. Empirically,
this change leads to behavior that is much closer to the stable examples: the likelihood gap per observation
decreases with Nobs, runВӯtoВӯrun variability reduces with sample size, and the ECM trajectories cluster tightly
around the benchmark likelihood. Compared to Example 3, the improvement is consistent with the fact that the
observed likelihood is evaluated smoothly as a sum/integral over latent configurations, rather than being driven
by whether reconstructed Z happens to be feasible for a particular intermediate R. In other words, Example 5
indicates that many of the practical problems in Example 3 are not caused by lack of information in the data
alone, but by how the latent structure is represented and optimized numerically.
Another important distinction is whether the CTMC dynamics (the generator Q) are assumed known or must be
estimated. When the CTMC structure is fixed and the latent dynamics are simple, inference focuses mainly on
the reward parameters and the optimization problem is typically more stable. When Q allows backjumps (as in
Examples 3 and 5), the parameter space becomes larger and the likelihood surface becomes harder to optimize.
The examples show that joint estimation can be successful under appropriate structural assumptions, but it is
computationally demanding and sensitive to numerical tuning (e.g. step sizes, innerВӯloop iterations, truncation
controls, and integration grids).
A key practical conclusion from the numerical results is that the main limitation in the difficult settings is often
optimization rather than statistical efficiency. In particular, Example 3 shows that an ECMВӯtype algorithm can

converge numerically while still terminating at a suboptimal solution because it becomes trapped in a local maxВӯ

the interpretation that improving the numerical treatment of latent variables and strengthening the CM/ECM

This suggests that better conditional maximization steps, stronger lineВӯsearch or trustВӯregion strategies, alterВӯ

challenging examples, multiple starting values are therefore not only a robustness check but may be required

The thesis has primarily focused on derivations and modelВӯspecific likelihood constructions, and the impleВӯ
mentations were designed to follow the theoretical development closely. This leaves room for further work on
computational improvements and optimization routines for the CM steps. Such improvements could reduce
computation time substantially and may also improve numerical accuracy by allowing tighter tolerances and
more reliable convergence diagnostics.
From an applied modelling perspective, the results emphazise that the practical usefulness of MPHвҲ—models
depends on how much structure is available from the application. In many settings (for example in engineered
systems), parts of the reward structure may be known or can be constrained using domain knowledge. When
reward rates are known or strongly restricted, the connection between observed accumulated rewards and
latent dynamics becomes stronger, which simplifies inference and reduces ambiguity. If all of R is known, it
could lead to a deterministically known sum of soujon times, which would provide an absorption time. That
could mean that the latent CTMC could be defined with the already usable EM algorithm.
Overall, the examples illustrate that inference for MPHвҲ—models is closely tied to modelling choices and the
amount of structural knowledge available. The results highlight that there is no single oneВӯsizeВӯfitsВӯall estiВӯ
mation strategy: different model structures lead to different requirements for feasibility handling, conditional
expectations, and numerical optimization. The distinction between settings that rely on direct reconstruction
of latent variables and settings that integrate them out smoothly has clear practical consequences for stability,
computational cost, and sensitivity to local optima. Successful application of rewardВӯbased phaseВӯtype models
therefore requires not only appropriate statistical methodology, but also careful numerical optimization and a
clear understanding of the underlying system being modelled.
Several open questions remain. In particular, it would be valuable to understand how much structural informaВӯ
tion is required to ensure reliable inference in general, and how such structure can be imposed without making
the model unrealistically restrictive. Another important direction is the development of more robust optimization
strategies for the CM steps, especially in settings where feasibility boundaries or mixture representations create
multimodal likelihoods. Addressing these questions would be an important step toward making MPHвҲ—models
more broadly applicable in practice.

Further research with MCMC
A natural direction for further research is the development of inference procedures for MPHвҲ—models that go
beyond special structural cases and can be applied in more general settings. In this thesis, estimation methods
have been studied in Examples 1 and 2, where the EвҖ“step becomes tractable due to the specific structure of
the model, and in Examples 3, 4, and 5, where some form of approximation of the conditional expectations is
required, and some numerical assumption had to be made to solve nonВӯclosed form integrals and infinity sums.
The central question that follows is whether a more general inference mechanism can be developed that avoids
these strctural problems, where the EВӯstep contains estimation of analytical unsolvable settings. One direction
is the use of Bayesian methods combined with Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques.
Bayesian inference combined with Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods provides a natural framework
for parameter estimation in models with complex latent structures. A contribution in this direction for phaseВӯtype
distributions is due to [24], who develop a Bayesian data approach for univariate PH models. Their key idea is to
augment the observed absorption times with the full latent continuousвҖ“time Markov chain (CTMC) trajectories
and perform inference on the joint posterior distribution of the model parameters and the latent paths.
A central technical result of [24] is that CTMC sample paths can be efficiently simulated conditional on a fixed
absorption time using a MetropolisвҖ“Hastings algorithm. Conditioned on the latent path, the completeВӯdata
likelihood factorizes linearly in the sufficient statistics (Bi, Zi, Nij, NiA), allowing the conditional posterior disВӯ
tributions of the parameters to be sampled in closed form under conjugate priors. This could be interesting to
investegate how this could work to simulate from accumulated rewards.

Conclusion
This thesis has investigated likelihoodВӯbased parameter estimation for phaseВӯtype (PH) models and their mulВӯ
tivariate extension with accumulated rewards, known as multivariate phaseВӯtype distributions with rewards
(MPHвҲ—). For standard PH distributions, the EM algorithm is a wellВӯestablished and effective estimation method,
and this thesis confirms its suitability as a starting point for the implementation and extension to structured
MPHвҲ—models.
The main conclusion of this thesis is that likelihoodВӯbased inference for MPHвҲ—models is feasible within an
ECM framework only under strong structural assumptions on the underlying CTMC and the reward matrix,
together with numerical assumptions when conditional expectations are analytically intractable. In particular,
the absence of general closedВӯform expressions for the observedВӯdata likelihood and the required conditional
expectations constitutes a fundamental limitation for general MPHвҲ—models. Through five example stuctures
with different assumptions on the CTMC structure and the reward matrix R, it was shown that the tractability of
the conditional expectations required for maximizing the expected logВӯlikelihood depends critically on the model
structure. Even in lowВӯdimensional settings and under restrictive assumptions, the derivation of completeВӯdata
likelihoods and conditional expectations proved to be technically demanding.
Within these limitations, the thesis demonstrates that the ECM algorithm provides a practical and numerically
stable estimation framework for structured MPHвҲ—models. By decomposing the maximization step into simpler
conditional updates, the ECM approach allows analytical and numerical optimization steps to be combined,
thereby avoiding intractable joint maximization problems. For the five examples considered, the resulting Q
functions exhibited strong parameter coupling, which motivated the use of conditional maximization steps and
numerical optimization for the reward parameters. When the required conditional expectations could be comВӯ
puted or accurately approximated under assumptions, the ECM algorithm was observed to converge reliably
and to produce accurate parameter estimates.
At the same time, the thesis shows that this ECMВӯbased approach does not extend naturally to general MPHвҲ—
models. When the reward structure is nonВӯinvertible, the model dimension increases, or the conditional expectaВӯ
tions cannot be evaluated explicitly, deterministic EM or ECMВӯbased likelihood inference becomes impractical.
Consequently, parameter estimation for general MPHвҲ—models remains an open problem.
In conclusion, this thesis establishes clear conditions under which likelihoodВӯbased estimation for MPHвҲ—models
is viable and clarifies the fundamental obstacles that arise outside these settings. The results provide a concrete
foundation for future research, in particular for the development of Monte Carlo or Bayesian inference methods
capable of handling the latent structure of general MPHвҲ—models when conditional expectations are analytically
intractable.
