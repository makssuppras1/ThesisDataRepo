Abstract
Purpose and objectives The aim of this thesis is to assess and evaluate the efficacy and safety of
glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) receptor agonists (RAs), sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2)
inhibitors and insulins in relation to low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, triglyceride (TG),
and c-reactive protein (CRP) levels, and their impact on cardiovascular disease (CVD) risk in
patients with type 2 diabetes (T2D). Furthermore, to expand upon the current meta-analyses
(MAs), by evaluate the impact of biomarkers and dose level effects on major adverse cardiovascular
event (MACE), achieved through a model-based meta-analysis (MBMA) to assess the various
effects.
Methods Eligible key phase III studies were identified through searches in the PubMed, Web of
Science, and Scopus databases. Randomized clinical studies were selected if they compared GLP-1
RAs, SGLT2 inhibitors, or insulins with placebos. A biomarker and CVD risk relationship were
investigated using the linear meta-regression technique. Biomarkers examined were placebo-adjusted
percentage change from baseline of glycated haemoglobin (hbA1c), body weight (BW), LDL, and
TG. Dose-response relationships were investigated using model-based techniques to assess the drug-
specific MACE risk. Cox proportional-hazards and logistic regression models were used to assess
the effects of a wider range of potential biomarkers based on baseline values from placebo data.
Results 45 studies were included in the MA. Of these, were 15 cardiovascular outcome trial (CVOT)s,
comprising eight with GLP-1 RAs, six with SGLT2 inhibitors, and one with insulin. Across all
three drug classes, random-effects MA models showed that the change from baseline of LDL were
decreased with 0.63 mg
dL (95% CI -1.86 to 0.6), and that TG were decrease with -5.15 mg
dL (95% CI
-8.09 to -2.22). Across GLP-1 RAs, the change from baseline of CRP were -0.48 mg
L (95% CI -0.87
to -0.09). An association between drug treatment and MACE risk was observed, (HR 0.91, 95% CI
0.87 to 0.96), with significant heterogeneity (I2 = 53%, p < 0.01). This heterogeneity was driven by
the insulin CVOT. Random-effects meta-regression analyses showed, for GLP-1 RAs, that hbA1c
(slope = 0.234, p = 0.033) and BW (slope = 0.078, p = 0.027) placebo-adjusted percentage change
from baseline was the best descriptor of MACE. This significant relationship was not observed for
LDL and TG, nor for SGLT2 inhibitors.
Increasing doses adjusted for relative potency of semaglutide showed to decrease CVD risk. The
same behaviour was not observed with SGLT2 inhibitors with dapagliflozin potency-equivalent
adjusted doses. A Cox model with age as time-scaled found gender, BMI, race, and diastolic and
systolic blood pressure as significant predictors for CVD risk. Through logistic regression, age,
gender, BMI, race, and pulse rate were found significant.
Conclusions This MBMA demonstrated that for GLP-1 RAs, hbA1c and BW reductions led to a
significant benefit for MACE risk. The effect of lipids on MACE were found inconcludable due to
the lack of summary level study data. A reduction in CVD risk with higher doses was observed
with GLP-1 RAs, while no such relationship was found with SGLT2 inhibitors.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Two types of diabetes mellitus exist: type 1 and type 2 diabetes (T2D). Diabetes disease in general,
is characterized by a lacking regulation of the movement of sugar into the body’s cells due to the
production lack of insulin (hormone) by the pancreatic beta cells.
Diabetes is a serious condition increasing the risk for cardiovascular disease [1]. Especially T2D,
the large majority of all diabetes cases, is considered to be responsible for a higher risk for cardio-
vascular (CV) mortality compared to non-diabetic individuals [2]. This is added to the well-known
increased prevalence of the risk factors such as hypertension, dyslipidaemia and obesity. A common
CV related endpoint is three-point MACE (major adverse cardiovascular event), consisting of i)
nonfatal stroke, ii) nonfatal myocardial infarction (MI), and iii) CV death events. When the latter
is extended to also include unstable angina (UA), then the endpoint is referred to as four-point
MACE (major adverse cardiovascular event-4).
The relationship between diabetes and cardiovascular disease (CVD) is complex. The interaction
between risk factors and their pathogenesis leading to an increase in CV risk is highly significant.
To explore this interaction, three distinct types of anti-diabetic medication with varying effects on
MACE have been chosen for the analysis in this thesis: insulin, glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1)
receptor agonist (RA) and sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitor. Insulin, which is
regarded as having no impact on MACE-3, GLP-1 RAs that have demonstrated a decreased risk
of MACE-3, and SGLT2 inhibitors, have shown CV benefits [3–5].
Most outcome studies involving any of these three treatments, primarily focus on improving gly-
caemic control, as well as reductions in body weight (BW). Current meta-analyses (MAs) have
studied their effects on the biomarkers: glycated haemoglobin (hbA1c) and BW, in relation to
MACE, while the effect of inflammatory biomarkers, such as cholesterol and c-reactive protein
(CRP), has not been investigated [6, 7]. The levels of CRP, low-density lipoprotein (LDL) choles-
terol and triglyceride (TG) reflects artery inflammation and is related to increased risk of CVD
[8, 9]. Furtherly, previous studies have emphasized the importance of adressing a reduction of
TG levels in the context of CVD across clinical studies [10]. An improvement in lipid profiles i.e.
controlled dyslipidemia, for T2D patients, can decrease the risk of CV complications, which has
been observed for GLP-1 RAs [11].
This thesis wishes to assess CV risk patients with T2D using multiple biomarkers, based on
available summary level literary data. The aim is to offer additional insights into overall changes in
lipid and CRP profiles for the three treatment classes. Given that these treatments operate through
different mechanisms, they will be be compared together and individually to provide guidance for
future therapeutic management strategies.
Determining the role of these biomarkers will become more challenging due to the limited mea-
surement and reporting of LDL or TG levels in phase III cardiovascular outcome trial (CVOT)s

In addition to reductions in lipid and CRP levels, evidence from multiple studies indicates an

PURPOSE & OBJECTIVES
2
Purpose & Objectives
The primary objective of this thesis is to assess the impact of insulin, incretin, more specifically
GLP-1 RA, and SGLT2 inhibitor treatments on MACE in T2D populations, with a specific focus
on key CV biomarkers, including LDL, TG, and CRP.
Furthermore, in this thesis it is wished to quantify the potential effect the dose of the various drugs
has on CV risk, in order to provide insights valuable for clinical decision-making and therapeutic
strategies. The latter objective will contribute to a deeper understanding of the nuanced relationship
between drug dosage and CV risk based on the large amount of hbA1c and BW information supplied
through clinical studies.
The main four objectives of this thesis are stated below:
• Study and evaluate the forms of MAs in a medical context, and summarize advantages and
pitfalls
• Perform a MBMA of key phase III studies of insulin, including GLP-1 RA and SGLT2
inhibitor drug classes, reporting effects on CRP and lipids in order to predict the effects of
the drug and dose levels in CVOT.
• Extend current MA of T2D data to investigate the effect of CRP and lipid levels on MACE
in a diabetic placebo-treated population.
• Quantify and evaluate the effects of the insulins, SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 RAs drugs on
MACE risk.

BIOLOGICAL BACKGROUND
3
Biological background
In this section, an overview is provided regarding T2D and its relation to CVD. Further three T2D
treatment drug classes are introduced and discussed: i) GLP-1 and GIP/GLP-1 RAs, ii) SGLT2
inhibitors, and iii) insulins.
3.1
Diabetes and cardiovascular disease
There are two types of diabetes mellitus, known as type 1 (T1D) and type 2 diabetes (T2D). Type 1
diabetes is a chronic disease characterized by immune-mediated destruction of the insulin producing
pancreatic beta-cells. While T2D is a condition where the regulation of movement of sugar into
the body cells is lacking because of insufficient insulin [29]. The lack of insulin in T2D patients is
caused by the beta cells of the pancreas insufficiently producing it. Further, in T2D patients the
cells show a poor response to the available insulin, leading to less sugar uptake by the cell. There
are several primary risk factors for developing CVD, including overweight, smoking, hypertension,
insufficient activity, hypertriglyceridaemia, dyslipidemia, and T2D [2].
Established T2D is observed as a major risk factor of CVD [9, 30]. The shared risk factors between
T2D and CVD and the fact that they can precede the development for each other, based on the
same underlying genetic and environmental causes, is termed by the ’common soil’ hypothesis [31].
Other identified risk factors for CVD observed across the T2D population are dylipidemias and
hypertriglyceridaemia [10, 32].
The occurrence of CVD, such as MI, in the context of dyslipidemia and hypertriglyceridaemia is
primarily attributed to the formation of occlusive thrombi on arterial plaque [8]. Arterial plaque
formation can be described by multiple key steps including sequential triggers and responses.
Hypercholesterolemia, a high level of LDL cholesterol, can initiate an inflammatory response in the
artery wall when infiltrated by LDL particles. The latter process triggers macrophages to migrate
to the inflamed site to assemble here to take up oxidized LDL particles. The antigen-presenting
macrophages initiates the activation of the body’s immune cells, leading to a subsequent synthesis
of inflammatory cytokines, such as interleukin-1β. In response to these circulating cytokines, the
liver is stimulated to produce the acute phase protein CRP. High levels of CRP are an indicator
for a worsening in CV progresion. Increased levels of circulating TG i.e. hypertriglyceridaemia, can
imitate LDL, initiating artery inflammation [9, 33].
Long term adherence to a healthy lifestyle can be an obstacle for T2D patients and often is also
insufficient for reducing the CV risk to acceptable levels, and so pharmacological intervention is
required [34]. In this thesis the effect GLP-1 RAs (including GIP/GLP-1 RAs and SGLT2 inhibitors)
and insulins have on improving CV related conditions is studied.

BIOLOGICAL BACKGROUND
3.2
GLP-1 RAs
GLP-1s are from the group of metabolic hormones called incretin, which stimulates insulin secretion
by the pancreas [35]. The secretion of the gut-produced peptide is regulated by nutritional, hormonal
and neural signals. Being released by the L cells in the intestine, the GLP-1 hormone can travel
to remote tissue like the pancreas and the pancreatic beta-cells via the circulation. Here the
GLP-1 receptors are highly expressed and by activation induce the secretion of insulin. Figure 1
shows a GLP-1 hormone secreting L cell into the interstitium, hereafter entering the circulation.
Another gut-produced incretin hormone called GIP is produced by K cells localized in the upper
gastrointestinal tract.
In T2D, the incretin effect is reduced or absent, leading to glucose intolerance. In order to induce
or revive the incretin effect GLP-1- and GIP RAs can be a solution. These two agonist can bind to
GLP-1 and GIP receptors in tissues of interest, causing a biological response similar to GLP-1 and
GIP hormones produced in healthy individuals [35]. Currently, available GLP-1 RAs have to high
homology to native GLP-1. While there are numerous GLP-1 RAs available on the market to apply
as treatment, GIP receptor activation is currently only achievable through the dual GIP/GLP-1

SGLT2 inhibitors
The glomeruli in our kidneys filters many grams of glucose daily. In healthy individuals all blood
glucose is actively reabsorbed into the circulation [5]. This transport of glucose across epithelial
cells occurring in both the kidney and the intestine is achieved by a family of membrane proteins
called sodium-glucose cotransporters (SGLTs) [5]. In the intestine the transport is achieved by
SGLT1 only, and in the kidneys by both SGLT1 and SGLT2. SGLT2 is over-expressed in the
epithelium of the proximal convoluted tubule (PCT) (see figure 2). These peptides are responsible
for the glucose reabsorption of >90% of the filtered glucose from the glomerulus. The transport
of glucose is sodium (Na+) dependent, hereby the name of the membrane proteins. The exit of
glucose into the interstitial fluid is facilitated by glucose transporters (GLUT) (GLUT2 in figure
2). If the amount of glucose in the circulation exceeds around 180 mg/dl, the excretion of glucose
via the urine increase,. This is often seen in patients with T2D, termed glucosuria [5].

4.1
Cox proportional-hazards model
In the context of MACE, hazard ratios (HRs) are pivotal in quantifying the relative risk of experi-
encing a certain event in the treatment arm with regards to another treatment or control arm in a
study. The HR is a measure used to compare the probability of the occurrence of events between
different groups over time. This quantitative comparison between groups is often accompanied with
a Kaplan-Meier curve, illustrating the risk of particular events during the duration of a study. This
representation of time-to-event (TTE) data can be displayed with time on the horizontal axis and
the risk rate or cumulative risk of events in percentage on the vertical axis. Data in such survival
analyses is characterized by: i) the patient-specific serial time, ii) their status at the end of their
serial time, and iii) the study group they belong to [39]. In the duration of a study, a subject can
experience the particular event of interest or be censored, i.e. the event is not observed before the
study is terminated.
HRs are often obtained using the stratified Cox proportional-hazards model, Cox model, which
allows for the analysis of data while adjusting for potential confounding variables. This allows for
stratification of data based on e.g. treatment arm, age or gender. The Cox model can be described
by a linear function for the log-hazard or as multiplicative effect on the hazard function (equation
2). In this semi-parametric model, covariates, also termed predictors, are entering linearly and the
baseline cumulative hazard function h0(t) can take any form [40].

Key assumptions undertaken by the Cox model are, i) proportional hazard assumption implying

4.3
Meta-regression
Meta-regression is a statistical technique used in MA and allows for the exploration of how covariates
are associated with effect sizes, such as hbA1c and BW. While the dependent variables in a MA
are obtained as study-level summary data rather than individual subject data, the approach is
similar to that of simple regression. Meta-regression can be performed under the assumption that
all studies share a common effect and that the variance in the observed effect sizes are due to
sampling error, or under the assumption that the observed study effect sizes differ because of real
differences, accounting for heterogeneity. This is termed fixed- and random effects meta-regression
[44].

5.1
Meta-analysis
This section addresses the different stages of a MA, including systematic literature search, study
selection, data extraction and analysis, assessment of heterogeneity, publication bias, and methods
used for meta-regression. The books Introduction to meta-analysis and Meta-Analysis with R are
used as references [44, 47].
5.1.1
Literature search
The research question to investigate and to be answered: Is there a CV benefit for T2D patients
treated with glucagon-like peptide 1 and glucagon-like peptide 1/glucose-dependent insulinotropic
polypeptide receptor agonists, sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors or insulins?
The effect of GLP-1 and GIP/GLP-1 RAs, SGLT2 inhibitors and insulins on the levels of LDL,
TG, and CRP in individuals with T2D is being investigated. The CV benefits are being interpreted
in terms of MACE risk.
5.1.2
Inclusion and Exclusion criteria
Preliminary to the literature search, eligibility criteria were formulated using the PECOS framework.
This framework serves as a tool for formulating eligibility criteria for a literature search. Addressing
the research question relative to the five categories: i) patient, ii) exposure, iii) comparison, iv)
outcome, and v) study design, helps direct and focus the literature search.
According to an update of the Guidance for Industry by the FDA, all studies published before 2008
should be excluded in this MA [48]. The updates include recommendations that new clinical studies
should, for example, include patients with a higher risk of CVD, such as elderly patient or patients
with renal impairment. Sponsors of phase II and III studies should also establish a committee to
adjudicate CV events such as MACE-3 or MACE-4.
The exclusion of studies by publication year was not defined in the database search strings but
was carried out later during the title and abstract screening. The PECOS framework and the five
categories are described in the following.
Patient Adult participants (≥18 years) with T2D.
Exposure Studies that considered the exposure of at least one of the drug classes GIP/GLP-1
RA, SGLT2 inhibitor and insulin are allowed. However, exposure combinations across
these drug classes was not allowed. Only placebo or standard of care (SOC) controlled
studies were allowed.
Comparison Studies that were controlled, which means that they included at least one
treatment arm and one control arm. Accepted control arms were either placebo or SOC.

Outcome Studies, that considered any of the CV risk factors LDL, TG, or CRP, or assess
the risk of CVD summarized as HRs for MACE-3 or MACE-4. Reporting of baseline
and endpoint or change from baseline with variation of the CV risk factors.
Study design Randomized controlled clinical phase III cohort studies.
The specific PECOS terms for the literature search are detailed in table 20 in Appendix B. A

The studies to be included in the final MA were randomised clinical phase III studies with adult

characteristics of participants across intervention and control groups. Only full-text articles were
included in the final MA. Studies with cohort treatment or control groups with other drug classes
or not involving placebos or SOC were also excluded.
5.1.3
Search strategy
The search of studies in two or more databases was of high importance for minimizing the risk
of missing eligible studies. The search queries for each database are in Appendix A. The PECOS
specific outcome wasn’t included as a search term in order to minimize the exclusion of potential
important studies for this MA. The same applies for patient age, since this information is rarely
defined in the title or abstract of studies.
After title and abstract screening, full text screening was performed against the eligibility criteria
to find studies reporting either one or more of the outcomes: MACE, LDL, TG and CRP. Clinical
trial stages were checked using the UMIN Clinical Trials Registry Chinese Clinical Trials Registry
(ChiCTR), the European Union Clinical Trials Register (EudraCT) and the ClinicalTrials.Gov
register and database.

5.2
Modelling
For the modelling part, the open-source software program R version 4.2.0 (2022-04-22) was used for
data file processing, exploratory data analysis, depictions, and modelling. The following describes
the different modelling approaches and applications from linear to nonlinear modelling. Methods are

explorative analysis, followed by a nonlinear dose-response relationship modelling based on covariate

5.2.1
Biomarker-driven meta-regression for MACE effect
The impact of biomarkers, including hbA1c, BW, LDL, and TG, on MACE is not directly studied
with the different treatments, as treatment effects on MACE could be correlated to treatment
effects on the biomarkers without a causal relationship. Nevertheless, it is hypothesised that some
of these biomarkers may drive effects on MACE and, therefore, investigated through meta-regression
analysis.
Linear univariate meta-regression models were created using the lm() function in R to investigate
each biomarker’s effect on MACE-3. For this analysis, the independent covariates were investigated
separately to the log-scaled HR. Each model was weighted with the inverse variance method for
random-effects meta-regression with weight w∗. The between-study variance, T2, was calculated
manually and validated with the R package meta (version 5.2.0).

6.1
Literature search
The systematic literature search encompassed PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Science conducted on
the 4th of January 2024.1
Initially, 5,006 articles were identified for inclusion, with an additional study suggested by the
supervisor Rune Viig Overgaard, making a total of 5.007. After employing the citation software
Mendeley and manual filtering for duplicates, 4,123 articles remained for further screening.
Following title and abstract screening, and application of inclusion and exclusion criteria, 660 arti-
cles were retained. Subsequent full-text screening reduced this number to 45 articles, all meeting
the eligibility criteria outlined in section 5.1.2. These 45 studies were included in the qualitative
synthesis, comprising open-label and double-blind phase III randomized clinical studies.
Among these, 15 studies were concerning CV safety and benefits in the form of MACE risk. Of these
studies eight were concerning the safety of GLP-1 RA drugs, six were concerned with the safety
of SGLT2 inhibitor drugs, and one was concerned with the safety of insulins. Lipid information,
such as baseline LDL and TG, and changes from baseline accompanied by variance information
were reported in eight of the CVOTs. Notably, none of the 15 CVOTs included information on CRP.
A final check of included studies based on inclusion criteria was performed on the 20th of January
2024. The various steps performed in the data cleaning and filtering process are depicted in figure 4
in the form of a PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses)
flow chart. An overview of the baseline characteristics for each study can be seen in Appendix
D table 22, 23, and 24. No supplementary information was obtained to replace the missing data
values, despite attempts to contact the corresponding authors of the studies.

7.1
Systematic literature search
Two literature searches were conducted for this project, the first during the initial phase and the sec-
ond on January 4th, 2024. The initial search revealed a greater number of eligible studies identified
through other MA compared to those found through systematic database search. In the subsequent
search, 45 studies met the eligibility criteria through database search alone, compared to 19 in the
first search. The differences between the two searches can be attributed to variations in search
queries. Notable modifications included the addition of ’t2dm’ in the study title as an alternative
to ’type 2 diabetes’, the inclusion of ’randomi*’ in either the title or abstract, not inclusively in
the title, and the application of a clinical trial filter. These adjustments in the second search led to
the identification of a greater number of studies in the initial phase, as depicted in the PRISMA
flow diagram (figure 4).
During the full-text screening process of the first search, studies were exclusively included if they
provided baseline and change from baseline or endpoint information related to LDL, TG, and
CRP. Consequently, CVOTs with HRs but lacking lipid or CRP information were excluded. While
the primary focus was on lipid-related information rather than CV outcomes, it was considered
necessary to include CVOTs without LDL, TG, and CRP information to explore CV benefits with
different treatments in general. As a result, ’MACE’ was added as an inclusion criterion.
Not all studies identified in the first search were retrieved in the second search. This discrepancy
may be attributed to the application of the clinical trial filter in the second search. It is worth
noting that this filter, while typically added by authors, was not present in a few studies identified
during the initial search.
As described in the section 5.1.4, only studies from the year 2008 to the present were intended for
inclusion. However, this criterion was not explicitly incorporated into the search string, leading
to the retrieval of 768 studies with publication years dating prior 2008. These studies represented
a significant portion of the total 4,123 unique studies obtained from the database search. It is
plausible that a few eligible studies containing lipid or CRP information were unintentionally ex-
cluded due to this criterion. Nevertheless, it is unlikely that a substantial number of CVOTs were
excluded due to this criterion, given the new guidance for CVD in diabetes studies provided in the
FDA guidelines in 2008. If studies predating this guideline were included, the number of insulin
studies might have been greater than the single insulin study ORIGIN identified. Notable, insulin
glargine and listpro received FDA approval in 2000, while insulin detemir was approved in 2005.
In comparison, semaglutide was approved in 2017. Generally, the approval timeline indicates that
insulin sanctioned much earlier than GLP-1 RAs and SGLT2 inhibitors. Therefore, without this
criterion, it would have been anticipated that the insulin class would have been more extensive.

7.2
Literature findings
Lipid and CRP change from baseline values differences between treatment and control arms were
examined across all studies using forest plots. Upon reviewing forest plots for LDL and TG (5,
6), it was evident that overall treatment significantly reduced TG levels from baseline, while a
similar insignificant pattern was observed for LDL. A reverse pattern was observed in a network
MA including GLP-1 RA treated T2D population study only [11]. If subgroup analyses, based on
drug class, were conducted for LDL and TG, similar patterns as in the network MA would have
been expected for GLP-1 RAs. It is important to note that while the results obtained in this MA
are somewhat comparable to the study, variations are expected due to the inclusion of different
studies. Additionally, this thesis included studies involving the GIP/GLP-1 RA tirzepatide in the
GLP-1 RA class, distinguishing it from previous analyses, including GLP-1 RAs only.
It is well-documented that levels of LDL remains constant or increases with exposure to SGLT2
inhibitors [5]. This trend was also evident in forest plot (figure 5), and in the exploratory biomarker
analysis (figure 11). Reduction in the levels of TG were observed across treatment effects. Though
significance was observed, the summary mean effect size of -5.15 mg/dL would have been expected
in a subgroup analysis based on drug class. This difference may be attributed to reported lipid
information from the ORIGIN study involving insulin carrying a substantial weight under the
random-effects model. For both LDL and TG, the mean difference and CI between treatment and
SOC overlaps with the vertical zero-response line. Given its significant weight, this study might
be influencing the pooled summary effect toward the null-effect line. By excluding this study from
the forest plot analyses, a higher significance for the differences in lipid levels between treatment
and control would be anticipated, favoring treatment. This was seen in the forest plot of HR for
MACE-3 (see forest plot 8), were the ORIGIN study was identified as the main source of hetero-
geneity, leading to its removal.
In comparison to the number of phase III studies reporting lipid levels over time, CRP was not
frequently reported. While elevated CRP is associated with an increased risk for CVD, few stud-
ies have reported values for this marker. Baseline and change from baseline levels of CRP were
only present in a total of three studies. Treatment showed superiority compared to placebo by
reducing the levels. During the full text screening process of eligible studies, it was observed that
CRP levels were reported more often in phase II studies. In the three phase III studies, STEP 2,

7.3
Biomarker driven MACE risk
In this thesis, significant reductions in the relative risk for MACE-3 were identified through re-
ductions in hbA1c and BW for GLP-1 RAs. This was in agreement with the observations from
previously conducted MAs, investigated the effect of several anti-diabetic drug classes combined
and alone [6, 7]. A great overlap between the MAs and this MA was seen in terms of included
studies, making the analyses comparable. Through univariate random-effects meta-analysis, one
of the studies found that SGLT2 inhibitors didn’t contribute to significant MACE risk reduction,
aligning with the similar results found in this thesis [6].
The relationship exploration between MACE and different covariates, were analysed based on
treatment. This separation into either GLP-1 RAs and SGLT2 inhibitors was performed, even
it similarities were observed in the pooled HRs in forest plot 9. The cause for this are based on
previously analyses discovering significant effect size differences between these two classes [6].
Distinct relationships were observed between the drug classes for hbA1c and BW. An increase in
placebo-adjusted percentage change from baseline of hbA1c and BW was significantly associated
with a reduction in HR for MACE-3 for GLP-1 RAs, while the opposite trend was observed for
SGLT2 inhibitors. However, CVOTs concerning lipid information was either lacking or incomplete,
making it challenging to draw conclusions regarding their relationship to MACE-3 through HR.
The observed effects of LDL changes for SGLT2 inhibitors showed variance homogeneity across
studies. If more CVOTs were to be added to the analysis, same pattern was to be expected, as
LDL for this drug class over time was shown constant (figure 5). In conclusion, the analysis of LDL
or TG could have been performed on a dataset consisting of all drug classes instead of separate,
increasing statistical power.
Another solution to the issue of missing data within the studies could have been mitigated by
imputing values derived from the existing dataset. In the case of LDL and TG, the observed het-
erogeneity was deemed statistically insignificant across all drug classes, suggesting that imputation
for these parameters could have been conducted using the complete lipid dataset. However, in the
case of CRP, the proportion of studies lacking this information was notably high in comparison to
the number of studies requiring an imputed observation. Nonetheless, imputation for lipid levels
could have been feasibly executed.

During the explorative analysis, two outlier CVOTs, one from each drug class, were identified

CVOTs might be identified as outliers as well due to the already small amount of included studies
and low statistical power. Furthermore, if new data were to be fitted to the same model, this might
also be classified as outliers. The implementation of alternative models beyond the log-linear model,
may offer improved capacity to capture some of the unexplained heterogeneity seen across studies.
Examination of data beyond linear regression are of interest in order to explain data, but maybe
the top priority of future analyses is to address the lack of data.
7.4
Dose-response
The hypothesis of dose being a mediator for MACE was investigated. For the sake of easier inter-
pretation and investigation, drugs within each drug class were scaled based on potency relative
to either semaglutide and dapagliflozin. The unique drug potency scaling was based on the great
amount of hbA1c and BW information across all 45 studies, including the CVOTs. Due to the
observed reversed effects between GLP-1 RAs and SGLT2 inhibitors on hbA1c and BW in relation
to MACE-3 risk, the dose-response analysis was held separated between drug classes, despite being
investigated together in a previous exposure-response analysis [28].
For GLP-1 RAs, the BW information for efpeglenatide was missing, while the Emax models for
SGLT2 inhibitors couldn’t converge with enavogliflozin added. Enavogliflozin, highly potent, was
administered at a dose of 0.3 mg in the study NCT04632862. The lack of data for studies with

DISCUSSION
During the systematic literature search, 252 records did not adhere to the eligibility criteria defined,
thus were excluded. If hbA1c and BW information from those records were included, an increase in
the statistical power for the Emax models and different model outcomes would have been observed.
Ultimately, this would have affected the relative doses of semaglutide and dapagliflozin versus
MACE-3 analysis results. While it is not expected that another conclusion from this analysis would
have been drawn, the results could have been more refined. Currently, the ED50 estimated for
semaglutide was 0.37 mg and 1.6 mg for the hbA1c and BW Emax models, respectively. These
doses fall within the current range of recommended dosing, starting at 0.25 mg with a maximum
dose of 2 mg QW.
After adjusting doses for the relative potency of semaglutide or dapagliflozin, the association between
the MACE-3 risk and dose was analyzed. For GLP-1 RAs, the four models performed similarly based
on AIC, while for the SGLT2 inhibitors, the simple linear and log-log linear models showed the best
performance. The study exploring exposure-response of multiple anti-diabetic drugs, demonstrated
best fit based on linear regressions of log-scaled maximum concentration (pmol/L) versus log(HR)
for MACE [28]. Here it was concluded that higher CV benefits of GLP-1 RAs may be observed
with higher exposure. The same was observed in this thesis, where GLP-1 RAs appeared to be
more sensitive to increasing dose in terms of reducing the HR.
For SGLT2 inhibitors, the Emax models showed poor performance with an estimate of zero for
ED50 interpreted as that at a dose of 0 mg 50% of maximum effect is reached. Beside this being
impossible to obtain through medications, these models also showed to be the worst models in
explaining HR through dose. It might be necessary to analyse potential contributes to the unex-
plained heterogeneity among this drug class, in order to identify other mediators than investigated
here.
7.4.1
SGLT2 inhibitor effect
Although there was a slight maximum reduction in hbA1c and BW based on meta-regression and
dose-response analysis (here indicated by Emax estimates), the SGLT2 inhibitor drug class also
demonstrated a slight improvement in MACE as seen in forest plot 9. One study investigated
whether this CV effect of SGLT2 inhibitors was a drug class effect or if there were differences
between them [86]. The conclusion was that differences exist within the drug class, as evidenced
by results showing empagliflozin treatment in EMPA-REG OUTCOME to be superior to, for
example, ertugliflozin and dapagliflozin. This conclusion was based on the inclusion of studies such
as EMPA-REG OUTCOME and DECLARE-TIMI 58. Additionally, they concluded that these CV
benefits seen through the risk of HR and CV death, whereas this thesis focused solely on MACE-3
risk.
The HR for EMPA-REG OUTCOME study was available for both 10 and 25 mg empagliflozin,
where as the pooled HR was found to be significant for superiority. However, when comparing the

7.5.1
Cox model
The first choice of time-scale in weeks violated the assumptions of proportional hazards, resulting
in the analysis of survival with time-scale age based on age of inclusion to age of the occurrence of
an event or censoring instead. The age-variation for subjects allowed the other covariates, included
in the Cox model, to vary with this time-scale, resulting in a more nuanced picture of how different
covariates impact CV risk at different stages of life, which has been observed in a previous study [91].
The findings of increased CVD risk associated with increasing systolic and diastolic blood pressure
aligns with existing literature [91]. An increase to above 140 mm Hg, termed hypertension, which,
as mentioned in section 3, is a risk factor for increased prevalence of CVD, especially seen in com-
bination with T2D [2].
The reduction of BW was found to be significant in explaining the log(HR) in the meta-regression
analysis. Likewise were BW, through BMI, found to be a significant predictor for CVD in the final
Cox model, where higher CVD risk was observed with increasing BMI for Caucasians. From the
final Cox model it was observed that a 10% BMI reduction resulted in the lowering of relative
CVD risk in terms of HR. However the HR reduction observed from the meta-regression analysis
with a 10% weight loss, where significantly greater. For REWIND the placebo-adjusted percentage
change from baseline of BW and BMI observed with 1.5 mg dulaglutide was -1.66% and -1.64%,

Logistic regression
Logistic regression models did not, as the Cox model, take time such as inclusion age to event
or censoring age, into account. Even with great differences in outcome and methodology the
significant predictors discovered in the final models had similarities. Both the Cox and the logistic
regression models found that the inclusion of interaction and quadratic terms significantly improved
performance. The decision was made though the first Cox and logistic regression model complied
with the model assumptions. However, through decile analysis, it was found that the final logistic
regression model, with interaction and quadratic terms, performed better in terms of predictability
of the probability of success, i.e. CV event.
Gender, BMI, and race were found as significant predictors in both models. In the logistic regression
model PR was found to explain the odds ratio, while blood pressure in general was found to explain
parts of the relative CVD risk in the Cox model. This supports that general cardiovascular function
is important in terms of CVD risk.
8
Conclusion
Through systematic literature search, 45 phase III studies were found eligible and included in this
MA. Of these, eight studied the CV safety in a diabetic population of GLP-1 RA, six studied
the CV safety of SGLT2 inhibitors, and one studied the CV safety of insulin were identified. The
comparison of insulin treatment effects within this drug class, and between drug classes were not
investigated due to the lack of insulin studies.
This MA added crucial novel information to current MAs by showing that treatment effects of the
three drug classes were highest in terms of TG reductions. GLP-1 RAs showed significant effects
by lowering CRP level and MACE-3 risk compared to placebo. SGLT2 inhibitors only showed
significance by lowering the MACE-3 risk. Random-effects meta-regression analysis revealed that
among the four selected biomarkers (hbA1c, BW, LDL, and TG), the change in hbA1c and BW
from baseline were the most significant explanatory factors for MACE-3 incidence.
From dose-response analysis comparing potency adjusted doses within the two drug classes GLP-1
RA and SGLT2 inhibitor, it was observed that GLP-1 RAs demonstrated the greatest maximum

Future research perspectives
The primary constraint in this thesis has been the limited availability of data. The quantity of
phase III CVOTs for T2D is insufficient, and those that are accessible lacks extensive lipid and CRP
information. By exclusively incorporating placebo-controlled studies in the MA, any noticeable
distinctions identified can be compared across various studies and drug classes. An extension of this
approach could involve conducting a network MA encompassing alternative forms of control. This
expansion is likely to augment the number of studies or treatment arms with insulin as the primary
response. Notable, the SURPASS-3, -4, and AP-Combo studies have investigated the GIP/GLP-1
RA tirzepatide in comparison to insulin [92]. The SUSTAIN-4 and -11 investigated semaglutide
versus placebo [93]. These are just a few examples of phase III studies without placebo or SOC as
control arms.
An alternative approach for future research to implement in order to augment the number of insulin
arms with lipid information would involve the inclusion of studies published prior to 2008. The
inclusion of additional studies could also facilitate an examination of the impact of tirzepatide,
within its specific drug class (GIP/GLP-1 RA). Currently, the solve tirzepatide study, SURPASS-
5, within this MA was categorized as a fully GLP-1 RA, assuming that its findings are directly
comparable on a 1:1 basis with other GLP-1 RAs.
The impact of dosage was only explored using Emax modelling. Another approach to identify the
relation of dose to CVD risk could involve conducting subgroup analyses within each drug class
based on dosage. Such analysis could provide more detailed information of the optimal dosing
regimen for each drug class to help guide clinical decision-making.
Considering the results from the supplementary survival analyses, it was evident that other biomark-
ers, which were not in the focus of the meta-regression and dose-response analyses, may hold greater
potential as mediators for CVD risk. Notable, hbA1c did not emerge as a significant predictor in
the survival analyses, in contrast to its significance in the meta-regression analysis. Future analysis
of placebo data through survival analysis could benefit from including change from baseline or
endpoint information as a more compelling measure for CVD risk analysis.
