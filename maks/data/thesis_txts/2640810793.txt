Abstract 
Risk identification is essential for risk assessment of construction projects. A widely used tool 
for risk identification is the risk register, which can be used as a database for organizational 
learning. However, sourcing information from such relatively unstructured text data is 
problematic. Natural language processing is a branch of artificial intelligence used for text 
analytics. However, using natural language processing with Danish textual data in risk 
identification marks a gap in the existing literature. Filling this literature gap and improving the 
completeness of risk identification in building projects at DTU Campus Service, a natural 
language processing tool was designed and presented. 
This thesis endeavoured into researching and developing the artificial intelligence design process 
for project risk. During the research, different artificial intelligence methods and their 
requirements were reviewed, natural language processing was selected, the most promising 
methods were determined, and various ways of employing those methods were explored. Finally, 
a user interface tool was developed and tested obtaining feedback from the users. Through the 
artificial intelligence designing process, DTU Campus Service's risk register was used as the data 
source to train different word embedding models, and different model architectures. Moreover, 
large open-access pre-trained word embedding models were also used, such as Wikipedia, 
Common Crawl and Danish Language and Literature Society. Models were compared in the 
word pair similarity experiments and correlated with human evaluation scores. Word similarity 
experiments results show that risk register data could be trained with a specific algorithm among 
the algorithm options, which also had the highest F measure results, which also had the highest 
F measure results. Among three large Danish word embedding models, DSL demonstrated the 
most consistent correlation with human results. After the prototyping, user feedback interviews 
showed that Power BI tool could be used as a database tool and does not require any 
programming skills. However, there is room for improvement regarding data quality and tool 
skills in the current prototype version. 
This thesis contributes to both literature and practice. It contributes to practice by illustrating a 
method for implementing the natural language processing algorithm in a database tool. This 
could improve the completeness of risk identification in construction projects by enhancing 
search quality of the risk register database with a similar word search function. The thesis's 
contribution to literature is using natural language processing in Danish for risk identification 
and comparing different model algorithms, and pre-trained large models. Finally, the output of 
the thesis also provides input and recommendations for future studies.  
Keywords: Machine Learning, Natural Language Processing, Risk management, Building 
Projects, Risk Register, Risk Identification.

Motivation 
This is my master's thesis, master of science in industrial engineering and management in 
specialization with production and project management at DTU. I have been encouraged and 
motivated by my supervisors for the research topic, and mostly I did this work by finding internal 
motivation because of my belief on a tangible improvement to a case company and contribution 
to scientific literature. 
In the research process, I was employed as a part-time junior consultant at CK&CO, which gave 
me much insight into consultancy. One semester before I worked as a teaching assistant for 
42433 Advanced Engineering Project, Program and Portfolio Management Course with 
Associate Professor Josef Oehmen at DTU, it motivated me to research the practical cases in 
project and risk management. Thanks to Associate Professor Josef Oehmen and Karim Cedric 
Meaouia, I have decided to research natural language processing and risk identification with 
CK&CO and DTU Campus service. 
Finding out current problems in risk identification and solving some of them was also one aspect 
of my motivation. Overall, the motivation is learning the existing risk identification literature 
and natural language processing applications in the area and contributing both to risk literature 
with my thesis and practice by providing useful research for CK & CO in later consultancy 
projects. 
The primary aim of this thesis is to provide a risk register database tool for DTU Campus Service 
employees and exploring the possibilities of natural language processing application in risk 
identification, which can be useful for similar consultancy projects of CK&CO. Besides, finding 
out the expectations of project managers and implementation options of the machine learning-
based tool in their decision-making process is going to be a managerial perspective of the thesis 
aim. 
In addition, being out of my comfort zone and doing exploratory research was quite self-
motivating. I mainly learned essential machine learning and natural language processing 
concepts and doing basic programming to use algorithms in risk management. 
Further consultations with project practitioners, subject matter experts and researchers revealed 
that natural language processing in Danish and risk identification is an under-researched topic. 
Thus, this thesis on this topic at CK&CO and DTU CAS became an enriching journey. I was 
motivated to make the prototype of a tool that can improve the risk identification of DTU Campus 
Service by including the database of registered risks and an easy way to search for information.

1 Introduction: Understanding the Context and Research 
Questions  
This chapter outlines the overall framework of the thesis. The thesis scope is introduced in 
Section 1.1, the research questions in Section 1.2, the outline of the thesis in Section 1.3 and an 
introduction to the companies and context in Section 1.4. 
1.1 Problem Formulation and Thesis Scope  
Risk identification is a key step within risk assessment. According to Project Management 
Institute (PMI, 2018, p 409), risk identification is “the process of identifying individual project 
risks as well as sources of overall project risk and documenting their characteristics”. In the risk 
identification process, overall risks sources are documented during the project for risk 
assessment. Therefore, a project team can appropriately respond to the risks by using the 
information throughout the project life cycle (PMI, 2018, pp. 409–419). 
Without risk identification, project cost and delay increase and the quality of work decreases (J. 
Wang & Yuan, 2017). Even though time is spent on risk identification, risks can be overlooked 
If a project manager lacks experience. According to Maytorena et al. (2007), there is a correlation 
between project managers' (PM’s) experience level and overlooking project risks. Therefore, 
documenting and reusing lessons learned can contribute to risk management, and risk register 
databases (RRDs) can be used for this purpose in organizations (Munier, 2014, pp. 61–90). In 
addition, using databases can increase the efficiency and quality of risk identification and 
potentially decrease project cost and delay (Md Sarwar, 2017; J. Wang & Yuan, 2017). 
Organizational experiences can be lost if learned lessons are not registered and reused (Abd El-
Karim et al., 2017; Sanchez-Cazorla et al., 2016). Moreover, overlooking risks can cause 
resource waste and loss of experience (Singh et al., 2020). RRDs are commonly used in risk 
identification (Siraj & Fayek, 2019). When PMs encounter a threat, they might use these 
databases to see if such a risk has been encountered before to get inspiration about mitigation 
actions (Da Silva et al., 2019). However, it is not always easy for PMs to find the data they need 
since risk registers can be unstructured text data (Da Silva et al., 2019). In addition, how people 
search and what they find can differ, since the information can be coded to text in different ways 
(R. Goodfellow et al., 2014; Leva et al., 2017).  
Since risks are registered according to different features such as the project phase and risk 
category, risks can be filtered with those features. This is why risk registers can be useful to find 
information. To check if a word is used in the software document, the exact word match (control 
+ F) function can be used. However, what people search for and how this information is coded 
to the text can differ. Searching for exact keywords can be inefficient and ineffective when 
searching for risks in risk register databases since different PMs register risks using different 
wording and phrasing (Loizides & Buchanan, 2008). 
==PAGE:13==
Master’s Thesis 
 
 
10 
 
When an exact keyword match is not found in the document, searching with similar keywords 
can help locate information. There are different ways to find similarities between words, such as 
searching for synonyms. However, one word can have many synonyms, and it can be impractical 
to search for all the synonyms of one word due to the time wasted on unmatched searches. 
Different words can be similar according to the context of the document. Finding the similarities 
among words according to context can be helpful in finding overlooked risks in risk databases. 
This is why finding the similarities among words according to the database is more important 
than finding and searching with semantically similar words to avoid unsuccessful searches 
(Sugawara et al., 2016; Loizides & Buchanan, 2008). Finding similar risks from the database can 
bolster the risk identification process because it enables learning from similar projects, 
potentially reducing overlooked risks. Searching risk databases mostly assists inexperienced 
PMs because they gain access to a database of past experiences (Maytorena et al., 2007). 
It is time consuming to find information in unstructured data sources (Eskildsen et al., 2020). 
However, machine learning (ML) algorithms in natural language processing (NLP) can be used 
to analyze unstructured text (Fan & Li, 2013). NLP can be used to improve the process of 
searching for risks in risk databases (Sugawara et al., 2016). Additionally, NLP can be used with 
different languages, including Danish (Mehdi et al., 2017). According to Branscombe (2017), 
developing an assistant tool for searching unstructured text sources such as risk databases can be 
helpful to practitioners (Branscombe, 2017). Different software tools can be used as interfaces 
between programming languages and practitioners (Wade & Wade, 2020, pp. 151–172).  
DTU Campus Service (CAS) has risk registers about the renovations, new building risks and 
mitigations from its 20 projects in 2018 and 2019 (Appendix-B-1). The risk and contract manager 
(RCM) of DTU CAS required a risk register database for improving the completeness of the 
organization’s risk identification process in building projects. 
In this research, risk identification practices in DTU CAS are explored, and risk identification 
problems are detected. Available risk registers from DTU CAS were used to develop a database 
tool, and NLP was used as an artificial intelligence (AI) method for searching function in the 
tool. 
CK&CO is a consultancy company which developed a risk register tool for DTU CAS in 2018. 
This thesis sought to collaborate with the company in developing a database tool from risk 
registers and used CK&CO’s experience in developing the tool. 
1.2 Research Questions 
Three research questions were defined to follow a structured approach to understand and detect 
issues and provide a solution. 
Research Question 1 (RQ1): "How can natural language processing improve the completeness of 
risk identification in building projects at DTU Campus Service?"

To answer this research question, I mapped the current risk identification process of DTU CAS 
and analyzed it based on interviews. Furthermore, I reviewed NLP applications to risk 
management in the literature. 
Research Question 2 (RQ2): "Which type of NLP algorithms is best suited to improve the 
completeness of risk identification in building projects at DTU Campus Service?" 
To answer this research question, I conducted experimental research and prototypical application 
of the principles. In this experimental research, different NLP models were trained and compared 
with each other and with available pre-trained models. Furthermore, subject matter experts 
assisted in comparing models. Details with this research question answer can be seen in 
Appendix-A-1.2. 
Research Question 3 (RQ3): "How can an NLP algorithm be implemented to the current risk 
identification process at DTU Campus Service?" 
The final research question is about utilizing the findings from previous questions and deciding 
on the most suitable intervention for the current risk identification system of DTU CAS. To 
answer this question, I investigated tools and interventions earlier implemented in the 
organization through interviews with practitioners. Furthermore, a workshop was conducted to 
obtain feedback about the prototype tool and its implementation.

2.2 Systems Engineering 
I used systems engineering (SE) as a general research method in the thesis because this 
methodology is considered a technology development methodology, and it allows solving 
complex problems with iterative development (Züst & Troxler, 2006). Walden et al. (2015) 
define SE as the top-down iterative development process for a real-world system operation 
(INCOSE, 2007, p. 11). 
In this methodology, there is a problem-solving cycle, which has three large and three small 
steps. It starts with searching for problems and goals with situation analysis and goal definition 
and then searching for solutions through concept analysis and synthesis. The final major step is 
a selection of concept and evaluation (Züst & Troxler, 2006, p.38). In my research, I began with 
understanding the problems of risk identification in DTU CAS. There were multiple problems 
such as overlooking risks due to the lack of a central database of risk data from previous projects, 
retrieving information from a low-quality dataset and time-consuming risk management. 
Following the problem definition, I searched the literature for solutions, and I found that NLP 
could be applied to searching risks from a database and has features that could solve some of the 
problems and improve DTU CAS’s risk identification practices. Among different algorithm 
options, I trained different NLP models and used pre-trained models. Finally, I evaluated the 
models according to their correlation with human similarity scoring, utilizing two groups of 
domain experts and non-experts to help identify whether the models were finding meaningful 
similarities. According to the user feedback, I suggested an implementation plan. Figure 4

study, large, pre-trained word embedding models’ word pair similarity scores are compared with 
human similarity scoring. Therefore, NLP models can be evaluated by the correlation with 
human scores. I found this methodology applicable for my research to choose the most

trained models as Finkelstein et al. (2002) and Schneidermann et al. (2020) to evaluate Danish 
word embedding model performance in a risk-specific word pair similarity experiment. Model 
training and pre-trained models are explained in Chapter 5. 
I designed a similar experiment to that of Schneidermann et al. (2020), which was the only word 
similarity experiment in Danish except for the translation of Word353 data from Danish to 
English (Finkelstein et al., 2002; Nielsen, 2016). In order to obtain word similarity data from 
participants, I used survey instruction as a similar version of Simlex999 (Hill et al., 2015) I asked 
participants to score word pairs similarity between 0 and 6. 0 means unrelated, and 6 means the 
most similar. I gave three examples of scoring to guide the participants (Schneidermann et al., 
2020; Appendix B-9). 
2.4.5 Word Pair Selection and Similar Word Concept 
I did not differentiate between similarity and relatedness concepts in word pair selection. 
Relatedness can be as important as a similarity when similar word search is aimed. This concept 
is explained in Section 3.1.20. I used NLP algorithms for measuring similarity between word 
pairs. 
In the models’ performance evaluation, I measured their capabilities in finding similarity 
between word pairs which are separately used in different risk descriptions with similar 
meanings. When people search for keywords in a text using an exact word match search 
(ctrl+f+keyword), they search for an exact match for what they searched (Wu et al., 2014). 
Choosing the words from the same sentence can be unnecessary because when users find one 
word from a sentence, that sentence is shown in the search list. Thus, it was important to choose 
and compare word pairs from different sentences; however, word pairs are used in a similar 
==PAGE:25==
Master’s Thesis 
 
 
22 
 
meaning in different sentences (Leva et al., 2017). 
Finding similar words according to the text content can be valuable due to finding more risks 
from the database than what people search with exact word match. Therefore, searching with 
similar words can be helpful (Sugawara et al., 2016). There are different ways to find word 
similarities. One word can have many different meanings. This is why finding the contextual 
similarity according to the database content is more important than finding the most similar 
words semantically. Because semantically similar words may not be in the text, searching with 
those words can cause unsuccessful searches. A simple example clarifies this concept: 
1. Dog eats meat. 
2. Cat drinks milk 
When these two sentences are considered in the large database, finding similarity between “dog” 
and “cat” can be meaningful. When people search with “dog” as a keyword, they can find the 
first sentence as an exact match but not the second. When they search for semantically similar 
words such as “mammal”, “animal” or “pit bull”, there is not a match in this example. 
Nevertheless, when the algorithm finds a similarity between “dog” and “cat” and automatically 
searches for both words, both sentences are shown as matches. Therefore, cat and dog words can 
be a pair for model evaluation. I chose the word pairs with this criterion. In section 6.4.1 I explain 
more details with the word pairs selection from the dataset. 
2.4.6 Choosing the Sample Group 
Similarity scores of the word pairs were calculated from each model via calculation of the cosine 
similarity distance between word pairs for evaluation (Sugawara et al., 2016). Furthermore, a 
sample of people was selected to rank the similarity scores of word pairs. I arranged two groups 
of people (i.e., a test and control group). There are different sampling methods in the business 
research area. I used convenience and purposive sampling methods to choose the participants for 
the word similarity experiment. Convenience sampling is useful for short-term or pilot projects, 
and when there is a shortage of time, the researcher chooses the sample group from the available 
network. Therefore, convenience sampling was useful in control group selection. Purposive 
sampling means that researchers use their judgement to choose a sample group related to the 
particular cases or grounded theory. This method is useful for very small sample groups in 
qualitative studies; therefore, it was useful for test group selection (Greener Sue, 2008, pp. 69–
70). 
The test group consisted of five employees at CK&CO and a risk manager from DTU CAS. Their 
scoring was vital because I assumed their scoring would be closer to that of the PMs due to their 
interest in and experience with risk management. On the other hand, the test group consisted of 
18 people deliberately chosen from non-risk experts. This means that they are not related to and 
have no experience with risk management. The two groups were also combined and evaluated 
as a combined group to compare the results of the test and control groups.

2.5.2 Correlation Methods 
Correlation coefficients help to measure the ordinal correlation between two variables. 
Conventionally, researchers, who conduct word similarity experiments, use the Spearman rank 
correlation coefficient because it captures the correlation between ordinal data and similarity is 
measured on continuous scale (Dror et al., 2018, pp. 1383–1392) The Spearman rank correlation 
coefficient can differentiated from other correlation coefficient such as Pearson, Kernel because 
it is non-parametric measure. For instance, there is no assumption about the distribution of the 
data inherent in the Spearman rank. Therefore, the Spearman rank correlation coefficient returns 
the same results as long as ordinal formation remains the same no matter how large or small the 
values in the data mapping are. Thus it is seen as a reliable metric for evaluating similarity task 
results (Dror et al., 2018; Schneidermann et al., 2020). Moreover, the Spearman rank correlation 
coefficient allows a direct comparison between model similarity scores and human similarity 
scores (Hill et al., 2015). According to Bakarov (2018), word similarity correlation between NLP 
models and human results is the most representative method for evaluation, which uses human 
judgements of word similarity scoring in the evaluation. 
NLP word embedding models are evaluated with word similarity experiments. After cosine 
similarity distances between word pairs are calculated with NLP models, people score the same 
word pairs. The similarity scores of NLP models are compared to human scoring results. 
Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient is widely used method in word similarity experiments 
for correlating similarity results between NLP models and human similarity scores 
(Schneidermann et al., 2020; Kliegr & Zamazal, 2018; Hill et al., 2015, pp. 665–695; Dror et al., 
2018, pp. 1383–1392). Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient is calculated with this formula 
(Marden et al., 1992): 
ρ = the Spearman rank correlation 
di = the difference between the ranks of corresponding variables 
n = the number of observations 
Equation 3: Spearman rank correlation coefficient 
 
Pearson’s correlation coefficient can also be used in model evaluation. However, there is some 
difference between the usage of these correlation methods. Pearson’s correlation coefficient 
evaluates the linear relationship between variable pairs, and it assumes data has a normal 
distribution. However, Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient evaluates the monotonic 
relationship between two variables. When the value of the variable increases and leads to the 
increase of another value while the relationship is non-linear, the Pearson correlation coefficient

3.1 Fundamental Concepts in the Current Literature 
In this section, I introduce fundamental concepts used in the research and the necessary

3.1.1 Risk Identification and Management 
Understanding risk management as a general concept is essential for understanding risk 
identification, which is a part of risk management. The risk is generically defined as "the effect 
of uncertainties" (ISO 31000, 2018, p.1). In the thesis, I focused on risk identification in 
construction projects. Standards are explained in PMI (2017) and ISO (2018). According to   PMI 
(2017, p. 395), project risk management consists of the following six steps:  
1. Plan risk management 
2. Identify risks 
3. Perform qualitative analysis 
4. Perform quantitative risk analysis 
5. Plan risk responses 
6. Control risks 
The first step is planning risk management activities. The second step is the risk identification 
process in which risks are detected and registered during the project. The third step is assessing 
the likelihood of risks with qualitative techniques such as prioritizing risks with risk matrices. 
This is followed by quantitative analysis with modelling methods. At the end of the analysis, 
project managers have basic information about risks and mitigation strategies and conduct plan 
risk responses. In the final step, stakeholders continue to track the risks, identify new risks and 
evaluate the risk process' effectiveness (PMI, 2017, p. 395). 
Risk identification is a key step in risk assessment. According to PMI (2018, p 409), risk 
identification is "the process of identifying individual project risks as well as sources of overall 
project risk and documenting their characteristics". The primary benefit of risk identification is 
documenting overall risks sources during the project for risk assessment. Therefore, the project 
team can use the information to appropriately respond to the risks throughout the project life 
cycle (PMI, 2018, pp. 409–419). Risk managers should determine potential risks during risk 
identification. In this process, potential threats and opportunities can be identified regarding their 
impacts, their phase in the project lifecycle and risk owner stakeholders (J. Oehmen et al., 2020; 
Josef Oehmen et al., 2014).  
==PAGE:34==
Master’s Thesis 
 
 
31 
 
Construction projects are mostly risk-oriented, and identifying risks can minimize cost and delay 
and increase stakeholders' profit and gain (M. S. Ali, 2017). According to Maytorena et al. 
(2007), the risk identification process can be challenging because finding information from risk 
registers can be time consuming. Finding information from risk databases for risk identification 
is correlated with project managers experience level. This concept is also described by Winch 
(2010, p. 360), who stated that risk identification is mostly conducted and depending on 
"experience of older hands." According to Da Silva et al. (2019), risk identification is an essential 
factor affecting project success criteria such as quality, budget, scope, and customer satisfaction. 
Forty-five percent of PMs experience difficulties with unidentified risks during their career. 
According to ISO 31000 (2018, p. 14), risk management processes begin with defining scope, 
context, and criteria. Risk assessment and treatment continue with communicating with 
stakeholders and getting a consultation, monitoring, and reporting on the process. This 
framework can be applied to different management and organization levels, including strategic, 
operational, and project levels. Figure 15 illustrates the risk management process.

Communication and Consultation 
Communication and consultation are the main parts of the risk management process. It is crucial 
to understand project risks by internal and external stakeholders to implement necessary 
mitigation actions in the project lifecycle. While communication is intended to increase 
understanding of the risks, the purpose of consultation is to receive feedback and support.

Scope, Context and Criteria 
This process aims to customize risk management. It starts with defining scope, objectives, and 
decisions in order to make an appropriate risk assessment. Establishing context means 
understanding the environment inside and outside the organization. Inside the organization 
means the policies, culture, vision, and mission of the organization. Outside the organization, or 
the external environment includes customers, external stakeholders and suppliers. Criteria can 
be set with relevant parameters for evaluating risks, decisions and their severity. It can also 
include potential consequences of the risks. 
Risk Assessment 
Risk assessment is the central part of the framework, including risk identification, analysis and 
evaluation. It is a systematic and iterative process. Risk identification means identifying various 
sources of risk and their impacts on objectives. The purpose of risk analysis is to detect the 
severity and level of risks such as risk probability and impacts. Risk evaluation involves 
evaluating the response options and capabilities of the organization according to project

3.1.6 Problems with Finding Information from Risk Registers 
As mentioned in the previous section, risk registers and central databases are useful in risk 
identification. Furthermore, as mentioned in the problem formulation, it is not always easy for 
PMs to find the data they need since risk registers can be unstructured text data (Da Silva et al., 
2019). When people search for keywords in a text using exact word match search (ctrl+f), they 
search for an exact match for their search (Wu et al., 2014). In addition, how and what people 
search for can differ, and the information can be coded to text in different ways (R. Goodfellow 
et al., 2014; Leva et al., 2017). Searching for exact keywords can be inefficient and ineffective 
when searching for risks in the risk registers because different PMs register risks using different 
wording and phrasing (Loizides & Buchanan, 2008).  
When an exact match of a keyword is not found in the risk database, searching with similar 
keywords can help to find information. There are different ways to find similarities between 
words, such as looking for synonyms. However, one word can have many synonyms, and it can 
be impractical to search for all the synonyms of one word due to the time wasted on unmatched 
searches. Different words can have similarities according to the context of the risk description. 
Finding the similarities of the words according to context can be helpful in locating overlooked 
risks in risk databases. This is why finding the similarity between words according to the 
database is more important than finding and searching with semantically similar words to avoid 
unsuccessful searches (Sugawara et al., 2016; Loizides & Buchanan, 2008). Finding similar risks 
from the database can help the risk identification process because it enables learning from similar 
projects, potentially reducing overlooked risks. Searching risk databases mostly assists 
inexperienced PMs because they gain access to a database of past experiences (Maytorena et al., 
2007). 
It is time consuming to find information in unstructured data sources (Eskildsen et al., 2020). 
However, ML algorithms in NLP can be used to analyze unstructured text (Fan & Li, 2013). 
NLP can be used to improve the process of searching for risks in the risk databases (Sugawara 
et al., 2016). Additionally, NLP can be used with different languages including Danish (Mehdi 
et al., 2017). According to Branscombe (2017), developing an assistant tool for searching 
unstructured text sources, such as risk databases, can be helpful to practitioners (Branscombe, 
2017). Different software tools can be used as interfaces between programming languages and 
practitioners (Wade & Wade, 2020, pp. 151–172). I introduced the basic concepts of AI to 
explain the relation between ML, NLP and risk identification.

3.1.9 Issues with Using Machine Learning in Risk Management 
According to Aziz and Dowling (2019), various ML techniques can be used in the risk 
management area; however, there are also some practical limitations such as appropriate data 
management, confidentiality and lack of competent employees in the firms. Classification 
algorithms such as SVMs, decision trees and unsupervised word embedding algorithms are 
examples of these techniques. Especially in the financial risk management area, ML techniques 
are used to predict credit risk and market and operational risk. The most crucial issue with ML 
algorithms is finding suitable datasets since data is mostly held separately among departments 
and kept in different databases with different regulatory restrictions about sharing data. 
Sometimes important data may not be recorded but rather kept as a company secret to avoid 
losing competitive advantage.  
Another critical issue is finding available skilled staff to implement AI techniques. A survey was 
conducted with 1,000 companies in the USA and revealed that firms have a common concern in 
implementing AI solutions about their employees’ readiness and competence to understand and 
apply AI solutions. They also agreed that training employees for AI solutions could require 
resources and time (Wilson et al., 2017, pp. 14–16). 
According to Aziz and Dowling (2019), transparency is also a significant issue for implementing 
AI solutions. Although ML techniques are increasingly popular, algorithms are mostly kept 
black-boxed, which prevents effective risk oversight and decreases the implementation of AI 
solutions among firms. However, AI solutions will continue to decrease the time consumption 
and cost of project risk management processes due to AI’s increasing usage.

3.1.13 Word Embeddings 
Word embedding is a type of distributive representation model which embeds words into a 
dimensional vector space. Therefore, each word is represented by a numerical vector with 
multiple dimensions in the vector space, and their distance from each other represents their 
semantic relations (Goldberg, 2016, pp. 345–420). Textual data sources can be converted into a 
vector space in which each vector represents a unique word from the data. Features of these 
vectors include the coordinate points in the vector space, which are encoded as a statistical 
association of words in the data context. These vector spaces are word embedding models and 
models differentiated according to their encoding differences such as training parameters and 
algorithm architectures (Lenci, 2008). 
Distance between two-word vectors in the vector space is called cosine similarity. Cosine 
similarity can be measured between -1 and 1. Each word has 1 cosine similarity value with itself. 
This means that similar words have higher cosine similarity to each other. According to the 
correlation between the cosine similarity of word pairs and the human perception of word 
similarity, models can be compared. Different studies have been conducted to evaluate word 
embedding models in comparison with human perception of language (Daniel & Martin, 2018, 
Chapter 8).

from the list. This is why finding similar words in data is more critical than finding semantically 
similar words from the dictionary. 
3.1.21 Preprocessing Practices 
Preprocessing is conducting for preparing data for word embedding. (Goyal et al., 2018; Kurdi,

Lowercase conversion: converting all letters from capitals to lowercase. 
Punctuation removal: removing all punctuation from the text. 
Stopword removal: removing all stopwords such as “I”, “my”, “a”, “an” and “the”. 
Number removal: removing all numeric characters. 
Space removal: removing all extra spaces between words.

NLP word embedding algorithms can process the texts and find similarities between words 
according to their usage in the context as mentioned in Sections 3.1.12 and 3.1.13. Thanks to

tasks in databases. Searching risks with similar keywords from the database can help the risk 
identification process because PMs can find similar risks with their keywords which they cannot 
find with exact word match (Section 3.1.20). Figure 38 visualizes searching risks comparison

6.4.1 Preparing the Word-Pair Dataset 
As mentioned in Section 2.4.5, I selected a list of word pairs from the risk description section of 
the DTU CAS risk register. Section 5.1 introduces that, as risks are registered in the database, 
they can be described in different ways such as with a synonym or related word, and therefore 
they can be difficult to find with exact word match search. Since NLP models can detect 
similarity between words in the text context, they can subsequently help to find similar words 
and search risks. The word pairs are chosen from different risk descriptions from the same risk 
category. I choose the word pairs from the same risk category because the category already 
suggests a possible similarity; for example, the category of “time management” is likely to have 
risks which are related. The reason for choosing word pairs from different risk descriptions is 
that when project managers search with a keyword, they can find the exact word match. 
However, when the risks are registered to the database with a synonym or a related word, they 
can be more difficult to find. That is why the word-pair list tests the models’ capabilities of 
detecting similarity from the text context. To summarize, I chose the word pairs from the risk

7.3.1 User Feedback and SWOT Analysis 
I conducted four semi-structured interviews with DTU CAS stakeholders RCM, HOS, PM1, and

stakeholders shared. Interview quotes from the SWOT Analysis are located in Appendix-B-14. 
In the following section, I summarise the opinions of the stakeholders. 
Strengths 
According to user feedback, the RRD tool can improve the completeness of the risk identification 
process at DTU CAS without radically changing current practices. The RRD tool can be used as 
a DTU CAS risk database. One of the tool's most critical strengths is in how it shows a user risks 
that can be overlooked with an exact-word-match search. Project managers can use the database 
themselves instead of meeting with their managers for risk identification, which can decrease the 
time that project managers used to meet with experienced managers for risk identification. 
Moreover, organisational learning can be documented in a single database to benefit future 
projects through the RRD Tool. New risk data can be added after each quarterly meeting, and 
the database can be enlarged. Stakeholders also stated that it is good to see their colleagues’ 
actions before responding to a given risk, providing an idea and increasing internal 
communication between project managers.  
Stakeholders perceive the tool as “the google search” of their own documents. Additionally, the 
similar-word-search idea was found useful because finding data from a database can be 
challenging, and the RRD tool provides such a service through categorization filters and the 
similar-word search, which enlarge searching outputs.  
Weaknesses 
Stakeholders stated that risk register data in the tool is limited. Data quality should be improved 
in future registered risks. For example, risk and mitigation descriptions should be elaborated so 
that data quality can increase, and the tool can be more useful in practice. In particular, 
stakeholders emphasised that mitigation descriptions, such as the result of mitigation actions, 
should be enlarged with the evaluation. 
Moreover, The RRD Tool is only a document search engine. It should be used in projects, and 
its practical contribution should be assessed after pilot project trials. The tool’s search bar layout 
can be improved. One stakeholder stated that there are also irrelevant risk matches with keyword 
inputs that should also be improved. Furthermore, the tool showcases the most common and 
costly risks from the database for project managers.

Opportunities 
The RRD tool can add value to DTU CAS, especially considering its contribution the 
identification practices of inexperienced project managers. It can also improve the quality of 
quarterly meetings because project managers can use the database for risk identification without 
help from other manager, meaning that it can decrease time waste in quarterly meetings. The 
database also allows PMs to contact the risk owners of previous projects directly and learn risk 
experience. 
It is helpful to realise the similarity between risks and to expand the user's view of the search. 
Eventually, more risk data will be registered to the tool. There can, then, be new connections 
between risks, and the tool can continuously be improved. It can motivate PMs to register risks 
to the database in greater detail in order to improve the data quality in time. Moreover, it can be 
widely used among project managers at DTU CAS, and people can view it as the ‘Google’ of 
their database.  
Threats 
Since the DTU CAS Project managers did not previously use a database, they could take issue 
with taking on a new practice. The tool can additionally be perceived as not user-friendly and as 
an extra workload. It should therefore be well-advertised as a benefit to the organization. 
Otherwise, PMs might see only the time-consuming nature of registering new risks rather than 
see it as an investment in organisational learning. They may not perceive the long-term benefits 
of enlarging the dataset. 
Project managers should still be responsible for risk identification in their own projects, and the 
tool may decrease that feeling of responsibility. Some PMs might perceive it as the full extent of 
the potential risk, though new risks may well arise during the project. Using only the database 
can decrease creativity and lead to tunnel vision. A PM must first think themselves rather than 
read of all potential past risks.

to the database. Particularly after each quarterly meeting at DTU CAS, new risks can be 
registered in the tool and the NLP model can be retrained by simply refreshing the tool page after 
each new risk register. This means that data quality and model performance can be improved

When I compared large, pre-trained Danish model correlation results from the control group with 
the Danish Similarity Dataset (Schneidermann et al., 2020) and the Word Similarity 353-Danish 
(Nielsen, 2016), the results were within +/- approximately 47%. However, when comparing with 
risk experts results, the correlation was a magnitude of 10 different. Thus, risk experts rate

Like Schneidermann et al. (2020), I created my own word pairs according to the DTU CAS 
dataset. However, my research differs from Schneidermann et al. (2020)’s because I trained 
different NLP models – specifically the DTU CAS dataset – while Schneidermann et al. (2020)

both specifically trained models and large, pre-trained models. I hypothesised that the models I 
trained explicitly with risk register data would perform better than the large models that used 
different datasets in word similarity tasks and corrected with word-pair search experiments.  
Another differentiation to be considered is the similarity concept. The dataset used in 
Schneidermann et al. (2020) provided a method for measuring similarity rather than 
relatedness/association. However, I did not separate similarity and relatedness in this research 
because I used NLP to locate similar risks using keywords from the DTU CAS risk register 
database, and relatedness between words was useful for the task as well as for the similarity.  
 
8.1.4 Risk register Tool Usage 
Semi-structured interviews with DTU CAS Stakeholders showed that the DTU CAS risk register 
practices support the literature, as in collecting risk data with risk registers prior to project 
practitioners' quarterly meetings. After handling a risk, project practitioners should abandon the 
‘post syndrome’ and address the learned lesson via the risk register throughout the project’s life 
cycle. Therefore, knowledge will remain even after the project team members leave, as Grant 
(2009) stated. Moreover, the RRD tool should be utilised during the entire project life cycle, and 
tools reliability should be confirmed and updated, as Dikmen et al. (2008) stated. 
As the literature review (Dikmen et al., 2008; Dunović et al., 2013; Leva et al., 2017; Tah & 
Carr, 2001) points out, DTU CAS plans to use RRD Tool as a repository of organisational risk 
knowledge. Therefore, the tool is going to increase communication between project managers 
through the sharing of risk experience. Moreover, as Dunovic' et al. (2013) stated, DTU CAS is 
going to use the RRD Tool as a second-level risk register (central risk register), including 
previous risks from all previous projects. Due to this central risk register, project practitioners' 
capability to identify and respond risks improves, which in turn increases the completeness of 
risk identification. Although every project faces different risks, the central risk register or risk 
register database tool can be useful in risk management steps, and each project can contribute to

8.2 Contribution to the literature 
This study fills the literature gap between risk identification and natural language processing 
with a danish risk data set from the construction industry. This study is also a contribution to 
danish word-similarity literature from a risk management perspective.   
My overall contribution lies in using Danish NLP in a real-world application. Some of the first 
work on Danish NLP word similarity was conducted by Finn Årup Nielsen (2016) who translated 
the work of Finkelstein et al. (2002) using Wordsim353. Another dataset, called the Danish 
similarity dataset (Schneidermann et al., 2020), was developed. This thesis also contributes to 
Danish NLP by working with a unique dataset. Furthermore, my dataset is unique because it uses 
domain-specific knowledge of risk management. Therefore, it contributes to Danish NLP that is 
specifically focused on the risk identification perspective with specially trained NLP models 
using the risk register corpus from a case study. Details about this section can be seen in 
Appendix-A-8.2. 
8.3 Contribution to practice 
This study shows that the completeness of risk identification at DTU CAS can be improved with 
a risk register database tool. This means that a small change to the DTU CAS project 
practitioners’ current practices can improve the completeness of risk identification. Especially in 
similar word search, project practitioners can see overlooked risks from the database through an 
NLP tool. Moreover, project managers are able and willing to benchmark risk response from 
earlier projects. Risk registers can be used on two levels and the RRD tool as a central risk 
database for project practitioners. After handling a risk, project practitioners should abandon the 
‘post syndrome’ and use the risk register to address the learned lesson throughout the project life 
cycle. Therefore, knowledge will not be lost after leaving the project team members or partners. 
Moreover, the RRD tool should be utilised during the project’s entire life cycle, and tools 
reliability should be confirmed and updated.  
This thesis contributes empirical knowledge of stakeholder expectations to artificial intelligence 
capabilities in risk management. The interviews showed practical examples of project managers' 
expectations of the risk database tools and the gap between what can be done and what is 
expected from artificial intelligence. For example, HOS and PM2 expected an RRD tool to show 
the mitigation action evaluation and risk evaluation according to cost and frequency. However, 
I used the word-embedding algorithms to create a vector model for a similar word search feature 
in the tool. To satisfy the demands of project practitioners, then, different NLP algorithms should 
be researched for future work. 
Power BI is a functional interface for project practitioners. It integrates Python scripts and the 
power query table of the tool, allowing project practitioners to use NLP tools without having

8.4 Reflections on the Thesis Process 
This thesis was a rewarding process. Both CK&CO and DTU CAS showed an interest and 
appreciation during the process and following its results. Final interviews and company meetings

Overall, it was a rewarding process. In the last five months, I have learned basic ML and NLP

8.5 Conclusion 
The purpose of this study was to provide a solution for improving the completeness of current 
risk identification practices at DTU CAS. I used NLP algorithms to improve their current 
practices with a risk register database tool. Although there is a small change to the current 
practices, it could be a big step for improving the completeness of risk identification in building 
projects at DTU CAS. I answered three research questions during the research.  
RQ1: " How can natural language processing improve the completeness of risk identification in 
building projects at DTU Campus Service?" 
To answer this question, I mapped current risk identification practices at DTU CAS and 
conducted a literature review about risk identification and NLP studies. NLP can be useful in the 
word similarity tasks, and word-embedding models can aid document retrieval for project 
practitioners. This decreases the necessity of long meetings and leads to use the effective use of 
previous experiences due to one of the database’s search tools, as explained in Section 4.1 and 
4.4. DTU CAS does not use a central database for storing previous risk experiences organization; 
instead, HOS and RCM aid PMs in risk identification and risk response practices with monthly 
and quarterly meetings. Project managers preparing risk registers for each project and risk 
registers are likewise updated quarterly. These risk registers are combined in a database. Since 
risk information can be coded differently by different PMs, there are often challenges in locating 
data from these databases (Section 3.1.6). Therefore, a database search tool was developed using 
NLP algorithms, as explained in Section 7. NLP word-embedding algorithms can find the 
similarity between words in textual data; by using this feature, a similar word search is 
implemented in the risk register database tool (Section 3.1.10). Therefore, project practitioners 
can search the risks with keywords and the RRD will show similar risks according to them, thus 
decreasing the number of overlooked risks from the database and allowing project practitioners 
to use the risk experience gained from previous projects. This can improve the completeness of 
risk identification in building projects at DTU CAS and save costs and time for future projects. 
RQ2: "Which type of NLP algorithms is best suited to improve the completeness of risk 
identification in building projects at DTU Campus Service?" 
Details about this section can be seen in Appendix-A-8.5. 
RQ3: "How can an NLP algorithm be implemented in the current risk identification process at 
DTU Campus Service?" 
To address this question, I built upon the responses to previous questions, using them to help 
determine and implement the most suitable intervention to DTU CAS’ current risk identification 
practices. I used semi-structured interviews to understand their current practices, prototype 
feedback, and stakeholders’ implementation ideas (Section 4,7.3 and 7.4, respectively). I 
concluded that without a dramatic change in current practices, the NLP algorithm could be 
implemented using a tool with an interface between an NLP algorithm and a data source. The 
literature review and CK&CO’s experiences with tool development showed that Power BI app

8.6 Limitations 
There are limitations to the thesis research. First, the research was completed amid COVID-19 
restrictions, which affected the number of meetings with stakeholders and which required that I 
hold all meetings and interviews online rather than in-person. Moreover, since the risk registers 
at DTU CAS are documents meant only used by project managers to prepare for quarterly 
meetings, data quality was limited and unclear for other project practitioners. Furthermore, I was 
unable to communicate directly with DTU CAS employees beyond the workshop interviews. 
Most of the feedback was taken second-hand from CK&CO’s co-owner. This limited the number 
of iterations through the tool development process. 
Language limitation should also be considered in the research, although I have been learning 
Danish for two years. Since I am not a native Danish speaker, my understanding of data was 
limited and depended on translation quality. Additionally, it could have limited my word-pair 
choices for word similarity and word-pair search experiments. The 40-word pairs are a small 
sample size from the data. According to the dataset, the number of word pairs would have ideally 
been larger, chosen by a native Danish speaker, and given a more extensive presentation. Data 
size also limited this research. Since DTU CAS risk register data is relatively small, semantically 
unrelated word matches appeared with models that created noise. 
The experiment’s participants sample size could also be large. Twenty-three people is a small 
number for a survey, which limits the validity of the research. I attempted to include all project 
and section managers at DTU CAS in the research. However, outside circumstances prevented 
this. 
I could only compare seven NLP word-embedding models – essentially the two main 
vectorization algorithms variations. It is also possible to train models with Glove (Sarmady & 
Rahmani, 2017). However, I was unable to use it because of errors on my computer and the time 
limitations. Lacking a computer science education background also increased the time required 
to learn basic coding practices and concepts.

8.7 Future Work 
According to Dikmen et al. (2008, p. 50), the repository risk tools should be tested in 
organisations with different projects and its reliability for risk management should be confirmed. 
Therefore, after the roll-out period, the RRD tool’s validity and reliability should be tested by 
DTU CAS project practitioners. In future studies, NLP algorithm performances can be tested 
using similar experiments with larger risk datasets and sample groups. More interviews can be 
conducted with stakeholders who utilised the RRD tool, and tool development can continue. 
Different programming languages and business intelligence platforms can be tested for the same 
purposes, such as R, C++ and Microsoft Azure. Classification algorithms can be implemented to 
the RRD tool to develop new abilities such as predictions of risk phases and categories of new 
risk inputs. The classification algorithms should then be tested to determine the highest algorithm 
performance using DTU CAS risk register data. The RRD tool can also be more proactive with 
the addition of a new feature, such as one that allows users to change the matched words' 
similarity score. This would increase a similar risk search quality that is initially tested by 
CK&CO.
