Learning objectives 
The master thesis should let students plan a project and complete it while achieving certain 
learnings. The authors of this report have set up the learning objectives below. 
A student who has met the objectives of the project will be able to: 
1. Describe the main social sustainability factors that manufacturing companies take into 
account when making design decisions for product development.  
2. Identify direct and indirect social impacts involved in product development. 
3. Understand the main drivers and barriers to the adoption of social sustainability assessment 
in product development. 
4. Compare existing social sustainability assessment methods, tools and frameworks. 
5. Evaluate the current regulatory environment and global initiatives around social sustainability 
and its implications for product development. 
6. Analyse the applicability of social sustainability indicators for product development. 
7. Analyse limitations and uncertainties of social sustainability assessment tools. 
8. Develop qualitative and quantitative methods for integration of social sustainability 
considerations in product development. 
9. Design case-specific decision support tool(s) to guide social sustainability integration in 
product development. 
10. Understand how the insights generated by a social sustainability assessment tool can inform 
strategic decision-making.

ABSTRACT 
This thesis aims to address the challenge of integrating social sustainability into product 
development within manufacturing companies. To identify needs, gaps and unveil current 
practice, a literature review has been conducted, complemented by an empirical study in a 
Danish enterprise. The literature review investigated tools and frameworks associated with 
social sustainability assessments, while the empirical study focused on the company’s social 
sustainability awareness and potential for integration into product development. As a response 
to the preliminary findings, a practical screening tool - the Social Indicator Selection Tool (SIST) - 
has been developed. Following the development of SIST, the tool was then evaluated by 
professionals across various manufacturing companies in Denmark and the Netherlands. 
Evaluations confirmed SIST as an effective and easy-to-use tool, capable of facilitating both 
social performance and risk assessments. Room for improvement is left in the development of a 
structured methodology to social product requirement creation, a clear link between social 
indicators and legislation and finally, in the development of sector-specific indicators. 
Keywords: Social Sustainability, Product Development, Manufacturing Companies, Social Risk, 
Social Life Cycle Assessment, Social Performance, Social Indicators, Database, Screening Tool

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Sustainability means meeting the needs of the present without imposing greater burdens on 
future generations (WCED, 1987). It can be conceptualized as three interconnected pillars: 
environmental, social, and economic. The environmental pillar focuses on the conservation of 
natural resources and ecosystems. The social pillar is about people and communities, equity, 
justice, and the quality of life. Last, the economic pillar seeks to build a strong and sustainable 
economy that supports the environment and society. Real sustainability can only be achieved by 
balancing all three of these pillars and knowing that they are interdependent as well as mutually 
reinforcing (Elkington, 2004).  
While companies have increasingly integrated the environmental and economic dimensions into 
their business activities, the social pillar of sustainability often lags behind. Within this 
framework, social sustainability is not simply a challenge to be managed, but also an opportunity 
that should be embraced. Businesses and product developers that proactively integrate social 
considerations into their core strategies can reap a multitude of benefits, like new avenues for 
innovation, brand reputation and a competitive edge (Mesquita & Missimer, 2021; Rodrigues et 
al., 2016; Yang et al., 2017). Through the prioritization of policies like fair labour practices and 
ethical product development, companies can build stronger relationships with stakeholders, 
fostering a more inclusive and equitable society while also leading positive change through their 
technologies (Haseeb et al., 2019). 
In recent years, social sustainability and more generally the social impact of corporations has 
been an issue that has come to light through regulations. In Europe, through the EU Green Deal, 
the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) came into being. The CSRD is a piece of 
legislation aimed at improving the quality and consistency of sustainable development reporting 
for companies operating in the EU. CSRD introduces great changes to reporting practices of 
corporate environmental and social impacts, since it applies to all large EU companies and 
demands more detailed and deliberate disclosures, through the adoption of specific reporting 
standards  (EY, 2024).  
Therefore, either for the sake of competitive advantage or due to reporting obligations, a clear 
need for the integration of social sustainability in the corporate world has arisen.

1.2 Problem Statement  
While social impacts have been widely addressed by the technical world, there is a lack of 
harmonized considerations of social impacts in product development. 
Despite a growing recognition of manufacturing companies' broad societal impacts and the 
acknowledged influence of their technological innovations, there remains a significant and 
multifaceted challenge in effectively integrating social sustainability considerations into the 
early stages of product development (Sandin et al., 2011). While environmental and economic 
sustainability assessments benefit from more established methodologies, the evaluation of 
social impacts is hindered by several key factors (Richter et al., 2023). These include the inherent 
subjectivity in defining and measuring social aspects, a lack of standardized data collection 
methods and consistent weighting approaches (Ahmad et al., 2018; Matthews et al., 2019) and 
the difficulty in achieving a unified measurement framework that encompasses the ecological, 
economic, and social dimensions of sustainability (Ahmad et al., 2018; Assefa & Frostell, 2007; 
Richter et al., 2023).  
Furthermore, the selection, normalization, weighting, and aggregation of social indicators are 
fraught with uncertainty and a lack of consensus, particularly concerning the balance between 
universal and context-specific metrics (Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021; van Dulmen et al., 2025; van 
Haaster et al., 2017).  Existing social assessment frameworks often struggle to provide 
actionable guidance and practical tools for anticipating and mitigating potential negative social 
consequences of emerging technologies during their formative phases (Hallstedt, 2017; Schöggl 
et al., 2017; van der Schoor & Göhlich, 2023). This is intensified by the epistemological 
challenges of incorporating qualitative social science approaches alongside traditional 
quantitative lifecycle assessment methods, necessitating a multi-paradigm approach that can 
handle the inherent uncertainties and normative dimensions of sustainability (Matthews et al., 
2019). 
Moreover, the need for meaningful stakeholder engagement throughout the assessment process 
is regarded as a necessity in order to ensure that diverse perspectives and values are considered 
(Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021; Hempel et al., 2013; Matthews et al., 2019; Mesquita & Missimer, 
2021). However, current practices often lack systematic approaches for such engagement, 
potentially leading to incomplete or biased assessments (Mesquita & Missimer, 2021).The 
challenge is only amplified by the need to visualize and communicate the complex interplay of 
social impacts to inform decision-making effectively (Matthews et al., 2019; Sarancic et al., 2022; 
Schulte & Hallstedt, 2018). 
In summary, the core problem is the absence of robust, pragmatic, and adaptable frameworks 
and tools that can be seamlessly integrated into the early product development process within 
manufacturing companies to proactively identify, assess, and address the full spectrum of social 
sustainability implications across the product lifecycle, while acknowledging inherent 
uncertainties, incorporating diverse stakeholder perspectives, and facilitating informed and 
accountable decision-making. This deficiency hinders the ability of companies to ensure their 
technological innovations contribute positively to social well-being and sustainable 
development in a holistic and systemic manner (Kühnen & Hahn, 2018).

2. Research methodology 
The structure of the thesis will follow the Design Research Methodology (DRM), as presented by 
Blessing and Chakrabarti, 2009. The DRM presents four separate phases: Research Clarification 
(RC), Descriptive Study I (DS I), Prescriptive Study (PS) and Descriptive Study II (DS II), making the 
design research process more rigorous.  
The breakdown of each stage can be seen below: 
• 
Research Clarification (RC): The first stage focuses on the definition of the research 
problem, as well as the clarification of the current understanding of the topic. This is 
achieved mainly through initial exploratory literature reviews. Its main outcome is the 
creation of an overall research plan covering main research criteria, questions and 
hypotheses. The results of the RC phase were presented in the Introduction chapter of 
this thesis. 
• 
Descriptive Study I (DS I): The goal of this stage is to understand the current state of 
knowledge related to the research problem. To do so, it promotes the collection and 
analysis of data from a variety of sources, like literature reviews, case studies or expert 
interviews. For this project, a comprehensive literature review shall be conducted, in 
order to identify social sustainability methods, frameworks, indicators and metrics 
relevant to product development. Simultaneously, empirical studies in the form of 
interviews and observations with the partner company shall be performed to identify 
current practices, processes and relevant actors. 
• 
Prescriptive Study (PS): This stage addresses the development of potential solutions to 
the research problem. It can involve the creation of design concepts, prototypes and 
experiments or simulations to test effectiveness. For this case, PS will encompass the 
process of conceptualizing and developing a screening tool for the integration of social 
sustainability into product development. 
• 
Descriptive Study II (DS II): This final stage entails the evaluation of the effectiveness of 
the proposed solutions in a real-world setting. It encompasses the collection of data 
regarding the performance of the solutions, the analysis of the results and finally the 
deduction of certain conclusions regarding the solution’s impact. For this project’s 
purposes and at this stage, the tool's practical application shall be tested in a corporate 
environment, with the participation of relevant stakeholders. Afterwards, the usefulness 
and utility of the tool shall be evaluated via a survey to the user, with the intent to identify 
limitations and propose future improvements for the tool. 
The specific methods used for DS-I, PS, and DS-II, such as interviews and literature reviews, are 
further detailed at the beginning of their respective chapters in this thesis.

Introduction to partner company 
For the empirical investigation of the thesis objectives, a medium sized manufacturing company 
from Denmark has been chosen as a case. The company belongs to the electronics sector, 
specifically working in developing broadband equipment. The goal was to understand the needs, 
gaps and overall interest of the company in social sustainability and evaluate the potential 
integration of social sustainability considerations in their product development process. It 
should be noted that the aforementioned company also took part in the practical

3. Research Clarification 
This chapter encompasses the research questions and objectives, as well as the 
research scope based on specific terminology definitions. 
3.1 Research Questions 
Based on the above, the project will be guided by the following research question: 
• 
How can the integration of social sustainability into product development be supported 
by a screening tool? 
This, in turn, is supported and further defined by the sub-questions: 
1. How do manufacturing companies currently incorporate social sustainability in their 
product development process? 
2. To what extent are existing methods, indicators, and frameworks for social sustainability 
assessment adequate for product development? 
These questions are designed to explore the practical needs of manufacturing companies and 
identify opportunities for a tool-based intervention that can help with social sustainability 
integration and identify the social impacts that may arise from product development. 
 
3.2 Research Objectives 
The aim of this project is to develop a practical screening tool to drive proactive social impact 
assessment in product development. This tool will equip companies with the capability to both 
anticipate and mitigate potential social risks, and to strategically identify and cultivate 
opportunities for boosting positive social outcomes. This will enable them to design and 
manufacture products that reduce and avoid adverse social consequences, while actively 
contributing to social well-being, rather than solely focusing on economic and environmental 
factors. 
The overall objectives in chronological order are the following: 
1. Review existing literature regarding social sustainability in product development  
2. Evaluate the potential for application of social sustainability methods/frameworks and 
social impact indicators in product development in manufacturing companies.  
3. Assess current practices and identify barriers and drivers for social sustainability 
implementation in manufacturing companies.  
4. Consolidate the results of the previous steps and create an initial version of a tool to 
couple social sustainability and product development.  
5. Implement the tool in a case of the company partner and other interested parties and 
extract feedback on the tool’s effectiveness. 
6. Assess the tool’s performance and deliberate on potential advancements that could 
follow on the field of integrating social sustainability in the corporate sector.

3.3 Scope 
As mentioned before, this project aims to investigate the potential of introducing social 
sustainability into the product development process at manufacturing companies. This scope 
was particularly chosen due to a significant gap of social sustainability assessment methods in 
the design phase of products instead of the later stage assessment of finished products or 
processes. Therefore, to define the scope of the project, a clear definition of social sustainability, 
product development and manufacturing companies will have to be given. 
Social Sustainability 
Social sustainability is observed to have many different definitions, both in academic and 
corporate environments. For the purposes of this thesis and on the context of product 
development, social sustainability shall be defined as thus: 
Social sustainability is the practice of designing, manufacturing, and distributing products in a 
way that supports the wellbeing and quality of life of all stakeholders throughout the product's 
entire lifecycle (Sandin et al., 2011; van Dulmen et al., 2025). This involves considering the social 
impacts on workers, consumers, local communities, and wider society, ensuring fair labour 
practices and decent work, respecting human rights, promoting health and safety (Rodrigues et 
al., 2016) and striving to create positive social outcomes while mitigating potential negative ones 
associated with the product (Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021). Ultimately, it is about integrating social 
considerations into the product development process to contribute to a more just and equitable 
society, both now and in the future (Assefa & Frostell, 2007; van der Schoor & Göhlich, 2023). 
Product Development 
Product development is defined as the comprehensive process of conceiving, designing, and 
bringing a product to life, which inherently includes its manufacturing, the procurement of raw 
materials, and its distribution across its entire life cycle (Fontes et al., 2018; Rodrigues et al., 
2016). This viewpoint recognises the substantial impact of design choices on supply chain 
performance, encompassing the selection and oversight of suppliers, and the implications of 
obtaining and processing materials, and the nature of manufacturing operations (Hannouf et al., 
2024; Reuter, 2016). Overall, product development aims to create products that are not only 
functional for their intended use but are also produced and delivered responsibly throughout 
their entire existence (Schulte & Hallstedt, 2018; Vilochani et al., 2024).  
Manufacturing Industry 
In the case of this project, the boundaries of investigation shall be contained within the 
manufacturing industry. The definition of a manufacturing company for the purposes of this 
project is a company that transforms raw materials or components into finished goods or 
products.  It should be noted that no distinction shall be made on whether the company is 
personally involved in extraction of materials or whether the assembly of the final product is done 
within the premise of the company’s own facilities. The process of transformation of raw 
materials into something new can take many forms, with that for example being physical, 
mechanical or chemical, all of which are deemed acceptable. The result ought to be a tangible 
product that may be sold to the consumers, to other businesses, or even be used as a component

systematic literature review and an empirical study involving interviews with the partner 
company. The chapter is structured into four main sections: Firstly, section 4.1 outlines the

4.1.2 Interview design 
The interview questionnaire was developed to understand the overall capability of the company 
to implement social sustainability criteria in product development. The company’s ESG manager 
was selected as the most suitable person to interview, since she is the person responsible for 
bringing any sustainability considerations into the organization, whether it is at product level or 
organization level. To succeed in grasping the capabilities of the company around social 
sustainability, 8 areas of questions have been developed, namely social sustainability 
awareness, organizational mechanisms, social sustainability in product development, 
procurement, value chain (life cycle perspective), responsibility, competence and support tools.  
This questionnaire was largely based on the work of (Schulte & Hallstedt, 2018) who developed 
a self-assessment tool, based on 4 sustainability maturity methods, to facilitate companies 
towards sustainability integration in product innovation. They created a detailed questionnaire 
and conducted interviews with people involved in product innovation from 4 Swedish 
manufacturing companies, to assess their capability in sustainability integration. Their work 
could be considered unique since none of the selected literature has an extended reference into 
questionnaire design nor such coverage on different elements comprising a company’s 
capability in integrating sustainability. The authors based their questionnaire on 8 key elements 
for successful integration of sustainability in product development, namely ensuring senior 
management support, sustainability perspective from the early stages of design, procurement, 
life cycle perspective, allocation of responsibilities, knowledge sharing and competence building, 
tools to guide decision making and tools that involve backcasting approach from defining what 
is considered success for the company.  
The questionnaire used for this study, for the interview with the ESG manager, can be found 
below (A-H):

4.2 Findings: Literature Study 
The insights from the literature study are divided into four categories, related respectively to tools, 
frameworks, indicators, and adoption barriers and drivers. These four topics are expanded upon 
in the following subsections. 
4.2.1 Tool Overview 
The literature paints an image of various tools used for social sustainability assessment. This 
chapter shall serve as an overview of the most prominent ones, explaining their function, their 
way of implementation, as well as any limitations they encountered. 
1. Social Impact Effect Analysis (SIEA) 
The SIEA  (Armstrong et al., 2024) is a novel method designed to evaluate engineered products 
based on social impacts, belonging to 11 different social impact categories. It is considered an 
ex-ante approach, with the intent of being implemented during product design. It considers both 
positive and negative as well as continuous and discreet social impacts, accounting for a diverse 
set of impacts and taking into account various uncertainties.  
The process of implementing the tool can be describes as such: Firstly, comes the identification 
of all stakeholders group who shall be affected by the product. Then, all relevant product 
attributes must be listed in detail. Following that, the impacts of the product on each stakeholder 
are identified. All social impacts should then be assigned to one or more of the 11 social impact 
categories. Then comes a description of how each product attribute leads to a social impact. The 
social impacts must then also be characterized as positive or negative and as continuous or 
discreet. A set of rankings the occurs, where the impacts are ranked in a scale of 1-10 three times, 
based on their magnitude, occurrence likelihood and uncertainty accordingly. Due to that, the 
Social Impact Risk Priority Number (IPN) of each impact can be calculated using specific 
formulas and taking into account if the impact is positive or negative. From this, designers can 
now see which impacts have the biggest positive or negative value and subsequently which ones 
of them need to be immediately addressed. The team then assigns roles to each of its members 
with the intent of improving the positive and decreasing the negative impacts. The previous 
scores will consequently be adjusted to reflect expected changes resulting from these impact 
management measures. Finally, a follow-up is scheduled to ensure that the implemented 
measures had the intended effect. 
The final output of SIEA is a ranked list of potential impacts, which helps designers prioritize 
certain areas to be given additional consideration in the design process. 
(Armstrong et al., 2024) states that SIEA encounters many challenges and limitations in its 
implementation. Firstly, the team experience and expertise can significantly affect their ability to 
identify relative social impacts and quantify uncertainties and occurrences. The ranking can also 
prove to be very subjective, rooted on the team’s own perspectives and prejudices. Issues can 
also be found in time consumption and data availability and reliability. Finally, there are currently 
no industry-specific standards or lists of common impacts for SIEA, although the authors state 
that Social Impact Assessment and Social-Life Cycle Assessment lists could potentially be of 
use.

2. ORWARE 
ORWARE (Assefa & Frostell, 2007) is a computer-based tool, tasked with the analysis of technical 
systems, such as waste management or energy systems, in the context of any of the three pillars 
of sustainability. It was developed at the Royal Institute of Technology in Stockholm in 
collaboration with several Swedish research institutes. The tool is designed from a life-cycle 
technology assessment perspective. It provides a framework for structuring concepts and 
methods to simultaneously assess the ecological, economic, and social performances of 
technologies used by certain technical systems. The social aspect of the tool is approached from 
the angle of social sustainability. Left undefined by the authors, and based on the simplicity of 
the approach, the tool was considered fit to be implemented both ex-ante and ex-post.  
The general implementation process takes the following form: Three relevant social indicators 
are chosen for the assessment (in the case provided these were Knowledge, Perception and Fear, 
based on the premise of social acceptance). After that, the study area is adequately defined (in 
the case provided, that being the municipality of Kil in west central Sweden, which is described 
in terms of its area, population, and environmental work). Then the technologies or technical 
systems are described in a way relevant to the scope of the assessment (the case focused on 
energy technologies). What follows is the development and use of questionnaires, focusing as 
much as possible on maximizing the response rate and enhancing the quality of information (with 
the case using a mailed survey questionnaire). Finally comes the presentation and aggregation 
of results which is unfortunately left undefined by the authors, who claim that further 
developments of concepts from life cycle assessment (LCA) and disability adjusted life years 
(DALY) should first take precedence to facilitate this last step. 
Implementing the tool, the authors encountered difficulties in achieving a consensus on the 
specific components of social sustainability and due to reliance on very limited social impact 
indicators. They also report low levels of public knowledge, leading to low information reliability, 
and even inability to properly collect data, with many of the surveys left unanswered. 
 
3. S-LCA based tool by Bonilla and Fu 
(Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021) developed a Social Life Cycle Assessment (S-LCA) tool with the intent 
of evaluating the potential social impacts of products throughout their life cycle. The authors 
claim that the tool can provide both an ex-ante and ex-post assessment. 
Implementation of the tool goes as follows: Firstly, the goal and the scope of the analysis is 
defined, namely the objective of the study and the system boundaries. The tool also includes the 
option of classifying the analysis as either informative, comparative or enchantment. 
Additionally, this step can also incorporate the nature of the product being studied, its 
functionality and even the spatial scale of the analysis. Then the list of social impact categories 
and indicators ought to be determined. The tool provides an indicator set structured based on 
the S-LCA presented in the United Nations Environmental Programme (UNEP) guidelines for this 
selection, with each indicator having a distinct type and belonging to a precise impact category 
==PAGE:22==
 
20 
 
and a stakeholder group. For the actual impact assessment then comes the selection of the 
Performance Reference Point (PRP), basically the reference threshold value, for the quantitative 
indicators. The indicators are subsequently graded (based on calculation procedures that have 
to be determined) on a scale between 0-1, where 0 represents the lowest social performance 
while 1 represents the best social performance. Finally, the results are visualized, and 
recommendations are made. 
For limitations, the authors claim that there is a risk of social impact misinterpretation, as case 
specific context and the perspective of different groups or individuals can cloud otherwise 
distinct definitions. Additionally, while it is recommended that the list of indicators is verified by 
using stakeholder input, determining relevant stakeholder and acquiring their contribution may 
be challenging. 
 
4. Sustainability toolset by Farrukh and Holgado 
(Farrukh & Holgado, 2020) developed a set of management tools to integrate sustainability 
thinking into early-stage technology assessment (an ex-ante approach). Its general purpose is to 
help companies effectively explore technology opportunities by combining methods for 
assessing new technology and identifying sustainable value, both in an environmental and a 
social context. It is also designed for flexibility, being fit to accommodate both a market-pull and 
a technology-push perspective. The toolset encompasses four main workshop-based 
templates: 
• 
Benefits Mapping, for identifying the overlap between what the technology can do and 
what problems or needs it can solve. 
• 
Industry Structure Analysis, where a supply chain is broken down in order to identify 
potential niches or disruption points for a new technology. 
• 
Value Mapping, for identifying value exchanges among multiple stakeholders, with the 
intent of uncovering new value opportunities. 
• 
Performance Dimensions Mapping, which breaks down a technology offering into parts 
and assesses how their importance changes based on different markets or applications. 
The implementation of the tool is configurable based on the type of technology assessment being 
conducted, of which the authors define three: Technology prospect, where a company 
possesses a new technology but doesn’t yet know its application, Technology Application, where 
a company has a new technology application idea but needs to evaluate its value, and 
Technology-Enhanced Offering, where a company has a new technology to replace an existing 
one and wishes to maximize benefits for all stakeholders. Depending on these assessments, 
implementation goes as follows: 
For Technology Prospect:  
1. Benefits Mapping is firstly used to explore potential solutions or benefits the technology 
can provide. This involves bringing together technical and commercial personnel to 
assess potential value opportunities. 
==PAGE:23==
 
21 
 
2. Industry Structure Mapping then contextualizes the opportunities in market terms, 
analysing the supply chain for disruption points. 
3. Value Mapping takes the form of a pre-workshop discussion to brainstorm preliminary 
ideas for sustainable value thinking, factoring in the company's sustainability vision. 
4. Performance Dimensions finally reflects on and compares performance dimensions 
derived from all previous templates, focusing on key requirements for technology 
adoption. 
For Technology Application: 
1. Benefits Mapping is used to initiate the discussion from a technology-pull, market-push, 
or solution-driven perspective. 
2. Value Mapping identifies value exchanges among the stakeholders in order to uncover 
new opportunities. 
3. Performance Dimensions is performed as the final step for reflection and comparison 
and to come up with action plans. 
For Technology-Enhanced Offering, a simple Value Mapping is conducted to analyse ways to 
enhance sustainable value creation for a wider range of stakeholders. 
Regarding limitations, the toolset encountered difficulties with its fast-paced nature that often 
does not allow for in-depth exploration of each template.  Difficulties in narrowing down the 
topics both before and during the workshops proved to be detrimental to the tool’s effectiveness. 
The authors also report that the toolset requires a significant amount of facilitation to be 
implemented while access to quantitative data is often limited or unreliable. Finally, getting 
stakeholders involved is hard while a lot of them prove to be unfamiliar with such management 
tools and templates. 
 
5.  Sustainability Criteria and Compliance approach by Hallstedt 
(Hallstedt, 2017) developed an approach for identifying sustainability criteria that can be used in 
early product development, separating criteria into product life-cycle phases and socio-
ecological sustainability principles. To achieve its objective, the approach consists of the 
following tools: 
1. Sustainability criteria matrices used to map out product life cycle phases and reviewing 
each phase in relation to certain sustainability principles, identify strategic sustainability 
criteria. 
2. Absolute sustainability criteria matrix, which is basically a reduced version of the 
overarching sustainability criteria matrix. This tool encompasses short-term and urgent 
design guidelines originating from legislation and aims to provide a quick scanning to 
decide whether a product early in its development should continue being developed as 
is, change direction or cease being developed altogether. 
3. Sustainability compliance index (SCI) matrix which assesses concepts qualitatively and 
in the context of design guidelines. Each criterion in the generic sustainability criteria 
==PAGE:24==
 
22 
 
matrix is divided into four SCI levels. The SCI matrix can then identify sustainability 
hotspots and guide improvements to a concept's socio-ecological sustainability profile. 
The author reports that while their approach covers both ecological and social sustainability 
perspectives, the social aspects are covered with less detail and explicitly than the ecological 
ones. Additionally, problems with time management are mentioned, due to the overwhelming 
labour setting up some of these matrices may involve. 
 
6. SDG-based social sustainability assessment tool by Heide et al. 
The methodological tool developed by (Heide et al., 2023) aims to assess social sustainability 
throughout a product's entire life cycle, based on the UN's Sustainable Development Goals 
(SDGs). Its objective is to translate the broad social concepts within the SDGs into practical steps 
and to align these with efforts to reach specific environmental targets. To do so, it is designed to 
evaluate a sector or product's contribution to the social SDGs through the selection of social 
sustainability indicators. It is acknowledged as a tool to facilitate both ex-ante and ex-post 
approaches. 
The implementation of the tool takes shape in the following steps: 
1. Definition of the goal and scope, basically what is the study’s purpose, the specific sector 
it addresses, the product type and the functional unit. 
2. Identification of life cycle stages of the product, as well as the geographical locations 
these are being performed. Potential social hotspots based on these locations can be 
identified using the Social Hotspot Database. 
3. Identification of stakeholders and SDG-related issues. An overview of who the 
stakeholders are, as well as the risks and opportunities associated with them based on 
each life cycle stage. These risks and opportunities can then be translated to appropriate 
indicators. 
4. Indicator validation, where the indicators are validated of their importance by the 
stakeholders based on criteria like relevance, measurability, acceptability, additionality, 
available resources etc. 
5. Formulation of the final indicators, based on the modifications made on the previous step 
and by defining their measurable units. 
6. Set up of targets, by dividing indicators into qualitative and quantitative and determining 
the absolute targets of each of these indicator types. Quantitative targets can be defined 
through statistics, standards or expert opinion while qualitative may require different 
methods. 
On the tool’s limitations, Heide et al. identifies issues with subjectivity on the account of 
stakeholders as well as issues with limited indicators that can also prove to be inflexible to 
accommodate for different sectors.

7. Social Impact Assessment (SIA) method by Hempel et al. 
The approach developed by (Hempel et al., 2013) is a method for conducting Social Impact 
Assessments (SIAs) of security technologies. It encompasses a bottom-up approach that aims 
to systematise the informal assessment dimensions of security technologies. The goal is to 
enhance the accountability of decision-making by stressing conflict and divergent perspectives. 
Since the method advocates for early and continuous assessment, while also acknowledging the 
need for re-assessment throughout the life cycle of a project, it was considered both an ex-ante 
and ex-post approach. 
Implementation is done in the following way: Firstly, comes the identification of conflicting 
criteria in the premise of security technology projects. Then the application context of the 
security technologies must be analysed. This step requires an awareness of the drivers that 
shape decisions regarding the needs, design, and practices of such technologies. Then, all this 
informal assessment material will reform into four core assessment dimensions: Security (the 
ability to perform and maintain system functionality, resist change and manipulation, and 
address issues such as reliability, robustness, and data integrity), Trust (how people perceive 
and experience security technologies, including concerns about hygiene, intimacy, and body 
integrity), Efficiency (economically related issues, like life cycle costs, time and space 
requirements etc) and Freedom Infringements (the potential impacts on individual freedoms and 
the need for transparency and awareness). Subsequently, comes the establishment of a 
moderated space for discussion, involving all potential stakeholders. From this, the various 
inconsistencies and antagonisms may arise, bringing into the attention of the stakeholders the 
broader social impacts of their decisions. 
Overall, the method recognises that the shaping of technology development and implementation 
depends heavily on the power relationships involved. Therefore, by creating a moderated space 
for negotiation and discussion, it aims to make the criteria subject to reflection. 
Regarding limitations to the method, the authors recognise that certain value conflicts are often 
not resolvable, regardless of negotiation efforts, often due to fundamental differences in values 
and perspectives between the stakeholders. A lack of knowledge and willingness to cooperate 
between stakeholders is also detrimental to the method. Also, there is often high uncertainty 
about relevant assessment criteria in terms of definitions and how to interpret them. Finally, 
social impact assessment is considered a complex process, requiring consideration of a wide 
range of factors (social, economic, financial, political, legal, ethical, psychological). Such 
complexity can therefore make it challenging to conduct a comprehensive and meaningful 
assessment. 
 
 8. Constructive Sustainability Assessment (CSA) 
The Constructive Sustainability Assessment tool (Matthews et al., 2019), was designed to 
evaluate the sustainability of emerging technologies through the incorporation of sustainability 
considerations in the early stages of technology development (ex-ante). Based on the authors, 
implementation takes form in 3 steps: 
==PAGE:26==
 
24 
 
1. Formulation: Relevant stakeholders ought to be identified and engaged in the form of 
workshops and/or surveys. Then deliberation through discussion ought to clarify the 
sustainability concept in need of being deployed, basically what the stakeholders would 
like to achieve through this process. This can include activities like pinpointing potential 
technological futures and scenarios of interest, clarifying the object, level and system 
boundaries of the analysis, exploring stakeholder worldviews and discussing data 
sources and interpretation to ensure the results are understandable and legitimate. 
2. Evaluation: Firstly, the methods for assessing the sustainability aspects of the analysis 
need to be selected. Any potential uncertainties regarding the data ought to be 
acknowledged and relevant assumption, exclusions and limitations will have to be 
recorded. To account for uncertainty, methods like the Monte Carlo simulations can be 
employed.  
3. Interpretation: The results are consolidated and presented to the stakeholders, with the 
value-judgements involved, being made completely apparent. The empirical results 
gathered in the evaluation phase need to be related to the formulation stage through 
deliberative activities like workshops. Topics for reflection can be the meaning and 
significance of the results (including unexpected outcomes or hotspots), the limitations 
of the results and potential recommendations and actions to be taken now or in the future 
This final stage also adds the opportunity of restarting the whole process by assessing 
ambiguities and alternative interpretations, giving this tool an iterative character. 
Matthews et al. recognises limitations of their tool, namely on issues with data availability and 
uncertainty, claiming that at low technology readiness levels (TRLs), data is often limited. Their 
tool is also subject to subjectivity, with the potential risk of inaccurate results. Additionally, 
identification and engagement of relevant stakeholders also proved challenging. Finally, the 
authors acknowledge that CSA can’t provide the underlying incentives for sustainable 
development, which often need to be nurtured elsewhere and before CSA implementation takes 
place. 
 
9. Sustainability requirements integration method by Quernheim and Schleich  
Quernheim & Schleich (2024) mention that due to the Corporate Sustainability Reporting 
Directive (CSRD) effect, sustainability must become an integrated part of product development 
processes. The authors suggest a systematic procedure to align product development with 
sustainability indicators from GRI standards due to the strong relation to Triple Bottom Line (TBL) 
perspective and the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS). The researchers 
employed life cycle thinking to link the life cycle stages of an exemplary product to relevant GRI 
Standards, and they proposed that GRI indicators should be included in product requirements. 
It is stated to be an ex-ante approach. 
Implementation follows the following steps: 
1. Creation of a sustainability requirements catalogue.  This should include guidelines for 
sustainable product development, like circular economy (CE) strategies, Sustainable 
==PAGE:27==
 
25 
 
Development Goals (SDGs) and Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) Standards. The authors 
advise that the establishment of the catalogue be done in SQL. 
2. Performance of a Hotspot Analysis. This step will include the definition of the goal and 
scope, the collection of data and the incorporation of expert insights. Boundaries ought 
to be drawn based on life cycle phases (raw materials, production, transportation, usage 
and end-of-life stages). 
3. Filter of the catalogue. This needs to be done by product type and by product level. GRI 
standards can then be linked to the categories they affect. 
4. Definition of indicators based on the selected hotspots. Sustainability indicators can be 
selected based on the requirements and recommendations of the filtered GRI catalogue. 
An ad-hoc method can also be used, with indicators being selected based on expert 
knowledge and previous research. 
5. Integration of sustainability goals in product requirements. Basically, sustainability 
indicators ought to be integrated into the requirement structure by creating or revising 
product requirements. Trade-offs between these indicators and conventional 
performance metrics will need to also be assessed and balanced so that sustainability 
enhancements don’t jeopardise general performance. Instead of requirements altering, 
it is possible that processes at the product system level will need to be optimized, to 
realize further value potential. 
Main identified limitations of the study were that the researchers didn’t provide a list of the exact 
GRI indicators to be involved in product development and the exemplary application focused 
exclusively on environmental aspects, without providing any additional information or guidance 
towards linking social sustainability into product development. 
 
10. TOPSIS modified by Rafiaani et al 
Rafiaani et al. (2019) present their modified version of a multi-criteria decision called Technique 
for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS). The tool is designed with the 
intent of identifying and ranking the most relevant stakeholders and indicators, with the end 
goal of informing relevant companies on assessing social sustainability of CCU technologies. 
The modified TOPSIS method is used for the analysis of the results of a survey and is considered 
a flexible ranking technique, applicable on various fields. Left ambiguous by the authors, this is 
considered both an ex-ante and ex-post applicable approach. The implementation steps of the 
modified TOPSIS tool are the following: 
• 
Creation of a decision matrix for each of the participants. 
• 
Normalization of the decision matrix. 
• 
Creation of the weighted normalized decision matrix through the multiplication of each 
column of the normalized decision matrix with the corresponding weight of the impact 
category. 
• 
Specification of the most relevant indicator (positive ideal solution) as well as the least 
relevant (negative ideal solution). 
• 
Calculation of the Euclidean distances of each indicator from the positive and negative 
ideal solutions. 
==PAGE:28==
 
26 
 
• 
Calculation of the relative closeness of all the indicators from the positive and negative 
ideal solution points. 
• 
Final ranking of the indicators and selection of the indicator with maximum closeness as 
the most relevant one. 
The authors report several limitations, namely the lack of systematic social impact research and 
general difficulty in quantifying social aspects. There is also a limited company perception 
regarding the relevance of social concerns as well as what kind of indicators need to be 
considered in the decision-making process, which is also partly to blame on the inconsistency of 
indicators lists for SLCA for different sectors. Problems with participants were also observed, 
such as low response rates and lack of expertise. Finally, the low technology readiness level of 
CCU technologies makes them not mature enough to gather sustainability data on social 
sustainability concerns.  
 
11. Catalog of social sustainability criteria by Richter et al. 
(Richter et al., 2023) developed a tool for assessing social sustainability by systematically 
structuring social issues and corresponding stakeholders. Such structure can then be used to 
facilitate sustainability assessment planning, decision-making and sustainability reporting. The 
catalog aims to improve transparency, traceability, and comprehensibility in assessing social 
sustainability and external reporting, by measuring the positive and negative impacts of 
corporate actions through the use of specific indicators. 
The catalog consists of four interconnected levels: 
• 
Stakeholder groups, characterized by their specific relationship with the company and 
can include workers, business partners, local communities etc. 
• 
Supercategories, representing the grouping of social issues and exclusively linked to their 
respective stakeholders. They can include items like working conditions, local well-being 
etc. 
• 
Subcategories, representing social issues, with 36 such issues grouped under the 
supercategories. 
• 
Indicators, used to measure the impact of social issues on stakeholders, primarily 
originating from GRI and the Roundtable Handbook. 
Richter et al. describe the implementation process as a five-step procedure: 
1. Definition and analysis of goal, scope and requirements. 
2. Identification, definition and analysis of stakeholders. 
3. Review and adaptation of criteria, with them being formulated from the perspective of the 
stakeholders. 
4. Application of the adapted catalog of criteria by providing measures for the indicators. 
Aggregation procedures and weighting may be applied if deemed necessary. 
5. Control and revision of the catalog of criteria, assessing the criteria on their usefulness, 
applicability and fulfillment of specified requirements.  
==PAGE:29==
 
27 
 
On limitations, Richter et al. considered the catalogue’s generic nature an issue, with no specific 
differentiation on whether it is used for reporting or assessment. The aggregation methods for 
turning social impacts into one quantitative value is also admittedly left vague and up to 
interpretation. 
 
12. Business, Environmental and Social Screening Tool (BESST) 
(Sarancic et al., 2022) developed BESST, a tool made to support decision-making through ex-ante 
screening of Product-Service System (PSS) concepts for manufacturing companies. It focuses 
on the triple-bottom-line (TBL) sustainable value potential over the whole of the PSS life cycle, 
examining PSS elements on the three sustainability dimensions, and subsequently providing 
insight into the benefits and costs of various concepts. 
The proposed process for implementing the tool is visually presented in four sheets, designed to 
support the screening of a PSS concept. 
• 
Sheet A is considered the pre-step and involves gradually building up the four dimensions 
(PSS elements, Triple Bottom Line, Value and Life Cycle) throughout the implementation 
process. 
• 
Sheet B deals with the actual examination of the PSS elements over their life cycle on the 
three sustainability dimensions. It offers a holistic picture of the benefits and costs of a 
given concept, in support of decision-making. 
• 
Sheet C utilizes vertical coherence, aligning the cost and benefits across the three pillars. 
• 
Sheet D addresses the hotspot identification phase, by discerning the cost and benefits 
drivers that most significantly affect the concept in question. 
The authors claim that their tool is suffering from being a static and severely fragmented 
qualitative assessment. They are also concerned that the screening involves an aggregation of 
unconnected pieces that build up the whole PSS concept.  
 
13. Checklist for Sustainable Product Development (CSPD) 
(Schöggl et al., 2017) developed the Checklist for Sustainable Product Development (CSPD), a 
qualitative ex-ante decision support tool intended for use in the early phases of automotive 
product development. Its purpose is to guide designers and engineers in enhancing the 
sustainability performance of their technologies by providing a structured and iterative process 
to identify and address sustainability aspects across the entire product life cycle, seeking to 
promote collaboration and awareness. 
Implementation of the tool can take the following simple form: 
• Completion of the questions found in the checklist, with the intent of acknowledging existing 
measures and noting down required actions. 
• Evaluation of the relevance of each individual question to the technology being assessed. 
==PAGE:30==
 
28 
 
• Review of the automatically generated task list and evaluation sheet to identify areas requiring 
improvements. 
• Initiation of follow-up sessions aimed at defining and monitoring the implementation of 
improvement tasks. 
• Reapplication of the checklist iteratively to track progress and further refine sustainability 
considerations and targets. 
As limitations of the tool, the authors mention the necessary presence of a sustainability expert 
in order to implement the checklist in a proper and efficient way. Additionally, the tool is limited 
to the early stage of product development, while it could expand to include subsequent stages 
as well as suppliers. Multiple stakeholders from different departments may be required to 
participate depending on the product’s complexity, adding to difficulties in engagement. Finally, 
the time of implementation was not measured, which could prove problematic if allocated time 
was exceeded significantly. 
 
14. Τechno-sustainability assessment (TSA) tool by Schoubroeck et al. 
(Schoubroeck et al., 2021) introduce their version of an integrated techno-sustainability 
assessment (TSA) ex-ante tool, designed for evaluating the sustainability of emerging (biobased) 
technologies and products, with an emphasis in early development stages. The tool’s aim is to 
help decision-makers identify sustainability challenges and opportunities by comparing the 
potential sustainability performance of various scenarios. It combines methods to facilitate 
indicator selection through the combination of technological with environmental, economic, and 
social data, and uses multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to handle data uncertainty and 
finally compare the scenarios in question. 
The tool is implemented by performing the following steps: 
• Definition of the goal and scope of the assessment, including market study, system boundaries 
and the functional unit. 
• Selection of relevant indicators (environmental, social, and economic) based on literature 
review and expert opinion. 
• Creation of process flows and mass and energy balances, based on gathered technological 
information. 
• Quantification of the selected indicators for each scenario, linking them to the mass and energy 
balance whenever possible. 
• Conduction of uncertainty and sensitivity analysis to identify potential connections between 
indicators and input data. Based on the results, this may lead to adjustments in process 
parameters. 
• Performance of a multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) using a stochastic outranking 
approach to integrate all sustainability indicators and identify the most and least sustainable 
==PAGE:31==
 
29 
 
scenarios. This may involve pairwise comparisons, preference structure definition, and 
weighting schemes. Generally, flexibility of assessment in this step is provided. 
Schoubroeck et al. report limitations on the aspects of data availability and quantification of 
social indicators. Another limitation is the lack of transparency, namely the fact that the tool 
provides just a ranking system of scenarios as output, and it may be hard to explain to external 
interested parties what this result means or how it came to be. Furthermore, the list of preference 
structures and weighting schemes provided for the last implementation step is not exhaustive 
and other methods may fit better into different cases and provide more accurate results. 
 
15. SAM4SIP 
(Schulte & Hallstedt, 2018) created the Self-Assessment Method of capabilities for Sustainability 
Implementation in the Product innovation process (SAM4SIP). It was designed to enable 
companies to assess and visualize their current capabilities for integrating sustainability into 
their product innovation process. It facilitates identification of areas of strength and potential 
improvement, clarification of sustainability goals, and the establishment of a roadmap for 
systematic implementation. The whole tool is based on 8 fundamental elements that ought to be 
taken into account:  
1. Ensure organizational support from senior management.  
2. Efficiently introduce a sustainability perspective early in the product innovation process. 
3. Utilize knowledge and experience of procurement staff in the earliest phases of the 
process. 
4. Include social aspects across the product life cycle and its value chain. 
5. Assign responsibility for sustainability implementation in the product innovation process. 
6. Have a systematic way to share knowledge and build competence in the sustainability 
field to inform decisions taken in future product development projects. 
7. Utilize tools for guiding decisions as a complement for assessment tools. 
8. Utilise tools that incorporate a backcasting perspective from a definition of success. 
The following steps are suggested for the implementation of this tool: 
• Deliberation on each aspect of the eight key elements. 
• Evaluation of the level that best corresponds to the company's current performance for each 
aspect. 
• Indication of level of certainty for each assessment on a scale of 1 to 10. 
• Addition of supporting documentation or experiences to reinforce the validity of the 
assessment of each element. 
On the matter of limitations, Schulte and Hallstedt report issues with subjectivity, both on the 
matters of evaluating as well as defining different aspects. Stakeholder engagement and time 
management also prove to be a challenge. Finally, while the tool was considered useful for 
gaining an overview of capabilities and identifying improvements, it was considered lacking in 
directly clarifying business benefits originating from sustainability.

16. Sustainable Value Analysis Tool 
(Yang et al., 2017) developed the Sustainable Value Analysis Tool to assist companies in 
discovering opportunities to create and capture value through sustainability. The tool functions 
by analysing value captured and uncaptured for key stakeholders throughout the product life 
cycle. It can be performed on a triple bottom line context and transform said value into 
opportunities for sustainability-focused innovations on the business model. 
Implementation of the tool is done based on the following steps: 
1. Definition of the product of analysis (whether a product or service). 
2. Definition of the life-cycle stages of the product of the analysis. 
3. Identification of the stakeholders of each of the life-cycle stages. 
4. Identification of the value captured for the stakeholders at each of the life-cycle stages, 
whether that value is of environmental, social or economic nature. 
5. Identification of value missed or destroyed at each lifecycle stage. This can include 
recognized negative outcomes or perceived risks of value destruction or even created 
positive value that is however not captured. 
6. Identification of value that is surplus or absent at each lifecycle stage. This can include 
underutilised tangible or intangible resources, excessive benefits, and unmet needs of 
the company and its stakeholders. 
7. Identification of value opportunities. Such opportunities must contribute to reducing 
negative value, capturing unmet value or increasing already captured value. 
8. Assessment of sustainability and feasibility of the value opportunities. Opportunities are 
then ranked based on their value and feasibility of implementation. 
On limitations, Yang et al. admit that the tool provided no guidance on how to incorporate 
opportunities into the business model and simply rests on identifying them. This leads the 
authors to speculate that their tool ought to be combined with other tools or frameworks to have 
a holistic effect. Additionally, the need for a facilitator to help implement this tool is considered 
a negative to its usability. Finally, accounting for the size and complexity of the business model 
and the product of the analysis, the workshops may be very lengthy and complicated.   
 
Summary of findings from tool overview 
The review of social sustainability assessment tools brings into light certain insights and trends. 
First of all, most of these tools are designed as ex-ante approaches. Even those tools who were 
not explicitly stated to be ex-ante, or considered themselves to be applicable both for ex-ante 
and ex-post approaches, heavily advocate for implementation at the early stages of product or 
technology development. While ex-ante, many tools (like ORWARE, the S-LCA based tool by 
Bonilla and Fu, the SDG-based social sustainability assessment tool by Heide et al.) aim to 
assess social sustainability throughout the product's entire life cycle. 
The development and implementation of these tools reportedly encounters challenges related 
to defining and measuring social sustainability. For instance, the authors of ORWARE reported 
==PAGE:33==
 
31 
 
difficulties in achieving a consensus on the specific components of social sustainability while 
the S-LCA tool by Bonilla and Fu notes the risk of social impact misinterpretation. Data 
availability and reliability are also addressed as limitations, with many tools struggling with 
indicator selection and stakeholder engagement. 
Regarding the actual context of the tools, there are instances where tools (like the sustainability 
toolset by Farrukh and Holgado or Hallstedt's approach) explicitly aim to integrate social 
considerations alongside environmental and economic aspects of sustainability. The degree of 
success in such endeavours however is generally not satisfactory, with Hallstedt’s approach for 
example acknowledging that social aspects are covered with far less detail than environmental 
ones. Many tools (SDG-based tool by Heide et al., S-LCA tool by Bonilla and Fu etc.) demonstrate 
an effort to align with established frameworks and guidelines (the UN's Sustainable Development 
Goals and the UNEP guidelines for S-LCA accordingly) in order to increase their own 
effectiveness and validity. Finally, not all tools are intended to be used on a generic context, with 
many of them only applicable for specific industries, just how TOPSIS is specifically designed for 
assessing the social sustainability of Carbon Capture and Utilisation (CCU) technologies. 
For implementation, most tools focus significantly on stakeholder involvement, since it is 
considered vital to assess the relevance of social impact categories and indicators. 
Implementation also seems to be, for most cases, structured around workshop-based 
templates, seeking to facilitate a collaborative approach to sustainability thinking integration. 
The results of this initial analysis of tools lead us to two other possible avenues worthy of further 
investigation. Firstly, the frameworks many of these tools were based upon for their structure and 
overall goal, and secondly the social indicators many of these tools used, how did they come to 
be and how effective was their implementation.  
 
4.2.2 Frameworks Overview 
As shown previously, the literature review revealed various tools developed for assessing social 
sustainability. Many such tools were based on preestablished frameworks, either in their 
structure or their use of social indicators. Therefore, to effectively evaluate the validity and 
applicability of these tools, a detailed understanding of these foundational frameworks upon 
which they are built is considered essential. 
 
Social Life Cycle Assessment Framework 
The social LCA framework is built upon the traditional environmental LCA framework, which 
consists of 4 discrete phases, namely goal and scope definition, life cycle inventory, life cycle 
impact assessment and interpretation. The life cycle approach enables the analysis of social 
impacts across the full life cycle of products and systems, but also in specific parts of the life 
cycle, for example cradle to gate, gate to gate and so forth. The S-LCA framework follows a 
stakeholder perspective in the assessment of social impacts, dividing in 6 stakeholder groups, 
namely workers, local community, value chain actors, consumers, society and children, where 
either positive or negative social impacts can occur. A main methodological consideration on the 
==PAGE:34==
 
32 
 
S-LCA framework has to do with the definition of impacts in actual and potential social impacts. 
In the first case the assessment is based on observed data, while on the latter case the analysis 
rests upon the likelihood of occurrence of a social risk. Such as in traditional LCA, the S-LCA 
practitioner should establish a functional unit, which is a quantitative reference unit representing 
the output value of the product or system and based on which the social impacts will be 
attributed in different phases or processes of the life cycle. Another important aspect is to 
specify system boundaries which eliminate the scope of the assessment, in tangible stages and 
processes to be considered in social impact assessment. Cut-off criteria could be also used to 
limit the scope of the study, for example by identifying and including only relevant stakeholders 
in the assessment based on the significance of potential social risks that could occur for these 
specific groups. The S-LCA method could be also supplemented with other stakeholder groups 
under assessment if relevant. The social impact indicators can either be qualitative, quantitative 
or semi-quantitative. Data sources for S-LCA use could be derived from interviews, 
questionnaires, audits, literature references, databases and global statistics. There are two 
approaches in assessing social impacts in S-LCA. The first is by assessing social impacts 
according to a reference scale (RS-SLCIA), and the second by adopting an impact pathway 
assessment analysing the mechanisms that generate social risks (IP-SLCIA). The RS-SLCIA is 
more relevant when social performance or social risks are to be addressed while the IP-SLCIA is 
suitable when the cause-effect chain of social impacts is to be analysed. The S-LCA methodology 
can be used in companies for the development of social policies, support decision making, 
managing social risks and facilitate supply chain materiality assessment, and support the 
creation of non-financial disclosures.  A certain limitation of S-LCA is gaining access to site 
specific data, especially on the edges of the studied systems (i.e. raw material extraction). In that 
case, generic data from available databases such as PSILCA and SHDB could be used to 
complement the study, but this comes with a cost challenge. 
 
Mention and utilization of the S-LCA framework in the literature 
Elements of the S-LCA framework are prevalent in the majority of the explored literature. Of the 
ones who explicitly cite S-LCA as the cornerstone of their own research and tool development, 
the following papers are particularly relevant: 
(Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021) directly relates to S-LCA through their development and evaluation of 
a challenge-derived S-LCA tool. Their research began with a systematic mapping of the social 
assessment field to identify recurring challenges in performing such assessments. Then, based 
on these challenges, they proposed a novel S-LCA method that maps these challenges to the 
different stages of an S-LCA (goal and scope, inventory analysis, impact assessment, and 
interpretation of results) and provides a holistic database of methods to overcome them. 
(van Haaster et al., 2017) integrates S-LCA in the development of a methodological framework 
for social life-cycle assessment on novel technologies. Recognising that the general framework 
for S-LCA was still in its early stages, the paper undertakes a systematic discussion of general 
considerations and seeks to construct a consistent and operationalised method entailing 
several indicators. The methodology involves defining the scope through a review of social 
sustainability concepts, identifying main areas of concern for social well-being, and selecting 
==PAGE:35==
 
33 
 
operational social indicators, all acknowledged to be directly inspired by the methodological 
foundations of S-LCA. 
(Hannouf et al., 2024) presents a new taxonomy of social subcategories and a step-by-step 
process for conducting S-LCA, specifically tailored for technology systems at different 
Technology Readiness Levels (TRLs). Their work addresses the gap in applying S-LCA at earlier 
stages of technology development (TRL 4-7), where opportunities to influence social 
performance are higher. In these early stages, by identifying social aspects relevant to 
technology features and the development process, they provide guidance to proactively assess 
and improve the social performance of emerging technologies. 
(Rafiaani et al., 2020) focuses on identifying social indicators for the sustainability assessment 
of Carbon Capture and Utilisation (CCU) technologies. Recognising a lack of systematic research 
on the social aspects of these technologies, the study aims to fill this gap using an S-LCA 
perspective. By employing the UNEP/SETAC guidelines, a key framework for conducting S-LCA, 
the research identifies relevant stakeholder groups (workers, consumers, local communities) 
and associated social impact categories and indicators. The goal is to provide a basis for future 
social sustainability assessments of CCU technologies and to inform producers about the most 
and least relevant social aspects of their activities, providing a direct contribution to the practical 
application of S-LCA in this specific sector. 
Finally, the volume by (Zanchi et al., 2020) is entirely dedicated S-LCA, bringing together various 
perspectives and contributions from the 6th International Conference on S-LCA. It addresses key 
aspects of the S-LCA framework, such as the definition of the Functional Unit, the development 
of impact pathway indicators, as well as approaches to weighting and scoring. Furthermore, it 
explores alternative theoretical perspectives within S-LCA and presents case studies applying S-
LCA methodologies in different sectors like automotive components and agriculture. 
Additionally, the book acknowledges the importance and increasing interest in S-LCA for both 
private and public sectors. 
 
Roundtable for Product Social Metrics 
(Fontes et al., 2018) present another organized effort to create a systematic methodological 
framework that facilitates social impact assessment, the Roundtable for Product Social Metrics. 
The Roundtable for Product Social Metrics is a cross-sector initiative that was formed to build 
consensus around how to assess both positive and negative social impacts along the life cycle 
of a certain product or service. Its’ end goal is to improve the lives of workers, users and local 
communities by better insights that enable more balanced decision-making and, using data and 
evidence, to evaluate the success or failure of measures taken. The framework was brought 
together in 2013 by several companies who wished for a shared credible and broadly accepted 
methodology to measure their social impacts. According to Fontes in particular, the primary aims 
of the Roundtable were to: 
• 
Consolidate principles for assessing social sustainability at the product level and 
harmonize different approaches. 
• 
Align with other global initiatives in the field and facilitate knowledge sharing. 
==PAGE:36==
 
34 
 
• 
Develop practical solutions for common implementation challenges. 
The primary output of the Roundtable is the Handbook for Product Social Impact Assessment, 
which describes a consensus-based methodology to assess positive and negative social 
impacts of products and services on four stakeholder groups: Workers, Local Communities, 
Small-scale entrepreneurs and Users. The methodology consists of four key components, those 
being the stakeholder group, the social topics (like the sub-categories found in the S-LCA 
framework), the performance indicators and reference scales to assess impact. For the actual 
evaluation, the handbook offers two distinct approaches, a quantitative one and a scale-based 
one. The handbook also discusses the hotspot identification step to understand risks in the value 
chain and the process of assessing social impact using 5-point reference scales. A methodology 
report providing background and explanations of the methodology, as well as an implementation 
guide are also included. 
The Roundtable's work is emphasized to be led by principles such as applicability and business 
relevance accessibility (with the handbook freely available under a Creative Commons license) 
with the intent of producing factual and coherent assessments with disclosed assumptions and 
verifiable claims. Practical feasibility was particularly deliberated upon, since participating 
companies recognized that resources like the UNEP/SETAC (2009) guidelines lacked reference 
values and scales, which are considered essential for product social impact assessment. 
Therefore, the development of the handbook aimed to build upon international standards and 
company approaches while filling these critical gaps. 
 
United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 
The UN's Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) represent a comprehensive framework 
established by the United Nations in 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable 
Development. This framework consists of 17 interconnected goals designed to address the most 
pressing global challenges across environmental, social, and economic dimensions. The 
primary aim of the SDGs is to foster coordinated international efforts towards achieving 
sustainable development worldwide. They serve as a "normative anchor point" for discussions 
and actions related to sustainability, providing a common set of objectives for governments, 
businesses, and civil society.  
The scope of the SDGs is broad, encompassing a wide array of issues such as poverty, hunger, 
health, education, gender equality, climate action, and sustainable consumption and 
production. The SDGs are structured according to a "wedding cake" model, which illustrates the 
foundational role of the biosphere, upon which society depends, and which in turn provides the 
basis for the economy. This hierarchical structure underscores the interconnectedness and 
interdependence of the different dimensions of sustainability. 
The SDGs are intended to guide and inform policy-making, business strategies, and research 
efforts related to sustainable development. While being a global framework, they can be applied 
and adapted at national, regional, and even project-specific levels. For instance, they can be 
used to identify hotspots by assessing if and how activities conflict with specific SDG definitions, 
or as a foundation for developing sustainability assessment tools and frameworks in various 
sectors, including product development and the built environment. 
==PAGE:37==
 
35 
 
 
Mention and utilization of the SDG framework in the literature 
As another influential framework, SDGs have been used in a variety of ways in the researched 
literature.  
• 
(Ehling, 2020) compares literature on social sustainability objectives with the SDGs to 
distinguish sub-dimensions and objectives. 
• 
(Heide et al., 2023) presents a framework for operationalising the social aspects of the 
SDGs in the Danish building sector, using them to define goals and indicators. 
• 
(Matthews et al., 2019) suggests the SDGs as a "normative anchor point" for discussing 
sustainability. 
• 
(Rahimpour et al., 2024) directly aligns its assessed social indices (gender equality, 
informal employment, labour income share, employment rate) with specific SDGs (1, 5, 
and 8) 
• 
(Zanchi et al., 2020) explains how Social Hot Spot Assessment links findings to SDG 
keywords to identify conflicts with specific goals 
 
4.2.3 Social Impact Indicators 
There is a variety of social impact/performance indicators that companies can use to assess their 
social footprint. What follows are the findings of the literature review on social screening tools, 
particularly regarding indicators. 
Types of indicators 
Overall, indicators can be either qualitative or quantitative. Qualitative indicators are often non-
numerical measures or descriptions of various aspects (Sandin et al., 2011). They can relate to 
the circumstances leading to the outcome of an indicator (van Haaster et al., 2017). On the other 
hand, a quantitative indicator is generally defined by its numerical nature and its ability to be 
measured numerically. It provides information in the form of counts, amounts, or ratios 
(Hallstedt, 2017). 
Social impact indicators can be qualitative, semi-quantitative, or quantitative and are often 
based on or heavily aligned with established frameworks and guidelines, such as Social Life 
Cycle Assessment (SLCA). A review (Ahmad et al., 2018) shows that many reviewed P-SPD and 
SPD tools are quantitative, using quantitative and/or qualitative data processed into quantitative 
results. However, some tools use a synthesis of quantitative and qualitative parameters for their 
assessments. Based on (Zanchi et al., 2020) Social Life Cycle Assessment mainly makes use of 
quantitative data and indicators. However, instances can be found of an SLCA method using both 
qualitative and quantitative indicators in their dataset, as can be seen in the method developed 
by (van Haaster et al., 2017) which utilizes six quantitative indicators assessed with an input-
output model and five qualitative indicators mapped through expert elicitation and literature 
review. Finally, (Fontes et al., 2018) specifically describe performance indicators as quantitative 
and qualitative markers of performance, but the rest of the literature seems to use the terms 
“social impact indicators” and “social performance indicators” interchangeably. 
==PAGE:38==
 
36 
 
 
Categorization of indicators based on social topic 
Over the reviewed literature, indicators are used to measure many different aspects of social 
sustainability. Therefore, besides the way they measure impacts, a categorization of indicators 
can also be made based on the kind of particular impact they attempt to measure. A display of 
the most common aspects found can be seen below. 
• 
Working Conditions encompasses elements such as number of working hours, 
minimum salary paid (Fontes et al., 2018), occupational diseases, freedom of 
association, collective bargaining (Zanchi et al., 2020) and workplace accidents and 
illness (Schoubroeck et al., 2021). 
• 
Human rights with examples such as child labour, discrimination (Zanchi et al., 2020) 
and equal opportunities. 
• 
Health and safety, concerning indicators that measure human toxicity (Schoubroeck et 
al., 2021), secure living conditions (Rafiaani et al., 2020), occupational safety and health 
measures (Zanchi et al., 2020). 
• 
Community relations comprising of local employment  (Rafiaani et al., 2020), 
community support and involvement (Schoubroeck et al., 2021), local value creation 
(Wallbaum et al., 2012) and community engagement (Zanchi et al., 2020). 
• 
Governance, which incorporates social policy compliance (Hallstedt, 2017), 
compliance with ethical guidelines, corruption (Rodrigues et al., 2016), transparency 
(Schoubroeck et al., 2021), and governance performance (Kühnen & Hahn, 2018). 
• 
Socio-economic repercussions, with the inclusion of income level (Schoubroeck et al., 
2021), access to essential services, societal investment and fair trading (Rodrigues et al., 
2016). 
• 
Consumer, relating to consumer health and safety (Hallstedt, 2017) product 
transparency (Schoubroeck et al., 2021) and end-of-life responsibility (Rafiaani et al., 
2020).  
 
Influence from frameworks/guidelines 
The overwhelming majority of the reviewed literature makes use of established frameworks and 
guidelines from where indicators for the proposed tools are either heavily inspired or directly 
extracted. The most commonly encountered frameworks are listed below. 
• 
UNEP/SETAC Guidelines for Social Life Cycle Assessment of Products, providing a 
methodological framework and list of sub-categories and stakeholder groups (workers, 
local community, society, consumers, value chain actors) that quite often form the basis 
of a social indicator set (UNEP, 2020). 
• 
The Global Reporting Initiative standards offer a common language/list of economic, 
environmental, and social indicators for organizations to effectively communicate their 
impacts. An example of its use can be found in the work of (Quernheim & Schleich, 2024) 
where the GRI catalogue was filtered in order to select relevant sustainability indicators. 
==PAGE:39==
 
37 
 
• 
The Roundtable for Product Social Metrics Handbook consists of an overview of 
indicators, social topics, and assessment methods for product social impact 
assessment, taking into consideration the UNEP SETAC Guidelines and other corporate-
level standards. The social topics and performance indicators entailed are directly 
related to worker, consumer, and local community stakeholder groups (Fontes et al., 
2018). 
• 
The Social Hotspot Database is a database made available for social inventories, aiming 
to provide data that levels the social aspect of sustainability with the economic and 
environmental ones. It was developed based on the UNEP/SETAC Guidelines and is 
generally used for identifying potential social risks associated with specific economic 
sectors and countries (Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021) 
• 
ISO 26000 can also be seen used as a guidance standard on generic social criteria for 
evaluating strategy (Kühnen & Hahn, 2018). 
 
Indicator Limitations 
It is also possible to identify from literature challenges and limitations in the selection and 
general utility of social indicators for use in screening in product and technology development 
projects. 
• 
Lack of a universally agreed objective definition of social sustainability, which makes 
it difficult to identify specific aspects and come up with appropriate metrics for 
evaluating them (Rahimpour et al., 2024). 
• 
Subjectivity, since lack of such a definition promotes subjectivity in the way people 
interpret and assess social aspects (Matthews et al., 2019). The context-specific nature 
of social aspects remains a key challenge for developing generally applicable 
frameworks, partly attributed to the fact that social well-being might often give rise to 
contradicting interests among societal groups (van Haaster et al., 2017).  
• 
Lack of standardisation and uniform approaches for corporate social performance in 
the context of life cycle assessment and supply chain sustainability assessment, in stark 
contrast to economic and ecological performance. A trend of researchers consistently 
calling for generalised and standardised social performance measurement and social 
indicators along supply chains and product life cycles can be observed, with 
practitioners often lacking a common and adequate standardized framework (Kühnen & 
Hahn, 2018). 
• 
The challenge of indicator selection is considered to be a major source of uncertainty 
for experts in S-LCA, with no universal list of indicators to choose from. On the other hand, 
while some believe that a uniform set would be more robust, others argue against it due 
to the need to incorporate local aspects (Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021). 
• 
Quantification of social indicators. Many social issues and data are difficult to quantify 
(Owodunni, 2018). There are no measurements for social issues similar to dollars for 
economic issues or CO2 emissions for environmental impacts (Pack et al., 2020). 
• 
Issues with data availability/quality. Data availability can pose difficulties, especially 
at a low TRL (Schoubroeck et al., 2021). Gathering extensive sensitive primary data also 
==PAGE:40==
 
38 
 
poses challenges, relying on stakeholder cooperation, with companies sometimes 
reluctant to share (van Dulmen et al., 2025). Low response rates and various missing data 
can also be a problem in studies that engage the public (Assefa & Frostell, 2007) 
• 
Normalisation/Weighting/Aggregation Uncertainty exists regarding the if and the how 
of the implementation of normalisation, aggregation, and weighting aspects in social 
assessment (Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021). While used by many researches and on many 
different tools,(Reuter, 2016) claims that aggregation of social data should be avoided to 
prevent the loss of detailed information. The weighting process for aggregating 
performances across impact categories in S-LCA faces limits such as a lack of 
transparency, implicit choices, no standard weighting method, and failure to account for 
the uncertainty of value choices (Zanchi et al., 2020). 
• 
Definition of system boundaries. On the context of S-LCA, defining the system 
boundaries is often highly subjective, with mostly qualitative criteria. Expanding the 
system boundaries can lead to different social assessment results. Subsequently, a 
problem arises where extensive boundaries can be beneficial but might make the 
analysis prohibitive, while narrow boundaries might leave out crucial impacts (Bonilla-
Alicea & Fu, 2021). 
• 
Connecting social impacts with products rather than solely with the conduct of the 
companies producing them can be challenging, particularly when it comes to defining 
relevant stakeholders (Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021). 
• 
The context-specific nature of social performance can hinder generalisability due to 
factors like regional socio-economic conditions, company sustainability orientation, 
stakeholder expectations and the industrial sector (Kühnen & Hahn, 2018). Social 
indicators are often time, region, and circumstance specific, making prediction difficult 
(van Haaster et al., 2017). 
• 
Misinterpretation of social impacts is a considerable risk of for various stakeholders, 
as local context is an influential factor and perceptions can vary significantly between 
different groups. 
• 
Difficulty in capturing differences at the product level. Social indicators are often not 
able to reflect technical differences between product design alternatives in terms of 
social sustainability, as opposed to environmental and economic indicators (Zanchi et 
al., 2020). 
Overall, the systematic literature review makes evident that there is a vast number of social 
indicators available, of all types, and covering many different social topics. While availability of 
indicators is not the problem, the selection and development of indicators is. There is an absence 
of a holistic list of indicators from which stakeholders can extract and use social indicators for 
generic purposes, while the act of reviewing literature and developing own indicators is proven 
to be complex and inefficient. Nonetheless, the connection of social indicator selection with 
relevant frameworks is regarded as a useful base and is something that ought to be considered 
for the tool development phase of this project.

4.2.4 Companies’ Adoption of Social Sustainability Practices 
The literature reveals various insights as to the extent social sustainability is adopted and 
currently implemented in the corporate world. (Kühnen & Hahn, 2018) claims that the integration 
of social indicators into sustainability performance measurement systems is a recent 
development in research and corporate practice, with companies however still lacking a 
universal understanding of indicators that would allow them to truly grasp their social impact. 
(Mesquita & Missimer, 2021) study of three Sweden-based product development organisations 
(PDOs) revealed that social sustainability considerations are in fact not consistently included in 
the decision-making process of product development. Of those companies that do incorporate 
social sustainability, it is shown to be a discordant and disparate endeavour. For example, some 
of them have a defined at top management level code of conduct, lacking however any clear 
processes to implement it, suffering from uncoordinated efforts at the tactical level and 
unreliable collaboration between functions. On the other hand, other companies exhibit 
effective vertical information flow and horizontal collaboration by anchoring social sustainability 
alongside environmental sustainability at the strategic level.  
Based on the research conducted by (Vilochani et al., 2024) the assessment of the 
manufacturing industry's integration of social sustainability within Sustainable Product 
Development (SPD) reveals a somewhat mixed picture. While the study acknowledges that SPD 
aims to minimise impacts considering environmental, social, and economic aspects, the overall 
results indicate that most SPD management practices are applied at a low capability level. 
Specifically, regarding social sustainability, the research observes that although companies are 
showing increased interest in corporate sustainability strategies and measuring sustainability 
performance, there is a possibility that the focus tends to be more on environmental and 
economic aspects rather than a balanced approach encompassing all three pillars. The authors 
conclude that the systematic and robust integration of social considerations into product 
development processes is still lagging behind in many manufacturing companies. 
 
4.2.5 Summary of Literature Study 
To summarize the systematic literature review findings, it is evident that numerous ex-ante tools 
exist for social sustainability assessment in product development, often aiming for life-cycle 
coverage. However, their development and implementation are hindered by challenges in 
defining and measuring social sustainability, ensuring data availability and reliability, and 
effectively engaging stakeholders. The review also highlighted that the integration of social 
aspects alongside environmental and economic considerations is frequently uneven, and 
although many tools align with established frameworks like S-LCA, the Roundtable for Product 
Social Metrics, and the SDGs, a universally agreed and standardized approach to social impact 
assessment and indicator selection is lacking, leading to subjectivity and difficulties in 
quantification and comparison. Furthermore, the adoption of social sustainability practices in 
manufacturing companies is still in its early stages, often overshadowed by environmental and 
economic concerns in product development. These findings for the thesis underscore the need 
for a practical and user-friendly screening tool that addresses the identified challenges in 
definition, measurement, data, and standardization by potentially incorporating a relevant set of 
==PAGE:42==
 
40 
 
social indicators and aligning with established frameworks to facilitate a more consistent and 
robust integration of social sustainability into manufacturing companies' product development 
processes. 
 
4.3 Findings: Empirical Study 
The empirical review on this project was done on the context of the case company and based on 
interviews of employees and gathering of real-world data, to understand drivers and barriers to 
implementation of social sustainability initiatives in the organization and in product level.  
4.3.1 Interview with ESG manager 
An interview with the ESG manager of the company was conducted, mainly in order to assess 
various facets of the company's approach to social sustainability. It aimed to gain insights into 
the company's maturity, awareness, integration, and practical implementation of social 
sustainability principles. From this, the following insights can be reported. 
PILAR A: Social sustainability awareness 
The company acknowledges that its maturity regarding social sustainability is low. Reporting on 
social sustainability is performed only regarding their own workforce as this is required by the 
board of the company. Social sustainability reporting in the company includes aspects like work 
accidents, participation of women in the total workforce and managerial positions, but it does 
not extend to the supply chain or the customers. It was also mentioned that customers of the 
company do not require social sustainability data and given that social sustainability cannot be 
considered as a selling point and thus there is a lack of motivation to include it in the company’s 
agenda. Another point that the ESG manager highlighted was that the overall motivation around 
social sustainability could be enforced by regulatory requirements, but since the EU Omnibus 
regulation is about to come into effect, a measure that significantly weakens the scope of ESG 
reporting, this puts social sustainability aside for the years to come. Furthermore, the interview 
revealed that there is a lack understanding on the definition of social sustainability, a fact that 
makes implementation of social sustainability related initiatives even more difficult. The 
conclusion of pilar A “Social Sustainability awareness” is that the company has a very low 
awareness in social sustainability in all aspects of its operations, and very limited motivation to 
include social sustainability in any case.  
PILAR B: Organizational mechanism 
While the company reports having good hiring practices and diversity, it does not actively engage 
with the supply chain beyond a pro-forma code of conduct. Additionally, social risks   are not 
being actively evaluated in any systematic way. The ESG manager believes that social 
sustainability will be important in the future, especially considering the underrepresentation of 
women in the IT sector and core business areas. They highlight that a lack of diversity can lead to 
missed talent and innovation that doesn't reflect the end-user needs (accessibility for disabled 
individuals, etc). The manager also notes that strategic and regulatory drivers, such as clients not 
meeting their own diversity goals and legislation requirements (like the ones imposed by CSRD), 
could push the company towards greater interest in social sustainability. However, the future 
==PAGE:43==
 
41 
 
impact of recent legislative changes in the EU, such as the Omnibus regulation, is 
uncertain. Currently, the company does not consider social sustainability when making 
decisions either in general or specifically in product development, and there is no evidence that 
management is aware of decisions' impact on social sustainability. On the context of their own 
company’s operations, the manager recognizes some positive social impacts, such as job 
creation and fair salaries in their R&D facilities, along with bringing a “Danish management style”, 
characterized by a positive culture and good working conditions. Positive impacts in Denmark 
include recruiting people of different nationalities. Negative social impacts potentially include 
high rates of long sick leave in some of the companies’ facilities and although the reason for that 
is unclear, there is fear of this being related to the companies’ operations and is under 
investigation. Additionally, a lack of procedures and support for neurodiverse employees is 
acknowledged, which could lead to anxiety and low efficiency. Regarding their products, there's 
uncertainty about positive or negative social sustainability outcomes, although they have 
addressed environmental issues at the product level, by conducting environmental life cycle 
assessments and developing environmental product declarations. So far, they have received 
customer queries related to disability access and parental control features, indicating a growing 
demand for these aspects. From an organizational mechanism perspective, there is a goal to 
have a diversity index, which can be seen as a commitment to social improvement, but there is 
no public commitment from the management side. The conclusion of pilar B “Organizational 
mechanism” is that the company has nor strategic plan neither a strong commitment to 
integrating social sustainability in their operations.  
PILAR C: Social sustainability in product development 
Social sustainability aspects are completely out of the scope for product development in the 
company, with risk avoidance being the primary consideration in resemblance to incorporating 
social considerations. The definition of social sustainability within the company seems unclear, 
and the ESG manager highlighted that it would be very difficult to transfer social sustainability 
aspects in the R&D process due to high subjectivity and lack of tangible metrics to be measured 
or predicted by the product developers. There are no specific social sustainability requirements 
in product development beyond a code of conduct for suppliers, which is not actively checked 
or enforced, acting more as a formality. While reputable suppliers are initially selected, no 
auditing occurs to ensure ongoing compliance. The conclusion of pilar C “Social sustainability in 
product development” is that the companies’ R&D process is very far from reaching social 
sustainability related product requirements when developing new products, and this would be 
impossible for the technical personnel in product development to comprehend since they work 
in a very structured and narrow scope in their everyday tasks.  
PILAR D: Procurement 
Regarding procurement, the company has a code of conduct for suppliers that mentions a few 
limited aspects of social sustainability, although exclusively for workforce-related topics, like 
safety and health in the workplace. As mentioned, the code of conduct lacks prioritization, and 
control mechanism and is often seen as “paperwork” when an agreement with suppliers is close 
to realization. The ESG manager expressed her wish to update this supplier code of conduct with 
clearer “red flag” requirements and enforcement mechanisms. Overall, there is an interest in 
==PAGE:44==

developing more effective ways to set and control social sustainability requirements for

pilar D “Procurement” is that social sustainability considerations could be a more serious part 
of an updated suppliers’ code of conduct, with the overall goal of minimizing risks.   
PILAR E: Value chain  
The company is aware of potential social risks connected to its operations, such as potential 
human rights violations on the upstream side of their value chain, although no critical feedback 
from customers has been observed yet. They are also aware of the risk of political events in 
specific geographies of their operations. Looking at the value chain, the company has limited 
knowledge of the origins of raw materials and associated working conditions, making it difficult 
to assess social aspects deeply. They feel it is challenging to go beyond average global or regional

sustainability. Additionally, and unlike environmental sustainability, the manager claims it would 
be distasteful to associate company’s operations with global average negative social outcomes, 
when there is always a chance that their supply chain is completely “clean” of such 
violations.  The conclusion of pilar E “Value chain” is that the company would like to know more 
on potential ways to assess supply chain risks, but their motivation remains low.  
PILAR F: Responsibility  
The ESG department is responsible for sustainability implementation, with a belief in integrating 
ESG considerations into the development of future products. The ESG department conducts 
several types of assessments including environmental life cycle assessments, environmental 
product declarations, materiality assessments, resource circularity analysis, GHG accounting, 
sustainability reporting, and more, leading the overall sustainability efforts for the company. 
Right now, a social sustainability agenda has not been developed and initial thoughts have 
emerged on the integration of environmental LCA in early-stage design choices for lower carbon 
footprint, without any actionable outcomes yet. The conclusion of pilar F “Responsibility” is that 
the ESG department is in charge of bringing any kind of organization or product specific social 
sustainability aspects.  
PILAR G: Competence 
The ESG department has set a training agenda for managers within the company to raise 
awareness around environmental sustainability and communication of environmental 
assessments findings. There is no motivation to do the same with social sustainability right now, 
since there are no driving forces from the market or regulation.  
PILAR H: Support tools 
Currently, there are no specific support tools at the product level for guiding decisions on social 
sustainability. The company utilizes specific software for environmental assessments in product 
and organization level. Regarding social sustainability, the company uses an ESG reporting 
platform to report on its own workforce as part of the overall annual reporting, without any 
broader social sustainability aspects.

4.3.2 Interview with Chief Product Officer (CPO)  
A more informal interview was also conducted with the chief product officer (CPO), with the goal 
of understanding the process of R&D and the general stages of product development. This 
endeavor revealed that the company’s product development process consists of 5 main steps: 
 
Figure 1. The stage-gate model of the assessed company (empirical study) 
a. Roadmap: The developers and top management gather and discuss the trends in the market. 
Next generation of products should follow certain standards set by the Institute of Electrical and 
Electronic Engineers (IEEE), to ensure compatibility, reliability, and interoperability with other 
network infrastructure essential for the operation of company’s products. The technical 
characteristics of future product are discussed there based on the standards.  
b. Concepting: In this step an initial design of next generation products is developed based on 
the outcomes of the previous step. The developers contact potential vendors to investigate 
whether they already produce components with certain characteristics that could fit into the next 
generation products. The vendors might not develop these components in that point of time yet. 
The design of the product which proceeds to further development stages will be a combination 
of what components are available in the market, and potential suppliers are assessed based on 
economic criteria, their maturity in developing the specific components and whether they have 
already evolved to mass production. It should be noted that the company does not possess a 
standardized process for assessing different suppliers, but their choice is exclusively based on 
the experience internally in the company. Additionally, the company collaborates with suppliers 
in a very limited geographical area (Taiwan). The outcome of this step is a decision on which 
manufacturer is finally chosen to implement the design for the company. 
c. Engineering Validation: The first samples of the product are produced, and they are sent to 
the testing facilities of the company, to assess the physical layout and whether the product 
fulfills the technical requirements as expected, based on the specification set by the IEEE. 
d. Design Validation: If the product fails to meet the technical requirements with the specific 
components, then other similar components from other vendors will be tested, new samples will 
be produced, and an engineering validation iteration follows. In case this also fails, a new design 
follows, and the overall process is repeated.  
e. Production Validation: After the engineering validation, the successful candidate product will 
be produced in a small scale (200 – 300 samples), and the samples will be sent to the testing 
facilities of the company for the final assessment of the product. After that, a mass scale 
production can follow.

To summarize the interview with the ESG manager, the company seems to not be motivated or

4.4 Tool Requirements 
Through both the literature and empirical studies, insights into what the industry requires from a 
social sustainability integration tool can be derived. The frame of the tool will therefore consist 
of the following elements: 
Social Risk Assessment 
Based on both the literature and empirical review, there is an evident need for methods that 
facilitate social risk assessments.  
The interview with the ESG manager shows that the industry values the importance of considering 
social risks, especially during raw material extraction and processing. Regarding product 
development, risk avoidance is mentioned as the primary consideration when incorporating 
social concerns. More specifically, the manager expresses concerns regarding the risk level of 
certain geographical locations, with the interview with the CPO revealing all component 
suppliers are based in Taiwan. 
Such a focus on assessing risks of geographical sectors is quite reminiscent to the definition of 
“social risk assessment” as presented in the SLCA framework. Specifically, social risk 
assessment, based on S-LCA principles, involves evaluating the probability of adverse social 
effects on stakeholders arising from an organization's activities, recognizing that this probability 
can vary significantly across different geographical locations and sectors. It serves to identify 
potential social issues that may arise through the establishment of a company’s operation in 
certain regions and inform proactive risk management. 
From the literature review, an example of social risk assessment comes from (Heide et al., 2023), 
specifically employing the Social Hotspot Database (SHDB) 2018, to identify countries and 
associated sectors with high social risk that Danish construction companies should be aware of. 
Therefore, while the importance of direct social impact assessment can’t be ignored, a social 
risk perspective is definitely an aspect that the upcoming tool ought to incorporate. 
 
==PAGE:47==
 
45 
 
Life Cycle Stages 
Incorporating life cycle stages is considered of paramount significance for a sustainability 
assessment tool to provide a comprehensive and accurate evaluation (Ahmad et al., 2018). 
Through the consideration of all phases of a product's existence, from raw material extraction 
through manufacturing, use, and end-of-life, the tool can avoid suffering from a limited 
perspective that could inadvertently shift social burdens from one stage to another (Waage, 
2007). Such a holistic approach ensures that the true sustainability performance is understood, 
allowing for the identification of cumulative impacts and a more informed basis for decision-
making (Ahmad et al., 2018; Schöggl et al., 2017). For instance, focusing solely on the 
manufacturing stage might overlook significant social risks in the raw material supply chain. 
Furthermore, a life cycle perspective is essential for identifying sustainability hotspots and 
enabling early design influence. Such a focused approach allows for the prioritization of 
resources and efforts towards the areas where the greatest sustainability improvements can be 
achieved (Waage, 2007). Moreover, considering the life cycle early in the product development 
process empowers designers to proactively integrate sustainability aspects into their concepts, 
preventing the need for costly and less effective alterations later in the product's life (Hallstedt, 
2017; Schöggl et al., 2017; van der Schoor & Göhlich, 2023). Finally, the inclusion of life cycle 
stages ensures alignment with established sustainability assessment methodologies and 
facilitates a broader understanding of the product's value chain (Schöggl et al., 2017). 
Finally, the inclusion of life cycle stages ensures alignment with established sustainability 
assessment methodologies and facilitates a broader understanding of the product's value chain  
(Schöggl et al., 2017). Methodologies such as Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) and Social Life Cycle 
Assessment (S-LCA) inherently adopt a life cycle perspective. Therefore, by mirroring this 
approach, the tool allows for potential integration with more detailed assessments and 
facilitates comparability across different studies and products (Ahmad et al., 2018; Rafiaani et 
al., 2020). 
 
Easy-to-use 
The literature provides many cases of successful tools which were designed to be used by 
management and individuals without technical experience to foster understanding and buy-in. 
(Sandin et al., 2011) explicitly states that their tool aims to "enhance the product development 
team’s understanding in the field of social sustainability and engage the team in the 
sustainability assessment", implying a need for accessibility beyond technical experts. 
Furthermore, (Schulte & Hallstedt, 2018) found that their self-assessment method was highly 
useful for "starting conversations about sustainability within the company and creating 
management attention". This highlights that a user-friendly tool can serve as a catalyst for 
broader internal dialogue and secure the crucial support of management, which is less likely if 
the tool is overly complex or requires deep technical knowledge. 
Naturally, besides appeal and increasing engagement, the practical implementation and 
adoption of a sustainability assessment tool are also contingent on its ease of use for a wider 
audience. (Ahmad et al., 2018) identify a "disappointing success and lack of well-established 
==PAGE:48==
 
46 
 
examples of commercial tools and industrial applications" and suggest that future tools should 
be "simple and user-friendly, adaptable, able to clearly show the results of analysis and provide 
guidance to make sustainability improvements, and less resource and time intensive". Similarly, 
(Farrukh & Holgado, 2020) address "challenges in the adoption and use of management tools" 
and aimed to create a toolset that was "configurable and practical while delivering tangible 
benefits", implying that usability is a key factor in overcoming adoption hurdles among various 
users, including non-technical management. 
Finally, for management to effectively integrate sustainability into strategic decision-making, the 
assessment tool must provide clear and easily interpretable outputs, demanding a level of 
simplicity that bypasses technical expertise. (Ehling, 2020) for example points to the popularity 
of the "Strategic Technology Position Portfolio" as a "matrix tool that provides an easily 
interpreted and communicated overview of current and future technology positions", indicating 
the value of user-friendly presentation for management-level tools. (Farrukh & Holgado, 2020) 
also note that their toolset intends to support decision-making and that the templates are "easy 
to apply, helping to structure and guide discussion, promoting a consensual way forward and 
leading to practical action plans". 
 
Integrated list of social indicators 
Through the literature, it has become clear that a social sustainability screening tool 
necessitates a prepared set of indicators accompanying it in order to be as effective as possible. 
The main support of this statement comes from the current lack of a unified and standardized 
approach in social impact assessment (Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021; Kühnen & Hahn, 2018; Sandin 
et al., 2011). Without a prepared set, users would face the significant challenge of identifying 
relevant indicators themselves, potentially leading to inconsistent and incomparable 
assessments (Kühnen & Hahn, 2018). The existing landscape of social indicators is fragmented, 
with various frameworks and studies using different, often non-equivalent, measures (Kühnen & 
Hahn, 2018; Sandin et al., 2011). This lack of clarity makes it difficult for manufacturing 
companies to effectively evaluate the social impacts of their technology development processes. 
Furthermore, creating effective and relevant social indicators from scratch is a complex and 
resource-intensive task, often requiring specific expertise and stakeholder consultation (van 
Dulmen et al., 2025). A prepared set of indicators within the screening tool can provide a valuable 
starting point, drawing upon existing research and established frameworks such as the 
UNEP/SETAC guidelines (Owodunni, 2018; Rafiaani et al., 2020; Sandin et al., 2011) or the social 
topics identified in handbooks for Product Social Impact Assessment (Fontes et al., 2018; Zanchi 
et al., 2020). Such an approach can be regarded as more time-effective for the users while also 
ensuring that the initial assessment considers a range of potentially significant social aspects. 
Finally, a prepared set of indicators can significantly enhance the comparability and consistency 
of social sustainability assessments conducted using the tool (Pack et al., 2020). By providing a 
common basis for evaluation, the tool can facilitate benchmarking and the identification of 
trends within and across companies. While the tool might allow for the inclusion of additional, 
context-specific indicators (Assefa & Frostell, 2007), the core prepared set ensures a degree of 
==PAGE:49==
 
47 
 
uniformity, addressing a key limitation of current social performance measurement and 
contributing to more meaningful and actionable insights for sustainable product development. 
 
Top-down Approach 
For a tool to be successful and impactful, it will also naturally require the support of upper 
management. Eling (2020) claims that for the development of business model innovations in 
existing companies, the support of top management is mandatory due to their strategic 
importance and company-wide nature. While the process can be initiated bottom-up, it should 
ultimately be aligned top-down. This suggests that for significant changes like integrating 
sustainability through new tools, direction and backing from higher management levels are 
crucial for successful implementation and impact across the organization. 
Describing the development of their Self-Assessment Method for Sustainability Integration in 
Product Innovation (SAM4SIP), Schulte and Hallstedt (2018) mention that a "top-down approach 
was applied, using backcasting from an ideal vision on all aspects" to define how companies 
ideally need to operate to comply with sustainability principles. Their argument is that 
establishing a sustainability vision and direction from a higher level within the organization can 
provide the framework and targets against which current capabilities and future improvements 
are assessed by the tool. 
Finally, a top-down approach combines well with the high usability factor as described above, 
where an easy-to-use tool can also effectively engage upper management and secure their 
support.

5.1 Tool development 
Based on the gaps observed in the literature review, the needs expressed by the partner company 
and the tool requirements as shown in the previous chapter, the support developed for this 
project was a comprehensive social sustainability indicator selection tool. Consisting of a 
database of social sustainability indicators and a series of filtering criteria, the tool is designed 
to aid in the assessment of social sustainability in product development. The purpose of the 
Social Indicator Selection Τool (SIST) is to provide an easy way for companies to identify a number 
of social indicators based on their preferences and assessment purposes, which can then be 
used to set social product requirements, create the criteria for the evaluation of the supply chain, 
communicate social impacts with the customers, and many other actions.  
SIST helps companies find and filter indicators according to several criteria: internal social 
impacts or external social risks, qualitative or quantitative outputs, relevance to specific 
stakeholder groups, and relevance to life cycle stages of a product. This way, companies can 
assess various aspects of social sustainability, satisfying purposes ranging from sustainability 
communication with clients to risk mitigation. SIST however, is also intended to provide a basis 
for integrating social requirements in the product development process, by providing the 
necessary indicators to assess the social impact of an existing or future product.

5.1.2 Filtering criteria definitions 
As mentioned before, all social indicators in the dataset are divided based on certain filtering 
criteria, to aid in their selection and prioritization by the companies. What follows is an overview 
of said criteria and their overall purpose. 
• 
Stakeholders and their sub-categories: Taking the context of SLCA into account, 
stakeholders are individuals, groups, or entities that can be affected by or have an 
influence on the social performance of a product's life cycle. Each of these stakeholder 
groups consists of sub-categories, represent specific social issues or topics of interest 
under each stakeholder category. They provide a more detailed breakdown of potential 
social impacts that can be assessed, and it is in each of these sub-categories that all 
social indicators of the dataset are assigned to. The indicators were filtered in such a 
fashion so that to enable a more specific and targeted understanding of how different 
social impacts affect distinct groups and to facilitate the assessment and management 
of these impacts from the perspective of those stakeholders. A summary of all possible 
stakeholders and their subcategories can be found in the following sub-chapter. 
• 
Type (Qualitative or Quantitative): These types of indicators are defined exactly as they 
were perceived during the DS-I stage and during chapter 4.2.3. In summary, quantitative 
indicators are those that can be measured numerically, providing information in the form 
of counts, amounts, or ratios. In the dataset for example, many such indicators are 
followed by units like "(%)", "thousands", "per 100'000 workers", or are presented as rates. 
On the other hand, qualitative indicators are non-numerical descriptions of various 
aspects of social sustainability, often relating to the circumstances leading to an 
outcome or the presence/absence of certain conditions. In the dataset, qualitative 
indicators are often phrased as statements about policies, practices, or presence of 
certain mechanisms within an organization or general environment. Examples of such 
can be “Presence of regular check-ups and frequency of animals welfare conducted by 
specialists” on the Ethical Treatment of Animals subcategory, all the way to “The 
company or facility has a policy to address the local community’s queries and grievances” 
for the Community Engagement subcategory. 
• 
Context (Internal or External): This filtering option has a double interpretation, relating 
to the source of the data and assessment for the various indicators, as well as whether 
the indicators address impacts of the company and its supply chain or general societal 
conditions. Internal indicators are those where the data is generated and monitored from 
within the organization itself. These indicators reflect the organization's own policies, 
practices, and performance on various social aspects. External indicators are those 
where the data comes from sources outside the organization and most often involve the 
assessment of a general geographical area or a broader societal context. 
• 
Life Cycle Stages: Based on the principles of Life Cycle Assessment, and for the 
purposes of manufacturing companies, the indicators can also be categorized based on 
their association of certain life cycle stages of the product. These stages are “upstream”, 
“manufacturing”, “use” and “after use”. 
1. Upstream encompasses all activities that occur prior to the manufacturing 
process. For example, this can include raw material extraction and processing, 
==PAGE:53==
 
51 
 
component production, as well as the transportation of those raw materials and 
components 
2. Manufacturing refers to the processes involved in the production of the final 
product. This stage can include the assembly (putting together various 
components to create the final product), processing and fabrication (further 
transformation of materials into the desired form), packaging and all other 
possible activities taking place in the premise of a manufacturing facility. 
3. The Use stage covers the period during which the consumer or end-user utilizes 
the product for its intended purpose.  
4. After Use consists of all the activities that occur once the product has reached 
the end of its intended lifespan. These can include disposal (landfill, incineration 
or 
any 
other 
way 
the 
product 
is 
discarded), 
recycling, 
or 
repurposing/remanufacturing (giving the product a new life or using its parts in 
new products). 
Dividing the indicators in the dataset into life cycle stage categories allows for a focused 
assessment of social impacts at specific phases of a product's journey. This enables the 
identification of social hotspots and a more nuanced understanding of where 
interventions might be most effective. 
Overall, the filtering criteria for the Life Cycle Stages and the Context were conceptualized to 
cover the tool requirements of Social Risk Assessment and Life Cycle Stages, as seen on chapter 
4.4. The stakeholder/sub-categories criteria were chosen due to the prevalence of the SLCA 
framework in the literature, with the intent of it being a convenient and perhaps familiar way to 
focus the assessment of the user. Finally, the Type criteria was included to provide the user 
options to determine whether they wish to pursue measurability and comparison or nuance and 
depth, as well as facilitate to their data gathering capabilities and preferences. For time 
efficiency purposes and to reduce the risk of excessive filtering leading to an insufficient number 
of indicators, no other filtering criteria were included.

5.1.3 Stakeholder groups and related indicator sources 
There are 6 stakeholder groups suggested by the S-LCA framework, which are also used on SIST. 
Namely these are Worker, Local Community, Value Chain Actors, Consumer, Society and 
Children. Each group and subcategory are described below, alongside the sources for the 
indicators in each subcategory.

Worker 
For the stakeholder group “Worker” there are 11 social impact sub-categories based on the SLCA 
framework.  
W1 - Freedom of association and collective bargaining 
All workers have the right of participation in organizations to promote their interests and 
negotiate freely without any interference. The social impacts on this sub-category are captured 
via KPIs relevant to trade union density, worker strikes and lockouts and the presence of 
governance mechanisms in organizations that allow collective bargaining. For the assessment 
of external risks, the international labor organization (ILO) database can be used which 
incorporates several relevant indicators in different geographies and sectors and the 
international trade union confederation (ITUC) global rights index which captures the violations 
against workers’ rights at a global level. For social performance assessment of the company, 
KPIs relevant to restrictions to collective bargaining, company policies against workers 
associations and resolution procedures can be used. KPIs found in SLCA and RPSM are 
exclusively qualitative and are more relevant to manufacturing and upstream life cycle stages of 
products.  
 
W2 - Child labor 
Any kind of work that harms children’s mental, physical, social development, but also work done 
by children below the national minimum age for employment or below 15 years old or any kind of 
work that prevents children from attending school or completing compulsory education is 
considered child labor. For this sub-category KPIs are related to the nature of work done by 
==PAGE:55==
 
53 
 
children, hours spent by children at work, age of workers and the presence of governance 
mechanisms in organizations to prevent child labor. For the assessment of external risks, the 
international labor organization (ILO) database can be used which incorporates several relevant 
indicators in different geographies and economic activities, and also the SDG 8.7.1 – Proportion 
of children engaged in economic activity. For a social performance assessment of the company, 
quantitative or qualitative KPIs relevant to the records of workers details and presence of action 
plans and policies against child labor can be used and have been found to be more relevant to 
manufacturing and upstream life cycle stages of products.  
 
W3 - Fair salary 
A fair wage is defined by fair and reasonable financial compensation for the nature of a specific 
service provided by workers, and is based on the establishment of a minimum wage representing 
a fair wage for such service. The assessment of the external environment of the company is done 
by using KPIs representing the amount of earnings, whether there is statutory minimum wage, 
and capturing wage gaps for specific geographies. SDG indicator 1.1.1 – Working poverty rate, as 
well as indicators from the ILO database are useful to assess wage conditions in different 
geographies and economic activities. Regarding social performance (internal assessment in the 
company), indicators can be both qualitative and quantitative and are mostly relevant to 
upstream and manufacturing life cycle stages, and include documentation of payments, 
earnings of workers compared to a living wage, industrial standards or legal requirements and 
international poverty line rates.  
 
W4 - Working hours 
According to the definition in the SLCA framework, which is largely based on ILO standards, the 
hours of work should comply with laws and industry standards, with overtime being voluntary 
and paid at a premium rate. The ILO database can be used to assess the external environment of 
the company by using national statistics on weekly working hours by employees in different 
geographies/sectors. For the social performance assessment, quantitative and qualitative KPIs 
can be used, relevant to working hours, holidays, agreements with employees and flexibility 
provided by the company. The KPIs found via the SLCA framework are relevant to the upstream 
and manufacturing life cycle stages with respect to product development.  
 
W5 – Forced labor  
When people undertake involuntary work or work under the menace of a penalty, this is 
considered forced labor. Social risks for forced labor can be assessed via datasets provided by 
the NGO “Walk Free” and capture prevalence of modern slavery and government response to 
end modern slavery. Qualitative indicators as found from SLCA and RPSM can be used for social 
performance assessment concerning employment terms, action plans and policies to prevent 
forced labor.

54 
 
 
W6 – Equal opportunities/ discrimination 
Everyone has the right to access equal opportunities and be treated fairly. When people are 
treated less favorably than others for no specific reason, or due to specific attributes or 
characteristics, this is considered discrimination. The social risks here can be assessed by using 
the ILO database via KPIs relevant to employment/unemployment by different population 
characteristics. For the social performance assessment, quantitative and qualitative KPIs 
related to policies, transparency and action plans of the company against discrimination can be 
used and are relevant mostly to the upstream and manufacturing life cycle stages of the product 
life cycle.  
 
W7 – Health and safety 
All workers have the right to a safe and healthy workplace. KPIs used to describe this impact 
subcategory, referring to the external environment of the company can be found in the ILO 
database and are related to the presence of accidents in the workplace, days lost due to injuries 
and inspections of workplaces. The internal assessment indicators found in the SLCA and RPSM 
are related to policies, safety measures and occupational health standards that the company 
apply to promote health and safety in the workplace.  
 
W8 – Social benefits / social security  
According to the definition provided by the SLCA framework, social benefits refer to non-
monetary employment compensation while social security refers to social insurance and 
assistance. Since the nature of this impact sub-category is inherently correlated to specific 
company procedures, only one KPI found corresponding to the external environment of the 
company, which can be captured via SDG indicator 1.3.1 Proportion of population covered by 
social protection floors/ systems. For social performance, both quantitative and qualitative 
indicators can be used reflecting the company commitment and procedures to enhance social 
security and benefits for employees.  
 
W9 – Employment relationship  
According to the definition found in SLCA framework, employment relationship is the legal link 
between employers and employees, and this is the condition that determines the application of 
the labor and social security law provisions addressed to employees. An indication of the health 
of employment relationships can be pictured through the WJP rule of law index, which is a generic 
index, geographically distributed, regarding enforcement of governmental actions towards 
ending corruption, open government, securing fundamental rights and justice. All the KPIs 
related to social performance found via the SLCA framework are qualitative and are connected 
to the contracts signed between employees and employers.

W10 – Sexual harassment  
According to the definition found in the SLCA framework, sexual harassment is a sex-based 
behavior that is unwelcome and offensive to its recipient. The only KPI found for the assessment 
of social risk on this sub-category was based on an EU research, where interviewed women were 
asked whether they experienced sexual harassment during the last 12 months. This research was 
conducted in 2014 and had a sample of 42000 women, but it cannot be considered 
representative of todays’ society. When it comes to the assessment of social performance, both 
quantitative and qualitative indicators can be used, such as number of incidents or descriptions 
of efforts of the organization to reduce risks for sexual harassment respectively.  
 
W11 – Smallholders including farmers  
While there is no commonly accepted term describing smallholders, the definition of 
smallholders in the SLCA framework describes low-technology or very small-scale farms, 
companies and partnerships, specifically regarding workers. The social risks to smallholders can 
be captured by national statistics derived from UN food and agriculture organizations with 
respect to wages, sales to local markets and other indicators describing the work life of 
smallholders. In the social performance side, quantitative and qualitative KPIs can be used 
regarding access to domestic services, ownership, standards and procedures reflecting the 
working life of smallholders. These can better represent upstream and manufacturing life cycle 
stages.  
 
Local community

LC1 – Access to material resources 
Local communities have a strong interest in preserving quantity and quality, and benefit by local

suitable for social performance assessment relate to actions and policies that the company 
could follow to improve access to material resources for local communities. These metrics can 
relate to all life cycle stages of the product.

government expenditure on education as a percentage of GDP (%). As for social performance 
assessment, KPIs describing initiatives and policies of companies to secure access to 
immaterial resources can be used. The identified indicators are quantitative and qualitative and 
cover different life cycle stages of product life cycle.  
 
LC3 - Delocalization; Migration 
Delocalization and migration occur when local populations are dispossessed from their land and 
resources due to large developments in their areas. National statistics can be used as an 
indication of delocalization and migration, as is for example inflows and outflows of foreign 
populations, which can be found in the OECD, ILO and the International Organization for 
Migration (IOM) databases. For social performance assessment, qualitative and quantitative 
KPIs such as policies and procedures related to resettlement, or number of displaced people due 
to the operations of the company can be used and are connected to several life cycle stages.  
 
LC4 - Cultural heritage 
This subcategory includes language, social and religious practices, craftmanship, and practicing 
traditions in general that are respectful to human rights. The only relevant indicator found to 
describe cultural heritage safeguarding with respect to the external environment of the company 
was a UNESCO KPI-percentage of persons in cultural employment. For the social performance 
assessment KPIs with respect to policies, programs and actions to secure cultural heritage can 
be deployed, all of which are qualitative and based on the SLCA framework.  
 
LC5 - Safe and healthy living conditions 
Several safety and health concerns for local communities may arise due to operations of an 
organization, from accidents, diseases, pollution to land use changes that lead to natural 
disasters. For the assessment of external risks, KPIs related to public health and environmental 
conditions can be used through open databases such as OECD or World Bank while for the social 
performance assessment, KPIs regarding policies, actions and mechanisms to promote healthy 
living conditions for local communities can be used. Indicators found in SLCA and RPSM are 
exclusively qualitative and are relevant to several life cycle stages.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:59==
 
57 
 
LC6 - Respect for indigenous rights 
According to the SLCA definition, respect of indigenous rights includes the right to lands, 
resources, cultural integrity, self-determination, and self-government. Historically, states have 
denied many indigenous populations these rights. Regarding external risks it was not possible to 
identify organized databases describing impact indicators to indigenous people. For 
performance assessment, KPIs from the SLCA framework can be used for both quantitative and 
qualitative assessment, relevant to several life cycle stages.  
 
LC7 - Community engagement 
According to the definition provided by the SLCA framework, community stakeholders include 
individuals or community groups that may be affected by the actions or products of an 
organisation, and thus the organisation should attempt to engage with a broad range of 
stakeholders that represent balanced community interests.  It was not possible to find KPIs 
representing external risks for community engagement. Both quantitative and qualitative 
indicators have been found in the SLCA and RPSM frameworks, suitable for a social performance 
assessment of the company. These KPIs are relevant to all life cycle stages and refer to actions, 
plans, initiatives and policies that a company can follow to enhance community engagement.  
 
LC8 - Local Employment 
Open communication and trust within a community can be empowered by hiring and training 
local employees in technical and transferable skills. Since for the risk assessment on the 
external environment, KPIs related to work and unemployment may also relate to local 
employment by assessing share of locally hired people and the nature of their work, ILO datasets 
also apply here. With respect to social performance assessment, both quantitative and 
qualitative indicators can be used highlighting company strategies, policies and actions towards 
enhancing local employment, with them mostly related to the upstream and manufacturing 
stages.  
 
LC9 - Secure living conditions 
While states have the primary responsibility to protect human rights, organizations must also 
respect and protect these rights. Organizations may employ security forces to protect their 
employees, assets and surrounding communities. For the assessment of external risk, KPIs from 
the World Bank database can be used such as the percentage of firms paying for security in 
specific geographies, while KPIs of the SLCA framework (for social performance assessment) 
can be used, reflecting on policies of the organization to secure living conditions. These are 
closer to the upstream and manufacturing phases.

Value Chain Actors 
For the stakeholder group “Value chain actors” there are 5 social impact sub-categories based 
on the SLCA framework.  
 
VCA1 - Fair competition 
This impact subcategory addresses anti-competitive behaviour, anti-trust and monopoly 
practises that are generally described us unfair business practises. For the assessment of 
external environment, the OECD product market regulation indicator can be used or other 
indicators from the World Bank database. For the social performance assessment, indicators 
describing legal actions and formalised procedures depicting the commitment of the company 
against unfair business practises can be used and are mostly related to upstream and 
manufacturing life cycle stages. 
 
VCA2 - Promoting Social Responsibility 
According to the definition of the SLCA framework, organizations can achieve the goal of creating 
social and corporate value by introducing the interests of their stakeholders into the core 
business practices. This subcategory is inherently related to the internal operation of the 
company and thus no external (risk) related indicators have been found. Quantitative and 
qualitative indicators regarding code of conducts, and initiatives promoting social responsibility 
have been identified through the SLCA framework and are related mostly to the upstream 
lifecycle stages. 
 
VCA3 - Supplier relationships 
According to the definition of the SLCA framework, supplier relationships are defined as 
affiliations with organizations that supply another organization with goods and services. 
Procurement decisions could bring unintended consequences to suppliers and should always 
be made with diligence to minimize negative impacts. KPIs describing the external environment 
of the company have not been found and this can be justified due to the fact that supplier 
relationships are mostly related to internal management practices. Social performance 
indicators for supplier relationships address the absence of coercive communication with 
suppliers, lead time and volume fluctuations, with them reasonably related to upstream life 
cycle stages.  
 
VCA4 - Respect of intellectual property rights 
According to the definition of the SLCA framework, intellectual property rights refer to the general 
term for the assignment of property rights through patents, copyrights, and trademarks that allow 
the holder to exercise a monopoly on the use of the item for a specified period. The only KPI 
identified to describe risk for intellectual property violations was the World Bank - charges for the 
==PAGE:61==
 
59 
 
use of intellectual property (in US$). Regarding social performance assessment, only two KPIs 
have been found regarding the organization policy and the use of local intellectual property. 
These indicators relate to the upstream, manufacturing, and use life cycle stages.  
 
VCA5 - Wealth distribution 
This social impact sub category addresses whether wealth is fairly distributed to all actors of the 
value chain. Only qualitative KPIs for social performance assessment have been found and relate 
to the presence of contractual instruments within the supply or value chain that ensure the 
distribution of the value among the actors and definition of a fair price. Depending on the 
indicator, different life cycle stages apply. 
 
Consumer 
For the stakeholder group “Consumer” there are 9 social impact sub-categories based on the 
SLCA framework.  
 
CO1 – Health and safety 
Consumers expect the products they buy to fulfil their function without compromising health and 
safety. Only one quantitative indicator for the assessment of external environment (social risk) 
was identified via the OECD database - number of recalled products. For social performance 
assessments, quantitative and qualitative indicators have been found concerning use and after 
use life cycle stages and relate to the presence of quality and/or product safety management 
systems, evidence on product safety and consumer awareness.  
 
CO2 – Feedback mechanism 
Feedback mechanisms are communication channels between consumers and organisations 
that help reveal customer satisfaction. Only qualitative indicators for social performance 
assessment have been found that can be used internally in the company. These indicators 
address the presence of mechanisms for customers feedback, management measures and 
practices related to customer satisfaction.  
 
CO3 – Consumer privacy 
Consumer privacy concerns include protecting the confidentiality of consumer data, and limiting 
personal information gathered from customers. For the assessment of external risks, KPIs from 
the United Nations Trade and Development (UNCTAD) can be used and relate to governmental 
and non-governmental mechanisms designated to protect consumer privacy. Regarding the 
social performance assessment, quantitative and qualitative KPIs can be used from the SLCA 
and RPSM frameworks. These KPIs are exclusively addressing the Use life cycle stage, a few 
==PAGE:62==
 
60 
 
examples of which are whether a company has a policy and procedures to secure consumer data, 
or the number of complaints regarding breach of privacy.  
 
CO4 – Transparency 
Transparency is essential for the customer to be able to make informed decisions, and 
organizations should focus on simplifying communication with customers to assist in this 
endeavor. Regarding the external environment, only one indicator has been found - the country 
business data transparency index by INFOPROFF. For internal evaluation, quantitative and 
qualitative KPIs can be used, for several life cycle stages, such as whether the product complies 
with standards and regulations regarding transparency, communication policy and company 
ratings in sustainability indices.  
 
CO5 – End-of-life responsibility 
Product end of life management may pose significant environmental risks, and thus 
organisations should be held responsible for an effective handling of waste to minimise these 
risks in the context of extended producer responsibility. For the assessment of external risks, the 
environmental policy stringency index (OECD) can be used, while for the internal social 
performance assessment a quantitative (Annual incidents of non-compliance with regulatory 
labelling requirements) and a qualitative (Do internal management systems ensure that clear 
information is provided to consumers on end-of-life options) KPI can be used. These KPIs refer to 
the After Use life cycle stage.  
 
Society 
For the stakeholder group “Society” there are 7 social impact sub-categories based on the SLCA 
framework.  
 
SO1 - Public commitments to sustainability 
This subcategory relates to the public commitment of an organization towards its stakeholders 
to contribute to sustainable development goals and reduce negative impacts arising from its 
activities. This subcategory is associated to the internal mechanisms of the company and thus 
there is no connection to external risks. Qualitative KPIs have been found, for example whether 
the company participates in the UN Global Compact list, or whether exists documentation of 
promises on sustainability topics and mechanisms for realization of promises. Sustainability 
commitments can relate to every life cycle stage of a product.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:63==
 
61 
 
SO2 - Contribution to economic development 
Directly or indirectly, organizations can foster economic development via revenue generation, 
job creation, education and training, investments and research. Several KPIs from the World 
Bank can be used to assess the general environment (external environment) of companies with 
respect to economic development, for example - manufacturing, value added (annual % growth) 
or Industry (including construction), value added (annual % growth). An example of a KPI that 
could be used for internal assessment is contribution of the product/service/organization to 
economic progress (e.g., annual growth rate of real GDP per employed person) that can relate to 
every life cycle stage of the products life cycle.  
 
SO3 - Prevention and mitigation of armed conflicts 
According to the definition of the SLCA framework, conflicts can be defined as a tense situation 
between different parties caused by different interests, aims or value systems and can be 
classified depending on their intensity. For the risk assessment, KPIs that could be used include 
the Violence Intensity Indicator developed by the Heidelberg Institute for International Conflict 
Research (HIIK), and military expenditure based on national statistics that can be found in the 
World Bank database. For social performance assessment, examples of KPIs include 
organization’s role in the development of conflicts and whether the organization produces 
disputed products.  
 
SO4 - Technology development 
Organizations have a significant potential for technology development and technology transfer 
via capacity building, enabling environment, financial and institutional support. The external 
environment (risk) can be assessed via KPIs found in the World Bank database, such as research 
and development expenditure (% of GDP) and number of researchers in R&D. Mostly related to 
the upstream and manufacturing lifecycle stages, KPIs for social performance assessment relate 
to research partnerships, technology transfer programs and investments of companies in 
technology development.  
 
SO5 – Corruption 
According to the definition given in the SLCA framework, corruption is the misuse of power for 
personal advantages. Different types of corruption exist, including bribery, embezzlement, theft 
and fraud, extortion, abuse of discretion, favoritism, nepotism and clientelism, conduct creating 
or exploiting interests, and improper political contributions. The World Bank provides a data 
catalogue with KPIs related to corruption and are appropriate for the assessment of corruption-
related social risks. Examples of these include bribery depth (% of public transactions where a 
gift or informal payment was requested) and the Corruption Perceptions Index. Qualitative KPIs 
for social performance assessment can be relevant to all life cycle stages and include 
==PAGE:64==
 
62 
 
commitments and initiatives that the company is involved in, with the purpose of eradicating 
corruption.  
 
SO6 - Ethical treatment of animals 
According to the definition given in the SLCA framework, ethical treatment of animals focuses on 
the well-being and/or welfare of animals that are affected by product systems and/or the 
organizations’ behavior. Ethical treatment of animals should secure the animal’s physical and 
mental state, ensuring that an animal fulfills its natural needs and desires. No representative 
KPIs have been found for risk assessment. For social performance assessment, quantitative and 
qualitative KPIs have been found via the SLCA framework, covering aspects such as quality, 
dimension and hygiene of livestock farming conditions, actions in response to complaints or 
serious unforeseen cases putting the lives or welfare of the animals at risk, presence of any label 
certifying the fair treatment of animals, presence/number of behavioral disorders or 
occupational diseases reported by workers, animal specialists, and/or civil society members. 
 
SO7 - Poverty alleviation 
According to the definition given in the SLCA framework, poverty is caused by the lack of 
opportunity, education, health and security and can be “absolute” where people are unable to 
fulfill their basic needs and “relative” where poverty is measured compared to other people’s 
standing in the economy. Organizations and governments should proactively act to alleviate 
poverty. For external risk assessment KPIs from ILO, OECD and World Bank can be used. 
Examples of such indicators include hours of work needed to escape poverty, low pay rate end 
income share held by lowest and highest 10%. For the social performance assessment, KPIs 
could relate to whether the organisation has a formalised commitment to reduce poverty, 
existence of poverty alleviation programmes and planning mostly relevant to the upstream and 
manufacturing life cycle stages.

According to the definition given in the SLCA framework, education is defined as a process of

Education can be formal or informal and is a powerful tool for children’s harmonious 
development. For the assessment of external risk, UNICEF KPIs can be used, such as out-of-
school rate for children of primary school age and completion rate for children of primary school

physical and psychological development during which young people’s physical, mental and

5.1.4 Function of the tool and selected indicators 
While the primary goal of this project was to create a method to integrate social sustainability 
into product development, SIST does not entail that as its sole purpose. For the sake of keeping 
the developed tool easy to use as well as relevant to as many companies as possible, there is no 
specific goal in place for what the extracted indicators will be used for. Instead, the context of 
the indicator’s application is left completely at the hands of the user, with SIST only mentioning 
some recommended uses, without those however being exclusive in any way. The recommended 
objectives and goals following the extraction of indicators are the following: 
• 
Develop strategies for social sustainability 
• 
Identify social risks in a regional context 
• 
Assess the social performance of products 
• 
Assess the social performance of the company

5.2 Tool implementation 
The implementation of SIST is effectively tied to the selection of the proper filtering options. It 
incorporates a recommended order of filtering criteria, general information about social 
sustainability and criteria, as well as suggestions on which options are appropriate for different 
contexts. SIST was implemented as a Microsoft Excel spreadsheet and is available as 
supplementary material. 
 
Figure 4. Overview of steps for the application of SIST 
More details regarding the stages in the implementation process are provided below. 
Stage 1: Stakeholder groups and sub-categories 
First comes the selection of the appropriate stakeholders and sub-categories. Each sub-
category is defined, and the user is given information on what is the exact context of said sub-
categories based on whether they seek to perform an internal or external assessment (according 
to the context criteria above). 
To facilitate the selection process of the stakeholders, the user is presented with certain 
questions, aimed at assisting them in prioritizing and narrowing down on specific stakeholders. 
The questions provided are the following: 
• 
Who are directly affected by your core business operations (both positively and 
negatively)? 
• 
Whose actions can significantly impact your ability to achieve your business objectives? 
• 
Who has expressed an interest in your social performance? 
• 
Who are particularly vulnerable or marginalized groups that your operations might affect? 
• 
Which stakeholders are most critical to your long-term success and sustainability? 
With the appropriate stakeholder(s) selected, another series of questions is provided with the 
intent to potentially narrow down further on specific stakeholder subcategories, to eventually 
provide the user with a manageable amount of indicators, more relevant to their needs. This next 
series of questions can be seen below. 
• 
What are the most significant social aspects related to your industrial sector? 
• 
Which social issues are most material to your stakeholders? 
==PAGE:67==
 
65 
 
• 
Where do you have the greatest potential to create positive social change or mitigate 
negative social impacts? 
• 
Which social impact areas align most closely with your company's mission, values and 
strategic goals? 
• 
Are there any specific social impact areas that are subject to regulations or industry 
standards relevant to your business? 
 
Stage 2: Context  
Afterwards, the user must determine whether the company is in need of internal or external 
indicators. It is highly advised that this filter is not skipped, to avoid having the user being 
overwhelmed by too many indicators. Overall, internal indicators are used for assessing social 
performance while external indicators are used for assessing social performance. 
• 
Internal indicators are optimal in case the primary goal of the user is to understand and 
improve their company's own policies, practices, and performance on social aspects, or 
more broadly, their social performance. Social performance refers to the principles, 
practices, and outcomes of a business’ relationships with its stakeholders (including 
people, organizations, institutions, communities, and societies). It encompasses the 
deliberate actions of businesses towards these stakeholders, as well as the unintended 
externalities of their business activities. Social performance is typically measured 
against a known standard and often uses company-specific data or close proxies, aiming 
to ascertain the actual social impact of a company's operations. Subsequently, internal 
KPIs ought to be prioritized if the assessment is primarily for internal use, to inform 
management decisions and track progress on internal goals. Finally, internal data is tied 
to the company’s ability to gather data on their own operations, with any difficulties that 
may bring, all of whom should be considered in advance.   
• 
External indicators on the other hand are considered more appropriate if the goal is to 
understand the social risks a company might face based on the broader social context in 
which it operates. Social risk is defined as the probability of adverse social effects on 
stakeholders occurring through a company's activities or business relationships, as well 
as the consequence of such an occurrence. It is often measured using generic, sector, or 
country-level data and provides a general risk level of encountering detrimental and/or 
positive social impacts rather than a precise measure of social performance. Social risk 
assessment helps contextualize social performance by indicating the inherent 
challenges or opportunities related to social issues within a specific industry or 
geographic location. Therefore, external KPIs often reflect broader systemic issues that 
the company might have limited direct control over. It should also be noted that, unlike 
internal KPIs, external data are freely available from established sources and require no 
structured data gathering mechanism from the company’s side in order to be synthesized. 
  
 
 
==PAGE:68==
 
66 
 
Stage 3: Types of indicators 
After the environment of the assessment is selected, the user must then determine whether they 
are looking for qualitative or quantitative indicators. It should be noted that if the external 
environment option was selected, the only option for this filter is that of quantitative indicators. 
Generally, if the user considers both types of indicators useful, they are encouraged to skip this 
filtering criteria and procced with both qualitative and quantitative KPIs. 
• 
Qualitative indicators are recommended if the primary goal of the user is to engage 
stakeholders and communicate the “story” of an impact, qualitative indicators can be 
more compelling and relatable than raw numbers.   
• 
Quantitative indicators are advised to be used if the objective is to portray 
accountability and demonstrate measurable progress on specific targets. 
 
Stage 4: Life-Cycle Stages  
Finally, the user can pick whether he wants to receive indicators linked with specific life cycle 
stages of a product or not. It is advised that if a company has observed certain social hotspots in 
its supply chain, it should select the stage of the product these hotspots belong to in order to 
investigate the issue further. Notably, this filter applies only for internal indicators and not all 
such indicators necessarily belong to a life cycle stage, with many of them not being applicable 
to the actual manufactured product. General recommendations for which life cycle stage a 
company should focus on are provided based on the specific industry a company can belong to. 
• 
Upstream: In the context of manufacturing companies, this stage most likely 
encompasses the raw materials extraction phase. Therefore, companies related to 
minerals/mining, agriculture, textiles or even forestry should most likely focus on this 
particular stage, since many such industries can have impacts related to land rights, 
displacement of communities, child labor or indigenous populations. 
• 
Manufacturing: Some industries that ought to pay particular attention to this stage are 
apparel and footwear (industries often infamous for sweatshop labor, poor working 
conditions, low wages, long hours, safety hazards), electronics, automotive and heavy 
machinery (with concerns like worker safety in factories, fair labor practices) and food 
and beverage industries (with issues like worker rights in processing plants). 
• 
Usage: This stage demands attention from industries with products that significantly 
affect society during use. These industries can include those tied to consumer goods 
(with issues like product safety, marketing ethics and accessibility for all consumers), 
pharmaceuticals (access to medicines, affordability, impact of drug use on individuals 
and communities), energy (access to affordable and clean energy, health impacts of 
energy consumption). 
• 
After Use: As a stage affiliated to waste disposal and end-of-life processes, it is 
particularly relevant to companies dealing with electronics (with e-waste leading to 
hazardous working conditions for informal recyclers in developing countries and 
environmental pollution), fast fashion (with the vast amount of textile waste and lack of 
recycling systems), packaging (with excessive or non-recyclable packaging leading to 
==PAGE:69==
 
67 
 
waste management issues) and even nuclear power (with nuclear waste having profound 
social and environmental implications). 
 
5.3 Alignment with requirements and comparison to reviewed tools 
Before the implementation of SIST with the partner company and potential others, a preliminary 
review is done to assess if the tool requirements determined on the DS-I phase have been met, 
as well as determine whether the tool design covers gaps found on the tools that the literature 
review brought forth. 
First and foremost, SIST is a guideline to assist companies to facilitate social sustainability 
assessments, by providing them with the appropriate social indicators from the incorporated 
indicator dataset. It is therefore evident that the requirement of an “integrated social indicators 
list” is met in full. The “life cycle stages” requirement inclusion comes from the indicator filtering 
option of the four aforementioned life cycle stages, for the user to be able to assess certain 
stages using appropriate indicators. Additionally, both “social performance” and “social risk” 
assessments can be supported by SIST, thanks to the filtering of the indicators based on whether 
they assess the internal or external environment accordingly. SIST was also developed with the 
explicit intent of being “easy-to-use" by taking the form of excel sheets and providing information 
and step-by-step implementation support. Finally, it is intended for upper management to come 
up with the desired indicators, since they are presumed to be more familiar with the company’s 
needs and objectives, hence satisfying the “top down” requirement. 
While the requirements can be considered satisfied, it should also be investigated whether the 
developed tool (and the requirements-made-features) are considered novel compared to its 
counterparts as seen in DS-I. For this purpose, a screening of the reviewed tools was performed 
to assess if they themselves satisfy the requirements of having a “life cycle perspective”, 
providing a “social indicator dataset” to assist in their respective objectives, as well as whether 
they facilitate “social performance” assessments, “social risk” assessments, or both. The 
results can be seen on Table 2 below.

5.5 Development limitations 
SIST is burdened by various limitations, all of which should be made known to the users (to set 
their expectations and improve their experience) and to future researchers (to direct future 
research). Said limitations are listed below: 
• 
There can be many instances of outdated data on external databases. Calculating many 
indicators relies on often old databases, offering therefore no temporal validity of the 
necessary data. 
• 
The external databases offer data that is not divided into a specific sector context. This 
means that they provide generic data per geography and not data specific to an industry 
or economic activity, making them potentially misleading and ineffective for certain users. 
Additionally, internal (or social performance) indicators also have no sector specific 
categorization.  
• 
The validity of data retrieved from external databases in the case of risk assessment 
cannot be guaranteed. Certain external KPIs have been selected from databases and 
reports from NGOs and thus the quality of data cannot be considered a given. Internal 
indicators also suffer from validation issues. Several internal KPIs have been selected 
from the Roundtable for Social Product Metrics, a handbook that has been developed for 
companies by companies, without the backing or endorsement by research institutions 
and universities. 
• 
The categorization of the internal indicators into the various life cycle stages was done 
subjectively by the authors and is not based on a specific methodology. 
• 
The introduced tool offers no official guide for making use of the extracted social 
indicators, leaving future actions to the inclinations of the user. 
• 
The assembled indicators are all generic and not categorized in a sector-specific manner.

6.3 Interpretation of results 
Overall, SIST was found to be an effective tool to facilitate both social performance and social 
risk assessments and the tool was determined to be easy to use. However, usability could 
improve further by replacing the excel format in a clearer form, like that of a website. The filtering 
options were all considered important additions to the tool, with nonetheless some reported 
occasions of SIST providing limited indicators, forcing the user to reiterate the tool with more 
relaxed filtering. SIST also proved to be time effective in its use and achieved its secondary 
objective of being a comprehensive introduction of social sustainability to companies, which 
proved to be of high importance to most users. 
On the negatives that were uncovered from the evaluation process, the sector agnostic nature of 
the tool has been found to be limiting for certain users, with the extracted indicators being 
unsatisfactory. Regarding application goals, it is clear that additional support is required for the 
users to utilize their extracted indicators, with such guide being beyond the current reach of SIST. 
Additionally, user requested goals like facilitating sustainability reporting are altogether not 
supported by design. Finally, the link to the product development process was determined to be 
unclear, with the indicators not intuitively being capable of reaching objectives like the 
establishment of social product requirements.  
 
6.3.1 Implementation limitations 
Unlike the limitations made apparent by the authors during the development phase of SIST, it is 
imperative to also list the limitations illuminated by the evaluation process. The relevant 
limitations can be seen below. 
• 
The tool does not provide any support or is linked directly to legislation like the CSRD and 
the overall sustainability reporting process. 
• 
The integrated indicator database can be found lacking (in quality or quantity) for certain 
filtering options and in accordance with the user’s desired objective. 
• 
There is no direct connection with product development, with users requiring further 
support to integrate the extracted indicators into the product development process. 
• 
The nature of the tool is exclusively excel based, which often leads to usability issues.

6.3.2 Comparison of findings to the DS-I chapter 
The evaluation process of DS-II reinforced many of the insights provided of the DS-I chapter, 
while refining or even being antithetical to others. In particular, the following can be derived: 
• 
Regulatory and reporting alignment as a key driver: During the empirical review of DS-
I, the case company's ESG manager expressed uncertainty about their future impact and 
even noted that the EU Omnibus regulation might weaken ESG reporting scope. In 
contrast, DS-II reveals a strong and immediate desire from multiple company 
professionals for social sustainability tools to be directly aligned with legislation like 
CSRD and CSDDD, specifically to aid in sustainability reporting and to explain concepts 
to stakeholders. This indicates that regulatory compliance is a more pressing and active 
driver for companies than previously conveyed, pushing them to seek tools that support 
these obligations. 
• 
Indicator tangibility: DS-I highlighted a general lack of understanding of social 
sustainability definitions and the difficulty in transferring abstract social sustainability 
aspects into the R&D process due to high subjectivity and lack of tangible metrics. DS-II

just starting their social sustainability journey. 
• 
Product development stage alignment: DS-I noted the difficulty in finding tangible 
metrics for social sustainability in product development. DS-II reinforces this by showing 
professionals explicitly calling for features that would align extracted indicators to 
specific stages of product development to establish social product requirements more 
effectively. Furthermore, it was reported in DS-II that social KPI integration in product 
development processes should follow a social baseline setting framework. Overall, 
certain steps have been better refined on how to achieve social sustainability integration,

• 
TBL integration: DS-I concluded that social aspects are often covered with less detail

Potential for Geographical Risk Assessment: While the DS-I case company had limited

• 
Usability by professionals: DS-I identified a general desire for user-friendly tools that 
could foster understanding and management buy-in. DS2 explicitly confirmed that 
sentiment, with SIST being described "usable by engineers not familiar with

7. Discussion 
This final chapter of the project contains reflections regarding the development and the 
limitations of SIST. Afterwards, starting points are set on how certain limitations can be 
addressed, and finally, recommendations for future research are provided. 
 
7.1 Reflections on SIST 
At the start of this project, the overall goal was to create a screening tool that would facilitate the 
integration of social sustainability into the product development process. However, through both 
the literature and the empirical review, this goal appeared harder and harder to accomplish.  
The majority of the tools investigated either facilitated general social impact assessment without 
any focus on product development or had a focus on product development but offered an 
unstructured methodology for product requirement creation. The common limitation that was 
the lack of appropriate social indicators compelled the authors to look into the SLCA and other 
frameworks, where it was unfortunately confirmed that most commonly available indicators 
were organizational, therefore difficult to relate to social assessment of products.   
Additionally, both empirical and literature review revealed a limited awareness for social 
sustainability in corporations and a general perception that social sustainability refers to 
organizational level and ESG reporting exclusively.  
All this lead to the conclusion that integration into product development before acceptance and 
understanding of social sustainability was running the risk of being highly comparable to running 
before being able to walk. 
And so, SIST came into being. With no desire to abandon the original goal of the project, while 
simultaneously needing to make something useful and understandable by companies immature 
to social sustainability, it was determined that the developed support should be an indicator 
selection tool with digestible informative characteristics. SIST was designed to educate the user 
on the topic of social sustainability (as well as types of assessment, lifecycle stages etc.), cover 
the much reported limitation of suboptimal indicators with its extensive integrated dataset and 
allow the user to pursue many different goals (including assessment of their product 
development) based on their interests and capabilities. 
7.1.1 Main gaps 
While SIST proved successful as an educational piece and an effective method to acquiring 
indicators, it had its problems. The most relevant (and most impactful) of which were the lack (in 
quantity or quality) of appropriate indicators for certain filtering options and goals and the lack of 
a distinct connection of the indicators to any goal relevant to the product development process. 
Unfortunately these two limitations left the authors at an impasse. In particular, no apparent 
progress could be made on the matter of limited indicators given the fact that SIST uses 
indicators from all available free and credible sources that could be found, making further 
indicator additions impossible. In the same light, no structured method to translating the 
==PAGE:85==
 
83 
 
indicators into social product requirements could be found and creating one run afoul of the time 
schedule of the project, while it could also potentially overburden the tool. 
7.1.2 Application form of SIST 
As mentioned in 4.1.3, during DS-I, many of the examined tools reported the nature of their 
implementation problematic. In particular, the workshop method most tools used was hindered 
by the need to bring in an external specialist to facilitate the process, as well as the need to 
identify and earn the participation of many different stakeholders. SIST attempted to circumvent 
this problem by being a tool that requires no facilitation (since it integrated all the information 
needed to get introduced to its concepts and mechanics) and through its extended scope, usable 
by any professional that would like to get involved with social sustainability. 
With the conclusion of DS-II however, it remains unclear if that was the correct approach. In 
particular, there were users who reported struggling with understanding certain filtering options, 
and required further clarification. Another example involves a user who originally complained on 
not receiving any indicators through their filters, which was eventually uncovered to be due to the 
fact that they chose more than one life cycle stage on step 5, an action that SIST specifically 
advises against, yet has no way to actively prevent. In retrospect, there is a chance that such 
usability misunderstandings or missteps could have been avoided by a third-party facilitation 
process instead of overall reliance to the instructions integrated into the tool. 
Additionally, for two out of the seven cases (case E and G), a presentation of the tool firstly took 
place, where the authors had the chance to introduce the users to the tool before they tested it 
later, at their own time. While no apparent connection of this process with overall improved 
evaluation results can be determined, the results themselves did allude to a higher level of user 
understanding of the tool’s functions. More than that, the users provided more detailed answers 
on how the extracted indicators would (and the tool itself) potentially connect themselves with 
the product development process.  Overall and in retrospect, a quick run-through of the tool 
presented to all potential users may have improved usability, assuming the users themselves 
had the time to undergo such a process. 
 
7.2 Bridging the gaps: starting points based on current research 
With all the above in mind, some inspiration can be provided regarding how the two 
aforementioned limitations can be resolved, based on the literature review performed on the DS-
I chapter. 
7.2.1 From extracted indicators to socially responsible product 
development 
Out of the aforementioned possible objectives for implementing SIST, the literature reviewed in 
the DS-I chapter can provide some clarity over how social sustainability product requirements 
can be implemented. Therefore, this subchapter investigates how a social impact assessment 
using the extracted indicators can lead to integrating social sustainability into the product 
development process. 
==PAGE:86==
 
84 
 
Translation of Insights into Social Product Requirements 
For this purpose, and assuming the user decides to utilize internal indicators, the insights gained 
from their future social impact assessment will have to be translated into concrete and 
actionable social product requirements. 
As can be derived from the work of (Quernheim & Schleich, 2024), the process starts by initially 
looking at the current product requirements to see if any of the selected social indicators are 
already being addressed. This helps to avoid redundancy and identify areas where existing 
requirements might need to be enhanced from a social sustainability perspective. On the case 
where social indicators are not currently covered, new requirements need to be created. 
Alternatively, social indicators can be integrated into existing conventional requirements. For 
example, a general requirement about product durability could be expanded to include 
considerations about the social implications of a product's lifespan on consumers or waste 
management workers. 
If the user employed the life cycle stage filtering option and has indeed acquired indicators 
related to certain stages, their evaluation can provide insights into potential hotspots at specific 
life cycle stages. These insights can, again, be translated to stage-specific requirements. 
Examples of these can be found in (Owodunni, 2018), specifically: 
• 
Product Design adheres to requirements related to safety, accessibility, and ethical 
sourcing. For instance, designing a product to be easily repairable could have positive 
social impacts on consumers by extending its usable life and potentially creating repair 
jobs. 
• 
Manufacturing Processes include requirements related to fair labour practices, worker 
health and safety, and community impacts at the production stage. These requirements 
can for example specify adherence to certain labour standards or the implementation of 
safety protocols. 
• 
Material Sourcing with requirements that address issues like child labour, fair wages, 
and community rights in the supply chain. This could involve specifying preferred 
suppliers or requiring certifications that ensure ethical sourcing. 
• 
Supply Chain Management involves establishing systems to monitor and ensure 
socially responsible practices throughout the supply chain. Requirements might include 
supplier audits or the implementation of codes of conduct. 
• 
Product Use requirements can focus on user safety, accessibility for diverse users, and 
the social implications of how the product is used. For example, instructions for safe use 
or features that enhance accessibility for people with disabilities could be mandated. 
• 
End-of-Life requirements can consider the social aspects of product disposal and 
recycling, such as the safety and working conditions of waste management workers or 
the impact on local communities where disposal occurs. In this case, design for 
disassembly or the use of recyclable materials can be linked to social requirements. 
 
Integration of Social Requirements into Product Development 
Following the work of (Quernheim & Schleich, 2024) newly defined social product requirements 
must be integrated into the broader product development process. Firstly, the social product 
requirements identified in the previous step need to be formally documented and integrated into 
the product specifications and design briefs. This ensures that the development team is explicitly 
==PAGE:87==
 
85 
 
aware of these requirements and that they are considered throughout the design and engineering 
phases. 
It is of paramount importance that social requirements should be considered across all relevant 
stages of product development. As can be seen from the empirical review of this project, 
specifically in the interview with the CPO, many typical R&D process steps are outlined, 
indicating points where social considerations can be introduced. Additionally, (Waage, 2007) 
suggests integrating sustainability questions into the "understand," "explore," and 
"define/refine" phases of the design process. For example, during the "explore" phase, designers 
can consider how the product can improve human health and fulfil social needs. 
It should be noted that integrating social requirements may involve trade-offs with other product 
attributes or business objectives. It is therefore essential to have a framework for assessing and 
balancing these trade-offs. Based on (Assefa & Frostell, 2007) understanding stakeholder 
priorities (e.g., reliability, price, environmental friendliness, social aspects), can inform these 
decisions. Overall, the process should aim for solutions that optimize overall sustainability, 
considering environmental, social, and economic dimensions in a holistic way. Nevertheless, 
social product requirements should contribute to the broader sustainability goals of the 
company, which might involve aligning with strategic sustainability plans (Schulte & Hallstedt, 
2018).  
For more guided support in this endeavor, a company could make use of specific tools and 
methodologies, as some of those presented in the DS-I chapter of this project. Examples of these 
entail the Checklist for Sustainable Product Development (CSPD) by (Schöggl et al., 2017), 
SAM4SIP by (Schulte & Hallstedt, 2018) and BEEST by (Sarancic et al., 2022). 
 
7.2.2 Overall indicator development guideline  
The dataset contained in this tool was designed to be as holistic as possible, and with the intent 
of being a generic collection usable by any manufacturing company. However, it is 
understandable that in some cases companies would prefer indicators exclusive to their 
particular industry (as can be seen on the collection produced by (Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021) and 
perhaps also wishing to screen through indicators based on their own specific criteria. Based on 
all this, it becomes clear that some support is necessary for the users that feel dissatisfied with 
the proposed dataset and wish to develop their own collection, as well as some proposed steps 
as to how the assessment can continue after the relevant KPIs have been determined.  
While researchers employ unique approaches to indicator selection to develop social screening 
tools for product assessment, a general methodology can be synthesized from their collective 
work. The synthesis was performed by documenting the steps of various indicator selection 
approaches of the literature, merging those of similar context, and adding them all up into a 
single list. Differences on the chronological order of some of these steps were resolved based on 
which order garnered more support from the literature compared to the opposing one.  This 
generic step-by-step guideline can be seen below.  
1. Define the Goal and Scope of the assessment by clearly articulating the purpose of the 
KPIs and the specific area or process that ought to be measured (Ahmad et al., 2018; 
Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021; Heide et al., 2023). Understanding of what these indicators 
need to achieve is crucial, taking into account which aspects of sustainability will be 
==PAGE:88==
 
86 
 
considered and if relevant, the potential stages of the product’s lifecycle (Heide et al., 
2023). More specifically, (Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021) advocate for defining the goal and 
scope in the context of Social Life Cycle Assessment (S-LCA), noting that this provides 
valuable guidance for the rest of the assessment.  
2. Review of existing indicators and frameworks by exploring relevant literature, 
standards and guidelines in order to comprehend what KPIs are already being used for 
similar purposes (Ahmad et al., 2018; Kühnen & Hahn, 2018; Owodunni, 2018). This is 
considered the most important step towards building a foundation and at the very least 
establishing relevant metrics categories. Examples from the literature include (Rodrigues 
et al., 2016) conducting a systematic literature review to gather available KPIs for product 
development. (Kühnen & Hahn, 2018) also followed a systematic literature review to 
identify trends and gaps in social performance measurement. Examining frameworks like 
the GRI Standards (Quernheim & Schleich, 2024), UNEP/SETAC guidelines (Heide et al., 
2023; Owodunni, 2018) and the Roundtable for Product Social Metrics handbook (Fontes 
et al., 2018) are held to be optimal starting points.  
3. Generation of a comprehensive list of potential KPIs that could measure the identified 
criteria or aspects that will need to incorporated (Rodrigues et al., 2016), taking into 
consideration both quantitative and qualitative measures. For example, (Rodrigues et al., 
2016) initially identified 830 process-related indicators in their review.  
4. Definition of criteria for indicator selection. To narrow down the KPI list from the 
previous step, a variety of criteria ought to be considered. The most common of those 
found in the literature are relevance (relation to defined goal/scope), measurability (how 
assessable it is using a defined scale), data availability (if data are reasonably 
accessible/obtainable), understandability (how easy it is to interpret), actionability (how 
insightful to decision-making), timeliness (if the data from the KPI can be made available 
in time) and consistency (if the KPI is consistently measurable over time) (Bonilla-Alicea 
& Fu, 2021; Fontes et al., 2018; Richter et al., 2023).  
5. Selection and refinement of the indicators. The previously defined selection criteria 
are applied to the general indicator list, narrowing down to the most suitable KPIs. 
Modifications to the KPIs in the form of altering their wording to ensure clarity and 
precision also apply here (Fontes et al., 2018; Heide et al., 2023).  
6. Designation of units of measurement and data collection methods, where each 
selected KPI is assigned a unit of measurement, like percentage, number, or score and 
methods for data collection, like surveys, audits, existing records etc. (Bonilla-Alicea & 
Fu, 2021; Rodrigues et al., 2016). 
7. Establishment of Performance Reference Points (PRPs) or Baselines to gain proper 
insight from quantitative indicators. This basically refers to defining a benchmark value 
for each of the indicators, indicating a threshold between values that are considered 
acceptable and unacceptable for the specific goals and purposes of a company. Said 
benchmarks can be based on industry standards, best practices, or internal goals. For 
example, (Bonilla-Alicea & Fu, 2021) discuss defining PRPs for quantitative indicators, 
drawing on the Handbook for the Social Impact Assessment of Products.  
8. Validation of the KPIs before full implementation will be required in order to assess their 
practicality, relevance, and the feasibility of data collection (Fontes et al., 2018; Hallstedt, 
2017).  
9. Implementation and Monitoring, where the selected KPIs are finally integrated into the 
regular reporting and monitoring processes, with their performance tracked over time 
(Ehling, 2020).   
==PAGE:89==
 
87 
 
10. Review and Iterations. After implementation, the KPIs will need to be periodically 
reviewed, in order to make certain of their continued effectiveness and relevance to the 
assessment’s goals (Richter et al., 2023). Based on this review, it may be wise to revise 
certain KPIs or add additional ones, as the needs and context of the assessment may 
evolve (Ahmad et al., 2018). 
 
7.3 Website version of SIST 
A functional limitation of SIST, as revealed by the Descriptive Study 2 chapter, was the tool’s 
excel format. With the reported results displaying usability issues, as well as the realization that 
an excel format may not be ideal to maximize the shareability of the tool, it became apparent that 
an alternative format was required. For this purpose, a version of the tool was translated into a 
website format. 
The website is freely available and can be found on: https://sisply.eu/ 
It should be noted that due to time constraints, the website version of the tool was not tested by 
any of the users found on DS-II, while development of this version is still to be finalized. 
 
7.4 Recommendations for future research 
Concluding this project, from the above, as well as the implementation limitations observed on 
chapter 6.3.1, certain recommendations for future research can be derived. 
• 
A structured methodology to social product requirement creation would need to be 
created. Unlike preexisting work, this method should focus exclusively on social 
requirements (instead of environmental) and based on companies’ feedback create an 
easy-to-follow guide for generic use. An attempt to connect preexisting (or slightly 
modified) social indicators with certain R&D processes may be an initial stepping stone 
to this endeavour. 
• 
Link between indicators and legislation, as was requested by the evaluations of SIST. 
While the authors didn’t have time to add such a feature on SIST, a general association of 
which indicators are relevant for which sustainability reporting requirements could prove 
of great value to companies, making their job easier while assisting them in improving 
their social impact. 
• 
Development of customized sector/industry specific indicators. From the 
evaluations of SIST, it became clear that the current sum of social indicators may not be 
of the utmost use to companies, regardless of their goal. A first step to combating this 
would be to create more relevant industry specific indicators, while ideally validated by 
governmental bodies or academic institutions.
