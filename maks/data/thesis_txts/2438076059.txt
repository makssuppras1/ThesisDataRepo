Abstract
Global warming is projected to increase the intensity, frequency and duration of extreme
meteorological events such as heat-waves (T > 35◦C) in certain regions of the world. To
investigate the eﬀect of short-term temperature changes on biotic and abiotic processes in
shallow aquatic environments, intact sediment cores and water from a shallow marsh in An-
dalusia (southern Spain) were studied for the eﬀect of a winter to heat-wave temperature
gradient (10-42 ◦C for 4 days) on net metabolism, nutrient cycling and denitriﬁcation rates,
with emphasis on the release of the greenhouse gas nitrous oxide (N2O). Increasing temper-
atures stimulated sediment metabolism (O2 consumption) and nitrate (NO−
3 ) uptake and
narrowed the oxygen penetration depth up to 34 ◦C. At higher temperatures, O2 and NO−
3
consumption rates declined and O2 penetration depth expanded. A zone of net N2O pro-
duction was evident in sediments between 18 and 34 ◦C, resulting in accumulations of N2O
up to 18 µM in the oxic-anoxic interface. Eﬂuxes of both molecular dinitrogen (N2) and
N2O from sediment to overlying water increased with temperature, with maximum values
for for denitriﬁcation at 26 ◦C. The ratio of N2O to N2 was not constant and varied with
temperature, increasing from 0.025 to 0.22 between 26 and 34 ◦C. The results suggest that
temperatures over 30 ◦C may induce an unbalance between nitrite (NO−
2 ) reduction to N2O
and the subsequent reduction of N2O to N2 during denitriﬁcation, resulting in the net ac-
cumulation and release of N2O. The increase in temperature during heat-waves appears not
only to alter rates of microbial processes, but also modiﬁes balanced processes and reactions,
causing a disproportional production and release of greenhouse gases such as N2O.

Introduction
Global surface temperature has increased by 0.9 ◦C relative to the 1951-1980 average (GIS-
TEMP Team 2018; Hansen et al. 2010) and predictions suggest an increase of at least 2 ◦C
by the end of this century (IPCC 2014). In the region of Andalusia, southern Spain, the evo-
lution of annual mean temperature likewise shows an upward trend of 0.10-0.30 ◦C/decade
since 1950 (Figure 1). Temperature is known to be an inﬂuential parameter of processes in
biological systems (Parmesan et al. 2003), although the slow and steady temperature increase
is unlikely to trigger dramatic temperature responses. However, associated to the increase in
global temperature is the projected increase in intensity, frequency and duration of extreme
meteorological events, which amplify the tales of rainfall and temperature (Fischer et al.
2015). Already evident is the increase in the number of heat-waves (T>35 ◦C) in regions
such as central Europe and the Mediterranean (Meehl 2004), where the eﬀect can be observed
in some Andalusian cities, in where the number of days with an average temperature above
35 ◦C shows a signiﬁcant increase (Figure 2).
Temperature is an eﬀective controller of biotic and abiotic processes in aquatic environ-
ments (e.g. Stadmark et al. 2007; Veraart 2012; Canion et al. 2014), where shallow aquatic
systems such as marshes, shallow lakes and estuaries will typically respond faster to atmo-
spheric temperature changes, than deeper water bodies, making them more susceptible to
eﬀects of the intensive warming that occurs during heat-waves. These shallow environments
are also highly productive systems that play an important role in nutrient cycling (Herbert
1999). Several studies have investigated the eﬀect of temperature, and consequently global
warming, on nutrient cycling in systems such as drainage ponds, lakes and estuaries (e.g.
Stadmark et al. 2007; Veraart et al. 2011; Klein et al. 2017), with temperature ranges be-
tween 10-25 ◦C, probably due to being located at higher latitudes (e.g. Holland and Sweden)
or because they considered the average temperature increase as a control factor. The eﬀect
of heat-waves (temperatures that exceed 35 ◦C for several days) on nutrient cycling and
biochemical processes in shallow aquatic environments is therefore virtually unknown.

Estuaries, marshes and inshore coastal waters have in recent years become subject to
elevated nutrient inputs from anthropogenic activities such as agriculture, aquaculture, and
domestic and industrial waste disposals (Paerl et al. 2006). Today, many parts of the world
are left with a surplus of reactive nitrogen that leaches into surface waters (Verhoeven et al.
2006), which has resulted in a 2-20 fold increase in riverine nitrogen ﬂuxes during the past
century (Howarth 2008). Since generally being a limiting nutrient, the surplus of nitrogen to
surface waters has supported an excessive growth of phytoplankton (eutrophication) followed
by a decline in rooted macrophytes, hypoxia in bottom waters and consequently biodiversity
loss and a decline in ecosystem services (Howarth 2008; Diaz et al. 2008).

A substantial part of the nitrogen that is exported by streams and rivers from land arrives
to estuaries and coastal zones and they therefore receive the highest nutrient input per unit
surface area of all aquatic ecosystems (Smith 2003). Estuaries and coastal systems serve as
important nutrient recycling sites and are generally considered hot-spots of greenhouse gas
production (Bange 2006), namely carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and nitrous oxide
(N2O). N2O is produced as an obligate intermediate during denitriﬁcation, a process that
removes reactive nitrogen by creating gaseous end products that diﬀuse to the atmosphere
(Zumft 1997). Denitriﬁcation has been proposed as a natural control mechanism of coastal
eutrophication (Seitzinger 2006), where rates of denitriﬁcation have increased in response
to elevated nitrogen supply, removing up to 50 % of the incoming nitrogen (Veraart 2012).
On average, denitriﬁcation produces 1-5 % of its gaseous end products as N2O (Seitzinger
2006) and enhanced denitriﬁcation rates have therefore been suggested to act with a positive
feedback on global warming. The temperature increase during heat-waves is expected to have

signiﬁcant impacts on the biogeochemistry in shallow coastal and estuarine environments and
thus, on the cycling of nutrients and greenhouse gases.
1.1
The nitrogen cycle
Nitrogen is an essential component to all living organisms. The un-reactive transformation
of dinitrogen gas (N2) to reactive nitrogen, namely nitrogen ﬁxation, is so far biologically
only known to be performed by specialized groups of prokaryotes containing the enzyme ni-
trogenase (Howard et al. 1996). Nitrogenase reduces the N2 molecule to ammonium (NH+
4 )
supplying reactive nitrogen to the environment. Reactive nitrogen species include, in ad-
dition to NH+
4 , organic compounds such as proteins and nucleic acids (DNA and RNA) as
well as reduced and oxidized inorganic nitrogen forms such as nitrate (NO−
3 ), nitrite (NO−
2 )
and nitrous oxide (N2O). The conversion between nitrogen compounds of diﬀerent oxidation
states forms the basis for many microbial metabolisms, that in turn frequently control the
availability and forms of nitrogen in the environment (Herbert 1999).

1.1.1
Ammoniﬁcation
In living and dead organic material (OM), nitrogen predominantly exists in the form of
proteins and nucleotides (e.g. in amino acids and DNA) that, when broken down by het-
erothrophic bacteria, eventually release nitrogen in the form of NH+
4 through a process termed
ammoniﬁcation (Canﬁeld 2005). Ammoniﬁaction can occur both in oxic and anoxic environ-
ments, where oxic environments include (but do not cover) the ventilated coastal water body
and surface sediment while anoxic environments exist in subsurface sediments and bottom
waters of stratiﬁed systems (Herbert 1999). The importance of aerobic and anaerobic am-
moniﬁcation depends to a certain extent on the depth of the oxic water column, which controls
the time lapsed before sinking organic particles enter the anoxic environment. In shallow and
productive coastal areas, anaerobic ammoniﬁcation generally accounts for at least 50 % of
nitrogen mineralization (Canﬁeld 2005), or signiﬁcantly more than in the open ocean, where
less than 1 % of organic nitrogen reaches anoxic sediment layers (Suess 1979). Partly de-
pending on location, the NH+
4 released from ammoniﬁcation is either utilized by organisms
for construction of biomolecules, or oxidized to NO−
2 and NO−
3 by nitrifying organisms in
oxic environments (Herbert 1999).
1.1.2
Nitriﬁcation
Nitriﬁcation is the step-vise oxidation of NH+
4 to NO−
3 and thereby links the most reduced
and the most oxidized compounds of the nitrogen cycle, where the harvested electrons and
protons are used to ﬁx inorganic carbon in the absence of light (chemolithoautothrophy).
The oxidation of NH+
4 to hydroxylamine (NH2OH) and ﬁnally to NO−
2 produces N2O as a
byproduct, making nitriﬁcation in marine environments represent a considerable source of
atmospheric N2O (Dore et al. 1996). The yield of N2O relative to NO−
2 during nitriﬁcation
has been shown to correlate with O2 concentration, going from 0.25 % at high O2 (220 µM) to
nearly 10 % at low O2 (5 µM) (Goreau et al. 1980). Optimum growth temperature for typical
mesophilic nitrifying bacteria in pure culture ranges from 25 to 35 ◦C and growth rates decline
below 15 ◦C (Canﬁeld 2005). Additional factors such as O2 and NH+
4 availability, sulﬁde
concentration, pH and salinity all regulate the rate of nitriﬁcation (Herbert 1999). Lower
O2 solubility and higher benthic respiration rates during warmer periods reduce sediment O2
8
==PAGE:10==
penetration depth and might therefore in combination with increased competition for NH+
4
limit nitriﬁcation rates despite optimal temperatures (e.g. Henriksen et al. 1981).
1.1.3
Dissimilatory nitrate reduction and denitriﬁcation
In anoxic or even low oxygen environments, microorganisms can utilize NO−
3 as a terminal
electron acceptor in respiration to harvest energy for growth. NO−
3 is reduced to NO−
2 , where
after the fate of NO−
2 determines which of three possible processes will follow: (1) the reduc-
tion to N2O and N2 by denitriﬁcation, (2) reduction to NH+
4 by dissimilatory NO−
3 reduction
to NH+
4 (DNRA), or (3) reduction to N2 coupled to the oxidation of NH+
4 in the process
termed anammox (Herbert 1999). Denitriﬁcation (and annamox, however not concerned in
detail here) is an essential process in the nitrogen cycle removing reactive nitrogen species
by creating inert gaseous products (N2O and N2) that diﬀuse to the atmosphere. Complete
denitriﬁcation consists of following reduction steps:
NO−
3 →NO−
2 →NO →N2O →N2
Denitriﬁcation is performed by a suite of microorganisms, most of which are facultative
anaerobic heterothrophs consuming easily degradable carbon sources, and some of which
grow as chemolithoautotrophs utilizing components such as H2 and H2S. The recently de-
scribed pathway of NO−
2 driven methane (CH4) oxidation further couples the oxidation of
CH4 to NO−
2 dismutation, a process bypassing the intermediate of N2O (Ettwig et al. 2010).
Complete heterothrophic denitriﬁcation (NO−
3 to N2) is considered most common, however,
under certain conditions, the last step from N2O to N2 is not completed, either because the
organisms do not possess the necessary enzyme, or it is nonfunctional due to molecular de-
fects (Canﬁeld 2005). Denitriﬁcation rates are usually highest in the anoxic sediment zone
right below the oxic-anoxic interface, especially if the O2 penetration depth is shallow and
the diﬀusion distance for NO−
3 from the overlying water thus is small (Rysgaard et al. 1994).
Here, the denitriﬁcation can be tightly coupled to nitriﬁcation occurring in the oxygenated
surface sediment (Jenkins et al. 1984), where the strength of the coupling partly depends on
nitrogen sources and availability. In drainage ditches and lakes receiving a surplus of nitrogen
from agriculture, denitriﬁcation has been shown to be able to remove 20-50 % of the incoming
9
==PAGE:11==
nitrogen (Veraart 2012; de Klein 2008). Denitriﬁcation rates are controlled by factors such
as organic matter and NO−
3 availability, sediment type, dissolved O2 and temperature (Yoon
et al. 1992; Veraart 2012; Klein et al. 2017; Palacin-Lizarbe et al. 2018), where denitriﬁcation
can occur between 2-50 ◦C and optimum temperatures lie within the 25-35 ◦C range (Can-
ﬁeld 2005). Reported rates vary widely in space and time, and the factors controlling them
may diﬀer among systems and seasons. For example, denitriﬁcation rates in coastal areas
typically range from 10 to 130 µmol N m−2 h−1 (Piña-Ochoa et al. 2006), with considerable
seasonal variability (Christensen et al. 1988; Hasegawa et al. 2004), whereas denitriﬁcation
rates of 300-400 µmol N m−2 h−1 have been measured in organic rich drainage pond sedi-
ments (Veraart et al. 2011). Reaction rates have traditionally been assumed to double for
every 10 ◦C increase in temperature (Q10 = 2) (i.e. Arrhenius rate law), but Q10 values in
more recent literature span a range from 1-10 (e.g.Rysgaard et al. 2004; Veraart et al. 2011).
1.2
Temperature eﬀects on nitrogen cycling
Temperature aﬀecting both abiotic and biologically catalyzed processes in aquatic environ-
ments, where each individual process often shows a distinctive temperature response (e.g.
Rysgaard et al. 2004). A change in temperature will therefore not only aﬀect overall rates of
turnover, but might also shift the relative importance of each process. Higher temperatures
may for example increase the production of NH+
4 by elevated mineralisation rates and further
increase the conversion rate of NH+
4 to NO−
2 and NO−
3 by nitriﬁcation. However, on the con-
trary, temperature increase might lower the concentration of dissolved O2 in the sediment to
such a degree that it will limit nitriﬁcation (Jenkins et al. 1984). These multiple interactions
involved make it challenging to predict eﬀects of warming on environmental processes in
aquatic systems, especially since certain responses might counteract each other to buﬀer the
eﬀect, whereas a synergy between eﬀects might cause an ampliﬁed signal in other situations.
In a controlled batch reactor study, denitriﬁcation showed only a moderate response to tem-
perature (Holtan-Hartwig 2002), while studies on constructed wetlands and drainage ponds
(Veraart et al. 2011) propose a stronger eﬀect of temperature on denitriﬁcation rates. This
suggests that the eﬀect of temperature on denitriﬁcation may arise from coupled temperature
dependent processes that are excluded in a bioreactor study but captured in a more natural
10
==PAGE:12==
setting. Previous studies using batch reactors thus appear unable to well predict eﬀects of
warming in environmental processes, which stresses the importance of warming studies that
do not disrupt the natural gradient of substrates and redox in sediments and thus maintain
the chemical microenvironment of the microorganisms to be investigated.
1.3
Objectives
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate the eﬀects of extreme climate events such
as heat-waves on the net metabolism and nutrient cycling in aquatic shallow environments
with emphasis on the processes related to release of greenhouse gases such as N2O.
The eﬀect of a wide temperature range on O2 and nutrient dynamics, denitriﬁcation
rates and N2O eﬂux from sediments was quantiﬁed in a shallow inter-tidal estuarine zone
in southern Spain. Produced as an intermediate gas during denitriﬁcation, N2O can escape
the ﬁnal reduction step to N2 during denitriﬁcation.
Since temperature both inﬂuences
gas diﬀusivity and rates of biologically catalyzed processes controlling the production and
consumption of N2O, we wanted to explore how the conditions that arise during heat-waves
might facilitate elevated N2O eﬂuxes, and whether the increase in N2O (gas) diﬀusion with
temperature could potentially lead to a disproportional increase in N2O eﬄux, compared
to the expected increase caused by elevated denitriﬁcation rates alone.
In addition, the
production of N2O during nitriﬁcation is likely aﬀected by the increase in temperature. In
this case, N2O is not a metabolite but a byproduct or secondary compound and the eﬀects
of prolonged high temperatures on the production and release of this gas are unknown.

Materials and Methods
2.1
Site description and sampling
Sediment and water was collected from a shallow mud ﬂat in an inter-tidal zone in the estu-
ary of Rio Guadalete (36.6252◦N, −6.1331◦E), southern Spain, in February and May 2018.
The sampling site receives discharges from a waste-water treatment plant and seasonal runoﬀ
from surrounding agriculture ﬁelds. Sampling was performed during low tide, where the sed-
iment surface at sampling point was nearly or completely exposed to direct sunlight. The
surface water temperature was measured 14 ◦C and 19 ◦C in February and May respectively
and salinity was 3 and 1 o/oo. In May, heavy spring rain during previous months transported
sand from upstream that covered the sampling area with a 15-20 cm thick layer. The sec-
ond sampling was therefore performed slightly closer to the riverbank (about 5 to 10 m),
where no sand had settled. During each sampling, sediment cores (n=24) were collected by
hand and transferred back to lab where they were distributed between ﬁve 50 L temperature
controlled containers mounted with water pumps and covered with water (∼40 L) retrieved
from sampling site. Cores rested in darkness at in situ temperature for two days (weekend)
prior to temperature manipulation. Initial conditions were achieved from 4 cores (Monday)
and the other 20 cores were equally distributed between the ﬁve containers. To cover a po-
tential winter to heat wave temperature gradient, after the measurement of initial conditions,
containers were either cooled down or heated up (10◦C, 18◦C, 26◦C, 34◦C and 42◦C) con-
tinuously during the four following days (Tuesday to Friday), where exact temperature was
monitored daily. For simpliﬁcation, each experiment day will hereafter be denoted as ’day 0’
to ’day 4’, day 0 referring to Monday; before temperature modiﬁcation, and day 4 to Friday;
four days after the initiation of temperature treatments.

O2 and N2O sediment proﬁles
Of the four cores in each temperature-controlled container, one was dedicated to proﬁle O2
and N2O sediment distribution.
Each day, O2 (n=3) and N2O (n=2) microproﬁles were
measured in each core using Clark type microelectrodes (Revsbech 1989; Andersen et al.

calibrated using O2 concentrations in overlying water as 100 % O2 saturation and the signal
at anoxic depths as zero. The N2O sensor had a tip diameter of 60-80 µm and an oxygen
front guard of alkaline ascorbate to prevent signal interference from O2. The N2O sensor
was calibrated daily for each targeted temperature. O2 ﬂux into the sediment based on O2

Water sampling
Water samples were withdrawn from the core using a syringe mounted with a PVC tube.
Samples for dissolved N2O (n=1) and Dissolved Inorganic Carbon (DIC, n=2) were trans-
ferred to 60 mL glass vials and 6 mL exetainers (Labco, UK) respectively. Both samples were
ﬁxed with concentrated formalin to a ﬁnal concentration of 1-2 % and sealed immediately
without headspace. Samples were kept upside down at room temperature until analysis.
Samples for NO−
2 , NO−
3 and NH+
4 (n=2) were ﬁltered through a 0.7 µm glass ﬁber ﬁlter into
a polyethylene vial and stored at -18◦C.

Analytical
Dissolved N2O was determined by gas chromatography with an electron capture detector.
A 25 mL headspace was added to sample while ﬂushing He through septa. Headspace gas
(20 mL) was then witdrawn from sample while supplying high-density (KCl) liquid to water

gas, where DIC sample (100 µL) was acidiﬁed with 50 mM HCl (ﬂow = 0.8 mL/min).

4
Discussion
The objective of this project was to investigate the eﬀect of temperature variation on bio-
geochemical cycling in shallow sediments by simulating the temperature situation that may
arise in such environments during heat waves (T>35 ◦C), events occurring with an increasing
frequency in Andalusia, Spain (Figure 2). The eﬀects of warming on O2 and nutrient dynam-
ics, denitriﬁcation rates and N2O eﬄux were measured in sediments from a shallow marsh
receiving discharge from a waste-water treatment plant and seasonal runoﬀfrom surrounding
agriculture ﬁelds. The eﬀect of warming on O2 consumption followed an expected path for a
biological process (Figure 10), by increasing and subsequently decreasing with temperature,
where the distribution leaned towards higher temperatures with an optima around 35 ◦C.
Nutrient dynamics (Figure 5) (February and May). DIC consumption in the water column
(Figure 12; left) (May) suggested nitriﬁcation to be a dominating process in water and/or
surface sediments at temperatures above 18 ◦C in both months. Both denitriﬁcation rate and
N2O sediment eﬄux increased with warming to 26 and 34 ◦C (Figure 14 & 13), but no cor-
relation was observed between denitriﬁcation and N2O ﬂux, suggesting a complex regulation
of N2O production. Microbial activity generally decreased over time at temperatures above
34 ◦C, suggesting inhibition at the high temperatures reached during extreme heat waves.
4.1
Net metabolism
Temperature is one of multiple factors that regulates benthic O2 consumption rates (Tham-
drup et al. n.d.), where other important controls include O2 concentration in overlying water,
28
==PAGE:30==
infauna activity and the input of organic matter. In all incubations, total O2 uptake was ∼
1.5 times diﬀusive O2 uptake (Figure 10), with the diﬀerence most likely caused by infauna
activity (e.g. visible worms). OM content of ∼100 mg g−1 in surface sediment resembled the
values reported from nearby marshes and estuaries (90 and 110 mg g−1 (García-Robledo et al.
2008; Garcia-Robledo et al. 2016), but were higher than the 80 mg g−1 reported in a recent
study on temperature eﬀect on O2 consumption and denitriﬁcation rates in drainage pond
sediments, where the measured sediment O2 consumption rates also were 1.5-2 times lower
(same δT) than calculated here (Klein et al. 2017), supporting that a higher OM content
increases O2 demand.
Sediment O2 penetration depth mirrored the response in sediment O2 uptake to temper-
ature (Figure 11), responding to elevated respiration rates by decreasing with warming to 34
◦C followed by a increase again at 42 ◦C. 42 ◦C being close to the upper temperature limit
for typical mesophilic aerothrophs (Canﬁeld 2005).
The O2 consumption in sediments is
traditionally ∼1000 times higher than O2 consumption in water (Kruse 1993), which agrees
with current measurements. O2 consumption in water decreased with time (Figure 12) and,
judging from the visible decline in turbidity, was likely caused by the decrease in particulate
organic compounds in water with time. Overall, the temperature increase enhanced net O2
consumption, until a maximum rate at 35 ◦C, similar to previous studies (Thamdrup et al.
n.d.). Prolonged exposition to high temperatures (> 35 ◦C) resulted in a decrease in the net
consumption, probably caused by inhibitory eﬀects of temperatures exceeding the optimal
range of mesophylic microorganisms.
DIC production and consumption rates were roughly an order of magnitude higher than
O2 consumption and nitriﬁcation. Since being analyzed on an IRGA that measures the total
infrared absorption spectra, DIC may have been overestimated due to high concentrations
of other greenhouse gases such as CH4 and N2O, which unit wise correspond to 25 and 298
CO2 equivalents, but usually exist at way lower concentrations compared to CO2 (nM vs.
mM). The concentration of CH4 and N2O in the water was only obtained in February, where
N2O ranged from 0-190 nM and CH4 ranged between 0.1-50 µM. It may thus be that N2O
and CH4 contributed to the observed variation between, and high rates of, DIC consumption
and production in the water in May (Figure 12). Nevertheless, assuming for the interference

indicated a development of a net autotrophic system in the water based on nitriﬁcation.

4.3
Denitriﬁcation
Denitriﬁcation rates in sediments are typically between 10-500 µmol N m−2 h−1 (Seitzinger
2006; Piña-Ochoa et al. 2006; Rosales Villa et al. 2018; Veraart 2012) and shown to depend on
factors such as OM availability and NO−
3 load (Silvennoinen et al. 2008). Several studies have
looked at the eﬀect of temperature on denitriﬁcation rates in shallow aquatic environments,
for example, in near shore temperate sediments (0-42 ◦C) (Canion et al. 2014) and drainage
ponds (10-25 ◦C) (Veraart 2012; Klein et al. 2017), where Ea values ranged 104-179 kJ
mol−1 and Q10 were 2.5-11.6, in agreement with current estimates. Several other biochemical
reactions that preceed denitriﬁcation may be directly aﬀected by warming. For instance,
nitriﬁcation rates in overlying water (Figure 6) increased the concentration of NO−
3 and
consequently NO−
3 inﬂux to the sediment (Figure 8), where a narrowing of the O2 penetration
depth with warming (Figure 11) additionally decreased the diﬀusion distance for NO−
3 to the
denitriﬁcation zone.
The isotope pairing technique allows for a partitioning between the
denitriﬁcation that uses NO−

is linked to nitriﬁcation occurring in the sediment (Dn). However, the analysis of 15N2 may
have saturated the mass spectrometer making the partitioning into the two N-sources produce
unreliable values and Dw and Dn were therefore not calculated. However, judging from the
large inﬂux of NO−
3 and NO−

most likely coupled to the inﬂux of NO−

4.4
N2O production and emission
Estuaries are eﬀective nutrient recycling sites and often considered hot-spots of greenhouse
gas production (Bange 2006), where N2O gets formed as a byproduct of nitriﬁcation and
as an intermediate during denitriﬁcation. Water in all incubations except 42 ◦C was su-
persaturated with N2O compared to atmospheric equilibrium values, reaching 200-270 times
atmospheric equilibrium values in temperatures between 18 and 34 ◦C. Since the sediment
surface in each container was negligible compared to the water volume (85 cm−2 to ∼40 L),
the high concentration of N2O measured in the mixed water indicated that nitriﬁcation was
contributing to N2O production in the water. A study looking at the eﬀect of temperature
(13-20 ◦C) and NO−
3 concentration (20-300 µM) on N2O production in pond sediment re-
ported a signiﬁcant positive relationship between temperature and N2O production, however
only at NO−
3 levels above 130 µM (Stadmark et al. 2007), indicating substrate limitation of
the N2O producing process. No NO−
3 amendment was performed in this experiment and it
is thus possible that at low temperatures (10 and 18 ◦C), where nitriﬁcation is less active,
denitriﬁcation and the consequent N2O production was rather limited by NO−
3 availability,
than temperature. Of the NO−

fraction increasing up to 34 ◦C (Figure 15).

2 to N2O occurred at a faster rate than the
following step of N2O reduction to N2, creating and unbalance between the two processes and
allowing for N2O to diﬀuse out of the cell and accumulate in the sediment. A proportion of
the N2O entering the sediment was still consumed by other microbial community members,
decreasing the concentration towards the sediment-water interface (Figure 9). However, a
substantial part seemed to escape to overlying water, with the fraction of N2O to N2 emitted
from the sediment shifting from 2.5 % at 26 ◦C to 22 % at 34 ◦C. This might be explained
by a disproportional increase in gas diﬀusivity compared to the increase in enzymatic ac-
tivity with temperature, where the increase in diﬀusivity seemingly exceeds N2O reduction
rate in the interval between 26 and 34 ◦C. In addition, the respiratory enzymes involved in
each reduction step during denitriﬁcation: nitrite reductase (nirS/nirK) and N2O reduc-
tase (nosZ), might have responded diﬀerently to temperature, supporting unbalanced N2O
production and consumption rates and contributing to the ineﬃcient reduction of N2O at
temperatures above 26 ◦C. The increase in temperature during heat-waves appears not only
to alter rates of microbial processes, but also to modify balanced processes and reactions,
causing a disproportional production and release of greenhouse gases such as N2O.
