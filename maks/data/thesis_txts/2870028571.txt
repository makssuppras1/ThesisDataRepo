Abstract
This thesis investigates the optimization of transformer-based models for sewer level
predictions, and the enhancement of anomaly detection methods in Urban Drainage
Systems (UDS). The study addresses several key research questions, including the
application of a Transformer model for multi-step forecasting, the role of synthetic
anomaly generation in benchmarking anomaly detection techniques, and the develop-
ment of a multi-feature approach for detecting system faults. Additionally, a novel tar-
geted iterative refinement method is introduced to reduce false-positives in anomaly
detection.
The optimized Transformer model demonstrated superior performance in sewer level
forecasting, outperforming both weak and strong baselines, including the LSTM
model. The synthetic anomaly generation was shown to provide a robust framework
for evaluating anomaly detection algorithms, revealing critical insights into model
behaviour across different types of synthetic anomalies. The multi-feature anomaly
detection approach, which combined multi-horizon forecast residuals, the final hid-
den state of the Transformer model, and integrated gradients with the unsupervised
Isolation Forest algorithm, demonstrated superior generalization and achieved higher
detection performance compared to individual models. Furthermore, the targeted
iterative refinement method showed promise, with ongoing improvements in anomaly
detection; however, additional experimentation is required to fully evaluate its effec-
tiveness.
This research contributes to the field by providing an optimized transformer-based
model for UDS forecasting, a comprehensive anomaly detection framework, and valu-
able insights into the effectiveness of a multi-feature approach for improving anomaly
detection performance in complex systems. The findings pave the way for future ad-
vancements in anomaly detection in complex systems, with potential applications in
other time-series forecasting domains.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Context
The primary purpose of UDS is to manage and control the movement of water in
urban areas, particularly stormwater and wastewater. These systems are typically
large and complex, serving multiple functions, such as [1]:
• Maintain public health and prevent contamination by carrying away wastewater
to treatment facilities.
• Prevent flooding during heavy rain events by collecting rainwater and directing
it away from the streets.
• Protect the environment from untreated stormwater and wastewater entering
natural water bodies.
Hutton et al. (2007) [2] also showed that the benefits of investments in urban drainage
systems far outweigh the costs in all developing regions - indicating that it is a highly
cost-effective investment. This was primarily due to time savings, easy access to wa-
ter, reduced health care and preventable deaths.
However, as urbanization continues to rise [3, 4], environmental regulations tighten
[5], public interest grows [6], and climate change brings even more unpredictable
weather patterns [7, 8], these systems are experiencing an increased stress and a higher
demand for management. To maintain system health and control while achieving the
objectives of the UDS, digitalization and hydroinformatics plays a crucial role [9, 10,
11].
1.2
Problem Statement
To accommodate the complexity of UDS, robust and reliable monitoring is essential.
This is facilitated through a network of sensors that are strategically placed around
different sewage structures [12]. However, under harsh environments and extreme
conditions, the data collected by these sensors is susceptible to various types of faults,
including: 1) process faults such as blockage, corrosion and bursts, and 2) technological
faults such as sensor drift, hardware malfunctions, miscalibration and environmental

interferences (e.g., debris or bio-fouling) [12, 13, 14, 15]. Errors in sensor data can
greatly affect the reliability of analytical models, operational decisions, and predictive
maintenance strategies.
In this context, anomaly detection plays a crucial role in maintaining the operational
eﬀiciency and reliability of Urban Water Networks (UWN), which include wastewa-
ter treatment facilities, stormwater management systems, and distribution networks.
These systems often face unexpected deviations or faults that can lead to serious
consequences. By enabling effective and timely detection, it is possible to minimizes
the risk of system failures, costly repairs, environmental damage, as well as improve
policy assessments, flood forecasts, resource allocation and labor-intensive Quality
Assurance and Control (QAQC) [16, 17, 18, 19].
Addressing these data errors is crucial for ensuring the reliability and usability of
sensor data. Traditional methods for detecting and correcting errors, which remain
practical and relevant, often depend on manual inspection, threshold-based rules, or
statistical techniques and models [12, 20]. While these approaches can be somewhat
effective, they tend to be labour-intensive, time-consuming, and not well-equipped
to manage the volume, variety, and speed of data generated in modern UDS. Re-
cent advancements in data science and Artificial Intelligence (AI) present promising
alternatives - and are shown to outperform traditional methods. In particular, unsu-
pervised methods have the potential to automatically detect anomalies and correct
errors without requiring extensive manual labelling or prior knowledge of system be-
haviour [21, 22, 23, 20, 13].
Developing such methods requires careful consideration of multiple factors, including:
1. Selecting appropriate models that can understand the system dynamics and
behaviours.
2. Utilizing effective anomaly detection mechanisms to identify and address un-
wanted faults.
3. Crafting robust evaluation frameworks to assess real-world applicability.
The subjects above will form the basis of the literature review.
1.3
Literature Review
Prediction models and surrogate modelling are particularly well-suited to address the
challenges of fault management in UWN [24, 25]. As emphasized by Liu et al. (2023)
[13], intelligent fault detection involves a three-step process:
1. Detection of faults: identifying faults as soon as they occur.
2. Diagnosis: determining the underlying causes of the detected faults.

3. Prognosis: estimating the future evolution of the fault to predict its impact and

1.3.1
Predictive Modelling of UWN and Beyond
Simulation and knowledge-based predictive models are highly specialized and time-
consuming, which makes it challenging to manage overly complex systems and imple-
ment adaptive adjustments. Similarly, statistical methods and traditional machine
learning models have limitations when it comes to representing complex systems and
capturing long-term trends. These are some of the motivations for turning towards
deep models, which offer several advantages in handling complexity and generaliza-
tion, although they typically require large datasets. [26, 13, 21, 23, 20].
In a review by Fu et al. (2022) [21], the authors provide a comprehensive summary
of the latest developments within water systems. The review explores various mod-
els, including Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN), Long Short-Term Memory net-
works (LSTM), Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), and Graph Neural Networks
(GNN), autoencoders, among others. The review highlights that LSTMs are favoured
for forecasting water demand, quality, flow, and levels due to their ability to cap-
ture long-term dependencies. However, the authors also note that GNNs outperform
LSTMs and autoencoders in detecting simulated attack scenarios, as GNNs can bet-
ter leverage the inherent interdependencies in the data. Finally, they predict that a
combination of CNNs and LSTMs will drive future research in the field.
In another review by Garzón et al. (2022) [22] on surrogate modelling of UWN, the
authors suggest that GNNs are superior for this task due to their ability to handle
graph data structures.
Lastly, a more recent review by Boughandjioua et al. (2024) [23] highlights that the
LSTM architecture is the current State of the Art (SOTA) approach for predicting
water levels, discharge levels, and flood forecasting.
More recent papers include a GNN implementations by Li et al. (2024) [27] on
water quality predictions in Water Distribution Networks (WDN) achieving accurate
predictions across the entire system. However, they do not benchmark the results.
Li et al. (2024) [28] applied a combined CNN–Attention–LSTM model to water-level
predictions and demonstrated that it outperforms all its counterparts, highlighting
the significant role of the attention mechanisms in enhancing prediction accuracy. In
a related study, Chiaburu et al. (2024) [29] utilized SOTA interpretable time series
models, specifically Neural Hierarchical Interpolation for Time Series (N-HiTS) and
TFT, to predict wastewater levels in rain tanks. However, evaluation is limited, as

methodologies. The survey begins by discussing traditional statistical models, such

However, with advancements in DL, models like the LSTMs emerged as top per-
formers. LSTMs excel in capturing long-term dependencies within time series data,

highlights the impact of the development of Transformer models. Transformers have
largely replaced LSTMs in many applications due to their superior ability to han-
dle both long-range dependencies, but also dependencies between the inputs. Unlike
LSTMs, which process data sequentially, Transformers leverage parallelized compu-

has been a steady progress of fusion between Transformers and e.g.
GNNs for a
spatio-temporal approach, and also foundation models to support broad applications
and transfer learning between areas.
Finally, the emerging architectures that are challenging the Transformers, these in-

et al. (2024) [35].

needed:
• Utilization of Transformers: While traditional DL architectures such as
LSTMs and CNNs are commonly used in water system forecasting, the broader
AI field has increasingly shifted focus toward the Transformer architecture. This
shift is largely driven by its effective global attention mechanism, which is par-

1.3.2
Anomaly Detection in UWN and Beyond
In a review by Liu et al. (2023) [13] on literature in Wastewater Treatment Plants
(WWTF), the authors note that research in fault management is still in its early
stages, with only 255 relevant articles identified over the two recent decades. Yet,
with an increase in recent years [36]. The detection of faults is the primary focus
in the 255 studies, as it typically precedes the more complex tasks of diagnosis and
prognosis. They also note that the most commonly used detection techniques include
Principal Component Analysis (PCA), NN and a combination of such methods - all
of which are largely dependent on traditional anomaly detection methods which are
supervised and need labelled datasets.
Some examples of such approaches include Rodriguez-Perez et al. (2020) [25], who
evaluated both semi-supervised and supervised methods using labelled water quality
data, and applied residual thresholding on neural network predictions for detection.
And Bellamoli et al. (2023) [37] also used labelled data from treatment plants and
found that tree-based ensemble models, such as XGBoost, yielded the best results.
Though there have been promising efforts in labelling anomalous data more effec-
tively by e.g. using active learning on environmental data [38], the process is cum-
bersome and often hindrance in development [13, 39]. To address this challenge of
limited labelled datasets in UWN, there have been efforts in exploring unsupervised
approaches.
Russo et al. (2021) [40] compared supervised and unsupervised methods for anomaly
detection in environmental time series datasets.
They concluded that supervised
methods performed better in predicting labelled anomalies. Additionally, they found
no significant performance difference among unsupervised methods like autoencoders,
Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM), Isolation Forest, and Local Outlier Factor (LOF).
They also observed that unsupervised methods fall short when dealing with complex
datasets, particularly where anomalies are contextual, ill-defined, or change over time.
In such cases, labelling becomes necessary, as also highlighted by Russo et al. (2020)
[38].
Teh et al. (2021) [39] proposed an unsupervised approach on environmental datasets,
where anomalous periods were defined heuristically for evaluation.
Their method

the reconstruction error.
Nicholaus et al. (2021) [41] detect anomalies from water levels in water tanks, where

They used a deep au-

Qiu et al. (2024) [43] propose an unsupervised method to detect anomalies in sewer

that integrates one-class SVM, isolation forest, and LOF.
In the broader field of AI, Schmidl et al. (2022) [44] contradict this claim of super-
vised methods being superior to unsupervised [40, 38] by providing a comprehensive
evaluation of 71 anomaly detection algorithms on 1.548 different time series datasets.
They use the Area Under the Curve (AUC) as a metric for comparison, due to its
threshold independence, robustness to class imbalance, and wide usage in literature.
Their results show that there is no one-size-fits-all detection algorithm for the differ-
ent anomalies. However, they suggest that simple tree-based models are effective for
extremum anomalies, while forecasting models (among others) are more suitable for
the more challenging anomalies such as trend and pattern-shift. They conclude by
emphasizing the need for further research on hybrid or holistic approaches, they are
not well represented in their current studies.
In a more recent survey, Zamanzadeh Darban et al. (2024) [20] provide an overview
of 48 popular open-source time series datasets for anomaly detection - of which three
are related to UWN. These include the WADI, SWaT and GECCO IoT. They argue
that there is a lack of unsupervised methods and also suggest more research into deep
learning approaches for anomaly detection in time series data. They believe that DL
approaches could help address challenges such as data non-stationarity, high dimen-
sionality in multivariate data, and the reduction of false positives.
In both surveys, most of the DL methods utilized thresholding techniques on anomaly
scores, such as the residuals.
The following points highlight areas where further investigation and development is
needed:
• Increased Feature Utilization: Most existing methods primarily focus on
using the raw data, reconstruction errors or residuals to detect anomalies. How-
ever, it is possible to leverage the rich information embedded within deep learn-
ing models, such as the hidden states, attention mechanisms or explainability

features. Since these models process a wide range of inputs to generate outputs,
the intermediate information — such as the processed representations of multi-
variate time series — can be extracted and utilized for further analysis. This
untapped potential, remains largely unexplored in literature.
• Using Hybrid Approaches: To detect anomalies, most approaches either
rely on Machine Learning (ML) algorithms or DL models. Each approach has
its trade-off: ML models excel at capturing extremum and irregularities, while
DL models are more effective for long-term multivariate anomalies [44]. But, by
combining both methodologies, such as using the residuals from the predictive
model as input to the unsupervised anomaly detection algorithm, it is possible
to leverage the strengths of both approaches. This could help in generalizing
across the different types of anomalies.
• Increased Standardization: Most methods commonly report F1, recall, and
precision scores for anomaly detection. However, these metrics are threshold-
dependent and may not fully capture the performance of detection algorithms,
as a poor threshold could undermine results. A more robust alternative is the
AUC score, which is threshold-independent, and also more widely used in other
areas of research. Additionally, anomalies typically occur over intervals rather
than at individual points.
Therefore, a more comprehensive analysis of the
detection behaviour, feature changes and threshold settings could strengthen
the results.
• Broader Evaluation: Most approaches identified in the literature review ei-
ther rely on manually labelled datasets, heuristic-based labels, or visual inspec-
tions. Few utilize synthetic anomalies in real datasets to validate the detection
performance. Synthetic anomalies would also address the limited availability of
UWN datasets and provide more control.
• Reduction of False Positives: While deep learning methods show promise
in reducing false positives [20], the data in UWNs is heavily biased toward dry
weather conditions, with limited representation of wet and extreme conditions
where data fluctuations are more pronounced. These fluctuations often increase
the likelihood of false positives, even when the data is not anomalous. Notably,
no method in the literature specifically addresses false positive reduction, which
is crucial for preventing error accumulation and further complicating QAQC.
1.4
Research Questions
This section presents the specific research questions that form the foundation of the
study. Inspiration is drawn from the identified gaps in literature review.
==PAGE:22==
8
1 Introduction
First, the thesis will focuses on developing a Transformer model optimized for sewer
level predictions, which is benchmarked against both weak and strong baselines.
Second, it establishes a synthetic anomaly generation framework to benchmark and
assess the performance of anomaly detection methods in a controlled environment.
Third, in order to detect these anomalies, a Multi-Feature Anomaly Detection ap-
proach is implemented and tested. It combines the rich information contained within
the Transformer model with Isolation Forest algorithm, and investigates the effect of
various anomalous features, and their contributions to detecting specific faults. These
features include the target value, multi-horizon forecast residuals, final hidden state
of the transformer model and integrated gradients.
Last, a novel approach named targeted iterative refinement is implemented and tested
in order to further enhance anomaly detection and potentially reduce the amount of
false positives. This method involves iteratively dividing the data into subsets based
on predicted anomalous and non-anomalous instances, and then fine-tuning a model
on each subset. This creates multiple subsets representing expected and unexpected
system behaviours, allowing each fine-tuned model to focus on the nuances of its
specific subset. The anticipated trade-off is a reduction in false positives, as each
fine-tuned model will become better at distinguishing: 1) anomalous data from rare
but valid data, and 2) subtle anomalies from normal behaviours.
Research Questions
On Optimal Modelling:
RQ1 How can transformer-based models be optimized for sewer level predic-
tions?
On Robust Evaluation Frameworks:
RQ2 What role does synthetic anomaly generation play in benchmarking
anomaly detection?
On Effective Anomaly Detection:
RQ3 How can a multi-feature approach improve the identification and gener-
alization of systematic faults?
RQ4 Can targeted iterative refinement improve anomaly detection perfor-
mance and reduce the amount of false positives?

Structure of the Thesis
This thesis is structured into six main chapters, each addressing a critical aspect of
the research.

refinement, and finishes with a discussion of the overall results.

discusses its limitations, and suggests directions for future work.

2.1
Models
The forecast models include the LSTM and the Transformer model, and for anomaly

2.1.1
The LSTM Model
RNNs are powerful models for sequential data, but they suffer from vanishing and
exploding gradient while learning long-term dependencies. LSTM is an architecture
first proposed by Hochreiter et al. (1997) [45], aimed at eliminating the drawbacks of
RNNs by introducing gating mechanisms that control the flow of information inside
the network.
2.1.1.1
LSTM Architecture
As depicted in Figure 2.1, the LSTM cell has a both a cell state ct and a hidden state
ht that carry information through the network and control the long- and short-term
dependencies respectively - hence the name.

Figure 2.1: Overview of the LSTM cell operations [46].
This long-term memory ct is controlled via three different kinds of gates: the forget
gate ft, the input gate it, and the output gate ot. Every gate is part of a sigmoid
activation function; hence their outputs lie between [0, 1] and can be thought of as the
degree of allowance or forgetfulness with regards to the information passing through.
The operations in an LSTM cell at time step t are described as follows [46, 45]:
1. Forget Gate: The forget gate decides what part of the previous cell state
information ct−1 should be retained or discarded, helping the model focus on
relevant information.
2. Input Gate and Candidate Cell State: Control what new information is
added to the cell state. A candidate cell state ˜ct is computed based on the
current input and previous hidden state, representing potential new memory
content.
3. Cell State Update: The updated cell state ct is obtained by combining the
scaled previous cell state with the modulated candidate cell state.
This ad-
ditive update helps maintain long-term dependencies by preventing vanishing
gradients during back-propagation.

4. Output Gate and Hidden State: Regulates the final output of the LSTM
cell by filtering the updated cell state. The resulting hidden state ht serves as
both the output for the current time step and the input to the next LSTM cell.
In all, the LSTM model architecture is robust to capture both long- and short-term
dependencies in the sequential data, making well-suited for time series prediction.
However, at the expense of computational cost, both in terms of training and memory
due to its many matrix multiplications.
2.1.2
The Transformers Model
Introduced by Vaswani et al. (2017) [30], the Transformer model revolutionized se-
quence modelling by replacing recurrence with self-attention. Though it was originally
developed for Natural Language Processing (NLP), its flexible architecture made it
applicable in many other areas, including time series analysis, which requires captur-
ing dependencies over both short and long horizons.
Figure 2.2: Overview of the self-attention and multi-head attention operations [30].
The components of the Transformer architecture mainly consist of: scaled dot-product
attention (also called self-attention), a multi-head attention mechanism and position-
wise feed-forward networks. The self-attention mechanism enables the model to weigh
the importance of different tokens within an input sequence, capturing both local and
long-range dependencies eﬀiciently. Multi-head attention generalizes this by enabling
the model to simultaneously attend to many representation subspaces, improving its
ability to capture diverse aspects of the input. Position-wise feed-forward networks
enhance the expressiveness of the model by applying the same non-linear transforma-
tion independently to each tokens representation.

2.1.2.1
Self-Attention Mechanism
The core of the Transformer architecture is the self-attention mechanism, also known
as the scaled dot-product attention (fig. 2.2). This mechanism allows each element
in the input sequence to dynamically assess and weigh the relevance of every other
element when constructing its own representation — also known as context.
Concretely, each element in the sequence computes three vectors: the query, key,
and value. These are obtained by multiplying the input element by three learned
weight matrices, WQ, WK, and WV , respectively, which are shared across the entire
sequence:
Q = XW Q,
K = XW K,
V = XW V
where:
• X ∈Rn×dmodel is the input sequence (e.g. sequence length of size n and then
dmodel input variables or embedded dimensions).
• W Q, W K, W V ∈Rdmodel×dk are learnable weight matrices that project X into
query, key, and value representations.
The attention scores are then computed using scaled dot-product attention, defined
as:

To understand this equation intuitively (see fig. 2.3), the input can be thought of as
either a sequence of words, where each word has its own embedding. The query can
be thought of as a question, while the keys represent possible answers. By computing
the dot product between a given query vector Qj (representing a single element in
the input sequence), and all key vectors in that sequence, the model determines a
similarity score for each pair. These scores are then passed through the softmax
function, normalizing them into a probability distribution. This distribution repre-
sents the weights assigned to each value vector of that sequence, which are summed
together and finally added to the original embedding of the considered element. This
means that the context of the whole sequence informs and potentially modifies the
individual element considered - forming an updated contextual representation - and
this updated representation is what is called the hidden state.
Comparing this intuition to time series data, the words would represent each time
step, while the embeddings would consist of the values of the variables at that time,
such as the sensor value and precipitation.

This global perspective allows the model to effectively capture both short-range and
long-range dependencies, making it particularly well-suited for tasks such as time
series analysis and NLP, where interactions and correlations between distant elements
are crucial.
2.1.2.2
Multi-head Attention
To capture various relationships and features, the Transformer uses multi-head atten-
tion (fig. 2.2). Instead of computing a single attention representation as described

above (one head), the model projects queries, keys, and values into multiple subspaces

where each head is computed with the previously mentioned Attention formula.
This allows the model to attend to information jointly from various representation
subspaces, essentially giving the model the capacity to learn the many distinct ways
that context changes meaning [30].
2.1.2.3
Positional Encoding
Unlike recurrent architectures, the Transformer does not inherently encode the order
of the input sequence elements, as it processes the entire sequence in parallel using
matrix multiplications. While eﬀicient, this causes the model to have no sense of the
order of elements within the sequence - which is crucial for time series.
To overcome this limitation, positional encodings can be added to the input embed-
dings to inject information about either the relative or absolute position of elements
in the sequence. These encodings are often represented by sine and cosine functions
that are concatenated with the input embeddings.
2.1.2.4
Architecture and Applications
The Transformer model typically consists of a stack of layers, each layer with a multi-
head self-attention mechanism and a position-wise feed-forward network. Residual
connections and layer normalization are applied around each sub-layer to enable train-
ing of deep architectures.
Although the original Transformer was designed in an
encoder-decoder configuration, most applications, including time series forecasting,
anomaly detection, and classification, adopt either encoder-only or decoder-only vari-
ants.
2.1.3
The Isolation Forest Algorithm
Isolation Forest is an unsupervised anomaly detection algorithm developed by Liu
et al. (2008) [48] that can be applied directly on numerical dataset with multiple
features. Unlike traditional methods that model normal instances and then detect
deviations, Isolation Forest operates on the notion that anomalies are few and dif-
ferent, and therefore easier to isolate. They implement this by creating an ensemble
of isolation trees, which recursively partition the data space using randomly chosen
features and cut values (see fig 2.4).
==PAGE:31==
2.1 Models
17
Figure 2.4: Principles used to identifies anomalies by Isolation Forest [49].
Each isolation trees keeps partitioning the space this way until it either reaches a
single data point or attains some predefined depth.
These partitions are selected
based on randomly chosen features and split values, which helps in isolating the data
points more effectively. An important observation is that that normal points, which
lie in the dense regions of the feature space, take longer to get segregated, meaning
their average distance from the root to the terminating node is generally longer.
Anomalous points, on the other hand, are usually sparse and unique, are segregated
with fewer splits, resulting in shorter path lengths. These path lengths are used to
derive an anomaly score: if a point’s average path length across all trees is short
relative to an expected value, it is more likely to be an anomaly. Mathematically, the
score s(x, n) for a point x is computed as:
s(x, n) = 2−E(h(x))
c(n)
(2.1)
where:
• The path length h(x), is the total number of edges you traverse from the root
node to the leaf node for a given data point x on a randomly generated tree.
• E(h(x)) is the average path length across an ensemble of randomly generated
trees.
• c(n) represents a baseline, and is the average path length of unsuccessful searches
in a Binary Search Tree.
Additionally, c(n) is derived from the properties of binary trees and involves harmonic
numbers:
c(n) ≈2H(n −1) −2(n −1)

The s(x, n) formula returns a value between 0 and 1, where anomalies stand out with
scores near 1, and normal points cluster towards 0. Classification can be performed
by thresholding the score or estimating the percentage of contamination.
Overall, Isolation Forest is highly eﬀicient and scalable to high-dimensional data.
Since it avoids computing pairwise distance or densities, is has a linear time complex-
ity, making it highly applicable.

2.2.1
The Integrated Gradients Method
Interpreting the predictions of complex models, such as deep neural networks, re-
mains one of the foremost challenges in modern machine learning. Among various
techniques developed for explainability, Integrated Gradients has emerged as one of
the most conceptually grounded, powerful techniques for attributing a model’s predic-
tion to its input features. Introduced by Sundararajan et al. (2017) [50], Integrated
Gradients offers an axiomatic framework that is particularly appealing for its theo-
retical guarantees and practical applicability.
2.2.1.1

Integrated Gradients is a gradient-based attribution method. It captures the central
idea of measuring the contribution of each input feature by aggregating the gradients
of the model output along a continuous path from a baseline input representing an
absence of signal to the actual input (see fig. 2.5).

2.2.1.2
Theoretical Foundations
Integrated Gradients relies on two important axioms that help make its attributions
meaningful and consistent:
• Sensitivity: For any feature where the difference between the input and the
baseline input is non-zero, and changing that causes a non-zero difference in the
output of F, the attribution to that feature should be non-zero. Another way
to express this: changing the input can change the prediction, which, in turn
should be captured in the integrated gradient.
• Completeness (or Summation-to-Difference): The sum of the integrated
gradients over all features is equal to the difference between the output at the
actual input and the output at the baseline:

IGi(x) = F(x) −F(x′).
This property guarantees that the attributions completely explain the difference
in predictions between the baseline and the real input, providing a type of
conservation of relevance.
The theoretical properties behind these axioms also form practical intuitions for in-
terpretations on model prediction. By enforcing completeness, one can assert that no
relevant information is lost in the explanation process.
2.2.1.3
Summary
In summary, Integrated Gradients shows you how much each input feature contributed
to the model’s prediction, by measuring the effect of gradually changing the input
from a baseline to its actual value. It highlights which features were most influential
in the model’s decision.

Most binary classification metrics depend on a fixed decision threshold, which requires
careful tuning but is often overlooked. The Receiver Operating Characteristic Area
Under the Curve (ROC AUC) metric mitigates this issue by evaluating the perfor-
mance of a binary classifier across all possible classification thresholds - making it
threshold independent.
Specifically, the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) illustrates the trade-off be-
tween the True Positive Rate (TPR) and the False Positive Rate (FPR) at varying
decision thresholds (see fig. 2.8). The TPR, also known as recall or sensitivity, mea-
sures the proportion of actual positives correctly identified by the model, whereas the
FPR represents the proportion of actual negatives incorrectly classified as positives.
An ideal model balances these two metrics, achieving a high TPR while maintaining
a low FPR.

The AUC, as the name suggest, represents the area under the ROC curve, effec-
tively providing a measure of performance irrespective of thresholding. It quantifies
a model’s ability to distinguish between the two classes, where an AUC of 1 represents
a perfect classifier, and an AUC of 0.5 suggests performance no better than random

Data Preparation
In this section, we outline the steps for preparing the data before model training. The
data preparation process involves loading, cleaning, splitting, scaling, and formatting
the data into suitable structures for time series modelling.
The Bellinge dataset includes readings from multiple sensors. However, two specific
sensors are initially considered:
- Target variable: Level sensor G71F04R_Level2.
- Exogenous Variable: Precipitation data from rain gauge 5425.
The sensor G71F04R_Level2 is selected as the target sensor for modelling due to its
simplicity compared to other types of sensors, such as flow or power sensors. Addi-
tionally, its strategic placement within the sewage system, relative to the available
data creates unique opportunities. Specifically, it is situated between two other level
sensors: G71F05R_LevelInlet upstream and G71F06R_LevelInlet downstream (see
Figure 3.2 in 3). This positioning creates unique opportunities for relational modelling
when optimizing the transformer in the later stages.
The rain gauge sensor 5425 is selected mainly because of its proximity to the tar-
get sensor G71F04R_Level2. Additionally, cross-correlation analysis shows that 5427
has a negative lag (Figure 3.10 in 3), meaning that 5425 tends to measure rainfall
earlier. This is important for accurate modeling, as it is essential for rainfall to be

- Data Loading: The time range is restricted to the latest start time and the

- Data Splitting: The data is split chronologically into three sets for training, val-

The proportions are 70% for training, 15% for validation and 15% for testing.

- Target: 1-minute ahead forecast.

minutes, it suggest that observations within a time window of larger than 82 minutes

120 minutes ensures the model captures both immediate and delayed dependencies

longer forecasts can be obtained with auto-regression by iterating predictions over
multiple time steps.

minutes without missing values (NaNs) are considered. This requirement ensures that
both the input sequence (120 minutes) and the target value (next-minute prediction)
are fully observed, minimizing data gaps that could compromise model performance.
However, this restriction may result in data loss.

4.1.2.2
Optimization Strategy
There are numerous hyperparameters, design choices, and other factors to consider
when optimizing a transformer model on sewage data.
Since testing all possible
combinations of these parameters is computationally infeasible, the process is bro-
ken down into manageable stages. Therefore, a stage-wise approach is implemented
to eﬀiciently identify the best combinations of parameters, ensuring a thorough yet
eﬀicient exploration of the optimization space. The order of the stages is carefully
structured to maintain coherence and maximize the effectiveness of the optimization
process.
Model Complexity: The first stage focuses on identifying appropriate model complex-
ities, which is crucial for the model to capture all the system dynamics. The hidden
size was evaluated with values of 32, 64, and 128. Additionally, various configurations
for the transformer layer were explored by adjusting the number of attention heads
and layers. The following sets of parameters for (num_heads, num_layers) were
considered for each hidden size: (1, 1), (1, 2), (2, 2), (2, 4), and (4, 2).
Input Features: With the complexity in order, this steps aims to identify additional
data variables that the model could benefit from. Two engineered variables were eval-
uated as inputs: time_of_day, and day_of_week. Both decomposed into their sine
and cosine components. Additionally, two sensors were considered: the upstream level
sensor G71F05R_LevelInlet and the downstream level sensor G71F06R_LevelInlet
relative to the target sensor. These variables underwent the same processing steps.
Training Parameters: This step incorporates multiple parameters that play a crucial
role in improving convergence. Different batch sizes were evaluated, specifically 32
and 128. Dropout in the transformer encoding layers were tested with values of 0.1
and 0.3. A learning rate scheduler using a cosine annealing strategy was employed,
with a maximum epoch of 16 for one cycle, and the following maximum learning rates:
0.001, 0.0005 and 0.0001.
Modelling Choices: The modelling choices explore ways to enrich the data or enhance
comprehension. The experiments include: 1) applying a log transformation to the
input before modelling, 2) explicitly incorporating positional encoding into the em-
bedded sequence, 3) aggregating all hidden states by computing their mean, rather
than selecting only the last hidden state, and 4) predicting the change in from the
previous value, i.e. the difference, instead of predicting the target value directly.

4.3.2
Anomaly Detection Model
In order to detect whether data is anomalous or not, the Isolation Forest algo-
rithm is selected. Pilot experiments also proved that other algorithms, like the LOF
and 1-class-SVM, needed significantly more time to train.
A key feature of the Isolation Forest algorithm is its ability to detect anomalies
eﬀiciently, even in large and high-dimensional data, making it particularly scalable for
diverse applications. It is also unsupervised, meaning it does not need labels and can
therefore be applied directly on the dataset. For simplicity, the algorithm is fitted on
the extracted features in the training set, using the default hyperparameters without
modification 1. The algorithm outputs both the decision function values and a binary
classification via automatic thresholding. In this context, the decision function is
multiplied by −1 so that high scores indicate anomalies (and will be referenced as the
anomaly score later on).
Anomalies are detected for each individual feature to evaluate their individual perfor-
mance and relevance. Additionally, the features are combined to assess whether their
joint application improves overall performance and enhances the ability to generalize
across the different anomalies.
Finally, the ROC AUC score, Timing metrics and other measured are used to assess
the detection performance.

4.4.1
Methodology
The assumption behind the targeted iterative refinement approach, is that it en-
hances anomaly detection by progressively refining the transformer model on each
predicted subset: i.e. subsets are targeted based on the predicted anomalous and
non-anomalous, resulting in two splits to fine-tune on. With repeated cycles of data
splitting, the fine-tuned model could potentially start to understand the difference
between anomalies and normal system behaviour. Specifically, one could assume that:
• The models that are continuously fine-tuned on subsets predicted as non-anomalous,
the model might get better at differentiating between normal data, and subtle
that could typically go unnoticed.
• The models that are continuously fine-tuned on subsets predicted as anomalous,
the model would start to differentiate between true anomalies and extreme but
valid event, as the extreme event still follow the system dynamics - of which the
anomalies do not.
However, while this process is computationally expensive, it is mostly designed for
an offline setting of QAQC. And if it leads to significant improvements in detection
accuracy, its application remains highly relevant. The approach can be described as
follows:
==PAGE:71==
4.4 Targeted Iterative Refinement
57
1. Initial Anomaly Detection: Apply the base anomaly detection method to the
polluted dataset. This involves:
- Fitting the transformer model.
- Extracting relevant features.
- Applying the unsupervised detection model to classify anomalous data.
2. Dataset Splitting: Divide the dataset into two subsets based on detection results
(anomalous and non-anomalous), creating separate training, validation, and test
sets for each.
3. Target Iterative Refinement: This approach is similar to the initial anomaly
detection:
- Instead of training a new transformer model, fine-tune the existing one on
each created subset.
- Extract updated features and apply the detection model to generate two
new subsets from each existing subset.
- Run the Targeted Iterative Refinement on each subset with their respective
fine-tuned model.
4. Global Comparison: To evaluate improvements over the initial iteration, then
for each iteration, combine the extracted features across its subsets, then apply
a new detection model to assess the refined results.
5. Stopping Criteria: Continue iterations until a predefined limit is reached, or
when subsets become too small to provide meaningful results.
The performance across the iterations follow the same evaluation approach as the
Multi-Feature Anomaly Detection.

On the other hand, the LSTM model, despite being optimized for complexity, re-

that different.
Despite the faster convergence in terms of epochs, the total training time remains

contribute to its faster convergence. This also means higher memory requirements

Detection of Synthetic Anomalies
The objective of this section is to investigate and compare the impact of different
input features on the detection of synthetic anomalies, specifically addressing RQ3
(sec. 1.4). The main steps can be summarized as follows:
1. The original time series data from the G71F04R_Level2 sensor will be polluted
with synthetic anomalies (5.23%) as described in the Synthetic Anomaly Frame-
work section.
2. A transformer model will be trained on this modified dataset, utilizing the opti-

section.
3. At each time step, multiple features will be extracted from both the data and the
model, including the target value, the model’s final hidden state, multi-horizon
forecast residuals, and features derived from the integrated gradients.

Forest model, both individually and in combination, to classify each time step
as anomalous or not.
5. The resulting anomaly detection models will be evaluated to assess their per-
formance, with their results benchmarked against a baseline Sigma-3 model,
which applies thresholding based on three standard deviations of the 1-step-
ahead residuals.
For the dimensionality of the extracted features, please refer to the Methods section.
In general, the features are either single values or vectors.

5.3.4
Qualitative Analysis of the Multi-Feature Model
Note: Similar to the arguments provided in the previous section, the following re-
sults focus solely on the test set.
The qualitative analysis of the detection model is divided into three sections: 1) the
most accurately detected synthetic anomaly, 2) the least accurately detected syn-
thetic anomaly, and 3) the most certain detection interval that contains no synthetic
anomaly.
Technically, the most accurate detection is determined as follows: For each injected
anomaly that has at least one predicted true-positive, a coverage score is computed
alongside two distance measures. The distance measures are somewhat similar to
that of the timing scores, only it measures when the anomalous score drops below
the threshold before the start and after the end for an anomalous period. This helps
avoid the influence of high anomalous scores that may arise from external factors,
such as a rainfall events, potentially obscuring the true detection performance. The
detections are then ranked by first sorting them based on the highest coverage values,
followed by the combined distance measures.
This process is performed for each type of synthetic anomaly, where the overall most
accurate detection is determined by selecting the anomaly with the highest average
anomalous score across the types.
A similar approach is used to determine the least accurate detection, where the
anomaly with the lowest average anomalous score across the types is selected.

Overall, the qualitative analysis indicated that the model tends to classify rain events
as anomalous, which is undesirable. It is crucial for operations to function effectively
during rain events, and if these events are incorrectly classified as anomalies, it com-

whether a detected anomaly needs to be addressed, or is simply a result of rainfall.

that the occurrence of a drift anomaly coinciding with a rain event. This could lead
to a compounded increase in the anomaly score. Additionally, the PCA visualization
of the input features reveals that the anomaly trajectory shows notable jumps toward
a region where many high anomaly score data points are concentrated. This region
may correspond to areas where the target sensor has high values.
On the other hand, the least accurate detection only identified the end of the Frozen
anomaly as anomalous. As mentioned earlier, Frozen anomalies typically exhibited
a lower AUC score, and could in some cases resemble dry weather conditions, which
could explain the detection challenge. The multi-horizon forecast residuals also re-
mained relatively low during this period.
Finally, the most certain non-synthetic anomaly also indicated a heavy rainfall event,
with almost the entire time window being detected as anomalous. The multi-horizon
residual plot further confirmed this, as the values were consistently high, as the model
might struggle to make accurate predictions in turbulent environments.
Since the Multi-Feature model uses both complex features and residuals as input,
it would be interesting to investigate whether the behaviour of detecting rain event
as anomalous is handled differently by other models, such as the Final Hidden and
Integrated Gradients models. Regarding Integrated Gradients, one might expect that
the model would still focus on the same input data to make its predictions. If that’s
the case, the rain event would likely be treated similar to normal events, though this
requires further exploration.

the subset that was consistently classified as anomalous continued to contained high
proportions of the synthetic anomalies. However, on the training set, the proportions
of synthetic anomalies appears more balanced - yet the predicted anomalous propor-

the data, it was less susceptible to anomalous patterns. It would be interesting the
see what anomalies reside in each subset.
Figures 5.40 and 5.41 present the AUC performance of each iterative model. On the
test set, iteration 1 showed slight improvements over iteration 0, but further itera-
tions do not yield significant gains. On the training set, the final iteration achieves
the best performance, particularly in detecting long-term anomalies. Although the ex-
periment was stopped at iteration 3, it would be valuable to explore further iterations
to understand whether additional improvements could be observed.
A possible explanation for the decrease in performance of the irregular spikes and
noise anomalies, compared to the frozen, offset, and drift anomalies, could be that,
since the model retrained on each subset, the residuals naturally decrease as the inter-
nal representations in the model (e.g., the hidden states) adjust. This aligns with the
fact that the Residuals model was highly effective at detecting irregular anomalies
due to its focus on residual errors, whereas the Final Hidden and Integrated Gradient
models were better at capturing long-term trends and structural shifts - as discussed
in the Detection of Synthetic Anomalies section.
On the other hand, the performance on the predicted intervals on the test showed no
clear distinction between iterative models. On the training set, iteration 1 showed
the best improvements in identifying intervals with true-positives, however, the last
iteration slightly decreased. These results contrast with the AUC scores, and could
imply that for the last iteration, each predicted interval is more qualitative with more
true-positives, however quantitatively it is less precise.
The distribution of anomaly scores in Figures 5.44 and 5.45 highlights key trends.
On the test set, the anomaly score distribution transitions from a unimodal to a flat-

anomalous instances - especially since the threshold was set between the modalities.
The optimal threshold increases, suggesting that the Isolation Forest model becomes
more sensitive to anomalies as iterations progress. In contrast, on the training set,
the distributions exhibit only minimal changes, but the optimal threshold follows a
decreasing trend similar to the test set. This suggests, that while the decision bound-
ary shifts, the underlying score distribution remains stable.
The time series plots in Figure 5.46 illustrate qualitative differences in anomaly de-

the rain event remains consistently classified as highly anomalous. This suggests that
while the iterative refinement process adjusts decision boundaries, it does not drasti-
cally alter the classification of anomalous instances.

5.5
Overall Discussion
Overall, the results and discussions have explored multiple aspects of: transformer-
based modelling for sewer level predictions, anomaly detection and targeted iterative
refinement.
The results shows that the transformer model outperformed both the strong and
weak baseline in steps-ahead forecasting under various weather conditions. Though
it underwent significantly more optimization, the validity of the results are further
enhanced by comparison with the results of the work of others [29]. However, trans-
former models are generally quite data hungry, and though the Bellinge dataset spans
a decade of observations, this might not always be the case for other datasets.
On the other hand, the multi-stage optimization approach demonstrates that the
performance of the transformer model significantly improved when incorporating ad-
ditional data features, such as time of day, as well as the upstream and downstream
sensors relative to the target sensor. This suggests that incorporation of contextual
information may alleviate the data-intensive requirements, potentially offering a bal-
ance between performance and data dependency.
The Multi-Feature anomaly detection approach was shown to outperformed individual
detection models by leveraging the information in the target value, multi-horizon
forecast residuals, final hidden state, and integrated gradients. The model exhibited
better performance on the training set, both on average and across each anomaly type,
and consistently achieved higher AUC scores than the baseline Sigma-3 model. These
results suggest that incorporating a variety of information-rich features together with
Isolation Forest enhances its ability to generalize detection capabilities.
However, while the Multi-Feature model performed best on average, other models
demonstrated specialization in detecting certain types of faults. This raises the ques-
tion, of whether the trade-off between a more generalized approach and model spe-
cialization is desired.
Yet, combining information from both the generalized and
specialized could to some degree explain what the type of anomaly is being detected.
Yet, the qualitative results indicated that the detection model tends to classify rain
events as anomalous, which is an undesirable outcome. This issue highlights the need
for further refinement in future work.
Overall, the targeted iterative refinement approach demonstrated an improvement in
performance - especially on the training set. Specifically, the final iteration on the
training set had highest average AUC score, indicating that to some degree, it is
detecting better and better the synthetic anomalies. Though, it did show a decrease
in performance on the predicted intervals with true-positives.
Significant differences were observed between the training and test sets, particularly
in the splitting process and the distribution of anomaly scores. One possible explana-
tion, could be that the test set, being unseen by the model, may have made it more
prone to predicting anomalies in each subset. This could also account for the bimodal

Conclusion
6.1
Contributions
This thesis aimed to optimize a transformer model for multi-step sewer level forecast-
ing (RQ1), assess the role of synthetic anomaly generation in benchmarking detection
methods (RQ2), evaluate the effectiveness of a multi-feature anomaly detection ap-
proach (RQ3), and investigate the impact of targeted iterative refinement in improv-
ing anomaly detection performance (RQ4). The results provide several key insights
into these aspects.
6.1.1
Transformer-Based Sewer Level Predictions (RQ1)
The optimized transformer model demonstrated superior performance over both weak
and strong baselines. Notably, it achieved nearly half the MAE of the LSTM model
for one-step forecasting, highlighting its ability to capture long-term dependencies
and temporal trends. While the LSTM required more epochs to converge, its over-
all performance was not significantly better than that of a simple linear regression
model - considering its computational cost. These findings suggest, that transformer-
based models are applicable to UDS, and when optimized appropriately, can enhance
forecasting accuracy.
6.1.2
Synthetic Anomaly Generation for Benchmarking (RQ2)
Synthetic anomaly generation provided a robust framework for evaluating anomaly
detection models. The results showed that this controlled approach helped assess
model performance for various anomaly types, revealing critical differences in the
behaviours of the detection models. These findings underscore the value of synthetic
anomaly generation as a benchmarking tool, allowing for standardized and repro-
ducible evaluations.
6.1.3
Multi-Feature Anomaly Detection (RQ3)
The multi-feature anomaly detection approach outperformed individual detection
models by leveraging multiple features, including multi-horizon forecast residuals,
final hidden state information, and integrated gradients. By combining the multiple
==PAGE:112==
98
6 Conclusion
features, the model was able to generalize across the different anomalies, increasing its
overall performance. The model also exhibited strong performance across both train-
ing and test sets, achieving higher AUC scores than the baseline Sigma-3 model. This
highlights the potential of hybrid approaches that combine an optimized transformer
model with a robust detection mechanism to enhance anomaly detection.
Additionally, complex input features like the final hidden state and integrated gra-
dients proved particularly effective in capturing long-term anomalies — cases where
residuals alone might be insuﬀicient. The differences between the detection models
could be used as a explainability measure for the nature of the detected anomalies.
6.1.4
Targeted Iterative Refinement for Anomaly Detection (RQ4)
The iterative refinement approach revealed important trends in anomaly detection
performance across iterations. On the test set, the method seemed to isolate the
synthetic anomalies into the targeted anomalous subsets, however, the performance
improvements were marginal. On the training set, there were noticeable improve-
ments in both the AUC and the predicted intervals.
Whether further iterations
would continue to improve the detection performance, or if the performance would
plateau, remains uncertain.
6.1.5
Additional Contributions
Beyond addressing the research questions, this thesis also introduces a substantial
update to the processing pipeline of the Bellinge dataset, making it more accessible
and useful for future research.
Considerable effort was also dedicated to setting up the entire framework, and all
code is fully reproducible and publicly available on GitHub1.
Finally, some of the analysis methods applied to the detection models may contribute
to greater standardization and broader evaluation within the field.
6.2
Limitations
Overall, the report is quite detailed and extensive, covering a wide range of exper-
iments, results, and insights. The primary objective was to evaluate how different
input features contribute to the detection of synthetic anomalies, and how the perfor-
mance would adjust with iterative refinement. However, the thesis also extends the
improved data processing pipeline and the optimization of the Transformer model.
To some extent, data processing and model optimization can overshadow the level of

detail in the anomaly detection results, both in terms of the thesis content and time

However, proper data processing and model optimization were deemed essential to en-
sure the validity of the detection results. The injection of synthetic anomalies assumes
that the rest of the data has been thoroughly cleaned, preventing any interference
with the true detection performance. Similarly, for the multi-feature approach, which

The Bellinge dataset spans over a decade of observations, which is often not the case

Additionally, the transformer model was optimized solely for one-step-ahead predic-

performance can be generalized to other sensors, or if the model is capable of han-
dling multiple sensors simultaneously, as these scenarios were not tested. However,
investigating this is crucial, as performing such extensive optimizations for a single
sensor would be impractical for large and complex UDS.
In general, the thesis focuses on synthetic anomalies that are observed during both
training and testing. However, it remains uncertain whether the same results would
apply to new, unseen anomalies, as this has not been tested. Moreover, most of the
synthetic anomalies used are relatively simple and applied solely to the target sen-
sor. In contrast, in real-world scenarios, anomalies may be correlated across multiple
sensors and may exhibit behaviours that differ from those of the synthetic anomalies
used in this study.
The optimal thresholds were also determined using the known synthetic anomalies.
This approach served two purposes: to demonstrate how the detection would per-
form under ideal conditions and to ensure consistent threshold selection for a more
fair model comparison.
While Isolation Forest provides its own threshold, it was
deemed less effective in this context. However, in most cases, where the true anoma-
lies are unknown, the threshold needs to be set manually. This was not been explored
in this study and comes down to how many false-negatives one is willing to accept.
The features in the anomaly detection model were limited, as detections for each
time step only included data relevant to that specific moment. However, incorpo-
rating features from neighbouring time steps could have improved the performance.
Additionally, the model only utilized the target value of the sensor, whereas includ-
ing data from the rain gauge could have further enhanced performance, potentially

Future Directions
This section outlines some of the main future directions, by integrating insights de-
rived from the literature review, methodology, experiments, analysis, and limitations,
all of which are directly relevant to the scope of this thesis.
6.3.1
Transformer Model Improvements
In general, further research could focus on the following areas to enhance model
performance and robustness:
• Multi-Sensor Prediction: Extend the model to predict multiple sensor out-
puts simultaneously, as this would make the be highly applicable, and could
potentially improve it understanding of the system dynamics.
• Architectural Enhancements: Assess other transformer architectures that
are specifically designed for time series sensor data. These could be in the form
of SOTA models, transfer learning or even foundation models for improved
generalization.
• Expanded Model Configurations: Investigate alternative configurations,
including the integration of additional sensor measurements, advanced feature
engineering techniques, variations in input sequence length, and data augmen-
tation strategies.
• Long-Term Forecasting and Imputation: Conduct a more detailed evalua-
tion of the performance in long-term forecasting scenarios. These results could
be useful for both imputation of missing data, but it could also be used for
scenario testing.

6.3.2
Benchmarking Expansion
Future research could explore the following areas to improve the modelling of sewage
system sensor data:
• Comprehensive Optimization of the LSTM: To enable a more accurate
comparison between the widely used LSTM and the newer Transformer model,
a thorough optimization of the LSTM is essential. The results could influence
future model selection, particularly since the Transformer is know to be good
at scaling up to more complex data.
• In-Depth Comparison with Alternative Architectures: A rigorous eval-
uation should include comparisons with other architectures, such as the recent
SSM Mamba model, GNNs, and their hybrid variants. These alternative archi-
tectures may offer advantages that are better aligned with the characteristics
of UDS data.
6.3.3
Anomaly Detection Approaches
Future research could explore the following areas to improve anomaly detection of
sewage system sensor data:
• Rain Event and Anomalies: One of the main issues observed in the Multi-
Feature detection model was its inability to distinguish between rain events
and synthetic anomalies.
To address this issue, future improvements could
focus on refining its ability to differentiate between natural weather patterns
and disturbances that do now follow the natural system dynamics.
• Additional Input Features: Since the final hidden state and the PCA re-
duced integrated gradients proved to be useful, it would be valuable to explore
their corresponding detection models in greater depth. Particularly integrated
gradients, as the PCA reduction may have led to some information loss. Future
research could also examine the impact of incorporating neighbouring features
from adjacent time steps or explore other potentially valuable features, such as
attention weights in Transformer model or Grad-CAM, to enhance detection
performance.
• Alternative Detection Algorithms: In this study, Isolation Forest was se-
lected for its effectiveness in handling high-dimensional data and large datasets.
However, more advanced models, such as Autoencoders, could potentially im-
prove performance. Additionally, exploring ensembles of the different detection
models investigated in this study could be a viable approach, as they were
shown to specialize in detecting specific types of faults.
• Improved Thresholding: Thresholding plays a crucial role in anomaly clas-
sification. Future research could explore ways to enhance this process, such as

• Synthetic Anomalies: Simulating more complex, multi-sensor anomalies could

6.3.4
Alternative Refinement Approaches
The directions outlined in the previous section provide a solid foundation for reducing
false positives. However, future research on refinement approaches could focus on the
following areas to further improvements:
• Targeted Normal Data: Rather than fine-tuning separate models for pre-
dicted anomalous and normal data, one could fine-tune only on the data identi-
fied as normal. This approach is beneficial because the data classified as normal
are likely to most closely resemble typical operational conditions.
• Even Splitting: Another approach to explore is iteratively splitting the subset,
ensuring that the predicted anomalies are evenly distributed across the subsets.
A form of this strategy was observed on the training data, where the iterative
models showed improved performance for each iteration - in contrast to the test
set where the performance improvement was marginal.

Conclusion
This thesis has contributed to the field of sewer level forecasting and anomaly de-

The experimentation of the Transformer model for sewer level predictions, demon-
strated, that when optimized properly, it can outperform traditional baselines such
as the LSTM - making it a promising tool for time-series forecasting in UDS.
The role of synthetic anomaly generation as a benchmarking tool was also reinforced,
providing a robust framework to assess and compare anomaly detection models under
controlled conditions.

Processing Pipeline
Provided Processing Pipeline
The provided data processing pipeline follows the procedure of 1) appropriate data
loading with regards to each SCADA system, 2) flagging and removing probable errors,
3) resampling the data into 1 minute intervals and 4) interpolating data gaps shorter
than 5 minutes. Thus, the provided processed the data is on a 1-minute interval with
the following columns: raw data with errors, cleaned data, and cleaned forward-filled
and interpolated data - respectively named raw_value, value_no_errors and depth_s.
An illustrative example of how a time series goes from raw to processed is visualized
in Figure 1. Here, the first figure (fig. 1a) visualizes some typical data scenarios of
errors, data gaps and outliers, as well as simulating the aforementioned characteristics
within SCADA system. Then, Figures 1b, 1c and 1d illustrate the available data series
after using the provided processing script.

In Figure 1, we see that only the processed raw data (fig. 1b) is affected by the in-
dicated errors in the original series (fig. 1a) - whilst the other series effectively avoid
them. The final series in figure 1d forward-fills data gaps due to the iFIX system,
and also interpolates less than 5 minutes of missing values. However, note that the
true data - indicated by the green line - does not seem to align very well with the in-
terpolation in Figure 1d. And upon closer examination, a few additional refinements
and clarifications that have become evident, that were not explicitly addressed by the
authors. They are summarized as follows:
1-minute resampling of the data results in a significant drop in detected errors when
comparing the raw time series (fig. 1a) to the processed versions. This occurs be-
cause the resampling method only flags a minute as erroneous if all sample within that
minute are erroneous. Such behaviour is not ideal, because the processed raw_value
series (fig. 1a) is still influenced by intermittent errors — such as occasional zero-
valued 10-second samples from the iFIX system — which causes the resampled minute
to underestimate the true value. Similarly, spikes or outliers in the original data will
skew the resampled values, introducing bias in the resampled series. As a result, this
resampling approach would lead to an underestimation of the error rate. For data
practitioners looking to use the raw_value series to identify real errors and build mod-
els, it is important to recognize this. This can be addressed by flagging a resampled
minute as erroneous if there is any error within the minute.
Forward-filling is applied to the iFIX data series due to its data-saving function.
While forward-filling might be suitable during dry periods with no rainfall — because
measurements are unlikely to fluctuate and can be assumed constant — it is less
appropriate in dynamic environments with rainfall, where linear interpolation would
be more suitable.
Interpolation should only be performed if it can effectively close the data gap being
interpolated over; otherwise, the results may introduce inaccuracies or distort the
overall data trend. Yet, the opposite can be seen at the end of the series in Fig-
ure 1d - where it is impossible to know how the data behaves - but it is interpolated
nonetheless. The authors use a 5-minute limit on interpolation to prevent unnecessary
extrapolation; however, this approach can be enhanced by introducing an additional
step of discarding interpolations that are unsuccessful in closing the data gap.
The following section will provide an overview of the adjusted data processing
procedure, by incorporating the discussed strategies to optimize the reliability and
quality of the data.

Data Management
The processed files are originally in separate files based on the SCADA system they
were recorded in, meaning some sensors were split up into 2 or more files. However,
for effective modelling, the ability to load data quickly, selectively and in a flexible
manner is essential. To achieve this, each sensor file is merged into a single, complete
time series. Thereafter, all sensor series are aligned based on their timestamps and
joined into a comprehensive and combined dataset. To further streamline access, the
data is stored in a single HDF5 file. This format allows for selective querying, i.e.
both by time and by specific columns - without loading the entire dataset.
Figure 5 illustrates the structure of the HDF5 file. The bools data represents the
error indicators and the data-saving values. This consistent data format throughout
the analysis and modelling phases improves reproducibility and lowers the barrier for
anyone looking to work with the dataset.

Data Quality
The dataset contains six key indicators of data quality, each specified and flagged by
the bools data. Nedergaard Pedersen et al. provide functions to identify these quality
stamps, which can be summarized as follows:
• Data-Saving: minutes that are not recorded unless they change by more than
a specified threshold.
• Manual Removal: deemed unreliable due to maintenance or start-up periods.
• Quality Stamp: stamped as low-quality by the iFIX SCADA system.
• Out of Bounds: values outside physically possible or meaningful ranges.
• Outlier: abnormal spikes based on their height and duration.
• Frozen: values remaining constant for a specified period.
Note, that data-saving periods are not directly errors, and that the distinctions be-
tween a Quality Stamp, Out of Bounds, and Outlier can be quite unclear, and there
are often instances where the occur together.
