Abstract 
The development within Internet of things is presenting many new possibilities with many appliances that 
are functioning as stand-alone outside. All of these need a power source and are often powered by battery 
or cables. By using solar panels as the power source, both cabling and battery-life can be changed. A 
challenge to outside products is the antenna, and by integrating an antenna into a solar panel, the 
possibilities for stand-alone appliances increases. 
This thesis presents proof of concept for a photo voltaic integrated printed antenna which is using the free 
space between two rows of solar cells in an existing solar module design used in a Bike Stand from out-
sider A/S. From a literature study, limited examples of production methods of entire solar modules with 
integrated antennas and resulting consequences are found.  
To determine the optimal antenna length for 868 MHz, the printed antenna was tested while its length 
adjusted within the bike stand. The resonant frequency of 868 MHz was satisfactory with a Voltage 
Standing Wave Ratio around than 2. The length was confirmed on another printed antenna, and afterwards 
two modules was built and encapsulated. The encapsulation resulted in a frequency reduction of 270 MHz. 
By adjusting the antenna length to a frequency of 1040 MHz two new modules were build. By analysing the 
two different modules a final antenna length was found and two final modules made. 
With the antenna length found to be less than half the module length, a dipole antenna was created. This 
the length of each pole being a little longer giving a possibility to change length. As no clear resonant 
frequencies were present, three dipole antennas were encapsulated and tested afterwards. 
The antennas were compared to results from a reference antenna, all placed within the bike stand during 
measurements. The bike stand was placed outside, and the antennas was pointed in the cardinal directions 
(N, S, E and W), while connected to a transceiver. The antennas were compared in number of packets sent, 
Received Signal Strength Indicator and Spreading Factor. 
It is concluded that the encapsulation of the solar panel reduces the resonance frequency. This 
encapsulation is dependent on the thickness of the materials used for encapsulation. 
A proof of concept has been established with a 50 mm monopole antenna grounded to the bike stand and 
with a dipole antenna with a length of each pole of 52 mm. The dipole antenna must however be tested 
further while using a balun, as the results left room for improvement compared to the monopole antenna. 
When deciding on production a few prototypes must be tested, as the materials can be of different 
characteristics and thickness influencing the resulting resonance frequency.

Introduction 
Imagine yourself going for a run in the forest in the evening time. Instead of a mobile phone you are 
wearing a watch with the same functions. Now you start running and along your path light starts shining, 
showing the path, possible directions and maybe even a pacer. A way to make this work is by having the 
lights as stand-alone, powered by a small solar module, and fitted with antennas that connects to your 
watch and reacts to your preferences. All of this is possible within the near future, as the development of 
Internet of Things (IoT) is accelerating. This is only one simple example as the possibilities are endless. 
Already IoT is implemented over a wide scale, but as it is, IoT is dependent on power sources such as 
batteries with a limited lifespan or cabling. By integrating the antenna in a solar module, it will be possible 
for a self-supplying long-time solution. This way the solar module can function as the path of least 
resistance to the antenna signal.  
IoT is not a new thought and has existed in theory since the 1980s, but along with the evolution of the 
internet, electric devices, and new possibilities IoT began to emerge around 2010.  
“The main theme of the Internet of Things is to embed short-range 
mobile transceivers in various gadgets and daily necessities to enable 
new forms of communication between people and things, and between 
themselves.”  
Wikipedia on Internet of Things1 
To integrate an antenna in a solar panel a microstrip antenna is ideal, as the PCB it is printed upon can act 
as a stabilising backplane. Microstrip antennas has been around for a long time, but the integration into 
solar panels is investigated significantly less. In the following different studies on this topic will be 
presented. A lot of the initial work focus on PV antennas for microsatellite applications, where the available 
area for the solar cell is limited [1]. 
In this study the integration of an antenna into a solar panel is investigated, as a solar panel can function as 
the window to the outside world for different applications. The possibility of placing an antenna within the 
solar panel opens new possibilities of communicating with different appliances through IoT, relieving 
installations of excessive cabling for power or networks.  
 
Literature study 
This thesis is based on the integration of a microstrip antenna into a solar panel. Microstrip antennas has 
been around for a long time, but the integration into solar panels is investigated significantly less. In the 
following different studies will be presented.  
In 1994 Tanaka, M. Suzuki, Y. and Araki, K. integrated an antenna into a solar panel, in terms of 
investigating how an antenna could be integrated, without reducing solar panel area on a micro satellite 
[1]. Since it is supposed to be used in space, there are no encapsulation of the solar cells, and most of the 
antenna is placed underneath the solar cells. The paper show that there is no impact on the produced 
power by implementing the antenna. Furthermore, both return loss and radiation patterns were nearly

equal, with and without solar cells, showing that solar cells including cabling had little influence on the

Another method of implementing antennas in micro satellites [2] presents two other prototypes based on a 
slot and a transparent antenna.  
The slots are made to correspond with the gaps between the solar cells. One slot is straight while the other 
is formed as a cross. To isolate electrically, a small layer of resin was placed upon the antenna, but 
otherwise it was as before, with no further encapsulation. When testing in an anechoic chamber the 
additions of solar cells, did not change the radiation pattern and the return loss much. 
A transparent antenna was tested in different levels of transparency. Placed upon the solar panels, it 
resulted in a loss of less than two percent at 90 percent transparency. With the transparent antenna a 
meshed patch yielded the best results. 
An array slot antenna combined with solar cells is presented in [3]. This is a study that presents a solution 
for satellites. For this a-Si:H thinfilm cells are placed in the areas between slots. The panel power density of 
this design is very low as lots of room are left without coverage of solar cells. It is however suggested, that 
with thinfilm most of the area could be covered resulting in a much better power density. This panel is not 
encapsulated, as it is focused on space. This study proves validity of concept as the antenna functions and 
the solar cells produce enough power to supply the antenna. 
Returning to earth the following studies are considering integrated antennas in solar panels for IoT or the 
influence of dielectrics to the resonance frequency. 
To investigate an antenna for IoT, a slot antenna was tested for a solar panel [4]. This solar panel consisted 
of 156x156 mm cells separated by 25 mm. The slot was placed in the space between cells, but the ground 
area reached under the nearby cells. This project also suggests more than one antenna in each panel to 
improve signal strength in a certain direction. As spacing of 25 mm between cells would not be normal in a 
solar panel, as it would compromise the power density of the panel. 
Several methods of integrating an antenna with solar cells have been tested in [5]. The two main methods 
are based on a patch and a dipole antenna placed upon the solar cell, and a dipole antenna placed within a 
parabolic solar reflector. The patch and dipole antenna placed upon the solar cell, both results in 
shadowing of the solar panel and a reduction in realised power. However, the possibility of using the 
aluminium back of the solar cell as ground, is found to give acceptable results. Along with this a transparent 
patch antenna placed on a a-Si solar panel was found to be successful for a 2,19 GHz antenna. None of the 
antennas was encapsulated. 
Further examination of using the aluminium back of a solar cell is made using a patch antenna [6]. Here it is 
observed that a reduction in gain could be a result of semiconductor properties of the solar cell. This 
examination also has a patch antenna placed on top of the solar cell, resulting in reduced efficiency. Only 
the antenna and cell were tested, and no encapsulation was applied. 
Using a GaAs with a gap for a slot antenna it is concluded that a very efficient antenna for IoT is achieved in 
[7]. The antenna in the article is not encapsulated but is said to be perfectly suitable for IoT applications. 
Because of the area left open in the GaAs layer, the area of PV is limited, while this application also needs 
an AC-blocking circuit to reduce current flowing from the antenna to the PV cell because of the design. 
In the following studies antennas have been encapsulated in other materials than the usual glass and EVA 
used in solar panels. Nevertheless, the results are relevant to this project.

In [8] a dipole butterfly antenna is placed upon a thermoplastic polymer, ABS, and is covered by plexiglass. 
The antenna is tested for both 900 and 1800 MHz and it is concluded that the length of the dipole antenna 
must be reduced when it is placed between the mentioned dielectrics. A frequency reduction of around 
12% due to embedding is observed. 
F. Benmeddour, C. Dumond, and E. Kenane [9], investigates the effect on antenna operating frequency and 
bandwidth. It is described that both thickness and permittivity influence the resonant frequency. The 
results indicate that the dielectric cover decrease the resonant frequency giving room for a reduction in the 
patch size. Furthermore, it is observed that the effects are more noticeable with a thicker superstrate as 
well as with an increased ratio between the sub- and superstrate dielectric constant. 
The observations in [9] is backed in [10] which study the effects of a superstrate on different antenna 
designs. Here it is concluded that the superstrate affects more than just the frequency. Among others 
Voltage Standing Wave Ratio (VSWR) and bandwidth is affected. Especially the resonant frequency is 
reduced when the superstrate thickens. The study on which this article is based are using sub- and 
superstrates with the same dielectric constant. 
[11] studies how thickness of the dielectric substrate influences the resonant frequency along with how the 
permittivity changes due to frequency. When the dielectric increase in thickness a reduction in resonant 
frequency is observed. One of the dielectrics that is tested is FR4 which is used in this study.

Motivation 
IoT devices placed in the outdoor environment are often well suited for being PV powered. The projects 
described above are all investigating the possibility to design antenna solutions that can work with PV or 
investigates the influence of surrounding dielectrics. Many of the projects offer solutions, where a final and 
practical implementation are not fulfilled. 
In this study a PCB antenna is integrated into a solar module to provide proof of concept for an installation 
into a bike stand by out-sider. The final product is compared to an off the shelf rubber antenna which is 
placed within the same bike stand.

The study is based upon the existing product Bike Sun, built by out-sider A/S2 (see Figure 1). This is a bike 
stand fitted with three solar panels, that charges a battery which supply power for bike stand lights. These 
bike stands can, along with other out-sider products, be placed everywhere as they do not rely on an 
external power source. By integrating a communication unit in the products, out-sider can monitor 
functionality, alter lighting periods and update software, without having to be present at each device. 
 
Figure 1 - Bike stand by out-sider. Picture from www.out-sider.dk 
 
Out-sider is in the process of developing the ideas, on how to use a communication device with their 
products, as IoT offers new possibilities. The knowledge gained from this study might give room for 
development of other devices, where the antenna integration into PV will be very practical. 
To comply with the European market, the antenna is supposed to function at 868 MHz, and if possible, also 
at 915 MHz which is used on the American market. Both these frequencies are without tariffs and are used 
for the internet of things (IoT). 
As the literature study indicates, some research has gone into integration of antennas in solar panels. 
However, the studies described lack implementation on earth, while it seems that it has already happened 
in space.  
In this project the function of different types of antennas are tested while being encapsulated and installed 
in a bike stand made by out-sider.

Theory 
An antenna either converts electric power into electromagnetic radiation or do the opposite. When 
working as a receiver the antenna transform waves into a voltage and current. To receive only a certain 
frequency derived from the wavelength an antenna needs to have a certain length which is dependent of 
both the antenna type and the materials surrounding the antenna. The same applies when an antenna 
functions as a transmitter, transmitting at a certain frequency. 
Most people are aware of the FM band which operates between 88 and 108 MHz, and by now no one can 
miss the 5 GHz that have been implemented for TV signals. In this assignment the IoT band are used which 
covers the bandwidth of 863-870 MHz in Europe. 
In the following some basic antenna theory and important expressions are presented.

Gain 
The gain described above is “the ratio of intensity, in a given direction, to the radiation intensity that would 
be obtained if the power accepted by the antenna were radiated isotropically”[13]. 
As the antenna in this assignment is mostly like a mobile phone antenna, it is assumed to have a low gain as 
it is supposed to interact with several receivers. As an opposite, the receiver stations that receives IoT 
signals will probably have a high gain directed in a low angle, as transmitters are placed near to 
ground.[12], [14]

Resonant frequency 
The frequency that is used in any appliance is the resonant frequency. It resonates when reactants of an 
impedance are very low and thus offering very little resistance. In this project the antenna is supposed to 
function at 868 MHz, which is within the European channel plan from 863-870 MHz. This frequency 
spectrum is unlicensed, meaning that it is free to use without paying additional fees or having special 
permits. 
In America this channel plan is between 902-928 MHz, and a reference frequency of 915 MHz is used to 
consider if the antenna is usable within USA as well.

Improving the signal 
When the impedance of the antenna differs from the impedance of the supply and coax cabling it might 
require a matching circuit to decrease the mismatch. This matching circuit are called a balun and especially 
a dipole antenna requires this.  
A BalUn is short for balance to unbalance, and when observing a dipole antenna, the dipole is symmetrical 
and thus balanced. But the coax cable leading to the antenna is unbalanced as currents can flow on both 
the inner and outer side of the shield, see Figure 5. The balun can both ensure matching of the antenna 
impedance to the supply, and also remove the possibility of the current I3. When the current path for I3 is 
removed the coax cable will also be balanced.

unballanced coax cable. The balun will choke the current path for I3 and thus also I3.

LoRaWAN test 
LoRaWAN is a Long Range Wide Area Network designed to transmit small amounts of data from appliances 
while only using a limited amount of power giving them years of durability. It can be operated in license 
free bands of which both 915 and 868 MHz are a part. LoRaWAN is ideal to transmit sensor data and has 
works in a greater range than other communication networks.[17]  
To test the antenna, a transceiver is connected to the coax cable attached to the antenna placed in the bike 
stand (Figure 17). Four tests are conducted with the antenna tip oriented towards the four cardinal 
directions (N, E, S and W). In Figure 13 a view from CIBICOM the LoRaWAN provider is shown. Here 
different information is given and the Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI) along with the Spreading 
Factor (SF) will be considered in comparing antennas. Furthermore, the number of transmissions, packets, 
are found when analysing the data and will be the primary source of comparison.  
SF informs about the quality of the connection between transceiver and gateway and is given in a scale 
between 7 and 12. SF has an influence on data rate and time-on-air as well as battery life and receiver 
sensitivity. At level 7 the power used to send the signal is lowest, while the signal will travel shorter than at 
SF12. When sending a signal at SF12 the time-on-air, will be longer than if the same signal is sent at SF7. 
[17] 
RSSI indicates if the signal is received with enough strength. If so, SF can be reduced, and if not, SF will 
increase. On this background, the value of RSSI have influence to signal strength and battery life. 
RSSI is measured between -40 to -149 dBm. Here -40 is a perfect signal strength and -149 is unusable [18].

Antenna length 
Three methods have been applied in determining the antenna length. The two first are used on the PCB 
and antenna alone and the last is used to predict the necessary length of the antenna when it is 
encapsulated. It is important that the length is investigated while being in the right surroundings. When the 
antenna is encapsulated, there is no longer a proper way of reducing length without altering the dielectrics 
around. 
The first method to determining the length is to cut of pieces of the antenna while observing the resulting 
impedance and S11 on the VIA. The cut offs must be removed to ensure an undisturbed signal [21]. Figure 
25 show an example of reducing the length of an antenna. Here the optimal length is found before 
encapsulation, and based on the resulting shift in frequency, a new antenna can be made. As is seen later, 
the result of encapsulation increases resonant frequency, enabling shorter antennas.  
In the second method Equation 5 is used to calculate the length by comparing measured frequency and 
length to desired frequency and length. This formula is however an approximately formula and thus it is 
necessary to use the formula more than once. The iterative way of using the formula and measurements is 
elaborated further under discussion.

Connection 
The first PCBs were made for a UFL connector as seen in Figure 26. The ULF connector gives possibility for a 
more compressed design, but a UFL connector is not made for a lot of reconnections. The same applies for 
the connector on the cable, which became loose [22]. 
The bad connection resulted in varying measurements with a difference that removed reliability of the 
results. When the connector is kept connected, this should not represent a problem, but during a design 
phase a high number of dis- and reconnections require another connector type.  
The other type of connector is a SMA connecter, Figure 26. This connector is stabilised on the PCB in the 
four corners and connected to the antenna through the middle pin. This connector is much better for

Cutting cells 
The cells for the module are cut using the Laser Scribing and Clevage (LSC) method, where a solar cell is 
placed on a scribing platform held fast by vacuum and cut by moving the cell underneath a fixed laser. The 
cell position is noted in the belonging computer along with cutting directions. In the process of cutting the 
cells each wafer was cut ten times in the same place increasing the possibility of a lower efficiency [24]. 
During the separating process, several cells cracked in different ways across, indicating that the cutting 
process could be optimized with a better platform. Had Thermal Laser Separation (TLS) been an option, the 
structural integrity of the cut cells should be better with less micro cracks [25]. 
To ensure the wafer is fixed, tape is applied. It is found that when the tape is cut by the laser it appears to 
melt into the cutting groove gluing the pieces together. This results in an increased number of uneven 
breaks as seen below (Figure 50). a show a cell with two cuts before separation of the small pieces. b shows 
how the cut made under tape results in an uneven break while the other has a clear cut.

Discussion 
In this section the final methods will be discussed as challenges described earlier led to this stage. However, 
the initial challenges are a part of both validity and uncertainties. 
 
Validity of results 
Thorough the design and testing phase the VIA has been used as the main instrument. Early on the results 
on the VIA was compared to results from a similar VIA. Both showed results that was very close while 
regarding the uncertainties of the tests. The comparison between the two VIAs is seen in Figure 52. The 
difference between the two is very small and in insignificant compared to variations during production. The 
immediate variations in production can derive from the ability to cut antennas in the same length, the 
lamination process where cells can change their positions a little, and the soldering process when 
connecting the SMA. Appendix 2 presents the data sheet of the VIAs. Again, the measuring uncertainties 
are considered insignificant due to differences from production.

Based upon observations of huge similarity in measurements of antennas with the same length, sub- and 
superstrate, it was decided that two examples of each type was enough to give reliable results. Figure 53 
exemplifies this by comparing the two modules of the same type where very little difference is seen. 
 
Measurement and design uncertainties 
The design and testing phase has been divided into four parts, as some major influences on measurements 
was realised. In part one, connection problems influenced the results. In the end of this testing phase the 
influence of the connection was realised, and the initial data was thus disregarded.  
Part two ended up showing how the coax cable touching the bike stand could influence the results. By 
acknowledging the chance in measurements, the results had to be discarded. This difference in results 
happens because of electromagnetic coupling between the coax cable and the bike stand. The 
electromagnetic coupling can happen since the coax cable is made with a braided shield and not a tube. 
This gives possibility to the magnetic and electric field in the shield to couple with the fields within the bike 
==PAGE:39==
January 2022 
Master thesis: Lasse Roer Wahlgreen  
Page 38 of 45 
stand and change the coax or antenna impedance [23].  
Part 3 is the finalising of the antenna module and test through VIA and phase four is the test of signal 
quality. 
During the testing phases other uncertainties had a possibility to play a role. These are not researched 
thoroughly but have been considered meanwhile. The ones that had the highest influence are: 
- 
Placement of the PCB or entire module in the bike stand. When not grounded properly, the 
antenna is very susceptible to disturbance from objects placed near to the antenna. For example, a 
hand, a camera, a phone, or tools used during testing. 
- 
Placement of the bike stand can, as before, have an influence on the antenna, by changing the size 
of ground. 
- 
Placement of the conductor connecting the two rows of PV. The high frequency on the antenna will 
induce voltages and resulting magnetic fields in metals placed around the antenna. This might 
result in a magnetic field acting against the signal. 
- 
Influence of light on the PV and the resulting electric field. The influence of the electric field from 
the induced voltage of the PV along with the conducting PV have some importance but should be 
insignificant [1][2]. 
- 
Disturbance from placement of buildings, trees among others during signal tests. As the 
measurements were conducted at a test site and not in an anechoic chamber, the surrounding 
environment and weather can play a role [26]. 
 
Antenna length 
To estimate the correct length of an antenna at a certain frequency Equation 5 can be applied. The results 
in Figure 35 where a cutting method is compared to an iterative length analysis based on Equation 5. The 
graphs are very alike with some offset, most likely derived from the possibility to cut at the exact same 
lengths. What is seen, however is a very uniform curve made from fewer measuring points. What is also 
seen, is that the Equation 5 must be used more than once, and for each time the result gets closer to the 
optimum length ending up with length differences being too small to change. 
The optimal length of the monopole antenna is found to be around 50 mm while the dipole antenna 
presents similar results with a length of 52 mm pr pole. In Figure 39 the resonant frequency is a little higher 
than 868 MHz, but the bandwidth covers both 868 and 915 MHz. This can be optimized a bit by increasing 
antenna length a little. 
The dipole antenna also has a resonant frequency a bit higher than 868 MHz. However, it is not 
immediately possible to increase the antenna length without making significant design changes, as the 
length of the antenna is already reaching the ends of the PCB. 
 
Influence by dielectric materials of a PV module 
The influence on the signal from the different parts of a PV module has been investigated. There was no 
clear indication on the influence by the EVA. Apart from the dielectric properties the influence of 
superstrate thickness is important [9], [10]. This gives reason to the limited effect by EVA and the high 
change in frequency by glass. A study has also shown that the permittivity og EVA decrease significantly at 
higher frequencies [27].

The placement of solar cells seems to have an influence on the impedance of the antenna. The changes are

antenna. 
 
Influence of the distance between antenna and solar cells 
As earlier where little difference was seen between a module with and without PV, the changes in reflected 
power are very low, leading to the conclusion that the PV cells have little influence on the total dielectric 
constant. On the signal efficiency there is however a possibility, that a larger distance between PV and 
antenna leaves room for a better signal. When the cells had moved towards the antenna, the impedance 
increased and measurements on the VIA got worse. Thus, the modules with least cell movements and best 
results on the VIA were used in the final signal tests. 
The difference between measurements of the signals is presented in Table 2. Here the antenna with the 
smallest cell area, Final 3, yields mostly better results than Final 1 with the original cell area. The results 
seem better but must be accounted for as an indication rather than a conclusive result.  
 
The final products 
Below the final products models are discussed. The results of the signal test are made within short time 
intervals as only one bike stand was available. Since weather can affect the signals, the short intervals 
reduce the possibility of influence from weather shifts. The statistical significance is thus not present; 
however, the results are indicating that both the monopole and dipole antennas could be good alternatives 
to an off the shelf rubber antenna. 
Comparing the antennas with a prefabricated product some changes are noted. The size of cells within this 
panel measure 19,5 x 28,5 each and the distance to the sides are 4 mm. In these final products, the cell size 
is 18 x 28,5 mm and distance to the sides are 3 mm. This will result in a reduced power output from the 
solar cells, but more room for the antenna.

Dipole antenna 
This antenna was made very late in the study and is not measured in the same weather conditions as the 
other antennas. The dipole antenna was tested towards the final monopole antenna, Final 3.  
The number of packets between the dipole antenna and the gateway are considerably lower than with the 
monopole. Nonetheless, as the dipole is in an unbalanced circuit, this could be expected. To properly test 
the dipole antenna a balun must be included. By balancing the entire circuit, the dipole antenna is expected 
to be a better alternative to the monopole, as the dipole can function without an extra connection to the 
bike stand. 
When observing the average RSSI and SF, RSSI is around the same level, while SF is a bit lower, indicating 
that less power is needed for the dipole to transmit the packets. 
 
Other antenna designs 
Ground in plane with antenna 
This antenna was not investigated further since a proper signal around 868 MHz could not be achieved. By 
looking at the VIA measurements in Figure 37 a, the resonant frequency at 560 MHz is amplified while 868 
MHz is dampened significantly. What happens to this antenna is due to reflections from the bike stand, as 
the antenna give very good readings outside the stand. It can be considered to test this antenna in a test 
stand with slant edges. Perhaps with 45 degrees angle the signal might be reflected away from the 
antenna, resulting in a working antenna. 
Another design of an antenna with ground was tested with the VIA. This design resulted in the same 
amplification and reductions and was thus not pursued further. 
Due to results from these tests, the PCB with antenna and ground is not regarded as a possibility for the 
bike stand.

Conclusion 
Two antenna designs for an out-sider bike stand are offered. A monopole antenna of 50 mm with the bike 
stand acting as ground and a dipole antenna. Both yielded results of RSSI and SF similar to an off the shelf 
rubber antenna.  
Both antennas are connected through a SMA connector, and for the monopole antenna a connection 
between the SMA shield and the ground is made.  
The suggested antennas cover a sufficient bandwidth to embrace both the European 868 MHz and 
American 915 MHz band with a reasonable efficiency. 
Other antenna designs did not produce satisfying results in the bike stand, but have great potential in other 
applications, where the antenna is surrounded by other materials than metal.  
During the study, the influence of the EVA is found to be close to nothing, while main influence on the 
signal comes from the protective layer, which in this case is glass. It is established that the encapsulation 
decreases the resonant frequency. For a solar panel, strong indications are observed that the glass layer has 
the strongest influence on the frequency, while EVA and PV barely change the result.  
As proof of concept is achieved some further tests are acquired when outsourcing. Some panels produced 
by the coming producer must be tested for both resonance frequency and signal strength before a final 
production is established. In the first modules produced by others, the antenna must thus be a little longer 
to enable length reduction. Most likely an antenna which is a couple of millimetres longer than 50 mm is 
necessary as the thickness of the PCB will decrease to fit the bike stand. 
Finally, it has been established that a multipurpose antenna is unreachable within a solar panel, as the 
environment in which the antenna is installed, have great influence. It is however expected, that when the 
materials surrounding the antenna has very low contents of metal a multipurpose antenna could be 
produced. Each new product needs a unique antenna and a design phase. This study gives room for some 
ground rules and design methods for an easier design phase with directions to a faster and better result.

Future works 
 
“It is quite true what philosophy says that life must be understood 
backwards. But above that one forgets the second sentence, that it 
must be lived in advance.” 
Søren Kirkegaard 
 
This study presents a lot of inspiration to future works, as it spans over several different tests that indicates 
rather than concludes. As Søren Kirkegaard says above, the knowledge obtained during the study would 
have changed the study, had it been known before. Below suggestions to further works are presented, 
beginning with the most necessary works before production. 
To balance the circuits baluns must be made for each type of antenna. This will most likely increase 
efficiency and signal strength. When a balun is installed, signal strength test must be made anew.  
As the final signal test is made with only one test stand and during a short period, new test could be 
conducted that include two or three test stands. This way, comparing the off the shelf, the monopole and 
the dipole antenna over a long period is possible increasing statistical significancy.  
After applying a balun to the antennas, they could be tested in an anechoic chamber while being installed 
in the bike stand. This could give directions to how the bike stand influences the radiation. 
Since the monopole antenna design with ground in plane showed promise outside the bike stand, this 
design could be tested towards other products. Here out-sider already have another bike stand called Jello 
Sun fitted with PV and light. As the PV in this bike stand is larger a dipole antenna should also be tested. 
As the antennas are encapsulated by EVA and glass while being in the vicinity of solar cells, the influence of 
these materials should be investigated further, to present a clear reference when designing the antennas. 
Among these tests a thorough test of the influence to the signal by solar cell size, along with the influence 
of conducting solar cells, should be made.  
When the solar cells were measured it happened at direct radiation, and thus the panel was not analysed 
for other angles of the sun giving only the best possible result. This could be tested to analyse the efficiency 
during a day. This test could be expanded to include shadow analysis at the installation site. 
If further projects are to be made with integrated PV antennas Nordsense7 and Sweco8 are both testing 
smart dumpsters with metal lids. Here the proposed solar panel antennas could be implemented as they 
would be surrounded by metal.
