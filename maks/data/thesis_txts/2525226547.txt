Abstract
Cardiac arrhythmias are abnormal heartbeats caused by incorrect electrical signals
in the cardiac tissue. The disease can be dangerous and potentially result in sudden
death.
Some arrhythmias can be treated by catheter ablation procedures, where
coagulation necrosis is induced to arrhytmogenic locations in the myocardial tissue.
The purpose of this thesis is to establish a numerical model predicting the coagu-
lation of myocardial tissue by a 980 nm wavelength laser, in order to avoid thermally
induced complications to catheter ablation procedures seen in clinic.
The model is based on Monte Carlo modelling of light distribution, finite element
heat transfer, and a first-order Arrhenius damage integral. After it was established,
it was used to numerically explore different laser settings, such as pulsation and
Gaussian and Laguerre-Gaussian01 beam modes.
Tissue parameters, µa = 0.74 cm−1, µs = 220 cm−1, g = 0.92, Thermal Con-
ductivity = 0.4841 · T + 0.0013 W/m/K/◦C, Thermal Diffusivity = 0.1270 · T +
0.0005 mm2/s/◦C, Arrhenius pre-exponential factor = 163000 kJ/mol, and Arrhe-
nius Activation Energy = 3.47 · 1022 s−1 were found based on literature studies and
an ex vivo experiment on swine myocardium.
The proposed model revealed, that for same coagulation geometries, it was possible
to reduce the peak temperature and risk of vaporization by using a wide laser focus
and a Laguerre-Gaussian01 beam mode.
Based on the numerical results, suggestions for different laser properties were
proposed for different typical arrhythmogenic locations within the myocardium.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The normal electrophysiology of the heart (Figure 1.1) ensures an effective flow of
blood around the body.
Cardiac arrhythmias are abnormal heartbeats caused by
disturbance of the normal cardiac electrophysiology. Some arrhythmias are harmless
while others can be life threatening.
When present, common symptoms include palpitations, dyspnea, angina, and
weakness. Other symptoms can be anxiety, fatigue, dizziness, syncope, and sweating.
Many types of cardiac arrhythmias exist and require different kind of treatments.
The term arrhythmia covers both bradycardia (Heart Rate (HR) < 60 beats per
minute (bpm)), tachycardia (HR > 100 bpm), and irregular heartbeats. Atrial Fibril-
lation (AF) is one type of arrhythmia, which can be treated by ablation procedures.
Many arrhythmias are asymptomatic and therefore not fund, but an estimate of AF
prevalence in Europe and US is around 1-3% of the population [1].
1.1
Treatment options for arrhythmias today
Arrhythmias are treated with medication if possible.
If the medical treatment is
insufficient, other treatment options include: Cardioversion, catheter-ablation, and
implantable devices. If neither medication nor cardioversion is effective, a catheter-
ablation procedure can be performed. The aim of ablation procedures is to induce
coagulation-necrosis on specific locations within the heart to create scar tissue. Scar
tissue does not conduct electrical activity, meaning that the origin of the arrhythmia
can be blocked, and a normal heart rhythm can be restored. The scarring can be
made with either heat or extreme cold.
When treating arrhythmia, coagulation necrosis is the goal. Ablative processes
such as vaporization and carbonization (see Figure 1.2) creates side effects, and re-
duces the safety of the treatment.
The medical procedure is termed an ablation
procedure in the clinic, albeit any physical ablative processes are unwanted.
Clinical preparations: Before the ablation procedure, the physician inserts a
catheter into the femoral vein in the groin.
Then the catheter is guided through
the veins into the lumen of the heart. The origin of the arrhythmia is located by
electrophysiological mapping and the coagulation treatment is initiated. The coag-
ulation necrosis is generated using either Radio Frequency (RF)-currents (heating),
Cryo (cooling), or most recently; lasers (heating) [3].

Figure 1.1: Under normal conditions, an action potential is generated in the SA-node (1)
and innervate the atria to contract.
The potential passes through the AV-node (2) and
AV-bundle, which extends through the fibrous skeleton. With a short delay, the potential
passes through the branching bundles (3) and out through the Purkinje fibers (4), where it
innervates the ventricles to contract and the cycle is completed [2].
RF coagulation of myocardium works by resistive heating. A sinusoidal current (of
500-750Hz [4]) travels from the electrode tip of the catheter through the myocardium,
intervening tissues, and down to the grounding patch electrodes, which are adhered
to the patients back. The resistive heating results in coagulative necrosis close to the
catheter, and reversible hyperthermia in surrounding areas.
Cryo treatment works by rapid cooling of the catheter tip with a cryogenic gas
such as pressurized liquid nitrous oxide (N2O). As the liquid inside the catheter tip
vaporizes, it absorbs heat from surrounding tissues, thereby freezing the myocytes.
The electrical properties of the myocytes are reversibly blocked, when reaching a
temperature of approximately −30◦C. If the temperature is further decreased to −60
to −80◦C for 4 minutes, the damage is permanent [4]. This makes it possible for
physicians to better control the impact of the treatment.
Laser coagulation works by photothermal interactions. When the photons are
absorbed in the tissue, the radiant energy is converted into thermal energy, which
dissipate throughout the tissue. Dependent of the wavelength, pulsation and beam
shape, the absorption (and coagulation process) can be deeper and with less surface
coagulation, which could prove beneficial to e.g. ventricular treatments.

Complications
Several complications to the ablation procedure can occur. The complications include,
but are not limited to, thromboembolisms, cardiac tamponade, atrio-esophageal fis-
tula, pulmonary vein stenosis, Phrenic Nerve Injury (PNI), vagus nerve injury, and
AtrioVentricular (AV)-block [6, 7]. The incidence of these complications seems to de-
crease with the experience of the operator [8], which suggests that better understand-
ing and surveillance of the damage and temperature development, during ablation
procedures, is needed. The mentioned complications are in some cases due to uncon-
trolled coagulation or vaporization during the procedure. One risk of vaporization
is the steam pop. A steam pop is an explosive rupture of the cardiac wall, which
can lead to new arrhythmias, intracardial hematoma, thromboembolic events, and
cardiac tamponade among others.
1.2
Hypothesis
The hypothesis in this thesis is, that a novel laser treatment option for cardiac ar-
rhythmias can be developed using a numerical model based on Monte Carlo (MC)
modelling for light distribution, finite element analysis for heat propagation, and
Arrhenius damage integral for denaturation. The numerical model should be able
to predict steam pop generation, damage profiles, and temperature distribution in
the tissue at different laser properties. A laser treatment recommendation for differ-
ent cardiac arrhythmias should be developed to make the treatment more specific,
minimize the doctors’ learning curves, and mitigate the thermal risks seen in clinic
today.

CHAPTER 2
Materials and
Methods
This chapter describes the numerical model setup, tissue properties, experimental
approach, and data extraction.
2.1
Numerical model setup
The numerical model is built on the MCmatlab software. MCmatlab is a 3D MC
light transport solver with heat diffusion and tissue damage developed by the DTU
Biophotonics team [9]. The MC modelling of light distribution in tissue is a computa-
tionally intensive but very accurate numerical model. The temperature evolution is
based on light absorption and finite element heat propagation throughout the volume.
The damage is determined by a first-order Arrhenius equation dependent on time and
temperature. The basic setup of the code can be seen in Appendix A.
The numerical model is based on a homogeneous cuboid of myocardium. The
input parameters for myocardial properties can be divided into three groups: 1: The
optical properties, including the absorption coefficient (µa), scattering coefficient (µs),
and anisotropy factor (g), 2: The thermal properties including Thermal Conductivity
(TC) and Volumetric Heat Capacity (V HC), and 3: The kinetic properties for dam-
age including Arrhenius activation energy (Ea) and Arrhenius pre-exponential factor
(A). The thermal and kinetic properties are tissue specific. The optical properties are
both tissue and wavelength specific. In order to understand the myocardial function
and properties, the histology of the tissue should be considered.
2.1.1
Cardiac histology
The heart consists of three histologically distinct layers:
The endocardium, my-
ocardium, and epicardium, of which only the middle layer is of interest in this thesis.
The myocardium is the most prominent layer of the heart. It mainly consists of car-
diac myocytes (see Figure 2.1), loose connective tissue, and capillaries.
==PAGE:16==
6
2 Materials and Methods
Figure 2.1: Histology of cardiac myocytes [2]. Cardiac myocytes usually have a single
(centered) nucleus, myofibrils with repeating sarcomeres, and many mitochondria. The cells
are often branched and connected by junctions within the intercalated discs.
The myocytes are branched and connected by intercalated discs, where gap junc-
tions allow the action potential to spread. This electrical coupling makes is possible
for the muscle cells to contract in synchrony. The cardiac myocytes contains my-
ofibrils with repeating sarcomeres, and have many mitochondria, enabling a high
contractile force [10]. They cannot divide effectively, meaning that they are rarely
replaced, once they are lost. This is the property exploited in coagulative procedures,
as the coagulated tissue should not be replaced by new arrhythmogenic tissue.
Specialized cardiac myocytes, called Purkinje fibers, running in the subendocardial
layer, only have few myofibrils, no intercalated discs, many mitochondria, gapjuctions,
and contains much glycogen. This makes their electrical conduction much faster than
regular myocytes [11]. The conduction in the Purkinje fibers has major impact on the
synchronous beating of the heart, and it is within this system that many ventricular
arrhythmias are initiated [11].
The microstructure of the cardiac myocytes are different from skeletal and smooth
muscle cells, and the optical properties of this tissue differs slightly from other muscle
types, mainly due to the amount of mitochondria, which, together with the nucleus,
have very strong scattering properties [12].

conducted from existing literature on myocardial tissue. This section describes the
optical, thermal, and kinetic parameters found in literature.

2.1.2.1
Optical
Three optical parameters for the wavelength λ = 980 nm are used for the model;
absorption coefficient (µa), scattering coefficient (µs), and anisotropy factor (g). The
attenuation of light in tissue is dominated by scattering over absorption in the 600–
1600 nm spectral range [12]. This is seen in Table 2.1, where the scattering coefficient
of myocardium is around two orders of magnitude higher than the absorption. The
λ of 980 nm for the laser is chosen due to its appreciable amount of absorption and
scattering in the tissue as calculated below.
Scattering
The scattering properties found in literature are summarized in Ta-
ble 2.1. The scattering properties of light in tissue can be described by µs and g.
The scattering coefficient µs is the probability per cm that a photon is scattered.

count on the SSR is investigated in order to estimate an appropriate amount of
photons to simulate, when using the model.

2.3.1.2
Irradiation procedure
The initial model parameters and laser geometry was used to make the protocol for
the irradiation.
It was decided to investigate the steam pop generation to get a
conception of when steam pops arise, since these are unwanted side effects of ablation
procedures.
Different power and duration settings were modelled using the initial model to
get an approximate range for the experiment. Considering the obtained maximum
temperatures and damage profiles in the simulations, a protocol for the experiment
was formed with Continuous Wave (CW) power of 2.5 −4 W and irradiation times
between 7.5−40 s. 21 experiments were performed in total, all with start temperatures
of T0 = 23 ± 1◦C. Four of the experiments were replicates, so a total of 17 different
set settings were used. The uncertainty of the settings were ±0.05 W for the power
and ±4 ms for the duration.

After irradiation, Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) was performed on each sam-
ple to check for signs of vaporization. Then, the samples were snap-frozen and sliced
with a scalpel in order to obtain cross sectional measurements of the damage. These
measurements were obtained by image processing in ImageJ.

Snap-freezing
Snap freezing hardens the tissue and preserves its morphology and dimensions, so

5.3
Ablation and coagulation
The definitions of coagulation and ablation in literature are ambiguous. Thomsen et
al. (2011) [31, p. 496] differentiates between tissue ablation and functional ablation.
Tissue ablation is the removal of tissue mass, while the functional ablation is the re-
moval or modification of tissue function. When the ablation term is used in the clinic,
it is usually of functional meaning, which for arrhythmia is achieved by coagulation.
In this thesis, the ablation term was used as tissue ablation. It was chosen to look
for vaporization signs in the OCT as indications of ablation. The sample without
vaporization signs on the OCT, still showed ablative signs in its cross-section shown
in Figure 5.1.
The dark area in the center of the lesion is surrounded by a white band of co-
agulation. The tissue whitening is an indicator of thermally induced necrosis to the
tissue and is thus the goal of the coagulation procedure. The darkening of the center
indicates that other thermal responses might occur within the tissue. These responses
include carbonization and caramelization.

5.3.1
Laser-tissue interactions
Typically, laser treatment of tissue either exploit the photothermal, photomechani-
cal, or photochemical interactions. In the NIR, the photochemical interactions are
insignificant [31, p. 619], and the photomechanical stresses only occur with short laser
pulses < 10−6 s [41]. J. Pearce (1990) [42] suggests that the thermal biological effects
includes different thermodynamic processes, of which only the lower-temperatures
(< 150◦C) follow the first-order kinetics of damage accumulation.
The numeri-
cal model proposed in this thesis is therefore only applicable for treatments with
Tmax < 150◦C. The experiments used for the model development all have estimated
Tmax > 190◦C as seen in Table 3.3 and the first-order kinetics used in the numerical
model is thus not accurate for the damage prediction. The table in Appendix D is re-
produced from S. Thomsen (1991) [41], and shows the temperature onset of different
pathological photothermal effects.
5.3.1.1
Vaporization
When the water in the tissue reaches temperatures above 100◦C, it undergoes a
phase change from liquid to gas. When the rate of vaporization becomes faster than
the rate of vapor diffusion throughout the tissue, the photothermal effects become
water dominated [41]. Phase change energy, tissue desiccation, vacuole formation,
and changes of mechanical properties due to stress confinement, are all parts of the
vaporization processes [43, 31].
These processes change the thermal development,
and the kinetics of damage accumulation from following simple first-order processes
to become much more complicated [31, 41].
When the steam pressure from the phase transition, together with the pressure
from the dissociation products of denaturation, exceeds the tensile strength of the
tissue matrix, vacuoles are formed within the tissue [31, p. 498]. When the vacuoles
grow larger, they can be seen as either bubbles or detaching on the OCT-images
==PAGE:45==
5.3 Ablation and coagulation
35
depending on their location. The vacuoles exerts a rising pressure on the surrounding
tissue as they grow, increasing the thermoelastic tensile stresses to surrounding areas
[43]. If the heating continues, a steam pop will eventually occur due to bubble collapse.
Jet formation upon bubble collapse as described by Vogel et al.
(2003) [43] can
result in complications such as cardiac tamponade or even fistulas, if it happens
close to the epicardium. Atrio-esophageal fistulas are rare, but the mortality rate
is higher than 80% and survivors are often left with serious disabilities [6].
The
temperature development, vaporization processes, and bubble formations needs to be
closely monitored in order to mitigate this risk. If the heating is discontinued right
after vacuoles are detected, the steam from the vacuoles should diffuse out of the
tissue. The tissue will then either collapse the bubble and restore the normal matrix
at the vaporization site or retain the bubble structure, dependent (among others) of
the pressure the vacuole exerted on the surrounding tissue [31].
If the rate of vaporization is slower than the rate of vapor diffusion, the vapor
will evaporate from the tissue surface, while the vaporized water in the tissue will be
replenished by diffusion from the surrounding tissue [44]. This effect is seen Figure 3.3,
where the tissue surface has sunken towards the center.
The protein denaturation and membrane dissociation can be described with sim-
ple first-order kinetics such as the Arrhenius equation, but ablation, vaporization,
and carbonization processes are complicated physicochemical processes, that are not
accounted for in the numerical model.
In Section 3.4, it was attempted to find a predictor of vaporization through the
numerical model. The Ωmax was chosen as the best parameter for this, as it takes
both the temporal and thermal approximations as inputs.
The analysis showed that for this specific laser used with the power and duration
range specified in the experiment, bubble-formation would happen when the simula-
tion predicts an Ωmax > 106, with steam pops occurring around Ωmax ≈107. When
vaporization begins, the numerical model will overestimate the temperatures in the
tissue. This yields much higher Tmax than what is occurring in the tissue, and there-
fore also a much higher Ωmax than what gives physical meaning in form of protein
denaturation as discussed above. Nevertheless, when experimenting with lasers with
other beam geometries or power settings than used in this thesis, it could prove use-
ful to use the numerical model to establish an approximate Ωmax for the transition
between coagulation and explosive ruptures.
Vaporization processes should be monitored during treatment, which could be
done by measuring impedance, as it is done for RF-ablation procedures [45], or black-
body radiation as suggested by Franz et al. (2020) [40]. The latter have shown to
detect ex vivo tissue temperatures > 70◦C in laser applications [40], which could
prove useful for further development of the model.
5.3.1.2
Pulsation
Typical effects of laser pulsation is based on thermodynamic and phase change pro-
cesses [31], which are not included in the numerical model. The dynamical processes
==PAGE:46==
36
5 Discussion
critical for correct modelling of pulsation effects includes the above-mentioned phase
transition processes, photomechanical effects, and optical property changes.
Bogni et al. (2010) [37] investigated CW vs. pulsed energy deposition in order to
evaluate the temperature distribution within a vascular soldering joint. By pulsing
the laser, it should be possible to modulate the damage process in the tissue. When
using the numerical model established in this thesis, the pulsation of the laser does
not change the damage profile in comparison to CW.
The model established by Bogni et al. (2010) [37] was composed of different layers
with different thermal and optical properties, making them able to optimize the energy
deposition in relation to the different diffusion rates. In the numerical model proposed
in this thesis, only one tissue type was modelled, so this heat deposition rate between
diffusion rates could not be established.
A possible extension of the model could have been to add an endocardial layer
on top of the myocardium with different properties in order to investigate if it would
be possible to coagulate the myocardium without damaging the endocardium.
If
this extension had been made, the pulsation might prove to create different damage
profiles than the CW, as seen in [37].
The damage predicted by the numerical model for pulsed lasers is not reliable.
When vaporization happens within the tissue, a pressure is built up. If this pressure
generation happens faster than it can diffuse, bubble formation starts to happen
which eventually leads to the unwanted steam pops.
If the pulses are short enough (<1ms), and generating high temperatures (>
100◦C), photomechanical interactions such as shock wave emissions become significant
for the damage [43, 31]. The thermoelastic stresses to the surrounding tissues due to
photomechanical interactions lowers the tensile strength [31].
Neither vaporization nor mechanical effects are considered in the numerical model,
which means that the pulsation study is not representative of a clinical outcome.
By including the damage-dependent scattering properties in the model, one might
simulate results from the pulsation of the laser which have different damage geometries
than the ones irradiated with CW. This might be in better correspondence with the
effects seen in [37] and discussed in [31, p. 632], where the damage due to thermal
diffusion is minimized.
Pulsed lasers should give a high peak damage, but the surrounding tissue will, due
to rapid diffusion, not be as damaged as with CW. The application might be useful
for small point ablations in the clinic, but these effects are yet to be modelled.
5.4
Treatment recommendations
As mentioned in the introduction; many types of cardiac arrhythmias exist and require
different kind of treatments. Typical sites for ablation treatment are the cavotricuspid
isthmus for atrial flutter [46], the pulmonary veins for atrial fibrillation, the AV-node
for AtrioVentricular Nodal Reentrant Tachycardia (AVNRT), around the tricuspid
valve for AtrioVentricular Reentrant Tachycardia (AVRT), and in the lower chambers
==PAGE:47==
5.4 Treatment recommendations
37
for Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) [47]. When coagulating dysrythmias at each of these
sites, different surrounding areas are in risk of unwanted treatment.
The cavotricuspid isthmus is in anatomic proximity to the right coronary artery
[48]. If the coronary artery is occluded, the SinoAtrial (SA)-node and AV-node can
become ischemic, and thus disrupting the cardiac rhythm even more. It is therefore
of importance not to coagulate too deep, which can be difficult as the thickness of
the isthmus changes during the cardiac cycle. A wide and superficial coagulation
geometry might be of use in this situation. A LG01 beam type with a large waist
might prove beneficial in order to coagulate on the cavotricuspid isthmus without a
heating the underlying coronary artery.
Isolation of the pulmonary veins takes a long time, as four veins should have
circumferential coagulation. CardioFocus has developed a laser balloon [49], which
makes isolation of each of the veins faster than point-by-point ablation. There is a
risk of diaphragmatic paralysis, when performing Pulmonary Vein Inclusion (PVI)
of the right pulmonary veins, since they are situated very close to the coagulation
target [49]. In this case, a superficial coagulation should be targeted with a steep
temperature gradient, making sure not to damage the underlying phrenic nerves. It
might thus prove beneficial to use a Gaussian beam with a small width, with a high
power for a short time in order to only treat superficially near the nerves.
The coagulation treatment of AVNRT, comprises of isolating one of the two path-
ways within the AV-node [47]. The AV-node is small, and if treating either both
pathways, or the AV-bundle, the signals from the atria can no longer travel to the
ventricles. This incident is called a complete heart block, and the patient would need
an implantable device to restore the heart rhythm [47]. It is thus important to have a
narrow beam without too much thermal diffusion in depth and to surrounding areas.
The treatment recommendation for AVNRT is the same as for the PVI of the right
pulmonary veins.
AVRT includes the ortho and antidromic reentrant tachycardias, and the Wolf-
Parkinson-White (WPW)-syndrome, which are caused by accessory pathways be-
tween the atria and ventricles [47]. For accessory pathways near the right free wall,
the coagulation should be broad to prevent post-procedural recovery of the pathway.
A LG01-beam with a broad width could prove useful in order to assure that the electri-
cal isolation persists. For accessory pathways near the septum though, it might prove
useful to decrease the width of the LG01-beam in order not to disturb the AV-signal.
VT-ablation is either performed by the endocardial approach as considered through-
out this thesis, or by an epicardial approach, where the catheter is inserted through
the chest and into the pericardium. The choice of approach is based on the anatomical
origin of the arrhythmia. If an endocardial procedure either could result in transmural
coagulation in the thick ventricles, or if it could ensure epicardial ablation without
damaging the endocardium, this would be a less invasive procedure than an epicar-
dial approach. None of the results obtained in this thesis was able to have this deep
coagulation (0.5-1cm) without extreme temperatures with risks of steam pops. If
increasing the fiber radius or using a pulsed laser though, one might be able to get
deeper coagulation, but this is yet to be investigated.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
A numerical model of the myocardium was established with the MCmatlab software
for a wavelength of 980 nm, and used for exploring the effect of different laser beams
on tissue.
The parameters for light propagation, heat transfer, and damage integral for swine
myocardial tissue were determined with ex vivo experiments and literature studies.
This was done with a methodical fitting procedure, reducing the residuals from model
to experiment. The changing thermal properties during treatment were included, but
the changing optical properties observed still remain to be modelled.
The model can be used for exploring the spatial and temporal effects that different
laser settings can have on the denaturation process when internal tissue temperatures
is kept below the vaporization threshold, which is approximately 100◦C. Above this
temperature, the vaporization kinetics were found to be much more complex than
what could be described by the simple first-order reaction rate included in this model.
The model predicts that the LG01 beam shape can obtain the same coagulation
profile as the Gaussian beam with lower peak temperatures and thus with lower risk
of vaporization.
The numerical model can predict the optimal beam waist for a specific laser fiber
radius and treatment endpoint. For the proposed laser fiber, with a diameter of 4
mm, a beam waist of 20-100 µm resulted in much lower peak temperatures than the
small 0.35 µm waist, resulting a lower risk of steam pops for the same treatment
dimensions.
Treatment recommendations for specific arrhythmias were proposed based on the
results obtained from the numerical model and typical complications reported in
literature.
The treatment of arrhythmias in the clinic, could use a numerical model like the
one presented in this thesis for damage prediction. It should not stand alone though,
as it is based on homogeneous tissue structures, and therefore does not account for
the variances in tissue structures and properties in the individual treatment targets.

CHAPTER 7
Outlook
The numerical model should be further developed in order to establish a more accurate
model considering the dynamical changes during the laser treatment of tissue.
The next steps could be:
a) The numerical model should be developed to include the effect of fractional
damage on the optical properties. This should result in a more reliable model
for the denaturation dynamics anticipated during treatment. For the scattering
properties it might be modelled as opposite logistic functions of µs and g.
b) A new experiment should be performed and a new fitting should be conducted
with the same procedure as proposed in this thesis. Using the established nu-
merical model, a protocol for the experiment should be established, where the
expected maximal temperatures are below, or in proximity to, 100◦C. This
should be done in order to minimize the inaccuracy caused by vaporization,
which is not accounted for in the model.
c) The model geometry should be expanded to include the endo and epicardial
layers and maybe even water flushing on top.
The treatment effect of the
different optical and thermal properties for each layer could then be investigated.
