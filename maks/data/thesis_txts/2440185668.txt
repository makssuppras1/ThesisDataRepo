Abstract
Background and purpose: An important problem in combined PET and CT (PET-
CT) which is yet to be fully addressed is the propagation of CT artifacts to the
PET image during the CT-based attenuation correction (AC) procedure.
By the
reduction of such errors an improvement in cancer assessment, treatment and response
evaluation is likely to follow. In this thesis, we have investigated the clinical value of
a combined dual energy CT (DECT) and inpainting-based metal artifact reduction
(IMAR) technique for AC of PET in presence of CT metal artifacts. head and neck
cancer (HNC) patients with metallic dental fillings or implants were the focus of our
investigation.
Methods: Our work was based on a dental phantom setup and scans of 108 patients.
A model was developed to transform dual energy (DE) virtual monoenergetic (VM)
reconstructions at the 130 keV level to the 120 peak kilo Voltage (kVp) energy domain.
The model was applied to images acquired using combined VM and IMAR (VM-
IMAR) and used for AC of PET after a transformation (VMt-IMAR). The method
was evaluated in comparison to a gold standard using the phantom setup. Patient
data was used to investigate the relative PET error (∆%,PET) as a function of CT
error (∆CT). An analysis of tumor volumes was performed based on absolute volumes,
Jaccard Index, mean surface distance (MSD), Hausdorff distance, standard uptake
value (SUV) and ∆%,PET.
Results: In presence of metals, VMt-IMAR reduced ∆%,PET from a range of [min;max]
[−47.3; 63.6] % to [−19.9; 35.3] %. The technique had a positive impact on tumor de-
lineation as measured using tumor volumes and MSD (p = 0.029 and p = 0.008,
respectively). VMt-IMAR had no effect on the SUVmean and SUVmax of the tumor
volume. We found an approximately squared relationship between ∆%,PET as a func-
tion of ∆CT. In presence of metals, the average maximal ∆%,PET ( ¯∆%,PET, max) in a
cohort of 19 patients was increased from [mean ± sd] 7.17 ± 6.96% to 17.63 ± 12.10%
(p = 0.014). We estimated ∆%,PET,max = 5% in the tumor at 7 cm from the metal.
Conclusion: We have proven the feasibility of using combined DE and IMAR recon-
structions for AC after applying a transformation of the VM image to the 120 kVp
energy domain. While there is room for further development on the applied trans-
formation, the clinical impact of the technique on metal artifacts was considerable,
especially if patients are scanned for radiotherapy purposes. Our results indicate,
that VMt-IMAR was useful for reducing the clinical impact of metal artifacts in
HNC patients scanned with PET-CT.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This thesis is concerned with reducing the error in combined PET and CT (PET-CT)
caused by CT metal artifacts during PET attenuation correction (AC). The combina-
tion of two metal artifact reduction (MAR) approaches will be used: dual energy CT
(DECT) virtual monoenergetic (VM) extrapolations and an inpainting-based metal
artifact reduction (IMAR) algorithm. The combined method is abbreviated as VM-
IMAR. The motivation for studying this topic is described in Section 1.1 and the aim
of this work is presented in Section 1.2. In Chapter 2, some of the fundamental ideas
and principles used underway in the thesis are reviewed.
1.1
Motivation
As a major success of modern medicine, high-income countries have seen a strong
reduction in the burden of infectious disease in the 20th century. This development
has left society with non-communicable disease as the most common cause of death,
and cancer as one of the largest and most complex public health challenges of our
time.
According to the World Health Organization (WHO), cancer is the second
leading cause of death globally with 8.8 million deaths in 2015.1
In this work we will focus on cancers of the head and neck region.
Three of
the most common types of head and neck cancer (HNC) are located in the lip, oral
cavity and pharynx. In 2012 these three cancers were responsible for 300.000 annual
deaths globally. Smoking is the most common cause of incidence2 [1]. In Denmark
the number of HNC patients has been increasing considerably over the past 40 years
with an incidence rate of 9.1 pr 100.000 citizens in 1980 to 17.4 pr 100.000 in 2014.
This development is explained mainly by a rise in oropharyngeal and hypopharyngeal
cancers which in turn is largely attributed to increasing human papilloma virus (HPV)
infection rates in high-income countries [2, 3, 4]. By 2020, the annual incidence of
HNC could surpass that of cervical cancer [5].
The management of cancer depends on the stage of disease at the time of diagnosis
and state-of-the-art cancer therapy requires the involvement several specialties. Op-

2.1
Imaging with photons
Computed tomography (CT) and positron emission tomography (PET) have the prin-
ciple in common that photons are detected and used to reconstruct images. In PET,
the measured photons originate from annihilation of a positron which collides with an
electron after emission from a radioactive molecule, which is injected into the body.
The γ-rays that originate from annihilation radiation, exist at a specific energy and
are referred to as monochromatic or monoenergetic (henceforth the latter term will
be used). Contrary to PET, CT is based on measurements of a polyenergetic beam
of photons originating from an X-ray tube traversing through the body to a detector
on the other side Figure 2.1.
2.1.1
Photons in CT: Brehmstrahlung
Brehmstrahlung is the emission of a photon beam, which occurs upon the rapid
deceleration of charged particles (in this case electrons) in a dense metallic object
such as Tungsten. The phenomenon is used to create a photon beam in an X-ray
tube. Brehmstrahlung results in a polyenergetic ray typically in a range of 0 to ∼120
kilo electron Volt (keV). Because low-energy photons are absorbed before penetrating
the body, an aluminum filter (or more advanced filters) is used to remove low-energy
photons. The result is a photon beam of energies in a range of typically ∼40 to ∼120
keV.
==PAGE:34==

For convenience, polyenergetic X-ray beams are referred to by the tube voltage
setting used to produce them. The polyenergetic spectrum referred to as 120 peak
kilo Voltage (kVp) typically consists of a spectrum of energies ranging from ∼40 to
∼120 keV with an intensity peak around two thirds of the peak kV, in this case ∼80
keV.
2.1.2
Photons in PET: 511 keV γ-rays
Georg de Hevesy was awarded the Nobel prize for his contribution to the discovery
of methods to chemically synthesize radiotracers. Versions of such radiotracers are
used in PET imaging today.
The most commonly used radiotracer is 2-deoxy-2-18F-fluoro-D-glucose (18F-FDG)
which consists of a radioactive 18-F molecule bound to a glucose molecule. Positrons
emitted from the nuclear core of the radioactive 18-F atom are annihilated almost
immediately upon emission by interaction with an electron, causing the emission of
two photons (γ-rays) separated by approximately 180 degrees at an energy of 511
keV.
A solution containing 18F-FDG can be injected into the patient. The radioactive
sugar molecules are physiologically transported into cells. This process results in a
distribution of 18F-FDG in the body depending on the cellular activity in a given
tissue. Examples of active healthy tissues with a high sugar uptake are the heart,
excited muscles, the brain and kidneys. Pathological areas presenting a high sugar
uptake could be tumors or infected tissues.

2.2
Attenuation correction (AC)
Unless a sample (for example an FDG-injected patient) consists of exactly one molecule
and is surrounded by vacuum, a γ-ray will inherently be attenuated by the material
present in the sample itself. This phenomenon is referred to as ”self-attenuation”. In
the case of diagnostic PET-CT the self-attenuation is caused by the patient tissue
through which the rays must traverse in order to reach the detectors. Self-attenuation
depends completely on patient anatomy and changes for every scan. In order to cor-
rect the PET signal for the effect, an accurate map of µ(x, y) in the line of flight of
each γ-ray is necessary.
Because CT images are fundamentally estimates of µ(x, y), a modification of the
images can be used for AC of PET data. However, as CT photon beams are polyen-
ergetic, a transformation is needed from µ(E = 120kVp) to µ(E = 511keV).

2.4
Artifacts in CT
The most important factors causing CT metal artifacts are under-sampling and non-
linear effects such as beam hardening, scatter and noise [21]. Metal artifacts depend
on the volume, atomic number Z and geometry of a given material, and hence artifacts
are more articulate in presence of metals such as iron, platinum, lead and quicksilver
and less pronounced in metals such as aluminum and titanium.
2.4.1
Under-sampling
High Z metals can cause the full photon beam to be attenuated in a region, hence
causing bright streaks in the reconstructed image. Low amplitudes measured in neigh-
boring detectors will cause dark streaks, as the measurements will be amplified inher-

2.4.2
Beam-hardening
Beam-hardening is effectively a filtration of the polyenergetic spectrum caused by
the scanned object. In measurements performed using a polyenergetic beam, lower
energies are preferentially absorbed. In effect, slightly varying spectra depending on
the projection angle are the basis of estimates of µ(x, y). The beam spectrum will be
displaced towards a higher effective energy Eeffective, causing the beam to ”harden”.

2.4.3
Scatter
Scatter arises due to the Compton scatter effect which is largely continuous through-
out the CT spectrum as discussed in Section 2.3. The effect is responsible for streak
artifacts in each projection in the direction of highest attenuation, which are very
similar to beam hardening artifacts. Even small amounts of scatter causes significant
streaking artifacts. Between several metal (or other dense) objects, light and dark
streaks will connect the metals. In addition, a dependency on the size of the scanned
object is observed [24].

2.6
Inpainting-based metal artifact reduction (IMAR)
The fundamental idea behind IMAR is using a simple threshold for metal detec-
tion followed by a rejection of all values affected by metals in the sinogram domain.
The rejected values are estimated by interpolation using measured intensities from
neighboring detectors [35]. The resulting images yield significantly improved images,
however a considerable drawback is the blurring of HU-values in proximity of high Z
materials. This problem was solved to a considerable extent by Meyer et al. in 2010,
using the idea of voxel-wise normalization by a prior image decomposed into bone
and soft tissue, followed by interpolation and de-normalization, see Figure 2.4 [36].
In a further effort to preserve information around bone edges in proximity of metals,
a frequency split approach was introduced by the same research group in 2012. In
this work, the high frequency information from a standard (non-MAR) image is used
in proximity of the metal in combination with the low-frequency information from an
IMAR [37]. Using this approach, useful information from the original image is pre-
served. The resulting method is a combined in-painting, normalized and frequency
split method. Frequency split normalized metal artifact reduction (FSNMAR) is one
==PAGE:42==

mon names MAR or IMAR. In this thesis, the inpainting-based MAR reconstructions
were performed using a commercial implementation of FSNMAR (iMAR, Siemens,

2.7
Combined DECT VM and IMAR
The effect of using combined VM and IMAR (VM-IMAR) extrapolations and IMAR
was studied by Bongers et al. [38] in 46 patients with either dental or hip prosthetics,
finding that a clear effect of IMAR and an incremental improvement by combining
the technique with VM extrapolations. I have also previously studied the combined
effects of the two techniques in a Radiotherapy context in Kovacs et al. [34] (see
Appendix A). Based on evaluations by five clinical experts, this study showed, that
VM-IMAR images were preferred over standard IMAR of 120 kVp images in presence
of artifacts from dental fillings and spinal screws. In addition, the most accurate
contours in a phantom study were performed using VM-IMAR. The study agrees
with Bongers et al., that the improvement from MAR is strongest. However, we found
in some cases that artifacts were reduced more effectively using VM extrapolations
at the 130 keV level. Especially in presence of spinal screws the technique showed
significantly improved results in comparison IMAR only. Illustrative examples of the
effects of IMAR and VM-IMAR are presented in Figure 2.5.

The main idea in this work is to use VM extrapolations from DECT in order to remove

hence to use such images to reduce the CT errors and their propagation to the PET
image during AC.
2.8
Summary
In PET-CT, scattering and attenuation of the PET signal is corrected using a bi-linear
transformation of the CT image, approximating the linear attenuation coefficient
µ(x, y) for each point in space.
In this process, artifacts in CT caused by dense
objects, especially metals such as prostheses and dental fillings, cause under- and
overestimates in the attenuation corrected PET image. A number of methods exist
to reduce these CT metal artifacts. We will use a MAR method which combines a dual
energy virtual monoenergetic extrapolations at the 130 keV level and an inpainting-
based approach (VM-IMAR). We will attempt to use such artifact reduced images
to achieve a more correct AC of PET data in order to increase the probability of
accurate clinical decisions using PET-CT.

3.7.1
Reference image in phantom analysis with metal
To create a gold standard reference image, it was necessary perform a rigid registration
between the phantom scans with and without metal. The image without metal was
used as the reference image R and the image with metal as the template image T .
The registrations were performed using crosscorrelation based registration followed by
a manual adjustment. For all registration, minc was used3. Using the transformation
of T to R, the metallic tooth was segmented and the HU-values from this region
were copied to R, creating the gold standard image Rgold. An inverse transformation
was performed of Rgold creating the gold standard image in the image domain of T
resulting in the final Tgold reference image in the metal domain. Tgold was used for
the phantom analysis with the presence of metal.

Statistics

4.3.4
Summary of phantom study results
In order to make possible attenuation correction using VM, a method using a PWL
model was presented to transform VM images to the ACCT domain, enabling the
possibility of using VM-IMAR for AC. The method was evaluated in a two section
phantom study with and without metal. In addition to the error introduced by using
the more noisy VM images, we presented the effect of errors from beam-hardening
artefacts in a a standard ACCT reconstruction. These errors ranged up to a maximum
of 4.5%, while the maximal errors in the VMt image ranged up to -13.5% due to
underestimates around the teeth. In the study without metal, underestimates in the
dental region were found. This error was reduced using the developed transformation
model. In the study with metal, a celar propagation of metal artifacts to the µ-map
and subsequently to the PET signal are seen. It is found, that the combined VM-
IMAR technique transformed to the ACCT domain reduces ∆%,PET,max from 64%
to 35%. An example of the transformation in a patient case is shown in Appendix F

scanned using standard and DECT. All scans were reconstructed using combined
VMt and IMAR (VMt-IMAR) according to the procedures outlined in Section 3.4.
21 % of patients (23 of 108) were identified with a visible PET-positive tumor. In
12 % of the total cases studied (13 of 108), it was not possible to evaluate the tumor
on the PET image due to a combination of weak PET signals and heavy artefacts.
This cohort was not studied further in this work. For the 23 PET-positive patients,
raw PET data was unavailable in two cases. In an additional two cases, the data was
not successfully reconstructed in e7tools tools. Hence a total of four patients were
excluded. Of the remaining 19 cases, 12 patients were identified with an overlap in
the axial direction between the pet-positive tumor region and metal artifacts. Of the
PET-positive group, 63% were categorized (12 of 19) as having an overlap between
the tumor and metal in the axial direction.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
The aim of this thesis was to evaluate the ability of a novel MAR method to address
PET errors originating from metal artifacts in the CT image used for AC. It has
previously been shown, that CT artifacts impact PET hence decreasing diagnostic
confidence and hampering the use of PET-CT in patients with malignant tissues in
proximity of metal implants [15, 41]. HNC patients have been pointed as a cohort
where a specific need exists for the problem to be addressed [42, 43, 44]. The extent
of the challenge of metal artifacts in HNC is emphasized by our finding in this work,
that 83% of 108 patients scanned over a period of 102 days had dental metal fillings
or implants. In some cases, the metal implants were small and insignificant to AC,
however, visible metal artifacts were identified in the µ-map in 23% (n = 25) of the
scanned patients.
In this work, we have proven, that it is feasible to use a combined DECT VM and
IMAR technique for AC of PET with results indicating a positive clinical impact.
In search of a MAR solution we turned to previous experience as well as peer-
reviewed literature and selected the combined VM and IMAR method. As several
authors point out, the majority metal artifacts are reduced by the IMAR technique
[38, 44].
However, this technique leaves clearly visible cupping biases and beam-
hardening artifacts for which reason we chose to use the VM-IMAR approach [34, 31,
38, 13, 26, 27].
We needed a method which allowed reconstruction of PET using VM images at the
130 keV level using the PET-CT scanner of e7tools. This was done by transforming
the 130 keV VM images from virtual monoenergetic domain to the standard ACCT
energy spectrum.
Models were developed using three separate data sets, and the
patient-based model was selected due to the certainty derived from the large number
of patients used (n = 102). Additional arguments for this choice were the observed
non-linearity in the phantom-based data and the similarity of the model to the manual
measurement-based model (the reader might compare equations (4.1) and (4.3)).
Using our implementation of VMt-IMAR for AC had the benefit that it was pos-
sible to limit the use of DE to regions with metals. Hence, we were able to reduce
patient dose from DE and to limit the decreased noise properties of 130 keV VM
images to regions with metals. Notably, the property of beam-hardening reduction
was preserved in the transformed VMt-IMAR images.
We found, that metal artifacts did not change the overall tumor SUVmean and
SUVmax. However, a mean change was not necessarily expected, because CT artifacts
==PAGE:94==
66
5 Discussion
are equally likely to result in over- and underestimates in the µ-map and hence in
the PET signal. In addition, artifacts typically only occur in a small part of the total
tumor volume, reducing the effect on the SUV in the full tumor volume. Similarly,
measurements of the impact of artifacts SUVmax have the weakness, that the SUVmax
voxel could be geographically located in a region unaffected by artifacts.
Tumor volumes were compared by the absolute volumetric differences, Jaccard
index, MSD and the Hausdorff distance.
We considered it important to include
all four metrics because a volumetrically small geometrical change, with a branch-
like geometry, which could have a significant impact on patient treatment, might
not be captured by simple volumetric or Jaccard index-based comparison [45]. To
address such differences, the Hausdorff distance and MSD are useful. Intuitively, the
Hausdorff distance answers the question: ”What is the largest distance between two
compared volumes?”. Analogously, the MSD answers the question: ”How large is the
distance between the two volumes on average?”.
Finally, we considered the relative PET error ∆%,PET in tumor volumes. The
results of the analysis can be used conveniently to estimate ∆%,PET,max in a given
region. For example, the derived model can be used to estimate ∆%,PET,max = 5%
can be expected within 7 cm of the metal in a given tumor volume. In other words,
when areas such as the oral cavity, tongue, hard and soft palate, lips, salivary glands
and the neck between the oropharynx and the hard palate are of diagnostic interest,
MAR methods for AC should be considered.
Others have investigated the use of MAR techniques for AC. However, to the best
of my knowledge, this is the first study of VM-IMAR reconstructions for AC of PET.
In addition, we are the first to evaluate the effects of CT MAR methods for PET AC
in a patient cohort and for tumor delineation.
A combined DE VM and IMAR approach for ACCT were studied in a dental
phantom setup by Schabel et al. in 2017 [44]. In this work, no additional value was
found using VM extrapolations at the 190 keV level. This finding might be attributed
to Schabel et al. using a frequency split approach to VM image computation, in
which optimum contrast 70 keV images are combined with higher keV level images in
the frequency domain. I have previously shown, that 70 keV images increase metal
artifacts, which could explain why the DE approach used by Schabel et al. did not
show an impact [34].
In 2017, Vos et al. studied the effect of an inpainting-based MAR method on
PET in 21 patients [46]. The group found an added value using an iterative MAR
algorithm (with no use of DE scans), and a significant influence on the SUV was found.
We added knew knowledge to this finding by showing an approximately squared
relationship between the absolute CT metal artifact error and the relative PET error.
In addition, we present a clinically useful relationship between CT and PET error,
which shows that voxels with a given value of ∆CT decays rapidly as a function of
distance from the metal. In a scenario, where a tumor is located within one cm of
the metal, the physician can use our result to predict that more than a third of all
voxels in a region will be affected by more than 5% PET error.
Nahmias et al. concluded in 2008, that considerable CT artifacts did not impact
==PAGE:95==
5 Discussion
67
clinical quantification of the PET signal [47].
We agree, that the PET signal is
relatively robust to metal artifacts, however, we have also shown a clear propagation
of CT artifacts to the PET signal.
Especially when tumor delineation technique
involves an SUV threshold-based approach, over- and underestimates of the tumor
signal had an impact on the contoured volumes as seen for example in Figure 4.23.
Our work has a number of limitations. CT based AC assumes, that a bi-linear
scaling can be used to calculate µ as done by Carney et al. [18]. This assumption
is acceptable for soft tissue and bone; however, it is violated for materials such as
iodine, gold, quicksilver, lead and amalgam due to the photoelectric effect related k-
edge [17, 18]. These challenges can be corrected using DE for material differentiation
by utilizing the property, that two different materials appearing with approximately
the same HU-values at one energy (such as bone and iodine at 120 kVp) will appear
with different HU-vales at another energy. This was previously been done by Kinahan
et al. in 2006 [48].
For the scope of this thesis, metals are altogether categorized in one group, with a
CT HU-value of approximately 3000, which is the same value of µ for metal which is
used in clinical standard ACCT. We restricted the transformation of CT values to HU-
value range covered by the measurement-based model (approximately [-1000 to 1500],
see also Figure 4.2). In addition, we chose to use time-of-flight PET reconstructions
(PSFTOF), which has previously been shown to reduce PET metal artifacts [49, 50].
While correct metal values should be considered in future work, our results show
clearly, that over- and underestimates in PET are largely geometrically aligned with
over- and underestimates in the µ-map used for AC, confirming the impact of the
error originating from CT voxels with artifacts.
The 130 keV VM technique had the drawback, that it increased image noise.
Further, we found that standard ACCT reconstructions contained beam-hardening
artifacts which were not visible in corresponding VM images. However, we our VMt
based AC resulted in an increase of ∆%PET in the standard ACCT µ-map.
One
approach to reduce the effect is by sinogram smoothing as proposed by Xia, Alessio,
and Kinahan [51]. However, it was our assessment, that the effect of this noise was
relatively minimal when considering the smoothing applied to the µ-map. The error
in the VM was within in an acceptable range for this work, however, there is room
for improvement in the presented method.
It was a limitation of our work, that we used the patient-based model to transform
the phantom CT images which were acquired at 100 kVp. This caused an inherent
visible bias of the bone region, even after transforming the VM image. In future
work, the model should be adjusted to fit for 100 kVp exactly. However, even with
this expected bias we were able to show a considerable decrease in ∆%PETusing the
VMt-IMAR technique.
It should be noted, that using the patient-based model, the assumption was made,
that a transformation developed based on a large cohort of patients was effectively
transforming the images to the 120 kVp spectrum. In reality patients were scanned at
80, 100, 120 and 140 kVp, which explains the increasing variance in the patient-based
model residuals (as seen in Figures 4.2 and C.11). However, the assumption is justified
==PAGE:96==
68
5 Discussion
to some degree by the empirical observation, that the majority of patients in the
cohort were scanned at 120 kVp and by the clear observed similarity between ACCT
and VMt-IMAR reconstructions in regions without metal artifacts. It is possible to
modify the model to take into consideration each energy level, and this should be
done as a part of future work.
With regards to the patient study, we rely on the assumption, that VMt-IMAR
images can be used as a acceptable reference in lack of a gold standard.
While
these reconstructions are visibly not perfect, the artifact reduction is clear, and our
assumptions are justified by the result in the phantom study, that the CT as well as
the PET error is reduced using the novel technique.
With regards to future work, the idea of AC based on VM-IMAR should be further
improved by 1) avoiding the amplification of residual metal artifacts in the VM-IMAR
images, 2) using a unique transformation depending on the acquisition energy in each
image, and 3) using DE to correct CT HU-values of metals. In spite of the need for
these improvements, we were able to show, that the new method yields significantly
reduced errors in the PET signal.
In summary, the overall aim of this M.Sc. thesis was to evaluate the ability of
novel MAR methods to solve clinically relevant challenges in PET-CT associated with
CT metal artifacts. We limited our examination to the specific problem of artifacts
propagating to the PET image through the µ-map, and presented a solution which
allowed for the use of VM-IMAR reconstructions for AC. The effect of our method
was evaluated in a phantom study and based on scans of 108 HNC patients.
In
the phantom study, we have shown using a gold standard, that VMt-IMAR reduced
PET error considerably. In our patient study, we were able to show that the relative
PET error increased approximately with the square of ∆CT. With respect to clinical
significance, we have shown that metal artifacts do not result in a change in the overall
tumor SUVmean and SUVmax, however the variability in tumor delineation and tumor
signal increased. In effect small tumors in regions of artifacts might not be detected
on rare occasions. Based on the 19 analyzed PET-positive HNC patients included in
this work, CT artifacts will impact PET-based delineation and hence treatment in
about 15% of the scans.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In a novel method, a combined DECT and IMAR method for metal artifact reduction
was implemented and used for AC of PET. The technique was evaluated and shown
to reduce the propagation of CT metal artifacts to attenuation corrected PET data.
Our results indicate, that the new method has a positive clinical impact on PET-
based tumor delineation variability in presence of metals.
We were able to present a direct relationship between the PET error in tumors
and the distance from the tumor to the metal. Using the relationship, we estimated a
maximal PET error in the tumor volume of more than 5% within 7 cm of the metal.
We recommend that a MAR technique for attenuation correction of PET is im-
plemented for HNC patients, especially if performed for radiotherapy planning. The
use of the technique should be further investigated in cohorts of patients for other
types of metallic implants.

Background and purpose: Two techniques for metal artefact reduction for computed tomography were
studied in order to identify their impact on tumour delineation in radiotherapy.
Materials and methods: Using specially designed phantoms containing metal implants (dental, spine and
hip) as well as patient images, we investigated the impact of two methods for metal artefact reduction on
(A) the size and severity of metal artefacts and the accuracy of Hounsﬁeld Unit (HU) representation, (B)
the visual impact of metal artefacts on image quality and (C) delineation accuracy. A metal artefact reduc-
tion algorithm (MAR) and two types of dual energy virtual monochromatic (DECT VM) reconstructions
were used separately and in combination to identify the optimal technique for each implant site.
Results: The artefact area and severity was reduced (by 48–76% and 58–79%, MAR and DECT VM respec-
tively) and accurate Hounsﬁeld-value representation was increased by 22–82%. For each energy, the
observers preferred MAR over non-MAR reconstructions (p < 0.01 for dental and hip cases, p < 0.05 for
the spine case). In addition, DECT VM was preferred for spine implants (p < 0.01). In all cases, techniques
that improved target delineation signiﬁcantly (p < 0.05) were identiﬁed.
Conclusions: DECT VM and MAR techniques improve delineation accuracy and the optimal of reconstruc-
tion technique depends on the type of metal implant.

Modern advances in image-guided radiotherapy (IGRT) have
increased the accuracy of dose delivery and decreased the need
for large planning target volumes (PTV) [1]. The high level of accu-
racy in delivery has, however, increased requirements for accuracy
in target delineation. Even deviations on a scale of a few millime-
tres may result in increased irradiation of organs at risk (OARs) or
geographic target miss, and hence have signiﬁcant negative impact
on patient outcomes. Today, it is recognized that target and OAR
delineation variability is a major source of uncertainty in radio-
therapy (RT), and considerable work has been done in order to
reduce factors that lead to this variability [2–6].
A major cause of delineation variability is streaking and beam
hardening artefacts from metallic implants. Metallic artefacts are
a signiﬁcant clinical issue in RT, causing decreased delineation con-
ﬁdence, decreased dose calculation accuracy, and increased time
spent on manually delineating pixels affected by the artefacts
[7–11].
The two main methods described in the literature for reduction
of metal artefacts are dual energy computed tomography (DECT)
virtual monochromatic (VM) extrapolations [12] and iterative
metal artefact reduction (MAR) algorithms [13–15]. DECT VM
images between 95 and 150 kilo electron volt (keV) levels have
been found to reduce beam hardening artefacts from various
metallic prostheses effectively [16–18], while VM images around
40–70 keV show some clinical value by improving contrast to noise
ratios (CNR) between soft tissues [19,20]. MAR algorithms also
show clinical value by reducing metal artefacts [21,22] and
improving dose calculation accuracy [23]. Using DECT and MAR
in combination may enable a further reduction of artefacts [24].
In this work, we focused on the impact of metal artefact reduc-
tion on target delineation, hypothesizing that DECT and MAR tech-
niques
can
reduce
RT-speciﬁc
uncertainties.
We
evaluated
different combinations of DECT VM and MAR techniques both
quantitatively and qualitatively in order to identify the optimal
solutions for radiotherapy imaging in different anatomical regions.
Furthermore, we investigated the potential of DECT VM and MAR
to improve the accuracy of target delineation in patient and phan-
tom images.

Materials and methods
We compared six different reconstruction methods using den-
tal, spine, and hip implant phantoms and corresponding patient
cases. The six evaluated reconstructions were (1) 120 peak kilo
voltage (kVp) (standard-of-care) (2) 120 kVp MAR (3) 70 keV DECT
(4) 70 keV DECT MAR (5) 130 keV DECT and 6) 130 keV DECT MAR.
Image acquisition details are described in the supplementary
material. 70 and 130 keV levels were selected because they repre-
sent the mean energy of the polychromatic spectrum in a 120 kVp
image while maximizing the CNR and the optimal balance between
beam hardening artefact reduction and soft tissue contrast, respec-
tively [17,25].
These reconstructions were evaluated according to (A) the
size/severity and HU-values of the artefacts, (B) the resulting image
quality as evaluated by ﬁve observers and (C) the delineation vari-
ability from ﬁve observers.
Phantoms
Three phantoms (Fig. 1) with metal implants were constructed
to represent common causes of metal artefacts as seen in clinical
practice: (1) a set of human teeth ﬁxated in parafﬁn wax with a
removable amalgam-ﬁlled tooth (in-house construction) (2) surgi-
cal spine screws (Globus Revere Pedicle Screw System and K2M
titanium rod) (3) a hip implant (Zimmer Segmental System
Proximal Femoral Provisional and Femoral Head Provisional).
Low contrast targets were arbitrarily shaped of polycaprolac-
tone (Polymorph, Thermoworx Ltd.) and placed in a water-tank
together with the respective implants. The metal implants could
be removed without affecting the positioning of the other objects
in the tank. CT scans of each phantom were acquired with and
without the presence of the metal implants.
Patients
This study was approved by the institutional review board and
the regional ethics committee (H-15006887). Patients who were
referred for radiotherapy, had metal implants and were older than
50 years were offered inclusion. Informed consent was obtained for
experimentation with human subjects. The DECT scans were per-
formed immediately after the treatment planning CT scan (64-
slice single-source CT scanner, Siemens Somatom Deﬁnition AS,
Siemens Health Care, Forchheim Germany). Images were then
reconstructed using the MAR algorithm (iMAR on VA48A SW, Sie-
mens AG, München Germany). VM images were reconstructed
using
the
Dual

application
(syngo.CT
Monoenergetic
2016, Siemens AG). Detailed settings and scanning procedure are
described in the supplementary material (section ‘‘Image Acquisi-
tion” and Table A1).
Study A: Artefact quantiﬁcation
Phantom scans with and without metal present were acquired
within one imaging session. The low-contrast targets and the sur-
rounding water were segmented in the images without metal.
These contours were then transferred to images with metal.
A program was written (MATLAB 2015b, MathWorks Natick
Massachusetts, U.S.A) to evaluate the area and severity of the arte-
facts, as well as the accuracy of HU-value representation of the
water and low-contrast target within a region surrounding the
metal implant.
The artefact area, HU-value median and interquartile range was
calculated, where the latter was interpreted as an expression of
artefact severity.
The 95% conﬁdence intervals (CI) of the pixel intensity values of
water and of the low-contrast target regions were estimated from
the non-metal reference. If pixel intensity values in the image with
metal were within the expected 95% CI for each corresponding
region, the pixels were considered ‘‘accurately represented”. Pixels
outside this range were considered ‘‘artefact”.

Study B: qualitative assessment of patient images
The six reconstruction methods were evaluated by ﬁve obser-
vers (three oncologists and two radiologists) in CT scans of three
patients with dental ﬁllings, a spine implant, and a hip implant
respectively. A program was developed in MATLAB for pairwise
side-by-side comparison of the six reconstructions. The program
presented the observers with all possible unique combination pairs
of the six reconstructions in random order. Each combination was
presented twice (ﬁrst left to right, then right to left resulting in a
total of 30 comparisons). By default, images were displayed in a
standard soft-tissue window (centre 40 width 350). Observers
were able to change the window setting and to simultaneously
scroll through both reconstructions in order to evaluate each axial
slice with metal. For each comparison, the observers were asked to
choose the image they would prefer for RT delineation.
The comparison of a reconstruction to each of the ﬁve other
reconstructions was considered an individual observation, and a
preference for a reconstruction in the pairwise comparison was
considered a positive outcome. A multivariate logistic regression
analysis was conducted to identify factors (VM energy and/or use
of MAR) signiﬁcantly contributing to the probability of observers
having a preference for a given reconstruction. Standard-of-care
reconstructions (120 kVp) were considered the baseline, to which
all other reconstructions (120 kVp MAR, 70 keV DECT, 70 keV DECT
MAR, 130 keV DECT and 130 keV DECT MAR) were compared.
Interaction between the factors (energy and use of MAR) was
included in the analysis as well.
Study C: delineation accuracy
Prior to delineation, the order of presentation of reconstructions
was determined according to a ranking of the results of study (A)
and (B). This was necessary to prevent information from previous
reconstructions with better visibility inﬂuencing results on subse-
quent reconstructions. The images were presented to the ﬁve study
observers in the determined order in standard delineation software
used in our clinic (Eclipse version 13.6, Varian, Palo Alto, California,
USA). The observers were blinded to the reconstruction type and
delineation on the reference image was performed last.
In order to obtain the true contour of the low-contrast target,
delineation was performed on a high-quality scan of the low-
contrast target in air, and then transferred to the phantom images.
A standard of care 120 kVp phantom scan without metal was
included for reference. In order to provide metrics for both posi-
tional and volumetric differences between contours [26], we mea-
sured
the

A Friedman-test was used for primary analysis and a pairwise

Discussion
In this work, we evaluated six reconstructions from the per-
spective of accurate HU-value representation, preference of clinical
observers in patient images and delineation accuracy. To the best
of our knowledge, this is the ﬁrst study to identify the optimal
use of DECT and MAR techniques in clinical routine for treatment
planning CTs and to quantify the gain in terms of delineation
accuracy. While 130 keV DECT and MAR reconstructions showed

One strength of this study is that images were ranked based on
pairwise combinations of the reconstructions presented in random
order. This allowed the observers to compare several distinct
anatomical regions, such as bone edges and different soft tissues
in all slices of both images, allowing for a more informed relative
ranking of all reconstruction methods. Additionally, we eliminated
potential bias which could have arisen from the order in which
images were evaluated. Other studies have used a qualitative scale
to evaluate and rank image quality [21,28], which may limit the
information on relative image quality (e.g. if the scale lacks dis-
criminative power).
Under some circumstances, such as in a study by Riegel et al.
[30], a reduction of delineation volume is considered an improve-
ment, as this reduces the risk of treatment side-effects. However,
in the present study we do not measure the tumour volume, based
on reasoning clearly illustrated in Fig. 4: In several cases, the vol-
ume of the observer contours (green) are smaller than the true
contour (red). Hence, volume reduction should not be considered
an improvement.
One limitation of this study is the use of a single source scanner,
which required that the patient was scanned twice for the DECT
scan, once for each energy. A dual source scanner would be superior
for clinical applications; however, the bore size of current dual
source scanners is too small for some radiotherapy set-ups. Though
this was not explicitly tested, careful visual evaluation did not reveal
artefacts caused by shift in position. In general, it is a limitation of
DECT techniques that the decision of whether or not to obtain a
DECT scan must be made prior to the scan – in contrast, it is a beneﬁt
of the MAR technique that these reconstructions can be created
based on raw-data from the standard-of-care scans. Hence, to make
a decision about whether or not to use DECT, information about
patient implants should be acquired prior to treatment planning
CT scan acquisition. In addition, for dose calculation on DECT VM
images, the treatment planning system HU-value-electron density
curves must be speciﬁcally calibrated for each desired VM energy
with an electron density phantom. These factors make DECT more
cumbersome to implement than the MAR algorithm. However, the
results of this study suggest that DECT 130 keV is preferable to
MAR in presence of spine implants, and that combined DECT and
MAR is the preferred modality in some cases for dental and hip
implants. To optimize metal artefact reduction in the presence of
all types of metallic implants, the implementation of DECT VM
images for treatment planning should be considered.
DECT VM and MAR techniques improved the accuracy of HU-
value representation and had a positive, statistically signiﬁcant
inﬂuence on observers’ preference. In addition, the techniques sig-
niﬁcantly improved delineation accuracy, and reduced delineation
variability between observers. The artefact reduction technique of
choice depends on the type of metallic implant, and a software
environment where multiple reconstructions can be evaluated
simultaneously is recommended.
