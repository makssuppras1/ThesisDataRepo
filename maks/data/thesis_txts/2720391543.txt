Abstract
In this report predictions for the wind­induced transverse responses for a standard tall building from
the draft of the Eurocode will be compared to a wind tunnel experiment. The Eurocode draft contains
two annexes (G and H) which contain difference methods to predict the response acceleration and an
equivalent static load. The results from the experiment carried out using the standard building model,
Commonwealth Advisory Aeronautical Research Council (CAARC) building, show that the predictions
from annex G are twice the value of the responses gained from the experiment whereas the predictions
from annex H are slightly lower than the experimental results. It should be note that the CAARC building
model does not fulfill the slenderness requirement for annex H, whereas the building model is a perfect
fit for annex G.
The report covers the wind tunnel experiment along with the experimental data validation, visualization
of results along with discussion and comparison to similar experiments.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1
Introduction
Ever since Alan G. Davenport introduced the wind loading chain, which is used as the fundamental basis
for determining the loads on structures in codes and standards around the world, engineers and researcher
have moved the development further. In the draft of the Eurocode [1] two annexes have been added both
with the purpose of determining the wind­induced transverse vibration for tall slender buildings.
The scope of this report is to compare calculated values for wind loads and wind­induced vibrations
transverse to the wind direction for tall buildings, based on a 50­year return event wind, with results from
a wind tunnel experiment at Svend Ole Hansen ApS performed using a scaled model of the Common­
wealth Advisory Aeronautical Research Council (CAARC) building. This building is a standard building
model which have been used in several wind tunnel experiments around the world and therefore gives a
broad amount of comparable information. The experiment is therefore not the first of it kind but it has
not been possible to find a comparison of an experiment like the CAARC model with the Eurocode’s
two new annexes. Holmes [2] have however made comparison between an experiment with a similar
model to the CAARC building model and three standard codes: The Hong Kong Code of Practice, The
Australian/New Zealand Standard, and The American Standard (ASCE 7).
Holmes concludes that the Australian/New Zealand Standard is the best fit whereas the two other stan­
dards are producing lower values in the along­wind response comparison. In the across­wind response
Holmes only compares the experiment with the Australian/New Zealand Standard as it is the only stan­
dard to have a method for prediction of the across­wind response. Again the Australian/New Zealand
Standard has great predictions production results slightly greater than the wind tunnel experiment does.
The report will go though the calculations for both annexes in the Eurocode draft and describe some con­
cepts surrounding the annexes, here the information background material for annex G has not been found
in the same amount as for annex H, therefore some of the assumptions for annex G is based on Svend
Ole Hansen’s knowledge within wind engineering. Furthermore, the report will describe the wind tunnel
experiment and the instruments used, as well as the methods used to validate the data and analyze the
results.
A large part of the report is used to validate the data gathered in the wind tunnel experiment by comparing
it to similar wind tunnel experiments with the CAARC building model. This is done to make the report’s
conclusions as trustworthy as possible and for others to use the data without worries. Later in the report
the measured pressure during the experiment is visualized as a function of time to give the reader an idea
about the pressure as a result of vortex­shedding later described in Section 2.2.
Lastly analysis of the of the experimental results are made and compared to similar experiments. These
analyses contain a Power Spectral Density analysis to determine the very important Strouhal number St
which is linked to the vortex­shedding and a modal analysis of a Single Degree of Freedom system to de­
termine the acceleration of the CAARC building model. These analyses results are used in a comparison
with the predictions of the across­wind responses from the two annexes from the draft of the Eurocode.
1.0.1
CAARC building model
The experiment is carried out on a model of the standard CAARC building constructed by Svend Ole
Hansen ApS. The CAARC building was proposed by Wardlaw and Moss in 1969 [3] as a simple model
for comparison between experiments done with simulating the natural wind in various wind tunnels. The
hope was that a direct comparison of the model pressure measurement and dynamic response, would lead
to development of better techniques as well as a more considerable volume of test data being obtained
from wind tunnel testing world wide.
The full­scale CAARC building is a tall building measuring 600 ft in height and sides of 100 ft by 150
ft (183.88 m × 30.48 m × 45.72 m) shown in Figure 1.1. It is a rectangular with smooth surfaces on all
sides and a flat roof keeping the structure simple.

Chapter 2. Wind theory
2
Wind theory
This chapter contain some of the basis theory for fluid dynamic and introduce important concept such as
the Reynolds number Re, vortex­shedding Strouhal number St and scaling from the wind tunnel mea­
surement to the full scale of the CAARC building model.
2.1
Reynolds number
One of the most important concept within fluid mechanics is the Reynolds number, first introduced by Sir
George Stokes and later popularised by Osborne Reynolds. The Reynolds number is a ratio between the
inertial and viscous forces in the fluid and is used to determine in which regime the fluid is. The equation
of calculating the Reynolds number is shown in Equation (2.1),
Re = D · U
ν
(2.1)
where D is the diameter (or width) of the object, U is the flow velocity, and ν is the kinematic viscosity
of air as a fluid. For low (Re < 2300) Reynolds number the flow is laminar meaning that the flow layers
are moving smoothly with no to little mixing with each other. The flow is typical kept laminar in tubes or
pipes to lower the energy usage or in ventilation systems to lower the noise. Fluids with a high kinematic
viscosity like syrup can give an idea about a laminar flow. Reynolds numbers between 2300 and 2900
the flow is in a transition state between laminar and turbulent flow which means that the flow is neither
completely laminar or completely turbulent. The flow will in this transition regime have some of the
characteristic for both laminar flow and turbulent flow. For Re > 2900 the flow is fully turbulent where
the fluid particles are moving in a chaotic system and the different flow layers are completely mixed. Due
to the low kinematic viscosity of air and the relative high speed, the Reynolds number of natural winds
which will relevant to buildings are in the turbulent regime.
2.2
Vortex­shedding
One important phenomenon is vortex­shedding, which takes place in the flow regime around an object
when the Reynolds number, Re, is greater than 40. Vortex­shedding occurs when vortices from each side
of the object start to influence each other as they become unstable due to increased turbulence in the flow.
This is accomplished by the stronger vortex dragging the weaker vortex across the wake. Because the
two vortices rotate in opposite directions, the flow supply to the strong vortex is cut off, and the vortex
is shed. A new vortex is then formed in its place, and the previously weaker vortex becomes the strong
vortex, which continues to drag the new vortex across the wake until the flow supply to the newly strong
vortex is cut off and it is also shed. This process continues on as shown in Figure 2.1 where an example
of vortex­shedding around a square cylinder is shown.
As a result of this process, the pressure around the object changes, causing the drag and lift (across­flow
force) acting on the object to vary over time. The interesting aspect of this phenomenon for this project
is the lift forces acting on the object perpendicular to the flow. The frequency of vortex­shedding is
dependent on the Strouhal number, St, as expressed in the Equation (2.2),
St = fv · D
U
(2.2)
where fv is the vortex­shedding frequency. The Strouhal­number is affected by the shape of the object
placed in the flow due to the separation point of the vortex which will change and therefore influence
how the vortexes are shedding. Correlation length describes the length of which the vortex­shedding

2.3
Scaling

aforementioned phenomenon need to be present in the wind tunnel. The Reynolds number will often not
be in the same regime as the scaling between the size of the wind tunnel model and the real size model is
not the same scaling used for the wind velocity. In this case the CAARC building mode is scaled 1/383,

number is not scaled which could mean that some of the phenomenons that happens as a function of the
Reynolds number in different regimes such as the drag crisis is not present in the wind tunnel but in the
real world or the other way around.

Chapter 3. Eurocode numerical methods
3
Eurocode numerical methods
In this chapter the two numerical methods described in the Eurocode draft [1] will be carried out on the
CAARC building model. The end results for the two methods, given in Eurocode draft annex G and
annex H respectively, are both given as a peak acceleration and a peak value of the equivalent static
across­wind load per unit length. Annex G is based on Japanese and Korean codes which have become
from experiments both in real and in wind tunnel testing. It has not been possible to find much background
information about the terms and how or what the equations are based on, instead the details have been
discussed with Svend Ole Hansen and his knowledge about the annex and within wind engineering areas.
Annex H is based on Dyrbye and Hansen’s book [5], chapter 8 especially, which determine wind loads
on bridges, the annex uses the fact that a high­rise building is similar to a vertical cantilever bridge.
One of the difference between annex G and H is the involvement of aeroelasticity which is when a fluid
flow effects a flexible structure. This affect is included in annex H and not in annex G, the question is
how much the aeroelasticity adds load to the building. As annex H is based on very slender structures
these structures might have a more elastic body similar to an airplane wing. Whereas annex G is based
on slender buildings that are heavy and might have a high enough stiffness to avoid large vibrations.

4
Wind tunnel experiment
This chapter explains the purpose of the experiment made for this thesis, the experimental arrangement
used to complete the experiment, and the different components and instruments used in the experiment.
The experiment is done in a wind tunnel at Svend Ole Hansen ApS which has been used since 1991 and
has formed the basis for several publications. The wind tunnel is used daily for projects at Svend Ole
Hansen ApS, furthermore the wind tunnel is also checked by third­party companies to ensure the wind
tunnel creates the right flow to match reality.
4.1
Purpose of experiment
The purpose of this wind tunnel experiment is to compare the transverse loads on a simple high­rise
building measured during an experiment with the Eurocode draft [1] annex G and H, the two annexes
are described in chapter 3. To compare the annexes with the experiment, some assumptions for the
experiment are needed. Since the pressure cannot be measured all over the surface of the CAARC model
but only at given points the accumulated forces will not be completely accurate. This can be tested in an
High­Frequency Base Balance (HFBB) experiment with a model on a scale which then measures the three
dimensional forces and moments. However the two models would have to be exposed to the same wind
in the wind tunnel to be comparable. The HFBB experiment was not chosen to be a part of this project.
The wind tunnel experiment will also provide raw data which after being processed can be compared to
some of the intermediate calculations made in both annexes. This might lead to a broader understanding
of how the wind behave on the CAARC building model and other high­rise buildings. As mentioned
before several wind tunnel experiments has already been carried out on the CAARC model in different
scales. This experiment contribute more data from a verified wind tunnel which has been used for several
decades on project around the world. The data can also to be used in the future for Svend Ole Hansen
ApS in research or in comparison with future projects.
4.2
Experimental arrangement
In this section the wind tunnel experimental arrangement will be descried along with the instruments used
in the experiment. Some of the components are made by Svend Ole Hansen ApS for their wind tunnel
to cooperate with their in­house software. These components will not be described in details due to the
company privacy. The wind tunnel testing was all done in one day right after each other to minimize
errors. The testing was organised by the author of this report with the help of staff members of Svend Ole
Hansen ApS to set up the wind tunnel software and the arrangement of the wind tunnel boundary layer
set­up.

Pitot tube
A pitot tube is used to measure the flow velocity of the wind in the wind tunnel. The pitot tube is pointed
in the direction of the incoming wind flow, this flow will create a pressure which will push the fluid inside
the pitot from its stagnation point. The dynamic pressure is the difference between the total pressure and
the static pressure and is determine using a diaphragm inside a closed container. The static pressure on
one side of the diaphragm will push the fluid inside the pitot tube and the total pressure on the other side
will force the fluid the other way. This will mean that the fluid will move from its stagnation point to
an known location and determine the dynamic pressure which with Bernoulli’s equation can be used to
determine the flow velocity. Pitot tubes are widely used in the aircraft industry to determine the speed
of the airplane and it known to be very accurate. An explaining figure is shown in Figure 4.3 for better
understanding.

Chapter 5. Validation of experimental data
5
Validation of experimental data
In this chapter, the results gathered from the wind tunnel experiment are shown along with a preliminary
analyze to validate the results. The pressure data measured has been analyzed with the help of in­house
functions from Svend Ole Hansen ApS. These functions have been used to export the pressure data to
later do mathematical analyses. Some of the functions are used as part of the mathematical analyses such
as a Fourier transform or tube correction. These functions have been used to reach the goal for the project
without spending unnecessary time making new functions. The analysis in this chapter is used to verify
the pressure data from the experiment, compare them to other similar experiments, and comes with partial
conclusions. This has been given a lot of attention, as bad results might lead to the incorrect conclusions,
and furthermore this creates a confidence for the reader. The chapter briefly mentions an error in some
of the data collected in the experiment, this error is used to verify the some of the other data collected.
5.1
Pressure data from wind tunnel experiment
The output from the wind tunnel experiments consist of the pressure data that was measured on the
CAARC building model while the wind was blowing and the measured pressure during an offset. Addi­
tionally, there is data of the voltage applied to the fan and data from digital furness control that measures
the wind pressure from the pitot tube inside the wind tunnel. These data sets are all recorded for each of
the angles α tested, as shown in Table 4.1 and will have to be verified in order to see which angle is best
suited for the two center lines.
The first step is to calibrate the measured wind pressure while the fan was turned on with the offset mea­
surements recorded while the wind was still. This is necessary as the offset varies through experiment
which can be seen in Figure 5.1 note the big jump at the 20 second mark where the turntable has been
turned and the measurement of a new angle is starting.

correction process that takes the length and size into consideration. This process changes the frequency of
the measured wind pressure data but does not change the mean value of the pressure. The tube correction
is necessary to account for the dynamic response in the measurement system and is based on research
conducted by H. Bergh and H. Tijdeman in their report ’Theoretical and experimental results for the
dynamic response of pressure measuring system’.

qm(z)

Chapter 6. Experimental analysis
6
Experimental analysis
In this chapter further analysis of the experimental data is made. These analysis consist of Power Spec­
tral Density analysis to determine the important Strouhal number, St, for the two angles chosen in the
chapter 5. A visualization of the measured wind pressure on the surface of the CAARC building model
is also displayed in this chapter. This has been done to show the reader how the wind pressure fluctuate
with the vortex­shedding frequency, which is used to determine the Strouhal number.
6.1
Strouhal numbers
As described in section 2.2 the Strouhal number is crucial factor in characterizing the vortex­shedding
frequency using non­dimensional expressions. The vortex­shedding frequency, fv, used to determine
the Strouhal number as shown in Equation (2.2) is obtained through Power Spectral Density analysis or
Power Spectrum analysis. The Power Spectral Density analysis uses a Fourier Transformation to convert
the across­wind force fcross(z, t) from a time domain to a power spectrum in a frequency domain. This
conversion transforms the input signal, which is the force as a function of time, into a force as a function
of frequency by breaking down the signal into individual sine waves. The across­wind force fcross(z)
is calculated by subtracting the force per unit length on one transverse side from that on the other. For
α = 0 deg the across­wind force fcross(z) = fE(z) −fW (z), where fE(z) and fW (z) is the force per
unit length found in Equation (5.6) for the East side and West side respectively. For α = 90 deg the cross
force fcross(z) = fN(z) −fS(z).
The power spectra are displayed in Figure 6.1a and Figure 6.1b showing the power as a function of the
frequency for α = 0 deg and 90 deg respectively for the weighted sum of the force. The forces are
weighted by its height. The vortex­shedding frequency will be the most dominate frequency in the power
spectrum. The reason that there can multiple frequencies where vortex­shedding is taking place is due to
fluctuations in the turbulence wind flow by adding this fluctuation wind speed u′ to Equation (2.2) the
equation changes to Equation (6.1),

7.3
Experimental sources of error
Experimental data is only as good as the quality of the equipment that it is collected with, and this wind
tunnel experiment is no exception. The experiment was conducted in a wind tunnel that is used regularly,
along with the accompanying equipment. These things are routinely tested and calibrated by Svend Ole
Hansen ApS and checked by third­party companies to ensure the equipment is in good working order. It
is therefore deemed that any potential errors in the equipment or the wind tunnel itself are of negligible
size in relation to the conclusions drawn from this study.
The experiment was carried out with minimal interference inside the wind tunnel to minimize the possi­
bility of human errors. Any human errors that may have occurred would likely stem in the calculations
performed during the analysis of the experimental data, which the author thoroughly checked with exter­
nal supervisors to reduce the chance of error to the lowest possible level.
Many of the inputs in the numerical method calculations of the Eurocode draft have been replaced with the
actual values measured during the wind tunnel experiment, such as the turbulence intensity and Strouhal
number. This ensures the methods are compared with the experimental results instead of more inaccurate
assumptions, even though they are based on scientific justification.
It is possible that using a finer mesh for collecting pressure data would have improved the results. Mea­
suring the three axial forces and moments at the same time as the pressure with a finer mesh around the
surface of the CAARC building model could have resulted in a more precise result. The experiments
conducted in Melbourne [4] used a similar pressure data gathering mesh method.
Taking the sources of error into consideration, it is believed that the results are credible.

Chapter 8. Conclusion
8
Conclusion
The wind tunnel experiments conducted at Svend Ole Hansen ApS to find the wind­induced transverse
responses have been successful, as the pressure data collected from the wind tunnel experiment on the
CAARC building model has been validated and compared with several similar experiments. These com­
parisons include important wind engineering factors such as the Strouhal number, pressure coefficients,
drag coefficients, and power spectra, all of which have shown to be within acceptable deviations.
Calculations following the draft Eurocode two annexes regarding across­wind responses have been per­
formed by the author and quality assured by supervisors. The predictions from the annexes are compared
in­depth throughout the discussion by comparing power spectra, using different mode shapes, and static
responses (forces and accelerations). This has ensured a scientific justification comparison between the
two annexes and the performed wind tunnel experiment.
The response results from the comparison show that the predictions from annex H fit best to the wind
tunnel results, while annex G predicts more conservative responses for both accelerations and equivalent
static forces. This is the case for both with linear and hyperbolic mode shapes, whereas the predictions
from annex H are near spot on. As Holmes [2] expresses, ”Ideally the codes and standards should produce
predictions slightly above the expected values from a wind­tunnel test” this is not the case for the predic­
tions from annex H, which in some cases are have negative deviations, even when adding a conservative
background response factor.
Determining which of the two annexes is best suited to predict design loads and accelerations for tall
buildings is a paradox. The predictions from annex G mean that tall buildings like the CAARC building
would be designed for load much greater than they would ever experience in a 50­year period. On the
other hand, the predictions of Annex H are very accurate, but some of the results are below the values
for the response obtained from the wind tunnel experiment, which would mean that the tall buildings
designed using this annex might be designed for too low loads.
It should be noted that the CAARC building model does not comply with the slenderness requirement
specified in annex H, therefore annex H was not meant to be used for the CAARC building. While the
CAARC building model aligns right into annex G, it is concerning that the resulting responses obtained
from this annex are much greater than the responses from from the experiments. The power spectra from
annex G appear to match well with the experimental power spectra, and the expression for the Strouhal
number is close to the actual value. This indicate that the significant deviation is located in the calcula­
tions that follow the power spectra.
One solution could be to examine the Australian/New Zealand Standard, which Holmes have compared
with wind tunnel testing on the CAARC building with favorable outcomes.
