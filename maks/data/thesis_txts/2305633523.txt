Abstract
Many restaurants have smooth rigid surfaces made of wood, steel, glass, and concrete. This often
results in a lack of sound absorption. Such restaurants are notorious for high sound noise levels
during service that most owners actually desire for representing vibrant eating environments, al-
though surveys report that noise complaints are on par with poor service. This study investigated
the relation between objective acoustic parameters and subjective evaluation of acoustic comfort
at ﬁve restaurants in terms of three parameters: noise annoyance, speech intelligibility, and pri-
vacy. At each location, questionnaire surveys were ﬁlled in by customers, acoustic parameters
were measured, and recordings of restaurant acoustic scenes were obtained with a 64-channel
spherical array. The acoustic scenes were reproduced in a virtual sound environment (VSE) with
64 loudspeakers placed in an anechoic room, where listeners performed subjective evaluation
of noise annoyance and privacy and a speech intelligibility test for each restaurant noise back-
ground. It was found that subjective evaluations of acoustic comfort correlate with occupancy
rates and measured noise levels, that survey and listening test results agreed well and that, in the
VSE, speech reception thresholds were similar for the ﬁve reproduced restaurant backgrounds.
Prediction models for noise annoyance, quality of verbal communication and sense of privacy,
were proposed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
More and more restaurateurs are following the ideal of having smooth rigid surfaces made of
wood, steel, glass and concrete in their restaurants. This general aesthetic design is modern and
simple, while surfaces are easy to clean. A negative effect of rigid surfaces is lack of absorption
which consequently keeps sound longer in the room. Restaurants are notorious of having very
high sound pressure levels during service and this can be explained by the fact that many owners
desire as much noise as possible. Noise is associated with an exciting dynamic environment
and therefore many restaurant owners overlook the problems that too high noise levels can result
in. High noise levels can cause guests to feel uncomfortable and mentally exhausted, and staff
undergo the risk of getting hearing damages when working long hours. In many countries the
noise level is required to be below 85dB during an 8 hour work shift by law and this limit is often
reached if not exceeded. Sound levels easily reach more than 80 dB during peak hours (Battaglia
2015), (Rindel 2010), (To and Chung 2014), due to guests being packed into small conﬁned
spaces. The evolution of the restaurant business has resulted in increased noise complaint rates,
and some places noise is a bigger concern to guests than poor service which was traditionally
the biggest source of complaints until recent times (James 2013). Besides the health of the staff
and guests complaining there is also an economic incentive to keep a close eye on the acoustics
in restaurants. The survey (James 2013) reported that 72% of restaurant goers actively avoid
establishments that are too loud. It is inarguable that restaurant noise is an important problem
that must be attended, which gave motivation for doing this study.
1.2
Overview of previous work
Previous research has been done regarding this topic, and different opinions have been presented.
(To and Chung 2014) reports that noise level depends on occupancy rates rather than dimensions
like ﬂoor area or ceiling height and this is supported in (Astolﬁand Filippi 2003) where speech is
reported as the main noise source in eating establishments. The article (Rindel 2010) concluded
that noise levels depent on the number of people and the equivalent absorption area per person.
Later the revised article (Rindel 2012) proposed a prediction model for quality of verbal com-

1.3
Research objectives
This study will attempt to describe various factors that inﬂuence the acoustic comfort, so that
restaurateurs in the future will have a better foundation to create a restaurant that can provide
the best possible experience for both staff and customers. In order to do so, 3 major parameters,
when evaluating acoustic comfort in restaurants, will be addressed: noise annoyance, speech
intelligibility and sense of privacy. Based on this scope, the research objectives are:
• Noise: To determine the main cause of noise annoyance and how this is subjectively per-
ceived. To construct models for predicting noise level and noise annoyance in restaurants.
• Speech: To investigate speech quality and what it is dependent on. To construct models
for predicting quality of verbal communication in restaurants.
• Privacy: To bring clariﬁcation on the term "sense of privacy" in restaurants.
This will be done by comparing objective acoustic parameters with subjective evaluations of
comfort. Relationships between acoustic properties and subjective ratings have been difﬁcult to
establish so far due to very high variations in the answers of questionnaires. In order to get more
controlled subjective evaluations a laboratory experiment will be performed trying to simulate
the event of being in a restaurant.

Theory
In this chapter the various theoretical methods and procedures used throughout the project are
presented. This includes: measurement of acoustic parameters, theoretical basis for constructing
surveys, speech intelligibility and the sound reproduction technique "ambisonics".
2.1
Room acoustic parameters
The goal of the project is to understand how acoustic comfort is achieved in restaurants. Sim-
plifying the scenario, one have a room and a certain amount of people producing speech shaped
noise. In reality there are many other noise sources, but speech noise is the dominant source at
least in modern restaurants with many people situated in a relatively small conﬁned space. The
surfaces and obstacles in the rooms either absorb or reﬂect incoming sound waves, and one way
to describe how a room reacts to noise is by producing an impulse response and measuring how
energy decays. The most known acoustic factor is reverberation time and many other factors
are based on this parameter. The sections below describe the theory behind the chosen acoustic
parameters.

2.2
Questionnaires
To evaluate the subjective reaction to a stimuli a survey must be conducted and this can be done
online, through telephone, via personal interview or in other ways. In this project the ﬁndings
of the article (Fields et al. 2001) were used as a basis for designing a questionnaire chosen to be
printed on paper and administered by the staff at each restaurant. The article suggests a method
for conducting surveys and provides a reliable measure of general reactions to noise in residential
environments that are comparable internationally and between languages. The method suggests
an interval-level measurement scale with equally spaced reaction answers giving the opportunity
to do linear regression.

questions ask the respondent to imagine a window of time in the past and how he feels about a

2.2.2
Development of noise-reaction questions
The earlier mentioned recommendation for a noise-reaction question was developed through a
series of steps. Previous research had to be looked trough to see what have been done before and
what kind of wording was used. The wording in particular had to be judged and translated by
experts in linguistics and people with no expertise in language had to verify that the wordings
seemed natural. Last a study was conducted to provide empirical data on the scale and wording of
the verbal answer possibilities. The research done and thoughts given to create the recommended
noise-reaction question is presented below.
Question
The questions seen in Figure 2.1 are direct, they mention a source of annoyance, a time frame
and ask directly about the respondents feelings towards the noise source. The questions are also
closed meaning the respondent have a set of possible answers they can give, in this case; the
degree of annoyance. Since residential noise is assumed to be negative or neutral - which is
widely accepted in community surveys (Fields et al. 2001) - the questions are phrased with on a
unipolar scale ranging from not annoying at all to extremely annoying. This furthermore forces
the respondent to acknowledge that the question is referring to the negative inﬂuence of noise in
residential areas.
Answer scale
Figure 2.2 illustrates the chosen answer scales, a 5-point verbal scale and a 0-11 point numeric
scale. The basis for choosing a verbal scale was to enhance the communicative ability of the
survey between respondents and identities that will purposely use the results. Using general
known words as a modiﬁer for noise annoyance increases the understanding of people involved
more than numeric scores do. However, adding a numeric answering scale will improve reso-
lution and hence better support the use of linear regression. In general, using two scales rather
than one increases the reliability of results and reduce errors regarding misinterpretation in the
question-answer task. The basis for choosing a verbal scale with 5-points rather than 3-, 4- or
6-points was that the 3- and 6-point scale where rejected and the 5-point scale proved to better
ﬁt a noise-reaction survey than the 4-point scale. The 3-point scale where considered not to give
enough information and the 6-point scale was too long for telephone interviews. The 5-point
scale is superior to the 4-point scale in terms of resolution, it gives an extra alternative towards
respondents trying to avoid end of scales and because the most widely used 4-point scale in pre-
vious research ("not at all, a little, moderately, or very" did not match a linear scale in terms of
the intensity of the words. The intensity scores of the the four words where later found to be
1-13-44-77 and not equally distant as 0-33-66-100. The numeric scale was decided to go from
0-10 giving the respondent a clearly divided scale with the score of 5 in the middle and with 0 in
the end expressing "not at all" better than an end of 1 would do.

2.3.1
MUSHRA
The MUSHRA (MUltiple Stimuli with Hidden Reference and Anchor) test (BS.1534-3 2015) is a
tool developed for rating the quality of a given stimuli. The test was originally created to evaluate
the sound quality degradation of reducing the bit depth of a sound ﬁle, but has since been used
widely to do subjective evaluation of a given factor of interest. In this case 5 different sound ﬁles
can be played back by pressing button A through E, and the slider is then used for rating how
much the noise in each scenario is annoying the test subject. The reference signal can be used in
different ways, e.g. by denoting an extreme or end points. See Chapter 3 for more details.
2.3.2
Speech intelligibility
Every day people put themselves in a situation where they have to understand speech and often
this is done with background noise masking the transferred information. The amount of infor-
mation that is successfully transmitted from the talker to the receiver can be expressed as speech
intelligibility. More precisely this can be deﬁned as the percentage of words transmitted through
the systems channel that are correctly understood. The general way of conducting experiments
regarding speech intelligibility is to have a talker reading up sentences which the receiver has to
repeat. The talker then compares the answers with the actual words and note how many words
are correctly understood. When a background noise is introduced to the channel, one can then
determine speech intelligibility as a function of the SNR. The outcome of such a relation is a
psychometric function shown in Figure 2.4.
Figure 2.4: Example of different psychometric functions. Redrawn from (Malmberg 1970).
Speech Reception Threshold
According to (IEC 645-2 1993) the SNR at which 50% of speech is understood correctly is
deﬁned as the threshold of speech or the SRT. This value of 50% is the previous mentioned
standard’s deﬁnition but other percentages can be used as threshold of speech.

2.3.3
Sound ﬁeld reproduction
The previous mentioned techniques for subjectively evaluating acoustic comfort was carried out
in the Audio Visual Immersion Lab (AVIL) at the Technical University of Denmark DTU. The
laboratory consisted of an anechoic room with a spherical loudspeaker array for spatial sound
reproduction. Ambisonics has been widely studied for some time now due to its ability to pro-
vide spatial information beyond the horizontal plane making acoustic scenes more realistic, and
furthermore in this case with a loudspeaker array the directional cues due to the pinna are main-
tained for each individual test subject. Below the theoretical background for ambisonics is brieﬂy
summarized for this particular laboratory setup. For details on ambisonics see (Marschall 2014),
(Moreau et al. 2006) and (Meyer and Elko 2004).
Ambisonics
Ambisonics is a sound ﬁeld reproduction technique that describes a given sound ﬁeld by use
of spherical harmonic functions. When using a spherical microphone array one samples sound
pressure around a sphere and this is used to estimate the spherical harmonic components, which
describe the sound ﬁeld in a spherical region of space. When an estimation of a sound ﬁeld is at
hand one can reproduce that by expressing it as a decomposition of plane waves which, in this
case, would be signals emitted from loudspeakers assumed to be in the far ﬁeld compared to area
of reconstruction (the sweet spot). An encoded sound ﬁeld can be reconstructed using different
loudspeaker arrangements and this process is called decoding. In the following part a spherical

CHAPTER 3
Method
This chapter will present the methods and procedures used in the project. It describes how the
measurements of the acoustic parameters were carried out, how the questionnaires intended for
the restaurant guests were formed and how the psychoacoustic experiments in AVIL were created
and executed.
3.1
Overview of restaurants
In order to investigate the relationship between measurable objective acoustic parameters and
the subjective evaluation of the acoustic comfort in restaurants, a set of eating establishments
willing to participate was found. In total, 5 different restaurants were investigated with varying
features like size, capacity, reverberation time and general purpose. Furthermore all 5 restaurants
were located in Copenhagen, all designed with surfaces mainly consisting of concrete, wood,
metal, glass and leather. The reason for choosing these types of establishments was that they
represent the modern way of designing a restaurant with a tendency of using hard rigid surfaces,
hence the observations made would presumably lean towards the extreme case with very high
noise levels and high noise complaint rates. Evaluating these extreme scenarios and successfully
establish a guideline for improving the acoustic comfort will therefore be useful in many types
of restaurants.

Measurements of objective acoustic parameters
Objective acoustic parameters were measured by recording impulse responses at several different
source-receiver positions in compliance with (ISO 3382-2(en) 2008). This was done by using a
setup with a laptop, a loudspeaker and a microphone. The recordings were done in the restaurants
off-hours, and only a few staff members were present.
3.2.1
Equipment
A laptop with the room acoustic software DIRAC from B&K was used for producing output
signals and processing input signals. The sound source was a Dodecahedron speaker connected
to an ampliﬁer of which was set to 0 dB gain. The receiver of the impulse response was a
microphone of model AKG C34 which was connected to a control box of model AKG S42
and further connected to a microphone preampliﬁer of model RME Quad Mic 4-Channel. The
preampliﬁer gain was set to 35dB. The directional pattern of the microphone was adjusted using
the control box. See ﬁgure A.1 in appendix for setup overview.

Settings
The settings of the equipment were adjusted in the ﬁrst restaurant, and turned out to satisfy the
measurements for all 5 restaurants. In accordance to (ISO 3382-2(en) 2008) the sound source
must produce an impulse sound that allows a decay curve to start at least 35dB above back-
ground noise level for calculations of T20 and at least 45dB above background noise levels for
calculations of T30. The speaker ampliﬁer, the microphone ampliﬁer and the built in ampliﬁers
in DIRAC were adjusted so the peak level of the impulse sound resulted in a SNR of at least
35dB. These settings allowed to calculate T20 but also proved to be the limit of the system,
where no clipping was observed. For improved SNR an exponential sweep (e-sweep) was cho-
sen. This was possible because the sound source satisﬁes the criteria for spectrum and directivity
(ISO 3382-2(en) 2008). Furthermore the e-sweep was found to be more comfortable for the
staff present during the measurements. In all the restaurants, the reverberation time was initially
measured to test which sweep length was necessary and it turned out to be enough with a sweep
length of 1.37 seconds. According to (ISO 3382-2(en) 2008) air attenuation in large rooms is neg-
ligible if the reverberation time is below 1.5 seconds at 2 kHz and below 0.8 seconds at 4 kHz.
For all restaurants, that was the case. Since it was not possible to know the reverberation time on
beforehand the temperature and relative humidity were monitored during the measurements, and
no signiﬁcant deviations were observed with respect to air attenuation.

3.2. MEASUREMENTS OF OBJECTIVE ACOUSTIC PARAMETERS
19
Source-Receiver positions
The sound source and receiver were positioned respectively 1.3 and 1.5 meters above ground,
and preferably 1 meter away from any reﬂecting surfaces if possible. The reason for positioning
the source and receiver at this height was to prevent reﬂections from tables, chairs, pillars or
other interior. 3 decay curves were measured at each source-receiver position, and the receiver
positions for each source position were placed at least half a wavelength apart.1 If possible, 12 or
more different source-receiver positions were measured to satisfy the requirement for precision
method in (ISO 3382-2(en) 2008). See Appendix C for overview of measuring positions.
Measurements
For all source-receiver positions a set of decays were recorded and stored for later analysis. In
order to measure LF each impulse response was recorded twice. First with the microphone set
to omnidirectional pattern and second with the microphone set to ﬁgure-of-8 pattern. When
recording with ﬁgure-of-8 pattern the microphone was positioned as stated in (ISO 3382-1(en)
2009), with the null pointing towards the source and the two lobes pointing towards the horizontal
plane.
Sources of uncertainty
Sources of uncertainty were observed during the measurements.
• Distance from source or receiver to a reﬂecting surface: In certain measurements it were
not possible to follow the ISO standard in terms of keeping the recommended distance
between microphone and nearby surfaces above 1m. This was due to interior items like
tables and chairs being very densely packed.
• Source-receiver positions: Due to time constraints in the measurement phase, restaurant
measurements did not conform with the 12 different source-receiver positions required for
the precision method. Instead at least 6 different source-receiver positions were measured
which is the requirement for the engineering method.
• SNR: Even though the SNR between the peak sound pressure level of the impulse and the
background noise was monitored to exceed 35 dB at all time - and mostly in the interval be-
tween 40-50 dB - there were often bursts of noise coming from staff and kitchen. Attempts
to avoid this were made by redoing the measurement, but noise did potentially inﬂuence
the measurements.

3.3.1
Construction of questionnaires
Noise reaction questions for restaurant survey
The goal was to construct a questionnaire that used widely accepted practices and methods, and
was comparable with other noise-reaction surveys. This was done by using the same framework
as suggested in (Fields et al. 2001). To begin with a question for evaluating noise annoyance
was developed which would later set the basis for the questions about speech intelligibility and
privacy. First a question was made and then scale and answer words were chosen. The suggested
verbal question in Figure 2.1 names a time horizon and a location for the respondent to imagine
him or herself in, then noise from a speciﬁc source and ﬁnally asks the respondent about to which
degree he or she is annoyed by the noise source. The same procedure was used when making the
question but the time horizon and location were combined to [Thinking about your dinner,], the
noise source was formulated as [noise from the restaurant] and question about how the respondent
feels about the noise was kept the same. It was assumed that restaurant guests perceive noise as
either a neutral or a negative inﬂuence and hence the scale was chosen to be unipolar, ranging
from extremely to not at all annoyed. The response modiﬁers; Extremely, Very, Moderately,
Slightly and Not at all, were chosen because they cover a continuous scale equidistantly between
0 and 100 as shown in Figure 2.3. The possibility of doing a linear regression on the answers
was useful for comparing the objective room acoustic parameters. The translation of the question
to danish was done as direct as possible with regards to maintaining a phrasing that seemed to
be natural. How understandable and natural the question was, was evaluated by asking several
people with different background.
Translation of response modiﬁers
The danish version of the response modiﬁer was translated by overviewing previous research
(mainly in the health sector) with same unipolar scale and similar context of the question like
[how much does the pain annoy you?]. Different 4-, 5- or 6-point scales were overlooked and
the end points were in general consistent. The neutral point [Not at all] was widely accepted
to be [Slet ikke], the negative end point [extremely] was widely accepted to be either [Særdeles
meget] or [Rigtig meget], the point [Very] when before [Extremely] was widely accepted to be
[Meget], the most natural and widely used mid point was [Noget/Nogen] and the point [Slightly]
most often translated to [Lidt]. [Slet ikke] was chosen because it is widely accepted and almost
the only words used for a neutral point, [Lidt] was chosen because it was more natural then e.g.
[Ringe grad] or [Minimalt], [Nogen] was chosen because it was more natural than e.g. [Moderat],
[Meget] was chosen because it is widely accepted to be a followup to [Særdeles/Rigtig meget]
and [Særdeles meget] was chosen because it is stronger than [Rigtig meget]. The word [Lidt]
was often used in a 4-point scale presumably with an intensity score of 33 which might shift
the word intensity away from the supposed 25 score. See Appendix E for examples of surveys
previously done.

Questions for evaluating speech intelligibility and privacy
The questions for speech intelligibility and privacy were assumed to be related where the sense of
privacy, to some degree, was understood as the inverse of speech intelligibility. It was therefore
decided that the questions should be similar regarding context, scale and answer modiﬁers. Since
the scale was unipolar going from negative to neutral a contextual anchor was chosen to be the
degree of difﬁculty of performing some act. The question about speech intelligibility therefore
evaluated the degree of difﬁculty of hearing and understanding other people around the table
and the question about privacy referred to the difﬁculty of having a private conversation. These
questions allowed the scale itself, the direction of the scale and response modiﬁers to stay the
same securing continuity in the survey questions, and making the answering task easier for the
respondents. The risk of respondents making mistakes was reduced by making the questionnaire
as easily understandable and straight forward as possible.

average utilization of seats was reportedly around 25%, and therefore the staff was instructed
to hand out questionnaires at all times. In restaurant 5 the utilization of seats was reportedly
around 50% on average, and therefore the staff was instructed to hand out questionnaires when
the restaurant was approximately half full. All staff were instructed not to read out load or repeat

3.4.2
Reproduction of background noise
Material
At each restaurant background noise was recorded for a duration of 25-35 minutes. The record-
ings were listened through to ﬁnd suitable segments for the psychoacoustic measurements. The
chosen segments were steady in level and consisted of: continuous babble with no recognizable
danish words or sentences and common restaurants related noises e.g cutlery hitting plates and
noises from chairs. The reason for not choosing material with recognizable words was to control
the amount of informational masking in the psychoacoustic experiments. Here informational
masking is deﬁned as cognitive interferences like spoken words. In other words, masking which
is not energetic. Recognizable words interfere cognitively, making the test subjects ability to
focus on the task more difﬁcult. Informational masking is very difﬁcult to control because the
strength of the maskers, like understandable speech, varies greatly with the test subject due to fac-
tors like ability to focus on a task and linguistic skills. Furthermore was it not possible to record
material where the distance to the nearest guest was exactly the same in all restaurants. Figure 3.8
illustrates the method of achieving segments that were steady in level and content. It was chosen
that the linear equivalent A-weighted level LAeq SPL of the segments should not vary more than
1.5dB from the mean, giving a range of 3 dB. Segments should also not contain any signiﬁcant
bursts of noise or other distinguishable/characteristic sounds. LA was monitored continuously
and in real time in the program PULSE LabShop from B&K with a CPB analyzer. The properties
of the analyzer was set to include sound from 63Hz up to 16kHz and smoothing was done with
exponential averaging with a time constant of 500ms. After the sections were chosen another
method of elimination was done by calculating and plotting the continuous sound pressure level.
In total, 3 different sections of 30 seconds duration, was chosen for each restaurant.

3.4.5
Privacy evaluation
There is no easy way to evaluate the subjective opinion on sense of privacy, especially not when
this is done remotely from the actual location of interest. An attempt to evaluate sense of privacy
was made with a MUSHRA test, where the subject was asked how private it felt when listening
to a monologue in the recorded restaurant noise. This method did not take into consideration the
actual distance to the nearest patron not in the group, the general feeling of the spatial distribution
of speech or that people dynamically change their vocal effort depending on how important it is
to transfer the message without loss of information. The results would therefore be based on the
test subjects own intuition. This exact method has most likely not been used before and could
successfully give information on the rather broad deﬁnition of sense of privacy.

Presentation
This experiment is positioned at the beginning of the whole session. The presentation is the same
as in the noise annoyance evaluation. Again 20 test subjects participated, each subject rated each
restaurant 6 times giving a total of 120 ratings per restaurant.
Instructions
The subject was instructed to rate how difﬁcult it was to keep a conversation private, by asking:
"Privatliv test - Hvor svært er det at føre en privat samtale?"3 . The subject then pressed the
reference button and listened to the speaker only and was informed that this referred to a situation
where the subject is alone with the friend and that this should trigger the response "not at all
difﬁcult to keep a conversation private". The test subject was then instructed to listen through all
the scenarios before proceeding to the next page.
3.4.6
Calibration
In this section a detailed description of the how the audio system was calibrated is presented. For
measuring levels in the center of the loudspeaker array a B&K SPL Meter - type 2250 was used.
The SPL meter was initially calibrated with a B&K Sound Level Calibrator - type 4230.
Background noise
In order to reproduce acoustic scenes as realistic as possible, it was important that no level of pre-
sentation in the lab should differ from the level recorded on site. The build-in calibration device
of the spherical microphone array made it possible to read out the pressure at each microphone
during the recordings and from these values a reference sound ﬁle was constructed. A 5 seconds
segment of one of the recordings was chosen and an A-weighting ﬁlter was applied. In order to
get a single reference level, averaging over the 64 microphones had to be done. This was done
by taking the rms value of the pressure of each channel, giving 64 single rms values for each mi-
crophone on the sphere. The mean of the 64 rms values was chosen be be the reference pressure
level resulting in: LAeq = 59.7dBA SPL. The reference ﬁle was constructed by looping the 5
seconds into a 2 minute long sound ﬁle. A sound pressure level meter was then positioned in the
center of the loudspeaker array, and the reference ﬁle was played back. It was determined that
the reference playback amplitude should be upscaled by a factor of 13.93 to induce a read out on
the 2250 of 59.7dBA. This scaling factor was later on used in all the experiments for setting the
background noise level to be equal to the level measured in the restaurants.
MUSHRA test
In the MUSHRA test the test subjects gave a subjective rating on noise annoyance and privacy
difﬁculty based on the background noise and the speaker. The speaker was used to simulate
the event of going to a restaurant and talking to a friend. According to (Lazarus 1987) a SNR

between speech and ambient noise of -3dB is required to gain "sufﬁcient" quality of verbal com-
munication. To get the most natural presentation of speech in noise a set of different SNRs were
listened through and it was chosen that all monologues should be normalized to 0dB SNR. This
sounded the most natural and speech was clear and understandable. The normalization to have a
0dB difference between speech and noise was done through a series of steps now presented.
The reference ﬁle mentioned before was used for constructing a general mechanism for nor-
malizing a monologue with the 5 different background noise levels on each page in the test. Now
a reference monologue was chosen and the Z-Weighted rms pressure of the monologue was nor-
malized with the A-weighted rms pressure of the reference ﬁle. In order to gain an SNR of 0dB
in the center of the loudspeaker array a scaling factor for the reference monologue needed to be
determined. This was done by measuring the A-weighted sound pressure level of the reference
monologue in the center of the lab and scale the amplitude to gain a read out on the SPL meter
of 59.7dBA which was the level produced by the reference ﬁle. The scaling factor correspond-
ing to a SNR of 0dB was found to be 26.5. The reason for normalizing the monologues with
the A-weighted rms pressure of the background noise samples was to account for very strong
ﬂuctuations in low frequency energy found in the recorded samples. An alternative would be to
normalize without any weighting. This was tried out but resulted in the monologues to sound
either much higher or lower than the background noise. Another alternative would be to create
a unique ﬁltering, but since A-weighting very well compensate for the spectral differences in
perceived loudness it was chosen to be suitable.
SRT test
Maskers
Using the simple up-down method with varying target levels, one have to ﬁx the
level of the masker in order to determine the SNR at threshold and this is easily done with e.g.
energetic maskers like white noise or pure tones, but since the maskers in this experiment were
dynamic in level and furthermore not subjective to normalization it was necessary to establish a
criterion for the maximum level difference allowed. The criterion was as follows: The range of
the linear equivalent A-weighted sound pressure level had to be within 3 dB for each respective
restaurant scenario. For each restaurant, 3 samples with a duration of 30 seconds were initially
chosen and these samples were then cut into 18 samples of 5 seconds duration. The A-weighted
level of all samples were measured and to respect the previously mentioned criterion some of
the samples had to be eliminated. Table 3.3 below shows the number of samples used for each
restaurant, their mean LAeq and their respective standard deviations.

Measured and calculated parameters
In this section basic parameters as volume, ﬂoor area, seat capacity and density of patrons are
presented. In the following, two scenarios are constructed: (1) A general set of measures for
comparison with survey results gathered from guests. This includes an overall estimate of pa-
rameters of all rooms in every restaurant. (2) A speciﬁc set of measures for comparison with
laboratory results. This include speciﬁc data observed in the room that recordings took place in,
where all other rooms are excluded from the data. This results in smaller volumes/ﬂoor areas and
modiﬁed patron densities based on direct observations.
4.2.1
General measures - Surveys
Table 4.2 presents the general measures used for comparison with subjective evaluation of noise
annoyance, speech difﬁculty and privacy difﬁculty through surveys. The surveys are distributed
to patrons sitting everywhere in the restaurants and therefore measures include all areas. The
utilization of seats are set to 90% in restaurant 1, 2 and 3, 25% in restaurant 4 and 50% in
restaurant 5. These values are based on reported procedures of seating the guests. In restaurant
1 patrons are ﬁrst seated near the entrance and patrons arriving later are placed next to current
patrons. This seating procedure results in utilization of 90% of seats in the different rooms
when empty rooms are excluded. This furthermore results in patron density to be ﬁxed and not
dependent on how many guests are in the restaurant. In restaurant 2 the same procedure is used
but there is only 2 rooms in the restaurant. A 90% utilization is still proposed since the ﬁrst room
reportedly is full every day of the week. The procedure mentioned in restaurant 1 and 2 also
apply to restaurant 3. Restaurant 4 and 5 only had one room and here the seating procedure is the
opposite of restaurant 1,2 and 3. Patrons are preferably seated away from each other and hence
patron density is minimized as opposed to restaurant 1, 2 and 3. The utilization rate is set to 25%
and 50% because the restaurant owners were able to estimate the average number of guests on a
daily basis. When looking at the data it is apparent that dimensional data differ a lot between the
restaurants, whereas patron density and absorption area per patron are more similar.

Subjective ratings from surveys can be difﬁcult to analyze if sample sizes are small and do not

can be used. In order to identify whether survey and laboratory ratings were normally distributed
the Anderson-Darling test (Johnson et al. 2011) was used to do a preliminary screening. The tests
showed that all populations, except laboratory ratings of restaurant 1,2 and 5, were very far from
being normally distributed, and therefore it is from now on assumed that non-parametric tests
are suitable for further analysis. To evaluate whether there is a signiﬁcant difference between the
subjective ratings in the 5 different restaurants Mann-Whitney tests were performed. This non-
parametric test, which is based on ranked sums, can be applied when populations are not normally
distributed and it is an alternative to the unpaired two-sample t-test. To evaluate differences,
Mann-Whitney tests were performed on every combination of restaurant rating means (Johnson
et al. 2011). P-Values are calculated adjusting for ties.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
This chapter will include discussions on subjective ratings, an evaluation of what acoustic com-
fort is dependent on and ﬁnally a discussion on speech intelligibility in restaurants.
6.1
Subjective ratings
The two methods of measuring subjective ratings for noise annoyance, privacy difﬁculty and
speech difﬁculty showed the same trends but laboratory ratings was in general higher in all
circumstances. The data analysis proved that ratings are dependent on particularly the noise
levels, patron densities and utilization of acoustic capacity. An increase in density of people will
ultimately increase the noise levels due to the lombard effect (Lazarus 1987). Both survey and
laboratory results proved this relation to be true, but laboratory ratings were approximately twice
as high as the survey ratings. This can be explained by the fact that people in the restaurants get
used to noise over time whereas in the laboratory test subjects go from almost absolute silence to
very high noise levels in a matter of seconds. Furthermore is it reasonable that restaurant guests
expect some degree of noise, because a vibrant somewhat noisy environment is associated with a
busy and popular restaurant. This sort of positive association with noise is difﬁcult to bring into a
virtual environment, and could therefore be a reason why the ratings in the lab were higher. That
said, the most apparent reason why the ratings in the lab were much higher than in the surveys
is that tests subjects in the laboratory seemed to screen the noise levels in the 5 restaurants and
then base answers on the differences between the 5. Figure 6.1 below shows a histogram of the
answer frequency in the 5 different restaurant scenarios.

6.2
Evaluation of acoustic comfort
In the following a discussion on acoustic comfort is presented. Noise annoyance ratings proved
to be well correlated with both speech and privacy difﬁculty ratings. It was expected that higher
noise levels would result in a higher degree of difﬁculty regarding having a conversation, but also
privacy difﬁculty proved to be correlated with noise annoyance.
6.2.1
Noise and quality of verbal communication
Correlation between patron density and noise annoyance were proven to be very strong. This was
expected from reviewing previous research. It would have been very useful to have measured the
ambient noise levels while restaurant guests were ﬁlling out the surveys, but was not practically
possible. Furthermore, it would have been interesting to investigate quality of verbal communica-
tion for restaurants where the ambient noise levels exceed 75-80dB. According to (Pickett 1956)
word intelligibility can decrease by up to 50% as a result of increased vocal force above 80dB. A
vocal force of 75-80dB corresponds to "raised voice" or even "shouting" according to (Lazarus
1987). Also in this article quality of verbal communication is suggested to decline with increased
vocal effort above 75dB. When intelligibility declines as a result of extreme vocal effort people
tend to shorten the distance between their heads. This affects the "group size" (Rindel 2010),
where group size is the average number of people per speaking person. When people shorten the

distance between them the group size is potentially decreased to 2, meaning that every second
person in the restaurant is speaking as opposed to a situation where average group size is 3 where
every third person is speaking in the restaurant. Dynamic group sizes are a result of the lombard
effect, where increased noise levels result in decreased group sizes or more people speaking at
the same time. When looking at Figure 5.3 from the previous chapter, noise levels in small to
medium size restaurants would exceed 80dBA when the patron density is greater than 1 P/m2. It
was reported from restaurant 3, with a patron density of 0.62-0.84 and noise level recorded to be

with less than 1 second reverberation time, and patron density of 0.8-0.9 P/m2 produced am-
bient noise levels of 78-84dBC which corresponds to the values measured in this project when
assuming that A-weighting is more attenuating than C-weighting. It is therefore plausible that a
density of 1 P/m2 and associated noise levels of about 80dBA would be the absolute limit for a
pleasant restaurant environment. In Chapter 5 a linear model for noise annoyance as a function

6.2.2
Privacy
The results obtained in this project indicates that sense of privacy is correlated with noise levels
and patron density. Logically a higher patron density will result in people sitting more closely
together reducing the distance between them. What is not logic is that an increase in noise should
reduce the sense of privacy. Previous research on privacy is very ambiguous and ﬁndings depend
strongly on the situation of which you wish to increase sense of privacy. Speech Transmission
Index STI can be used to describe privacy and in the paper (Rindel and Pop 2005) privacy is
proven to increase when you decrease STI. You can decrease STI by either remove absorption or
increase background noise, it would therefore seem plausible that in an eating establishment an
increase in noise levels would increase privacy. In the same paper it is suggested that the measure
DL2
1 which is a measure for spatial decay can effect privacy, and this seems reasonable since
speech is attenuated more spatially by a higher degree of the measure DL2. On the other hand
an increase in spatial decay must come from increased absorption and this ultimately increase
STI, and the question is then which factor DL2 or STI is dominant. In the article (Jeon et al.
2014) it was proven that privacy in high speed train cabins increased when the train was at a
hold at the stations. The ambient noise level when driving at high speed was 66.0dBA and
56.7dBA when at a hold. This indicates that a decrease of noise, at least in this noise level
range, will increase sense of privacy. In the same article an analytical model for privacy distance
rP
2 is suggested where privacy distance rP is increased when the noise level is decreased. It
is apparent that more research has to be done before the term privacy is fully understood. The
results in this project suggest that noise affect privacy negatively when asked: "How difﬁcult
was it to maintain a private conversation with the other guests at your table; Extremely, Very,
Moderately, Slightly or Not at all?". It is not clear how respondent interpret the question, whether
they focus on how easy it is for other people to hear their voice, to what degree they can hear
others voices or if speech from other tables just disturb their own conversation both by energetic
and informational masking. If one assume that noise affects privacy negatively (Jeon et al. 2014)
and absorption affects privacy positively (Rindel and Pop 2005) it is clear that an increase in
absorption is advisable when trying to increase sense of privacy. This will also reduce the degree
of noise annoyance and make it less exhausting to have a conversation due to reduced vocal force.
1DL2 is the level reduction in dB when distance from source is doubled.
2privacy distance rP is the distance between source and receiver where STI ≤0.2.

6.3
Speech intelligibility
The goal of conducting SRT tests was to identify whether noise level, interior and acoustic prop-
erties have an effect on speech intelligibility. From the data analysis done it was not possible to
ﬁnd any signiﬁcant difference between the 5 restaurants in terms of SRT. The accuracy of the
given CIs of SRT is considered to be on the 95% signiﬁcance level. This is based on the fact that
only one sample population, see Table 5.5, was identiﬁed to be non-normally distributed. One
sample population (mean speech level at threshold, restaurant 3) showed three outliers which
might have caused the anomaly of restaurant 3’s SRT value which was around 2dB lower than
the others, but still with no statistical signiﬁcant difference. The SRT values of around -8dB
corresponds well with tests presented in other literature. In the article (Hochmuth et al. 2015)
a similar test was conducted with the same matrix test, but with 5 words to be identiﬁed as op-
posed to 4 words in this project’s test. SRT values were measured in four different languages
with multi-talker babble noise as masker. The SRT measured in the test was: German (-6dB),
Spanish (-4dB), Russian (-9.5dB) and Polish (-9dB). In the article (Preis et al. 2013) the same test
with 5 words to be identiﬁed gave an SRT value of -7.9dB for babble noise masker. The results
obtained in this project lies within the range of these article’s equivalent results, but language
and the fact that the current test only had 4 words to be identiﬁed instead of 5 might have had an
effect on the SRT levels found.
The most dominant noise source in a modern restaurant like the ones in this project is speech
noise from guests. Other noise sources are cutlery, chairs, bar, kitchen and music. The acoustic
scenes used in this project varied in all these aspects, where some had music, some had an open
kitchen very close to the seating area. Even though the acoustic scenes and the acoustic prop-
erties varied it was not possible to identify a difference between them in terms of an SRT test.
It is clear that restaurants of this size should emphasize on absorption and by this decrease the
ambient noise level and hereby give the guests some relief in terms of vocal effort. It is possi-
ble that this insigniﬁcance in SRT is only reproducible in small to medium size restaurants, and
that bigger eating establishments with longer RT’s will show difference as a function of acoustic
parameters.
6.4
Limitations
This study was limited to restaurants with RT’s under 1 second, relatively small volumes and an
ambient noise level measured below 80dB. Furthermore was the interior design equal to what
is often seen in western eating establishments. If a similar study is conducted it is advisable to
include restaurants with more variation in terms of RT, volume, noise level and interior design.
The term "ﬁne dining" is often associated with carpets, tablecloths, upholstered chairs and low
noise levels. This type of restaurant was not included in the study and would most likely produce
different results. It is also likely that geographical and cultural aspects will have an effect on
subjective evaluations, and this was not considered. Future research would beneﬁt from compar-
ing scenarios where patron density or acoustic capacity is ﬁxed, to analyze whether or not other

6.5
Summary
Subjective evaluations from surveys proved to be very difﬁcult to analyze due to insigniﬁcant
differences between mean ratings in certain restaurant combinations. The ratings obtained in the
laboratory showed less variation and it was possible to identify a signiﬁcant difference between
the 5 restaurants, except between restaurant 1 and 2. Since both methods showed same tendency
the laboratory results support the claim that survey ratings are different between the restaurants
allowing for linear regression. There was a clear trend that ratings in the lab were dependent
on the dynamic range of the noise levels. A strong correlation between noise annoyance and
patron density was found and a linear model for the relationship was proposed. Speech quality
proved to be dependent on the term "acoustic capacity" and on patron density. A linear model for
quality of verbal communication was proposed. The results obtained in the study indicates that
sense of privacy in restaurants are dependent on patron density and the associated noise levels.
Experiments showed that restaurants with these dimensions and speech as main source of noise
produces similar SRT values.
6.6
Putting into perspective
Other applications are large gatherings of people in conﬁned spaces with speech as dominant
noise source. This includes conference halls, exhibitions, indoor markets etc. With this in mind
the ﬁndings do not only apply to eating establishments, but can prove useful for various instances
of organizing events or constructing new buildings.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this study it was found that the main cause of noise annoyance is when restaurateurs seat
guests too closely together. Noise annoyance correlated well with patron density and the asso-
ciated noise levels produced. Furthermore did surveys prove that relatively high noise levels i.e.
76.7dBA lead to a mean response of slightly/moderately annoyed. 76dBA corresponds to the
noise from a vacuum cleaner or standing 50ft. away from a highway at 10am (Comparitive Ex-
amples of Noise Levels @ONLINE 2016). This indicates high noise tolerance in restaurants. A
prediction model for noise annoyance as a function of Rho was proposed. It showed that a patron
density of 1 will produce an average response of "moderately annoyed", which consequently will
lead to a signiﬁcant amount of guests rating "extremely annoyed".
Quality of verbal communication was found to be dependent on amount of people, volume and
RT as suggested in (Rindel 2012), and the term "acoustic capacity" was used to construct a pre-
diction model. One should note that Rho was an equally strong predictor for speech quality as
acoustic capacity, and further investigations are needed in order to determine which predictor
is the most reliable. SRT values for the 5 different restaurants proved not to vary, and results
corresponds to previous research done with babble noise.
The term "Sense of privacy" is still very ambiguous, but there is evidence that it is dependent
on noise levels. This was supported by the fact that both restaurant surveys and laboratory exper-
iments produced the same results when respondents were asked "How difﬁcult was it to maintain
a private conversation with the other guests at your table?". This ﬁnding matches previous inves-
tigations, but there is still room for further research on the topic.
Reproduction of acoustic restaurant scenes in a VSE proved to be useful and supported the sur-
veys. It was found that SRT measurements in a VSE did not produce a different result compared
to traditional methods where headphones are used.
