Abstract
Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA) is the most common chronic rheumatic disease in childhood,
often involving the temporomandibular joint (TMJ) and leading to facial asymmetry and jaw
misalignment. Early, objective, and reproducible assessment of TMJ involvement is essential to
prevent long-term functional impairment. This thesis presents an artificial intelligence-based
framework to diagnose and monitor jaw misalignment severity in JIA patients using 3D facial
landmark data.
A dataset of 3,132 3D facial scans from patients aged 0 to 20 years (with some outliers up to 32)
was processed, including landmark extraction, asymmetry calculation, and feature engineer-
ing. Multiple classical machine learning algorithms (Random Forest, Support Vector Machine,
Multi-Layer Perceptron) were initially tested, followed by the development of a deep neural
network trained with extensive hyperparameter optimization through grid search. Performance
was evaluated using macro-average and weighted-average F1 scores over 300 independent runs.
Results showed robust performance for identifying healthy or balanced jaw conditions, with
weighted-average F1 scores reaching 0.88 for the Left side predictions. However, macro-average
F1 scores were lower, around 0.63, highlighting challenges in reliably classifying moderate and
severe TMJ involvement due to data imbalance and biological complexity.
The pipeline demonstrated high reproducibility, solid interpretability, and clinical relevance
by correlating facial asymmetry with TMJ diagnostic categories derived from orthopantomo-
graphic evaluations. Furthermore, a holistic prediction target combining left and right joint
involvement was explored to match clinical decision-making more closely.
The main limitation was reduced accuracy for moderate and severe TMJ categories, primarily
due to data imbalance. Nonetheless, this thesis significantly contributes by establishing a robust
AI-based framework capable of objectively quantifying jaw misalignment, laying the ground-
work for enhanced clinical validation and potential adoption in pediatric rheumatology prac-
tices.
Future directions include enriching the dataset, refining landmark extraction algorithms, ex-
ploring advanced architectures, and potentially integrating 2D-to-3D reconstruction to make
the method more accessible. Ultimately, with further improvements, this approach could re-
duce dependence on radiographic imaging, lowering patients’ X-ray exposure and providing a
faster, non-invasive tool to support TMJ diagnostics in JIA patients.

Introduction
3.1
Background
Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA) is the most prevalent chronic rheumatic disease in childhood,
characterized by persistent joint inflammation. Among its manifestations, temporomandibular
joint (TMJ) involvement is particularly significant, impacting jaw alignment and function. Early
detection and effective monitoring are essential to mitigate long-term functional impairments
and improve quality of life, as face asymmetry exponentially grows if not treated in time (figure
1).
Figure 1: Example growth in individual with JIA and unilateral TMJ involvement.
3.2
3D Surface Scanning
Three-dimensional surface scanning, also called 3D photos, is a non-invasive and radiation-
free technology capable of capturing the detailed external morphology of the human face. Un-
like traditional radiographic imaging, 3D surface scanning preserves high-resolution spatial
information without exposing patients to ionizing radiation, making it particularly suitable for
repeated examinations in pediatric populations. Modern 3D scanning systems employ pho-
togrammetry or structured-light techniques to generate precise surface meshes of the craniofa-
cial region, which can then be used for quantitative analysis.
In the context of Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis, 3D surface scanning allows clinicians and re-
searchers to assess facial asymmetry and jaw misalignment over time, supporting both diagno-
sis and treatment monitoring. When paired with advanced landmark detection methods and
AI-based analysis pipelines, these scans can yield objective and reproducible measurements of

jaw deformities, improving the accuracy of TMJ involvement assessments. This thesis leverages
such 3D scanning data as the basis for landmark extraction and downstream machine learning
models.
3.3
Motivation
Current diagnostic procedures for assessing TMJ involvement and jaw misalignment are pre-
dominantly manual, qualitative, and subject to considerable inter-observer variability. Leverag-
ing Artificial Intelligence (AI) offers the potential to enhance objectivity, accuracy, and repeata-
bility in diagnostics and monitoring of treatment efficacy. Specifically, AI-based techniques can
systematically analyze complex three-dimensional craniofacial landmarks to provide precise
quantification of jaw misalignment [1], as presented in figure 2.

Untreated JIA with involvement of the TMJ can develop into a facial type, examplified in figure 3,
[2], with micrognathic or retrognathic mandible with posterior rotation in relation to the cranial
base. In general, unilateral TMJ arthritis leads to retruded and wider chin, shorter mandibular
height and prominent cheek, while bilateral involvement is even more dramatic with retruded
chin, shorter mandibular height, prominent upper cheeks and narrower perioral region. Early
diagnosis and proper medication inhibit the development of these traits.

3.4
Objectives
This study aims to develop and evaluate an AI-driven methodology for diagnosing and quan-
tifying jaw misalignment associated with JIA. The primary objectives are:
• To develop an AI model capable of accurately identifying and classifying the degree of
jaw misalignment from 3D facial landmark data.
• To assess the effectiveness of the AI model in monitoring treatment progression in ado-
lescents with JIA.
3.5
Research Questions
The study addresses the following key questions:
1. How effectively can AI techniques identify and quantify TMJ-related jaw misalignment?
2. Can AI-driven diagnostics reliably track treatment progression and outcomes over time?

Literature review
4.1
Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis and Temporomandibular Jaw Involvement
Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis is an autoimmune, inflammatory disease characterized by persis-
tent arthritis in one or more joints lasting at least six weeks, primarily affecting individuals un-
der 16 years of age [3], [4]. Although the precise etiology of JIA remains unclear, genetic predis-
positions and environmental factors are implicated in its pathogenesis [4]. Temporomandibular
join involvement is particularly concerning due to its significant impact on craniofacial growth
and function, potentially leading to severe deformities such as mandibular asymmetry, retrog-
nathia, micrognathia, and impaired jaw function [5]–[7].
4.2
Current Diagnostic Methods and Limitations
Currently, TMJ arthritis diagnostics largely rely on clinical evaluation, conventional radiogra-
phy, magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and computed tomography (CT) [7], [8]. However,
these techniques present critical limitations including subjectivity, high inter-observer variabil-
ity, potential radiation exposure, limited sensitivity to early morphological changes, and diffi-
culties in repeated longitudinal monitoring [5], [7], [8].
4.3
3D Surface Scanning Technology
Three-dimensional (3D) surface scanning methods such as stereophotogrammetry have sig-
nificantly advanced the assessment of craniofacial morphology, offering a radiation-free, non-
invasive solution ideal for pediatric patients [3], [8]. These techniques deliver highly accurate,
rapid capture of facial topography, enhancing clinicians’ ability to detect subtle morphological
deviations and assess disease progression effectively [5], [9].
4.4
Facial Landmarking and Asymmetry Quantification
Quantifying facial asymmetry accurately is crucial for evaluating the severity and progression
of JIA-related craniofacial anomalies [3]. Manual landmarking is laborious and prone to signif-
icant variability [5]. Recent innovations in automated landmarking, such as Deep Multi-view
Landmarking (Deep-MVLM), utilize deep learning frameworks to improve landmark accuracy,
reproducibility, and efficiency [9]. Deep-MVLM applies multi-view convolutional neural net-
works (CNNs) to robustly localize landmarks from multiple perspectives, significantly enhanc-
ing the precision of craniofacial assessments [9].
4.5
Deep Learning Applications in Medical Diagnostics
Deep learning, a subset of artificial intelligence, has increasingly demonstrated potential for im-
proving medical diagnostics through sophisticated pattern recognition and quantitative image
analysis [10], [11]. Models such as U-Net have revolutionized biomedical image segmentation
by enabling precise anatomical delineation and diagnosis [12]. Convolutional neural networks,
in particular, have been successfully applied to various medical imaging modalities, including
radiography, MRI, CT, and 3D surface scans, enabling precise anatomical feature detection and
disease classification [9], [11]. Recent advances have explored transformer architectures, fur-
ther extending AI capabilities in medical imaging analysis through improved interpretability
and robustness [13], [14].

4.6
Research Gap and Justification
Despite significant advancements, a critical research gap remains regarding fully automated,
robust, and reliable methods for precise quantification and monitoring of TMJ-related jaw mis-
alignments in pediatric JIA patients [5], [8]. Most existing approaches still rely heavily on man-
ual or semi-automated assessments, lacking consistent reproducibility across longitudinal stud-
ies [6], [9]. The present study aims to bridge this gap by leveraging state-of-the-art deep learn-
ing technologies, providing a fully automated, precise, and objective diagnostic and monitoring
solution tailored specifically for children and adolescents with JIA.

Methodology used in the thesis
This section provides a comprehensive theoretical foundation for the methods and techniques
utilized throughout the thesis, including landmark placement, data preprocessing, dimension-
ality reduction techniques, deep learning principles, hyperparameter optimization, data split-
ting strategies, model evaluation metrics, and relevant Python libraries and techniques.
5.1
Facial Landmark Placement
Facial landmarks serve as anatomical reference points for quantifying facial asymmetry. These
landmarks were automatically generated using the Deep-MVLM algorithm [9]. This software
places 73 unique landmarks on the patient’s face.
5.1.1
Face Analyzer
The Face Analyzer (in-house software built as an extension to the Landmarker software [15]) is
responsible for deforming the symmetrical template face, also called an atlas, into the patient’s
facial geometry, computing deviations in millimeters as part of this process. A 3D facial scan is
represented as a surface mesh composed of many small triangular facets that describe the de-
tailed external geometry of the face. Because these scans can be acquired in slightly misaligned
orientations, the software must first align the scan with the reference template.
To achieve meaningful alignment, only the landmarks located on the upper face, for example,
those near the eyes, nose and temples, are used as anchor points. This approach is critical be-
cause the lower face and jaw are precisely the regions of interest for asymmetry analysis, and
using their landmarks to align the face would bias the analysis. Once alignment is achieved, the
face is compared to a perfectly symmetrical template, and the amount of deformation necessary
to match the patient’s surface is quantified, providing a measure of local shape differences. This
procedure is illustrated in figure 4.
Following deformation, asymmetry is calculated based on a cross-sectional symmetry plane
(the midsagittal plane), as shown in figure 5. For each landmark, the software computes a vec-
tor between the observed location on one side of the face and its mirrored counterpart on the
opposite side, producing a set of asymmetry measures in four dimensions: the norm (mag-
nitude of asymmetry) as well as its decomposition into the transverse (x), vertical (y), and
sagittal (z) components. This quantitative representation supports downstream analysis and
model development focused on classifying and monitoring jaw asymmetry in JIA patients.

5.3
Deep Learning Fundamentals
Deep learning utilizes neural networks consisting of interconnected layers of artificial neurons.
Each neuron combines inputs, applies weights, adds bias, and passes the result through a non-
linear activation function. By iteratively updating these parameters through backpropagation
and optimization algorithms, neural networks learn complex patterns from data.
Key hyperparameters influencing deep learning performance include:
• Network Architecture: Structure determining layers, neurons, and connections.
• L2 Regularization: Adds a penalty term to the loss function, which is proportional to the
square of the magnitude of the weights
• Dropout Rate: Fraction of neurons randomly disabled during training to mitigate overfit-
ting.
• Learning Rate: Magnitude of updates to model parameters during training.
• Batch Size: Number of samples processed before a gradient update.
• Epochs: Complete iterations through the entire training dataset.
• Patience (Early Stopping): Number of epochs allowed without improvement in valida-
tion loss before terminating training, preventing overfitting.
Early stopping, guided by the validation loss metric, was employed to halt training once perfor-
mance ceased improving, thus ensuring optimal model generalization.

5.4
Dimensionality Reduction Techniques
Dimensionality reduction techniques are used to transform high-dimensional datasets into lower-
dimensional spaces while preserving important structural relationships, with visualization pur-
poses. These transformations allow for more intuitive visualization of the data and can help
detect underlying structures, clusters, or separability that may not be obvious in the raw feature
space.
Three techniques were selected for this purpose: Principal Component Analysis (PCA), t-distributed
Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE), and Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projec-
tion (UMAP). Each method captures different aspects of the data’s structure and is based on
different theoretical assumptions.
• Principal Component Analysis (PCA): PCA is a linear dimensionality reduction tech-
nique that identifies directions (principal components) along which the data varies the
most. These directions are orthogonal, and the first few components typically capture
the majority of the variance. PCA is fast, interpretable, and useful for obtaining a base-
line understanding of global variance structures. It is particularly appropriate when data
relationships are expected to be linear or when simplicity and speed are prioritized.
• t-SNE (t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding): t-SNE is a nonlinear technique
that focuses on preserving local structure. It computes pairwise similarities in the high-
dimensional space and attempts to map those similarities into a low-dimensional embed-
ding. t-SNE is highly effective at capturing local groupings and is especially suited for
small to medium-sized datasets, as is the case in this thesis. However, it does not preserve
global distances and can be sensitive to hyperparameters such as perplexity and learning
rate.
• UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection): UMAP is a more recent
nonlinear method that constructs a high-dimensional graph representing the data man-
Page 15 of 54
==PAGE:20==
page 16
ifold and optimizes a low-dimensional layout that preserves both local and global rela-
tionships. UMAP is computationally efficient, scalable to larger datasets, and typically
requires fewer adjustments to hyperparameters than t-SNE. It serves as a useful comple-
ment when seeking to understand broader structures in the data while still maintaining
local integrity.
These dimensionality reduction techniques were applied as exploratory tools during the anal-
ysis phase of the project. Their goal was to provide qualitative insight into the geometry of
the feature space and to assess the suitability of the extracted features for classification tasks.

Data Preprocessing Pipeline
Data preprocessing was executed through the Python script clean_data.py, comprising vari-
ous custom functions that transformed the raw data into a structured format suitable for model
training. The goal of these functions not only is it to prepare and set the data in readable formats
for the python libraries used, but also to give the AI model the most insightful data possible,
as this is a key step in the process. If the input of the model is insightful data, with the needed
configurations the results will be great. However, if the input data does not contain any relevant
information, the output will not be reliable not accurate.
• clean_data_parameters(): Parses the original data, splitting embedded landmark and
asymmetry tuples into numerical columns. It also encodes the gender from ’M’ and ’F’ to
1 and 0 respectively. Finally, it also removes any scan values that are not ’0’, ’2’ or ’3’, to
make sure all data is valid. Returns a data frame.
• remove_bad_scans(df): Removes corrupted or unreliable scans from the dataset, of a
manually selected list by a specialist. Returns a data frame.
• drop_landmarks(df, drop_landmarks): Removes specified landmarks and their associ-

as well as the amount of parameters.
• template_delta_dist(df): Calculates the distances between landmarks on patient scans
and their template counterparts. Adds these values as new columns to the data frame and
returns it. The function only calculates for the landmarks present on the input data frame.
This function aims to provide additional input information, that will specifically magnify
values on the lower part of the fame, helping a lot with the classification task.
• dist_landmarks(df, pairs): Computes distances between selected pairs of landmarks.
Adds these values as new columns to the data frame. Returns a data frame. This function
aims to add precise additional information to help the classification task, as it can provide
information about certain landmarks in the jaw, like the chin, that are specifically relevant.
• angle_between_lines(df, list_angles): Measures angles defined by combinations of

5.6.1
Training and Validation Performance Curves
To track the model’s learning process, two key metrics were recorded at each epoch: loss and
accuracy, both for training and validation sets. These metrics were plotted to create learning
curves, providing insight into how well the model was optimizing and generalizing over time.
• Training Loss: Measures the error between the model’s predictions and true labels on the
training data.
• Validation Loss: Measures the same error on the validation set; increases may signal over-
fitting.
• Training Accuracy: Tracks the percentage of correctly classified samples in the training
set.
• Validation Accuracy: Tracks generalization ability to unseen data in the validation set.
The expected behavior of these curves is as follows:
• During early epochs, both training and validation loss should decrease, and accuracy
should increase.
• If the model starts to overfit, training loss may continue to decrease while validation loss
begins to rise.
• Ideally, validation loss plateaus or decreases smoothly, indicating good generalization.
• A large gap between training and validation curves typically suggests overfitting, while
closely aligned curves indicate a well-regularized model.
• Most commonly, the validation loss will have higher value than the training loss. Simulta-
neously, the validation accuracy is expected to take lower values tan the training accuracy.
These plots are used later in the thesis to visualize training dynamics and identify whether a
model was underfitting, overfitting, or learning appropriately. They also guide early stopping:
if validation loss does not improve for a specified number of epochs (patience), training halts
automatically to avoid unnecessary overfitting.

6.2.1
Facial Landmarks and Symmetry Features
Facial landmarks play a crucial role in quantifying jaw misalignment and craniofacial asym-
metry, serving as the foundation for both feature extraction and downstream model training.
In this study, each facial scan is annotated with a set of 73 anatomical landmarks, automati-
cally placed using Deep-MVLM [9]. This deep learning-based tool leverages multi-view stereo
matching and convolutional neural networks to accurately project predefined points from a ref-
erence face (the template) onto the surface of each patient’s 3D facial scan.
The reference template used for landmark projection is a perfectly symmetrical 3D mesh. Each
of its 73 landmarks is mirrored across the sagittal (midline) plane, ensuring consistent place-
ment across patients and enabling meaningful comparisons between the left and right sides of
the face. This symmetrical template allows for precise calculation of asymmetry by compar-
ing each landmark to its mirrored counterpart. Consequently, paired landmarks have the same
asymmetry magnitude but opposite directional signs, effectively encoding bilateral discrepan-
cies.
For each landmark, seven values are stored in the dataset:
• 3D spatial coordinates: x, y, and z.
• 4 asymmetry descriptors: asymmetry magnitude and directional components along the
x, y, and z axes.
While the Deep-MVLM tool provides automated and standardized landmark placement, the
reliability of individual landmarks can vary. Certain points, such as those located on rigid and
well-defined facial structures (e.g., the chin and upper jaw), are consistently reproducible and
robust across scans. For instance, landmark 68 (chin), landmark 63 (top of the right upper jaw
and landmark 73 (top of the left upper jaw) are considered particularly reliable. In contrast,
landmarks situated in soft or deformable tissue regions—such as the inner lips or around the
center of the eyes—are more susceptible to scan variability and placement inaccuracies. This
variability in reliability should be considered when interpreting model predictions and feature
importance.
Overall, the landmark system provides a structured, interpretable, and biologically grounded
representation of facial geometry and asymmetry, making it well-suited for the task of jaw mis-
alignment classification. Figure 11 displays some examples, including the perfectly symmetrical
template face.

6.3.1
Selected Landmarks and Justification
Out of the 73 available landmarks, only a specific subset was retained in the final dataset. These
included landmarks corresponding to the jawline, nose, and mouth regions, which are the most
structurally relevant areas for evaluating mandibular asymmetry and the shape, which is the
core diagnostic focus of this thesis. In contrast, landmarks located in less stable or unreliable
regions, such as the inner lips and areas near the eyes, were excluded due to their greater sus-
ceptibility to movement and scan variability.
The retained landmarks were:
• Nose and midface: 35–46 (center of the face and upper lip).
• Jaw: 63–73 (lateral and posterior jaw structure).
• Mouth outline: 47–56 (excluding inner lip points).
These choices were based on both anatomical relevance and empirical testing. The jaw land-
marks (63–73) are particularly important as they define mandibular geometry. The central
landmarks around the mouth and nose provide symmetry reference points. Inner lip points
were discarded due to their instability across scans. Because this landmark combination was
the best performing one, it was decided to use in the final pipeline.

6.3.3
Unused functions
The function rotate_landmarks_randomly(df) was considered to force the model to identify
the patient’s facial structure, but it was not used in the final version as it worsened the model
performance. Because the model had to spend more resources in identifying each patients facial
structure and then compare them, the model actually performed worse (contrarily to expected),
which is why it was finally not used. In a way, by unsorting the faces, I was undoing the work
face_analyzer had done, and the network had to do that job again.
The function drop_landmarks(df) was also omitted, as keep_landmarks() provided a more
straightforward mechanism to retain only the desired features. Essentially, both functions can
provide the same output, so it was simply decided to use the other one.
This final preprocessing setup allowed the model to focus on the most consistent and diagnos-
tically relevant facial structures while reducing noise and dimensionality. It struck a balance
between anatomical insight and computational efficiency, enabling more robust classification
of jaw asymmetry.
6.3.4
Preprocessing Strategies: SMOTE, Scaling, Encoding, and Stratification
In addition to feature engineering, several general preprocessing techniques were applied to
improve model robustness and handle common challenges in supervised learning, such as class
imbalance, feature scaling, and label formatting.
• SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique): SMOTE generates synthetic
examples of minority classes to address class imbalance. While its application was tested
in preliminary experiments, it was ultimately not used in the final pipeline. Empiri-
cal results showed that excluding SMOTE produced equal or better performance in the
context of deep learning models, while maintaining greater data authenticity. When us-
ing SMOTE, the model tended to overfit the data, and the macro f1 score was not high
enough. Because the minority classes are so small compared to the majority one, using
this technique made the model overfit the training data and perform poorly on the test
data, with macro f1 scores of around 0.3 and 0.4.
• StandardScaler: Standardization of numerical features (zero mean, unit variance) was
essential for models like SVM and neural networks. This ensured uniform feature scaling,
stabilized gradient descent updates, and improved convergence rates.

Model
This section presents the final neural network architectures used to classify jaw asymmetry in
adolescents with Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis. Three distinct models were built, each corre-
sponding to one of the prediction targets: Left, Right, and Diagnose. While the overall struc-
ture of the networks was consistent, hyperparameters were fine-tuned individually through a
grid search to optimize performance for each task.
8.1
Architecture Overview
All models were constructed using the Keras Sequential API within the TensorFlow frame-
work. The architecture consists of:
• An InputLayer matching the number of input features, which is 175.
• Two fully connected Dense layers with ReLU activations.
• BatchNormalization applied after each dense layer to stabilize training.
• Dropout applied after activation to reduce overfitting.
• A final Dense output layer with softmax activation to produce class probabilities across
three diagnostic classes (0, 2, and 3).
L2 regularization was applied to the kernel weights in each hidden layer to penalize overly
complex models and improve generalization. The choice of dense fully-connected layers rather
than convolutional architectures was motivated by the data structure: the facial landmark and
asymmetry features are best represented as flat, structured vectors rather than 2D or 3D grids.
The use of ReLU activation allows efficient non-linear modeling, while softmax at the output
layer provides an appropriate probabilistic interpretation for multi-class classification. The se-
lected network depth and width were designed to balance learning capacity with the risk of
overfitting, guided by experiments and grid search optimization.
8.2
Training Configuration
All models were compiled using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate specified per target.
The loss function was sparse_categorical_crossentropy, which is appropriate for multi-class
classification problems where the labels are integer-encoded. The models were trained using
early stopping with a patience of 40 epochs, monitoring validation loss to prevent overfitting.

10
Discussion of results
This chapter discusses the results presented in Chapter 9, linking them to the original research
questions and the clinical context of Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA) with temporomandibu-
lar joint (TMJ) involvement. The discussion highlights strengths, limitations, sources of error,
and future perspectives.
10.1
Revisiting the Research Questions
The study aimed to answer two main research questions, as introduced in Chapter 3:
1. How effectively can AI techniques identify and quantify TMJ-related jaw misalignment?
2. Can AI-driven diagnostics reliably track treatment progression and outcomes over time?
The results presented in Chapter 9 provide evidence that deep learning models can effectively
classify jaw asymmetry from 3D landmark data, achieving robust macro-average F1 scores,
particularly for healthy or balanced cases. However, challenges remain in accurately classi-
fying moderate and severe TMJ involvement, reflecting the complexity of subtle asymmetry
patterns and limited training data for these classes. Regarding treatment monitoring, the frame-
work demonstrates high potential by providing reproducible, quantitative metrics that could be
tracked over time, although further longitudinal studies would be needed to fully validate its
application for monitoring treatment progression.
10.2
Interpretation of Results
The experiments showed that the proposed deep learning models were able to reliably detect
and classify jaw misalignment in adolescents with JIA, with best macro-average F1 scores ex-
ceeding 0.7 for the Left side prediction task. This confirms the feasibility of applying artificial
intelligence to quantify TMJ-related asymmetry using 3D landmark data, supporting the main
thesis objective. The consistently high precision and recall for the majority class (healthy or bal-
anced) demonstrates that the landmark and asymmetry features are robust in distinguishing
clear-cut cases without significant TMJ involvement.
However, the models performed less effectively on minority classes, in particular for moder-
ate (class 2) and severe (class 3) involvement. The macro-average F1 scores were considerably
lower than the weighted F1, which is expected in the presence of strong class imbalance. The
confusion matrices showed that moderate and severe cases were frequently confused with the
healthy class. This is likely due to both the underrepresentation of those categories and the sub-
tlety of jaw asymmetry patterns in borderline cases, which reduces class separability. Addition-
ally, intra-class variance may be high among patients with TMJ involvement, further challenging
the model.
It is also worth noting that the Left side consistently showed better macro-average F1 scores
compared to the Right side. Several explanations could account for this: more reliable landmark
positions on the left, fewer scanning artifacts, or perhaps more consistent clinical scoring of
the left TMJ by the experts. This observation deserves further investigation to clarify whether
systematic biases exist in either the scanning protocol or the diagnosis itself.
The precision and recall plots (Figure 20) provide valuable insights into how well each class is
Page 49 of 54
==PAGE:54==
page 50
predicted individually. For all three prediction targets (Left, Right, and Diagnose), the major-
ity class (class 0 or balanced condition) exhibited high precision and recall consistently above
0.9, indicating robust predictive performance. In contrast, the minority classes (moderate and
severe involvement) displayed significantly lower precision and recall values, typically ranging
from 0.3 to 0.6 with greater variability. This suggests that the model often struggled to distin-
guish between moderate and severe cases and the healthy condition due to overlapping feature
distributions and class imbalance.
The confusion matrices (Tables 12a, 12b, and 12c) further elucidate these findings. Misclassifi-
cations occurred predominantly between minority classes and the healthy class, indicating the
model’s limited ability to accurately identify subtle or borderline cases. Specifically, moderate
and severe cases were frequently misclassified as healthy, underscoring the difficulty of reliably
identifying less pronounced morphological deviations.
Regarding the interpretation of the weighted-average F1 score, the mean score of 0.88 for the Left
side predictions does not imply that each individual patient would be correctly classified 88%
of the time. Rather, this metric represents the average model performance across all instances,
with weights proportional to class frequency. Consequently, the high weighted F1 score pre-
dominantly reflects excellent accuracy for the majority class, masking the poorer performance
for minority classes. In clinical terms, this means the model is very reliable when predicting the
absence of TMJ involvement (class 0), but much less so for identifying patients with moderate
or severe involvement (classes 2 and 3).
In contrast, the macro-average F1 score, which assigns equal weight to each class regardless of
their frequencies, provides a more balanced perspective on the overall performance. Thus, clin-
icians should interpret the macro-average F1 (around 0.63 for the Left side) as a more realistic
estimate of the model’s overall predictive reliability across different clinical severities.
These interpretations highlight the importance of examining multiple metrics to fully under-
stand the strengths and limitations of predictive models, particularly in imbalanced classifi-
cation contexts. Future developments should focus explicitly on improving performance for
minority classes to enhance clinical usability.
10.3
Patient diagnosis
An important methodological decision in this thesis was the introduction of the combined ‘Di-
agnose‘ target, in addition to separate predictions for the ‘Left‘ and ‘Right‘ sides individually.
Clinically, while detailed classifications of the left and right temporomandibular joints sep-
arately provide valuable anatomical insights, the overall treatment and clinical management
strategies typically do not differ significantly between moderate (class 2) and severe (class 3)
involvement. Thus, a holistic diagnostic prediction, integrating both sides into a single classifi-
cation, can offer greater clinical relevance and simplify decision-making.
The ‘Diagnose‘ prediction aims precisely at capturing this holistic view, distinguishing between
balanced (healthy), left-dominant, and right-dominant cases. Although its macro-average F1
score was intermediate compared to separate left- or right-side predictions, it provides a more
practical clinical metric by directly reflecting treatment-relevant distinctions. This holistic ap-
proach acknowledges the practical reality of clinical management and treatment planning, where
specific severity differences within an affected side may matter less than the general presence
and side dominance of TMJ involvement.

Therefore, despite slightly reduced performance compared to the left-side-only model, the holis-
tic prediction approach represented by the ‘Diagnose‘ target provides meaningful complemen-
tary information. It aligns better with clinical workflows, potentially increasing the clinical util-
ity of the model by simplifying the diagnostic process without compromising essential clinical
details.
10.4
Sources of Error and Limitations
Beyond class imbalance, multiple other sources of error could have contributed to lower scores
or misclassifications. First, the data is not homogeneous in terms of age: patients range from
infancy to young adulthood, with a few outliers above 20 years old. Since craniofacial morphol-
ogy changes significantly with growth, a single model might struggle to generalize over all these
developmental stages. Second, the dataset includes both male and female patients, whose facial
structures may systematically differ, as well as patients from different ethnic backgrounds, in-
troducing further variability in craniofacial features. These demographic factors may confound
the learned relationships between landmarks and the TMJ diagnostic labels.
A particularly important limitation arises from the way the ground truth is established. The
diagnostic classes are based on expert evaluation of orthopantomographic (panoramic) radio-
graphs. While JIA involvement of the TMJ typically results in mandibular asymmetry, asym-
metry can also appear in patients whose TMJ is healthy (score 0). In other words, a patient
may have a naturally deviated chin but no TMJ inflammation. Conversely, mild TMJ arthritis
might be present without visible chin deviation. Figure 21 illustrates this: on the left, a girl with
JIA but without TMJ involvement shows symmetrical features, while on the right, a girl with
JIA and unilateral TMJ affection shows clear asymmetry. This imperfect biological link between
facial shape and TMJ diagnosis adds unavoidable error to the classification task.

Finally, technical sources of error must also be considered. Both the face analyzer software
(for aligning and deforming the atlas) and the landmarking algorithms introduce small but
systematic inaccuracies. These errors propagate through feature engineering and ultimately
affect the training of the neural network, reducing the upper bound of achievable accuracy.

While the use of robust pipelines and validation steps helps mitigate these issues, their presence
cannot be fully eliminated.
10.5
Future Perspectives
Overall, the results confirm that deep learning holds strong promise for diagnosing and mon-
itoring TMJ-related facial asymmetry in JIA patients. However, several future directions could
enhance the model’s performance and clinical utility. Increasing the number of samples, es-
pecially in the moderate and severe classes, would be critical to improving class balance and
reducing bias. Incorporating advanced data augmentation or synthetic oversampling tailored
to 3D landmark data might further help mitigate data imbalance. Another avenue could in-
volve domain adaptation or transfer learning, leveraging knowledge from related craniofacial
datasets to improve feature representations.
Additionally, future models could explicitly include patient-specific information such as age,
gender, or ethnicity to better control for demographic differences, and potentially move toward
a more personalized prediction framework. Finally, improving the landmarking and alignment
algorithms, for example with more advanced registration methods or uncertainty quantifica-
tion, could further reduce the impact of technical errors in the preprocessing pipeline.
10.6
Summary
In summary, the developed pipeline achieved robust classification of jaw asymmetry in JIA pa-
tients, showing strong performance for the majority class and promising but still limited perfor-
mance for minority classes. The discussion highlights the complexity of the problem, reflecting
both biological and technical limitations that deserve continued attention. Nevertheless, this
thesis demonstrates the feasibility of automated, AI-based diagnostics in this clinical setting
and lays a foundation for future improvements in precision, interpretability, and broader clini-
cal application.

Conclusion and Future Work
This thesis aimed to develop an artificial intelligence pipeline capable of diagnosing and moni-
toring jaw misalignment in adolescents with Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis using 3D facial land-
mark data. The proposed deep learning framework demonstrated promising potential, achiev-
ing robust performance especially in distinguishing patients without temporomandibular joint
involvement from those with mild to severe conditions. Specifically, the developed models
reached macro-average F1 scores exceeding 0.7 for the left side predictions, demonstrating that
landmark-based features and carefully engineered geometric measures can effectively capture
relevant clinical patterns of facial asymmetry.
However, the results also highlighted significant challenges. Classification performance varied
noticeably among different levels of TMJ involvement, with the lowest accuracy observed in
moderate and severe categories. Macro-average F1 scores for the right-side predictions and the
combined diagnosis were lower than for the left, ranging between 0.56 and 0.63. This variability
is attributed primarily to the scarcity of data points in the more severe categories, the subtlety
of asymmetry patterns associated with intermediate clinical states, and inherent limitations in
the clinical labeling process based on orthopantomographic imaging.
One crucial finding is the consistently high weighted-average F1 scores across all prediction
tasks (ranging from 0.86 to 0.92), indicating that the model excels at identifying the majority
class (healthy or balanced). Yet, the disparity between weighted-average and macro-average
metrics underscores the difficulty of accurately classifying minority classes. This imbalance
poses a significant obstacle for deploying the current model directly into clinical settings with-
out further refinements.
Moreover, an important biological limitation was identified: facial asymmetry does not al-
ways indicate TMJ involvement, and conversely, TMJ involvement may not always result in pro-
nounced facial asymmetry. Thus, the model inherently faces a diagnostic ambiguity rooted in
biology itself. Technical sources of error, such as inaccuracies stemming from facial alignment
and landmark extraction software, further contribute to these diagnostic challenges.
Despite these limitations, the presented pipeline forms a robust basis for future research and
has strong potential for clinical utility if further developed. With continued improvements in
model accuracy and generalization, it could substantially reduce reliance on traditional diag-
nostic imaging methods, such as orthopantomographs, thereby decreasing patient exposure
to ionizing radiation from X-rays, particularly important for pediatric populations undergoing
repeated assessments.
Future Work
To address the identified limitations and build upon the findings of this study, several avenues
for future research are proposed:
• Data expansion and stratification: Acquiring additional data points across all diagnos-
tic classes, particularly moderate and severe categories, would significantly enhance the
model’s ability to generalize. Future datasets should ideally include stratification by age,
gender, and ethnicity, enabling more personalized diagnostic models that explicitly ac-

• Advancements in input data technology: Developing computer vision algorithms capa-
ble of reconstructing accurate 3D facial surfaces from standard 2D photographs could sub-
stantially increase the accessibility of the diagnostic pipeline. Such advancements would
facilitate widespread adoption of the model in clinical practices lacking specialized 3D
imaging equipment.
• Algorithmic innovation and enhanced modeling strategies: Experimenting with more
sophisticated model architectures, such as graph neural networks or attention-based meth-
ods, might better exploit the relational structure among landmarks, potentially improving
accuracy in minority classes. Additionally, advanced data augmentation techniques and
customized loss functions specifically targeting minority classes could help address cur-
rent imbalances.
• Longitudinal validation studies: Conducting longitudinal research to assess the model’s
performance in tracking treatment progression over time would better address the second
research question posed in this thesis. This would validate the model’s clinical relevance
beyond single-time-point classification and provide valuable insights into treatment out-
comes.
• Improving landmarking accuracy and reducing technical errors: Investing in the refine-
ment of facial alignment and landmark detection software will reduce propagated errors
and enhance the reliability of the entire diagnostic pipeline. Such enhancements are cru-
cial to increase clinician trust and facilitate broader clinical adoption.
In conclusion, although the current model is not yet ready for direct clinical application due to
limitations in accurately predicting moderate and severe TMJ involvement, it provides a solid
foundation for further development. With targeted improvements addressing class imbalance,
algorithm robustness, and biological complexity, future iterations of this AI-based diagnostic
tool could substantially reduce the need for radiographic diagnostics, decrease patient expo-
sure to radiation, and offer a precise, reproducible alternative for assessing TMJ-related facial
asymmetry in patients with JIA.
