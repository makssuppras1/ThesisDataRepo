Abstract
The two main structural elements composing the jacket structure are the chords and the braces
while the tubular joint is the point of connection of the two. In structural analysis of o’¼Ćshore
structures, joints are frequently assumed completely rigid, hence local distortions of the chord
circular cross section are assumed to be negligible. Nevertheless, this has been con’¼ürmed to be a
limiting assumption in several research works in literature.
Tubular joints exhibit a considerable ’¼éexibility in both elastic and plastic range of response. This
’¼éexibility can be de’¼üned as Local Joint Flexibility (LJF), and it is an intrinsic feature of tubular
joints that a’¼Ćects both the global static and dynamic responses of an o’¼Ćshore structure.
This thesis project focuses on the creation of a simpli’¼üed ’¼ünite element (FE) model for the tubular
joint that can take into account of its ’¼éexibility by the introduction of a ŌĆ£special joint elementŌĆØ
incorporated in the simpli’¼üed FE model of the tubular joint. The characteristics of the special joint
element are obtained through the use of a Di’¼Ćerential Evolution best/1/bin optimization algorithm.
The optimization algorithm guarantees the minimization of the di’¼Ćerence between the response
results obtained from an optimized simpli’¼üed model and those coming from a correspondent shell
model. The latter represents the benchmark model, since it already takes into account of all the
e’¼Ćects given by LJF.
The optimization provides an optimal simpli’¼üed model that is able to mimic the correspondent
shell response in terms of eigenfrequencies and records static responses closer to the shell model as
well. Additionally, the use of this optimized model has positive e’¼Ćects also once it is plugged in a
full beam structure model, it remarkably narrows down the gap in eigenfrequencies with respect to
a full shell model of the corresponding structure studied.
For this thesis project the tubular joints types studied are the following: T-, K- and TY- joints.

Introduction
Tubular structures such as the jacket substructure of both o’¼Ćshore platforms and o’¼Ćshore wind
turbines are generally fabricated by welding circular hollow section members, though also other
methods are possible. The location where the welding takes place is the connection between the
chord member the brace members. This connection is called tubular joint. In Figure 1, an example
of tubular joint is presented together with the names used for each part of the welding.
Figure 1: Two-planar tubular DK-joints in an o’¼Ćshore jacket structure [3].
The tubular joint has been the subject of numerous research works aimed to ’¼ünd an appropriate
formulation to de’¼üne the ŌĆ£Local Joint FlexibilityŌĆØ (LJF) typical of tubular joints. The LJF is one
of the factors a’¼Ćecting the behavior of a tubular joint and consequently a’¼Ćecting the global static
and dynamic responses of a jacket structure. Many investigations have shown the not-negligible
e’¼Ćect of LJF in both the elastic and plastic ranges of response of the overall structure.
Conventionally, in structural analysis of o’¼Ćshore platforms, jacket structures are modeled by con-
sidering tubular members rigidly interconnected to each other at nodal points by beam elements.
However, rigid connections mainly used in practice are not capable of modeling the multi-axial
responses of the joints, such as the progressive ŌĆ£ovalizingŌĆØ of the chord cross section which is rather
a shell-like behavior.
As a consequence of what has been said so far, the scope of this thesis is to create a simpli’¼üed ’¼ünite
element model that can take into account of the LJF.
After a review of the main literature, the organization of this project starts with a study on the
e’¼Ćectiveness of di’¼Ćerent geometrical parameters on the static response of the joints considered.
This is required to verify what has been read in literature and in addition it helps to choose the
geometries for the T,K and TY joints to be used for next stages of the project.
The creation of the simpli’¼üed model starts only after having explained the necessity to include
a ŌĆ£special joint elementŌĆØ inside the simpli’¼üed model.

2.1

Welded steel tubular joints are the kind of connection used extensively in the construction of ’¼üxed
o’¼Ćshore steel structures. However, the use of tubular connections in circular hollow sections is not

3.1
FE method to create three types of tubular joints
The ’¼ünite element method is a numerical technique used to ’¼ünd approximate solutions to problems
described by di’¼Ćerential equations. In structural engineering it is used to analyze load subjected
structural systems that are not easy to be be calculated analytically. It is possible to model a struc-
tural system by interconnecting elements provided of physical properties so to obtain the desired
outputs like: displacements, rotations, reaction forces, stresses strains.The type of elements used
in this project are the beam and shell elements.
Regarding the beam element used, it consists of 2 nodes each having 3 DOFs hence 1 horizontal,
1 vertical displacements and 1 rotation. The classical Euler-Bernoulli beam theory has been used
in this project for beam elements, so to exclude the cases when transverse shear strain needs to be
taken into account.
Accordingly to what has been chosen for the beam, the shell element considered in this project is
a ŌĆ£thinŌĆØ shell element. A shell is a structural part in which the thickness is much smaller than the
other two dimensions and it is able to carry loads in all directions so it can have both in-plane and
out-plane deformations. The number of degrees of freedom per each node for a shell element is 6,
meaning 3 translations and three rotations, since the shell element is a combination of plate and
membrane element.
Shell problems generally fall into one of two categories: thin shell problems and thick shell prob-
lems. Thin shell problems assume that transverse shear deformation is small enough to be neglected
while thick shell problems assume that the e’¼Ćects of transverse shear deformation are important
to the solution.
As previously said, in this project the thin shell element is used and this sets a limit for the thickness
of the shell element modelled which needs to be 1/15 of the characteristic length. If the thickness
is larger, then the shell is considered thick and the Kirchho’¼ĆŌĆÖs plate theory wonŌĆÖt be applicable
anymore.

4.1
Di’¼Ćerential evolution optimization best/1/bin (DE best/1/bin)
The optimization strategy is the main tool constantly used throughout this thesis. The optimiza-
tion works by using both the results obtained from the beam and shell models to contribute to
reach the aim of the thesis by satisfying the objective functions de’¼üned. More details about how
this optimization tool is used in this thesis will be explained in the next subsection, while in this
subsection the optimization algorithm is explained in its logic and process.
The optimization strategy used is the ŌĆ£Di’¼Ćerential evolution, strategy best/1/binŌĆØ (DE best/1/bin)
from the Python SciPy optimization package. This optimization ’¼ünds the global minimum of a
multivariate function.
As can be intended from the name ŌĆ£Di’¼Ćerential evolutionŌĆØ, it is a type of evolutionary algorithm,
hence it uses mechanisms inspired by the theory of evolution. In other words, the ’¼üttest individuals
of a population are the ones that produce more o’¼Ćspring, which in turn inherit the good traits of
the parents. This makes the population improve over time, generation after generation thanks to
di’¼Ćerent genetic mechanisms such as mutation, recombination and selection.
The Di’¼Ćerential Evolution algorithm can be considered as a sequence of steps: initialization of the
population, evaluation, mutation and recombination, replacement and ’¼ünal evaluation.
The main items to de’¼üne before explaining each phase of the optimization algorithm are: popula-
tion, variables, bounds and objective functions.
ŌĆó Population: number of vectors (or individuals) available for the optimization.
ŌĆó Variables: parameters to be optimized, they de’¼üne the dimension ŌĆ£dŌĆØ of the optimization
problem. Variables are the set of values composing each single vector (or individual) of the
population.
ŌĆó Bounds: domain of validity for variables.
ŌĆó Objective function: it is the function to minimize, so it de’¼ünes how good a vector of the
population is for the optimization scope.
It is important to notice that this optimization method doesnŌĆÖt always guarantee to ’¼ünd the global
minimum but increases the chances to reach it.
The di’¼āculty of ’¼ünding the optimal solution
increases exponentially with the dimension ŌĆ£dŌĆØ (dependent on the amount of variables).
This
problem is called ŌĆ£curse of dimensionalityŌĆØ.
For example: letŌĆÖs suppose that the variables composing each vector of the population can have only
a binary value (i.e. either 0 or 1), the number of combinations to be studied by the optimization are
2d. Where 2 are the possible values a variable can have (i.e. either 0 or 1) while ŌĆ£dŌĆØ is dependent
on how many variables are contained in a vector.
To solve the problem of ŌĆ£curse of dimensionalityŌĆØ it is possible to increase the population size or
increase the number of iterations as much as required so to let the optimization result converge
always to the same value.

4.1.1
Step 1: Initialization
The ’¼ürst step in every evolutionary algorithm is the creation of a population with vectors (or in-

The initial population is composed of the ’¼ürst vector that represents the ŌĆ£initial guessŌĆØ inputted
by the user, while the remaining vectors of the population are composed of random values for each
variable. Though being random values, they still remain within the given bounds set by the user.

Step 2: Evaluation
After having created the population, the objective function needs to be used to evaluate which
vectors of the population are better than others to reach the minimization of the objective function.
The best vector is then selected and represents the staring point for the next step, hence mutation
and recombination.

Comparison and ’¼ünal comments
It is relevant to highlight that the study conducted in this project is especially useful for tubular
structure with small bays lengths and heights, since this means that the joints are closer one to the
other and their ’¼éexibility have an important impact on the response. This statement is con’¼ürmed
further by the results in subsection 3.3 (in Table 15) where the impact of joint ’¼éexibility got reduced
as the chord length increased.
It can be concluded by comparing Table 55 and Table 57 that in case of the structure composed of
tubular joints, shown in Figure 42, it is possible to optimize singularly each joint element and then
plug them in the structure to obtain still an important improvement in terms of eigenfrequencies for
the structure. Thus, what has been done in subsection 6.1 could be preferred instead of optimizing
the full structure as it has been done in subsection 6.2, since it can be traduced into solving less
computationally heavy optimizations and possibly use the same optimized joint models for other
structures containing them. Further details in the next section.

7
Conclusion and further work
In literature it has been highlighted many times the necessity to take into account of the ’¼éexibility
of the tubular joint when working with this type of connections. The usual error is to consider
this joint as rigid since it is an easier solution for the modelling but at the same time it causes not
negligible errors in the ’¼ünal outputs, such as static responses and eigenfrequencies results.
Therefore, the purpose of this project was to create a simpli’¼üed model that could take into account
of the tubular joint ’¼éexibility e’¼Ćects typically recorded in a shell model but missed by a beam
model with rigid connection. This aim has been achieved by creating a beam model for a tubular
joint that could include a new element called ŌĆ£special joint elementŌĆØ, whose characteristics could be
customized as desired, independently from features of the rest of the joint. In this project 3 types
of tubular joints have been considered, hence: T joint, K joint and TY joint and for all of them the
ŌĆ£special joint elementŌĆØ to be optimized has been considered as shown in Figure 28. The parameters
of the special joint elements to be optimized are 4, the diameter (djoint), thickness (tjoint), length
(ajoint) and YoungŌĆÖs modulus (Ejoint).
The optimizations performed for all the 3 types of joints in this project have shown that it is
possible to get the optimized values for the special joint elementsŌĆÖ parameters in order to have an
optimized beam model with eigenfrequencies as close as possible to the correspondent shell model.
It has been found that the eigenfrequencies are an important output on which the optimization
should be based since they are not load dependent compared to an eventual optimization based on
static cases which may lead to results valid only for a speci’¼üc load case.
The results obtained have shown that for all the 3 joint types the optimized beam models have
been able not only to reach the eigenfrequencies of the corresponding shell models, but also have
registered some improvements in the static response for two load cases, with respect to the not
optimized beam models using rigid joint connection. The results just mentioned can be found in
section 5.
Moreover, some additional conclusions can be made once the optimized beam models found for the
3 joint types have been implemented in a full structure. Their substitution in a full beam structure
instead of the rigid connections have improved remarkably both the eigenfrequencies for the ’¼ürst 4
modes of the structure, and the static response for a speci’¼üc load case studied. This means that for
the considered full structure it can be preferred to separately generate an optimized beam model
for each tubular joint composing the structure and then substitute the optimized beam models in
the full structure. This is far more advantageous with respect to start a new optimization running
for the entire structure and optimizing the joints all together in the same optimization. This latter
case leads to optimizations with high number of variables and consequently higher computational
times required.
What stated in the last paragraph opens the horizons to some future work that can be done to
continue this study. A ’¼ürst suggestion for future work is to apply the optimized beam models for
the T,K and TY joints obtained in section 5 in other structures. This can be done to check whether
an improvement in eigenfrequencies and static response happens not only for the structure studied
in section 6 but also for other structures.
In case this future work leads to positive results, then it may be really useful to look for some
regression functions that can ’¼ünd the optimized parameters for the special joint element to be

optimized by simply receiving some input data about the geometry and material of the tubular
joint. By ’¼ünding a regression function it will be even not necessary to use the optimization algorithm
but the optimized beam model for the joint will be even easier to be obtained.
In this project, the parameters used for the optimization are 4 as stated before. Then, an additional
idea for a further work is to substitute the ’¼ürst optimization parameter i.e. djoint with the cross
section area and moments of inertia instead. This means that after the optimization of the cross
section area and moments of inertia, the resulting optimized special joint element has not a tubular
cross section but a generalized one. However, this optimization can lead to optimization results
leading optimized beam models even closer to the shell models outcomes.
Finally, all these future works may be performed not only for T,K and TY joints but also with
other joint types, especially the double plane joints like the DK-joints. The double plane joints
have braces on to planes as shown in section 1 in Figure 1, this means that their study allows to
extend the analysis done in this project to a 3D environment.
