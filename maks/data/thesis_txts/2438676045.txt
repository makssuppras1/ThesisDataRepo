High Frequency and High efficiency Sigma 
DC-DC converter 
Abstract 
There has been a long desire to achieve both high power density and high efficiency. LLC resonant 
converter has been proved as a good candidate to satisfy these requirements by increasing its 
switching frequency and integrating its magnetic components. It has become a popular converter 
and been used in many applications. 
To shrink the size, the resonant inductor is usually integrated into the transformer. Besides, it has 
also been proved that LLC can achieve highest efficiency at resonant frequency since there is no 
circulation loss. The resonant inductor can be very small and is capable to be replaced by the 
transformer leakage inductance. However, it cannot regulate the output voltage due to the low 
inductance ratio. The converter operating in this state is called DC transformer. A boost converter is 
connected with the LLC in series in primary side and in parallel in secondary side, which only takes 
a very small portion of power to regulate the output voltage. Therefore, the whole system can 
achieve both high efficiency and regulation purpose, because large power is transferred by the LLC 
resonant converter and the boost converter is capable to regulate the output voltage. The topology 
of the combination of the LLC and boost is called sigma converter. This converter is supposed to 
regulate the output power ranging from 100W to 800W. 
This paper focuses on the optimal design of LLC resonant converter, including circuit parameters 
design, magnetics integration and winding techniques. The operating principle of LLC converter is 
==PAGE:4==
III 
 
first introduced and detailed magnetics integration method and winding layout are given next. A 
customized core design guideline is proposed and no via connection on secondary SRs to eliminate 
the termination loss. Finally, a prototype with stacking structure operating at 1MHz 390V input and 
12V/800W is built to test theory design. The 96.2% highest efficiency and 105W/in3 power density 
are expected.

LLC resonant converters can achieve its highest efficiency at resonant frequency. Therefore, they 
are designed as a DC transformer to maintain the highest efficiency in most applications [10]-[14]. 
The resonant inductor is integrated into the transformer by using its leakage inductance, as shown 
in Fig. 1. The output voltage should be regulated by changing the converter frequency. This causes 
additional circulation loss, and LLC cannot operate as a DC transformer. An alternative to obtain 
high voltage gain with narrowed operating frequency range can be achieved by designing a large 
resonant inductor to create a low inductance ratio of magnetizing inductance to resonant inductance. 
If discrete magnetic components are used for LLC resonant converter, the large inductor may require 
extra winding loss and core loss, as well as large size of the component [15]-[19]. Therefore, the 
resonant inductor is usually integrated into the transformer by utilizing its leakage inductance to 
increase its power density and efficiency especially when LLC converter operates at high frequency 
[20]-[23].  Literature [24] proposes a new method to create a larger resonant inductance by using

If interleaving strategy is implemented to decrease both leakage inductance and AC resistance, the 
regulation capability is sacrificed. The power loss can be very severe at light load condition.  
In order to achieve both high efficiency and regulation capability, the topology of the sigma 
converter is proposed and illustrated in Fig. 2. It can be seen that a boost converter is connected 
with the LLC in series in primary side and in parallel in secondary side. A small portion of power is 
taken by the boost converter for regulation purpose, while large power is transferred by the LLC 
resonant converter operating as a DC transformer.

Stage 2 (t1≤t≤t2): After Q1 turns on, the resonant inductor Lr and resonant capacitor Cr continue in

Chapter 3 LLC resonant converter design  
Considering the large turn ratio, a large current is flowing throw secondary devices. Single 
center-tapped rectifier cannot handle this large current. Thus synchronous rectification and 
secondary windings in parallel with each other should be taken into consideration.  
3.1 Topology selection for LLC resonant converter 
There are two structures to choose for LLC resonant converter, which are shown in Fig. 7. The 
synchronous rectification (SR) devices have limited current capability due to packaging and 
thermal constrain. For server applications, one should consider paralleling four to eight SRs to 
reduce conduction loss as illustrated in Fig. 7 (a). However, when paralleling a large number 
of SRs, both static and dynamic current sharing are difficult to achieve. For example, each SR 
has different on resistance, and the turn-on and turn-off time may be different due to different 
Drain-to-Source capacitance. Consequently, each SR conducts different current. In addition, it 
can be seen that four secondary windings are in parallel with each other. Considering PCB 
layout, these four parallel windings should be connected through vias. The high-frequency 
operation causing high di/dt ac current must flow through these vias, which are marked using 
red dots in Fig. 7 (a). This will generates large termination losses and hot pots[22]. Therefore, 
structure shown in Fig. 7 (b) is commonly used in this case. The turn ratio 16:1:1 is divided 
into 4×4：1：1. Each SR follows one secondary winding. No current sharing problem occurs. 
Besides, this structure also eliminates termination loss because no via is needed to be connected 
to parallel secondary winding.

4.3 Vias and Paralled Winding Consideration 
The paralleled secondary winding is a common practice to reduce current density and further reduce 
winding loss. 3-D FEA simulations is conducted to investigate the current sharing problem due to 
paralleled secondary winding at 1MHz. As shown in Fig. 18, the top and bottom secondary winding 
are connected through vias. The current density distributed on the top winding is obviously lower 
than that on the bottom winding. Therefore, the large winding current cannot be simply decreased 
by using paralleled winding method in high frequency transformer design. In addition, current is 
not uniformly distributed in these vias to connect the bottom and top winding. High power losses 
and hot pots occurs[22].

4.5 Power devices selection for LLC resonant converter 
GaN devices are selected for LLC primary switches because they has low output capacitance Coss 
and drain-to-source resistance Rds_on. Low Coss requires small discharging current and the large 
magnetizing inductance can be designed. As discussed in section 3.2, both primary and secondary 
RMS current are inversely proportional to magnetizing inductance. LLC resonant converters with 
large magnetizing inductance has low conduction loss. GS66508B from GaN systems is determined 
as primary switches.  
Several power devices for the LLC resonant converter secondary side are illustrated in TABLE II. 
The GaN device EPC2023 does not show much benefit. Both drain-to-source resistance Rds_on and 
output capacitance are higher than silicon MOSFET. In addition, Rds_on of EPC2023 has larger 
variation under different junction temperature. Besides, both BSC011N03LS and BCS0500NSI 
have larger drain pads with SuperSO8 package, which indicate better thermal characteristics.

d. NCP4308 
The maximum frequency NCP4308 can operate is 1MHz. In this case, this chip is operating at its 
maximum frequency and problems may occurs. Both resonant frequency and switching frequency 
decrease to 500 kHz, and the transformer with PCB winding and the other with liz wire are 
implemented to solve this problem. 
However, 8 NCP4308 are hard to work synchronously. The author has tried every method to tune 
the surrounding resistors of each chip, which requires much effort to tune each controller. Although 
all the controller work synchronously, the low frequency noise still exists when the supply voltage 
(from the output) is higher than the UVLO of NCP4308. The resonant current waveform is seriously 
distorted and the converter cannot work.  
Another solution to solve this problem is to replace all synchronous rectification controller (8 
NCP4308) with gate drivers. Controller signals can be generated by FPGA or function generator. 
Since each 4 MOSFET conduct current at the same time, only 2 gate drives are needed. The ground 
plane for gate drives should be placed away from the area with primary winding on the bottom. 
However, this solution cannot be implemented in current PCB.

Power losses of LLC resonant converter is described in Chapter 3. Loss breakdown of LLC resonant 
converter is shown in Fig. 35, which is calculated under full load conditions. It can be seen that the

sigma converter at full load, the efficiency is expected to achieve 96.2% with power density

Chapter 7 Conclusion and Future Work 
7.1  Conclusion 
The concept of sigma converter is capable to achieve both high efficiency and regulation purpose. 
The LLC resonant converter is designed as a DC transformer to achieve high efficiency, while the 
boost converter regulates the output voltage with negligible penalty on total system efficiency.  The 
proposed magnetic core and winding structure for LLC resonant converter avoid the paralleled 
winding practice and vias connections. Detailed design procedure of the customized core, optimal 
LLC parameters design, discussion of synchronous rectification and components selection are given 
in this thesis. The expected efficiency of the sigma converter is 96.2% 
7.2  Future Work 
The main problems for the converter are the large winding loss and low frequency noise. Solutions 
for these two problems are given separately. In addition to the main problems mentioned above, 
another research for sigma converter is the effectiveness of stacking structure and the control taken 
by the boost converter.  
a. Winding loss 
In high frequency and high current transformer design, paralleled winding practice and vias 
connection should be avoided as discussed in Section 4.3. Moreover, primary windings and 
secondary windings should be fully overlapped to achieve flux cancellation, which reduces AC 
resistance.  
==PAGE:57==
48 
 
b. Low frequency noise 
As discussed in Section 6.2, there are several potential reasons for the low frequency noise. The 
noise starts when 8 synchronous rectification controllers NCP4308 begin to work. Instead of using 
the controller, a potential solution is to use gate drivers and the input PWM signal can be generated 
by FPGA or function generator. It should be noted here that the ground plane for gate drives should 
be placed away from the area with primary windings on the bottom. 
c. Stacking structure 
The magnetic core is placed in the middle with power devices and gate drives on the top and bottom. 
The leakage flux from the magnetic core may pose a threat to the working performance of these 
components. Whether stacking structure is effective deserves further investigation. 
d. Control 
The whole sigma converter is capable to control the load steps with the low power transfer through 
the boost converter, but it is highly dependent on the efficiency of the LLC resonant converter. The 
boost converter can be replaced by other converters operating in buck-boost mode, for example, 
active clamp flyback converter.
