Abstract
This thesis investigates how geothermal energy can be integrated in Greater Copen-
hagen’s district heating system. Geothermal energy can play an important role if the
ambition of being carbon-free by 2025 should be fulﬁlled. The project focuses on a 74
MWth geothermal plant based on electricity-driven heat pumps. The plant is expected
to be located in Nordhavn and fully operational from 2020-2045.
The energy system is thermodynamically modelled in EES in order to optimize the
operating economy. Coupling heat pumps in series is seen as a prominent solution
to improve the system performance. The optimal conﬁguration is a system with 4 x
1-stage heat pumps with a payback time of 15.8 years (private-economic). A physi-
cal constraint is that the required condensing capacity for the optimal system is in the
range of ∼12 MWth pr. heat pump, while the currently largest compression heat pump
in operation is 4.5 MWth. Using 2-stage heat pumps as an alternative showed the cap-
ital investments were too high compared to the gain in performance. Cost-estimation
was performed using cost-correlations based on Danish trade business and compared
to ofﬁcial key indicators from the Danish Energy Agency. The economic results only
deviated by 8.7%. Connecting it to the transmission grid decreased the payback time
by 1.1 year and lead to higher heat production prices.
A feasibility study shows that the geothermal plant would reach a break-even for 6050
full load hours/year. The results conﬁrm that it suits best as a base load plant. Com-
parison with its variable heat production price shows that its competitive position in
daily heat planning is limited due to its requirement for paying taxes and tariffs for
electricity. Changing the regulatory framework by introducing a subsidy that excludes
PSO-tariff would decrease the payback time by 4.8 years. A sensitivity analysis indi-
cated that capital investments for drilling, electricity prices and system performance
had the largest impact on the operating economy.
Based on the ﬁndings, it is recommended to change the regulatory framework and
using the system optimized geothermal plant. Reducing capital investments require
more experience and expertise. These initiatives support heat generation using elec-
tricity and contribute to integration of geothermal energy. Furthermore, the geothermal
plant can utilize the excess electricity production from wind power, which is currently
exported to the Scandinavian countries and thereby generates a loss, as electricity is
exported without taxes and tariffs. The integration further contributes to liberalisation
from biomass, which can play an important role for the future Danish energy system if
biomass is expected to be a ﬁnite resource.

Resume
Dette speciale undersøger hvordan geotermisk energi kan integreres i Storkøbenhavns
fjernvarmesystem. Geotermisk energi kan spille en central rolle, hvis ambitionen om
at være CO2-fri i 2025 skal opfyldes.. Der fokuseres speciﬁkt på et 74 MWth geotermisk
anlæg bestående af elektricitets-drevne varmepumper. Anlægget forventes at blive
lokaliseret i Nordhavn og fuldt ud operationel fra 2020-2045.
Energisystemet er modelleret i EES for at kunne optimere driftsøkonomien. Kobling af
varmepumper i serier viser sig at være en lovende løsning for at øge systemydelsen.
Den optimale konﬁguration er 4 x 1-trins varmepumper med en tilbagebetalingstid på
15.8 år (privatøkonomisk). En fysisk begrænsning er at varmepumpernes varmeka-
pacitet er 12 MWth og den nuværende største varmepumpe i drift er på 4.5 MWth. An-
vendelse af 2-trins varmepumper viser at kapitalomostningerne er for store i forhold
til genvisten i ydelsen. Økonomien er bestemt ud fra kost korrelationer baseret på
danske industripriser og sammenlignet med Energistyrelsens nøgletal, som blot viser
en afvigelse på 8.7%. Forbindelsen til transmissionsnettet forværrer tilbagebetalingsti-
den med 1.1 år, da systemydelsen forværres under højere temperatur-løft. Endvidere
stiger varmeproduktionsprisen som konsekvens af dette.
Et feasibility studie viser at anlægget opnår en break-even for 6050 fuldlasttimer/år og
bekræfter dermed at anlægget tilpasses bedst som en grundlast enhed. Sammenligning
af anlæggets variable varmeproduktionspriser viser at dens konkurrenceevne i daglig
fjernvarme-planlægning er begrænset i forhold til biomasse eftersom anlægget skal be-
tale tariffer og afgifter til anvendelse af elektricitet. Ændring af de regulatoriske ram-
mer, eksempelvis ved fjernelse af PSO-tariffen, vil øge konkurrenceevnen betydeligt og
medføre en reducering i tilbagebetalingstiden på 4.8 år. En sensitivitetsanalyse indik-
erer endvidere at kapitalomkostninger til boring, elpriser og systemydelsen har den
største indﬂydelse på driftsøkonomien.
Baseret på disse resultater, er det anbefalet at ændre de regulatoriske rammer og an-
vende det optimeret geotermiske anlæg. Reducering af kapitalomkostninger er vur-
deret til at kræve mere erfaring og ekspertise på nuværende tidspunkt. Disse anbe-
falinger kan fremskynde integrationen af geotermisk energi og generelt store elektricitets-
drevne varmepumper. Integrationen vil kunne udnytte en stor andel af elproduktionen
fra vindenergi som ellers bliver eksporteret til de skandinaviske lande uden tariffer og
afgifter. Eksport af el uden tariffer og afgifter genererer et tab samfundsøkonomisk. In-
tegrationen af geotermi bidrager derudover også til liberalisering af biomasse, hvilket
kan spille en central rolle hvis biomasse forventes at være en ﬁnit ressource.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Denmark has set an ambitious goal to maintain its leading position in attaining an en-
ergy production based on renewable energy. By 2050, the aim is to cover the whole
energy supply (electricity, heating, industry and transport) by renewable energy. The
European community is becoming more dependent on imported fuels. Researching
in renewable energy can both contribute towards job creation and the economy of the
European community. Sustainable energy development involves increasing energy ac-
cess, security of supply and affordability with the objective to reduce negative environ-
mental impacts [1].
New challenges arise when the energy system needs to be based on ﬂuctuating re-
sources. This requires infrastructural changes in the energy system. Thorough research
and scenario planning ensure that the nation follows the optimal direction with mini-
mal economic costs. Several research projects undergo different strategies to fulﬁl these
targets. As of 2015, Denmark is in a front-position of producing electricity from wind
turbines and using biomass for Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plants.
The following project focuses on the heat production for Greater Copenhagen. Copen-
hagen has an even more ambitious goal of being the world’s ﬁrst carbon free capital by
2025. According to its climate plan (2012), the goal is to cover Copenhagen’s power and
heat generation by wind, biomass, geothermal energy and waste. The changes require
extensive planning and expertise [2] [3].
Greater Copenhagen’s district heating companies CTR, VEKS and HOFOR have car-
ried out "Heat Plan Greater Copenhagen - Part 3", which analyses the development of
the capitals district heating system towards 2050. The main purpose of the project was
to enhance the knowledge to end up taking the decisive decisions on which road-plan
the heating sector should follow in the metropolitan area. Even though there is an ac-
knowledgement of the geothermal energy potential, their plan deviates from using it
due to high capital investments and lack of experience of using large heat pumps in-
tegrated with geothermal energy. This leads to this project, which aims to investigate

1.2
Literature review
The study of integrating geothermal energy in Denmark has been researched for sev-
eral years through demonstration plants, energy-planning tools (STREAM, Energy-
Plan) and academic research. The aim of this section is to review published literature
related to this dissertation.
Harrestrup et al. (2014) showed that geothermal energy could play an important role in
the future Copenhagen’s district heating system with promising results. Especially due
to the question if biomass will continue to play a large role in a long-term perspective.
Biomass is seen as a ﬁnite energy resource, hence new alternative renewable energy
sources must be considered [5]. P. Østergaard et al. (2011) concluded that geothermal
plants could pay a signiﬁcant role in a future district heating system. The assessment
of the geothermal energy shows enormous potential for district heating application in
Denmark [6].
Greater Copenhagen’s heating companies carried out a Heat Plan - Part 1 (2009) that
contains different scenarios with integration of geothermal energy. They evaluated it
could decrease the dependence on biomass, although being a more expensive scenario
from a socio-economic perspective. They concluded that the price movement of fuels
and experience on geothermal energy could make it an attractive solution [7].
Heat Plan - Part 2 (2011) further discussed the possibilities of integrating large scale
geothermal plants with a capacity of 65 MWth based on electricity driven heat pumps.
Again, similar conclusions were drawn as for the ﬁrst heat plan. They concluded that
initiatives are required for changing the framework conditions of the high energy taxes
and tariffs on electricity [8].

1.3
Thesis statement
The objective of this thesis is to assess how geothermal energy can be integrated in
Greater Copenhagen’s district heating system by using vapour compression heat pumps
(VCHP). The assessment bases on creating a thermodynamic model of a geothermal
district heating system using the software Engineering Equation Solver (EES). The pur-
pose is to optimize and design the optimal conﬁguration while considering both econ-
omy and performance. In addition, a feasibility study will be performed to evaluate the
economy from a socio- and a private-economic perspective in order to make an overall
energy system assessment.
Research question
How can Greater Copenhagen’s district heating system integrate geothermal energy
using vapour compression heat pumps?
Project content
I. Integration of compression heat pumps in Greater Copenhagen’s district heating
system using geothermal energy.
• Thermodynamic modelling, design and optimization of a Geothermal Dis-
trict Heating System using vapour compression heat pumps.
• Finding optimal amount of heat pumps in series in terms of performance and
economy.
• Literature survey of large-scale heat pump components and economic evalu-
ation using component-based cost correlations.

Background
2.1
District heating in Copenhagen
District heating is an efﬁcient way to distribute heat through insulated pipes in a cen-
tralized and dense area. The District Heating Network (DHN) consists of several heat-
producing units that creates a ﬂexible system to fulﬁl the heat demand. The existing
DHN has already underwent changes in terms of fuel usage. The previous changes
are transitions from oil to coal and coal to gas and biomass. To fulﬁl the carbon free
targets, Greater Copenhagen is now experiencing a new transition to 100% renewable
energy. The district heating system in Copenhagen provides space heating and domes-
tic hot water to the urbanized and dense area of Copenhagen. The produced heat is
mainly from CHP plants, where some of them already have been renovated to oper-
ate on 100% biomass (Amager Plant Block 1, 2010). 75% of the carbon reductions are
planned to come from the capital’s energy system while the district heating system ac-
counts for much of it. The distribution temperature is 75◦C/50◦C during summer and
85◦C/50◦C during winter. Producing units which are directly coupled to the transmis-
sion grid has a forward temperature of 105◦C/55◦C during summer and 115◦C/55◦C
during winter.
Figure 2.1 shows a map of the current DHN in Greater Copenhagen. The colours rep-
resent the different supply sources. There are four supply companies: VEKS, CTR,
HOFOR and VF. The CHP plants distribute district heat to the three supplier compa-
nies CTR, HOFOR and VEKS, which are responsible for distributing it to consumers
or local supplier companies. The system provides 98% of Greater Copenhagen’s heat
demand. There have been discussions whether district heating would play a role in the
future due to a lower heat demand (i.e. passive houses, efﬁcient energy use). Gustavs-
sons et al. (2011) ﬁndings indicate that district heat would still play a major role in a
future energy system due to its high degree of ﬂexibility for implementing renewable
energy [13] [14]. M. Münster et al. (2012) observed similar ﬁndings, as an cost-effective
expansion of the DHN could contribute to sustainability and security of supply [15].

2.2
Geothermal energy for district heating
Geothermal energy is contained in the earth’s crust and produced from the geothermal
layers. Certain geological areas consist of water reservoirs, which can be used for both
heat and power production. In other areas, water is injected to the soil to produce
power. The temperature of the subsoil layers in Denmark increases by 25-30◦C for
every 1,000 m of depth. The extracted geothermal water is saline with a concentration
ranging from 10-20% salinity. The potential of geothermal energy in Denmark is large
enough to utilize for district heating purposes. DGDH estimates that the geothermal
potential in the metropolitan area is approximately 60,000 PJ (∼1/3 of Copenhagen’s
heat demand for up to 5000 years). It emphasizes the enormous potential to cover the
capital’s heating demand. The lack of knowledge and high capital investments halt
the development of geothermal energy in CPH. Thus, the following factors still make
geothermal energy worth considering:
• Renewable energy source
Geothermal energy categorises as renewable energy and has an enormous poten-
tial for heat production.
• Biomass is a ﬁnite source
Biomass resource is a limited energy resource in Denmark and may be a tempo-
rary solution until 2040. Denmark uses available land extensively for agricultural
purposes and thereby limit the production of biomass [6] [18].
• Experience lead to less risks and investment
More experience and research in the ﬁeld reduce the high capital investments
making it an attractive solution [19].
• Non-competitive fuel prices
Fuel prices may rise if supply does not match demand →market determined
price unlike a stable heat source as geothermal energy.
• Focus on using large heat pumps using renewable sources in Denmark
The Danish Energy Agency has initiated a task-force of specialist to establish
a collaboration for using large heat pumps to identify if there is a demand for
change in regulation from the industry1 [20].
The geothermal reservoirs can cover from 30-50% of the total district heating for sev-
eral thousands of years. Greater Copenhagen’s geothermal layers is called the "Bunter

The Danish concept of a geothermal plant
The concept of the Danish geothermal plant is brieﬂy explained here. A geothermal dis-
trict heating system includes a geothermal- and a surface loop consisting of heat pumps
and a primary heat exchanger. The geothermal loop constitutes of a submersible pump
which pumps hot geothermal water to the surface loop and an injection pump which
re-injects geothermal water to the subsoil. The surface plant includes a primary heat
exchanger and heat pumps to absorb more heat from the source.
Geothermal heat is accessed through deep reservoirs via drilled wells. Water situated
in the subsoil in aquifers are naturally heated up making it a stable heat source. The hot
geothermal water can be extracted by drilling into the porous rock layers. Two wells are
required: a production well accessing the hot geothermal energy and an injection well
for injecting the cooled geothermal water down to the subsoil. Injecting the geothermal
water is required for ensuring a safe pressure in the water reservoir. A bottom-hole
spacing is maintained to avoid premature cooling of the production well [21].
Drilling below 3 km is not feasible due to decreasing permeability with increasing
depth. Overlying sediment layers require a dramatic increase in the pumping work.
Three geothermal plants are today in operation in Denmark at Thisted (1984), Amager
(2005) and Sønderborg (2013)3. The plants use absorption heat pumps for extracting
heat and delivering high temperature district heating. The geothermal plant in Thisted
initially used electricity driven heat pumps. But they were exchanged with absorption
heat pumps in 1988 due to changes in taxation of electricity causing a reduced economic
viability.

The pros and cons of geothermal district heating can be summarized by following
bullet-points:
• Pro’s
 Renewable energy source with low CO2 emissions.
 Long technical lifetime and stable heat production.
 Low operation costs and ’fuel-free’ compared to gas and biomass.

2.3
Large heat pumps for district heating
Heat pumps are used in geothermal district heating to elevate the temperature satisfy-
ing the district heating demand. There are in general two different types of heat pumps:
a heat-driven heat pump (absorption) and an electricity driven heat pump (vapour-
compression). The geothermal plants in Denmark use absorption heat pump due to
high taxes on electricity. The taxes on heat production make an absorption heat pump
a favourable option. Recent changes in the Danish energy system give advantages in
using an electricity-driven heat pump.

The high capital investments lie in the initial phase of drilling wells to access the
geothermal reservoirs. Integrating a heat pump will allow for less drilling wells as
it increases the capacity relative to direct heat exchange. To give a proper example, the
geothermal plant at Amager integrates three absorptions heat pump in series that re-
sults to a heat capacity of 14 MWth. A simple calculation can reveal the difference from
not integrating a heat pump4. The geothermal plant at Amager produces 235 m3/h hot
water at 74◦C. Return temperature for Copenhagen’s district heating is approx. 50◦C.
This means from a direct heat exchange from 74 to 53◦C, the heat production will yield
nearly 5.7 MWth.
Integrating an absorption heat pump will cool down the geothermal water through
its evaporator to 17◦C, resulting in a heat production of nearly 14 MWth. This sim-
ple example reveals the importance of integrating heat pumps in a geothermal district
heating system. Omitting the heat pumps would require additional four drilling wells
that means higher risks and capital investments. The Danish experience shows that
heat pumps are a cost effective and an efﬁcient solution to utilize the geothermal heat
source based on the previous large-scale plants (Amager, Thisted and Sønderborg).
Cooling of geothermal brine can also contribute to reduced gas separation for water
and thereby reduce clogging in the injection well due to precipitation.
Comparison of absorption and vapour-compression heat pumps
An absorption heat pump is heat-driven and uses steam or ﬂue gas for operation. The
heat source for operation can derive from local CHP plants, which means a geothermal
plant based on an absorption heat pump need to be located nearby a CHP plant. This
limits the location of the plant and it should be considered if the geothermal potential
can cover the demand. Denmark has gained a lot of experience from using a heat-
driven heat pump unlike for the electricity driven heat pump. Its advantage lies in
using the waste energy from the process of heating district heating water. High amount
of energy is required in such a process and the wasted energy is usable for the heat-
driven. The COPH remains constant at around 1.75 [10].

2.4
Geothermal plant at Amager
Designing a new geothermal plant requires an in-depth understanding of the existing
design. Greater Copenhagen has already established a geothermal plant for demon-
stration purposes. It was commissioned in 2005. The plant is owned by Greater Copen-
hagen’s Geothermal Collaboration (HGS6) represented by HOFOR, CTR, VEKS and
DONG Energy.
The plant has two drilling wells with a depth of 2.6 km to access a geothermal tem-
perature of 73◦C. A 700 kW submersible pump extracts water with a rate of 230 m3/h.
A heat exchanger decreases the temperature to 17◦C before injecting it to the subsoil
using a 400 kW injection pump. Three absorptions pump are operating in series us-
ing steam delivered from the district heating network or Amager Plant Block 3. They
increase the temperature to 85◦C before distributing the heat to the consumers. Main
reasons for operating in series are to avoid too high operating pressure and thereby
yield a higher COP.

The plant has been utilizing a stable heat production capacity with a peak production of
13.5 MWth since 2005. Unfortunately, there have been periods with increased injection
pressure causing capacity reduction. These occasions happened due to missing steam
supply, periods with no heat demand, increased injection temperature from insufﬁcient

Evaporator
Type of heat source (gas, liquid or solid) can distinguish evaporators into different
types:
• Direct expansion (DX)
A DX evaporator uses a part of the heat transfer area to superheat the vapour.
Refrigerant is forced through the expansion valve and liquid in the compressor
is avoided through superheat. The heat transfer coefﬁcient in this region is low,
and therefore the superheat should be limited to 5-7 K. A too low superheat can
on the other hand induce oscillating evaporation temperature also referred to as
hunting.
• Flooded
A ﬂooded evaporator covers the entire heat transfer area with liquid refrigerant.
Refrigerant ﬂow is arranged to collect refrigerant after expansion valve in a low-
pressure (LP) receiver. Liquid and vapour are then separated where liquid is
drawn to the evaporator and gas is drawn to the suction line. The average heat
transfer coefﬁcient is higher compared to a DX evaporator[38].
A ﬂooded evaporator is preferred when using ammonia as the refrigerant side is com-
pletely wet making it possible to exploit ammonias high heat transfer coefﬁcient. The
LP receiver ensures pure vapour entering the suction line omitting the need of su-
perheat. Less compressor power avoids larger discharge temperatures. Due to these
advantageous reasons, a ﬂooded evaporator is chosen for this system. The tubes are
made of stainless steel as copper corrodes with ammonia.

Optimal system design for large temperature lift
Large temperature lifts equal to large difference between evaporating and condensing
temperature are required in district heating application. The large temperature lifts
cause undesirable operating conditions for the compressor. A large temperature lift
results to a large pressure ratio. Single-stage compression causes a drop in the efﬁciency
of the compressor and thereby lowers the performance of the system. Therefore, it must
be evaluated if the system should be a single-stage or multi-stage cycle. A multi-stage
cycle reduces the compression ratio and thereby increases the compressor efﬁciency.
In addition, the system performs better and has adequate discharge temperatures for
each compressor compared to a single-stage system [40].
Figure 3.9 shows the change in the ideal isentropic efﬁciency for different external pres-
sure ratios (pout/pin). It indicates that the compressor is designed to perform best
for a speciﬁc pressure ratio. Large pressure-ratios worsen the compressor efﬁciency
and must be avoided due to design limits for lubrication oils. Discharge temperature
should also be within an acceptable range, preferably lower than 160◦C in order to
avoid oil degradation [41].

Thermodynamic reference model
A thermodynamic reference model is deﬁned in this section and bases on a geothermal
district heating system using a simple 1-stage heat pump with ﬂooded evaporator and
a 2-stage heat pump with an open intercooler.
Framework for cycle calculations
Several assumptions are used that will have an impact in the end-result. The thermo-
dynamic model is modelled in steady state operating conditions under full-load oper-
ation. Heat exchangers are perfect insulated meaning no heat loss to the surroundings.
Fouling is not considered and same for change in kinetic and potential energies of the
ﬂuid streams. Fluid properties are unchanged and set to constant values based on an
average temperature from in- and outlet of each loop, respectively. The bullet-points
below summarize the assumptions:
• The district heating water is deﬁned as liquid water and pressurized in order to
avoid phase-change from evaporation and condensation.
• All rejected heat is useful (no heat loss considered).
• Pressure drop and heat losses from components are neglected. Consequently,
condensation and evaporation occur under constant temperature and pressure.
• Non-useful subcooling is not considered.
• Isenthalpic throttling for expansion valve.
The Geothermal District Heating System bases on the star plant concept and must be
situated in a dense and populated area in Copenhagen. Therefore, the fundamental de-
sign bases on the one presented on ﬁgure 2.9. Each process conﬁguration is designed
in full-load conditions with the aim to determine UA values of the systems heat ex-
changer, evaporator and condenser. The UA-values are then ﬁxed, while setting two
other parameters “free “(condensing and evaporating temperature). The method is
also referred to as designing the model in "Design" and setting constant UA-values to
simulate in "OFF-Design". It was earlier assessed that the system will use a ﬂooded
evaporator, and therefore no superheat is considered. Subcooling is set to 5K.

Framework for feasibility study
This section presents the framework for the performed feasibility study. The aim is to
identify the proﬁtability in commissioning the geothermal star plant with the use of

a private-economic perspective on the assumptions that the Danish Energy Agency’s

eters. The feasibility model is created in Microsoft Excel with built-in VBA macros.
Scope of the feasibility study
The analysis only concerns the geothermal star plant itself assuming that the energy
system is already given. The commissioning of this large scale plant is also set not to
affect any market prices and uses fuel price forecast provided by the DEA. Inﬂation
rate is set to a ﬁxed rate of 2% annually, while the district heating price increases with
a ﬁxed rate of 1%/yr based on prior experience (HOFOR). The plant is expected to
be fully operational in 2020 with a technical lifetime of 25 years. The energy plant is
deﬁned as a base load plant with 7000 FLH/yr. The investments are set to be paid by
2019 meaning that the valuation year is set from 2020.

Overview of cash ﬂows
Positive cash ﬂow
Since the district heating companies are a non-proﬁt organisation there is no real in-
come, but merely a shadow income. A positive cash ﬂow should thus be interpreted
as a utility value. The positive cash ﬂow is here deﬁned as the difference between
the current heat price they have to pay compared to the heat production price for a
new introduced energy system. The heat price paid by DH companies is unfortunately
conﬁdential, so assumptions are made in this case. It bases on the consumers price sub-
tracted the taxes governed by Government such as taxes for energy, CO2, SO2-/NOx
and VAT.
The reference price is deﬁned as:
Heat priceREF = Consumer price −Tariffs −VAT
(3.16)
The positive cash ﬂow then yields:
CFpos = Heat priceREF −Heat production price
(3.17)
Where heat production price is equivalent to the price they have to pay for producing
heat for the new introduced geothermal plant.
Negative cash ﬂow
The DH heating companies are required to pay taxes for the electricity according to the
"Heat element law", as they own and operate the system.
This means the electricity price used in the analysis is deﬁned as:
Electricity price = Spot price + Taxes + Grid tariffs
(3.18)
DEA grants subsidy to electricity use from the geothermal loop (pumps) as long the
energy output is either renewable energy or district heating. Electricity cost for the
pumps is therefore exonerated from taxes [56].
The negative cash ﬂow then yields:
CFneg = ∑OPEX + Electricity costs
(3.19)

Summary
The following bullet-points summarize the method of the project:
• A simple vapour-compression cycle was deﬁned along with its main governing
equations for performance. The overall geothermal energy system was modelled
in EES using several assumptions to ease the process.
• A validation of the model was performed through parametric simulation to anal-
yse the model’s behaviour and conﬁrm if it acts as expected compared to funda-
mental theory. Heat pump cycles were further validated using the commercial
software CoolPack.
• A literature-study of large-scale components was presented to identify which
type of components the heat pumps needed and the corresponding design limits.
• Economics for the heat pumps were attributed using cost-correlations using the
theory of thermoeconomics proposed by Bejan et al.
• A feasibility study scope was deﬁned for both a socio- and private-economic part
in order to assess if the geothermal system will be a beneﬁt for the municipalities
and the investors.

Figure 4.5 shows the change in PBT for both a 1-stage and 2-stage cycle with n amount

4.4
System analysis and physical constraints
It is important to mention some of the most important physical constraints that are
present for heat pump cycles in geothermal district heating application. Constraints
arise when a heat pump has to condense above 85 ◦C for ammonia (too high pressure).
The maximum evaporator sink outlet temperature for a screw compressor is ∼35-
40◦C [44]. Compressor discharge temperature should not be above 160 ◦C. Minimum
evaporator outlet temperature depends on the geothermal brine. Since the brine is not
pure water but 19% saline, the freezing point is -15.2◦C.
Figure 4.6 and 4.7 present a comparison of combined QT-diagram for the GDHS using

5.3
Variable heat production price (unit cost)
The Danish law for heat supply determines how the district heat price is structured for
all heat producing units. The law requires the price to base on all related cost involved
for producing the heat. The principle prohibits the heating companies from earning
more or less from the distributors. In principle, deviation in fuel and electricity prices
can cause the heat supplier to charge too much or less from the consumers. The heat
price is then adjusted the following year to accommodate for this. Another important
factor is the amount of full load hours that changes greatly the variable heat production
price.
The heat production price is set to be based on the average cost over the lifetime of the
project referred to as Unit Cost (UC). UC is a measure used for ”Day-ahead” district
heating planning by Varmelast, an organization by VEKS, HOFOR and CTR, which are
responsible for optimal district heat planning hour-by-hour. Daily operation of heat
producing units is optimized by producing heat with units that have the lowest UC.

New initiatives
Previous results indicate a difﬁcult competitiveness against biomass due to its emanci-
pation from taxes. There are generally three options to lower the variable heat produc-
tion cost:
• I. Prioritize the unit to have more full load hours.
• II. Reduce the huge capital investments related to drilling of wells and the corre-

• III. Introduce new initiatives for reducing taxes and tariffs on top of electricity
used for renewable heat production.

with ﬂuctuating wind power. Other initiatives such as energy storages also allow for

Summary
Based on the feasibility model for the optimal system, it was found out that:
• A geothermal plant is best suited as a base load plant. It reaches a break-even at
6050 FLH/yr (private-economic) and 5051 FLH/yr (socio-economic). The project
should be discontinued if it can’t reach its break-even.
• A cash ﬂow analysis illustrated the difference in PBT from both perspectives.
The payback times were PBTsocio = 12.3 years and PBTprivate = 15.8 years based
on ofﬁcial DEA projections for electricity prices, for a scenario with prioritized
production of 7000 FLH/yr.
• Heat production price is highly dependent on the amount of full load hours given
to the plant. Comparison with a future energy system shows that its competitive
position in the daily heat planning is harmed by the electricity taxes and tariffs.
• Implementing a geothermal star plant in Greater Copenhagen would suppress a
biomass consumption by 3.56 M.ton in 25 years.
• Alternative scenarios stress the sensitiveness linked to the proﬁtability when chang-
ing the framework for the electricity price and reducing capital investments.
• With current legal framework, the geothermal plant must be a politically priori-
tized unit.

6.3
Thermodynamic modelling approach
Thermodynamic modelling in EES involves several assumptions easing and quicken-
ing the modelling process. These assumptions should to some extend be subjected to a
critical review as proposed in the sensitivity analysis. The LMTD-method is an efﬁcient
method to design component sizes relative to a reference capacity. Thus, no heat trans-
fer correlations were used in this study to determine a heat transfer coefﬁcient U. The
parameter was required to determine the heat transfer area for each heat exchanger.
Using heat transfer correlations that consider pressure-drops would also alter the com-
ponent sizes and should be considered - in order to achieve more precise component
sizes. The purpose of the thermodynamic model was also primarily to provide rough
estimates for component sizes. Pressure- and heat losses could be added in the model,
but would prolong the calculation and modelling process.
Using the LMTD-method in EES also causes converging problems if well-deﬁned bound-
aries are not set. Problems occurred when performing part-load parametric simula-
tions. Resolving the issue was an iterative process to ensure LMTD equations had
proper initial guess values and boundaries. The integration of heat pumps in series is
also somewhat inefﬁcient when performing it in EES. A prominent alternative is the
modelling tool Digital Network Analysis (DNA) that is component-based.

6.4
Perspective
System optimization
The technical modelling results proposed several solutions for optimizing the surface
plant. A surface plant consisting of 4 x 1-stage heat pumps coupled in series was de-
termined as the optimal system based on economy and performance. The system does
not fulﬁl all physical constraints. The optimal system requires 4 x 1-stage heat pumps
with a condensing capacity of ∼12 MWth (see ﬁg 3.14). Increasing demand for larger
sizes of heat pump technology may increase the market availability in the near future.
The current barrier for large heat pump technology introduction is the lack of experi-
ence and the limited feasibility when comparing with CHP plants. An ongoing Dan-
ish research project “SVAF - Experimental development of electric heat pumps in the
Greater Copenhagen DH system (2014-2015) ” aims to establish two large electricity
heat pumps using a high temperature source (i.e. geothermal energy) and a low tem-
perature source (i.e. waste or seawater) [64]. The scale of the heat pump is ∼5 MWth.
The experience gained from the project will be crucial for determining the size of heat
pump technology for the geothermal star plant. It is vital that the heat pumps establish
a stable operation as a potential capacity reduction would harm the feasibility of the
project.
An increased plate heat exchanger gives a higher district heating capacity with no
trade-off for increased investment. Opting for this solution would require an assess-
ment of increased service and maintenance time. An enlarged heat exchanger may
potentially lead to capacity reduction, if the cleaning process becomes too comprehen-
sive. Should the plant maintain a reference capacity of 74 MWth, the best option is to
cost-optimize the heat pumps. The cost correlations reveal that the cost for an increased
heat exchanger and gain in capacity is higher than the reference system.
Connecting the system to the transmission grid causes reduced proﬁtability and system
performance, which leads to higher heat production prices. B.Bach (2014) did a com-
parison of connecting large heat pumps to transmission and distribution grid while
considering Greater Copenhagen’s energy system in BALMOREL. The decreased per-
formance when connecting to the transmission grid decreased the yearly operation
hours by 1000 [65]. The magnitude can naturally not be directly compared with this
energy system as it differs, but is emphasizes that the decreased performance will af-
fect competitiveness in daily heat planning.
In general, it is important to system optimize the geothermal system in order to reduce
the cost of heat and improve its competitiveness. The large capital investments and
the use of electricity with the current framework make it sensitive to these factors.
Improvements in the cycle design could also be of interest, e.g. investigating if adding
an internal heat exchanger versus the additional investment can be a beneﬁt.

How can Greater Copenhagen’s district heating system integrate geothermal
energy?
Referring back to the initial research question of this thesis and the obtained results,
this section aim to put the ﬁndings in context with the purpose of the study.
Investigating the proﬁtability highlighted the problems in renewable heat production
using electricity as energy input. The plant’s competitive position is greatly harmed by
the huge taxes and tariffs that account up to 75% of the total price. Alternative scenar-
ios demonstrated how change in regulatory framework could push the integration of
geothermal energy. The challenges concerning geothermal energy are that large risks
are associated in drilling and the current framework harms the proﬁtability. These two
focus area should be prioritized.
Therefore, to integrate geothermal energy in Greater Copenhagen, a new legal frame-
work must be created for the geothermal plant to make it a economic attractive solu-
tion. Reducing capital investments related to drilling require more know-how expertise
and can only be gained from commissioning the geothermal plant.
Regulatory framework
Large heat pumps have been a popular topic in the Danish energy system but no units
was set in operation in 2014 [Danish District Heating, 2014]. With the increasing share
of wind power, the heat pumps will be able to cover the excessive power for heat pro-
duction. Challenges cause a slow development due to the current framework that halts
the development and consequently making it harder for integrating geothermal energy
with the use of electricity-driven heat pumps. It can be somewhat critical opting for an
electricity-driven heat pump when the variable costs seems higher than an absorption
heat pump. Better system integration and avoiding missing steam supply are among
of one the reasons that make the electricity-driven heat pump superior. Thus, changes
are required to promote them even more and ensuring them to have a lower variable
unit cost.
Exporting the cheap generated electricity ultimately harms the socio-economic proﬁt.
Earnings from taxes and tariffs are lost from the exported electricity. Initial projections
indicate that even with a lower taxes and tariffs (to promote large heat pumps), the
government will still earn a proﬁt - rather than exporting it [66]. The lack of an overall
system integration between heat generation and electricity production is one of the
reason why integrating geothermal energy with electricity heat pumps seems at risk.
Change in regulatory framework can improve the competitive position against biomass.
The PSO tariff has been heavily debated, as it seems illogical that heat pumps that pro-
motes integration of renewables also have to pay a PSO tariff unlike biomass. The
alternative scenario without PSO shows how it could greatly improve the proﬁtability.
Considering that electricity-driven heat pumps use renewable sources (e.g. geothermal
==PAGE:85==

energy), it seems illogical for heat pumps to be subjected to a PSO tariff. Also consid-
ering that PSO tariff represents roughly 20% of the total power price. It is a substantial
amount of the operational expenditures that can be reduced in the technical lifetime.
Danish District Heating has drawn similar conclusions for the general observations of
large electricity driven heat pumps. A recent report by Danish District Heating recom-
mends removing PSO-tariff and lower electricity tariffs for large heat pumps [67].
Insurances for geothermal heat potential
The study presumes that the geothermal potential is steadily available. This is the
deﬁning success criteria for geothermal plants. The integration of geothermal plants
has been a slow process compared to fellow European countries such as France and
Germany due to lack of insurances. A recent insurance-policy has been enacted by the
Danish Government which helps promoting the integration of geothermal energy [68].
The policy is expected to be active from Fall 2015 with an insurance of ∼8 M.EUR (60
M.DKK) for the ﬁrst drilling. Viborg municipality experienced unsuccessful drilling
and other problems that ultimately caused the project to be abandoned. The total ex-
pense amounted to ∼18.76 M.EUR. The expense is now amortised by the households
(consumers) as their yearly district heat expenditure increased with 114 EUR/year [69].
Thus, risks in geothermal heat potential is an important dimension to consider - espe-
cially for public acceptance of geothermal energy and the interpretation of the obtained
results.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
7.1
Main Conclusions
From the obtained ﬁndings, it can be concluded that integrating geothermal energy can
be accomplished by commissioning a new large scale 74 MWth geothermal plant.
System performance was evaluated by creating a thermodynamic model. The model
was validated through parametric simulations to conﬁrm its behaviour compared to
fundamental theory. The system performance was greatly enhanced when using a sys-
tem design where several heat pump units are coupled in series. The optimal amount
was a 4 x 1-stage heat pumps. The optimal conﬁguration was best in terms of perfor-
mance and proﬁtability. A current technological constraint is that the required condens-
ing capacity is ∼12 MWth, and the current largest heat pump unit in operation is ∼4.5
MWth. Increasing the district heat capacity is possible by injecting to a lower temper-
ature. It increases proﬁtability even though the energy use for geothermal pumps and
heat pumps increase. Opting for this scenario would require better pump equipment
to ensure an acceptable wear. The geothermal system performs best at distributional
level as the system performance decreases for higher temperature levels. Decreased
system performance results to higher heat production prices. This could pose a prob-
lem, as the geothermal plant might not get sufﬁcient operation hours when considering
Greater Copenhagen’s energy system.
Cost correlations were compared with the Danish Energy Agency’s guideline and showed
that the obtained results were within a reasonable range, only deviating by ∼8.7%.
A feasibility study showed that the geothermal star plant is best suited as a base load
plant as initially projected with a break-even at 6050 FLH/yr (private-economic). The
heat production price is not fully competitive against energy systems using biomass.
This causes a problem from a socio-economic optimal scenario. The Danish Law of
Heat Supply requires heat planners to operate with units with the lowest variable heat
production costs. Current regulatory framework does not in general support large
electricity driven heat pumps that harms the development of this geothermal plant
too. Initiatives such as removing the PSO-tariff would greatly enhance the proﬁtability
73

and improve its competitive position. The geothermal plant would further decrease
a biomass consumption of 3.56 M. ton in its technical lifetime - thereby liberating the
Danish energy system from biomass.
A sensitivity analysis revealed highest impact for operating economy is capital expen-
ditures, electricity price and system performance. Focus should be on how to create
an attractive legal framework for renewable energy systems using electricity, main-
taining a high system performance and reducing capital investments. These measures
are needed in order to ensure the integration of geothermal energy, competitive op-
erating economy and gain public acceptance from consumers as they lead to cheaper
heat production prices and less risk. Creating the legal framework does not only pro-
mote the integration of geothermal energy, but also large electricity-driven heat pumps
that could use alternative renewable sources. This would lead to the ideal system in-
tegration with a Danish energy system consisting of a high share of wind power and
renewable energy systems utilizing the electricity for heat production.
7.2
Future work and recommendations
The ﬁndings only based on vapour compression heat pumps. A direct comparison with
a hybrid heat pump would be beneﬁcial to make - in order to conﬁrm the choice of heat
pump technology. Hybrid heat pumps are especially of interest when elevating to high
temperature levels as for this system. The comparison should reveal which heat pump
technology is best suited for geothermal application.
Implementing a seasonal heat storage improves the system integration. It gives ﬂexi-
bility and optimizes the interaction between ﬂuctuating renewable resources and heat
demand. Seasonal heat storage can be accomplished by heat injection in geothermal
reservoirs during cheap spot prices. However, it may potentially ruin the wells’ per-
meability. Danish Geothermal District Heating is currently investigating this solution
in a research project “Heho - Heat Storage in Hot Aquifers (2011-2015)" [68]. Dependent
on the established ﬁndings, a seasonal storage could be implemented in the thermody-
namic model to reveal better system integration.
The system integration analysis performed in the study only considers the geother-
mal plant unit and compares with variable heat production prices for alternative units.
Modelling of the whole district heating system in BALMOREL would be an interesting
study to determine the amount of full load hours the geothermal plant unit will achieve
in Greater Copenhagen energy system.
Another relevant study is to perform an exergy analysis on the modelled energy sys-
tem. It is a quantitative evaluation that takes the quality of the energy into account.
The aim is to determine real thermodynamic inefﬁciencies occurring in an energy con-
version that relates to exergy destruction. The analysis can be used for minimization of
losses with components having the highest exergy destruction. Thereby designing an
even more efﬁcient cycle to improve the geothermal plant’s operating economy.

A.1
Calculation of integrating heat pumps in geothermal sur-

Amager geothermal plant integrates three absorption heat pumps in series to utilize

A.3
Absorption heat pump
The absorption heat pump (AHP) uses water as working ﬂuid and generates heat with
thermal and chemical processes. The salt (LiBr) ensures low pressure in the evaporator,
while the concentration of the salt is varied in the working ﬂuid by absorbing refriger-
ant vapour.
The absorber consists of a mixture of LiBr and water (H2O) which absorbs steam gen-
erated from the evaporator. As it absorbs the steam, condensation to water occur while
heat rejection to district heating water occur. The cycle is continuously running through
the generator, which regenerates the concentration LiBr/H2O.
Regeneration of the mixture is done through use of heat or steam to boil water at 140-
180◦C. The LiBr/H2O mixture ﬂows into the absorber. The energy used for this process
is transferred to the district heating water, so it is seen as waste energy.
Performance for a AHP is denoted as COPH and includes the efﬁciency for the power
station of where the working energy is originated. Hence, a comparison of COPH and
COPVCHP is not possible. Following relation is valid (Granryd):
