Preface 
This study serves as the final thesis project concluding the Master’s (MSc) degree in Petroleum Engineering 
at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The project was conducted over a 5 months period in spring, 
2016 (25th January, 2016 – 25th June, 2016) at Center for Energy Resources Engineering (CERE) in
Chemical and Biochemical Engineering department under the supervision of Associate Professor Kaj 
Thomsen. The conducted research work is mainly theoretical and is based on electrolytic based 
thermodynamic modelling simulations. This emphasis on theoretical understanding has led to the 
development of a different approach towards applicability of CO2LSWAG (Carbon dioxide Low Salinity 
Water Alternating Gas) injection in carbonate reservoirs. 
This thesis consists of two introductory chapters. Chapter 1 describes the problem statement and motivation 
behind this research study. Chapter 2 explains the basic fundamentals required to understand the various 
terms and concepts used throughout the work. Chapter 3 consists of the theory behind WAG and provides 
understanding about the research work done on carbonate reservoirs using CO2LSWAG. Chapter 4 is the 
hypothesis proposed by Krishna Hara Chakravarty and his co-workers. This whole study revolves around the 
hypothesis proposed on SmW-EOR (Smart Water Enhanced Oil Recovery) and further has been applied in 
CO2LSWAG. Chapter 5 includes the model and methodology used to perform simulations and calculations. 
It describes the theory behind the Extended UNIQUAC Model proposed by Thomson et al., 1999. Chapter 6 
is the discussion section. Herein results from analysing different research papers produced from this thesis 
work are presented. The results are compared with the reported experimental data and correlation with the 
proposed hypothesis has been presented.  
The explanations given and critical correlations found are summarized and concluded in Chapter 7.  Chapter 
8 includes future scope of further work that can be done. Contribution made by other researchers has been 
cited at required places and a complete list of references is provided at the end of this research study.

Fines formation during CO2 Low Salinity WAG (CO2LSWAG) EOR in Carbonate Reservoirs 
by Nipun Garg 
Low salinity waterflooding (LSW) and water alternating gas injection (WAG) both have been universally 
accepted as a potential enhanced oil recovery (EOR) method for sandstone and carbonate reservoirs. Salinity 
variations of injected brine have also shown to affect oil recovery in carbon dioxide low salinity water 
alternating gas injection (CO2LSWAG). CO2LSWAG has been experimentally proved to be a promising 
enhanced oil recovery method for sandstone reservoirs. But, the efficiency of CO2LSWAG in carbonate 
reservoirs has not been proved completely yet. There are still uncertainties about the recovery mechanism in 
carbonate reservoirs even for low salinity waterflooding. Formation of fines and dissolution of carbonate 
rock has been reported many times while performing LSW and CO2WAG coreflooding experiments. But its 
effect on increase in recovery during CO2LSWAG has not been previously studied.  This study explores the 
possibility of fines formation and calcite dissolution during CO2LSWAG injection and attempts to correlate 
with an effect on oil recovery reported in laboratory and simulation experiments. The Extended UNIQUAC 
model has been used to calculate the respective dissolution and fines formation in CO2LSWAG modelling 
and experiments. Fines formation in terms of attrition has been observed in all the carbonate core plugs. Its 
primary reason is interaction of aqueous CO2, injected brine, mineral surface and formation brine. This 
attrition has not been observed or mentioned anywhere in the experimentation done before. Further, it is 
scrutinized that dissolution of calcite rock is not the only phenomenon behind observed calcium 
concentration in effluent. It may also come out in form of small fines i.e. attrition or precipitates of 
anhydrites. They have been observed to form consistently while performing CO2WAG with different salinity 
brines. In another observation, it has been found that the fines formed inside the core plugs are most of the 
times soluble at lower (atmospheric) temperature and thus considered as dissolution of calcite. It has been 
also seen that the fines formation is correlating to wettability alteration mechanism and thus to observed 
increase in oil recovery. Further experimentation to scrutinize this mechanism of fines formation along with 
observation of all the ions in effluent has also been recommended. Finally, suggestions and thoughts on 
future work have been discussed including an opinion on coreflooding experiment vs reservoir flooding for 
CO2WAG.

Introduction 
1.1  Background and Motivation 
Carbonate reservoirs holds a great economic importance in terms of Oil and Gas reserves around the world. 
It has been said that more than half of the world’s remaining oil exists in carbonate reservoirs. On the other 
hand these reservoirs are characterized by low primary recovery providing great opportunity to apply 
improved oil recovery (IOR) methods. According to an international database, gas injection has been the 
most commonly used enhanced oil recovery (EOR) method in past decade. These gas injection methods 
include both continuous gas injection and water alternating gas (WAG) injection.  
Waterflooding is a cheap recovery method and is applied worldwide for IOR and pressure maintenance. In 
beginning the composition of water was not considered as the most important factor. But over the past few 
decades, composition variation has attracted many researchers thus extensive studies were performed being 
lately applied on field scale. This composition variation or advanced ion management of injection water has 
been named as “Smart Waterflooding (SmW)”. It has proven positive effects on recovery for both 
sandstone and carbonate reservoirs.  
Several laboratory experiments and field tests have shown that low salinity water (LSW) injection 
improves oil recovery in sandstone reservoirs (Tang and morrow, 1999; Seccombe et al., 2008). This 
method in sandstone includes interaction between crude oil, brine (injected or formation) and rock. Several 
complex mechanisms have been proposed behind an observed increase in recovery. This primarily includes 
the presence of clay playing key role in recovery increase for sandstone reservoirs. In carbonate reservoirs, 
an absence of clay minerals made the recovery increase debatable for water based EOR’s. But, through 
extensive work on carbonate reservoirs, smart water flooding has also been established as a possible EOR 
method (Austad et al., 2005; Zekri et al., 2011; Gupta et al., 2011). An IOR by smart variation of the sea 
water in chalk reservoirs was first reported by Austad et al., 2005. Since then, numerous mechanisms have 
been proposed by researchers for the potential recovery increase in carbonate reservoirs. These mechanisms 
include wettability alteration, ion exchange mechanism, fines migration, mobility control, rock dissolution 
and formation of electric double layer (Zhang et al., 2007; Hiroth et al., 2010; Strand et al., 2008, Fathi et 
al., 2010). Still, no general consensus has been developed behind the observed recovery increase by SmW 
injection in carbonate reservoirs. 
Recently, Chakravarty et al., 2015 explained and have shown that wettability alteration is not the primary 
mechanism behind oil recovery increase in SmW-EOR, but an additional effect. They performed 
simulations for over 100 plus SmW-EOR coreplug experiments reported in literature. These simulations are

The significant advantages of combining LSW with other EOR techniques such as polymer or low tension

One of such novel techniques which have gained interest in recent years is low salinity water alternating

controlling the gas mobility and negating the low macro and micro sweep efficiency for waterflooding. 
Therefore CO2LSWAG promotes the alliance of the mechanisms underlying these methods that further 
enhances oil recovery. It also overcomes the problem of frequently encountered late production in 
conventional WAG. Dang et al., 2014 suggested that CO2LSWAG can be used in two strategies.

“As an effective IOR/EOR approach for green and brown oil fields by utilizing the advantages
between them to overcome the current challenges associated with LSW and CO2 WAG”.
2.
“As an agent that improves the conformance control by blocking off the high conductivity zones
and divert the injected fluid into unswept zones”.
As SmW-EOR, LSWAG is well understood in terms of applicability with the sandstone reservoirs. But, 
such work has not been done with the carbonate reservoirs. Moreover the mechanisms described have not 
been accepted worldwide and cannot explain the observed recovery increase for all the carbonate 
reservoirs. But as Chakravarty et al. 2015 justified the importance of fines formation in SmW-EOR, same 
has not been studied for CO2LSWAG. Though, salinity variations of injected brines have been shown to 
affect oil recovery for CO2LSWAG EOR. They also proposed a hypothesis similar to SmW–EOR 
explaining the importance of CO2 in increasing the fines formation. In this theoretical study they used the 
Extended UNIQUAC model as a mathematical tool to explain the effect of increasing aqueous CO2 
concentration, NaCl concentration, and presence of active ions like Ca2+, Mg2+ and SO4
2- in brine.
Chakravarty et al. 2015 also proposed that CO2 injection along with low saline water may have distinct 
effects like miscible CO2 can decrease the oil viscosity resulting in increased oil displacement and CO2 
dissolved in water may cause fines formation following the dissolution of CaCO3. These fines can alter the 
sweep efficiency by blocking pores and increase oil displacement through emulsification.  
Up till now, there is a lack of experimental evidence to conclude definitively that CO2LSWAG induces 
fines formation leading to enhanced oil recovery. This hypothesis by Chakravarty et al. 2015 only explains 
the theoretical aspect of it, but no experimental or simulation comparison has been made yet. Thus, this 
research project aims at more realistic assessment of this hypothesis necessary to understand CO2LSWAG 
better for carbonate reservoirs.

CO2LSWAG EOR in carbonate reservoirs. The main objectives are:

1.3  Considerations

3.2  Background on Oil Recovery methods 
The movement of hydrocarbons towards production due to a pressure difference between the reservoir and 
the production wells is the general mechanism of oil recovery (Donaldson et al., 1989). This oil recovery 
process is generally divided into three major categories namely primary recovery, secondary recovery and 
tertiary recovery or Enhanced Oil Recovery (figure 3).  
In the primary recovery process, oil is forced out of the reservoir using the natural pressure developed in 
reservoir fluids over a long period of time. While depleting the reserves naturally, when it is no longer 
possible to make use of the natural pressure to make hydrocarbons move, the secondary recovery processes 
are used. At this time either gas or water is injected to augment or increase the pressure in the reservoir as 
to get continuous production of hydrocarbons. This pressure maintenance with the help of injection of 
water or gas in the reservoir is designated as secondary oil recovery processes. The combined recovery of 
original oil in place (OOIP) from reservoir generally ranges from 20-50% from the primary or secondary 
recovery processes. Now this is where tertiary recovery or Enhanced Oil Recovery comes in picture whose

3.4  Water Alternating Gas Injection (WAG) 
WAG is considered an enhanced recovery process categorized under gas injection as shown in figure 4. It 
has been used since 1960’s and was first reported in 1957 in Canada (Christensen et al., 2001). WAG 
injection involves drainage and imbibition taking place simultaneously or in cyclic alternation in the 
reservoir (Nezhad et al., 2006). This method was originally supposed or intended to improve sweep 
efficiency during gas flooding with in between slugs of water and gas followed by the same procedure in 
the reservoir. The variants used are different gases along with water saving important and valuable gases 
leading an economical way.  
Figure 6: A typical WAG process (Halland et al., 2011)) 
WAG process includes injecting a slug of gas into the reservoir followed by water serving as the "chasing 
fluid". This cycle is repeated as on requirement and foreseen options. The process basically involves two 
steps, first one being the gas injection which reacts and thereby swells the oil. It reduces the viscosity of oil 
present and consequently making it more mobile and easier to flow. The interfacial energy between gas and 
oil system is lower than water and oil system. It enables the oil to be displaced by gas which would not 
have been accessible by water alone. A brief pictorial process of WAG is shown in figure 6 with CO2 used 
as injecting gas. It also shows a very brief one of the many proposed mechanism responsible for oil 
recovery.

WAG combines both effects and benefits of gas and water injection. Waterflooding increases the macro 
efficiency and gas injection sweeps the oil in micro pores increasing the micro efficiency. WAG injections 
has successfully increased the recovery from 5 to 10 % of oil initially in place (OIIP) and very few field 
trials have been reported which were unsuccessful (Christensen et al., 2001). 
The WAG displacement can be optimized by playing with the mobility ratio (M). If an unfavourable 
mobility ratio is obtained, the gas will finger causing early gas breakthrough simultaneously decreasing the 
sweep efficiency. Other main reasons are reservoir heterogeneity, high permeable layers and premature 
breakthrough of the water phase. The reservoir properties affecting vertical sweep mostly include reservoir 
dip angle and variation in permeability and porosity. Normally, downward increasing porosity and 
permeability is advantageous for the WAG injection and increases the stability of the front. So, to conclude 
WAG recovery can be optimized by maximizing any or all of the above factors.  
3.4.1  Classification of WAG Injection 
WAG Injection can be classified in many ways. The most common classification as given by Christensen, 
Stenby and Skauge, 2001 is to distinguish between miscible and immiscible displacements. Miscible and 
immiscible injection are the function of properties of the fluid to be displaced in the reservoir, injected fluid 
i.e. displacing fluid and pressure, temperature of the reservoir. 
Miscible WAG Injection (MWAG) 
In this type of WAG the displacing and displaced fluid are supposed to be miscible. This is done by 
maintaining the reservoir pressure above the minimum miscibility pressure (MMP) of injecting fluid. MMP 
is the minimum pressure at which fluid becomes completely miscible without forming an interface. The 
miscibility occurs when both the displacing and displaced fluid mix in all proportions without interference. 
Most of the WAG field experiences are miscible. It has not been possible to isolate the degree of 
compositional effect on oil recovery by WAG injection. Further as analysed by Christensen et al., 2001, 
most of the miscible injection projects are found onshore. These early cases used less economical and 
expensive solvents like propane. They are not viable at current low prices. The miscibility can be lost if the 
pressure comes below MMP. So, the reservoir needs to be pressurized again to maintain the miscibility. 
From a number of core flooding experiments it has been shown that MWAG injection gives better recovery 
than Immiscible WAG injection. Overall, the gas which has been found most effective and economical 
during the lab and field experiments is carbon dioxide (CO2). 
Immiscible WAG Injection (IWAG) 
This type of WAG process has been applied practically in very few fields. This is mostly applied with the 
aim of improving frontal stability or contacting unswept zones where gravity-stable gas injection cannot be 
applied because of limited gas resources or because of high reservoir heterogeneity. However, sometimes 
immiscible gas injection results in poor recovery of oil because of very high mobility of gas owing to its 
low viscosity.

Hybrid WAG Injection (HWAG), simultaneous WAG injection (SWAG), foam assisted WAG (FAWAG),

hydrocarbon gases. 
3.4.2  WAG field studies
After the first successful review in Canada (1957), many laboratory investigations were made to evaluate 
the efficiency of WAG injection for different fields worldwide. Also in another work in 1958, Caudle and 
Dyne proposed a water-gas injection for oil recovery enhancement. In the laboratory experiments 
performed, they observed sweep efficiency of 90% as compared to 60% for pure gas flooding. Since then 
WAG has got increased importance and many laboratory, pilot and field scale implementations have been 
performed (Skauge et al., 2003). 
An extensive field experience review of WAG was given by Christensen et al., in 2001. In this publication 
more than 60 field implementations were reviewed for WAG. They concluded that miscible WAG 
contributes heavily to enhanced recovery and most of the field applications were successful. The field 
reviews on average showed an increase of 5% to 10% of OOIP. In some field cases an increase of even 
20% has also been reported. 
WAG injection has been a very popular EOR method for North Sea oil fields being efficient in improving 
oil recovery. Majority of the applications are in mature fields. One of the very old field implementation by 
Statoil was in Gullfaks field in Norwegian sector of North Sea. WAG pilot was first implemented there in 
1991. Then afterwards it was also applied in fields of Brage, Snorre, Statfjord, Veslefrikk etc. with mostly 
being hydrocarbon or produced gas injection. 
Ma and Youngren, 1994 reviewed WAG results in Kupark field in Alaska. In this field, storage gas 
injection and water flooding was applied. Then IWAG was followed by MWAG. In simulations they 
reported an increase of 1 to 3% of OOIP and field results were observed to follow the same trend. 
One of a very successful application of WAG was in Brent reservoirs at Statfjord field. Here 45% of the 
produced gas was reinjected and a very effective incremental oil rate was achieved. One of the typical 
effects observed during implementation of WAG was decreasing water cut, increasing oil rate and 
increasing gas oil ratio (GOR). The water cut reduction of 70% has been reported (Crogh et al., 2002).  A 
pictorial graph of increasing recovery is shown in figure 7.

3.5  Carbonate Reservoirs 
Carbonate (chalk and limestone) reservoirs hold a great economic importance. It is well known that more 
than half of the world’s oil and 40% of world’s gas reserves are held in carbonate reservoirs. For example, 
the Middle East is dominated by carbonate fields and is responsible for the major part of its oil and gas 
production. The exploration, evaluation and production methods are similar to sandstone reservoirs but 
because of highly varied properties they are challenging to produce. These challenges require new tools, 
techniques and recovery methods or innovation in current recovery methods to make use of these valuable 
oil and gas reserves.  
Carbonate rock is a type of sediment mainly consisting of Calcium Carbonate (CaCO3). In majority it 
contains three different type of carbonate rock minerals, which are Calcite (CaCO3), Dolomite 
(CaCO3.MgCO3) and Aragonite (CaCO3). Further these minerals can form three types of carbonate rock. 
The most common is limestone and other two are dolostone or dolomite rock and chalk. Chalk is mainly

These highly complex carbonated reservoir rocks are characterized by low primary oil recovery. It provides 
a huge opportunity for EOR methods to be employed and produce more oil. Exhibiting low porosity and 
usually fractured along with oil wet to mixed wet rock properties are causes of low hydrocarbon recovery.  
They are also relatively less permeable making EOR methods even more challenging.   
WAG is proven to be a good method to create incremental recovery in or after secondary recovery. But not 
many laboratory scale application are available with CO2LSWAG. The recovery mechanism here are 
different from sandstone reservoirs as it lacks clay. Since LSW has also been proved to be an effective 
method within carbonates (Chakravarty et al., 2015), CO2LSWAG should also produce positive results. 
3.6  CO2 Low Salinity Water Alternating Gas Injection (CO2LSWAG) 
Low salinity water flooding is an emerging EOR method leading to wettability alteration from oil-wet to 
water-wet conditions; whereas water-alternating-gas (WAG) injection is a proven method for improving 
gas flooding performance by controlling gas mobility. Therefore, LSWAG injection promotes a 
combination of mechanisms associated with these two methods that enhances the oil recovery further. 
Before proceeding and explaining experimental and simulation approaches it is important to understand and 
discuss the concept of Low salinity or Smart water EOR. 
3.6.1  Concept of Low salinity or Smart Water EOR 
There is no specific definition which has been stated so far in literature specifying the limit of low salinity. 
Though in many experimental works, terminology of optimum salinity has been defined. A work in 
University of Wyoming stated that injecting water with a TDS content of less than 5000 ppm resulted in 
highest oil recovery. Most of the literature work has been done on the basis of less saline water than the sea 
water or formation water, which has been considered as “Low Salinity”. The impact of brine composition 
variation on residue oil production was first introduced in the papers by Jadhunandan and Morrow in 1995. 
It was further studied in detail by Yildiz and Morrow in 1996. This began to shift the industry’s attention 
towards considering adjusting the ion composition and salinity of the injected fluid. This salinity variation 
has been reported to increase recovery and decrease in residual oil saturation. 
LSW is receiving an increasing attention in oil and gas industry. It is currently identified as an important 
EOR technique as it shows more benefits in terms of chemical costs, environmental impact, and field 
process implementation than conventional chemical EOR methods. Strand et al. in 2008 termed the 
chemically altered sea water as “smart water”. Although the benefits of LSW have been realized, the 
mechanism for incremental oil recovery by LSW is still an open topic for discussion. Several studies have 
been conducted in order to further understand this phenomenon (RezaeiDoust et al., 2009; Fadili, 2009; 
Fathi et al., 2011; Awolayo et al., 2014; Yousef et al., 2012). Since then, there several evidences have been 
given to support the fact that reduction in the salinity of sea water leads to higher oil production. A study at

3.7  Proposed Recovery Mechanisms for CO2LSWAG 
CO2LSWAG for carbonate reservoirs gives enhanced oil recovery. All experiments and simulations have 
their own explanations for that. There is a need to look at those explanations so that concluding evidence 
can be provided. Many authors have suggested more than one mechanism happening in the reservoir. As 
this novel technique is a synergy of worldwide accepted methods i.e. LSW and CO2WAG, the mechanisms 
proposed for these two should be valid with an additional effect. One thing can be definitely concluded that 
there is no authentic and worldwide accepted mechanism for this EOR method. A summary of the proposed 
mechanisms in literature are explained below. 
3.7.1  Wettability alteration and IFT reduction 
Wettability in general is a very effective approach to EOR. As explained in section 2 and also by Anderson, 
1986; Buckley, 1998, rock wettability alteration is dependent on many factors like composition of oil, brine 
composition, rock surface mineralogy, system temperature, reservoir temperature and pressure. Both low 
salinity and aqueous CO2 together contributes to wettability alteration. 
As investigated by Huang and Holm, 1988; Rao et al., 1992, one of the main mechanisms of enhancing oil 
recovery by miscible gas flooding is achievement of favourable wettability alteration. Al-Mutairi et al., 
2014 performed reservoir temperature and immiscible pressure condition wettability measurement on 
carbonate core. The results show that exposing oil wet carbonate rock to brine saturated with CO2 causes 
wettability alteration to an intermediate wet state. Also with increase in CO2 concentration the level of 
wettability alteration increases. Here low salinity water further contributes to increasing concentration of 
gas in brine enhancing the wettability alteration.  Teklu et al., 2014 performed calculations to predict the 
solubility variation of CO2 with pressure and salinity. They used two different models by Enick & Klara, 
1990 and Li & Nghiem, 1986 to predict the behaviour. The results are shown in figure 18.

causing improved oil recovery (Bennion and Bachu, 2008). Yang et al., 2005 also showed that along with

provides supporting evidence for oil recovery enhancement. Similar results were observed by Teklu et al.,

3.7.2  Ion exchange or substitution and Geochemical reactions 
Ion substitution takes place majorly because of ions present in injected low salinity water. As the ion 
substitution on calcite surface occurs, soluble sulphate gets adsorbed and leads to release of adsorbed 
carboxyl compounds. This changes the mineral wettability to more water wet state and enhances oil 
displacement. In supporting data to this mechanism, it has been shown that high concentration of SO4
2- ions
in injection brine corresponds to increase in oil production (Fathi et al., 2011). Also, increasing Mg2+ and
Ca2+ ions concentration in injection brine has shown increase in oil recovery (Fathi et al., 2010).
Decreasing NaCl concentration also leads to higher oil recovery (Zhang et al., 2006). During ion exchange, 
salt formation takes place which are less soluble at those conditions leading to precipitation. Hence, 
permeability alteration occurs which may lead to enhanced recovery.

3.7.3  Fines migration and Rock dissolution 
The concept of fines migration was first given by Tang and Morrow in 1999. Morrow and his research 
colleagues observed that fines (mainly kaolinite clay fragments) were released from the rock surface, 
resulting in an increase of spontaneous imbibition recovery with decrease in salinity for different 
sandstones. There have been few experiments performed at the laboratory scale in which fines migration 
has been observed in carbonates. Experimental investigation of CO2LSWAG by Ramanathan et al., 2015 
mentions about fines migration and emulsion formation in sandstone cores. But its contribution towards 
enhanced oil recovery has not been explained. When CO2 gets dissolved with the brine, it forms carbonic 
acid and leads to dissolution of calcite. Yousef et al., 2011 suggest that rock dissolution of carbonate cores 
could also be one of the contributing mechanisms. They based this hypothesis on NMR study performed 
before and after the low salinity flood.  
There are other several mechanisms which are proposed in clay formations that might be applicable in 
carbonate reservoirs. It includes local increase of pH because of varied brine composition, salting in effect, 
and electric double layer expansion. So, overall it can be concluded that different mechanisms have been 
explained, different explanations has been given on increase or decrease of recovery, still a worldwide 
accepted mechanism is missing.  
3.8  Factors affecting CO2LSWAG 
There are certain factors having a big impact on economics of this EOR method. They also affect the 
performance of CO2LSWAG. There are different factors mentioned and explained in the literature 
impacting the results of LSW and CO2WAG injection. They also make an impact while injecting the 
combination of both. Most important of them are explained as follows: 
Initial Wettability 
Initially wettability explains the nature of reservoir rock. Oil or mixed wet reservoir favours the wettability 
alteration of rock leading to EOR. Zekri et al., 2011 conducted simulation study to check favourable 
wettability to WAG method. Their result mentioned that reservoir wettability has significant effect on the 
optimization of WAG injection rate. In particular, Dang et al., 2014 mentioned that CO2LSWAG is not a 
good method when applied in reservoir with dominating water-wet rock.  If there will be sufficient high 
pressure i.e. enough reservoir energy more miscibility can be achieved with CO2 resulting in higher 
wettability alteration. 
Injected Fluid 
Injected fluid with low salinity concentration as compared to formation water will lead to ion substitution 
and wettability alteration. Moreover, presence of divalent ions is necessary to have geochemical reactions. 
Therefore, along with lower salinity, presence of active ions is necessary.

WAG ratio is the ratio of pore volumes of water and gas injected alternatively. Zekri et al., 2011 mentioned 
that recovery of oil during CO2LSWAG depends a lot on WAG ratio during different WAG cycles. As

Hypothesis (Fines formation and EOR) 
Previously, in water based EOR’s like WAG and SmW, precipitation of brine salts was not believed to 
have positive impact on sweep efficiency. However, substitution of Mg2+ and Ca2+ on calcite surfaces is a
very well accepted phenomenon leading to brine composition alteration and increasing the possibility of 
solids formation known as fine formation. This section of the research project explains the hypothesis of 
fines formation and its contribution to EOR, proposed by Chakravarty et al., 2016. 
Through extensive research in last decade, SmW-EOR has been established as a possible EOR method for 
carbonate reservoirs. In many literature studies, SmW-EOR has also been stated as Low Salinity Water 
Injection or Advanced Ion Management (Zekri et al., 2011; Gupta et al., 2011). As explained earlier, many 
mechanisms have been proposed over working of SmW-EOR, but none has been accepted worldwide. The 
most famous of all of them is wettability alteration. The proposed EOR mechanism believed to consists of 
two processes: (1) Substitution of calcium by magnesium on the calcite surface (2) followed by release of 
adsorbed carboxyl compound due to adsorption of sulphate ions on the rock surface thus making the 
surface more water wet. Also in the effluent water, a decrease in sulphate concentration shows that sulphate 
acts as a reagent in this process (Zhang et al., 2007). Zhang et al., 2006 suggested that precipitation of salt 
must be avoided as it can block the flow of the waterflood. Many reports have shown the significance of 
increased concentration of SO4
2- ions in the brine leading to increased oil recovery (Zahid et al., 2011), but
only up to an optimum concentration (Awolayo et al., 2014). So collectively successful EOR with 
completely water wet core plugs and no EOR for very high SO4
2- ions shows that wettability alteration is
not applicable for all conditions. 
In their hypothesis, Chakravarty et al., 2016 suggested that, it is possible for insoluble fines to have a 
similar behaviour in chalk as in sandstone reservoirs.  They established a correlation between amount of 
fines and observed oil recovery via laboratory experiments and modelling calculations. They also proposed 
that wettability alteration is not the primary mechanism behind EOR; it is the formation and migration of 
insoluble fines, which brings the wettability alteration. In the following sections, it is shown that how 
Chakravarty et al., 2016 have explained the previous understood mechanism and further introduced the 
importance of fines formation and interpretation in EOR.

4.2 Concept of Fines formation in Carbonate Reservoirs 
In sandstone reservoirs, the concept of fines formation is related to clay present in the reservoir rock and 
ions in the injected brine. It is similar in carbonate reservoirs. The importance of fines as the main 
mechanism was first explained by Chakravarty et al., 2015. In terms of EOR in carbonate reservoirs, it is 
the precipitation of different salts (mainly CaSO4) at different pressure and temperature conditions. It is 
also found in good relation to the dissolution of calcite rock. Attrition of carbonate rock has also been 
defined as a part of fines formation by Chakravarty et al., 2016. Solubility or dissolution of salt in water is a

majorly defined fines as anhydrite precipitation when calcium ions interact with sulphate ions present in the 
formation or injected brine. They explained three major reasons for the formation of fines inside the

SmW-EOR primary mechanism is fines formation and migration which lead to wettability alteration and 
thus enhanced oil recovery. A straight correlation has been found between the observed fines formation and 
improved recovery. To conclude, in SmW-EOR it can be said that the theory of fines formation in 
carbonate reservoirs and its contribution for EOR is well defined and proved via experimental study and 
simulations.  
In CO2LSWAG, simulations performed using the Extended UNIQUAC model (Thomsen and Rasmussen, 
1999) shows significant formation of fines and dissolution of calcite at various concentration of CO2. Here 
CO2 is playing a very important role and enhances both the phenomenons significantly. But the work done 
on CO2LSWAG does not cover experimental evidence and the correlation of increased recovery with fines 
formation. Further chapter aims at explaining and finding out the correlation between calcite dissolution, 
fines formation and oil recovery for carbonate reservoirs in CO2LSWAG.

Model and Methodology 
Scale formation in wells is a very common problem for Oil and Gas Industry while producing oil from 
reservoirs (Dyer and Graham, 2002). This scale can be defined as hard adherent mineral deposits that 
precipitate from brines. Its formation depends on many factors like degree of super saturation, pH of the 
solution, CO2 content, composition of formation and injection brine, reservoir conditions and kinetics of 
rock dissolution. These scale depositions has long been considered as causing serious damage in wells and 
reason for reduced flow area. It has also been considered as a major cause of safety issues by blockage and 
failure of valves (Graham and Mackay, 2003).  
During recent studies, formation of fines which is a similar phenomenon as scale formation, has caught 
attention as a possible mechanism behind EOR in various water based EOR methods like low salinity 
waterflooding (LSW), water alternating gas injection (WAG) etc. So, prediction of its behaviour before 
applying these EOR methods has become more important. Many mathematical models have been 
developed to understand these behaviours. The majority of the programs (Duan et al., 1996; Møller et al., 
1998) designed to predict scale tendency uses the framework provided by Pitzer (1991). For multi-
component solutions, Pitzer’s approach requires both binary and ternary parameters. This requires both 
binary and ternary experimental data for all the species in the mixture. Such data are not available for some 
systems of importance in natural waters and are particularly scarce at the high temperature and pressure 
conditions like in Oil and Gas industry. Also, in many cases the quantity and quality of the experimental 
data do not justify the use of many parameters.  
So, a method requiring a limited amount of experimental data but leading to accurate predictions for the 
solid–liquid–vapour equilibrium and precipitation of fines for electrolyte solutions is of great use. One such 
model is the Extended UNIQUAC model (Thomsen and Rasmussen, 1999) which is the thermodynamic 
model selected for this study of fines formation.  
A similar software named ScaleChem is developed by OLI Systems Inc. It uses a thermodynamic model 
similar to the Extended UNIQUAC model named OLI Aqueous model. It can also predict the speciation 
over a wide range of chemicals in water at high temperature and pressure ranges.

Several experimental and simulation data has been studied for fines formation, its migration and calcite 
dissolution during CO2LSWAG EOR. The objective was to investigate the impact of fines formation and

6.1  Dataset 1 (Mohamed et al., 2011, SPE - 140943) 
Mohamed et al., 2011 performed experimental study on 10 calcite cores (Pink Desert Limestone) to 
identify the impact on permeability for different salinity brines in WAG. Different salts were tested at four 
different concentrations of 0, 1, 5 and 10 wt. %. The salts used are NaCl, CaCl2, and MgCl2. One of the 
coreflooding experiments was also performed with de ionised water (D.I. water). The experimental 
conditions set were 2000 psi (137.89 bar) and 200 oF (93.33 oC). The permeability was measured using the
Darcy’s equation. Three WAG cycles each of 10 pore volumes (PV) (5 PV of CO2 and 5 PV of brine) were 
used for each experiment. A new core was used for each coreflooding and the concentrations of different 
ions were also measured in the effluent. Their experimental results show that CaCl2 has the major effect on 
dissolution and thus change in permeability. According to Mohamed et al., 2011, dissolution is the only 
mechanism occurring behind permeability alteration. In NaCl brine, there was no major dissolution as 
compared to other brines, thus they concluded this to be the reason behind no significant change in 
permeability. They also mentioned that increasing the concentration of MgCl2 in brine caused more damage 
to the core i.e. more dissolution occurred.  
All the above data is treated with the Extended UNIQUAC model to understand the mechanism and 
reasons behind the observed change in permeability. The results obtained and correlations found are 
discussed as follow:

Significant attrition (Fines formation) 
To calculate the amount of attrition and prove its significance, calculations have been made to get an 
optimized amount of dissolution that can take place over a span of 30 PV of WAG. Over an injection of 10 
PV one cycle of WAG gets completed. Different amount of CO2 will be soluble in different salinity of 
brines. The amount of dissolution taking place will depend on the amount of formation water displaced by 
the CO2 injected and consequently CO2 available to interact afterwards. To calculate an optimum amount 
of dissolution taking place in each cycle of WAG, different percentage of brine removal has been 
considered (figure 39-47 a).   
During first injection of WAG for 5 PV, CO2 injected into the core plug will allow its interaction with the 
formation brine. A fraction of the injected CO2 becomes soluble in the brine and thus causes dissolution 
and attrition. Reported high concentration of calcium in the effluent also indicates the same. The expected 
amount of dissolution and attrition will depend on the extent of formation brine being displaced on gas 
injection. After 5 PV of gas injection, the injection of brine will displace the gas from the coreplug. Herein 
again; extend of dissolution will influence the amount of CO2 that can become soluble in the injected brine. 
This directly influences the maximum possible dissolution of calcite during brine injection. If a small 
fraction of injected brine/gas is displaced, then the amount of CO2 soluble required over 5 PV of brine 
injection will not be available in the coreplug. Therefore calcite dissolution during brine injection will be 
adversely affected. On the other hand; if majority of brine/gas is displaced then the amount of brine 
available during CO2 injection will be constrained. Thus most of injected CO2 will not become aqueous and 
shall remain in gaseous state. Correspondingly, the amount of calcite dissolution will decrease during gas 
injection and will be adversely affected. Therefore calculation has been conducted for different mixing 
proportions to calculate the maximum amount of dissolution that can takes place during the WAG 
experiment.   
For example, assume that 95% of the brine/gas is displaced during each step in the WAG experiment.  
Therefore the majority of the pore volume in core plug will contain CO2 (g) on gas injection. This gaseous 
CO2 can become soluble in the subsequently injected brine; which will cause dissolution and produce 
considerable amount of calcite in the effluent. But on CO2 injection, 95% of the formation brine will also 
be displaced from the coreplug. This would lead to mixing of CO2 with the remaining 5% of formation 
brine available in the coreplug. Thus on 95% displacement, major dissolution during brine injection will be 
observed; while only a small fraction of calcite gets dissolved during gas injection.  
In another case, assume that 15% of the brine/gas is displaced during each step in the WAG experiment.  
Therefore the majority of the pore volume in core plug will contain brine on gas injection. This gaseous 
CO2 injected subsequently become soluble in the brine; which will cause dissolution and produce 
considerable amount of calcite in the effluent. But on brine injection, 15% of the CO2 will also be displaced 
from the coreplug. This would lead to mixing of brine with the remaining 85% of CO2 available in the 
coreplug. Thus on 15% displacement, major dissolution during CO2 injection will be observed; while only 
a small fraction of calcite gets dissolved during brine injection. This way, calcite dissolution has been 
shown to depend on interaction of gas and brine.

previously used brines. It is expected that ion exchange might have taken place, but as no data has been

complete ion substitution invariably exceeds the limit of calcium observed in effluent. So, it can confirm 
that complete ion substitution is definitely not the case here and for this reason calculation performed here 
does not involve ion substitution. But still this does not deny the fact of having attrition and ion substitution 
both, as brine can dissolve a limited amount of calcium as shown in calculations. 
Section 6.1.1 concludes that in all the cases, be it different brines or different salinities, a significant 
amount of fines formation because of attrition is taking place. This fine formation cannot be ignored 
although it does not have a consistent relationship with the varying salinity. It can be both beneficial or may 
have adverse effects on oil recovery. As it will be explained in section 6.1.2, these fines can be both static 
and mobile. Sometimes these fines can be misled with the dissolution as they are in soluble form at ambient 
temperature conditions. A significant formation of static fines can block the pores and may lead to decrease 
in permeability instead of flow divergence to other directions. These fines can also cause problems near the 
production or injection wells.

6.1.2  Supersaturated effluent 
As shown above, effluent brine contains significant amount of calcium more than what should have been 
obtained from dissolution. This is because of attrition. Attrition produces insoluble fines which can be

6.1.3 Permeability alteration 
Mohamed et al., 2011 claimed that calcium chloride has the main effect in calcium dissolution and change 
in permeability. They also mentioned that NaCl variation in brine does not change permeability, but CaCl2 
and MgCl2 injection has altered it significantly (figure 51).  In this figure, Kinitial and Kfinal are permeability 
measured before and after the coreflooding experiment. As it can be seen in figure 51, the permeability 
alteration is not having a consistent relation with salinity variation. It is increasing at 1 wt. % for CaCl2 and 
then decreasing significantly.  
Figure 51: Permeability change while doing WAG experiment (Mohamed et al., 2011) 
The dissolution shown in experimental study does not have direct correlation to permeability alteration. 
The reason behind this is that attrition has not been considered in the discussion. Calculations done with the 
Extended UNIQUAC model showed that significant attrition is taking place i.e. release of small fines. 
These small fines do not get mixed with the injected or effluent brine. The reason is that the effluent brine 
is already saturated with the calcium ions. As, it cannot take up more ions, the attrition ions  flows along 
and sometimes blocks the flow path by sticking at the pore throats thus resulting in the reduction of 
permeability. Sometimes the size of these fines is so small that it comes out along with the effluent brine 
and becomes soluble at the ambient temperature (section 6.1.2). These are mobile fines and the one’s which 
stay in the core are static fines which were mobile once. So, attrition leads to both sides of the permeability 
alteration. It is hard to predict the actual result. This is the case here with the formation of fines in the 
coreplug. At some wt. % of brine it led to increase in permeability and others resulted in decrease. Thus it 
can be concluded that, dissolution surely leads to increase in permeability because ions get dissolved and 
comes out with the effluent brine, but attrition may have both negative and positive effects on permeability 
alteration.

6.2.1  Dissolution, Attrition or Ion Substitution? 
Chakravarty et al., 2016 explained that amount of ion substitution (Ca2+ by Mg2+) that takes place is
dependent on both the injection rate of brine and on type of coreplug. They mentioned an example of 
experiments conducted with outcrop chalks from Stevns Klint, Denmark. An immediate ion substitution 
has been reported on this coreplug which has not been observed in reservoir coreplug experiments 
conducted by Zahid et al., 2012. The coreplug used in this experimental study is a Pink Desert limestone. In 
literature, no data has been reported regarding ion substitution taking place or an extent up to which ion 
substitution takes place in this type of limestone.  
All the 7 WAG coreflooding experiments performed here uses brine with magnesium ions. But as no data 
has been reported for the concentration of magnesium ions in effluent, it is hard to predict weather ion 
substitution has taken place or not. The calculations performed shows that, values obtained after ion 
substitution are in fair range of the calcium ion concentration observed in effluent. So, it becomes 
important to analyze both the cases here in Dataset 2 unlike the case of MgCl2 brine used in Dataset 1. 
Considering an injection pattern and calculations sequence as explained in section 6.1.1, an analysis has 
been done for two cases; with ion substitution and without ion substitution. This is done to analyze if the 
observed calcium concentration in the effluent is solely because of dissolution (as mentioned by Mohamed 
et al., 2011) or a combination of dissolution, ion substitution and fines formation (attrition and anhydrite 
formation). The calculations performed over a sequence of 7 core flooding experiments are shown in figure

variation and uncertainty despite of using similar core plugs. 
In core 1 and core 4 different brine injection rates have been used keeping other parameters unchanged.

concentration observed in the effluent. A possibility could be that the ion substitution took place will be 
less and attrition rate was higher at high injection rate and vice versa for low injection rate. On contrary 
high dissolution is expected at lower injection rate as brine interacts for longer time with the mineral 
surface (Mohamed et al., 2011).

Conclusion 
In this thesis study, analysis of more than 20 coreflooding experiments has been performed on different 
type of limestones. Simulation models for CO2LSWAG have also been compared with the calculations 
done by the Extended UNIQUAC Model. The major finding of this study is that dissolution is not the only 
phenomenon behind the observed concentration of extra calcium obtained in the effluent while applying 
CO2WAG with different brines. Aqueous CO2 plays a significant role in calcium release either in dissolved 
or insoluble form thus increasing oil recovery. Also fines formation correlate to the enhanced oil recovery 
in CO2LSWAG. Based on the available experimental evidence (Mohamed et al., 2011; Teklu et al., 2014; 
Amin et al., 2012; Dang et al., 2014; Zekri et al., 2015) the Extended UNIQUAC calculations were 
performed. The results given were compared to previously proposed mechanisms and an impression of the 
possible behaviour of soluble salts and fines was found.  
There are many mechanisms that have been proposed in the literature explaining the recovery increase in 
water based EOR’s like CO2LSWAG. But no general consensus has been set yet. The mechanism of fines 
formation has proved to be explaining the observed increase in recovery with reservoirs having different 
wettability, different type of limestone and variable injection brine compositions. But, the main aim of this 
work was to perform a realistic assessment of fines formation necessary to understand CO2LSWAG better 
for carbonate reservoirs. Based on above research study following conclusions have been made: 
1.
Reasons of fines formation
While performing calculations using the Extended UNIQUAC Model, various reasons have been
found causing fines formation. They are as follows:

CO2LSWAG has been seen to cause release of small fines from the mineral surface due to
interaction of CO2 (aq.), injected brine, formation brine and rock. This is called attrition.

Interaction of soluble CO2 (aq.) with the calcite causes dissolution of CaCO3 followed by
anhydrite formation because of super saturation of CaSO4.

Change in brine speciation followed by ion substitution on the mineral surface causing
release of increased calcium ions.

Interaction between injected brine with the formation brine or interaction of two
individually soluble injected brine.

Variation in pressure and majorly temperature condition of the core plug during
coreflooding experiments.

2.
Effect of aqueous CO2
Soluble CO2 has been observed to effect fines formation to a great extent. There are brines which
get precipitated even without any available aqueous CO2. But nonetheless it has shown to enhance
the fines formation. There are examples like Teklu et al., 2014, where it has been observed that
there was no fines formation with only injected brine, but as soon as it comes in contact with
aqueous CO2, fines formation takes place.  Also it has been proven many times that reaction of
carbon dioxide and calcite causes its dissolution by forming bicarbonate ions. With decrease in
salinity, the solubility of CO2 increases and thus increases dissolution. This causes increased fines
formation, if sufficient amount of sulfate ions are present either in formation brine or injected
brine. It also has a great impact on WAG ratio. If more brine is injected than CO2 i.e. WAG ratio is
high towards brine injection, then there might be case when brine does not have much aqueous
CO2 available and thus no fine formation takes place.
3.
Dissolution-not the only phenomenon
An effluent data plays the most significant role in scrutinizing the fines formation during
coreflooding experiment. Significant dissolution has been reported in 16 coreflooding experiments
performed by Mohamed et al., 2011. Reanalyzing these experiments i.e. effluent data using the
Extended UNIQUAC Model has shown that significant attrition has taken place instead of only
dissolution. Irrespective of ion exchange, all the experiments have shown increased calcium
concentration in effluent. This is because the effluent brine can take up to a limited amount of
soluble calcium with it. An increased amount beyond supersaturation shows the attrition. In other
coreflooding experiments where effluent data has not been provided, attrition has also been
predicted, but to concretize effluent concentration is required. Fine formation as attrition has been
seen to affect the permeability both negatively and positively. This shows that no clear inverse
correlation of attrition can be made to the observed oil recovery.
4.
Precipitation of injected brine
In literature review, precipitation of salts during core flooding has been perceived to have a
negative impact on the oil production. Therefore, the amount of precipitation of injected brine is
also studied like for Zekri et al., 2015. It has been observed that the precipitates formed can
produce significant oil recovery. But in some cases, high precipitation of salt has an adverse effect
(Chakravarty et al., 2016). This gives an idea that this precipitation has no clear inverse correlation
to the observed increase in oil recovery; while the amount of fines formation taking place after ion
substitution and interaction with aqueous CO2 in the reservoir has been seen to correlate
consistently with oil recovery.
5.
Fines formation and observed recovery
Several studies from literature reports the effluent brine compositions measured at ambient
conditions and attempts to show it as dissolution thus reason for enhanced oil recovery.
Reanalyzing these brine compositions at experimental conditions show that substantial quantities
of fines were released from the core plugs. In many cases, as experimental conditions were

unaccounted fines have been seen to directly correlate with the observed oil recovery. This 
observed co-relation between produced fines and oil recovery show that insoluble salts (mobile 
fines) can pass through the pores and be produced in the effluent. Thus, the real amount of soluble

mistakenly overestimated as seen in Mohamed et al., 2011. 
These studies collectively shows that calculations carried out using the Extended UNIQUAC Model can 
predict behaviour of different brines injected along with CO2 over a varied range of temperature and 
pressure. Importantly, fines formation shows a good correlation with observed increase in oil recovery 
caused by CO2LSWAG. Though, this would need further experiments to be performed to develop further 
understanding and scrutinize the mechanism.

Future Scope of Work 
This research study provides significant correlations necessary to be considered while injecting brine along 
with CO2. A variety of experimental coreflooding and simulations shows enhanced recovery of oil. Fines 
formation shows direct correlation with wettability alteration thus enhanced oil recovery. But many of them 
lack necessary data to proof the explained hypothesis. Thus more experiments are needed to be performed 
that can be used to scrutinize the hypothesis over a wide range and variety of carbonate cores. Further work 
that can be done and studied is as follows: 
1.
Experimental investigations need to be done with varying salinity of injection brine while
observing effluent concentrations of different ions. This should be done along with performing
solubility and precipitation calculations using the Extended UNIQUAC Model (Thomsen et al.,
1999). These effluent concentrations are required in order to back track the fines formations and
condition of produced effluent at reservoir conditions. This is similar to predicting what would
happen if a particular brine will be injected in the reservoir.
2.
Calculations using the Extended UNIQUAC Model can be performed by making a strategy for
implementing WAG ratio and WAG timing and then study the recovery correlation with the fines
formation.
3.
This proposed mechanism should also be scrutinized from, petrophysical, geological, and flow
behavior angles. Particularly the grain size of the formed fines, their distribution in the coreplug
(during and after flooding) and their exact pattern of interaction with the residue oil are phenomena
which require further experimental investigations.
4.
Effect of CO2 on behavior of sea water and brines used in different fields can be studied. This will
provide information about how ion concentration and speciation changes as temperature and
pressure conditions changes.  It will be beneficial in deciding ion concentration of injected brine
while being used with WAG.
5.
Calculations can be performed to find an optimum amount of fines formation for different
conditions. Here, optimum amount means a brine composition which produces ample amount of
fines at reservoir conditions beneficial for enhanced oil recovery.
6.
Coreflooding experiments have been done from long time to get an understanding of the reservoir
behavior while applying an EOR technique. For these experiments, the reservoir rock is used to
mimic the behavior. In case of water based EOR’s like SmW EOR or CO2LSWAG, it might be a
different scenario and an update or better technique is required to predict the actuality. A better

coreflooding system design or understanding is required which can depict the reservoir behavior 
more precisely. It is explained in brief as follow: 
Coreflooding experiment 
In Coreflooding, the general procedure followed to saturate the core to its irreducible conditions 
works well for most of the experiments. The problem comes when the core is flooded with the 
desired brine or gas and then output is measured. In case of brine injection as in this research 
project, the phenomenon of fines formation i.e. dissolution and attrition is observed. A core is 
generally a piece of reservoir rock and is a micro level reservoir. So, initially the brine is injected 
at the ambient conditions and then it enters the core at desired reservoir conditions. The brine 
interacts with the core causing dissolution, ion substitution, attrition and several other 
phenomenons. This makes the brine saturated resulting in saturating brine coming out of the core 
along with mobile fines if any. The path that has been travelled here by the brine is generally very 
short. All predictions made are based on the output that is measured. 
Reservoir Flooding 
The injection of similar brine in actual reservoir will behave differently. To understand this 
phenomenon, imagine a reservoir consist of n cores as used in coreflooding experiment.  The first 
core gets flooded with the injected brine or CO2 in alternative positions. As explained in above 
experimentations, fine formation will take place. The brine injected will also dissolve some 
calcium and becomes saturated at some point. This saturated brine will interact with the second 
core. But here, mobile fines which might be of small size, will flow along the saturated brine and 
eventually becomes bigger to alter the mobility of the fluids in coreplug. They can change the ions 
speciation significantly. Further these fines will block some of the pores and will alter the 
permeability and coreplug will behave differently than it was expected to. Moreover brine will be 
injected from different wells in real reservoirs. It might be possible that brines will interact with 
each other and will create some new species which gets precipitated and will alter the flow. So, 
here it can be very different from what was predicted in coreflooding experiment. 
So, coreflooding experiments does tells about the behavior but it’s not completely true for actual 
reservoirs and thus better techniques needs to be find out for better predictability.
