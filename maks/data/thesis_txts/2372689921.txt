Abstract
This research proposes an autonomous and intelligent robotic arm system for the
accomplishment of constrained task in an uncertain environment. The investigation is
motivated by the vision of future Human-Robot collaboration. Our solution includes
knowledge from different areas such as control theory, robot modelling, path and trajectory
planning, computer vision, sensor system integration, collision avoidance, and decision-
making theory. In this report, we combine these aspects to a complete real-time control
architecture across different level of abstraction in a teleoperation scenario. The software is
implemented in a ROS framework, in order to support a clear and modular design, suitable
for future extensions and integration on different hardware components. The experiments
show a prototype capable of achieving the goal the 99% of time in uncertain environment
constantly interrupted by external agents. We can conclude that an adaptive robotic system
capable of reacting to external stimuli in an uncertain environment is potentially obtainable.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The vision of the research is to design an intelligent and autonomous robotic arm
system for the accomplishment of constrained tasks in an uncertain environment.
This investigation is motivated by our previous research ( Capolei et al. (2017)) about
the control of an industrial robot arm employed in surgical application. In that introductory
analysis the robot was unconscious of the surroundings populated by humans and of the
tool it was manipulating. That problem led us to question more about the concept of
human-robot collaboration, and in particular about the constrained motion in uncertain
working scene.
Traditionally, the autonomous and safe moves were not required in real world appli-
cations, because the robotic manipulator was frequently used in industry with static and
known external constraints. That implies the use of pre-computed trajectories executed
in fenced areas. Only in the Nineties, the robots were equipped with cameras and other
exteroceptive sensors in order to sense the surroundings and the object they were manipu-
lating. These endowments enabled the increase in the detection of external surroundings,
but were not enough to introduce them in the human vicinity.
In 2005, a new complete generation of robots emerged: the collaborative robots, or
cobots. Cobots address to most of those tasks which until now couldn’t be fulﬁlled
because of security reasons ( European Parliament (2017), International Organization for
Standardization (2017)). Moreover, it has been proved that the human-robot cooperation
leads to a drastic reduction of the workplace accidents and to an optimization of the
production process in different commercial sectors ( Hägele et al. (2002), Bauer et al.
(2008), Kaplan et al. (2016)). In the last decade, this new trend is leading to a signiﬁcant
progress in the design of new types of robots, and the development of advanced collision
avoidance methods.
As a matter of fact, the biggest concern of human-robot collaboration is the safety ( Had-
dadin et al. (2010), Haddadin et al. (2009), Heinzmann and Zelinsky (2003)). Human

CHAPTER 2
Idea and Concept
The constrained motion problem is not new in Robotics, and it consists of:
• connecting the start to the goal conﬁguration;
• ﬁnding a collision-free path for a robot in an environment containing rigid bodies;
• respecting other constraints, for example task constraints;
• possibly optimizing certain criteria.
Although, for mobile robots the safe and reactive motion is largely solved in obstacle
vicinity ( LaValle (2006)), there are still few algorithms developed for robot arms ( Siciliano
and Khatib (2016), LaValle (2006)). In general, the constrained planning problem is
unmanageable for robot manipulator. In practice, it is possible to ﬁnd adequate solution
depending on the context. The most crucial aspects are the high computation efﬁciency
and memory requirements. In particular, the approximation of the 3D objects into point of
the conﬁguration space and the map-checking are computational expensive, while the data
processing has to be fast in order to have rapid reaction to speciﬁc external events.
On the other hand, the procedures to resolve the safe human-robot collaboration can
be mainly branched into:
• pre-collision safety strategies : that are concerned into measuring the human-robot
physical distance and consequentially enable the robot to take precautionary action
whenever this distance is below a limit ( Balan and Bone (2006), Flacco et al.
(2012), Kuli´c and Croft (2007), Shin et al. (2010));
• post-collision safety strategies: that are involved into the reduction of the concussion
or damage in case of collision ( Haddadin et al. (2008));

component of the planner is an RRT* sampling based motion planner in the 3D workspace.

2.1.2
Constraints Classiﬁcation and Inﬂuence
Li et al. (2008) introduce a motion planning method based on trajectory analysis and
waiting strategy for robot manipulator in a dynamic environment. The most interesting
feature of this methodology is the classiﬁcation of the obstacles into four groups up to
their trajectories: Piecewise Linear obstacles without automatic avoidance ability, Piece-
wise Linear obstacles with automatic avoidance ability, Piecewise Nonlinear obstacles
without automatic ability, and Piecewise Nonlinear obstacles with automatic ability. The
“Piecewise Linear/Nonlinear” deﬁnition refers to the kinematics behavior of the obstacle
in fragments of its trajectory. Each obstacle category is related to a different safety factor,
that scale the inﬂuence of the obstacle on the action of the manipulator.
While in contrast, Wang et al. (2010) propose an on-line motion planning algorithm
for 7-degree of freedom robot manipulator that avoids the collision of the end-effector
and the Elbow link. The method consists on using an additional constraint named Elbow
angle. The obstacles detected by the camera are modeled as plane. One side of the plane
is deﬁned as safe area, whereas the other side is the dangerous area. The Elbow angle is
on-line decided in relation to the planes built from the environment information. Once the
constraint is updated the kinematics is solved.

2.2
Problem Statement and Solution Requirements
In general the control of a robot arm relies on the elaboration of data from Interoceptive
sensors, that give a rough estimate of the robot position with respect to a ﬁxed and known
frame. There is no need for a more complex control structure if the robot arm is working
in a obstacle-free environment executing repetitive action with static target. Though, the
functional architecture gets more elaborated if the planning scene contains external rigid
bodies that possibly move around the robot, in particular if those bodies are humans, or
robots. Thence, it is required that the robot moves in the direction of the target avoiding
any potential collision, and if an obstacle is approaching while is manipulating a tool, the
robot should be able to prevent any probable impact in order to protect the object. To
fulﬁll all these problems, the control system should be endowed with a number of tools
that provide:
• information about the state of the system;
• the real-time model of the working environment containing only the important
features for the task purpose;
• the real-time determination of likely collisions;
• a reactive control;
• control of the end-effector movements in a collision-free manner;
• the prevention of collision respecting the task constraints.
2.3
Solution Justiﬁcation
What this project wants to achieve is a robotic control system capable of extracting
useful information from the environment, reasoning about it in order to move cautiously

and to accomplish the requested task. To accomplish that, it is proposed a rule-based
supervision system which will be in charge of regulating the separate components of the
system in order to achieve a goal. The rules-based approach guarantees a continuously
reactive system suitable in dynamic environment, such system can change goals and
intention depending on the situation. The high-level control system has access to all the
current internal and external states, such as the working scene information, the planning
and execution status and the user desires. The information from the environment is
collected by a stereo vision sensor, and processed via speciﬁc algorithms that extract
only few but important features without overloading the computer memory. To solve the
obstacle detection problem, it is introduced a fast algorithm based on spherical coordinates
system. The method assumes the dynamic obstacle as static for a inﬁnitesimal range
of time. Moreover, the implemented positioning algorithms achieves the most common

The problem of the reasoning capabilities of an autonomous manipulator is the topic
of this chapter. Robot autonomy is of high relevance for Human-Robot collaboration, in
such uncertain environment, the robot must not only reason about itself but also reason
about the surroundings in order to execute appropriate actions. This capability of properly

are able to reach their goals or react to observed situation with planned actions that are
functions of some psychological attitudes, such as beliefs, desires and intentions. Since

about it, resulting in a elaborated goal-directed and reﬂective behaviour.

via state diagram. Then, the reasoning system architecture and operation are described

CHAPTER 5
Positioning Methods
Mechanically, an industrial robot arm can be synthetically described as a kinematic
chain of rigid bodies, i.e. links, coupled through revolute joints. At the extremity of the
chain there is the base, which is constrained to a ﬁxed substructure, and the end-effector
that is free to move within the robot reaching radius. Considering that the ﬁnal motion is
the result of the movement of the coupled links, the corresponding position and orientation
of the end-effector is deﬁned with respect to the overall chain. The goal of this chapter is
to describe the method used to derive the end-effector pose, and to introduce the different
algorithms employed to manipulate this pose. First, the kinematics of a six degrees of
freedom robot arm is discussed. Then in section 5.2, the positioning algorithms to translate
and rotate the end-effector are presented. It follows a description of the technique employed
to position a speciﬁc tool around a remote center of motion, or fulcrum point. The chapter
ends with the method used to position the robot when an obstacle is approaching.
5.1
Kinematics
Since the kinematic analysis of a six-links manipulator can be extremely complex, the
Denavit-Hartenberg ( DH) convention is used. The DH convention allows to compute
easily the forward kinematics of an open chain manipulator taking advantages of its
recursive nature. The complex problem is decoupled into the individuation of the frames
describing each link, and the determination of the coordinate transformation that represents
two consecutive frames, namely the Homogeneous Transformation matrix Ai. Hence, the
aforementioned matrix contains information about the position and orientation of the frame
oixiyizi with respect to the frame oi−1xi−1yi−1zi−1. The Homogeneous Transformation
matrix Ai is a function of four parameters ( ergo thetai, di, ai, αi) and is mathematically
obtained from the matrix product of four basic transformations:

Control Architecture
In this chapter the functional architecture of the control system is treated. In the ﬁrst
part, the overall hierarchical and modular structure of the system is described. Succes-
sively, the hardware architecture is presented, it includes a description of the employed
manipulator and the stereo vision camera speciﬁcation. The chapter ends with the software
components overview: from the programming environment description to the illustration
of each module of the software.
In the general assessment, the user communicates with a computer running an open
source software speciﬁc for robotic application. The computer is used to elaborate all
the data necessary for controlling the robot, such as the user’s command, the kinematics
information, the robot trajectory and the perception data. A stereo vision camera is
employed to acquire the information from the environment. A six degrees of freedom
industrial robot arm is chosen to execute the tasks.

the robot model is ﬁltered from the Point cloud, in this way the system does not see the
robot as a possible collision object in the scene, also because the robot will not move in
case of collision.

Octomap Node. Hither, the OctoMap library ( Hornung et al. (2013)) is employed
in order to convert the Point-Cloud into an efﬁcient 3D occupancy grid map using oc-
trees1. The Octomap is able to model in real time arbitrary environment without prior
assumptions about it. The main advantages of using the Octomap for collision avoidance
application are the drastically reduced runtime and memory requirements derived from
the probabilistic approach and the data compression method. The only drawback is that
due to its probabilistic nature is not directly suitable for dynamic environment. Thus, we
used additional procedure in order to update the Octomap periodically without loosing its
efﬁciency.
Scene Builder Node. The Scene Builder collects the Octomap data and other pre-
implemented objects, such as the holder of the tool and the tool itself, and adds them to
the working scene as collision objects. It also creates the interactive markers that are used
to move the pre-implemented collision object in the scene.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusions
The research presents an intelligent and autonomous robotic system capable of moving
smoothly and safely in an environment containing external rigid bodies. The developed
system provides a solution for the generic teleoperation tasks of the robot end-effector,
and additional algorithms focused on positioning a speciﬁc tool around a remote center
of motion. The control system demonstrates to react efﬁciently to external disturbances
in respect to the pre-determinate task constrains, in particular, the experimental results
exhibit a high success rate of 99% out of the total planned trajectories.
The use of an industrial robot arm allows a Large-Scale application. Moreover, the
integration of the software components in ROS supports the implementation of a clear
and modular design, suitable for future extensions and application on different hardware
platforms.
The future work should focus on improving the system performance through fault
diagnosis, moreover It could be of great interest to boost the spatial reasoning capabilities
of the control system through the implementation of a human-tracking algorithm based on
the employment of several stereo vision camera. The robotic tasks could be extended with
pick up and place duties, that extension could lead to a more sophisticate and vast system
data base that could include additional A.I. techniques.
