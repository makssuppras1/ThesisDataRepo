Compliance of containerships with international standards for 
carbon intensity with a focus on methanol 
Abstract 
This paper investigates the performance of 16 container ships from a large Danish shipping company and 
their respective compliance with international standards for energy efficiency and carbon intensity. IMO 
has adopted short-term measures on carbon intensity for ships that set requirements for the coming 
years. The European Union is expected to adopt measures to regulate the shipping industry with the 
FuelEU initiative. The proposal regulates the energy of the fuel used on-board ships sailing in the EU. The 
paper investigates how effective methanol is as a FuelEU compliance option. This paper also investigates 
the possibility to comply with the IMO regulation on CII, as this has recently been amended to include a 
number of correction factors, one of which concerns the transport of refrigerated containers on-board. 
Here, a series of technical and operational compliance options in combination with the use of methanol 
is considered. An optimization model helps finding the best, cost-effective solutions for CII compliance for 
each sample ship. For FuelEU compliance, green e-methanol is a viable solution until sometime in the 
2040’s. LNG-based methanol, however, performs even worse than the traditional fossil fuels used today. 
For CII compliance, it is more difficult to meet the requirements. Methanol is ineffective, and other 
expensive compliance options such as a waste heat recovery system will for many sample ships be 
required before 2030. However, a soon to be adopted change to the CII regulation considers green 
methanol as a zero-emission fuel. This adjustment aligns the FuelEU proposal and the CII regulation, and 
green methanol thus becomes a viable solution in both regulations in the next-coming decades. 
1. Introduction 
The transition to a maritime shipping industry that drastically reduces greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions 
requires many different solutions that aggregated achieve the targets. It is a complicated process that 
covers a broad range of fields such as the technological and scientific research fields as well as the political 
and the juridical fields. The International Maritime Organization (IMO) is in a continuous process of 
regulating the industry to achieve a greener future. Furthermore, the European Union (EU) has recently 
proposed a series of initiatives to regulate the maritime industry. 
1.1 Regulation of the International Maritime Organization 
In 2018, the IMO provided a regulatory framework with a set of internationally mandatory, short-, 
medium-, and long-term measures to improve energy efficiency on ships. The “Initial IMO GHG Strategy” 
sets out a vision to phase out GHG emissions from maritime industry as soon as possible. The overall levels 
of ambition of the initial strategy are as follows (International Maritime Organization, 2022a): 
1. Carbon intensity of the ship to decline through implementation of further phases of the energy 
efficiency design index (EEDI) for new ships: to review with the aim to strengthen the energy 
efficiency design requirements for ships with the percentage improvement for each phase to be 
determined for each ship type, as appropriate.

2. Carbon intensity of international shipping to decline: to reduce CO2 emissions per DWT-miles, as 
an average across international shipping, by at least 40% by 2030, pursuing efforts towards 70% 
by 2050, compared to 2008. 
3. GHG emissions from international shipping to peak and decline: to peak GHG emissions from 
international shipping as soon as possible and to reduce the total annual GHG emissions by at 
least 50% by 2050 compared to 2008 whilst pursuing efforts towards phasing them. 
Point 1 refers to the requirement of complying with certain regulatory standards in the 
implementation of new ships to the fleet. It strengthens the requirement for the already known Energy 
Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) – an index to estimate the performance of a new ship under perfect 
conditions. The formula for the index is based on technical design parameters of the individual ship, where 
a lower score is a better score. In June 2021, IMO adopted a similar measure, EEXI, addressing the 
environmental performance and technical efficiency for existing ships in a fleet (International Maritime 
Organization, 2022b). For point 2, a carbon intensity indicator (CII) such as the annual efficiency ratio (AER) 
measures the operational performance of the ships as CO2 emissions pr. DWT-miles. Point 3 sets the goal 
for the overall volume of emissions. According to the roadmap for the implementation of the initial 
strategy, the adoption of a revised strategy will take place in the Spring of 2023 (International Maritime 
Organization, 2018).

1.2 Regulation of the European Union 
The European Commission launched the European Green Deal in December 2019. The deal pledges the 
union to reach climate neutrality by 2050 and it is the direct follow-up from the EU to the Paris Agreement 
agreed upon in the UN in 2015 (European Council, 2022a). The goal of climate neutrality is followed by 
the legislative “Fit for 55” package, which is the approach to turn the ambitions into law. The FuelEU 
Maritime initiative is one of the proposals in the package alongside other important regulations such as 
the Alternative Fuel Infrastructure Regulation (AFIR) and the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS). The 
AFIR sets out a framework of common measures among member states for the deployment of the 
infrastructure that secures the supply of the alternative fuels and electricity needed in European ports 
(European Council, 2022b).

2. Literature study 
This study is a continuation on the findings in the paper by Schroer et al. (2022) titled “An evidence-based 
assessment of IMO’s short-term measures for decarbonizing container shipping”. Shortly described, the 
paper investigated the possibility of installing and using a series of technical and operational options for 
container ships to comply with the CII regulation. The paper investigated the potential in decreasing fuel 
consumption and thereby CO2 emissions using these compliance options and their corresponding costs. 
2.1 The compliance options 
The analysis in this paper uses the exact same calculation methods for reduction potentials for the below 
presented compliance options as described by Schroer et al. Also, the analysis in this paper uses the same 
cost estimations for installing the compliance options as researched by Schroer et al. 
The compliance options are: 
• 
Engine power limitation (EPL): achieving emission reductions by reducing the speed through 
water by limiting the engine power. The calculated reduction potentials are in the cases of using 
60%, 50% or 40% maximum continuous rating (MCR), where 100% corresponds to the maximum 
power output of the ship. The tables and solutions in the later analysis refer to these options as 
“EPL 60%”, “EPL 50%” and “EPL 40%”. EPL is studied by Rutherford et al. (2020). 
• 
Turbocharger cutout (TCCO): achieving emission reductions through cutting out the turbo 
charger at engine loads where the turbocharger is not effective. TCCO is studied by Guan et al. 
(2015). 
• 
Waste heat recovery system (WHRS): achieving emission reductions from utilizing the waste heat 
from the main engine and transforming it to electricity. WHRS is studied by Singh and Pedersen 
(2016).

One of the conclusions by Schroer et al. (2022) is that compliance with the CII is not straightforward,

alternative fuel. 
The following analysis in this paper assumes that the fuel reduction potentials when installing the above

methods for the reduction potentials of the compliance options can be used if combusting methanol in 
the main engine. This might not exactly be the case, but an exact evaluation of this requires more research. 
2.2 Methanol 
Several studies have reviewed possible pathways to decarbonization using the potential of alternative 
fuels. Liquefied natural gas (LNG) has been the most researched alternative fuel within the last two 
decades, but recently other fuels have been trending in the research such as methanol, ammonia and 
hydrogen fuels (Xing, Hui et al., 2021). 
Figure 3 shows that LNG still has a small but a noticeable share of the fuel consumption by ships 
worldwide. In recent years, the use of LNG has grown significantly. One of the advantages of LNG is its 
capability to reduce environmentally harming nitrogen oxide (NOx) and sulfur oxide (SOx) emissions.  
However, the reduction of CO2 is much less impressive at only around 15-20 % compared to heavy fuel oil 
(HFO) making it hard to address the serious climate change observed by only focusing on LNG. 
Hydrogen and ammonia-based fuels can reduce CO2 emissions significantly. For hydrogen, the total life 
cycle emissions can be close to zero and no CO2 is emitted during combustion. At low amounts, it blends 
with conventional fuels.

2.2.1 Production 
The Fuel EU GHG intensity calculation includes the well-to-tank (WtT) emissions. Thus, the life cycle and 
corresponding emission potential of a fuel before it reaches the fuel tanks are important. Methanol can 
be produced from a great variety of feedstocks. LNG and coal are today the most used feedstocks for 
methanol production. The effect of methanol to reduce GHG emissions depends largely on the feedstock 
used in the production. The use of fossil feedstocks contributes with significant WtT GHG emissions in the 
production process of methanol. This makes fossil-based methanol an alternative, which is just as bad as 
the conventional fuels used today. This is reflected in the very large CO2 equivalent WtT emission value of 
31.3 g CO2 eq. / MJ for methanol produced from LNG, which is much higher than that for HFO and MDO 
as seen in Table 1.   
As of 2020, approximately 65% of global methanol production came from natural gas whereas 
approximately 35% came from coal. Less than 1% was produced form biomass and renewable sources. 
Methanol produced from coal and natural gas is considered high carbon intensive and is called brown or 
grey methanol. Methanol produced from renewable feedstocks is called green if the carbon capture is 
obtained from renewable industrial processes, and it is called blue if the carbon capture is obtained from 
fossil fuel combustion industrial processes of any kind. The green and blue kinds are considered as low 
carbon intensity fuels (The International Renewable Energy Agency, 2021).

an electrolysis process. There is a current scalability problem in the industry, but the production of 
hydrogen from green electrolysis processes is expected to increase significantly in this decade (The 
International Renewable Energy Agency, 2021). 
2.2.2 Technology readiness 
Methanol has the important ability that it can be used in conventional internal combustion engines (ICE) 
due to a high-octane rating in the fuel that does not damage the engine. This can be as a drop-in fuel or 
as a complete substitute. Methanol can be stored at normal temperatures and pressure levels, which is 
not the case for ammonia, liquid hydrogen and LNG.  
For safety handling, especially fire monitoring and firefighting systems need installations as methanol-
based fire burns invisibly. Fuel tanks and pipes should be double-walled and have gas freeing systems for 
extra security and complete water tightness. In general, the technologies required for the bunkering 
station, the safety handling in the fuel tanks and pipes as well as in the main engine are considered mature. 
The conclusions for the technology readiness and description of the functional requirements are based 
on a report from “Det Norske Veritas group” (DNV) to the IMO in 2016.  For further technical details, 
please see the DNV report. 
2.2.3 Costs 
The Methanol Institute of The International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) (2021) has estimated the 
production costs of methanol based on the different types of production. Combining the costs of the 
BECCS and the DAC processes, green e-methanol currently has a production cost of 1200 to 2400 US 
dollars pr. ton. This is considerably higher than fossil-based methanol, which has a production cost around 
100 to 250 US dollars pr. ton. However, mature production cost levels are projected to go down to a cost 
interval of 250 to 630 US dollars pr. ton for green e-methanol, which then makes it a lot more 
approachable as a solution. Xing et al. (2021) estimates the cost of production of renewable methanol, 
where the feedstock mainly is biomass, to being 1.5 to 4 times higher than production of fossil-based 
methanol. The future price of green e-methanol also depends on the production costs of green hydrogen, 
which depends on the price of renewable energy power. Furthermore, electrolysers are currently used at

can be converted to handle methanol at a relatively low capital cost level (Lindstad et al., 2021). The DNV

to be at a cost of 10,500,000 US Dollars, which in September 2022 prices corresponds very close to the 
same amount of euros. This is the number used in the following analysis. However, it might be an 
estimation that is too low. 
3. Objectives 
The analysis looks at a range of example container ships from a large Danish shipping company to evaluate 
the overall environmental performance of the ships and the fuels. The analysis takes its starting point 
where Schroer et al. (2022) made their conclusions. This includes using same calculations methods for the 
fuel reduction potential given by the investigated compliance options presented in Section 2.1 and the 
corresponding researched costs. The analysis of this paper adds the new correction factor for reefer 
containers to the CII calculations as presented in Section 1.1, which Schroer et al. (2022) did not include 
in their evaluation of a ships’ performance. The ships’ general performances are evaluated using the AER 
as the CII. Furthermore, the analysis considers the consequences of the FuelEU regulation and investigates 
the compliance possibility of methanol use. The performance of methanol is evaluated with the GHG 
intensity indicator proposed in the FuelEU initiative. Moreover, the analysis considers how the methanol 
consumption required by the FuelEU regulation affects the compliance possibilities for meeting the CII 
regulation. This includes an investigation of when to install the technical and operational compliance 
options in combination with the use of methanol to meet the CII requirements for each sample ship. An 
operations research model proposes solutions to this optimization problem. An in-depth, techno-
economic analysis is exemplified for one of the sample ships. Specifically, the analysis considers the 
following statements: 
• 
Examine how the inclusion of the reefer correction factor for the CII affects the compliance with 
the CII regulation for the sample ships. 
• 
Investigate the compliance possibilities offered by methanol for the FuelEU GHG intensity 
requirements considering the corresponding costs.

Pooling ships together is also an opportunity to meet GHG intensity targets. However, with the very similar

4.2 Meeting the IMO short-term measures 
This analysis uses the AER as the CII as presented in Section 1.1. The AER value together with the EEXI 
value constitute the evaluation of each sample ships to decide whether compliance options and 
alternative fuels are needed to comply with the CII requirements. 
Section 1.1 shortly presented the EEXI and AER measures. The EEXI is a technical index used as a 
benchmarking mechanism for a one-time check in 2023. It measures the energy efficiency of the ship. The 
calculation of the EEXI is available in the MEPC78 Annex 12 (2022). The reference company provides the 
attained EEXI performance of the sample ships for this analysis. If the attained EEXI performance is below 
the EEXI requirement as presented in Table 4, an EPL solution with a minimum limitation of 50% MCR is 
used as a solution for the sample ship from 2023. This is the approach presented by Schroer et al. (2022), 
and EPL is an effective approach to reduce the attained EEXI performance of the ship. 
The calculation of the AER uses the fuel consumption of the sample ships as provided by the data through 
the IMO DCS. As the specific fuel consumption for reefer containers is not given in the dataset, this paper 
uses an estimation based on the number of reefers on-board as described in the calculation for estimated 
fuel consumption of reefer containers in MEPC78 Appendix 1.

correction factor is to give a more fair evaluation of the ship that carries many reefer containers. It is

how to achieve the required label C or above, as soon as they have a D label. If possible, a ship should

to 2019.

4.4 Solutions to comply with the CII requirements 
This section provides a year-by-year solution for Ship B on which installations to make and how much 
methanol to use. The solutions are designed to meet the CII requirement line every year to not receive a 
D rating for the ship. As Figure 7 shows, the best performance ship B achieves is well below the 2030 
requirement. Therefore, several combinations are possible to meet the requirements year by year. 
The solution depends on the costs of the different compliance options, which Appendix 1 presents. Table 
18 in Appendix 3 shows an action plan to comply for the rest of the sample ships. 
4.4.1 Solution 1: Install compliance options when needed without FuelEU requirements 
The first solution installs the necessary compliance options as late as possible, i.e. at the time the limit for 
CII requires a better performance of the ship – and not earlier than this. As a starting point, the approach 
considers a cheaper compliance before considering a more expensive compliance option to deliver a 
proposed solution. Furthermore, the payback time for installing an expensive compliance option like a 
WHRS is evaluated, if a WHRS is required at a given time. 
Firstly, the one-time check for EEXI has to be considered. Table 4 shows that ship B meets the required 
EEXI, and a 50% MCR is thus not required from 2023.  
The ship initially performs at 11.19 g CO2 / DWT-miles. This is sufficient until 2024 where the requirement 
is a maximum of 10.98 g CO2 / DWT-miles. Installing the VFDs that do not require a WHRS already installed 
– namely seaweater cooling pumps and low temperature freshwater pumps – is sufficient as it gives a CII 
performance of 10.93 g CO2 / DWT-miles. 
In 2024, the requirement is 10.74 g CO2 / DWT-miles. Installing the two cheapest options, AEE and TCCO, 
will reduce the performance of the ship to 10.84 g CO2 / DWT-miles. Therefore, one of the expensive 
options such as a WHRS, EPL or methanol is required. A WHRS is only relevant if the payback time is 
shorter than the remaining lifetime of the ship when comparing the fuel savings to the investment and 
maintenance costs. For ship B, it is approximately 8 years with the used fuel prices for fuel oil and marine 
diesel oil. However, as the ship was introduced in 2016, it is assumed to sail in 2032, 8 years after 2024, 
with the assumed lifetime of a ship of 25 years. It is thus not a problem to install the WHRS. This results 
in a performance of 9.71 g CO2 / DWT-miles, which is sufficient until 2030. 
In 2030, installing the last possible VFD, turbogenerator condenser pumps, is not sufficient to meet 
requirements. EPL is required. The EPL solution that limits the engine the least is “EPL 60%” corresponding 
to limiting the engine to 60% MCR. This results in a performance of 8.60 g CO2 / DWT-miles, which is way 
below the limit of 8.96. However, a 60% MCR for ship B results in an additional working time of 489 hours 
per year as shown in Table 13 in Appendix 1. This is a considerable amount of extra hours due to a very 
high average engine load for ship B. A better solution would be to limit the engine power to a higher level 
than 60% of the full power output, but 60% is the lowest limitation currently considered in this analysis. 
Notice lastly in Table 6 that the AER with the correction factor increases in the years from 2025 to 2029, 
even though the compliance options installed are the same. This is due to the reduced effect of the reefer 
correction factor for each year after 2023.

4.4.3 Solution 3, minimize total cost from 2019-2030 with OR model 
The operations research model depicted in Appendix 2 formulates a mixed integer problem that finds the 
solution that Table 10 displays. The procedure in solution 1 and 2 is cumbersome, and it requires a 
substantial amount of manual calculations to find the optimal solution for each sample ship. The 
operations research model addresses this issue.  
The model is designed to consider the same factors for reduction potentials and costs as in solution 2 
above. It is written in the programming language Julia. The solver used is the state of the art Gurobi solver, 
which solves the problem for all 16 sample ships individually in about 5 seconds on a 2016 Intel core I5 
Macbook Pro. The free and open-source GLPK solver also easily finds the optimal solution. 
The objective function: 
Similar to the other solutions, the objective of the model is to minimize costs: one-time installation costs, 
maintenance costs, operational costs and fuel costs for each individual sample ship. However, the model 
does not wait to install compliance options for each single ship until it is strictly necessary for the ship to 
meet the CII requirements. In most cases, the model decides to install compliance options early to gain a 
larger total amount of fuel savings in the time span of years from 2019-2030. Explained shortly, if the 
payback time for a compliance options is shorter than the time span of 12 years, which are the years 
currently considered by the model, the model will install the compliance option. This minimizes the total 
cost for the entire period from 2019-2030. 
The decision varibles: 
The model considers two types of decision variables: binary variables for the compliance options and 
continuous variable for the fuel consumption of the different fuel types; fuel oil, diesel oil and methanol. 
A binary variable decides the installation year of a compliance option and a similar binary variable decides 
whether the compliance option is available for a ship in any given year – the compliance option is available 
in all years after the installation of the compliance option. Here, the only difference is EPL, which can be 
turned on and off in any year. The continuous variables measure the fuel consumption of the different 
fuel types of the ship. The fuel oil and diesel oil consumption depends on the compliance options installed 
and the amount of fuel oil and diesel oil that is converted to methanol in a given year. 
Main constraints: 
The main limiting constraint is to secure that a ship’s performance is below the AER limit in any year. 
Similar to solution 1 and 2, the model secures that the ship installs compliance options or converts to 
methanol to be below the limit. The model is designed to include the reduction potentials from switching 
to methanol or installing compliance options. Energy balance constraints secure that a ship uses the same 
amount of total energy as in the baseline year of 2019, unless compliance options that reduce the fuel 
consumption are installed. The model includes the reefer correction factor for the CII performance, the 
methanol requirements from FuelEU, and it calculates the fuel and emission reductions similarly to the 
manual approach in solution 1 and 2.

Using an operations research model to find the combination of compliance options and the amount of 
methanol to use has several advantages that make it easier for large-scale analysis. 
Advantages of OR-model: 
• 
Finds the cheapest combination of compliance options and methanol consumption for each 
individual ship while meeting the CII requirements every year in the time span that the model 
considers (currently 2019-2030). 
• 
The model automatically handles calculations for payback time, fuel cost savings, energy 
conservation when switching to methanol etc. 
• 
Finds the best solution for each individual ship quickly. 
• 
Given the correct input data, calculation mistakes do not appear.  
Limitations of OR-model: 
• 
The solution depends entirely on accurate input data for compliance options reduction potentials 
as well as the costs for installations and prices of fuels. 
• 
The model currently only analyzes the time span 2019-2030. However, as several of the sample 
ships are expected to sail after 2030, this is an important factor to notice. Input data for e.g. CII 
requirements after 2030 are necessary to expand the model.

• 
Lacks the ability of making qualitative decisions. For example: if a sample ship has 1-3 years of its 
life time remaining, and it requires methanol retrofitting or a WHRS to continue sailing, it might 
be preferable to scrap the ship early or find another solution. The OR-model does not make 
considerations like this. 
5. Discussion 
In the 2018 OECD report “Decarbonising the Maritime Transport”, three main recommendations were 
stated as being crucial in achieving a zero-emission future in maritime transport (OECD, 2018): 
• 
Set a clear, ambitious emissions-reduction target to drive decarbonization of maritime transport. 
• 
Support the realization of emissions-reduction targets with a comprehensive set of policy 
measures. 
• 
Provide smart financial incentives to advance the decarbonization of maritime shipping. 
The FuelEU proposal from the EU and the CII requirements from IMO separately set clear targets as stated 
in point 1 and 2 above. FuelEU directly sets financial incentives with the penalty of non-compliance. 
However, the different targets and requirements of the two regulations result in conflicting incentives for 
ship-owners. 
The most significant difference in the FuelEU proposal and the current regulation for CII is the lack of well-
to-tank requirements of the fuels for CII. Using methanol clearly shows this difference. While it according 
to the FuelEU GHG intensity calculation is even worse to use LNG-based methanol than the traditional 
fossil fuels, it has no effect on the CII performance whether a ship-owner uses green e-methanol or an 
LNG-based methanol. This incentivizes ship-owners to use the green methanol in the EU only. Outside of 
the EU, the price of the methanol type will have the largest incentive factor. Methanol is mostly produced 
from fossil fuels today; either LNG or coal, which is considerably cheaper. There is a risk that the grey 
methanol will dominate the market outside of the EU, unless the incentive structure is changed. The WtT 
issue is discussed in the IMO, and several alternatives for a new GHG fuel standard are proposed by the 
member countries (International Maritime Organization, 2022c).   
Figure 5 illustrated that green e-methanol meets the requirements for FuelEU GHG intensity until 2040 
and possibly a few years more depending on the surplus banking of the ship. For CII performance, 
however, Figure 7 illustrated that many sample ships would be on the limits of the requirements in 2030 
if converting to methanol even with all investigated compliance options installed.  
The two regulations present two main dilemmas: the type of methanol is crucial only for FuelEU, and the 
period of compliance when converting to methanol is vastly different for the two regulations. A new-built 
ship today probably sails for 25 years within the requirements inside the EU if sailing on green e-methanol, 
but it only meets the CII requirements for approximately 10 years.

5.2 Alternative solutions 
As previously mentioned, the OR-model does not consider the situation where a ship is at the end of its 
lifetime and an expensive compliance option is required to meet the CII requirements in the next-coming 
years. In this situation, possibly none of the solutions presented in this paper are relevant. Potential other 
solutions are solutions such as network optimization or early scrapping. Operations research has a 
potential in network optimization to reduce the distance sailed or the sailing speed of a given ship or a 
fleet of ships. The solution is free in technical installation costs but could have an effect on revenue 
streams if routes are changed. Network optimization solutions are continuously being developed by the 
industry.  
The second solution is to scrap a ship early. An example of this could be the ship M. It is built in 2005 and 
therefore has an expected lifetime until the end of 2030. In 2030, the prediction is that it has been in 
category D for three consecutive years unless it convert to methanol completely. However, as it does not 
sail in the EU, methanol consumption is not required. In this situation, it might be advantageous to scrap 
the ship 1-2 years early instead of going through the full retrofitting process of the ship. Nonetheless, the 
scenario of early scrapping requires more investigation. Finally, e-diesel or any other drop-in fuel that 
does not require retrofitting could be an alternative solution for this kind of. 
For the long-term solutions, other alternative zero-emission fuels like ammonia and hydrogen should be 
considered soon. The FuelEU regulation gives the opportunity to pool ships, which makes it easier for ship-
owners to try different technologies and different fuels. If pooling two ships together and one of them 
performs well while the other does not, compliance can still be reached.  
Conclusion 
This paper uses the conclusions in the paper from Schroer et al. (2022) as a starting point for further 
analysis. Schroer et al. investigated the reduction potential in fuel consumption using a series of technical 
and operational compliance options. This paper uses the research behind the reduction potentials of the 
compliance options, the corresponding calculations and the corresponding costs of installation. 
Specifically, this paper expands the analysis to examine a larger ship sample size, it considers the 
implications of the FuelEU regulation with methanol as a possible solution, and finally it considers how to 
optimally combine methanol consumption with the compliance options investigated by Schroer et al. to 
meet the short-term requirements set by the IMO. 
Green e-methanol is good solution to comply with the FuelEU regulation. If applying green e-methanol, a 
ship directly complies with the regulation until 2040. The possibility to bank any surplus from the previous 
years extends the period for about 5-7 years. However, this is the best possible scenario where the 
production of the methanol is assumed carbon neutral. A scenario with methanol produced from LNG 
performs worse than the traditional fossil fuels in the GHG intensity calculation of the FuelEU proposal. 
Thus, the type of methanol used is crucial.

With the current regulation for CII, it is relatively hard to meet the requirements towards 2030 with the 
investigated compliance options and the use of methanol. Several of the sample ships are on the limit of 
compliance in 2030. For the ships that are expected to continue sailing in the years after 2030, this would 
require other solutions.  
Generally, the most expensive compliance options are methanol consumption, installation of a WHRS and 
the use of EPL. Increasing the use of methanol to meet CII requirements is generally not cost-effective.  
For sample ship B used for demonstration purposes, the analysis shows that increasing the methanol 
consumption is very expensive at any time. The methanol consumption for ship B is therefore based on 
the methanol quantity required by the FuelEU. Figure 6 shows that to comply with the FuelEU 
requirements, 37% of the energy in the main engine should be based on green e-methanol from 2030 to 
2035 for ship B. This number is similar for the rest of the sample ships. For CII compliance, though, many 
of the ships require full methanol consumption in the main engine to comply in 2030, even if other 
expensive compliance options are installed. This makes the solutions very expensive for many sample 
ships. 
The IMO acknowledges that it is difficult to comply with the CII regulation by using methanol. Currently, 
the type of methanol used on-board does not affect the CII performance. This is in sharp contrast to the 
incentives imposed by the FuelEU proposal, where ship-owners are incentivized to use green methanol 
and not fossil-based methanol. The clear difference in the incentive structures of the two regulations 
results from the FuelEU GHG intensity calculation considering the WtT emissions while the CII calculation 
only considers the combustion phase. To acknowledge the problem, IMO soon adopts a change in the 
regulation that decreases the CO2 conversion factor for combusting green methanol from 1.4 g CO2 / g of 
green methanol to 0.0 g CO2 / g green methanol – i.e. green methanol will be considered a zero-emission 
fuel like ammonia and hydrogen. This adjustment significantly changes the possibility for the sample ships 
to comply with the CII regulation. Without the adjustment, many sample ships are on the limit of 
compliance in 2030, but with the adjustment all the sample ships easily comply. An optimization model 
quickly finds the best solution in these different scenarios. 
Assuming that the investigated compliance options have the same reduction potentials for methanol 
consumption as they have for the traditional fuels, an optimization model helps finding the optimal and 
cheapest way of combining compliance options with methanol consumption for each sample ship. The 
model in the analysis minimizes the total cost of compliance options installations, maintenance costs, 
operational costs and fuel cost in the analyzed time-span 2019-2030 for each example ship. The advantage 
of the model is that it finds the best solution for each example ship quickly. The main limitation is that it 
relies entirely on correct input data for reduction potentials and costs. To improve the model, more 
research into the entirety of the costs of installing compliance options on the ships is required. 
Installations of compliance options or including the necessary retrofitting to burn methanol is expensive. 
For ships that perform on the limit of the CII requirements and only have a few years left in their life-time, 
low-investment solutions should only be considered. An example could be using bio-fuels as drop-in fuels 
that do not require any significant retrofitting. A purely operational measure as network optimization is 
also a potential solution. The more drastic measure of early scrapping is also an option that deserves 
consideration.
