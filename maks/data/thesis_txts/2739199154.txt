Abstract
This thesis aims to bridge the gap between emission inventories and impact assessment mod­
els for sparingly soluble metal compounds in freshwater ecosystems, with a focus on en­
hancing the accuracy of environmental impact assessments. The research addresses the
limitations of traditional methodologies that aggregate metal forms, which can lead to over­
estimations of toxicity due to the assumption that all metal forms are geoavailable. Metal
oxides are chosen as relevant emissions with low geoavailability and the study employs a sys­
tematic approach to calculate the factors regarding their fate (Fate Factor­FF) and exposure
(Bioavailability Factor­BF) in freshwater based on their equilibrium and kinetic dissolution. The
suggested methodology was tested on oxides of copper, nickel, and zinc and their speciation
was simulated using PHREEQC across 7 European freshwater archetypes. The key findings
reveal that accounting for specific forms of metal emissions does not significantly alter speci­
ation or estimated factors in equilibrium conditions. On the other hand, the factors calculated
in the kinetic base are considered more realistic and strongly dependent on the dissolution
extent (expressed as Geoavailability Factor­GF), where particle size and kinetic constant play
a major role. The potential impacts are accounted as Comparative Toxicity Potential (CTP),
the ratio of kinetic­based to equilibrium­based one has a positive linear correlation with the
dissolution extent. Ultimately, this study shows the importance of broadening the dissolution
studies to environmentally relevant concentrations and media for metal substances with en­
vironmental significance. This will increase the applicability and robustness of the results of
the methodology established in this thesis so that they can be used in Life Cycle Impact As­
sessment (LCIA) models, such as USEtox, and encourage more detailed and consistent metal
emission reporting in Life Cycle Inventory (LCI).

Introduction
1.1
LCA and metals
The Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a systematic analysis used to quantify the potential en­
vironmental and health impacts of products and services during the entire life cycle and its
data can be separated into two phases. The Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) phase determines the
resource and emissions flows for every process associated with the life cycle of a product or
service. The Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) phase links those flows to the potential
environmental impacts. [1, 2, 3]
In LCIA, the ecotoxicological impacts of a chemical emission are estimated with compound­
specific Characterization Factors (CF), also known as Comparative Toxicity Potentials (CTP)
[2, 3]. CTP of a chemical substance in freshwater is calculated as a product of the Fate Factor
(FF), which represents the residence time of the substance in the freshwater environment,
the Exposure Factor (XF), which is its time and space integrated exposure to organisms in
freshwater, and the Effect Factor (EF), that reflects its ecotoxicity, expressed as a potentially
affected fraction (PAF) of freshwater species [1]. This is incorporated into USEtox, which is a
steady­state fate model that provides the possibility of developing consistent and compatible
estimation of characterization factors for thousands of substances (both organic and inorganic)
and complies with the recommendations from UNEP­SETAC’s Life Cycle Initiative regarding
the characterization of toxic impacts in Life Cycle Assessment [2, 4].
Metals are highly relevant compounds due to their high persistence, bioaccumulation and tox­
icity (PBT). These characteristics enable the trophic transfer of these compounds in aquatic
and terrestrial food chains introducing important hazards for wildlife and human health. En­
vironmentally relevant metals (and metalloids) of concern depending on their abundance and
potential toxicity in organisms include Cr, Ni, Cu, Zn, Cd, Pb, Hg, and As [5, 6]. Thus, based
on the current LCIA methods, metals are often placed among the most toxic compounds [7].
However, the developed methods are based on non­polar organic substances and neglect the
different behavior of metal compounds.
Compared to the organic substances, which are studied to a greater extent, the metal ones do
not degrade, so the conventional measures of persistence used for organic substances do not
apply. Additionally, contrary to most organic emissions, metals are released to nature in sev­
eral physical­chemical states [7]. Furthermore, they can be found in very high concentrations
due to naturally occurring processes, such as mineral weathering and volcanic eruptions, and
anthropogenic ones including various industrial and agricultural activities. Hence, their expo­
sure depends on both the emission rate and the background levels, which can vary significantly
across different geographical areas and environmental media. Another interesting character­
istic is that some metals (such as manganese, iron, copper, and zinc) are essential to life in
limited concentrations but can be poisonous in excess. At the same time, their uptake and
toxicity are highly influenced by their speciation which depends on the ambient chemistry and
the emission form [3].
Metal speciation refers to the distribution of a metal over different forms. This can occur through
adsorption and desorption, ion exchange, precipitation and complexation of organic and inor­
ganic ligands. The speciation of metal compounds depends on their specific properties, which
are highly linked with the metal form, their concentrations and the water chemistry, such as
the pH, redox potential and the presence of abiotic ligands. [8]. Speciation influences metal
fate, bioavailability and toxicity depending on the environmental conditions. Initially, when this

To mitigate this issue, the total metal amount contained in the compound should be divided

1.2
Mineral dissolution
The geoavailability reflects the extent of its dissolution of the metal compound, also addressed
as the mineral, which is influenced both by thermodynamics, in equilibrium base, and kinetics.
Equilibrium dissolution. Minerals in water can either dissolve or precipitate based on their
solubility, which is dictated by the equilibrium between the solid phase (mineral) and its dis­
solved ions in water, expressed as equilibrium constant, K. This property is influenced by the
nature of the compound and environmental parameters. The most significant environmental
parameter affecting the equilibrium constant is temperature. According to the van’t Hoff equa­
tion, the change in the equilibrium constant with temperature depends on the enthalpy change.
Generally, the solubility of most minerals increases with temperature (endothermic reaction),
while that of gases decreases (exothermic reaction). Additionally, the ionic strength, which
depends on the ions in the solution, can alter the apparent equilibrium constant. Higher ionic
strength typically reduces the activity of dissolved ions, which can decrease the solubility of
minerals. [11]
When the ion activity product (IAP) of the dissolved species in water equals the K, the system
is at equilibrium, meaning no net dissolution or precipitation occurs. The Saturation Index (SI)
is usually used to assess the state of mineral equilibrium. It is defined as SI = log(IAP/K).
• If SI > 0, the water is supersaturated with respect to the mineral, favoring precipitation.
• If SI < 0, the water is undersaturated, favoring dissolution.
• SI = 0 indicates equilibrium.
Kinetic dissolution.The dissolution of minerals is a complex chemical reaction that consists
of a series of parallel and sequential elementary reactions. [11, 12] The main sequence of re­
actions includes mass transport of solutes (H+, OH−, ligands) to the mineral surface, surface

1.3
Research questions
Acknowledging that dissolution, both in equilibrium and kinetic level is highly related to the
compound­specific properties, the metal generic toxicity potentials, that are currently used,
create an inevitable gap between emission inventories and impact assessment results in LCA
and other emission­based chemicals assessment frameworks. Consequently, the aforemen­
tioned statements and the Clearwater Concensus’ recommendations lead to forming the fol­
lowing research question:
How can the metal compounds be included in the current impact assessment models, to bring
LCI and LCIA together?
To answer this question, we break down the work into the following sub­questions:
• Which compounds should be primarily included in the study?
• Which time horizon should be considered for the dissolution process? Is equilibrium
representative or kinetics should be considered?
• How determining the specific form of the metal emission can influence impact factor

2
Methods
To accomplish the goal of the thesis, two cases were assessed; the first one refers to the
inclusion of a metal compound considering its equilibrium dissolution while the second one ac­
knowledges its kinetic dissolution for a relevant timeframe. The work is performed according
to the following steps (summarized in Figure 2.1). At the beginning, a systematic LCI database
overview is performed for the selected metals. This guides to a relevant compound selection
for this study as it showcases the compounds with low geoavailability that are currently reported
and not properly assessed with currently used LCIA methods. At the same time, one can find
the limitations that the current metal emission reporting is facing. The next step includes the
selection of speciation software which should contain the selected compound dissolution and
precipitation alongside dynamic simulations. Its performance is tested by comparing its results
with other software to ensure the robustness of its results. Then, the equilibrium dissolution of
the selected metal compounds is simulated based on thermodynamic data so that their specia­
tion patterns are assessed to be included in the proposed LCIA framework. Besides, the kinetic
dissolution of the selected metal compounds is simulated based on kinetic data collection and
harmonization. Their speciation patterns are assessed for a relevant time horizon in order to be
included in the proposed LCIA framework. Additionally, modifications to the current framework
for metals were proposed in order to calculate compound­specific CTPs. Those modifications
are divided into the equilibrium­based impact factors and the kinetic­based ones that are time­
integrated. Ultimately, sensitivity analysis was performed on the relevant parameters for both
cases. The purpose of this analysis is to assess the variability of the speciation results on the
calculated factors based on relevant environmental scenarios.

2.1
Metal compound selection
For illustrative purposes, the focus of this study is on emissions that include copper, nickel
and zinc. A systematic LCI database overview was performed to select the relevant group of
compounds to assess in this study. The starting point of this overview is a chemical list of all
compounds that contain the aforementioned metals (Ni, Cu, Zn) in their canonical SMILES for­
mula. This list is constructed based on the extracted data from the U.S. Environmental Protec­
tion Agency (US­EPA) and European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) databases. Their identifiers,
CAS number, names and formulas, are used to cross­check which compounds are included
in the elementary flows reported in the most common LCI databases (Ecoinvent, Simapro,
LCA for Experts databases). The matching compounds were presented in Table A.1. It is
important to mention that only inorganic mass­measured emissions were accounted for. The
elementary flows containing metal form were analyzed from Simapro (9.4.0.3), LCA for Experts
(10.7.1.28) and Ecoinvent (3.10) database. For consistency, previous versions of Ecoinvent
were also checked (2.2­3.9.1). As seen in the Appendix A.1.1, metal ions prevail at the report­
ing in the LCI inventories, followed by salts (e.g. chlorides, sulfates), oxide and the elemental
form alongside the aggregated forms (e.g. Zinc compounds). Comparing different databases,
the inconsistency in the reporting is highly noticeable between even metals and the focus is
mainly on the total ionic form of the metal, for which LCIA methods already exist to assess it,
such as USEtox 2.1 [4, 19] which accounts only for the ions Zinc(II), Copper(II) and Nickel(II).
The criteria for compound selection are based on their environmental relevance, which is cap­
tured by the emission reporting, but also on their limited solubility, as the proposed methodol­
ogy aims to represent metal compounds that are considered sparingly soluble and are over­
looked with the current methods. The definition of sparingly soluble compounds reflects the
significantly less release of the metal cations or metal­bearing species into solution and their
solubilities are low enough that a solubility product can be calculated [20]. In this view, the
metal oxides are the most relevant forms to study in this project as they are considered to
be insoluble/sparingly soluble based on the standards testing schemes and they are already
reported in inventories. Nickel oxide (NiO) is not reported but is highly relevant, e.g. from
combustion processes and incineration plants [21]. Its lack of inclusion in the LCI databases
may be a result of the already­mentioned inconsistencies.
2.2
Speciation modelling
Speciation of metals in natural waters has a crucial role in their impact factors estimation as
described in section 1.1. Therefore, the focus of this section is the selection of a software
and thermodynamic database that can properly simulate the selected metal oxide speciation
in freshwater conditions.
2.2.1
Software selection
Speciation is modeled in PHREEQCI 3.7.3­15968 [22] and not WHAM 7 [23] or MINEQL+
[24] as suggested in similar studies [2, 3, 25]. This software is used for simulating chemi­
cal reactions and transport processes in natural or contaminated water. Its advantage over
the other two programs reflects the fact that it includes multiple thermodynamic databases,
that can be chosen to achieve a better representation of the simulated system, and it can
model non­equilibrium reactions including kinetic reactions. Additionally, oxidation­reduction
reactions are considered for systems in which multiple oxidation states of the same element
coexist (eg Cu(I), Cu(II)) as well as surface reactions. It can, also, be used for speciation
modeling when the possibility of mineral dissolution or precipitation needs to be known due to
its feature for the simulation of mineral equilibrium. However, some relevant limitations of the
software include the poor representation of organic carbon and the inadequacy of specifying
ionic concentrations in solution chemistry (only total amounts of the elements can be set).

2.2.2
Thermodynamic database and modification
The thermodynamic data file (TDF) chosen for the thesis is the ”minteq.v4.dat”, which is de­
rived from MINTEQA2 version 4 (EPA­approved database) and uses mostly the Davies equa­
tion. It includes around 1990 species and specializes in metals [22]. It operates well between
temperatures 0.01 to 100 °C, pressure 1 bar and ionic strength less than 1 M [26]. These
characteristics and its consideration of major and minor river species make the database ca­
pable of properly representing metal speciation in freshwater. Another important factor that
was taken into account for this selection was that it includes the desired mineral phases (metal
oxides) as well.
As described previously, most of the thermodynamic databases used in PHREEQC including
minteq.v4, lack of data for organic carbon binding. To overcome this drawback of the software,
surface species of colloidal humic substances ­HS (humic acids – HA and fulvic acids ­ FA)
were used to modify the database. Model VII (WHAM 7.0), which describes cation­HS com­
plexation, was implemented as presented in Tipping et al [23]. The coupling of Model VII to
PHREEQC was achieved as described in Marsac et al publications [27, 28]. This modification
is applied to simulate the binding sites of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and the particulate
one that is a part of the total suspended solids (TSS). The latter ones are assumed to contain
binding sites in metal surfaces (MeOHx) as well. Iron (FeOHx), manganese (MnOHx) and
aluminum (AlOHx) surfaces were taken into account based on the parameters used in WHAM
7 [23]. In PHREEQC databases there are some binding constants for FeOOH, however in or­
der to have similar results with the already used methods, the addition of WHAM species was
decided. A detailed description of database modification is presented in the Appendix A.1.2.
2.2.3
Archetype simulation
The speciation of the metal emissions is studied for the 7 freshwater EU Archetypes, as they
have been described in [2]. The water chemistry is presented in the Appendix A.1.2 (Table A.2,
A.3 and A.6). Additionally, since PHREEQC software (and its thermodynamic databases) ac­
knowledges redox reactions, expressed as pE (pE=Eh/0.05916) should be considered as a
parameter for each archetype. The redox potential (Eh) in rivers and lakes fluctuates between
0 and 0.7 V [29]. Its value is chosen based on the trophic level of each archetype, defined by
the DOC concentration; in eutrophic waters, the environment is more reducing due to organic
matter oxidization while in oligotrophic waters the conditions are oxidative, and the system is
close to equilibrium with atmospheric O2 [30]. Details on this assumption are included in the
Appendix A.1.2.
The water parameters (pH, pE, Temperature and major/minor species) are modeled as ”SOLU­
TION” for each archetype, while DOC and TSS are modeled as ”SURFACE” and its sorption to
different aquatic species is considered to be a surface reaction. The simulation of total metal
emission is initially performed, as studied in [2, 3], to assess the robustness of the model
compared with published results and different speciation software (WHAM 7).

2.4.2
Simulation of kinetic dissolution
For the simulation, it is assumed that the speciation with the water elements is occurring fast
compared with the oxide dissolution [7, 32]. Therefore, the dynamic simulation is used only for
the dissolution part, while for the rest of the speciation analysis, the equilibrium one is chosen.
The kinetic system for the metal oxide dissolution and precipitation is modeled in PHREEQC
software using thermodynamic data from the modified minteq.v4.dat (e.g., equilibrium con­
stants and activity coefficient models) and kinetic parameters. The program allows the user
to describe the rate equations (“RATES”) and define the input parameters. Then, by using
the specified water chemistry (“SOLUTION”, “SURFACES” and fixed values for pH and pE)
for each archetype, one can add the input parameters for each system (“KINETICS”). Those
were selected to be the SSA0 and kobs, additionally, the time duration, the timestep as well as
the initial metal oxide concentration (background concentration, Table 2.1) are specified. The
existing databases only contain limited rate equations, and they did not include the compounds
of interest, so this modeling was based on literature values, and it was implemented in easy­
to­use formats. The value of the rate constant is chosen to be archetype­specific and how it
is derived is explained implicitly in paragraph 2.4.3. SSA0 was calculated for each compound
for 5 particle sizes (10 nm, 100 nm, 1 μm, 10 μm, 100 μm) in order to assess a relevant range
for the emitted compounds in the environment [33, 34, 35].
The setup of the model was tested with experimental values to assess its accuracy and validate
its results. The medium composition as described in [32] was simulated in PHREEQC based on
its ion composition. Some organic ligands used (eg POPSO buffer) could not be represented
accurately. The average dissolution of 5 experiments was accounted for. The results of the
model validation are presented in Appendix A.1.5.
2.4.3

For the research kinetic dissolution of metal oxides, data regarding the kinetic constant are

experiments in freshwater conditions are scarce and show high variability. Therefore, system­

can fit in the Equation 2.2.
Data collection. To have a consistent dataset for the estimation of the rate constants, data

data were gathered regarding the particle size, Me release/rate and Me concentration at the
equilibration, as well as experiment data such as initial MeO concentration and study duration.

2.5
Proposed framework for impact factor calculation
This study aims to examine if specifying the compound in the emission inventories would be
essential for the CTP, in the fate (FF) and the exposure (BF) phase. The effect (EF) phase
is assumed not to be influenced by the emission compound since the free ion/truly dissolved
metal is considered to be the most toxic form, as already assumed [2, 3]. To achieve that,
archetype­specific factors and partition coefficients were calculated based on the results of
the speciation model. Partition coefficients refer to KpSS (L/kg), which is the partition coeffi­
cient between particulate and dissolved phases, and KDOC (L/kg), which is the partition co­
efficient between colloidal and dissolved phases. KpSS, KDOC and BF are derived directly
from the speciation calculations, while FF is calculated by using USEtox 2.1. To achieve that,
archetype­specific KpSS, KDOC and DOC concentrations replaced default values in USEtox.
Apart from these three parameters, all other parameter values are kept at their default values
in the USEtox main database and inorganic database for FF calculation.

Results
This chapter presents the results regarding the two cases when considering the equilibrium
and kinetic dissolution of the metal oxides in freshwater. The focus is on how the dissolution
influences the impact factor calculation in each case.
3.1
Equilibrium base results
The robustness of the speciation results was checked by using WHAM 7, as speciation soft­
ware, with the same input parameters (both water chemistry and metal amount are consid­
ered as total concentrations) to compare the calculated factors. The BFs in the two models
show good alignment. The main discrepancies are spotted for copper with a deviation of 20­
40%. Those can be explained by the lack of including Copper(I) species in WHAM alongside
differences in thermodynamic databases and calculation methods used in the software. In
PHREEQC, the binding constants used and BF calculation, only refer to Copper(II); Copper(I)
is considered not bioavailable as it speciates quickly and bound particles [38]. A similar im­
age is also shown for partition coefficients, where the main differences can be explained by
the estimations that need to be made to modify minteq.v4 database so that surface binding is
included in a comparable way with WHAM. For those metals in the chosen concentration, no
precipitation is expected while reaching equilibrium. The visual representation of the software
comparison is presented in Appendix A.1.3.

4
Discussion
4.1
Applicability
This study provides methodological steps to include characterization factors in impact assess­
ment methods that are compound­specific for metal emissions and not broad for total metal
forms. The key consideration is the inclusion of their dissolution, in equilibrium or kinetic base.
Copper, nickel and zinc are selected to assess the methodology developed to include metal
emissions at the compound level in LCIA framework. The common ground of those metals,
apart from their environmental relevance, is that they all belong to the d­block of the periodic
table, and are divalent (+2) metals and transition metals. Despite their similarities, compounds
containing each of them have unique properties regarding their solubility, size, possible oxida­
tion states and dissolution kinetics. This can generalize the methodology to be used to other
metals that belong to this category and possibly different ones, such as post­transition metals
(Pb, Cd). However, a mechanistic validation of this assumption is recommended before the
adoption of the methodology.
Furthermore, the focus of the study is on compounds that have limited solubility and are cur­
rently reported in LCI databases. In emission inventories, inorganic metal compounds are
mostly repressed as salts (e.g. chlorides, sulfates), oxides and elemental forms. It is consid­
ered that the high solubility of the salts (such as sulfates, chlorides, and nitrates), makes them
readily soluble and available (near instantaneous and complete dissolution) in the ionic form
to speciate [40], while the elemental forms are probably total metal form, that may not belong
to the elemental one but to various forms not specified explicitly. Thus, this study develops
a methodology for the metal oxides which are considered sparingly soluble, sometimes even
characterized as insoluble, and undergo non­oxidative dissolution. This selection delimits the
scope of the thesis for relevant emission compounds, in which the dissolution mechanism is
extensively studied. In this project metal oxides could be considered proxies of compounds
with similar dissolution mechanisms, as the considered kinetic equation represents the general
rate law for mineral dissolution/precipitation reactions [11].
Considering their equilibrium dissolution, the selected compounds in their background con­
centration do not substantially influence the speciation patterns compared to the total metal.
This can explain why the equilibrium speciation of metal oxides does not differentiate from the
current practice. This behavior should be tested separately for other metals/metal compounds
that are not studied in the thesis. A similar approach and explanation would be considered
on the fact that the speciation of the selected compounds, based on equilibrium and kinetic
dissolution, does not lead to precipitation of any metal mineral.
Kinetic­based factors should be preferred over equilibrium­based factors as they more accu­
rately represent the speciation within realistic timeframes. Equilibrium scenarios often depict
a worst­case scenario that might span multiple years, which is not representative of the actual
residence time in freshwater systems. Our findings indicate that both the FF and BF decrease
with the increase in particle size and decrease of rate constant (kobs). Regarding the latter,
copper and zinc oxides have dissolution rates of the same magnitude, while nickel oxide is
expected to have much slower dissolution. This aligns well with the relative order of reactivity
at a constant pH CuO ∼ZnO > MnO > CoO > NiO for the first block of transition metal ox­
ides. For those metals, the rates are expected to be controlled by short­range forces and the
order of reactivity (Cu+2 > Zn+2 > Mn+2 > Co+2 > Ni+2 for transition metals) [41]. The rates
of dissolution of the simple oxides and rate constant of water exchange rate, because of the

similarities of the average bond distances and coordination numbers in the simple oxides and
aquated ions [12, 41].
For the studied metal oxides, the archetype differentiation, which is based on their pH values,
does not have a big influence due to the high data variability which leads to an increased
uncertainty in kobs estimation. This can also be explained by the fact that the pH influence is
expected to be crucial in highly acidic (pH <5) and basic environments (pH> 8) [15]. On the
other hand, the importance of reporting particle size in metal emission studies is underscored,
as has already been suggested [20]. It can lead to a factor decrease over a factor of 10 in
some cases.

Comparison with literature
The comparison between equilibrium base factors of total metal and metal oxides and kinetic
base ones has already been discussed in Chapter 3. Thus, here, it was considered a more
straightforward comparison between the factors which are calculated in the current study for
the total metal in the equilibrium base and the ones published in Y.Dong et al [2] and currently
used in USEtox. The latter uses WHAM 7 for the speciation calculations, while in this study,
PHREEQC is used. Comparing FF and BF with the published results of Y.Dong in Figure 4.1
showcases the possible incompatibilities of speciation modeling choices to the established
method.

them with the published values, some minor differences were observed (Table A.10) due to
rounding errors and updates in the USEtox model after the paper’s publication.
BF values show larger discrepancies and underestimation in Figure 4.1. Zinc values vary be­
tween 8­80%, nickel 20­80% and copper 30­100%. The values for copper and Archetype 7
appear to have the highest misalignment. Some of those differences can be explained be­
cause of the software differences as described in Section 3.1. However, the main ones come
from a difference in water chemistry simulation. It appears that in the framework described
in Y.Dong’s paper, the concentrations of the water components (Ca+2, Mg+2, Na+, etc) are
considered as free ion concentrations and not as total (as confirmed in Figure A.4, A.5). This

4.3
Limitations
The study faced several limitations that could impact the accuracy and applicability of the
methodology. One significant limitation is the software constraints, particularly regarding water
chemistry and its incapability to include its major/minor species as free ions, as used in the
current framework with WHAM 7. Additionally, the speciation results are highly dependent on
the thermodynamic database used and its equilibrium constants which do not account for the
particle size dependence, potentially skewing the equilibrium dissolution predictions.
Regarding the kinetic dissolution modeling, studies on metal oxide dissolution in freshwater
metals are scarce both regarding the mechanism description and data collection leading to as­
sumptions and uncertainties. The kinetic equation employed also has limitations, often based
on idealized assumptions, thoroughly discussed in Appendix A.1.5, that may not hold in real­
world conditions. For example, the surface area of a solid was calculated based on a smooth,
geometric surface of an average grain; in reality, the grains have pits and irregularities that
increase the surface area [11]. Additionally, the data available for the study was limited, with
most existing data relevant only to specific conditions, often referring to nanoparticles, and
typically involving high initial concentrations not representative of environmental scenarios.
Those limitations are summarized below:
• Initial concentrations. A large number of relevant studies refer to the dissolution much
higher than the environmentally relevant ones, higher than 1 mg/L, which usually leads to
MSc Thesis
27
==PAGE:38==
mixture saturation and incomplete saturation. This may cause both mechanistic and nu­
merical errors when extrapolating them for the desired concentrations. Studies show that
the dissolution kinetic rate constants increase with decreased initial MeO concentrations
and show less dependency on the water chemistry [43].
• Particle size. The plethora of the studies included is representative of low particle size
in the nanoscale (higher surface area), which may lead to an overestimation of the rate
constants.
• pH. The dataset for zinc oxide is underrepresented for pH<7.2 leading to higher uncer­
tainties in the calculation of the kobs at archetypes EU 5, 6 and 7.
• Nickel oxide. For NiO more dissolution studies for environmentally relevant media need
to be found. Since only one is included, with 6 data points, those results should be
interpreted with precaution.
4.4
Future research
To address the limitations identified and improve future studies, several recommendations are
proposed. A more extensive kinetic dataset should be developed through a comprehensive
database and literature research, along with more relevant laboratory tests. it is essential
to increase the dissolution studies using relevant concentrations and particle sizes in order
to increase the accuracy of the fate and exposure models. Although this task is beyond the
scope of the current thesis, it is crucial for improving accuracy.
Some further suggestions to improve the link between emissions and LCIA methods in future
research projects can include the following:
• Further studies should investigate the potential influence of considering specified factors
for each compound to EF, which is considered dependent only on the ionic toxic form
since dietary uptake is neglected.
• The assumption that readily soluble compounds can be aggregated with total metal
should be further tested in order to be validated.
• The methodology should be extended to calculate dissolution rates for more metals and
metal forms that undergo non­oxidative dissolution, such as insoluble salts and carbon­
ates.
• A similar methodology should be constructed for oxidative dissolution processes, includ­
ing sulfides and elemental forms of metals.
• Suggesting how metal compound­specific characterization factors for the other environ­
mental compartments (soil, sediment, air, marine water) can be included in LCIA meth­
ods.
This will broaden the applicability of the framework, enhance its robustness and lead to a more
systematic emission reporting instead of aggregated total metal forms.
4.5
Recommendations
Summarizing the findings of the study, the current chapter ends with recommendations regard­
ing the potential utilization of the proposed methodology. The use of this methodology, when
its results represent more accurately each metal compound, is intended to calculate BF, KDOC
and KpSS offline and include them in USEtox for estimating the freshwater CTP of each com­
pound separately. The kinetic­base impact factors should be preferred and adjusted based
on the time horizon of freshwater residence time used in USEtox (139 days) as presented in

Figure 3.10. Additionally, as mentioned before, the constraint imposed for KDOC is based on
organic compounds. In the case of metal compounds, this parameter is calculated offline for
metals and not the way it is estimated for organic ones, it could be removed in future itera­
tions. Furthermore, it is recommended to refine the water chemistry representation of each
archetype in PHREEQC.To achieve that, the water chemistry is suggested to be described

way to shortly arrive at this result would be to calculate this value by WHAM, however, this

their particle size. This would assist in estimating the compound amount that is emitted in each
representative freshwater archetype and ultimately extrapolate the archetype­specific impact

5
Conclusion
This thesis attempts to bridge the gap between emission inventories and impact assessment
models in freshwater ecosystems by introducing a methodology that includes metal com­
pounds with limited dissolution (low geoavailability). The oxides are chosen as the focus of this
study, as relevant representatives of sparingly soluble compounds, and the 3 metals, copper,
nickel and zinc, were used to test the framework. The influence of the emission specification
was assessed on the Fate and Bioavailability Factor (FF and BF) in equilibrium and kinetic
base in comparison to the traditional LCIA approach addresses the impact of total metal forms,
which assumes that all of it is geoavailable and potentially overestimates their potential toxicity.
Archetype­specific impact factors are calculated after simulation in PHREEQC and are repre­
sentative of European freshwater. The findings underscore that in equilibrium, for the selected
compounds, accounting for the specific forms of metal emissions does not lead to a signifi­
cant change in speciation and the estimated factors. However, this depicts an infinite timeline
which is not realistic of the actual residence time in freshwater systems. Thus, the consider­
ation of kinetic base factors is proposed and it is highlighted how the compound dissolution
kinetics influences their metal availability and its potential for accumulation in aquatic life. The
particle size of the emissions is a parameter that shows substantial influence for all the studied
compounds. The higher the size the lower the geoavailability (limited extent of dissolution).
This effect is combined with the kinetic constant (kobs) which depends on the nature of the
compound and the surrounding environment. This parameter, due to data limitations, shows
high uncertainty and should be further studied to be included in the LCIA models.
A positive linear correlation was found between the kinetic­base toxicity potential (CTPT ) and
the equilibrium­base one (that has GF=1) and geoavailability fraction. When the dissolution
extent is higher (due to smaller particle size and/or higher rate constant), the values for ki­
netic and equilibrium tend to be closer, while lower dissolution results in a broader CTP ratio,
indicating more varied kinetic behavior. This suggests that the general linear fit may not be
applicable when the dissolution rate constant falls below a certain range, highlighting the need
to include more metal compounds to evaluate the validity of this regression.
Looking to the future, research should aim to test the methodology, considering relevant kinetic
studies, to a wider variety of metal and metal forms that can fit into the suggested methodol­
ogy. This could involve, also, developing methods for studying oxidative dissolution processes,
including sulfides and elemental forms of metals. Such efforts would not only broaden the ap­
plicability of the framework established in this thesis but also strengthen its reliability, leading to
more systematic and accurate reporting of emissions that are compound­specific and include
particle size data.
Ultimately, incorporating precise dissolution data into environmental assessments is essential
for promoting realistic sustainable practices and guiding effective policy decisions regarding
metal compounds. By enhancing our understanding of how specific metal forms behave in
freshwater ecosystems, we can better predict their environmental impacts and develop tar­
geted strategies for preventing and addressing pollution. This research serves as a founda­
tion for a more comprehensive approach to environmental impact assessments, highlighting
the need for ongoing investigation and refinement in the study of metal compounds in aquatic
environments.

The coupling of Model VII to PHREEQC was achieved as described in Marsac et al [27, 28].
The model is a discrete binding site model which takes electrostatic interactions into account.
Only the aqueous cations (Mn+) and their first hydrolysis products (MOH(n−1)+) are considered
to bind to HS with the same binding constants [28]. Model VII does not consider the binding
of Na+ to HS sites unlike Ca+2 or Mg+2, which complexes with HS in solution. However, Na+
is considered to control indirectly the complexation as it influences the ionic strength and the
electrostatic effects [27]. Cation­HS (Humic Substances) complexation (including H+) in Model
VII follows the following reaction Equation A.1.
Mn+ + HS ⇀
↽HSM (n−1)+
(A.1)
Generic parameters for a wide range of metal ions and humic substances were determined
by fitting experimental datasets. These generic parameters capture “average” behavior. Lin­
ear free energy relationships (LFER) are commonly used to determine the cation­HS binding
parameters for the different metals based on experimental data. For the modeling default con­
stants are used with are more internally consistent and are derived from the average estimates
of the binding parameters. [23]
Eight sites are considered and divided into an equal number of type A sites (carboxylic groups)
and type B sites (phenolic groups). Proton binding is described by the two median intrinsic

A.1.5
Kinetic model
Kinetic mechanism
The kinetic dissolution of MeO is described based on the mechanism presented in [32] for
ZnO nanoparticles. The surface reactions of MeO dissolution include proton­promoted and
ligand­promoted dissolution reactions and the precipitation of free metal ions (Me+2) back to
the solid phase Figure A.6.
The assumptions considered for the kinetic model are summarized below:
• Dissolution kinetics was controlled by reactions at the surface rather than the transport
of ions away from or to the surface as expected for sparingly soluble compounds.
• Complexation of dissolved Me+2 by inorganic and organic ligands (e.g., OH−, Cl−, and
functional groups in NOM) is fast compared to the dissolution reactions on the MeO
surface.
• All surfaces of MeO particles were available for dissolution
• The reaction of MeO reprecipitation is first order with respect to [Me+2].
• The particles are a spherical shape so that the surface area of MeO decreased by a
power factor of 2/3 in relation to the mass of the undissolved ZnO NPs. [32]
• Only dissolution occurs without aggregation. Agglomeration of some metals is mostly
observed in small/nanoparticle sizes and for increased concentrations of solid materials
[20, 45]. Αggregation is usually relevant at Ionic strength higher than 10 mM (in my case
IS is 0.6­9mM). Overall, in freshwater conditions (high NOM and low IS) the aggregation
can be considered negligible since high stability of particles is expected [32, 45].
