Summary (English)
The goal of the thesis is to quantify the ﬁdelity of simulated wind resources.
Machine learning approaches will be used to predict the uncertainty with the
goal to create uncertainty maps over large ares. If such a map can be gener-
ated it can be great addition to wind atlas data sets. In addition, if the error
can be predicted, the modelled wind can be corrected and therefore no further
downscaling needed to gain better estimates of win resources. Furthermore an
attempt will be made to gain a physical understanding of the machine learning
behaviour.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Wind resource assessment is one of the most, if not the most, important step
in wind farm development. The wind resource describes the wind conditions
at the site of interest and varies between sites due to diﬀerences in terrain and
roughness etc. Wind farm developers rely heavily on the wind resource estimates
for the choice of turbines and the power they will produce[20]. Making the wrong
choice can have severe consequences e.g. if the wind resource is over estimated
with only 1%, one can expect a loss of 2% in power production. Furthermore, a
wrong turbine choice could eﬀect the maintenance cost and decrease the lifetime
of the turbine.
Ideally, one would want to measure over a very long period, at multiple locations
on the site[12]. Doing so will result in having experienced all possible variations
of the wind and thus, have a almost perfect wind resource estimate. This is
however not practical and measurements are not always available. Therefore,
researchers need to rely on Mesoscale modelling. Mesoscale models are high res-
olution numerical weather prediction (NWP) models and are widely used within
the wind energy industry to predict wind resources. The wind ﬁeld is predicted
on a gridded area, referred to as the model domain, with a typical grid spacing
larger than one kilometer. These simulated gridded wind ﬁelds will however, not
agree to what is measured at a point at the same site, mainly because the model
grid points cover the mean wind ﬁeld over several km while the observation takes
place in one point. For this reason, the models will not predict the smaller scale

variations caused by orography and roughness variation etc. Therefore, further
postprocessing is needed to account for these eﬀects. An example of such a
method is described in Badger et al.[2] where the mesoscale model outputs are
postprocessed with a statistical–dynamical downscaling method, referred to as
generalization. In the generalization process, all topography eﬀects are removed
from the mesoscale outputs which allows them to be used as input to higher
resolution ﬂow models to introduce the microscale features. The generalization
is important so that the topography eﬀects are not accounted for twice.
Because that the wind energy industry relies on reliable wind resource estimates
for turbine siting, it is of great importance to investigate the causes of the
diﬀerences of modeled and observed winds with the goal to be able to quantify
and predict likely size of the diﬀerences. This could be achieved by developing
a machine learning approach to ﬁnd patterns between modelled and measured
wind based on topography information on diﬀerent resolution.
1.1
Methodology
To be able to ﬁnd patterns between errors in simulated wind resources and the
actual, a lot of data is required. The US National Weather Service’s (NWS)
Automated Surface Observation Systems (ASOS) is a dense network of mete-
orological stations distributed across the USA. The stations measure surface
climate, at 10 m a.g.l, and serve as the primary source for surface climate moni-
toring in USA. The ASOS network currently consists of more than 900 stations
and the data is publicly available. The ASOS data will be used to compare with
wind resource simulations(at 10 m a.g.l) over the eastern USA in attempt to
predict the simulation error. The simulations have already been created using
the Weather Research and Forecasting model (WRF). .
Elevation map with global coverage, extracted from the Global Wind Atlas
(GWA), will be used to extract elevation maps of the ASOS sites. Land use
(LU) classiﬁcation models, created by the European Space Associations (ESA),
will be used to convert LU classes to roughness length. From the extracted
elevation maps and LU models, parameters to describe the site topography will
be derived and used to train a machine learning model to map the parameters
to the simulation error. In the training process, diﬀerent approaches will be
tested in attempt to obtain reasonable results.
If the cause of the simulation error can be found and the magnitude quantiﬁed,
a further downscaling approach might not be needed.

resolution uncertainty map could be generated as an addition to wind atlas

Modelling
A good, ideally perfect, estimate is crucial when it comes to wind farm plan-
ning. If the resource is under estimated, a potential site for a wind farm could
be overlooked and if over estimated, developers could suﬀer a great ﬁnancial
loss. Wind measurements are not always available when it comes to estimat-
ing the wind resource at a given site. Therefore, researchers must use diﬀerent
approaches to give an estimate of the wind resource. Numerical Weather Predic-
tion (NWP) models are widely used for such a task. NWP’s solve mathematical
models of the atmosphere to predict the climate, i.e. the governing equations
of ﬂow dynamics[20]. The model solves The Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes
equations (RANS). Due to the chaotic behaviour of the atmosphere the equa-
tions cannot be solved exactly and will introduce errors. A benchmarking study
quantifying the skill of mesoscale models for wind climate assessment has been
described in Olsen et al[16].
The Global Wind Atlas (GWA) is a tool, collection of datasets and methodology,
developed at DTU Wind Energy[1]. The main purpose is that the user has access
to estimated high resolution wind resources all over the globe. The GWA uses
large-scale global reanalysis data sets that are generalized before introduced to
microscale eﬀects through the Wind Atlas Analysis and Application Program
(WAsP), also developed at DTU Wind Energy. The generalization process is

important in order to remove eﬀects of topography in the reanalysis data set so
that they are not accounted for again when introduced to the microscale ﬂow
modelling.
2.1
Weather Research and Forecasting
Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) Model is a type of NWP model that
produces mesoscale atmospheric simulations.
Mesoscale models have higher
resolution than planetary scale general circulation models (GCMs) and smaller
resolution than microscale or local scale[19]. A typical size for mesoscale grid
cells is 1 km - 10 km while microscale range from few meters up to 1 km[20].
Mesoscale models are considered suﬃcient for weather forecasting and are used
by weather services and institutions around the world for forecasting and climate
research. However, to obtain high resolution, mesoscale models require coupling
with microscale ﬂow models, e.g through Statistical–Dynamical Downscaling as
described by Badger et al[2].
In addition to solving the RANS equations, mesoscale models describe atmo-
spheric thermal processes in great details[20]. This allows the model to capture
large scale eﬀects such as gap ﬂow, channeling and sea breeze etc. Gap ﬂow are
low-level winds that occur when the wind is pushed through gaps in the terrain
e.g when two mountains meet or at the opening of a valley. When the wind ﬂows
through a valley, it is forced to ﬂow with the axis of the valley. This ﬂow eﬀect
is known as channeling. Sea breezes occur at coastlines or at large lakes. Water
has a higher heat capacity than the land surface and takes therefore longer time
to heat up and cool down. The diﬀerence in temperature forces the wind to ﬂow
from the water and in over the land surface or vice versa.

2.2
Microscale Modelling
Microscale modelling is ﬂow modelling with grid resolution from a few meters
up to 1 km, typically[20][12]. Microscale models have smaller grid spacing than
mesoscale, and thus resolve ﬁner features of the topography. The higher res-
olution allows for modelling of the local ﬂow eﬀects, e.g. orographic speed-up
and roughness change.
The Wind Atlas Analysis and Application Program
(WAsP) is a software developed at DTU Wind Energy. WAsP is a linearised
ﬂow model, meaning that the model uses a linear approximation of the Navier-
Stokes equations[12]. It performs well in attached ﬂow cases, in simple (i.e. not
complex) terrain. As a measure of the orographic complexity of the site, WAsP
uses the so called Ruggedness Index (RIX). The RIX is deﬁned as the fraction of
the terrain that is steeper than a critical slope, WAsP default 0.3 or 17◦[15]. As
mentioned, the WASP linearized ﬂow model is limited in its validity to attached
ﬂow and thus, any ﬂow separation will have a great impact on prediction error.
Therefore, it is recommended to run the WAsP model on sites that have very
low RIX values, ≈0 representing very simple terrains.
2.3
Topography
Good description of the topography is important to achieve accurate results
from ﬂow modeling. Topography is referred to as the whole description of sur-
face properties, elevation, roughness length, obstacles etc.[20]. In wind energy
terminology, topography is usually divided into two categories, orography and
surface roughness[20][1][12]. Orography describes the surface height, or eleva-
tion, variation and has a direct inﬂuence on the ﬂow through speed-ups over hills
and ridges and channeling etc. Sudden changes in orography, e.g steep hills may
cause ﬂow separation. The second eﬀect is the surface roughness length (z0).

2.3.2
Roughness
To estimate the roughness length on microscale, two land use data sets are
considered, Globcover2009 (GC) and ESACCI (LC), both developed by the Eu-
ropean Space Agency (ESA). The Globcover data set was created by converting
Medium Resolution Imaging Spectrometer (MERIS) Surface Reﬂectance (SR)
data into 22 land cover classes. The land cover classes are deﬁned in accor-
dance to the United Nations Land Cover Classiﬁcation System (LCCS). The
GLobcover data is provided with global coverage at 300m resolution (available
at, http://due.esrin.esa.int/page_globcover.php).
The second model,
ESACCI, is a result of ESA’s Climate Change Initiative (CCI) Land Cover
project. As with the Globcover model, the ESACCI uses MERIS to estimate
the 22 LCCS land cover classes. In addition, the ESACCI model uses regional
information, if available, for more detailed surface description. This allows the

Furthermore, the ESACCI provides the land cover data as stable and as time

Chapter 3
Wind Resource Assessment
Measurements
The best way to estimate wind resources is through measurements, so called
measurement campaign. Ideally, one would want to measure over a very long
period at multiple locations to capture all possible variations of the wind at
the site of interest[12][13]. This, of course, is not considered practical when a
measurement campaign has a deadline and a budget. To overcome this prob-
lem, researchers have used modelling, as discussed in Chapter2, or nearby met
stations, if available, to estimate the long term variability. Researchers in the
ﬁeld of wind energy rely heavily on good wind resource estimations. Therefore,
guidelines have been developed to ensure high quality measurements. The Mea-
suring Network of Wind Energy Institutes, called MEASNET, is a collaboration
of some of the most experienced institutes in the ﬁeld of wind energy[14]. The
objective of MEASNET is to provide guidelines to ensure that the institutes
perform measurements of equal quality. According to MEASNET[14], all wind
speed measurements are to be performed in accordance with IEC 61400-12-1[9].
Wind measurements shall be made by high quality anemometers, calibrated by
approved institutes, at a height at least 2/3 of the height of interest, wind turbine
hub height for wind farm planning. Furthermore, according to MEASNET[14],
measurements are considered incomplete if measurement period does not cover
at least 12 consecutive months with data availability of less than 90%.

Preparing a Machine
Learning approach
Instead of using the experience and human decision making, parameters describ-
ing the site in term of its microscale and mesoscale properties will be introduced
to Machine Learning. The term Machine Learning describes a group of statisti-
cal algorithms that can be applied to large sets of data and learn from it[7]. A
learning algorithm uses the information from the data provided to it, to generate
a mathematical model that are used to predict outcomes of new data.
Machine Learning can be broadly classiﬁed into two classes, unsupervised and
supervised learning[7]. Unsupervised learning algorithms aim to identify struc-
tures in the data without connecting it to a response or a result. Unsupervised
learning is widely used for clustering. Supervised learning algorithms aim to
identify patterns between input variables, referred to as predictors, to a desired
outcome, response. There is a wide range of tasks that Learning algorithms can
be applied for. However, only two of the most common tasks will be covered,
regression and classiﬁcation.
Regression and classiﬁcation models are very similar in many cases. The main
diﬀerence is that of which form the desired response is, numerical or categorical.
Regression models predict a continuous response by relating one or more input
predictors to a desired response. A regression could e.g. be predicting a cars
==PAGE:38==
32
Preparing a Machine Learning approach
fuel economy, knowing its weight, model, engine size etc. Classiﬁcation models
However, predict a discrete response based on the input predictors. an example
of a classiﬁcation is e.g. predicting if a person is a man or a woman, based on
height and weight etc.
5.0.1
Decision Trees
Classiﬁcation and Regression Trees (CART), typically referred to as decision
trees, are supervised learning algorithms that can be used to solve both clas-
siﬁcation and regression problems[5].
Decision trees are relatively simple to
interpret and visualize and can handle both numerical and categorical inputs.
Decision trees can be thought of as trees that are upside down i.e the root is at
the top, the starting node of the tree. The tree grows downwards by creating
branches that split on internal nodes, as the tree grows, and ﬁnally making a
prediction at a so called leaf node. For each split, the learner uses the pre-
dictor, from the input predictors, that gives the best estimate to the desired
response. The tree continues to grow until a certain stopping criteria is reached,
e.g for classiﬁcation problems, when the prediction class stops varying or when
the prediction error stops decreasing for regression problems. In addition, some
stopping criteria can be user deﬁned, as will be discussed further in Chapter 6.
When a tree has been fully grown, it can be introduced to previously unseen
data to predict the outcome. Because of their ﬂexibility, decision trees are used
in a variety of applications and are among the most used machine learning al-
gorithms, e.g Microsoft Kinetics uses decision tree based algorithms to detect
body parts and movements to control their Xbox game console[6].
Although decision trees are fairly easy to work with, they do have some compli-
cations. As is stated in Hastie et al.[8], "trees have one aspect that prevents them
from being the ideal tool for predictive learning, namely inaccuracy". Decision
trees have the tendency to over-ﬁt data when allowed to grow to their deepest
possible level, particularly if the data is limited and/or noisy, which is normally
the case of the real world. In the case of over-ﬁtting, the decision tree can make
near to perfect predictions for the training data but very poor predictions for
previously unseen data. Over ﬁtted tree performs as if it remembers the data
it was build with, one can imagine that the tree is actually ﬁtting to the noise
in the data rather then the patterns. Many methods have been developed to
prevent over-ﬁtting and will be discussed further in Chapter6 in the process of
building the models.

5.1.1
Terrain Ruggedness as a input to ML
As discussed in 2.2, all of the sites are located at or near an airport and are
therefore in relatively ﬂat terrains thus, the ruggedness index RIX = 0 for most
of the sites and RIX ≈0 for the others. To have some description of the site
complexity another approach is used.
The method is proposed by Riley et
al.[18], where the orthographic complexity is calculated as the sum change in
elevation between a grid cell and its eight neighbours. This method, referred
to as the Terrain Ruggedness Index (TRI), provides information on the relative
elevation change at the sites and is calculated as
TRI =
qX
(xi,j −x0,0)2
where x0,0 is the grid cell to evaluate and xi,j is the elevation of each neigh-
bouring cell. The results are then averaged over a desired area to get the total
TRI. According to Riley et al.[18], the TRI levels can be classiﬁed into following
classes.
• 0-80 m level terrain
• 81-116 m, nearly level

• 162-239 m, intermediately rugged
• 240-497 m, moderately rugged

In the case of relatively ﬂat terrains, the value of RIX = 0 as has been discussed.

The TRI however, as can be observed in Tables A.1 and A.2, always gives a
value greater than 0 and thus, some measure of complexity that can be useful
as an input to the ML approach, even though the values all fall within the ﬁrst
two classes, level terrain and nearly level.

From the roughness maps explained in Chapter2.3.2, some statistics are calcu-
lated. The mean roughness length over the area is calculated for the site as
well as the corresponding roughness variation. Furthermore, a calculation of
roughness for a distance of 500 m from the station is calculated at microscale,
here referred to as the point roughness. The point roughness at mesoscale is
extracted from the model grid description as the cell LU index and converted
to roughness length.
5.1.3
Speed up as a input to ML
Although that the sites are all considered to be in relatively ﬂat terrain and
orographic speed-ups of the wind are not likely to occur, a method is proposed
to introduce a measure to the ML model. To estimate if the station is on a hill
or not, the second derivative is derived from the microscale elevation map with
distance of 500m from the station. If the second derivative is a positive value,
the station is located at a point relatively higher than the surroundings. As can
be observed in Table A.1 and A.2, the speed-up values ,presented as isHill, even
if positive are extremely low with the highest positive and negative values of
the order of 10−4. The low values indicate and support the observation that all
stations are located in a very ﬂat terrain.

When the models have been trained, one will need to evaluate the performance.

In classiﬁcation problems, using a confusion matrix (CM) to evaluate the model

on regression, it will be used as a measure of performance of the models. The
output predictions are divided into 10 equally sized bins of 20% from 0%-200%,
to represent the error classes in the CM.
6.2
Shallow Trees
At ﬁrst, the DT algorithm will be restricted to grow shallow trees. This is done
to get an understanding of how the algorithm learns from the data used for
training. Furthermore an attempt will be made to connect the learning process
to the physical understanding of the wind. Figures 6.2 and 6.3 present the DT
and the corresponding predictions based on the WRF12 and WRF4 respectively.
Both DTs are trained with only one predictor and the restriction of performing
only one split at the root note. The predictor used is the microscale area mean
roughness length. Both models predict that a lower roughness length results in
lower simulation errors. According to Kelly et al.[10], this behaviour could be
expected due to roughness uncertainties which have higher eﬀect on simulated
winds at sites with higher roughness.

So far, the DT seems to be making decisions consistent with an understanding
of the situation. However, allowing only one split, results in only two results, a
high or low uncertainty estimate. Therefore the next step of the process is to
present a new predictor to the ML for training and this time allow three nodes
to split. The second predictor is the mesoscale point roughness. The mesoscale
point roughness was chosen whereas it is assumed to be the one most related to
the microscale mean roughness, i.e representing the same area.
Observing the tree trained on the WRF12 data, in ﬁgure 6.4(a), one can notice
that the DT algorithm does indeed use mesoscale roughness to predict. The
mesoscale roughness however, is only used to split one node, on the left where
there are lower microscale roughness values. The highest predicted error is from
the left-left branch.
This node represents low microscale and low mesoscale
roughness. At ﬁrst this might seem diﬃcult to understand but if one takes a
look at the root, the microscale split values are much higher than the mesoscale.
Therefore one can assume that the left-left branch has much lower roughness
values than the left-right branch which is in the direction of the microscale. If
looking at the values in Table A.2, the lowest microscale mean roughness value
is 0.068 m which supports that assumption. On the right site of the root, for
high microscale values, the DT makes a decision to make another split for even
higher values. The DT makes the same prediction as in the previous model, for
one split only, for lower microscale roughness, the errors are lower.
On the other hand, for the DT trained on the WRF4 data in Figure6.5, the
mesoscale roughness appears on the right branch where the higher microscale
roughness is. The split for the mesoscale happens at a higher value than for the
previous model and again the prediction might seem odd at ﬁrst, whereas the
right-left branch predicts higher errors than the right-right branch. However,
if the values in Table A.1 are observed, the highest mesoscale roughness value
that is less than 0.55 m is 0.1 m. The right-left branch has thus, much higher
diﬀerence between the microscale and mesoscale values. Still the DT seems to be
making decisions which are fairly easy to interpret but is still not very accurate
because, the DT can only predict four results.
Until this stage, the DT’s have been controlled by the user. The predictors have
been chosen and the DT’s are only allowed to grow to a certain level. The next
DT models build, will also have restrictions on the depth but the algorithm
will have full control over the choice of predictors, with some limitations. The
predictors presented to the DT’s are

6.3
Building complexity of the trees
Now when some understanding of the DT behaviour has been established, it
is time to grow the DT models to the deepest possible level. The predictors
introduced to the DTs are the same as in the previous step and the all restrictions
on model complexity are removed. The DT algorithm uses the data to gain as
much information as possible, resulting in complex tree structures. The model
PIs and predictions over actual are presented in Figures6.10 and 6.11 for the
WRF12 and WRF4 respectively. This time, the tree structure is not presented
because, it simply does not ﬁt on a page and would be to hard to read because
of the complexity. One might also notice that the CM is not presented either
and is because of the model performance as will be discussed.
Ideally a DT performs at its best when grown to its deepest possible level. To
achieve that however, requires a very large data set. Data sets that are large
enough are not always available as is the case here. Furthermore the data is
assumed to be very noisy due to rough estimates of e.g the roughness values.
As was discussed in Chapter5.0.1, this can result in the tree over-ﬁtting to the
training data or the noise in it, when over complex, and the model becomes
unstable when introduced to new data.
When observing the predictions in
Figures6.16(b) and 6.17(b), it is not very clear at ﬁrst that the model is unstable
and one might assume that the model is just producing more outcomes and
seems to be converging towards the 1:1 line. However, keeping in mind that
the DT is expected to be most accurate when at its deepest level, one could
expect that, if more data would be available for testing, that the predictions
would behave similar and scatter all over. To test this, the model is introduced
to the training data and is presented in Figure6.12 for WRF4 only. The model
produces nearly perfect predictions on the training data, indicating that the
model is indeed over-ﬁtted.
As has been discussed, DTs are popular for ML problems because of their ﬂex-
ibility and relatively easy interpretation and implementation.
For DTs, and
other ML algorithm, there are several ways to prevent over-ﬁtting e.g restrict-
ing the tree from growing beyond a certain level and or trimming a complex
tree, called pruning, to a certain desired level. Some of the most used methods
will be discussed in the following subsections.
6.3.1

are pruned to a certain level and thereby reducing the model complexity. The

6.3.2
Cross Validation
When partitioning the data into training and testing data sets, one would want
as many data points as possible in each set. When not having enough data, this
introduces a trait oﬀbetween the two bins, whereas it is desired to have enough
data points in each bin that are statistically representative for the whole data set.
Finding the best split of the data, can be a problematic and a time consuming
process by trial and error. A method called Cross Validation performs such a
task[11]. Cross validation is a powerful tool when it comes to reducing the risk
of over-ﬁtting, when enough data is available. Cross validation allows the use
of the whole data set for both training and validation.
A popular cross validation method is the k-fold cross validation[8]. Figure6.13
shows how the k-fold method is performed. The data is split into k number
of bins, of equal size, which are then used to train k number of learners using
k-1 bins for training. Predictions are then made on the bin that was not used
in the training process. This method can help reducing the risk of over-ﬁtting
whereas, ideally, one or more of the k trained models will not over-ﬁt and can
therefor be used for predicting response from unseen data. A so called Leave
one out cross validation (LOOCV) is a special type of k-fold cross validation,
where the k = n, n being the number of data points. When using the k-fold
method, it can be diﬃcult to ﬁnd the best value of k because the higher the
value the longer time it takes to train the model. Typically, in practice, k = 10
is considered suﬃcient.
6.3.3
Random Forest
Random forests (RF), as the name implies, are models that consist of multiple
DTs[4]. RF is a machine learning ensemble algorithm which improves stability
and reduces variance for higher accuracy. What makes the RF diﬀer from other
DT algorithms, is that it uses makes random decisions when growing the trees.
Random forests select random subsets from the training data and as a result,
random forests prevent the model to over-ﬁt the training data. One popular

The bagged RF algorithm takes a training data of size N as an input[3]. Samples

sample from the original training set may occur more than once in the new
training set. This method of selecting data is often referred to as bootstrap.
Furthermore, instead of using all the predictor variables to decide on how to
split a node, the random forest selects a random set of predictors which results
in a variety of trees. Each tree is grown to its largest with no pruning. When
introduced to new data, the random forest makes the decision by aggregating
the predictions from all the trees. For classiﬁcation problem, the predicted class
is the on that gets the most votes from the RF and for regression the ﬁnal
prediction is the average of all the predictions in the RF. One disadvantage of
random forests is that the user has very little control of the algorithm and that
it can appear as a black box when it comes to analyzing.

Chapter 7
Uncertainty Map
Theoretically, the predictive model constructed in Chapter5 can be applied to
any site in the world. As long as data from simulated wind resources is available,
the model can be used to predict the model errors in each grid cell. Applying
this feature to the whole, or a part of, model domain results in uncertainty maps
describing the predicted model error in each grid cell
7.1
Method
To generate the uncertainty maps, the information for each grid cell needs to be
provided to the model. The method follows the same methodology as described
in details in Chapter5.
There are however, some diﬀerences in the order of
preparation. Now, the simulation domain controls which sites are chosen, i.e.
For each grid cell, a corresponding site is chosen from the land use and elevation
maps so that the sites will represent the same area as the model domain. The
site parameters are estimated and a table is created with all the predictors. The
table is then used as an input to the predictive model.

Chapter 8
Discussion
The predictive models constructed and evaluated in Chapter6, seem to have
reached a limit of how accurate the predictions can be, based on the data used.
For the research, only the mean wind speeds of each station for the year 2008.
Only having the mean wind speed over one year is not considered good in
practice, because it probably is not climatologically representative for the sites
due to annual variability. The simulated wind resource is based on reanalysis
data that is considered to be representative, and therefore ﬁnding connection
and patterns between the simulated and measured wind is very diﬃcult with
only one year.
Furthermore, the roughness length values derived, are very coarse estimation
and only 14 categories were introduced with 12 unique values. No detailed site
inspection was carried out. It is thought that if the roughness values were to be
described in more detail, the model might perform better. Also, no information
was given on the dominating wind direction. If such information would have
been provided the roughness could have been weighed diﬀerently according to
the direction. For further research on this topic, one might want to investigate
the the winds in sectors to gain more detailed information on the directions and
wind speed distribution. Also, investigating the time series could be useful to
gain information about the variation and atmospheric stability etc.
This research was limited to constructing predictive models based on decision

trees. As was discussed, the trees diﬀer from being inaccurate if data is limited

However, even though not perfect, the models constructed in this report are
capable of making predictions. More detailed research on this problem could
result in models that are comparable to microscale ﬂow models, or at least give

Chapter 9
Conclusions
Machine learning methods can be used to predict uncertainty of simulated wind
resources. Even though the models constructed here need much more research
they did show better performance in each step. The model performance of the
RF models is listed in Tables 6.2 and 6.3 where one can see how the models
evolve. Furthermore, the ML approach can be used to make a correction on the
simulated wind. To evaluate the model performance, a confusion matrix was
used. As was discussed in Chapter6, the confusion matrix (CM) is developed
for classiﬁcation problems and the results of this research supports that CMs
are not suitable as a performance measure for regression problems whereas some
good predictions are misclassiﬁed as poor.
A physical understanding can be interpreted from the DTs, at least until they get
too complex to follow. That is where the predictor importance comes in, even
though it gives no information on how each predictor was used, it gives an idea of
how important it is for the predictions. For example, the microscale roughness
variation, in almost all cases, has the highest score on the PI and the other
roughness’s score high as well. This indicates, as expected, that the roughness
is important. In addition, the terrain ruggedness seems to be important as well,
also expected, thus, orography has direct inﬂuence on the wind ﬂow.
One of the parameters however, is diﬃcult to interpret, the elevation. When
introduced to the ﬁnal version of the RF, the elevation, for both micro and
