Abstract
A Locally Recoverable Code (LRC Code) is a type of error-correcting code, where each
codeword symbol can be recovered by accessing a small number of other codeword symbols.
This allows local recovery of erased codeword symbols in numerous applications, such as
distributed storage, where certain parts of the codeword may be unavailable.
In this thesis a detailed description of LRC codes is given, emphasizing the key parameters
length, dimension, locality and minimum distance. An upper bound on the minimum
distance of LRC codes is proven, detailing the proof provided by Tamo and Barg in [10].
Specific constructions of LRC codes are presented based on the work in [10] and of Barg,
Tamo and Vlǎduţ [2].
The LRC code family of [10] resembles classical Reed-Solomon
codes, and both a general construction of such optimal LRC codes as well as multiple
extensions are described in this thesis. Proofs of all parameters are included. In addition,
several new examples of specific codes obtained using the constructions are provided and
the local recovery process shown.
Likewise, the family of algebraic geometry LRC codes from [2] is also presented. Special
attention is given to codes constructed over the Hermitian curve, and several clarifications
and elaborations are given with respect to the original article. Examples of Hermitian
LRC codes are constructed to illustrate the different methods and properties described.

1 Introduction
We live in a world where deserted shopping centres and empty bank oﬀices does not stop
you from buying one more brand new dress that you will probably only wear once. Where
being ordered to self-isolate in a foreign country with all airplanes grounded does not mean
that you can not speak to your family whenever you want. Recent times have shown all
of us exactly how digitalized our society has become, and given an indication as to the
importance of digital data systems in the future. In a world where life does not come to
a hold even in the face of global lockdowns, it is clear that we are all increasingly reliant
on electronic means of communication and information. With this awareness comes an
appreciation of reliant ways of transmitting and storing data, along with an emphasis on
the necessity of robust data systems.
The reliability of data systems is exactly the issue addressed by error-correcting codes,
which constitute a way of processing data to avoid the data being compromised by errors
and erasures. It is a basic condition in all electronic information systems that parts of the
data may be altered by exposure to some sort of noise, or that parts of the system may
fail, thus causing a loss of data. In order to minimize or altogether exclude the destructive
effects of these circumstances, error-correcting codes work by cleverly adding redundancy
to the data. The additional information then allows the parts of the data affected by errors
or erasures to be reconstructed. The field of error correction in electronic communication
channels has been well-documented in literature since its introduction by Shannon in 1948
[7]. Error-correcting codes are often constructed by exploiting different algebraic or geo-
metric structures, and studying them thus requires some knowledge of abstract algebra.
At the present time, error-correcting codes are widely used in a broad range of applica-
tions, while improvements are ongoing as new technologies and requirements emerge.
The subject of interest of this thesis is a particular type of error-correcting codes called
Locally Recoverable Codes (LRC codes). These codes are able to recover lost parts of data
from just a small amount of available data. This is advantageous in applications such as
distributed storage, where e.g. power loss may lead to limited data access. If the missing
parts of the data can be reconstructed from the locally available data, system failures can
be avoided.
The aim of this project is to understand and convey the specifics of LRC codes, including
defining properties and limitations. In addition, some recent constructions of LRC codes
utilizing structures from algebra and geometry are closely examined. The families of LRC
codes considered in this thesis were introduced by Tamo and Barg in 2014 [10] and by
Barg, Tamo and Vlǎduţ in 2015 [2]. The presentation of the LRC codes provided in the
following is intentionally elaborate with the hope of clarifying certain details of the con-
structions, thus facilitating a better understanding of the different aspects of the codes.
A more explicit formulation of part of the construction in [2] is provided, allowing an
alternative method to be used to ultimatively improve the estimate of specific parameters

2 Background on Error
Correcting Codes
Error-correcting codes have countless applications in the fields of data transmission and
storage. The codes are implemented such that some degree of redundancy is added to the
original data prior to the data being stored or transmitted; a process called encoding. The
idea is that the additional information contained in the extra data allows parts of the data
to be reconstructed if lost or altered while in transmission or storage. Upon accessing or re-
ceiving the (possibly erroneous) encoded data, decoding of the received data is performed.
This process aims to reconstruct the encoded data by eliminating any errors. Since data
can not be completely protected from noise alterations or storage failures, error-correcting
codes are very important for the robustness of data systems.
In this section a brief introduction to error-correcting codes is provided. The codes consid-
ered in this thesis are all block codes, and the key parameters of such codes are presented.
Some widely used measures of the performance of error-correcting codes will also be intro-
duced before the family of Reed-Solomon codes is detailed.
2.1
Parameters and Performance
Block codes are a type of error-correcting codes, where blocks of data are encoded by
adding some redundancy. For a specific block code C, the size of the blocks that it is
capable of encoding is fixed. The number of data symbols in each such data block is called
the dimension k of the code, and is one of the defining parameters. The code is then
further defined by how much redundancy it introduces to each data block. The number
of data symbols resulting from encoding an entire block of data is called the length n ≥k
of the code.
The data symbols are assumed to belong to some finite alphabet Σ. Usually this alphabet
is a finite field Fq, where q is some prime power. A data block m ∈Σk is called a message,
and intuitively encoding of any distinct message m should result in a distinct data block
c ∈Σn called the codeword corresponding to m. Both the messages and codewords can
be seen as vectors with elements from Σ of length k and n respectively. This gives rise to
the following definition of a block code with dimension k and length n:
Definition 2.1.1 (Block Code). A block code C with length n and dimension k is the
image of an injective mapping Σk →Σn.
This means that a block code C can be understood as just a finite set of codewords of
length n. Any codeword can be written as c = (c1, ..., cn), where the symbol ci ∈Σ for
i = 1, ..., n is the i’th coordinate of the codeword c, and the set of coordinates of any
codeword of length n is simply denoted by [n] = {1, ..., n}. Because only certain elements
of Σn are recognized as codewords, it is possible to use the code to identify whether errors

have occurred.
A type of block codes of particular interest are linear block codes, which are codes where
the defining map is linear. Such codes are denoted (n, k) codes. An (n, k) block code C is a
k-dimensional subspace of Σn, which means that the all zero-vector is always a codeword
in a linear code, and that all codewords can be represented as linear combinations of at
most k other codewords. Moreover, any linear block code can be defined in terms of a
generator matrix. A generator matrix G is a k × n matrix, whose rows form a basis of
the k-dimensional vector space corresponding to the code. Encoding of a message m is
then done by computing the vector-matrix product c = mG. In this way the codeword c
corresponding to m is easily obtained as a linear combination of the k basis vectors of the
code [4].
Given an (n, k) block code C the restriction of C to a subset of coordinates I ⊂[n] is
denoted by CI and describes a set of partial codewords of C where only the symbols at
coordinates in I remain. A set of partial codewords CI itself forms a block code, and will
be referred to as a local code. The parameters of such a local code is (nI, kI), where kI ≤k
and nI ≤n. The relationship between the length and dimension of a block code C can be
quantified as follows:
Definition 2.1.2 (Code Rate). The rate R of a (n, k) block code is given by R = k
n.
If the code rate is high, the amount of redundancy is relatively low compared to the amount
of original data. Since the codeword can then be seen as more compact with respect to
the initial information, implementation of the code requires just a small increase in the
amount of data sent or transmitted. This is of course desirable in circumstances where
the space available for the data is limited.
At the same time introducing more redundancy can intuitively be used to reconstruct
larger parts of the original message since more information is then available. This makes
it clear that some trade-off between space and error-correction capability occurs. In order
to establish some measure of how many errors a code can correct, the minimum distance
of the code is very useful.
Definition 2.1.3 (Minimum Distance). The minimum distance d of a block code C is the
minimum number of positions on which any two distinct codewords c1, c2 ∈C differ.
For a linear block code the minimum distance can be reduced to the minimum number of
non-zero elements of any non-zero codeword [4].
Because the minimum distance quantifies how different any two codewords of a specific
code C can be, it makes sense that this can be used to examine if and what errors have
occurred when storing or transmitting a codeword. If the received data does not coincide
with any of the codewords of the code used, then at least one error must have occurred.
In order to identify and correct these errors, the concept of maximum likelihood (ML)
decoding is applied. This principle is used under the assumption that it is less likely for
a codeword symbol to be erroneous than for it to be correct. In other words that it is
most likely that most of the data has not been lost or altered. The idea is then that the
codeword most likely to be the one originally stored or transmitted, is the one that most
closely resembles the available data. As a result the code can correct errors only up to the
point where so many errors have occurred, that the data no longer resembles the correct
codeword the most, but instead resembles some other codeword more.
Because the difference between any two codewords is at least the minimum distance, it
follows that a block code C with minimum distance d can correct any t errors iff. t < d

The clear relationship between the minimum distance d and the error-correction capability

and dimension of a linear (n, k) block code C as follows:
d ≤n −k + 1.
(2.1)
Because the aim of error correcting codes generally is to be able to correct as many errors
as possible at the smallest possible cost (in terms of the amount of data), it is desirable to
create codes with minimum distance as close as possible to the Singleton-bound. Codes
that attain the Singleton-bound can therefore be seen as optimal with regards to being
able to correct the most errors using the least added redundancy.
Definition 2.1.4 (MDS Code). A maximum distance separable (MDS) code is a linear
block code C whose minimum distance d meets the Singleton-bound (2.1) with equality.

in 1960 [6] and have since then been widely used in numerous different applications. In
the following a brief description of the general construction of RS codes is given, serving
as a frame of reference for the codes described in Section 4.

3 Background on Locally
Recoverable Codes
Locally Recoverable Codes (LRC codes) are types of error-correcting codes, where each
codeword symbol can be understood as a function of a small number of other symbols
of the same codeword. This allows reconstruction of the codeword symbols from only
a small number of the other symbols, contrary to standard error-correcting codes where
many symbols generally must be taken into account when attempting to reconstruct the
codeword.
This property can be very useful in applications where not all parts of a
codeword are guaranteed to be available at the same time. In e.g. distributed storage
failures may occur in a single node of the storage system, while other parts remain intact.
Locally recoverable codes aim to be able to eﬀiciently recover the lost data by accessing
only a small number of the remaining nodes.
The codes work for erasure-type channels where some parts of the data are erased or in
other ways become unavailable. This means that it is known what part of the data has
failed and thus exactly what part of the codeword should be reconstructed.
Different
constructions of LRC codes allow flexibility with regards to the exact recovery process.
Some codes are constructed such that every codeword symbol can be recovered in multiple
ways. This means that the recovering process can be successful without being dependent
on the availability of specific symbols.
In this section some of the basic terminology related to LRC codes are introduced. Some
common generalizations of LRC codes are described before some useful bounds on the
performance of LRC codes are presented. In this context a detailed proof of a Singleton-
like bound for the minimum distance of LRC codes is given based on the proof provided
in [10], however certain necessary clarifications are included.
3.1
General Terminology
The LRC codes considered in this report are instances of the block codes described in
Section 2. However, for LRC code a specific property called the locality of the code is
particularly important.
Definition 3.1.1 (Locality). An LRC code C is said to have locality r if any symbol
ci, i ∈[n] of an arbitrary codeword c ∈C can be recovered using a set of at most r other
symbols of c.
A code of length n and dimension k with locality r is called an (n, k, r) LRC code. The
parameter r is called the locality parameter, and the r coordinates of the symbols needed
to recover the i’th symbol must not differ between the different codewords in the code.
Indeed, if the coordinates needed for recovery did differ, one would need to know the
codeword in order to correctly recover it, but then recovery in itself would be pointless as

7
==PAGE:16==
the codeword would already be known.
Definition 3.1.2 (Recovering Set). The specific set of coordinates of the at most r symbols
needed to successfully recover symbol ci is called the recovering set for ci and is denoted
Ii.
For some recovering set Ii and symbol a ∈Fq the subset of partial codewords in CIi where
the symbol a is the i’th coordinate of the codeword is denoted by CIi(i, a).
Lemma 3.1.1. Iff. there exist a recovering set Ii ⊂[n] \ {i} where |Ii| ≤r for all i ∈[n]
such that for two distinct symbols a and a′
CIi(i, a) ∩CIi(i, a′) = ∅,
then the code C has locality r.
Proof. When attempting to recover the symbol at coordinate i the information available
will be exactly one of the partial codewords in the set CIi. If any of the partial codewords
in CIi coincide for distinct symbols a and a′ (that is, the two sets CIi(i, a) and CIi(i, a′) are
not disjoint for a ̸= a′), then there is no way of distinguishing between these two symbols
in the recovery process, and the correct value of the i’th symbol can not be uniquely
determined. If, on the other hand, the intersection of CIi(i, a) and CIi(i, a′) is empty, then
the information contained in the recovering set Ii can only be in one of the sets, and so it
is possible to determine whether the symbol at coordinate i should be a or a′.
Clearly any error-correcting code with an error-correcting capability of at least one (or,
interchangeably, a minimum distance of at least 2) is also an LRC code with r ≤n −1
since any such code can recover the lost symbol by accessing at most all of the remaining
codeword symbols.
However, this bound is not very tight, and so further bounds are
established:
Lemma 3.1.2. For any linear LRC code, it holds that 1 ≤r ≤k.
Proof. The lower bound is clear since r = 0 would require the lost symbol to be recovered
from no information at all, which is obviously impossible - and the code would then not
be an LRC code.
That r ≤k can be seen as any (n, k) linear code C has a corresponding k × n generator
matrix whose rows form a basis for C. Since they form a basis, the rows of the generator
matrix must be linearly independent, and therefore the rank of the matrix is k. This
means that of the n columns only k are linearly independent. As a result any column can
be written as a linear combination of at most k other columns. Each column represents
a unique coordinate of the codewords of C, which means that if column i is a linear
combination of at most k other columns, then the i’th coordinate of any codeword is a
linear combination of at most k other coordinates.
This does not exclude the possibility of one coordinate being linearly independent from
all of the other coordinates, since the corresponding column would obviously still be a
linear combination of 0 < k other columns. However, the existence of such a column i
would imply the existence of a codeword with only one non-zero symbol, namely the one
at coordinate i. This again implies that the minimum distance of the code is 1, and so
the code would not be able to recover the i’th coordinate at all, and would therefore not
be an LRC code.
In all other cases the coordinates corresponding to the up to k columns in the linear
combination for coordinate i are exactly the recovering set Ii, and it then follows that the
i’th codeword symbol can be recovered using at most k other codeword symbols.

For a linear LRC code C the restrictions CIi∪{i} are local codes for all i ∈[n]. In the
general setting the recovering sets are assumed to be minimal, i.e. to contain only the
smallest number of coordinates required for successful recovery. A consequence of this is
that the error correction capability of each local code is just 1, and so the code is not able
to locally recover any two erasures if they happen to occur within the same recovery set.
LRC codes can be generalized to remedy such scenarios. This is done by requiring the
minimum distance of each local code CIi∪{i} to be ρ ≥2, thus allowing ρ −1 erasures
to be corrected within each recovering set. The size of the recovering sets is increased
proportionally, such that they each consist of at most r + ρ −1 coordinates. Thus, the
locality of the code is still r.
Definition 3.1.3 ((n, k, r, ρ) LRC Code). If for any codeword symbol ci, i ∈[n] of an
(n, k) block code C there exists a set Ii ∈[n] \ {i} of size r + ρ −1 such that the local code
CIi∪{i} has minimum distance at least ρ, then C is an (n, k, r, ρ) LRC code.
Clearly ρ = 2 reduces to the standard LRC codes capable of correcting just 1 error in
each recovering set. Beside the obvious advantages of being able to correct multiple era-
sures, (n, k, r, ρ) LRC codes also provide more flexibility with respect to exactly which
coordinates are required for recovery of a single symbol. This is the case since any r of
the r + ρ −1 coordinates of the recovering set can be used to recover all of the remaining
ρ −1 symbols.
Another noteworthy generalization of LRC codes are codes with multiple disjoint recover-
ing sets for each symbol.
Definition 3.1.4 (LRC(t) Code). If for any codeword symbol ci, i ∈[n] of an (n, k) block
code C, there exists t disjoint sets Ii,j ∈[n] \ {i}, j = 1, ..., t of sizes r1, ..., rt respectively,
such that ci is a function of the symbols in Ii,j then C is an (n, k, {r1, ..., rt}) LRC(t) code.
Obviously, multiple recovering sets allow each codeword symbol to be recovered in more
than one way, which means it may be possible to recover a symbol even though some or
all of the symbols in one of the recovering sets are unavailable. Multiple recovering sets
are also advantageous in ”hot data”; applications where multiple users frequently access
the same data. In this case each codeword symbol can be made available to the different
users by accessing separate parts of the remaining data.

In this section a family of LRC Codes resembling Reed-Solomon codes is described. The
construction was first presented by Tamo and Barg [10]. The main construction yields a
family of linear, optimal (n, k, r) LRC codes constructed over a finite field Fq, where q is
some prime power. This construction will be detailed before some illustrative examples

The main construction can be generalized in a number of ways presented in [10]. The
generalizations provide more flexible constructions, thus improving the applicability of the

modifications of the main construction to obtain both (n, k, r, ρ) LRC codes and LRC(t)
codes.

4.2
Generalizations of the Main Construction
The construction just described is seen to closely resemble the classical Reed-Solomon
codes. However, the code family can be extended by using a more general algebraic for-
mulation of the evaluation map as described in Section 4.2.1. Moreover, the construction
of LRC codes described so far relies on the parameters satisfying certain constraints. This
obviously limits the construction of different codes. It turns out that some of these re-
quirements can be relaxed, allowing for a more flexible code construction. In this section
only LRC codes with single recovering sets will be considered, and based on [10] a few ap-
proaches to loosening some of the constraints of the main construction will be introduced.

Orthogonal Partitions
An important part of the algebraic construction is that the partitions of the set of evalu-
ation points A are orthogonal. In this section a number of different requirements for the
existence of such orthogonal partitions are introduced.
Taking the coset partitions described i Section 4.1.1 as starting point, the most obvious
extension would be to use cosets of distinct subgroups of the field Fq to obtain two or
more different partitions. However, in order to ensure that the coset partitions generated
from any pair of subgroups of Fq are orthogonal, the following additional requirement is
necessary.
Lemma 4.3.2. The coset partitions of two arbitrary subgroups H, G ⊆Fq are orthogonal
if the intersection of H and G is trivial; H ∩G = e where e is the group identity element.
Proof. Assuming that H and G are the coset partitions generated by H and G, then any
two distinct elements x and y in the group over Fq can only be in the same cosets of H
and G if Hx = Hy and Gx = Gy. This implies that the group element xy−1 is also in
both subgroups H and G. Since x and y are distinct, x−1 ̸= y−1 and so the element
xy−1 is different from the group identity element e.
This means that the intersection
H ∩G contains at least two elements, namely the identity element which is always in any
subgroup as well as the element xy−1. From this it is seen that no two distinct elements
can be in the same coset of both H and G unless the intersection of these two subgroups
contains more than one element.
For additive groups of Fq subgroups intersecting trivially as described are easy to find,
since any additive group of a finite field can be seen as a direct product of smaller groups.
These smaller groups intersect only by the zero element, and therefore the coset partitions
generated will be orthogonal.
If the group is cyclic, the requirement stated in Lemma 4.3.2 is equivalent to the following:
Lemma 4.3.3. The coset partitions of two arbitrary subgroups H and G of a cyclic group
over Fq are orthogonal if gcd(|H|, |G|) = 1.
Proof. This can be seen since, as before, any two distinct elements x and y only belong
to the same coset in both partitions H and G if xy−1 ∈H ∩G. This means that xy−1 is
both a |H|’th root of unity and a |G|’th root of unity, which is only possible if the order of
xy−1 divides both |H| and |G|. Since xy−1 ̸= e the order of xy−1 is greater than 1, and so
gcd(|H|, |G|) > 1. Again the conclusion is that the two distinct elements x and y can not
be in the same coset in both partitions H and G, and the two requirements are therefore
identical.

5 Theoretical Background of
Algebraic Geometry Codes
Error-correcting codes constructed over algebraic curves are well-documented. These codes
are called algebraic geometry codes (AG codes), and result from evaluation of algebraic
functions in points of an algebraic curve. It is also possible to construct locally recoverable
AG codes, and a specific construction of such codes will be introduced in Section 6. In
the following the necessary theoretical terms are introduced. The theoretical background
is mainly based on Stichtenoth, 2009 [9], in particular the concepts presented in Chapter
1. Additionally, Appendix B of [9] details the relations between the general theory of
algebraic function fields and the specifics of algebraic curves.
5.1

The basis of all AG codes is the concept of algebraic curves.
In the following these

algebraic curves are detailed to provide a starting point for the construction of the codes
presented in this thesis.
Definition 5.1.1 (Aﬀine Algebraic Plane Curve). An aﬀine algebraic plane curve over

gC. In AG codes this property turns out to be highly relevant for estimation of the key
parameters of the codes. This means that by providing the value of the genus, or at least
a good estimate thereof, examination of the code parameters becomes possible.
For the two types of algebraic codes already introduced, the genus is well-known. The
projective line is an algebraic curve of genus 0, while the genus of the Hermitian curve is
q0(q0 −1)/2 [9, p. 230].
One way of estimating the genus of some curve C is to use Baker’s theorem which provides
an upper bound on gC [3]. Baker’s theorem relates the genus of the algebraic curve to
the number of interior integral points of the Newton polygon arising from the defining
polynomial of the curve in question. As will be seen shortly, this number can be computed
quite easily, thus providing an estimate of the genus of the curve.
Denoting the defining polynomial of some algebraic curve C by f(x, y) the Newton polygon
∆(f) is the convex hull of the set of pairs of exponents of x and y appearing in f(x, y).
Denoting the number of interior integral points of ∆(f) by N, Baker’s theorem states the
following bound on the genus of the curve:

Function Fields
For any algebraic curve C defined over the field K, a particular algebraic function field
can be associated. Since there is a correspondence between an algebraic curve and the
associated function field, the curve can be identified with and further examined through
the function field.
Definition 5.2.1 (Field of Functions on Algebraic Curves). Given some aﬀine alge-
braic plane curve C defined over the field K, the field of rational functions on C is de-
noted by K(C) and consists of all ratios p1(x, y)/p2(x, y), p2(x, y) ̸= 0, where p1, p2 ∈
K[x, y]/⟨f(x, y)⟩and f(x, y) is the defining polynomial of C.
The function field over C can also be written as K(C) = K(x, y), where both x and y
have to be transcendental over the field K and satisfy f(x, y) = 0. Both x and y can
be understood as functions C →P1(K), mapping points on the curve to the first and
second coordinate respectively. Counting with multiplicity, the degree of x is then the
number of points on the curve sharing the first coordinate, i.e. deg(x) = degy(f(x, y)),
while the degree of y is the number of points on the curve sharing the second coordinate
deg(y) = degx(f(x, y)).

n and consists of ratios of polynomials in y with degy ≤n and coeﬀicients in K(x). That

In this section a number of LRC codes constructed over algebraic curves are detailed. The
codes are algebraic geometry codes (AG codes, or Goppa codes [9]), and arise from the
same general construction. This construction was introduced by Barg, Tamo and Vlǎduţ
[2].
In the following the general construction is first presented, before the specific example of
LRC codes constructed over the Hermitian curve is addressed. A variety of such Hermitian
LRC codes is presented, and a number of examples are also included.
In particular,
explicit formulations not provided in [2] of Hermitian LRC codes capable of correcting
multiple erasures as well as Hermitian LRC codes with small locality are given, including
specified equations for the algebraic curves involved. Moreover, construction of LRC(2)
codes arising from the general construction is presented, again focusing on codes over the
Hermitian curve. The description of this type of code also benefits from the specified curve

6.1
General Construction
In the following a general method for constructing LRC codes over algebraic curves is pre-
sented. Just as the RS-codes presented in Section 4, the codes arising from the following
construction are evaluation codes. Again a particular encoding polynomial is attributed
to each possible message, and the corresponding codeword is then obtained by evaluation
of this polynomial in a number of evaluation points. For AG-codes these evaluation points
are points of an algebraic curve.
The foundation of the construction presented in [2] are two smooth projective algebraic
plane curves, X and Y , defined over a finite field Fq for some prime power q. The corre-
sponding function fields are denoted by Fq(X) and Fq(Y ). In addition, a rational, separable
map g : X →Y of degree r + 1 is needed. Because g is separable, the primitive element
theorem [5, p. 243] implies that a function x ∈Fq(X) exists such that Fq(X) is the simple
extension of Fq(Y ) by x. This means that Fq(Y ) ⊆Fq(X), and so any element in the
function field over Y can also be considered an element of the function field over X.
Notably, since g has degree r + 1 there are up to r + 1 points P ∈X for which g(P)
evaluates to some specific point P0 ∈Y . These sets of points on X for which g is constant
are called the fibers of g. Evaluation of any function f ∈Fq(Y ) in a point of X can be
understood as evaluating f in just the ”Y -part” of that point, which means that any such
f is constant on the fibers of g.

6.2.2
Generalizations of the Main Construction
The main construction of Hermitian LRC codes presented in Section 6.2.1 can be gener-
alized in a number of ways. In the following only generalizations applying directly to the
Hermitian LRC codes are considered. These can all be extended even further to apply for
other families of codes arising from the general construction of algebraic geometric LRC
codes. In particular, a family of codes on Garcia-Stichtenoth curves is considered in [2].
The first generalization presented extends the Hermitian LRC codes to be able to correct
more than one erasure within each recovery set. The second generalization relaxes the
requirements of the locality parameter, allowing a more flexible code construction.
Hermitian LRC Codes Correcting Multiple Erasures
The construction of Hermitian LRC codes can be adjusted such that it is possible to correct
more than one erasure within the same recovery set. This requires the minimum distance
of the local codes, denoted by ρ, to increase. In codes constructed using Theorems 6.2.1
and 6.2.3 the minimum distance of the local codes is 2, which means that only one erasure
in each recovery set can be corrected. In the following the construction is extended to
apply for ρ ≥2.
The starting point of this generalization is an (n, k, r) Hermitian LRC code. Such a code
can be altered slightly to be able to correct ρ −1 erasures in each recovery set. This is
done by denoting the degree of the map g : H →P1(Fq) by r + ρ −1. If e.g. considering
the construction using the projection on y this means that deg(gy) = q0 = r + ρ −1, and
as a result that the locality of the code is r = q0 + 1 −ρ, which is a reduction for all
ρ > 2. This decrease seems desirable, since recovering more symbols from less information
is the aim of any error correcting code. However, in order for the construction to work,
the dimension of the code also has to decrease, as will be seen shortly.
The (n, ˜k, r, ρ) Hermitian LRC is constructed by following the steps of the construction
of the codes of Theorems 6.2.1 and 6.2.3. The divisor of Y = P1(Fq) is again denoted
D = lQ∞for l ≥1, and a basis for the linear space L(D) is given by {f1, ..., fl+1}. The
space of encoding polynomials, V , is now obtained as:
V = span{fjxi, i = 0, ..., r −1, j = 1, ..., l + 1} ⊆Fq(X).
Note that for the projection on x, the monomials should be in y instead of x. It is seen
that the restriction of any encoding polynomial F ∈V to a fiber Aj of g can be considered
a polynomial of degree at most r −1. This means that the decoding polynomial can be
determined using interpolation through any r points of Aj. Since each fiber consists of
r + ρ −1 points, the codeword symbols corresponding to all of the remaining ρ −1 points
can then be computed by evaluation of the decoding polynomial.
This shows that the code obtained has locality r and can correct ρ −1 ≥1 erasures in
each recovery set as desired. The length of the code is unchanged with respect to the main
construction, but the dimension of the code has been reduced. Since the dimension ˜k of
the code equals the dimension of V , it is seen to be ˜k = (r −1)(l + 1). Considering again
the construction with the projection on y, the dimension of Vy was seen to be (q0−1)(l+1),
50
Locally Recoverable Codes
==PAGE:59==
but for this construction it is seen to be (r −1)(l + 1) = (q0 −ρ + 1)(l −1) which is a
reduction for all ρ > 2. An analogous result is easily obtained for the projection on x.
This means that the cost of being able to correct more erasures within each recovering set
is a decrease in the dimension of the code.

7 Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to examine a type of error-correcting codes known as Locally
Recoverable Codes (LRC codes). Such codes were seen to be characterized by their ability
to successfully recover a missing codeword symbol using just a small number of other avail-
able codeword symbols. The locality property could be extended in several ways, resulting
in codes able to locally recover multiple codeword symbols as well as codes with multiple
different options for recovery of each codeword symbol. As a result it was clear that LRC
codes could be useful in numerous different applications, including distributed storage.
Just as other error-correcting codes, LRC codes were described by the length n and di-
mension k. In addition, the locality r of the code was seen to be an important parameter
conveying the number of codeword symbols needed for successful recovery.
The rela-
tions between these parameters were examined and described in detail. Afterwards upper
bounds on both the rate R and minimum distance d in terms of the parameters of the LRC
codes were given in Theorem 3.2.1, indicating the performance attainable by the codes.
An extensive proof of both bounds was provided, containing elaborations and small nec-
essary adjustments with respect to the proof given in [10]. The upper bound (3.2) on the
minimum distance of LRC codes was seen to resemble the Singleton bound (2.1), and LRC
codes with minimum distance equal to this upper bound were denoted optimal.
A family of optimal LRC codes was presented in Section 4 based on the construction by
Tamo and Barg [10]. These codes were seen to be constructed much like the classical
Reed-Solomon codes, but required the set of evaluation points to be carefully chosen, thus
allowing a partition into disjoint sets for which a polynomial constant on each set could be
found. Furthermore, it was seen that such a partition and corresponding good polynomial
could be identified by utilizing the structure of additive and multiplicative subgroups of
Fq. The specifics of the construction of such RS-like LRC codes was detailed, emphasiz-
ing the parameters of the codes obtained by providing proofs of both length, dimension,
locality and minimum distance. Generalisations of the main construction to more flexible
constructions were then given. In particular, the extension to both (n, k, r, ρ) LRC codes
and several kinds of LRC(2) codes were explained and the code properties proven. New
examples of both the encoding and local recovery process were provided for multiple dif-
ferent members of the RS LRC code family.
Though the initial construction of the RS LRC codes was seen to be somewhat restrictive
in terms of the parameters of the codes, the many generalisations do provide much more
flexibility in the code construction. This is clearly advantageous, as it extends the number
of applications where LRC codes of this type could be of use. However, there are still
some combinations of parameters that can not be achieved by the constructions presented.
If e.g. considering codes over fields with characteristic 2, as would often be interesting

for implementation purposes, it can be seen that certain localities are unattainable when
relying on the partitions in Section 4.1.1. One example of this is locality r = 5, which
would imply r + 1 = 6 = m · 2t and thus m = 3 and t = 1. In this case t is not a multiple
of the smallest possible l satisfying 2l mod 3 = 1, since l is then 2. In [10] it is shown that
this issue can in some cases be overcome by constructing the code over a suﬀiciently large
finite field. Nonetheless, this small example does show that the construction have certain
limitations. At the same time the construction also has many advantages, perhaps most
notably the optimality of the codes. The resemblance to standard RS codes could also
prove very useful, in that it makes the codes easily recognisable and understandable for
anyone familiar with RS codes. It is not diﬀicult to imagine that this resemblance could
also be useful for implementation, as much of the work already done on implementing RS
codes eﬀiciently could very likely be reused.
The second family of LRC codes presented in this thesis was based on the construction
by Barg, Tamo and Vlǎduţ [2], and was seen to be a type of algebraic geometry (AG)
code, relying on the structure of algebraic curves and function fields.
The theoretical
foundation necessary for understanding the construction was provided in Section 5, where
key concepts such as the genus of an algebraic curve, a divisor of a function field and the
corresponding Riemann-Roch space was defined. A constructive approach to estimating
the genus of certain algebraic curves using the theorems of Baker and Pick were presented
in Section 5.1.1. This allowed a new formulation of the dimension of some of the codes in
the AG LRC code family to be obtained, resulting in a more exact estimate than provided
in [2].
Construction of the AG LRC codes was presented in Section 6, where special attention
was devoted to codes defined over the Hermitian curve (5.1). Due to the nature of the
Hermitian curve (many Fq-rational, smooth points) it was seen to serve as a good founda-
tion for the code construction, with codes with reasonable parameters arising from both
natural projections. It was further established that Hermitian LRC codes with small local-
ity could be obtained by introducing additional requirements to the natural projections.
This extension was not specified in [2], where only a much more general extension to AG
LRC codes with small locality was briefly presented. The parameters of the Hermitian
LRC codes with small locality resulting from the construction presented in this thesis were
estimated and proven. Likewise, a generalisation to (n, k, r, ρ) codes was also described for
the specific case of the Hermitian curve, as opposed to the more general (n, k, r, ρ) LRC
codes over Garcia-Stichtenoth curves mentioned in [2]. Several new examples of LRC codes
over the Hermitian curve were shown to concretize the different constructions. Finally, a
construction of AG LRC(2) codes was presented, once again with focus on specifying the
construction over the Hermitian curve.
The choice to center the description of the AG LRC code family around codes on the Her-
mitian curve was made to avoid complicated abstract definitions and explanations, and
instead aim to provide more tangible examples. In doing so, more explicit formulations of
some of the code constructions were obtained, with the intention of facilitating the under-
standing of the code family. At the same time some of the generality of the presentation in
[2] was lost. Given more time, it could have been interesting to consider a more in-depth
explanation of the general constructions as well.
Another obvious point of interest is the performance of the AG LRC codes. Though the
minimum distance of the codes was presented in this thesis, no statements were given as
to the performance of the code family. It is clear from the minimum distance that the

AG LRC codes are not optimal, but computing the gap to the Singleton-like bound (3.2)
would give an indication as to how far from optimal they actually are. An example of
this was seen in [2], where the gap for the main construction on the Hermitian curve was
seen to be relatively small. A more practical approach could also be used to shed further
light on the code performance, by implementation and testing of some of the codes. This
would constitute an interesting extension of this project, and allow codes from the two
code families presented to be experimentally compared.
