Abstract 
Problem and aim: Obesity and metabolic syndrome comprise a great load worldwide. Two of the 
many factors associated with those conditions are early-life exposure to either under- or 
overnutrition, and lipotoxicity. The aim of this work is to illuminate whether early-life 
programming of central energy regulation potentially operates through hypothalamic ceramide 
levels.  
Design: The impact of three prenatal, and two postnatal nutritional challenges have been 
investigated in a 3 x 2 factorial design intervention study in sheep. The outcomes are the 
hypothalamic ceramide level and composition, measured two years after ended intervention in the 
2.5 years old sheep. At late gestation, 36 twin-pregnant ewes were divided into three intervention 
groups; LOW – 50% energy and protein, NORM – 100% energy and protein, and HIGH – 150% 
energy and 110% protein. After three days of lambing, each twin group was split into the two 
postnatal groups; conventional diet (CONV) and high-carbohydrate-high-fat diet (HCHF) until six 
months of age. Here after, the 37 out of 44 lambs included in this work were put on a conventional

diet for two years, before their hypothalamuses were removed. The remaining seven lambs came 
from an external control group, which yielded a seven group comparison in total.  
Analyses and results: No differences were found in total ceramide levels between groups from the 
one-way ANOVA analysis. However, the prenatal diet interventions tended to yield different total 
ceramide levels (p-value = 0.09544) when five outliers were excluded. This was a result of a two-
way ANOVA analysis. Principal component analysis revealed a higher prevalence of short acyl-
CoA chain ceramides (SACCs, C14-C18) in the LOW/HCHF and HIGH/ HCHF groups, and a two-
way ANOVA analysis confirmed that the three postnatal HCHF groups contained higher levels of 
SACC than the three CONV groups (p-value of 0.0391). In the appetite regulating mRNA 
expression comparison, AgRP mRNA levels correlated positively with SACC levels (0.02418). All 
analyses were conducted in R or LatentiX.       
Discussion: Increased expression of the two ceramide synthases CerS6 and CerS5, or impaired 
expression or activity of acid ceramidase were suggested to induce the elevated SACC levels in the 
postnatal obesogenic diet groups.  
Conclusion: Permanent hypothalamic ceramide composition appears to be a factor through which 
early-life programming of central metabolic regulation operates. The mechanisms of the ceramide 
composition set point are unknown, but likely include the level of expression of some hypothalamic 
regulation enzymes, such as CerS5, CerS6, and acid ceramidase.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Problem definition 
Obesity is a continuously growing concern worldwide (Finucane et al. 2011) hitting people in the 
developing countries the most. It seems to have reached a steady state in the United States with an 
obesity percentage of approximately 35% for the last decade (Ogden et al. 2014). The World Health 
Organization estimates more than 20% Europeans to be obese at present time (Bollars et al. 2013). 
Overweight and obesity are associated with metabolic disorders such as type 2 diabetes mellitus, 
cardiovascular disease (CVD), and fatty liver (Seppala-Lindroos et al. 2002) all tightly related to 
the metabolic syndrome (Kaur 2014). But also psychiatric illnesses have been correlated to obesity 
(Petry et al. 2008) and some metabolic syndrome symptoms (Dortland et al. 2013). Interestingly, 
CVD has been decreasing in Western Europe for the last 25 years (Mladovsky et al. 2009), whereas 
diabetes is estimated to increase further. In 2013 10.8% of worldwide total health expenditure was 
spent on diabetes. It is the fourth biggest cause of death in Europe and thus constitutes a great load

(International Diabetes Federation 2013). On the surface, overweight, obesity, and metabolic 
syndrome are issues concerning amounts of calories consumed. However, digging closer into the 
reason leads to the emergence of much greater complexity implicating factors such as genotype 
(Vogler et al. 1995), metabolic programming (McMillen, Robinson 2005), developed and deep-
rooted habits (Everitt, Robbins 2006), behavior and personality traits (Velazquez-Sanchez et al.

One proximate path in coming closer to the origin of developing obesity and metabolic syndrome is 
looking into peoples early-life exposures, such as the type and availability of nutrition, and how 
those factors affect central metabolic programming. A growing body of evidence suggests fetus 
nutrition availability and birth weight to be influential on non-communicable disease development 
risk later in life (McMillen, Robinson 2005). The knowledge of how and by which mechanisms 
prenatal exposures are affecting long term metabolic programming, are important pieces in the 
puzzle of the understanding of the development of metabolic syndrome, that seems to be initiated 
centrally from the hypothalamus (McMillen, Robinson 2005). In continuation of this, lately, greater 
attention has been drawn to the influence of some hypothalamic lipid levels, such as ceramides 
(Contreras et al. 2014), on the whole body metabolism control capacity of hypothalamus. However, 
it has not yet been reported whether modulated metabolic programming involves permanently 
altered ceramide levels.  
1.2. Aim of project 
The aim of this project is to investigate the potential link between pre- and postnatal nutrition and 
hypothalamic ceramide level and composition later in life. Thus, this work illuminates whether 
early-life programming of central energy regulation potentially operates through hypothalamic 
ceramide levels.  
Is there a correlation between pre- and early postnatal nutrition availability and the level and 
composition of hypothalamic ceramides in sheep?  
- Is the ceramide level different between the different diet intervention groups? 
- Are there differences in ceramide composition between groups? 
- Do the ceramide levels correlate with hypothalamic appetite regulating mRNA expression?

2. Theory 
2.1. Introduction to ceramide related metabolic alterations 
The metabolic syndrome is a designation for an impaired metabolic state characterized by a bundle 
of symptoms that are correlated with fatty liver, obesity, CVD, and type-2 diabetes. Those 
symptoms most often include; insulin resistance (fasting glucose level >100 or 110 mg/dL), 
elevated triacylglyceride (TG) level (>150 mg/dL), decreased high density lipoprotein cholesterol 
(HDL) (<35-50 mg/dL, sex and definition dependent), elevated blood pressure ≥130/85 or 140/90 
mmHg, and increased waist circumference or waist-to-hip ratio (>0.90 for men and >0.85 for 
women) (Kaur 2014). This altered metabolic state is often accompanied by leptin resistance (Banks 
2004) and a chronic low-grade inflammatory state peripherally (Hotamisligil et al. 1995) and 
centrally, occurring with excess energy consumption (Posey et al. 2009) and elevated ceramide 
levels (Kolak et al. 2007).   
Elevated ceramide levels in insulin resistance were first seen by Turinsky and colleagues in Zucker 
rats. At that time this correlation was thought of as unlikely (1990). Since then ceramides have been 
shown to play an important role in the development of insulin resistance. Ceramide inhibits, among 
others, the key signaling protein kinase B (PKB) (Pickersgill et al. 2007), also called Akt, through 
protein kinase C-zeta (PKC-ζ)- (Powell et al. 2003) and protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A) (Stratford 
et al. 2004) activation. The inhibition of Akt impairs the insulin signaling cascade, and affects 
gluconeogenesis and the synthesis of proteins, fatty acids, TAGs, and glycogen (Galadari et al. 
2013). It also affects the activity of the lipid degrading enzyme, hormone sensitive lipase (HSL) 
(Choi et al. 2010). Furthermore, C2 ceramide analogue mediated Akt inhibition alters dopaminergic 
and serotonergic neurotransmission (Riddle et al. 2003), and C16 ceramide mediated Akt inhibition 
is connected to depression in mice (Gulbins et al. 2013). Finally, ceramide may inhibit GLUT4 
gene transcription (Long, Pekala 1996). Some Akt mediated metabolic pathways are schematized in 
figure 2.1.1.

also are promoted by some pro-inflammatory agents, as tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α) and

Straczkowski et al. 2007), and fatty liver and adipose tissue inflammation (Kolak et al. 2007) 
compared to control groups. However, depletion of ceramide seems to be just as detrimental as its 
excess, since it, as well, induces glucose intolerance and insulin resistance in lean mice, treated with 
myriocin, a first step blocker of the de novo ceramide synthesis pathway (Yang et al. 2009).

2.2. Hypothalamus in relation to metabolism 
Human anatomy 
Hypothalamus is a ventral part of the diencephalon (interbrain) and is located below thalamus. It 
weighs approximately 8g (Clarke, Lemon 2009). The floor and lower lateral walls of the third 
ventricle (3V) are bounded by hypothalamus, whereas the higher walls are bounded by thalamus. 
The pituitary stalk (infundibulum) is descending from the basal hypothalamus forming a connection 
with the pituitary gland (Byrne 1997, Clarke, Lemon 2009). The basal hypothalamus contains the 
median eminence (ME), which constitutes the inferior boundary and the blood-brain barrier (BBB) 
(Banks 2004, Mullier et al. 2010), although ME contains fenestrated capillaries through which 
peripheral signal compounds may enter (Rodríguez et al. 1986). Adjacent to ME the arcuate nucleus 
(ARC) (also called the infundibular nucleus in humans, which is why it is abbreviated “inf” in 
figure 2.2.1) is located (Baroncini et al. 2012). The location of ARC is often called mediobasal 
hypothalamus (MBH). The signal compounds are most likely detected in ARC and this nucleus is 
important in appetite regulation. It projects to the ventromedial nucleus (VMH) and the lateral 
hypothalamic area (LHA) both seen in figure 2.2.1A. All three latter nucleuses are located in the 
tuberal region of hypothalamus, and they all project to the paraventricular nucleus (PVH) located in 
the anterior hypothalamic region (PVH is abbreviated “Pa” in figure 2.2.1B). PVH further projects 
to the limbic brain parts that among others constitute the reward system (Byrne 1997).

Hypothalamic development in relevant mammals  
In rats hypothalamic nuclei differentiation initiates about day 10 in embryo, and neuronal projection 
pathway development continues until day 20 of postnatal life. Rat gestation lasts 19-21 days (Grove 
et al. 2005, Tobet, McClellan 2013). Normally, a postnatal leptin surge occurs in rodent 
hypothalamus around day 10 as part of the neuronal projection development (Ahima, Prabakaran & 
Flier 1998). However, prenatal growth restriction combined with neonatal weight catch-up is 
associated with a later leptin surge at day 16 and subsequent leptin resistance (Yura et al. 2005).

Physiology 
ARC contains orexigenic neurons producing hunger-inducing neuropeptides, and anorexigenic 
neurons producing satiety inducing neuropeptides. The orexigenic peptides are called neuropeptide-
Y (NPY) and agouti-related-protein (AgRP), which is why their producing neurons are called NPY-
AgRP. NPY is an agonist for the NPY receptors in the LHA and the PVH inducing hunger, whereas 
AgRP is an antagonist to both the MC3 and MC4 receptors explained below suppressing satiety. 
See figure 2.2.4. The anorexigenic neurons are called POMC/CART because they produce 
==PAGE:13==
Arwen U. M. Stankowska

s073359 
 
 
 
13/59 
proopiomelanocortin (POMC), which is the precursor to the melanocortins; γ-melanocyte-
stimulating hormone (γMSH) and α-melanocyte-stimulating hormone (αMSH). αMSH and γMSH 
bind to melanocortin MC3 receptors in ARC and to melanocortin MC4 receptors in PVH and 
VMH, respectively, as seen in figure 2.2.4. Stimulation of those receptors inhibits food intake. 
POMC/CART neurons also produce cocaine- and amphetamine-regulated transcript (CART), 
working as an antagonist on the NPY receptors in the LHA and PVH suppressing hunger (Neary, 
Goldstone & Bloom 2004, Byrne 1997). Appetite regulating hormones such as leptin and ghrelin 
bind to their responding receptors in the elucidated ARC neurons in order to conduct their effect 
(Byrne 1997), but leptin and ghrelin receptors are to be found sporadically in the hypothalamus and 
brain (Perello et al. 2012).

2.3. Early-life metabolic programming 
Associations in humans 
There is a U-shaped correlation between birth weight and the risk of developing obesity, metabolic 
syndrome and the often accompanied diseases, type-2 diabetes and CVD (Yu et al. 2011, Harder et 
al. 2007, Silveira, Horta 2008, Risnes et al. 2011). 
In the seventies Ravelli and colleagues made an investigation on 300.000 young men who had lived 
through the Dutch famine (1944-1945) as fetuses, and found an association between early 
gestational nutrition deprivation and increased risk in developing obesity (1976), as opposed to the 
development of glucose intolerance, that seems to be programmed in mid and late gestational 
phases (Ravelli et al. 1998). Early gestational exposure to the Dutch famine has also been 
associated with a tendency to choose more fatty foods in grown up life (Lussana et al. 2008). In the 
late eighties the association between low birth weight and increased blood pressure later in life was 
found (Barker et al. 1989b), together with the correlation of low birth weight and increased death 
risk from ischaemic heart disease in adulthood in men from Hertfordshire (Barker et al. 1989a). In 
extension of the Hertfordshire study Hales and colleagues found a correlation between impaired 
glucose tolerance or newly discovered diabetes and low birth weight in some of those Hertfordshire 
men (1991). Studying people in developing countries has shown that intrauterine stunting is 
associated with a greater risk in developing type-2 diabetes, due to lower insulin production 
capacity, compared to low birth weight without stunting, which is more correlated to insulin 
resistance (Fall et al. 1998). Decreased β-cell mass induced by restricted fetus growth may cause 
this association (Bouwens, Rooman 2005). Furthermore, it has been observed that women with low 
birth weights but with above average weight at the age of one year are at the greatest risk of dying

from CVD, in contrast to men, in who the greatest risk in CVD death rate is correlated to low birth 
weight and below average weight at one year of age (Osmond et al. 1993). Further, it seems that 
men with low birth weight who encounter rapid weight gain after the first year of life are at the 
highest risk in dying from CVD (Eriksson et al. 2001). These latter studies indicate that fast weight 
catch-up form a CVD risk factor in both men and women. This is in accordance with an American 
cohort study showing childhood obesity to be associated with infant weight catch-up (Stettler et al.

calories than infants from lean and overweight mothers (2005), and that infants with higher BMI 
show lower energy expenditure, which seems to correspond to correspondingly lower lean body 
mass (2008). See figure 3.3.1 for overview. 
Central programming of metabolism and appetite  
Animal research suggests leptin and insulin levels to be essential hypothalamic programming 
factors during the hypothalamic development time frame in early life. Increased insulin levels in the 
infant plastic hypothalamus, permanently alter hypothalamic neuronal structure (Plagemann et al. 
1999) yielding an obesity- and metabolic syndrome-prone phenotype (Plagemann et al. 1992). 
Leptin deficiency in rodent dams results in disrupted projections from ARC in the offspring 
hypothalamus, which may permanently alter ARC response to leptin in an appetite increasing 
manner (Bouret, Draper & Simerly 2004). Diet-induced obese rat dams produce hyperphagic 
offspring with hyperleptinemia and leptin resistance despite being on a standard chow (Kirk et al. 
2009), probably, at least partly, due to diet-induced permanently alteration of response to appetite 
signals such as NPY in ARC (Kozak et al. 2000). Similarly, neonatal rats treated with leptin (d0-10) 
during hypothalamus development also end up being leptin resistant and hyperphagic (Toste et al. 
2006). Pregnant ewes overnourished during late gestation (d115-124) results in lambs with higher 
milk consumption than control with attendant decreasing ARC leptin receptor expression along 
with increasing fat mass (Muhlhausler et al. 2006). Interestingly, intrauterine undernutrition with a 
subsequent diet independent weight catch up (during d0-10) produces leptin resistant rodents as 
well (Yura et al. 2005). In this study prenatally undernourished mice fed a high fat diet from the 
ninth week of age showed to have an obesity-prone phenotype compared to intrauterine 
normalnourished controls fed the same high fat diet from week nine. Thus, overnutrition and 
undernutrition combined with weight catch up during hypothalamus development seem to yield 
inadequate hypothalamic appetite signal response, which may be caused by diet-induced abnormal 
levels of leptin, among others. Furthermore, even though the calorie intake did not markedly differ

altered appetite signal sensitization. Most likely, both of these metabolic alterations arise from 
hypothalamus, and both may be connected to elevated ceramide levels. See next section for details.

2.4. Role of hypothalamic ceramide 
Production 
There are three major pathways in ceramide production; The de novo pathway, the 
sphingomyelinase pathway, and the salvage pathway. They may all be connected to diet, metabolic 
regulation, and weight, which make them relevant in this work.  
De novo pathway 
The first and rate-limiting step of de novo ceramide generation in endoplasmic reticulum (ER) 
consists of a condensation of palmitoyl-CoA (C16-CoA) and L-serine catalyzed by serine 
palmitoyltransferase (SPT). Depending on the SPT active subunit, SPT can also use myristoyl-CoA 
(C14-CoA) or stearoyl-CoA (C18-CoA) as substrates. The product is 3-ketosphinganine, which is 
subsequently reduced to dihydrosphingosine (sphinganine) by 3-ketosphinganine reductase. In the 
ER and mitochondria an N-acylation occurs mediated by the acyl-CoA chain length specific 
ceramide synthase (CerS) to form dihydroceramide. CerS specifications are shown in table 2.4.1. 
Ceramide is finally made by a desaturation forming a trans-4,5 double bond in the sphingosine part 
catalyzed by dihydroceramide desaturase (Hannun, Obeid 2008, Kolesnick, Kronke 1998, Tidhar, 
Futerman 2013). See figure 2.4.1.

Physiology 
Ceramide metabolism in ARC has significant impact on the effect of leptin (Gao et al. 2011) and 
ghrelin (Ramírez et al. 2013). Most likely, leptin and ghrelin carry out their appetite regulating 
effects through altering the levels of malonyl-CoA (Gao et al. 2007), and carnitine 
palmitoyltransferase 1 (CPT-1). The CPT-1 types a and b normally mediate the transportation of 
long chain fatty acids through the translocase transporter to mitochondrial matrix for the purpose of 
oxidation (Berg 2007). CPT-1a and the brain specific CPT-1c has been stated as key mediators of 
central ceramide metabolism (Carrasco et al. 2012, Gao et al. 2011, Mera et al. 2014, Gao et al. 
2013c). Gao and colleagues (2013c) investigated the effects of long-term CPT-1a expression in rat 
VMH and observed hyperphagia. Mera and colleagues (2014) found similar results accompanied by 
elevation in the ceramide types Cer14:0 and Cer18:1 in MBH. Gao and colleagues (2011) found 
that rodents with ARC CPT-1c overexpression consumed more food and had elevated ARC NPY 
and ceramide levels compared to control following a fasting night, and the effects of leptin and 
malonyl-CoA such as ceramide decrease and anorectic behaviour were attenuated under those 
conditions. The effects of CPT-1c overexpression were abolished when fed ad libitum. Conversely,

2011) nor ghrelin (Ramírez et al. 2013) treatment, but at the same time had decreased ARC 
ceramide levels (Gao et al. 2011) and no ghrelin-induced AgRP and NPY increase (Ramírez et al. 
2013). The CPT-1c KO mice did not show ghrelin-induced ceramide increase compared to control, 
and myriocin mediated inhibition of hypothalamic de novo ceramide synthesis prevented ghrelin-
induced orexigenic effect (Ramírez et al. 2013). Further, it has been seen that ceramide analog 
administration into rat ARC blocks the anorexigenic effects of leptin, including down regulation of 
NPY (Gao et al. 2011). Gao and colleagues (2011) used ciliary neurotrophic factor (CNTF) 
exposure to clarify if metabolic effects of CPT-1c and ceramide are malonyl-CoA mediated, as 
CNTF triggers anorexigenic effects bypassing the malonyl-CoA metabolic pathway (Watt et al. 
2006), and is therefore expected not to be blocked by central infusion of malonyl-CoA

3.5. Data analysis 
Introduction to LC-MS 
For the fraction 2 (ceramides and DAGs) compound analysis Liquid Chromatography-Mass 
Spectrometry (LC-MS)[2] was used. See appendix I list for LC-MS details. 
In overall LC-MS is a combination of High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) and 
Mass Spectrometry (MS). In the LC process the sample compounds are separated from each other. 
In practice the sample liquid or mobile phase flows through a column that contain a fluid called the 
stationary phase. The stationary phase is the compound separator due to its ability to retard different 
compounds with different rates, for instance, so that all ceramide 16:0 exit the column separated 
from all the ceramide 18:0 molecules. The column exit time is measured by a diode array detector 
(DAD). This corresponds to the retention time, which is the time at which the compound is detected 
in the MS. 
In the MS process the sample fluid with the separated molecules enters the electrospray ionization 
(ESI) zone that nebulizes the fluid and subsequently evaporates it by means of a heated vaporizer 
zone and nitrogen, and ionizes the analytes through high vacuum and electrical potential. However, 
the ionization efficacy varies from compound to compound, which is why specific compound 
standard curves are needed. Subsequently to the ESI, the ionized analytes enters the quadrupole 
time of flight (Q-TOF) measuring system. First, the quadrupole only enables molecules with a 
selected mass to pass. Second, the ionized molecules pass on to the TOF that measures their flight 
time, which enables the determination of the mass-to-charge-ratio, and ultimately the mass. 
Additionally to the flight time, the TOF detector also measures the relative amount of each specific 
molecule, which is correspondent to the area size in the mass spectrum of each molecule peak. 
Though, standard curves are required to verify the compound quantity.

4.1. Analysis of total ceramide level between groups 
To test the first outcome, if the intervention diets had any effect on the hypothalamic ceramide 
content, one-way ANOVA test was used. For this test only the sum of ceramides for each sheep was 
employed. Based on the R search for outliers (see appendix III) and the Grubb’s test (p-value = 
0.177) no outlier was initially excluded. However, the outlier search pointed towards five potential 
outliers; hyp 5, 15, 32, 36, and 41, seen in the plots as cases; 4, 14, 29, 33 and 38 respectively.   
The one-way ANOVA test yielded a p-value of 0.6881, which does not indicate any effect of the 
interventions. However, in the boxplot in figure 4.1.1 a tendency of elevated ceramide amounts can 
be observed in both of the prenatal HIGH groups, and in one of the prenatal LOW groups, 
combined with the postnatal high-fat-high-carb diet compared to the others. The boxplot seem to 
reveal a difference in variances, which led to the Bartlett’s test, testing for equality of standard 
deviations. The test result yielded a p-value of 0.1207, which is big enough to trust the equality and 
the test result. A power test of the data calculated in R resulted in 0.19, meaning that 19% of the 
time the test will show significance if there is one.

5. Discussion 
In this study no differences in long-term total ceramide content were found in sheep hypothalamus 
when different pre- and postnatal diet interventions were compared. The study size of only forty-
one samples divided into seven groups yielding a power of 0.19, entails an increased risk of a type 
II error. The outcome data of this study was marked by five outlying samples with pronounced 
elevations of selected ceramide species. Most likely the striking elevations were random, as they 
appeared in five different groups and one of them being the external control group. Therefore they 
are believed to be either interpreting errors by Bruker software or the result of the laboratory 
processes conducted by a novice. Since the error was not found and they were not identified as 
outliers by the Grubb’s test, those five samples were partly included in the analysis. However, data 
with such a potential error also gives rise to questioning the reliability of the rest of the data. All 
statistically significant results discussed below are found from data excluding the five problematic 
samples.  
The vitamin and mineral mix given to the ewes twice a week during the intervention may have been 
a factor reducing the effect of the prenatal diet interventions. Another type II error risk factor is the 
prenatal intervention time frame. The literature indicates that the neuronal pathway projections are 
established in sheep fetuses at least at day 110 of gestation (Muhlhausler et al. 2004), which is 
approximately 40 days prior to delivery. Unfortunately, the prenatal diet intervention of the present 
study is initiated approximately six weeks (42 days) prior to term. Thus, the intervention diet may 
not have had sufficient influence on the hypothalamic development and programming, as it is 
probably conducted outside the crucial developing time frame3. However, in a prior study 
conducted by the same group, with a similar design, diet intervention, and intervention time frame, 
the newborn lambs from the LOW/HCHF group had a higher intake and preference of the HCHF 
diet compared to NORM/HCHF, but no increased diet intake was seen in the LOW/CONV lambs 
(Nielsen et al. 2013). This increased preference for calorie dense food resembles the food addiction-
prone hyper reward state (Stice et al. 2011) mimicking the reward deficiency syndrome (Blum et al. 
1996) behaviour. This could be connected to blunted response to leptin and insulin and/or increased 
response to ghrelin (Figlewicz, Sipols 2010). More relevantly, elevated ceramide levels could play a

Principal component analysis revealed a tendency for the HIGH/HCHF and LOW/HCHF groups to

were included in the NORM/HCHF group, which is too little in order to trust the PCA pattern. 
Additionally, no significant differences in SACC content between the prenatal diet interventions 
were found. Only a high risk type II error tendency occurred in total ceramide level between the 
prenatal groups. However, a higher prevalence of SACC was correlated with the three postnatal 
HCHF groups (p-value = 0.03755) compared to the postnatal CONV groups. This finding is in 
correspondence with the intervention diet for those three groups that consisted of 50% cream. 
Regarding the relative fat composition of cream, among others, it is comprised of approximately 
11% C14:0, 31% C16:0, 12% C18:0, and 24% C18:1 (Saxholt et al. 2009). Does this mean that 
excess of C14-C16 fatty acids in the tissue are enhancing CerS6 activity? Analysing the fractions 1 
(TAGs) and 3 (FFAs) from the fractionation process would help clarify the relevance of this 
question. However, the diet interventions had ended two years prior to autopsy, indicating early-life 
programming of hypothalamic long-term ceramide composition, through excess calorie mediated 
alteration of activity and/or expression of some ceramide synthases or ceramidases. In this case 
CerS6 and possibly CerS5 (table 2.4.1) appear to exhibit increased activity. Impaired acid 
ceramidase could also result in increased SACC (table 2.4.2). mRNA expressions of those enzymes 
could be measured in order to test this theory. Should those SACCs be remains of the intervention 
diet, this would additionally point in the direction that excess fatty acids in the organism mediates 
central ceramide production, which could result in increased central lipotoxicity, as it is seen in 
some rodent studies (Posey et al. 2009, Borg et al. 2012).

Existing literature suggests early-life overnutrition to be a programming factor for increased 
appetite and increased risk of obesity, and thus the accompanied disease risk factors. The elevated 
Cer14 and C16 ceramides in the postnatal overnourished groups of this study are in correspondence 
with this literature. The correlation between hyperphagia and elevated Cer14:0 and Cer18:1 in 
MBH (Mera et al. 2014), and oxidative stress caused by elevated Cer16:0 levels (Zigdon et al. 
2013) have previously been pointed out in this report. Altered activity of acid ceramidase or CerS6 
and possibly CerS5 may account for one programming mechanism increasing susceptibility for 
obesity in early-life overnourished mammals.  
The pooled SACC levels from all groups were positively correlated with the mRNA levels of the 
orexigenic AgRP neuropeptide (p-value = 0.02418, figure 4.4.1). This correlation evokes a riddle of 
causality. Do altered presumed CerS6 or acid ceramidase activity and thereof the correspondingly 
increased SACC level promote the gene expression of AgRP, or is it the other way around. The fact 
that particularly Cer16:0 impairs Akt (Osawa et al. 2005) that are generally involved in several 
signaling pathways (Boyle 2005, Galadari et al. 2013) including gene transcription (Matsumoto et 
al. 2002, Price, Proud 1994) (figure 2.1.1) draws the riddle towards the first potential theory. The 
AgRP expression promoting properties of C16/14 ceramides could be tested by means of 
measurement of AgRP mRNA levels in a CerS6 and CerS5 depleted/overexpressed model.  
The previously mentioned hyper reward similar behaviour manifested in the LOW/HCHF lambs 
may be speculated to be caused by a combination of prenatal energy deprivation-induced alterations 
in the mesolimbic system and early postnatal diet-induced long-term alterations in ceramide 
regulation. In order to test this hypothesis a new batch of newborn LOW and NORM lambs could 
be scanned by means of functional magnetic resonance imaging while being fed both a HCHF or 
CONV diet to test for brain activity differences. Alternatively, the mesolimbic brain parts from the 
present study sheep could be tested for differences in neurotransmission markers. The latter has 
already been done in prenatal energy restricted rodents, where long-term differences in dopamine 
and serotonin transmission markers have been found in hypothalamus (Manuel-Apolinar et al. 
2014).  
For future studies the advantage of testing for both the mesolimbic reward system- together with the 
appetite regulating markers should be kept in mind, in order to minimize holes in the puzzle of 
central metabolic programming, as those two systems operate intertwined. Further, those results 
should be compared to metabolic risk markers, such as, for instance, blood lipid and glucose levels.

6. Conclusion 
The aim of this project was to investigate the potential link between pre- and postnatal nutrition 
availability and hypothalamic ceramide level and composition in grown up life in sheep. This 
question was investigated in order to illuminate whether early-life programming of central energy 
regulation potentially operates through hypothalamic ceramide levels.  
One-way ANOVA analysis revealed that late gestational over- and undernutrition combined with a 
six months postnatal calorie dense diet did not alter total ceramide level in grown up sheep 
hypothalamus. However, the postnatal diet, comprised of 50% cream and 50% milk replacer, 
altered the long-term hypothalamic ceramide composition, regardless of the prenatal interventions, 
in a short acyl-CoA chain ceramide (C14-C18) increasing manner. This was first discovered in a 
PCA plot and subsequently confirmed through a two-way ANOVA analysis where the y variable 
consisted of the pooled short chain ceramide levels and the two factorial variables consisted of the 
separated pre- and postnatal interventions. The test showed a significant difference between the two 
postnatal diets (p-value = 0.0391). Hence, the postnatal obesogenic diet-induced alteration in 
ceramide composition indicates chronically altered activities of either CerS6 and possibly CerS5 or 
acid ceramidase.  
Finally, a correlation between the short chain ceramides and AgRP mRNA expression was found 
when all groups were pooled into one correlation test (p-value = 0.02418). This correlation may be 
connected to a hyper reward similar behaviour manifested in the prenatally undernourished and 
postnatally overnourished lambs from a previous corresponding study, when potential intrauterine 
nutritional restriction-induced mesolimbic reward sensitivity alteration is taken into account.   
In conclusion, permanent hypothalamic ceramide composition appears to be a factor through which 
early-life programming of central metabolic regulation operates. The mechanisms of the ceramide 
composition set point are unknown, but likely include the level of expression of some hypothalamic 
regulation enzymes, such as CerS5, CerS6, and acid ceramidase, all affecting short chain ceramide 
regulation.

•

Extraction of brain homogenate  
NB! Make one blind-extractions  for each extraction series, which go through exactly the same methods! 
•

•

•

(keep on ice all the time, add methanol to the sample directly after weighing of the tissue).  
•

• 
Add 3 ceramic beads. 
• 
Transfer to the samples to building 221 on ice.  
• 
Homogenize the samples using in the ice-cold metal block 
• 
Transfer the tubes back to 224.  
• 
Whirl the samples  
• 
Transfer the homogenate to a clean screw-capped tube labeled “polar” on ice. 
•

transfer to the tube

Fractioning of lipidextracts on aminopropyl-cartridges 
Based on Kaluzny 1985, Bodennec et al, 2000 and Fauland 2013 (Only changed in step 8) 
• 
Wash the cartridges with 3 ml diethylether followed by 1.5 ml CHCl3:MeOH (2:1) (Can be done in advance). 
• 
Activate the cartridges with 2x500 µl hexane (do not let the cartridge dry before sample application). 
• 
Dry down the lipid extracts and dissolve them in 200 µl CHCl3. 
• 
Put clean glasses into the manifold and apply the sample to the cartridge, the flow-through is discarded.  
• 
Elute TAG and cholesterolesters using 3.0 ml hexane:chloroform:ethylacetate (100:5:5) (Fraction 1). 
• 
Elute cholesterol, DAG and ceramide using 3x2.0 ml CHCl3:MeOH (23:1) (Fraction 2). 
• 
Elute FFA with 5 ml 2% acetic acid in diethylether into a new glass (Fraction 3). 
• 
Put a clean glass in the manifold and elute zwitterionic phospholipids (PC, PE, Sphm and alternatively 
glycolipids) with 10 ml CHCl3:MeOH (2:1) (Fraction 5). 
• 
Put a clean glass in the manifold and elute acidic phospholipids (PG, CL, PS, PI and PA) with 10 ml 
CHCl3:MeOH (4:1) + ammonium acetate (final concentration 0.2M) (Fraction 6). 
• 
Dry down the fractions under nitrogen (for total PL is fraction 5 & 6 mixed).
