Portfolio Optimization using Mixed-Integer and Linear Programming
Abstract
This thesis develops and empirically validates a three-stage stock portfolio construction framework
that (i) screens the S&P 500 stocks via fundamental filtering scores, (ii) generates return scenarios
with multivariate Monte-Carlo simulations, and (iii) allocates capital through a mixed-integer linear
program that maximizes Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). It is aimed to represent a retail investor
who rebalances the portfolio on a monthly basis, considering cardinality constraints and Revolut’s non
premium transaction cost structure.
Stocks are filtered monthly using three financial fundamental scores: the Piotroski F-Score, Mohanram
G-Score, and the Quality-minus-Junk (QMJ) factor. Only stocks whose scores exceed pre-defined
thresholds progress to optimization. Monthly return scenarios are simulated for every rebalancing
date, using 5,000 joint paths over a 21-day horizon, generated from multivariate normal (MVN),
exponentially weighted moving average with three decay factors (λ ∈{0.99, 0.97, 0.94}), multivariate
Student t-distribution (MVT) with volatility scaling (using both the most recent and average 21-day
volatilities), and three GARCH-based models (GARCH cDCC, DCC and GJR). When applying a
single modeling approach across the entire multi-period optimization and rebalancing framework, the
MVN model emerged as the most promising overall, capturing a good trade-off between total return
and risk adjusted performance. The analysis also highlighted the potential value of the MVT with
21-day volatility scaling for short-term risk management.
Both the G-Score with a threshold of 7 and the F-Score with a threshold of 8 proved to be promising
filtering mechanisms when combined with the MILP optimizer. The most effective configuration
used a CVaR confidence level of β = 15%, a minimum expected return of 3.75% per month, and
the multivariate normal (MVN) model for return simulation. In the in-sample period, the F-Score
8 portfolio achieved a cumulative return of 152% over five years, compared to 74% for the S&P
500—representing roughly 50% excess wealth. The Sharpe ratio improved from 0.57 (S&P 500) to 1.38,
while the maximum drawdown was reduced from 14% to 16%. Under the same settings, the G-Score 7
portfolio also outperformed the benchmark, achieving a total return of 123% and a Sharpe ratio of
1.061.
Out-of-sample results reinforced the robustness of these findings. The F-Score 8 portfolio delivered a
total return of 130% with a Sharpe ratio of 0.709, while the G-Score 7 variant performed even better,
yielding a 158% return and a Sharpe ratio of 0.99. Statistical tests across both sample periods show
that the F-Score 8 strategy achieved a p-value of 4.82%, while G-score 7 achieved 5.32%, confirming
that the combined filtering and optimization process adds significant value beyond pure screening, and
that transaction costs do not erode the strategy’s advantage. To compare: The S&P 500 had a total
return of 99.3% and a Sharpe ratio of 0.83.
This study brings together fundamental-based stock selection, scenario-based risk estimation, and
mixed-integer CVaR optimization into a single, repeatable framework. In doing so, it addresses a gap in
the existing literature around integrating fundamental analysis with MILP-CVAR optimization models.
The results show that careful data selection combined with modern portfolio optimization techniques
can effectively turn fundamental signals into practical, real-world investment strategies.

1
Introduction
Over the past decades, financial markets have become significantly easier to access. With the rise
of low-cost trading platforms, like Revolut, and abundant financial information, more and more
private investors are entering the stock market. Yet, the challenge remains: how can individual
investors—especially those without professional expertise—build a stock portfolio that not only selects
the "right" stocks but also allocates capital in a risk-aware and optimized way?
Constructing a robust portfolio involves making two critical decisions: which stocks to include, and
how much capital to allocate to each stock. While ETFs 1 offer a simple and easy entry point, they
leave little room for customization. Many retail investors seek more tailored strategies that incorporate
financial logic, but struggle with questions like: "Which stocks should I choose?", "How can I balance
risk and return?" or "How should I adjust my portfolio over time?". These questions can be translated
into two topics: fundamental stock selection (selection of stocks based on key fundamental data such
as profit margin) and quantitative portfolio optimization through risk management.
On one hand, fundamental stock selection can be done through fundamental scores such as the F-
Score, G-Score, and QMJ (Quality Minus Junk), which bundle key financial fundamental data into an
aggregated score, and have shown empirical success in identifying financially strong companies. On the
other hand, quantitative portfolio optimization methods—such as Mixed-Integer Linear Programming
(MILP) using Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) — employ a risk measure that is both theoretically
sound and computationally practical, and has been adopted by regulatory institutions, most notably
the Committee on Banking Supervision (2016) [26], within the Basel III framework for setting minimum
capital requirements for market risk. In the academic literature, these two aspects have often been
studied in isolation, and a practical and integrated approach that combines these two dimensions is
missing from the literature (as detailed in section 2.4.1).
This thesis aims to fill that gap. It develops a comprehensive portfolio construction framework that
begins with filtering S&P 500 stocks based on fundamental scores, models return distributions using
multivariate statistical techniques, and applies a Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) algorithm
to optimize portfolios under a CVaR objective, incorporating transaction costs and monthly rebalancing.
The proposed methodology is described in figure 1 and is structured in three stages: (1) filtering stocks
using fundamental scores; (2) Return Modeling and scenario simulation using multiple approaches:
multivariate normal (MVN), MVN with Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (EWMA), multivariate
Student t, and several GARCH-based models (cDCC, DCC, and GJR); (3) monthly rebalancing the
portfolio using a CVaR-based optimization model that incorporates transaction costs and takes the
simulations as input.

1.1
Objective and Investor Profile
Objective
The objective of this thesis is to develop a practical, three-stage portfolio optimization framework
(as shown in Figure 1) that enables individual investors to construct stock portfolios capable of
outperforming the S&P 500 index. The framework emphasizes risk-awareness, cost-efficiency, and
implementability in real-world settings. It integrates fundamental score-based filtering, multivariate
return modeling, and CVaR-based Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) optimization with
monthly rebalancing and transaction costs.
Investor Profile
This work is motivated by the growing accessibility of financial markets to retail investors through
platforms like Revolut. However, most retail investors 2 lack the tools or expertise to build optimized
portfolios that account for risk, cost, and performance in a systematic way. To bridge this gap, the
thesis considers a representative investor profile: a 20–30-year-old using a standard (non-premium)
Revolut account with an initial capital of $10,000. It is assumed that this investor covers transaction
costs only in the first period, rebalances monthly, and does not inject additional capital over time. The
investment universe is limited to S&P 500 constituents with at least five years of historical daily return
data available for simulation purposes.
Defining the investor’s risk-aversion profile is critical for tuning model parameters and selecting

portfolios. Different profiles may lead to different conclusions—for instance, younger investors may

drawdown minimization, ultimately leading to different portfolio choices. Accordingly, this study
evaluates strategies using a weighted scoring approach based on two performance metrics: total return

These bounds help prevent excessive concentration in a single position, while also avoiding allocations

asset pricing.
1.2
Research Question
To guide the development and evaluation of the proposed framework in figure 1, this thesis is structured
around the following research questions. These questions are designed to explore the effectiveness of
fundamental filtering strategies and their integration into a risk-aware portfolio optimization process
that aims to outperform the S&P 500 benchmark:
1. What is the most promising fundamental score, according to a given investor profile, and its
corresponding threshold to filter a list of stocks and create a market cap weighted portfolio
compared to the S&P500.
2. What is the most promising fundamental score, according to a given investor profile, and its
corresponding threshold to filter a list of stocks and create a MILP optimization model using
CvaR compared to the S&P500.
3. What is the most promising return modeling technique, according to final portfolio returns, to
perform monte carlo simulations for a MILP optimization model using CvaR.
1.3
Limitations
Like any empirical study, this work faces limitations—chief among them, data quality. The quarterly
fundamental data from CRSP had a considerable number of missing values, especially for variables
used in the G-Score and QMJ-Score. While standard cleaning methods and imputation techniques (e.g.
cross-sectional or time-series averages) were applied, these may reduce the precision of the composite
scores and affect the accuracy of the resulting portfolio rankings.
A second constraint lies in the universe of securities examined. Although the S&P 500 consists of
five hundred constituents by design, roughly eight percent of the index had to be excluded at each

rebalancing date due to the requisite of five years of available data. The analysis, therefore, rests on a
slightly reduced sample, introducing a degree of survivorship and availability bias. Furthermore, by
restricting the investigation to the S&P 500 the thesis is, de facto, confined to a single currency and a
single economic environment; results cannot be generalized to other equity markets without additional
evidence.
Because statutory tax rates and the availability of deductions vary widely across jurisdictions—and can
be further altered by an investor’s legal form, residence, or the use of tax-advantaged vehicles—this

covers January 2015 to June 2025. These windows capture the COVID-19 shock of early 2020 but lack
other major stress episodes such as the 2008 global financial crisis and the 2000–2002 dot-com collapse.

in origin or market structure from the pandemic.
Finally, computational resources impose practical boundaries on model complexity and calibration. All
portfolio simulations were executed on a consumer-grade HP laptop equipped with an AMD Ryzen 7
5700U CPU (1.8 GHz base clock) and 8 GB of RAM; a complete back-test consisting of sixty quarterly
rebalancing dates required up to three hours of computing time. Parameter fine tuning was off-loaded
to the Technical University of Denmark’s high-performance computing cluster (HPC), where access to
GPUs enabled parallel execution but did not remove the fundamental trade-off between search depth
and turnaround time. On average a portfolio with 60 rebalancing dates took approximately one hour
to finish. This computing time was similar between the private computer and the HPC, however the
HPC was able to run all experiments in parallel.
While these constraints are not expected to overturn the principal conclusions, they delimit the scope
of inference and are therefore acknowledged explicitly.

Literature Review (LR) is defined by Hart (2001) [40], as "the selection of available documents (both

In determining the most suitable LR approach for this thesis, both the Critical Literature Review
(CLR) and the Systematic Literature Review (SLR) were considered. On one hand, according to Hart
(2001) [40], "A Critical Literature Review involves evaluating existing research to identify strengths,
weaknesses, gaps, and contradictions, thereby establishing an informed argument or context for new
research". On the other hand, according to Petticrew and Roberts (2006) [54], "A Systematic Literature

2.4.1
Foundations of Portfolio Optimization
What is the rationale for using portfolio optimization, and how has Modern Portfolio
Theory (MPT) shaped its development?
The primary rationale for employing portfolio optimization in modern financial management stems
from the desire to move beyond simplistic asset allocation rules, such as equally weighted (EW) or
value-weighted (VW) portfolios. While EW portfolios offer inherent diversification and VW portfolios
naturally align with market capitalization, neither actively manages the inherent risk-return tradeoff of
investment decisions. Portfolio optimization, in contrast, provides a formal framework for rational asset
allocation under risk, allowing for a disciplined and quantifiable approach to balance expected return
with desired risk levels. This systematic methodology aims to maximize returns for a given level of
risk or minimize risk for a target return, enabling investors to make informed decisions that go beyond
arbitrary allocations.
The conceptual groundwork for portfolio optimization was fundamentally laid by Markowitz (1952)
[46] through his pioneering Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT). Markowitz’s seminal work introduced
the groundbreaking concept of the risk-return tradeoff, defining a portfolio’s risk quantitatively as its
projected variance σ2
p = wT Σw, where σ2
p is the portfolio variance, w is the column vector of asset
weights, and Σ is the covariance matrix of asset returns. This formulation led to the derivation of the
efficient frontier, a graphical representation of optimal portfolios that offer the highest expected return
for each level of risk, exemplified by portfolios such as the minimum variance portfolio or the tangency
portfolio. Early theoretical work further supported mean-variance optimality under specific conditions
on investor utility functions or asset return distributions Tobin (1958) [63].
Considering solely the mean return and variance of a portfolio is, of course, a simplification when
compared to including additional moments that might more completely describe the distribution of
returns. Consequently, researchers like Lee (1977) [42] (on investor utility with skewed returns), Kraus
et al. (1976) [13] (on incorporating skewness into portfolio choice), Fama and French (1965) [35] (on
the empirical properties of returns), and Elton and Gruber (1974) [32] (on portfolios with non-normally
distributed returns) offered alternative portfolio theories that aimed to incorporate more complex
characteristics, such as skewness or more realistic descriptions of return distributions. Nevertheless,
despite these conceptual alternatives and simplifications, mean-variance theory has remained the
foundational cornerstone of modern portfolio theory, continuing to guide the development of more
advanced optimization models. Its initial framework provided the indispensable language and tools for
a systematic approach to portfolio construction, setting the stage for subsequent advancements in risk
modeling and constraint handling.
What optimization techniques are available for portfolio construction, and what justifies
the use of Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP)?
A wide range of optimization techniques exists for portfolio construction, spanning from classical
approaches such as the Markowitz Mean-Variance model to more advanced, constraint-aware frameworks.
The original formulation by Markowitz is based on Quadratic Programming (QP), where portfolio risk

is quantified by return variance. While QP remains foundational in Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT),
its quadratic objective function introduces computational challenges and limits the model’s flexibility
in accommodating real-world investment constraints, such as cardinality rules, minimum trade sizes, or
sector allocation caps. These constraints often require discrete decisions that cannot be easily modeled
within a pure QP framework and instead call for a Mixed-Integer Quadratic Programming (MIQP)
approach, which is considerably more computationally demanding.
To overcome these challenges, practitioners and researchers increasingly employ Mixed-Integer Linear
Programming (MILP), which naturally accommodates both continuous variables (e.g., asset weights)
and discrete variables (e.g., lot sizes, number of positions). MILP offers a more flexible and scalable
framework for modeling the practical constraints encountered in institutional portfolio management.
Moreover, these models are capable of handling hundreds of assets and complex investment rules within
reasonable time frames, making MILP a highly practical choice. Furthermore, linear risk measures
such as the Mean-Absolute-Deviation (MAD), as described in Konno et al. (1991) [12] and Conditional
Value-at-Risk (CVaR) Rockafellar and Uryasev (2002) [58] integrate seamlessly into MILP models,
offering robust and regulator-aligned alternatives to variance-based risk metrics.
In addition, robust and distributionally robust optimization techniques can be seamlessly integrated into
MILP formulations to hedge against estimation uncertainty in key inputs such as expected returns and
the covariance matrix Fan et al. (2024) [7]. While these frameworks provide valuable protection against
model risk, they typically involve significant computational overhead and implementation complexity,
often requiring nested optimization or ambiguity set calibration—which made them impractical for the
scope of this thesis.
To conclude, MILP stands out as a highly adaptable and computationally tractable optimization
framework for real-world portfolio construction. Its ability to handle realistic constraints, combined
with compatibility with linear risk measures like CVaR, makes it the method of choice in this study.
What risk-based objectives are used in MILP portfolio models, and what is the preferred
one, given the described investor profile?
Within the MILP framework, several risk, based objective functions are commonly employed to quantify
and manage portfolio risk. These measures are designed to linearize the risk component, making the
optimization problem tractable. The Mean, Absolute Deviation (MAD), proposed by Konno et al.
(1991) [12], replaces variance with the mean of the absolute deviations of portfolio returns from their
expectation. This measure is linear, scales efficiently to large portfolios, and demonstrates empirical
robustness to heavy, tailed return distributions. However, its main drawback is that it penalizes upside
and downside deviations equally, which may not align with an investor’s asymmetric risk preferences. A
closely related extension is the Mean-Gini model, as proposed by Shalit and Yitzhaki (1994) [59], which
replaces variance with Gini’s mean difference as the risk measure. Like MAD, it preserves linearity
while better capturing inequality in the return distribution. However, the Gini coefficient is not always
coherent, as it may violate subadditivity—a key axiom of coherent risk measures—particularly for
certain return distributions, as discussed in Szego (2002) [62].
Other linear or mixed-integer linear risk measures have been proposed, but each presents limitations

that make them less suitable for the current study. For instance, the Conditional Drawdown-at-
Risk (CDaR) model Chekhlov, Uryasev, and Zabarankin (2005) [25] constrains the average depth
of drawdowns using a linear CVaR-like formulation on the running maximum equity curve. While
attractive for wealth-preservation mandates, CDaR introduces time-based variables that increase with

for high-frequency strategies with short horizons rather than monthly-rebalanced equity portfolios, as
considered here.
The Minimax Loss model, Young (1998) [66], minimizes the worst-case historical loss, offering robust
protection against extreme events. However, its extreme conservatism often leads to trivial solu-
tions—such as cash-heavy allocations or benchmark replication—that lack practical relevance for active
portfolio construction. As a result, it is more commonly used for stress testing rather than as a primary
optimization criterion.
Ratio-based models, such as the Sharpe or Sortino ratios, optimize reward-to-risk trade-offs. While they
are widely used in performance reporting, they are difficult to implement cleanly in optimization. The
Sortino ratio, which focuses on downside risk using semi-deviation, is linearizable but highly sensitive
to the investor-defined target return, often leading to unstable solutions. Furthermore, these models do
not offer direct control over tail risk and are generally not favored in institutional risk management
settings.
Lastly, Maximum Drawdown Minimization directly limits the worst peak-to-trough loss by introducing
binary variables to track regime shifts.4 Although attractive in theory, its computational cost grows
rapidly with longer horizons. As a result, it is mostly used in specialized strategies or as an add-on
constraint to simpler models.
To conclude, while several linear and mixed-integer linear risk measures exist—including CDaR,
Minimax Loss, ratio-based models, and maximum drawdown minimization—each presents important
limitations. These range from high computational complexity (as with CDaR and drawdown models), to
overly conservative allocations (Minimax Loss), or instability due to parameter sensitivity (Sharpe and
Sortino ratios). In contrast, Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) offers a coherent, convex, and regulator-
endorsed approach that directly targets expected tail losses beyond a given quantile β Rockafellar and
Uryasev (2002) [58]. Its intuitive interpretation as the “average of the worst β% outcomes” makes it
particularly well-suited for downside-focused portfolio construction. Given its mathematical soundness,
regulatory approval by Committee on Banking Supervision (2016) [26], empirical effectiveness, and
compatibility with mixed-integer formulations, CVaR was chosen as the core risk measure in this thesis.

Fundamental Score-Based Filtering
What is the empirical basis for fundamental filtering?
Fundamental analysis helps assess a company’s true value and financial health. Analysts use models like
discounted cash flow (DCF), residual income, and sum-of-the-parts to estimate intrinsic value, especially
for complex or diversified firms. This is complemented by financial statement analysis, including
ratio and trend evaluation of metrics like liquidity and profitability. Modern techniques incorporate
machine learning to enhance earnings forecasts and valuation accuracy. Qualitative factors—such as
management strength, competitive advantage, and industry trends—are also considered to provide a
complete investment picture.
Scoring models simplify financial data into interpretable indicators of risk or strength.
These systems aid screening and consistency but don’t directly indicate valuation. Classical models
remain widely used, though recent machine learning tools offer more adaptive, data-rich approaches to
support modern portfolio strategies.
Piotroski (2002) [55] developed a fundamental screening model known as the F-Score, designed to
distinguish strong from weak value stocks using historical financial statement data. The F-Score is
based on nine binary signals related to profitability, financial leverage/liquidity, and operating efficiency,
with the total score ranging from 0 (worst) to 9 (best). His empirical results demonstrate the power of
this approach: “A strategy of buying high F-Score firms and shorting low F-Score firms within the high
book-to-market (BM) portfolio generates a 23.3 percent annual return between 1976 and 1996” (p. 3).
Mohanram (2005) [48] introduces the G-Score, which serves as the growth stock counterpart to
Piotroski’s F-Score. This fundamental scoring model is specifically designed to evaluate growth firms,
which traditional value metrics (like book-to-market ratios) often fail to assess accurately. The G-Score
aggregates eight financial indicators across three dimensions: profitability (e.g., return on assets, cash
flow from operations), stability (e.g., earnings variability, accruals), and accounting conservatism (e.g.,
R&D and capital expenditures). The study shows that a long-short strategy—buying stocks with
high G-Scores and shorting those with low G-Scores—consistently generates significant excess returns.
Specifically, a one-point increase in G-Score is associated with a 3.9% increase in size-adjusted returns,
even after controlling for common risk factors such as size, book-to-market ratio, and momentum.
Economically, this translates into a 20.3% annual return spread between portfolios of high versus low
G-Score firms, highlighting the predictive power of financial statement analysis in identifying mispriced
growth stocks. Despite its effectiveness, the G-Score has seen less widespread adoption in practice
compared to other fundamental strategies.
Asness, Frazzini, and Pedersen (2017) [22] define quality by characteristics investors should be willing to
pay a higher price for, such as profitability, growth, and safety. Conversely, "junk" refers to unprofitable,
stagnant, or poorly managed companies. They introduce the Quality-Minus-Junk (QMJ) factor, a
strategy constructed by going long high-quality stocks and shorting low-quality stocks. This strategy
has consistently earned significant historical risk-adjusted returns in the United States and across 24
other countries. The paper demonstrates that high-quality stocks deliver significantly higher excess
returns than junk stocks, with even larger risk-adjusted returns (four-factor alphas). Furthermore, it

finds that a low "price of quality" predicts a high future return for the QMJ factor, highlighting its
predictive power. However, the QMJ factor may have exposure to other unidentified risk factors and
could potentially underperform during certain market regimes or periods of irrational exuberance for
"junk" stocks.
In conclusion, the empirical basis for fundamental filtering is robustly established by various academic
studies demonstrating its effectiveness in identifying mispriced securities and generating superior returns.
Models such as Piotroski’s F-Score, Mohanram’s G-Score, and Asness, Frazzini, and Pedersen’s QMJ
factor consistently show that systematic analysis of financial fundamentals can differentiate between
high-performing and underperforming stocks. Therefore, leveraging these empirically proven methods
of fundamental filtering provides a strong justification for their application in investment strategies.

In the previous section, it was identified that fundamental filtering aligns conceptually with identifying
strong investments. However, its explicit integration into portfolio optimization frameworks remains
surprisingly limited. A literature search on DTU Findit using the query ("fundamental analysis" OR

2.4.3
Modeling Returns
Should tail risk be estimated via Monte Carlo simulations or historical methods?
The estimation of Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) for portfolio optimization critically depends on
the input scenarios that model future asset returns. These scenarios can be generated through historical
methods or by fitting statistical distributions and subsequently performing Monte Carlo simulations
Yan (2018) [65].
The historical method generates scenarios directly from past observed returns. This approach involves
collecting a series of historical returns for the assets in the portfolio and using these past observations
as proxies for future possible scenarios Yan (2018) [65]. A key advantage of this method lies in its
model-free nature. It does not require assumptions about the underlying statistical distribution of
returns, thereby capturing any non-normal features, such as fat tails or skewness, exactly as they
appeared historically. However, its significant limitation for tail-risk estimation is its inherent reliance
on historical data. It can only generate scenarios that have occurred in the past, making it incapable
of modeling unprecedented extreme events or exploring a broader range of potential future market
conditions that have not yet been observed. This can lead to underestimating tail risk if the historical
period did not include sufficiently severe market downturns.
In contrast, Monte Carlo simulations involve generating a large number of random scenarios for asset
returns based on a specified statistical distribution or a set of distributions, along with estimated
parameters (e.g., mean, covariance) Yan (2018) [65]. This method allows for the creation of a vast
array of hypothetical future market states, extending beyond historical observations. Moreover, Monte
Carlo simulations offer greater flexibility in capturing complex features of return distributions, such as
fat tails, skewness, time-varying volatility, and the crucial interdependencies and correlations between
assets. However, Monte Carlo simulations are reliant on accurate distributional assumptions and
parameter estimation, introducing model risk and complexity. Furthermore, they are computationally
more intensive than historical methods, necessitating sufficient simulations for stable CVaR estimates.
To conclude, as CVaR focuses on the accurate estimation of tail risk for robust portfolio optimization,
the choice of scenario generation method is paramount. Monte Carlo simulations are notably superior
to historical methods in this regard, primarily due to their capacity to generate diverse and plausible
scenarios, including those extreme events that may not have occurred in historical data. This forward-
looking capability, allowing for the comprehensive capture of tail risk, makes Monte Carlo simulations
the preferred methodology for this study’s CVaR estimation.
What return modeling techniques exist and why choose Multivariate Normal, Multivariate
Normal with Exponentially Weighted Moving Average, and Multivariate Student t?
The Multivariate Normal (MVN) distribution was selected as a foundational scenario-generation
approach due to its central role in modern portfolio theory and its analytical tractability. As emphasized
by Bodie et al. (2014) [6], the assumption of joint normality of asset returns implies that any portfolio
composed of those assets will also exhibit normally distributed returns, an important property for
deriving closed-form solutions in risk and optimization models. Beyond theoretical elegance, the MVN
serves as a widely accepted benchmark against which more sophisticated models can be compared.

From a modeling perspective, the choice to use a multivariate (rather than univariate) distribution is
essential for capturing the dependence structure among assets, including co-movements and correlations,
which are critical for portfolio-level risk assessments. While empirical evidence often suggests that
financial returns deviate from strict normality—exhibiting characteristics such as skewness and excess
kurtosis Sinha and Karandikar (2012) [60]—the MVN remains a reasonable first approximation for
average market conditions.
The decision to incorporate the Multivariate Normal (MVN) distribution with Exponentially Weighted
Moving Average (EWMA) covariance estimation is rooted in addressing the dynamic nature of finan-
cial market volatility and correlations. Traditional covariance calculations for multivariate normal
distributions, such as the historical sample covariance over a fixed window, implicitly assume that all
observations within that window are equally relevant and that market dynamics are stationary. How-
ever, financial markets are inherently dynamic, exhibiting phenomena like volatility clustering—where
periods of high volatility tend to be followed by more high volatility, and vice versa. This means that
recent market movements often provide a more accurate reflection of current risk than older data points.
Consequently, static or equally weighted historical calculations can fail to capture these evolving risk
profiles, leading to inaccurate risk assessments and potentially suboptimal portfolio decisions.
This is precisely where the Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (EWMA) comes into play. EWMA
addresses the limitations of traditional methods by assigning exponentially declining weights to past
observations, thereby giving more importance to recent data. This methodology gained significant
traction through the RiskMetrics framework, developed and popularized by J.P. Morgan in the mid-1990s
Morgan and Reuters (1996) [50]. J.P. Morgan introduced RiskMetrics with the objectives of promoting
greater transparency in market risk and establishing a benchmark for market risk measurement. The
EWMA approach within RiskMetrics allows the model to be highly responsive to current market
conditions, effectively capturing phenomena like volatility clustering and adapting to non-stationary
market dynamics.
It is also worth noting that the EWMA model yields another significant advantage due to its close theo-
retical relationship with the Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity (GARCH(1,1))
model. Specifically, the EWMA model can be viewed as a constrained version of a GARCH(1,1) model
Morgan and Reuters (1996) [50]. This theoretical link provides a robust econometric foundation for
EWMA’s ability to model time-varying volatility and correlations, making it a powerful tool for scenario
generation in dynamic risk management contexts.
It is widely documented in financial econometrics that asset returns exhibit specific empirical regularities,
often referred to as stylized facts. Among these, excess kurtosis and fat tails are particularly prominent,
meaning that extreme market movements occur more frequently than predicted by the Gaussian
(Normal) distribution Cont (2001) [28]. As a consequence, relying solely on the Multivariate Normal
distribution, as discussed in previous sections, can lead to an underestimation of true tail risk. A more
appropriate alternative for capturing these heavy tails is the Multivariate Student-t distribution (MVT).
Its inherent ability to model excess kurtosis makes it particularly well-suited for applications like CVaR
optimization, where accurate estimation of extreme losses is paramount, thereby leading to more robust
and realistic risk assessments.

While the Multivariate Student-t distribution offers these desirable properties for modeling financial
returns, its practical application, particularly fitting it to large datasets, can be computationally
extremely challenging. However, detailed frameworks exist to overcome this complexity. Notably, the
methodology proposed by Hu et al. (2010) [9] provides a sophisticated, albeit intricate, approach that
makes the proper fitting of a multivariate Student-t distribution feasible for a substantial number of
assets, such as in the range of 400-500 stocks, which was used and described in section 3.6.4. This
demonstrates that despite its computational demands, the benefits of the Multivariate Student-t for
accurate tail risk modeling can be realized through advanced techniques.
Beyond these selected distributions, other models offer additional capabilities but introduce increased
complexity. The Skewed Multivariate Student-t distribution, for instance, can capture both fat tails
and asymmetry (skewness) in returns, offering an even more comprehensive empirical fit Jondeau and
Rockinger (2003) [41]. However, its parameter estimation becomes substantially more complex and
computationally intensive, especially in high-dimensional settings, making it less practical for very large
portfolios within the scope of this study. Jump diffusion models, such as those introduced by Merton
(1976) [47], allow for the modeling of sudden, discontinuous price movements (jumps) in addition to
continuous diffusion. While these models can provide a more realistic representation of crisis events,
their increased complexity in calibration and simulation often outweighs their incremental benefits for
the primary objective of CVaR estimation in this context.
Why GARCH? Which GARCH models exist, and why GARCH cDCC, DCC and GJR?
The GARCH (Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity) was originally introduced by
Engle (1982) [33] as ARCH and later generalized by Bollerslev (1986) [24], has become a cornerstone
in modeling the time-varying volatility of financial time series. At its core, GARCH assumes that
volatility is not constant but conditional on past information, which aligns more closely with empirical
evidence from financial markets. This framework allows the conditional variance at time t to depend
on both past squared returns (shocks) and its own lagged values, capturing the phenomenon known as
volatility clustering—a feature where large changes in asset prices tend to be followed by large changes
(of either sign), and small changes follow small ones.
Such clustering is a violation of the homoskedasticity assumption inherent in traditional models like
those based on the multivariate normal (MVN) distribution, which assume constant variance over time.
As shown in Figure 2, periods of market turbulence demonstrate persistent high variance, while calm
periods reflect sustained low volatility. This persistence in volatility shocks is a hallmark of financial
time series and motivates the need for conditional heteroskedastic models like GARCH.
Another key advantage of GARCH models is their tractability and adaptability. The basic GARCH(1,1)
model, often sufficient in practice, is simple to estimate and interpret, yet powerful enough to explain
many empirical regularities in asset return data. Over time, however, more sophisticated variants have
been developed to address specific limitations of the original GARCH. For instance, standard GARCH
models are symmetric in the sense that they respond equally to positive and negative shocks. This
symmetry fails to capture the leverage effect, where negative returns often lead to disproportionately
higher future volatility compared to positive ones. Models like GJR-GARCH Glosten, Jagannathan,
and Runkle (1993) [37] extend GARCH by introducing an asymmetry term to better account for this
Page 16
==PAGE:30==
Portfolio Optimization using Mixed-Integer and Linear Programming
behavioral phenomenon.
In addition, univariate GARCH models, while effective in modeling individual asset volatilities, fall
short in portfolio or multivariate contexts where co-movement and time-varying correlation among
assets are critical. This has led to the development of multivariate GARCH models, such as Constant
Conditional Correlation (CCC-GARCH) and Dynamic Conditional Correlation (DCC-GARCH), which
separately model conditional variances and the evolution of correlations. DCC, in particular, is widely
used in financial econometrics due to its ability to adapt to changing correlation structures over time,
which is crucial for dynamic portfolio optimization and risk forecasting.
Still, models like DCC can struggle with convergence and scalability in high-dimensional settings. To
address this, the composite DCC (cDCC) model was proposed to improve estimation stability and
computational efficiency, especially in large asset universes (e.g., N ≈400), as shown in recent studies.
Despite these improvements, even advanced GARCH models are not without limitations. They often
perform poorly under time aggregation, are ill-suited to capture long-memory effects in volatility, and
can suffer from overfitting in high-dimensional settings unless appropriately regularized.
To conclude, GARCH was included to overcome volatility asymmetry (described in Figure 2) and
model time-varying correlations that distributions like MVN ignore.

GARCH CCC was excluded for its inability to model dynamic correlations, while ARCH and standard
univariate GARCH were excluded because they lack multivariate structure. Instead, the univariate
standard GARCH is used as a pre-step, which the DCC builds on top. Finally, other multivariate
GARCH models (e.g., BEKK, VEC, Scalar MGARCH) were excluded since their main advantages
were assumed to be represented through GARCH DCC and GARCH GJR.
It is also worth noting that despite their advantages, GARCH models exhibit limitations such as
poor performance under time aggregation, inability to capture long-memory volatility effects, and
susceptibility to overfitting in high-dimensional settings without strong regularization Connor, Goldberg,
and Korajczyk (2010) [27].

3.6.1
Specifications for Monte Carlo simulations
All return data used in this analysis refer to log returns, which are standard in financial time series
modeling due to their time-additive properties and compatibility with statistical assumptions. It
was also assumed that the data were i.i.d. (independent and identically distributed) for Multivariate
Normality (Both with equal and exponential weights), in accordance with JP Morgan’s RiskMetrics
methodology. Additionally, the mean vector and covariance matrix of asset returns were estimated
using a 5-year window of historical data for all distributions. This was done to balance between
capturing long-term trends and maintaining relevance to current market dynamics, assuming to provide
a sufficiently large sample size to estimate covariances robustly while avoiding excessive dilution from
outdated data. These inputs were computed for each first trading day of the month (as provided by
CRSP) using a rolling window of 5 years. Only assets with complete data availability throughout the
5-year window and confirmed S&P 500 membership at that point in time were included, as mentioned
in section 1.1. This filtering process excluded, on average, approximately 8% of the companies from the
analysis in each rebalancing date. However, this trade-off was considered acceptable, as the goal was to
prioritize the reliability and stability of the inputs over maximizing sample size. Including incomplete
or short-lived return records would have led to noisy and unreliable simulations, undermining the
quality of the risk modeling.
A total of 5000 Monte Carlo simulations were used to approximate the distribution of future portfolio
returns. This number was chosen as a balance between computational efficiency and statistical accuracy.
In the context of CVaR (Conditional Value-at-Risk) and other risk-sensitive optimization methods,
a larger number of simulations improves the stability of tail estimates and reduces sampling error
Glasserman (2004) [36]. However, beyond a certain threshold (typically in the low thousands), the
marginal benefits of additional simulations begin to diminish. This aligns with the convergence properties
of Monte Carlo estimators, which improve at a rate of O(1/
√
N) [36]. As such, 5000 simulations were
considered sufficient to achieve robust results without incurring excessive computational cost. Empirical
tests showed that increasing the number of simulations to 7,500 or 10,000 led to negligible differences
in portfolio composition and risk metrics, while increasing computation time. The simulations were
carried out over a 21-day horizon, rather than 30 days, to better approximate monthly returns given
a standard year of 252 trading days (Note that 252 days divided by 12 months equals 21 days per
month). Furthermore, visual inspection of the simulated return distributions indicated that 30-day
horizons introduced longer tails (i.e., more extreme outliers), whereas 21-day simulations more closely
resembled the distribution of observed historical monthly returns.
To properly take advantage of the benefits of using Monte Carlo over the historical method (e.g.
simulate future events that might not have occurred historically), it is crucial to determine the most
appropriate way to model returns and therefore the tail risk. In this way, the next sections focus on
the methodology for using Multivariate Normal, Multivariate Normal with Exponentially Weighted
Moving Average (EWMA), Multivariate Student t and GARCH models.

3.6.3
Multivariate Normal with Exponentially Weighted Moving Average
Traditional covariance calculations, such as the previous historical sample covariance over a fixed
window, implicitly assume that all observations within that window are equally relevant and that market
dynamics are stationary. However, financial markets are inherently dynamic, exhibiting phenomena
like volatility clustering—where periods of high volatility tend to be followed by more high volatility,
and vice-versa. This means that recent market movements often provide a more accurate reflection of
current risk than older data points. Consequently, static or equally weighted historical calculations
can fail to capture these evolving risk profiles, leading to inaccurate risk assessments and potentially
suboptimal portfolio decisions.
This is where the Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (EWMA) comes into play. EWMA addresses
the limitations of traditional methods by assigning exponentially declining weights to past observations,
giving more importance to recent data. The methodology gained significant traction through the
RiskMetrics framework, developed by Morgan and Reuters (1996) [50].

3.6.4
Multivariate Student t
It is well-documented in the literature that financial asset returns exhibit excess kurtosis and fat tails,
making the multivariate Normal distribution an inadequate model, as seen in previous test results.
A more appropriate alternative could be the multivariate Student t distribution, which captures the
heavy tails observed in real-world return data. In this way, since CVAR optimization focuses on tail
risk the Multivariate Student t would allow to better model this risk and achieve more accurate results.
To do so, it was used a framework inspired by methodology proposed by Hu et al. (2010) [9], which
suggests using elliptical distributions such as the multivariate Student-t to better capture fat tails in
financial data. To do so, they suggested a two step approach:
1. Univariate volatility filtering with GARCH(1,1) models.
2. Multivariate distribution fitting using the Student-t distribution via the Expectation-Maximization
(EM) algorithm.
In order to reliably fit a multivariate Student’s t distribution (the second step in the approach), the
input data must approximate an i.i.d. (independent and identically distributed) process. Raw log
returns often violate this assumption due to volatility clustering, i.e., periods where returns exhibit
persistently high or low variance. To address this, a univariate GARCH(1,1) model with normally
distributed innovations is fitted to each asset return series, and the standardized residuals are extracted.
These residuals serve as the de-volatilized returns used for multivariate modeling (first step of the
approach).

Volatility Scaling of Simulated Returns
After fitting the multivariate Student-t distribution on standardized residuals obtained from GARCH
filtering, it was necessary to rescale simulated return paths to reflect asset-specific volatility levels.
The multivariate distribution fitted via the EM algorithm captures the dependency structure and tail
behavior, but by construction, its marginals are standardized with unit variance. As a result, direct
simulation from this distribution would produce return paths that misrepresent the true volatility of
each asset.
To address this, each vector simulated from the fitted multivariate Student-t distribution was multiplied
element-wise by a volatility vector obtained from univariate GARCH(1,1) models. Specifically, the
most recent estimated conditional standard deviations (i.e., the final column of each GARCH volatility
matrix) were extracted and used for the volatility scaling. These volatilities were previously computed
in R using the rugarch package and exported to CSV format for each monthly fitting date as a
byproduct of the EM algorithm. It is worth noting that it was used 2 scaling approaches, one that
used the most recent GARCH volatility estimate (to reflect the most current market conditions at each
rebalancing point), and other that took the average volatility over the past 21 trading days (with the
goal of providing a smoother and more robust scaling factor and aligning with the rebalancing period
of one month with 21 trading days).

3.6.5
GARCH
To estimate the conditional covariance matrix of asset returns without relying entirely on a static five-
year historical window, a GARCH-based forecasting approach is implemented. Specifically, the model
uses the univariate standard GARCH model (sGARCH) for each asset return series and then combine
them within a multivariate composite cDCC (Dynamic Conditional Correlation) framework. This setup
allows to dynamically model time-varying volatility and correlation structures in a computationally
tractable way. The sGARCH specification was chosen for its simplicity and efficiency in high-dimensional
settings. The error distribution is assumed to follow a multivariate normal distribution, consistent
with the assumptions of the Monte Carlo simulation framework used later. The portfolio contains
more than 300 assets, so a full likelihood-based DCC fit on the N(N −1)/2 correlations becomes
computationally prohibitive. Therefore the composite DCC (cDCC) variant of Aielli (2013) is used,
implemented in rugarch as model = "cDCC". cDCC preserves the standard DCC recursion while
factorising the likelihood, which cuts both memory requirements and runtime by an order of magnitude.
Variance targeting is enabled to reduce the number of free parameters and enhance numerical stability.
Specifically, in each GARCH(1,1) equation the intercept is replaced by ωi = ¯hi (1 −αi −βi), where ¯hi
is the unconditional sample variance of asset i. ARMA(0,0) mean specification is kept and, consistent
with evidence that daily expected returns are negligible relative to volatility, fix the mean at µ = 0 by
setting include.mean = FALSE. All series are first standardized to unit variance for estimation. The
forecasted correlation matrices are then re-scaled by the original sample standard deviations so that
Ht is expressed in the units of raw log-returns.
For each rebalancing date, the cDCC-GARCH model is fitted using five years of historical data up to
the day before the rebalancing date. The model then forecasts a sequence of 21 conditional covariance
matrices—one for each trading day in the upcoming month. These forecasts are used to simulate daily
return shocks in the Monte Carlo engine. The function dccforecast with n.ahead = 21 allows for the
generation of this forward-looking covariance structure.
Because daily shocks in the simulation are drawn from time-varying distributions, having access to a
distinct covariance matrix for each day significantly enhances the realism of the return paths. Each
forecasted matrix is rescaled to match the original units of the data. For every rebalancing period,
5,000 multivariate normal return paths are simulated, matching the structure of the earlier Monte
Carlo experiments and enabling a consistent comparison of results.
This approach balances model complexity and computational feasibility, allowing for robust forward-
looking risk estimation across a large cross-section of assets.

3.7.1
Value at Risk (VaR) and Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR)
Value at Risk (VaR) is a widely used risk measure in financial risk management that estimates the
maximum expected loss over a given time horizon at a specified confidence level. Formally, the VaRα at
level α is the α-quintile of the loss distribution. For instance, a 5% VaR of −10% implies that there is
only a 5% probability that losses will exceed 10% over the selected period. However, VaR has notable
limitations—it does not provide any insight into the magnitude of losses beyond this threshold and
lacks sub-additivity, which means it may underestimate risk when combining portfolios.
To overcome these shortcomings, Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR), also known as Expected Shortfall,
was introduced as a more informative and theoretically sound risk measure. CVaR represents the
expected loss given that the loss exceeds the VaR threshold. In essence, it answers the question:
“If things go badly, how bad can the average outcome be?” This makes CVaR especially useful in
tail-risk-sensitive applications. According to Mansini et al. (2014) [14], CVaR is a coherent risk
measure—it satisfies the four desirable properties of risk measures: monotonicity, subadditivity, positive
homogeneity, and translation invariance. The coherence of CVaR as a risk measure is discussed in
Section 7.4. This coherence ensures that CVaR behaves well in portfolio construction, particularly when
assessing diversification benefits and optimizing under uncertainty, unlike VAR. Figure 16 illustrates
the distinction between these two risk measures on a normally distributed return profile with a mean
of -2% and standard deviation of 5% .

This second model extends the previous CVaR optimization by incorporating transaction costs,
introducing a trade-off between minimizing downside risk and reducing turnover-related expenses. The
decision to separate the model into two stages is intentional and reflects the assumption that the investor
prefers to construct the initial portfolio purely based on the optimal risk-return trade-off, without the
influence of transaction costs. Starting with transaction costs would prevent the portfolio from ever
reaching a fully optimized CVaR allocation, as trading frictions would constrain asset selection from
the outset. By first establishing a clean, risk-optimal baseline, transaction costs can then be considered
in subsequent periods to minimize losses, while accounting for real-world trading frictions.
To do so, three new decision variables are introduced: tj (a binary indicator of whether asset j is
traded), γj (the trade volume in USD), and tcj (the transaction cost for asset j)—all required to reflect
Revolut’s pricing structure as described in Section 3.7.
While both zj and tj are binary variables associated with asset selection and trading, they serve distinct
Page 50
==PAGE:64==
Portfolio Optimization using Mixed-Integer and Linear Programming
purposes in the model. For example, consider a case where asset j was already part of the portfolio
in the previous period with a weight of 10%, and it remains in the portfolio at the same weight. In
this case, zj = 1 (the asset is held), but tj = 0 since no trade occurred. Conversely, if asset j was not
held previously but is now bought at a positive weight, both zj = 1 and tj = 1. Similarly, if an asset
is completely sold off (weight reduced to zero), then zj = 0 and tj = 1. This distinction allows the
model to track not just the presence of assets in the portfolio, but also when trading activity—and
thus transaction costs—should be applied.
Constraints (16) and (17) define γj as the absolute change in the monetary allocation of asset j between
periods. Constraints involving tj ensure that any nonzero trade triggers the activation of transaction
costs. These costs, are defined as the maximum between a fixed fee FC · tj and a variable fee V C · γj,
consistent with Revolut’s standard commission scheme.
Unlike the initial model, where the portfolio weights sum to 1, this formulation allocates total capital
C between asset investments and transaction costs, as captured by the adjusted budget constraint (15).
This allows for scenarios where less than 100% of the capital is invested due to incurred trading fees.
The return and risk constraints are also modified accordingly. The CVaR constraint (22) penalizes
losses net of transaction costs in each scenario, and the expected return constraint (23) requires that
the return after deducting transaction fees exceeds a predefined threshold.
To ensure continuity over time, the asset universe is expanded to include all assets that might appear
in any rebalancing period (approximately 600 stocks), even if some were not present in the current
date’s S&P 500. The eligibility of each asset is governed by A0j, indicating availability of historical
return data, and B0j, which filters assets based on fundamental criteria.
This modeling approach allows for realistic multi-period portfolio management by explicitly quantifying
the performance impact of transaction costs. For example, if an investor allocates $900 of a $1,000
portfolio to assets yielding 1% and spends $100 on transaction costs, the net return is 900×0.01−100 =
−91, illustrating how trading frictions can erode gains. The model applies this logic systematically
across assets and scenarios.
It is worth noting that Constraint 13 was not included in the model without transaction costs for two
main reasons. First, the restriction imposed by A0j is implicitly handled, as the stock universe in the
model without transaction costs is already limited to the assets available at each rebalancing point.
This differs from the model with transaction costs, where a larger, consistent universe is needed across
time. Second, while the B0j constraint is not explicitly enforced in the initial model, it is assumed that
the investor prefers to begin with a pure CVaR risk-based optimization when constructing the initial
portfolio and only after apply fundamental filters.

Tickers and Permnos
Most data for this thesis is derived from the website Wharton Research Data Service through an API
from the Wharton database. It combines access to CRSP for price data and beta as well as Compustat
database for fundamental data and the consumer price index.
Additionally, there was the objective to obtain data for the constituent companies of the S&P 500 at
specific historical points in time, as mentioned in section 1.1. As WRDS did not provide access to
this information for past index compositions, Bloomberg was selected as the primary data source for
identifying the index composition throughout time.
Using Bloomberg’s ticker symbols as the primary identifier for retrieving historical data from CRSP
was assumed to be suboptimal due to their lack of stability and uniqueness over time. In other words,
tickers are not persistent identifiers, since they can be reassigned, modified, or even reused by entirely
different companies. For instance, the ticker symbol A is currently associated with Agilent Technologies
but was historically used by other entities, such as Anaconda Copper. Such reuse can lead to significant
data inaccuracies, particularly in this study where consistency and coherence across time are essential.
In contrast, the CRSP permanent number PERMNO offers a robust alternative. Each PERMNO
serves as a unique and time-invariant identifier assigned to a specific security. This identifier remains
fixed throughout the life of the security, regardless of corporate actions such as name changes, mergers,
or de-listings. As a result, PERMNO ensures accurate and consistent referencing across different time
periods.
In the present analysis, PERMNO is used not only to retrieve reliable CRSP data but also to facilitate
integration with firm-level data from the Compustat database. Since Compustat employs the GVKEY
identifier, the use of permno in conjunction with the CRSP-Compustat Merged (CCM) link file allows
for precise matching between the two datasets.
To identify the PERMNO corresponding to each Bloomberg ticker, both the tickers and their associated
8-digit CUSIPs (as provided by Bloomberg) were used to query the CRSP database. The results were
then filtered to match the relevant year based on the Bloomberg ticker, and the appropriate PERMNO
was selected accordingly. For companies without a standard ticker—i.e., those listed only with an
internal Bloomberg identifier (e.g., IHS Markit Ltd: 2499073D)—the associated 8-digit CUSIP was
used to retrieve the PERMNO. If neither a valid ticker (beyond the internal Bloomberg code) nor a
corresponding CUSIP was available, or if the ticker could not be found in CRSP and no CUSIP was
provided to verify it, the company was excluded from the analysis. In total, this filtering process led to
the exclusion of 16 companies in total from the analysis over the 2015-2024 period: 15 were excluded
from the backtesting set and 6 from the validation set.

Merging of permno and GVKEY:
As discussed in Section 4.1, stocks were identified using permnos. However, the permno identifier is
not used within the Compustat database for company-level financial data. This distinction exists for

To address this, Compustat uses its own unique company identifier: the GVKEY. The link between

linked to multiple GVKEYs, each corresponding to different time periods. In these instances, manual
inspection of the link’s start and end dates was performed, and duplicate mappings were resolved by
retaining only the ones whose dates were more compatible with the rebalancing time frame of this
paper (2015-2024).

4.3.2
Missing values
To accommodate missing values in fundamental variables, a progressive imputation approach was
applied. The core idea is to fill empty cells using the average of previously reported values. This is
done iteratively: in each step, the algorithm expands the historical window, using an increasing number
of past quarters to compute a rolling mean.
The process begins by checking whether the value can be imputed using just the most recent past
quarter. If that is not sufficient, it proceeds to use the average of the last two quarters, then three, and
so on—up to a maximum of eight quarters, which corresponds to two years of historical data.
The choice of two years (8 quarters) balances data availability and relevance. On one hand, using a
longer window improves the chances of having enough valid historical data to fill missing values. On
the other hand, financial performance can change significantly over time, so limiting the window to two
years ensures that the imputed values are still reflective of recent company behavior. This trade-off
helps avoid biasing the data with outdated information while still maximizing data completeness.
For firms with insufficient historical records—i.e., those that have fewer than two years of quar-
terly data—missing values remain unfilled. This avoids imputing based on too little or potentially
unrepresentative data.

5.1.2
Value-weighted portfolios with transaction cost
To enable a fair comparison across strategies, this section also includes value-weighted portfolios with
transaction costs. The transaction cost logic mirrors that used in the optimization framework: for each
trade, the model applies the higher of a fixed $1 fee or 0.25% of the transaction value. These costs
directly reduce the available capital for the next investment period, making it essential to understand
the mechanism before interpreting the results of the value-weighted experiments.
The methodology used to compute combined score portfolios remains unchanged in this experiment.
However, the value-weighted logic, combined with transaction costs, has important implications: if
even one stock enters or exits the portfolio, all weights must be rebalanced accordingly. As a result,
particularly for portfolios with low thresholds (and thus many changes), the model frequently incurs
nearly $500 in transaction costs per period due to this constant rebalancing.
Therefore, the focus in this section is on higher thresholds, where portfolio turnover is lower and the
results more realistically reflect investable strategies. Additionally, these experiments serve to establish
a baseline for comparing value-weighted portfolios with optimization-based portfolios that include
transaction costs, discussed in later chapters.

5.2
Optimization without Transaction Cost: µ0, β and norm
In this subsection, parameter optimization is carried out on the model without transaction costs and
without applying any stock pre-filtering. The aim is to first identify the best combination of µ0, β,
and return modeling techniques. Then, using these results, the optimal threshold and scoring method
are determined a posteriori. While, in theory, both steps should be optimized jointly, doing so would
require running the code for 30 days in the HPC (8×8×7×63/40/24)—where the first three dimensions
correspond to the number of scores and thresholds (excluding the last threshold, which failed cardinality
constraints), 63 combinations arise from the variations in µ0 and β, divided by 40 models which can be
run simultaneously with the HPC and 24 hours to obtain the number of days. This sequential approach
is also logically sound, as the choice of µ0 and β, and return model primarily governs the risk-return
trade-off, while the threshold and score selection fine-tune stock selection. As such, this simplification
is not expected to materially impact the overall conclusions. It is also worth noting that all return
modeling techniques were considered, with the exception of GARCH-based models, which failed to
converge when applied to the full universe of unfiltered stocks.

5.9
Statistical Tests for Experimental Desing
While performance metrics such as average returns and drawdowns provide a general overview of each
strategy’s behavior, they do not indicate whether observed differences are statistically meaningful or
could plausibly occur due to chance. To complement these metrics, statistical hypothesis testing was
conducted on the monthly return differentials between strategies. The main goal of this section is
to aid in answering the three research questions with additional statistical rigor. Note that a more
detailed explanation of these tests can be found in Appendix Section 7.6. That section also highlights
the importance of using a sufficient number of data points to obtain reliable p-values. For instance, in
the Paired One-Sided t-Test, the test statistic includes the term
p
1/n in the denominator, meaning
that with fewer observations, the resulting p-values tend to be higher, reflecting increased statistical
uncertainty. This underscores why relying exclusively on in-sample or out-of-sample data may lead to
misleading conclusions.
Two main tests were employed:
• Paired one-sided t-test: A parametric test used when the distribution of return differences
satisfies normality and independence assumptions.
• Wilcoxon signed-rank test: A non-parametric alternative that does not require the assumption
of normality, making it more robust for skewed or heavy-tailed distributions.
The null hypothesis in all cases is that the alternative strategy performs no better than the benchmark
strategy, i.e., the mean or median of the return differences is less than or equal to zero (for Paired
one-sided t-test and Wilcoxon signed-rank test, respectively) :
• Null hypothesis (H0): µalt−benchmark ≤0
• Alternative (H1): µalt−benchmark > 0
Page 109
==PAGE:123==
Portfolio Optimization using Mixed-Integer and Linear Programming
It was conducted diagnostic tests to assess the validity of the assumptions underlying the t-test:
• Normality: Both the Anderson-Darling test and the Shapiro-Wilk test were used to evaluate
whether return differences follow a normal distribution.
– H0: Return differences are normally distributed. Therefore Paired one-sided t test is used.
– Rejection of H0 implies that parametric tests such as the t-test may not be appropriate. In
this case, for a 95% confidence interval, Wilcoxon signed rank test is applied.
• Autocorrelation: The Ljung-Box test was used to detect serial dependence in the return
differences.
– H0: Return differences are independently distributed (no autocorrelation).
– Rejection of H0 implies potential bias in the results of tests assuming independence.
These tests produce a p-value, which reflects the probability of observing results as extreme as those
in the sample, assuming the null hypothesis is true. For instance, a p-value of 0.03 means there is only
a 3% chance that the observed outperformance occurred randomly. A common threshold is 5% (i.e.,
p-value < 0.05), suggesting that such an outcome is unlikely due to noise and is instead statistically
significant.

6
Conclusions
This thesis investigates the integration of mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) with fundamental
factor-based screening as a pre-filtering mechanism in portfolio optimization. Specifically, it examines
the impact of applying fundamental filters—namely the Piotroski F-Score, the Mohanram G-Score, and
the Quality-Minus-Junk (QMJ) Score—on key portfolio performance metrics, including total return,
Sharpe ratio, and maximum drawdown.
To assess the effectiveness of the proposed approach, a series of structured experiments were conducted
to identify systematic improvements resulting from variations in key model parameters. To benchmark
performance against the S&P 500 index, portfolios were constructed by applying different threshold
levels to the fundamental scores. Given that the Piotroski F-Score and Mohanram G-Score were
originally designed to target firms in the top and bottom 30% of the book-to-market (BM) ratio
distribution, two additional portfolio types were developed. The first portfolio type consisted of 50%
high BM firms evaluated using the F-Score and 50% low BM firms evaluated using the G-Score. The
second type extended this logic by applying the F-Score to the top 30% BM firms, the G-Score to
the bottom 30%, and the Quality-Minus-Junk (QMJ) Score to the remaining firms in the middle BM
segment.
The first research question aimed to identify which fundamental score, in combination with which
threshold, yields the most promising performance relative to the S&P 500, based on various risk-return
metrics. Among the tested configurations, the Piotroski F-Score with a threshold of 8 demonstrated
the strongest performance. Without considering transaction costs, over the in-sample period from
2015 to 2020, this strategy achieved a cumulative return of 141%, a Sharpe ratio of 1.289, a Sortino
ratio of 2.477, and a maximum drawdown of -16%. The portfolio returns were found to be statistically
significantly higher than those of the S&P 500, with a p-value of 0.00175. This strong performance
was confirmed in the out-of-sample period, during which the F-Score 8 portfolio achieved a cumulative
return of 175%, a Sharpe ratio of 1.203, and a maximum drawdown of -18%. However, when transaction
costs were incorporated, the portfolio’s performance declined markedly, highlighting the substantial
impact of transaction costs on the net returns of the value-weighted strategy.
The second research question examined which fundamental score, along with its respective threshold,
is most effective when used as a pre-filtering mechanism for portfolio construction via a MILP-
CVaR optimization framework.
The primary objective was to evaluate whether portfolios built
from fundamentally screened stocks and optimized for downside risk could outperform the S&P 500
benchmark. Excluding transaction costs, the filtered and optimized portfolio—based on the F-Score
with a threshold of 8—significantly outperformed the benchmark, achieving a total return of 152%, a
Sharpe ratio of 1.394, a Sortino ratio of 2.764, and a maximum drawdown of -16%. This result was
statistically significant, with a p-value of 0.0296. Notably, all performance metrics surpassed those of
the S&P 500 over the same period, which recorded a total return of only 74% and a similar level of
risk, with a maximum drawdown of -14%.
When transaction costs were introduced, performance declined as expected; however, the portfolio

The third research question sought to determine the most suitable return modeling technique for
generating Monte Carlo simulation scenarios within a MILP-CVaR portfolio optimization framework.
The analysis compared several approaches based on their impact on final portfolio performance, with
the objective of identifying which method yields the most realistic and robust return distributions for
risk-aware optimization. Specifically, the following models were tested: multivariate normal (MVN),
exponentially weighted MVN with three decay factors (λ ∈{0.99, 0.97, 0.94}), multivariate Student
t-distribution (MVT) with volatility scaling (using both the most recent and average 21-day volatilities),
and three GARCH-based models. When applying a single modeling approach across the entire multi-
period optimization and rebalancing framework, the MVN model emerged as the most promising overall,
capturing a good trade-off between total return and risk adjusted performance. While GARCH-based
models also showed potential, the statistical evidence supporting their outperformance remained
inconclusive. The analysis also highlighted the potential value of the MVT with 21-day volatility
scaling for short-term risk management. Its ability to capture fat tails and higher kurtosis, combined
with a comparatively lower maximum drawdown compared to all other return modeling techniques,
positions MVT as a strong candidate for return modeling during periods of heightened market risk.
However, this last hypothesis was beyond the scope of this thesis and remains to be empirically tested
and validated.
In conclusion, the combination of applying a fundamental filter using the F-Score (threshold 8) or
G-Score (threshold 7), followed by portfolio construction through a MILP-CVaR optimization model
with a risk level of β = 0.15 and a minimum expected return µ0 = 0.0375, and using multivariate
normal (MVN) return modeling, emerges as a promising strategy to outperform the market.
6.1
Future Work
This thesis has demonstrated that combining fundamental score screening with scenario–driven CVaR
optimization can generate materially superior risk–adjusted returns for a student investor. Nonetheless,
several dimensions merit deeper investigation before operational deployment. The avenues outlined
below refine, rather than broaden, the research agenda; each flows directly from empirical observations
made in the preceding chapters.
Alternative return modeling techniques
Section 5.4.1 showed that portfolio performance is highly
sensitive to the choice of return–generation model. Extending the menu beyond the Student-t, EWMA,
Normal and GARCH distributions could therefore unlock additional gains. As stated previously, the
student-t and GARCH models can be further explored to find better fitting parameters. Other methods
could include (i) non-parametric bootstrapping—both block and circular—to preserve serial dependence
Page 115
==PAGE:129==
Portfolio Optimization using Mixed-Integer and Linear Programming
without distributional assumptions, and (ii) supervised or self-supervised machine learning approaches
that learn joint return structures directly from data. Each technique should be tested in combination
with the F-, G- and QMJ-Score filters to isolate interaction effects, using the same CVaR MILP back
end for comparability. It could also be tested to use forecasting techniques to estimate the mean
returns, and then apply the covariance structure to perform the simulations.
Rebalancing frequency:
The experiments employed monthly rebalancing over a five–year horizon.
Because the underlying accounting variables update only quarterly, most scores remain static between
10-Q and 10-K releases. A natural extension is to align the optimization schedule with those reporting
cycles—i.e., quarterly trades—to test whether reduced turnover and fresher fundamentals offset the
loss of reactivity. Conversely, shifting to an annual frequency would exploit the superior reliability of
year-end statements and minimize transaction costs still further. A systematic comparison of monthly,
quarterly and annual schedules—holding all other parameters constant—would clarify the risk–return
trade-off between informational timeliness and trading frictions.
Broader investable universe:
The current study is restricted to the S&P 500, thereby focusing
on U.S. large-capitalization equities. Replicating the framework on mid-, small- and even nano-cap
universes could reveal whether fundamental-score filtering and CVaR control are equally effective
where analyst coverage is sparse and mispricing arguably larger. Parallel tests on major non-U.S.
benchmarks (Dow Jones, Nasdaq Composite, FTSE 100, Nikkei 225, DAX 40) would gauge currency and
market-structure robustness. Finally, constructing a single globally diversified portfolio—coupled with
systematic FX-hedging overlays—would show whether geographic breadth enhances risk mitigation or
merely adds new sources of volatility.
Regime-switching integration:
Performance differentials observed in Section 5.4.1 showed that the
return modeling techniques differ depending on market environments. Embedding a regime-detection
layer—based on macroeconomic indicators, volatility breaks or technical signals—and switching dy-
namically among pre-calibrated scenario models could attenuate tail losses in bear phases without
sacrificing upside in bull markets. Markov-switching filters, hidden semi-Markov models or simpler
threshold rules all warrant exploration, provided their parameters are learned strictly out of sample to
avoid look-ahead bias. Additionally, hedging strategies in this sense could also be applied.
Alternative fundamental filtering strategies:
This thesis employed three established scoring
methods to filter stocks, grounded in academic literature. However, numerous other fundamental
screening approaches merit further exploration. The current framework lays the foundation for extending
the analysis to include both conventional and unconventional strategies. In particular, non-academic
methods—such as Peter Lynch’s investment screening principles—remain underexplored in the empirical
literature and could offer valuable insights when assessed using the portfolio optimization framework
developed in this study.
By addressing these extensions, future research can more finely tune the interplay between information

content, model risk and implementation cost, thereby bringing the proposed framework closer to
real-world adoption.

7.4
Appendix: Proof of CVAR being a coherent risk measure
Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR), also known as Expected Shortfall, is widely recognized in the
literature as a coherent risk measure. A coherent risk measure must satisfy four axioms: monotonicity,
subadditivity, positive homogeneity, and translation invariance. These properties ensure that the risk
measure behaves consistently with sound economic and mathematical principles. As shown in Mansini
et al. (2015) [14], CVaR fulfills each of these properties, making it particularly suitable for portfolio
optimization.
Monotonicity implies that if portfolio A always yields returns less than or equal to those of portfolio
B across all scenarios, then the risk of portfolio A should be greater than or equal to that of portfolio
B. In mathematical terms, for two loss random variables X and Y , if X ≥Y almost surely, then
CVaRα(X) ≥CVaRα(Y ).
CVaR satisfies this property because it is defined as the conditional
expectation of losses in the tail: if one portfolio consistently has larger losses, its tail average will also
be larger.
Subadditivity ensures that diversification is rewarded. The risk of a combined portfolio should not
exceed the sum of the risks of its components. For any two portfolios with loss distributions X and Y ,
this means CVaRα(X + Y ) ≤CVaRα(X) + CVaRα(Y ). This property is essential in financial theory
because it ensures that aggregating portfolios leads to a lower or equal risk, aligning with the principle
that diversification reduces overall portfolio exposure to extreme losses. CVaR, being a convex and
expectation-based measure, inherently satisfies subadditivity.
Page 128
==PAGE:142==
Portfolio Optimization using Mixed-Integer and Linear Programming
Positive homogeneity means that scaling all positions in a portfolio by a positive constant λ > 0
should scale the risk linearly. That is, CVaRα(λX) = λCVaRα(X). CVaR satisfies this because both
the Value-at-Risk threshold and the tail expectation scale proportionally with portfolio size. This is a
critical property for portfolio optimization, as it ensures that the model reacts appropriately to position
sizing and leverage.
Translation invariance states that if a risk-free asset (i.e., constant payoff) with return r is added to
a portfolio, the overall risk should decrease by exactly r. Formally, CVaRα(X + r) = CVaRα(X) −r.
This holds for CVaR because adding a constant shifts the entire distribution, including the tail, and
thus the conditional expectation in the tail is also shifted by the same constant.
In summary, CVaR not only offers better tail-risk insight compared to traditional measures such
as VaR, but it also meets the theoretical criteria rigorously proven in [14, Chapter 5]. This makes
it particularly suitable for use in portfolio optimization models where robustness, consistency, and
theoretical soundness are critical.
