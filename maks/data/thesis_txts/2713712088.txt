Abstract 
 
Building obsolescence in industrial society is usually addressed by demolition resulting in the 
disposal of building materials in landfills and high emissions of carbon dioxide [26]. To limit this 
issue, Design for Deconstruction (DFD) was introduced to the structural sector to ensure the reuse 
of structural elements. Although many scientific efforts have been investigated in dismantled 
connections, there was no effective way to dismantle buildings without causing damage to the 
structural parts or a drop in the efficiency of prefabricated elements. 
The purpose of this thesis is to study the connection between a deck and other bearing elements 
(stabilizing wall, beam, and another deck) to verify if the decks can be dismantled by breaking the 
mortar in joints without causing damage to other bearing elements. 
This was accomplished using Autodesk Inventor to analyze a slim low-rise commercial building 
(50m * 5m) belonging to terrain 2 as per Eurocode standards that suits Denmark. The worst-case 
scenario was analyzed in the software to determine the mortar with the lowest strength capable 
of transferring the stresses without producing failure during the serviceability of the building. The 
findings revealed that 16 MPa mortar was sufficient, and that the height of the story and the 
mortar strength appeared to be related in a correlation that seemed to be linear. Moreover, two 
methods were performed to allow the disassembly of hollow core decks using flat jacks by 
calculating the resistance forces in the joints and the jacking force required to overcome them. It 
was noticed that dismantling the decks in the horizontal direction using three flat jacks of 818 kN 
each was safer for the structural elements. 
Finally, dismantling the building and reusing the decks reduced CO2 emissions by 55% and the 
total overall cost by 34%. This confirms that further reductions can be achieved if more elements 
are designed for disassembly in the building. 
 
 
 
Keywords: Design for Disassembly (DFD), Demountable Joints, Finite Element Method (FEM), 
Autodesk Inventor, hand calculations, slab reuse, flat jacks.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1. Background  
The environmental crisis, the world is now experiencing, has compelled individuals to reassess 
their approach to addressing problems of waste, consumerism, and pollution. Nearly 40 percent 
of the world's yearly CO2 emissions stem from buildings. Building operations account for 29 
percent of the overall emissions, while construction and building materials account for an extra 
11 percent as seen in Figure 1.1. Moreover, construction operations also require vast quantities 
of materials and natural resources; in the United States, for instance, the building construction 
industry utilizes around 60 percent of the natural raw material (Wagner, 2002). Therefore, it is 
worthwhile to reconsider how buildings are constructed [1]. 
 
Figure 1.1: CO2 emission distribution [2] 
 
Recently, environmentally friendly structures and construction technologies have seen an uptick 
in demand to reduce CO2 levels. Structural component reuse, which decreases waste and 
material consumption, is one method of achieving carbon neutrality [3]. These technologies are 
compatible with the "Zero Waste Hierarchy" principle that prioritizes and organizes waste 
management choices for both companies and individuals. Waste avoidance is given the highest 
priority, followed by re-use, recycling, recovery, and then discharge into the land, water, or air 
that harms human health and the environment. The five-level pyramid shown in Figure 1.2 
demonstrates how the hierarchy helps individuals rethink their relationship with waste based on 
five priorities listed according to what appears to be best for the environment [4]. 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
 
Figure 1.2: Zero-Hierarchy Pyramid [4] 
 
The term “disassembling” or “deconstruction” is rarely used in engineering practice when a 
structure reaches the end of its intended lifespan, whereas the term “demolition” is more 
common. When it comes to concrete structures, it is virtually a taboo topic. However, it is vital to 
get familiar with these terms to develop a sustainable process and discuss the circularity of 
concrete life. Does it make sense to refer to the structures as "long-term temporary structures"? 
To what degree should we spend time and money on a "ready to dismantle" constructing method? 
These queries may simply get more rhetorical while considering the environmental benefits and 
economic savings that can be achieved by exploring and developing new approaches targeting 
buildings with zero-waste hierarchy concepts.  Besides that, the concrete elements can last for 
approximately 250-300 years if manufactured and built properly. Therefore, this should be a 
warning sign for the designers, that adapting the reuse approach is not considered a utopia [5]. 
To replace the demolition practices with the disassembly strategy, structures must be designed 
using the Design for Disassembly (DFD) principle [6]. 
 
1.2. Definitions and Principles for DFD: 
In the field of deconstruction and renovation, the concept "Design for Deconstruction" (DFD) 
defines a design approach that facilitates and eases the recovery of components and materials. 
The Dutch professor in the domain of Construction and Environment Charles Kibert , described 
the concept as "the total or partial dismantling of structures to facilitate the recycling of materials 
and the reuse of elements at the end of a structure's lifespan" [7]. This allows whole buildings to 
be easily converted, expanded, or reduced in size, thus avoiding the elimination of whole 
structures [8]. To encourage a closed-loop system for structures components, disassembly has 
proven to be a fundamental tool [7]. The professor in the sector of science and education David 
T. Crowther stated four options for the reuse of disassembled building materials:   
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
1) Relocating the structure  
2) Reusing the elements 
3) Reprocessing of materials 
4) Recycling of structural parts 
Of these four, the reuse of structural components is preferred because they require less energy, 
and recycling and reprocessing are less desirable due to their limited contribution to a new 
product [7]. 
 
The implementation of DFD reduces environmental effects and optimizes economic value. 
Disassembly has several advantages, including a decrease in CO2 emissions and a decrease in 
the use of virgin/new building materials, hence reducing harmful effects on the environment (from 
processing to disposal). Additionally, DFD in buildings has the potential to drastically decrease 
the quantity of waste that ends up in landfills by redirecting it elsewhere. Structural Engineer 
Andria Carlsson has found that the reuse of prefabricated concrete elements in the construction 
of new buildings could save material costs by 56 % and carbon dioxide emissions by 68 % [9]. 
 
Although the use of DFD approach has revealed several benefits that contribute to the goal of 
sustainability, there are currently some restrictions on the usage of this system [7]. One of the 
major challenges is to design joints and connections that can easily be dismantled to allow the 
reuse of slabs. While dismount ability adds value to the building, it cannot have a negative effect 
on other essential characteristics of the joints. One of these characteristics is the ability to transfer 
loads. Even though improved demonstrability does not have a significant effect on the load 
transmission of axial forces, there is a doubt about the capacity of such connections to transmit 
shear forces that are often thought to depend on the friction produced along the section subjected 
to compression [3]. 
 
1.3. Problem Statement 
There is a clear debate over how to mitigate the environmental effect of building obsolescence 
and excessive use of the material, as well as considering novel ideas addressing and tackling the 
associated issue. Several attempts have been made to consider eco-friendly structures under the 
concept of design for disassembly, but there is still no effective approach to disassembling 
structural components by providing efficient joints capable of transferring and resisting expected 
loads, as well as dismantling the structure elements without causing distortion to their original 
condition and preserving their efficiency. 
 
1.4. Scope  
The objective of this research is to take a brief look at the history of structures designed for 
disassembly and to present a new approach for the demountable joints between the decks and 
between the deck and stabilizing walls or beams using hydraulic jacking, in order to facilitate the 
dismantling of joints and to reuse the structural components in future projects.

2. Literature Review and Site Visit 
 
 
 
This chapter will provide an overview of some of the literature related to the evolution of the 
building life cycle and the development of construction techniques for structural elements and 
joints. In addition, different types of investigated DFD connections will be presented, followed by 
a documentation of a site visit that includes a review of the observed connections.  
 
 
2.1. Building Life Cycle 
Historically, the building construction industry has been criticized for its lack of innovation and 
improvement, especially when compared to the technological sector [10]. The "Cradle-to-Grave" 
linear life cycle model, represented in Figure 2.1, has been the traditional method for designing 
structures that can stand for a very long time. It is used to assess the environmental and 
economical effects associated with all stages of a product's life, including raw extraction of natural 
resources, processing and production, distribution, consumption, repair, and finally disposal [11]. 
This methodology prohibits the reuse and adaptation of concrete structures. Despite the fact that 
there have been very few initiatives to reuse concrete construction materials, there is no effective 
approach to securely deconstruct buildings without altering or distortion the structural elements. 
One possible explanation for this is that the way concrete structures are constructed makes them 
inflexible and resistant to change (Durmisevic, 2010) [10].

According to Duffy (1998) and Brand (1995), the structure itself can last for 300 years as illustrated 
in Figure 2.2, thus a modified model was required to reap the benefits and minimize the 
environmental impact as well as the cost associated with the old building life cycle [10]. 
 
Figure 2.2: Building layers and their expected lifetime [12] 
 
Therefore,  the mono-directional Cradle-to-Grave model was modified to Cradle-to-Cradle 
closed-loop life cycle as illustrated in Figure 2.3. This approach is starting to gain popularity in 
building design nowadays in which different building layers with different life spans can be 
exploited and avoided disposal before their life spans expire through:  
 
Refurbishing the entire building that has been in use for some time so that it may be used for 
similar purposes, such as labs, residences, etc. 
Reusing the standard design components, such as slabs, beams, and columns, in the construction 
of a new structure or elsewhere within the same building. 
Recycling the old construction materials to produce new building components or other 
manufactured products [13].

To enable simple disassembly of structural parts throughout the transition to the new model, it 
was necessary to develop the way of casting structural elements and creating accessible joints 
[14].

2.2. Development of Construction Methods  
Over the past years, cast-in-situ concrete has been the dominant procedure in the construction 
industry.  Conventional concrete is placed in certain on-site formwork and cured to gain the 
strength of RCC components. This method is adapted by the older model as it yields monolithic 
structures with permanent joints (wet joints), as shown in Figure 2.4, in which all functions are 
assembled into single interpenetrating entity preventing the dismantle and reuse of structural 
components in future projects [15]. 
 
 
Figure 2.4: Cast in situ joints [17]    
 
As soon as World War II ended, nations throughout the globe started rebuilding their 
infrastructure to restore what had been lost. Prefabricated reinforced concrete elements were 
proven to be the most practical and effective alternative for reducing the time and effort involved 
in this procedure. However, the difficulties arise during the period of on-site assembly, when 
structural integrity must be assured via in-situ cast hybrid connections (Figure 2.5) or via dry 
connections (Figure 2.6) in restricted areas. These connections are critical to the entire structural 
behavior and, therefore, to be effective, they must be ductile, strong, and capable to transfer the 
designed loads while also complying with the building sequences for the specific structure. The 
usage of these connections gives flexibility and enhances the structure's ability to be designed for 
disassembly [16]. 
 
 
Figure 2.5: Hybrid Connection [17]

2.3. Investigated DFD Joints  
Different types of demountable connections that were examined are summarized as follows: 
 
2.3.1. Beam-Column Connections: 
Joint 1: [20] 
 
The end-plate beam-column joint shown in Figure 2.12 can be dismounted by simply removing 
the bolts. Under quasi-static cyclic loading, the hysteretic performance of the connection 
was investigated experimentally. Some horizontal and flexural shear cracks were observed in the 
precast beam, indicating that it exhibits the same structural behavior as a wet-jointed/monolithic 
concrete frame structure (rigid moment joint). Also, it was discovered that the beam was damaged 
when the connection was disassembled.  
 
 
Figure 2.12: Beam-Column Joint with embedded steel end plate [20] 
 
Joint 2: [18] 
 
Figure 2.13 depicts a similar demountable connection to the first joint, with a small modification 
on the embedded steel end plate where it has been replaced with two bolted angle plates. 
According to the experimental findings of the connection when subjected to quasi-static cyclic 
loading, the behavior of a monolithic reinforced concrete frame structure can be attained. Where 
the performance of subassemblies with steel angle bolted joint was shown to be largely reliant on 
varying connection specifications. 
 
The literature provided some suggestions for the connection specifications:  
• 
The beam edge distance should exceed 5 times the diameter of the steel reinforcements.  
• 
Horizontal stirrups should be added to the joint. 
• 
Non-shrink grout might be used if needed. 
• 
The gap between the column and the beam should be avoided. 
• 
The bolts should have adequate pre-tension force to protect the joint from sliding before 
the yielding of the steel reinforcements. 
 
 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
Issues encountered during structural disassembly: 
 
• 
The difficulty associated with the removal of the tilted threaded bolts due to the joint 
sliding. 
• 
The difficulty associated with the removal of the grout from the steel angle holes. 
 
 
Figure 2.13: Beam-Column Joint with two bolted angle plates [18] 
 
Joint 3: [21] 
 
Another demountable girder-column joint is presented in Figure 2.14 with steel anchors and 
mountable steel plates incorporated into each of the precast concrete elements. During 
construction, steel bolts were utilized to secure the embedded steel plates. FEM modeling 
(ANSYS) was used to evaluate the connection behavior when subjected to progressive loading. 
The results of tests carried out on a full-scale model with several trials and modifications showed 
that the connection can sustain 3-4 times more load compared to the expected loads. 
Furthermore, the ability to absorb energy is a key feature of this joint because it limits the intensity 
of dynamic impacts and eliminates the outdoor noise (from industrial activities, buildings, and 
traffic) below the threshold of audibility, improving indoor environmental quality and permitting 
construction projects in areas with higher noise and traffic levels. The system can also be used in 
areas where natural earthquakes are likely to occur. 
 
 
Figure 2.14: Girder-Column Joint with steel anchors and mountable steel plates [21] 
 
 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
Joint 4: [41] 
    
Figure 2.15 illustrates another type of beam-column connection that is designed for dismantling. 
The joint employed a “Z” shaped pads made of fiber-reinforced grout and high-strength steel 
reinforcement bars. The findings from the tested experiments on the joint subjected to cyclic 
behavior indicated that the joint performed well in terms of enhancing the joint shear capacity, 
energy absorption, and ductility, with minimal destruction to the connected parts. Moreover, 
since there is a lack of steel reinforcement along with the brackets of the column that hold the 
beam, the joint was found to be simple to assemble and easy to repair after any medium-intensity 
seismic act. This column-beam joint test is the milestone for an experimental study program for 
prefabricated concrete constructions. 
 
 
Figure 2.15: Beam-Column Joint with “Z” shaped pads [41] 
 
Joint 5: [22] 
 
The precast concrete beam-column joint demonstrated in Figure 2.16 consists of anchor rod pre-
casted in the column and a steel plate fastened to the embedded anchor bolts. A threaded bar 
connects the beam end to the steel plate. Initial findings of the ongoing experimental studies and 
tests showed that precast concrete beam-column joints function seismically better than cast-in-
place joints in terms of ductility and deformation capacity. The capacity of a precast joint to 
distribute energy due to anchor rod yielding was extremely efficient. Where the joint system was 
discovered to be particularly effective in transferring the loads from high-strength threaded bars 
to the transition steel block, then from the steel block to the pre-tension A490 steel bolts, which 
then transfer the loads to the anchor rod, and finally to the concrete in the joint. 
 
 
Figure 2.16: Beam-Column Joint with anchor rod and threaded bar [22]

2.3.2. Beam-Beam Connections: 
Joint 6: [23] 
 
Beam-to-beam joint is a type of DFD connection where a precast column with the protruding 
beam is connected to the DFD beam by welding the steel reinforcement together as presented in 
Figure 2.17 and then the joint is casted with concrete on site. Five fully scaled tests were 
performed under cyclic and static loads for two different types of concrete, recycled aggregate 
concrete (RAC) and normal aggregate concrete (NAC). It was deemed that more research was 
necessary for this type of joint. However, the following conclusions were derived from the current 
investigation results:   
 
DFD elements showed the same stiffness deterioration, crack distribution, and energy absorption 
as monolithic elements, but with a higher ductility ratio and greater capacity for an initial flexural 
cracking moment and ultimate moment. Moreover, the RAC specimen developed more fractures 
than the NAC specimen, mainly around the connection, and the specimens behaved more like a 
monoclinic structure. 
 
Figure 2.17: Beam-Beam Joint with welded steel reinforcement [23] 
 
2.3.3. Wall-Wall Connections: 
Joint 7: [24] 
 
The vertical wall-wall connection seen in Figure 2.18 is a joint composed of a steel plate and steel 
bolts used in earthquake-prone regions for low-rise constructions. A preliminary study was 
conducted on the cyclic behavior of this joint, and further investigations required in the future, 
from the experiments with these conclusions were obtained: 
• 
The bolts might rotate and slide if the concrete was severely crushed or the holes were 
damaged. 
• 
The bolt sequential distribution was unable to increase the load-bearing capacity of the 
connections because side bolts of the connection caused the steel plate to spin 
significantly at a stage of high deformation. 
• 
Steel bolts might come loose, and steel plates could crack as well.

2.3.4. Slab-Slab Connections: 
Joint 8: [25] 
 
Another approach that can be considered a viable solution to sustainability is the Lego-inspired 
building. To create the desired structure, multiple bendable blocks are joined together by pure 
dry joints as shown in Figure 2.19. These couplings are designed to withstand the applied forces

only hindrance to the efficiency of the system is the structure's buildability, which can be 
considered time-consuming. Therefore, additional research is being made to incorporate robots 
into the system in order to enhance the efficiency of the system.  
 
 
Figure 2.19: Lego Slab-Slab Connections [25]

disassemble. However, no verification for the dismantling possibility is provided in the article.

3.5. Wall Analysis 
After finding the horizontal line load on each floor level, the Rigid Floor Method was used to 
estimate the reaction R (kN) on each stabilizing wall given the following assumptions: 
 
▪ 
All the stabilizing walls are located in the same position on all the stories. 
▪ 
The floor diaphragms act as infinite rigid horizontal beam in order to distribute the 
horizontal wind line load on the stabilizing walls. 
▪ 
The walls act as stiff columns that are fixed into the foundations. 
▪ 
The wind load is resisted only by the stabilizing walls despite the fact that part of it is actually 
resisted by floors, façade, columns, beams, and building diaphragms. 
▪ 
The wind load is distributed on the walls depending on their stiffnesses and positions.

5.2. Usage of Flat Jacks in DFD Building  
In this part of the study, flat jacks are used in a temporary work to dismantle the slab panels of 
the DFD building by applying the required force (Fjack) needed to break the mortar in the joints 
between the slab panels and other structural elements without causing deterioration.   
Hollow core slabs of HCS (EX 18, 5L 9.3) are used in this study equivalent to SL-18 decks selected 
in Section 3.3. The reason for this change is the challenging and conservative disassembly of HCS

before dismantling. For instance, according to [33], the fire resistance capacity of SL 18 deck is 4 
hours while it is significantly lower in HCS where it is about 25 min [43]. Thus, additional steel 
reinforcement (stirrups of min Y 10) is provided in the joints between HCS, as shown in Figure 
5.6, to enhance the fire resistance capacity. To be more conservative, the width of the HCS was 
decided to be 1.2m rather than 2.4m, with three joints instead of one with 16 Mpa mortar.

7. Discussion 
 
 
 
When designing a building, it is necessary to pay close attention to the way in which the various 
structural elements are connected to each other. This is especially important when designing for 
the building's disassembly because it helps to ensure that the building retains its durability as well 
as the ability to transfer loads efficiently. The majority of previous research has focused on DfD 
systems that rely on mechanical connections to transfer loads, with mortar used to cover and 
protect the joints from damage. The joint design presented in this thesis, on the other hand, 
employs low-strength concrete binding material to transfer loads between bearing elements and 
to ensure that the slabs can be easily disassembled after the structure has served its purpose. 
 
Several assumptions were considered to ensure a conservative building design, especially when 
computing the lateral wind load where only shear walls were assumed to be responsible for 
resisting wind load, ignoring the important role of other bearing and non-load bearing elements 
(facades, non-load bearing walls, columns, end beams, and building geometry) in resisting a 
portion of this load. 
 
Moreover, after validating Autodesk inventor, the investigation was conducted on a 
representative section of the building because it was difficult to investigate a full building model 
using this software. Consideration was given to the wind load's effect on the entire structure; these 
forces were calculated using Cast software. When all forces were applied to the four different 
Autodesk Inventor models, the stress results indicated that placing shear walls at the edges of the 
building results in a more conservative design than walls placed in the middle of the building. This 
refers to the fact that a structure is more uniform and rigid when the center of gravity of the shear 
walls is closer to the building's center. 
 
To guarantee that the structural elements can be disassembled with minimal effort, mortar of 
fck=16Mpa was selected as the lowest compressive strength concrete needed to withstand the 
stresses applied on the building during its service. According to the linear relationship between 
story height and stresses presented in Section 4.2.3, mortar with a lower compressive strength 
can be selected for shorter stories, as the critical story height in this thesis was assumed to be 6 
meters for conservatism. 
 
On the basis of the mortar's determined strength, additional study was conducted to determine 
the necessary jacking forces needed for the disassembly of the HCS. The feasibility of a vertical 
and horizontal disassembly was explored. According to the results, four 170 KN capacity flat jacks 
were sufficient to guarantee disassembly in the vertical direction, while three 818 KN capacity flat 
==PAGE:73==
72 
 
jacks were required to guarantee disassembly in the horizontal direction. This enormous 
difference in capacity is due to the high tensile splitting resistance of the mortar during horizontal 
disassembly relative to its shear resistance. Additionally, shear resistance at the mortar level itself 
is greater than shear resistance at the interface between the mortar and the contact surface of the 
structural elements. This implies that the shear failure will become apparent at the interface 
between the mortar and the structural components. 
 
Results from the feasibility study showed that dismounting the HCS in the horizontal direction is 
feasible and do not result in any deterioration of other bearing elements. In this method, the 
assumption that the safety factor for resisting shear stresses is 2 could be exaggerated. An 
experimental test is required to ensure that the model has the necessary factor of safety (FOS) for 
resisting shear stresses against beam and column tilting during disassembly. 
 
Regarding disassembly in the vertical direction, the outcome was quite encouraging as it showed 
cracking of the mortar. Although the study did not address the design of beams, columns, and 
walls, it was discovered that the stresses on them exceeded their designed compressive strength 
indicating crack formation in these structural elements as illustrated in Figure 5.18 in Chapter 5. 
To avoid cracking in the column, either cross sectional area can be increased, extra steel 
reinforcement can be added, or carbon fiber sheets can be used to wrap the column as seen in 
Figure 7.1 [44]. 
 
Figure 7.1: Column wrapped with carbon fiber sheet [44] 
 
According to the results, punching stress was observed on the beams and walls at the location 
where the concentrated jacking forces were applied. In reality, the jacking force is applied as a 
uniformly distributed load across the thrust area of the flat jack, resulting in significantly lower 
stresses than concentrated force. To avoid this issue, a steel plate, like that shown in Figure 7.2, 
can be added to the beam and wall in the place where there is high stress to avoid any expected 
damage. 
 
Figure 7.2: Steel plates [45] 
==PAGE:74==
73 
 
Based on the above observations, it appears that horizontal disassembly is the most practical 
technique, as it does not require additional design steps for some of the structural elements in 
DFD buildings. FJ35 flat jack from HYDRA Capsule [42] seen in Figure 7.3 can be used for 
horizontal dismantling where this type of flat jacks has a capacity of 100 tons (>818 kN). Since 
the closed height of these flat jacks is 32mm, gaps of 35x350 mm2 should be filled with polystyrene 
before pouring the mortar in the joints between the slabs during construction. Polystyrene can 
then be removed with knife or sprayed with chemical materials to make place for the installation 
of the flat jacks. After the appropriate jacking forces have been applied, the HCS is free and ready 
to be removed using a crane.   
 
Figure 7.3: FJ35 flat jack [42] 
 
The edge beam and steel reinforcement at the joints between the slabs and other structural 
elements were not part of the shear and jacking force calculations. They were assumed to be 
removed or cut with a diamond saw prior to dismantling. Couplers seen in Figure 7.4 can also be 
an alternative solution instead of the expensive diamond saw where it allows the removal of steel 
reinforcement without wasting them by simply hammering the portion where the couplers exist.  
 
 
Figure 7.4: Couplers [40]  
 
Finally, the estimation of CO2 emissions and cost revealed that when only the decks were reused, 
CO2 emissions were cut in half (55.12%) and the total cost was reduced by a third (33.28%,). This 
confirms that additional savings are possible if more structural elements of the building are 
designed for disassembly to be reused. 
==PAGE:75==
74 
 
 
8. Conclusion   
 
 
 
This thesis focused on a new paradigm for dismantling HCS within a concrete building using 
hydraulic flat jacks to break the binding material in the joints. Therefore, the feasibility of 
constructing joints with weak mortar capable of transmitting stresses from decks to other 
structural elements during the usage phase of the building was investigated.  
The building and its joints were analyzed using Autodesk Inventor software that showed an 
accuracy of 99% when compared to the analytical calculations. To determine the maximum 
stresses that were imposed on the joints, the analysis was conducted on four models with different 
wall locations and different deck spans. The findings showed that concrete strength of 16 MPa 
for the mortar was sufficient to transfer the stresses from the slab to the walls and beams. 
Furthermore, dismantling of HCS in the horizontal direction was found to be safer for the HCS 
and the adjacent structural components to prevent their cracking. The jacking force needed to 
overcome the shear and splitting stresses, formed in the joints during dismantling, was found to 
be 2455 kN assuming that the steel reinforcement in the joint would be cut with a diamond saw. 
This force was applied to the slab-slab joint using three flat jacks and the results showed  
successful cracking of the mortar on all three sides of the HCS.  
Last but not least, the CO2 emissions were cut in half and the overall cost was cut by a third 
when the decks were reused. This demonstrates that additional savings are possible if more 
components of the building are reused.

History of building construction   
 
In 1985, the Netherlands became a pioneer in the field of precast concrete designed for 
disassembly by introducing building systems to improve overall building performance 
(Bestcon-30, MXB-5, CD-20, SMT, and Moducon-2000). Despite the fact that these 
systems have significant weakness in sustaining seismic stresses due to beamless 
structures, in addition to the difficulties in changing any defective elements parts, they 
raise the prospect of applying disassembly in the construction sector across the entire 
European construction industry, which could be achieved if certain conditions are 
met.[21] 
 
CD-20  Building systems  
 
The structural joint in this system is formed with four short and embedded pens on both 
sides of the columns, as well as grouting slots in the floor elements. to ensure the 
horizontal stability of the connection grouting is provided in the slots. [21] 
 
   
 
                 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Bestcon-30 & SMT systems  
 
Both systems utilized four reinforcement bars extend from the upper section of the 
column and positioned to fit the holes from the corners of each  precast slabs. The 
connection is sealed with mortar once the floors are properly placed. [21] 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
MXB-5 building system 
==PAGE:81==
80 
 
 
The system is designed based on the dry-mounted concept, with steel plates embedded 
on both ends of the precast columns used to host anchor bolts that connect the columns 
with the floor slots. [21] 
 
 
                
 
               
Moducon-2000 system 
 
The connection consists of four screws in the upper section of each column. The Screws 
are oriented to be inserted through the holes in the corners of the precast panels. To 
ensure the continuity of load transfer through the connection, holes are located at the 
bottom of each upper column to host the bolts from the lower one. [21]
