ABSTRACT 
 
This report includes a study of passenger behaviour in the Danish train 
system (S-tog/Metro). In recent years, there have been several examples of 
passenger behaviours leading to personal injuries and train delays. Such 
circumstance has substantial socioeconomic consequences.  However, little research 
has been conducted on this specific topic and the implementation of safer S-
tog/Metro operations requires a behavioural study.   
In the current study, an S-tog/Metro passenger behaviour questionnaire, 
comprising 29 behaviours, and a train driver questionnaire, comprising 14 
behaviours, is used for data collection. In total, 137 S-tog/Metro passengers and 
140 train drivers are included in the survey. Data culled from passengers is used 
in a factor analysis, an anova and regression analysis focusing on boarding and 
alighting behaviours, and a correlation analysis.  
 
- 
The factor analysis reveals a four-factor solution explaining 20 behaviours: 
Factor I Distracted and clumsy, Factor II Jeopardous, Factor III Careless, 
and Factor IV Presumptuously and audacious.  
- 
The anova analysis shows that in 75% of the questions, men and women 
had significantly different understanding of the rules and consequences of 
boarding and alighting behaviours.  
- 
The linear regression shows that the behaviour boarding and alighting after 
the departure signal is equally affected by subjective norms and attitude.  
- 
The correlation analysis shows that age correlates with Factor II. 
 
Data culled from train drivers is used in a risk assessment. The risk 
assessment, identifies 8 high-risk behaviours. High-risk behaviours demonstrate 
that Factor IV has the highest risk loading.  
 
The results set the stage for two recommendations. First, another 
questionnaire-based study should be completed focussing only on the 8 high-risk 
behaviours. This study should also include observations and interviews for optimal 
data.  
Second, if solutions are needed, the factors should be considered in the 
following order: Factor IV, Factor III, Factor I and Factor II.

1 INTRODUCTION 
In 1864, the first metro line was created in London (Transport For London, 
2011). Ever since, the development of train systems within cities has been nonstop 
around the world. Today, train systems can be found in 148 cities, spread over 540 
lines, with 9000 stations and an average of 150 million passengers per day 
(Advancing Public Transport, 2014). Train systems in cities are becoming 
increasingly popular (Transport For London, 2016; Villacorte, 2013). Train 
systems have their own track and are not affected by cars, bicycles, or pedestrians. 
They are therefore considered reliable as they depart on time and are highly 
punctuality (NetworkRail, 2016; MetroTrains, 2016; DSB, 2016; Metro, 2015; 
Goverde, 2015).  
 In Denmark, as in many other parts of the world, the S-tog/Metro system 
is one of the most preferred modes of commuting of the public transport in greater 
Copenhagen. It accounts for 50% of public commuting (Københavns Kommune, 
2011). In 2015, the operational stability was extremely high with 98,8% of the 
metro and 98% of the S-tog departures were on time (DSB, 2016; Metro, 2015). In 
the fourth quarter of 2015, the average daily number of passengers was 506.922 for 
the S-tog/Metro (Danmarks Statistik, 2016), and the number is increasing (ibid).  
 
The first S-tog/Metro line opened in Greater Copenhagen in 1934 (Laine, 70 
år med S-tog, 2014). Greater Copenhagen has, ever since, been under constant 
development. Today, the system counts seven S-tog lines and two Metro lines, and 
is expected to expand. With a new upcoming Metro line and the new Copenhagen-
Ringsted railway, parts of the Copenhagen railway system are set out to hit their 
maximum capacity (Behrendt, 2014). With the new Metro and railway expected 
to open in 2018 and 2019 respectively, the number of passengers is expected to 
increase to 752.293 passengers/day (Danmarks Statistik, 2016; Metro, 2016). 
Moreover, a steadily increasing population in Greater Copenhagen also contributes 
to the increase in the number of passengers. Figure 1 shows the increasing number 
of inhabitants in different parts of Copenhagen from 2004 to 2015 (Københavns 
Kommune, 2016). From 1995 to 2014 the population has increased from 471.300 
to 580.300, and the population projections for 2027 says 684.400 (ibid).

The histories of the first metro systems demonstrate that cities expand train 
systems to handle capacity problems, among other problems. Greater Copenhagen 
is not an exception. There are already some plans for new future lines, but some 
are not expected to be realized until 2040 (Marfelt, 2013). However, there is not 
much room to build and expand inside Copenhagen. As such, an expensive solution 
to the expected capacity problems is to build future train systems deeper in the 
ground. A cheaper solution to the capacity problems is to increase the number of 
trains and departures. Already though, the metro has gone from operating every 
3,5 minutes to every 2 minutes  (Laine, Oversigt over metrodriftens historie, 2009). 
The S-tog has also increased the number of departures in the past years (DSB, 
2009).  
However, with an increasing number of departures, smooth train operation 
relies even more on passenger behaviour. To have a smooth, efficient train 
operation, there must be no outside interference. This is why passenger behaviours 
which do not follow common travel rules (CTR) when using S-tog/Metro can 
interfere with optimal conditions for train operation (Din Offentlige Transport,

As mentioned only very few previous studies have addressed the mechanisms 
behind S-Tog/Metro passenger behaviours. However, based on previous research 
in the area of transport behaviour, it is reasonable to believe that transport 
behaviour varies according to demographic differences.   
Cowi completed an investigation in 2015 attempting to elucidate whether 
there were significant differences in accident rates and traffic figures when road 
users' age, gender, income, education, and other social and economic conditions 
were taken into account. The study shows that there is a significant difference in 
accident rates depending on age, gender, income, and education (Cowi, 2015). 
Reason, Manstead, Stradling, Campbell, and Baxter (1990) was a study 
investigating the difference between driver behaviours across sex. It consisted of a 
questionnaire with 520 participants. The questionnaire deduced three factors: 
violation, dangerous errors, and harmless lapses. It showed a difference in violations 
(men reported most) and harmless lapses (women reported most). It showed that 
violations are caused by motivational and social factors, and harmless lapses are 
accounted for by the information-processing characteristics of an individual 
(Reason, Manstead, Stradling, Campbell, & Baxter, 1990).  
Steg and Brussel (2009) did a study in Netherland investigating 147 young 
moped riders. It showed a difference across gender. It also showed that people felt 
a significant correlation between subjective norms and speed. Moped riders were 
more prone to speed when others expected them to do so (Steg & Brussel, 2009).  
Sullman, Meadows & Karl (2002) investigated pedestrians' attitudes towards 
traffic violations. They concluded that, young men reported more violations than 
elderly men, and men reported more violations than women.  
In addition to demographic factors previous knowledge from other areas of 
human behaviour indicate that “the lemming effect” may influence passenger 
behaviour. For instance, in the book Infostorms: Why do we 'like'? Explaining 
individual behaviour on the social net the lemming effect is illustrated using 
examples of individuals who are uncertain of the correct action in a given situation 
and tend to follow others (Hendricks & Hansen, 2016). Transferred to the area of 
S-Train/Metro passenger behaviour the lemming effect could be illustrated by a 
situation in which a person wants to board a train after the departure signal. If 
this person sees one or several other persons boarding the train, the person tends 
to just follow and do the same as the others. Previous studies indicate that 
behaviours within transport is strongly influenced by social norms.  
- 
The study Peer influence on speeding behaviour among male drivers by 
Møller & Haustein (2014) indicated a strong influence of social norm on 
speeding behaviour of younger drivers (Møller & Haustein, 2014).  
- 
The study Social Behaviour in Public Transportation by Hult, Munch &  
Johansen (2011), showed that passenger rely on social norms and rules when

using the public transportation in Copenhagen and London (Hult, Munch, 
& Johansen, 2011). 
Finally, previous studies indicate that the perceived consequences of a given 
behaviour as well as the risk of being caught by the police in case of violations 
strongly influence transport behaviour (Vaa, 1997). Although this has not been 
investigated in relation to S-Train/Metro passenger behaviour it is likely that these 
aspects also influence such behaviour. More specifically, lack of awareness of the 
consequences of violations of CTR may contribute to the occurrence of such 
behaviour. This hypothesis, is supported by, personal experience of the author of 
this project.  
 
Based on the above, the goals of the current study are to:  
- 
Gather knowledge and understanding about S-tog/Metro passenger 
behaviour with a particular focus on boarding and alighting behaviours. 
- 
Examine the correlation between passenger behaviours and demographics. 
- 
Identify high-risk behaviours.

2 METHODOLOGY  
This chapter begins by presenting and justifying methods used in the current 
study. The methodology uses triangulation, in other words, employing multiple 
methods to gather data on, or investigate, a topic (Zauszniewski & Bekhet, 2012). 
The chapter start of by describing the data collection for each method: A 
qualitative method (interview) and a quantitative method (questionnaire). Then, 
methods used for data analysis are presented: A qualitative risk assessment (risk 
matrix) and statistical method (factor analysis); in addition to a presentation of 
the interpretation of the data collected.  
To provide a simple overview of the explanation, a process overview is 
illustrated in Figure 3.  
 
Figure 3 Illustration of the process, tasks, and methods applied throughout the project. 
2.1 
DATA COLLECTION 
For the current project, data was collected using two questionnaires. One was 
completed by train passengers and the other by train drivers. Participants for the 
train passenger questionnaire were recruited through Facebook, e-mail, and a 
commuter forum (S-tog pendlere) in the form of an anonymous questionnaire link. 
The sole criterion for participating was an age restriction of +16. After 7 days, a 
total of 156 people answered the questionnaire. Participants who did not fully 
complete the questionnaire were removed from the sample. Thus, the number of 
participants was 137.  
Participants for the train driver questionnaire were recruited by a 
spokesperson from Danske Statsbaner (DSB) who distributed the questionnaire to 
S-tog drivers. The sole criterion for the Train drivers’ participation was: Currently 
employed and working as an S-tog driver. After 16 days, a total of 182 Train 
drivers answered the questionnaire, with a 140 of them being valid for analysis.

2.1.1 PASSENGER QUESTIONNAIRE

correlation between passenger behaviour and demographic and secondly to 
examine if boarding/alighting behaviours are influenced by attitude (ATB),

gaining a better understanding for preventive measures. The questionnaire was a

ATTITUDE, SUBJECTIVE NORM, AND PERCEIVED BEHAVIOURAL CONTROL OF 
BOARDING AND ALIGHTING 
The TPB was introduced in 1967 under the name theory of reasoned action 
(TRA) by Martin Fishbein and Icek Ajzen. It is based on the assumption that an 
individual act rationally, using available information, considering the consequences 
of performing or not performing a certain behaviour, and then deciding to engage 
in the behaviour or not. One problem, however, is that the information an 
individual possesses will not always be correct or complete. This can lead to, in 
the eyes of others, irrational behaviour (Ajzen, 2005).  
 
==PAGE:24==
 
METHODOLOGY 
24 
 
 
Figure 4 The relation between components in the theory of reasoned action (TRA). 
 
As shown in Figure 4, a behaviour is determined by an individual’s intention 
of performing a behaviour. So, the higher the intention, the more likely that the 
behaviour is carried out. Intention is influenced by two factors: attitude toward 
the behaviour (ATB) and subjective norm (SN). The former refers to the way a 
behaviour is evaluated: positively or negatively, while SN refers to the perceived 
social pressure to engage or not to engage in a behaviour (Ajzen, u.d.). Examples 
include:

of performing this 
behaviour. 
 
3. Your attitude is that you should not catch the 
train after the departure signal while you think

Shown in the examples much like what Martin Fishbein and Icek Ajzen aim 
to show— are the different components which play a part in a behaviour. However, 
==PAGE:25==

TRA has been criticised for lacking components which, besides ATB and SN, may 
prevent a person from performing a behaviour. For example,  
 
5. Your attitude is that you will catch the train after the departure signal and 
your friends also think (SN) this would be a good idea, but you do not have 
the ability to do so.   
 
This describes a situation where an individual has a high intention of 
performing a behaviour but lacks full control of a situation. Considering this 
criticism, Ajzen developed an expanded version of the TRA, called the theory of 
planned behaviour in which perceived behavioural control (PBC) is added to 
account for aspects beyond the control of an individual, illustrated in Figure 5 
(Ajzen & Fishbein, 2010).   
 
 
 
The concept of the TPB is to predict the intent of performing a certain 
behaviour, as in the example of a person’s intention to catch a train (Cope, Ingram, 
Harju, & Wuensch, 2010).

2.1.2 TRAIN DRIVER QUESTIONNAIRE 
The purpose of the questionnaire is to collect data to create a Risk Matrix 
(see section 2.2.3 Risk Matrix). To create a Risk Matrix, information is needed 
about the likelihood and consequence of passengers’ behaviours. To collect this 
information, a questionnaire was distributed to train drivers. The questionnaire 
used for the train drivers is based on a questionnaire used in the previous Chinese 
survey (Wan, Li, Yuan, & Schonfeld, 2015). 
2.1.2.1 SEMI-STRUCTURED INTERVIEW 
Some adjustments had to be made to ensure the questions suitability for train 
drivers and facilitate the collection of information about likelihood and consequence 
of passenger behaviours. This included a four-step process with semi-structured 
interviews (Cohen & Crabtree, 2006). This was, unlike the quantitative passenger 
questionnaire which depended on a larger, randomly selected sample, purposeful 
sampling. This suggests purposely picking information-rich samples, in this case 
train drivers, to gather information concerning a study (Patton, 1990). Then, these 
qualitative sample interviews were used to improve the quantitative questionnaire. 
 
1. First, two semi-structured interviews were conducted with one train driver 
and one train driver instructor (Sørensen H. H., 2016; Sørensen O. , 2016). 
The aim was to determine the correct terminology and delimit passenger 
behaviour within their area. 
 
2. The second step was to adjust the original questionnaire based on the 
interviews.  
 
3. The third step was to distribute the new questionnaire draft to the same 
interviewees.  
 
4. The fourth and final step was to implement their feedback and as such 
complete the questionnaire.

2.2.3 RISK MATRIX  
As presented, there are 28 different passenger behaviours. However, 
behaviours that constitute the biggest risk can be hard to determine (passenger 
injury and train delays risks). A risk matrix is a qualitative assessment tool used 
to identify the level of risk from, in this case, passenger behaviours (Altenbach, 
1995). The advantage of using this method is that given a limited amount of 
resources, the risk matrix prioritizes the focus area. In this project, the risk matrix 
is constructed from the train drivers’ questionnaire which contains information 
about the likelihood and consequence (train delay and personal injury) of different 
types of passenger behaviours (see Figure 8). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The assessment of the likelihood is based on the train drivers’ subjective 
assessment and estimates. The purpose of using a risk matrix was to determine 
how much of a risk a behaviour possesses regarding passenger injury and train 
delay. In this case, passenger behaviours listed in Table 6.  
Figure 9 shows the scales of the matrix. The results from the train driver 
questionnaire help by placing different passenger behaviours from Table 8 within 
the risk matrix. Each question, which can be seen in appendix 03 Train driver

4 DISCUSSION 
This section discusses the results presented throughout the report. Beginning 
with a brief description of the report’s object and discussion of the results, followed 
by a discussion of the methods.   
Despite the fact that undesirable passenger behaviour during boarding and 
alighting often causes train delays and, at times, passenger injury, information 
about this topic is limited (Møller & Haustein, 2016). As such, the current study 
began with little information. The goal of this study was to:  
 
- 
Gather knowledge and understanding about S-tog/Metro passenger 
behaviour with a particular focus on boarding and alighting behaviours. 
- 
Examine the correlation between passenger behaviours and demographics. 
- 
Identify high-risk behaviours. 
 
OBJECT GOALS, HYPOTHESES, AND FINDINGS 
Indicators from other behaviour studies concerning what to expect in the 
findings included following: Certain demographics have particular behaviours 
within transport, commuters’ indifference of consequences (The Lemming Effect) 
and Passenger awareness that some undesirable passenger behaviour rarely leads 
to any personal financial consequence. This study ended up showing a similarity 
to the other studies. 
 
In the passenger behaviour analysis, this study extracted four latent variables 
(factors). Table 42 shows that this study’s four factors and the Chinese study’s 
three factors were similar. This study did, however, have two factors explaining 
one of the Chinese studies factors.

To gather more knowledge and understanding concerning S-tog/Metro 
behaviours, with particular focus on boarding and alighting, the following two 
behaviours were further investigated:  
 
- 
Boarding or alighting after the departure signal.  
- 
Forcing the S-tog/Metro door open to board or alight. 
 
Considering the goal gather knowledge and understanding about S-tog/Metro 
passenger behaviour with a particular focus on boarding and alighting behaviours, 
this was to some extent possible, though, there was not enough data to analyse 
forcing the S-tog/Metro door open.  
  
The result from the analysis showed that the attitude and subjective norm 
weighted evenly on the behaviour.  The factor PBC was removed due to low 
correlation and not contributing significantly to the model in a linear reggresion. 
This gave the equation: 
 
 
Behaviour = 0,348 · Attitude + 0,334 · Subjective Norm 
(2) 
 
The equation was for both sexes. Due to time limits, it was not possible but 
is suggested to run a Tukey analysis to highlight any differences between the sexes, 
and thereby to determine an equation for men and women. The same Tukey 
analysis could be applied to the data shown in Table 28 as it displayed a large 
difference in how men and women interpret CTR. It can be hard to determine 
exactly what causes the attitude and subjective norm. However, by looking at Table 
26 and Table 28, there was some indicators as to what attitudes may be caused by. 
It showed that a clear majority thinks:  
- 
It is harmless to board or departure the S-tog/Metro after the departure 
signal.

- 
There are no consequences regarding being fined or causing injuries, despite 
being the actions being illegal.  
- 
The actions cause train delays (This means passengers are indifferent of the 
consequences of their behaviours).  
 
In summary, these results suggested that while S-tog/Metro passengers 
understand the consequences of their behaviour, they are indifferent. Table 28 
showed that participants had the perception that people were neither pleased nor 
annoyed if someone boards after the departure signal.   
This can be considered in a circular way. If people’s attitude toward the 
behaviour is “there are no consequences”, then it would become more common with 
time to consider the behaviour acceptable. This could lead to two things:  
 
1. Groups may expect others to board or alight after the departure signal. 
2. Perception is key. When in doubt, do as others do (the lemming effect).  
 
Another object of this study was to identify high-risk behaviours. It was 
possible to identify eight high-risk behaviours within passenger injuries and train 
delays. Compared to examples of UPB in Denmark from Table 1, these behaviours 
are the same, however, with some additional behaviours. This indicates that train 
drivers’ knowledge used in this study is credible. There were 29 passenger 
behaviours in the beginning which were narrowed to eight high-risk behaviours.

campaign alongside fining passengers would probably have a better effect. 
Currently, people know that the only fine you are likely to get is from traveling 
without a ticket. Again, a new extended study is suggested with focus given to 
high-risk behaviours and follow up interviews to learn about their roots.

DATA COLLECTION 
A common approach when conducting behavioural studies is collecting data 
by using questionnaires. This study had, as described, no financial resources, a 
time limit, and no staff. With such restrictions, questionnaires are an effective, if 
not the best way to gather knowledge. The main advantage of using questionnaires 
is that a large amount of data, can be collected in a short time and in a cost-
effective way (Ackroyd & Hughes, 1992). 
 When using a questionnaire, it is important to investigate the questionnaire’s 
validity and reliability. Does the questionnaire measure what is desired, and is the 
questionnaire a reliable method for measuring this? The advantage of reusing a 
questionnaire, is that it has usually been:  
 
1. Face validated. 
2. Pilot tested. 
3. Tested reliable  
 
The Chinese questionnaire had already undergone all three points, and the 
adjustment to the Danish system was done by using interviews and the official 
travel rule book (Xie & Parker, 2002; tools4dev, 2014; Collingridge, 2015; Din 
Offentlige Transport, 2016). The Chinese study tested if questionnaire was reliable, 
by a manual inspection of each response, to ensure the reliability. The same method 
was used in the present study. During the manual inspection, invalid answers were 
removed.   
Despite being validated and reliable, some of the feedback on the passenger 
questionnaire was: 
 
- 
The questionnaire was too long. 
- 
The questionnaire was too complex. 
- 
Some of the questions in the questionnaire lacked an explanation. 
 
These types of biases are discussed in the report Biases in questionnaire 
construction: how much do they influence the answers given? (Quelhas, et al., 
2010). This report accounted for some of these biases which did not have as much 
influence as normally anticipated, and usually only affected an insignificant portion 
of the participants. With this in mind, the questionnaire was assessed as the best 
fitted choice for data collection when retrieving information about passenger 
behaviours.  
 
The purpose of the questionnaire for train drivers was to collect data to 
calculate a mean of their answers (Likert 1-5 scale). The first questionnaire was 
designed for passengers. As such, the questionnaire needed to be adjusted to train

PASSENGER BEHAVIOUR 
A problem with a rather small sample size is that it can be difficult to extract 
latent variables (Trninić, Jelaska, & Štalec, 2012). It is also difficult to determine 
the appropriate minimum variance to be explained when completing a factor 
analysis. R. A. Peterson suggests that the normal average of the variance that 
should be explained is around 56,6% (Peterson, 2000). This study had 137 
participants, and 47,7% of the total variance was explained. Compared to the 
Chinese study which had 579 participants and explained 53,95% of the total 
variance, the current study does not greatly differ. However, it is on the lower end 
compared to the normal average. This could be caused by the small sample size.   
Another limitation of factor analysis is the factor naming which can be quite 
problematic (Young & Pearce, 2013). All behaviours within a factor may not fit 
the rest of the variables. The Chinese study did not have this problem as they 
simply removed factors which did not fit the rest (Wan, Li, Yuan, & Schonfeld, 
2015), which was not done in this present study.  
 
BOARDING AND ALIGHTING BEHAVIOUR  
To gain a better understanding of some boarding and alighting behaviours, 
the theory of planned behaviour was applied to the behaviours boarding or 
alighting after the departure signal and forcing the S-tog/Metro door open to board 
or alight.  
It was not possible to derive anything from the questionnaire regarding the 
latter behaviour. This is because it did not occur among these participants. 95% 
answered never to the question how often do you forcw the S-tog/Metro door open 
to board or alight. Some participants were asked how they interpreted the question: 
“How often do you force the S-tog/Metro door open to board or alight”. Everyone 
agreed, saying they thought it meant blocking a door that was about to close and 
==PAGE:54==
 
DISCUSSION 
54 
 
forcing it open to catch the train. The idea behind the question was, however, that 
the train door should be completely closed. So, it is assessed that the question had 
a misunderstanding bias and that the 5% who answered something other than 
never to the question may have misunderstood the meaning of the question.  
It was generally difficult to derive statistically telling results from the 
questionnaire regarding the two boarding and alighting behaviours. This was an 
anonymous questionnaire. It is suggested that follow-up research uses a mixed 
method study with questionnaires and semi-structured follow-up interviews. The 
questionnaire provides information and patterns within a large sample and semi-
structured interviews can provide greater insight concerning individuals (Coiro, 
Knobel, Lankshear, & Leu, 2008).  
 
RISK MATRIX 
By using the risk matrix, it was possible to identify risks which posed more 
significant hazards. However, to use the method, some changes according to normal 
procedure had to be made. Using a risk matrix which consists of a frequency and 
consequence axis is often based on statistics. This means the likelihood for 
something to happen is known. This is precise statistical information (qualitative). 
Unfortunately, no statistic exists as to how often different behaviours occur in 
Denmark. For this reason, train drivers’ empirical knowledge was used to 
determine an estimate of the likelihood. This made the risk matrix more 
quantitative. Assigning a predicted likelihood by using expert knowledge instead 
of statistics is an approved alternative and does not cause not cause large 
deviations from known statistics (Elmonstri, 2014). Because of this, the risk matrix 
is considered credible. However, it does lack some behaviours because of train 
drivers’ limited periphery. A study with focus on additional behaviours could be 
conducted in another report.  
 
PAIRING RISK ASSESSMENT AND PASSENGER BEHAVIOURS  
By combining passenger behaviours and risk assessment, it was possible to 
derive high-risk passenger behaviours. This is not a method in it itself, however 
the two methods have been accounted for individually. It was not possible to 
correlate the eight high-risk behaviours and demographics within a reasonable cut-
off limit. It can be discussed if 0,4 is too high. Others behavioural studies use values 
that vary from as low as 0,2 to 0,4 (Schmitt, 1996; Hunecke & Haustein, 2007). 
Again, as noted, the higher value is based on work in At what sample size do 
correlations stabilize? (FelixS, 2013).  
Another problem was the skewness of most of the high-risk answers. This was 
the first questionnaire regarding passenger behaviours in S-tog/Metro in Denmark, 
of is kind. As such, there was little information to determine outcomes before

the high-risk behaviours, it is now known that there is a large probability that 
participants answer never or rarely to how often… questions. Therefore, an ordinal
