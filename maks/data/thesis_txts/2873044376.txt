Abstract
This thesis presents a structured model that integrates psychological behavior change
theories into sustainable design processes, with a particular focus on the ideation phase.
Aimed at non­expert designers, the model offers a card­based toolkit that helps translate
psychological mechanisms into actionable strategies across four key behavioral stages:
Trigger, Action, Maintenance, and Ensure. Through literature review, theory selection,
and iterative prototyping, the study identifies twelve core psychological principles suitable
for early­stage sustainable design. A prescriptive matrix and co­creation game interface
are developed to support reflective and theory­informed decision­making. Evaluation with
participants confirms the model’s usability and highlights opportunities for improving clarity
and long­term strategy.

Terminology
• Behavioural Design: An approach that systematically analyses triggers, motiva­
tors, and capability factors to apply design strategies—such as optimised feedback,
reward systems, and scenario building—in order to influence and change target be­
haviours (behaviourDesign.info).
• Behaviour: The observable, purposeful response of an individual to intrinsic or
extrinsic stimuli in a given situation, including actions, speech, and mental reactions
(Skinner, 2019).
• Sustainable Design: Design that takes into account environmental, economic and
social impacts throughout the product life­cycle (Bhamra and Lofthouse, 2007).
• Sustainable Development: Development that meets the needs of the present with­
out compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs, main­
taining healthy economic, ecological, and social systems (WCED, 1987; Mensah,
2019).
• Design for Sustainable Behaviour (DfSB) The field that investigates how design
can influence user behaviour during the use phase to reduce negative social or envi­
ronmental impacts, applying behavioural theory to products, services, and systems
(Bhamra et al., 2011; Lilley & Wilson, 2017).
• Psychology: Scientific study of mind and behaviour (VandenBos, 2007).
• Intervention: A coordinated set of activities—such as prompts, incentive systems,
feedback mechanisms, or environmental restructuring—designed to alter or redirect
the causal processes regulating behaviour (Michie et al., 2011).
• Mechanism of Action: Internal psychological processes—such as enhancing mo­
tivation, simplifying execution, and optimising triggers—through which behaviour
change techniques exert their effects (Michie et al., 2011). Referred as Mechanism,
Behavioural Mechanism, or Behaviour Change Mechanism in this paper as well.
• Framework: A system of concepts, assumptions, expectations, beliefs, and theo­
ries that supports and informs research, identifying presumed relationships among
key constructs to be studied (Ravitch & Riggan, 2017).
• Tool: Artefacts that not only augment practitioners’ capabilities for carrying out in­
tended actions, but also guide their perception and understanding of problems and
solutions (Dalsgaard, 2017).

Introduction
1.1
Background of the Study
The concept of sustainable development highlights the importance of meeting the needs
of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own
needs (WCED￿1987). It covers the three pillars of environment, economy and society
and requires human activities to be carried out within the ecological carrying capacity of
the planet (Mensah, 2019; Hák et al., 2016). With the intensification of crises such as
climate change, biodiversity loss and resource depletion, sustainable development is no
longer a visionary goal, but an urgent issue that requires realistic action.
Studies have shown that up to 65±7% of global carbon footprint is directly linked to house­
hold consumption behaviours, including daily activities such as energy use, dietary choices
and product purchases (Ivanova et al., 2016). However, although the public’s awareness
of environmental issues is increasing, there is still a huge gap in individual behavioural re­
sponses, with consumers slow to adopt more sustainable behaviours, and the behavioural
changes they make are often short­lived (Lilley, 2009). This ‘ knowing­doing gap’ poses
a great challenge in the field of DfSB (Design for Sustainable Behaviour). Although de­
signers are equipped to develop products and services that directly impact society and
the environment (Papanek, 1972), the design interventions on the use phase in product’s
life cycle are often overlooked(Tang, 2010).
As an emerging field of research in sustainable design, DfSB focuses on influencing user
behaviours through design strategies to reduce negative consequences of use at the so­
cial and environmental levels (Bhamra et al., 2011). In recent studies, there has been a
shift from emphasising physical attributes, such as material substitution and product ef­
ficiency, to the mechanisms of interaction between users and products, considering the
physical form, interaction patterns and the presentation of information to guide users to­
wards more sustainable behaviours (Lilley & Wilson, 2017). However, the integration of
DfSB in the design process still faces difficulties, especially the lack of mature method­
ologies and systematic decision support tools. (Tang, 2010)
In parallel, the field of behavioural sciences has provided a lot of structured theories to
explain the formation and transformation of behaviours, such as the Theory of Planned Be­
haviour (Ajzen, 1991), the Fogg Model of Behaviour (Fogg, 2009), and Self­Determination
Theory (Ryan & Deci, 2000), etc. These psychological theories are widely used in the
fields of health, finance, and education, and their core mechanisms cover the forma­
tion of motivation, control of external triggers.(Levy et al., 2023; Zhang & Hale., 2022;
Theopilus et al., 2023) However, in the design field, especially when designers do not
come from a background in psychology, it is hard to transfer these theories to specific
design decisions and strategies due to the complexity of the technical terminology and
the length of the mechanism chain. Furthermore, even though there has been attempts
in the literature to propose strategy models (e.g., ecological feedback, behavioural guid­
ance, intelligent systems, etc.) (Lilley, 2007; Tang, 2010), there is still a lack of practical
frameworks that can support designers to “’determine intervention points”, “select theo­
retical mechanisms”, and “’adapt strategies”’ for a wide range of applications in practice.
Therefore, this study focuses on bridging the gap between psychological behavioural the­
ories and sustainable behavioural design practices, translating psychological theories into

Research Aim, Objectives and Questions
1.2.1
Research Aim
The overall aim of this study is to develop and validate a psychology­behavioural mecha­
nism oriented Design for Sustainable Behaviour (DfSB) tool to help designers in practice to
identify users’ current behavioural stages more systematically, to clarify the design areas
that can be intervened in and to match the appropriate psychological theory of behavioural
change with design strategies.
1.2.2
Research Objectives
To achieve the above, the following verifiable objectives were set:
• Research Objective 1 (RO1): Define the scope of the study.
• Research Objective 2­a (RO2­a): Validate the contribution of psychological theo­
ries to the key behavioural stages in DfSB through a systematic literature review.
• Research Objective 2­b (RO2­b): Construct a Theory­Mechanism­Design Strat­
egy mapping matrix (30+ theories such as TPB/Nudge/SDT) to identify design gaps.
• Research Objective 3 (RO3): Develop a decision­making tool to match psycholog­
ical theory/cases with behavioural mechanisms.
• Research Objective 4 (RO4): Conduct design workshops to evaluate the usability
and effectiveness of the tool in real­life scenarios.

Scope and Direction
1.3.1
Scope
The central topic of interest in this study is how to influence the sustainable behaviours
of users through design intervention strategies, with the goal of providing designers with
basic design skills with a systematic tool to facilitate the translation of psychological theo­
ries into the language of design. (Figure 1.2 ) The user behaviours explored in the study
focus on decisions and habits in daily life that are closely related to environmental sus­
tainability, such as energy and water conservation, waste management, and the use and
purchase of sustainable products These behaviours are high­frequency, easy to observe,
and easy to be influenced by design, and thus are widely regarded as key entry points
for designing sustainable behavioural interventions. The actors are end users (i.e., users
of products or services), and do not include the behaviour of policy makers, community
managers, producers or designers themselves, unless they are involved in the research
as users of the tools.
Figure 1.2: Scope of the study

This study is conducted based on Design Research Methodology (DRM), and its focus is
established in two stages:
In the Research Clarification phase, the research focuses on the intersection of psychol­

In the “Descriptive Study I” stage, the study identifies design gaps from the perspectives

methodology.

In the Prescriptive Study, the design of a psychological theory­behavioural mechanism­

1.4
Theoretical Background
1.4.1
Behavioural Design
Behaviour is the observable, purposeful response of an individual to an intrinsic or extrin­
sic stimulus in a given situation, such as actions, speech, and mental reactions. (Skinner,
2019) Behaviour design aims to influence and change target behaviours by systematically
analysing triggers, motivators and capability factors and then applying design strategies to
reduce barriers and enhance cues. Designers promote desired behaviours through tech­
niques such as scenario building, optimised feedback and reward systems to enhance
well­being and health (behaviourDesign.info). Due to the complexity of motivations for
shifting behaviour, different Due to the complexity of motivations for shifting behaviour, dif­
ferent levels of interventions are made by linking the design approaches to the behavioural
change elements accordingly to ensure behavioural and habitual change in energy and
resource consumption. change in energy and resource consumption. (Tang, 2010)
1.4.2
Sustainable Design
Sustainable development refers to meeting present needs without compromising the abil­
ity of future generations to meet their own, and sustaining a healthy economic, ecological
and social system for human development. ￿WCED￿1987￿Mensah, 2019￿Sustainable
design strives to minimise the environmental, economic and social impacts of a prod­
uct throughout its life cycle by employing strategies such as material selection, process
optimisation and recycling. (Lilley, 2009).
1.4.3
Design for Sustainable Behaviour
Design for Sustainable Behaviour is an emerging field that aims to investigate how design
can influence user behaviour to reduce negative social or environmental impacts during
the use phase (Bhamra et al., 2011).It is concerned with the application of behavioural
theory to understanding users, and behaviour changing strategies to design products,
services and systems that encourage more sustainable use.(Lilley & Wilson, 2017) Cur­
rently, Design for Sustainable Behaviour research and industry practice is still in its in­
fancy. Academic research has focused on conceptual models and empirical cases, such
as interventions in energy efficiency and waste management, while industry applications
are increasingly using smart home systems and mobile apps for real­time behavioural
monitoring and feedback (Froehlich et al., 2010).
1.4.4
Psychological Theories
Psychology, defined as the scientific study of mind and behaviour (VandenBos. 2007),
provides the basis for behavioural design for understanding and changing human be­
haviour. The rise of behaviourism at the beginning of the 20th century , Watson (1913)
shifted the focus of psychology from unobservable mental processes to measurable be­
haviours, followed by Skinner (1965) through his operant conditioning theory, which eluci­
dated how environmental feedback reinforces stimulus­response associations. Bandura’s

(1977) Social Learning Theory, which introduces the concepts of observational learning
and self­efficacy, drives behavioural design beyond simple stimulus control to a cognitive
mediation model.The cognitive revolution of the 1960s, represented by Neisser (1967),
refocused attention on the internal mental processes of attention, memory and reasoning,
and provided a new intervention for design. goals. The Social Cognitive Theory devel­

such as ’lazy cognition” to change user behaviour and enhance healthy choices.
1.4.5
Interventions and mechanisms
“Behaviour change interventions” can be defined as coordinated sets of activities de­
signed to change specified behaviour patterns (Michie et al., 2011). Typical interven­
tions include prompts (e.g. push notifications), incentives (e.g., point rewards), feedback
systems (e.g., progress visualisations), and environmental reconfigurations (e.g., default
option settings) (Fogg, 2009). Mechanisms refer to the internal psychological processes
by which these techniques work ­ such as enhancing motivation, streamlining execution
and optimising triggers ­ which are critical to explaining and predicting intervention effects
(Michie et al., 2011).

Methodology
2.1
Introduction to Design Research Methodology
This study adopts Design Research Methodology (DRM) as a core methodological frame­
work (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009) to systematically guide the development of a psy­
chological theory driven design tool. DRM is chosen because it supports both theoretical
understanding and practical tool development, which fits the goal of the study. The tool is
designed to empower designers to more effectively guide users towards sustainable be­
haviours with psychological theories, thereby enhancing the role of design in sustainable
behaviour change. The research process is divided into four stages: Research Clarifica­
tion, Descriptive Study I, Prescriptive Study and Descriptive Study II. (Figure 2.1 )
Figure 2.1: Research process
2.2
Research Clarification
The objective of this phase is to clarify the research objectives and elucidate why the
integration of psychological behavioural theories into Design for Sustainable Behaviour
(DfSB) is important and necessary. Towards this end, a research unit ­ Workpackage1 ­
is created to provide an in­depth insight into the relevant literature of the interdisciplinary
fields of psychology and DfSB to conclude this stage of the study, specifically by sorting
out the core theoretical underpinnings, identifying gaps in existing practice and arguing
how psychological insights can be an effective way of reinforcing the design of sustainable
behaviours.
2.3
Descriptive Study I
The objective of this phase is to validate the contribution of psychological theory to the crit­
ical behavioural stages in Design for Sustainable Behaviour (DfSB) through a systematic
literature review and identify design gaps.
This phase not only clarifies the “what goal” of behavioural interventions, but also lays the
groundwork for subsequent tool development, clarifying the “why intervene”, “where to
intervene”, and “how” behavioural change occurs. ‘How behaviour change occurs’. Three
work packages (Work Package 2, Work Package 3, and Work Package 4) are included
in this phase, which are structured around the core questions of Research Question 2
(RQ2): How can psychological theories be systematically mapped to behavioural change
design strategies, and what behavioural mechanisms enable effective translation from
theory to practice?
2.4
Prescriptive Study
After completing a systematic review of the relationship between psychological behavioural
mechanisms and existing DfSB strategies, this study enters the Prescriptive Study phase,

==PAGE:18==
aiming to develop a decision­making tool to match psychological theory/cases with be­
havioural mechanisms. This tool is designed to help designers translate abstract psy­
chological behavioural theoretical structures into design strategies that can be adapted
to specific contexts, in order to enhance their behavioural interventions in practice, thus
responding to RQ3: How can a behavioural mechanism­oriented ”psychology­to­design”
tool enhance designers’ capability to design context­adaptive interventions?
2.5
Descriptive Study II
The objective of this phase is to conduct design workshops to evaluate the usability and
effectiveness of the tool in real­life scenarios. It focuses on the testing and reflective
evaluation of the tool’s application in real or almost real design contexts. Designers will
participate in workshops to evaluate the tool’s support for strategy selection, mechanism
understanding, and behavioural insights. Mixed methods are used in this phase: including
questionnaires, interviews, user behaviour modelling, comparative experiments (e.g. A/B
testing), etc., as well as reflecting on the boundaries and limitations of the tool in different
contexts, design phases, and target behaviours, in order to provide a basis for future
versions of the tool, and to further advance the process of integration between psychology
and sustainable design.

3
Descriptive Study I
3.1
Introduction to Descriptive Study I (DS­I)
The objective of Descriptive Study I is to validate to what extent psychological theory
contributes to the key behavioural stages in DfSB through a systematic literature review.
(O2a) And to construct a “psychological theory­behavioural mechanism­design strategy”
mapping matrix to identify design gaps. (O2b) In the Clarification Study section, the in­
tersection of psychological theory and DfSB is examined and the value of psychological
insights for enhancing sustainable behavioural design is identified. Yet, to better under­
stand the objectives of this study and to set the stage for the Prescriptive Study stage, this
chapter answers the following sub­research questions: how can psychological theories be
systematically mapped to behavioural change design strategies, and what mechanisms
enable effective translation from theory to practice?
For in­depth investigation to evidence how psychological insights can effectively reinforce
the design for sustainable behaviours, this chapter provides a literature study of existing
applications of psychological theories in the field of behavioural change, and a systematic
literature review of the extent to which Design for Sustainable Behaviours (DfSB) practice
fits with psychological theories and the mechanisms of action.
Stage of Descriptive Study I consisted of three work packages (Work Package 2, Work
Package 3, and Work Package 4).In workpackage 2, the study involved mapping psy­
chological theories of behaviour and extracting the corresponding mechanism pathways,
which were subsequently matched to a behavioural stage model to provide a structured
basis for the subsequent design of mechanism pathways. In workpackage 3, a system­
atic literature review is conducted to analyse the extent to which Design for Sustainable
Behaviour (DfSB) practices fit with psychological mechanisms, pointing out that current
DfSB is insufficient in translating psychological mechanisms, and further highlighting the
need for the development of design tools. In workpackage 4, the study identifies key op­
portunities for psychological intervention design to enhance the long­term effectiveness
of interventions by extending the lifecycle of behavioural change (from triggering to action
to maintenance and assurance), and models the fit between psychological mechanisms
and design outputs .
3.2
Methodology of DS­I
3.2.1
Existing applications of psychological theories in behaviour change
The first work package of Stage 2 aims to systematically review the practical applications
of psychological theories in the field of behavioural change and mechanisms of action.
To achieve this goal, the first step involves establishing a foundational understanding of
psychological theories, followed by a literature review. The research began with a thor­
ough reading of introductory psychology books, including Psychology: Themes and Vari­
ations (Weiten, 2007) and A History of Modern Psychology (Schultz, 2013). This enabled
researchers from design backgrounds to develop a global view of the schools of psy­
chology (Figure 3.1 ), their developmental lineage, and the factors that influence human
behaviour, and to correct the common misconception that behavioural change theories
originate in a single school of psychology. In fact, behaviour change theory is dispersed
across multiple schools of cognitive psychology (e.g., Theory of Planned Behaviour), so­
cial psychology (e.g., Norm Activation Theory), and behaviourism (e.g., Operant Condi­
tioning), rather than a particular school of psychology.

A literature review was conducted using the search formula ‘psycholog* + ‘behavio* de­

of behaviour change. Psychological theories that clearly included a description of be­
havioural change and had at least 1 empirical study demonstrating the effectiveness of the
designed intervention were screened. Theoretical definitions, application cases, mecha­
nisms of action, and stages of influence were summarised and mapped, laying the foun­
dation for subsequent screening as well as research on applied psychological theories.
3.2.2
Systematic literature review of Design for Sustainable behaviour
As global sustainability issues are growing critical, design is undertaking an increasing
responsibility for sustainability. This chapter aims to reveal the current state of integration,
application efficacy, and systemic shortcomings of psychological theories in research and
practice through a systematic review of research in the field of Design for Sustainable
Behaviour (DfSB) between 2021 and 2025, and to provide empirical evidence for the
construction of a theoretically­driven interdisciplinary design framework.
The systematic review adopted the PRISMA­P framework to systematically analyse the

3.3.1.1 Overview
Initially 24 psychological theories that have been applied to the field of behavioural design
were identified. Following the addition of Workpackage 3 (a systematic review of the litera­
ture related to DfSB) this is expanded to 27, and the psychological theories were analysed
by mapping the psychological theories to the theoretical mechanisms, the main types of
mechanisms of action, and the stages of behavioural influence, respectively (Figure 3.4 ).
Figure 3.4: Review of psychological theories applied to the field of behaviour design
The psychological theories involved are as follows:
• Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA): Predict individual intentions to engage particu­
lar behaviors at a specific time and within diverse contexts . A given behavior can
be predicted by intentions (i.e., motivations) to engage in that behavior. Behavior­
specific attitudes (i.e., evaluations or appraisals) and subjective norms (i.e., per­
ceived pressure to engage in behavior from significant others) determine intentions.
(Ajzen and Fishbein, 1980)
• Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB): The theory of planned behavior was developed
from the theory of reasoned action. In addition to attitudes and subjective norms
which comprise the theory of reasoned action, the TPB’s key contribution is the
concept of perceived behavioral control, defined as an individual’s perception of the
ease or difficulty of performing the particular behavior (Ajzen, 1985).
• Theory of Interpersonal behaviour (TIB): Behavior, in any situation, is a function of
intention (consistent with other behavior models) as well as the strength of habit
of the behavior and the various facilitating conditions. Further, behavioral inten­
tion is explained by social factors as well as rational and affective beliefs about the
behavior. In the model, social factors comprise of norms, roles and self­concept.
And, affect construct comprises of both positive and negative emotional responses
towards a decision and is distinct from rational­instrumental evaluations of conse­
quences (perceived consequences construct). (Triandis, 1977)
• Norm Activation Theory (NAT): Awareness of consequences, ascription of responsi­
bility, and personal moral norms activate pro­environmental behaviour. (Schwartz,
1977)
• Model of Pro­Environmental Behaviour (MPB): Considers both internal factors (moti­
vation, knowledge, values, attitudes) and external contextual factors (infrastructure,
policies). (Kollmuss & Agyeman, 2002)
• Goal Theories (GT): Highlights how setting and pursuing goals can lead to sustained
behaviour change. (Presseau et al., 2010)

• Emotions Theory (ET): Stresses the role of emotions in decision­making; emotional
experiences can directly trigger or inhibit behaviours. (Kahneman & Tversky, 2000)
• Unconscious Processes Theory (UPT): Focuses on the impact of non­conscious,
automatic processes on decision­making; many behaviours occur without deliberate
thought. (Newell & Shanks2014)
• Enjoyment (Hedonic) Approach (EA): Suggests enhancing the pleasure or fun as­
pect of a target behaviour to promote behavioural change. (Dolnicar et al., 2020)
• Motivation­Opportunity­Ability Model (MOA): Posits that behaviour occurs if an in­
dividual has sufficient motivation, opportunity, and ability. Lack in any factor can
prevent behaviour change. (Ölander & Thørgersen, 1995)
• Fogg’s Behaviour Model (FBM): States that for behaviour to occur, a trigger must
be present at the right moment, and the user must have sufficient motivation and
ability. There is a trade­off between ability and required motivation. (Fogg, 2009b)
• Hooked Model (HM): Outlines a four­step cycle (Trigger, Action, Reward, Invest­
ment) to build user habits. Repeated cycles with user investment increase the like­
lihood of habit formation. (Eyal, 2013)
• Behaviour Change Wheel (BCW): A comprehensive framework that links behaviour
(via Capability, Opportunity, Motivation) with intervention functions (education, per­
suasion, incentive, etc.) and policy categories. (Michie et al., 2011)
• Goal Framing Theory (GFT): Proposes that behaviour is influenced by three types of
goal frames: normative (altruistic), gain (economic), and hedonic (emotional). The
dominant frame varies by context. (Lindenberg & Steg, 2007)
• Comprehensive Action Determination Model (CADM): Integrates elements from TPB,
NAT, habit theory, and others; behaviour is determined by habitual, intentional, and
situational factors with norms indirectly influencing intentions. (Klöckner & Blöbaum,
2010)
• Health Belief Model (HBM): The HBM views health behaviour change as based on
a rational appraisal of the balance between the barriers to and benefits of action.
(Munro et al., 2007)
• Information­Motivation­behavioural Skills (IMB): This theory focuses on three com­
ponents that result in behaviour change: information, motivation and behavioural
skills. (Munro et al., 2007)
• Prospect Theory (PT): Describes how people choose between probabilistic alterna­
tives that involve risk, where losses are felt more acutely than gains. (Dasgupta,
2017)
• Social Learning Theory (SLT): Social Learning Theory suggests that people learn
behaviours, attitudes, and emotional reactions through observation, imitation, and
modeling of others, rather than direct experience alone. Learning occurs in a social
context and is influenced by rewards, punishments, and role models. (Bandura,
1977)
• Self­Determination Theory (SDT): SDT is a psychological needs­based motivational
theory that distinguishes between intrinsically motivated behaviour and various forms
of extrinsic motivation along a continuum of self­determination. Behaviour is driven
Designing for Sustainable Behaviour
13
==PAGE:24==
by intrinsic interest or extrinsic motivation, and the higher the autonomy, the more
persistent the behaviour. (Webb et al., 2013)
• Cognitive Dissonance Theory (CDT): The Cognitive Dissonance Theory states that
when there is a conflict between beliefs, attitudes, or behaviours, individuals expe­
rience psychological discomfort and tend to adjust their cognition or behaviour to
reduce this discomfort. (Festinger, 1957)
• Operant Conditioning (O­C): Operant Conditioning Theory states that behaviours
are shaped by rewards and punishments, meaning reinforced behaviours are re­
peated, while punished behaviours decrease. (Skinner, 1938)
• Behavioural Priming Theory (BPT): Behavioural Priming Theory suggests that prior
stimuli (e.g., words, images) can unconsciously influence subsequent behaviour.
• Theory of Delay Discounting (TDD): This theory states that people prefer immediate
rewards over delayed ones, and the perceived value of a delayed reward decreases
over time. (Bargh et al., 1996)
• Social Cognitive Theory (SCT): Social Cognitive Theory (SCT) is a behavioural the­
ory that explains learning and behaviour change through observational learning,
social influence, and self­efficacy. (Belinskiy, 2024)
• COM­B Model (COM­B): The COM­B model provides a structured framework for
designers to understand and influence behaviour by analyzing users’ Capability,
Opportunity, and Motivation. (Pillan, 2022)
• Dual­Process Decision Model (Dual): The Dual­Process Decision Model proposes
that human decision­making involves two systems: a fast, intuitive, and automatic
system (System 1), and a slower, deliberate, and analytical system (System 2).
(Leeuwis, 2022)

3.3.2.2 Discussion
This systematic review reveals key challenges and opportunities in the field of Design
for Sustainable Behaviour (DfSB). Current DfSB practice is found to have developed a
rich set of intervention tools and technology prototypes, but there are still clear shortcom­
ings in the construction of systematic design processes, long­term behavioural support
mechanisms, methodological multiplicity and theoretical translation capabilities. Product­
orientated design preferences and individual­level intervention tendencies limit the impact
in complex sustainability issues. Uneven coverage of behavioural stages, especially the
lack of long­term maintenance mechanisms, severely limits the sustainability of interven­
tion effects.
In terms of theoretical application, although behavioural science is gradually penetrat­
ing design practice (62% of strategies integrate elements of psychology), there is a clear
theoretical narrowing. Even though the instrumental translation of a few theories, such

cepts (e.g., “cognitive dissonance”) still lack an effective vehicle. The disconnection in
theory­practice translation stems in part from the absence of an interdisciplinary system,
making it difficult for abstract psychological concepts to directly inform design practice.
Based on these findings, this study suggests that future research should focus on the de­
velopment of stage­appropriate multi­tiered intervention tool chains to bridge the ”trigger­
maintenance” gap; the construction of a theory­design mediation framework to facilitate
the visualisation and operationalisation of psychological concepts; and the expansion of
systematic design methodologies to enhance the ability to intervene at the policy and
community levels. The development of these directions will help to establish a more com­
plete and effective methodological system of sustainable behaviour design to address the
increasingly complex challenges of sustainable development.

Concluding notes
This chapter explains the core theoretical transformation pathway by establishing a map­
ping mechanism between psychological theories and sustainable behavioural design strate­
gies.
Firstly, by considering stage fitness can help designers filter out appropriate psychological
theories. For example, when the target behaviour only needs to trigger an initial Action,
cognitively driven theories (e.g. Theory of Planned Behaviour) can provide effective sup­
port through belief reconstruction. If a stable behavioural pattern (Maintain) needs to be
established, then emotionally driven (e.g., motivational reinforcement) and contextually
driven mechanisms (e.g., embedded social norms) need to be integrated. Ensure, how­
ever, relies on the design of multiple contextual cues from unconscious­driven theories
(e.g., habit formation models).
Secondly, current DfSB practice is over­concentrated in the areas of resource efficiency
(38%) and health and wellbeing (41%), with significant research gaps in the areas of
low carbon transition (24%) and circular systems (14%). The distribution of hotspots in
empirical research could provide designers with directions for choosing design areas.
Finally, behavioural mechanisms can be used as a bridge between psychology and DfSB.
Among the eight types of behavioural mechanisms verified in this study, motivational reg­
ulation (30%), contextual shaping (33%) and habit solidification (15%) are the ones that
have a better fit for design carriers, and the other behavioural mechanisms can also sig­
nificantly enhance the accuracy of the application of psychological theories.
These findings not only build a bridge between psychological theory and design practice,
but also point out the direction of research to achieve systematic intervention of sustain­
able behaviour through stage diagnosis, domain calibration and mechanism translation.

Prescriptive Study
4.1
Introduction to Prescriptive Study
After a systematic review of the mechanisms of psychological behaviour in relation to
existing Design for Sustainable Behaviour (DfSB) strategies, this study entered the Pre­
scriptive Study stage.
The core objective of this phase is to develop a decisional support tool to assist designers
in translating abstract psychological theories into design strategies that can be adapted
to specific contexts.
This stage begins with the construction of a “Psychology–Design” mapping matrix and
continues with iterations of an MVP prototype. The aim is to empower designers in the
design research process, specifically addressing Research Question 3:
How can a behavioural mechanism­oriented “psychology­to­design” tool en­
hance designers’ capability to design context­adaptive interventions?
4.1.1
Defining the Scope of the Model
This model aims to incorporate behavior change principles from psychology into sus­
tainable design processes, with a particular focus on the early ideation phase. Its goal
is to provide designers with a structured toolset that enables them to revisit and reflect
on their sustainable design concepts through a psychological lens—before entering the
testing stage. This early­stage focus is intentional: the model assumes that designers
already have a preliminary direction, such as a rough concept or target group, and aims
to strengthen their decision­making by clarifying behavioral mechanisms and theoretical
rationales at a point when design intentions are still flexible.
This model is particularly relevant for designers working on consumer­facing sustainable
products and services—such as home energy monitors, eco­friendly appliances, food­
saving solutions, or sustainable lifestyle platforms. It also applies to UX/UI designers
and service designers who develop interventions aiming to shift everyday behaviors (e.g.,
nudging healthier food choices, encouraging waste separation, promoting water­saving
habits). These designers often operate under tight development timelines, limited access
to behavioral science expertise, and the need to quickly generate and test actionable con­
cepts. The model equips them with a psychologically­informed toolkit to make informed
design decisions early in the process.
The scope of the model is intentionally focused on sustainable behaviors at the individual
or household level (e.g., saving energy, reducing consumption, minimizing food waste),
as these behaviors are more susceptible to psychological influence and are more closely
aligned with product and service design contexts. Broader systemic issues such as policy,
governance, or supply chains are beyond the scope of this tool.
Rather than replacing traditional design methods or subsequent user testing, the model
seeks to enhance psychological sensitivity within the design process. It is intended to
support designers without a background in psychology in asking the right questions, iden­
tifying key behavioral mechanisms, and translating theory into actionable strategies dur­
ing concept development. As such, the model is positioned as a guided decision­making
framework aimed at bridging the gap between abstract psychological theory and practical
design application.

4.1.2
Design Strategies
The design intent of this model is not simply to translate psychological theories into strat­
egy suggestions, but to guide designers in systematically reflecting on the following: What
behavior needs to change? Why should it change? And how can an effective intervention
be created?
It adopts a gamified, card­based structure as a guiding mechanism, encouraging constant
iteration, real­time adjustment, and collaborative exploration. This structure was chosen
over more linear or questionnaire­based tools because it supports divergent thinking and
playful testing in early ideation stages—especially when the problem framing is still fluid
and solutions need to remain open­ended. At its core, the model represents a shift in how
early­stage challenges in sustainable behavior design are framed. The model is built on
the premise that behavioral problems in design are not merely the result of user resistance,
but are often driven by overlooked cognitive, emotional, and contextual factors.
As such, the underlying logic of the model encourages designers to view behavior as a
decomposable system, where each phase of user behavior carries its own identifiable
characteristics.
The model is built upon three key assumptions:
• Behavior can be mapped to intuitive mechanisms of influence; this assumes that
psychological mechanisms such as motivation, emotion, or perceived benefit are
relatable enough to guide design without requiring deep scientific expertise.
• Design can serve as a tool for cognitive and behavioral reconstruction; because
the way information, cues, and feedback are presented can actively shape users’
interpretations and habits.
• Psychological theories should act as proactive tools in the early stages of design,
rather than being used solely for post­rationalization; early integration of theory can
help identify the root causes of behavioral gaps and prevent superficial or short­lived
solutions.
With this logic, the model functions as a cognitive framework that helps designers translate
complex behavioral challenges into clearer psychological diagnoses, thereby informing
more targeted design directions.
This model, therefore, represents an anticipatory approach to design—one that empha­
sizes the importance of understanding problem structures as the foundation of any solu­
tion.
4.2
Methodology of Prescriptive Study
The development of this model is a process of gradually transforming psychological theory
comprehension into actionable design tools, aimed at supporting decision­making in the
early stages of design. The overall process begins with the construction of a psychology–
design mapping matrix, which systematically organizes empirically supported behavioural
theories and links them to common behavioural challenges in sustainable design. This
is followed by the creation of a contextual adaptation mechanism, which enhances the
model’s applicability and interpretability across diverse user situations, helping designers
accurately assess the relevance of psychological mechanisms in varying contexts. Finally,
based on this theoretical framework, a user­friendly prototype tool is developed in the
form of a gamified card system—designed to support both collaborative co­creation and
independent use.

Based on a detailed review of existing psychological theories, this study initially screened
12 core psychological principles (Figure 4.3) from the 27 candidate theories collected
from the DS­I literature reading as the underlying theoretical framework for the first proto­
type tool. The selection process was guided by a multidimensional assessment covering
the distribution of each theory across key behavioural stages (Trigger, Action, Mainte­
nance and Ensure), with preference given to theories with clear operational pathways
(e.g., Comparative Feedback Design in Social Comparison Theory), and with a focus on
designing for feasibility of translation. Theory selection prioritises theories with clear op­
erational pathways (e.g. Comparative Feedback Design in Social Comparison Theory)
and focuses on the feasibility of design translation.
Theory selection prioritised balanced stage coverage, taking into account the high con­
centration of theories for the Action stage (27 theories) and the relative scarcity of theories
for the Maintenance (13 theories) and Ensure stages (10 theories). In order to mitigate
the potential bias of design towards short­term interventions and to elevate the focus on
sustained behavioural outcomes, this selection deliberately included more theories that
could be shown to be applicable to the later stages (e.g., Theory of Interpersonal be­
haviour and Cognitive Dissonance Theory) and removed the theories that only play a role
in the triggering of behaviour and the psychological theories that work only on a particular
behavioural mechanism in the action phase (e.g. Prospect Theory and Emotions Theory).

of the user behaviour intervention mechanism(s) involved in the theory in a sustainable
behaviour design or intervention situation. Psychological theories with well­defined, ac­

and anticipatory emotions­that have established practical relevance were considered first,
whereas Health Belief Model, Comprehensive Action Determination Model and other the­
ories that focus too much on specific domains such as medical and environmental psy­

4.2.2 Guidance on adaptation scenarios
Designing for behaviour change is not merely about applying psychological theories mechanically—
it is more about making adaptive judgments based on specific target users. Therefore,
this model introduces a ”Psychological Principle Selector” during the development phase
to help designers choose more appropriate psychological strategies based on different
user characteristics and behavioural contexts and build sustainable behavioural design
approaches.
This mechanism guides designers through a series of questions that prompt reflection
across five key dimensions:
Behavioural Awareness: Are users already aware of the existence and seriousness of
the behavioral issue? If not, any design intervention may face the risk of weak cogni­
tive foundations, low motivation, or psychological resistance. This question determines
whether the intervention should begin at the stage of “problem awareness building.”
Frequency and Repetition: Is the behaviour a one­time decision (e.g., switching elec­
tricity providers), or a repeated behaviour (e.g., using plastic bags daily)? Behaviour
frequency affects memory load and intervention sustainability. Repetitive behaviours rely
more on internalization and habit formation strategies.
Motivational Direction: Are users primarily driven by intrinsic motivation (e.g., value
alignment), or by external incentives (e.g., rewards, punishment, or social pressure)?
Different motivational mechanisms call for different design emphases—on internal self­
building or external social/environmental triggers.
Social Influence Sensitivity: Are users easily influenced by others’ actions or norms? If
users care about peer evaluation, social norms and role models can be effective mecha­
nisms. If users are more autonomous, individual intrinsic motivation should be reinforced.
Barrier Type: Are users restricted by explicit external barriers (e.g., time, cost, complex­
ity), or mainly by psychological factors (e.g., fear, anxiety, uncertainty)? Designers must
distinguish whether the barrier is cognitive, emotional, social, or structural to apply the
right psychological mechanism.
To assist designers—especially those without formal training in psychology—in quickly
identifying the appropriate theoretical foundation for a given design task, a set of ques­
tions is developed around the above five dimensions. This decision­making tool guides
designers in evaluating key psychological variables such as motivation type, behaviour
type, social influence, and environmental constraints. Based on their answers, it automat­
ically recommends (or filters out) corresponding psychological theories using a system of
“Open” and “Close” labels.
Each question is based on common influence mechanisms in behavioral psychology and
is mapped to a theory number on the card. Designers answer each question based on the
characteristics of their target users in their projects. If they select one of the two options,
34
Designing for Sustainable Behaviour
==PAGE:45==
they will obtain a set of recommended theory numbers (marked as “Open”), thereby nar­
rowing the initial search range and improving strategy identification efficiency. Through
these contextual cues, the tool achieves flexibility and applicability across different design
tasks, enabling designers without a background in psychology to make effective theoret­
ical choices and transform psychological reasoning into design­driven decision­making
paths.
4.2.2.1 Are users aware that this is a problem worth changing?
This question aims to assess whether users have already developed basic awareness of
the behavioural issue. If users have not yet recognized the existence or severity of the
problem, any behavioural intervention will face the risk of weak cognitive foundations, low
motivation, or resistance. Therefore, this question helps determine whether the interven­
tion should begin at the stage of raising awareness and building intention.
When users answer “yes,” indicating that they are already aware of the problem, the
activated theories include: TPB and TDD. These two theories were selected because:
• Both are based on the assumption that users already have certain goal orientations
or intentions to change.
• They emphasize users’ weighing of the consequences of their actions (expected
benefits and costs), making them suitable for design scenarios where users are
already aware of the problem.
• TPB includes two elements: attitude and subjective norm, while TDD emphasizes
the cognitive process of delayed gratification.
When users answer “no,” indicating they have not yet recognized the problem, the acti­
vated theories include: TRA, O­C. These theories were selected because:
• They focus on establishing the prerequisites for behavioral motivation (e.g., attitude
formation, loss­driven motivation).
• TRA assumes that behavioral intent has not yet formed, making it more suitable
for early­stage strategies; O­C theory emphasizes the shaping of behavior through
rewards and punishments, which can help stimulate initial interest or motivation.
Therefore, the design objective of this problem is to assess whether users currently pos­
sess the cognitive foundation required to accept design interventions. The matched theo­
ries all assume that users possess a certain level of subjective willingness and preliminary
judgment capacity, enabling them to enter the strategic design phase.
4.2.2.2 Is this behaviour you aim to change a one­time action or a long­term habit?
This question helps distinguish whether the design target is a single action or a repeti­
tive habit, in order to determine whether habit­maintenance psychological theories should
be applied. Long­term behaviours typically require stronger motivational stability, self­
regulation, and social reinforcement structures, whereas one­time behaviours rely more
on immediate motivation and external triggers.
When users respond with “long­term habits,” the activated theories include: FBM and
SCT. These two theories were selected because:
• They emphasize long­term maintenance pathways involving repetitive behavior, en­
vironmental feedback, or modeling learning.

• They are applicable to design strategies aimed at habit formation (e.g., building
social support systems, providing continuous reminders, etc.).
When users respond with “one­time behavior,” the activated theories include: TIB, Dual­
process model. These two theories were selected because:
• TIB incorporates “emotion and habit” into behavioral explanations, suitable for one­
time triggered behaviors;
• Dual emphasizes rapid, unconscious System 1 decision­making, suitable for simple,
one­time behaviors.
4.2.2.3 Are users mainly driven by internal motivation or external rewards/social
pressure?
This question helps identify whether users’ behaviours are mainly influenced by intrin­
sic motivation or extrinsic drivers such as rewards, punishment, or social pressure. This
distinction is critical, as most behavioural theories differ significantly in their assumptions
about motivation. Intrinsic motivation is better for designing long­term behaviour align­
ment, while extrinsic motivation is often used for short­term activation.
When users respond with “internal motivation,” the activated theories include: GFT and
CDT. These two theories were selected because:
• Both focus on the internal drive for consistency between behavior and individual
cognition.
• GFT emphasizes how people frame actions as moral, pleasurable, or socially re­
sponsible; CDT emphasizes that people seek cognitive consistency and thus ac­
tively adjust their behavior.
When users respond with “external motivation,” the activated theories include: O­C, SCT.
These two theories were selected because:
• O­C encourages behavioral change through “reward/punishment” mechanisms;
• SCT enhances the influence of external motivation through observation, modeling,
and other methods.
4.2.2.4 Are users influenced by what others do or think?
This question assesses whether users are sensitive to social influence, such as peer
opinions or group behaviour. In many sustainable or health design contexts, social norms,
modeling, and group belonging play a crucial role in behavioural change. This question
helps determine whether social­level psychological mechanisms should be introduced.
When users answer “yes” (i.e., users are susceptible to social influence), the activated
theories include: TPB, SCT. These two theories were selected because:
• TPB emphasizes the influence of subjective norms on behavioral intentions;
• SCT provides design inspiration for “group­guided” approaches such as imitating
others and social feedback.
When users answer “no” (i.e., users make decisions more individually), the activated the­
ories include: CDT, TDD. These two theories were selected because:
• They are more applicable to the cognitive balance between internal motivation and
future expectations;

• They can be used to design strategies oriented toward personal values and long­
term planning.
4.2.2.5 Are there clear external barriers to action (e.g., time, money, complexity)?
This question determines whether behaviour execution is constrained by external con­
textual barriers such as time, cost, or complexity. These barriers are key in translating
intention into action. Identifying them helps determine whether the intervention should
focus on structural changes rather than just psychological motivation.
When users answer “yes” (i.e., there are clear external barriers), the activated theories
include: COM­B and BCW. These two theories were selected because:
• Both are based on system structure and executive function, and are applicable to
strategies for structural optimization, capacity enhancement, or resource provision.
For example, public facility support, process simplification, etc.
When users answer “no” (i.e., external barriers are not significant), the activated theories
include: Dual­process model, FBM. These two theories were selected because:
• They are applicable to design scenarios with low cognitive load, such as habit for­

• FBM also aligns with the motivation and capability matching pathways in accessible
scenarios.

4.3.1.3 Preliminary Feedback and Observations of MVP prototype I
In pilot tests, nearly all participants showed signs of confusion at different stages. While
the behavioural stage classification was generally understood, the transition from identi­
fying behavioural problems to generating strategies lacked fluency. Several participants
noted that Step 3 felt too similar to Step 2, resulting in diminished engagement and unclear
functional differences between phases.
Regarding the theory cards, most participants found the cues too abstract, making it dif­
ficult to translate them into actionable design strategies. Many requested at least one
concrete example or contextual scenario per card.
Despite these issues, most participants appreciated the card format and workshop setting,
stating that the tool “stimulated reflection” and “sparked curiosity about user behaviour.”
However, they also offered clear critiques:
• The tool lacked gamified elements, especially in the later stages where engagement
dropped;
• Without a facilitator, completing the full process independently was difficult, sug­
gesting the need for improved instructions or a self­guided flow.
These insights provided concrete directions for the second prototype, particularly regard­
ing stage structure, card guidance, and solo usability support.
4.3.1.4 Conclusion of MVP prototype I
The first MVP validated the feasibility of structuring the psychology–design logic into a
“cards + process map” format, showing that this pathway could indeed help designers
analyze behaviours and anticipate interventions. Its limitations also revealed opportunities
for refinement in structure and visual communication for the next iteration.

In the Stage Perception stage, the designer will combine the results of his/her preliminary
user research, and assist in judging the target user’s current behavioural stage (Trigger,
Action, Maintenance or Ensure) through the Behavioural Stage Dial. Each turn of the dial
triggers a trigger. Each turn of the dial triggers a question that the designer has to answer

they showed in the pilot, and strengthens the basis of decision­making that focuses on
the real user.
Step 2: Mechanism Matching
At this point, the designer will make a brief statement based on the user insights from the
project, in terms of three key dimensions: whether the user is aware of the value of the
behaviour, whether they have tried the behaviour before and why they failed, and their
perception of the attitudes of others.
As the pilot study found that designers tended to share as many design ideas or findings
as possible with their fellow designers or Facilitators, the initial ‘5 whys’ were condensed
into 3 questions in this prototype, and the length of sharing was limited to 5 minutes per
question to ensure that the content was focused. Facilitator will record key information
during the process and help designers to identify the psychological mechanisms behind
the behaviours in conjunction with the Mechanism Definition Framework. In the end,
designers will be provided with 3 to 6 behavioural mechanism cards, which will be used
as a reference base for theory matching in the next stage.

This chapter focuses on the core task of the normative research phase, which is to trans­
late psychological theory of behaviour into a decision support tool that can be used in the
early design phase. Through the construction of a ‘psychology­design mapping matrix’
and a mechanism matching system, the abstract principles of behavioural science were
disassembled into perceptible, judicious, and applicable design elements, and eventually

integrated into a card­based toolkit with operational procedures and collaborative mech­
anisms.
The MVP Prototype III iteration not only responds to key feedback from the pilot study,
such as the difficulty in understanding mechanisms and the ambiguity of strategy paths,
but also introduces a clear division of roles and phases to enhance the tool’s guiding power
and co­creation. Through the five steps of behavioural stage identification, mechanism
matching, theory guidance, contextual perturbation and strategy combination, the tool
encourages designers to think systematically about the psychological logic in sustainable
behavioural interventions from the perspective of user behaviour and embed the theory
into the specific strategy generation process.
The core contribution of the tool is that it not only helps designers to identify the deep psy­
chological barrier of ‘why users don’t change’, but also stimulates the thinking of strategy
construction of ‘how to translate into intervention paths’, and thus introduces a theory­
supported judgement mechanism in the early stage of design. In particular, through the
‘Mechanism ­ Intervention Path’ strategy, the mechanism of judgement supported by the­

combination of ‘mechanism­theory­field’, the tool provides a structured path to dismantle
complex problems, and opens up a new entrance to intervention design for designers with
non­psychological backgrounds.
However, in the process of developing the tool, a number of issues have gradually emerged
that are worthy of further exploration. For example, although the card system enhances
the readability and engagement of the tool, designers may still experience uncertainty

accompaniment of a facilitator; furthermore, although this study attempts to introduce
unconventional strategy combinations to expand the design boundaries, their practical
feasibility and acceptance in real project settings remain to be verified. These issues will
be further developed and discussed in the evaluation study in Chapter Five.
In summary, this chapter demonstrates the process of translating psychological theory
structures into a design language that is ‘dialogueable, collaborative and embeddable’. It
not only serves as a response to research question three, but also provides a solid foun­
dation for subsequent evaluation studies and strategy iterations. Through this normative
research process, designers are no longer just passively selecting theories, but become
active participants in the application of theories and the construction of intervention strate­
gies.

5.1
Introduction to Descriptive Study II (DS­II)
This pilot study aims to validate the feasibility and logical consistency of the “three­step
method” tool among designers, observe the quality of sustainable behavior intervention
strategies generated by designers based on this process, and assess the impact of these
strategies on the perceptions and behavioral intentions of target users. In addition to fo­
cusing on the application effectiveness of the process, the test was also used to identify
specific issues in interface design, language expression, and interaction processes, while

5.2
Methodology of DS­II
This study employs a mixed­methods approach to systematically explore how users in­
teract with the tool, which parts are intuitive and easy to understand, which parts are
confusing, and whether the tool can truly help users develop effective behavior change
strategies. The test invited four design­related students with diverse backgrounds to par­
ticipate, providing a variety of quantitative scores and qualitative feedback.
The reason for adopting a mixed­methods approach is that the study’s objectives include
both validating functionality and usability, as well as gaining a deep understanding of the
psychological mechanisms and thought processes involved in the tool’s usage. Quantita­
tive data (such as Likert scales) provides intuitive numerical feedback to identify obvious
shortcomings or prioritize optimizations; qualitative data (such as open­ended feedback
and user quotes) helps researchers understand users’ actual thought processes, sources
of confusion, and potential strategy insights. Therefore, mixed methods are particularly
suitable for the dual purposes of structurally validating early prototypes and exploring
conceptual aspects.

Limitation
Despite the promising feedback, this pilot test had several limitations. First, the number

which may limit the generalizability of the findings to broader professional contexts. Sec­

selection of psychological principles was pre­set rather than fully open­ended, which may
have constrained users’ exploration. Lastly, the random event mechanism, while engag­
ing, may have introduced variability that impacted the consistency of user evaluation.
5.3.5
Ethical considerations
This study involved multiple stages related to psychological mechanisms, user cognition,
and co­creation behavior during its development and evaluation process. Therefore, the
following ethical considerations were thoroughly taken into account in the design and
testing of the tools:
1. Transparency and informed consent
All participants received a clear and concise informed consent form prior to the start
of testing or workshops. This form outlined the research objectives, tool content, ac­
tivity procedures, data usage, and participants’ rights (such as the option to withdraw
at any time). The consent form emphasized that participation was entirely voluntary
and that no sensitive or personally identifiable information would be collected.

2. Ethical boundaries in psychological intervention design
Psychological principles such as “social norms,” “anticipated regret,” and “commit­
ment mechanisms” used in the tool are widely applied in behavioral design. How­
ever, in educational and experimental settings, it is essential to avoid inducing, ma­
nipulating, or creating emotional burdens. Therefore, this study clearly labels the
mechanism of each strategy card and guides participants in workshops to discern
their applicable boundaries, preventing the misuse of psychological interventions
that could influence users’ decision­making freedom.
3. Participants’ Cognitive Load and Experience
Considering participants’ disciplinary backgrounds and cognitive differences, the
tool avoids jargon and uses concrete visual cues and card examples whenever pos­
sible. Additionally, all testing activities are kept within a reasonable timeframe (no
more than two hours) and provide ample breaks and open­ended question oppor­
tunities to reduce cognitive load and anxiety.
4. Data Anonymity and Feedback Mechanisms in Co­creation Processes
In co­creation workshops, all recorded discussion content and strategy outputs are
identified with anonymous codes. Any use of participants’ spoken content requires
their written authorization. Participants may freely choose whether to retain their
strategy outputs after the activity and have the right to access research summaries
or feedback on outcomes to enhance their sense of participation and trust.

• Help designers identify the key behavior stages at which user decisions occur,
specifically: triggering attention, forming motivation, taking action and maintaining
behavior;
• Build behavior intervention strategies with a stronger psychological foundation;
• Lower the barrier to using interdisciplinary theories to enhance engagement and
systems thinking.
Additionally, the study found that the tool currently has shortcomings in interface mapping,
language translation, and user persona adaptation. Future versions are recommended to
iterate on “mechanism visualization,” “user module customization,” and “multi­stage path
tracking.”

strategic planning in sustainable design.

Discussion
6.1
Aims and Objectives
With the aim of ‘systematically introducing psychological theory into the design process
of sustainable behaviour’, this research is dedicated to solving the long­standing prob­
lems of disconnected theory, unclear strategy and lack of early judgement in design prac­
tice. Through the step­by­step advancement of the DRM (Design Research Methodology)
framework, we have constructed a ‘theory­mechanism­design strategy’ mapping matrix
and transformed it into a set of tools that can be used, discussed and iterated upon to
address the core challenges of the four research questions.
Firstly, regarding RQ1: Why is it important and necessary to integrate psychological the­
ories into sustainable behaviour design, the need for psychological theory integration, we
systematically analysed the existing DfSB literature to identify the limitations of its cover­
age of behavioural phases and the use of theory types, and revealed the heavy reliance
on cognitive and motivational theories in current practice, neglecting the potential of social
and situational mechanisms. It is in this context that this study is developed, proposing a
two­dimensional classification of stages and mechanisms at the level of system architec­
ture, providing a new organising logic for theory integration.
Secondly, for the construction of RQ2 (How can psychological theories be systematically
mapped to behavioural change design strategies, and what mechanisms enable effective
translation from theory to practice): System Mapping Approach, we developed a mech­
anism mapping matrix and an adaptation pathway guide, forming a set of cognitive tools
that ‘guide strategy generation in a structured way’. In contrast to the past card tools that
only serve as inspiration, this study emphasizes the systematic coherence of ‘theoretical
mechanism­user profile­behavioural barriers­intervention strategy’, so that design deci­
sions can be based on theoretical deduction rather than empirical intuition alone. This
enables design decisions to be based on theoretical reasoning rather than empirical intu­
ition.
In RQ3: How can a behavioural mechanism­oriented ”psychology­to­design” tool enhance
designers’ capability to design context­adaptive interventions? Validation of the tool’s
efficacy, we verified the positive effects of the tool in increasing the density of theory use,
the recognition of behavioural barriers, and the diversity of strategies through two rounds
of designers’ workshops and the feedback of four in­depth interviews with the participants,
and obtained empirical support for the mechanism of ‘anterograde cognitive constructs’.
Although the sample size is still limited, the results initially show the positive effect of the
tool on improving the quality of early judgements in the design process.
Finally, RQ4: To what extent does the proposed framework improve accessibility and ef­
fectiveness in real­world scenarios? Practical Value explores the potential of the tool in
two typical scenarios: in classroom teaching, as a ‘cognitive visualisation mediator’ to
help students understand the practical application of abstract behavioural theories; and in
corporate workshops and project collaboration, as a co­creation support system for cross­
role collaboration. On the other hand, in corporate workshops and project collaboration, it
serves as a co­creation support system, providing a shared context and decision­making
baseline for cross­role collaboration, which significantly improves the efficiency and inter­
pretability of strategy generation.

6.2
Contributions
The main contribution of this study is the development and practical validation of a ‘theory­
mechanism­strategy’ mapping system to support the systematic integration and strategic
translation of psychological theory in sustainable behaviour design. The framework not
only responds to the long­standing problem of ‘theory narrowing’ in design research, but
also provides an actionable cognitive support tool for the DfSB field, complementing the
current fragmentation of design practice in the use of theory.
In contrast to the isolated use of single theories such as Fogg’s model and TPB in the
existing literature (see 3.2.4), this study for the first time structurally groups twelve repre­
sentative psychological theories according to the stages of user behaviour (e.g., triggering
period, maintenance period) and the attributes of the mechanisms (cognitive, motivational,
social, etc.), and proposes a ‘Stage­adapted Theory Cluster’ and ‘Mechanism­adapted
Theory Cluster’. Two types of strategy units are proposed, namely ‘stage­adapted theory
group’ and ‘mechanism­complementary combination’. For example, combining the the­
ory of planned behaviour with the theory of social norms is more effective in addressing
the cognitive­behavioural gap, while the synergy between self­determination theory and
goal­setting theory is more suitable for behaviour maintenance and internalisation.
It is worth emphasising that this study not only integrates psychological theories at the
theoretical level, but also methodologically transforms from a static theory base to a dy­
namically designed decision support system through the design of the ‘Psychological Prin­
ciple Selector’ (4.2.2). This transformation is not only a technical optimisation, but also an
intervention in the cognitive process of design: it enables designers to construct hypothe­
ses and calculate intervention paths at the conceptual stage, leading to the generation of
preemptive and structured strategies.
By embedding theoretical mapping mechanisms into a system of gamified design tools,
we facilitate the construction of an “evolutionary cognitive model”. Instead of viewing de­
sign as a static problem solving process, the system views design as a multiple interaction
process between ‘designers­users­society’, emphasising the closed­loop logic between
hypothesis testing, strategy adjustment and behavioural feedback. We believe that this
shift in design cognition not only helps to improve the interpretability of the design process,
but also provides a point of convergence between theory and practice for the mediation of
interdisciplinary knowledge exchange (especially psychology, behavioural sciences and
design).
6.3
Inspiration and Implications for Design Practice
From the perspective of design practice, the most significant value of the tool system
is in its strategy perception and decision support in the ‘early conceptual stage’. While
traditional DfSB methods often rely on late­stage behavioural testing or intuition­based
intervention guessing, the tool developed in this study enables designers to structurally
identify and set strategies for user behavioural barriers at an early stage through mecha­
nism mapping matrices, psychological decks, and a user persona system.
The empirical test results show that the tool significantly improves designers’ ability to per­
ceive the types of behavioural barriers (78% coverage, compared to 52% in traditional in­
terview analysis) and their frequency of citing literature theories during the design process
(3.2 times increase in theoretical density), which verifies the tool’s utility in improving the
design cognition and strategy expression ability. This improvement is not only reflected
in the data level, but more importantly reflects a paradigm shift in design cognition: from
70
Designing for Sustainable Behaviour
==PAGE:81==
the linear thinking of ‘problem­solution’ to the systematic modelling of ‘mechanism­path­
feedback’.
Further, the tool’s extensibility and modular architecture also give it a strong potential
for adaptation. Its card mechanism and behavioural stage model can be extended with
content based on specific domains (e.g. food waste, public transport, mental health),
while the visualisation strategy in the interface design provides an intuitive and action­
able pathway for non­professional background users. Although the initial test users were
mainly designers, the feedback indicated that the tool was also a good knowledge bridge
in multidisciplinary collaboration, especially in facilitating dialogue and consensus building
between design and research, planning and implementation.
6.4 Reflections and Limitations
Despite the progress made in this study in building a system of structured psychological
tools, several key limitations and challenges still need to be faced. Firstly, in terms of the­
oretical integration, this study focused on 12 classical psychological theories and did not
yet encompass cutting­edge areas such as emotion dynamics, neural design or metacog­
nition. While this choice ensures that the theories are operational and representative, it
also inevitably compresses the breadth of the theoretical dimensions and may not fully
cover behavioural patterns in complex contexts.
Secondly, in terms of mapping between user profiles and behavioural barriers, we tried to
establish a ternary path of ‘behavioural stage­user type­barrier mechanism’, and designed
a number of typical user role cards. However, in the actual test, some users reported that
their behavioural motivations and scenarios were difficult to be covered by the existing
cards, which suggests that the current user persona database is still insufficient in terms
of breadth and diversity. In particular, there is insufficient support for marginalised groups,
cross­cultural users or special contexts (e.g., post­disaster environments, ageing back­
grounds), which poses a challenge to the adaptability of the tool.
In addition, in terms of interaction mechanisms, although the gamification design en­
hances the user’s sense of participation, the test also revealed that certain operation logics
have high cognitive load. For example, the behavioural phase transition logic, event trig­
gering and strategy linkage created comprehension barriers for some users, especially
the simultaneous processing of a large amount of textual information, which resulted in
a more burdensome experience. Therefore, future versions need to introduce more con­
crete example diagrams, guiding animations and multimodal information assistance in the
interactive interface to enhance comprehension and the willingness to continue using.
Finally, in terms of the evaluation methodology, although we collected initial feedback
through designer workshops and individual interviews, the overall sample size was small
and focused mainly on participants with design backgrounds, and a systematic evaluation
of cross­domain usage effects has yet to be formed. Therefore, in the future, we need to
introduce A/B tests on a larger scale and with more diverse populations, supplemented
by behavioural data tracking and longitudinal studies, in order to assess the effectiveness
of the actual intervention and the continuity of its use.
6.5 Comparison and Positioning with Existing Studies
This study is located at the intersection of Design for Sustainable Behaviour (DfSB) and
psychological theory translation. On the one hand, compared with existing single­theory
driven design tools such as TPB and Fogg (e.g., Design with Intent Toolkit), this study
builds a multi­theory integration model that can be used across phases and mechanisms;

validation’, we pay more attention to ‘prospective construction’ and ‘strategy translation’.
On the other hand, compared with the empirical research path that emphasises ‘a pos­

In addition, from a methodological point of view, most of the existing tools focus on user

7
Conclusions and future work
7.1
Fulfilment of the Learning Objectives
The purpose of this section is to assess how well the study has achieved the initial learning
objectives throughout the DRM research path. Each learning objective has been mapped
to the different stages of the study, the core outputs, and analysed and reflected upon in
relation to the empirical results.
1. Assessing the effectiveness and limitations of existing Design for Sustainable
Behaviour (DfSB) practices
In the descriptive research phase I (Chapter 3.2), through a systematic literature
review, this study revealed significant shortcomings in DfSB practices in terms of
behavioural stage coverage, methodological systematicity and theoretical integra­
tion capabilities. In particular, intervention preferences are focused on the product
level and short­term behavioural triggers, with the behavioural maintenance and as­
surance phases notably absent. At the same time, the use of psychological theories
tends to be ‘narrow’, with only a few theories being widely adopted, which provides
an important motivation for this study to propose a systematic theory integration
framework.
2. Exploring opportunities for integrating psychological strategies into the de­
sign of sustainable behaviours
Through the analysis of theoretical mechanisms (Chapter 3.3) and the modelling of
behavioural mechanisms (Section 3.1.2), the study identifies that motivational reg­
ulation, contextual shaping, and habit building are under­utilised in DfSB, but have
high potential for design translation. These findings form the basis of the subse­
quent Theory­Mechanism­Strategy mapping model, and define the key points at
which psychological theory can intervene in the design process.
3. Identifying and reflecting on psychological theories used in current sustain­
able design practice
The study systematically identified 27 behavioural psychological theories and anal­
ysed their citation frequency and depth of application in current DfSB research
(Chapter 3.3). The results show the dominance of TPB and Fogg’s model, and
the low frequency of use of theories critical to behavioural persistence, such as
cognitive dissonance and self­determination, suggesting that most of the theories
have not yet been effectively operationalised. This study accordingly selected 12
theories as the core of the instrument to ensure a balance of theoretical coverage,
mechanism diversity and behavioural stages.
4. Using psychological theory to identify drivers and barriers to sustainable be­
haviour
In the Research Clarification (RC) phase, eight categories of core behavioural mech­
anisms and their pathways to sustainable behaviour were identified through litera­
ture and mechanism modelling. These mechanisms not only provide a structural
basis for the construction of the tool, but are also embedded in the design decision
pathways, which are used to help designers diagnose behavioural problems and
match intervention strategies.
5. Designing sustainable behavioural intervention strategies that facilitate the
Designing for Sustainable Behaviour
73
==PAGE:84==
application of psychological theory
In the normative phase (Chapter 4.1), a “psychology­design” mapping matrix was
constructed and an intervention tool based on this logic was developed. The tool
system translates abstract theoretical structures into design recommendations for
specific behavioural problems through stage delineation, mechanism matching and
strategy recommendations, which significantly improves the translation of theory
into practice.
6. Testing the proposed strategies and tools in real scenarios
In the descriptive research II phase (Chapter 5.2), the effectiveness of the tools in
supporting strategy generation, mechanism identification and user behaviour judge­
ment was verified through the Designer Workshop. The research tasks cover typical
domains such as energy consumption behaviour, shopping behaviour, etc., and ini­
tially verify the tool’s adaptability in different design problems.
7. Optimising the tool system based on application results and user feedback
Based on the feedback from user tests and interviews (Chapter 5.3), the tool system
has gone through a number of version iterations, including optimisation of the con­
tent of the theory cards, enhancement of the interface guidance and diversification
of the user role cards. This process reflects the iterative path of user­centred design
and reinforces the usability and transferability of the tool.
8. Constructing a decision framework for systematic mapping of psychological
theory to sustainable behaviour design
The complete theory mapping framework is constructed in Chapters 4.1–4.2, which
helps designers systematically diagnose user behavioural phases, identify mecha­
nistic barriers, and match psychological theories with feasible strategies. The frame­
work not only provides a theoretical support path for DfSB, but also provides a struc­
tured guide for early strategy judgement in the design process.
9. Developing tools and methods to support designers to implement psycholog­
ical insights
Through the MVP prototype development (Chapter 4.3), the research has devel­
oped a complete tool flow covering ‘diagnosis­matching­adaptation’, which employs
paper cards, question guides and user simulations to help designers to implement
psychological insights independently or collaboratively.
10. Identifying and reflecting on the limitations and room for improvement of the
developed framework
In the user testing and practical feedback (Chapter 5.3), the tool showed that there
is still room for improvement in terms of terminology understanding, user profile
matching, and stage path guidance. Based on these findings, the study proposes
directions for future iterations of the version, including enhancement of card visual­
isation, optimisation of mechanism labelling and collaborative path recording, etc.,
in order to further expand the applicability boundaries and cognitive accessibility of
the tool.
7.2
Future Research Directions
The Theory­Mechanism­Strategy mapping system constructed in this study, together with
its derived gamification tools, provides a new entry point for early strategy formulation in
sustainable behaviour design. However, as an experimental prototype system, there is
still plenty of room for future evolution, and we hope to further improve and expand the
tool system in the following directions:
74
Designing for Sustainable Behaviour

In the short term, researchers can focus on expanding the library of psychological theory
by incorporating relevant paradigms that have emerged in recent years, such as neural
design, behavioral economics, and emotion regulation, in order to enhance the tool’s
explanatory power and intervention support for complex behavioural phenomena.
At the same time, it is suggested to develop a digital collaboration platform to enable
modular and traceable online operation of the card system. This would address real pain
points such as “difficulty in collaboration”, “insufficient records”, and “lack of tracking”,
thereby enhancing the feasibility of applying the tool in design education and remote co­
creation contexts.
In the mid­term, the research focus will shift to the data­driven intelligent evolution mech­
anism. On the one hand, it is suggested to introduce user interaction data into the mid­
term study to establish a causal inference model of “theory–intervention–behavioural re­
sponse”, so as to promote the prediction of the effect of the intervention strategy and the
verification of the mechanism.
On the other hand, systematic testing of cross­cultural user groups can be conducted
to explore the strategy suitability, mechanism sensitivity, and linguistic ambiguity of the
tool in a multicultural context, aiming to improve the ecological validity and cross­domain
applicability of the tool.
From a long­term perspective, the tool system can evolve into a cognitive­linguistic­
strategic integrated language system that supports sustainable behavioural design. This
system would not only serve designers’ strategy generation, but also facilitate cross­
disciplinary communication and decision­making co­creation.
The envisioned system could include the following modules: a standardised mechanism
language, a library of typical behavioural pathways, a situational consensus model, and
combinable intervention morphemes, thereby supporting systematic collaboration and de­
sign judgement in complex social issues.
In addition, researchers can explore the integration path of the system with AI models,
psychological databases, and educational platforms. By using large language models
for theoretical semantic analysis and scenario generation, or by embedding the tool into
teaching and industry practice workflows, the adaptability and sustainable value of the
system in education, healthcare, and policy­making can be further extended.
7.3
Conclusion
Design is not just about “making change”, but also a cognitive act of identifying potential,
organising hope, and conveying understanding. This study hopes to provide designers
with not only a set of tools, but also a mode of thinking, a method of communication, and
even the possibility of a path of understanding to a more complex world. It may not be
able to solve complex behavioural problems directly, but it will enable designers to more
consciously ask, interrogate and debug their intervention hypotheses in order to build
a more strategic and ethical framework for design practice in the midst of uncertainty.
It is hoped that this project will not only serve as a starting point for designers to think
about complex sustainability issues, but will also evolve into a bridge between theory and
practice, between strategy and heart.

Q1. Are users aware that this is a problem worth changing?
Scenario 1: Yes, users are aware of the problem.
Open:
1. Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA): When users already recognize the importance of
the problem, they hold a positive attitude towards behavior change and care about
others’ expectations. TRA emphasizes that attitude and subjective norms jointly
determine behavioral intentions (Fishbein Ajzen, 1975). When users evaluate the
behavior change positively (attitude) and believe important others expect them to
change (subjective norms), their willingness to act is stronger.
2. Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB): When users are aware of the problem, TPB can
better predict behavioral intentions. In addition to attitude and social norms, TPB in­
troduces perceived behavioral control, i.e., the user’s perceived ability to change. If
users recognize the problem, they may have already formed an intention to change.
TPB helps analyze the influence of attitude, social pressure, and self­efficacy on
that intention (Ajzen, 1991).
3. Fogg Behavior Model (FBM): When users are aware of the problem, they usually
have some motivation to change. FBM states that behavior occurs when there is suf­
ficient motivation, ability to perform the behavior, and an appropriate trigger (Fogg,
2009). For users who are aware of the issue, their existing motivation can be lever­
aged, and behavior can be promoted by increasing ability (reducing difficulty) and
setting triggers.
4. Cognitive Dissonance Theory (CDT): Users who recognize the problem but have not
yet changed their behavior may experience cognitive dissonance—a psychological
discomfort resulting from a conflict between their behavior and values or health be­
liefs. This inner conflict motivates them to reduce discomfort by changing their be­
havior (Festinger, 1957). Thus, CDT can strengthen their internal motivation: when
behavior contradicts cognition, the discomfort prompts them to adjust behavior to
restore consistency.
5. Temporal Discounting Theory (TDD): Even if users realize the importance of the
problem, they may delay action due to the delay in benefits or losses. People often
prefer immediate rewards and underestimate future outcomes, known as present
bias (Ainslie, 1975). For users aware of the problem, if the behavior yields long­term
benefits (e.g., health), TDD explains why they may delay or ignore change. Hence,
instant feedback or short­term incentives are needed to counteract this tendency.
6. COM­B Model: This model states that behavior depends on three factors: Capa­
bility, Opportunity, and Motivation (Michie et al., 2011). When users recognize a
problem, they usually have psychological capability (awareness) and some inter­
nal motivation. What’s needed is to ensure they have the actual opportunity and
skills to change. COM­B helps assess whether user skills or environmental support

Scenario 2: No, users are not aware of the problem.
Open:
1. Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB): If users are unaware, intervention should shift
their perception of consequences. TPB’s beliefs and attitude components can be
influenced through information to help them understand the risks and benefits of cur­
rent behavior (Ajzen, 1991). Lowering perceived difficulty and boosting self­efficacy
also help users consider change.
2. Behaviour Change Wheel (BCW): When users lack awareness, multi­level inter­
ventions are needed. BCW’s core COM­B model highlights psychological capability
(e.g., knowledge and awareness) (Michie et al., 2011). It provides a framework for
using education, promotion, and policy to raise awareness.
3. Goal Framing Theory (GFT): For unaware users, appealing to their hedonistic or
gain­related goals (e.g., immediate pleasure or savings), or normative goals (e.g.,
moral or social obligations) can increase perceived relevance of the issue (Linden­
berg & Steg, 2007).
4. Operant Conditioning (O­C): For users lacking intrinsic motivation, extrinsic rewards
or punishments are powerful initial drivers. Incentives or mild punishments help
them start considering the change (Skinner, 1953).
5. Temporal Discounting Theory (TDD): Unaware users may undervalue future risks or
long­term benefits. Highlighting immediate consequences or short­term gains helps
overcome this present bias (Green & Myerson, 2004).
6. Social Cognitive Theory (SCT): Many people recognize problems through social
comparison or observation.
Public role models and peer discussions can raise
awareness. SCT’s verbal persuasion and supportive environments can enhance
self­efficacy (Bandura, 1977).
Q2. Is the behavior a one­time action or a long­term habit?
Scenario 1: Yes, it is a one­time behavior.
Open:
1. Theory of Interpersonal Behavior (TIB): TIB integrates emotions and habitual pro­
cesses, which are particularly suitable for scenarios involving quick or singular be­
havioral triggers. For users making a one­time decision (e.g., buying an eco­product
once), TIB highlights the impact of emotions, social norms, and facilitating conditions
(Triandis, 1977).
2. Dual­Process Decision Model (Dual): This theory distinguishes between intuitive
(System 1) and analytical (System 2) thinking. For one­time decisions, users of­
ten rely on intuition or quick judgments. Hence, strategies such as nudges, visual
prompts, or emotional cues can effectively influence behavior without deep deliber­
ation (Kahneman, 2011).
Close:
1. Behaviour Change Wheel (BCW): BCW emphasizes multi­level interventions and
structural changes. For short­term or one­off actions, its broad framework (e.g.,

Scenario 2: No, it is a long­term habit.
Open:
3. Behaviour Change Wheel (BCW): BCW is more suitable for long­term behavioral
change. It supports continuous improvement through policy, environment, and so­
cial interventions. It helps design gradual nudges and reinforcement mechanisms
to maintain long­term behavior (Michie et al., 2011).
4. COM­B Model: For repeated behaviors, COM­B is helpful in analyzing psychological
capability (e.g., habit memory), physical opportunity (e.g., availability of options),
and reflective or automatic motivation. It helps sustain behavior change across time
(Michie et al., 2011).
5. Theory of Interpersonal Behavior (TIB): Although TIB includes habit elements, its
main strength lies in explaining initial decisions influenced by emotion and social fac­
tors. For long­term habit formation, more structural frameworks are needed. TIB’s
lack of systematic reinforcement limits its role here (Triandis, 1977).
6. Dual­Process Decision Model (Dual): While this theory is useful in understand­
ing spontaneous decisions, long­term behavior maintenance relies more on system
planning than momentary intuitive choices. Dual­processing has limited utility when
deliberate planning dominates (Kahneman, 2011).
Q3. Is the user intrinsically motivated? Scenario 1: Yes, they are internally motivated.
Open:
1. Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB): When users have intrinsic motivation, TPB can
help clarify how attitude, social norms, and perceived behavioral control interact to
enhance intention. At this point, the main focus should be on removing obstacles
and reinforcing their intention to act (Ajzen, 1991).
2. Cognitive Dissonance Theory (CDT): Intrinsically motivated users may experience
dissonance if their current actions do not match their inner values. CDT can help re­
inforce this internal inconsistency and drive them to take action to reduce discomfort
(Festinger, 1957).
3. Social Cognitive Theory (SCT): Internally motivated users are more likely to ini­
tiate action. SCT emphasizes self­efficacy and observational learning. Peer sup­
port, feedback, and positive social feedback can further enhance internal motivation
(Bandura, 1977).
Close:
1. Goal Framing Theory (GFT): If the user is already intrinsically motivated, there’s
no need to stimulate behavior with additional goal framing. Reframing could dilute
existing intrinsic motivation or create confusion (Lindenberg & Steg, 2007).

Scenario 2: No, they are not internally motivated.

Q4. Do users believe they are capable of taking action?
Scenario 1: Yes, they believe they are capable.
Open:
1. TPB: When users believe they are capable, the perceived behavioral control com­
ponent of TPB is strongly activated, enhancing intention and likelihood of behavior
change (Ajzen, 1991).
2. SCT: Self­efficacy is at the core of SCT. High self­efficacy not only promotes ac­
tion but also increases persistence and resilience when facing difficulties (Bandura,
1977).
3. COM­B: If users believe they are capable, the psychological capability and motiva­
tion elements are activated. Designers can now focus on optimizing environmental
opportunities to support action (Michie et al., 2011).
Close:
1. BCW: BCW includes structural­level interventions. If users already have confidence
in execution, there’s no need to initiate large­scale environmental changes. Simple
behavior support is more efficient (Michie et al., 2011).
2. O­C: For users already confident, external incentives may be unnecessary or even
counterproductive. It’s better to maintain their internal drive (Skinner, 1953).
Scenario 2: No, they don’t believe they are capable.

Q5. Is the user influenced by social norms or peer pressure?
Scenario 1: Yes, they are influenced.
Open:
1. Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA): Subjective norms are one of its two core com­
ponents. When users are concerned about others’ expectations, social influence
directly affects their intention (Fishbein & Ajzen, 1975).
2. TPB: TPB includes subjective norms and perceived social pressure. When users
are sensitive to group attitudes or expectations, TPB helps explain and shape their
behavioral intention (Ajzen, 1991).
3. SCT: Social influence is one of SCT’s core mechanisms. Peer modeling, feedback,
and social reinforcement help promote behavioral change (Bandura, 1977).
Close:
1. CDT: CDT focuses more on internal inconsistency.
If users’ behavior is driven
mainly by social expectations rather than inner belief, CDT is less relevant (Fes­
tinger, 1957).
2. Dual Process: In strong social influence situations, users may rely on fast, au­
tomatic responses (System 1), but deeper rational analysis is limited. Therefore,
dual­process reasoning is less applicable here (Kahneman, 2011).
Scenario 2: No, they are not influenced.
Open:
1. CDT: When users are uninfluenced by others, internal belief and consistency be­
come the main motivation. CDT helps highlight the tension between inner values
and existing behavior (Festinger, 1957).
2. Dual Process: Independent users may rely more on rational (System 2) thinking.

Appendix C: Future guidance
1. Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA): Emphasize positive attitudes and supportive so­
cial norms to strengthen the user’s intention, as strong intentions are key predictors of
actual behavior (Fishbein, M., & Ajzen, I. (1975). Belief, attitude, intention, and behavior:
An introduction to theory and research. Addison­Wesley).
2.Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB): Boost positive attitudes, supportive social norms,
and the user’s perceived control, as TPB finds these factors jointly strengthen intentions
and help translate them into behavior change (Ajzen, I. (1991). The theory of planned
behavior. Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 50(2), 179–211).
3.Theory of Interpersonal Behavior (TIB): Ensure the environment supports the in­
tended action by removing barriers and providing cues, as TIB emphasizes that favorable
facilitating conditions (along with intention and habit) are necessary for the behavior to
occur (Triandis, H. C. (1977). Interpersonal Behavior. Brooks/Cole Publishing).
4.Fogg’s Behavior Model (FBM): Present a trigger for the target action at moments when
the user has high motivation and sufficient ability, because FBM states that behavior oc­
curs only when a prompt coincides with enough motivation and ability (Fogg, B. J. (2009).
A behavior model for persuasive design. In Proceedings of the 4th International Confer­
ence on Persuasive Technology (pp. 1–7). ACM).
5.Behavior Change Wheel (BCW): Use a structured approach (e.g. the BCW) to di­
agnose whether the target behavior is limited by capability, opportunity, or motivation,
and then select intervention techniques addressing those specific needs, as this model
links behavioral diagnosis to effective strategies (Michie, S., van Stralen, M. M., & West,
R. (2011). The behavior change wheel: A new method for characterizing and designing
behavior change interventions. Implementation Science, 6(42), 1–11).
6.Goal Framing Theory (GFT): Frame the desired behavior in terms of a goal that matters
to the user (for example, highlighting personal gains, enjoyment, or moral reasons), since
GFT shows people are more likely to act when the message aligns with their gain, hedonic,
or normative motivations (Lindenberg, S., & Steg, L. (2007). Normative, gain and hedonic
goal frames guiding environmental behavior. Journal of Social Issues, 63(1), 117–137).
7.Cognitive Dissonance Theory (CDT): Prompt users to notice when their actions con­
flict with their beliefs or goals, as the discomfort of cognitive dissonance will drive them to
change their behavior to restore consistency (Festinger, L. (1957). A theory of cognitive
dissonance. Stanford University Press).
8.Operant Conditioning (O–C): Immediately reward desired user behaviors (or remove
negative outcomes) to reinforce those actions, as operant conditioning shows that be­
haviors followed by positive consequences are more likely to be repeated (Skinner, B. F.
(1953). Science and human behavior. Macmillan).
9.Theory of Delay Discounting (TDD): Highlight short­term benefits or provide instant
feedback for performing the target behavior, because TDD finds that people tend to un­
dervalue rewards that are delayed and favor immediate outcomes (Ainslie, G. (1975).
Specious reward: A behavioral theory of impulsiveness and impulse control. Psycholog­
ical Bulletin, 82(4), 463–496).

10.Social Cognitive Theory (SCT): Build users’ self­efficacy through small successes
and let them observe positive peer examples (e.g. testimonials or social sharing), since
SCT proposes that people learn from others and act when they feel confident in their
abilities (Bandura, A. (1986). Social foundations of thought and action: A social cognitive
theory. Prentice­Hall).
11.COM­B Model: Identify which prerequisite is lacking—Capability, Opportunity, or Motivation—
and design your solution to strengthen that element, as the COM­B model posits that be­
havior change requires addressing all three components (Michie, S., van Stralen, M. M.,
& West, R. (2011). The behavior change wheel: A new method for characterizing and
designing behavior change interventions. Implementation Science, 6(42), 1–11).
12.Dual­Process Decision Model (Dual): Make the desired action intuitive and easy
to encourage fast, automatic responses, while also providing clear information for more
deliberate consideration, as dual­process theory shows both impulsive “System 1” and
reflective “System 2” thinking shape our decisions (Kahneman, D. (2011). Thinking, fast
and slow. Farrar, Straus and Giroux).
