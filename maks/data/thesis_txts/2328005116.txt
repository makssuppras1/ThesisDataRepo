Abstract
The increasing demands from various industries of calculation tools for analysing beam-like struc-
tures with complex geometry consisting of general anisotropic materials, have the latest decades
motivated the development of several numerical calculation tools for determination of cross-section
properties based on either 2D or 3D ’¼ünite element discretisations. An approach of analysing a
3D slice of a beam for the determination of the corresponding cross-sectional properties, has been
developed more recently, and gives room for improvements of present formulations to be developed.
This thesis covers the formulation of a calculation tool for determination of cross-section properties
for straight prismatic beam slices with arbitrary cross-sections consisting of general anisotropic
materials.
The ’¼ürst part concerns a general static description of a straight prismatic beam for which it applies
that six states of static equilibrium can be formulated. The distribution of sectional forces for the
six equilibrium states are used for the description of the sectional elastic energies along the beam,
which is found as a product of the six by six cross-section ’¼éexibility matrix and the internal
sectional forces. By consideration of six load cases, for a discretisation of a beam slice, which leads
to internal force distributions corresponding to the ones of the six equilibrium states, the beam
’¼éexibility matrix is derived from the consideration of equivalence between the external and internal
elastic energies of the full beam slice.
In the second part a FEM-model for the discretisation of the 3D beam slice is developed, by
establishment of a ’¼ünite element with cubic 4 point Lagrange interpolation of the displacement
’¼üelds in the lengthwise direction. A clear model structure is introduced and the Lagrange method of
under-determined multipliers are presented and used for the further establishment of displacement
constraints.
In the last parts of the thesis the six load cases for the beam slice, leading to internal force
distributions corresponding to the ones of the six equilibrium states, are established by imposing
deformation to the end sections of the discretized beam slice, by enforcement of constraints using
the Lagrange method of under-determined multipliers. The earlier established energy equivalence
is then used in the determination of the beam ’¼éexibility matrix, from which the cross-section
’¼éexibility properties can be derived due to a well known distribution of the sectional internal
forces along the beam slice. Ultimately the 21 independent cross-sectional sti’¼Ćness component are
determined by inversion of the cross-section ’¼éexibility matrix.
The developed procedure is programmed in Matlab using a program structure of an earlier de-
veloped ’¼ünite element program, and four calculation examples are ’¼ünally carried out in order to
illustrate the use and validity of the procedure.

Introduction
The elastic beam theory was initiated centuries ago, by GalileoŌĆÖs(1564-1642) observation and de-
scriptions of beams made in the 17th century, and HookeŌĆÖs(1635-1703) later identi’¼ücation of the law
of proportionality for elastic materials, see [1]. Since then the mathematical theory of elasticity has
been developed by several actors up through the centuries. After the invention of the computer
and the development of the Finite Element Method(FEM), calculation procedures build on the
discretisation of beams by ’¼ünite elements have been developed in numbers. Typically those cal-
culation procedures have been based on a separation of the kinematics and statics into lengthwise
and cross-sectional behaviour, giving rise to a 2D cross-sectional problem and a 1D beam problem
to be solved. In the recent decades an increasing number of calculation methods for solving the 2D
cross-sectional problem have been developed. This tendency is seen as a results of the increasing
demands, from various industries, of lighter and more simple calculation procedures. One of those
industries is the wind energy industry which in the attempt of reducing the cost of energy of their
wind turbines, see Figure 1.1, are constructing larger rotor blades, consisting of increasingly more
complex cross-section geometries and composite materials. In general the interest in using compos-
ite materials with speci’¼üc ’¼über lay-ups and dimensions has increased in the construction industry.
The use of composite materials allow the user to design a structure with speci’¼üc intentional prop-
erties. In the case of wind turbine rotor blades this could for example be, to design a ’¼über lay-up
that couples bending and twist of the blade, which potentially reduces the load felt by the blade
due to the change in angle of attack, from the wind, on the twisted blade. Other examples of
industries which are demanding tools for calculation of the static and dynamic behaviour of beams
with complex geometries and material compositions, are the airplane industry that are attempting
to lower the weight and material consumption in airplanes, and also in the design of bridges where
there is a tendency of designing more sophisticated bridges with slender cross-sections of various
shapes, which for example is the case for the increasing number of constructed pedestrian bridges.

There exist several tools on the market which are able to cope with the analysis of more complex
beam structures, many of which uses shell elements to make full 3D analysis of the structures, see
for example Abaqus [3]. The full three dimensional Finite Element analysis is however computa-
tional expensive and demands big e’¼Ćorts in the mesh generation. When the analysed structures
are slender and displays beam-like behaviour a separation of the 3D beam problem into a 1D
problem in the longitudinal direction and a 2D problem for the orthogonal sections, are possible,
stated back in 1981 by Berdichevsky, see [4]. This separation potentially ease the mesh generation
and computational costs. The most widely recognised calculation procedure using this approach
is today, the one implemented in the production and design tool VABS(Variational Asymptotic
Beam Sectional Analysis) which were initiated by Hodges ’¼ürst mentioned in [5], and are later
described in the userŌĆÖs manual [6]. The procedure is an asymptotic exact methodology based on
geometrically non-linear three dimensional elasticity, restrained by having small strain relative to
unity and maximum dimension of the cross-section small relative to the length scale of the full
elasticity problem. VABS allows analysis of beams with arbitrary ’¼über orientations, initial curva-
tures and pre-twist which has made it highly adaptive for the analysis of for example wind turbine
rotor blades, see [7]. Another earlier developed method, presented by Giavotto et al. [8], also
separates the 3D problem into a 1D and 2D problem, and is developed from the principle of virtual
work where the displacement ’¼üelds are discretized by planar ’¼ünite elements. This approach were
’¼ürst implemented in the computer program ANBA(Nonhomogeneous Anisotropic Beam Section
Analysis) by Borri see [9], and has recently been used in the development of the cross-section
analysis tool BECAS(Beam Cross section Analysis Software) for anisotropic and inhomogeneous
beam sections of arbitrary geometry, see [10]. Through series of calculated examples there have
been found good agreement between the cross-section properties obtained by the two calculation
procedures BECAS and VABS, which therefore are chosen as the comparison base in many of the
later calculated examples using the present developed calculation tool.
A di’¼Ćerent way of dealing with the determination of the 2D cross-section properties is to consider
the equilibrium states of a 3D slice of the beam, for which the elastic energy can be determined
and used in further derivations of the beam ’¼éexibility components. This approach has been used
in the newly developed cross-section analysis procedure by Couturier et al. [11], which is based
on the concept of the establishment of six equilibrium states for a beam, earlier used by Krenk
and Jeppesen [12] and H├Ėgsberg and Krenk [13]. By imposing suitable end section displacements
in order to achieve displacement ’¼üelds giving rise to internal force distributions corresponding to
the ones of the six equilibrium states, the cross-section sti’¼Ćness parameters have been determined
through energy consideration. The same principle of the establishment of six deformation modes
has earlier been used by Jonnalagadda and Whitcombe [14]. For both approaches a special ad-
ditional correction has been needed for the two imposed shear deformation modes representing
an internal force distribution of constant shear forces along the beam slice.
Contrarily to the
equilibrium states of homogeneous tension, torsion and bending, the two equilibrium states of ho-
mogeneous shear couples with other internal forces, namely the bending moments that due to the
shear forces varies linearly over the beam slice. Jonnalagadda and Whitcombe tried to cope with
this problem by the establishment of boundary conditions from the consideration of a center unit
cell in a long beam exposed to constant shear forces, while Couturier et al. used the two bending
deformation modes to predict the in-plane contractions of the two shear deformation modes, while
simultaneously ensuring that the shear deformation modes remain orthogonal to the other four
deformation modes in terms of produced energy.
This thesis covers a reformulation of the calculation procedure developed by Couturier et al.[11],
from the use of ’¼ünite elements with cubic Hermitian lengthwise interpolation, to ’¼ünite elements
based on 4-point Lagrange interpolation in the lengthwise direction. The possibilities of simplifying
the boundary conditions in general and for the shear modes in particular are investigated, and the
calculation procedure is sought to be clari’¼üed in order to create an accessible calculation tool.
A new ’¼ünite element, for the discretisation of a 3D beam slice, is developed in order to achieve
cubic representation of the displacement ’¼üelds in the lengthwise direction, by use of 4-point La-
==PAGE:13==
3
grange interpolation. The choice of cubic interpolation in the lengthwise direction is made in order
to achieve exact representation of the lengthwise displacement ’¼üelds of the beam slice, allowing
the analyse, of deformation modes to be performed using only one element in the longitudinal
direction. The new ’¼ünite element is implemented in the FEM-program, developed for isotropic
beam elements in the NL-Solid package [15], programmed in MATLAB. The program is further
extended to support general anisotropy in order to analyse more complex beam structure consisting
of composite materials with various ’¼über lay-ups. In correspondence with the calculation procedure
developed by Couturier et al., six linear independent deformation modes will be established for
a discretisation of the 3D beam slice in order to obtain internal force distributions corresponding
to six considered equilibrium states of the beam slice. The deformation modes are established by
enforcing constraints between the two end sections of the discretized beam slice through the in-
troduction of Lagrange multipliers. The dimensions of the Lagrange multipliers are then analysed
in order to determine the nodal force distributions corresponding to the established deformation
modes. Having established the six deformation modes and found the corresponding nodal forces,
the elastic energy equivalence for the 3D beam slice is considered, and used in the determination of
the full 6 by 6 ’¼éexibility matrix and ultimately the cross-section sti’¼Ćness matrix which eventually
could be used for further 1D beam analysis.
The advantages of the presented method for determination of the cross-section sti’¼Ćness components
lay in particular in the avoidance of any special 2D theory for the stress and strain distributions
and in the simplicity of the calculation procedure. Moreover the use of 3D element allow the user
to de’¼üne anisotropy and material discontinuities more directly.
In the last part of the thesis a number of calculation examples are carried out in order to highlight
the use and validity of the developed calculation tool. Moreover a user guidance and presentation
of the general program structure can be found in the Appendix - 8, which serves the purpose of
making the program more accessible for the reader to use and eventually develop.

The new cross-section analysis procedure builds on the discretisation of the beam by a three-

of two end nodes and two midsection nodes in the x1-direction. In the cross-sectional x2x3-planes
the quadratic variation of the displacement ’¼üelds are achieved by 3-point Lagrange interpolation

cross-sectional plane this misrepresentation vanishes. This issue was ’¼ürst dealt with by Watchpress
in [21] and have later been investigated in [17]. In [17] the convergence of respectively a 8-node
and 9-node quadratic element has been investigated, where it was found that both elements gave
rise to exact solutions to a simple beam problem, when non-distorted elements were used. When
the element were distorted the 8-node element contrarily lead to quite wild stress ’¼éuctuation and
misrepresentation of the center line de’¼éection, while the use of the 9-node element still gave ex-
act results. In order to avoid the misrepresentation and achieve smooth solution for all types of
modelled cross-sections it have been chosen to include the 9ŌĆÖth center node in the element.

Constraint Load Cases -
Deformation Modes
By enforcing six sets of constraint load cases to the established FEM beam model, using the
Lagrange method of under-determined multipliers, it is the aim to establish six linear independent
deformation modes. The six deformation modes are established in order to achieve a well known
distributions of the internal forces, corresponding with the internal force distributions of the earlier
presented six equilibrium states. In chapter 2 the energy equivalence between the external work
done by the nodal loads and displacements, and the internal elastic energy of the beam was outlined.
From this the beam ’¼éexibility matrix H was further derived on the condition that the six load
cases, leading to the internal force distributions corresponding to the six equilibrium states, was
achieved.
This will here be used for the derivation of the ’¼éexibility matrices of varies beams
by formulating a general way of establishing those six load cases from the consideration of their
corresponding deformations.
The considered six deformation modes are denoted as a case of homogeneous extension, two cases
of homogeneous shear, a case of homogeneous twist and at last two cases of homogeneous bending.
In the following the six deformation modes are described in terms of the relations between the two
beam end sections displacements, which later will be used as the deformations to be imposed to
the FEM beam model.
In the following the Greek subscripts ╬▒, ╬▓ = 2, 3 allowing repeated summation over repeated Greek
subscripts are used for achieving a more compact notation. A permutation symbol e╬▒╬▓ is further
used, which takes the following values based on the indices e23 = 1, e32 = ŌłÆ1 and e22 = e33 = 0.
5.1
Rigid Body Constraints
As just stated it is the aim to establish the six deformation modes primarily through general
formulations of the relations between the displacements of the two beam ends. These formulations
therefore has to include the displacements to be imposed as well as the constraints needed in order
to prevent the beam from performing rigid body motions. It is attempted to formulate general and
similar constraints, for the prevention of rigid body motions, for as many of the six deformation
modes as possible. The beam is positioned in a 3D space, which means that it can perform rigid
body translation along each of three axis and rigid body rotations about each of the three axis,
giving a total of six possible rigid body motion performances to be constrained.
It has been chosen to describe the rigid body motion constraints for the deformation modes exten-
sion, twist and bending, and the shear modes separately, since the shear modes di’¼Ćers from the
other deformation modes due to the non periodicity of the displacements and the couplings with
the bending moments.

Conclusion
The main aim of the present thesis was the reformulation of the calculation procedure developed
by Couturier et al.[11] from a procedure based on ’¼ünite elements with Hermitian lengthwise in-
terpolation to a procedure based on ’¼ünite elements with 4 point Lagrange interpolation in the
lengthwise direction. The method is based on the consideration of six load cases associated with
six deformation modes of homogeneous extension, shear, twist and bending established for a 3D
slice of the analysed beam, discretized by ’¼ünite elements. The six deformation modes are estab-
lished by enforcing constraints by use of the Lagrange method of under-determined multipliers,
and the possibilities of simplifying those constraints, has further been investigated, in order to
achieve a more accessible calculation tool for the determination of cross-section properties.
The ’¼ürst step lay in the development of a ’¼ünite element to create base for the remaining estab-
lishment of the calculation procedure. The number of nodes in the longitudinal direction were not
for discussion since exact cubic representation of the lengthwise displacement ’¼üelds were an initial
demand, claiming 4 nodes in lengthwise direction when using 4-point Lagrange interpolation. In
the transverse directions several node con’¼üguration were investigated, leading to both linear and
quadratic transverse displacement ’¼üeld representations, where the last were chosen for obtaining
more exact representations of displacement ’¼üelds with the use of fewer elements. The ’¼ünal outcome
given by the present ’¼ünite element, has 36 nodes divided out on four transverse sections, where
the last 9ŌĆÖth node in each transverse section were included in order to avoid possible distortion of
an element not having full quadratic interpolation of the transverse sections.
The second step of the developed calculation procedure were the formulation of the deformations
to be imposed to the discretized beam slice in order to achieve internal force distributions cor-
responding to the six states of static equilibrium.
Several possible solution were initiated and
investigated, since the secondary aim of the thesis were the simpli’¼ücation of the constraints to be
enforced for the establishment of the deformation modes. It was found most convenient to enforce
the constraints mainly on the two beam end sections, after attempts of establishing formulations
based on volume preservation. Attempts were further made by imposing deformations through
the sum of the displacements and rotations of the end sections, which were found possible for the
most simple deformation cases of extension and bending, but problems arose for the shear and
twist deformation modes due to the parabolic distributions of the nodal forces, which couldnŌĆÖt be
restored from the Lagrange multipliers, since only one multiplier were evaluated for each imposed
deformation making it able of restoring only constant or linear nodal force distributions. It was
therefore found, that the deformations were to be imposed by de’¼üned increments between each
corresponding nodal degree of free at the two end sections, giving rise to Lagrange multipliers for
each corresponding degrees of freedom, from which exact nodal force distributions can be evaluated.
The shear deformation modes gave rise to extensive complications due to the natural coupling with
the bending moments. After attempts of imposing average transverse displacement increments and
average rotations, giving rise to ill-conditioned equation systems, it was ’¼ünally found necessary to
express the shear deformation modes as a combination of transverse displacement increments plus
the contribution from the in-plane contractions due to the linear variation of the bending moments.
The shear modes coupling to the bending moments further made it necessary to ensure orthogo-
nality to the remaining four deformation modes, which were accomplished in terms of the shear
deformations not to produce work with respect to the force distributions of the four deformation
modes of homogeneous extension,twist and bending.

Conclusion
The ’¼ünal outcome, of the analysis of di’¼Ćerent possible constraints enforced for the establishment
of the six deformation modes, is found to closely relate to the constraints established by Couturier
et al.[11]. The present developed procedure however di’¼Ćers through the attempts of keeping the
procedure of enforcing constraints as simple as possible, by no reorganisations of the equation
systems, and thereby only the establishment of speci’¼üc relations between corresponding nodal
degrees of freedom at the two end sections.
The new calculation procedure for determination of cross-section sti’¼Ćness properties for straight
beams of any anisotropic material and arbitrary cross-section geometry has successfully been devel-
oped. The procedure based on the consideration of a 3D slice of the investigated beam, discretized
by one layer of ’¼ünite elements with 4-point Lagrange lengthwise interpolation, is found advanta-
geous in comparison to earlier developed 2D cross-section analysis procedures, due to the avoidance
of the establishment of any 2D theory for the stress and strain distributions. When one has estab-
lished the displacement ’¼üelds of the six deformation modes leading to internal force distributions
corresponding to the six states of static equilibrium, the rest of the analysis simply lay in the
consideration of the energy equivalence for the six corresponding load cases. Moreover the 3D
representation of the analysed cross-section simpli’¼ües the process of discretisation of any arbitrary
cross-section geometry and material compositions with for instance several layers of arbitrary ’¼über
lay-up orientations.
It has been shown that the sti’¼Ćness components evaluated for simple isotropic rectangular cross-
sections show clear quadratic convergence towards analytically found solutions, when an increasing
numbers of elements in transverse directions are used in the beam slice discretisation. Further it
was shown for beams of more complex geometries and material compositions that the sti’¼Ćness
components accurately can be evaluated from beam slice discretisations consisting of relatively few
’¼ünite elements in the transverse directions. This ease the mesh generation and make the procedure
suitable for design space exploration, where for instance several ’¼über lay-up sequences rapidly can
be investigated for ’¼ünding an optimal structural design with the maximum intentional strengths.
At last the developed calculation procedure has been programmed in the open programming lan-
guage Matlab and made available for the reader to use and develop. Natural extensions of the
present program could lay in the implementation of more types of elements with 4-point Lagrange
interpolation in the lengthwise direction, in order to ease the mesh generation and representation
of complex cross-sections further. An additional extension could further be the implementation
of the evaluated cross-section properties, from the present calculation procedure, in a 1D beam
analysis, allowing analysis of full 3D beam structures. This could further be widen out to include
inertial loads associated with dynamic problems, for execution of non-static analysis.

Appendix - SABS-Properties
A calculation procedure for determination of cross-section properties for beams with arbitrary
cross-sections and material compositions has been developed. The present thesis contains a detailed
assessment of the theory and numerical solution method used for the development of the procedure.
It has been intended to make the developed calculation tool accessible, allowing the reader to use
and eventually further develop the present program. This appendix therefore serves the purpose of
being and extensive guidance to the developed program in order to clarify the di’¼Ćerent calculation
steps and program functions.
The programming of the calculation procedure is based on the program structure contained in
the program toolbox NLSolid presented and developed by Nielsen et al., for which a detailed
description and user guidance can be found in [15].
Some functions contained in the NLSolid
toolbox have been led unchanged while others have been slightly modi’¼üed in order to serve the
present purpose. New functions and script ’¼üles have further been added to the toolbox while some
former functions and script ’¼üles have been removed since they served no purpose in the present
calculations. The content of the new program has been added in a new toolbox folder named SABS-
Properties(Straight Anisotropic Beam Section - Properties), for avoidance of any confuses regarding
the content of the former NLSolid Toolbox and the new developed SABS-Properties Toolbox. The
new toolbox is divided into three folders containing respectively driver ’¼üles, example ’¼üles and tool
’¼üles. An overview of the di’¼Ćerent programs and script ’¼üles together with a short description are
